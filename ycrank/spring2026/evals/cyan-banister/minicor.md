# Minicor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are all shipping computer-use capabilities in their foundation models right now, and Minicor's own website acknowledges the 80–85% click accuracy these models already achieve. Minicor claims 93–96% — a meaningful gap today, but one that narrows every time a foundation model company pushes an update. This is the defining tension of Minicor's existence. Every infrastructure bet I've made — Crusoe, Substrate, SpaceX — involved building something that got *harder* to replicate over time. Crusoe's portable data centers in North Dakota oil fields couldn't be copy-pasted by a software update. Minicor's core agent capability lives on top of models that the companies supplying them are actively improving. The managed layer — self-healing orchestration, SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA compliance, observability — is real, but it's a thinner moat than physical infrastructure. When I run the ten-year question on Minicor, I see a plausible future where foundation model accuracy hits 98%+ and the orchestration layer becomes a feature inside the model provider's own platform offering. That's a structural risk that no amount of execution can fully outrun.

Faizaan Chishtie and Saheed Akinbile met at the University of Ottawa, co-founded Software For Love together, and share the kind of long-standing co-founder relationship I look for — they've been building alongside each other for years, not assembling a team for a market opportunity. Faizaan has prior YC experience through Phoenix (W21) and grew Homeboard Technologies to mid-five-figure ARR. Saheed's journey from Lagos to senior engineering roles at Ford and Morgan Stanley demonstrates genuine resourcefulness. But when I ask my first question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I'd hear is a professional pivot, not a biographical forge. Minicor exists because Laminar (their prior company, legal entity still Laminar Run, Inc.) encountered a customer who needed to connect to an on-premise Windows-based medical record system with no API. The problem found them through building, which is better than spreadsheet market analysis. But "we were building integrations and hit a wall" is not the same as a founder who lived inside the broken system and emerged unable to ignore it. Neither founder appears to have been trapped by a legacy EHR, crushed by an opaque vendor lock-in, or personally scarred by the system Minicor attacks. The forge is professional, not biographical.

Legacy desktop software vendors — Epic, Cerner, CDK Global, SAP — represent one of the most genuinely opaque, extractive systems in the American economy, and Minicor's website explicitly notes that some vendors are *actively restricting* third-party API access. Healthcare providers, auto dealers, logistics companies are locked inside these walled gardens with no programmatic way to get their own data out. This is the invisible system pattern I respond to most viscerally — gatekeepers exploiting information asymmetry against people who have no alternative. Minicor is picking the lock on these gardens, and that structural instinct earns real points in my framework. But here's where I pause: Minicor's customers are AI companies who need to read/write data in their end-customers' legacy systems. The power shift goes from legacy vendors to AI intermediaries, not directly to the healthcare workers or patients trapped inside Epic. Affirm made lending terms transparent *to the borrower*. Carta made cap tables accessible *to the startup founder*. Minicor makes legacy systems accessible *to other technology companies*. The invisible system is real, but Minicor's relationship to the people inside it is mediated by an additional layer.

UiPath (NYSE: PATH), Automation Anywhere ($6.8B+ raised), Skyvern, Induced AI, Browserbase ($40M Series B at $300M valuation) — Minicor operates in a well-capitalized, consensus-attractive space. AI automation is one of the most eagerly funded categories in venture right now. The specific niche of native Windows desktop and Citrix automation is unglamorous enough to deter the browser-focused players like Skyvern and Induced AI, and I appreciate the plumbing-level focus. But this is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs. Nobody at a dinner party would be uncomfortable hearing about Minicor the way they'd be uncomfortable hearing about Anduril (defense AI) or Contraline (male birth control). The 300-investor-pass test doesn't apply here — this is a category where capital is flowing freely and the competitive landscape is crowded. Minicor's SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certifications are meaningful barriers at pre-seed — compliance is expensive and time-consuming — and the 25,000 patients/day customer with six logos on the website shows production-grade reliability. These are real signals. But compliance moats are achievable, not compounding. Every well-funded competitor in the space will eventually get SOC 2 and HIPAA if the market demands it.

The strongest bull case for Minicor is a Flexport-style bet: attacking an opaque, locked-down system (legacy desktop software) with modern technology at precisely the moment foundation models crossed the usability threshold for computer-use automation (Anthropic launched computer use October 2024). The 25,000 patients/day customer proves the technology works in production. Faizaan's prior YC experience and Saheed's enterprise engineering background at Ford and Morgan Stanley — both target verticals — provide genuine founder-market fit. The Known Exception I'd check is RTFKT: can "cultural inflection point timing" substitute for infrastructure-grade defensibility? But Minicor isn't a cultural moment — it's enterprise infrastructure, and enterprise infrastructure needs compounding moats. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Minicor is building a proprietary data asset from the workflows it executes — a corpus of desktop UI patterns, vendor-specific interaction models, or workflow telemetry that compounds with each customer and cannot be replicated by foundation model companies training on general web data. If Minicor's 25,000 patients/day generates a data flywheel that makes its agents structurally better than raw foundation model computer-use, and that gap *widens* rather than narrows with each model update, that changes the entire thesis.

Minicor lands in Neutral territory for me. The invisible system angle is the strongest dimension — legacy vendors actively restricting API access and trapping their customers is the kind of structural opacity I respond to from my own experience navigating systems that exploited people without power. But the biographical forge is absent (professional pivot, not personal inevitability), the space is consensus-attractive rather than contrarian, and the commoditization risk from foundation model companies is the central structural concern that overrides the genuine strengths. The two dimensions most decisive: Founder's Biographical Forge (no evidence of biographical inevitability connecting either Faizaan or Saheed to this problem domain) and Infrastructure-Grade Defensibility (active commoditization pressure from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google on the core capability).
