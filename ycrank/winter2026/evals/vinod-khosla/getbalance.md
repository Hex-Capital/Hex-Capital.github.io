# Balance -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's my problem with Balance: nobody thinks this is a bad idea. Digits has raised $100 million. Finally has $305 million. Pilot has $325 million. Truewind, Puzzle, Haven -- the list goes on. Every well-funded fintech team on the planet has noticed that LLMs can categorize transactions and that accountants are expensive. When Cisco's CTO told me they'd never build a TCP/IP router, I knew Juniper was onto something. When the food industry laughed at plant-based meat, I knew Pat Brown had found a real opening. The signal I look for is experts dismissing the idea as impossible. With AI accounting, the experts are *funding* it. Balance is arriving at a party where the room is already full of people holding $100 million checks.

The consequence of success here is what stops me cold. If Balance wins -- wins completely, takes everything -- what happens? SMBs pay less for bookkeeping. Financial statements get produced faster. Tax filings happen more smoothly. That's a real business. Pilot already does $27 million in revenue serving a thousand customers doing roughly this. But nobody's life is fundamentally different. The accounting industry doesn't become obsolete; it gets automated at the bottom end. Compare this to what gets me out of bed: fusion energy replacing fossil fuels, AI systems that match a doctor's diagnostic capability, plant-based proteins that could reclaim billions of acres of farmland. Making reconciliation 80% cheaper is a fine outcome for the founders. It is not a consequence worth the effort of the world's best talent.

The technology assessment reinforces my concern. The dossier states it plainly: "LLM capabilities for categorization and reconciliation are accessible to any well-funded team." Balance's AI agent "Bea" runs on the same foundation models available to every competitor in this space. The "AI plus human review" architecture is already implemented by Haven, which hit $1 million in revenue and profitability at pre-seed. There is no technology here that makes the incumbent approach structurally unviable. Traditional accounting firms can license the same LLMs. Intuit could bolt this capability onto QuickBooks tomorrow -- they don't because entering services would create channel conflict with their accounting firm partners, not because the technology is out of reach. That structural gap between software and services is real, but it's a business model observation, not a technology moat.

To be fair to the bull case: the team has genuine founder-market fit. Mathias was a CFO at a YC company and founded an accountancy himself -- he has lived inside this problem. Gus built production AI systems at Orbital Witness with Oxford NLP research behind him. Emil shipped product at two YC companies and has McKinsey rigor. The founding team maps cleanly to the three competencies this venture needs: accounting domain knowledge, AI engineering capability, and startup operations. If the question is "can this team build a functioning AI accountancy that serves SMBs profitably?" -- yes, almost certainly. Haven proved the model works at small scale. The multi-jurisdiction play across Denmark, the US, and the UK is ambitious and could create real switching costs if they crack cross-border tax automation. And the conversational interface through Slack and WhatsApp is a genuinely different UX from dashboard-centric competitors. If you believe that execution quality and geographic breadth can differentiate in a crowded market, Balance has the ingredients.

But that's exactly the problem. Everything I just described is a competent team executing a well-understood playbook in a consensus market. Mathias is an industry insider -- a former CFO and accountancy founder -- which means he knows what the industry considers impossible and is likely constraining his ambition accordingly. The team hasn't done anything the experts reject. They haven't attempted something that made investors look at them funny. They've reduced the probability of failure -- pick a proven model, apply available LLMs, add human review for quality, serve a known market -- to the point where success means a moderately successful professional services firm with technology leverage. The money doesn't expire or produce a transformational outcome. It produces a middle-ground business, and that middle ground is precisely what I structure my portfolio to avoid.

The services scaling constraint seals it for me. "Human accountants review and sign off on all outputs and file tax returns" -- this means hiring qualified accountants in every jurisdiction they operate. Their gross margins are structurally tethered to labor costs. Pure software scales to 85% margins. An AI-augmented services business might reach 60% if the automation is exceptional. This is a fundamentally different economic structure than the companies I back. When I invested in Square, the technology replaced the POS terminal entirely -- no human stood between the merchant and the payment. Balance still needs the human in the loop, and that human needs professional qualifications in each country's tax code. That's not a flaw in their execution; it's a structural constraint of their model.
