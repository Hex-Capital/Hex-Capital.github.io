# Stilta -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I can't get past: five competitors raised between $6.5 million and $55 million in the last twelve months in this exact space. Solve Intelligence already has eight-figure ARR and 400+ customers. &AI — also YC-backed — is building a nearly identical agentic patent search product with $6.5 million from First Round. There are 25+ AI patent tools on the market right now. When I look at this landscape, I see the definition of consensus. Every investor and their partner has already decided that AI for patent work is a category. The pricing power has been competed away before Stilta even gets to scale. My whole career has been built on finding the deals nobody else wants — SpaceX when private space was considered delusional, Contraline when male contraception made VCs squirm. This is the opposite of that. This is a crowded table where I'd be paying full price for a seat everyone already wants.

The founder story is where I always start, and it's where my concern deepens. Four engineers from McKinsey and QuantumBlack, top Swedish universities, Goldman Sachs, math competitions. These are obviously accomplished people — Oskar Block bootstrapped two sports betting companies to over a million in ARR, which tells me he can sell and execute. But when I ask my core question — "Tell me your story, not the business. How did you get here?" — I don't hear a biographical thread that connects these founders to patent law. I don't see a patent attorney on the team. I don't see someone who spent years drowning in prior art searches and thought "there has to be a better way." What I see is consultants who identified a large addressable market through their professional network and built a product for it. That's a perfectly valid way to start a company, but it's not how the founders I back tend to find their problems. The founders I write checks for are usually solving something that kept them up at night long before they saw a TAM slide.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. Patent search is genuinely complex — it's a high-stakes, regulated domain where getting it wrong has material financial consequences, and where the audit trail requirements create a meaningful bar that general-purpose AI tools can't clear. That's the kind of hard space I normally get excited about. The McKinsey and QuantumBlack backgrounds mean this team knows how to build and deploy enterprise AI systems for exactly the kind of large organizations that buy patent tools. They already have paying customers on two continents. If I squint, I can see a version of this that rhymes with Carta — taking an opaque, spreadsheet-driven process and making it transparent and auditable. And the team's pre-existing working relationships from McKinsey should help them navigate the inevitable founder stress of a four-person startup. If everything breaks right — if they out-execute in a market that's big enough for multiple winners, if their agentic architecture creates switching costs before competitors catch up — this could be a solid enterprise SaaS company.

But "solid enterprise SaaS company" isn't what makes me write a check from my own money. What's missing is the thing I can't quite put into words but always feel when it's there — that jolt. The sense that this company is doing something nobody else sees yet, something that looks a little bit crazy, something that connects to a founder's deepest conviction about how the world should work. Stilta is building efficiency tools for well-paid patent attorneys at leading firms. It doesn't create economic access for people who lack it. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to those who've been excluded. It makes an expensive system faster for those who can already afford it. That's a business — it's just not my kind of bet. The "Cursor for patent practitioners" framing says it plainly: this is mapping an existing popular metaphor onto a new vertical. That's the opposite of magically weird.

The four co-founders sharing McKinsey as a common employer is actually one of the stronger signals here — they've worked together, they understand each other's styles, and that matters when things get hard. But I've also seen that four-way founder dynamics create decision-making complexity, especially when there's no clear biographical reason why one person is the visionary and the others are supporting that vision. Oskar is CEO, but the team reads as a group of peers who decided to start a company together, not a founder with a burning mission who recruited the team he needed. I'd want to understand how they resolve disagreements before I'd get comfortable.

I'm passing on this one. Not because the team isn't capable — they clearly are — but because everything about this company signals consensus: the market is crowded and well-funded, the approach maps an existing paradigm onto a validated category, the founders found the problem through professional analysis rather than personal necessity, and the product serves an already well-resourced customer base. I invest my own money in the things that look wrong to everyone else but feel right to me. Stilta looks right to everyone. That's exactly when I step back.
