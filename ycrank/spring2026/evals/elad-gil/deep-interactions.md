# Deep Interactions -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Deep Interactions is a research-credentialed Oxford HCI team selling a thin multi-stakeholder layer into a category where Lovable just added **$100M of revenue in a single month** with 146 employees (TechCrunch, Mar 2026) and Replit just closed **$400M at $9B post** (Mar 2026) -- so the analytical signature here is "weakest on index position, least bad on single-miracle isolation," which is the shape of a feature, not a company. Sruthi Viswanathan's "12 businesses live, 50K+ usage hits" sits next to Lovable's $400M ARR run-rate as the dossier's most load-bearing comparison, and that ratio is the entire evaluation.

**Anduril** (2022) is the high rung of my market re-opening ladder because Ukraine was a *specific, named, 24-month* catalyst that re-priced defense procurement. Deep Interactions' "why now" reduces to "95% of businesses abandon their AI pilots" -- which is a generic pain statement, not a catalyst. A catalyst names what *changed* in the last 24 months; pilot abandonment is a steady-state failure rate of enterprise software, true in 2018 and true now. Lovable, Bolt, Replit, and v0 are themselves the catalyst for this category, and Deep Interactions is downstream of *their* re-opening, not the author of one. Compare to **Decagon** (2023), which I funded because Whisper + GPT-4 was a named capability step-function that re-priced the customer-support stack within an 18-month window -- Deep Interactions has no equivalent. The dossier's catalyst slot is empty.

**Stripe** (2010) is the canonical "index on a compounding category" and the relevant contrast for Deep Interactions' lowest dimension. Stripe sits in the workflow path every e-commerce entrant touches; Deep Interactions sits as the #4 or #5 entrant in a workflow path *Lovable, Replit, and Bolt already own*, and the dossier names Replit Agent 4's parallel-agent capability (Replit blog, Mar 2026) as already shipping the multi-agent piece. The low-rung failure mode here is **Lyft** Series C -- a #2 position in a category with declared leaders where you must win share *against* the leaders rather than ride the category. Deep Interactions' "collaborative vs solo prompt" wedge is a feature axis Lovable can copy in a sprint, not a structural moat. The dossier's own risk section concedes this: "Lovable, Replit, and Bolt could each extend into the team/stakeholder workflow." When the bull case for differentiation is something the incumbent can ship in a quarter, the index position is backwards.

Revenue velocity compounds or it doesn't, and on this axis Deep Interactions discloses **12 customers** and **50K usage hits** with no MRR, no MoM, no named enterprise logo, and a Calendly link as the funnel (deepinteractions.ai, 2026). The 18-month mechanism: either those 12 design partners convert to paid contracts averaging $50K+ ARR (a $600K base compounding 25% MoM gets you to a real Series A story) or they churn back to Lovable/Replit because the collaboration layer doesn't survive the moment a PM realizes Replit Agent 4 already does parallel agents. In an AI cycle where "if you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss Show #863, April 2026), 12 customers in an 8-month launch window against Lovable adding $100M/month is not slow -- it's a different category of company.

The counterfactual for founder shipping rhythm: Deep Interactions' score would flip a tier if the dossier named even *one* shipped artifact -- a Sruthi side project on GitHub, a Neil research demo with usage, a Beni shipped product at Virgin Media O2 Moonshots beyond a title. Instead the dossier surfaces "no public repos found" for both technical co-founders and lists Sruthi's history as Oxford DPhil candidate, UX Research Scientist at Naver Labs Europe, UX Researcher at Google India -- four research/UX titles, zero named shipped consumer artifacts. **Perplexity**'s Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with what we'd discussed already built; that's the shipping signature I underwrite. A team of three Oxford-affiliated researchers and an ex-CEO of an early-stage fintech is the *opposite* archetype -- thoughtful, well-credentialed, slow to ship.

Single-miracle is mechanically Deep Interactions' least bad dimension because LLM code-generation accuracy *is* a validated capability (Lovable's $400M ARR proves it). The problem: Deep Interactions stacks a second miracle on top -- a "context layer" synchronizing humans + agents + codebase across Slack, Gmail, Chrome, Miro, Figma, GitHub, Vercel, Supabase, and CLI -- which is integration complexity that compounds adversely with every customer's idiosyncratic toolchain. **Color Genomics** (2015) worked because I structured it as workflow on commodity sequencing -- the chemistry was solved, the workflow was the bet. Deep Interactions structures itself as workflow on solved code-gen, which is the right pattern, but the workflow itself requires nine integrations to deliver on the launch claim, and that's a multi-miracle GTM execution problem masquerading as a product wedge.

My 36-month forecast: Deep Interactions is acqui-hired by Lovable, Figma, or Notion for under **$30M** by Q2 2028, or quietly winds down -- the Oxford HCI research depth is the asset, the multi-stakeholder layer becomes a Lovable feature within 12 months, and Sruthi's team ends up running collaboration product inside an incumbent. This is the standard fate for #4-or-later entrants in a category where the leaders are compounding at the **Lovable** rate, and it's consistent with the high/low signature -- a defensible team without a defensible position.

The strongest bull case: Deep Interactions could be the **Decagon** of internal AI app development -- a workflow infrastructure layer in a category the dossier correctly identifies as having no real multi-stakeholder competitor, with Tom Blomfield as YC partner (a meaningful signal), and Sruthi's Naver/Google UX research depth being exactly the discipline required to design a humans-in-the-loop layer that Lovable's solo-dev DNA can't natively build. That case stings because Decagon also looked like "a feature" until enterprise customer-support buyers started paying for orchestration. The dossier evidence that wins for me: Decagon at this stage was already showing named enterprise logos and contract sizes; Deep Interactions discloses neither, against incumbents with 100x the distribution. The verifiable flip condition: Deep Interactions hits $1M ARR with three named Fortune 500 logos by end of 2026 -- at that point the "collaboration wedge" is "just working" rather than "false signal" (TechCrunch, Nov 2025), and I'd reconsider at the Series A.
