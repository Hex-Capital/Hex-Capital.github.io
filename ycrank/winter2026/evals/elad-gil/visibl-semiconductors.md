# Visibl Semiconductors -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Visibl is the structural conflict sitting at the center of the EDA market. Synopsys, Cadence, and Siemens EDA collectively control roughly 75% of the market, and each is investing in AI capabilities within their own tool suites. But none of them can build a cross-tool coordination layer -- because Synopsys building something that orchestrates Cadence outputs (and vice versa) would undermine each company's strategy of capturing the full design flow within its own ecosystem. That structural conflict creates a genuine opening for a neutral third party to own the integration layer across competing toolchains. This is the dynamic that created Bloomberg Terminal in financial data: competing banks couldn't build neutral infrastructure, so a third party did. When I see incumbent structural conflict preventing the obvious solution, I pay attention.

But the market is the question. "AI for chip design" is no longer non-obvious -- ChipAgents has raised $74 million and is deployed at 50 semiconductor companies. Silimate is generating revenue with enterprise customers. Chipmind, Maieutic, Synopsys DSO.ai, Cadence Cerebrus -- the list goes on. This has moved from "too boring for most investors" to consensus venture bet inside of 18 months. The specific "coordination layer" framing is somewhat differentiated from the point-tool approach most competitors are taking, but it's a conceptual distinction, not a proven market category. Nobody is paying for chip design coordination today -- the buying behavior doesn't exist yet, and creating a new budget line item inside risk-averse semiconductor procurement organizations is a different game than selling a better verification tool against an established category. The structural moment around LLM capability crossing the threshold for interpreting EDA outputs is real, but it's shared across every company in this space. What made Harvey's timing thesis work was that almost nobody else saw the GPT scaling curve in 2022 -- here, everybody sees it.

The product-to-distribution potential is the most compelling theoretical argument for Visibl. If you own the coordination layer that sits across all EDA tools and ingests the full design context -- code, specs, scripts, tool outputs -- you've created an integration point that could distribute additional products: design analytics, optimization recommendations, compliance tracking, team performance tooling. The platform trajectory from coordination layer to design intelligence infrastructure is structurally similar to how Stripe moved from payments API to Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. But this is purely aspirational at pre-seed -- the founders haven't articulated multi-product distribution thinking in any public materials, and the on-prem deployment model, while appropriate for semiconductor IP security requirements, inherently limits the velocity at which distribution can compound. You can't do self-serve distribution when every deployment requires on-premises installation and integration with proprietary design environments.

On the team: Jordon Kashanchi's credentials are the real signal here. Silicon design at Microsoft Azure Maia, RTL design at Arm, graphics hardware at Intel, MS ECE from UT Austin with integrated circuits specialization -- he's personally lived inside the coordination problem that Visibl is solving. That's the kind of founder-market fit I weight heavily. But the CEO, Bryce Neil, comes from Deloitte's consulting AI practice, not from semiconductor design. In enterprise semiconductor sales, the first five conversations happen with engineering leadership who evaluate whether the founding team understands their workflow deeply enough to be trusted with design IP. Having the CEO without semiconductor domain experience creates friction in those conversations. The domain expertise is concentrated in a single founder, which is fragile.

For the bull case: what would have to be true for this to become a defining company? First, the "coordination layer" would need to prove out as a real product category that semiconductor teams will pay for separately from their EDA tools -- not just a feature that Synopsys and Cadence add to their existing suites. Second, the incumbent structural conflict would need to persist long enough for Visibl to build a customer base before one of the big three decides to build a cross-tool orchestration product anyway (business model conflict doesn't last forever when $6 billion in AI-augmented EDA revenue is at stake). Third, the team would need to close enterprise semiconductor deals with a 2-person team doing on-prem deployments, which is an execution challenge that goes beyond product quality. If all three hold, you get a company that becomes the neutral infrastructure layer for chip design coordination -- a PagerDuty-like outcome where something that seems like a feature becomes deeply embedded enterprise infrastructure. The incumbent conflict makes this more plausible than it appears, but it requires a lot to go right in sequence.

The plan structurally requires multiple sequential breakthroughs: build AI that accurately interprets complex EDA outputs across multiple vendor toolchains, get semiconductor design teams to adopt a coordination tool from a two-person startup, do this through on-prem enterprise sales against a competitor with $74 million and 50 existing customers, and create a new budget category in notoriously conservative procurement organizations. That's not one miracle -- it's three or four. The coordination problem is real and the CTO has credible domain depth, but the gap between a genuine market insight and a fundable company at this stage is too wide for me to write a check from my own money.
