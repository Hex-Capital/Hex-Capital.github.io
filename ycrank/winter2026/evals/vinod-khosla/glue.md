# Glue -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking feature of Glue is not what it's building but what it's building against. Cursor sits at a $29.3 billion valuation with $2 billion in annualized revenue. Lovable raised $330 million in a single round and crossed $200 million ARR. Vercel has $563 million in funding. Figma commands a $12.5 billion valuation. And Glue -- two people, no visible open-source repository despite claiming to be open source, a possible recent pivot from something called "Willow" -- wants to carve out the "visual design iteration layer for coding agents" from this field. The competitive asymmetry here is not just large, it is categorically different from what I look for. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he faced ULA and SpaceX, but he had a genuinely differentiated vehicle architecture and no one else was building small-launch. Glue's "agent-first canvas" concept can be built as a feature sprint by any of five well-funded companies already in the space. That is the difference between attacking an industry and filling a seam in someone else's platform.

Let me apply my first question: if Glue succeeds completely, does the world operate differently? The answer is no. Software developers build frontends more efficiently. The design-to-code handoff gets smoother. That is a workflow improvement, not an industry transformation. The software development tools market is $7 billion -- a perfectly fine market for a venture-backed company, but not the kind of consequence magnitude that justifies a bet with 90% failure probability. Compare this to Impossible Foods targeting $1.7 trillion in animal agriculture, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems targeting the entire fossil fuel energy system. Glue, at its absolute ceiling, is "Figma for the AI era" -- a big business, not a changed world. They have narrowed their scope to a "layer" -- their word -- which is exactly how companies reduce the probability of failure while making the consequences of success manageable. That is the opposite of what I fund.

The timing is the one genuinely interesting signal. AI coding agents are on an exponential curve right now. Claude Code, Codex, Cursor -- the trajectory is unmistakable. The "vibe coding" movement coined by Karpathy in early 2025 is collapsing the traditional EPD workflow. If you believe the primary way software gets built in three years involves AI agents as first-class participants in design, then someone needs to build the surface those agents work on, and building it now -- before the paradigm is consensus -- is the right timing. I used this exact rate-of-change logic when I backed OpenAI in 2019: ignore current performance, watch the slope. Glue is positioned ahead of a real curve. But here is where it breaks down: being early to a concept is not the same as being early to a technology. When I invested in OpenAI, nobody else could write a $50 million check into AGI research -- the barrier was conviction and capital. With Glue, the concept of an agent-first design canvas requires no breakthrough to replicate. Cursor could ship this in a quarter. Lovable could pivot into it. Vercel could extend v0. Being early only matters if you can build a moat before the well-funded players arrive, and I see no evidence of a structural moat forming here.

The founders are competent engineers from Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta. Perbhat Kumar's work on Kindle Fire launcher redesign and Microsoft Copilot Studio RAG agents is relevant. Tejas Priyadarshi's Meta and Azure experience gives solid engineering depth. But this is precisely the profile that concerns me: industry insiders executing a well-understood playbook in a consensus space. Building an AI-powered developer tool in 2026 is not contrarian -- it is the most crowded category in venture capital. Where is the irreverence, the foolish confidence, the outsider attacking a problem the experts dismissed as impossible? Nobody is dismissing AI dev tools. Everyone is funding them. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never work in public telecom, that was my signal to write the check for Juniper. Who is telling these founders their idea is crazy? Nobody -- because it is entirely reasonable. Reasonable ideas in crowded markets produce reasonable outcomes.

The bull case, which I want to engage honestly: if the "builder" role truly replaces the traditional engineering-product-design triad, the canonical design surface for that new paradigm becomes critical infrastructure. Figma's architecture is built for designer-to-designer collaboration, not agent-to-builder workflows. Rebuilding for agent-first interaction may require a ground-up approach that Figma cannot deliver without cannibalizing its existing product. In this world, Glue's early positioning and open-source distribution could create the de facto standard before incumbents react -- the way Linux became infrastructure despite Microsoft's dominance. For this to work, three things must be simultaneously true: the paradigm shift must be real and fast, incumbents must be architecturally unable to adapt, and Glue must ship a compelling open-source product that attracts community contributions before competitors close the gap. The first condition is plausible. The second is uncertain. The third is unsubstantiated -- there is no public repository, no community, no traction data of any kind.

This is a pass. The consequence of success is a good business in a viciously competitive market, not a transformed industry. The founders are capable but not contrarian. The technology is an integration play, not a disruption. The timing is favorable but insufficient to overcome the absence of structural defensibility. I tell founders like this: you are building a feature, not a company. Find the problem underneath this problem -- the one that terrifies you and that the experts say cannot be solved -- and come back.
