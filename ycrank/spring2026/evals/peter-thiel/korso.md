# Korso -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything I need to know about the absence of a secret. Five funded startups -- Paperless Parts with $30 million, Uptool with $6 million from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins -- are building AI quoting and RFQ automation for manufacturers. Add Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI, and you have a market that is a textbook illustration of mimetic rivalry. Every player has converged on the same thesis: frontier LLMs can now extract structured data from manufacturing documents, therefore someone should build a vertical AI tool for quoting. When every venture-backed team in a sector is working from the same insight using the same underlying technology, that is not a secret. That is a consensus dressed in startup clothing. Girard would recognize this instantly -- companies imitating each other's theses, converging on identical product visions, competing their way toward mutual destruction. The structural outcome is predictable: margin compression, feature parity, and a race to the bottom on pricing that no one wins.

What is Korso's secret? I cannot find one. "Manufacturers waste time on manual quoting" is an observation anyone who has walked through a machine shop could make. "LLMs can automate document extraction and pricing" is a capability every AI engineer now understands. The combination of these two facts does not constitute a contrarian truth -- it constitutes a market opportunity that dozens of teams have simultaneously identified, which is precisely the condition under which monopoly becomes impossible. If I asked the founders my standard question -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- and they answered "AI can transform manufacturing operations," I would point out that Khosla Ventures, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins already agree, which means it is no longer a secret. It is a shared bet.

The technology compounds this problem rather than resolving it. Korso is built on RAG-powered AI and "frontier AI research" per the website, but the dossier explicitly notes that "the underlying LLM/RAG technology is broadly available" and that differentiation will depend on domain training depth and integration quality rather than proprietary model architecture. This is 1-to-n work -- applying an existing capability (large language models) to an existing market (manufacturing quoting) -- not 0-to-1 creation. There is no evidence of a 10x technological discontinuity. A RAG pipeline over pricing history and RFQ documents is competent engineering, not a proprietary breakthrough. Uptool can build the same thing. So can any team with access to GPT-4-class APIs and a few months of domain data collection. When the technology is available to everyone, the technology cannot be a moat.

The strongest bull case would require Korso to execute a small-market domination strategy with extraordinary speed -- pick a specific manufacturing vertical (say, CNC machine shops processing WhatsApp RFQs in Southeast Asia), lock in every customer in that niche before competitors notice, accumulate enough pricing data to make the AI genuinely irreplaceable, and then expand concentrically. The WhatsApp ingestion channel is actually interesting here -- it suggests awareness that manufacturing communication in many global markets runs through messaging apps, not email. If Korso deliberately targeted WhatsApp-heavy manufacturing corridors in emerging markets where Paperless Parts and Uptool have no presence, there could be a geographic wedge strategy. The data flywheel is real in principle: each manufacturer's accumulated pricing history and customer relationship data would increase switching costs over time. But I see no evidence that Korso has identified a specific beachhead, no evidence of early traction in any segment, and no evidence that the founders have articulated why their particular niche creates compounding advantages. The go-to-market appears to be generic "Contact Sales" outbound, which is the opposite of monopoly thinking.

The founding team is three recent or current UPenn undergraduates with internship-level experience. Martin Pan's stint at General Motors provides some manufacturing context, and Daichi Hiraoka's ML research on radiation therapy treatment planning shows genuine technical capability. Alex Liu's dropout and claimed research paper consumption signals intellectual seriousness. But I see no evidence of the kind of definite, specific vision that distinguishes a founder who knows exactly what the future looks like from one who is iterating toward product-market fit. Building "the intelligence layer for manufacturing" is a tagline, not a plan. Zuckerberg at 19 could tell you exactly what Facebook would become and refused a billion dollars to protect that vision. I need to see that kind of specificity and conviction here, and I do not.

This is a pass. The thesis is consensus, the technology is commoditized, the market is crowded with better-funded competitors pursuing identical approaches, and the founders -- while technically capable -- have not articulated a secret or a definite plan that distinguishes Korso from the half-dozen other teams building the same product. Manufacturing needs better software. Everyone knows this. Knowing it is not enough.
