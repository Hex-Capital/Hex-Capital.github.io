# Standard Signal -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Michael Royzen is a credentialed hacker — UT Austin Turing Scholar, Phind YC S22, Microsoft Research at seventeen — running solo at a hedge fund that needs SEC registration, qualified custody, and an audited track record before any LP wires $100,000. Standard Signal's signature in my portfolio is the inversion of what usually works for me: high hacker genealogy, zero co-founder, and a regulated-capital problem the founder has never personally suffered. That pairing — strong on the axis I most over-index on, weak on the structural axes the business actually requires — is what I have to evaluate, not the 12ms latency number or the 2,847 asset pairs on the landing page.

On the dimension Standard Signal scores highest — hacker genealogy — the obvious anchor is Patrick Collison, who had won Ireland's Young Scientist prize for Lisp work before **Stripe** (2010) and had already shipped **Auctomatic** (2007). Royzen has the analogous shape: a real prior YC company in Phind, a 150M-search artifact, training runs on "top coding LLMs (2023-2024)." Where Standard Signal diverges from the Collison rung is the GitHub footprint — 24 followers, 10 repos mostly forked from `onnx` and `popper` — which is a much thinner public hacker trail than I'd expect from someone pitching frontier financial LLMs. Phind itself is the load-bearing artifact, not the personal commits. That's enough to clear the hacker bar but not enough to clear it the way the Collisons did.

The lowest-dimension ladder is co-founder configuration, and the failure mechanism I keep returning to is the one I wrote about in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010): solo founders run out of the second brain they need when the work bifurcates. **Airbnb** (2009) needed Chesky for narrative and Blecharczyk for the stack simultaneously during the cereal-box months; if Chesky had been solo, the stunt and the product could not have been run in parallel. Standard Signal is a single founder running a hedge fund — meaning Royzen is simultaneously the model trainer, the LP fundraiser, the compliance owner, and the risk officer, while the two open roles posted (ML Research Engineer, Quantitative Researcher) are employee slots, not co-founder slots. That's the exact configuration I flag as a Red Flag, and the dossier offers nothing to override it.

Organic-idea fit — this is where Standard Signal compounds badly over 18 months. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT; Patrick Collison had hacked commerce flows since he was a teenager. Standard Signal's dossier is explicit: "no public hedge-fund, trading, or quantitative finance employment history found." Royzen did not lose money to a discretionary fund and decide to build an LLM to replace it. The mechanism by which absent founder-problem fit kills companies is that the founder cannot tell when the product is wrong, because they have never lived as the customer; in trading, where the customer is also the loss function, that gap shows up as overfitting to backbacktest narratives rather than market microstructure. 87.3%–94.2% "confidence levels" on the landing page is exactly the language someone uses when they have not personally been stopped out of a position.

Schlep zone — counterfactual: the score flips up a tier only if the dossier had named a registered investment adviser filing, a prime broker relationship, or a named compliance hire. **Coinbase** (2012) cleared the schlep bar because Brian Armstrong was visibly running toward the BSA/AML grind nobody else in his batch wanted; Standard Signal's site lists ML and quant roles, no general counsel, no compliance officer, no custodian named. The schlep is real — autonomous AI executing trades inside an LP vehicle is a regulatory minefield — but Standard Signal has not yet picked it up. Structurally present, behaviorally absent.

Animal resourcefulness pre-funding — the single-anchor parallel is Brian Armstrong, who got rejected from YC and then shipped an Android Bitcoin wallet to overturn the decision. Standard Signal's equivalent pre-funding artifact is… Phind, a different company. There is no cereal-box, no demo of the trading system running on paper money against a public benchmark, no LP soft-circle with a named family office. The launch tweet from @MichaelRoyzen and two open job posts are not animal behavior; they are normal-founder behavior. Royzen passed the animal test once already to build Phind to 150M searches, which is why this isn't a zero, but the animal has not shown up specifically for Standard Signal.

Forecast: in 36 months Standard Signal is not operating as a hedge fund. The mechanism is that LP capital at $100,000 minimums requires either an audited multi-year track record or a brand-name pedigreed trader, and Standard Signal has neither; the YC check and Royzen's Phind reputation get the doors open at family offices but do not close allocations against Citadel and Renaissance. The most likely 2029 state is a pivot to selling the LLM research stack as B2B tooling to existing funds — adjacent to QuantConnect's positioning — at sub-$2M ARR, or quietly wound down with capital returned. **Quantopian** (shut down 2020) is the closest cautionary case in the dossier: a technically credible team that could not produce returns the market would underwrite.

The strongest bull case is the **Loopt** (2005) exception — I funded Sam Altman before mobile location was usable infrastructure because force-of-personality overrode the timing problem. Royzen has the Phind exit-trajectory shape that maps roughly onto Altman's Loopt-to-YC arc, and "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) tells me that the best ideas look initially like bad ones; "a solo founder running an LLM hedge fund" looks exactly that dumb. The bear rebuttal is structural: Loopt was a consumer product where Altman could iterate against users; Standard Signal is a regulated capital pool where the iteration loop runs through SEC filings and LP redemption gates. Animal force-of-personality does not compress fund-formation timelines. The verdict flips to Invest if Standard Signal posts a public, third-party-verified six-month live track record at the stated Sharpe target, or recruits a co-founder with named buy-side risk-management experience before the YC demo day.
