# Ndea -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

François Chollet is probably the single most credentialed human being alive to attempt what Ndea is attempting. He built Keras -- 63.9K GitHub stars, used by YouTube, Waymo, Spotify, ByteDance -- and then built the benchmark that literally defines what it means to measure AGI progress. Then he left Google after nine years as Senior Staff Engineer and said, publicly, that LLMs will never get us to AGI. Mike Knoop co-founded Zapier at YC, scaled it to millions of users, and brings the operational credibility to complement Chollet's research depth. The sane-person-insane-idea calibration here is among the strongest I've encountered. Chollet is maximally sane -- and telling the entire $100B+ AI industry that their architectural bet is fundamentally wrong is genuinely insane. When I backed Solana at $0.05, Anatoly Yakovenko was a Qualcomm systems engineer saying he could build a faster blockchain when most of crypto was in a winter. Chollet has that same structural conviction -- deep technical credibility wielded against consensus.

Here's where my framework starts grinding against this company. I've been saying publicly since 2024 that AI is a bad startup investment -- that the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution, and that incumbents like Apple, Google, and Meta will capture the value. My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? Ndea fails that test completely. The business IS AI. There is no physical-world operation, no installed hardware, no operational complexity that software amplifies but cannot replace. There is no Craftwork-style moat where crews and trucks and scheduling create barriers to entry. Ndea's defensibility is purely intellectual capital -- Chollet's unique understanding of program synthesis, a specialized team, and accumulated tacit knowledge. If program synthesis demonstrates real breakthroughs, Google DeepMind has the formal reasoning expertise and the hundreds of billions in compute to redirect resources toward this paradigm overnight. $43M against that asymmetry is a pebble against an avalanche.

But I have to be honest about the bull case, because it's substantial. If program synthesis is to intelligence what proof-of-stake was to blockchain consensus -- a genuinely new computational primitive that enables an entirely different ecosystem -- then Ndea isn't an AI application. It's a protocol-layer bet. Chollet isn't building a wrapper on GPT. He's proposing that the entire paradigm of continuous neural network training is the wrong abstraction, and that discrete program search is a fundamentally different primitive for machine cognition. His blog says program synthesis in 2025 looks like deep learning circa 2012 -- if that analogy holds, you're looking at a Cambrian explosion in the next few years. And Chollet literally wrote the book on deep learning, so he has more pattern-matching authority on that analogy than anyone. The scenario where Ndea establishes program synthesis as a new layer that others build on -- where its system becomes infrastructure for scientific discovery across drugs, energy, robotics -- that IS an infinity story, and it IS a protocol position. The narrative ceiling is genuinely unbounded in a way that almost nothing else is.

The problem is that the bull case requires everything to go right on the research frontier AND for Ndea to maintain its lead through commercialization -- and there is zero evidence of either yet. No product. No revenue. No pricing model. No go-to-market. Not even a defined path from "research breakthrough" to "commercial value." This is a pure research bet dressed up in startup clothing. The ARC-AGI 2024 competition moved scores from 33% to 55.5%, which is progress, but the target is 85% and the gap between benchmark performance and real-world scientific discovery is enormous. Chollet's own comparison to "deep learning circa 2012" is simultaneously the most exciting and most terrifying framing -- it took deep learning a decade to become commercially dominant, and the companies that won weren't the 2012 research labs but the platforms with distribution that absorbed the technology.

There's also something structurally uncomfortable about a founder who created the benchmark and then started a company to beat it. The ARC Prize Foundation board seats give Chollet and Knoop an unusual position -- they're defining the goalposts and running toward them simultaneously. That's not fraud, but it's a conflict that could undermine the benchmark's credibility as an independent measure, which in turn undermines one of Ndea's key defensibility claims.

I keep coming back to the Birchbox lesson: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome. Ndea has the most magnificent narrative imaginable -- literally building AGI through a paradigm nobody else is pursuing -- and the weakest moat structure I can identify. If this works, it becomes consensus and gets absorbed by players with ten-thousand-times the capital. If it doesn't work, there's nothing underneath it. No revenue floor, no asset cushion, no operational flywheel. I respect the founders enormously and I think the intellectual thesis is fascinating, but my framework says this is a spectator sport for me, not an investment.
