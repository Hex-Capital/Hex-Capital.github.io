# Foaster -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out immediately: "AI-native consulting firm" is possibly the single most consensus startup idea in Spring 2026. Harvard Business Review ran pieces last fall about consulting firms cutting graduate hiring because AI automates junior analyst work. McKinsey has its own internal AI tools. Distyl AI has $202M in the bank doing forward-deployed AI engineering. When the entire industry is already moving in your direction, you're not pursuing an insane idea -- you're pursuing the most obvious idea in the room. My entire framework starts with finding a sane person doing something most people think is crazy, and I cannot find the crazy here. "Let's use LLMs to do consulting interviews cheaper and faster" is what every second-year MBA wrote in their applications essay this cycle.

The founders have real technical chops -- I'll give them that. Combes building Repondia and handling 60,000 AI-conducted calls is directly relevant operational experience, if it's real (no independent web presence or press to verify, which nags at me). The Werewolf benchmark getting shared by OpenAI's president and attracting xAI's attention shows these two know how to design and evaluate AI agent interactions. They're sharp ENSAE students who've published work that got millions of views. But sharp AI researchers building an AI consulting product is a "sane person, sane idea" -- it's the most logical thing they could do, which is exactly why it doesn't excite me. The pairing lacks tension. Compare this to Teamshares, where the founding team's operational credibility met an idea that sounded logistically impossible. There's no friction here between founder and idea that generates non-consensus signal.

Now let me run the AI kill test, which is where this really falls apart. My core question is always: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? Foaster's entire value proposition IS AI -- strip it away and you have two 23-year-olds with no consulting industry credentials trying to sell process mapping to enterprises. The business without AI is literally nothing. That's the photographic negative of what I look for. Craftwork is a painting company that happens to use software for dispatch optimization -- the moat is the crews, the trucks, the operational complexity. Foaster is a software product that happens to call itself a consulting firm. The dossier itself notes the core technology stack "uses generally available foundation models." When GPT-6 or whatever comes next makes organizational analysis trivially cheap, what defends Foaster's position? Client-specific operational maps create mild switching costs, but they're siloed per engagement -- no network effect, no compounding data advantage across clients. Every well-funded competitor in that landscape table can build an interview agent. This is features competing against features, not structure competing against structure.

The strongest bull case I can construct: the innovator's dilemma is genuinely operative here. McKinsey cannot offer 3-5x cheaper consulting without destroying its own leverage model -- partners billing out junior analysts at enormous markups is the structural backbone of the entire industry. If Foaster becomes the Vanguard of consulting -- the low-cost disruptor that incumbents can't respond to without self-cannibalization -- the TAM is $300 billion and the land grab is historic. And the "operational map as persistent asset" idea has a germ of something interesting: if you build comprehensive maps of how hundreds of companies actually function, that dataset is unprecedented. No one has that. You could potentially build a primitive -- an organizational intelligence layer that other tools query -- rather than just a consulting service. But that primitive vision isn't what's being built. What's being built is an engagement-based services firm with a "book a call" CTA and founder-led sales. Services businesses scale linearly, not exponentially. Revenue requires proportional engagement delivery. That's not an infinity story -- that's a good small business at best, and at worst it's a feature that Distyl AI adds to their platform next quarter.

Two final structural problems. First, the competitive capitalization gap is alarming: you've got $202M (Distyl), $136M (PromptQL), $101M (Aily Labs), and $57.6M (Skan.ai) already deployed in overlapping territory. A two-person pre-seed team isn't "David versus Goliath" when Goliath has AI capabilities too -- it's an underfunded entrant in a market that's already attracted serious capital. Second, enterprise sales of AI-conducted employee interviews raises data sensitivity issues that require trust, legal sophistication, and compliance infrastructure that a two-person team of 23-year-old engineering students simply hasn't had time to build. This isn't a knock on their intelligence -- it's a structural mismatch between the go-to-market motion and the team's current capabilities.

I pass. The founders are technically talented, but the idea is consensus, the moat is nonexistent by my framework, there's no primitive or protocol position, and the competitive landscape is already capitalized. If the founders pivoted toward building an organizational data primitive -- a protocol-layer standard for how companies represent and share operational knowledge -- I'd look again. But an AI consulting firm in 2026 is to me what a subscription box was in 2014: obvious enough that everyone does it, which means no one defends it.
