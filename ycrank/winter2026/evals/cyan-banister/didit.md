# Didit -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Didit: this is a company born out of a metaverse project. Alberto and Alejandro Rosas built Gamium -- a SocialFi platform with a token -- and Didit was the identity layer they needed for that vision. When the metaverse narrative cooled, the identity piece became the company. Their GitHub org is still named "didit-protocol," the whitepaper still lives at its subdomain, and $GMM token holders were offered airdrops tied to Didit's growth. I don't fault founders for pivoting -- pivots can be powerful -- but when I ask my foundational question, "tell me your story, not the business," I need to hear why *this* problem is the one you'd walk through fire for. What I hear instead is a technically capable team that discovered their side infrastructure was more commercially viable than their main product. That's smart business instinct, not biographical mission. The founders are former professional tennis players who studied mathematics, built ML systems, and worked at Oracle. Those are real credentials. But identity verification isn't their wound -- it's their wedge.

The space itself concerns me more than the founders do. Identity verification is about as consensus as enterprise infrastructure gets. Persona has raised $417 million at a $2 billion valuation. Veriff has raised $200 million at $1.5 billion. Entrust acquired Onfido for $650 million. Sumsub has somehow built $85 million in revenue on just $7.5 million raised. When I look at the companies that have changed my life as an investor -- SpaceX, Crusoe Energy, Contraline -- they were all operating in spaces where other investors actively didn't want to be. Nobody was competing for deal flow in male contraception or mining Bitcoin on gas flares. Didit is entering a ring with four well-funded, well-established fighters, and their primary weapon is price. A free tier and $0.03 per check is aggressive, but it's not a moat -- it's a margin compression strategy that any well-capitalized competitor can match when they decide to. This is the kind of bet where you need to believe the team can out-execute everyone with far less capital, and I don't see the biographical fire that sustains that kind of fight.

The strongest bull case is genuinely compelling, and I want to be honest about that. Seven hundred B2B customers with 20% month-over-month growth and near-profitability on just $2 million raised is exceptional capital efficiency. If those self-reported numbers hold, Didit is tracking toward a Sumsub-like trajectory -- building a real business without burning through venture dollars. The 300% net revenue retention at month six suggests that once businesses integrate, they expand usage dramatically. The no-code workflow engine, MCP server for AI agents, and the breadth of 14,000+ document types across 190 countries represent real infrastructure investment. If I imagine the world where Didit becomes the Stripe of identity -- the default API that every startup integrates because it's free to start and simple to scale -- that's a multi-billion dollar outcome. The LATAM sales presence hints at a smart geographic wedge where legacy providers are weaker and emerging fintechs need affordable KYC. For this to work, Didit needs the traction numbers to be real, the pricing to be sustainable, and Persona and friends to move too slowly downmarket. It's not impossible. It's just not a bet shaped like mine.

The one dimension where Didit genuinely lights up for me is the co-founder dynamic. After HQ Trivia taught me that even the right product at the right time can't survive broken co-founder relationships, I probe this relentlessly. Alberto and Alejandro are identical twins who have been building things together since they were twelve years old. They were professional athletes together, studied mathematics together, co-founded Gamium together, and now run Didit together. That's not a partnership formed in a co-founder matching event -- it's a relationship forged over a lifetime, with conflict resolution patterns that have been stress-tested in ways most startup teams never experience. If this company hits existential crises -- and in a market this competitive, it will -- these two are less likely to fracture than almost any founding team I can think of. The broader team of twelve also shows thoughtful construction: a CFO with finance depth, a full-stack developer with biometric ID experience since 2018, and regional sales coverage.

Ultimately, Didit is a competent team executing well in a crowded, well-understood market. There's nothing unpopular about what they're building, nothing that makes other investors uncomfortable, nothing that requires me to see what nobody else can see. The pricing disruption is smart but not weird. The technology is solid but not breakthrough. The market is large but well-addressed. I invest in the spaces where the consensus hasn't formed yet, where the founders have a personal stake so deep that quitting isn't an option, and where the idea itself makes people slightly uneasy. Didit doesn't trigger any of those signals for me. I respect the execution, I like the twins, and I wish them well -- but this isn't my kind of bet.
