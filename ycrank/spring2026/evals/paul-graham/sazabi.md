# Sazabi -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Sazabi is that "AI observability" is a category where everyone is tripping over each other to throw money. Arize just raised $70M. LangSmith hit a $1.25B valuation. ClickHouse acquired Langfuse. Datadog — a company with $2.1B in annual revenue — is bolting AI features onto its existing platform. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where this much capital has already concentrated, my instinct is to walk away. The best startup ideas are the ones that make smart people say "that will never work." Nobody is saying that about AI observability. They're saying "I want in." That's the opposite of the signal I look for.

But then I look at Sherwood Callaway's trajectory, and something more interesting emerges. He built observability infrastructure at Brex — not as a product manager who read dashboards, but as the engineer who wired the systems together. Then he founded Opkit, one of the first AI phone calling products for healthcare, got it through YC, and sold it to 11x. At 11x he managed the engineering team building Alice, which is described as one of the largest agentic systems in production. So he's not someone who read a TechCrunch article about AI agents and decided there must be a business there. He literally built observability infrastructure, then built an AI product, then managed a large-scale AI agent system, and presumably watched the existing observability tools fail him in real time. That's as clean a "live in the future, then build what's missing" origin story as you'll find. It reminds me of how Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive — except Callaway's version involves two separate careers (infrastructure engineering and AI systems) converging into a single insight.

The specific technical thesis — "logs are all you need" — is where the contrarianism actually lives. Not in the category, which is consensus, but in the architectural claim. The entire observability industry is built on three separate pillars: logs, metrics, and traces, each priced and sold independently. Datadog makes a huge chunk of its revenue by charging separately for each pillar. Callaway is arguing that AI can reconstruct the other two from logs alone — three pillars for the price of one. Most observability engineers I know would call this naive at production scale. And that's exactly the kind of reaction that interests me, because if Callaway is right, every incumbent has a structural conflict of interest in adopting his approach — their billing models depend on the three-pillar architecture being necessary. That's not a technical moat, it's a business model moat, which is rarer and potentially more durable. Whether the thesis is actually correct is an empirical question I can't answer from a dossier, but the shape of the bet is right.

What genuinely concerns me is that this appears to be a solo founder. I've said many times that starting a startup is too hard for one person. The YC page lists only Callaway. He has 10 people on the team but no identified co-founder or technical co-lead. At Brex, at Opkit, at 11x — he worked with other people. Now he's going alone against Datadog, one of the most well-executed enterprise software companies of the last decade? The angel investor roster is impressive — people from Anthropic, Vercel, Brex, Replit, LangChain — and the compliance certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001) show someone who understands what enterprise buyers will demand. But angels and certifications don't substitute for having a co-founder who can argue you out of a bad decision at 2 AM. I've seen too many single founders burn out or make avoidable mistakes to be comfortable here.

If I steelman this: the bull case is that Callaway is a Stripe-shaped opportunity wrapped in a consensus-looking category. Patrick Collison tackled payments infrastructure — a schlep that everyone knew needed fixing but nobody wanted to touch because of PCI compliance and bank integrations. Callaway is tackling observability infrastructure — a schlep where the incumbent (Datadog) has locked in a pricing architecture that structurally prevents it from offering the simpler, cheaper, AI-native approach. The "logs are all you need" thesis, if correct, is the kind of insight that can't be adopted by the incumbent without cannibalizing its core business model. And Callaway has the exact right background to execute on it: he built observability, he built AI systems, he saw where they collide. The 10-person team and compliance certs suggest he's moving fast with purpose. If this thesis plays out, Sazabi could be the company that restructures how engineering teams think about production visibility in an AI-generated-code world.

But I keep coming back to two things: the hot category and the solo founder. The category heat means that even if Callaway executes perfectly, he's competing for the same customers against teams with $70M and $125M war chests. And the solo founder structure means he's bearing all of that pressure alone. I'd feel differently if I saw a technical co-founder with complementary strengths, or if the category were one that repelled capital instead of attracting it. Callaway's founder-market fit is unusually strong — probably in the top decile of what I see at this stage — but fit with the problem doesn't compensate for structural vulnerability in the company itself.
