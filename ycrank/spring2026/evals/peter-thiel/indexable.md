# Indexable -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Indexable is not the technology — it is the competitive landscape. E2B has $43.8 million in funding, 88% of the Fortune 100 as customers, and over 500 million sandbox sessions. Daytona raised $31 million and offers fork-and-snapshot capabilities — the same core value proposition Indexable is building. Modal Labs is valued north of a billion dollars. Fly.io has raised $120 million. Google has open-sourced an agent sandbox for Kubernetes. When I look at this market, I see five or six well-funded players converging on the same thesis — that AI agents need isolated compute environments. This is not a secret. This is consensus dressed in infrastructure jargon. When every smart VC in San Francisco agrees that "agentic infrastructure" is the next big thing, the Girardian alarm should be deafening: mimetic convergence produces exactly the kind of competitive destruction that annihilates returns. The surface of this market resembles the food delivery wars more than it resembles Facebook at Harvard.

And yet. There is something buried beneath the consensus layer that is worth excavating. The deeper thesis is not "agents need sandboxes" — that is the obvious truth E2B has already validated at scale. The potential secret is more specific: that full-state forking of a running virtual machine in under 30 milliseconds — not just filesystem snapshots, but processes, memory, databases, everything — represents a fundamentally different computational primitive, one that enables agent architectures nobody has built yet. If AI agents evolve toward tree-search-style exploration, branching thousands of execution paths to evaluate parallel hypotheses, then the difference between 26 milliseconds and 200 milliseconds is not a performance improvement. It is the difference between feasible and infeasible. At 200ms, you can fork occasionally. At 26ms, you can fork continuously — forking becomes a core operation, like a function call. Version-control semantics on compute state, where every mutation creates a commit and branches are first-class citizens, is not just "a faster sandbox." It is closer to "git for running programs." That is a genuinely new category, if the market demands it.

Andrew Gazelka's technical profile is precisely the kind I take seriously. Hyperion — a Minecraft engine processing 1,000 players in 455 microseconds, targeting 170,000 concurrent players — is not a toy project. It is a demonstration of obsessive systems engineering at the microsecond level, exactly the skill set required to build a custom VMM that outperforms Firecracker by an order of magnitude. His stack reads like a hardware engineer's wishlist: custom VMM on KVM, content-addressable storage engine with cross-tenant deduplication, libfabric transport layer, a custom fork of Nix rewritten in Rust, a modified Linux 6.19 kernel. This is building from the metal up. The xAI experience gives him exposure to production agent infrastructure needs. He has the technical horsepower. What I do not see, however, is a definite vision beyond the technology itself. Where is the specific plan for how this transforms the world? What does Gazelka believe about the future of computation that most people would argue against? The website says "pay for what you use" and offers a waitlist. The go-to-market appears to be generic developer SDK adoption — the same playbook every infrastructure company runs. A website translated into four languages at pre-seed, with no visible traction, suggests broad targeting rather than the disciplined narrowness that monopolies require. Facebook started with Harvard. Stripe started with 7 beta users who were developers at startups. Where is Gazelka's Harvard?

The bull case is real and I want to state it explicitly. If we are entering an era where AI agents do not merely execute tasks sequentially but explore branching decision trees — forking execution state thousands of times per task, evaluating parallel paths, backtracking, diffing outcomes — then Indexable's architecture is not competing with E2B. It is creating a new category that E2B's Firecracker-based architecture literally cannot serve without a complete rebuild. In that world, the competitive landscape is irrelevant because the competition is playing a different game. The Stripe analogy would hold: Stripe was not a "better PayPal" — it was a developer-first payments API that created an entirely new market category. Indexable could be "version-controlled compute" rather than "faster sandboxes." And Gazelka's extreme systems engineering talent — demonstrated by Hyperion, not just claimed — is the kind of founder-technology fit that produces genuine 0-to-1 companies. A three-person team that has already built a custom VMM, a content-addressable storage engine, and a modified Linux kernel is not iterating toward product-market fit. They are building something with conviction.

But the bull case requires multiple things to be true simultaneously. The agent tree-search paradigm must materialize broadly, not just in research demos. The performance gap between 26ms and 90ms (Daytona) must prove decisive rather than merely nice-to-have. Gazelka must find or become the commercial leader who can sell enterprise infrastructure against E2B's installed base. And the company must survive long enough — with YC-scale capital against competitors with $30-120 million — to reach the future it is building for. The technology is genuinely impressive. The secret, if it exists, is narrow and conditional: it depends on a specific evolution of agent architectures that has not yet occurred at scale. What I cannot identify is the kind of structural, self-reinforcing monopoly path that would make this a power-law outcome. Deep technology without clear monopoly dynamics produces valuable companies, but not category-defining ones. The switching costs and data gravity from SDK integration are real but modest — the same moats every infrastructure company claims.

I find myself respecting the technical achievement while remaining skeptical of the market structure. Gazelka has built something genuinely hard — the kind of deep systems work that most engineers cannot do and most startups will not attempt. But building something hard and building a monopoly are different things. The question I keep returning to is: does the world need version-controlled compute badly enough that one company can own the category? Or is this a feature that gets absorbed into broader platforms — Modal, Fly.io, even hyperscalers — once the underlying techniques are understood? The custom VMM creates a real engineering moat, perhaps two to three years of architectural advantage. Whether that is enough time to establish the kind of durable market position that compounds over decades is the bet.
