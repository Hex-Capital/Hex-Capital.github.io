# REV1 -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about REV1 is that the incumbents aren't dismissing this idea — they're already building it. SolidWorks shipped a beta Auto-Generate Drawing command in its 2025/2026 release. When I backed Juniper Networks, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router — that dismissal was the signal. When I invested in Impossible Foods, the food industry thought plant-based heme was absurd. The moment an incumbent validates your approach by building the same feature into their existing platform, you've lost the contrarian advantage entirely. SolidWorks isn't mocking automated drawing generation; they're shipping it. That's not a market signal — that's a competitive death sentence for a two-person pre-seed startup trying to sell into the same enterprise accounts that already pay for SolidWorks seats.

Let me apply my first-order filter: if REV1 succeeds completely — automates every 2D engineering drawing from every 3D CAD model — what is the consequence? Mechanical engineers reclaim 25% of their time currently spent on documentation. That is a real pain point. I don't dispute it — Alex Rivero lived it at Tesla and Apple. But saving engineers time on paperwork does not transform how products are designed, manufactured, or delivered. It does not make an existing trillion-dollar system obsolete. It makes an existing workflow incrementally more efficient. If I'm being honest, this is the archetype of the company I pass on: a team that is too talented to spend their careers making a tedious task slightly faster. The consequence of total success is a nice SaaS business in a narrow sub-segment of the $24 billion CAD market. That is not a consequence worth all the effort.

The competitive picture makes this worse, not better. DraftAid, a YC W24 company, is building the identical product with a two-year head start — 210,000 drawings created, $150,000 in paid pilot contracts. Leo AI has raised $9.7 million and serves Scania, HP, and Mobileye with a broader engineering copilot. REV1 differentiates through a "native review/edit interface and PLM synchronization" versus DraftAid's plugin approach. That is a feature distinction, not a structural advantage. When your differentiation from a direct competitor can be described in a single sentence about UI architecture, you are in a features race, not building a category. And you're in that race while SolidWorks, Siemens NX, and PTC Creo are all adding AI capabilities natively.

The bull case would require REV1 to be a wedge into something much larger — the AI-powered interface between design and manufacturing, replacing not just drawing creation but the entire post-CAD workflow: BOM generation, tolerance stack-up analysis, drawing change management, manufacturing communication. A CAD-agnostic platform that becomes the standard layer between any 3D model and any factory floor. If that were the stated ambition, and if the founders demonstrated they were thinking at that scale, the calculus changes. The multi-CAD angle is genuinely interesting — no incumbent has incentive to support competitors' file formats, so a CAD-agnostic tool could serve environments that run SolidWorks, NX, and Creo simultaneously. That structural insight could be the foundation of a real business. But the dossier describes a team focused on automating 2D drawing generation, not transforming the design-to-manufacturing pipeline. The ambition, as stated, is calibrated to be manageable. And a manageable ambition is precisely what I avoid.

The founders are technically capable — Alex brings direct domain experience from Tesla and Apple, Louis brings ML and platform engineering. The pairing of a mechanical engineer who has personally created hundreds of manufacturing drawings with an ML researcher who can build the automation is logical and well-constructed. But these are industry insiders executing the obvious playbook. Alex saw the pain at Tesla, Louis had the ML skills to solve it, and they're building exactly what you'd expect two Tesla engineers to build. There's no outsider irreverence here, no attempt at something the experts consider impossible. The experts consider this very possible — SolidWorks is already doing it.
