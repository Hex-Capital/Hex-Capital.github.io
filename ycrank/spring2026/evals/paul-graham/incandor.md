# Incandor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Incandor is the profile I see most often and fund least often: a Stanford CS pair attacking a genuine schlep — bank fraud, GDPR-regulated biometric capture, displacement of BioCatch's $185M ARR channel — with no pre-funding artifact and no fingerprint that says they bled on this problem themselves. Matthew Yekell came out of Vista Equity, Cboe, and Stanford Management Company; Luc Rosenzweig came out of NVIDIA, Apple, and Epic Games. That's a finance-buyer-credible CEO paired with a competent systems engineer who has, as far as the dossier shows, never written a line of fraud code in anger. The market is real, the work is unpleasant, and that is exactly the kind of bet where I lose money when the founders are credentialed instead of animal.

On animal resourcefulness, the HIGH rung is Airbnb selling 1,000 cereal boxes at $40 each to fund the company; the MID rung is Drew Houston's Dropbox demo video lighting up Hacker News before the seed check; the LOW rung is the polished MBA AI-wrapper team I keep rejecting because there's no shipped artifact behind the deck. Incandor sits just above LOW: the dossier has zero open jobs on the YC page, no Product Hunt launch, no design partner named, no benchmark backing the ">99% accuracy" claim on incandor.com, no GitHub trail for either founder. Animals leave tracks, and the Incandor track is a Stanford profile page and a LinkedIn that reads "Vista Equity Partners." When the dossier is this silent on what Yekell and Rosenzweig did before the institutional check, I assume they did nothing remarkable, because the founders who do remarkable things tell you about it in the application.

On organic founder-pain, the HIGH rung is Drew Houston forgetting his USB stick at MIT; the MID rung is the Collisons at Stripe who had been hacking commerce flows since Auctomatic at ages 17 and 19; the LOW rung is the spotted-a-market-opportunity AI-wrapper pitch. Incandor sits at LOW. Nothing in Yekell's Vista/Cboe/Stanford-endowment path or Rosenzweig's NVIDIA/Apple/Epic Games path puts either of them inside a bank fraud-ops chair watching mules drain accounts. The category was almost certainly chosen because behavioral biometrics is growing 26.95% CAGR and BioCatch hit $185M ARR — that is competitive analysis, not USB-stick frustration. When I ask "what do you wish someone would make for you?", the honest answer for Incandor founders is probably not "a programmable behavioral map for fraud teams."

Incandor's strongest dimension is the schlep test. The HIGH rung is Coinbase's bitcoin compliance grind that Brian Armstrong actually wanted to do; the MID rung is Stripe's manual integration work the Collisons embraced; the LOW rung is the pure-software wrapper with no offline exposure. Incandor lands between MID and HIGH: continuous keystroke and mouse capture is GDPR-regulated biometric data, bank procurement cycles are slow and political, and the displacement target is BioCatch with Wells Fargo and 90 new 2025 logos plus NuData bundled into Mastercard. This is unpleasant work that mainstream YC applicants in the same batch are routing around for AI agents and dev tools. If anything in the Incandor dossier is going to flip me, it is this — the schlep is named, it is sized, and it is real.

On hacker genealogy, the HIGH rung is Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize at 16 before Stripe; the MID rung is the Reddit hacker dorm of Huffman, Ohanian, and Swartz; the LOW rung is the credentialed-but-non-shipping CS-degree team. Incandor lands at MID-LOW: Stanford CS with NVIDIA/Apple/Epic on Rosenzweig's resume is real systems work, but the dossier explicitly says "No public GitHub repos found" for both founders and no patents, no papers, no open-source. Hackers leave trails, and the absence of one in 2026 is louder than it was in 2007. On cofounder configuration, the HIGH rung is Huffman and Ohanian rooming together at UVA; the MID rung is the Collison brothers; the LOW rung is solo. Incandor sits at MID — two Stanford CS founders, plausibly acquainted through the department, no documented multi-year collaboration. That's the default, not a positive signal.

My 36-month forecast: Incandor reaches $1–3M ARR with two to four mid-market fintech logos, gets out-distributed by Sardine's broader fraud-plus-compliance bundle and BioCatch's bank channel, and exits via acquihire into a mid-tier fraud platform — Sardine, Alloy, or LexisNexis — for $20–60M. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a real schlep market combined with founders who chose the category instead of suffering through it produces companies that build credible technology, win a few design partners on the cold-start pitch, and then run out of the relentless distribution stamina required to displace a $185M-ARR incumbent that already sells to Wells Fargo.

The strongest case against my pass: Incandor's "programmable behavioral map" framing is structurally analogous to Stripe's developer-API framing in a market that BioCatch and NuData treat as a black-box score, and Rosenzweig's systems credentials at NVIDIA and Apple are exactly the latency-and-signal-processing background that label-free behavioral clustering at scale demands. Sardine raised $145M and grew ARR 130% YoY by being the fintech-first developer pitch versus the bank-first incumbent pitch — Incandor could ride the same lane. The Known Exception that almost rescues this is Stripe itself, where I funded credentialed-looking Collisons because they had previously shipped Auctomatic. Incandor has no Auctomatic. Yekell has Vista Equity slides; Rosenzweig has Epic Games shipping experience but no fraud artifact and no public GitHub. The bull case dies on the missing pre-funding artifact — the one thing that flipped Houston, Armstrong, and the Collisons for me. What would flip my verdict: a public benchmark on a labeled fraud dataset (IEEE-CIS or a named bank's red-team data) showing Incandor's cold-start unsupervised model beating a supervised BioCatch-equivalent baseline by a margin Yekell and Rosenzweig built themselves before raising.
