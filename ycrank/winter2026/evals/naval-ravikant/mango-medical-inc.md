# Mango Medical -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting number in this dossier is also the least informative: a $10M ARR letter of intent from an unnamed "top orthopedic company." At the pre-seed stage, an LOI of that magnitude is either a signal that the product solves something so painful that a major OEM wants to lock it down before FDA clearance -- or it's a conditional expression of interest that evaporates the moment regulatory timelines slip. I've seen both. The fact that it's unnamed and non-binding, contingent on 510(k) clearance that hasn't been submitted, makes it impossible to weight heavily. If I could call the device company's VP of product and hear them say "we need this yesterday," that changes everything. Without that, it's a letter.

The specific knowledge question is where this deal gets honest. Adrian Kilian is a dentist -- not an orthopedic surgeon, not a trauma specialist, not someone who has spent a decade in the OR watching fracture reconstruction bottleneck on 3D planning. Dentistry shares architectural DNA with orthopedic planning: imaging, 3D reconstruction, workflow automation. I can see how someone who understood Invisalign's transformation of dental workflows would recognize the analogous opportunity in orthopedics -- and the company's own "Invisalign for bone fractures" framing confirms that's exactly the pattern they're applying. But pattern recognition across adjacent domains is not the same as non-transferable knowledge. A smart team from any medical AI program at a top university could identify the same gap. The six years of work since the TU Munich spinoff in 2020 accumulates some proprietary understanding -- clinical edge cases, surgeon workflow preferences, training data for fracture-specific models -- but it's not the kind of deep, irreplaceable founder knowledge that made Evan Williams the inevitable person to build Twitter. Jorge Padilla Pérez brings legitimate AI and medical imaging chops from TU Munich, and having a genuine technologist on the founding team clears my bar there. But the skills are trainable. What's being built here could be assembled by any competent medical AI lab with the right clinical partnerships.

The leverage architecture is where I find something to like. Turning a multi-day, technician-dependent planning process into a minutes-long AI pipeline is real leverage -- code replacing labor, scaled across thousands of surgical cases. One engineering team building a system that serves every orthopedic surgeon who needs fracture planning. That's the right kind of ratio. But the leverage is bounded in two ways. First, the market: the U.S. orthopedic surgical planning software SAM is $24.63M. Even the broader AI-in-orthopedic-surgery TAM is $307M today. These aren't the kind of numbers where a 10% market share produces venture-scale returns at angel entry. Second, the distribution: selling through orthopedic device OEMs means you're a component in someone else's ecosystem, not a platform. Stryker or Zimmer Biomet sets the terms. You're building a pick for someone else's mine.

For the bull case to work, several things have to be true simultaneously. The fracture reconstruction niche -- which is genuinely less contested than hip or knee replacement planning, where Formus Labs already has FDA clearance and 3,000+ cases -- has to expand into a broader orthopedic planning platform. The $10M LOI has to convert, which means 510(k) clearance has to come through in a reasonable timeframe. And the "agentic AI" approach has to produce meaningfully better plans than what Enhatch and Brainlab already offer, creating enough clinical pull that device OEMs compete to integrate rather than dictate terms. If all of that happens, you have a company that could be an acquisition target for one of the four major orthopedic device firms -- Stryker, Zimmer Biomet, Smith+Nephew, DePuy Synthes -- at a price that reflects the strategic value of owning the planning layer. The precedent exists: Brainlab acquired TraumaCad, Materialise acquired OrthoView. But that's an acquisition thesis, not a compounding thesis. It's a scenario where the exit is a multiple, not a magnitude.

The timing isn't contrarian. AI automating medical imaging workflows is one of the most consensus themes in healthtech. Twenty companies are building variations of this. The fracture-specific focus narrows the field, which is smart positioning, but it's not the kind of bet where most intelligent people think you're wrong. It's the kind of bet where everyone agrees the direction is right and the question is execution and regulatory timing. I get paid for being right when others think I'm wrong, not for being right when everyone agrees.

I respect the persistence -- six years from TU Munich spinoff to YC W26, the willingness to relocate from Munich to San Francisco to attack the U.S. market. That signals genuine commitment to the problem, not to the identity of being a founder. The regulatory moat, if they achieve 510(k) clearance, would create real defensibility -- a 12-24 month window that fast followers can't shortcut. And the academic origin at TU Munich likely means there's real research underneath, not just a wrapper on open-source segmentation models. But the competitive picture is sobering: Formus Labs has clearance, revenue, and Zimmer Biomet distribution. Enhatch has $15M in funding and Smith+Nephew. Mango Medical is entering a race where the other runners started earlier and are carrying more resources. I'd need to see the technology demonstrate a qualitative leap -- not incremental improvement, but a fundamentally different capability -- to overcome that gap.
