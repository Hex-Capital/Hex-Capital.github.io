# Advocate -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Baran's mother is a care coordinator. That detail, buried in his a16z Speedrun profile, is the most interesting thing in this entire dossier — because it sits in tension with everything else about his background. Columbia class president, Oxbridge scholar, McKinsey due diligence, part-time at Citadel, healthcare growth equity at Radian Capital. That's the resume of someone who identifies opportunities by analyzing markets, not someone who builds things because a problem is driving them crazy. The mother detail is supposed to bridge that gap — to transform "I saw this billing gap in my deal flow" into "I grew up watching my mom leave money on the table." Maybe it does. But the way Advocate describes itself — "no new hires, no workflow changes, pure margin" — is the language of an investor pitch, not a care coordinator's frustration. Drew Houston didn't describe Dropbox as "frictionless file synchronization for enterprise productivity." He said he kept forgetting his USB drive.

Advocate's niche passes my schlep blindness test, and I want to give that its due. Care coordination billing at skilled nursing facilities and behavioral health centers — CPT 99490 at roughly $62 per patient per month, documentation requirements that scare off the lazy — is exactly the kind of problem that makes polished founders say "ugh." The big RCM players like Waystar ($1.1B revenue) focus on physician encounter coding at health systems. CodaMetrix ($109M raised) automates multi-specialty coding for hospitals with 200+ locations. Neither is going to build a voice-note-to-billing tool for a 40-bed skilled nursing facility in Queens. The channel mismatch — facility administrators versus hospital CFOs — is a real structural barrier that could protect Advocate's niche the way bank integration complexity protected Stripe's early position. But here's the critical difference: Patrick Collison was a developer who felt the payment integration pain in his own hands. Baran felt this problem through spreadsheets at Radian Capital, or through dinner conversations with his mother. That's not nothing, but it's not the same thing. Stripe's founders could build the product AND were the users. Advocate's founders can probably build the product — Justinas Banys built data systems at Garner Health, a legitimate unicorn in care navigation — but neither founder has personally coded a CMS billing packet or spent months navigating documentation requirements as a care coordinator.

What I can't find anywhere in this dossier is a story. Not a credential, not a market sizing, not a competitive positioning — a story. When someone asks me how I evaluate founders, I say I ask them to tell me about something hard that happened and what they did about it. The Airbnb founders had the cereal boxes. Patrick Collison had Auctomatic at seventeen. I look for the narrative of creative problem-solving under constraint, and Advocate gives me none. No shipping history I can verify — Baran claims co-founding Leilo, but public sources credit Sol Broady and Ethan Blattman as the founders, and the company was acquired by Fiji Kava in 2022 without Baran's name appearing in the coverage. No public GitHub repos for either founder. No working prototype I can see — advocatewellbeing.com redirects to chiefofhealth.com, which shows almost nothing. No early users, no revenue signals, no obsessive beta testers using a crappy v1. The absence of stories is itself a data point. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes the way spinning objects generate centrifugal force — it's a byproduct of the quality itself.

The strongest bull case for Advocate: the regulatory schlep creates a genuine moat. Getting automated billing right under False Claims Act exposure — where upcoding errors can trigger massive legal liability — is hard enough that most AI companies won't touch it. If Advocate nails compliance, facilities won't switch. The CMS expansion of reimbursable care management codes (CCM, PCM, behavioral health integration) is a real tailwind that's widening the addressable billing pool. And Banys's time at Garner Health means the CTO has built healthcare data pipelines that actually work in production. If Advocate can sign five or ten facilities, prove the revenue capture is real, and build an audit trail that survives CMS scrutiny, they'd have something defensible. The niche is small enough — low single-digit billions SAM — to discourage Abridge and Suki from prioritizing it. All of that could be true. But I'm being asked whether to invest my own money based on signals I can see today, and today what I see is a well-credentialed team that identified an opportunity from the investor's chair, dressed it up with a personal origin story, and has not yet demonstrated the obsessive building quality that separates founders who execute in regulated healthcare from founders who get stuck in compliance limbo.

Advocate lands in Neutral territory for me, held back most by the absence of resourcefulness evidence and the analytical rather than organic relationship between these founders and their problem. The schlep is real — care coordination billing at facilities is genuinely unglamorous work that Waystar and CodaMetrix won't chase — and that single signal is what keeps this from being a clear Pass. But Baran's profile pattern (McKinsey, healthcare VC, polished institutional credentials) is precisely the archetype I've learned to be cautious about. The best founders I've backed built things before they had a business reason to. I need to see Advocate's product working in a facility, capturing real revenue, with care coordinators who refuse to give it back. Until then, I'm watching, not writing a check.
