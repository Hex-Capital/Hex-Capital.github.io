# Minicor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep circling back to with Minicor: this is a space where everybody can see the opportunity. Legacy desktop apps with no APIs, AI models that can now see and click on screens, enterprises trapped behind old software -- the pitch writes itself, and that's actually the problem for me. UiPath is a $13 billion public company already shipping its own AI Healing Agent. Automat just raised $19.25 million from Felicis. Anthropic's Computer Use API is in public beta and available to anyone with an API key. When I think about the investments that have defined my career -- SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares made people back away from the table -- they all shared that quality of being deeply unpopular and genuinely difficult to assess. Minicor is neither. Everyone I know in the Valley right now is talking about AI agents automating desktop workflows. That consensus signal is loud, and it makes me cautious.

What I do respect is the operational seriousness these founders bring. SOC 2 Type II certification and HIPAA compliance at the pre-seed stage -- that's not something you stumble into. That's months of unglamorous work building audit trails and security controls while competitors are shipping demos. It reminds me of what drew me to companies operating in regulated spaces: the compliance work itself becomes a moat because most startups don't have the patience or discipline for it. And the claim of architecture handling 25,000 patients per day suggests they've been in production, not just running demos. If I were evaluating this purely on operational maturity and willingness to do hard things in healthcare, I'd be more excited.

Saheed's story has threads I'd want to pull on in a real conversation. Born and raised in Lagos, came to Ottawa, built a lottery system and payment processor in Nigeria before working deep inside Ford and Morgan Stanley's legacy enterprise environments. That trajectory -- growing up in a place where infrastructure gaps force creative workarounds, then spending years inside the exact kind of legacy systems Minicor is automating -- that's the kind of biographical thread I look for. But the dossier doesn't go deep enough for me to feel the personal fire. I don't know what drove Saheed from Lagos to Ottawa, what building payment systems in Nigeria taught him about systems that don't talk to each other, or whether this problem kept him up at night before it became a company. Professional fit is clear. Personal stake -- the kind where the founder and the mission are inseparable -- I can't confirm it from what's here.

The co-founder relationship is genuinely strong by my standards. Faizaan and Saheed met in 2019, built Software For Love together during the pandemic -- a nonprofit where they chose to use their engineering skills to help organizations that couldn't afford software -- and then started Minicor in 2023. That's five-plus years of working together, including building something where the motivation wasn't money. After watching HQ Trivia implode because two co-founders couldn't resolve their differences despite having the right product at the right time, I now probe co-founder dynamics before almost anything else. These two have a foundation. The nonprofit also tells me something about their values -- they built for people who needed help before they built for profit.

Let me give the bull case its due, because there's a version of this that works. If you believe -- and I think it's reasonable -- that the next five years will see a massive wave of AI companies trying to sell into healthcare, automotive, and supply chain, then someone needs to build the infrastructure that lets those AI companies actually connect to the legacy systems of record. Minicor is positioning itself as that infrastructure layer: not the AI itself, but the managed plumbing that makes the AI usable in production. The SOC 2 and HIPAA certifications make them credible in healthcare specifically. The deterministic-code-with-AI-recovery architecture is clever -- it sidesteps the reliability concerns that plague pure AI agent approaches. And the MCP interface for coding agents is a developer-distribution play that could create organic adoption. If they can become the default way AI companies integrate with legacy desktop systems, the switching costs compound quickly once they're embedded in production workflows. That's a real business. But it requires the "AI companies selling into legacy industries" wave to materialize at scale, and it requires Minicor to stay ahead of UiPath's AI capabilities and Anthropic's own potential to move up the stack.

Ultimately, this doesn't trigger the intuitive response I've learned to trust. The founders are competent, the co-founder relationship is solid, the compliance work is impressive, and there's a path to making invisible systems visible -- trapped healthcare data, locked automotive records -- in a way that resonates with my pattern of backing companies that create transparency where opacity exists. But competence in a consensus market isn't what I invest my own money on. I'm looking for the founders and ideas that make other investors uncomfortable, and Minicor makes everyone comfortable. That's the signal I can't get past.
