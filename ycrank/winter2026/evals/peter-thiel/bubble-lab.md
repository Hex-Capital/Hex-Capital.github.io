# Bubble Lab -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive structure of this market tells you everything you need to know before you examine anything else. n8n has raised $253 million and reached $40 million in ARR building workflow automation. Gumloop has $24.5 million. Dust has $21.5 million and Sequoia backing. Activepieces exists in the same space. And then there is the entity that actually owns the platform Bubble Lab depends on -- Salesforce -- which has announced Agentforce 360 to position Slack itself as an "agentic OS." Bubble Lab is building a product on a platform whose owner is building the same product, while surrounded by competitors with a hundred times more capital. This is not a market with a hidden secret waiting to be discovered. This is a market where the thesis -- "AI should automate operational workflows" -- is so thoroughly consensus that billions of dollars are already chasing it. When I ask my question -- what important truth do very few people agree with you on? -- the answer here appears to be "Slack is a good interface for workflow automation," which is something Salesforce itself agrees with so strongly that it is restructuring its entire enterprise AI strategy around it.

The absence of a secret is fatal, but the structural dynamics make it worse. Bubble Lab has open-sourced its core workflow engine under Apache 2.0. In the abstract, open source can build community and distribution -- but it only works as a monopoly strategy when the open-source layer creates lock-in for a proprietary layer above it (Red Hat's model, Stripe's developer ecosystem). Here, the open-source engine *is* the product. Anyone can fork it, self-host it, and extend it without ever becoming a Bubble Lab customer. The company has simultaneously given away its technology and built its product on a platform controlled by a competitor. This is the architectural opposite of monopoly -- it is a strategy that structurally guarantees commoditization from both above (Salesforce) and below (the open-source community).

The founders are young, technically capable Cornell Tech graduates who previously built a consumer nutrition app together. The pivot from gymii.ai to B2B operations automation is itself a signal I weight negatively -- it suggests exploration rather than conviction, iteration rather than a definite plan. When Zuckerberg built Facebook, he was not pivoting from a food-tracking app. He had a specific vision for how identity-based social networking would reshape human interaction, and he held that vision against every pressure to sell or diversify. I see no evidence that Selina Li or Zach Zhong have articulated a definite view of the future that this company creates -- a world in which Bubble Lab's existence makes something previously impossible suddenly inevitable. "Your ops super-employee in Slack" is a tagline, not a vision. Neither founder has documented experience in the operations domain they are targeting, and the domain shift from consumer health to enterprise ops suggests they are following market signal rather than executing against a deeply held thesis about how the world should work.

The strongest bull case would require several things to be simultaneously true: that Salesforce's native AI features remain permanently tethered to the Salesforce ecosystem and overpriced, leaving a durable gap for tool-agnostic automation; that the Slack-native interface creates such superior user experience that operations teams refuse to use any non-Slack alternative; and that long-term memory and personalization create switching costs deep enough to resist competition from better-funded players. If all three held, Bubble Lab could potentially own the "Slack-native, tool-agnostic ops agent" niche. The 4,000 users and named customers like Dyna Robotics suggest genuine utility. But even in this optimistic scenario, the outcome is a modest SaaS business at $29.99-$99.99 per month, not a monopoly. The power law demands investments where success means returning the entire fund. A workflow automation tool competing against n8n and Salesforce, priced at $30 a month, does not pass that test even in the best case.

The underlying technology -- an LLM that calls APIs on behalf of users -- represents no discontinuity whatsoever. Every major AI lab and hundreds of startups can build this. The 25 integrations are engineering work, not proprietary technology. The long-term memory feature is a modest personalization layer, not a 10x improvement over anything. Nothing here makes a previously impossible thing possible. This is a 1-to-n business in a market already crowded with 1-to-n businesses, each copying the others into mutual margin destruction. The Girardian dynamics are textbook: every player is imitating every other player, differentiation is cosmetic (Slack-native vs. visual builder vs. drag-and-drop), and the inevitable outcome is a race to the bottom on pricing while Salesforce captures the premium tier for free as a bundled feature. I would not invest my own money here.
