# AICE Power -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been building clip-on CT sensors with AI-powered load disaggregation for commercial buildings since 2011, raising $41.8M+ along the way. Infogrid has raised $148M for broader smart building intelligence. Facilio has $45.9M and covers 25,000+ buildings with its software-only BMS aggregation platform. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where multiple well-capitalized players have been operating for a decade-plus using fundamentally the same technical approach, my first question isn't about the founder or the product -- it's about what structural change makes NOW different from 2015, or 2018, or 2021. AICE Power's answer is the EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive recast adopted in 2024, declining IoT connectivity costs, and post-2022 European energy prices. These are real tailwinds, but they're incremental tightenings of trends every energy-sector investor and operator has been tracking for years. The EPBD is not analogous to GPT-4 crossing a capability threshold that unlocked legal AI after a decade of failures -- it's a regulatory evolution in a well-watched space. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by consensus; when I backed Harvey, legal AI was considered a graveyard. Energy monitoring for commercial buildings is neither dismissed nor a graveyard -- it's a category with hundreds of millions already deployed into it. That's the opposite of nonobvious.

AICE Power's sharpest strategic insight is the segment focus: small-format multi-site commercial venues -- E.Leclerc supermarkets, Accor hotels, fast food chains -- that traditional BMS vendors like Honeywell and Siemens structurally cannot serve. These incumbents sell through specialized integrator channels where the economics require high-touch, multi-week deployments. AICE Power claims 5% of BMS cost and 30-minute installation per site. That business model conflict -- where incumbents would cannibalize their integrator relationships and compress their own margins to compete -- is a real dynamic. I've seen it work in payroll (Gusto disrupted ADP at the SMB tier) and in fleet operations (Samsara went where legacy providers couldn't). But Samsara at its earliest stage was building a platform with broad sensor types, developer APIs, and cross-category fleet intelligence. AICE Power is a monitoring tool for a single use case. There's no evidence of API-first architecture, third-party integrations, or a platform layer that other businesses would build on. It sits at the application layer, not the infrastructure layer, which limits compounding switching costs to the physical installation of commodity hardware.

Grégoire Chomette brings energy domain research from EPFL -- a 2018 publication on biogas supply chain optimization -- and computational modeling from MIT and NASA, where he assessed asteroid impact risk. Rémi Bouteiller brings exactly the hardware product experience AICE Power needs: he led electronics, firmware, and Shenzhen manufacturing at Amira Health and worked on embedded systems at EssilorLuxottica and Balyo. Bouteiller's French Eurobot championship and hackathon wins signal strong technical execution capacity. The combination of Chomette's energy domain knowledge and Bouteiller's end-to-end hardware engineering covers the critical skill surface. But neither founder has operated inside a company during hypergrowth -- neither has navigated the 10-to-1,000-employee transition that defines whether a hardware-plus-software company can scale distribution, field support, and supply chain simultaneously. AICE Power's 10+ sites tracking 5+ MW represent genuine early validation with recognizable logos (E.Leclerc, Accor, NASA, Station F), but for a two-person team approaching a market that requires physical deployment to hundreds of sites, I need to see evidence that this team can build and scale a field organization. Hardware scaling complexity is not a nice-to-have concern -- it's the central execution challenge.

The single-miracle test lands poorly for AICE Power. The underlying technology isn't the miracle -- clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware, and non-intrusive load monitoring is an active research area with open-source implementations. AICE Power needs at least two things to go right simultaneously: hardware manufacturing and supply chain must scale reliably across hundreds of sites, AND distribution to fragmented multi-site commercial operators must convert at rates that support the unit economics. Unlike Harvey, where the technology miracle was happening exogenously via foundation model improvements and legal firms were already proven technology buyers, AICE Power faces execution miracles in two domains (hardware logistics and enterprise sales) that don't compound favorably for a two-person team starting from 10 sites.

The bull case deserves articulation: the EPBD recast creates a regulatory mandate that will force millions of small commercial buildings in Europe to implement energy monitoring for the first time, Verdigris's $41.8M was deployed targeting large commercial buildings and explicitly avoided the small-commercial segment, and AICE Power's 5% cost position combined with 30-minute installation makes it potentially the only viable solution for compliance in this newly mandated tier. If AICE Power becomes the default compliance solution for EPBD in small commercial, the regulatory moat compounds -- similar to how I've seen compliance complexity protect early movers in legal (Harvey) and financial regulation (Coinbase). The E.Leclerc and Accor logos show enterprise procurement is already happening, and a single procurement decision with a 500-location chain creates instant scale. Under this scenario, AICE Power looks like "boring but critical" infrastructure for regulatory compliance, the exact pattern behind Gusto and Checkr. But this bull case requires the EPBD enforcement timeline to be aggressive enough to create near-term demand, and it requires AICE Power to have a meaningful technical or operational advantage beyond price -- and the dossier shows no proprietary technology, no patent portfolio, and commodity hardware. Anyone can clip a CT sensor onto a panel. The condition that would flip me: if AICE Power demonstrated that EPBD enforcement is creating a hard compliance deadline within 18 months for their target segment AND they showed 100+ contracted sites with a named chain operator, I'd revisit. That would convert a regulatory tailwind into a structural forcing function with proven distribution.

AICE Power operates in a real market with genuine regulatory tailwinds, but nothing about the timing is nonobvious, the positioning is application-layer rather than infrastructure, and the execution path requires two simultaneous scaling miracles for a two-person team with no prior hypergrowth experience. The two dimensions most decisive for my assessment: Nonobvious Market Timing, where the EPBD is well-publicized and the market has $200M+ already deployed into competing solutions; and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning, where AICE Power is a vertical monitoring tool rather than a platform layer that other businesses build on. These are the two criteria that carry 55 of my 100 points, and AICE Power scores below my defaults on both.
