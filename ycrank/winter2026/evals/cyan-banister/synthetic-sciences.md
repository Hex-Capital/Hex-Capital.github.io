# Synthetic Sciences -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Synthetic Sciences is that this is one of the most popular categories in tech right now. Google launched an AI Co-Scientist. Edison Scientific raised $70M with Jeff Dean on their cap table. Elicit already has 400,000 researchers using their platform. Every frontier lab is building research assistance tooling. When I look at the investments that have defined my career -- SpaceX when everyone said Elon was burning money on rockets nobody asked for, Uber when ride-sharing was flatly illegal, Crusoe Energy when the pitch was literally "let's mine Bitcoin on burning gas in North Dakota" -- the common denominator is that those ideas repelled other investors. AI for scientific research in 2026 repels nobody. It's on every VC's thesis slide. That's a problem for how I invest. The most promising companies are not popular, and this category is extremely popular.

When I sit down with founders, I don't want the business first. I want to know how they got here -- what happened in their life that made this particular problem impossible to ignore. Aayam Bansal and Ishaan Gangwani are clearly brilliant. Fifteen-plus publications at ICML, NeurIPS, and ACL by age 18 is extraordinary. USACO Platinum and IOAI qualifications are real signals of intellectual horsepower. Research stints at CMU, Harvard, MIT CSAIL, Berkeley -- these are serious credentials for anyone, let alone teenagers. But when I listen for the biographical thread that connects this founder to this specific problem, what I hear is: "We are researchers, and research workflows are fragmented." That's an observation, and it's a correct observation, but it's not the kind of lived-experience conviction I've learned to trust. They attended elite schools in Kolkata and Pune. They had the resources to do summer research at the world's best institutions. There's no survival story here, no adversity that forged an unbreakable commitment to this particular problem. That doesn't make them bad founders -- it means they don't trigger my primary filter. Bansal's claimed prior exit of an AI healthcare company is intriguing but completely opaque -- no acquirer, no terms, no details. At 18, I can't evaluate what I can't see.

The pivot from InkVell to Synthetic Sciences during the YC batch itself gives me pause. InkVell was a focused product -- turning research into publication-ready documents. Synthetic Sciences is an enormously ambitious platform: end-to-end research execution, GPU orchestration across providers, four distinct modes spanning biology, ML, writing, and iterative model improvement via RL training data. That scope expansion for a two-person team competing against Edison Scientific's $70M and Elicit's 400K users isn't scrappy ambition -- it's a surface area problem. The benchmark claim of 92% on "BixBench Verified" would be remarkable if true, but the published BixBench results show frontier models at 17% accuracy, and the "Verified" variant appears to be a different evaluation protocol that couldn't be independently corroborated. When a key credibility signal can't be verified, I note it.

The bull case I'd want to believe: these two are the kind of prodigies who compress ten years of learning into two. The data flywheel they describe -- product usage generating process-level training data that improves agent capabilities -- could theoretically create a proprietary dataset no competitor can replicate. If inference costs keep dropping and their RL training infrastructure produces measurably better research agents, they could carve out a wedge that neither Google nor Edison would prioritize the same way. And maybe the real "weirdness" here isn't the idea but the founders themselves -- two 18-year-olds from India, dropping out of college, going head-to-head against teams with fifty times their funding. That does take real conviction. But conviction without a biographical anchor to the problem, in a consensus category, against entrenched and well-capitalized competitors, doesn't clear my threshold. I've seen too many brilliant people chase hot categories and get outgunned. The founders I back are solving problems that chose them, not problems they chose because the market was large.

I also don't see the economic access dimension that draws me to investments. Uber created income for millions of drivers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people locked out of the system. Postmates put money in the pockets of delivery workers. Synthetic Sciences makes research faster for computational scientists who already have GPU access and institutional resources. The pricing tiers at $50-200 per month target well-funded labs, not people who are currently excluded from research. There's nothing wrong with that as a business -- it's just not the kind of impact that gets me to lean forward.

This is a pass for me, and I want to be clear about why: it's not a judgment on the founders' intelligence, which is clearly exceptional. It's that my framework is built around backing outsiders with biographical stakes in non-consensus problems, and Synthetic Sciences is a consensus bet by academically elite founders in one of the most competed categories in technology. I wish them well -- they may build something significant -- but this isn't my kind of bet.
