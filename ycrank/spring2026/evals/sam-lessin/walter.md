# Walter -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Walter is a vertical AI agent for manufacturing ERPs pitched by a CDTM dropout (Nikolas Keller) and a CTO (Lukas Postulka) with no verifiable public footprint, claiming "multiple 9-figure customers" in packaging and defense — which makes Walter the textbook 2026 YC consensus shape (AI agent + enterprise vertical + ERP write-back) lightly redeemed by industrial traction I cannot independently price. Walter's analytical signature is **modest founder execution-conviction paired with zero owned distribution in a category I publicly de-rate as "just more internet"** — and that pairing is what determines the verdict, not the customer logos.

Compare Walter to **Sublime Security** on the round-pricing axis: Sublime was pre-consensus enterprise security at a moment when most security capital was still chasing late-stage SOC SaaS, so the seed entry sat below the category's eventual clearing price. Walter is the inversion — vertical AI agents into SAP/NetSuite/Dynamics 365 are precisely where the 2026 YC batch is densest and where Gartner's "40% of enterprise apps embed task-specific agents by end of 2026" line is generating exactly the kind of consensus heat I treat as deselection. The Walter dossier publishes no round size, no post-money, no lead, no passes — silence on round dynamics from a YC company in a hot sector is not neutral information; it is the default-priced SAFE I cannot generate alpha against, and the absent-evidence prior pins Walter near the floor of this dimension.

The lowest-rung failure mechanism on founder-as-brand is the canonical Pass Example founder — "no public writing I can find on why their take on agents is non-obvious" — and Walter reproduces that pattern almost line-for-line. Keller's public artifacts are a personal site referencing a fine-dining culinary background and a CDTM life-paths tool; Postulka has no confirmed public profile at all. The only published Walter conversion path is a Cal.com demo with the CEO, the Twitter and LinkedIn follower counts aren't even retrievable, and there is no newsletter, no Discord, no industry-specific community Walter built before the company. This is the inverse of what the $60M Slow Creator Fund was designed to underwrite, and the inverse of the John Fish / Bookshelved check — Walter has zero distribution on the cap table and depends entirely on outbound enterprise sales the founders have not demonstrated they can run.

On capital structure, the 18-month compounding question is whether Walter's "90%+ accuracy after 10 documents" claim translates into per-customer fine-tuning that lets Walter expand seats inside packaging/defense accounts without raising again — or whether SAP, Oracle, and Dynamics integration depth plus SOC 2 / ISO 27001 still only "in progress" force three more rounds of GTM and compliance burn before Series A. The hiring ad ($125K–$200K, 0.10–1.00% equity) is a standard SaaS cap table, not a Teamshares-style or revenue-share instrument I would write a non-consensus check into. Walter is bootstrappable-looking on the surface given the customer claims, but the ERP enterprise motion is structurally capital-hungry — this is not the venture-shape-without-software-cap-table profile that would make me reach.

Counterfactually, what would flip founder-conviction from a low-mid score to a real signal? Keller would need to have published opinionated long-form writing specifically on why template-OCR-derived cognitive capture (Rossum, Hyperscience, Workist) structurally fails for non-English manufacturing documents — three years of it, ideally a Substack the procurement directors at the 9-figure packaging customers actually read. Instead, the dossier surfaces a pivot from "customer support and software development" automation to manufacturing back office, which reads as opportunity-chasing rather than mission. The CDTM dropout signal is real but mild; against Drop.io's seven-year arc to a Facebook acquisition, Walter's conviction footprint is the response of a smart generalist to a market opening, not the temperament of an operator who has been working on this problem since before LLMs crossed threshold.

On category sleeve, parallel to **Ro**: I made Ro a known exception because 50-state prescription-rail regulatory complexity was a genuine "venture moment" that only VC capital could clear, and capital intensity was the moat rather than the failure. Walter's analog would be that defense-grade document classification plus deep ERP write-back across SAP/NetSuite/Dynamics constitutes a similar schlep moat that horizontal AI agent platforms won't underwrite. The dossier supports the *shape* of that argument — Workist is the only direct competitor, defense customers are slow-moving and sticky — but "AI is just more internet" is my baseline frame and the Walter pitch is not crypto, not DTC, not creator-economy, not employee-equity rollup. It is the dominant YC sleeve I am explicitly re-weighting away from, and the vertical-ERP wrinkle is not enough to overcome it.

My 36-month forecast: Walter reaches $3–8M ARR on 4–8 named industrial accounts, then is acquired as a feature by an ERP incumbent (Microsoft Dynamics or Oracle NetSuite) or by Rossum/Hyperscience consolidating the document-AI category, for $40–90M — a fine outcome for the founders, a non-event for a seed entry at YC consensus pricing. The mechanism producing this is the high-low pairing in Walter's signature: modest execution-conviction is enough to close a handful of 9-figure packaging accounts and survive, but zero owned distribution plus a consensus category cap the upside well below the venture-moment threshold the round price will require.

The bull case is the one that would sting: Walter's per-customer fine-tuning genuinely compounds, the "90%+ after 10 documents" claim becomes a real data flywheel inside each ERP installation, defense-vertical procurement cycles produce 5-year contracts that horizontal AI vendors can't touch because Rossum and Hyperscience never built the SAP write-back path with industry-specific schemas, and Walter becomes the Workday-of-manufacturing-back-office at $80–150M ARR by 2029. The dossier facts that defeat this — Workist is already positioned identically as "AI doc automation for manufacturing," Hyperscience raised $300M+ before Walter existed, SOC 2 and ISO 27001 are still "in progress" which gates the very defense customers Walter claims, and the founders pivoted into this market within the last 18 months — make the bull case a structural underdog. The verdict flips only if I learn Walter's round is sub-$8M post-money with a visible tier-1 pass AND Keller has 3+ years of pre-Walter public writing on manufacturing automation that I missed. Absent both, Walter is a pass.
