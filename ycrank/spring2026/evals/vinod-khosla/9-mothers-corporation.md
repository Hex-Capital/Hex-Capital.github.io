# 9 Mothers -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 9 Mothers is the scope calibration. Cheap suicide drones are genuinely transforming warfare -- Ukraine proved that a $500 FPV drone can destroy a $10 million armored vehicle, and that asymmetry will reshape every military on earth. That is a massive, consequential problem. But EDDA is positioned as a small, portable kinetic turret targeting only Group 1 drones under 20 pounds. The company has deliberately narrowed its aperture to the most constrained version of the counter-drone problem -- smaller than ACS's Bullfrog, which handles Groups 1 through 3 with heavier weapon integrations. I understand the logic: go small, go portable, get to the individual soldier. But when I see a startup that has already scoped down to a specific weight class and form factor before it has a single government contract, I worry they've traded consequence magnitude for perceived feasibility. If EDDA succeeds perfectly, you get a good defense product company -- maybe a $3-5 billion acquisition target for Raytheon or L3Harris. That's a real business. But nobody's rewriting how militaries operate. The counter-drone fight will be layered -- EW, kinetic, directed energy -- and EDDA becomes one component in that stack, not the thing that makes the old approach obsolete.

Allen Control Systems is the elephant in this evaluation that a generic analyst might underweight. ACS raised $30 million from Craft Ventures, won the Army's xTechOverwatch competition, secured SOCOM maritime contracts, and tripled its Austin operations -- all by early 2026. They are building fundamentally the same thing: an AI-guided autonomous kinetic turret. In most technology markets, I'd say a better-funded competitor doesn't matter because the outsider with a different approach can leapfrog. That's what Juniper did to Cisco -- TCP/IP routers were architecturally different from what Cisco was building. But EDDA and Bullfrog are not architecturally different. They're both AI-guided kinetic turrets. The differentiation is "smaller and cheaper" versus "broader capability." In defense procurement, where the customer is a single buyer (DoD) with strong switching costs and relationship-driven contract awards, being 12-18 months behind a direct competitor with Army and SOCOM relationships is not a trivial gap. It's potentially fatal. This isn't a market where you can sell directly to end users and bypass the gatekeeper.

The strongest bull case, which I want to take seriously: the military doesn't need hundreds of Bullfrog-class systems mounted on vehicles -- it needs tens of thousands of cheap, portable C-UAS units distributed to every squad, every forward operating base, every logistics convoy. If EDDA is genuinely a fraction of Bullfrog's cost and can be carried by two soldiers, that's a different market segment entirely. The Army's $500 million-plus annual C-UAS budget is growing, and the affordable-at-scale segment may be underserved by ACS's heavier approach. The timing is undeniably right -- AI computer vision has crossed the real-time autonomous engagement threshold, fiber-optic drones are making EW solutions obsolete, and every NATO military is scrambling. If 9 Mothers can get to a working prototype and an OTA contract before ACS moves downmarket, the portable-kinetic niche could be enormous. And Smith has built and scaled a venture company before -- Rainforest QA at $41.2 million raised with 141 employees isn't nothing.

The team is interesting but incomplete for the risks they face. Smith and Khomenko worked together for eight years at Rainforest QA -- Smith as CTO, Khomenko building production ML and computer vision pipelines. That working relationship and Khomenko's direct CV expertise are the technical foundation for EDDA's autonomy stack. Pyzh's cybersecurity background from Leviathan Security Group maps to hardening defense systems against adversarial attacks, which is a real concern for autonomous weapons. These are smart people with relevant technical skills making a genuinely courageous domain leap from QA software to weapons systems. But I see no one on the team -- no founder, no advisor, no board member -- with defense procurement experience, weapons engineering credentials, or military operations background. For a company whose entire revenue depends on selling to the DoD, that's not a gap you can close with learning rate alone. You need someone who has navigated SBIR programs, testing and evaluation cycles, and program-of-record transitions. The gene pool hasn't been engineered to address the venture's most critical non-technical risk.

I keep coming back to the question I ask every founder: if this succeeds, does the world operate differently? With 9 Mothers, the honest answer is: soldiers have a better tool in their kit. That matters -- lives are saved, tactical effectiveness improves. But it's a product win, not a system transformation. The rate of change in the underlying technology (AI/CV for autonomous targeting) is genuinely accelerating, and the timing window is excellent. But excellent timing in a consensus market -- defense-tech venture funding doubled to $49.1 billion in 2025 -- means you're skating to where everyone else already sees the puck going. The outsider founders bring fresh thinking but lack the institutional access that defense demands. This is a credible company attacking a real problem at the right time, but the ambition is calibrated below what I need to write a check from my own money.
