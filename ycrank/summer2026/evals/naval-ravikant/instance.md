# Instance -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Instance is a clean MIT technical pair selling a physics-violation scanner into the narrowest possible B2B buyer set -- frontier video-world-model labs like the NVIDIA Cosmos team and a handful of robotics-foundation startups -- which means Instance's highest dimension is founder integrity reads (Lucy Cai and Claire Mao, middle-school friends, no flags) and its lowest is leverage architecture, the dimension I read first. That pairing tells me what Instance is: a competent, quiet, technically-credible team operating in a labor-and-capital-leverage business that requires permission from a small group of gatekeeper customers. That is the inverse of the architecture I fund.

The leverage check runs first and Instance fails it cleanly. Twitter (2007) was media -- anyone could post, the platform charged nothing, marginal cost of replication was zero, and the network metabolized its own distribution. Instance's distribution is direct enterprise sales to frontier labs plus "human judgment at scale," which is the labor-leverage half of the four-leverage taxonomy I wrote against at nav.al/product-media. The dossier itself confirms it -- no Product Hunt launch, no open-source repo, no GitHub presence from either Lucy Cai or Claire Mao, no developer-pull signal, and a landing page at tryinstance.app that reads "working on world models" with no self-serve hook. When the academic alternatives (T2VPhysBench, WorldModelBench, VideoPhy-2, LikePhys) are free arxiv papers, the commercial wedge has to be either a proprietary dataset compounding or a permissionless distribution channel, and Instance has neither yet.

The marketplace-network dimension is where I want to think hardest, because the low-rung failure mode here is single-sided SaaS where each customer is economically independent of the next -- and that is exactly what Instance looks like. Each frontier lab buying physics-violation scanning gets value from the tool, not from the previous lab's usage. The only path to network economics is a proprietary labeled-violations dataset that compounds into a benchmark standard model labs are forced to measure against -- the Encord-style data-flywheel argument -- but the dossier has zero disclosed customers, zero disclosed dataset size, and Encord itself ($110M raised, 300+ teams including Toyota Woven and Skydio per TechFundingNews 2025) is structurally ahead on exactly that flywheel.

Lucy Cai's MIT Lincoln Laboratory work on ML for 2D-to-3D point cloud generation is the specific-knowledge anchor and it is *adjacent* to world-model physics evaluation rather than load-bearing inside it -- vision-to-3D is not rigid-body simulation, not energy conservation modeling, not the LikePhys-grade research the dossier itself flags as unsolved. Compare to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity (2022): a Berkeley ML PhD specifically in retrieval, the exact substrate of the product. Instance's founder-product-market fit is one ring out from the core, and the integrity-exception I made for Clearview AI required *extreme* specific knowledge that no Stanford clone could replicate -- Instance's MIT MEng-plus-Lincoln-Lab-internship profile does not clear that bar. It clears a competent-pre-seed bar, which is different.

The market dimension compounds the leverage problem over 18 months in a specific way: the buyer set is so narrow (Cosmos team, Genie/Veo teams, maybe a dozen robotics foundation-model startups) that any one of them building eval in-house removes 8-15% of TAM, and most of them will, because evaluation infrastructure is precisely the kind of thing frontier labs treat as core IP. Pivot room exists in theory -- horizontal synthetic-data QA, robotics-sim eval, generalist labeling -- but each of those adjacencies lands Instance in Encord, Surge AI (raising at >$15B valuation per Sacra July 2025), and Meta-owned Scale's territory with no structural advantage.

The founder trio reads clean -- Lucy Cai (SpaceX, AWS, Lincoln Lab) and Claire Mao (MIT Math+CS, Council for Math Majors president, The Tech contributor), middle-school friends, no contested exits, no ethics-volunteering -- which is the 10-year-partner profile in the Buffett-trio sense. The recent pivot from "Instance: AI data analyst for databases" in January 2026 LinkedIn posts to physics-aware synthetic video QA by Summer 2026 batch is the kind of fast iteration I want to see early; first ideas never work and pivot-velocity is a positive signal at this stage, not a negative one. But integrity-and-energy alone do not save a check when the leverage architecture is wrong -- the Pinterest pass calibrated me to that direction, not this one.

Forecast, 36 months out: Instance either shuts down, raises a flat extension and pivots horizontally into a generalist eval-infrastructure play that gets out-resourced by Encord and Meta/Scale, or acqui-hires into one of the frontier labs (NVIDIA, Meta, an Anthropic-class robotics buyer) for sub-$25M as a talent-and-dataset tuck-in. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: clean technical founders + capital/labor leverage business + narrow buyer set converges on acqui-hire as the modal outcome, because the founders are good enough to be hired but the architecture is not built to compound.

The strongest bull case is the Perplexity exception -- if the synthetic-video category breaks the way AI search did in 2023, and Instance has accumulated even one frontier-lab contract that ratchets into a benchmark standard, the dataset flywheel could clear three of five gates (specific knowledge, pre-consensus timing, and a quasi-API distribution to model labs) and waive the network requirement the way Perplexity did. The bear answer that wins: Perplexity's founder had a Berkeley ML PhD in retrieval and shipped against a billion-user query surface; Instance has an MIT MEng internship-grade founder shipping into ten enterprise buyers, and the dossier lists *zero* signed customers, zero pricing, zero revenue. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: one named frontier-lab customer (Cosmos, Genie, Veo, or a robotics foundation-model startup) publicly adopting Instance as their evaluation gate within 9 months, plus a proprietary violation-labeled dataset disclosed at >100K clips -- without that pair, this is a pass.
