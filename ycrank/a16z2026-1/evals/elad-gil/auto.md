# Auto -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Dave Evans and Sam Hare are one of the strongest founder pairs I've seen for a camera AI company -- maybe the strongest. Two separate acquisitions by Snap (PlayCanvas in 2017, Seene in 2016), years running Snap's camera engineering stack (Evans as Director of Engineering for Mini-Programs, Games, SnapKit; Hare leading the Lens Experience team), and Hare holds a PhD in computer vision specializing in real-time applications. If I were drafting the ideal team to build an AI camera platform, I'd describe these two. But my framework starts with market structure, not founders, because I've seen exceptional operators get crushed when the market timing is wrong -- and with Auto, the market timing concern isn't that it's too early. It's that it's too obvious.

The structural shift powering Auto -- multimodal vision-language models crossing accuracy thresholds for reliable photo understanding -- is real. GPT-4V shipped October 2023, Gemini followed in December 2023. But this shift is about as consensus as it gets in 2026. Google Lens processes 12 billion visual searches per month, up 250% from 2023. Apple launched Visual Intelligence as a built-in iOS feature in December 2024 and has already expanded it to iPhone 15 Pro. OpenAI sells camera-capable ChatGPT Vision for $20/month. When every trillion-dollar platform company is actively building in your category, you're not in a nonobvious market -- you're in the most obvious market in technology. Compare this to when I backed Coinbase in 2012: crypto infrastructure was dismissed as fringe by virtually every mainstream investor. The whole point of the nonobvious framework is that your alpha comes from seeing a structural shift before consensus forms. Here, consensus arrived years ago and is backed by hundreds of billions in incumbent R&D.

Auto's "Frame" abstraction -- turning each photo into a persistent, task-specific mini-app rather than a one-shot answer -- is a genuine product insight. Google Lens returns search links, Apple Visual Intelligence returns static facts, ChatGPT Vision requires conversational back-and-forth. The Frame concept captures existing photo-taking behavior and channels it into ongoing task workflows, which maps to my thesis about capturing existing behavior in a new medium. But a product insight is not a structural moat. The dossier is explicit: "The underlying multimodal AI models (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) are available via API to any developer. Building a 'photo → structured action' pipeline is achievable by well-funded competitors." Auto is an application sitting on top of contested infrastructure layers it doesn't control. That's the pattern I actively avoid. When I back Stripe, I'm backing the payments API that thousands of businesses integrate into; when I back Harvey, I'm backing a platform that embeds in law firm workflows. Auto is a consumer app dependent on third-party model APIs, competing for App Store downloads against free alternatives from Alphabet, Apple, and OpenAI. The user-generated Frame ecosystem could eventually create switching costs, but there's no public data on Frame creation volume -- the network effect is aspirational, not demonstrated.

The single-miracle test compounds my concern. Auto needs a distribution miracle to acquire users against Google Lens's 1.5 billion monthly users and Apple's zero-cost bundled alternative. It needs a retention miracle to keep consumers paying $9.99/month for functionality they can approximate for free. And it needs a platform miracle for the Frame ecosystem to reach critical mass before incumbents replicate the UX pattern. That's at least two independent breakthroughs -- distribution against free incumbents AND subscription conversion at scale -- with a third needed for the platform thesis to work. Each individually is hard; combined, the odds compound downward. The technology miracle is happening exogenously, which helps, but the distribution math against free, pre-installed competitors is the primary challenge, and nothing in the dossier addresses it beyond App Store optimization and the a16z Speedrun network.

The bull case I have to take seriously: Auto's Frame ecosystem could become the "App Store for visual AI" -- a platform layer that neither Google Lens (optimized for ad-linked search results) nor Apple Visual Intelligence (optimized for hardware attachment, no third-party extensibility) has incentive to build. If thousands of user-created Frames produce discovery loops and switching costs, Auto transcends the application layer and becomes infrastructure. Evans built the engine powering Snap Games; Hare built the Lens Experience. They know how to build creator platforms inside camera products. If Frame creation hits an inflection point before incumbents react, and the $9.99/month pricing holds because users genuinely replace three separate app subscriptions (as one early App Store reviewer claimed), the margin architecture works -- pure software subscription with 70%+ gross margins and potential expansion revenue. For this to materialize, the Frame ecosystem needs to demonstrate traction within 12 months, and Apple needs to not kill the app through policy changes or Visual Intelligence feature expansion. Neither condition has evidence supporting it yet.

Auto's 5-star App Store rating from 5 reviewers is not traction data -- it's a rounding error. No download counts, no user counts, no revenue figures, no Product Hunt launch, no press coverage. That's normal for a company three weeks post-v1.0 launch, and I'm not penalizing the absence at pre-seed. But the absence of any distribution signal means my evaluation rests entirely on the structural market analysis and founder quality. The founders score near the ceiling of what I look for: dual exits, scaling experience at a hypergrowth company (Snap), deep technical domain expertise (CV PhD, 3D engines, AR product engineering), and a clear co-founder relationship forged through overlapping Snap tenure. Evans and Hare are the strongest dimension in this evaluation. But my framework is explicit: "I tend to be more product market centric than founder centric." Market structure timing and infrastructure positioning are more heavily weighted because I've seen strong founders fail in hostile market structures more often than mediocre founders succeed in favorable ones. Auto has exceptional founders building a consumer application in the most obvious AI market of 2026, against free competition from the three largest technology companies on earth. The founders deserve a better market structure to fight in.
