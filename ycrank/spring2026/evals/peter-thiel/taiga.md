# Taiga -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Taiga is the dimensional inverse of the bet I want to make: a regulated-sector wrapper around a consensus AI thesis. The single most interesting fact in the dossier is that Nanda Guntupalli and Adam Wax both grew up inside independent physician practices, which is a clean founder-market-fit story, but the regulated-customer texture sits on top of what is otherwise an "AI for X" pitch in a category that has already absorbed $99.5M into Candid Health and $95M+ into Adonis. The signature here is regulated-floor plus contrarian-ceiling: the moat substrate exists, the secret does not.

The Stripe (2010) comparison is the one Taiga is implicitly inviting, and it is the one that breaks under load. Patrick Collison's secret was that the incumbent ? PayPal, the company I had run ? had a structural blind spot toward developer experience because its DNA was consumer trust and fraud, not API ergonomics. The mechanism was a named incumbent with a named architectural failure mode the market had stopped questioning. Taiga gestures at the analogous claim ? that traditional billing firms cannot cannibalize their own labor margins by automating headcount away ? and that is a real observation, but it is not a secret. Candid Health raised $99.5M and Adonis raised $95M+ saying the same sentence. When three funded competitors articulate the same incumbent failure mode, it is no longer contrarian; it is the consensus 2025 RCM thesis. Taiga's wedge ? independent practices rather than multi-site groups ? is a segmentation tactic, not a Zero to One (2014) answer to the question of what important truth few people agree with you on.

Pure-SaaS-no-moat is the canonical pass pattern, and the way Taiga avoids that pattern is the one place I give credit. Healthcare RCM sits inside HIPAA, payer-specific adjudication rules, and federal fraud liability on miscoded claims ? the same family of regulatory texture that made Palantir (2003) a 15-year hold rather than a feature. The 98% touchless claim rate Taiga self-reports is exactly where that texture bites: if AI-generated codes mis-adjudicate at scale, the founders are inside False Claims Act exposure, not a refund conversation. That is real, and it is why the atoms-heavy/regulated dimension does not collapse to the software-only default. But integration with ten EHRs and fifty payers is not a certification moat ? it is plumbing that Candid Health and Adonis already have. The regulatory ceiling is high; Taiga is not yet near it.

On monopoly trajectory, the compounding question is what Taiga looks like at month 18 against three competitors with multi-year head starts. Akasa at $205M raised serves 650+ hospitals; Adonis posted 4x revenue growth in 2025 with net retention above 130%; Candid Health grew revenue ~250% YoY in 2024 across 200+ healthcare organizations. The percentage-of-collections service model implied by Taiga's "service" framing has linear unit economics ? margin scales with headcount unless the AI genuinely zeroes the human review layer, which the 98% touchless number claims but no third party has validated. Eighteen months of compounding in this market structure does not produce monopoly pricing; it produces a tuck-in.

On definite plan, the counterfactual that would move Taiga a tier is a named two-to-five-year architectural commitment Guntupalli and Wax refuse to pivot from ? a specific payer-class certification depth, a proprietary denial-feedback corpus the founders commit to owning end-to-end, a stated refusal to expand beyond independent practices for 36 months. The dossier shows none of that. Two-week onboarding and "AI-native operations" are velocity claims, not definite-optimism claims. Absent that signal, I apply the iteration-mode default.

The single-anchor parallel for founder lineage is Dustin Moskovitz at Asana (2010), the Known Exception where I overrode missing structural moat because the founder had already shipped Facebook (2004) to scale. Guntupalli is listed as Class of 2028 at Penn ? he has not yet graduated college, let alone shipped a product to scale ? and Wax's record is the same shape. Founder-market fit through physician parents is genuine domain proximity, which is not nothing, but it is the Paul Graham weighting, not mine. Determination plus earnestness without operator pedigree or contrarian credentialed-outsider identity does not trigger the Asana override. There is no PayPal Mafia tie, no acquired side project, no Olympiad-class technical signal in the dossier.

Thirty-six months out, Taiga is acquired by Candid Health or Adonis as an independent-practice channel tuck-in for under $30M, or it winds down ? not because Guntupalli and Wax are unserious, but because the category is consolidating into the funded incumbents before Taiga can build the proprietary denial-corpus that would create switching costs. The mechanism is straightforward power-law math: in a market with three $95M+ competitors already shipping, the fourth entrant captures the long tail, and the long tail does not return a fund.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-pass is the SMB-independent-practice wedge as a genuine blind spot ? Candid Health's 200+ customer base is multi-site groups, Akasa's is hospitals, Adonis's is broader orchestration, and none of them have the cost structure to profitably serve one-doctor offices the way an AI-native service can. Both founders' parents run exactly that customer profile, which is rare domain exposure. If that wedge is real and Taiga compounds a denial-feedback corpus the incumbents structurally cannot replicate because they do not have the data shape, this becomes the Lyft (2010) override ? not monopoly, but durable second-place positioning in a category that supports two winners. That case stings because the customer-segment blind spot is plausible. It loses because no Known Exception in my file maps cleanly: Lyft had driver-side network effects from day one, Asana had Moskovitz's Facebook pedigree, Quora had Adam D'Angelo as CTO ? Taiga has neither network compounding nor operator pedigree, only segmentation. The condition that would flip my verdict is fifty signed independent-practice contracts with net retention above 120% by month 18, demonstrating the wedge compounds rather than commoditizes. Until then, pass.
