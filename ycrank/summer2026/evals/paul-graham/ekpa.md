# Ekpa -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Ekpa is a competent MIT pair with a polished retail-portfolio idea and no visible stunt, no shipped product, no schlep -- the exact inversion of the pattern I trust. Yuga Patel and Anant Asthana are two undergraduates whose LinkedIn headlines read "CS/AI + Econ @ MIT" and "Student -- Massachusetts Institute of Technology," which tells me their credentials and nothing about what Ekpa has actually built between the two of them. The site is a waitlist. Pricing is "forthcoming." That is a deck, not an artifact.

The Airbnb cereal-box story is the high rung on my animal-resourcefulness ladder because Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia sold Obama O's at $40 a box before an investor would look at them; Drew Houston shipped a Dropbox demo to Hacker News before the check; Brian Armstrong hand-built an Android wallet after Coinbase's rejection. The mechanism is the same in each: revenue or usage generated from nothing, before permission was granted. Ekpa's dossier gives me the exact opposite -- a pre-launch read-only mode, no waitlist size disclosed, no Product Hunt, no Discord, no press, no company Twitter, no GitHub for either Patel or Asthana. When I search for the tracks an animal leaves and find none, I do not assume the animal was quiet. I assume there was no animal.

The pass example I keep in my head is the two-MBA AI-wrapper team with the glossy deck and the $50B TAM slide, and Ekpa's failure mode rhymes with it even though these are MIT undergrads rather than MBAs. The mechanism there is: idea sourced from spotted opportunity, not from personal pain. Retail investors juggling brokerage holdings and SEC filings is a real problem for someone -- but does it read as a problem Yuga Patel personally has? He is an undergraduate. The dossier gives one flicker in the right direction: Anant Asthana wrote a blog post called "Autonomous AI Agents and Financial Trading: What's Real Today vs What's Hype," which is at least evidence he thought about this before he pitched it. That is a real point in Ekpa's favor. It is not a USB stick left at MIT. Drew Houston forgot the stick; Anant wrote a survey post. Different intensity.

Coinbase's compliance grind is the reason I keep returning to schlep blindness as a moat -- Armstrong ran toward the thing everyone else routed around. Ekpa is doing the reverse. The dossier notes that goekpa.com carries a disclaimer that Ekpa is "not a broker-dealer or investment adviser" while the product delivers "share-count recommendations tied to user holdings." That is flinching at the exact schlep that would have been the moat. If Ekpa had told me they were pursuing IA registration and building the compliance surface no LLM-wrapper competitor would touch, I would be leaning forward. Instead they are disclaiming it away, which means any of the five competitors named in the dossier -- Composer, Numerai, AlphaSense, LinqAlpha, QuantConnect -- or Robinhood's in-app AI can do the same thing tomorrow.

Ekpa's hacker genealogy would need to counterfactually include a public GitHub, a competitive-programming placement, or a shipped open-source artifact for the score on that dimension to flip a tier. Patrick Collison had the Young Scientist Lisp prize at 17 before Stripe; Aaron Swartz co-wrote the RSS specification before Reddit. The Ekpa dossier explicitly reports "No public repos found" for both Patel and Asthana and no Twitter account for either. MIT is a credential, not a hacker lineage. I have funded plenty of MIT students; the ones who worked were the ones whose GitHub predated the pitch.

The co-founder configuration is the one dimension where Ekpa lands cleanly in the mid-range of my portfolio. Two founders, both at MIT, presumably classmates -- Reddit was two MIT-adjacent roommates, Stripe was two brothers, and the shape of a strong pair usually comes through in the room. There is no dossier signal that Patel and Asthana dislike each other, and no signal they built anything together before Ekpa either. This is the default score, not a positive one.

My 36-month forecast for Ekpa: dead or acquihired into a mid-tier retail broker like Public.com or SoFi for low single-digit millions after failing to cross $500K ARR, because Robinhood and Fidelity will ship the same per-holding AI research summaries natively inside the brokerage app where the user already is, and the read-only overlay Ekpa proposes has no reason to be a separate product. The mechanism is the one from Black Swan Farming inverted: an idea that looks reasonable, with founders who have not yet demonstrated the animal test. That is the shape of the companies I lose money on.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-pass I can build for Ekpa: AlphaSense just closed a $350M round at $7.5B in July 2026, and LinqAlpha raised a $22M Series A in the same month with 70+ institutional customers, so AI-agents-reading-SEC-filings is a category with real dollars behind it and Ekpa could be the retail counterpart. I do not find this convincing. AlphaSense and LinqAlpha are priced by institutions that price accuracy; the retail-subscription-research SKU has historically belonged to Motley Fool and its imitators, not to LLM overlays, and the disclaimer language on goekpa.com tells me Ekpa is dodging the exact regulatory surface that would separate them from an LLM API contract. None of my Known Exceptions apply -- there is no cereal-box stunt overriding my idea skepticism, no force-of-personality Altman-style animal read, no prior Auctomatic-scale exit. Ekpa would flip to Invest for me on one verifiable condition: a shipped prototype with a public backtest of per-holding recommendation accuracy against 10-K filings, plus founders who have chosen to pursue IA registration rather than disclaim around it. Until then, pass.
