# Sequence Markets -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything you need to know before you evaluate anything else. Talos has raised $196 million and is valued at $1.5 billion. FalconX has raised $430 million and is valued at $8 billion. CoinRoutes has been operational since 2017. 1inch has raised $190 million. Every one of these companies is built on the identical thesis: digital-asset liquidity is fragmented across venues, and smart order routing aggregates it. When I ask "what is the secret?" -- what important truth does Sequence Markets see that Talos, FalconX, CoinRoutes, and 1inch do not see -- I get silence. The fragmentation of crypto liquidity is not a secret. It is the most obvious structural feature of the market. Every participant, every exchange operator, every institutional trader already knows this. A thesis that every well-funded competitor shares is not a thesis at all -- it is a consensus dressed in startup clothing.

The deeper problem is that smart order routing is not a zero-to-one technology. The dossier itself acknowledges that SOR is "a well-understood concept from traditional equities markets." This is a 1-to-n transfer -- taking execution infrastructure that has existed in equities for decades and porting it to crypto. Citadel Securities, Virtu, and the entire electronic trading ecosystem solved order routing in traditional markets long ago. Applying those ideas to a new asset class can be a viable business, but it is not a technological discontinuity, and it does not create monopoly. The claimed "sub-millisecond execution latency" and "AI-powered routing" are engineering claims, not architectural breakthroughs. Any well-capitalized competitor -- or any exchange itself -- can build comparable routing logic. When the barrier to replication is moderate engineering effort rather than a fundamental insight, you are in a competition, and competition destroys value for everyone involved.

The bull case would require something specific to be true: that the gap between incumbents focused on large institutions (Talos requires enterprise-scale commitments, FalconX bundles prime brokerage) and retail-accessible DeFi aggregators (1inch, which ignores centralized venues) creates a structural opening for a platform that serves both segments across both CeFi and DeFi. Peter Bai's market microstructure experience from TMX Group and a $12.8 billion pension fund gives him genuine domain knowledge -- he understands execution quality from the inside, which is more than most pre-seed crypto founders can claim. If the team could monopolize a specific underserved segment -- say, mid-tier crypto trading firms too small for Talos but too sophisticated for retail platforms -- and build a data flywheel that compounds routing quality with volume, there is a path to a defensible business. But a defensible business is not what I invest in. I invest in monopolies that can return the fund. The execution infrastructure segment of crypto trading is not a monopoly opportunity -- it is a feature that will eventually be absorbed by exchanges themselves or commoditized by open-source routing protocols.

The founder signal compounds my concern. Peter Bai has relevant experience, but the vision articulated -- "fastest and smartest trade execution infrastructure" -- is an optimization claim, not a definite plan for the future. His co-founder Muhammad Awan's YC tagline is "I like building cool projects." That sentence is the distilled essence of indefinite thinking. Where is the specific vision of what the world looks like if Sequence wins? Where is the founder who refuses to sell, who has a concrete theory of why this company will be the last mover in execution infrastructure twenty years from now? I do not see the intellectual conviction that Zuckerberg had about identity networks or that Musk had about reusable rockets. I see competent people solving an obvious problem in a crowded market -- which is precisely the profile of a company that will compete rather than monopolize, and competition is where returns go to die.

This is a pass. The thesis is consensus, the technology is incremental, the market is crowded with well-funded players who share the identical insight, and the founders -- while possessing relevant domain knowledge -- have not articulated a secret or a definite vision that would overcome the structural headwinds. A pre-seed company entering a market where competitors have raised a combined $850 million needs a genuinely non-obvious advantage to justify the bet. I see operational competence but no secret, and a company without a secret is a company without a monopoly.
