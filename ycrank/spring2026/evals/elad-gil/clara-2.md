# Clara -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Clara is George Favvas's second act at multi-state telehealth primary care -- the Circle Medical (YC S15) playbook re-run with an LLM drafting layer in front of the clinician -- which means Clara scores high on operator fit (the trio shipped to $100M ARR at Circle) and low on index position (D2C primary care is a market where Hims is already guiding to $2.7-2.9B in 2026 revenue with its own Labs AI agent). The signature is operator-led D2C category re-entry into a market where winners are not declared but consensus is forming -- not the Stripe (2010) "index on e-commerce" template, not the Anduril (2022) re-opening template, but closer to a Hims-flanker with better data integration and worse capitalization.

Founder execution is where Clara loads up on the ladder. The Brex (2017) archetype I track in *High Growth Handbook* (2018) is the operationally-disciplined CEO who has run the exact P&L before -- and George Favvas at Circle Medical scaled to $100M annualized and profitability over ten years with Caitlin Swift running clinical ops through ~1M appointments/year and 425 providers. That is not a pedigree pitch; that is the *prior shipped artifact* I screen for. Where Clara diverges from the HIGH rung is rhythm: I have no signal in the dossier that Favvas is shipping new product surface between investor meetings the way Aravind did at **Perplexity** (2022), and the two open roles (Data Engineer, Product Engineer) suggest a team still building the v1 surface rather than iterating one in flight. Operator pedigree, not founder-ships-between-meetings -- the Brex slot, not the Perplexity slot.

Lyft Series C is the failure pattern I keep returning to: a #2 position in a market where the #1 captures a multiple of the #2's economics, dressed up as a contrarian bet (20VC interview). Clara is not exactly Lyft -- D2C primary care is not winner-take-all by network effect, it is share-take by CAC and brand -- but the structural concern is the same. Hims is at $2.7-2.9B 2026 revenue guidance and shipped Labs AI in 2026; K Health raised $439M and has Northwell B2B2C distribution; Hippocratic AI is at $3.5B post-money selling into health systems; One Medical is owned by Amazon at $3.9B. Clara has $12M pre-seed and a 150,000-source EHR connectivity claim that almost certainly routes through Particle or Health Gorilla -- meaning the integration "moat" is rentable infrastructure. This is the Lyft-shape problem: Clara is fighting for share, not riding a rail.

LLM clinical reasoning crossed a practical threshold in 2025 and Epic/Athena/Cerner data portability matured in the same window -- that is a real catalyst, but it is the *same* catalyst every other AI-health pitch in this YC batch is invoking. The mechanism over the next 18 months: as the catalyst becomes consensus, the marginal entrant captures less of it; OpenEvidence already reports ~40% of US physicians using AI tools daily, which means the catalyst has been priced into incumbent roadmaps. Clara is entering a re-opened market roughly 18 months after the re-opening, not at the front edge the way Decagon hit post-LLM customer support in early 2023. Real catalyst, late entry.

Counterfactual on revenue velocity: to flip Clara from neutral to invest on this axis, I would need to see >20% MoM patient acquisition with 6-month retention matching Hims's membership cohorts, or a signed payer/employer distribution deal that converts the D2C funnel into B2B2C. The dossier has neither -- "No public pricing displayed on askclara.com," no user counts, no app-store data, YC Launch post live but no engagement metrics disclosed. In the AI cycle, silence on growth is itself a negative signal because real growth is easy to disclose ("if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" -- Tim Ferriss Show #863). Spring 2026 batch is early; this is the absent-evidence default, not an active red flag, but it does not earn a benefit-of-the-doubt premium either.

Single-miracle test, against the Decagon (2023) template where one validated capability (LLM accuracy at support) carries the rest. Clara requires: (1) LLM clinical accuracy good enough for routine sign-off, (2) clinician labor margin that doesn't collapse the D2C unit economics, (3) D2C CAC economics in a category Hims has bid up, (4) regulatory non-blowup across 50 state medical boards plus LegitScript. That is not one miracle -- the dossier itself flags that "Every recommendation requires a licensed clinician's review, capping the gross-margin advantage AI typically offers." This is structurally closer to Color Genomics (2015), where I architected workflow on top of commodity sequencing to *isolate* the miracle -- except Clara hasn't isolated it. Multiple miracles, compounding small odds.

Forecast, 36 months: Clara reaches $15-35M D2C ARR with healthy retention from a niche of complex-condition patients, gets squeezed between Hims (D2C scale + brand) and Hippocratic (B2B AI agent infrastructure), and either (a) sells to One Medical/Amazon or Hims for $150-400M as a vertical bolt-on, or (b) pivots from D2C to B2B2C white-label AI primary care infrastructure for self-insured employers and regional payers. The Favvas team is too disciplined to flame out and too constrained by the unit economics to compound into a defining company.

The bull case stings: Circle Medical alumni are the only team that has scaled multi-state telehealth primary care to $100M and stayed profitable -- everyone else is either condition-based (Hims), B2B2C-dependent (K Health), or enterprise-facing (Hippocratic), which leaves a genuinely vacant D2C AI primary care slot. The 50-state licensure plus LegitScript footprint took Circle Medical years to build and Clara inherits the institutional muscle; if AI clinical accuracy compounds faster than incumbent telehealth providers can rebuild their P&Ls around clinician-as-reviewer rather than clinician-as-revenue, Clara's "AI drafts, clinician signs" architecture becomes the format other incumbents have to retrofit against. That is the Anduril (2022) software-on-hardware pattern transposed onto healthcare: don't beat the practice, *be* the practice with the inverted labor model. The dismantle: AI Market Clarity (blog.eladgil.com, Nov 2025) flags D2C health as a category where revenue winners are already at scale, and Clara's unit economics are explicitly clinician-bound by the dossier's own admission -- that is margin-compression bet, not single-miracle bet. **Flip condition**: if Clara discloses >20% MoM revenue with retention curves at or above Hims membership cohorts within nine months, or signs an employer/payer distribution channel converting D2C into indexed B2B2C, I'd revisit and likely lead.
