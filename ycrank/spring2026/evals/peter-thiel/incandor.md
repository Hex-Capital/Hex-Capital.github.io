# Incandor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Incandor is a technically-articulated pure-software API attacking a regulated-customer category already owned by incumbents with $239M-$347M war chests and 60+ patents -- the rare profile where the "secret" is real but the monopoly geometry is upside-down. Matthew Yekell and Luc Rosenzweig have named a specific architectural blind spot in BioCatch and Feedzai (supervised label dependency, multi-week enrollment) and proposed a label-free behavioral map that works "from session one." That is a thesis. What it is not is a moat. Incandor's highest dimension is the contrarian secret; its lowest is monopoly trajectory; and that pairing describes a clever feature destined for acquisition, not a power-law company.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy for Incandor's contrarian-secret dimension because Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent failure mode -- PayPal's developer-hostile integration surface -- in a category the consensus had already declared closed. Incandor's claim that BioCatch and NuData structurally cannot ship label-free detection because their supervised-model contracts depend on historical fraud labels is the same shape of argument: an institutional reason an incumbent cannot cannibalize itself. But Incandor diverges from Stripe on a load-bearing detail: Collison's secret was about the *buyer* (developers as a new economic actor inside enterprises), while Incandor's secret is about *technique* (unsupervised vs supervised). Technique secrets get copied in nine months by any incumbent with capital; buyer-channel secrets compound for a decade. Yekell and Rosenzweig have a real thesis -- it is just not the kind that bends a market.

The pass-example pattern -- "better model" moats that any competitor replicates within a release cycle -- is exactly where Incandor's monopoly trajectory sits. BioCatch was acquired by Permira for $1.3B in May 2024 with 60+ patents; Feedzai raised $75M at a $2B valuation in October 2025 bundling behavioral biometrics with device fingerprinting and malware; NuData ships inside Mastercard rails as a default. Incandor's dossier surfaces zero patents, zero disclosed customers, zero published benchmarks behind the ">99% ATO accuracy" claim, and no evidence of a data network effect yet. The future-moat hypothesis -- cross-customer behavioral baselines compounding into ring detection -- is the same data-network promise every fraud-API pre-seed has pitched since 2015, and it requires hundreds of integrated platforms before it bites. Incandor enters a category where the consolidation already happened.

On definite plan vs indefinite iteration, no evidence appears in the dossier -- no multi-year roadmap, no named technical milestones, no architectural commitments Yekell has publicly refused to pivot from. The site says "Request early access." Applying the absent-evidence default, Incandor scores near the indefinite-optimism floor; a Spring 2026 YC company with a launch video and no disclosed customer dialog will, by base rate, iterate toward whatever the first three design partners ask for. That is the modal YC trajectory, and it is the modal Thiel pass.

Founder lineage is the dimension where Incandor most resembles the median YC peer rather than the Affirm/Asana operator-pedigree pattern. Rosenzweig's NVIDIA, Apple, and Epic Games tenure is real systems exposure -- relevant to the low-level mouse and keystroke telemetry capture problem -- but no shipped-to-scale product is attached to his name, and his GitHub shows one repo. Yekell's Vista and Cboe background is enterprise-financial-software-adjacent but not operator-pedigree in the Levchin or Moskovitz sense. Counterfactually: if either founder had shipped a fraud or auth product at NVIDIA, Stripe, or Plaid that I could point to, this dimension flips a tier and the overall verdict tightens. They have not.

**Anduril** (2017) is the single-anchor parallel for the atoms-heavy/regulated dimension, and the comparison cuts against Incandor. Palmer Luckey's defense customer was regulated *and* the product was hardware-capex-heavy -- the moat stacked at both the customer and the asset layer. Incandor's customer is regulated (banks subject to SR 11-7 model risk, GDPR Art. 9 biometric data, BIPA exposure) but the product is a JavaScript SDK and an API. The regulation cuts both ways: it slows Incandor's sales cycle without giving Incandor a certification moat any well-capitalized incumbent cannot also clear. Software-only in a regulated sector is a tax, not a barrier.

In 36 months Incandor is acqui-hired by a mid-tier fraud platform -- Sift, Socure, or a payments processor wanting an unsupervised-detection bolt-on -- for $20-60M, with the team absorbed into a larger product surface; the less likely tail is a pivot to bot-detection-as-API for non-bank SaaS where the regulated-buyer cycle does not gate them. The mechanism is direct: Incandor's secret is genuine but technique-shaped, the incumbents have the distribution and the labels, and a label-free behavioral SDK is a feature any acquirer would rather own than compete with.

The strongest bear-of-my-bear case is that BioCatch's supervised-model lock-in is structurally real -- their entire enterprise contract motion depends on historical-label consulting engagements that they cannot abandon without breaking revenue -- and that the next wave of neobanks, crypto exchanges, and embedded-finance platforms have no historical fraud labels to feed a supervised model, creating a label-free greenfield Incandor uniquely fits. The Stripe-shaped read is that Yekell is selling to a buyer the incumbents structurally cannot serve. Against this: Feedzai already raised $75M explicitly to extend into greenfield fintechs, and Mastercard's NuData is bundled free with rails the neobanks already use. The condition that would flip my verdict is a signed enterprise contract with a named tier-1 bank or a Stripe Radar / Plaid distribution partnership disclosed in the next twelve months -- absent that, Incandor is a feature, and features get acquired, not compounded.
