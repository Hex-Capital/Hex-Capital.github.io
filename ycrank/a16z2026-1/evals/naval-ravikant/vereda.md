# Vereda -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural dynamic that makes Vereda worth studying is the principal-agent problem embedded in its competitive landscape. Orbia — the dominant platform in Brazilian ag inputs — is owned 68.8% by Bayer, 14% by Yara, and 17.2% by Bravium. These are input manufacturers and distributors. They cannot disintermediate their own distribution networks without cannibalizing the revenue that funds their existence. This is the exact architecture that let Perplexity challenge Google: incumbents who possess the capability to build the better product but are agents of their own P&L, structurally unable to cannibalize themselves. João Souza and Pedro Galindo, as principals with no distributor relationships to protect, can move where Orbia's owners cannot. The R$167 billion distribution layer sitting between manufacturers and farmers is intermediary rent — and intermediary rent is what principals exist to eliminate.

Specific knowledge here is genuine but bounded. João grew up in a farming family and executed over $1 billion in agribusiness transactions at BTG Pactual. Pedro invested in agriculture at Patria, the most active PE firm in LatAm ag. They understand how input distribution actually works — the markups, the credit structures, the seasonal purchasing cycles, the document requirements (CAR, CCIR). This knowledge would take a Silicon Valley team years to accumulate. But it's relationship and market-structure knowledge, not the kind of deep technical specific knowledge that took Aravind Srinivas years at Google Brain to build. Another team with strong ag banking backgrounds could identify the same opportunity. What elevates Vereda above that objection is that they headquartered in Rio Verde — a Goiás agribusiness hub, not São Paulo or San Francisco. Agents optimize for their own comfort. Principals go where the problem lives. That location choice is a revealed preference worth more than any pitch deck slide.

The leverage audit reveals a hybrid architecture, which is where my enthusiasm moderates. The WhatsApp AI agent "Vera" is permissionless leverage on the demand side — zero marginal cost to reach the next farmer, zero app download friction, meets 99% of Brazilian smartphone users where they already live. But the supply side requires manufacturer-by-manufacturer negotiation, document verification, and demand aggregation that depends on operational execution. "Human support backs the AI agent" is the tell. Vereda is AI on the front end and operations on the back end. The comparison to my own AngelList investment is structurally precise: both aggregate fragmented demand from an underserved population, both disintermediate a gatekeeping layer, both make the supply side pay. But AngelList's back-end was software — deal flow, SPVs, and rolling funds all scaled through code. Vereda's back-end requires negotiating with Syngenta and Bayer's sales teams, which is labor leverage no matter how much AI you wrap around the farmer-facing side. The 6-8 person team claiming $155M GMV across 50+ cities in 45 days is extraordinary output-per-person if real — but "potential revenue" of $8M is not revenue. The word "potential" does significant work in that sentence.

Where Vereda hits hardest on my framework is access democratization. Small and mid-sized Brazilian farmers currently access inputs through distributors who capture roughly half the distribution flow. Vereda gives these farmers direct manufacturer pricing — claiming up to 30% cost reduction — for zero cost. Free to farmers, supplier-funded. This is the AngelList pattern: give the underserved side free access, charge the supply side for aggregated demand they couldn't reach alone. The WhatsApp-native channel is the distribution insight that web-first competitors like Agrofy ($66M raised) have missed — rural Brazilian farmers don't download apps, they send WhatsApp messages. When the channel matches the behavior, distribution becomes the moat.

The strongest bull case: this is a market where the principal-agent problem at incumbents is permanent and structural, the distribution insight (WhatsApp over web/app) is correct and defensible through speed of farmer relationship accumulation, and the embedded finance optionality — lending against proprietary demand and credit data — could transform a 5% take-rate marketplace into a fintech with durable margins. The compound interest test is promising: every farmer transaction generates credit data, every aggregated order strengthens manufacturer pricing, every satisfied farmer refers neighbors via the same WhatsApp channel they already use daily. If Vereda reaches critical mass in Goiás before Agrofy pivots to WhatsApp, the demand-side network effect could lock. The risk that would flip me: if the 45-day GMV sprint turns out to reflect a single safra purchasing window with no retention across crop cycles, or if manufacturers refuse to bypass distributors at scale due to contractual or relationship constraints. No public evidence of signed supplier agreements exists in the dossier, and a marketplace with $155M in stated GMV but zero confirmed supplier commitments is a demand aggregation engine without a supply engine.

Vereda earns an Invest lean driven primarily by access democratization — the structural parallel to AngelList's model of unlocking restricted resources for an underserved population — and the permanent principal-agent disadvantage facing incumbents like Orbia. The specific knowledge score reflects real but replicable domain expertise: João's farming roots and BTG deal flow are meaningful but not the non-transferable technical depth I look for in my highest-conviction bets. The leverage architecture's hybrid nature — AI front-end, operational back-end — prevents this from reaching the upper tier. At pre-seed with 6 people in Rio Verde, the founders have earned the right to prove the supply side works.
