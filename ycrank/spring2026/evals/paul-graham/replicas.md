# Replicas -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Replicas is that it's building in a space where competitors have collectively raised over a billion dollars. Devin alone sits on $900M at a $10B valuation. Factory has $50M. Augment Code has $252M. Normally I'd say the size of the opposition is irrelevant — Stripe won against established payment processors with orders of magnitude more resources. But Stripe succeeded because the schlep of PCI compliance, bank integrations, and regulatory paperwork created a moat that repelled everyone. The schlep was the strategy. What's the equivalent schlep here? Spinning up a VM, cloning a repo, running a CLI agent? Any competent infrastructure engineer can build that in a weekend. And the actual agent providers — Anthropic, OpenAI — can add native cloud execution to their own products trivially. OpenAI's Codex already runs in a cloud sandbox. This isn't a schlep that repels competitors. It's a feature request that attracts them.

Connor Loi is clearly a builder. The project list — a Monte Carlo ray tracer, a space RTS with gRPC/QUIC networking, a PR stacking tool, meaningful contributions to Helicone's AI Gateway — shows someone who builds things because that's what he does, not because someone told him to. That's a real signal. His 8 months on the Helicone core team shipping LLM observability infrastructure is directly relevant experience. He's a hacker building for hackers, which is my favorite configuration. But he's a solo founder, and I've said publicly many times that starting a startup is too hard for one person. You need someone to argue with, someone to cover when you're sick, someone who notices the thing you missed. Every time I've seen a solo founder succeed at scale, there was something almost superhuman about them — and the dossier doesn't show me that level of intensity. Connor's GitHub has 34 followers and his side projects have single-digit stars. That's fine for a student, but it's not the signal I look for when someone is going alone into a $10B competitor's market.

The deeper problem is that this idea sounds good to everyone. "Run AI coding agents in the cloud from Slack" — who disagrees with that? Name one smart engineer who hears this pitch and says "that will never work." You can't, because it obviously will work. That's why a dozen companies are already doing it. My entire framework for angel investing is built on the premise that the best ideas initially look wrong to most people, and the returns from ideas that look right to everyone have already been competed away. The "agent-agnostic" positioning is the one potentially interesting angle — if you're an engineering team that wants to use Claude Code for some tasks and Codex for others, you need a neutral orchestration layer, and neither Anthropic nor OpenAI will build that for the other's agent. But "neutral platform" is not a moat. It's a positioning statement. Open-source projects like Ruflo already combine these agents. Neutrality is table stakes, not a wedge.

The bull case would require something like this to be true: the major agent providers stay fragmented for years, none of them builds adequate cloud execution for their own tools, enterprise teams become deeply committed to multi-agent workflows where Replicas' Slack and Linear integrations create genuine switching costs, and Connor either finds extraordinary co-founders or proves to be one of those rare solo operators who can scale alone. If all of that holds, the "workflow integration layer" between engineering teams and their AI agents could be a meaningful business. I can see the shape of the opportunity — it's similar to how Retool became the default for internal tools even though anyone could build their own. But Retool worked because building internal tools was the schlep that repelled engineers. Cloud VM orchestration does not repel engineers. It excites them. That's the difference.

I also notice the absence of an origin story. The dossier doesn't tell me Connor kept running into this problem at Helicone and finally snapped and built the solution himself. It doesn't describe a specific moment of frustration. The problem framing uses market language — "engineering teams want to..." — not personal language. Maybe the story exists and just didn't make it into the research, but in my experience, founders who discovered their problem organically can't stop talking about the moment it clicked. The fact that one of his three named customers is his former employer Helicone reinforces my suspicion that this started more from proximity to a trend than from personal pain.

Connor has real technical talent and a genuine builder's disposition, and the market is unquestionably real. But a solo student founder with no prior founding experience, building in a consensus-hot space against billion-dollar incumbents, without a clear schlep advantage or organic discovery narrative — that's not a bet I'd make with my own money. The idea needs to be either wrong-looking enough to give him runway before competitors notice, or hard enough to give him a moat once they do. It's neither.
