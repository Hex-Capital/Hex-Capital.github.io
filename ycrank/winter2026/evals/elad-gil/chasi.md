# Chasi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Equipment dealers -- the companies that sell, rent, and service forklifts, cranes, and excavators -- operate in a trillion-dollar global distribution industry that most tech investors have never thought about for more than thirty seconds. Fleet utilization sits below 60%. Dealer staff are buried in phone calls and manual quoting. Billions in assets sit idle because the human bottleneck in the sales process means leads get dropped, quotes arrive late, and follow-ups never happen. This is the archetype I've seen produce outsized returns: a market that triggers instant dismissal from other investors. When I backed PagerDuty, "operations alerting" sounded boring. When I backed Gusto, "payroll software" sounded boring. Equipment dealer revenue optimization sounds boring too. That's the signal, not the noise. The question is whether the structural moment is real and whether this specific team can capture it.

The "why now" here has three legs, and they're all concrete. First, LLM capabilities crossed the reliability threshold for customer-facing voice and text interactions in 2024-2025 -- you couldn't have built autonomous AI agents that handle equipment sales calls with sufficient accuracy two years ago. Second, cloud-based DMS adoption hit 54% of new deployments in 2024, which means for the first time, a majority of dealer systems have API-accessible endpoints that AI agents can actually integrate with. Third, the U.S. construction boom ($223 billion in manufacturing construction, $40 billion annual run rate in data centers) is creating unprecedented demand on dealer capacity while labor remains constrained. These three forces converging at the same time create a genuine structural inflection -- not a static TAM that's been sitting there for a decade, but a market where the enabling conditions arrived in the last 18 months.

What I find most compelling about the founders is the specificity of their domain expertise. Akash and Sarman were both part of the founding team at Axion Ray, which is essentially "Datadog for manufacturers" -- they've already deployed AI systems at Cummins, Boeing, Harley Davidson, and Medtronic. They understand industrial procurement cycles, the integration challenges of connecting to fragmented legacy systems, and what it takes to earn trust from companies that move slowly and demand reliability. This isn't a team that read about equipment dealers last month and decided to build an AI wrapper. Sarman built supply chain orchestration software as a founding engineer at Tesla. Akash designed tractors and industrial automation before running GTM for industrial AI products. The claim that they're "live across equipment businesses in the US and Europe" is consistent with a team that can leverage existing relationships to get early deployments quickly.

The competitive dynamics deserve honest examination. Quipli launched Quinn, an AI revenue engine trained on "millions of transactions" from their existing customer base -- that's a real data advantage with built-in distribution. But Quinn is only available to Quipli's own customers, which constrains its market. Chasi's integration-first approach -- plugging into any existing dealer stack rather than requiring platform migration -- is a fundamentally different distribution strategy. It mirrors the Stripe playbook: don't force merchants to switch payment processors, just give developers a better API that works with everything. The fragmented DMS landscape (CDK, Texada, InTempo, Baseplan, and dozens more) actually favors the integrator over the bundled platform, because no single DMS vendor has more than a fraction of the market. That said, building and maintaining integrations across this fragmented ecosystem is engineering-intensive, and Chasi is a two-person team. Execution velocity matters enormously here -- the window to build integration depth before larger players enter is probably 18-24 months.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I see latent upside that might not be obvious from the current product. If Chasi's AI agents are embedded in a dealer's customer-facing interactions -- handling sales inquiries, rental requests, service scheduling -- they occupy the most strategically valuable position in the dealer's workflow: the revenue intake funnel. From that position, expanding into fleet utilization optimization, dynamic pricing recommendations, parts ordering intelligence, or even financing and insurance referrals becomes a natural extension through the same integration. The dealer relationship becomes a distribution channel for multiple revenue streams. The founders haven't explicitly articulated this multi-product vision in available materials, but the market structure supports it, and the Axion Ray experience (which expanded from quality detection into adjacent industrial workflow products) suggests they understand platform-building in industrial contexts.

The concern I can't fully resolve is commoditization risk. LLM-powered sales agents are broadly available technology. The domain-specific integration work and data accumulation create a moat, but it's a moat that takes time to build, and a two-person team has finite capacity to build integrations across dozens of DMS platforms while simultaneously acquiring customers and improving the AI agents. The single miracle here is something like "can we build enough integration depth and dealer trust fast enough to create switching costs before this becomes a feature that DMS vendors add to their own platforms?" It's achievable, but it requires speed. The parallel to Harvey is instructive -- Harvey was building workflow and integration depth ahead of the capability curve, so when GPT-4 shipped, they were positioned to capture the moment. Chasi is similarly positioned at the capability threshold, and the founders' industry relationships from Axion Ray give them a plausible path to rapid deployment. I'd want to see customer count and engagement depth to move from leaning-in to full conviction, but the structural setup is sound.
