# Ruma Care -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ruma Care is who else is in the room. Cohere Health has raised $200 million. Waystar is public with a billion dollars in revenue. Develop Health just closed a $14 million Series A. Basys.ai is training on Mayo Clinic data. And these are just the named competitors — the CMS prior authorization mandate (CMS-0057-F), effective January 2027, is a public signal visible to every healthcare entrepreneur, every EHR vendor, and every incumbent revenue cycle management company simultaneously. When I ask "what is the secret here?" — the truth about the world that most intelligent people actively reject — I cannot find one. That prior authorizations are broken is not a secret. It is the most consensus view in healthcare technology. That biologics are expensive and denials are costly is obvious to anyone who has spent five minutes in a rheumatology clinic. When a federal regulation creates the "why now," there is no informational edge — the timing advantage belongs to everyone.

The founders exhibit what Paul Graham would recognize as strong "schlep blindness" — Meng Fei Shen embedded himself as a medical assistant in a rheumatology clinic, and Christina Huang has spent seven years navigating biologics denials as a patient. This is genuine domain immersion, and in Graham's framework it would score highly. But domain immersion is not the same as a contrarian secret. Knowing the pain of a broken process deeply does not mean you have a non-obvious insight about how to create a monopoly from fixing it. The founders' plan — extract payer rules, auto-fill forms, learn from denial patterns — is a well-specified workflow automation. It is definite in the sense that they know exactly what they are building. But it is definite in the way an engineering project is definite, not in the way SpaceX was definite. Musk did not merely automate an existing rocket production workflow; he redesigned rocketry from first principles against the active opposition of every aerospace engineer alive. The Ruma founders are automating faxes.

The technology question compounds the concern. They report a 20x reduction in staff time per authorization and a 50% reduction in denial rates — impressive operational metrics if sustained at scale. But the underlying capability is rule extraction, form auto-fill, and pattern recognition on insurance denials. This is competent machine learning applied to a structured domain. It is not a technological discontinuity. It does not make a previously impossible thing possible. Any well-funded team with EHR integration access and healthcare ML expertise could build a comparable system within eighteen months, and the dossier itself acknowledges this. The CMS FHIR API mandate will further commoditize the integration layer by 2027, lowering barriers for every subsequent entrant. When regulatory standardization is the enabling force behind your product, the technology moat is being eroded by the same policy that created your market.

The strongest argument for Ruma Care, and the dimension where my framework gives them the most credit, is their beachhead strategy. Biologics in infusion clinics is a deliberately narrow niche with extreme willingness to pay — $80-150K per patient per year makes even a small improvement in authorization success enormously valuable to a clinic. Starting in rheumatology, dermatology, gastroenterology, and neurology clinics in California and Nevada, then expanding to other specialty medications, is the concentric-circles approach I advocate. If Ruma could truly monopolize biologics PA before anyone else builds comparable depth in formulary intelligence, they would own a small but lucrative market. The Ruma Formulary Navigator as a free top-of-funnel tool is a clever distribution mechanism. For this to work as a great investment, several things would need to be true: the biologics PA niche would need to be sufficiently distinct from general PA that horizontal competitors cannot easily specialize into it; the denial pattern data would need to compound into a genuine proprietary advantage that grows faster than competitors can replicate; and the EHR vendors would need to remain uninterested in building native biologics-specific PA tools. I find none of these conditions particularly likely. Epic alone could render the entire category moot with a product update.

This is a company built around a consensus problem with competent execution and good market positioning. In many investment frameworks, that would be sufficient. In mine, it is not. The absence of a genuine secret means the company will compete — and competition, by definition, erodes the monopoly profits that produce power-law returns. A company that makes prior authorizations faster is valuable. It is not zero to one.
