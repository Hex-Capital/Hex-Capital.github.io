# qomplement -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about qomplement isn't the document filling idea itself — it's the competitive structure of the market they're entering. Nanonets is at $100M revenue. Rossum has $104.6M raised and $44.9M in revenue. Reducto just closed a $75M Series B. These companies all process the same document types qomplement touches, and the distance from "extracting data from a PDF" to "filling data into a PDF" is an incremental product feature, not a separate market. When I see a pre-seed company positioning itself as the inverse of what well-funded incumbents do, I have to ask: is this a structural market opening, or is it a feature gap that closes the moment one of those incumbents adds a "fill" button? The dossier itself flags this: "extraction vendors could extend downstream." I've seen this pattern repeatedly — a startup identifies a real workflow gap, builds a point solution, and then the adjacent platform player absorbs it as a feature update. The question is whether qomplement can build enough workflow stickiness before that happens, and nothing in the evidence gives me confidence they can.

The market timing test is where this falls apart for me. The structural shift here — LLMs reaching accuracy thresholds for document processing — is real but completely obvious. Every IDP company in the competitive landscape is riding the exact same wave. There's no nonobvious window. When I backed Perplexity, the structural catalyst (AI capability crossing a search-quality threshold) was visible to me but invisible to most investors who'd been burned by prior "AI search" failures. Here, the catalyst is visible to everyone — Nanonets, Rossum, Reducto, and dozens of others are already capitalizing on it. The "filling vs. extraction" framing is a product positioning choice, not a structural shift. And the local inference angle (quantized models on 8-16GB RAM) is interesting architecturally but creates deployment friction — requiring enterprise users to have specific hardware specs is a support burden that works against the frictionless adoption you need at pre-seed.

On the infrastructure test, qomplement is plainly an application, not a platform layer. Compare to Reducto, which is explicitly an "API-first infrastructure layer for other AI companies" — that's the picks-and-shovels play in document AI. qomplement builds on top of infrastructure layers; it doesn't provide one. The workflow builder with database connectors adds some integration depth, but this is standard SaaS capability, not a platform that other companies build their products on. I look for companies that become progressively harder to remove from customer workflows — Stripe embedded in payment flows, Gusto embedded in payroll operations. A document filling tool with nine database connectors doesn't reach that level of integration depth, especially when competing workflow automation platforms (UiPath, Microsoft Power Platform) already offer adjacent capabilities.

The strongest bull case I can construct: document filling is genuinely a neglected workflow — the "last mile" problem where extracted data needs to flow back into regulatory forms, tax templates, and compliance PDFs. If that last mile is painful enough and specific enough that extraction vendors deprioritize it, qomplement could own the niche. The local-first architecture creates a real compliance advantage in regulated industries where cloud data residency is a barrier. And the Latin American market entry is tactically smart — less competition, genuine enterprise pain, and Kerim's Contalink experience gives him authentic exposure to LatAm document workflows. If they could demonstrate that enterprise customers in regulated industries are choosing them specifically because of the local execution model, that would shift my assessment. But the evidence isn't there — one transport client testimonial (savings figure, not revenue) and press coverage limited to Mexican publications. The pivot from a general-purpose computer agent to document filling is less than a year old, and the prior product metrics don't transfer.

The founder profile compounds my concern. Both founders graduated around 2024 from Tec de Monterrey. Kerim's Contalink exposure gives him relevant domain context, but Growsphere was a 5-person bootstrapped venture — not the kind of scaling experience that prepares you to navigate enterprise sales cycles against incumbents with $100M+ in funding. The CTO has no publicly identifiable prior ventures. I'm not dismissing young founders — I've seen first-time founders build extraordinary companies when the market structure is overwhelmingly in their favor. But when the market structure isn't offering a clear nonobvious opening, founder experience becomes the tiebreaker, and it doesn't break in their favor here. This is a team that needs to out-execute well-funded incumbents in a market where the structural advantages aren't stacked for a new entrant.

Pass. The filling niche is real but too easily absorbed by adjacent players. The timing is obvious, not nonobvious. The company is an application, not infrastructure. I'd rather invest in the company building the document AI infrastructure layer that both extraction and filling tools depend on.
