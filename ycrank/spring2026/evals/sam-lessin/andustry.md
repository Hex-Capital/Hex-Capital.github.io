# Andustry -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the fundamental problem with Andustry: the founder is sane, and the idea is also sane. Johann Sturken has a family background in industrial goods trading, mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM, McKinsey aerospace consulting, Airbus strategy work -- this is someone who genuinely understands how industrial procurement works from the inside. Het Dave brings SaaS-to-AI transformation experience. These are credible, well-credentialed operators. But "AI to automate procurement" is not an insane idea. It's the single most obvious application of LLMs to enterprise workflows. AI procurement adoption is projected at 86% by end of 2026. Matchory raised $8.86 million. LightSource Labs raised $33 million at a $130 million valuation. Cavela raised $8.6 million. MESH Works has $884 million in RFQs on their platform. When I look at this landscape, I see a sane person walking into a crowded room where everyone already had the same idea. The non-consensus premium -- which is where all my returns come from -- is zero.

Now run the AI kill test. This is where it gets ugly. The entire value proposition is AI-powered: AI searches suppliers, AI generates RFQs, AI follows up, AI compares quotes. Strip out the AI and there is literally no business. That's the opposite of my "cherry on top" framework. The AI isn't the cherry -- it's the cake, the plate, and the table. When I invested in Craftwork, the moat was painting crews on ladders. Software made them more efficient, but no competitor could clone the operational infrastructure overnight. With Andustry, every piece of the stack -- LLM-powered RFQ generation, supplier matching, specification extraction -- is reproducible by any team with API access to the same foundation models. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." The 500K+ verified supplier database is the one potential moat, but the dossier notes its "proprietary vs. aggregated status" is unknown. My strong prior: if it were proprietary, they'd lead with that. Aggregated public data is not a moat.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the industrial machinery vertical is real and underserved. Xometry went public at a $2.1 billion market cap, proving manufacturing marketplaces can produce venture-scale outcomes. Johann's family connection to industrial goods trading is the kind of founder-market fit that you can't fake -- he likely understands the actual workflow pain of calling fifty suppliers for a CNC machine in a way that a Stanford CS grad never would. And EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume at pre-seed, with two people, is a real signal that someone is actually using this thing to move actual money. If Andustry can build a genuinely proprietary quality and trust-score dataset on industrial suppliers -- verified through actual transaction outcomes rather than scraped from directories -- that data network effect could eventually compound into something defensible. The question is whether they can reach that density before the five-plus well-funded competitors eat the same market from different entry points.

But I don't think they can, and here's why. Xometry's moat isn't software -- it's that Xometry actually manufactures things. It operates a network of machine shops. There's physical-world operational complexity that software alone can't replicate. Andustry is pure digital intermediation. It sits between buyers and suppliers adding an AI matching layer, but it doesn't control any physical infrastructure, doesn't hold inventory, doesn't perform quality inspections on-site (unlike MESH Works, which does in-person supplier audits in 40+ countries). As AI gets cheaper and more capable, the matching layer becomes less valuable, not more. Any supplier directory -- including Thomasnet, which already has massive buyer traffic -- can bolt on an AI RFQ agent in months. This is the structural position I warn about constantly: building where the software IS the business means you're building where AI will destroy you fastest. The industrial goods market is enormous and inefficient, but the right play here is a company with physical-world operations that uses AI as a tool -- not a company that is the AI tool.

On the primitive test: Andustry is an application, not a protocol. It's not creating a new financial primitive or a layer that other businesses build on top of. It's a marketplace, and marketplaces in high-value industrial purchasing face a classic disintermediation problem -- once a buyer finds a reliable supplier through Andustry, what stops them from going direct for the next order? The narrative can theoretically expand from industrial machinery to broader industrial procurement, but that narrative expansion runs directly into LightSource Labs and Keelvar, who are already executing on enterprise-wide procurement AI with significantly more capital. The infinity story here is actually quite finite.

I respect the founder credentials, particularly Johann's. In a different structural configuration -- say, if they were building an industrial goods company that used AI to optimize their own procurement operations, or if they were creating a quality certification protocol that other platforms had to integrate with -- I'd be much more interested. But an AI software layer on top of public supplier data, competing against five well-funded teams in the most consensus AI application category imaginable, is precisely the bet I've been publicly arguing against for two years.
