# Sponge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Sponge: Mastercard unveiled Agent Pay, Visa launched Intelligent Commerce, PayPal built an agent toolkit, Coinbase shipped agent wallet tools, and Stripe -- the founders' own former employer -- launched x402 for USDC agent payments on Base. All in the last twelve months. When I look at an investment, I ask myself: is this unpopular? Is it difficult to assess? Does it have real but surmountable technology risk? Sponge fails the first question decisively. This is one of the hottest categories in fintech right now. Four direct competitors have raised a combined $40 million. Every major payments incumbent is building in this direction. The weirdness has been completely arbitraged away. When everyone at the party is talking about the same opportunity, I've learned that's exactly when I should be looking somewhere else.

I want to know the founders' stories -- not their resumes, their stories. How did three people arrive at the conviction that AI agents need their own financial infrastructure? What I have instead is a set of credentials: Stripe Crypto, University of Waterloo, University of Toronto, internships at Facebook and Google. These are impressive on paper, but they tell me about capability, not about obsession. I don't see the biographical thread that connects these specific humans to this specific problem in a way that would sustain them through the inevitable valley. When I backed the Crusoe team, they had independently stumbled onto an insight about waste gas and computation that was rooted in something they'd personally witnessed and couldn't stop thinking about. With Sponge, what I see is three talented engineers who noticed a market category forming at their employer and decided to build the startup version of what Stripe might build internally. That's not the kind of founder-problem fusion that produces the companies I'm drawn to.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If you believe -- and it's reasonable to believe -- that autonomous agents will handle trillions of dollars in transactions within a decade, then someone needs to build the plumbing. These three founders literally built Stripe's stablecoin infrastructure. They know payments at the deepest architectural level. The two-sided marketplace design (Wallet for agents, Gateway for businesses, Catalog as discovery) is more complete than what any single competitor offers. And there's a version of this where Stripe's x402 becomes enabling infrastructure that Sponge builds on top of, rather than a competing product -- the way Twilio built on top of carrier infrastructure rather than competing with AT&T. If Sponge can establish the marketplace network effect before incumbents build the full stack, they could become the standard that agents transact through. The Amazon beta pilot is an interesting early signal. That's the scenario where this becomes a defining company.

But I keep circling back to my core concern: this looks like a consensus bet in a crowded category, built by founders chasing a hot market rather than solving a problem born from personal experience. When I think about the companies in my portfolio that worked -- Uber, SpaceX, Flock Safety -- the founders weren't responding to a category that VCs and incumbents had already validated. They were creating categories that didn't exist yet, often against active resistance. Sponge is building in a lane where Stripe, their former employer with millions of merchant relationships, could decide any Tuesday to ship a complete agent payments product. The Stripe Crypto background is simultaneously the strongest credential and the biggest vulnerability -- they know exactly how Stripe thinks, which means Stripe knows exactly how to build what they're building.

I also notice that this company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations in any obvious way. It's infrastructure for AI agents to spend money on behalf of enterprises and developers. That's valuable if it works, but it doesn't have the quality I look for where a company opens doors for people who were previously locked out. Uber gave drivers income. Affirm gave consumers transparent credit. Postmates created work for delivery people. Sponge makes it easier for software to transact with other software. That's a real engineering problem, but it doesn't produce the same visceral pull for me.

The co-founder dynamic is the one dimension where I feel genuine comfort. Three people who built together on the same team at Stripe Crypto, who chose each other deliberately. That shared working history matters -- they've already navigated deadlines, technical disagreements, and organizational politics together. It's not the same as surviving a real crisis, but it's substantially better than co-founders who met at a networking event. I just wish that strong team alignment were in service of a problem that felt more personal to them and less like a category play.
