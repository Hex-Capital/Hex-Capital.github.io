# Sponge -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Sponge: the idea isn't insane. Three ex-Stripe Crypto engineers building payments infrastructure for AI agents sounds like the most consensus pitch in Silicon Valley right now. Mastercard unveiled Agent Pay. Visa launched Intelligent Commerce. PayPal, Stripe, and Coinbase all shipped agent payment toolkits. Their own former employer launched x402 for USDC agent payments on Base in February 2026. When every incumbent in the payments stack is racing into your category, you're not pursuing a non-consensus idea — you're sprinting alongside the herd. My sane-person-insane-idea filter requires both components. The founders are clearly sane — building Stripe's stablecoin financial accounts and core money movement systems is about as credible a background as you can have for this exact company. But credible founders pursuing an obvious opportunity is precisely the setup I avoid, because the returns are already priced into the market's expectations.

The structural moat question is where this really falls apart for me. Sponge is a software product — a TypeScript SDK, an MCP integration, a marketplace listing page. Every component is replicable within months. The "two-sided marketplace network effect" argument is the standard pitch for why a thin software layer between two sides of a market becomes defensible, and I've heard it a thousand times. Sometimes it works. But the specific problem here is that Stripe already has millions of merchants. If Stripe decides their x402 integration needs a wallet product, a gateway product, and a catalog — and the founders know better than anyone how Stripe thinks about product expansion — Sponge gets absorbed as a feature. This is exactly the category where AI doesn't create a moat; AI IS the reason the category exists, and everyone with distribution will build into it. My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is definitively no. Without autonomous AI agents, there is no agent payments infrastructure to build. That's the reddest flag in my framework.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If you believe — and it's not unreasonable — that the agent economy will look more like the early internet than like a feature update to existing payments, then the right analogy isn't "Stripe adds a feature" but "PayPal emerges alongside eBay." In that world, Sponge's Wallet + Gateway + Catalog combination could become the transactional primitive for a genuinely new economic layer. The Catalog especially interests me — a marketplace of pay-per-use API services for agents could compound in the way that app stores compound, where more services attract more agents attract more services. The Amazon beta pilot, the AgentMail integration, the early supply-side partnerships — these are exactly the kind of seeds that, in the Venmo story, preceded explosive adoption. If I squint, this team from Stripe Crypto building the payment layer for a new economic topology rhymes with Anatoly Yakovenko leaving Qualcomm to build a new Layer 1. The difference is that Solana was building a genuine new primitive at the protocol layer. Sponge is building an application layer on top of existing payment rails and blockchain infrastructure that other people control.

That distinction — primitive versus application — is decisive for me. Stripe's x402 is the primitive. Coinbase's agent wallet tools are the primitive. Sponge is building a user experience and marketplace layer on top of these primitives, which means its ceiling is fundamentally capped by the platforms it depends on. I backed Venmo because peer-to-peer mobile payments were a new financial primitive. I backed Aleo because zero-knowledge proofs were a new cryptographic primitive. Sponge isn't creating a new way for value to move — it's creating a dashboard and SDK for value that already moves through Stripe, Coinbase, and Ethereum. The multi-chain architecture sounds differentiated until you realize that multi-chain support is table stakes for any crypto-adjacent fintech in 2026.

The competitive density is also alarming. Four funded competitors — Skyfire at $9.5M with a16z and Coinbase Ventures backing, Natural at $9.8M, Payman at $13.8M with Visa and Coinbase, Nevermined at $7M — all have more capital and most have deeper distribution partnerships. When a pre-seed company enters a category where multiple competitors already have eight-figure backing from strategic investors who ARE the payment rails, the margin for error disappears. The founders' Stripe pedigree is real, but it's not enough to overcome the structural dynamics of building an application layer in a market where the infrastructure providers are your most dangerous competitors. I'd want to see evidence that the Catalog is generating real transaction volume and organic supply-side growth before this becomes interesting to me.
