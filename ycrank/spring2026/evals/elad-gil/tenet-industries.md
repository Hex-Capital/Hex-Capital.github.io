# Tenet Industries -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Helsing has raised €1.37B and sits at a €12B valuation. Stark Defence has ~$100M in funding, a unicorn-founder CEO (Florian Seibel, ex-Quantum Systems), and Peter Thiel and Sequoia on the cap table. Rheinmetall is already producing 500-1,000 drones per month. Tenet Industries is a four-person team in Stockholm with YC-level funding entering this same market. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was considered uninvestable by Silicon Valley — the nonobvious window was wide open and well-capitalized competitors hadn't entered yet. Tenet Industries enters in 2026, after defense tech had "its best funding year ever in 2025," after Germany awarded €900M in drone brigade contracts to Helsing, Stark, and Rheinmetall, and after every major European VC has published a defense thesis. The structural shift that created this market — Ukraine's battlefield consumption of thousands of drones monthly, NATO expansion including Sweden's March 2024 accession, European commitments to 3.5% GDP defense spending — is real and documented. But it is no longer nonobvious. The timestamp on this market says "consensus as of mid-2025."

Tenet Industries' manufacturing-automation thesis — designing the drone around the factory rather than the reverse — is where the potential nonobviousness lives, and I want to take it seriously. Ukraine's war has revealed that the binding constraint on expendable munitions isn't drone sophistication but production volume at low unit cost. Helsing competes on AI autonomy. Stark competes on software-defined capability. Tenet Industries claims to compete on factory throughput across multiple airframe types: FPV quadcopters, fixed-wing, Shahed-type, and missile-class systems from a unified production platform. This maps loosely to the Gusto pattern I've seen work — boring, operationally critical infrastructure that prestige-seeking founders ignore. Payroll isn't glamorous, but its universality and regulatory complexity create defensibility. Similarly, the factory that produces 10,000 sub-$1,000 drones per month might be less exciting than the AI-guided autonomous platform, but it solves the actual operational bottleneck NATO procurement officers face. The infrastructure-layer test is partially met by the multi-form-factor platform claim — if Tenet Industries genuinely builds a manufacturing system that other programs depend on for airframe production, that's closer to picks-and-shovels than point product. But at pre-seed with four people, this is a slide deck claim, not demonstrated capability, and I score it accordingly.

Emil Falk is the most credible signal on the team. His WindowGlass Recycling won Venture Cup "Startup of the Year" in 2023 and deployed production machines at four Ragn-Sells facilities by autumn 2024 — taking hardware from concept through automated manufacturing to commercial operation. That's directly relevant to Tenet Industries' design-for-automation thesis. Hugo Frisk brings self-taught electronics design, which is table stakes for drone hardware. Fabian Andersson has no retrievable public credentials. What I don't see — and what matters enormously in defense — is anyone with procurement experience, military relationships, or defense industry credibility. When I look at Stark Defence, I see Seibel's track record building Quantum Systems into a defense drone company with government contracts. Tenet Industries has no visible defense network. Sweden's SEK 4B (~$440M) unmanned combat procurement program is a real beachhead opportunity, but winning government evaluation contracts requires navigating qualification processes, security clearances, and institutional relationships that take years to build. No advisors or board members with defense backgrounds were identified in the dossier.

Tenet Industries fails my single-miracle test. Count the breakthroughs required: (1) the manufacturing automation technology must work and deliver genuine cost/speed advantages over both purpose-built competitors and commodity Chinese/Ukrainian supply, (2) the team must win defense procurement contracts against competitors with 100x-1,000x more capital and established government relationships, (3) the products must achieve battlefield validation or equivalent credibility — Ukrainian competitors like Fire Point and Terminal Autonomy are battle-tested, White Stork drones have been delivered to Ukraine, and Tenet Industries has zero combat provenance, and (4) export controls under Wassenaar and EU dual-use regulations must be navigated across multiple NATO markets. That's at least three independent miracles compounding. My Anduril exception applied when the market structure signal was so overwhelming it overrode multi-miracle risk — but Anduril entered an empty field. Tenet Industries enters a field with Helsing, Stark, Rheinmetall, and a dozen Ukrainian startups already occupying it.

The strongest bull case: Tenet Industries is the manufacturing-layer play in a market where everyone else is competing on the autonomy layer. Sweden's NATO accession and $440M drone procurement create a geographic beachhead with home-country advantage. Emil Falk has proven he can take hardware from prototype to production deployment. The expendable munition market is growing at 19.9% CAGR to $13.26B by 2030, and the actual bottleneck is production volume, not AI sophistication. If Tenet Industries can demonstrate 5-10x cost advantage per unit through manufacturing automation, procurement officers will buy on price regardless of brand prestige. This is a genuine argument, and it's why I don't put Tenet Industries in Strong Pass territory. But the bull case requires the manufacturing miracle to be so dramatic that it overrides the capitalization, credibility, and procurement-access disadvantages — and there's no evidence in the dossier that the manufacturing platform exists beyond a YC company page description. One verifiable condition would flip me: a signed evaluation contract or LOI with Försvarsmakten (Swedish Armed Forces) tied to the SEK 4B procurement program. That would validate both the manufacturing approach and the procurement access in one signal.

Tenet Industries lands in the lower range of Neutral for me. The market structure is real but consensus — I'm scoring nonobvious market timing at 12/30, which is my default for absent evidence of nonobviousness because the defense drone market is now the most popular category in European venture. The manufacturing-platform thesis has infrastructure-layer characteristics that push above my default, but it's entirely unproven at this stage. The multi-miracle requirement — technology, procurement, and battlefield validation simultaneously — is the dimension that most constrains my score, compounded by a founder team that lacks visible defense procurement expertise or networks. The margin dynamics of expendable hardware munitions sold via government contracts are structurally reasonable (repeat consumption, switching costs post-qualification) but unknowable at pre-seed. This is a real market with a defensible thesis buried inside it, but the timing advantage has been captured by players with orders-of-magnitude more capital, and the team hasn't yet demonstrated the defense-specific capabilities needed to compete.
