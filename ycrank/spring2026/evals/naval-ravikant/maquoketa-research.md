# Maquoketa Research -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything about the leverage problem here. Helsing has raised €1.37B. Anduril has raised $3.5B+. Stark secured a €268M German contract despite failing all four test strikes. When a company that can't hit its targets still wins nine-figure procurement deals, the market is telling you that relationships, timing, and incumbency advantages dominate technical excellence. A five-person pre-seed team building attack drones is entering a game where the rules are written by procurement officers, not by physics. This is the opposite of the markets where I look for asymmetric upside -- it's a market where consensus capital has already arrived at scale, and the pricing of deals reflects that optimism. "Anyone in this business who's chasing hot markets gets killed."

The specific knowledge question is where this gets interesting, but only partially. Alejandro Hernandez's background at Berkeley Wireless Research Center specializing in phased array systems and RF design is legitimate non-teachable knowledge. You don't learn phased array antenna design from a MOOC or six months of self-study -- it's deep electromagnetics, years of lab work, the kind of knowledge that exists outside standard pipelines. If I'm evaluating pure technical depth, the CTO passes the test. But specific knowledge has to be evaluated against the full problem, not just one layer of it. Building autonomous attack drones requires RF electronics AND defense procurement navigation AND autonomous targeting systems AND manufacturing at scale AND ITAR compliance AND security clearances. Hernandez's knowledge covers one critical layer. Yeager's EECS background and trading-tech experience at SpiderRock covers general software capability, not defense. Williams has no public information at all. Nobody on this team appears to have military experience, defense industry tenure, or prior work with DoD procurement. The team has depth in one dimension and gaps in several others that matter enormously in this specific market.

The leverage architecture is my fundamental concern. Each drone is manufactured, assembled, tested, and sold as a physical unit. Revenue scales linearly with production -- more drones require more materials, more assembly, more quality control. This is the structural opposite of code leverage or protocol leverage. One engineer at Replit writes code that gives millions of people the ability to program. One engineer at Maquoketa helps build drones that must be individually produced and destroyed on use. The autonomy software layer could theoretically create compounding value -- if flight data from each deployment improves the next generation's targeting and EW resistance, that's a data flywheel. But there's no evidence of this architecture, and the "autonomous" framing may be constrained by DoD Directive 3000.09's human oversight requirements for lethal systems anyway. Even the Opendoor exception in my portfolio had algorithmic pricing creating qualitative new capability in an unleveraged industry -- here, the leverage case remains hypothetical.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. Defense procurement is genuinely restructuring. Germany's €9B+ went to startups over primes. AeroVironment can't scale Switchblade production fast enough, with their new factory not operational until late 2026 or 2027. The incumbent primes face business-model cannibalization -- Lockheed and Raytheon's profit models depend on high-margin platforms, making cheap expendable drones structurally unattractive for them. If Maquoketa's in-house RF and phased array capability produces drones with meaningfully superior EW resistance -- the ability to operate in jammed, contested electromagnetic environments -- that's a technical differentiator that matters on the battlefield in ways that procurement relationships alone can't replicate. The bull case is that in the next 24 months, the U.S. military realizes its supply of EW-resistant autonomous munitions is catastrophically insufficient, and a small team with genuine RF depth gets pulled into contracts through SBIR, OTA, or YC's defense network faster than the traditional procurement timeline would suggest. If that happens, the phased array moat compounds with deployment data and this becomes a serious company. For this to work, though, the team needs someone who understands how to sell to the DoD -- and there's no evidence of that capability today.

What I keep coming back to is the infrastructure-versus-application question. My portfolio bias is toward picks-and-shovels plays -- Alchemy over individual DeFi apps, Stack Overflow over individual developer tools. The equivalent play in defense drones would be building the autonomous navigation layer, the EW-resistance module, or the targeting AI as infrastructure that dozens of drone manufacturers integrate. Theseus (YC S24) is doing exactly this with GPS-denied navigation. Maquoketa is building the full airframe-to-electronics stack, which means competing head-to-head with companies that have raised a thousand times more capital. I'd rather back the layer that makes every drone smarter than back a single drone competing against Anduril's war chest. The team has genuine specific knowledge in the RF layer -- but they've chosen to build around it rather than leverage it as infrastructure.

This is a pass. Real technical depth in one founder, a real market with undeniable tailwinds, but wrong leverage architecture for my thesis, consensus timing that's already been priced by billion-dollar rounds, and critical gaps in the founding team's defense-specific knowledge. The CTO's phased array expertise is the kind of non-teachable knowledge I look for -- I'd be more interested if they were building an RF/EW-resistance platform that other drone companies integrate, turning that specific knowledge into a leverage layer rather than a feature of a single product line.
