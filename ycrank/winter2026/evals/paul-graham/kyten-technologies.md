# Kyten Technologies -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Kyten is how the idea was found. These founders didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's hot in defense tech." They spent seven and eight years at SpaceX building and deploying 6,000 battery packs to orbit. They saw the aerospace battery supply chain break from the inside — the 12-month lead times, the incumbents optimized for glacial procurement cycles, the terrifying dependency on Chinese cells for defense applications. This is organic discovery in its purest form. The problem grew out of their daily work, not out of a TechCrunch headline. That's exactly the signal I look for before anything else.

The schlep here is real and multi-layered. Custom pack geometry, thermal management, onboard BMS, aerospace qualification, NDAA compliance, dealing with defense procurement — this is the kind of tedious, regulatory, physically constrained problem that causes software engineers to unconsciously look away. Battery pack integration isn't glamorous. It's not AI. It's not a platform play. It's manufacturing hardware that has to survive the thermal extremes of low earth orbit or the inside of a military drone, and then getting it qualified by customers who have multi-year certification processes. The structural parallel to Stripe is interesting: every engineer who worked in aerospace knew the battery supply chain was broken, but nobody wanted to walk into the manufacturing facility and deal with the compliance paperwork. That unconscious avoidance is exactly what creates opportunity for founders who will.

The timing argument is unusually concrete. The DoD is projecting procurement of 200,000+ drones in 2027, with Army plans for a million over two to three years. NDAA compliance is forcing domestic sourcing while 90% of drone battery supply still runs through China. The Pentagon is developing a dedicated battery procurement strategy for 2026. These aren't speculative trends — they're policy commitments backed by defense budgets. When the demand curve is this steep and the supply constraint is this binding, the company that can actually deliver product wins. Kyten's claimed 3-week turnaround versus the industry standard of 12+ months is the kind of speed advantage that, if real, would make them the obvious choice for programs ramping urgently.

What concerns me is what I don't see. There's zero evidence that Kyten itself has built anything, shipped anything, or acquired a single customer. The 6,000 packs deployed to orbit were SpaceX packs, built at SpaceX, using SpaceX's infrastructure and capital. Starting a manufacturing company from scratch is a fundamentally different challenge than operating inside one of the best-funded hardware companies on Earth. The "software-defined automated manufacturing" claim sounds promising but I have no evidence it exists yet beyond a website description. And the capital intensity problem is severe — competitors have $50M to $425M in funding. A 2-person team with pre-seed capital faces a long road to building real manufacturing capacity, and every fundraising round between here and production-scale is a dilution event and an execution risk.

The bull case is strong enough to take seriously. If the defense drone procurement ramp materializes as projected, there will be a genuine supply crisis for NDAA-compliant battery packs. Incumbents like EaglePicher are structurally unable to serve this demand — their cost structures and sales processes are optimized for large prime contractors, not rapid-turnaround custom work for dozens of drone OEMs. The founders have exactly the right background: they've operated at the intersection of battery engineering and aerospace manufacturing at the highest level. And the schlep — qualification cycles, thermal engineering, defense compliance, manufacturing process development — creates a natural moat against casual entrants. If Cooper and Lucas can translate their SpaceX process knowledge into a lean, fast manufacturing operation before the demand wave peaks, this could be a very large outcome. The question is whether they can get from two people and a pre-seed check to production-scale manufacturing fast enough, and whether the defense procurement timeline holds.

I'd want to talk to these founders. The organic discovery is as strong as I've seen. The schlep willingness is evident in their career choices — you don't spend seven years doing battery pack test and manufacturing at Starlink unless you're comfortable with tedious, physical, unglamorous work. But I need to see evidence that Kyten the company has built something, not just that the founders built things at SpaceX. A prototype. A customer conversation. A letter of intent. Anything that moves this from "two excellent resumes with a thesis" to "a company that has started making something real." Without that, I'm scoring the potential, not the progress.
