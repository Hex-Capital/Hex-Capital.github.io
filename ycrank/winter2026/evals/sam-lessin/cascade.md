# Cascade -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Cascade is a sane team with a deeply sane idea, and that's the problem. Two Berkeley CS grads from the BAIR Lab building AI agent monitoring infrastructure -- in a market where Braintrust just raised $80M at an $800M valuation, Raindrop pulled in $15M from Lightspeed with a nearly identical "train custom models on production data" approach, Arize has $131M deployed, and ClickHouse, Snyk, and Anthropic have all made acquisitions in the exact category Cascade is entering. When I look at the competitive landscape table in this dossier, I don't see a market opening -- I see a market that's already been priced, bid up, and is consolidating before Cascade has a visible product. The pitch deck probably leads with that $196 billion agentic AI TAM number from Market.us. That's the tell. When the TAM slide writes itself, every fund has already seen the same deck twenty times, and the contrarian premium has evaporated completely.

The founders have genuine domain credibility -- I want to be clear about that. Adam's BAIR Lab research on agentic safety and graph reasoning models maps directly to the product thesis, and Haluk built production monitoring infrastructure at Netflix and Amazon before researching failure mode taxonomies for AI agents at BAIR. These are not tourists in the problem space. If I'm running my sane-person-insane-idea calibration, the "sane" part scores well. But the idea itself is what every infrastructure-focused VC in San Francisco is salivating over right now. AI agent observability is the consensus infrastructure play of 2026 the way "developer tools" was in 2021. When an idea is consensus, founder quality becomes table stakes rather than alpha -- you need to be not just credible but the absolute best team in the space, and Braintrust already has Notion, Replit, Cloudflare, and Ramp as customers while Raindrop is processing millions of events daily.

Let me run the AI kill test in reverse, because this is where my framework reveals something other investors might miss. The question I always ask is "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" For Cascade, the answer is unambiguously no -- without AI agents, there's nothing to monitor. This is an AI-first company where AI IS the business, which is the category I screen out most aggressively. But it's actually worse than a standard AI-first play, because the monitoring layer itself is vulnerable to AI commoditization from above and below. From above: model providers like Anthropic and OpenAI have enormous incentive to build evaluation and safety tooling natively into their agent frameworks -- Anthropic already acqui-hired HumanLoop. From below: the custom evaluator models Cascade wants to train are themselves ML models that will get cheaper and easier to build as the underlying infrastructure matures. The moat is software monitoring other software, which is exactly the category where I've been saying the walls are dissolving fastest.

The bull case would require believing that Cascade's custom evaluator models create genuine compound learning -- that the data flywheel from observing each customer's production agent runs generates switching costs deep enough to resist both the well-funded competitors and the gravitational pull of platform incumbents. If that flywheel works, and if the market fragments enough that being the best evaluator for legal-reasoning agents or customer-support agents creates defensible vertical niches, Cascade could carve out a meaningful position. The Airtable exception in my own portfolio proves that something that looks like SaaS on the surface can actually function as a new primitive if it becomes the substrate other workflows build on. If Cascade's evaluator models became the standard by which enterprises certified their agents for production deployment -- effectively a compliance layer -- that would transform it from an observability tool into infrastructure. But nothing in the dossier suggests that ambition. The positioning is monitoring and evaluation, not certification or compliance primitives. And the data flywheel is entirely prospective with zero demonstrated customer evidence. Raindrop is building the identical flywheel with thirty times the capital.

Two more structural concerns. First, this is pure subscription SaaS -- usage-based monitoring infrastructure with no physical-world anchor, no protocol position, no regulatory lock-in. Craftwork has painting crews. Metropolis has parking hardware. Cascade has API calls watching other API calls. When I say software is a business tool, not a business model, this is what I mean. Second, the brand problem is non-trivial -- "Cascade" returns a strategy execution platform, a materials handling corporation, Windsurf's coding agent, and a data automation tool before you get anywhere near runcascade.com. That's a distribution tax on every marketing dollar spent, and distribution is already the hardest problem for a two-person pre-seed company competing against an $800M-valued incumbent.

I respect the founders' technical preparation, and the market timing for agent reliability tooling is real. But real market timing plus consensus recognition plus well-funded competition equals a compressed opportunity where being early doesn't generate the returns my framework demands. This is exactly the kind of bet where the narrative magnitude belongs to the category, not to this specific company. Pass.
