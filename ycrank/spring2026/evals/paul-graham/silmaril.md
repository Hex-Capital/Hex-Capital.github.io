# Silmaril -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Five AI security startups sold for a combined $1.4 billion in 2024-2025 — Protect AI to Palo Alto Networks for $500M, Robust Intelligence to Cisco for $400M, Prompt Security to SentinelOne for $180M, Lakera to Check Point, CalypsoAI to F5 for $180M. That acquisition wave is the single most important fact about Silmaril. It's simultaneously the strongest argument for and against investing. For: the acquirers validated the market, cleared the field of independent competitors, and proved that build-vs-buy favors buy in this space. Against: those acquirers now own AI security capabilities bundled into enterprise platforms with thousands of existing sales reps. Silmaril is entering a market where the previous generation of standalone vendors was systematically absorbed. The question is whether Silmaril's self-healing approach — autonomous red-teaming agents generating synthetic attack data through RL environments — represents a genuine generational leap over what Lakera and Prompt Security built, or whether it's an incremental improvement entering a market that's consolidating into platform plays.

Aum Upadhyay built the security and privacy framework at AWS that "prevented over $1.8B in damages." Eduardo Velasco led low-latency ML models at Amazon "generating $400M in annual revenue." Those are impressive corporate credentials, and Silmaril's founder-market fit is genuinely strong — you don't often see a pre-seed team where both founders have directly relevant experience at exactly this intersection of security and ML infrastructure. But corporate accomplishments are not the same as startup resourcefulness. When I backed the Airbnb founders, the signal wasn't their résumés — it was the cereal boxes. The story of creative problem-solving under constraint when no one believed in them. Silmaril's dossier contains dollar figures from Amazon tenure but zero stories of hacking, scrapping, or building something before anyone asked them to. The GreenDragon open-source project — an intentionally vulnerable AI app for security training, currently at 10 GitHub stars — is the closest thing to an unprompted building signal, and it's thin. I'm not saying Aum and Eduardo lack the animal quality. I'm saying the dossier gives me no evidence of it, and truly resourceful founders usually generate notable anecdotes.

Silmaril's technical approach — a multihead classifier evaluating execution states rather than screening inputs, asking "does this execution lead to a harmful outcome?" rather than "does this input look malicious?" — is legitimately differentiated from the pattern-matching guardrails it aims to replace. The claimed 99% detection at 20ms latency, if real, would be a meaningful technical achievement; Lakera reported 98%+ at sub-50ms before its acquisition. But security professionals in the YC LinkedIn comments raised concerns about "validation methodology and deterministic reproducibility in an adaptive system," and no independent third-party verification exists. Eduardo's low-latency ML experience at Amazon maps directly to the 20ms constraint — this is the right person to build a fast classifier. The data flywheel is theoretically compelling: more deployments generate more diverse attack patterns, training a better classifier for all customers. Stripe had a similar compounding dynamic with payment fraud data. But Silmaril has no public revenue data, no disclosed customer count — just one testimonial claiming "8 breaches prevented, $28M in damages blocked." Stripe at its earliest had developers using it because the Collisons physically installed the integration on their laptops. I need to see that kind of desperate early user love, not damage-prevention testimonials.

Silmaril's 5-line SDK integration into LangGraph targets the developer-led adoption motion I've seen work at Stripe, Replit, and Retool — hackers building infrastructure for hackers. That pattern is my sweet spot, and Silmaril fits it. The shift from single-turn LLM calls to multi-agent workflows with tool access (MCP, function calling) creates novel attack vectors that static guardrails genuinely weren't designed for. The market timing argument has real substance. But here's what troubles me: AI security in 2026 is consensus-good. Everyone agrees this matters. The TAM slides write themselves — $34 billion growing to $213 billion. When the idea sounds great to everyone, the returns have been competed away. The best YC investments — Airbnb, Coinbase, Dropbox — all sounded wrong or weird when they started. "Let strangers sleep in your apartment." "Build a company around internet money that might be illegal." Silmaril's pitch — "defend AI applications from prompt injection" — sounds obviously correct. Nobody at a dinner party would say "that will never work." That's not disqualifying on its own, but it means Silmaril has to win on execution rather than on an insight that repels competitors.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Silmaril has the best founder-market fit possible in a market with a $1.4B acquisition-validated exit path, entering at precisely the moment when the competitive field has been cleared by consolidation. The self-healing RL-based approach is genuinely novel. The founders' combined AWS security and low-latency ML expertise is the exact skillset required. Garry Tan personally shared Silmaril's launch post. The agentic AI explosion in 2025-2026 creates attack surfaces that previous-generation guardrails cannot cover. And the developer-tool GTM motion has repeatedly produced venture-scale outcomes. Does a Known Exception apply? Silmaril somewhat parallels Stripe — developer infrastructure tackling a regulatory-compliance schlep — and I invested $15K in Stripe for 2% based on founder quality. But the critical difference: I knew Patrick Collison for years before Stripe. I had a deep, multi-company signal on his quality. With Aum and Eduardo, I have Amazon résumés and LinkedIn profiles. The Stripe exception was triggered by extraordinary founder evidence accumulated over time, not by domain credentials. I'd need to see Silmaril's founders in a 10-minute working session — watch them respond to hard questions, see if they have the "running back" quality — before the Stripe parallel would activate.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Aum and Eduardo have the adaptive, creative-under-constraint quality that I can't evaluate from a dossier. Concretely — if they showed me they'd built and deployed Silmaril's core classifier while still employed at Amazon, using nights and weekends, because they couldn't stop thinking about the problem, and had three companies using it before they'd raised a dollar. That would move Founder Resourcefulness from 14 to 22+ and change the entire calculus. Alternatively, disclosed revenue showing rapid weekly growth would provide indirect evidence of the iteration velocity I prize. What keeps Silmaril in Neutral territory is the gap between strong domain credentials and observable startup-building signals. Organic Problem Discovery scores slightly above default at 11/25 because Aum's AWS security work is genuine professional immersion in this problem space — not McKinsey trend analysis — but it's not Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive either. Idea That Repels scores 7/20 because AI security is consensus-hot, saved only by the specific technical skepticism about adaptive systems' reproducibility. The two dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Founder Resourcefulness (14/30, held at default by the absence of startup-specific stories despite strong corporate credentials) and Idea That Repels (7/20, because the space is too obviously attractive). Silmaril is a competent team in a real market with genuine domain expertise — but nothing in this dossier activates the pattern-matching instinct I've developed over thousands of YC interviews.
