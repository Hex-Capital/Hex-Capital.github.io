# Korso -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately about Korso is that this is an idea everyone thinks is good. AI quoting for manufacturers? Paperless Parts raised $30M to do cloud quoting. Uptool just pulled $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins — four of the best firms in the business — to do AI quoting for SMB manufacturers. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all in the same water. When I see five funded startups converging on the same problem with the same underlying technology (LLMs doing RAG over pricing history), I don't see validation. I see that the returns have already been competed away. The whole point of my Black Swan framework is that the best investments are the ones where knowledgeable people's first reaction is skepticism. Nobody is skeptical about "AI for manufacturing workflows" in 2026. It's on the cover of every trade publication. That's the tell.

Now, could the right founders win a crowded race? Absolutely — Stripe wasn't the first company to do online payments. But when Patrick Collison tackled payments, he'd already built and sold a company. I'd known him since he was a teenager. I had years of evidence that he had the specific combination of technical brilliance and relentless execution that could overcome Braintree, Authorize.net, and eventually PayPal. With Korso, I have three UPenn undergrads with internship-level experience, no prior startups, no public GitHub repos, and zero traction signals. I can't find a single story of creative problem-solving under constraint. When I evaluate founders, I'm looking for the X-ray — the stories that reveal the skeleton beneath the presentation. There are no stories here. Martin Pan's stint at GM as an automations engineer is the strongest signal, and it's thin. One year at a manufacturing company doesn't give you the deep operational intuition of someone who's spent a decade quoting jobs on a shop floor.

The origin story concerns me. Where's the personal frustration? Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Octopart founders were physics students who couldn't find electronic parts. With Korso, I don't see "we were working in a machine shop and spent four hours every day doing quotes manually and thought this is insane." I see a team that identified a market opportunity — probably through research, given Alex Liu's self-description of reading "more research papers after dropping out than while in school." That's an interesting biographical detail, but it's a declaration about inputs, not a demonstration of outputs. The way to get startup ideas is not to read research papers about startup ideas. It's to live inside the problem until the solution becomes obvious to you and invisible to everyone else. I don't see evidence that these founders lived inside manufacturing operations.

The strongest bull case: manufacturing is genuinely ripe for software disruption, these are young technical founders with real engineering chops (ML research, robotics, hands-on hardware experience at Penn Electric Racing), and Martin's GM background gives them at least some insider credibility. The WhatsApp ingestion angle — the fact that a huge amount of global manufacturing procurement runs on WhatsApp — could be a genuine non-obvious insight if they're targeting markets that the U.S.-centric competitors are ignoring. YC accepted them, and Andrew Miklas presumably saw something in the interview that doesn't come through in a dossier. Young founders with high learning velocity can surprise you. But for me to invest my own money, I need more than theoretical potential. I need to see the animal quality — the intensity, the stories of obstacles overcome, the crappy version one that someone is using obsessively. I see none of that here. The competitive landscape alone doesn't kill this — but the competitive landscape combined with no visible execution edge is what makes this a pass.

I keep coming back to the schlep blindness framework, but inverted. Schlep blindness creates moats when you're the founder who sees the unglamorous problem that repels everyone else. Manufacturing quoting was a schlep five years ago. Today it's a category with Series B companies and top-tier VCs writing seed checks. The schlep has been identified. The moat from being the only one willing to do the hard work doesn't exist when Khosla Ventures is funding your competitor to do the same hard work with more resources. To win this market now, you need either a dramatically better product, a distribution advantage, or a wedge that nobody else sees. I don't have evidence of any of these.
