# Heavi -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari scaled an agricultural supply chain marketplace to $250M+ ARR in India, exited, moved to San Francisco, and then went and got ASE T8 certified as a diesel mechanic. I want to dwell on that last part because it's the most important fact in this entire dossier. A Harvard MBA who already proved he could build a company at scale chose to go through the national certification program for heavy-duty truck service. That's not resume decoration -- you don't sit for the T8 exam to impress investors. You do it because you want to understand what happens inside a repair bay when a Class 8 Freightliner comes in with a failing aftertreatment system and the tech needs the right DPF clamp from a vendor who may or may not have it in stock. Dasari didn't just "identify an opportunity" in the technician shortage -- he went and lived in the problem. His father ran Ashok Leyland, one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers, so the industry is in his family's DNA. The combination of a founder who scaled a marketplace past $200M in funding, carries genuine diesel grease under his fingernails, and grew up watching commercial vehicles get built is the kind of founder-market fit that's hard to manufacture.

Michael Holkesvik brings the technical side -- Yale CS, built NCX's satellite imagery and ML marketplace as CTO, scaled it to 15,000 landowner members. NCX's story has a cautionary dimension: they raised $74.4M but their core carbon credit program was rejected by Verra, the industry's gatekeeper, followed by 40% layoffs. That failure mode is relevant to Heavi because the product depends on access to OE systems and aftermarket catalogs that could similarly restrict access. But Holkesvik learned something from NCX that most CTOs never learn: what happens when the external dependency you built around says no. I'd want to hear how he thinks about Heavi's data access differently because of that experience. The co-founder pairing covers the critical bases -- Dasari owns the domain and the customer relationship, Holkesvik owns the technical architecture -- though the absence of shared history between them is something I'd probe in a conversation.

Heavi's schlep factor is exceptional. Heavy-duty parts procurement involves thousands of OEM part numbers, cross-reference databases, fitment verification across model years, and a vendor landscape so fragmented that the current solution is literally a technician calling three distributors on the phone. This reminds me of what Stripe faced with payments infrastructure -- the Collison brothers tackled PCI compliance and bank integrations because that complexity repelled every other developer who could see the opportunity. Heavi is building the agent layer over an equivalently ugly backend, and the 65.5% shop understaffing rate and 67,000 projected technician shortfall mean the pain is structural and worsening. Most AI founders are building chatbots for knowledge workers. Building an AI procurement agent for diesel shops is the kind of idea that makes most YC applicants wrinkle their noses -- which is precisely why the competitive landscape is so sparse. Fullbay has 5,000+ shops and just acquired Pitstop for AI capabilities, but their architecture is shop management ERP with AI bolted on, not an agent-first procurement system. That's the difference between adding search to a filing cabinet and building Google.

The early traction validates the pain point: $100K in contracted revenue from 5 design partners, with those partners reporting up to 30% employee time savings and roughly 40% margin improvement. For a two-person pre-seed company, $20K average contract value from heavy-duty repair shops -- businesses that are notoriously resistant to new software -- is a meaningful demand signal. These aren't free pilots. The 40% margin improvement claim, if real, means Heavi is finding parts cheaper or faster enough to materially change shop economics. That's the kind of number that makes shop owners tell other shop owners, which is how you get distribution in a fragmented market without a large sales team.

Where I'm less certain is whether Dasari's problem discovery is truly organic or expertly constructed. He spent 2015-2024 on agricultural supply chains in India, not in American truck repair bays. The family connection to commercial vehicles is real, but he pivoted into this domain rather than stumbling into it through personal frustration. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive; Dasari read the ATRI technician shortage report and recognized, through his father's industry, that he understood the commercial vehicle world better than any other tech founder would. That's closer to "intelligent schlep detection" than "lived in the future and built what was missing." It's still a strong signal -- most founders can't even see schleps, let alone choose them deliberately -- but it lacks the raw authenticity of a founder who was personally losing money every time a part took three days to source.

The risk that would flip me is data access. If OE systems and major aftermarket catalogs (Dorman, Alliance Parts, Meritor) decide to restrict API access or charge prohibitive data licensing fees, Heavi's agent can't search across sources -- and the entire value proposition collapses. This is structurally similar to what killed NCX's core program when Verra declined certification. Holkesvik should have pattern-matched on this risk from his NCX experience, and I'd want to see evidence that Heavi has vendor data agreements or partnerships in place rather than relying on scraping. The $100K in contracted revenue suggests the product works today, but "works today" and "has durable access to its data sources" are different questions.

The two dimensions that most determine my verdict are schlep blindness and founder quality. Heavi is attacking a domain that makes sophisticated founders physically recoil -- OEM part cross-references, vendor phone trees, diesel fitment databases -- and that disgust reaction from the market is Heavi's moat. Dasari's ASE T8 certification combined with his $250M+ ARR scaling experience represents a founder profile I almost never see: someone who can both understand what a technician needs and build the organization to deliver it at scale. The idea won't win any pitch competitions, and that's exactly the point.
