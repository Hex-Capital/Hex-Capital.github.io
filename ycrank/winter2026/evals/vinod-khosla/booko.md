# Booko -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the question I cannot get past: if Booko succeeds beyond anyone's expectations -- becomes the dominant dynamic pricing layer for every service business on earth -- what changes? Yoga studios fill their 5pm Tuesday slots. Hair salons charge $2 less during slow Wednesday mornings. Medspas recover 20% more revenue. That is a nice business. It is not a reason to get out of bed. The consequence of total success here is that appointment-based businesses operate more efficiently. No industry becomes obsolete. No trillion-dollar system gets replaced. The world functions identically, except some small business owners see slightly better utilization rates. I have spent forty years looking for companies where success means the world operates differently. This is not one of them.

What concerns me more than the small ambition is the structural positioning. The dossier states plainly that "the core technology -- demand prediction for time slots using historical booking data -- is not novel at an algorithmic level. Any ML team with access to booking data could build a similar model." The founders haven't built something the incumbents cannot replicate -- they've built something the incumbents have already partially replicated. Mindbody launched its own dynamic pricing feature. Zenoti has dynamic pricing built into its enterprise platform. Booko's argument is that it does the same thing at a lower take rate, sitting as an overlay rather than requiring platform migration. That is a cost arbitrage position against a $1.9 billion incumbent, not a technology disruption. When Juniper attacked Cisco, the thesis was that TCP/IP routing would make Cisco's entire architecture obsolete -- not that Juniper could sell the same routers for 15% less. Booko is selling the same capability the platforms already offer, just unbundled and cheaper. That is an optimization of the existing system, not a replacement of it.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Revenue management transformed airline economics -- Sabre and Amadeus became multi-billion-dollar companies by optimizing perishable inventory. If you squint, Booko is attempting to bring that discipline to the vast, fragmented universe of service businesses: every doctor's office, every consultant, every tutor, every salon. That aggregate market is genuinely large. And Mindbody's 20% marketplace fee creates a real business model conflict -- the incumbent profits from the friction Booko aims to eliminate, which is the kind of structural opening that sometimes produces durable third-party companies. Post-COVID demand volatility has made utilization optimization more urgent. If someone built the "revenue management operating system for all services" and captured pricing intelligence across millions of businesses, the data asset could become genuinely valuable. But none of that is evident here. The product handles 4-9% discounts for yoga classes. The vision, as presented, is a feature attached to someone else's platform, not a new category of infrastructure.

On the founders, I have almost nothing to evaluate. Neither founder's background, domain expertise, or technical credentials could be confirmed through public sources. One may have attended Dartmouth and studied mechanical engineering. The product demonstrates some technical competence -- per-client ML models, SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance, API integrations with major booking platforms -- but I cannot assess learning rate, contrarian thinking, or first-principles reasoning from a factual dossier with this level of opacity. What I can observe is the choice itself: two founders chose to spend their careers building a pricing optimization overlay for fitness studios. That choice reveals how they think about consequence and ambition. The most talented people I have backed chose problems that frightened them and mattered at civilizational scale. Dynamic pricing for hair salons does not clear that bar.

The timing is adequate but unremarkable. ML infrastructure has matured enough for a two-person team to deploy per-client models without building their own training infrastructure. Mindbody's dynamic pricing launch has educated the market. These are favorable conditions for building a business, but there is no exponential technology curve creating a window that closes. Dynamic pricing algorithms are forty years old. The application to services is straightforward. This is not skating to where the puck will be -- the puck arrived decades ago in travel and hospitality, and Booko is applying the same principles to a smaller, more fragmented market. Nothing about the timing suggests that building this company in 2026 captures an accelerating trend that will look obvious in hindsight.

I pass. This is a company that has carefully minimized every identifiable risk -- proven technology, known market pain, established integration partners, compliance certifications -- and in doing so has constrained the consequences of its own success to a level I find inconsequential. Some problems demand that you take enormous risks because the outcome justifies it. Filling empty yoga class slots is not one of those problems.
