# Aemon -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Aemon is that AlphaEvolve was published by DeepMind in May 2025, and shortly after, two twenty-year-old researchers dropped out of school to build a commercial version of essentially the same idea. That timing pattern worries me. When I see a company whose genesis aligns with a prominent research publication rather than with a personal frustration the founders couldn't stop thinking about, I suspect the idea was conceived from the outside in. Richard Zhou's LinkedIn post is titled "Why I started a business at 20" -- not "why I couldn't stop working on this problem." And his stint as Founder in Residence at Afore Capital puts him squarely in the idea-search pipeline: someone who decided to start a company first, then went looking for what to build. That's the ordering I worry about most. The best founders I've backed built things because they couldn't not build them. Drew Houston didn't decide to start a startup and then brainstorm file sync -- he kept forgetting his USB drive.

Now, the bull case is real and I want to state it honestly. These two are genuine technical hackers. Publishing at ICLR and EMNLP before turning twenty is not something you can fake or buy. Beating DeepMind's AlphaEvolve on circle packing with under ten dollars of compute is a legitimately impressive result -- it's the kind of thing that makes you think "these kids actually understand the math at a deep level, not just the API calls." Twin brothers as co-founders is about as strong a co-founder relationship as you can get; there's zero risk of the kind of co-founder dissolution that kills most early companies. And the positioning is smart -- AlphaEvolve is internal to Google, so there's a genuine gap in the market for a commercial product that does evolutionary code optimization for external R&D teams. If you squint, this has the "picks and shovels for builders" shape that I've gravitated toward with Stripe and Replit.

But here's where my schlep blindness framework actually works against Aemon rather than for it. The whole point of schlep blindness is that the best opportunities are problems everyone can see but unconsciously avoids because the work is ugly. R&D optimization is not being avoided. DeepMind built AlphaEvolve. Sakana AI built ShinkaEvolve and got accepted to ICLR 2026. OpenEvolve is already on GitHub as open source. Lila Sciences raised $550 million. Periodic Labs raised $300 million. Cognition has over $900 million. This is a category where billions of dollars of capital are actively flowing in. When every well-funded lab and startup is already working on adjacent problems, you're not exploiting schlep blindness -- you're entering a gold rush. The fact that nobody has productized AlphaEvolve commercially yet doesn't mean nobody will. Google could flip that switch tomorrow. And the open-source implementation means the base technology is already replicable.

The evidence of user pull is thin even by pre-seed standards. One case study on legal retrieval optimization and a benchmark result on circle packing. The benchmark is technically impressive but it's not a customer. The legal retrieval case study at least shows the system working on a practical problem, but I don't see anyone pulling this product toward them organically -- no waitlist, no community, no revenue signal, no evidence of engineers discovering Aemon and telling their colleagues about it. The website has a "Book a Discovery Call" button, which is enterprise sales language for "we don't have inbound demand yet." At this stage I don't expect much, but I'd feel better if there were even one story of an engineering team that found Aemon on their own and started using it.

What would make me change my mind? If Ray and Richard could describe a specific research project where they personally spent weeks manually iterating on optimization approaches, felt the pain viscerally, and built the first version of Aemon to solve their own problem -- that would shift everything. The technical talent is clearly there. The co-founder relationship is iron-clad. If the idea turns out to be organic rather than inspired by AlphaEvolve's publication, this becomes a much more interesting bet. But based on what I can see, this looks like two genuinely brilliant young researchers who saw a hot paper, recognized they had the skills to build something similar, and decided to commercialize it. That's not a terrible starting point -- plenty of good companies start that way -- but it's not the kind of founder-problem fit that makes me reach for my checkbook. The talent here deserves a problem they discovered themselves.
