# Superset -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Superset: Anthropic has already shipped native multi-agent orchestration for Claude Code. The very company whose CLI agent drives a huge portion of Superset's use case looked at the same workflow pain and said, we'll just build this ourselves. That's not a hypothetical competitive risk sitting in some future scenario -- it's already happening. And that tells me something fundamental about the durability of this layer. When I invested in Flock Safety, the big incumbents in public safety had no structural incentive to build what Garrett was building. Here, the upstream platforms have every incentive and are already acting on it. Superset is building a beautiful house on rented land, and the landlord is expanding next door.

I want to hear these founders' stories, and that's exactly what's missing from everything I can see. Three ex-YC CTOs is a strong credential on paper -- Kiet shipped Onlook to 25,000 GitHub stars, which is genuinely impressive community building. Satya has the big-company engineering breadth from Google, Amazon, and Facebook. Avi brings interesting cross-domain depth from biotech and CAD. But when I ask my fundamental question -- how did you get here, what in your life made you the person who has to solve this specific problem -- the answer seems to be "we were CTOs who used developer tools and noticed a workflow gap." That's a professional observation, not a biographical imperative. The founders I back hardest are the ones where the problem and the person are inseparable -- where you can trace a line from some formative experience or genuine struggle straight to why they're building what they're building. I don't see that thread here. I see capable engineers who spotted an opening in the market's hottest category.

And that category is the core issue for me. AI developer tools might be the single most consensus investment thesis in venture capital right now. Cursor is valued at nearly $30 billion. Every fund in San Francisco is trying to get into AI coding infrastructure. The best ideas I've backed -- private space when people thought Elon was delusional, ride-sharing when it was literally illegal, male contraception that other VCs wouldn't touch -- they all shared a quality of being unpopular and difficult to assess. Superset is neither. It's a well-executed product in a category that every investor already understands and wants exposure to. The agent-agnostic positioning is a clever strategic choice, and I'll give the team credit for that insight -- there's a real structural argument that Cursor and Windsurf won't build a tool that commoditizes their own proprietary agents. But "clever positioning in a hot market" is a different signal entirely from "magically weird idea that nobody else believes in."

Let me be honest about the bull case, because the traction numbers deserve respect. Eight thousand GitHub stars in roughly five months, a number-one Product Hunt launch, testimonials from engineers at Microsoft, Netflix, and OpenAI -- that's real developer love, and it happened fast. If CLI-based agents truly become the dominant programming paradigm and fragment across multiple providers, the Switzerland-of-agents play could be enormously valuable. Kiet has proven twice now that he can ship open-source tools developers actually want, which is a rare and valuable skill. If I believed the orchestration layer would remain structurally independent -- that Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google would keep their CLI agents open and wouldn't build native orchestration -- then the founding team's shipping velocity and community-building instincts could make this a breakout company. But I don't believe that. The platforms are already moving.

This company also doesn't activate the part of my investing lens that cares about who benefits beyond the founders and investors. Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Affirm gave transparent credit access to people the banks ignored. Superset makes already well-compensated software engineers slightly more productive when running AI agents in parallel. That's useful, but it's not the kind of economic access creation or invisible-system-made-visible that I look for in my portfolio. I want my investments to leave the world meaningfully different for people who need it, and I don't see that dimension here.

Three CTOs from three different YC companies, with no shared employer or university and no publicly visible history of working through hard times together -- that gives me pause. My most painful investment lesson was HQ Trivia, where two brilliant co-founders destroyed a product that could have redefined media because they couldn't resolve their conflicts. Three technical co-founders without obvious complementary skill differentiation and without a long track record together is a dynamic I'd want to probe hard before writing a check. Maybe they've built deep trust through the YC network. But I'd need to see that, not assume it.
