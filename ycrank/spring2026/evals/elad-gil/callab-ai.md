# Callab AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Callab AI's core bet presents an unusual temporal problem: they're building AI voice agents for legacy on-prem PBX infrastructure -- a segment that is structurally *contracting* as enterprises migrate to cloud telephony. The dossier cites that 35% of Fortune 500 contact centers still route legacy trunks through cloud APIs (Synthflow blog, 2025), and there's no question this installed base represents real near-term demand. But my framework evaluates whether a structural shift is *opening* a market, not whether residual demand exists in a market that's closing. When I backed Coinbase, the structural shift in digital asset infrastructure was creating an expanding market -- every year, the addressable opportunity grew. Callab AI faces the opposite dynamic: every year, the legacy PBX installed base shrinks as cloud migration progresses. That's a fundamentally different risk profile. The question isn't whether Callab AI can serve today's legacy enterprises -- they likely can -- but whether the transition period is long enough to build a venture-scale business before the addressable market erodes beneath them.

Retell AI at $40M ARR, PolyAI at $200M+ raised with a $750M valuation, and Vapi at $20M raised within months of launch -- the AI voice agent market is one of the most consensus-obvious categories in the current AI wave. Callab AI's legacy PBX niche provides some differentiation from these cloud-native platforms, but "a niche within an obvious market" is categorically different from a nonobvious market. The dossier confirms Trillet AI already claims nearly identical legacy PBX integration with Avaya, Cisco CUCM, Mitel, and Asterisk (Trillet blog, 2026), which means Callab AI doesn't even own the niche exclusively. Cloud-first incumbents have a structural disincentive to re-architect for legacy -- that's real -- but the disincentive weakens as cloud-native AI voice platforms mature and legacy enterprises eventually migrate anyway. Callab AI is running a race against the market's own evolution.

Callab AI's infrastructure-layer positioning is where the framework pushes hardest against this investment. When I look at my highest-conviction bets -- Stripe building the payments API, Figma becoming the design platform, Retool building internal tools infrastructure -- the common thread is that these companies built layers *other businesses depend on*, creating compounding switching costs. Callab AI builds and deploys its own voice agents for contact centers. The features listed on their site -- knowledge-base referencing, post-call analysis, sentiment tagging, structured data extraction -- are application-layer capabilities, not platform APIs that other developers build on. Compare this directly to Vapi or Retell AI, which position as developer API platforms enabling others to build voice applications. Callab AI sits at the application layer, which means they compete on product execution rather than platform lock-in. On-prem deployment creates some switching costs once integrated into a PBX stack, but that's operational friction, not the compounding network effects that define infrastructure positioning.

Haithem Kchaou (CEO) and Chehir Dhaouadi (CTO) bring genuine technical depth -- their co-authorship of the 101 Billion Arabic Words Dataset (arXiv 2405.01590, 72 Hugging Face likes, 532 monthly downloads) is a credible research contribution and underpins Callab AI's Arabic dialect advantage. Dhaouadi built platforms serving 200,000+ users across Tunisian government entities, demonstrating real systems experience. Karim Beguir, co-founder of InstaDeep (acquired by BioNTech for ~$680M), backing them with $600K pre-seed is a meaningful endorsement from a credible operator. But neither founder shows evidence of operating inside a company during its scaling phase -- the 10-to-1000 employee trajectory that separates founders who've *seen* hypergrowth from those who haven't. Kchaou's LinkedIn shows a Project Manager internship at Mobioos; Dhaouadi was CTO at FivePoints. The 6+ years of working together at ClusterLab since 2020 is a positive signal, though the pivot from Reedz (Arabic audiobook summarization) to contact center AI represents a significant domain shift. Callab AI's founders have Arabic NLP expertise, not contact center operations expertise -- and those are different domains with different buyer dynamics.

The bull case is more interesting than I initially credited. Callab AI has clients across three continents including Dunkin' Donuts at pre-seed -- that's real enterprise pull, not vaporware. Enterprise telephony migration is genuinely slower than technologists assume; regulated industries (healthcare, financial services) face compliance barriers to cloud migration that could sustain legacy PBX demand for a decade. The Arabic dialect angle gives Callab AI a wedge into MENA markets where PolyAI, Vapi, and Retell have minimal presence, and MENA contact center demand is growing rapidly. If Callab AI used legacy PBX as a landing zone and expanded to cloud-native deployment over time, the shrinking-market concern diminishes. I've checked my Known Exceptions: the Anduril override applies when market timing is overwhelmingly positive, which isn't the case here. The BioAge override applies when deep personal domain expertise creates unique conviction -- the founders' Arabic NLP depth is real but doesn't override the structural market concerns.

Callab AI earns a Pass because the two dimensions I weight most heavily -- nonobvious market timing (30 points) and infrastructure-layer positioning (25 points) -- both work against this investment. The AI voice agent market is consensus-obvious, the legacy PBX niche within it is structurally shrinking, and Callab AI builds at the application layer rather than the platform layer. The single verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Callab AI is signing $100K+ ACV enterprise contracts with Fortune 500 companies specifically *because* of on-prem PBX integration requirements, combined with a demonstrable platform strategy (API access, developer ecosystem) that positions them as infrastructure rather than application. That would transform the thesis from "niche application in a declining segment" to "essential integration layer for a massive enterprise installed base" -- and I've seen that transformation work when the installed base is large enough and sticky enough to sustain a decade of revenue growth.
