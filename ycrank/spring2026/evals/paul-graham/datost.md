# Datost -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Datost is that it's an idea you could generate in fifteen minutes with a whiteboard and a list of trending categories. "AI + Slack + data analysis" — every word in that phrase makes investors nod. And that's exactly the problem. When I look at an idea and everyone's first reaction is "oh, that makes sense," I get nervous. The best startup ideas live in that uncomfortable zone where smart people's initial response is "that will never work." Datost lives in the opposite zone. It's the kind of idea that sounds great at a Demo Day but has already been competed into a crowded field: Julius AI has $10M and two million users, Defog.ai is open-sourcing text-to-SQL, Microsoft is shipping Copilot in Power BI, and — most critically — Salesforce owns both Slack and Tableau and has every strategic incentive to build this exact product in-house. When your landlord is also your competitor, you're in trouble.

The founders are 21, both software engineers who worked together at Traba. I've backed founders this young before — Patrick Collison was a teenager when I first funded him, the Bindwell founders were 18 and 19. But those founders had something I could see immediately. Patrick had already won Ireland's top science competition and built a company before Stripe. The Bindwell kids were applying AI to pesticide molecule discovery — a domain so unsexy and scientifically dense that no other YC applicants would touch it. That's schlep blindness in action. Building a Slack chatbot that generates SQL is the opposite of a schlep. It's the kind of project that gets posted to Hacker News every week. The dossier shows no evidence of the "animal" quality I look for — no stories of creative workarounds, no prior projects shipped, no open-source work with traction. One GitHub repo with zero stars. A LinkedIn headline that still reads "Software Engineer Intern." These aren't disqualifying facts individually, but together they paint a picture of founders who are early in their journey without a visible track record that would let me bet on trajectory.

I also can't find the organic problem discovery. The best companies start with a founder who has a specific frustration they can describe in concrete detail — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Octopart guys unable to search for electronic components. Where is Datost's version of that story? Were these founders data analysts stuck waiting on engineering tickets? Were they engineers buried under ad-hoc SQL requests from product managers? The dossier doesn't surface any personal origin story, and in my experience, founders who discovered a problem organically are eager to tell you about it. The absence of that narrative is itself a signal. It suggests this idea may have been chosen from a menu rather than discovered in the wild — the classic "identified an opportunity" pattern that I've learned to distrust.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Slack-native distribution is a genuine wedge: someone asks a data question in a channel, the bot answers it, and twenty people in that channel see the value without a sales call. That's real viral mechanics. The "own computer" execution environment — if they've actually built a reliable sandboxed compute layer — is more interesting than raw SQL generation, because it addresses the gap between generating a query and actually trusting the result. And the enterprise data trust barrier they'll need to climb (SOC 2, permissions, audit trails) could eventually become a moat if they do the hard compliance work that lighter competitors skip. If these founders turn out to have the relentlessly resourceful quality that the dossier simply doesn't capture, and if they execute faster than Salesforce's internal teams can coordinate between their Slack and Tableau divisions — which, knowing large companies, could take years — there's a path to a meaningful outcome. But that's a lot of "ifs" stacked on a team I can't yet read.

What I keep coming back to is that this company doesn't pass my schlep blindness test. The best opportunities hide behind work that repels most founders — regulatory complexity, physical-world messiness, scientifically hard problems. An AI Slack bot connecting to databases is a weekend hackathon project for a strong engineer. The hard part isn't building it; it's competing with everyone else who can also build it, including three of the largest software companies in the world. I need to see founders who are tackling something that makes other founders wince, and I need to see evidence that these specific founders have the determination and adaptability to win a competitive market. I see neither.
