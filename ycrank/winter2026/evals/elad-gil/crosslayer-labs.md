# Crosslayer Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The founding team didn't just study Internet security — they wrote the standard that now governs how every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate gets issued globally. The CA/Browser Forum's Ballot SC-067 mandating Multi-Perspective Issuance Corroboration went into effect March 2025, and these three founders invented it. That's not a credential. That's a structural position. When I invested in Coinbase, part of the thesis was that teams who deeply understand regulatory dynamics build moats that software-only investors can't even see. Here, the moat is even more extreme: the founders authored the regulation, it's deployed at Let's Encrypt, Google Trust Services, and Cloudflare securing 8 million certificates daily, and they now want to build the commercial monitoring layer on top of the infrastructure they helped shape. A Princeton professor with an ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award, a researcher who coded the first MPIC implementation in 2016, and a PhD student whose published work maps directly to the product's cross-layer detection capability — this is founder-market fit at the deepest level I've seen in a pre-seed cybersecurity company.

The structural moment is real but needs careful parsing. The MPIC mandate creates a market education tailwind — CAs and enterprises that never considered BGP-layer attacks on their infrastructure now know the threat exists because they had to comply with a new standard. That's genuinely useful for shortening sales cycles. But the mandate also partially solves the problem at the certificate issuance layer, which could shrink one category of attacks the platform detects. The broader "why now" is that cross-layer Internet attacks are increasing in sophistication (the 2022 KLAYswap $2M theft is a good case study), and existing monitoring tools watch individual layers in isolation — BGP monitoring here, certificate transparency there, JavaScript integrity somewhere else. Nobody correlates across layers. This fits the "too boring, too niche" archetype that I've repeatedly found asymmetric returns in. PagerDuty looked like niche alerting software; it was actually operations infrastructure for every company running software. The question is whether cross-layer impersonation detection is similarly a wedge into something much larger, or whether it stays narrow.

That's where my primary concern sits: the product-to-distribution trajectory. The best security companies — CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, even Datadog — started with a wedge product and expanded into adjacent capabilities through the same customer relationship. Can cross-layer monitoring become a distribution channel for broader security products? Structurally, the monitoring relationship with enterprise security teams could be expanded into threat intelligence, incident response, or broader attack surface management. The founders' relationships with major CAs give them an unusual distribution asset — they've already convinced Google, Apple, and Cloudflare to adopt their standard. But I see no evidence that the team has articulated a multi-product vision or thought about distribution beyond a Calendly demo link. At pre-seed this isn't fatal, but it's the difference between a $200M outcome and a $2B outcome.

The bull case I'd need to believe: cross-layer impersonation detection is the entry point into a comprehensive "Internet presence security" platform. Healthcare systems, crypto exchanges, and banks don't just need to monitor for DNS/BGP/TLS/JavaScript attacks — they need continuous validation that their entire Internet-facing infrastructure is authentic and uncompromised. If the cross-layer correlation engine is genuinely differentiated (and the 15+ publications at USENIX, ACM CCS, NSDI suggest it is), then Crosslayer becomes the system-of-record for Internet authenticity. The SAM expands from "cross-layer impersonation" into the full EASM market ($6.5B by 2029). The regulatory relationships become distribution channels. The academic credibility becomes enterprise trust. In this scenario, the narrow wedge was actually a distribution strategy all along.

The bear case is more concrete. The SAM for cross-layer impersonation detection is a fraction of the EASM market — likely low hundreds of millions. Incumbents like CrowdStrike and Palo Alto could add this capability as a feature within their existing platforms rather than losing deals to a standalone product. The academic-to-commercial transition requires building sales, customer success, and product operations simultaneously — and the team has zero open positions and no visible revenue or customer traction. The customer education burden is real: most CISOs are spending their budgets on ransomware defense, endpoint security, and phishing prevention, not cross-layer impersonation. Convincing them to prioritize this attack vector requires sophisticated technical selling that first-time founders from academia may struggle to execute. This feels like it requires at least two miracles — build a production-grade enterprise product AND educate the market on a threat category most security teams don't yet prioritize.

I'm landing at a cautious neutral. The founding team's technical credibility is genuinely exceptional — among the strongest I've seen at this stage. The regulatory inflection point is real and the "too boring" market archetype applies. But the market size uncertainty, the absence of any commercial traction signal, and the product-to-distribution gap make this a "watch and see if they can execute the commercial transition" rather than a "write a check today." If I saw evidence of a signed LOI with a healthcare system or crypto exchange, or evidence that the founders had thought about platform expansion beyond cross-layer monitoring, my conviction would shift materially.
