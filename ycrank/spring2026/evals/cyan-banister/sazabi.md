# Sazabi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: this is a developer tools company entering the single most crowded investment category in tech right now. AI observability. Every fund I know has a thesis slide about this space. Arize just raised $70M. LangSmith is at a billion-plus valuation. Langfuse got scooped up by ClickHouse before it turned two. Datadog -- a company worth more than $50 billion -- is bolting AI features onto its platform as we speak. And Sazabi is still on a waitlist, no product in the wild, asking me to believe they'll carve a moat in a field where well-capitalized players are already planting flags. When I think about where I've made money, it's never been in the space where every investor in the room is nodding along. This is the room where everyone is nodding.

When I ask "tell me Sherwood's story," what I get back is a career arc: Dev Bootcamp, Crunchbase, Brex infrastructure, then Opkit -- an AI phone calling company for healthcare that got acquired by 11x. After that, he ran engineering on Alice, one of the bigger agentic systems in production. That's a real thread, and the Opkit exit is proof he can build, ship, and reach an outcome. The a16z scout role tells me he's networked. The angel list -- people from Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, Replit, Brex -- is genuinely strong signal from the exact ecosystem this product serves. But I need to be honest: when I hear this story, I hear someone who saw a market gap from inside a series of relevant jobs. That's founder-market fit through career logic. It's not someone whose life cracked open in a way that made this problem *theirs* in a way nobody else could claim. The Dev Bootcamp origin in 2014 -- that's a self-taught thread I respect, because I recognize the resourcefulness in choosing that path. But it doesn't change the fundamental read: this idea found Sherwood through his LinkedIn, not through his biography.

The piece that genuinely doesn't work for my framework is the invisible system question. Observability tools are the *opposite* of opaque -- they exist to make things visible. Datadog isn't a cartel extracting value from people who don't understand the game. It's a well-priced product that engineers evaluate on clear criteria and choose with full information. Sazabi's manifesto argues that traditional "three pillars" create cognitive overload, that logs alone are sufficient if AI reconstructs the rest. That's a UX argument, maybe even a clever architecture argument. But it's not exposing a hidden tax. It's not making an invisible system visible. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity, there was a specific structure -- hidden interest, deferred charges, penalty fees -- that extracted value from people who didn't understand what they were paying. Where's that structure in observability? Engineers aren't victims of Datadog's information asymmetry. They're customers who can read a pricing page.

The strongest bull case, and I want to be honest about it: if AI coding agents really do write the majority of production code within five years, someone needs to build the observability layer that captures *why the agent decided what it decided*, not just what the code does. Traditional APM tools genuinely weren't designed for this -- they instrument human-authored code, not agent decision chains. If Sazabi's "logs are all you need" thesis holds up at scale, and they define the telemetry schema for how agent decisions get logged, that schema could become infrastructure in the way that OpenTelemetry standardized distributed tracing. The compliance certifications -- SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, ISO 27017 -- are unusually mature for a ten-person team and suggest operational seriousness that most pre-seed companies don't bother with. And the timing is real: this is an inflection point for how code gets written. If I squint, I can see a version of this where Sazabi becomes the agent-native successor to Datadog. But that "if" is doing enormous work, and the path runs directly through a $50 billion incumbent that's already moving.

I wish Sherwood well. He's clearly competent, well-connected, and building in a space he understands. But this is consensus AI in a consensus market, targeting an already-transparent industry, with well-funded competitors already at scale. There's no hidden structure to disrupt, no stigma keeping other investors away, no biographical inevitability driving the founder. It's the kind of company that might win through excellent execution -- but excellent execution bets in crowded markets aren't where I've made my money. My biggest returns came from writing the check that nobody else would write. Everyone wants to write this check.
