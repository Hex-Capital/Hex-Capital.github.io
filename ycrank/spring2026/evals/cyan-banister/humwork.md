# Humwork -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Tendem, backed by $72M from Bezos Expeditions and Nebius, already operates an MCP-based expert network with 10,000+ specialists — ten times Humwork's 1,000+ verified experts — and has existing relationships with Anthropic, the company that created MCP in the first place. This is the fact I cannot get past. When I backed Uber, there were 300+ investors saying no. When I wrote the SpaceX check, rockets were blowing up and nobody else wanted in. Humwork is entering a space where a well-capitalized incumbent already occupies the exact same technical layer, the YC Spring 2026 batch was "dominated by autonomous agent pitches," and every venture firm in San Francisco is tripping over itself to fund AI agent infrastructure. This isn't a hard space. This is the easiest space to pitch at a dinner party in 2026. The absence of contrarian friction is itself a signal — when everyone agrees the market is hot, the returns accrue to whoever got there first with the deepest moat, and Humwork is not that company.

Yash Goenka's founding story is what I'd call prolific but untethered. Instawrite.ai, Jarvys.ai, Iris BCI, Phonecall.bot, Qualify.bot, now Humwork — six startups, and the pivot from Qualify.bot (YC S25, launched July 2025) to Humwork (YC P26, launched April 2026) happened in roughly nine months, both operating under Orange AI Inc. When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," I need to hear why this specific problem found this specific founder. What I hear instead is a talented builder scanning the landscape for the next wave. Yash has real technical depth — LLM work since 2021, a patent on graphene supercapacitors, UC Berkeley rocketry — and Rohan Datta brings five years of data science at Microgrid Labs and an AI voice platform handling 1M+ call minutes. Their 16-year friendship and roommate history suggests a stable co-founder dynamic, which matters. But the biographical connection to Humwork's problem — AI agents getting stuck on edge cases — reads as observed market gap, not lived necessity. The Qualify.bot work (real-time AI-to-human handoff in loan qualification) does create structural familiarity with the problem shape, and that's worth something. It's not nothing. But it's the difference between a craftsperson who can build in any domain and a founder who would be working on this problem without funding.

Humwork's 87% resolution rate and 130% net retention in software engineering are genuinely impressive pre-seed metrics if independently verifiable — those numbers suggest real value being delivered. The <2 minute average first reply against a 30-second matching SLA shows operational discipline. Let me construct the strongest bull case: MCP is a genuine protocol inflection point, like APIs standardizing in the early 2010s, and whoever builds the highest-quality routing layer between agents and human experts at this exact moment captures switching costs that compound. Humwork's narrower focus on real-time agent debugging — coding agents looping on bugs, legal research agents hitting edge cases — could yield faster iteration and higher resolution rates than Tendem's broader task orientation. YC accepted Yash twice across consecutive batches, which is a meaningful institutional vote of confidence. The 130% net retention specifically suggests that software engineering teams who try Humwork expand their usage, which is early product-market fit signal. If Humwork could prove that narrow-vertical expert quality consistently beats Tendem's breadth-first approach, there's a wedge here. But — and this is where I come back to my framework — the MCP integration layer is technically replicable by any company with an expert network. Tendem has already done it. Upwork or Fiverr could build one. Anthropic itself could build first-party expert escalation into Claude. Humwork's defensibility hinges entirely on expert supply quality and matching speed, and at 1,000 experts versus Tendem's 10,000+, that moat is a speed bump, not a wall.

Humwork attacks no invisible system. When an AI agent gets stuck on an edge case, the developer knows it — the agent loops visibly, throws errors, makes the same bad architectural guess. The pain is real but transparent. There's no opaque structure extracting value through information asymmetry, no hidden tax being imposed on people who don't understand the system. Compare this to Affirm exposing credit card opacity, or Ownwell attacking property tax assessment opacity, or Carta making cap table management legible. Humwork is a speed and convenience improvement — reducing a multi-hour manual rescue to a 30-second expert handoff — and that's valuable, but it's not structural disruption. The people Humwork serves are software engineers and AI power users, among the most empowered and information-rich workers on the planet. The cascading impact is real but bounded: better AI agent reliability helps developers ship faster, and the expert side creates flexible work for domain specialists. But this doesn't expand access to something previously gatekept. It makes already-efficient processes more efficient for already-privileged users.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Yash and Rohan demonstrated a proprietary matching algorithm or resolution data asset that Tendem structurally cannot replicate — something where every resolution makes the next one faster and more accurate in a way that creates genuine compounding advantage — and if that moat were validated by a named enterprise customer with a signed contract, not just self-reported website metrics. That would change the defensibility calculus enough to overcome the consensus market and absent biographical forge. Right now, Humwork is a competent team building a useful product in the hottest, most crowded space in venture, against a competitor with ten times the resources and a head start. I wish them well. The founders are clearly talented builders. But this isn't a Long Journey bet — there's no biographical inevitability, no contrarian conviction required, no invisible system to expose, and no infrastructure moat that a well-funded competitor can't replicate faster.
