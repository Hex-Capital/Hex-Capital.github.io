# Panacea -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Panacea is a high-schlep, low-animal company: the FDA submission grind is the kind of unpleasant work I wrote about in "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012), but Haris Javed-Akhtar and Yaman Ziadeh have not, in this dossier, left the kind of pre-funding tracks that animals leave. The strongest fact on the page is the regulatory surface itself -- IND, NDA, BLA, 510(k), De Novo, PMA -- and the weakest is that the most resourceful act anyone at Panacea has performed publicly is launching a Cal.com booking link.

The schlep dimension is where Panacea places highest, and the ladder is short and clear. **Coinbase** (2012) sits at the high rung: Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind was itself the asset. **DoorDash** (2013) sits in the middle: last-mile delivery, unglamorous but not regulatorily exposed. The AI-wrapper team I described in my Pass Example sits at the bottom: pure software, no offline pain. Panacea is closer to the Coinbase rung than to the middle -- 4,049 510(k) submissions in FY2025, FDA averaging 117.27 days elapsed, ten distinct pathway acronyms in the YC blurb -- this is precisely the kind of work other Spring 2026 applicants are routing around. Fixed-fee pricing in an industry where MDI Consultants has billed hourly since 1978 is also a schlep-on-top-of-a-schlep, because Panacea is absorbing FDA timeline risk that incumbents successfully push onto clients. That is contrarian in the way I like.

The animal-resourcefulness dimension is where Panacea places lowest, and it is the dimension I weight most. **Airbnb** (2009) is the high rung -- the cereal-box stunt, 1,000 boxes at $40 each, financed the company before any institutional money. **Dropbox** (2007) is the middle rung -- Drew Houston shipped a demo video to Hacker News before the seed check, less theatrical than cereal but a shipped artifact. The MBA AI-wrapper team is the low rung -- glossy deck, no artifact. Panacea is barely above that bottom rung. The dossier reports a website last updated May 16, 2026, a Principal Consultant named Harun Sinha, and a customer testimonial about "12x faster" work -- but no Panacea-the-company submissions, no revenue figure, no signed LOI with a named biotech, no Armstrong-style hand-built prototype that overturned a "no." Recruiting one ex-FDA consultant is not nothing, but it is closer to a hire than to a stunt.

On organic-to-founder pain, **Dropbox** (2007) is the high rung (Houston's USB stick), **Stripe** (2010) the middle (Collisons hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007)), the MBA wrapper team the low. Panacea sits closer to the bottom: Haris carries an MD per his LinkedIn handle, which supplies authority, but the dossier contains zero evidence that either founder personally filed an IND or a 510(k) and got burned by the schlep. The pain belongs to their customers, not to them, and that is the failure mode "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) warned about.

On hacker genealogy, **Stripe** (2010)'s Patrick Collison won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work; **Reddit** (2005)'s Swartz co-authored the RSS spec; the wrapper MBAs had no GitHub. Yaman Ziadeh at UIC, then Bloomberg, then Anterior (a YC AI healthcare company) is a credible engineer -- mid-rung -- but the dossier explicitly notes "No public data found" on his GitHub or open-source footprint. Hackers leave trails; Yaman's trail is a resume, not a repo. On cofounder configuration, the **Airbnb** (2009) trio roomed together; the Collison brothers shared a childhood; my Red Flag is "founder pair met for the purpose of the startup." Panacea has two founders with "no shared employer or university overlap" per Phase 3 findings -- which is closer to the hackathon-pair anti-pattern than to the Collison brothers, though I'll grant the YC interview usually surfaces chemistry I can't see from a dossier.

My 36-month forecast: Panacea reaches $1?3M of fixed-fee revenue serving a dozen small biotech and medical-device clients, gets squeezed between Weave Bio's $20M Series A war chest above and incumbents like MDI and EMMA below, and either ends up acquired by a mid-tier consultancy for under $30M or quietly pivots to a pure-software regulatory drafting tool to escape the consultant-labor margin trap. The mechanism is the fixed-fee timeline risk: FDA averages 1.58 review cycles per submission, and Panacea is absorbing every overrun while Weave Bio sells software at SaaS margins.

The strongest case against me: Coinbase looked like this in 2012. Brian Armstrong was not a regulatory veteran when he started -- the regulatory schlep itself was the moat, and animals are revealed by the grind, not before it. If Panacea's fixed-fee structure forces them to actually ship submissions faster than MDI -- if the "12x faster" testimonial converts into named-biotech revenue -- the Coinbase analogy holds and I will have missed it. The reason I am not bending: Armstrong overturned his **Coinbase** (2012) rejection by hand-building an Android Bitcoin wallet, an artifact I could touch. Panacea's dossier contains no equivalent artifact -- no shipped tool, no public submission accepted by FDA under Panacea's name, no founder anecdote of personally suffering a Pre-Sub. The schlep is real; the animal is not yet visible. One verifiable condition would flip me: three named ex-FDA reviewers signed exclusively to Panacea plus three paying biotech customers cited by name within 90 days. Until then, pass.
