# Didit -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Didit is what it's not: a non-obvious market. Identity verification is a $14 billion market with multiple unicorns already in play -- Persona at $2B valuation with $141M in revenue, Veriff at $1.5B, Onfido acquired for $650M. There are no fewer than four well-capitalized competitors doing essentially what Didit does. When I ask "what has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this market possible now?", the answer is generic: deepfakes are getting better, regulations are expanding globally. Both of those shifts benefit every player in the market equally. There is no structural vacancy here -- no Project Maven cancellation creating a defense AI vacuum, no developer API gap that only one team saw. This is a consensus market where the competitive dynamics are already well-understood by every venture investor on Sand Hill Road. That's the opposite of where I find asymmetric returns.

The bull case centers on pricing disruption creating incumbent business model tension -- and it's worth taking seriously. Didit offers 500 free KYC checks per month with per-check pricing starting at $0.03. Entrust paid $650M for Onfido's per-verification fee revenue; matching a free tier would cannibalize that investment. This resembles the dynamic that made it hard for legacy payment processors to compete with Stripe's developer-first pricing. And the execution metrics are genuinely impressive: 700+ B2B customers, near-profitable on just $2M raised, claimed 300%+ NRR by month 6. If those numbers are real, this team has Sumsub-like capital efficiency -- Sumsub hit $85.6M revenue on $7.5M raised, proving KYC can be built lean. But pricing disruption alone isn't a structural inflection. Persona could introduce a free tier next quarter with $417M in the bank. A pricing moat at the low end is real but structurally caps you in the SMB segment, and the enterprise segment is where the margin lives.

The execution velocity deserves credit independent of my market concerns. Building a full-stack identity verification system from scratch -- 14,000+ document types across 190+ countries, ISO 27001/27017/27018 certifications, iBeta Level 1 -- on $2M in capital with a 12-person team is operationally impressive. Alberto Rosas's GitHub shows real ML depth (StyleGAN2 implementation with 143 stars isn't a toy project). The v3 launch with a drag-and-drop workflow engine in January 2026 shows the team is shipping, not just pitching. Twin co-founders who've been building together since age 12 -- that eliminates co-founder relationship risk entirely, which I treat as existential at pre-seed.

Where this falls apart for my framework is the product-to-distribution trajectory and the Web3 overhang. On distribution: the free-tier PLG funnel into paid features (AML screening at $0.20, active liveness, white label) is sensible, and the multi-product surface area (verification, biometrics, AML, proof of address, age estimation) shows platform thinking. But Persona already owns the deeper workflow orchestration layer for enterprises, and Sumsub already covers end-to-end KYC/KYB/transaction monitoring. Didit is competing on price in a market where the leaders compete on depth. On the Web3 question: the GitHub org is still "didit-protocol," the whitepaper still lives at whitepaper.didit.me, and $GMM token holders were offered airdrops tied to Didit's growth. Enterprise compliance teams doing vendor due diligence on their KYC provider will find metaverse tokens and SocialFi connections. That creates real friction in exactly the buyer segment where trust matters most.

The AI agent angle -- MCP server for programmatic identity verification -- is the most forward-looking element. If autonomous agents need to verify human identities at massive scale, verification volumes could increase by orders of magnitude. But Persona is already positioning for "the verified identity layer for an agentic AI world," and this opportunity is speculative enough that it doesn't change my current assessment. I'd want to see evidence that agent-driven verification volumes are materializing, not just that the infrastructure exists. The single miracle here is unclear to me: is it winning enough volume at rock-bottom prices to build data flywheel superiority before well-funded competitors respond? Is it converting SMB free-tier users into enterprise contracts? Multiple obstacles need to break right simultaneously -- volume, margins, competitive response, enterprise trust despite Web3 history -- and that starts to look like a multi-miracle plan.

I'm passing. The execution is real, the capital efficiency is notable, and the pricing strategy creates genuine incumbent tension. But identity verification is a well-understood, well-funded market with no non-obvious inflection that uniquely advantages Didit. The strongest companies in my portfolio entered markets that looked wrong to other investors -- PagerDuty's alerting seemed boring, Coinbase's crypto seemed speculative, Harvey's legal AI seemed impossible. Identity verification doesn't look wrong to anyone; it looks like exactly what it is, which means the returns are already priced in by the existing competition.
