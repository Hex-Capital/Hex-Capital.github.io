# Indexable -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft game engine called Hyperion that handles 10,000 concurrent players in a single world — over a thousand GitHub stars, #1 on r/rust for a day. That's not a side project. That's someone who looked at the limits of concurrent state management and decided the existing tools weren't good enough, so he'd build from the metal up. Now he's doing the same thing for AI agent sandboxes: Indexable is a custom VMM on KVM, a modified Linux 6.19 kernel with upstream-unsupported patches, a content-addressable storage engine with cross-tenant deduplication, Nix reimplemented in Rust. When I read that job posting for a Founding Engineer requiring C, Rust, and deep Unix, I see someone building real infrastructure, not skinning Firecracker and calling it a platform. Gazelka's prior exit with BountyBot and his time as Member of Technical Staff at xAI add credibility. But here's what I keep coming back to: when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — I get a résumé. I get credentials. I get Hyperion and xAI and University of Minnesota CS. What I don't get is the biographical crucible that makes sandbox infrastructure the problem Andrew was forged to solve. The technical obsession is visible; the personal inevitability is not.

Indexable's custom VMM approach — millisecond forking of entire running environments including files, processes, memory, and databases — is genuinely differentiated from E2B's Firecracker microVMs ($21M Series A, 88% Fortune 100 adoption), Daytona's Docker-in-Docker with ~90ms cold starts ($24M Series A), Blaxel's ~25ms cold starts ($7.3M Seed), and Modal's $1.1B-valued serverless GPU platform ($87M Series B). The technical approach is contrarian in method — nobody else is building their own hypervisor from scratch — but the space itself is consensus-hot. AI agent infrastructure is where every VC in my world wants to deploy capital right now. Five well-funded competitors are already selling to the same developer persona. This doesn't make sophisticated investors shift uncomfortably in their chairs; it makes them reach for their checkbooks. When I backed SpaceX, private rocketry was considered insane. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. Indexable is building in a space that VCs are fighting over, and the technical contrarianism of the custom VMM doesn't compensate for the consensus enthusiasm of the market category.

Indexable reminds me structurally of Crusoe Energy in one specific way: both companies built deep, ground-up infrastructure for a workload that other players were serving with off-the-shelf components. Crusoe built portable data centers when everyone else was renting from hyperscalers. Indexable is building a custom VMM when everyone else wraps Firecracker. The parallel is real — and Crusoe's custom infrastructure turned out to be the wedge into a much larger market (AI data centers via the Stargate project). If Indexable's millisecond forking and content-addressable storage become the foundation for something beyond AI agent sandboxes, there's a Crusoe-like transformation possible. But the parallel breaks in a critical place: Crusoe operated in a space with no well-funded competitors doing the same thing. Nobody else was putting data centers on flare stacks in North Dakota. Indexable faces five funded competitors who are already generating revenue — E2B at $1.5M as of June 2025, Daytona at $1M forward run rate in under three months. The infrastructure-at-inflection pattern is my highest-conviction unstated pattern, and AI agent adoption is clearly the inflection. But being at the right inflection matters less if you arrive after the land rush.

The invisible system test is where Indexable falls quiet for me. I look for companies that expose opaque, extractive structures — the taxi medallion cartel behind Uber, the credit card opacity behind Affirm, the property tax system behind Ownwell. Indexable is building better developer tooling. It's building faster, more capable sandboxes for AI agents. That's valuable, but it's not dismantling a hidden structure that exploits information asymmetry against people who don't understand it. Cloud infrastructure pricing has some opacity, but Indexable isn't attacking that — they're competing on performance within an existing market category. There's no invisible tax being eliminated, no gatekeepers being disempowered. The company serves developers and enterprises who are already sophisticated buyers. The cascading real-world impact follows the same pattern: better AI agent infrastructure may eventually enable AI agents that expand access to services for underserved populations, but that's several degrees of separation from Indexable's direct value proposition.

The bull case for Indexable is genuinely strong, and I want to name it clearly before I explain why I'm not writing a check. Andrew Gazelka has precisely the right technical DNA — Hyperion proves he can build high-performance concurrent systems from scratch in Rust, and his xAI experience gives him direct exposure to the AI infrastructure gap. The custom VMM with millisecond forking could deliver a 10-100x performance advantage over Firecracker-based competitors, and in infrastructure markets, that kind of performance gap can let a three-person team beat companies with $87M in the bank. The content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication could create a multi-tenant efficiency moat that compounds with scale — a genuine infrastructure moat, not a brand moat. And the [inferred] 12-18+ months to replicate the full stack creates a real window. If I apply my ten-year question — where does this go? — a hypervisor purpose-built for stateful branching could become foundational infrastructure far beyond AI agent sandboxes. But the biographical forge is absent, the market is consensus, and the invisible system criterion goes unmet. I would flip to Invest on one condition: if Andrew can show me that Indexable's fork semantics enable a capability that is structurally impossible on Firecracker — not faster, but impossible — and that a specific category of AI agent workload requires it. Performance advantages get competed away; capability gaps create moats.

Indexable scores highest on infrastructure defensibility — the custom VMM, modified kernel, and content-addressable storage are genuine technical barriers that would take competitors substantial time to replicate, and the inflection timing with AI agent adoption is real. Indexable scores lowest on invisible system disruption and contrarian conviction — it's better tooling in a hot market, not a structural revelation in a stigmatized one. The founder's technical capability is undeniable; the biographical inevitability is not. This is a competent team building real infrastructure in a space I can't get excited about through my specific lens, even though I recognize it may work through someone else's.
