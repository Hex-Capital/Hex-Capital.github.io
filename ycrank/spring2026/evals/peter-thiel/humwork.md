# Humwork -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Tendem, backed by $72 million from Bezos Expeditions and Nebius, already operates a 10,000-expert MCP-based network routing human specialists to AI agents — the identical thesis Humwork is pursuing with one-tenth the supply at 1,000 experts. The existence of a well-funded competitor executing the same playbook through the same integration standard is not incidental; it is definitional. When two companies build the same product on the same open protocol for the same customers, you are not looking at a monopoly candidate — you are looking at a market. Humwork's dossier itself contains what amounts to a competitive landscape slide listing Tendem ($72M), Invisible Technologies ($144M raised, $134M revenue), Surge AI ($1.2B revenue), and Braintrust ($242.5M). The presence of that slide is the clearest signal available: Humwork has failed to escape competition before it has meaningfully entered it.

Humwork's implicit secret — that AI agents will frequently stall and require real-time human rescue — is not a secret at all. It is the consensus view of every developer who has used a coding agent for more than twenty minutes. The YC Spring 2026 batch was, by press accounts, "dominated by autonomous agent pitches," which means the agent-failure problem is not hidden knowledge but ambient awareness. The 30-second matching SLA is an operational achievement, not a contrarian insight. Matching speed is a feature, not a secret — and features are competed away by anyone with capital and an expert bench. Toloka has spent years building precisely that bench across global, multilingual coverage. Humwork's 87% resolution rate and 130% net retention in software engineering are respectable execution metrics, but execution metrics describe how well you are running the race, not whether the race is worth winning. The question my framework asks first is whether being right about the underlying thesis produces structural dominance, and the answer here is plainly no — being right that agents need humans produces a competitive marketplace, not a monopoly.

Yash Goenka pivoted from Qualify.bot, his YC S25 company launched in July 2025, to Humwork by April 2026 — nine months between ventures, both operating under the same legal entity, Orange AI Inc. His personal site lists additional prior startups: Instawrite.ai, Jarvys.ai, Iris BCI, Phonecall.bot. This is the profile of a founder in search mode — testing ideas, measuring responses, iterating toward product-market fit. It is the lean-startup methodology in its purest expression, and it is precisely the indefinite thinking I reject. A definite founder does not cycle through five startups in four years looking for the one that sticks; a definite founder identifies a specific truth about the world and builds irreversibly toward it. The 16-year co-founder relationship between Goenka and Rohan Datta — former UC Berkeley roommates — does satisfy my prehistory criterion, and Datta's experience building an AI voice platform handling 1M+ call minutes demonstrates genuine technical execution. But shared prehistory and technical competence do not compensate for the absence of a definite plan. These founders are good at building things quickly; they have not demonstrated that they know what to build.

The bull case an advocate would construct is not trivial: Humwork sits at the intersection of two genuinely explosive trends — the proliferation of autonomous AI agents and the irreducible need for human judgment at failure points. MCP, released by Anthropic in late 2024, created a standardized integration layer that gives Humwork distribution across Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, and every MCP-compatible agent through a single 60-second server install. That distribution architecture is elegant — it is the closest thing in Humwork's dossier to a genuine insight. The 130% net retention suggests that teams who use Humwork expand their usage, which is the signature of real utility. And the founders' prior work on Qualify.bot — real-time AI-to-human handoffs for commercial lenders — is directly transferable domain experience. An advocate would argue that this is early enough in MCP's lifecycle that whoever builds the densest expert network first wins through matching-speed network effects, and Humwork's 30-second SLA represents a beachhead monopoly in real-time agent debugging before Tendem's broader task orientation catches up. Could this be a Lyft exception — a category creating so much total value that even the second player generates fund-relevant returns? The human-in-the-loop market projected at $16.4B by 2030 does not have the magnitude required for that exception. Lyft worked because ride-sharing was creating hundreds of billions in value; a $16 billion market split among multiple players with no winner-take-all dynamics produces modest outcomes, not power-law returns.

Humwork's MCP integration layer — the core of its technical architecture — is, by the dossier's own admission, "technically replicable" by "any company with an expert network." This is a pure software middleware company with no physical-world technology, no hardware, no regulatory barrier, and no proprietary data asset that compounds over time. The resolution data could theoretically train matching algorithms, but the dossier marks this moat as "nascent," and Tendem's 10x larger expert base generates 10x more resolution data from which to learn. More fundamentally, Anthropic created MCP and could build first-party expert escalation directly into Claude — at which point Humwork's entire distribution channel becomes a platform feature rather than an independent business. This is the structural fragility of building on someone else's protocol: you are one product update away from irrelevance.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: if Humwork secured an exclusive, multi-year integration agreement with a major AI platform — Anthropic, OpenAI, or Google — that made Humwork the default expert escalation layer rather than one of many MCP servers, that would create the kind of structural lock-in I look for. An exclusive platform partnership would transform Humwork from a competitive marketplace into an embedded monopoly, following the Palantir pattern of becoming operationally indispensable to the platform's users. Without that, Humwork is competing in the open market against better-capitalized players pursuing identical theses — and competition, as I have said many times, is for those who have already lost.
