# Valence -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The founding team here stopped me. Daniel Kasabov-Nouvion built FPGA systems at IMC Trading and worked at Optiver -- two of the most technically demanding market-making firms on the planet. Neo Wang did latency-sensitive strategy research at DRW and interned at Cubist Systematic Strategies and Citadel Securities. Arthur Zhou is a USAMO qualifier and USACO Platinum competitor. This isn't a team that read about prediction markets in TechCrunch and decided to build something. These are the people who build exchange infrastructure for a living. When I think about whether I'd describe these founders as "animals" at their craft -- yes, without hesitation. Daniel wrote an order book in Rust for his college finance club. Neo previously built something called "FTX Markets," literally an event-contract exchange prototype. The technical density per founder here is among the highest I've seen at pre-seed.

The idea passes my organic test convincingly. When you spend your career building low-latency trading systems at places like Optiver and DRW, and then prediction markets fragment across Kalshi and Polymarket with separate order books and execution infrastructure, you don't need a brainstorming session to see the opportunity. You see it because it's the same problem every mature asset class faces, and you already know how to solve it. Traditional equities have Bloomberg and Trading Technologies sitting between traders and exchanges. Crypto has 1inch aggregating across DEXes. Prediction markets have nobody doing this yet. Neo had already built an event-contract exchange before Valence existed. The problem found these founders through their lived experience -- they didn't go looking for it.

The schlep is genuine but worth examining carefully. Cross-venue aggregation in prediction markets isn't just connecting APIs -- it requires co-located servers at exchange data centers, smart order routing across fundamentally different market structures (CFTC-regulated Kalshi vs. crypto-native Polymarket on Polygon), and navigating uncharted regulatory territory as an intermediary that doesn't neatly fit existing classifications. Most smart engineers would unconsciously filter this out -- not because it's impossible, but because the combination of exchange politics, regulatory ambiguity, and infrastructure capital makes it feel like more trouble than it's worth. For these founders, the technical execution isn't the schlep -- that's their natural habitat. The real schlep is the business development side: convincing exchanges that a third-party aggregator routing orders across their competitors is in their interest.

That exchange dependency is the structural risk I can't ignore. Valence's entire value proposition sits on top of Kalshi's and Polymarket's willingness to provide API access. Kalshi just raised $1B at an $11B valuation -- they have every incentive to own the trader experience end-to-end. If Kalshi restricts API access or builds competitive aggregation features, Valence's moat vanishes overnight. This isn't like Stripe, which built its own relationships with card networks and banks. It's closer to building an OTA that depends on airlines choosing to share inventory. Airlines eventually pushed back. Exchanges might too. The mitigation -- that Valence brings incremental cross-venue volume exchanges wouldn't otherwise capture -- is plausible but fragile.

The traction claims deserve honest scrutiny. The website reports $200M+ monthly volume, 1B+ contracts traded, and 50% peak market share on individual events. For a three-person pre-seed company, those would be extraordinary numbers. One testimonial references a quant firm trading "$150M+ monthly" through the platform. But these are all self-reported with no independent verification, and the dossier flags this correctly. Meanwhile, the Twitter account has 331 followers, which creates cognitive dissonance against the volume claims. I don't want to assume the numbers are wrong -- if a quantitative trading firm is genuinely running $150M monthly through Valence's infrastructure, that's meaningful economic dependency of the kind I look for. But I'd want to verify before writing a large check. If the numbers are real, this is already a working business, not a prototype.

What makes this interesting despite the risks is the timing dynamic. Prediction market fragmentation is accelerating, not consolidating. Robinhood, DraftKings, FanDuel, and Crypto.com are all launching their own prediction products -- each a walled garden. Every new entrant makes cross-venue aggregation more valuable, not less. This is the opposite of most competitive dynamics, where more competitors erode your position. Valence gets stronger as the market fragments further. The founders are building picks and shovels during a gold rush, which is a pattern I've funded repeatedly -- Stripe for payments, Replit for development environments. The question is whether the pick-and-shovel analogy holds when the gold mines can refuse to let you sell shovels on their property.

The bull case is straightforward: three elite quant-trading hackers who came from exactly the right background, discovered exactly the right problem through lived experience, and may already have meaningful traction. The bear case is that exchange dependency makes this a business that exists at the pleasure of its upstream suppliers, and the traction is unverified. I lean invest, but at the lower end of my conviction range -- the founder-problem fit and technical depth are genuinely compelling, and the exchange dependency risk, while real, may be manageable in a fragmenting market where no single exchange can dominate.
