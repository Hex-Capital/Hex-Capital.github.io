# Asimov -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The structure of this problem is interesting. Everyone in robotics knows the data bottleneck exists. Language models train on the entire internet; robots train on a few thousand hours of teleoperation in labs. That gap has been visible for years. And the obvious solution -- put cameras on regular people doing regular tasks and sell the footage to robotics teams -- involves exactly the kind of operational ugliness that would make a typical ML researcher recoil. Shipping hardware to hotel workers in three countries, managing quality from non-technical contributors, building annotation pipelines that produce clean 3D pose data from shaky head-mounted cameras. That's schlep. In 2023, this would have been a textbook schlep-blindness opportunity.

The problem is, it's 2026 and the blindness has lifted. micro1 is already shipping Ray-Ban glasses for identical capture at $100M+ ARR. General Intuition raised $134M. Sensei is in the same YC network attacking from a different hardware angle. Scale AI looms over everything. When I wrote about schlep blindness, the power of the concept was that schleppy problems face structurally low competition because smart people unconsciously filter them out. But when a market becomes hot enough -- when humanoid robot companies raise billions and the data constraint becomes the topic of every robotics conference -- the schlep stops being invisible. It becomes a gold rush. And in a gold rush, the advantage shifts from "willing to do the hard thing" to "most capitalized to do the hard thing at scale." That's a race Asimov is poorly positioned to win against micro1's $35M war chest and Scale AI's $16B+ in cumulative funding.

The founder backgrounds have the right ingredients without the decisive signal. Anshul Verma did ML research at BAIR and worked at Scale AI, so he's seen data marketplaces from the inside. Lyem Ningthou is described as a defense tech robotics researcher who previously worked at a YC W24 company. These are relevant experiences. But the dossier contains no moment of organic discovery -- no "I was trying to train a robot and couldn't get the data" story that would tell me this idea grew from genuine personal frustration rather than a smart reading of market trends. Two Berkeley EECS undergrads looking at the robotics data gap and deciding to build a marketplace is a perfectly reasonable origin story. It's just not the kind that makes me confident they'll endure the five years of painful execution this will require.

What does impress me is the operational reality of 5,000+ contributors across three continents. At pre-seed. That's not a pitch deck -- somebody actually shipped hardware, recruited workers, and got footage flowing. It suggests real hustle. It reminds me of what I look for in terms of doing things that don't scale: founders who are in the field making it happen rather than sitting in a co-working space modeling TAM. But I want to know more about the demand side. "Trusted by frontier robotics teams" with zero named customers is marketing copy. The addressable buyer set right now is maybe a dozen companies -- Physical Intelligence, Figure, 1X, Skild AI, a few others. When your total customer universe fits in a conference room, a single lost deal can crater your revenue. I need to see that at least one of these labs is paying, or has signed an LOI, or is actively consuming this data in training runs. Without that, the contributor network is supply without proven demand.

The bull case is real and I want to acknowledge it honestly. If the humanoid robot market hits its projected trajectory -- $15B by 2030 -- the appetite for diverse, real-world human motion data could be enormous. And Asimov's approach captures something genuinely different from lab teleoperation: organic, unscripted human behavior in natural environments. A robot learning to cook from watching a thousand different people cook in their actual kitchens, with all the messy variation that implies, might learn better than one trained on staged demonstrations. If Asimov can lock in data supply agreements with enough diverse environments before competitors catch up, and if they can demonstrate measurably superior training outcomes from organic capture versus teleoperation or synthetic data, the data network effect could compound. The flywheel -- more diverse data attracts more robotics customers, more revenue funds more contributor deployments -- is sound in theory. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe Asimov can build a quality and diversity moat before micro1 and Scale AI replicate the contributor network approach with ten times the capital.

I don't believe that yet. The founders are smart and the schlep is real, but this is a consensus opportunity now, and the competitive moat depends on operational execution against better-funded rivals rather than on the kind of technical or insight advantage that lets small teams punch above their weight. This is closer to a category where capital wins than one where craft wins.
