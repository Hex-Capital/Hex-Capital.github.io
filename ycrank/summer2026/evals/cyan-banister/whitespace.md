# Whitespace -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Whitespace is a company whose strongest line — Alex Tung and Leon Yao "grew up in families running wholesale distribution businesses" — is the exact biographical claim I lean into, paired with a product I cannot touch unless I happen to run a Halton Foods or a Mercer & Co. That high/low pairing is the signature: an autobiography-shaped pitch wrapped around an ERP-integration abstraction, in a vertical where Pepper has already raised $128M and Choco $301M. The "grew up in" line is the one sentence in the whole dossier I want to be true. Everything else has to clear the bar that line sets.

Anduril (2017) is the right HIGH rung for founder origin: Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from a parents'-garage to scale before he ever touched defense, so when he showed up pitching counter-drone, the biography was already on the table. The shared mechanism with Whitespace is supposed to be the same — a founder who lived adjacent to the problem before the company existed. The dossier evidence cuts the wrong way, though. Alex Tung's last job was Chief of Staff / Head of GTM at Ankar AI, an Atomico-backed AI IP startup in London — that is a founder who learned the B2B-AI sales motion, not a founder who answered the phones at the family wholesale operation in frustration. If the distribution childhood were load-bearing, Alex would have left a warehouse, not an Index-adjacent London cap table. Leon Yao the dossier cannot even confirm. I read the "grew up in" claim as decorative rather than originating — the right words from founders who learned the right words.

The consensus YC AI-agents-for-vertical-X darling is the LOW rung for "magically weird," and its failure mechanism is exactly what I see in Whitespace: the deck arrives in a lane where six funded competitors have already drawn the map for the LP class. Proton.ai at $24M raised, Pepper at $128.1M with $30B GMV across 500+ distributors, Choco at $301M and a unicorn mark — those are not three obscure incumbents, those are three priced-in answers to the same question. Whitespace's "horizontal multi-agent OS" framing versus Pepper's vertical depth is the kind of distinction that reads sharp on a slide and dissolves in the field. I am supposed to be finding them before it becomes consensus, and the consensus here is already two financings deep on three different cap tables. Nothing in the Whitespace dossier — no flared-gas-pad equivalent, no ketamine-clinic equivalent, no rocket equivalent — pulls it off that rung.

Mechanism analysis on the "Single CEO" axis: with a 2-person team and zero disclosed equity split or title hierarchy, Whitespace is sitting in the default 50/50 zone where I have been burned before. Over 18 months that compounds the wrong way — the first enterprise distributor contract will demand a single name on the signature line, the first hire above the founders will demand a single reporting line, and Whitespace has neither disclosed. Silence on governance is not a missing footnote; it is the founders telling me they have not had the conversation yet. HQ Trivia (2017) is what unresolved tiebreaker authority looks like in slow motion, and the Whitespace dossier shows none of the protective structure that would inoculate against it.

Counterfactual on the "unsexy / capital-intensive" axis: Whitespace would flip up a tier if the dossier showed a hardware artifact at the distributor — a scanner, an inbox appliance, a depot-floor device — anything that compounds against time the way a Crusoe data-center modular unit does. As written, Whitespace is software-only sitting on top of Epicor, Infor, SAP, NetSuite, and spreadsheets. Wholesale distribution is satisfyingly unsexy — that is real — but unsexiness without capital intensity is just B2B SaaS in worse clothes. Epicor's own 2025 AI blog tells me the incumbents are already shipping the agents Whitespace wants to charge for; the moat math does not finish in Whitespace's favor.

Single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability: HQ Trivia (2017) is the override case where I let a tactile artifact eat a governance red flag — I could open the app on my phone and feel the viral pull before any metric existed. Whitespace is the inverse. I cannot install it, I cannot photograph it, I cannot hand it to a friend; the "customers" Mercer & Co., Halton Foods, and Brightway Retail read as demo data. The Whitespace front door is a marketing page with claims of "10 hours saved per person per week, 5% order-volume uplift, 1% margin improvement" attached to no named buyer and no third-party validation. The HQ Trivia override is not available here because the artifact is invisible to anyone who is not already a paying distributor.

Forecast, 36 months: Whitespace either stalls between $500K and $2M ARR competing for the same mid-market distributors Proton.ai is courting and shuts down or acqui-hires into Epicor / NetSuite for under $15M, or it narrows to a single vertical (UK food wholesalers is the geographic tell) and ends up the cheaper Pepper-shaped competitor in one country. The mechanism is the signature: the autobiography is decorative rather than originating, so the founders pattern-match on the YC AI-vertical playbook rather than on a wound they actually carry, and the product stays invisible to anyone outside the buyer set, which means the market — not the founder — sets the ceiling. That ceiling is Proton.ai's $24M raised in a five-year-older company.

The bull case that stings: HandUp (~2014) is my own precedent for writing a check because the founder's biography mirrored the wound, commercial defensibility be damned. If Alex Tung and Leon Yao genuinely answered phones at the family wholesaler as teenagers, the social license to walk into a regional distributor's warehouse is not a small thing — it is the Crusoe-roughneck dynamic in miniature, and Pepper's $128M does not buy that. Whitespace would deserve a second meeting on those grounds alone. What dismantles it is the trajectory evidence: Alex's last role is GTM at an Atomico-backed AI IP company, not operations at a wholesaler — the biography is invoked but not inhabited. The condition that would flip me: one signed annual contract with a named non-demo distributor doing >$50M GMV, with the founder able to describe the specific moment in the warehouse — not the slide — when they knew this had to be built. Until then, pass.
