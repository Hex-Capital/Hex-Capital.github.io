# Ditto Biosciences -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that caught my eye about Ditto is how they found this idea. These three scientists didn't sit down and say "autoimmune disease is a $125 billion market, let's find an angle." They spent three years together at Arcadia Science -- a biotech specifically focused on understudied organisms -- analyzing parasites, phages, and evolutionary biology. Borges was studying how bacteriophages evade CRISPR systems. Sun was working on evolutionary genomics of arthropods. Weiss was doing microbiology and genomics at the intersection of computational screening. The insight that parasites have spent millions of years perfecting proteins to manipulate the human immune system, and that those proteins could be isolated and engineered into drugs, grew directly out of what they were already doing every day. This is what organic discovery looks like in biotech: the idea didn't come from TechCrunch, it came from a microscope.

The schlep here is real and multilayered. Everyone in pharma knows nature produces great drugs -- aspirin from willow bark, GLP-1 agonists from Gila monster venom. But mining the genomes of parasitic worms and ticks for immunomodulatory proteins? That's the biological equivalent of what scared everyone away from payments before the Collisons showed up. It requires simultaneous expertise in parasitology, evolutionary biology, computational genomics, protein engineering, and immunology -- a combination almost nobody has. And the previous attempt at parasite-derived therapeutics (Coronado Biosciences, live pig whipworms, failed Phase 2 in 2013) left behind a "this doesn't work" narrative that gives most smart people permission to look away. Ditto's insight is that the failure was in delivering live organisms, not in the underlying biology of the proteins themselves. That distinction is subtle enough that it functions as a natural filter against competition. Over 98% of viral and parasite proteins remain uncharacterized. This is an enormous, ugly design space that nobody else is systematically exploring because the work is too interdisciplinary and too stigmatized by prior failure.

The founding team is the biotech equivalent of hacker-founders. All three have PhDs in directly relevant fields. Borges has 50+ publications and a patent. They co-built computational tools together at Arcadia -- PreHGT for horizontal gene transfer screening, ProteinCartography for protein analysis. Borges has actual bioinformatics code on GitHub. They've already built a platform that analyzed over a million parasite-derived proteins and demonstrated 1-2 nanomolar binding affinities. These aren't business people hiring scientists -- they are the scientists, and they've already built the core of the product themselves. The three-year working relationship at Arcadia also passes my co-founder test decisively. They didn't meet at a co-founder matching event; they published papers together.

Where I get stuck is that my entire framework -- weekly growth rates, doing things that don't scale in user acquisition, ramen profitability, default alive -- was designed for companies that can ship something to humans and measure whether those humans want it. Biotech drug discovery doesn't work that way. There are no "users" pulling the product toward them. There's no path to ramen profitability. The company will need to raise hundreds of millions of dollars over 10+ years before there's any possibility of revenue. My check at $5K-$50K is a rounding error in the context of what this company needs to reach clinical trials, let alone market. The "growth" metrics are scientific -- proteins screened, binding affinities measured -- not business metrics I can track week over week.

The strongest bull case is that this is a platform play, not a single-drug bet. If the computational screening approach works, Ditto could generate a pipeline of novel drug candidates across the entire autoimmune disease spectrum, each protectable by composition-of-matter patents -- the strongest form of pharma IP. The analogies to natural blockbusters (aspirin, penicillin, taxol, GLP-1 agonists) are historically grounded, not aspirational. The team's credentials are genuinely exceptional for a pre-seed company -- Miller Fellowship, NSF Fellowship, Nature Microbiology publications, Illumina experience. And the timing argument is real: AI-driven protein analysis at this scale was computationally impractical five years ago. If I were going to make a Boom Supersonic-style exception -- overriding my software priors for a capital-intensive, long-timeline bet -- this is the kind of company that would trigger it. But Boom had a "frighteningly ambitious" quality that captured something beyond the business case. Parasite-derived immunomodulatory proteins, while scientifically elegant, doesn't quite reach that threshold of ambition magnitude where I override my own priors.

The organic discovery is genuine, the schlep is formidable, and the founders are real scientists who build. But biotech drug discovery sits outside the core of my framework in ways that make it hard for me to evaluate with confidence. I know how to judge whether a software company's early users genuinely need the product. I don't know how to judge whether 1-2 nanomolar binding affinity translates to clinical efficacy ten years from now. The idea passes my organic test and my schlep test. The team passes my technical-founder test and my co-founder test. But the absence of anything resembling user pull or growth trajectory isn't just a pre-seed gap -- it's structural to the industry they're in, and it leaves me without the signals I rely on most to distinguish good bets from interesting science.
