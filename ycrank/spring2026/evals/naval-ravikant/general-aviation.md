# General Aviation -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Ben Frank is a licensed pilot and aircraft mechanic with an MIT aerospace degree who already exited Seer Aerospace to Aviation Week in 2023 and ran Rotabull as CEO -- and he is building satellite-based ATC that gets sold to the FAA. That sentence is General Aviation's entire signature: deepest-quartile specific knowledge wrapped around the most permission-gated distribution architecture I evaluate. The founder is real; the leverage structure is the opposite of the one I underwrite. I read the leverage architecture first, and General Aviation's primary distribution channel is the FAA Common Automation Platform shortlist, which The Air Current (2025) reports already contains Collins, Leidos, Thales, Indra, and Frequentis. That is not a permissionless code-or-media business. That is capital-and-labor leverage that *requires* permission, and the permission-granters have a thirty-year vendor relationship with the people General Aviation must displace.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung on permissionless leverage -- media protocol, anyone posts, zero marginal cost of replication, no gatekeeper between the user and distribution. General Aviation diverges from that mechanism at every single load-bearing joint. The dossier names FAA certification, spectrum allocation via FCC/ITU, System Wide Information Management integration, and safety-of-life avionics type certification as the gates Ben Frank must clear before a single aircraft connects. Aireon just got bought by Iridium for $366.7M in May 2026 *because* the space-based ADS-B surveillance constellation is the regulatory chokepoint, not because of viral adoption. General Aviation's website (generalaviation.com) is marketing copy claiming "~50% cost reduction" with no shipped product -- the architecture is permissioned-by-design, and I have no MetaStable-style three-year pre-consensus accumulation in satellite ATC to override it the way I overrode the same objection on Anchorage Digital.

The bottom rung on marketplace-with-compounding-value is single-sided SaaS where each customer is economically independent of the next -- which is structurally what air traffic control *is*, because the buyer set is national air navigation service providers and there is exactly one FAA. General Aviation's pitch of "connecting every aircraft, drone, rocket, and ground vehicle" gestures at a network, but the dossier does not describe a mechanism where the Nth ANSP customer makes the (N-1) prior customers more valuable -- and the $14.7B ATM market (GMInsights 2025) is sliced into roughly 200 sovereign procurement processes, not a compounding two-sided graph. Stack Overflow added every developer's question to the answer corpus for every other developer; General Aviation does not have that mechanic in the dossier and the customer geometry forbids it.

Ben Frank's specific knowledge is where General Aviation actually compounds. Stack Overflow (2008) was founded by practicing developers writing for developers -- Atwood and Spolsky -- and the parallel here is clean: Frank holds a pilot certificate and a mechanic license, shipped Rotabull (aviation parts SaaS) and Seer Aerospace (fleet analytics, acquired by Aviation Week 2023), and then went to Rotor Technologies on autonomous helicopters before starting General Aviation. That is the founder-product-market-fit profile I underwrite -- discovered the problem as a user, multi-year operator depth, prior clean exit. On this dimension alone General Aviation would clear the seed gate I applied to Notion (2013) and Replit (April 2023). The dimension is real and the dossier evidence is unambiguous.

The big-market test cuts both ways for General Aviation. The $32B FAA modernization appropriation (Federal News Network, Dec 2025) sounds platform-shaped, but the pivot vectors I can actually trace from a satellite-ATC starting point are narrow: drone UTM (where Unifly already operates), ground-vehicle telematics (capital-intensive hardware), spaceport coordination (one or two customers globally). For General Aviation to flip this dimension a full tier, the dossier would need to name an adjacent commercial market -- private aviation connectivity, Part 135 operator analytics, something with non-FAA buyers -- where Ben Frank could land seed-runway revenue while the regulatory cycle grinds. The dossier names none, and "the first idea never works" (Venture Hacks 2007-10) is the axiom General Aviation cannot afford if the only second idea is also FAA-gated.

The Buffett trio reads fine on General Aviation. Frank exited Seer to Aviation Week in 2023 cleanly, has shipped two prior companies, holds operational credentials, and Twitter at 85 followers means he is not running the founder-as-influencer self-promotion pattern I distrust. The only structural concern is that the YC page lists team size as 0 -- not a co-founder dispute, not a 50/50 split with unresolved CEO authority, but an outright absence. Picking a co-founder is the most important decision a founder makes (Venture Hacks ~2007-10), and Ben Frank has deferred it. For a satellite-ATC company that needs avionics engineers, RF systems, regulatory affairs, and federal sales simultaneously, solo at seed is not integrity-failing, it is energy-bounded.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation pivots away from FAA Common Automation Platform replacement toward a drone UTM or Part 135 operator analytics adjacency by 2028, lands under $500K ARR through that pivot, and the FAA CAP contract goes to one of Collins/Leidos/Thales/Indra/Frequentis from the existing shortlist. The mechanism producing this is the dimensional signature itself -- Frank's specific knowledge is portable to adjacent aviation markets, but the leverage architecture of the founding bet is permissioned in a category where I have no pre-consensus DCA position to override it, so the team survives via pivot and the original ATC thesis does not.

The strongest case against my pass is the Anchorage Digital precedent: I funded a regulated, permission-gated business when the founder's specific knowledge and category-timing both passed at top quartile, and General Aviation's founder credentials genuinely rival that bar. The bull mechanism is that the $12.5B initial FAA appropriation plus GAO-25-108162's urgency framing creates a procurement opening wide enough that even a sub-shortlist vendor could land a Part 23 or Part 135 connectivity contract through a teaming arrangement, and Ben Frank's Aviation Week and Rotor Technologies relationships are exactly the network for that. The dismantling fact is that Anchorage had two things General Aviation does not: a co-founding team that cleared the energy gate, and my own three-year accumulation in crypto since MetaStable (2014) that gave me category-timing conviction the market lacked. General Aviation is a solo founder in a category I have not been quietly accumulating into, and the Iridium-Aireon consolidation just closed the satellite-aviation whitespace at $366.7M. The verdict flips to Invest if Ben Frank names a credible technical co-founder with avionics or satellite-systems depth AND the company announces a non-FAA design partner -- a Part 135 operator, a foreign ANSP, a DoD logistics customer -- within twelve months.
