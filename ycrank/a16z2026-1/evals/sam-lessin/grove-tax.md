# Grove Tax -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Every time you get pitched "AI automates professional services workflow," you should ask one question: can the incumbent add this as a feature by next quarter? Grove Tax is building AI agents for the tax return lifecycle — intake, document extraction, delivery — targeting the exact platforms Uday Nandam used to build product for. Intuit owns ProConnect and Lacerte. Thomson Reuters owns UltraTax. Both have announced AI-powered tax compliance features already. Nandam literally worked at both companies. He knows where the inefficiencies are, sure. But so do they. And they have distribution to millions of tax preparers, decades of client trust, and AI budgets that dwarf any pre-seed company's total capitalization. Grove Tax at $30 per return is building a feature for Intuit, not a company against Intuit.

The mispricing test fails immediately. AI for accounting is the definition of consensus right now. Canopy just closed a $70M Series F at $292M total raised. TaxGPT pulled in $5.22M. Corvee raised $10M. Taxnova — literally in the same a16z Speedrun batch — raised a $1M pre-seed. The CPA shortage has been covered by every business publication in America. "75% of CPAs retired in the last decade" is on the grove.tax homepage, but it's also in Ramp's marketing materials and Insightful Accountant and a dozen other places. There is zero discomfort in this category among sophisticated investors. Nobody is saying "that's crazy" when you pitch AI for tax firms. They're saying "which one should I back?" When multiple VCs are actively bidding on the same thesis, the upside is already in the price.

Compare this to Metropolis, which I seeded. AI-powered parking management sounds boring as hell. But Metropolis has physical parking infrastructure — actual garages, actual hardware, actual relationships with real estate owners. An LLM cannot replicate a parking garage. Grove Tax has none of that. It's pure software sitting on top of someone else's calculation engine, automating a workflow that's well-defined enough for any competent engineering team to replicate. The dossier itself acknowledges that "LLM-based document extraction is increasingly commoditized." When the dossier is making my bear case for me, that's a problem.

Nandam's domain expertise is real — PM work across Intuit Link, Tax Scan & Import, ProConnect and Lacerte, with claimed UltraTax experience at Thomson Reuters. Gaurav Mathur brings Airbnb and Indeed engineering. These are capable people building a sensible product. That's exactly the configuration I find boring. Sane people, sane idea. The sanity-insanity ratio is inverted. Nobody hears "AI agents for tax preparation" and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "obviously." The strongest version of the founder-market fit argument is that Nandam built these exact systems at the incumbents and knows their technical debt intimately. Fine. But intimate knowledge of an incumbent's weaknesses is table stakes for any enterprise startup — it doesn't create a primitive, and it doesn't create mispricing.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Intuit and Thomson Reuters are structurally incapable of building AI workflow automation because their revenue models depend on per-seat licensing, and Grove Tax's per-return pricing creates a genuine business model conflict that paralyzes the incumbents for 3-5 years while Grove Tax captures the market. This is a real dynamic — incumbents often can't cannibalize their own business models. And $25-30 per return with no seat fees is a clever wedge. But "incumbent business model conflict" is a timing argument, not a moat argument. Even if Intuit hesitates for two years, Grove Tax still has to outrun TaxGPT, Canopy's AI features, Corvee's new "Instead" prep brand, Taxnova, and whatever else emerges from every accelerator class between now and then. Racing five well-funded competitors in a consensus category to capture a temporary incumbent hesitation window is not how I want to deploy capital.

The one genuinely positive signal is capital discipline. Two-person team, per-return pricing aligned with customer value, no minimums, free onboarding for early returns. The business model doesn't require blitzscaling to work. That's good. But capital discipline in service of building a feature that Intuit will ship as a product update is efficient allocation of resources toward a capped outcome. The core "Prepare & Review" module — the actual hard AI problem of drafting tax returns — is listed as "Coming Soon" on the pricing page. Grove Tax is currently selling intake and delivery at $30 per return without the central capability that would differentiate it. The decisive dimensions here are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability — both score near the bottom of their ranges because AI tax workflow automation is consensus-exciting and fails the incumbent replication test cleanly.
