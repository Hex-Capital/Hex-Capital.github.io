# Smartbase -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Conexiom has raised $170 million, serves sixteen of the top twenty industrial distributors, and generates roughly $75 million in annual revenue doing exactly what Smartbase proposes to do — automate purchase order entry for manufacturers. Workist has raised $11.6 million doing the same in Europe. OrderEase and Buddy occupy adjacent positions. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?", Smartbase's answer appears to be: LLMs now make document extraction cheap enough for small manufacturers. Every AI startup on the planet knows this. The dossier itself flags the commoditization risk explicitly — "the core technology — OCR/AI extraction from documents and ERP API integration — is technically reproducible" and "increasingly commoditized via LLM APIs." A company whose differentiator can be described as "the same thing the incumbent does, but cheaper and for smaller customers" has not identified a secret. It has identified a market segment. These are fundamentally different things, and only one of them creates a monopoly.

Sam Goldman has founded three companies across three unrelated domains in roughly three years: Sphinx Labs in smart contract DevOps ($2 million raised), Anytrace in AI support engineering (YC S25), and now Smartbase in manufacturing order entry (YC Spring 2026). Taira Fujioka, Smartbase's CTO, joined Goldman for the latter two ventures, giving them approximately one year of shared prehistory. The pivot pattern is the clearest signal in this dossier. "Lean is code for unplanned" — and three domain pivots is the purest expression of the indefinite founder archetype my framework screens against. Goldman and Fujioka describe their research methodology as "working on factory floors" and "sleeping at a factory." This is customer discovery — the lean playbook executed diligently. What it is not is a founder who arrived at manufacturing with a specific, pre-existing conviction about what the industry gets wrong. Smartbase was found through iteration, not built from conviction. Taira Fujioka's credential as the youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference is genuinely impressive technical pedigree, but technical capability in service of an indefinite search is not what I invest in.

The bull case deserves construction before dismissal. Smartbase could become the HubSpot of manufacturing operations — Conexiom's enterprise pricing and sales motion structurally cannot serve the approximately 250,000 small U.S. manufacturing establishments. Smartbase's nine named ERP integrations (Acumatica, Epicor, Infor, Dynamics 365, NetSuite, QuickBooks, SAP, SYSPRO, Sage X3) create real per-customer switching costs once business rules are configured. Five-figure annual contracts suggest the product delivers enough value that manufacturers pay meaningfully. If Smartbase captures even a fraction of the SMB segment at $30,000 average contract value, the numbers compound. The "why now" of LLM accuracy plus expanded ERP APIs is legitimate — this automation was genuinely harder to build three years ago. A patient investor might see a $500 million to $1 billion outcome through ERP vendor acquisition. I have considered whether my Lyft exception applies — whether the total value creation in manufacturing automation is so large that even the number-two player produces fund-relevant returns. It does not. The Purchase Order Software Market is $3.6 billion growing to $8.2 billion by 2033. Smartbase is competing for a subsegment of a subsegment. This is not ride-sharing creating hundreds of billions in new economic value. The power-law math fails: a realistic best case for Smartbase is a "nice $200 million business" — exactly the outcome that is structurally irrelevant to how venture returns are distributed.

Smartbase operates as a pure software abstraction layer with zero physical-world technology. No hardware. No proprietary dataset that compounds with scale. No regulatory moat. No government customer embedding. Compare this to General Matter, where I took a board seat — HALEU enrichment requires physical centrifuge cascades, NRC licensing, and a $900 million DOE contract that creates decade-long switching costs. Smartbase's moat is engineering effort across nine ERP APIs, which is real work but not structural defense. SAP, NetSuite, or Microsoft could build native PO ingestion into their own platforms and eliminate the integration layer entirely — the dossier notes that "ERP vendors historically treat order entry as a customer workflow problem rather than a product feature," but "historically" is not "structurally." One product manager at Microsoft Dynamics deciding to add an AI PO import button unravels Smartbase's entire position. The Conexiom "AI-Powered Ideal Order Platform" launch in January 2025 already signals the incumbent's direction.

Smartbase's distribution strategy, per the dossier, is inferred as "direct sales to U.S. manufacturers, starting with SMBs" with a "hands-on, high-touch onboarding model." No scalable distribution channel, no viral mechanism, no channel partnership with ERP vendors or manufacturing trade associations is documented. Manufacturing SMBs are among the hardest customer segments to reach in American business — fragmented across geographies, skeptical of software vendors, and often lacking dedicated IT staff. Smartbase at two people cannot build a sales organization to reach them. The company would need to discover a distribution insight as non-obvious as PayPal embedding in eBay auction listings — and nothing in the dossier suggests Smartbase has found one or is looking for one. Superior sales and distribution alone can create a monopoly, but Smartbase shows no evidence of thinking about distribution with the sophistication its product engineering apparently receives.

One verifiable condition would flip my assessment: if Smartbase demonstrated that its per-customer AI model creates a genuine cross-customer data flywheel — where each new manufacturer's PO formats improve extraction accuracy for all other manufacturers, producing accuracy that no competitor without Smartbase's installed base can match — that would be a network-effect secret hidden inside what appears to be a single-tenant SaaS product. The dossier's data-advantage claim is marked "[Inferred]" and describes a per-customer advantage, not a cross-network one. If Goldman and Fujioka could show me that their system learns manufacturer-to-manufacturer in ways that create compounding, irreversible accuracy gaps, I would revisit. Until then, Smartbase is a competent team executing the lean playbook in a competitive software market with no secret, no monopoly path, and no power-law magnitude. Competition is for losers, and Smartbase has chosen to compete.
