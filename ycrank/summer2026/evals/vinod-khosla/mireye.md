# Mireye -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Mireye is a geospatial MCP wrapper on public USGS, NOAA, and Census data -- a two-person team in San Francisco selling "federal-grade geospatial ground truth" via API into a surface where Google Maps Platform, Mapbox, CARTO, and xMap all shipped MCP servers in 2025-2026. The signature of Mireye is the inverse of what I fund: its highest-scoring dimension is a small founder-pivot signal (Chokshi's $250K ARR Poker Pit exit at 21), and its lowest is the dimension I weight at 30 points -- consequence magnitude. That pairing tells me what Mireye is: a competent two-person execution play on a commodity data layer, not a company that breaks an economic mechanism.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence -- the $50M check went in because the success-state was a civilizational redirection of cognitive labor, the "$15 trillion of GDP is labor" frame I made explicit in Fortune (2026-03-06). Mireye's success-state, as the YC page articulates it, is that "AI agents stop guessing about specific places" -- a grounding-quality improvement inside an agent stack whose foundation models are built by someone else. The GeoAI TAM the dossier cites is $62.88B by 2030 (MarketsandMarkets 2025); even at full capture Mireye does not collapse an incumbent regime, it grounds a query. Mireye diverges from the OpenAI rung at the only place that matters in my framework -- there is no named system that disappears if Mireye wins, no labor market displaced, no thermodynamic boundary moved. The dossier reads as "improve a workflow," not "make a mechanism break."

The failure mechanism of the **sales-call-summarizer** pass case from my own pass example is this: the success-state is a 20-30% efficiency gain inside one workflow, the required-capital profile is tiny, the founder profile is domain-expert continuity, and any Tier-1 generalist can fund it. Trace each clause through Mireye's dossier: the success-state is grounded geospatial answers for agents (workflow improvement); the capital profile is API/MCP self-serve economics; Kapoor spent six years in data infrastructure at Tools for Humanity and Lyra Health, and Chokshi led AI/ML at Seismic -- the data-infra-engineer-becomes-data-infra-founder pattern is the exact career continuity my TED Talk warned about when I cited the Berkeley 28,000-prediction study. Mireye is not a science miracle dressed as SaaS; it is SaaS, and the four MCP servers already shipped by Google, Mapbox, CARTO, and xMap mean the incumbent capture is happening in real time.

The technical-risk dimension compounds the wrong direction over 18 months for Mireye. CARTO has $28.9M ARR (Latka 2024) and shipped CARTO MCP Server in 2026; Mapbox raised over $220M historically and shipped Mapbox MCP Server plus DevKit; Google Maps Platform shipped MCP grounding for any LLM. As agent integrations standardize on MCP, the differentiating value migrates from "do you expose an MCP endpoint" (week-one parity) to "what proprietary, citation-quality, globally normalized data do you index that the incumbents do not" -- and Mireye's stated coverage is US-only federal datasets that are public domain. The mechanism that should compound moat (proprietary data acquisition, integration density, switching costs) is exactly the mechanism Mireye does not control.

Counterfactual on founder-defiance: to flip Mireye from a 6/20 to a 13/20 on this dimension, Chokshi or Kapoor would need to have publicly contradicted the prevailing thesis in geospatial AI -- for instance, a written argument that public federal datasets are sufficient and that the global POI/mobility coverage xMap and Mapbox emphasize is a category error. The dossier contains no such public position. Mireye's founders look like two competent data engineers building the obvious wedge, not instigators in the sense of my "12 Good Instigators" TED frame.

Single-anchor parallel on learning rate: **Slash** (2024) -- the $100M Series C I led on the strength of a documented Kanye-pivot story -- is the shape of evidence I weight here. Mireye's analog is Chokshi's Poker Pit, reported as zero-to-$250K ARR in four months before acquisition; that is a real prior exit, not a thesis pivot. The Slash pattern requires a public reasoning-update, not just a small exit. Mireye gets credit for execution velocity but not for the rarer signal -- founder thesis change under contact with reality.

My 36-month forecast on Mireye: acqui-hired by CARTO, Mapbox, or a vertical insurance-tech buyer for under $15M, or pivoted into a single vertical (wildfire/flood underwriting for property insurers) where federal risk data is regulator-required and proprietary distribution can be built. The mechanism is straightforward -- the MCP-server wedge collapses once incumbents have parity, and the only escape is a vertical where data citation is regulated and incumbent data scientists don't want to build it.

The strongest case against my pass on Mireye runs through **Glean** -- the Known Exception in my own framework where I funded a domain-expert founder (Arvind Jain, ex-Google search) building information-retrieval infrastructure that any incumbent could theoretically build. Glean won on focus, build velocity, and a real workflow the incumbents under-served. xMap's $4M+ pre-seed with $600K revenue (citybiz 2024) shows the wedge is fundable; Mireye could be the US-federal-data-quality version of that bet. The dossier evidence that dismantles this case: Glean was selling into Fortune-500 information asymmetry with consequence magnitude that justified the consensus-credential override, while Mireye is selling grounding endpoints into a market where Google Maps shipped the substitute in 2026. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed integration or revenue-share deal with Anthropic, OpenAI, or a foundation-model lab making Mireye the default geospatial MCP for that agent stack -- distribution capture that would matter more than the data-layer commoditization. Absent that, Mireye scores where the framework says it scores, and I pass the way I passed the sales-call summarizer.
