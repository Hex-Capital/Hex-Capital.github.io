# ZeroSettle -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about ZeroSettle isn't the product — it's the structural position of the founders. Two systems engineers who worked on Apple's operating systems are now building the plumbing to route around Apple's 30% commission. This is like if two former Goldman Sachs engineers built the tools for companies to bypass Goldman's underwriting fees. They know exactly how the system works from the inside — the StoreKit APIs, the App Review process, the compliance architecture. That insider knowledge is genuine and hard to replicate. But insider knowledge and organic problem discovery are different things.

Here's what concerns me about the origin story. The 30% App Store tax is not a problem hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness. Everyone knows about it. It's been on the front page of every tech publication for five years. Epic literally sued Apple over it. What kept developers from offering alternative billing wasn't unconscious avoidance of a painful schlep — it was that Apple made it illegal. The court removed that barrier in April 2025. So this isn't a Stripe situation where thousands of hackers knew payments were broken but nobody wanted to deal with the banks. This is a regulatory gate that opened, and now multiple well-funded companies are sprinting through. Neon Pay has $14M from Thrive and a16z. Paddle ($293M raised) explicitly cited Apple's ecosystem opening as a growth driver. Stripe published its own iOS alternative-payment guide the same month the ruling landed. ZeroSettle is entering a land grab, not discovering a hidden schlep. The compliance complexity across US, EU, and Japan is real — tax, fraud, entitlement syncing, region-specific checkout flows — but complexity that everyone can see isn't the same as complexity that everyone unconsciously avoids.

Now, Gabe Roeloffs did found a smart appliance startup and has written about building iPhone apps. If that earlier startup had an app generating revenue through the App Store, he would have felt the 30% cut personally. That's the seed of something organic. And the decision to build an SDK rather than, say, a consulting service shows they're thinking like infrastructure hackers — picks and shovels for the gold rush. The product looks real: documentation site live, dashboard running, SDKs in Swift, React Native, Flutter, and Kotlin. Two Apple systems engineers building iOS-level infrastructure is about as technically credible as it gets for this specific problem. Ryan's patent from Apple and their shared background at Cal Poly and Apple together suggests a co-founder relationship that predates the startup, which is exactly what I look for.

The bull case deserves a full hearing. If Apple is permanently barred from charging commissions on linked-out purchases in the US, the savings for developers are enormous — going from retaining $7 on a $9.99 sale to retaining $8.50-$9.50. ZeroSettle's Merchant of Record model means once developers migrate subscribers, switching back is painful. RevenueCat structurally cannot offer this product without jeopardizing its Apple relationship. Apple cannot offer it without cannibalizing $27 billion in annual revenue. That's a rare competitive dynamic where neither the dominant incumbent nor the platform owner can easily respond. If ZeroSettle moves fast and locks in developers during this transition window, the MoR switching costs create a real moat. The founding partner program — free for two years — is aggressive but sensible for land-grab economics. And they're a two-person team, which means they're lean enough that even modest transaction volume makes them default alive.

But here's the problem I can't get past: there's zero evidence anyone is using this yet. No customer names, no revenue, no user counts, no waitlist, no organic sharing — nothing. The documentation site exists and pricing is posted, but that's a storefront, not demand. At pre-seed I don't expect much, but I expect *something* — one developer saying "this saved me $50K," one testimonial, one sign that the product has touched a real user. The Founding Partner Program being free for two years also means revenue is structurally delayed. And the Stripe dependency is the kind of thing that keeps me up at night: ZeroSettle is a value-add layer on top of Stripe's payment infrastructure, and Stripe has already signaled interest in this exact market. If Stripe adds migration campaigns and entitlement syncing to their existing iOS payment guide, ZeroSettle becomes a feature inside Stripe, not an independent company. That's the "building on someone else's platform" risk, and here they're building on two platforms — Apple's and Stripe's — either of which could squeeze them.

The founders are technically strong, the timing is legitimately right, and the compliance schlep is real. But this reads to me as a well-executed response to a regulatory catalyst rather than an organic discovery born from years of personal frustration. It's the kind of idea two smart ex-Apple engineers would generate by reading about the Epic v. Apple ruling and thinking "there's a startup here." That's not nothing — sometimes timing and execution win — but it's not the kind of founder-problem fit that makes me write a check from my own money before seeing any demand signal at all.
