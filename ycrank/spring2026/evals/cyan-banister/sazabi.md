# Sazabi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Sherwood Callaway started the Infrastructure and Observability teams at Brex as employee #70, co-founded Opkit through YC S21, got it acquired by 11x in September 2024, then rebuilt their AI sales agent as Tech Lead before launching Sazabi. That's an impressive professional trajectory — repeat YC founder, prior exit, direct domain experience in the exact problem he's solving. But when I run my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is "I was at Brex and saw that observability was painful." That's a career insight, not a biographical crucible. Callaway studied history at Davidson College, went through Dev Bootcamp, worked his way up through engineering roles at Crunchbase and Brex. Resourceful, yes. Accomplished, absolutely. But there's no evidence in the dossier that this problem found him through lived experience that predates his professional career. The founding story for Sazabi reads like a talented engineer who spotted a gap in a market he knew well — and that's exactly the kind of origin story that makes me pause, because I've learned that founders who arrive at their problem through career optimization build differently than founders who arrive because their biography left them no choice.

Sazabi's architectural bet — rejecting the three-pillars paradigm of logs, metrics, and traces in favor of logs-only ingestion, then using AI to derive the rest — is genuinely contrarian at the technical level. Callaway argues that "fundamentally, logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events and traces are basically correlated events," which means the entire OpenTelemetry standard that Dash0, SigNoz, and the broader CNCF ecosystem are building around is unnecessary overhead. That takes courage to say when Dash0 just raised $155M at a $1B valuation and Datadog has 47,000 customers on the three-pillars model. But here's what I keep testing: would this idea make a room of sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs? Developer tools observability is one of the most consensus-attractive categories in venture capital right now. Nobody gets uncomfortable hearing "I'm building AI-native observability." The contrarianism is architectural, not social or regulatory. Sazabi isn't entering a space that's politically toxic like Anduril was, or stigmatized like Contraline's male birth control. It's entering a crowded, well-funded, investor-beloved category with a different technical approach. That's differentiation, not the kind of contrarian conviction that has driven my highest-returning bets.

The invisible system question is where Sazabi partially activates my framework, but doesn't fully light up. Datadog's pricing is famously opaque — per-signal billing where engineering teams get surprise invoices, separate charges for logs, metrics, and traces that incentivize the vendor to multiply telemetry types. There IS an extractive structure here: the three-pillars paradigm benefits vendors who bill per signal more than it benefits the teams paying those bills. Sazabi's logs-only approach could collapse that pricing complexity. But Callaway frames Sazabi's mission as "modern observability is bloated" — that's a product critique, not a systemic one. And Sazabi's target customers are "early-stage and growth-stage technology companies," meaning the people being freed from this invisible tax are other startup engineers, not vulnerable populations navigating systems they don't understand. When I backed Affirm, the invisible system was credit card opacity that extracted value from ordinary consumers who couldn't parse their interest rates. When I backed Ownwell, it was property tax appeals that homeowners didn't even know they could file. Sazabi is solving a real pain for a specific professional class, but it's not the kind of structural power shift that connects to my deepest conviction about what technology should do.

Sazabi's defensibility picture concerns me against the competitive backdrop. The SOC 2 Type II, SOC 1, ISO 27017, and ISO 27001 certifications are impressive execution signals for a pre-seed company with ten people — that's unusual discipline. The "Perfect Memory" feature, where Sazabi accumulates customer-specific log history and incident patterns, creates genuine switching costs over time. But the dossier is direct about the vulnerability: "The logs-only-plus-AI approach is architecturally reproducible. Any team with LLM infrastructure expertise could attempt a similar product." Dash0 already ships AI agents through Agent0. Datadog is shipping AI features on a base of $2.7B ARR. The moat depends on execution speed and data accumulation, not structural barriers — and when I look at my portfolio, my biggest failures (HQ Trivia, GameCrush) were precisely the companies where the moat was execution-dependent rather than structural. The timing inflection around AI coding agents — Claude Code, Cursor, Codex generating code that needs new observability — is real and gives Sazabi a genuine "why now." But timing without structural defensibility is what I learned from HQ Trivia: right time, right product, insufficient moat.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Callaway is the rare founder with direct experience in both observability infrastructure (Brex) and AI-agent architectures (Opkit, 11x) — the exact intersection Sazabi occupies. The backing from leaders at Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, Replit, and Browserbase isn't just capital, it's distribution channels into the exact developer communities Sazabi needs to reach. The logs-only bet mirrors how Crusoe Energy looked "wrong" against consensus — flare gas mining seemed like a gimmick until it turned out to be the infrastructure play hiding beneath the weird surface. Maybe Sazabi's logs-only approach is the same kind of hidden infrastructure bet: what looks like a product simplification is actually a fundamental rearchitecting of how observability works. That parallel is tempting, but it breaks down on a critical axis. Crusoe's founders were operating in a space nobody else wanted to touch — literal oil fields in North Dakota. Sazabi is competing for the same developer-tools dollars that every well-funded observability company is chasing. Crusoe had a physical moat (you need to actually go to the flare sites). Sazabi's moat is execution speed against incumbents who have billions in revenue and are already shipping AI features. The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Callaway showed me that the logs-only architecture enables a pricing model so transparent and predictable that it structurally undermines Datadog's per-signal billing opacity — turning the product bet into a systemic disruption of how observability is sold, not just how it's built.

Sazabi is a well-executed entry into a real market by a founder with directly relevant experience, and I expect it will attract capital from investors who weight domain expertise and market timing above the signals I look for. But it doesn't activate the dimensions that have driven my highest-conviction bets. The biographical forge is professional, not personal — a score of 15 where I need to see 22+. The space is technically contrarian but socially consensus. The invisible system angle exists but isn't the founder's driving motivation. What most locks this into Neutral for me is the combination of those first two dimensions: a founder who arrived at the problem through career insight rather than biographical inevitability, building in a category that VCs are eagerly funding rather than actively avoiding. My best investments have always been the ones where I was the first check because everyone else was too uncomfortable to write one. Sazabi has backing from Village Global, Agent Fund, Hawk Hill Ventures, Zypsy, and angels from Anthropic and Vercel — it's not looking for uncomfortable first checks, and that tells me something about where it sits relative to my framework.
