# Gojiberry AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing I can't get past is the TrustMRR listing. On March 9, 2026 -- one week after launching on Product Hunt and while actively participating in YC S26 -- Gojiberry AI was listed for sale at $11.15 million. I don't know what the intent behind that listing was, and maybe there's an innocent explanation. But the signal it sends is the opposite of earnestness. The founders I back are people who would rather die than sell their company at the stage when it's just starting to work. When the Airbnb founders were at that point, they were eating ramen and selling cereal boxes to buy more time. They weren't putting the company on a marketplace. A founder who simultaneously raises venture money and lists the company for sale is hedging in a way that makes me question which outcome they're actually optimizing for.

Set that aside for a moment and ask my first question: how did they find this problem? Pierre-Eliott and Roman ran CoCo AI, a WhatsApp marketing tool for Shopify stores. They sold it. Before that, Roman ran a Web3 game studio. Dylan sold an attendance SaaS called Edusign. These are serial builders -- people who construct software businesses, extract value, and move on. The Gojiberry idea came after a pivot: they first built an AI sales assistant for CRM auto-fill, got 2,000 people on a waitlist, and zero paying customers. So they switched to intent-based lead generation. That's not organic discovery. That's systematic market testing. The problem of small sales teams wasting time on cold outbound is real, but it's also the kind of problem you'd identify by reading a few SaaS blogs and looking at pricing pages for 6sense. Thousands of people have noticed this gap. The question is whether the schlep involved is hard enough to keep most of them out, and here the answer is clearly no -- the dossier itself lists five funded competitors doing variations of the same thing, and acknowledges that "LinkedIn signal detection and automated messaging are technically reproducible."

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about what's impressive here. These founders bootstrapped to $175K MRR spending $380 a month on marketing. That is absurdly capital-efficient. Roman's 12-channel distribution machine -- 5,000 cold emails a day, 800 LinkedIn DMs a week, Reddit posts, YouTube videos, SEO -- all running on essentially nothing, is the kind of scrappy execution that usually gets my attention. They pivoted hard when the first product had no paying customers and within 60 days had 100 paying customers on the new one. The churn is below 5%. The profit margin is 75%. If I'm evaluating these founders purely on the "relentlessly resourceful" axis, they score well. They know how to get from zero to traction in a way that most pre-seed teams never figure out. The $127 CAC with 1.3-month payback is the kind of economics that makes a company default alive almost immediately.

But resourcefulness in execution is not the same as building something that compounds into a defensible business. This is a crowded, hot category. Apollo just acquired Pocus. Clay hit $100M ARR. Lemlist is at $40M ARR. HeyReach got to $10M bootstrapped doing adjacent work. When I see a category where multiple bootstrapped competitors have already reached $10M+, that tells me the barriers are low and the outcome space is well-explored. The founders are competing on execution speed, not on insight. And execution advantages in sales tooling erode fast -- every new entrant gets the same LinkedIn APIs, the same LLMs for message generation, the same enrichment providers. The "data flywheel" claim sounds right in theory, but at $99/month serving small teams, the data per customer is thin. This isn't Stripe, where each customer's payment processing generates irreplaceable infrastructure lock-in. This is more like email marketing tools in 2015 -- a category where everyone can build a decent product and the winner is determined by GTM spend, not technical depth.

The CTO, Dylan, built Edusign from scratch starting at age 12 as a developer, and scaled it to 600K users. That's respectable. But his GitHub has 21 repos with negligible community traction, and the core technical challenge here -- stitching together LinkedIn scraping, enrichment APIs, and LLM-generated messages -- is integration work, not deep engineering. Nobody on this team is the kind of hacker who stays up all night because they're fascinated by the technical problem. They stay up all night because they're fascinated by the growth metrics. Those are different animals.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The founders would need to be building toward something bigger than a LinkedIn outreach tool -- some vision of an SMB sales intelligence platform that absorbs CRM, enrichment, and pipeline management into a single opinionated product, the way HubSpot absorbed marketing tools but starting from signal detection. If they can ride the current growth from $175K to $1M+ MRR while maintaining these economics, they'd have a real business regardless of venture dynamics. And three founders with three prior exits who know how to build and sell companies are unlikely to go to zero. The floor here is probably a $20-30M acquisition by one of the larger platforms. But that's the problem -- I'm not investing my own money for floor outcomes. I invest for the possibility that this becomes something that reshapes how business works. I don't see that path here. The market is too crowded, the schlep is too low, and the sale listing suggests the founders themselves may agree.
