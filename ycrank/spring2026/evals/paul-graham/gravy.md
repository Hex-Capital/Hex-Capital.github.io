# Gravy -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Gravy is a 2026 Mac app that shipped before it pitched, which I respect, but the two founders behind it -- Ali Tabba and Max Church -- left almost no hacker trail before they walked into Andrew Miklas's office. That pairing is the whole story: a working artifact at gravy.finance with "3 connected accounts and 3 automations" in the demo, attached to a founder pair where Max Church has no public profile data of any kind and Ali Tabba's LinkedIn reads "Founder (YC P26)" preceded by "Stealth Startup." Competent silent builders is a real archetype, but it is not the archetype that prints the **Stripe** (2010) outcome, and I read founders before I read products.

The highest dimension here is resourcefulness, and the load-bearing analogy is **Dropbox** (2007): Drew Houston shipped a demo video to Hacker News before the seed check, and the artifact itself was the application. Gravy has the artifact -- a downloadable Mac binary at gravy.finance with a Windows waitlist -- and that puts Gravy above the polished-deck AI-wrapper teams I pass on reflexively. But Gravy diverges from the **Dropbox** (2007) rung in a way that matters: Houston's video hit Hacker News and the hacker tribe vouched for it within hours. Gravy's dossier records no Product Hunt launch (the Product Hunt "Gravy" is an unrelated shopping product), no company Twitter (@social_gravy is an indie band), and no GitHub for either founder. Shipped but unseen is a softer signal than shipped and adored.

The lowest dimension is hacker genealogy, and the failure mechanism I keep watching for is the one the MBA-deck team in my own pass example exhibits: credentials without artifacts. Patrick Collison had won Ireland's 41st Young Scientist for Lisp work before **Stripe** (2010); Aaron Swartz had co-authored the RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005); even Brian Armstrong hand-built an Android Bitcoin wallet to rescue his rejected **Coinbase** (2012) application. Gravy's dossier lists Ali Tabba at the University of Warwick and Max Church with literally no public profile data surfaced -- no GitHub repos, no open-source contributions, no competitive programming, no Hacker News presence. The MBA-deck failure mode is "credentialed but never shipped." Gravy is the inverse and slightly better version -- they shipped -- but the absence of a tribal trail behind the binary is the same silence, and silence has predictive content.

On organic-pain fit, the mechanism question over eighteen months is whether Tabba and Church reach for the product themselves every morning the way Drew Houston reached for his USB stick. The dossier says nothing about either founder having personally drowned in subscriptions or fought Plaid OAuth flows in a prior life; the framing on gravy.finance is "consumers manage finances across fragmented accounts," which is market-spotting language, not USB-stick language. Without an origin story, the agent automations -- rent tracking, subscription monitoring, daily briefings -- become a feature list someone else could also assemble from Plaid + OpenAI + Electron, exactly as the commoditization-risk row of the dossier states.

On schlep, the counterfactual question is what would have to be true for Gravy to clear the **Coinbase** (2012) bar. Brian Armstrong won that bar because he ran toward bitcoin compliance grind while every other YC applicant routed around it. Gravy's local-first architecture and BYO-LLM-key model are friction -- asking a consumer to paste an OpenAI key is a schlep mainstream PFM users will not perform -- but it is friction Gravy imposes on the user, not friction Gravy absorbs on the user's behalf. For this dimension to flip a tier, Gravy would need to be doing the GLB / state-privacy / Plaid-tier-negotiation grind itself, and the dossier explicitly notes "no SOC2, privacy policy, or compliance posture is published."

On co-founder configuration, the single-anchor parallel is **Stripe** (2010): two-person team, brothers, with years of commerce-hacking shared history through **Auctomatic** (2007) before the second YC pass. Gravy is two people, which clears the solo bar from "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010), but the dossier records "no public data on co-founder history" between Tabba and Church. Two founders who passed the YC interview will read as chemistry-positive by default, so I stay near the neutral anchor and refuse to award the **Stripe** (2010) premium without the shared-history evidence.

My 36-month forecast for Gravy: under $300K ARR by mid-2029, then an acquihire to Monarch Money or Copilot for the local-first IP and the two engineers, or a quiet wind-down. The mechanism is that the BYO-LLM-key onboarding step caps consumer conversion an order of magnitude below Rocket Money and Cleo, Monarch's $95.5M war chest lets it copy the local-data feature without restructuring revenue, and the founders -- absent any animal anecdote in the dossier -- do not radiate the determination required to survive a five-year consumer-distribution slog against three funded incumbents.

The strongest bull case I owe Gravy is the **Airbnb** (2009) exception: local-first + BYO-LLM looks dumb to me the same way "strangers sleeping in your home" looked dumb in 2008, and "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) tells me to lean in when my gut says no. If Tabba and Church are animals, the contrarian architecture is the moat. The dossier dismantles this for me: the **Airbnb** (2009) override required cereal boxes -- a stunt that proved animal status before I wrote the check. Gravy's dossier contains no stunt, no pre-funding revenue, no Hacker News thread, no founder anecdote. Shipping a Mac binary is necessary but not sufficient; Brian Chesky shipped revenue from breakfast cereal. The one verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a Tabba or Church-built artifact -- pre-Gravy open source, a viral Hacker News thread on the local-first PFM architecture, or evidence either founder personally automated their own finances for years before incorporating -- surfacing in public. Until then, pass.
