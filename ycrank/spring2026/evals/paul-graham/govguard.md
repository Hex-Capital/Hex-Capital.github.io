# GovGuard -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Aditya Sabharwal and Gleb Hulting have no public GitHub repos, no Twitter presence, and no visible traction -- not a single paying customer, pilot, or LOI that made it into the public record. GovGuard's YC Launch page has 2 upvotes. I start with founders, and the problem here is that I can't see these founders at all. When I backed the Airbnb guys, I couldn't stop talking about their cereal box hustle -- that story told me everything I needed to know about their resourcefulness. When I invested in Patrick Collison, I'd known him since he was a teenager shipping Auctomatic. With GovGuard, I'm looking at two founders whose LinkedIn headlines read "BioE & CS @ UIUC" and "GovGuard," who co-founded something called BoardWatch together, and who have left essentially no public trace of the animal quality I look for. The prior co-founding experience at BoardWatch is a real signal -- they've worked together before, which means they've probably had the hard conversations about equity and commitment. Gleb also founded EGO Logistics. But prior founding alone isn't enough when I can't see what happened during those ventures.

GovGuard's problem space is where the analysis gets more interesting than the founders. Federal FOIA requests hit 1.5M in FY2024, up 25% year-over-year, while backlogs grew 33% to 267,056 requests. One organization alone filed 100,000+ FOIA requests using AI-assisted tools. This is a genuine arms race -- AI is creating the problem and AI is the only plausible solution. The incumbents, Granicus and CivicPlus, acquired their FOIA tools (GovQA and NextRequest respectively) and both are ticket-management systems, essentially Jira for public records. GovGuard is building the thing those tools don't do: the actual substantive work of searching archives, flagging PII, citing state-code exemptions, and drafting response letters. That gap between "managing the workflow" and "doing the work" is real, and it's the kind of structural opening I find compelling. Everlaw does AI-powered redaction but targets federal agencies at enterprise prices ($80.8M revenue, $2B+ valuation) -- GovGuard is going after the ~90,000 local government entities that can't afford Everlaw.

The schlep here is legitimate. Selling to municipalities means RFP processes, council approvals, SOC2 certification (in progress for GovGuard), potentially FedRAMP, and 6-18 month sales cycles. Building a state-code exemption library across 50 states is the kind of tedious regulatory work that makes most YC founders' eyes glaze over. This is the pattern I saw with Stripe -- payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled every other developer who was smart enough to build it. But the critical difference between GovGuard and Stripe is that the Collisons were developers building for developers. They lived inside the payment integration pain every day. GovGuard's founders have no public evidence of prior government, legal, or public-records domain expertise. Their MS CS backgrounds equip them to build AI document processing, but "can build the technology" and "deeply understand the customer's world" are different things. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas -- it's to live in the future and build what's missing. I don't see evidence that either Aditya or Gleb lived inside a city clerk's office and felt the pain of manually redacting PDFs with a physical marker.

The bull case for GovGuard is genuinely worth constructing. The "why now" convergence -- AI-generated requests surging while federal workforce reductions cause FOIA operations to cease -- creates demand that didn't exist 18 months ago. ARPA-H explicitly sought AI redaction software in 2025, signaling federal appetite. GovGuard's built-in state-code exemption library is a meaningful differentiator against both generic eDiscovery tools and the ticket-management incumbents. The ClassDojo precedent in my own portfolio shows I can get excited about schlep-heavy, non-developer markets when execution quality is exceptional. And these founders have shipped before -- BoardWatch exists, they're technical, they're in YC with Tom Blomfield. Maybe GovGuard is the ClassDojo of public records: a market that venture capital considers too unsexy and procurement-heavy to fund, attacked by builders who are willing to do the painful work of navigating municipal sales. But ClassDojo required "exceptionally high execution quality" as the override signal, and GovGuard has given me nothing to evaluate on that dimension. Zero customers. Zero revenue. Zero public GitHub activity. Two YC Launch upvotes. The schlep alone doesn't create the exception -- the execution has to be visible.

GovGuard sits in the space where the market thesis is stronger than the founder signal. The FOIA automation gap is real, the incumbents are structurally unable to cannibalize their own ticket-management products without retraining their entire sales force, and the demand curve is accelerating. But I'm investing my own money, and what I'm buying is founders, not market theses. I've seen dozens of companies with compelling market analyses and no evidence that the founders can execute against the specific, gnarly challenges of their chosen domain. GovGuard's founders need to show me one municipal customer using the product obsessively in its crappiest form -- "who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of?" That's the condition that would flip me. One city clerk who can't stop using GovGuard, even with bugs and missing features, because the alternative is physically marking up PDFs. That signal would tell me both that the product works and that the founders figured out how to navigate government procurement -- which would itself be a powerful resourcefulness story.

**Verdict:** Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint (no visible stories, no traction, no evidence of the creative workaround pattern that signals the animal quality) and Organic Problem Discovery (no domain background, no origin story rooted in personal frustration with FOIA processing). GovGuard is attacking a real schlep in a market with genuine structural tailwinds, but the founders are invisible to me, and invisible founders are a bet I don't take with my own money.
