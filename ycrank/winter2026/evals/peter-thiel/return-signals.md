# Return Signals -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask about any company is what secret it possesses -- what important truth about the world does it see that others do not? Return Signals' implicit thesis is that proactive, conversational SMS after delivery can prevent e-commerce returns. This is not a secret. Every apparel brand executive I have ever spoken to knows returns destroy margins. Every SMS platform vendor knows post-purchase engagement exists. The idea that an AI could intervene conversationally before a return is filed is, in 2026, the most obvious possible application of large language models to e-commerce. When I scan the competitive landscape -- Attentive at $500 million ARR, Klaviyo at $1.2 billion ARR, Loop Returns with $176 million in funding, Postscript with $117 million -- what I see is not a company that has escaped competition. I see a company that has voluntarily positioned itself at the intersection of multiple competitive forces, each of which has the resources, the integrations, and the incentive to replicate this functionality as a product extension. This is the opposite of monopoly thinking.

The strongest version of the bull case relies on a business model conflict argument: Attentive and Klaviyo are marketing platforms optimized for message volume, while Return Signals' value proposition is margin preservation through returns reduction -- orthogonal goals that create an innovator's dilemma for incumbents. This is a real dynamic and the most intellectually interesting element of the company. If Attentive's revenue scales with sending more promotional messages, perhaps they structurally cannot prioritize a product whose purpose is to send fewer, more targeted post-delivery conversations. But this argument collapses against Loop Returns, which already manages the returns workflow and faces no such conflict -- adding AI-driven proactive outreach is a natural feature extension for them, not a business model contradiction. And the history of enterprise software is littered with startups whose "business model conflict" thesis proved insufficient when incumbents simply launched the feature anyway, taking six months of engineering time rather than building a new company.

Ilya Valmianski has genuine technical credentials -- a physics PhD, medical conversational AI deployed at scale across Kaiser Permanente, a prior founding experience that allegedly reached $40 million ARR. These are real accomplishments. But the domain trajectory concerns me: he built sophisticated medical AI systems where the conversational stakes are high, regulatory barriers are substantial, and the technical challenge is immense -- and then pivoted to post-purchase SMS for apparel brands, where the conversational complexity is orders of magnitude lower. This pattern suggests a founder who built hard technology in a difficult market and then retreated to an easier application domain for commercial tractability. That is the opposite of what I look for. The founders I back -- Zuckerberg, Musk, Luckey -- move toward harder problems, not easier ones. The co-founder Alejandro Zaniolo brings business development experience from proptech companies with no visible connection to e-commerce, returns, or AI. I see no evidence of how these two met or why they share conviction about this specific future.

The technology itself is the weakest element. LLM-driven SMS conversations are not proprietary technology -- they are an application layer built on foundation models that any competent engineering team can access through API calls. The claimed 4-10x higher response rates compared to Attentive and Postscript are engagement metrics, not technology metrics. A well-crafted conversational prompt will outperform a marketing blast; this is not surprising and not defensible. Where is the 10x improvement that makes the old approach structurally obsolete? I do not see it. The data moat argument -- that accumulated sizing feedback and return-reason patterns create a defensible flywheel -- is theoretically sound but nonexistent at pre-seed. And even if it existed, the data advantage would be brand-specific and fragmented, not a platform-level network effect that compounds across the entire customer base the way Facebook's social graph did.

The company does execute one element of monopoly strategy correctly: starting with a narrow niche. Apparel, footwear, and accessories are the highest-return-rate categories in e-commerce, and focusing there rather than targeting all of e-commerce on day one shows some strategic discipline. The expansion path to other categories is visible. But a well-chosen beachhead does not compensate for the absence of a secret. You can monopolize a niche and still build a small business rather than a transformative one. The question is not whether Return Signals can win some apparel brands -- it probably can. The question is whether it can generate the kind of power-law return that justifies an angel investment. A company whose best outcome is a modest SaaS business serving mid-market e-commerce brands, perpetually threatened by feature releases from players with fifty to a hundred times its resources, does not pass the power-law test. I need investments where success means civilizational-scale impact or category-defining monopoly. This is a feature in search of a company.
