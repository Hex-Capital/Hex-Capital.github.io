# SideKit -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out to me about SideKit isn't the product or the founders -- it's the competitive landscape they're walking into. Firebase has 72%+ iOS penetration and near-total Android coverage. PostHog has raised $180 million, is open-source, and already bundles analytics with feature flags. Statsig just sold to OpenAI for $1.1 billion. LaunchDarkly has $330 million behind it. When I look at a company, I ask myself: is this unpopular? Is it difficult to assess? Does it make people uncomfortable? SideKit is none of those things. It's a developer tool that bundles analytics, version management, and AI-powered fixes into one SDK. Every investor in the Valley understands this category instantly. Nobody's going to call you crazy for building it. And that, for me, is not a great sign. The deals I've done well on -- SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline -- were the ones where people looked at me sideways when I described them. A lighter-weight PostHog for mobile doesn't generate that feeling.

I always want to hear the founder's story before anything else. Ashish Selvaraj built a sudoku app called Not Evil Sudoku that crossed 50,000 installs, and then built his own private analytics service for it because he didn't want to use Firebase. That's a legitimate "scratch your own itch" origin -- he actually lived the problem. I respect that. But it's not a survival story. It's not someone who was forged by the problem in a way that makes them the only person who could build this company. He's a sharp UWaterloo CS student who interned at Databricks and Microsoft, which is a strong technical profile, but it's also the profile of hundreds of founders in any YC batch. I'm looking for the biographical thread that makes it impossible for this founder to do anything else, and I'm not finding it here. Then there's Ethan Pronev -- and this is where I get genuinely uncomfortable. I can't find a LinkedIn for him. His personal website returns a 404. No public GitHub. The company website claims the team includes engineers from "Jane Street, Citadel, Databricks, Microsoft," and while Ashish's Databricks and Microsoft stints check out, the Jane Street and Citadel claims are unverifiable. I don't need polished credentials to invest -- some of my best founders had no credentials at all -- but I do need transparency. When I can't see who someone is, I can't read them, and reading people is how I make decisions.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because I owe it to these founders to take it seriously. The timing argument is genuinely interesting: AI code generation tools like Cursor and Copilot are creating an explosion of new developers who can build apps but have no idea how to take them to production. SideKit's tagline -- "It's never been easier to turn ideas into apps, it should be just as easy to take those apps to production" -- is speaking directly to a wave that could be massive. If the next two years produce millions of new indie mobile developers, and if those developers need a simple, opinionated toolkit that doesn't require them to stitch together Firebase plus Mixpanel plus LaunchDarkly plus custom version-check logic, then SideKit could be the right product at the right moment. The privacy-first angle has real regulatory tailwinds. And the self-healing AI feature -- where an agent processes user feedback and ships fixes as PRs -- is at least directionally novel. If I could feel confident that these founders had the grit and the weirdness to outrun PostHog and survive the inevitable Firebase feature update that bundles everything they offer, I'd lean in. But that confidence requires biographical conviction I don't have.

The deeper issue for me is that SideKit doesn't create economic access for anyone who currently lacks it. My best investments -- Uber, Postmates, Affirm, Thumbtack -- put money in the pockets of people who needed it or gave transparent access to systems that were previously opaque. SideKit makes life marginally easier for developers who already have laptops, coding skills, and the ability to ship apps to the App Store. That's a real business, and developer tools can be great businesses, but it doesn't activate the part of my investing brain that lights up when I see a company fundamentally changing who gets to participate in the economy. The "invisible system made visible" pattern I've backed with Carta and Flock Safety isn't here either -- analytics dashboards are among the most visible things in software. There's no structural opacity being disrupted.

I'm passing on SideKit. These are capable founders building a reasonable product in a real market, but nothing about this opportunity requires conviction over consensus. It's a well-understood category with well-funded incumbents, a founder story that's competent rather than extraordinary, and no clear moat beyond execution speed. The 7 GitHub stars and 227 LinkedIn followers at this stage are normal for pre-seed and I won't penalize that, but they also don't give me any early signal of the kind of organic pull that would tell me something special is happening. When I write a check, I'm looking for the people and ideas that make the rest of the world uncomfortable. SideKit makes everyone comfortable, and that's exactly the problem.
