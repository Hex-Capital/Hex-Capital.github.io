# Moda -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Moda: this is one of the most consensus markets I've seen in a while. LLM observability has attracted over $385 million in combined funding across competitors like Braintrust, LangChain, Langfuse, and Helicone -- and that's before you count the acqui-hires and the inevitable feature expansions from Datadog and the cloud providers themselves. When I look for investments, I'm drawn to ideas that make people uncomfortable, that other investors have passed on because they sound absurd. "We're building monitoring for AI agents" is the opposite of that. It's the kind of thing every technical founder in San Francisco is thinking about right now. The behavioral detection angle -- hallucinations, laziness, broken promises -- is a real product insight, but it's an incremental differentiation within an already-crowded category, not the kind of contrarian leap that gets me out of my chair.

When I sit down with founders, the first thing I want to hear is their story -- not the pitch, not the market size, but how they arrived at this specific problem in a way that makes them the only people who could solve it. Mohammed and Pranav are clearly talented engineers. University of Waterloo, Shopify internships, Pranav's time at Cerebral Valley working on LLM observability -- these are legitimate credentials. But credentials aren't the same as biographical inevitability. I don't see the personal thread that ties these founders to this problem in a way that would keep them grinding through the inevitable crises ahead. They identified a market gap, which is smart, but it reads more like pattern-matching the hot category than solving a problem that's been burning in their chest. When I backed the Crusoe team, it wasn't because they had relevant experience -- it was because they'd independently stumbled onto something nobody else saw, and their obsession with it was palpable and a little bit irrational. I'm not feeling that here.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. The shift from LLMs-as-API to agents-as-product genuinely does create a new class of semantic failures that infrastructure-level observability doesn't catch. If Moda can own "agent behavioral monitoring" as a category before Braintrust or LangSmith bolt it on, they could build a meaningful wedge. Langfuse reached $1.1 million in revenue and got acquired by ClickHouse with only $4.5 million raised -- proof that a focused, well-executed tool can win even in a crowded space. And Pranav's direct experience building LLM observability at Cerebral Valley means this team isn't starting from zero on domain knowledge. The timing is genuine: agents are shipping to production now, behavioral failures are expensive, and nobody has nailed this specific layer yet. If I were evaluating this purely on market timing and technical plausibility, I'd be more interested.

But that's exactly the problem -- evaluating on market timing and technical plausibility is consensus-based investing, not conviction-based investing. This company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations. It doesn't make an opaque system transparent for people who lack power -- it makes agent failures visible for engineering teams who already have a dozen tools at their disposal. It doesn't operate in a regulated or stigmatized space where others fear to tread. The dossier itself acknowledges that the underlying approach -- running LLM classifiers over conversation logs -- is "technically reproducible" by any well-funded competitor. That's not a moat; that's a feature roadmap item for Braintrust's next quarter. When I think about the companies in my portfolio that became truly important -- Uber creating income for drivers, Affirm making credit terms transparent, Flock Safety putting public safety data in communities' hands -- they all shared a quality of giving power to people who didn't have it before. Moda is a useful tool for a well-served audience, and that's a fundamentally different kind of bet than what I make.

The co-founder dynamic has modest positive signals -- shared university, shared employer at Shopify -- but I have no window into how Mohammed and Pranav handle real disagreement when things go sideways. At two people, this matters less now, but it'll matter enormously when the first real crisis hits and they're competing against teams with $100 million in the bank. I'd need to sit with them to understand that dimension, but nothing in the public record gives me confidence or concern either way.
