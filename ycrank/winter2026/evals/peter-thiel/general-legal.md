# General Legal -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Y Combinator published a Request for Startups in 2025 that explicitly told founders to "start your own law firm, staff it with AI agents, and compete with existing law firms." General Legal is that company. And therein lies the problem. When the most influential accelerator in the world publishes an open invitation for a specific business model, that model is not a secret. It is a consensus. The entire premise -- that AI makes lawyers dramatically more efficient and that this efficiency should be captured through a new kind of firm rather than a software tool -- is shared by Lawhive (which just raised $60 million to expand into the U.S. on exactly this thesis), by LegalOS (in the same YC batch), by Third Chair (also YC-backed), and implicitly by every law firm partner who has spent the last two years wondering what GPT-4 means for their practice. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?", the honest answer here is that very few people *disagree* with anything General Legal is doing. The market is crowded with agreement.

The founding team is the strongest signal in this dossier, and I want to be precise about why it is not sufficient. Walker, Qadrud-Din, and Mohler built Casetext together, which Thomson Reuters acquired for $650 million in cash. All three combine genuine legal practice (Fenwick, Cooley, Harvard Law) with genuine AI engineering (PhD in mathematics, published ML research, CTO-level technical leadership). This is rare dual expertise -- I cannot think of many teams better positioned to build an AI-powered law firm. But capability and secret are different things. The Casetext team knows how to build AI legal products; the question is whether this particular product creates a monopoly. A team with a $650 million exit knows how to execute, but execution advantage is not a structural moat. The experience gives them a head start, not an endgame.

The "$500 per contract with one-hour turnaround" pricing creates a genuine 10x improvement over traditional law firms charging $500-2,000 per hour with multi-day turnarounds. I take this seriously -- an order-of-magnitude cost and speed improvement is exactly what I look for. But the improvement is in the delivery model, not in the underlying technology. The AI workflows that enable this efficiency are built on the same large language models available to every competitor. Harvey has $750 million in funding. Spellbook has reviewed ten million contracts. Lawhive has $35 million in ARR and seven-times revenue growth. The dossier itself states plainly that "any team of experienced attorneys with AI engineering capability could build a competing firm." When your own competitive analysis describes your model as replicable, you have not found a secret -- you have found an arbitrage that will be competed away.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If these founders can monopolize commercial contracting for YC-backed companies and then expand concentrically -- first to all VC-backed startups, then to mid-market companies -- they could build something with real switching costs. Every contract reviewed teaches the AI more about a client's risk tolerance and business context. The regulatory barrier -- you must be a licensed law firm with barred attorneys to do this work -- means Harvey and Spellbook cannot trivially become competitors without fundamentally changing their business model. And the Stripe analogy is tempting: start with a narrow developer audience, deliver a dramatically simpler experience, expand into financial infrastructure. If General Legal becomes the default legal provider for the YC network and that network effect compounds, the outcome could be substantial. For this to work, they would need to achieve something approaching Stripe's lock-in -- where the integration cost of switching exceeds the benefit -- but professional services relationships are inherently more portable than payment APIs. A startup can switch law firms in a week. They cannot switch payment processors in a week.

The structural problem from my framework is that this is a labor-intensive service business dressed in technology language. Every contract requires a licensed attorney. Scaling from 20 clients to 2,000 requires proportionally more attorneys. The AI makes each attorney more productive, which is valuable, but it does not create the kind of zero-marginal-cost scaling that defines technology monopolies. Facebook's thousandth user cost nothing to serve. General Legal's thousandth contract requires an attorney's time. This is the difference between a technology company and a professional services firm with good technology -- and the distinction matters enormously for power-law returns. A very successful professional services firm generates solid returns. It does not return the fund.
