# Cedar -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$2.5M-$3M in contracted revenue with four people and no public-facing product — that's the number that stops me cold. At pre-seed, most companies are building prototypes and running pilots. Cedar claims enterprise contracts generating seven figures while still listing "🤫 in stealth" on LinkedIn. Even if these self-reported figures from the a16z Speedrun listing are somewhat inflated or represent multi-year contract values rather than ARR, the signal is hard to dismiss: real enterprises are writing real checks for AI-driven referral mapping before Cedar has a marketing site, a Product Hunt launch, or a single public case study. That revenue density per employee is the kind of pull signal I weight heavily at this stage — it suggests the problem is urgent enough that buyers don't need to be educated, they need to be served.

The structural market timing here requires careful parsing. The broad category — AI agents for recruiting — is consensus. Perfect raised $23M, Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce for $14.4M+, and the sector attracted $208M in the first eleven months of 2025. If Cedar were building another AI sourcing tool scraping LinkedIn profiles, I'd score this as crowded and move on. But Cedar's specific approach sits at a different layer: extracting private relationship data from employee conversations to build a proprietary graph that no public-data tool can replicate. The structural catalyst is real — LLM conversational capabilities crossed a threshold in 2023-2024 that made structured relationship-mapping interviews feasible at scale for the first time. Two years ago, you could not have an AI agent conduct a nuanced 15-minute call that surfaces trust-based professional connections. The nonobviousness is moderate: most AI recruiting investors are focused on sourcing from public data, and the private-graph approach is genuinely differentiated but not a fundamentally different structural read than the broader "AI agents unlock X" thesis being applied everywhere.

Greg Gunn and Beier Cai are exactly the founder profile I look for. They scaled Hootsuite from 5 to 1,500 employees and $0 to $200M+ ARR — Gunn as the first business executive, Cai as the founding engineer who wrote the first line of code. They've worked together for over a decade across three companies. That's not a co-founder relationship, that's a battle-tested operating unit. More specifically, they lived the exact problem they're solving: at Hootsuite's scale, referral hiring becomes operationally brutal, and the dossier cites Stripe spending 100 people and $10M/year to maintain referral programs. The advisory bench — Laszlo Bock (former Google SVP People Ops), Tiffany Fenster (former Stripe Head of Recruiting), Brendan Browne (former LinkedIn VP Global Talent Acquisition) — maps precisely to the enterprise buyers Cedar needs to reach. This reminds me of the Gusto pattern: an operationally boring HR infrastructure problem that experienced operators attack because they've personally felt the pain, while prestige-seeking founders chase flashier markets.

The infrastructure-layer thesis is where Cedar gets genuinely interesting — and where the biggest uncertainty lives. Every 15-minute employee call adds private relationship data to a proprietary graph that competitors cannot replicate from public sources. If Cedar achieves customer density, this graph compounds: each new enterprise client enriches the network for all clients, creating the kind of data network effect that makes a platform progressively harder to displace. LinkedIn owns the dominant professional graph but faces a structural business-model conflict — building an agent-driven referral extraction layer would cannibalize LinkedIn Recruiter's per-seat revenue and antagonize enterprise customers by surfacing their employees' private networks. That conflict is real and creates a window. The risk: Cedar's graph requires critical mass to generate cross-customer value, and right now with four people and undisclosed customer count, there's no evidence the graph has reached meaningful density. The Commit.dev history cuts both ways here — Gunn and Cai previously co-founded a "professional network for startup engineers" in 2019, raised $6M from iNovia and Georgian Partners, and the current status is opaque. They've tried to build a professional graph before. Either they learned exactly why it failed and Cedar's AI-agent approach is the corrective, or they're repeating a pattern that didn't work. I'd need a direct conversation to resolve that ambiguity.

The single-miracle test reads favorably. The technology miracle — LLMs capable of conducting structured conversational interviews — is happening exogenously and improving on its own curve. Enterprise buyers of recruiting solutions are proven payers with budget authority (no EdTech problem here). Cedar doesn't need a regulatory change or a distribution breakthrough. The one miracle required: enterprises must be willing to let AI agents call their employees and map their private professional networks. The contracted revenue suggests this miracle is already happening at meaningful scale, though data privacy concerns in regulated industries (GDPR jurisdictions especially) could limit the addressable market segment. The broader concern is whether the core process — AI-conducted relationship mapping calls — is defensible when the underlying LLM technology is commoditizing. The accumulated graph data is the moat, not the call technology itself, which means Cedar is in a race to build graph density before well-funded competitors like Perfect ($23M) decide to add a similar feature.

The strongest bull case for Cedar is that they've found a genuinely differentiated data asset in a category where most competitors are fighting over the same public profile data, and the revenue traction validates enterprise willingness to pay for this specific approach. The bear case: the AI recruiting market is crowded and consensus, the proprietary graph requires customer density they haven't proven, the technology layer (LLM-driven calls) is replicable, and Commit.dev's unresolved history raises questions about whether this team can build a lasting network business. My scoring is most influenced by the operator-scaler founder profile (exceptional for this specific problem) and the revenue traction signal, moderated by the consensus nature of the broader market and unproven graph density.
