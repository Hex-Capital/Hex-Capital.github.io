# Terranox AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did these founders find this problem? And with Terranox, the answer worries me. Neither founder comes from uranium exploration. Jade Checlair has a PhD in geophysical sciences, which sounds relevant until you read the details — her research was on exoplanet habitability and snowball states on tidally locked planets. That's geophysics of other worlds, not this one. Leeav Lipton spent eight years building AI/ML at a bank's research institute. These are smart, accomplished people. But the path from "planetary scientist and bank ML engineer" to "AI-powered uranium exploration company" has the shape of an idea that was assembled from trending ingredients — AI is hot, nuclear is hot, uranium supply is constrained, nobody's applying AI to uranium specifically — rather than discovered through lived experience. When I look for the specific moment where a founder personally encountered the pain they're solving, I don't find it. That's the failure mode I've written about extensively: brilliant people generating an idea that sounds compelling on a whiteboard but didn't grow from genuine need.

The schlep question is genuinely interesting here, though, and it cuts both ways. Uranium exploration is an enormous schlep — federal regulatory oversight from the NRC and CNSC, environmental review processes that stretch years, public opposition, capital-intensive drilling campaigns, and the physical reality of exploring remote terrain. Most software founders would unconsciously filter this entire category out of their mental search space. That's real schlep blindness at work. The vertical integration model — owning both the AI and the mineral claims rather than licensing software — multiplies the schlep further. In theory, this is exactly the kind of ugly, tedious complexity that creates structural barriers to competition. But here's the problem: the Stripe analogy only works because the Collison brothers were programmers who personally knew payment processing was broken and then chose to walk into the banks. The schlep was deterrent enough to keep competitors away, but the founders had the domain knowledge to navigate it. Terranox's founders have adjacent skills — geophysics and ML — but no publicly documented experience in uranium exploration, mining operations, or the nuclear fuel cycle. The schlep deters competitors, but it may also defeat the founders.

The co-founder relationship is one genuinely strong signal. They met in first-year physics and have collaborated for over a decade. That's not a pair who met at a co-founder matching event last month. Nine companies in one YC batch added strangers as co-founders and all nine imploded within a year — these two are clearly not in that category. And individually, their credentials are real: a UChicago PhD with NASA collaborations and BCG consulting on one side, eight years of production AI/ML engineering on the other. The combination of geoscience training and ML systems experience is directly relevant to building exploration models. I can see why YC admitted them. But credentials and relevance are different from organic founder-market fit. I'd need to hear them describe the specific moment they knew this was their problem — not "we noticed the uranium supply deficit," but something that reveals they've been living inside this domain.

Let me steel-man the bull case, because there's a version of this that works. KoBold Metals has raised over a billion dollars and is valued at nearly $3 billion applying AI to mineral exploration for copper, nickel, and cobalt. Earth AI — also YC-backed, also vertically integrated — has raised $20 million and claims a 75% discovery success rate versus the industry's 0.5%. If that model works for critical minerals, there's no physical reason it can't work for uranium. And nobody is doing it. The uranium-specific positioning is genuinely uncrowded. The timing is strong: AI data center demand is doubling electricity consumption, new reactors are coming online, uranium prices have hit $86/lb on long-term contracts, and supply is structurally constrained. If Terranox's AI can identify even one significant deposit in North America, the asset value could be enormous. This has some of the characteristics of my Boom Supersonic investment — frighteningly ambitious, capital-intensive, important for the country's energy security. The question is whether the founders' conviction runs as deep as Blake Scholl's obsession with supersonic flight, and I don't yet see evidence that it does.

The absence of any visible product is notable even at pre-seed. The website returned only font loading and a title. No demo, no prototype screenshots, no technical blog post describing their approach, no evidence that the claimed "AI trained on 70+ years of exploration outcomes" actually exists yet. For a company whose core value proposition is a technical capability — an AI model that can identify uranium deposits — showing nothing technical is a gap. Leeav has real ML engineering experience, but there are no public repos on GitHub, and Jade's GitHub shows mostly forks of scientific computing tools. At this stage I don't expect revenue or users, but I'd want to see evidence that someone has been writing code, training models, and getting results. The asset-heavy nature of the business model also creates a fundamental tension with lean startup economics — you can't reach ramen profitability by finding uranium deposits on $500K of pre-seed capital.

This is a Pass for me. The idea has the fingerprints of market analysis rather than organic discovery. The founders are smart and their collaboration is tested, but neither has operated inside the domain they're targeting. The schlep is real but may be beyond their current ability to navigate without uranium-specific expertise. And the capital requirements of physical exploration make it hard to see how the standard YC playbook — build fast, get users, grow weekly — applies here. If they came back with a working model that had been validated against known deposits, a geologist with 20 years of uranium exploration on the team, and a first mineral claim in hand, I'd look again.
