# Fed10 -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Fed10 is that these founders were actual lobbyists before they could legally vote. That's not a credential you can manufacture. Armand Iorgulescu was the youngest gubernatorial appointee in his state, drafted recommendations that became law, and was doing this work as a teenager. Winston Wei passed bills before voting age. All three co-founders "passed legislation together" before starting Fed10. When I apply the organic test -- did this idea grow from lived experience or from a brainstorming session? -- the answer is clear. These founders didn't read about policy consulting in a market report and decide to "disrupt" it. They were in the rooms. They know what it actually takes to track bills across fifty state legislatures, because they were personally doing the tracking and the lobbying. That's the kind of organic problem discovery I look for.

The schlep here is real but not as deep as it first appears. Monitoring fifty state legislatures, each with different procedures, data formats, and session calendars, is genuinely tedious work. The accuracy requirement is high -- if your AI tells a company that a bill doesn't affect them and it does, you've created a liability nightmare. Policy professionals charge $500/hour partly because the consequences of being wrong are severe. But this isn't Stripe-level schlep, where you had to negotiate with banks, build fraud systems, and navigate financial regulation. The underlying legislative data is public. The technology barrier to building a basic version -- feed bills into an LLM, generate summaries -- is low enough that FiscalNote already launched PolicyNote, Quorum is adding AI features, Plural Policy raised $14 million, and USLege exists. When I count five competitors doing variations of AI-powered legislative tracking, including a public company with $120 million in revenue, I get nervous. This feels like it's becoming a consensus category, and consensus ideas have already been priced.

The strongest bull case is that the incumbents are actually weak right now. FiscalNote's stock is down 97% from its merger price and the company is in organizational transition. These incumbents built their businesses around human analysts layered on top of databases -- their economics depend on professional services revenue that AI directly threatens. If Fed10 can build something accurate enough to genuinely replace the human consultant layer, the value proposition is obvious: seconds instead of days, thousands instead of tens of thousands. And the founders' domain expertise gives them something purely technical competitors lack -- they know what a good policy analysis actually looks like because they've done the work themselves. That's the same advantage the Collisons had: they were programmers who used payment APIs, so they knew exactly where the pain was. If Fed10's founders can translate their lobbying experience into an AI system that produces analysis indistinguishable from a senior consultant's, that's a real product with real demand.

But I keep coming back to the crowded market problem. The founders have genuine domain expertise, and two of the three have CS backgrounds, which gives them the technical-plus-domain combination I value. Zayan at Berkeley CS with an Amazon offer at 17 suggests real technical ability, and Winston adds CS from Williams alongside political science. That's a strong founding team composition on paper. Yet there's zero evidence of what they've actually built. No traction data, no user testimonials, no revenue signals, not even a Product Hunt launch. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but in a market with five funded competitors, I want to see some signal that their approach is differentiated enough to matter. The positioning as "AI agents that replace your policy consultants" is compelling copy, but I can't tell whether the product actually delivers consultant-quality analysis or just summarizes bills like any LLM wrapper could.

What holds me back from conviction is a pattern I've seen repeatedly: smart young founders with genuine domain insight entering a market that's simultaneously being flooded by both startups and incumbents adding AI features. The organic discovery is real. The schlep willingness is real. But when the same problem is being attacked from multiple directions by teams with more resources, more enterprise relationships, and more runway, the startup advantage narrows. These founders are college-age, which means they don't yet have the networks of enterprise buyers who would be their customers. The YC brand helps, but government affairs teams at Fortune 500 companies don't typically buy software from twenty-year-olds when FiscalNote and Quorum are adding similar AI capabilities to platforms they already use. The founders need to prove they can close enterprise deals in a market where relationships matter enormously -- and I haven't seen evidence of that yet.

I'd want to see one more thing before writing a check: proof that the AI output is actually good enough. In consumer products, you can ship something rough and iterate. In policy consulting, accuracy is table stakes. If a government affairs VP at a Fortune 100 company tries Fed10 and the analysis misses a critical provision or mischaracterizes a bill's impact, they'll never come back. The founders' lobbying experience should help them build the right evaluation framework, but building an AI system that matches senior consultant judgment across fifty different legislative contexts is a genuinely hard technical problem. I'd want to see a working demo that impresses actual policy professionals before I'd invest my own money.
