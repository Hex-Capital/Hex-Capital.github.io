# Arctic Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Arctic Health is a schlep-shaped company built by resume-shaped founders, and that mismatch is the whole evaluation. The work itself -- 300+ payer portals, all 50 states + DC, a 60-90 day credentialing slog governed by NCQA -- is exactly the kind of grind I wrote about in "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012). But Sam Slezek covered insurance companies at Morgan Stanley, Raghav Mehrish was a senior engineer at Airbnb and Zenefits, and Michael Clark ran inside sales at Instabase. Nobody in this trio woke up at 2 a.m. fighting a payer portal for their own practice. The schlep was spotted; it was not lived.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy for Arctic Health on the schlep dimension, and it's a flattering one. Brian Armstrong ran toward bitcoin compliance when every other YC applicant in his batch was routing around it; the regulatory grind became the moat. Arctic Health is staring at a structurally similar prize -- per-state licensure rules, per-payer idiosyncratic forms, NCQA standards that change without notice -- the kind of work Medallion ($130M raised) and Verifiable ($50.1M, Craft-led Series B, TechCrunch July 2023) have been climbing up-market to escape. Arctic Health diverges from the Coinbase analogy in one specific way that matters: Armstrong personally built an Android Bitcoin wallet to overturn his YC rejection. Arctic Health's dossier names no equivalent artifact -- no demo video on Hacker News, no first ten SMB practices onboarded by hand, no published per-portal reliability number. The schlep is identified; the running toward it is asserted, not demonstrated.

The pass-example MBA team from my own essay is the failure mechanism Arctic Health most resembles on animal resourcefulness. That team had a glossy deck, a $50B TAM slide, and a "hire engineers post-funding" plan; what they lacked was a pre-funding stunt that would have made me forgive everything else. Arctic Health's dossier reads similarly in shape: Scale AI / Instacart / Morgan Stanley / Airbnb / Zenefits / Instabase / 1Sharpe Capital -- a credentials parade with the $500K Menlo Ventures check already in -- and not one cereal-box moment. No Product Hunt launch, no press, no Twitter presence, no GitHub repos under any of the three founders, no named first customer. Arctic Health may be working very hard; the dossier just gives me no specific anecdote to prove it, and animals leave tracks.

Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT; the Collisons had been hacking commerce flows since **Auctomatic** (2007). Organic ideas compound over 18 months because the founder keeps finding the next unobvious sub-problem before any customer reports it -- they are still building for themselves. Arctic Health's founder-market fit is adjacent rather than organic: Slezek covered insurance as a sell-side analyst, Mehrish touched HR-benefits compliance at Zenefits. Neither of those is the same as having personally filed a CAQH application at 11 p.m. for your own practice. The compounding will be slower; the team will need real SMB customers to surface the edge cases Patrick Collison would have already known.

The hacker-genealogy axis would flip a tier if Mehrish surfaced a real GitHub footprint -- a maintained open-source project around browser automation, a non-trivial agent harness, anything matching the Patrick Collison Young Scientist Lisp prize (2005) shape. The dossier says "No public repos found" for all three founders. Raghav Mehrish has a UCLA MS CS and senior Airbnb tenure, which is hacker-credential but not hacker-artifact. Counterfactually: if Mehrish had shipped a public agent framework with hundreds of GitHub stars predating Arctic Health, I'd be at 11/15 here instead of 6/15, and the whole evaluation would lean toward Invest.

**Stripe** (2010) was two brothers with a decade of shared building behind them. Arctic Health is a three-person team -- CEO, CTO, COO, classic complementary coverage -- but the dossier explicitly says "no public data on co-founder history (shared employer/university not identified)." Three is the right number; the prior-relationship signal is missing. I score this at the default, because YC interviews surface chemistry better than dossiers do.

My 36-month forecast on Arctic Health: acquired by Medallion or Verifiable for a low-to-mid eight-figure outcome, or stuck at $1-3M ARR in the SMB segment while Certify (which just raised $40M Series B in 2025) and Assured ($6M seed, Fierce Healthcare) compete the wedge into commodity. The mechanism is the signature: a real schlep that the founders correctly identified but did not personally suffer, executed by a competent team with no pre-funding artifact to suggest they will outrun four better-capitalized incumbents and one direct AI-native peer. Schleps reward people who keep showing up after the romance dies. The dossier does not yet show me that Arctic Health's three founders are the kind who will.

The strongest bull case against me: Slezek led product at Scale AI, and Alexandr Wang publicly named my "Schlep Blindness" essay as Scale's seminal trigger. If Slezek absorbed Wang's operating system directly -- the willingness to run toward the labeling grind nobody else would touch -- then Arctic Health has imported the exact cultural genome that turned Scale into a $14B+ company, and the missing cereal-box moment is just a documentation gap I'm punishing unfairly. That stings. The counter is that Wang was the founder of Scale; Slezek led product teams inside the company Wang built. Watching Wang up close is not the same as being Wang, and the dossier gives me no Arctic Health-specific moment where Slezek behaved like Wang would have. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: Arctic Health publishing, within 90 days of YC Demo Day, a list of 30+ named SMB practices live in production with per-portal submission accuracy above 95% -- a stunt that would prove the animal exists and the schlep is being run, not just described.
