# Valgo -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

These founders wrote the textbook. Literally. "Algorithms for Validation" -- MIT Press, forthcoming -- authored by the founding team and their Stanford advisor. They teach the only course on validation of safety-critical systems at Stanford. Robert Moss spent seven years at MIT Lincoln Laboratory on the team that built ACAS Xa, the FAA-certified collision avoidance system that is now a worldwide standard in commercial aviation. This is not domain expertise acquired through career optimization. This is knowledge that took a decade to accumulate, that exists in perhaps a dozen people on the planet, and that directly maps to the product being built. When I evaluate specific knowledge, I ask whether a well-funded competitor could hire their way to equivalence. Here, the answer is close to no -- because the founding team IS the field. Sydney Katz's PhD on safe ML-based perception, Harrison Delecki's thesis on sampling and estimation for safety validation -- these aren't adjacent credentials, they are the methodological foundation of the product itself. The closest analogy in my portfolio is backing the developers of Stack Overflow, who understood developer knowledge infrastructure because they'd lived inside it for years. Valgo's founders understand probabilistic safety validation because they invented the modern framework for it.

The leverage architecture is what pulls me forward. Valgo is not building an insurance company. It's not writing policies. It's building the risk quantification infrastructure layer -- the probabilistic model that sits between autonomy companies generating simulation data and insurers who need actuarially usable loss estimates. This is a picks-and-shovels play, and my portfolio is dominated by them for a reason. AngelList was funding infrastructure, not a fund. Alchemy was blockchain developer infrastructure, not a DeFi app. Valgo is insurance infrastructure for physical AI, not an insurer. The API-based model means that once the probabilistic engine is built, each additional simulation dataset processed is near-zero marginal cost. And crucially, each new autonomy company's data has the potential to improve risk models for every insurer on the platform -- a data flywheel that compounds with adoption. One engineering team's work serves the entire emerging market.

The contrarian bet here is subtler than "smart people think this is wrong." The non-consensus position is structural: Valgo is wagering that simulation-derived loss estimates can replace actuarial claims history as the basis for underwriting autonomous systems. Every insurance actuary trained in the last century would tell you that you need historical claims data to price risk. Valgo says: the claims data will never exist in sufficient volume because autonomous systems are new, but simulation data -- which autonomy companies already generate at massive scale for their own testing -- contains equivalent statistical information if you know how to extract it. The people who know how to extract it are the people who wrote the textbook on extracting it. That's the bet. Koop Technologies took the more intuitive path -- become an MGA, write policies directly through Lloyd's, own the full stack. Valgo's bet is that the risk quantification layer is the durable position, and that insurers themselves will want to underwrite these policies once someone gives them a credible loss estimate. I find the infrastructure positioning more compelling than the vertically integrated approach, because it scales across all insurers rather than competing with them.

The honest bear case: this is a dual-sided market problem with regulatory uncertainty. Autonomy companies guard simulation data jealously -- it reveals failure modes, which are competitively sensitive. Insurers are structurally conservative about adopting non-traditional underwriting methods. State insurance commissioners may not accept simulation-based rate filings. And the immediate addressable market is small -- perhaps a few dozen autonomy companies that need commercial insurance today. Koop has a four-year head start and established relationships. These are real constraints. But the structural dynamic favors Valgo on the timeline that matters: autonomous trucks, delivery robots, and air taxis are crossing from pilot programs to commercial deployment right now. Every one of them needs insurance to operate on public roads. The demand is not speculative -- it's regulatory mandate. And the FAA certification experience Moss carries from ACAS Xa is precisely the credential that gives regulatory bodies confidence in novel risk frameworks. If autonomous deployment accelerates -- and the trajectory suggests it will -- the company that owns the risk quantification layer captures value regardless of which insurer or MGA writes the policy.

The public benefit corporation registration and the multi-year academic commitment before commercialization signal the kind of long-term orientation I look for. These founders could have started a company at any point during their PhDs. They chose to complete the research, write the textbook, and build the intellectual foundation first. That's patience. The team composition is right -- three technical PhDs from the same lab with deep collaborative history, plus an insurance industry veteran (Jon Qian, credentials described but unverified independently) to bridge the domain gap. The technology has genuine compounding properties: accumulated simulation-to-loss datasets become proprietary, model accuracy improves with volume, and the textbook authority creates a category-defining brand that competitors cannot replicate through engineering alone. I'd want to verify Jon Qian's insurance credentials independently and understand the specific data-sharing architecture before wiring money, but the core thesis is sound: non-teachable knowledge, infrastructure leverage, contrarian structural positioning, and technology that compounds. This is my kind of bet.
