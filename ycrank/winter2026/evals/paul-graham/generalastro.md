# General Astronautics -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about General Astronautics is that this company needs at least three miracles to work. Commercial space stations have to get built and become operational. Those stations have to allocate budget for third-party robotic lab equipment instead of building it themselves. And a two-person team of recent graduates has to successfully engineer autonomous robots that handle fluids in zero gravity — something nobody has done. Each of those is a substantial bet on its own. Multiplied together, they make for very long odds. I've always said you want a startup that needs zero miracles, ideally, or maybe one. Three is too many.

The origin of this idea concerns me. Schork interned at SpaceX working on Starlink laser optics testing, and he's genuinely accomplished in robotics — winning soft robotics competitions in high school, publishing in academic journals. Zhou comes from CMU's robotics program. These are clearly smart, technically capable people. But neither of them has worked in microgravity research. Neither has spent time pipetting samples on the ISS. The $130K-per-astronaut-hour statistic is a number you find in articles about the space economy, not a pain point you discover by living inside the problem. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, that was organic. When the Collisons got sick of integrating payment APIs, that was organic. When two robotics students decide that space lab automation sounds like a massive market opportunity, that's closer to what I've called a "made up" idea — not because it's wrong, but because it was arrived at through analysis rather than experience.

The schlep here is real, and it's significant. Fluid handling in microgravity is genuinely hard physics. Surface tension and capillary effects dominate when gravity disappears, which means terrestrial lab automation approaches don't transfer cleanly. Most robotics founders would unconsciously avoid this problem precisely because the engineering is so foreign. That part of the equation works in their favor. But there's a second schlep they may not fully appreciate: selling to this market. Your total addressable customer list is maybe five to ten organizations globally, all of which have multi-year procurement cycles, require flight heritage for hardware qualification, and are staffed by people who have been in aerospace for decades. You cannot do "Collison installation" with NASA. You cannot grow 5-7% per week when your pipeline has five names on it. The entire feedback loop that makes startups work — build, measure, learn, iterate weekly — is structurally broken in this market.

The bull case is that this is a Boom Supersonic situation: frighteningly ambitious, important for humanity, and potentially enormous if it works. And the platform-agnostic approach is genuinely smart — rather than building your own spacecraft like Varda or Space Forge, you build robots that could operate on any station. If Axiom and Vast and Orbital Reef all come online in the late 2020s, being the standard robotic platform across all of them would be a powerful position. GITAI has $129M but is pointed at satellite servicing, not lab automation — there's a window. And the technical founding team covers the right bases: mechanical engineering and aerospace from Caltech, CS and robotics from CMU. If I squint, I can see why this works.

But when I look at the founders more carefully, I see two people at the very start of their careers. Schork appears to be Caltech class of 2026 — meaning he may not have graduated yet. Zhou's graduation date is unknown but likely recent. Their experience is internships, not full-time roles. More concerning: I see no evidence they knew each other before starting this company. One is at Caltech, the other at CMU. In one YC batch, I saw nine companies where co-founders met specifically to start a company together, and all nine fell apart within a year. I don't know that's what happened here, but the absence of a shared history is a flag I can't ignore. And Zhou's GitHub — four public repos, all forks, one follower — doesn't show the kind of compulsive building I look for when I ask whether someone is "an animal" at their craft.

There's no evidence of user pull — no prototype in use, no letters of intent, no named customer conversations. At pre-seed that's not disqualifying on its own, but combined with everything else, it means I'm being asked to bet entirely on the founders' ability to navigate an extraordinarily difficult market with extraordinarily difficult physics while building relationships they don't yet have. The technical ambition is admirable. The problem is real. But the path from here to a working business requires the founders to develop domain expertise, industry relationships, and flight heritage essentially from scratch — and to do it before GITAI notices this adjacent market and pivots with a hundred million dollars behind them.
