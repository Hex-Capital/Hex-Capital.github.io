# Galactic Resource Utilization Space, Inc. (GRU Space) -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

A 22-year-old wants to build a hotel on the Moon. The experts will tell you this is insane. That's exactly why I'm paying attention. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routers would never work in telecom networks, I wrote the check for Juniper. When people laughed at plant-based hamburgers, I backed Impossible Foods before it was even a company. The signal I track is not whether the experts approve — it's whether the consequence of success is large enough to justify the attempt despite their dismissal. If GRU Space succeeds in constructing habitable structures on the lunar surface using in-situ resources, humanity has permanent commercial infrastructure on another world. That consequence is civilizational. No spreadsheet captures it. The question is whether this particular bet, at this particular price, gives me the option value I need.

The talent-flow signal here is what made me sit up. Dr. Kevin Cannon — one of the world's foremost published researchers on lunar regolith composition and ISRU — left a tenured-track professorship at Colorado School of Mines and his CTO role at Ethos Space to join a 22-year-old's startup as employee number one. When I evaluated OpenAI in 2019, I ignored the mediocre AI performance and tracked two things: the pace of model improvement and the acceleration of top talent joining the company. A world-class scientist abandoning the security of academia to join a pre-seed company is a signal about the technology trajectory that no market analysis can substitute for. Cannon knows what actual lunar regolith does at the molecular level better than perhaps anyone alive. His decision to join GRU, rather than stay at a university or work with ICON's $507 million war chest, tells me something about where he believes the technology is heading and who he believes can get there.

But I learned the hardest $160 million lesson of my career at KiOR: technology that works in the lab can be a trap when the economics don't close at deployment. KiOR produced biofuel. It worked. At $5-10 per gallon against a $3 market. GRU Space's geopolymer brick process has been demonstrated with simulated regolith on Earth. The 2029 demo mission is a 10-kilogram payload — orders of magnitude below what hotel construction requires. And between here and a functioning lunar hotel, the plan demands that ISRU technology scales on actual regolith, that CLPS landers deliver reliably (Astrobotic's Peregrine mission failed in January 2024), that Starship achieves its projected cost reductions, that life-support systems work for civilian guests, and that a legal framework for lunar commercial operations emerges from the current Outer Space Treaty ambiguity. That is five simultaneous low-probability requirements. My framework explicitly flags this: when the plan requires the technology AND the market AND the regulation AND the infrastructure AND the team to all work at once, the founder hasn't prioritized which risk to eliminate first.

The timing has genuine elements working in its favor. Trump and Isaacman announcing a permanent Moon base by 2030, the CLPS program commercializing lunar payload delivery, Starship's development trajectory — these create the conditions for lunar infrastructure companies to exist. GRU is positioned ahead of a curve. But here's what separates this from my OpenAI bet: AI in 2019 was on an exponential improvement trajectory. I could measure the rate of change in model capability and talent flow, and extrapolate with 60-70% accuracy where it would be in five years. ISRU is not on an exponential curve. It's a materials science and engineering challenge — fundamentally linear, requiring capital-intensive physical demonstrations on a surface 240,000 miles away. The enabling infrastructure (launch vehicles) may be on an improvement curve, but the core technology is not. I don't have a rate-of-change signal to anchor my timing thesis.

The bull case I take seriously: at YC pre-seed pricing, this is a pure option-value bet. Five hundred thousand dollars either expires or produces a company that holds the only dataset of actual lunar ISRU construction — information no government or private entity on Earth currently possesses. Skyler Chan's trajectory — Berkeley EECS graduated early, Air Force pilot at 16, Tesla engineer, NASA 3D printer launched to space — compresses an unusual density of achievement into 22 years. The "hotel" framing is clever marketing that generates $250K-$1M deposits, 15+ press hits, and a Trump family reservation, creating a capital-raising flywheel that funds underlying technology development. The real business may be government contracts for lunar infrastructure — roads, landing pads, warehouses — with the hotel as the vision that attracts talent and capital. If Cannon's geopolymer process works on actual regolith, GRU owns a capability that Lockheed, Boeing, and Northrop can't replicate from their cost-plus contract structures. The outsider advantage is real: aerospace primes are structurally incapable of pursuing speculative consumer hospitality on the Moon.

Where I land: the consequence magnitude earns GRU Space serious consideration. At option-value pricing, I can tolerate extreme failure probability when the upside is this vast. But the absence of an exponential technology curve — the thing that gave me conviction on OpenAI — and the simultaneous-breakthrough architecture of the plan temper my enthusiasm. This is not a company where I can track rate of change and predict where it will be in three years. It's a company where I'm betting on a single 2029 demonstration mission that requires raising tens of millions in additional capital from current YC-level funding, using third-party launch vehicles with a track record of delays. I'd write a small check because the problem is worth attempting and Cannon's presence validates the technology direction. But this is a $1M option, not a $50M conviction bet.
