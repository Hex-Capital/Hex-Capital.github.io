# Rubric AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Rubric AI is what it's walking into: a market with $29 billion Scale AI, $1.2 billion-revenue Surge AI, $10 billion Mercor, and Labelbox -- which has already shipped a product literally called "rubric evaluation." This is not an unloved corner of the world where a few dreamers are toiling away on something nobody else understands. This is one of the most competed, most consensus categories in all of AI right now. Surge AI's blog is titled "Benchmarks are broken." Mercor's blog says "Welcome to the era of evals." When multiple billion-dollar incumbents are publishing thought leadership about exactly the thing you're building, the contrarian edge has been fully arbitraged away. I've learned to trust the signal that comes from unpopularity -- SpaceX when private space was considered a joke, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal in most cities. Rubric AI is the opposite of that signal. Everyone in Silicon Valley already believes that post-training evaluation matters.

When I sit down with a founder, the first thing I want to know is their story -- not the pitch, not the market sizing, but how they ended up here, solving this particular problem. Pragya Saboo has a solid career: product management at Asana, where she led Time Tracking from inception to launch, PM work at Oscar Health, a stint as a principal at Climate Capital. She co-founded a DTC nutraceuticals company and gave a TEDx talk. These are real accomplishments. But when I look for the biographical thread that connects *this person* to *this problem* -- the thread that tells me she'll persist through the inevitable crises of building a post-training research lab for frontier AI -- I can't find it. Her publicly documented experience doesn't include ML research, RLHF, model training, or AI infrastructure. The company calls itself a "research and product lab," but I don't see the research credentials. And the second team member isn't even publicly identified, which means I can't evaluate their story at all. That blocks my entire primary assessment channel. I need to hear both founders' stories before I can gauge whether this team is built to survive.

I'll give the bull case its full hearing, because it's not nothing: Meta's acquisition of 49% of Scale AI in mid-2025 genuinely fractured the market. Frontier labs that were shipping sensitive training data to Scale now have real concerns about what Meta sees. That's a structural dislocation, and in theory, a nimble two-person startup could slip into that gap -- especially if the unnamed second founder is a deeply credentialed ML researcher who brings the technical gravitas that Pragya's product skills would complement. A small team focused exclusively on evaluation methodology for frontier labs, rather than managing thousands of annotators, could theoretically move faster on the research side than bloated platforms juggling government contracts and enterprise sales. And Gustaf Alstromer as YC partner suggests someone credible said yes. But for this bull case to work, I'd need to see evidence of that technical co-founder's credentials, some indication of a genuinely novel evaluation methodology, and ideally a conversation with a frontier lab engineer who says "these people understand something nobody else does." None of that evidence exists in the public record.

What I also don't see is any dimension of economic access or real-world impact. This is B2B tooling sold to the wealthiest companies on Earth -- OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind. The annotation workers doing the actual evaluation work might benefit from better conditions, but that's not what Rubric AI is building toward. When I backed Uber, I saw what it did for drivers like Roger. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit replacing hidden fees. When I backed Flock Safety, I saw neighborhood safety data going to communities that needed it. Here, I see infrastructure being sold to organizations that already have extraordinary resources. That's a fine business, but it's not the kind of investment that gives me that visceral feeling of positive-sum impact.

I'm passing on Rubric AI. A two-person team with no publicly visible traction, no evident technical research background in the founding team, an invisible co-founder, and a company name that overlaps with multiple existing entities -- entering the most crowded and consensus category in AI infrastructure. The market is real, the timing argument around Scale AI's fragmentation is real, but this is exactly the kind of hot-market positioning I've trained myself to avoid. The best investments I've made were the ones that made other people uncomfortable. This one would make other investors very comfortable, and that's the wrong signal for how I invest.
