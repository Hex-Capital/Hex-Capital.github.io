# Oddpool -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Oddpool is a software aggregator selling $30/mo and $100/mo API access on top of two public venue feeds, founded by a team whose LinkedIn headlines still read "Co-founder @ Benchspan (YC P26)" — a within-batch pivot whose strongest dimension is a 352-star GitHub repo and whose weakest is the absence of any physical-world barrier to replication. That pairing is the entire diagnosis: a developer-distribution layer with no atoms, no secret, and a comp transaction (Polymarket's acquisition of Dome) that already prices the upside.

On the monopoly secret ladder, Palantir sits at the top — the contrarian claim that intelligence analysts would let a software firm restructure their data graph if the embedding ran deep enough, a thesis the consensus actively rejected for years. Wish sits at the bottom — no secret, no moat, a $14B IPO that crashed 90% because the monopoly question was never answered. Oddpool sits closer to Wish than to the middle: the "secret" advanced in the dossier is that venues like Polymarket will not become neutral aggregators because of channel conflict, which is not a secret — it is the lesson every observer of the Dome acquisition already drew. The Oddpool insight is correct and consensus simultaneously, which is the failure mode I name most often. The market-structure gap is real; it is also visible to Prediction Hunt, FinFeedAPI, and the open-source pmxt project, all of whom are racing into the same opening.

On the atoms-versus-bits ladder, General Matter sits at the top — uranium enrichment, $900M DOE contract, my first new board seat since Facebook, because the physical-world barrier cannot be shortcut by capital. Palantir sits in the middle as a software company whose embedding into government workflows produced an atoms-equivalent moat. Wish sits at the bottom: pure software commodity, replicable in a quarter. Oddpool is below Wish on this axis, because Oddpool does not even own its inputs — Kalshi's REST/FIX endpoints and Polymarket's CLOB are public APIs whose terms either venue can rewrite tomorrow. The dossier admits this directly: "Product depends entirely on Kalshi and Polymarket public APIs." That is not a moat; that is a counterparty.

Definite founder conviction: SpaceX is the high rung — Musk announced reusable rockets and committed irreversible capital before any market validation. The low rung is the AI-CRM pivot pattern I describe in my pass example — founders chasing the heat of the week without a specific secret. Avi Arora and Ritesh Malpani sit near the low rung not because their resumes are weak — Microsoft Prompt Shields at 20B classifications/year and Bloomberg trading infrastructure at 100K+ TPS are real — but because Oddpool is a pivot from Benchspan inside the same P26 batch, and a pivot inside the batch is the cleanest possible signal of indefinite thinking. The founders are searching for product-market fit; they are not executing a 10-year plan they made irreversible commitments to in 2023.

Power law magnitude: Lyft is my known exception at the middle of the ladder — a #2 in a category so large that even the loser returned the fund. SpaceX is the top. Oddpool is well below Lyft because the ceiling of a "Bloomberg for prediction markets" data broker, with $44B annual notional and $30-100/mo seats, is a $50-200M ARR business — and the relevant comp is Dome, which Polymarket absorbed at an undisclosed but almost certainly sub-fund-returning price. Distribution architecture is the one dimension where Oddpool earns real credit — the 352-star Awesome-Prediction-Market-Tools repo, the free tier with no card, and @oddpool_alerts publishing live arbitrage on Twitter follow the developer-led playbook PayPal used through eBay seller embedding. It is not organic pull at PayPal scale, but it is a real channel and the founders have built it with intent rather than buying paid acquisition.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool is acquired by Kalshi or a fintech data vendor (CoinAPI, ICE, or Bloomberg's terminal-data arm) for $10-50M between months 18 and 30, at sub-$5M ARR, after either Kalshi or Polymarket tightens commercial redistribution terms and forces a forced sale. The mechanism is the Dome precedent operating in reverse symmetry — the venue that did not buy Dome buys Oddpool to neutralize the same aggregation threat, and Oddpool's two-venue dependency makes the founders rational sellers at any reasonable multiple.

The strongest bull case is precisely that Dome precedent: venues now demonstrably pay to neutralize neutral aggregators, the historical orderbook archive compounds with every passing month, and the Clearview AI exception in my own framework shows I have written checks where a proprietary dataset overrode clean monopoly structure. If Oddpool's March-2026-onward 1-5 minute orderbook archive becomes the de facto backtesting corpus for prediction-market quant funds, the dataset itself becomes the asset and a venue acquisition could clear $200M+. The bear answer wins because Clearview's dataset was billions of scraped images that were genuinely unreplicable; Oddpool's archive can be matched by any competitor who started collecting in March 2026 and runs in parallel for two years — Prediction Hunt and FinFeedAPI are already doing exactly this. An acquisition exit at any price is the explicit red flag in my framework — companies built to be acquired are companies that never achieved monopoly, and the power law demands independent dominance, not a Polymarket-style absorption. The verdict flips to invest only if Oddpool signs an exclusive multi-year commercial data redistribution agreement with either Kalshi or Polymarket that prevents Prediction Hunt and FinFeedAPI from offering equivalent coverage — a contractual moat the dossier shows no evidence of and the venue acquisition pattern actively argues against.
