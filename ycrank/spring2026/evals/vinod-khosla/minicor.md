# Minicor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

93-96% click accuracy versus 80-85% for raw foundation model computer-use. That's the core technical claim on Minicor's website, and it tells me almost everything I need to know about the shape of this bet. A ten-percentage-point accuracy improvement is not a technology discontinuity — it's an optimization. When I backed Impossible Foods, the innovation wasn't "15% better soy protein." It was synthesizing heme from scratch, a fundamentally different approach to producing meat. Minicor is building an orchestration and reflection layer on top of Anthropic's, OpenAI's, and Google's computer-use APIs. The foundation is someone else's technology, and those someones are among the best-funded research labs on earth, shipping improvements quarterly. Minicor's own website acknowledges these baselines. The accuracy gap that defines Minicor's value proposition today could close in two model generations. That's not technology risk — that's a countdown.

Minicor's consequence-of-success question lands with a thud. If Minicor fully succeeds, legacy Windows desktop applications in healthcare, automotive, and logistics become API-accessible via AI agents. That's useful plumbing. One customer processing 25,000 patients per day is real usage, and six logos on the website suggest early commercial traction. But Minicor is middleware — the transformative value accrues to the AI companies building on top of it, not to Minicor itself. Did the world change when better SFTP software shipped? When EDI got cheaper? Integration infrastructure is necessary but rarely consequential at the civilizational scale I care about. When I invested $50 million into OpenAI, I wasn't backing the most elegant API wrapper — I was backing the possibility that artificial general intelligence reshapes everything. Minicor, even at full success, produces a well-run infrastructure company serving enterprise customers. That's a fine outcome for many investors. It's not the outcome I write checks for.

Faizaan Chishtie has prior YC experience from Phoenix (W21), shipped a prior startup Homeboard to mid-five-figure ARR, and pivoted from the original Laminar integration platform after encountering the legacy-desktop problem firsthand with a healthcare customer. Saheed Akinbile brings enterprise engineering from Ford Motor Company and Morgan Stanley — relevant verticals but institutional experience, not the outsider naivety I gravitate toward. The pivot from Laminar to Minicor is a legitimate learning signal — they hit a wall, saw an opening, and redirected. That's exactly what I look for in founders: adaptation over planning. But mid-five-figure ARR on a prior startup and solid engineering jobs at large companies don't clear the bar of extraordinary. Compare this to Peter Beck building rocket engines in a New Zealand garage, or the Mach Industries founder dropping out of MIT at 19 to compete with Lockheed Martin. Minicor's founders are competent and adaptive, but the founder signal here is "good YC-caliber team" not "this person sees something nobody else sees."

The bull case writes itself, and I want to be honest about it. An advocate would argue: Minicor is the essential connective tissue that unlocks AI in healthcare. Epic and Cerner are actively restricting API access. Without Minicor, every AI healthcare company hits the same wall — they can't read or write to the system of record. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance took real time and money to achieve, creating a barrier against the dozen browser-automation startups (Skyvern at $2.7M seed, Induced AI at $2.3M seed, Browserbase at $40M Series B) that focus on web, not Windows desktop. The DoorDash exception in my own framework says: when consequence magnitude is planetary-scale and the founder demonstrates learning velocity, override the technical novelty requirement. Here's where the argument breaks: DoorDash's consequence was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people — a direct impact on how humans live. Minicor's consequence is making it easier for B2B AI vendors to integrate with legacy software. That's two layers removed from human impact. The DoorDash exception doesn't apply because the consequence magnitude doesn't reach the threshold that triggered the override.

$35.27 billion global RPA market growing at 24% CAGR is a large TAM number, and large TAM numbers are exactly the kind of spreadsheet fiction I ignore. UiPath is public. Automation Anywhere has raised $6.8 billion. Minicor is competing for redistribution of existing automation spend, not creation of new demand — one of my explicit red flags. My highest-conviction investments (OpenAI, Commonwealth Fusion Systems, Impossible Foods) created entirely new categories. Minicor is a better mousetrap in a category that already has very large mice. The self-healing claim is genuinely interesting as engineering, but "automations that don't break when the UI changes" is solving a maintenance problem, not bending the cost curve of a basic human need toward zero. The path to radical affordability here would require Minicor's technology to make healthcare itself cheaper — but Minicor charges the AI companies, who charge the healthcare providers, who charge patients. The cost reduction dissipates across too many intermediaries.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if Minicor demonstrated that its orchestration layer had produced a durable, proprietary data asset — millions of labeled desktop UI interactions across heterogeneous applications that no foundation model provider could replicate — creating a flywheel where accuracy improves with usage faster than Anthropic or OpenAI can close the gap from the model layer alone. That would convert this from an optimization layer into a proprietary technology moat. I see no evidence of that in the dossier today. Minicor is a competent team building useful infrastructure on a foundation that is actively commoditizing beneath them. The consequence of success is a profitable company, not a changed world. I pass.
