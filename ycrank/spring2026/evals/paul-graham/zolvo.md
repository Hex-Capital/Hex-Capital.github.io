# Zolvo -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I want to know about any company is how the founders found the problem. With Zolvo, the answer comes through an interesting channel: both founders were at Domu, a YC S24 company. Rodriguez was the first GTM hire and closed Domu's first million in revenue selling to financial institutions. Montes was the founding AI engineer building voice AI infrastructure that handled 50,000 to 100,000 daily calls. They were inside the machinery of fintech lending operations every day. That's not the same as Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive -- it's not a personal itch -- but it's meaningfully better than reading a TechCrunch article about market size and deciding to start a company. They saw the back offices of commercial lenders firsthand and realized these places are still running on spreadsheets and manual reconciliation. The problem found them while they were doing something else, which is how the better startup ideas tend to arrive.

What catches my attention is the schlep. Commercial lending servicing is the kind of problem that makes smart people's eyes glaze over. Reconciliation, verification, collections, regulatory reporting, SOC 2 compliance, bank examination requirements, multi-state lending regulations -- this is the un-glamorous plumbing of finance. Look at where the AI money actually went in this space: Salient raised $70 million for voice AI that automates consumer and auto loan collections calls. That's the version of this problem that looks good on a pitch deck. Automating phone calls is clean. Automating the full back-office workflow for commercial lenders -- mapping data pipelines across legacy systems, dealing with audit obligations, navigating bank-grade security requirements -- is the kind of ugly, tedious work that causes schlep blindness. The fact that a company with $70 million is going after the prettier adjacent problem rather than this one is itself a competitive signal. And the incumbents like nCino have a structural conflict: their large-bank customers have existing headcount and legacy servicing contracts, making it hard to sell them a product that eliminates back-office roles. Zolvo targeting mid-market lenders with $200 million in assets under management avoids that conflict entirely.

Tony Montes is a genuine builder. Dual engineering degrees from Universidad de los Andes, research stints at Cornell and UIC, publications at ACL and EMNLP -- those are real NLP credentials -- plus he built production AI systems at Domu that scaled to six figures of daily calls. He has 67 GitHub repos. At 22, the combination of academic research chops and production engineering experience is unusual. He's not assembling pre-built components; he's someone who understands the models underneath. Rodriguez complements him well on the sales side -- closing $1 million in revenue and landing Chubb as a client at Domu is a real credential for someone who was 23 at the time. She relocated to San Francisco with $3,000 to start this company, which is the kind of financial commitment that tells you something about conviction.

The bull case is that commercial lending servicing is a genuine pocket of schlep blindness in a multi-billion-dollar market, attacked by founders with relevant operational experience and a capable technical co-founder, at a moment when lenders are ready to adopt AI. The $6,000 in revenue within four weeks is a small number but a meaningful signal in B2B fintech, where sales cycles to financial institutions typically run months. Getting any commercial lender to pay you money in a month suggests they had warm relationships from their Domu days and the pain is acute enough for fast adoption. The Domu founders investing is also a positive signal -- these are people who worked alongside Rodriguez and Montes and chose to back them with their own money.

Where I hesitate is the technical defensibility. They're building on OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude -- commercially available models. The workflow integration and domain-specific data accumulation could eventually create a moat, but right now the barrier to a well-funded competitor replicating the core product is moderate. Salient expanding from consumer collections into commercial servicing is a plausible threat, and they have $70 million and $25 million in ARR as a running start. The question is whether Zolvo can accumulate enough customer-specific data and workflow depth in the commercial lending niche before someone bigger notices the opportunity. That's a race, and $500K of YC funding gives you limited runway to win it.

This is a company I'd watch carefully but wouldn't write a personal check for today. The schlep blindness pattern is real -- this problem has been hiding in plain sight, and the competitive dynamics favor a focused startup over incumbents with channel conflict. The founders have relevant experience and the CTO can genuinely build. But the organic discovery is one degree removed from personal pain, the early traction is too thin to confirm user pull, and the technical moat depends on execution speed rather than proprietary technology. If they come back in three months with $50K MRR and five commercial lenders who can't live without the product, that changes the picture significantly.
