# ARC Prize Foundation -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: this is the AI establishment, not the outsiders. When OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI have all adopted your benchmark, when Sam Altman is doing a fireside chat at your launch party at YC, when your donors include the co-founder of DoorDash and the CEO of Box — you're not the weird kid in the corner that nobody believes in. You're the popular kid. And I've learned through painful experience and wonderful experience that the most promising companies are the ones nobody wants to fund, the ones that seem ridiculous. ARC Prize Foundation is the opposite of that. It's credentialed, respected, and consensus before it even goes through YC. That's exactly the signal that makes me step back.

When I think about François Chollet and Mike Knoop, I see two extraordinarily accomplished people. Chollet created Keras — a framework used by millions of developers — and wrote the foundational paper on measuring intelligence. Knoop built Zapier to $310M in revenue. These are serious, impressive people. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is an elite French engineering education, a prestigious Google career, and a successful YC exit. I don't hear the survival story. I don't hear the hardship that makes this problem personal in the way that Travis Kalanick's taxi medallion obsession was personal, or the way the Crusoe team's discovery of wasted gas in North Dakota was born from something they'd encountered in the real world and couldn't let go of. Chollet's connection to ARC-AGI is intellectual — he wrote a paper in 2019 about how we should measure intelligence. That's a brilliant academic contribution, but it's not the kind of lived-experience founder-problem bond that I've learned to trust as my primary signal.

I also have to be honest about something structural: this is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. I invest my own money. I take extraordinary risk because I believe in asymmetric upside — the possibility that a weird little company becomes a $3 billion infrastructure play, like Crusoe did. With a nonprofit, there is no upside. There's no equity, no return, no participation in the value created. I deeply respect the mission of creating credible, independent benchmarks for AI progress. The world needs this. But my framework is built around backing founders with personal checks and sharing in what they build. A donation is a different calculus entirely, and it's not the kind of bet I'm set up to evaluate or make.

The strongest bull case is genuinely compelling, and I want to honor it: if artificial general intelligence is the most important technological transition in human history, then the institution that defines how we measure progress toward it could be as foundational as NIST or IEEE. Chollet may be the single most qualified person alive to build that institution. The coordination problem — AI labs can't credibly benchmark themselves — creates real structural positioning for a neutral nonprofit. And the team's self-funding through 2024 shows genuine personal commitment. If I squint, this looks like infrastructure at a technological inflection point, which is a pattern I've invested in before with SpaceX and Crusoe. But SpaceX had equity upside, and Elon Musk's story — the immigrant kid from South Africa who nearly went bankrupt three times to get rockets to fly — had that biographical survival quality that Chollet's career at Google doesn't carry.

What concerns me beyond the consensus problem and the nonprofit structure is the attention split and the conflict of interest. Chollet and Knoop are simultaneously co-founding Ndea, a for-profit AI lab building the very kind of system that ARC-AGI benchmarks are designed to measure. That's the people who make the ruler also making the thing being measured. No public governance policy addresses this separation. After HQ Trivia taught me how badly things can go wrong when structural tensions between founders aren't resolved, I've become very attuned to these kinds of misalignment risks — and this one sits at the organizational level, not just the interpersonal one.

This is a company I respect but cannot get behind as an angel investment. The founders are solving an important intellectual problem, but there's no biographical grit story connecting them to it through personal struggle, no anti-consensus weirdness to lean into, no economic access creation for underserved people, and no equity structure that lets me participate in the outcome. The AI benchmarking space is real and important, but it doesn't activate any of the signals I've learned to trust.
