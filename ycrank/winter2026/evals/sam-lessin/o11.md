# o11 -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The Product Hunt tagline tells you everything: "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works." I don't even need to read past that line. This company is self-identifying as a feature improvement on the incumbent's own product, built as a third-party add-in inside the incumbent's platform, competing for the incumbent's customers. This is the most perfectly distilled example of what I've been warning about for two years. Microsoft has 400 million paid Office 365 seats. They ship Copilot at $30/user/month. They control the add-in APIs, the AppSource distribution, and the entire platform surface o11 lives on. Building here is renting a room inside a landlord's house and then advertising that your room is nicer than his. Even if that's true today, the landlord has the keys.

The founders deserve credit separated from the idea. Ajay Misra leading AI at Cal AI -- a product that reached $40M ARR, 10M users, and got acquired by MyFitnessPal -- is a real credential. That's operational proof he can ship AI products that find scale. Aryah Oztanir as a 19-year-old Morehead-Cain scholar with multiple prior startups shows energy and ambition beyond his years. These are competent people. But competent people pursuing an obvious idea is precisely NOT my investment filter. "Sane person, insane idea" requires the idea to provoke genuine skepticism. "Let's make AI work better inside Office for finance professionals" provokes zero skepticism -- it provokes a thousand competitors. Gamma is at $100M ARR. Plus AI does basically the same thing. Microsoft itself is iterating Copilot quarterly. This is a consensus play in the most crowded AI category imaginable.

Run my AI kill test on this and the result is immediate: AI doesn't just threaten this business -- AI improvement by incumbents IS the threat vector. Every time OpenAI ships a better model and Microsoft integrates it deeper into Copilot, o11's differentiation narrows. The "unlimited context" claim is a technical capability that improves with every model generation -- for everyone, not just o11. The finance-specific chart types and Excel auditing are features, not structural moats. The in-app workflow lock-in from custom templates is real but thin -- switching an Office add-in takes minutes, not months. There is no physical infrastructure here, no regulatory position, no protocol that others build on. The software IS the business, which is exactly the category I've argued is getting destroyed. When I backed Craftwork, the moat was painting crews and scheduling operations that no amount of software could replicate. Here, the moat is... better prompting? Deeper API integration? Microsoft can match both with a product update.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Microsoft Copilot will remain inadequate for finance power users for multiple years, that o11 can build enterprise-grade security and compliance (SOC 2, data residency) with two people fast enough to close Wells Fargo and Bank of America as paying accounts, and that neither Gamma nor any well-funded competitor decides to build the same in-app add-in strategy. I'll grant that Microsoft has historically been slow to optimize for vertical use cases -- their incentive is horizontal adoption. And the pricing wedge ($20/mo vs $30/mo for Copilot) creates real room. If o11 could become the de facto financial modeling and presentation layer inside every investment bank's Office deployment before Microsoft closes the gap, that's a venture-scale outcome. But "racing to build a feature before the platform owner ships it" is not an investment thesis I can underwrite. The Birchbox lesson applies here in reverse: where Birchbox had narrative without moat, o11 has traction without structural defensibility. Both end the same way.

The featured logos -- Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Yale, Mayo Clinic -- likely represent individual users on free or Pro tiers, not enterprise contracts. A two-person team without SOC 2 certification is not closing enterprise procurement at tier-one financial institutions. I give the founders credit for generating real usage -- "hundreds of companies," 150 ratings at 4.8/5, solid Product Hunt launches -- but usage on a freemium add-in is qualitatively different from contractual enterprise revenue. The reference to "Series funding in March" on LinkedIn suggests momentum, but momentum in an AI feature company is fragile the moment the platform owner ships an update.

This is a pass. Not because the founders lack capability -- Ajay Misra in particular has shipped at real scale -- but because the structural logic of the business contradicts everything I believe about where value accrues in the AI era. An application-layer AI tool, on someone else's platform, competing with the platform owner's own product, in the most consensus category in technology, with no physical operations, no protocol position, and no moat that survives the next Microsoft product cycle. I'd rather back these founders building something genuinely weird.
