# Didit -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin of this idea is the most interesting thing about Didit, and it follows a pattern I've seen produce good companies. The founders weren't trying to build a KYC business. They were building Gamium, a Web3 social platform, and they needed identity verification to make it work. They looked at existing providers, presumably found them expensive and fragmented, and built their own identity layer from scratch. Then they realized the infrastructure they'd built was more valuable than the platform it was supposed to serve. This is organic discovery. It's how some of the best companies start -- you build something for yourself, and it turns out everyone else needs it too. The idea wasn't conceived in a brainstorming session about hot markets. It grew out of a real construction project.

The schlep here is enormous and genuine. Supporting 14,000 government-issued document types across 190 countries. Building deepfake and spoofing detection from scratch. Getting ISO 27001, 27017, 27018 certifications and iBeta Level 1 accreditation. Dealing with AML regulations across different jurisdictions. This is exactly the kind of ugly, tedious, regulatory complexity that makes smart engineers unconsciously look the other way. But here's where Didit departs from the Stripe analogy that might seem obvious: the problem isn't hiding in plain sight. Everyone can see identity verification is a mess. Persona has raised $417 million. Veriff has raised $200 million. Onfido was acquired for $650 million. The schlep is real, but this isn't schlep blindness -- the opportunity is visible and well-capitalized competitors are already in the room. What Didit is betting on is that the incumbents' pricing model -- charging premium per-verification fees from check one -- creates a structural opening for a free-tier model they can't easily match without cannibalizing themselves. That's a real insight, similar to how open-source companies attack proprietary software vendors.

The co-founders are twins who have been building together since age 12. I cannot overstate how much this matters. I've written about how cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate, and I've seen nine companies in a single batch fall apart because the founders didn't really know each other. Alberto and Alejandro Rosas have the deepest possible co-founder bond -- they're genetically identical humans who grew up building projects together, both became professional tennis players, both studied mathematics, and both transitioned into software engineering. Alberto's GitHub demonstrates genuine ML craft: a StyleGAN2 implementation with 143 stars is not a weekend project. Alejandro built AI systems at Oracle. These are technical founders who can build -- and did build -- the core product themselves. The full identity verification, biometrics, liveness, and fraud detection stack was constructed from scratch, which at this price point and feature breadth reflects real engineering intensity.

The traction, if the self-reported numbers are accurate, is remarkable for a company that has raised only $2 million. Seven hundred B2B customers with 20% month-over-month growth. Millions of monthly verifications. 300% net revenue retention by month six. Near-profitable at YC entry. These are not pre-seed metrics -- they're closer to Series A metrics achieved on a pre-seed budget. The concern is that every one of these numbers comes from the company's own blog and YC page with no independent verification. The external signals -- 16 Trustpilot reviews, 458 LinkedIn followers, 6 iOS app ratings, 10 GitHub stars on the demo repo -- feel thin relative to "1,000+ companies" and "millions of monthly verifications." B2B infrastructure companies often have weak public footprints, so this isn't necessarily contradictory, but I'd want to verify these metrics directly before writing a check.

The Web3 origin is the thing that gives me pause. The GitHub organization is still called "didit-protocol." There's a whitepaper. Token holders were offered airdrops tied to Didit's growth. Enterprise procurement teams doing due diligence will find a metaverse token project in the background. The founders navigated a smart pivot -- from Web3 identity infrastructure to B2B identity verification SaaS -- but the artifacts of the prior life linger. This is a real credibility risk when you're selling compliance infrastructure to regulated financial institutions. The best case is that they clean this up aggressively and the Web3 phase becomes just an origin story. The worst case is that enterprise buyers see it and choose Persona instead. The competitive pressure from well-funded incumbents compounds this: Persona is explicitly positioning itself for AI-agent identity verification with $417 million in backing. Didit's pricing advantage is real, but in enterprise compliance, cheapest doesn't always win -- regulated buyers often want to pay more for perceived safety.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If the traction metrics are real, these founders have built a near-profitable B2B infrastructure business with 700+ customers on $2 million in funding while competing against companies with 100x more capital. The organic discovery from Gamium is authentic. The schlep is massive and they've done it. The co-founder bond is as strong as any I've seen. The free-tier pricing model creates a structural advantage that incumbents can't easily match. This has the shape of something that could work -- capital-efficient founders with technical depth attacking an incumbent market from below on price. What holds me back from higher conviction is the competitive density and the unverified metrics. This isn't a hidden opportunity waiting for someone brave enough to tackle it. It's a visible, well-funded market where Didit's edge is execution efficiency and pricing disruption rather than the kind of insight-driven moat I look for in my strongest bets.
