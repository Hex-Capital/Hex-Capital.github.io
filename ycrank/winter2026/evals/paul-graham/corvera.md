# Corvera -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is the best part. Christopher Kong spent six years running Better Nature, a tempeh brand, across 5,000 retail locations in six countries. He didn't study the CPG software market and identify a gap. He lived inside the operational nightmare — processing orders from retailers who each send differently formatted PDFs, managing inventory across multiple warehouses, trying to forecast demand for physical products that spoil. When someone spends six years doing something painful and then builds software to make it stop hurting, that's organic problem discovery. It's the same pattern as Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, except Kong's version lasted six years and spanned six countries.

The schlep is also genuine, though not invisible. CPG supply chain operations involve parsing heterogeneous documents — emails, PDFs, EDI feeds — from hundreds of different retailers and suppliers, each with their own formatting quirks. It involves coordinating physical inventory across warehouse locations, dealing with the messy reality of goods that move on trucks and sit on shelves. Most AI startups right now are building copilots for knowledge workers in clean digital environments. Corvera is trying to autonomously execute supply chain operations where a single wrong order quantity damages a brand's relationship with Tesco. That's a harder problem, and the difficulty deters competition from the AI-native side. But here's where my schlep blindness framework only partially applies: this problem isn't hiding in plain sight the way payments were before Stripe. Crisp has raised $127M+. Unioncrate has been in CPG supply chain AI since 2016. Settle has raised over $500M. These companies occupy adjacent territory and could move toward autonomous execution. The problem isn't that nobody sees this — it's that the specific segment (sub-$500M CPG brands needing end-to-end automation, not just dashboards) hasn't been well-served. That's a real opening, but it's a narrower one than true schlep blindness creates.

What I'm looking for and not finding is the technical hacker signal. Kong is the operator-founder with deep domain knowledge, which is valuable. But his background is biochemistry and brand-building, not engineering. Breeuwer, the CTO, led data and AI work at Google and built multi-agent workflows there — relevant experience, and his 25 public GitHub repos suggest someone who actually writes code. Collins has a Princeton CS degree but his career has been in product management, not hands-on engineering. This is a competent technical team, not a team of hackers who couldn't stop themselves from building this. The difference matters. When the Collison brothers built Stripe, they were programmers building tools for programmers — they could evaluate every technical decision from the inside. Corvera's founding team has the right composition on paper (operator + AI engineer + product person), but I don't see evidence of the kind of technical obsessiveness that produces products people love rather than products that work adequately.

The bull case is straightforward and worth stating clearly. Kong's network of CPG founders is a genuine distribution advantage — he can pick up the phone and call people who trust him, who share his pain, and who will try his product because they know he understands their world. Fifteen design partners at pre-seed, before having a polished product, suggests this network is already working. If Corvera can convert even a third of those design partners into paying customers and demonstrate that autonomous order processing actually works reliably, the switching costs become real because the system sits in the order-to-cash workflow. CPG brands that hand over their operations to an AI system and it works won't switch back to spreadsheets. The question is whether they can reach that level of reliability with a four-person team before well-funded competitors like Crisp add similar capabilities to their existing 7,000-brand install base.

The traction signals are thin but appropriately so for pre-seed. Fifteen design partners and fifty-plus brands in pipeline tell me that people in CPG are willing to talk to Kong and try what he's building. Eight logos on the website. But there's no revenue data, no testimony about user dependency, and "design partner" is one of those terms that can mean anything from "actively using the product daily" to "agreed to a call next month." I'd want to know: are any of these brands actually processing real orders through Corvera today? Because if even three of them are, and they'd be genuinely disrupted if the product disappeared tomorrow, that changes my calculus significantly. Without that evidence, I'm left with a well-connected founder who has earned the right meetings but hasn't yet proven that the product delivers on its ambitious promise of hands-free operations.

This is a team with genuine founder-market fit building in a real problem space, but I'm not seeing enough signal to write a personal check. The organic discovery is strong — among the strongest I've seen in this batch for that specific dimension. But the competitive landscape is crowded, the technical founding team is competent rather than exceptional, and the "AI agents for X" framing, while grounded in real pain in Kong's case, is also the most consensus category in 2025-2026. When I said the most impressive companies I've seen recently aren't working on AI, this is partly what I meant — not that AI is bad, but that "AI agent for [vertical]" has become the default idea shape, and default ideas face default competition.
