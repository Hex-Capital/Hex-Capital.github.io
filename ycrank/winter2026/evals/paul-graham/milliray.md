# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Milliray is that the CEO's PhD thesis is literally the product. Matthew Moore spent years in a St Andrews lab building 94 GHz Doppler radar systems and publishing papers on drone micro-Doppler signatures. That's unusual. Most academic spin-outs have a gap between the research and the company -- the founders have to translate their work into something someone would buy. Here, the translation distance is nearly zero. Moore didn't study adjacent physics and then pivot toward drones. He built the exact radar, at the exact frequency, for the exact application. That level of founder-technology fusion is rare and it matters, because it means the core technical risk has already been partially retired inside a university lab.

But here's where I hesitate. The origin of this idea is a research program, not a personal encounter with the problem. Moore didn't have drones buzzing his critical infrastructure. He was assigned or chose a research topic in the millimetre wave group at St Andrews. That's different from Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the answer is "through academic inquiry" rather than "they couldn't stop thinking about it because they lived with it every day." University spin-outs can work -- they sometimes produce the deepest technical moats -- but the motivation structure is different from organic discovery, and it worries me in ways that are hard to articulate. The best founders are pulled toward a problem by something almost gravitational. Research commercialization can feel more like pushing.

The schlep, though, is genuinely massive. Building millimeter-wave radar hardware at 94 GHz requires specialized RF engineering that you can't hire off the street. Then you have to sell it to defense ministries and NATO procurement offices, which means multi-year sales cycles, security clearances, export controls, and the kind of bureaucratic endurance that makes most founders' eyes glaze over. This is exactly the kind of layered difficulty that creates schlep blindness -- not one hard thing, but five hard things stacked on top of each other. Thousands of engineers know small drones are a growing threat. Very few want to simultaneously solve the hardware physics, the signal processing, the defense procurement, and the manufacturing scale-up. That structural reluctance is a real moat, arguably more durable than any patent.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The timing argument is strong -- possibly the strongest I've seen for a defense startup at this stage. Over a billion dollars in counter-drone purchases closed in December 2025 alone. Ukraine is experiencing 1,600+ drone attacks monthly. NATO DIANA was specifically created to fund companies like this. If Milliray's millimeter-wave approach genuinely detects nano-drones that Robin Radar and Blighter miss at lower frequencies -- and the published research suggests this is physically plausible -- then they're not competing with DroneShield's $216M revenue machine so much as filling a capability gap that the incumbents cannot close without fundamental hardware redesign. The sovereign European angle matters too: UK and EU defense buyers increasingly want non-US suppliers. A technically superior, UK-based, NATO-backed radar company entering this market at exactly the moment budgets are exploding -- that's a scenario where a $500K bet returns 100x.

But I have two concrete concerns that prevent me from investing. First, the CTO is listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, an ion trap quantum computing venture. In a three-person startup, your CTO running a second deep-tech company isn't a minor scheduling conflict. It's a fundamental question about commitment. I've seen what happens when co-founders aren't all-in -- nine companies in one YC batch added uncommitted co-founders and every single one fell apart. I'm not saying Doherty is uncommitted, but the signal isn't good. Second, there is zero evidence of user pull. "Deployment-ready" is a claim on a website, not a customer pulling the product toward them. DASA and NATO DIANA are validation programs, not procurement contracts. At pre-seed I don't expect much traction, but I want some sign that a real buyer is leaning in -- a letter of intent, a paid pilot, even an email from a defense procurement officer saying "when can we test this?" I see institutional recognition of the founders' credentials, which is not the same thing as demand for the product.

The technical team is genuinely impressive -- three PhDs covering radar hardware, optics, and machine learning, all with Royal Academy of Engineering fellowships. Moore built the actual radar himself during his PhD research. That checks the "hacker founders who build" test as strongly as any deep-tech company I've seen. But technical excellence in a lab and commercial execution in defense procurement are different animals, and I see no evidence yet that this team has made the transition from researchers to founders who will do whatever it takes -- the unscalable, uncomfortable, door-to-door work of selling radar systems to military buyers.
