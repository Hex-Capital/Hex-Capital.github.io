# Concorda -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Concorda's $204K in annualized revenue within three months of operation with a two-person team is a legitimate early traction signal -- real law firms writing real checks. But that number needs to be read against the denominator: Harvey is at $190M ARR and serves eight of the top ten US law firms. Legal AI funding hit $4.3B in 2025, up 54% year-over-year. AI adoption among legal professionals surged from 19% to 79% in two years. This is not a stuck market waiting for someone to crack it open. This is a market that cracked open three years ago, and the players who were early -- Harvey, EvenUp at $2B+, Thomson Reuters with the federal judiciary contract -- already occupy the positions that matter. When I wrote my seed check into Harvey in 2022, legal AI was a graveyard and the GPT capability curve was about to cross a precision threshold that consensus hadn't recognized. Concorda is entering in 2026, when that same structural shift is fully priced in. The litigation-specific vertical angle has some logic, but Crimson, also YC-backed, is building a near-identical "AI associate for litigation lawyers" and already has A&O Shearman as a design partner. The nonobvious window here is closed.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Concorda falls furthest short of my framework. Concorda's four modules -- Case Command, Motions & Briefs, Discovery, Claim Charts -- are application-layer tools that lawyers use directly. No other businesses build ON Concorda. There's no API, no platform for third-party developers, no evidence that Concorda becomes a layer that compounds switching costs through network effects or integration depth. Compare this to Stripe, which didn't just process payments but became the payments API that thousands of companies embedded in their core architecture. Concorda's firm-specific voice models could create some stickiness over time, but that's learned personalization, not infrastructure lock-in. Harvey, with its broader platform covering contracts, compliance, due diligence, and litigation, is closer to an infrastructure layer for law firms than Concorda's litigation-specific application.

Samuel Oh's combination of Harvard CS and UChicago Law, plus Ke Ma's Northwestern JD and McKinsey operational experience alongside her Cook County ASA role, gives Concorda genuine founder-market fit for the litigation domain. Oh's quote -- "I spent years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way" -- reflects lived experience with the problem. These are lawyer-engineers building for a workflow they've personally executed, which I value. But neither founder shows evidence of operating at a company during hypergrowth, founding a prior startup, or building and scaling a team beyond two people. The dual services/SaaS model demands simultaneous execution across client delivery, product development, sales, and compliance -- that's a heavy operational load for two people with no visible technical co-founder or engineering team.

The services-plus-SaaS hybrid model is the structural concern that most activates my margin architecture framework. Concorda explicitly offers "AI-native litigation services" alongside platform access. Legal services are inherently low-margin, carry professional liability and malpractice exposure, and require state bar compliance that pure SaaS competitors avoid entirely. The $204K annualized revenue is impressive, but the dossier provides no breakdown between services and software revenue. If most of that $204K comes from services, the margin profile looks fundamentally different than if it's platform subscriptions. My High Growth Handbook principle is clear: high-margin, negative-churn businesses with good acquisition funnels win. Services revenue doesn't compound, doesn't scale without headcount, and doesn't command software multiples.

The strongest bull case for Concorda requires believing that litigation execution is sufficiently distinct from general legal AI that Harvey's breadth creates a durable opening for a depth-first competitor. Law firms do buy specialized tools alongside horizontal platforms -- they use both Westlaw and niche litigation software today. If Concorda's end-to-end case management (evidence mapping through USPTO integration) delivers materially better outcomes than Harvey's litigation module, and if the services wedge converts to SaaS retention, there's a path to a defensible vertical position. The 79% legal AI adoption rate means the buyer education problem is solved. But for this to work, Concorda needs Harvey to remain a generalist AND needs to win against Crimson in the litigation-specific niche AND needs to manage the liability exposure of its services model. That's at least two independent distribution and execution miracles beyond the technology foundation that's available to everyone. My single-miracle test says companies needing simultaneous breakthroughs in distribution against an entrenched incumbent AND regulatory/liability navigation face compounding failure probabilities that the early revenue signal doesn't resolve.

My pass is driven primarily by two dimensions. First, nonobvious market timing: legal AI in 2026 is the most consensus AI vertical in enterprise software, and the litigation sub-niche is already contested by Crimson and addressable by Harvey's expansion. There is no structural shift that Concorda is uniquely positioned to exploit that others have missed. Second, infrastructure-layer positioning: Concorda is building an end-user application, not a platform that other businesses depend on, which limits the compounding dynamics I look for in category-defining investments. The early revenue is real but doesn't override the structural analysis -- demand for legal AI tools is exactly what you'd expect in a market where adoption jumped from 19% to 79%.
