# Asendia AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The pivot tells me everything. Asendia launched twice on Product Hunt as a consumer job coaching product -- "Sarah, your AI Job Coach" -- earning respectable upvotes both times. Then they showed up at YC as a B2B agentic recruitment platform for staffing agencies. Now, a pivot can signal learning rate. I've seen founders who discovered the real opportunity sitting behind their initial product and had the courage to abandon what was working for what could be transformational. But that's not what I see here. I see a team that built a consumer product, couldn't find a business model, and pivoted to enterprise because that's where the revenue is. There's no evidence of B2B traction post-pivot -- no named agency customers, no revenue signal, no LOIs. They went from one consensus category (AI career coaching) to another consensus category (AI recruitment tooling), and neither represents the kind of problem whose consequence of success justifies extraordinary effort.

Let me be direct about the consequence magnitude. If Asendia AI fully succeeds -- captures a dominant share of the AI recruitment tool market for staffing agencies -- what changes in the world? Staffing agencies operate more efficiently. Recruiters save five hours a day. Fill rates improve. This is a perfectly reasonable business, and it is exactly the kind of investment I pass on. The AI recruitment market is $656 million growing at 7.2% CAGR. Even the more generous framing -- $1.7 billion for AI interview assistants -- describes a market where total dominance yields a mid-sized outcome. Nobody's life changes. No trillion-dollar system gets disrupted. Staffing agencies still exist, they just use better software. The company has narrowed its ambition to the point where success is manageable but inconsequential at the scale I care about.

The technology reinforces this concern. Asendia advertises four AI agents -- sourcing, SMS outreach, video interviewing, phone screening -- plus 50+ ATS integrations, all maintained by a three-person team. The dossier itself acknowledges the core issue: "the moat depends on execution speed, agency-specific workflow design, and ATS integration depth rather than proprietary AI." When your differentiation is integration breadth and workflow design rather than fundamental technology, you're in a feature war. Every foundation model API improvement benefits your competitors equally. hireEZ has 800 million candidate profiles. Humanly acquired three companies in 2025 to expand its platform. Workday bought Paradox. You're building a better mousetrap in a neighborhood where the cat is already moving in. This is the opposite of Juniper, where TCP/IP routing made Cisco's architecture structurally obsolete. Asendia doesn't make anything obsolete -- it automates existing steps in an existing workflow using commoditized AI capabilities.

The strongest bull case is timing and structural gap. Paradox's acquisition by Workday genuinely removes the largest independent player and creates an opening. Bullhorn and other staffing ATS incumbents face business model cannibalization if they bundle AI agents at no additional cost, which creates a structural window for a purpose-built AI layer. If Asendia can establish itself as the default AI middleware for mid-market staffing agencies during this window, the business could reach meaningful scale before incumbents respond. The founders' enterprise backgrounds -- Google Cloud, Microsoft -- give them credibility in B2B sales conversations that many YC teams lack. And the CTO's prior product (FAANG AI) demonstrates he can ship AI-powered recruitment tools. If the Paradox gap persists longer than expected, and if Asendia's multi-agent approach creates genuine switching costs through ATS integration depth, this could become a solid $100-200 million outcome. But solid outcomes in known markets with commoditized technology are precisely what I avoid. The probability of success might be higher than my typical investment, and the consequence of success is correspondingly smaller.

The founders are capable engineers from big tech applying established AI techniques to a known industry. That's the anti-signal. They're insiders -- Google, Microsoft, Infineon, TU Munich -- executing a playbook that the market already understands. I look for the biochemistry professor who doesn't know what the food industry considers impossible, or the New Zealander who doesn't know rockets are supposed to be built by aerospace veterans. What I see here are two experienced engineers building exactly what you'd expect experienced engineers to build: a well-integrated, multi-feature SaaS platform. No one would call this crazy. No expert would dismiss it as impossible. And that absence of friction is itself the warning sign.
