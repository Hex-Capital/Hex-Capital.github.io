# Zibra Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a sane-team-sane-idea infrastructure bet where the founders' best credential — building Ray Core at Anyscale — is also the exact reason the moat doesn't compound: they are pitching me a vertical wrapper around an open-source protocol they personally helped commoditize. Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer are real distributed-systems engineers (Ray Core, LinkedIn Venice, VMware) and they are competent on every technical axis I can measure. But "faster spot-instance scheduler for quant backtests" is not a primitive, it is not contrarian, and the AI Kill Test isn't even an AI question here — it's an "Anyscale Kill Test," and the answer is uncomfortable.

On Primitive Creation — the dimension where Zibra Labs sits lowest — the ladder runs from Venmo at the top (peer-to-peer payments became a verb) through Slack in the middle (real-time messaging as a genuine replacement primitive for email) down to "better CRM / AI-wrapper-for-industry-X" at the bottom, which is execution inside an existing market. Zibra Labs sits one rung above the floor. The dossier itself frames the company against QuantConnect's 16GB cap, Coiled's $26M Dask-on-cloud business, and QuantRocket's self-hosted Python — that is a comp set, not a primitive. "HPC clusters of 100–50,000 nodes, up to 6.4M parallel tasks" is a feature spec, not a new behavior. Nobody is going to say "I Zibra'd that strategy" the way they say "Venmo me." This is faster horsepower in a category that already has horsepower vendors.

On Consensus Mispricing — the dimension that triggers my entry point and where I score Zibra Labs near the bottom — the ladder runs from Solana at four cents (Ethereum-won consensus had priced it at zero) through Slack (hot deal, primitive override) down to AI copilot wrappers in 2025 where every Sand Hill firm is bidding. Zibra Labs is tagged on the YC page with "Artificial Intelligence, Fintech, Cloud Computing, Infrastructure" — that is literally four of the five hottest categories in the Spring 2026 batch. There is no rejection signal, no category discomfort, no "what if they're right?" There is only "what if they're faster?" That is a quality question, not a pricing question, and quality questions are how you lose money in venture.

On Structural Durability, the ladder runs from Solana (protocol consensus, network effects) through Metropolis (physical parking infrastructure AI cannot replicate) down to AI writing tools an Adobe ships next quarter. Zibra Labs sits at the low end with one mitigating factor. Ray is open source, 42.4k GitHub stars, and the upstream project is commercialized by Anyscale — the founders' former employer. AWS has already published a Coiled-on-AWS reference architecture for backtesting workloads. The mitigating factor is that quant backtesting is genuinely latency-sensitive and spot-orchestration is hard, but "hard engineering" is not a moat when the people best positioned to replicate you are your old team.

On Sanity-Insanity Calibration, the ladder runs from Raj at Solana (sane engineer pitching consensus-rejected protocol) through Butterfield at Slack (proven, sane, but messaging wasn't insane) down to networking-event co-founders with synthetic relationships. Rabbani and Policzer are clearly sane — Ray Core, Venice, real artifacts on GitHub, plausibly co-workers at Anyscale per both bios. The problem is the idea is also sane. "Quant funds need more backtest compute" is the most reasonable sentence I'll read this week. Sane person + sane idea = a respectable engineering services company, not a Slow check. On Capital Discipline I have minimal evidence, but a 2-person team with 0 YC job postings and infra-flavored economics suggests they treat dollars carefully — I'll give them the benefit of the default.

My 36-month forecast for Zibra Labs: acqui-hire by Anyscale or Coiled at $15–40M for the engineering team, or a pivot off quant-specific positioning into general AI training/inference batch infrastructure once they discover that hedge funds buy infra from people with quant pedigrees, not Ray pedigrees. The mechanism is straightforward — neither founder has a documented hour at Citadel, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, or Jane Street, and the Bridgewater/Two Sigma name-drops on zibralabs.ai are Ray users, not Zibra customers. Quant infra sales is a relationship business and the relationships aren't there.

The strongest bull case against my forecast: vertical specialization beats horizontal cloud batch, hedge funds are the most cost- and latency-sensitive buyers in tech, and a focused quant-native scheduler that wins five tier-one funds is a $30–80M ARR business with absurd gross margins and pricing power. The dossier's "hundreds of signals per day" framing is real — AI-generated alpha research has genuinely shifted the bottleneck to backtest compute. That's the steel-man, and it stings because vertical infrastructure done right is exactly the kind of physical-feeling moat I usually like. But it doesn't win against my framework: the buyers are exactly the buyers Anyscale already sells to, and Zibra Labs has zero disclosed customers, zero patents, zero quant-domain credibility, and zero pricing-page evidence of how they monetize. The flip condition is concrete: signed contracts with two or more tier-one quant funds (Citadel / Two Sigma / DE Shaw / Jane Street / Hudson River) at $1M+ ARR each within twelve months. Until then, this is Adobe-for-AI logic in quant-infra clothing. Pass.
