# Parrot -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Phillip Baek's resume says BME at UT Austin and EP Mapping Specialist at Boston Scientific; Liam McBride's says UIUC computer engineering and life-sciences computer vision at Alchemy AI. Nowhere in the Parrot dossier does anyone say "I grew up in my uncle's body shop in Bakersfield" or "I worked the phones at a State Farm supplement desk for two summers." That absence is the whole evaluation. Parrot is a clean-governance, technically capable duo who picked collision repair the way you pick a vertical from a Notion doc -- and a clean CEO line over an analytical founder-market origin is exactly the dimensional signature that pattern-matches to my Pass Example, not to my portfolio.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the high rung on founder-as-origin -- he was soldering VR headsets in a trailer before he ever wrote a defense memo, and the body of work *preceded* the company. Parrot's analogue would be a founder who had spent five years inside CCC ONE or estimating supplements at a 20-bay MSO; instead the prior co-venture between Phillip Baek and Liam McBride was Alchemy AI in life-sciences computer vision, and the LinkedIn titles still say "Alchemy" rather than Parrot. That is the rebrand of a pivot, not the surfacing of a problem the founders have been carrying around since their twenties. Supplement negotiation lives in state-specific insurance labor-rate disputes; I cannot find the moment in the Parrot dossier where either founder *felt* that pain firsthand, and silence here reads as "discovered in a memo."

Phillip Baek as named CEO with Liam McBride as named CTO, with a documented prior collaboration at Alchemy AI, is the cleanest thing about Parrot. This is the inverse of the HQ Trivia (2017) Yusupov/Kroll structure that later imploded -- Parrot has a delineation, not a 50/50 with no tiebreaker, and the two have already survived one company together. I do not get to penalize a pre-seed for not yet having a documented vesting-conflict protocol, so this dimension scores in the upper band; it is the one criterion where Parrot meaningfully clears the bar.

Crusoe (~2018) burning gas flares for Bitcoin in North Dakota was magically weird because two or three credible VCs had passed on aesthetic grounds and the founder was *of* the oil patch. Parrot's "AI-native operating system for auto shops" is unsexy in the right direction -- collision repair is a category most YC founders would rather die than touch -- but the wrapper is the most consensus pitch shape of Spring 2026, and Rote (YC) is selling the supplement-negotiation slice into the exact same buyer. When two YC companies in adjacent batches converge on "AI agents for body shops," the round is not underpriced and the category has stopped being a secret. Parrot is unsexy without being weird.

Collision repair plus insurance supplements *is* the unsexy-regulated lane I fund -- state labor-rate disputes, CCC ONE API gating, adjuster relationships -- and this is the dimension where Parrot has more thesis fit than its founders' backgrounds would predict. The mechanism that compounds here over 18 months is the proprietary corpus of supplement negotiations and parts-vendor call data; if Parrot accumulates that across 50 shops, the data moat starts to look real. The mechanism that fails is that CCC, Mitchell, and Solera Audatex sit on the API spigot and are themselves shipping AI features per Autobody News 2026, which means Parrot's "integrates with" posture is also "exists at the pleasure of." This is closer to Vigil Labs in regulated finance than to SpaceX, and without hardware capex the moat is contingent on data accumulation Parrot has not yet earned.

Flock Safety (~2018) I sourced by reading the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons -- the artifact was in the world, photographable on a telephone pole. Parrot's product is a voice agent inside a body shop's phone system; I would have to call a participating shop and listen for the bot to verify it exists. What I can verify is the 90-day pilot numbers -- $150K to $180K monthly revenue, supplement turnaround 5 days to 2 days, $34K supplement dollars processed per month -- and those are tactile in the sense that the cars actually got fixed faster. But it is one shop, 90 days, and the dossier reads "single-pilot evidence base," which is closer to founder testimony than to artifact-in-the-wild.

In 36 months Parrot lands in one of two places: acqui-hired into CCC ONE, Mitchell, or Solera Audatex for $10M-$30M as a feature tuck once the incumbents decide voice-agent middleware is table stakes, or under $2M ARR and dying in a knife fight with Rote and an in-house Better Collision Centers build. The mechanism producing this forecast is the founder-origin gap: Phillip Baek and Liam McBride can ship the software, but the buyer relationships and the regulatory intuition for state-by-state labor-rate disputes are owned by people who have lived in collision repair for fifteen years, and incumbents will outflank a generic AI-agent wrapper on relationships once they decide to.

The strongest case against the pass is the pilot itself. A single 90-day deployment moving a shop from $150K to $180K monthly revenue with supplement turnaround halved is not a deck slide -- it is dollars that landed in a shop owner's account. HandUp (~2014) is the exception I would reach for, where I overrode commercial defensibility for personal mission alignment, but Phillip Baek and Liam McBride do not have that biographical hook with collision repair. DeepMind (~2013) is the polymath exception, but Parrot's founders are competent specialists in adjacent fields, not Demis-Hassabis-grade cross-domain compounders. The pilot stings because the numbers are real, but one shop is one shop, and the dossier names zero collision-industry advisors, zero MSO operators on the cap table, zero insurance-adjuster hires. The verdict flips if Parrot lands five paying MSO chains within twelve months with named CCC-certified integration partnerships and a co-founder or VP added from the body-shop world -- short of that, I am watching from the curb.
