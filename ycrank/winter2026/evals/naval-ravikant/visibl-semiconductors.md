# Visibl Semiconductors -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Visibl is not the company itself but the structural gap it's trying to occupy. Synopsys cannot build a coordination layer that orchestrates Cadence tools. Cadence cannot build one that orchestrates Synopsys tools. Neither can build something that sits neutrally across the entire design flow without undermining their own ecosystem lock-in strategy. That's a genuine business model conflict creating a vacuum -- the same kind of structural opening that made AngelList possible when VCs couldn't build a platform that served their own competitors. If Visibl is truly building the neutral coordination infrastructure for chip design, the opportunity is real and the incumbents are structurally unable to fill it. That's the bull case, and it's not trivial.

But the founder-specific-knowledge test gives me pause. Jordon Kashanchi has real semiconductor experience -- Microsoft Azure Maia, Arm, Intel, an MS in Integrated Circuits from UT Austin. He's designed chips. He's used the EDA tools. He's presumably lived through the coordination breakdowns they describe. That's genuine and above-average for a pre-seed founder. But it's professional-pipeline knowledge -- the kind you acquire by being a good engineer at good companies for four years, not the kind that emerges from a decade of obsessive focus on a problem that nobody else sees clearly. Compare this to what I'd want: someone who spent years building internal coordination tools at a semiconductor company, who has non-obvious theories about why the existing workflow is fundamentally broken in ways that the people inside it can't articulate. The launch post says they "repeatedly saw coordination bottlenecks delay integration and tapeout." That's an observation any competent chip designer makes. The question is whether they possess insight into the coordination problem that would take a competitor years to discover. I don't see evidence of that depth yet. And the CEO brings software and data skills from Deloitte's consulting practice -- valuable for building the product, but the semiconductor domain knowledge is concentrated in a single founder.

The leverage architecture is where this deal gets ambiguous. A coordination layer that ingests full design context and sits across the entire EDA tool chain -- if that's what this becomes -- has genuine platform leverage. One platform serving thousands of design teams, each deployment making the system smarter about how chip design coordination fails. That's the infrastructure play I'm drawn to. But on-prem deployment for enterprise semiconductor companies with strict IP security requirements is the opposite of leverage. It's a two-person team deploying customized installations at Fortune 500 companies with twelve-month sales cycles. Every new customer requires proportional effort -- integration work, security compliance, design-flow customization. This could easily become a high-value consulting business disguised as a software platform. The leverage potential exists in the architecture, but the go-to-market reality pulls against it.

The timing concern is straightforward. "AI agents for chip design" is one of the hottest subcategories in enterprise AI right now. ChipAgents has raised $74 million, is deployed at 50 semiconductor companies, and has former CEOs of Synopsys, Cadence, and Mentor Graphics on its advisory board. Silimate from YC S23 is already generating revenue with enterprise customers. This is not a contrarian bet -- it's a validated market where the pricing already reflects optimism. Visibl's "coordination layer" positioning is differentiated from the point-tool approaches, but differentiated positioning within a consensus category is not the same as being non-consensus. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin, it was dismissed as nerd money. The best returns come from bets where smart people think you're wrong. Here, smart people are actively pouring capital into the same thesis.

For this to be a great investment, Visibl would need to prove that cross-workstream coordination is a genuinely different product category from point-tool optimization -- not just a marketing distinction but a technical architecture that the $74M competitors can't replicate by adding features. They'd need to show that Kashanchi's firsthand experience translates into product decisions that surprise domain experts. And they'd need to find a deployment model that scales beyond one-at-a-time enterprise installations. If all three prove true, the incumbent business model conflict creates a durable structural moat, and this becomes the picks-and-shovels play for the AI-in-chip-design era. But that's three simultaneous conditions, each uncertain, in a market where well-funded competitors have a significant head start in customer relationships and data. The asymmetric upside I look for -- where losses are capped at 1x and gains are uncapped -- gets compressed when the market is already crowded and the technology advantage is unproven.

The technology itself has reasonable compounding characteristics in theory -- each deployment ingests more design context, each tapeout cycle generates training data about coordination failures. But this is per-customer compounding, not network-level compounding. Stack Overflow's knowledge graph became more valuable with every question asked by any developer anywhere. Visibl's design context stays locked inside each customer's on-prem deployment. Without a mechanism to compound knowledge across customers, the defensibility is switching costs, not a deepening moat.
