# Indexable -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Indexable is the depth of the schlep. Andrew Gazelka didn't use Firecracker or Docker or any other off-the-shelf foundation. He built a custom virtual machine monitor on KVM, a custom Linux kernel with patches that haven't been upstreamed, a Rust-based reimplementation of Nix, and a content-addressable storage engine with RDMA transport. This is the kind of work that makes most founders' unconscious minds flinch -- not because they lack the skill, but because they instinctively sense the years of unglamorous kernel debugging ahead and look away. When I see E2B and Daytona using Firecracker and Docker respectively, I see companies standing on someone else's concrete. Gazelka is pouring his own. That structural willingness to do the hard thing is one of the strongest signals I know. Stripe worked for the same reason -- the Collisons didn't just build a nice payment API, they dealt with the banks, the fraud systems, the regulatory apparatus that every other programmer unconsciously avoided.

But here's my concern: how did Gazelka find this problem? He was at xAI, which plausibly means he experienced sandbox limitations firsthand while working on AI infrastructure. His Hyperion project -- a Minecraft engine in Rust handling 10,000 concurrent players -- shows he's been building systems for massively concurrent stateful environments for years, which is essentially the same problem domain as VM forking. His April 2023 blog post on GPT-4 as a hardware abstraction layer suggests he was thinking about AI-systems intersections before the current wave. These are good signals. But I can't find the moment of organic discovery -- the equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. The AI sandbox category is one of the hottest in infrastructure right now. Six funded competitors. Every cloud provider launching sandbox features. When I said the most impressive YC companies I'd seen recently weren't working on AI, I was pointing at exactly this failure mode: smart engineers entering a hot category because it's hot, rather than because they stumbled into a problem they couldn't stop thinking about. I can't tell which one this is, and that ambiguity matters.

What I can say with confidence is that Gazelka is an animal. The Hyperion project alone -- a custom game engine with 1,000 GitHub stars, targeting 10,000-player worlds -- tells you this person stays up all night building things because he can't help it. The Nushell contributions, the SwarmBot, 217 repositories on GitHub, the Starstruck achievement three times over -- this is a genuine hacker who builds for the love of building. The "do less, deeply" line in his GitHub bio suggests the right instinct about focus. His xAI tenure and prior exit with BountyBot show he can ship in demanding environments. When I evaluate whether a founder can build the core product themselves, this is about as high as you can score at pre-seed. The custom VMM already exists and claims 26ms full-environment forking -- that's not a pitch deck, it's a working system.

The problem is what's missing. No public users. No revenue. The website returns a 403 error. No mentions in any sandbox comparison article. No press. The GitHub org has zero public repositories and five followers. The "early access DM" model on Twitter -- to an account with 86 followers -- is founder-led sales at its most minimal. I have no evidence that anyone actually wants this product. "Make something people want" is not negotiable, and right now I'm looking at extraordinary engineering in search of users. Compare this to Daytona, which crossed $1M ARR within two months of relaunch, or E2B, which claims 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. The incumbents have not just more money -- they have proof of demand.

The strongest bull case is that 26ms stateful forking with database-level consistency is not an incremental improvement over 150ms ephemeral containers -- it's a categorically different capability. As AI agents get more sophisticated and need to branch entire stateful environments atomically, the companies built on Docker and Firecracker may hit a ceiling they literally cannot break through without rebuilding from scratch. The custom VMM becomes the moat, not a liability. In that world, Gazelka is doing what the Collisons did -- building the hard infrastructure that everyone else will eventually need but nobody else wants to build. The content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication could create a cost advantage that compounds with scale. And the fact that competitors have raised $30-100M while using off-the-shelf virtualization might mean they've optimized themselves into an architectural dead end. If that's true, Indexable wins despite being late and small, because the schlep scared everyone into easier but ultimately inferior approaches.

I want to believe that story. The technical excellence is real, and the schlep advantage is structurally sound. But I'm missing two things I need: a clear organic origin story that explains why this specific person is working on this specific problem beyond "I'm good at systems programming and sandboxes are hot," and any evidence at all that someone outside the building wants what they're making. The single-founder listing also concerns me -- the YC page names only Gazelka despite a team size of three, and the GitHub org shows one visible member. I've seen what happens when the entire technical vision lives in one person's head and the company needs to scale execution. This is a company I'd watch closely over the next two batches. If users start pulling the product toward them organically, the schlep advantage becomes decisive. But right now, it's brilliant engineering without proof of demand, in a category hot enough that being brilliant may not be sufficient.
