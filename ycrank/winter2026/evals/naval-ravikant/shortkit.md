# shortkit -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Michael Seleman spent six years building YouTube Shorts infrastructure at Google -- one of exactly two organizations on Earth (the other being ByteDance) that have solved short-form video delivery at planetary scale. That's the signal. You can't acquire that knowledge from documentation, courses, or even a few years at a smaller video company. The intuitions about how ML-driven buffering interacts with codec selection, how feed mechanics affect rebuffer rates at the tail end of the latency distribution, how chunked transfer encoding changes first-frame delivery -- these are things you learn by operating systems serving billions of views. When I backed Stack Overflow, part of the thesis was that Joel Spolsky and Jeff Atwood had accumulated non-transferable knowledge about how developers actually seek and share information. Seleman has the analogous knowledge for short-form video infrastructure. That's genuine specific knowledge. Neil Bhammar brings complementary operator experience from BusRight -- $7M raised, 23-state customer base, 700% revenue growth -- but his knowledge is about scaling B2B SaaS, not about video. The specific knowledge is concentrated in one person, which is both the company's greatest asset and its single-point-of-failure risk.

The leverage architecture passes my basic test: ShortKit turns what was a multi-engineer, multi-month build into an SDK integration. That's code leverage -- one team's infrastructure work serves every customer's video feed. Usage-based pricing means revenue scales with customer engagement, not with ShortKit's headcount. This is the infrastructure pattern I've backed repeatedly -- Alchemy for blockchain, CoinList for token launches, AngelList for fundraising. Build the picks and shovels while everyone else mines for gold. But unlike those investments, ShortKit lacks a network effect or protocol dynamic. Each customer's usage doesn't make the platform meaningfully better for other customers. There's no shared graph, no liquidity network, no composable protocol. It's a well-executed API business with switching costs that develop over time through integration depth, but switching costs are a retention mechanism, not a compounding one.

The contrarian test is where I get stuck. Short-form vertical video as the dominant content format is pure consensus -- the most consensus thing in consumer tech right now. The contrarian claim embedded in ShortKit's thesis is subtler: that every consumer app, publisher, and e-commerce platform will want its own TikTok-quality feed rather than relying on the major platforms for distribution. That's a genuine bet. Most smart product people would say "why would a fitness app build its own short-form feed when users already have TikTok?" ShortKit is betting that owning the video experience, the engagement data, and the ad revenue will motivate apps to bring feeds in-house. If that thesis is right, ShortKit is positioned at the infrastructure layer of a major platform shift. But "if" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The demand signal from non-social apps for embedded short-form feeds is unproven in the dossier -- no named customers, no revenue, no waitlist metrics. I can't distinguish between "building infrastructure for a wave that's coming" and "building infrastructure for a wave that never arrives."

The bull case deserves serious consideration. If the market for embedded short-form feeds materializes broadly -- if news publishers, e-commerce apps, education platforms, and fitness apps all decide they need TikTok-quality video experiences -- then ShortKit has founder-domain fit that's almost impossible to replicate at the speed the market would demand. BytePlus is the obvious structural threat, but it carries geopolitical risk that limits adoption by Western companies and operates as a large-enterprise vendor, not a developer-first SDK. Mux could extend into short-form, but building specialized feed mechanics, swipe-optimized pre-fetching, and vertical-native transcoding would require them to build and maintain what is effectively a separate product. The "Stripe for short-form video" analogy is seductive: everyone knew payments existed before Stripe, but Stripe made the developer experience so frictionless that it unlocked a market. If Seleman's YouTube Shorts knowledge translates into an SDK that makes short-form integration genuinely trivial, this could be a breakout infrastructure company.

But I keep coming back to the leverage ratio. The best companies in my portfolio didn't just remove friction from an existing process -- they enabled an entirely new category of person to do an entirely new type of work. Replit doesn't just make coding faster for engineers; it makes coding possible for non-engineers. AngelList didn't just streamline fundraising; it created permissionless access to startup investing. ShortKit makes it easier for engineering teams to embed short-form feeds -- that's valuable, but it's an efficiency gain for existing teams, not a new form of leverage for a new category of actor. The market may be narrower than the TAM numbers suggest: the companies that genuinely need embedded short-form video and can afford ShortKit's Scale tier pricing may number in the hundreds, not the thousands.

This is a competent team with real domain knowledge building infrastructure in a pattern I've backed before. Seleman's specific knowledge is the strongest signal -- it's rare, directly applicable, and non-teachable. But the market thesis is unproven, the competitive landscape includes a ByteDance subsidiary with the literal TikTok stack, and the leverage architecture lacks the network effects or protocol dynamics that turn good infrastructure companies into great ones. I'd want to see evidence that the demand for embedded short-form feeds exists beyond a few publisher case studies before committing capital.
