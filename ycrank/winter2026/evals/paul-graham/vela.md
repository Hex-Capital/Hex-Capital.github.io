# Vela -- Paul Graham Evaluation

AI scheduling is a graveyard. Clara Labs raised $12.6 million and got acqui-hired into Twitter in 2019. x.ai raised $44 million building "Amy Ingram" and eventually shut down. Every year at YC I see applications from teams building AI scheduling assistants, because scheduling is what you come up with when you sit down and try to think of AI startup ideas. It's the canonical example of a manufactured idea -- obvious, universally relatable, and seductive precisely because everyone has felt the pain of email ping-pong about meeting times. The question with Vela is whether LLMs have genuinely changed the underlying physics of this problem, or whether this is another team walking into the same trap with slightly better tools.

When I look at how the founders found this problem, I see credentials but not an origin story. Gobhanu came from BCG AI, Perplexity, and Deloitte. Saatvik came from AWS ML Supercompute. Both are clearly capable people. But neither background screams "I was drowning in scheduling coordination and couldn't take it anymore." The dossier contains no moment of organic discovery -- no equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. The problem wasn't hiding in plain sight due to schlep blindness; it's been visible for years and attracted hundreds of millions in venture capital. What kept those previous companies from winning wasn't that nobody saw the opportunity. It was that the technology couldn't reliably handle the ambiguity. That's a different dynamic than Stripe, where the schlep itself -- banks, fraud, compliance -- was the deterrent. Here the deterrent was technical feasibility, and LLMs may have removed it. But "the technology got better" is a commodity enabler, not a founder-specific insight.

The one thing I genuinely like is the multi-channel approach and the schlep it entails. Clara was email-only. Calendly is link-only. Vela is trying to negotiate scheduling across email, SMS, WhatsApp, Slack, Teams, and phone simultaneously. That integration surface is ugly, tedious work -- maintaining six different communication channel APIs, handling carrier SMS filtering, WhatsApp policy changes, enterprise Slack permissions. Most teams unconsciously avoid that kind of plumbing. There's real schlep here, even if the top-level idea is consensus. And the co-founder relationship is strong: these are siblings who built a microfinance nonprofit together starting at age 14 and scaled it to 77 countries. That's the opposite of co-founders who met at a hackathon. They've been working together for a decade. When I hear "siblings who dropped out of Wharton and UChicago Booth respectively," I see conviction and relationship durability.

The traction signals are ambiguous in a way that makes me cautious. Listing Netflix, LinkedIn, and AWS as logos is meaningful if those represent teams that depend on Vela daily. It's meaningless if one recruiter at each company tried it for a week. The dossier explicitly notes these may represent individual recruiters, not enterprise contracts. "Thousands of interviews scheduled each week" is a strong claim, but with no revenue data, no pricing page, and no independently verifiable growth metrics, I can't distinguish between a product people are pulling toward them and a free tool people try and forget. Product Hunt with 136 upvotes and #7 Day Rank is not a demand signal; it's a launch event. I need to see whether those enterprise recruiters would be genuinely upset if Vela disappeared tomorrow.

The bull case for Vela is this: LLMs have crossed a reliability threshold that makes autonomous scheduling negotiation actually work for the first time. The multi-channel approach creates real integration moats that compound with usage data. Paradox getting absorbed by Workday leaves an opening for non-Workday customers. The siblings have serious execution ability -- scaling a nonprofit to 77 countries as teenagers is not nothing, and Saatvik's CMU CS credentials plus AWS ML engineering background means the technical foundation is real. If they can establish themselves as the scheduling coordination layer before Google and Microsoft ship native equivalents, the enterprise switching costs from accumulated scheduling context could be substantial. That's a real company.

But my concern is structural. Google has Gemini plus Google Calendar. Microsoft has Copilot plus Outlook. Both are actively building AI scheduling features. When the platform owner ships a native version of your product, your standalone value proposition gets compressed to whatever gap remains between their 80% solution and your 95% solution -- and most buyers accept 80%. The underlying technology is accessible to any team with LLM API access and calendar integrations. The barrier is edge-case handling at scale, which is real but not a moat I'd bet my own money on at this stage. I look for problems where the schlep itself creates durable structural barriers. Here the schlep is integration work that any sufficiently funded team can replicate.

Saatvik is technically strong on paper -- dual CS degree from CMU in two years with highest honors, patent filed at 16, AWS ML engineering. But there are no public GitHub repos for either founder, and the job postings for India-based engineers suggest the core product development may already be distributed rather than founder-built. At pre-seed I want to see the founders' fingerprints on the code. The absence of visible technical craft doesn't condemn them, but it doesn't give me the signal I look for.
