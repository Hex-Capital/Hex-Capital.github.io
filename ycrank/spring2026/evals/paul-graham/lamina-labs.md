# Lamina Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The comparison that matters here isn't Synthesia or Runway. It's Manim. Grant Sanderson built Manim because he personally needed it to make 3Blue1Brown videos -- that's the paradigmatic organic discovery. He had a problem, he wrote software to solve it, and six million subscribers proved the demand existed. Lamina Labs is essentially saying "Manim is brilliant but requires Python expertise -- we'll make it accessible to everyone via natural language." That's a real observation. But it's one layer removed from the original discovery. Sanderson found the problem of visualizing STEM concepts. These founders found the problem of Manim being too hard to use. A real problem, yes, but a less fundamental one. And this distinction matters because Manim is open-source. If Grant Sanderson or the Manim Community decided to ship an LLM-powered natural language interface -- which any competent engineer could prototype in a weekend hackathon -- Lamina Labs would face an existential competitor with vastly superior brand recognition and distribution in exactly their target market.

The organic connection isn't zero, though. Both founders are international olympiad medalists from Nepal in physics, astronomy, and computer science. Rohan built curriculum at Caltech for teaching ancient astronomy. Sudip is doing ML research at MIT. These aren't people who read TechCrunch and noticed "AI video is hot." They've lived inside STEM education for years and genuinely understand what it takes to explain a physics concept visually. The problem is that their website frames the opportunity as "AI video went cinematic. Education got nothing" -- which reads like a market-gap identification, not a personal itch they couldn't stop scratching. Drew Houston didn't frame Dropbox as "cloud storage went enterprise, consumers got nothing." He said "I kept forgetting my USB drive." The framing reveals how the founders think about their own company, and this framing says "market positioning" more than "personal necessity."

The schlep question is the one I keep turning over. For Stripe, the schlep was dealing with banks, fraud systems, PCI compliance, and financial regulation -- ugly, boring work that thousands of hackers consciously knew was needed but unconsciously avoided. What's the schlep here? Building a deterministic rendering engine that handles arbitrary STEM domains correctly isn't easy, but it's also the kind of work that two olympiad-winning physicists would find genuinely fun. Nobody is unconsciously avoiding "build cool math animations" -- that's what smart technical people do for weekend projects. The barrier isn't schlep blindness; it's ambition blindness. Most founders look at STEM animation and think "that's a feature, not a company." The founders' bet is that it's a company. They might be right. But the competitive moat is thinner when the barrier is perceived market size rather than painful execution complexity.

Here's the bull case I'd want to believe: Pictor's sub-8-second deterministic rendering from text prompts, if it represents a genuinely novel rendering engine rather than an LLM wrapper around Manim, could constitute real infrastructure. The gap between academic prototypes (LASEV, TeachMaster) and a reliable production system covering all of STEM is substantial. If these founders have built something architecturally distinct -- a rendering pipeline optimized for speed and breadth that would take competitors years to replicate -- then the "feature vs. company" objection dissolves. And the timing argument has teeth: LLMs just crossed the threshold for reliable natural-language-to-structured-code translation, making this product category feasible for the first time. If Lamina Labs is six months ahead of the Manim community and eighteen months ahead of Runway's education mode, that lead time could compound. Two olympiad-level technical founders building their own product at MIT and Caltech is exactly the team profile I'd want for this bet.

Where I land is that the technical founder quality is genuinely strong -- olympiad medalists at Caltech and MIT who can clearly build, with domain knowledge spanning the exact intersection of physics, mathematics, and computation that this product requires. That's the strongest signal in the dossier. But the absence of any traction evidence whatsoever -- no waitlist count, no demo users, no Product Hunt launch, no social media presence -- leaves me with no way to test the most important question: does anyone actually want this enough to use it? "Make something people want" is the test, and right now there's no evidence either way. Combined with meaningful competitive risk from Manim's open-source ecosystem and well-funded AI video incumbents expanding into education, I can't get to conviction with my own money. The ingredients are interesting. The signal is too thin.
