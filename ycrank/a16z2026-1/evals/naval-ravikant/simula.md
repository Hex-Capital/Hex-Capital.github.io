# Simula -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Simula enters a space where ZeroClick has $55M and a founder who built Honey to a $4B PayPal exit, and Koah Labs has $26M+ with 2M MAUs and 35M+ ad impressions already serving. This is the competitive fact that dominates my analysis. "Escape competition through authenticity" — but authenticity requires specific knowledge that competitors cannot replicate, and Simula's interactive ad formats (brand ambassadors, sponsored mini-games) are design choices, not technical moats. Koah or ZeroClick can ship a gamified ad unit in a quarter. Ryan Hudson at ZeroClick has spent his career building consumer monetization infrastructure — that's specific knowledge. Simula's founders have impressive credentials in adjacent domains but nothing that would take a competitor years to accumulate in ad infrastructure specifically.

Yizhen Zhen built games since age 10, did SWE at Google, PE at Bain Capital, and graduated Dartmouth '24. Logan Nicholson founded Talsey AI in hospitality and has gaming industry connections from Mobidictum conferences. Where I struggle: neither founder's knowledge stack maps to ad tech. Game development experience is relevant to building interactive ad formats, and Bain Capital training sharpens commercial thinking — but ad infrastructure requires deep understanding of demand-side platforms, real-time bidding, attribution modeling, publisher revenue optimization. Zhen's prior startup (Fledge) was a recruiting CRM. Nicholson's was hospitality AI. These are smart people who identified "AI apps need monetization" as a market opportunity. That's analysis, not obsession. If Zhen disappeared tomorrow, would Simula's core insight — that interactive formats outperform banners in AI surfaces — survive? Yes, because it's a hypothesis anyone in ad tech could generate. That's the test, and Simula doesn't pass it. The no-shared-history co-founder pairing between Dartmouth/HBS and BYU with no employer or university overlap raises the principal-agent concern I watch for in early teams — co-founders without pre-existing trust are agents to each other before they're principals together.

The leverage architecture is Simula's strongest dimension. An SDK deployed once to publishers creates zero-marginal-cost distribution. Each additional publisher adds inventory that attracts advertisers, and each advertiser funds the payouts that attract more publishers — classic two-sided network effects. The $15+ annual ad revenue per DAU claim, if accurate, creates genuine publisher lock-in once revenue flows. This is the structural reason I haven't scored Simula lower: code-as-leverage in a network business is the right architecture. The Wish comparison haunts me here, though. Wish had marketplace leverage without specific knowledge, and I watched it collapse 95% from its IPO. Leverage alone — without the founder's irreplaceable expertise driving the product — creates a generic company in a competitive market. Format innovation in ad tech has historically been a temporary advantage: Facebook's News Feed ads were revolutionary until every platform copied them within two years.

The bull case requires three things to be simultaneously true: Simula's interactive formats genuinely produce 3x payouts (their claim versus traditional ads), publishers prefer experiential ad units over simpler contextual inserts (against historical mobile ad preferences for low-friction formats), and OpenAI and Google don't extend their own ad platforms to third-party publishers via SDK. The self-reported "5M+ DAUs and 100M+ downloads" pipeline is the pivotal data point — if those integrations go live and produce the claimed $15+ per DAU, Simula has a $75M+ revenue opportunity from existing commitments alone. That would make the competitive funding gap irrelevant. But "integrating into" is doing enormous linguistic work in that claim, and no independent verification exists. At pre-seed I don't penalize absent traction, but I do weight the distinction between "we're talking to publishers" and "we're serving ads and collecting revenue."

Simula does fit my access democratization pattern — giving long-tail AI app publishers access to ad monetization previously available only to platforms with direct advertiser relationships. That's the AngelList playbook applied to a different gatekeeping structure. But AngelList worked because I had a decade of specific knowledge about what both sides of the market needed. Simula is applying the democratization template without the founder-specific-knowledge engine underneath it. The team of five is lean, and the SDK model reduces the headcount scaling typically required in ad tech, but two-sided ad marketplaces ultimately require sales teams on both the publisher and advertiser sides — this isn't Notion, where the product is its own distribution.

The dimension that locks my verdict is Specific Knowledge Productization. Simula is a market-opportunity company, not a specific-knowledge company. Zhen and Nicholson are capable operators in a real and growing market — AI ad spending projected to reach $26B by 2029 — but capable operators in a crowded market without irreplaceable expertise is exactly the bet structure that produces median outcomes, not power-law returns. The leverage architecture partially compensates, keeping Simula above a clear Pass, but not enough to overcome the competitive reality of $81M+ in funding across direct competitors with deeper domain credentials.
