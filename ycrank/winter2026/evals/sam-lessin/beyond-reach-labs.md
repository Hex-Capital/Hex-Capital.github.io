# Beyond Reach Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what grabs me immediately: a guy who spent seven years at SpaceX leading human-rated parachute systems across 30+ Dragon missions -- the hardware that brought NASA astronauts home for the first time since Apollo -- teamed up with the PhD who literally invented the underlying technology under a NASA grant, and they've known each other since freshman year at Penn in 2013. That's thirteen years of relationship before co-founding. This is the kind of founder pairing I look for obsessively. Collins has lived inside the design-qualify-fly cycle for space hardware at the highest stakes imaginable. Fogelson didn't just study deployable structures -- he created the structural paradigm (PETS and HERDS), published it in peer-reviewed journals, and tested it in zero-g on NASA's dime. When I apply my "sane person, insane idea" filter, this calibrates beautifully. The sane part is almost absurdly credible. The insane part -- a dining table that unfolds into a football field in orbit using metamaterial geometry change -- sounds like science fiction until you realize it's backed by real physics, real testing, and real patents. Most VCs would look at a two-person pre-seed hardware company competing against Northrop Grumman and Redwire and say "pass." That's exactly the kind of investor skepticism that creates non-consensus opportunity.

What makes this particularly interesting through my lens is that this is the ultimate AI-resistant business. I've been pounding the table that software moats are dissolving -- "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore." Well, nobody is going to prompt an LLM and get a flight-qualified deployable space structure out the other end. The moat here is physics, patents, manufacturing know-how, and eventually flight heritage. AI might optimize the structural design process or improve manufacturing quality control, but it cannot replicate the core capability. This is Craftwork for orbit -- operational and physical complexity that technology enhances but cannot substitute. The patent protection on PETS and HERDS, combined with the engineering gap between published metamaterial theory and actual flight-qualified hardware, creates years of defensibility. And here's the kicker: once they fly the demo in Q2 2027 and accumulate operational hours, the flight heritage itself becomes a compounding moat. Space procurement is pathologically conservative -- customers weight demonstrated in-orbit performance above everything. Each successful deployment makes the next sale easier and makes it harder for anyone to catch up.

The primitive-versus-application question is where I have to think carefully. Space power generation is genuinely infrastructure -- it's the layer everything else depends on. You can't run an orbital data center, staff a commercial space station, or build a lunar outpost without dramatically more power than current arrays can deliver. If Beyond Reach Labs' deployment mechanism becomes the standard way power infrastructure gets built in space, they're creating a new primitive that the entire in-space economy builds on top of. That said, this is component hardware, not a protocol with network effects. It's more analogous to a critical piece of physical infrastructure than to Solana or Venmo. The compounding dynamics come from flight heritage and manufacturing scale, not from network effects where each additional user increases value for all users. That caps the primitive score relative to my pure protocol-layer investments, but doesn't eliminate it -- plenty of great infrastructure businesses compound through trust and qualification rather than network effects.

The infinity story is legitimately compelling. They cite in-space power demand growing from 50 MW to 500+ GW by 2035 -- a 10,000x increase. That projection depends on orbital data centers, mega-constellations, commercial space stations, and lunar outposts all materializing, which is a real question mark. But the narrative expands naturally: satellite power today, space station power next, lunar infrastructure after that, and ultimately the vision of abundant space-based energy production. Each milestone makes the next narrative expansion more credible. The risk is timeline -- deep-space hardware companies live on decade-long arcs. Helium took seven years from seed to unicorn, and that was deploying hotspots on Earth, not qualifying hardware for orbit.

The bull case I'd make if I were arguing against my own concerns: the $175M in LOIs, even though non-binding and from undisclosed counterparties, suggest that the demand signal is real. Space companies don't sign LOIs for fun -- the procurement process is heavy enough that expressing interest in a pre-flight technology requires genuine need. SpaceX's Starship is about to collapse launch costs, which will unlock power-hungry in-space applications that have been economically impossible until now. DARPA's NOM4D program is actively funding this exact technology category. The founders aren't guessing about what the market needs -- Collins spent seven years inside the company that's creating the demand (SpaceX's constellation and human spaceflight programs). The concern about multi-year qualification timelines is real, but that's precisely what creates the moat once you're through it. This is a business where being early and being patient are features, not bugs.

Where I land: this is a genuinely strong pairing of credible founders with a non-consensus idea in a domain where physical-world moats are real and AI-resistant. The team composition, the relationship depth, the patent-protected IP with NASA validation, and the structural position in space infrastructure all align with what I look for. The $175M in LOIs provide a meaningful traction signal for a pre-seed company. The risks are real -- qualification timelines, capital intensity, and dependence on the broader space economy materializing -- but those risks are exactly what keep consensus investors away, which is what creates the opportunity for a check from someone with structural conviction and patience.
