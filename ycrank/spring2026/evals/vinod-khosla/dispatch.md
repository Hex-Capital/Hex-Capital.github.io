# Dispatch -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Dispatch: they're building the FedEx trucks for a factory district that hasn't been built yet. The entire business depends on a chain of prior bets paying off -- that microgravity manufacturing works at commercial scale, that the companies doing it won't vertically integrate their own return logistics (as Varda already does), and that Dispatch can offer enough cost advantage to justify a standalone downmass layer. That's not one big technical risk I can underwrite. That's three or four simultaneous conditions that all need to resolve favorably, and the founder can't control most of them. When I backed Rocket Lab, the demand side was clear -- hundreds of companies needed to get satellites to orbit. Here, the demand side is a handful of companies, most of which are themselves pre-revenue startups.

Let me address the consequence of success honestly: if in-space manufacturing becomes a $60 billion industry by 2040 and Dispatch captures the return logistics layer, you have a real business -- maybe a multi-billion-dollar infrastructure company. That's not nothing. But it's not replacing how an industry works. It's not eliminating animal agriculture or making fossil fuel power plants obsolete. It's a component in a supply chain for a market that may never reach those projections. The TAM estimates I see -- $6.3 billion in 2025, $11.2 billion by 2030 -- describe the entire space manufacturing ecosystem, and Dispatch would capture a fraction. I keep asking myself: if this works perfectly, does the world operate fundamentally differently? The answer is no. Some pharmaceuticals and semiconductors get manufactured in a novel way, and Dispatch is the trucking company that brings them home. The problem isn't unimportant, but it's not the kind of magnitude that makes me write a check despite 90% failure odds.

The 100x cost claim on heat shield construction is the one detail that grabbed my attention. If that number holds at orbital scale -- and that's a monumental "if" -- it represents exactly the kind of economic disruption I look for: making the existing approach unviable. But a ground test behind a rocket engine in the Mojave Desert is a far cry from surviving sustained hypersonic heating, plasma dynamics, and the full reentry environment from orbit. KiOR taught me this lesson at the cost of $160 million of my own money: technology achievement in controlled conditions without validated unit economics at operational scale is seductive and dangerous. Dispatch has demonstrated ingenuity and scrappiness, but the gap between a ground heat shield test and a successful orbital return mission is not a gap you bridge with clever engineering alone -- it requires capital, regulatory navigation, and time, all of which their competitors have more of.

Now, the bull case I have to take seriously: someone will build the dominant downmass infrastructure for space manufacturing, and it might be cheaper to do it as a specialized provider than as a vertically integrated manufacturer. If that's true, the 2-person team with a genuine cost breakthrough could outmaneuver Varda's $329 million in vertical integration the same way a scrappy startup sometimes outmaneuvers a bloated incumbent. The Astranis pedigree gives these founders real spacecraft engineering knowledge -- they've built and operated satellites, which is directly relevant to reentry vehicle design. And the "autonomous space station" vision, if you squint, could become a platform play with genuine lock-in. But here's what I can't get past: this market has 15+ companies building reentry vehicles, Varda has already completed multiple orbital missions and generated $48M in revenue, and Inversion has $125 million in combined funding and contracts including the Space Force. Dispatch isn't the contrarian outsider that experts dismiss -- they're one of many teams chasing an opportunity that's already consensus enough to attract government contracts and hundreds of millions in venture funding.

The founding team is competent but doesn't trigger my pattern recognition for outlier outcomes. Both founders come from Astranis -- same company, similar orbit (pun intended). That gives them relevant technical depth but also a homogeneous perspective. I prefer founding teams that bring colliding worldviews: the scientist and the operator, the domain expert and the industry outsider. Here I see two aerospace engineers who decided to start a space company. That's the industry playbook, not the contrarian outsider approach that produced my best returns. There's no evidence in the dossier of rapid evolution in thinking, abandoned assumptions, or the kind of first-principles rethinking that separates the best athletes from the established wide receivers.

This is a competent team pursuing a real opportunity in a space that's becoming crowded fast. The problem isn't silly, the technology concept has merit, and the timing is reasonable. But the consequence of success doesn't reach the threshold where I'll tolerate high failure probability, the founders are insiders executing an industry playbook rather than outsiders reimagining the problem, and the competitive landscape has moved well past the moment where a $500K pre-seed entry gives you meaningful option value. I'd want to see one of two things change before I'd invest: either the cost breakthrough validated at orbital scale (which transforms this from a logistics company into a genuine technology disruptor) or evidence that the founders are thinking about this problem in a way that none of the 15+ competitors have conceived. Neither is present today.
