# Haladir -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing that catches my eye isn't the technology — it's the trajectory. Haladir's LinkedIn described them as an "AI-powered mainframe modernization suite" for COBOL migration, then "AI-led software development" correctness, and now "operational superintelligence" with RL for operations research. Three framings before a single customer. At pre-seed, pivoting is forgivable. But when I'm evaluating specific knowledge — the kind that can't be taught, that emerges from years of obsessive engagement with a problem — rapid repositioning is a tell. It suggests the founders possess a general technical capability (formal verification + RL) and are searching for the right application, rather than building from deep knowledge of a domain they've lived inside. Evan Williams didn't pivot to public communication — he'd been building Blogger for years. The Haladir team identified constrained optimization from the ML side, not the operations side. None of them have spent time inside logistics, supply chain, or manufacturing. Their insight is about what LLMs can't do, not about what operations teams actually need.

The underlying intellectual bet, however, has real structure. Using solver-verified rewards to train RL models on constrained optimization is a picks-and-shovels play for AI training infrastructure — and my portfolio reveals that I systematically favor infrastructure over applications, even when I don't say so publicly. If Haladir can formalize reward signals across multiple operational domains, they're building something that looks like Alchemy looked for blockchain developers: an infrastructure layer that every practitioner needs but nobody wants to build themselves. The ConstraintBench paper documents a genuine gap — 65% constraint satisfaction, 30.5% joint feasibility-and-optimality from frontier models — and the architectural response (solvers generating ground-truth for RL reward signals) is elegant. One formalization serves every model trained against it. That's leverage through code, not through headcount.

The strongest bull case: this team has identified a structural gap that exists because operations research experts don't know RL, and RL researchers don't know OR. Interdisciplinary gaps are where specific knowledge forms — not from any single discipline, but from the rare intersection. The ConstraintBench paper, co-authored by all four founders, is evidence they can operate at this intersection. If they own the benchmark that becomes the standard for evaluating LLMs on constrained optimization, they've built a compounding asset — similar to how Stack Overflow's knowledge graph became the infrastructure everyone built against. The "why now" is structurally sound: RLVR proved tractable for math and code in 2024-2025, OR has natural verifiability through solvers, and nobody has bridged them yet. That's not a hot market — it's a specific technical gap waiting for the right team.

But the specific knowledge bar isn't met at the level I require. These are four current or recent students — CMU, Princeton, UVA, UVA Wise — with no prior exits, no operating experience in the domains they target, and credibility resting on a single paper published the same month they appeared on YC's radar. Joseph Tso's Google Scholar profile and Princeton CS background suggest research aptitude, but research aptitude in ML is abundant. What's scarce is the person who has spent five years inside supply chain operations and also understands formal verification well enough to bridge the gap. That person would have specific knowledge that can't be trained into someone else. This team has academic knowledge that any strong interdisciplinary PhD group could replicate. The paper is good — but a benchmark is not a business, and the two-star GitHub repo suggests the community hasn't yet validated the contribution as essential.

The competitive landscape compounds my concern. Harmonic at $295M and Code Metal at $161.5M are operating in adjacent formal-verification-for-AI territory. If operations research becomes a priority training domain for any major lab — OpenAI, DeepSeek, Databricks — they already have RLVR infrastructure and could extend it. Haladir's moat is currently the specialized interdisciplinary knowledge of the team, which I've already argued is replicable. The technology doesn't yet compound because there's no product, no users, and no accumulated domain formalizations. The potential for compounding is real, but potential isn't defensibility.

I respect the research-first GTM approach — publishing a benchmark to establish credibility before selling a product is how Stack Overflow started, how Replit built developer trust. But the founders need to demonstrate they know something about operations that operations people don't know about themselves, not just something about LLMs that ML researchers haven't benchmarked yet. The intelligence and energy seem present. The integrity filter shows no red flags. What's missing is the irreplaceable founder-problem connection — the reason it has to be this team solving this problem. Right now, it could be any competent team at the intersection. That's a pass for me, though I'd want to watch where they land after the next iteration.
