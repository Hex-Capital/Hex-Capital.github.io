# Arzana -- Paul Graham Evaluation

What catches my eye first about Arzana is something most investors would gloss over: these founders moved *toward* the schlep. They were running a gourmet meal delivery service at Stanford — a perfectly respectable, fashionable startup — and they walked away from it to go build ERP connectors for manufacturing plants in Iowa. Nobody does that unless something real pulled them. Going from consumer food delivery to parsing handwritten RFQs and wiring up Epicor integrations is the opposite of what a team optimizing for fundraising would do. It's the opposite of what sounds cool at a dinner party. That vector — from glamorous to unglamorous, from Stanford campus to Okoboji — is itself a signal.

The schlep here is enormous and genuine. Eleven ERP integrations with a four-person team. Think about what that actually means: you're dealing with Epicor's API quirks, SAP's authentication nightmares, JobBoss's legacy data formats, and QuickBooks's limitations — simultaneously. Plus you're parsing unstructured manufacturing documents: handwritten notes, inconsistent PDFs, email threads with forwarded-forwarded-forwarded RFQs. This is exactly the kind of work that makes smart engineers unconsciously look away. The same dynamic that kept payments broken for a decade before Stripe — thousands of programmers knew the problem existed but couldn't stomach dealing with banks and compliance. Here, thousands of software engineers know manufacturing offices run on fax machines and spreadsheets, but nobody wants to spend their career integrating with mid-market ERPs. Arzana walked into that building.

The organic origin story is plausible but not airtight. Alexander grew up in Okoboji, Iowa — a small town where manufacturing is the economy, not an industry vertical you read about in a report. His field engineer is from the same town. The company maintains a physical presence there. The Brilla team consulted for Italian manufacturers before pivoting. I'd place this somewhere between "stumbled into the problem through lived experience" and "identified a smart market from adjacent exposure." It's not Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's not a brainstorming session either. The Iowa roots are doing real distribution work — getting Schneider National and Zeiss as customers when you're 21 and based in Okoboji suggests relationships and trust that a purely SF-based team couldn't manufacture.

Here's what genuinely impresses me: a pre-seed company with nine named customer logos, a Fortune 500 name on the wall, and a specific testimonial — "cut our TTQ from 5 days to 1, and our RFQ win rate skyrocketed." That's not hypothetical demand. That's a VP of Sales at a real company saying the product changed their business. Published pricing of $2,500-$7,000 per month means these aren't free pilots — someone is writing checks. Marshall Kools made ten thousand dollars last year. They're not living large; they're grinding. When I see a co-founder who was a D1 wrestler and state champion accepting $10K annual compensation to integrate ERPs in Iowa, that's a specific kind of determination I can recognize.

My biggest concern is one I take seriously: neither founder is a technical hacker. Alexander has taken one coding class. Kools studied management science and engineering. The entire technical capability of the company rests on two engineers — Politi and Vander Pol — with essentially no public track record. They're actively hiring a "Founding Full-Stack Engineer," which suggests the technical bench is thin. I've been consistent on this point throughout my career: the founding team needs to be able to build the core product. Dropbox's exception was that Houston himself was the builder. Here, the builders are employees, not founders. If Politi leaves, what happens? That's key-person risk I can't ignore, and it's the kind of structural fragility that becomes catastrophic during the inevitable hard periods. Endeavor AI has $7M and is targeting the same market — in a knife fight over enterprise manufacturing customers, the team that can ship faster usually wins, and that advantage typically belongs to companies where the founders write the code.

The bull case is compelling though. If the traction signals are real — 20+ plants paying $2,500-$7,000/month — that could be north of $50K MRR, which would make this one of the strongest revenue stories in any YC batch at pre-seed. The schlep creates a genuine moat: every ERP integration is switching cost, and the manufacturing-specific document parsing improves with every transaction processed. The "why now" is sound — LLMs crossed a reliability threshold for unstructured document parsing in 2023-2025 that makes this approach viable for the first time. And the founders demonstrated something I value: they tried something, it didn't work, and they came back with something harder and less obvious. That's the Emmett Shear pattern — failed at Kiko, came back with Justin.tv. The question is whether business founders with hired engineers can execute at the pace this market demands.

I'd invest, but with less conviction than I'd have if one of the founders could build the product themselves. The problem is exactly right — a massive, obvious schlep that everyone avoids. The early traction, if verified, is strong. The founders show resourcefulness and the right instinct of moving toward difficulty rather than away from it. But the non-technical team structure means I'm betting on their ability to recruit and retain great engineers rather than on their ability to build great software directly. That's a weaker bet, and it shows in my score.
