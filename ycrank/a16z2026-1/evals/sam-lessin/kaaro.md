# Kaaro -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran are second-generation railroaders who already built and scaled an AI agent company. That's the sentence that matters. At Toma — YC W24, a16z-backed, $17M Series A — they took AI voice agents from 20 dealership customers to hundreds. Now they're 20 years old, they've dropped out of Waterloo and UBC respectively, and they're running the exact same playbook against an industry their families actually work in. The founder-market fit here isn't the usual "I noticed a problem" — it's generational. They grew up watching railroaders chase phone calls and manually key data into green-screen terminals. And in an industry where vendor trust takes years to build, they walked in with CPKC — Canada's largest railroad — already engaged, CN in late-stage conversations, and Gulf & Ohio Railways building with them. That pipeline didn't come from cold outbound. That came from being insiders.

The mispricing question is moderately interesting. "AI agents for freight railroads" is not a category any VC at a San Francisco demo day is fighting over. There's essentially no funded competitor in North America — Optym does operations research for locomotive scheduling, Wabtec's RailConnect is a legacy TMS, Railinc is an industry data layer, and Ursico is European. Nobody is building AI workflow agents for the phone-call-and-email layer of railroad operations. That's real category neglect, and neglect creates pricing asymmetry. But it's not the kind of mispricing that gets me truly excited — railroads aren't a stigmatized category like cannabis delivery or adult content. Investors aren't avoiding this because it makes them uncomfortable; they're avoiding it because it looks small and boring. "Small and boring" mispricing is real but it caps the asymmetry. You're not getting Solana-at-four-cents pricing dynamics here.

The primitive test is where Kaaro loses me. This is not a new verb. Nobody is going to say "Kaaro me that railcar status." What Kaaro is building — AI agents that automate communication-heavy workflows in a vertical industry — is the Toma playbook applied to a different sector. It's vertical SaaS with an agent interface. Valuable? Probably. A new primitive? No. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavioral category. Patreon created direct creator monetization. Kaaro is automating existing railroad workflows more efficiently. That's execution in an existing operational category, not category creation. The $55B railway management system market already exists; Kaaro is competing for a slice of operational workflow spend within it.

Here's the tension I keep coming back to: my stated position is that AI is sustaining technology that benefits incumbents. Kaaro is an AI application-layer startup. Wabtec is a $25B public company that owns RailConnect, the backbone TMS for freight railroads, and they're actively modernizing from green screens to web interfaces. What stops Wabtec from shipping AI agents on top of RailConnect? That's the exact scenario I describe when I talk about Adobe adding AI features — the incumbent has the distribution, the data, and the customer relationships. The bull case is that Wabtec is a hardware-first conglomerate (locomotives, braking systems) that moves at conglomerate speed, that railroad IT procurement is relationship-driven in ways that favor these founders' family connections, and that the domain expertise gap — FRA regulatory requirements, interchange rules, carrier-specific terminology — is deep enough to buy Kaaro three to five years of runway before an incumbent catches up. I buy maybe half of that argument. The domain moat is real but it's a speed advantage, not a structural one. Metropolis works as an analogy because Metropolis owns and operates parking infrastructure — physical assets that can't be replicated by software. Kaaro owns no physical infrastructure. It's a software layer sitting on someone else's railroad. The physical moat belongs to CPKC and CN, not to Kaaro.

The capital picture is genuinely tight — $500K from a16z Speedrun, two founders, three railroad customers in the pipeline. The North American freight rail market is structurally concentrated: seven Class I railroads generate most of the ton-miles, and ~600 short-lines fill the gaps. That concentration means you don't need to blitzscale — you need to close a handful of enterprise deals with long procurement cycles. Sai and Gautham aren't trying to hire fifty people and burn through a Series A; they're trying to get CPKC from "working with" to "paying us" while keeping the lights on. That discipline is correct for this market. But the flip side of concentration is dependency: if CPKC doesn't convert, Kaaro's pipeline shrinks by a third.

If I'm wrong about this being a pass, here's what would have to be true: the AI agent layer for railroad operations becomes a genuine platform primitive — a new data and intelligence layer that sits between the legacy TMS and the operational workforce — and Kaaro's early relationships and domain data compound into an unassailable position before Wabtec wakes up. The CPKC merger (April 2023) did create unprecedented operational complexity across a Canada-US-Mexico network, and that complexity might be the wedge that makes an AI overlay indispensable rather than nice-to-have. If Kaaro captures the tribal knowledge of railroad operations at scale — the exception-handling patterns, the contact networks, the billing edge cases that currently live in people's heads — that data asset could become genuinely defensible. But "could become" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The founding team's Toma pedigree and railroad family ties are the strongest signals here. The category mispricing is real but moderate. The AI kill test from Wabtec is the most serious structural risk.
