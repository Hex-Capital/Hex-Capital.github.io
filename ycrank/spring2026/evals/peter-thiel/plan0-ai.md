# PLAN0 AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

PLAN0 AI is the canonical shape of a company I pass on without effort: a definite-sounding vision ("the CoStar or Bloomberg of construction," per the YC page) wrapped around a thesis ? AI parses architectural plans faster than humans ? that six other named startups are already executing in the same Robotics & Automation News February 2026 benchmark. Arash Barati has a real story to tell investors, but PLAN0 AI's highest dimension is its narrative conviction and its lowest is the one that actually matters in my framework: there is no secret. The CEO comes from Drake Real Estate Partners and Explora Ventures, which buys him buyer access; it does not buy him a monopoly.

The Monopoly Secret ladder is where PLAN0 AI fails most decisively. Palantir's secret was that intelligence agencies would let proprietary software ingest their classified workflows if the entity ratio between humans and machines was correctly designed ? a thesis the establishment rejected for years. Facebook's secret was that real-identity networks would dominate anonymous ones at the moment Friendster and MySpace looked unbeatable. Wish, my anti-anchor, had no secret at all ? just discount e-commerce competing on price with Amazon, ending in a 90% crash from a $14B IPO. PLAN0 AI sits adjacent to Wish on this ladder: Togal.AI claims 98% takeoff accuracy at $299/user/month, Beam delivers in 2-3 days, Kreo undercuts at $35/month, and Provision has already raised $7M USD specifically for "AI co-pilots for pre-construction estimates." The pitch deck's "competitive landscape" slide is the slide ? six funded entrants pursuing identical theses is the textbook definition of a market with no secret. PLAN0 AI's "quant finance inspired" 24-month cost forecast is a methodological flourish, not a contrarian truth ? Bloomberg-style commodity forecasting is consensus analysis in every quant shop, applied here with no proprietary data feed that CoStar, Autodesk, Procore, or Trimble do not already control.

The Definite Conviction ladder is where PLAN0 AI's CEO earns his only structural credit. SpaceX is the high rung ? a specific plan to land rocket boosters when aerospace consensus said it was physically impossible. Anduril sits at the mid rung ? a concrete defense-hardware thesis from Palmer Luckey's prior Oculus prehistory. The low rung is the indefinite YC AI-CRM wrapper iterating toward product-market fit. PLAN0 AI lands between mid and low: Arash Barati's "Bloomberg of construction" framing is genuinely definite ? he can describe what success looks like ? and his Drake Real Estate Partners economist background gives the forecasting layer authentic provenance. But Shervin Barati, the CTO, has no verifiable public footprint, no GitHub, no LinkedIn confirmed, and the dossier flags likely family-relationship as the only evidence of co-founder prehistory. "Founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together ? otherwise they're just rolling dice." Brothers count, but I cannot verify the technical half of this team executes on the definite plan the CEO articulates.

Power Law magnitude is structurally capped here. SpaceX is the high rung ? launch and reusability creating a category that is now worth $350B. Lyft is my mid-rung override ? the #2 in ride-sharing was still fund-relevant because the category generated hundreds of billions. The low rung is any vertical-SaaS niche tool. PLAN0 AI is pinned closer to the low rung by the GII Research figure of $5.65B preconstruction software TAM by 2032 across the entire category, fragmenting across at least six AI estimating entrants plus Procore, Autodesk, Trimble. Even monopolistic capture of this category produces a $1-3B outcome, not a fund-returner. Distribution & Sales Architecture sits between mid and low rungs: the high rung is PayPal embedding in eBay listings unprompted; the low rung is "build it and they will come" with paid ads. PLAN0 AI has Arash Barati's family-office and real estate PE network ? real but unproven ? and zero customer logos, no Product Hunt listing, no press hits beyond the YC page itself. Physical-World Moat: General Matter, with its $900M DOE contract and HALEU enrichment, is the high rung; Palantir's deep embedding is mid; an AI software wrapper is the low rung. PLAN0 AI is unambiguously the low rung ? computer vision over PDF plan files is a software layer a well-funded competitor rebuilds in months, exactly what Provision is already doing with its $7M head start.

PLAN0 AI's 36-month forecast: it reaches $1-3M ARR among mid-market general contractors, gets acqui-hired by Procore, Autodesk, or CoStar for $30-80M, or alternatively shuts down after Provision out-distributes it. The mechanism is straightforward ? in a category with six funded entrants and four incumbent data-owners, the structural ceiling is a feature acquisition, not an independent monopoly. This is the cleantech pattern recurring in proptech AI: founders swept up by the social trend of "AI-powered everything" rather than a hard contrarian analysis of why this specific vertical resists winner-take-all dynamics.

The strongest objection is the Lyft override: when the underlying category compounds fast enough, even the #2 player produces fund-relevant returns, and Arash Barati's investor-network distribution into real estate PE ? a buyer segment Togal and Kreo do not address ? could carve out a CoStar-adjacent data business worth multiple billions. CoStar itself trades at over $30B market cap on exactly this thesis applied to commercial real estate. The mechanism is real and stings. But it dies on the dossier: PLAN0 AI does not own the underlying cost data feeds ? CoStar, Autodesk, and Trimble do ? and "Bloomberg of construction" without a proprietary data acquisition path is the indefinite version of the strategy. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: PLAN0 AI signs an exclusive data-licensing or co-development agreement with a top-10 ENR general contractor or a major real estate PE firm within 12 months, creating a proprietary dataset Provision and Togal cannot replicate. Without that, the ceiling is acquisition.
