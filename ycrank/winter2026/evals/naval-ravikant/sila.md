# Sila -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything about timing. "Agentic team chat" is not a contrarian bet in early 2026 — it is the most consensus category in enterprise software. Glue raised $20M in October 2025 with David Sacks as co-founder, pursuing identical positioning. Dust has $7.3M ARR building AI agents alongside existing messaging tools. Slack shipped Agentforce. Microsoft Teams has Copilot with agent capabilities reaching hundreds of millions of users. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. The asymmetric upside comes from being right when the crowd is wrong — not from entering a market where five well-funded teams are already racing toward the same thesis. The crowd is not wrong about AI transforming team communication. The crowd is already building it.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Both founders are Cornell CS 2025 graduates who built an AI chatbot deployed across 16 university classes and co-founded an AI course marketplace called saiera. That demonstrates competence in building AI products — which is a teachable, increasingly commoditized skill. What it does not demonstrate is non-transferable knowledge about how teams communicate, why enterprise messaging architectures fail, or what the structural problem with Slack actually is at the protocol level. Their thesis — that messaging volume is overwhelming and AI should participate as first-class teammates — is an observation any knowledge worker could make after a week on Slack. Williams didn't just observe that people wanted to communicate publicly; he had spent years on Blogger understanding the compression of thought into new formats. The gap between "I noticed Slack is noisy" and "I understand communication architecture deeply enough to rebuild it from first principles" is the gap between a pitch deck insight and specific knowledge.

The leverage architecture has potential but remains theoretical. "AI Teammates" with defined roles operating 24/7 inside a messaging workspace could create genuine labor leverage — one human coordinating with multiple AI agents to produce output that previously required a team. That's a real leverage mechanism if executed well. But the same leverage mechanism is available to every competitor in this space. Glue offers MCP-connected AI within chat threads. Dust offers configurable AI agents connected to company tools. The leverage is inherent to the category, not proprietary to Sila. When I backed Notion, the leverage was in the product's unique compound document model — not just "productivity software with AI." The architecture of the tool itself was the moat. Here, I don't see what makes Sila's implementation of AI-in-chat structurally superior to the half-dozen other teams building the same thing with similar LLM capabilities.

The strongest bull case would require me to believe three things simultaneously: that the incumbents genuinely cannot rebuild their messaging architectures due to innovator's dilemma dynamics, that Sila's founders will out-execute Glue (which has Sacks's distribution network and $20M) despite having no comparable resources or enterprise relationships, and that being young and technically capable with no enterprise baggage is itself a form of specific knowledge about what communication should become. There is a version of this story that works — where Slack and Teams are structurally paralyzed by their installed base, Glue makes the wrong architectural choices, and two fast-moving technical founders build something so much better that teams switch despite enormous friction. But that scenario requires Sila to be not just good but categorically better, in a domain where the founders have no demonstrated edge. The LinkedIn profiles still listing saiera as their current company adds a minor but real concern about commitment and focus. This is a category where I want founders who have been obsessing about team communication for years, not founders who pivoted from education AI into the hottest enterprise category of the moment.

This is a pass. Two capable technical founders building in a consensus category against well-funded competitors, without the domain-specific knowledge that would make me believe they see something the rest of the market doesn't. The market is real. The timing is real. But the founders' edge relative to this particular problem is not differentiated enough for me to write a check with my own money. I want the founder who worked at Slack for five years, left in frustration because they understood exactly what was architecturally broken, and is now building the thing they couldn't build inside the incumbent. That founder isn't here.
