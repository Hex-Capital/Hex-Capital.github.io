# Veriad -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Veriad: this is a company where nobody is going to tell you the idea is crazy. AI-powered marketing compliance for large enterprises — that's a sentence any investor can parse in five seconds and nod along to. There's a published TAM, a growing CAGR, multiple funded competitors already in the space, and a clear procurement path. Sedric has raised $22 million, Haast has $7.2 million from Airtree, Red Marker was acquired after raising $37 million. When I look at a market where that many players are already well-capitalized and building overlapping solutions, and where the core capability is applying LLMs to check content against rule sets — something any competent engineering team can prototype in a weekend — I don't feel a jolt of energy. I feel the absence of one. The most promising companies are not popular and they're difficult to assess. Veriad is popular and straightforward to assess. That's a problem for how I invest.

I always want to hear the founder's story before the business. What I can piece together about Rohan and Anton is that they're professionally accomplished — Rohan was founding engineer at CoLoop, self-taught his way from accounting to software engineering, ran nightlife operations in Helsinki, shipped indie hacker projects. Anton closed seven-figure deals at Fortune 500 companies. They connected through CoLoop, noticed a compliance pain point while selling internal tools to enterprises, and decided to build a product around it. That's a perfectly rational origin story for a startup. But it's not the kind of story that keeps me up at night wondering what these founders might become. There's no biographical thread connecting either founder to compliance as a personal mission — no moment where the system failed them, no lived experience with the consequences of regulatory opacity. They identified a market opportunity and they're executing on it. That approach can build a solid business, but I invest in people who are solving problems that are etched into their identity, not people who are solving problems they spotted while selling adjacent software.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Red Marker's absorption into IntelligenceBank truly leaves a standalone market gap, and if Rohan and Anton's enterprise sales DNA lets them close Fortune 500 deals faster than a typical pre-seed team, this could become a meaningful enterprise SaaS company. The "$120M in ad spend covered" traction claim, if real, suggests they're already inside enterprise workflows — that's unusual for a two-person team at this stage. Anton's seven-figure deal experience is genuinely rare among pre-seed founders, and the clear CEO/CCO split gives them a natural division of labor. In a world where marketing compliance complexity keeps accelerating across jurisdictions, the company that builds the deepest rule engine and widest integration layer could create real switching costs. I can see why YC backed them. A disciplined institutional seed fund that values market timing and commercial execution should take this meeting seriously.

But that's not how I invest. When I look at Veriad through my particular lens, I see three things that keep me on the sideline. First, this company doesn't make an invisible system visible — it automates an existing review process for organizations that already have compliance departments and budgets. Affirm revealed hidden credit card fees. Carta made equity transparent to employees who had no idea what their shares were worth. Veriad makes compliance review faster for companies spending $50 million a year on advertising. That's efficiency for the already powerful, not access for the underserved. Second, the "0% false positive rate" claim on their website makes me uncomfortable — in compliance, where the stakes are regulatory fines and brand damage, that's either a carefully scoped metric that doesn't mean what it sounds like it means, or it's the kind of polish-over-substance signal I instinctively distrust. Third, and most fundamentally, the core technology here — wrapping LLMs with compliance rule sets — doesn't have the kind of moat that survives contact with well-funded incumbents. When Sprinklr or Adobe decides to add this as a feature toggle in their existing marketing suite, the standalone value proposition gets compressed fast.

I wish I could find the weirdness in this one, but I can't manufacture conviction where the signal isn't there. This is a competent team pursuing a logical opportunity in a recognized market. For a different kind of investor, that's sufficient. For me, it's a pass.
