# Ventura -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me immediately: when Deloitte is publishing reports quantifying exactly how many basis points of EBIT improvement GenAI will generate for wholesale distributors, you are not looking at a non-consensus idea. You're looking at the consensus crystallized into a consulting slide deck. Ventura is building AI agents that automate quoting and order entry for industrial distributors -- and so is Distro, and Endeavor AI, and Go Autonomous, and Parspec is adjacent, and SAP and Oracle are already shipping AI copilots into their ERP suites. Four-plus funded competitors, an obvious pain point, an enormous TAM that every pitch deck leads with. This is the exact pattern I warn about: when the market is "hot" and the value proposition is obvious, you're buying narrative at market price. The returns have been priced in before the first check clears.

The founders are genuinely strong, and that's what makes this evaluation interesting rather than a quick pass. Swen Koller already built and exited a company -- bravo -- doing essentially the same thing (digitizing ordering for wholesalers) in the food vertical. He knows the customer, the ERP pain, the sales cycle. Jack Collins is a serious ML engineer: 2.4K-star open source LLM framework, founding engineer at a company that hit $7M ARR in 14 months, nine years of production NLP work. These are competent, credible people with directly relevant experience. The "sane" side of my equation is well-satisfied. But a sane person with a sane idea is my least interesting quadrant -- it means the founders are legible, the opportunity is legible, and every other investor in the market can see exactly the same thing. When I backed Solana at five cents a token, Anatoly Yakovenko was a Qualcomm systems engineer proposing a new Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter. Most people thought he was crazy. Nobody thinks "automate data entry for distributors" is crazy.

My bigger structural concern is that this fails the AI kill test -- in reverse. I normally ask "does AI commoditize this business or strengthen it?" But Ventura IS the AI. Strip away the LLM capabilities and there's no company left. The dossier is admirably honest about this: "The core technology -- LLM-based document parsing, product matching, and ERP integration -- uses generally available AI capabilities." So the moat must come from somewhere else -- per-customer data accumulation and nine ERP integrations. I've seen this movie before. Integration depth creates real switching costs for individual accounts, but it doesn't prevent competitors from entering. And a two-person team maintaining nine bidirectional ERP integrations is a fragility flag, not a moat signal. When SAP ships native AI quoting -- and they will, because it increases platform stickiness -- Ventura's "live within days" deployment advantage evaporates against an incumbent that's already inside the customer's infrastructure. This is a software company whose moat is software, which is exactly the category I've spent the last two years arguing is dissolving.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I owe it to founders this credible. If Koller's distribution relationships from bravo translate to fast customer acquisition, and if Collins' engineering enables genuinely superior automation accuracy, Ventura could accumulate distributor-specific data -- pricing histories, catalog matching patterns, customer relationship context -- that creates real per-account lock-in before incumbents or competitors can replicate it. Industrial distribution is a $7.86 trillion market that is genuinely technologically backward, and the "live within days" claim, if real, represents a deployment speed advantage that matters enormously in a market where ERP implementations take months. Koller running the same playbook he ran with bravo but adding AI on top is, in theory, the "cherry on top" model I advocate -- a great distribution business enhanced by AI rather than constituted by AI. The problem is that bravo was itself a SaaS tool, not a distribution operation. The company doesn't own trucks, warehouses, or physical relationships. It's software all the way down. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews and scheduling operations that software optimizes but cannot replace. Nobody can replicate Craftwork's operations with a better model. Anyone can replicate Ventura's quote parsing.

This is not creating a new primitive. It's an application layer built on existing ERP platforms and generally available LLM APIs. There's no protocol, no standard, no infrastructure that other companies build on. The ceiling is a nice vertical SaaS business -- and I don't believe nice vertical SaaS businesses will survive the next three platform shifts. The narrative can theoretically expand from "AI quoting" to "AI operating system for industrial commerce," but that expansion path runs directly through SAP, Oracle, and Epicor's product roadmaps. I respect the founders and I understand the market opportunity. But this is a feature waiting to be absorbed by incumbents with distribution, which is exactly the outcome I expect for AI-first startups where the AI is obvious and the structural moat is absent.
