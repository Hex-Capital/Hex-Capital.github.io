# shortkit -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about ShortKit: it might be the most legible pitch I've seen this batch. Former YouTube Shorts infrastructure engineer leaves Google, builds an SDK that lets any app embed a TikTok-style video feed. Every investor in the Valley would hear that sentence and nod. And that immediate legibility is precisely the problem for me. The most promising companies I've backed were difficult to assess and deeply unpopular. SpaceX was dismissed as lunacy. Crusoe Energy sounded like a joke — mining Bitcoin on gas flares? ShortKit sounds like a perfectly rational product extension of one engineer's career at Google. Nobody would call this crazy. Nobody would argue about whether the market exists. And when nobody argues, when the idea slots neatly into an existing mental model, the contrarian premium that creates outsized returns simply isn't there.

When I evaluate a founder, I start with their story — not the business, the person. Tell me how you got here, and I mean the real version. With Michael Seleman, I see six years of deep expertise building YouTube Shorts infrastructure, which is genuinely impressive and rare. That's a credential, and a strong one. But a credential is not a calling. I don't see the biographical thread where this problem became personal, where some lived experience fused this founder to this specific mission in a way that would sustain them through the inevitable crises — the kind of thread I saw in the Contraline founder's years of obsessive academic research, or in Travis Kalanick's gravitas born from genuine struggle. Neil Bhammar co-founded BusRight as a college capstone project and grew it well, which shows operating competence. But student transportation software to video infrastructure is a pivot that reads as opportunity-seeking rather than problem-driven. I'd want to sit with both of them and ask the real question: why this, why now, why you two — and I suspect the answer would be about market timing and technical capability, not about something that keeps them up at night.

The strongest bull case here is genuine, and I want to be honest about it. Michael's YouTube Shorts experience is the kind of scarce, domain-specific knowledge that's incredibly hard to replicate. If you believe — and it's reasonable to believe — that every consumer app, publisher, and e-commerce platform will want a short-form vertical video feed embedded in their product over the next five years, then ShortKit is perfectly positioned to capture that wave with a founder who literally built the canonical version of the thing at scale. The ML-driven buffering, the codec optimization delivering 2-3x byte reduction, the feed-aware player mechanics — these aren't vaporware features, they're the kind of optimizations that only someone who spent years inside the YouTube Shorts stack would know to build. Add Neil's GTM chops from scaling BusRight to 23 states, and you have a complementary pairing that makes structural sense. If they execute well and the embeddable short-form video market materializes at scale, this could be a solid infrastructure business with real switching costs.

But "solid infrastructure business" isn't what I invest for. I'm looking for companies that create economic access for people who lack it, that make invisible systems visible, that operate in spaces others are afraid to enter. ShortKit helps well-resourced app companies add another engagement feature to their products. It doesn't unlock economic opportunity for an underserved population. It doesn't make an opaque system transparent. It doesn't operate in a stigmatized or regulated category that creates natural moats. It's a developer tool in a competitive landscape where Mux has $174 million in funding and $46 million in revenue, BytePlus has the full weight of ByteDance's technology, and any of these players could build a short-form-specific SDK extension if the segment proves valuable enough. The competitive dynamics here make me nervous — not because ShortKit can't compete today, but because the specialization moat is thin when incumbents with established developer relationships decide to prioritize the same niche.

I also have real questions about the co-founder relationship that the dossier doesn't answer. No shared university, no shared employer, no public story about how Neil and Michael found each other. After HQ Trivia taught me that even the right product at the right time gets destroyed by founder misalignment, I probe this relentlessly. A two-person team where one engineer carries the entire technical depth and the other handles business development — that's a configuration where misalignment becomes existential fast. I'd need to understand how they resolve disagreements, what happens when the product roadmap conflicts with what a customer wants, whether they've been through a genuine crisis together. Without that story, I'm flying blind on what I consider one of the most important dimensions.

This is a pass for me. Not because it's a bad company — it's a reasonable bet in a growing market with a technically credentialed founder. But it doesn't activate my framework in any dimension. It's consensus, it's legible, it serves well-resourced customers, and it operates in a space where the barriers to competition are engineering effort rather than regulatory complexity or network effects. I wish them well, and I suspect they'll find investors who love this pitch. I'm just not one of them.
