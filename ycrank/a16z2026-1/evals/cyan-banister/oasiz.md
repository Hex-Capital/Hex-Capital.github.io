# Oasiz -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Oasiz: a TikTok-style vertical feed for AI-generated casual games, backed by a16z Speedrun, with 6.5 million organic TikTok views and 230,000 plays. And I feel a very specific feeling — the same one I felt watching HQ Trivia explode. That company was the right time, the right product, and it was "transformative and could have been how we consume media in the future had they played their cards right." It failed. Not because the market was wrong, but because consumer attention products without infrastructure underneath are sandcastles. GameCrush and IRL taught me the same lesson. Oasiz's early viral traction is real — but viral traction on TikTok is the easiest metric to generate and the hardest to convert into something durable. When I look at the AI game generation layer — built on "generally available LLMs and generative models" per the dossier — I see a product that Rosebud AI ($15M from a16z and Khosla), CodeWisp, and Playabl.ai can all replicate. The social distribution layer is the bet, but it hasn't reached the compounding phase yet, and 21 App Store ratings in two months tells me the flywheel isn't spinning.

Abel Dagne is clearly talented — a Stanford student, Coca-Cola Scholar, Google STEP intern who built a prior app to 500,000 downloads and #1 on the App Store social charts. That's execution evidence, and I respect it. Jonathan Dinh brings genuine domain credibility as a top-0.01% competitive mobile gamer and Berkeley AI researcher from the ROAR lab. But when I ask my first question — "tell me their story, not the business" — what I get back from this dossier is credentials, not crucible. Where is the biographical moment that made Abel or Jonathan incapable of NOT building Oasiz? A young founder who saw that AI tools can now generate games in seconds and that distribution hasn't caught up — that's a market observation, and it's a correct one. But it's career optimization dressed as a mission. The dossier identifies "no shared prior employer or university" between the co-founders, and I have no information about how they found each other or why this particular team was forged for this particular problem. The absence of that narrative isn't neutral for me — it's data.

The space itself is the opposite of uncomfortable. AI gaming is consensus-attractive in 2026. Multiple accelerator-backed companies are building nearly identical tools. a16z is funding both Oasiz (through Speedrun) and Rosebud AI ($15M Series A). No investor shifts in their chair hearing about an AI-powered social gaming platform — they lean forward because it's the hot category. Compare this to when I wrote my check into Flock Safety, where surveillance cameras made civil liberties advocates nervous, or Anduril, where defense tech was politically toxic. Those were spaces where being the first check required courage. Oasiz requires no courage. The "why now" — AI capability improvements crossing the threshold for functional game generation — is the same thesis every competitor in this space is pitching. That's consensus, not conviction.

When I run my invisible system test, I can't find the hidden structure. What opaque, extractive system is Oasiz exposing? Roblox requiring Lua scripting is a skill barrier, not an information asymmetry that exploits vulnerable people. App Store distribution is already transparent. Game discovery algorithms may be imperfect, but they're not the kind of extractive cartel I look for — not the taxi medallion system that Uber attacked, not the credit card opacity that Affirm exposed. Oasiz is building a better distribution channel for casual games. That's a product improvement in an already-legible market, not a structural revelation. The Jemz virtual currency system introduced in v1.3 is a standard mobile gaming monetization mechanic, not a transparency innovation. I need to see a company making pricing, access, or information visible where it was previously hidden — and I don't see that here.

The strongest bull case: Abel's proven consumer distribution chops are rare at pre-seed. If the creation-plus-distribution-plus-community bundle reaches critical mass before Rosebud or Roblox absorb the value proposition, Oasiz could become the default social layer for AI-generated interactive content — a new platform category. The unconfirmed Atari IP offer, if real, suggests brand-level validation. And the ten-year question isn't absurd: if every person can create interactive experiences as easily as they post a TikTok video, the company that owns that social graph owns something genuinely new. But I've learned the hard way — three times — that "could have been" in consumer entertainment is the most expensive phrase in my portfolio. TikTok could add mini-games with its 1B+ users. Roblox, with 380 million MAU, could ship AI-assisted casual creation and absorb this entire category. The 5,000 playable experiences built in closed beta aren't a moat when the generation tools are commoditized. For this to work, Oasiz would need to build network effects so strong that creators and players couldn't leave, and nothing in the dossier — not the 7,000 user hours, not the 21 App Store ratings — tells me that's happening yet.

Oasiz has a capable young team operating in a real market at a genuine technological inflection. But it lacks what I need to write a check from my own money: a founder whose biography made this problem inevitable, a space that requires contrarian courage to enter, and a hidden system being torn open. What I see instead is a consumer attention product in a consensus-hot category with commoditized technology and nascent network effects — the structural profile of the bets that taught me my most expensive lessons.
