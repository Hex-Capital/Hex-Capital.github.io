# Grade -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Grade is that Lumanu has $20.6 million and Trolley has $44.4 million already deployed in this exact market. That tells me everything I need to know about where this company sits on my framework: nobody is dismissing creator payment infrastructure as impossible or crazy. The incumbents aren't mocking it. The market is validated. The concept is consensus. And when established players endorse the same thesis you're pursuing — when no one at a conference would laugh at your pitch — that's precisely the signal that the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening. My best investments were in companies the experts declared would never work. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing was a fantasy. The food industry said you couldn't make meat without a cow. Grade's competitors are saying "yes, this market is real" and spending tens of millions to prove it. That's not a good sign for a new entrant with $500K.

Let me address the consequence question directly. If Grade succeeds completely — becomes the dominant platform for performance-based creator payments globally — what changes? Marketing teams stop using spreadsheets to pay influencers. Creator payments arrive faster with less manual work. Finance departments get a cleaner invoice. That's a real operational improvement. It is not a transformation of how any industry fundamentally operates. Nobody's life changes at a civilizational level. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The meat industry still runs. Energy is still generated the same way. Healthcare isn't disrupted. We've built a better accounts payable workflow for a specific vertical. The $205 billion creator economy TAM is a large number that obscures the reality: Grade's actual addressable market is the payment processing layer within that economy — a fraction of the headline. And the consequence of winning that layer is a transaction-fee fintech business. That is the textbook case of reducing risk until the reward is proportionally diminished.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. The structural shift from flat-fee creator sponsorships to performance-based compensation is real and analogous to historical transitions — commission-based sales replacing fixed salaries, programmatic advertising replacing manual media buying. If performance-based pay becomes the default for the $1.3 trillion creator economy projected by 2033, the infrastructure layer that automates that link between performance data and payment execution could become essential plumbing. Stripe didn't look like a world-changing company when it was processing small payments for developers — it looked like a better API for something that already existed. The founders here have exceptional problem-person fit: they literally discovered this pain by scaling consumer apps with creators and hitting the spreadsheet wall themselves. Lotanna's trajectory from XPO (influencer fintech) to Creator Check (creator screening) to Grade shows genuine domain compounding. James shipped products that reached hundreds of thousands of users and hit $40K MRR within two months at Pray Screen. The 120% month-over-month growth in payout volume is a real signal. If you believe infrastructure layers in rapidly growing markets compound into category-defining businesses, and that these founders' lived experience gives them distribution advantages the incumbents lack — that's a coherent case.

But I keep returning to the technology question. The dossier states it plainly: "The core technology — connecting performance APIs to a payment disbursement layer — is technically reproducible." Grade routes payments through PayPal, Wise, Venmo, Payoneer, and Revolut. No proprietary payment rails. No novel technology. The differentiator is described as "a product design advantage rather than a deep technical moat." I've learned from painful experience — KiOR taught me that technology achievement without unit economics is a trap, but the inverse lesson is equally important: a workflow tool without technology differentiation is a feature, not a company. Any of Grade's better-funded competitors could add performance-based payment rules to their existing platforms. Lumanu's $2.1 million in revenue with 1,000 customers suggests they're already serving this market. Trolley's $44.4 million in funding gives them the engineering resources to replicate Grade's dashboard in a quarter. When your competitive advantage is UX and not physics, the capital-rich incumbent usually wins.

The founders are capable people building a logical product — and that's exactly what concerns me. They're industry insiders executing the obvious next step in a space they know intimately. Lotanna founded an influencer fintech company, then a creator screening tool, then a creator payment platform. That's not a contrarian leap; that's a product roadmap. I look for founders who bring outsider irreverence to problems the industry says can't be solved. These founders are bringing insider expertise to a problem the industry already knows exists and is already funding solutions for. They've optimized their probability of building a functioning business at the cost of building something that reshapes how value flows in the economy.

These are smart founders who should be working on a bigger problem. The creator economy needs better payment infrastructure the way any growing market needs better tooling — and someone will build it profitably. But "someone will build it profitably" is not a sentence I associate with the investments that define a career or change the world. I pass.
