# Bubble Lab -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a platform dependency that looks existential. Bubble Lab is building a Slack-native AI agent, and Salesforce -- which owns Slack -- has already announced Agentforce 360, explicitly positioning Slack as an "agentic OS" for enterprises. This isn't a theoretical risk. It's a declared strategy from the platform owner. When I invested in companies like Stripe or Harvey, the infrastructure they built on wasn't simultaneously becoming their competitor. Stripe built on the internet; the internet wasn't launching a payments product. Harvey built on GPT; OpenAI wasn't building a law firm. Bubble Lab is building on Slack while Slack is building Bubble Lab's product. The open-source workflow engine and visual builder provide alternative surfaces, but the entire positioning -- "your ops super-employee in Slack" -- is the value proposition. If you remove the Slack-native angle, what's left is an AI workflow automation tool competing against n8n ($253M raised, $40M ARR, $2.5B valuation), Gumloop ($24.5M raised), and Dust ($21.5M raised, ~$7.3M revenue). That's not a structural position; that's a feature fight with 100x better-capitalized competitors.

The market question is where this breaks down for me. "AI agents for workflow automation" is not a non-obvious market -- it's one of the most consensus categories in venture right now. The dossier cites a $24B workflow automation TAM growing at ~10% CAGR, and separately an AI agent market at $7.8B growing at 46% CAGR. These are the kind of static TAM slides I push back on hardest. The numbers are real but they describe a market that every investor already sees. When I look at my best investments -- PagerDuty, Gusto, Checkr -- those were markets other investors dismissed. "Operations alerting" sounded boring. "Payroll for small business" sounded boring. Nobody is dismissing AI workflow automation as boring in 2026. There are 20+ funded competitors building adjacent products. There's no structural inflection unique to Bubble Lab's approach. The "why now" -- LLMs can now orchestrate multi-step tool use -- is the same "why now" for the entire category. That's not an insight; that's a sector thesis.

The bull case I'd want to consider: Slack is genuinely where operations work happens at startups and growth-stage companies, and being native to that context could create a distribution wedge similar to how Stripe won by being native to where developers already worked. The 4,000+ users and named customer testimonials (Dyna Robotics reporting 10+ hours/week saved, PeakMojo reporting 40+ hours of engineering time saved) suggest something is working. Salesforce's AI features are likely oriented toward premium tiers and the Salesforce ecosystem, potentially leaving pricing space for a freemium tool-agnostic agent. If Bubble Lab could build enough switching cost through Pearl's long-term memory and workflow automation before Salesforce fills the gap, there's a path. And the open-source engine at 1.1k stars shows a community forming. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe (1) Salesforce won't build a competitive free/low-cost operations agent for non-Salesforce tools, (2) the Slack distribution wedge creates enough compounding adoption to survive a well-funded competitive field, and (3) the team can execute a B2B enterprise sales motion they've never run before. That's three things that need to go right -- which is my single-miracle test failing.

On execution, I'll give the founders credit: they've shipped a working product with 25+ integrations, obtained SOC 2 Type 1 and CASA Tier 2 certifications (non-trivial compliance work at pre-seed), built an open-source engine with real community engagement, and onboarded named customers with quantified value testimonials. That's solid velocity for a two-person team of recent graduates. Their prior startup gymii.ai won Cornell Tech's 2025 Startup Awards and reached 1,000+ users, demonstrating they can build and ship. But the domain pivot from consumer nutrition tracking to B2B operations automation is a real concern. Neither founder has documented experience in BizOps, RevOps, or enterprise sales. The customers who are giving testimonials -- Dyna Robotics, Corgi, Hattrick Ventures, Insforge, PeakMojo -- are not signal-carrying enterprise logos. When I back founders in B2B infrastructure, I look for people who lived the problem they're solving. Selina Li's JPMorgan quant background gives some exposure to operations-heavy environments, but quant research and RevOps are different worlds.

The core issue is that the underlying capability -- an LLM-powered agent that calls APIs on behalf of users -- is technically reproducible. This is what I'd call "software-aware" territory rather than "software-driven." Remove the AI layer and you have Zapier. Add the AI layer and you have what every automation company is doing right now. The software doesn't create a fundamentally different competitive position; it's an interface improvement on existing workflow automation. A strong product-to-distribution trajectory could overcome this, but the distribution channel here is Slack -- which someone else owns and is actively building competitive features into. The multi-product expansion path isn't clear either. What does Bubble Lab distribute through Pearl beyond more integrations? More integrations is a features roadmap, not a platform strategy.

I'm passing. The market is consensus, the platform risk is structural rather than speculative, the competitive landscape features players with orders of magnitude more capital and traction, and the founding team -- while showing solid execution velocity -- lacks domain expertise in their target buyer. The founders are capable builders who might find something, but the current positioning doesn't show me where the asymmetric returns live.
