# Ontora -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Max Arnold built agentic SAP process mining at Porsche, deployed it across four Volkswagen subsidiaries, and now wants to do it as a startup. That's real. That's not a slide deck — the guy actually shipped enterprise process discovery tooling inside one of the most complex manufacturing supply chains on the planet. If I were evaluating founder-market fit in isolation, I'd give Ontora high marks. But founder-market fit isn't the question I ask first. The question I ask first is: what is the market pricing this at, and is the market wrong? And the answer here is that "AI agents for enterprise operations" is the single most consensus-hot category in the entire 2026 YC batch. Every sophisticated investor at demo day is nodding along to this pitch. There is no mispricing. Ontora is operating in a category where the upside is already baked into the price — AI-disrupts-consulting has been a mainstream thesis since ChatGPT dropped, McKinsey layoffs are front-page news, and Celonis has raised $2.4 billion doing adjacent work. When every other investor is excited, there's no asymmetry left.

Ontora's pitch is "what used to take months and $500K, we do in hours for $50K." That's a 10x cost reduction and a 100x speed improvement — sounds incredible. But that's not a new primitive. That's a faster, cheaper version of something McKinsey has done since the 1930s: interview employees, map processes, deliver recommendations. The output is identical — process maps, automation roadmaps, ROI estimates. The method changed (LLMs instead of 25-year-old MBAs with PowerPoint), but the category didn't. Ontora isn't creating a new behavior that didn't exist before. Nobody will say "let's Ontora our operations" the way people say "Venmo me." When I backed Venmo, peer-to-peer mobile payments literally didn't exist as a behavior — after Venmo, it became a verb. Ontora's "Unified Context Layer" has a whisper of primitive potential — if it became the operational knowledge base that every enterprise AI tool depended on, that would be infrastructure, not an application. But right now that's speculative product roadmap, not demonstrated reality, and the dossier shows one customer testimonial from Vertiv, not a platform ecosystem.

Leon Iwanowitsch brings consulting-domain AI experience from St. Gallen, David Korn brings data pipeline chops from finance, and the three co-founders share a genuine origin story — quitting their jobs after a trip to Stockholm's fastest-scaling startups. That's a team that made a real decision together, not a synthetic co-founder match. But here's where my sane-person-insane-idea framework cuts against Ontora: these are sane people with a sane idea. "Let's automate management consulting with AI" is exactly what every smart operations-minded MBA is thinking right now. There's no moment where I hear the pitch and think "that's fucking crazy but what if they're right?" It's the opposite — I hear it and think "obviously, yes, of course someone should do that, and ten other teams will too." The ratio is wrong. I need a reasonable person doing something unreasonable. Ontora is a reasonable team doing something entirely reasonable.

Ontora fails my AI kill test more cleanly than almost any company I've seen this batch. The dossier states it plainly: "The core capability (LLM-driven structured interviews + synthesis) could be replicated by any team with access to frontier LLMs." Celonis, with $2.4 billion in capital and $771 million in revenue, can bolt on conversational process discovery as a feature next quarter. Microsoft can pipe this through Copilot. Accenture can build it internally and bundle it with their existing consulting relationships. Ontora has no physical-world operations, no protocol-level lock-in, no network effects that compound with scale. Compare this to Metropolis — AI-powered parking management that I seeded early, now valued at roughly $5 billion. Metropolis owns relationships with physical parking infrastructure that no LLM can replicate from a cloud server. Ontora's entire moat is "we prompted the LLM better and built a nice synthesis layer." That's a feature, not a company. The business model conflict argument — McKinsey won't cannibalize their $500K engagements — is the same argument people made about newspapers and online advertising. The newspapers didn't build the replacement, but Google and Facebook did. The question isn't whether McKinsey builds Ontora. It's whether well-capitalized tech companies do. And they will.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Ontora's consulting-replacement positioning mirrors how Teamshares approached small business acquisition — taking an old-world industry and applying a fundamentally different operational model. Max Arnold's Porsche deployment is genuine pre-seed validation that most AI startups can't claim. The incumbents face real business model conflict: automating a $500K engagement into a $50K product destroys the billable-hour model that funds McKinsey's marble lobbies. And if Ontora aggregates anonymized process data across hundreds of enterprises, they could build a benchmarking moat no competitor could replicate from scratch. That's real. But Teamshares owns actual small businesses with real employees, real revenue, and real physical assets — moats AI can't touch. Ontora owns a software pipeline that any well-funded team can rebuild in six months. The Slack exception — where I paid consensus prices because the primitive was genuine — doesn't apply because Ontora isn't creating a new behavioral category. It's executing faster in an existing one. The one condition that flips me to Invest: if Ontora demonstrates that its aggregated operational knowledge across 50+ enterprises creates a proprietary benchmarking and context layer that competitors cannot replicate without doing 50+ deployments first — a genuine data network effect, not a theoretical one — then the structural durability score changes everything. Show me that moat actually forming, not projected.

Ontora's capital posture is appropriate for pre-seed — three founders, lean team, $50K engagement pricing that could generate revenue without additional fundraising — but the 6-12 month enterprise sales cycle the dossier flags is a genuine tension with YC-stage runway. No revenue data is disclosed, and zero open job positions suggest they're early. On capital discipline alone, Ontora is neutral — neither burning recklessly nor demonstrating the "last money I'll ever raise" mentality I look for.

The dimensions that lock this verdict are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability. Ontora is a well-executed AI application in the hottest category in venture, priced at market enthusiasm, with no structural moat against commoditization. The founders are competent but the bet isn't contrarian. I need the market to be wrong about something for the risk/reward to work, and the market isn't wrong here — it's correctly excited about AI-driven consulting automation, and it's correctly pricing dozens of companies that will attempt it. Pass.
