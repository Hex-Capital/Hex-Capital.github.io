# Snapp Stats -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Shawn Tsao co-founded Caviar and rode it through a $410M exit to DoorDash — a genuine accomplishment that places him in rare company among repeat founders. But Caviar's insight was operational, not technological: aggregate restaurant delivery in a geography before the market consolidates. That playbook does not transfer to AI-powered sports video analysis, where the question is not "can you out-execute on logistics?" but "do you possess a technological discontinuity that makes the existing approach obsolete?" Snapp Stats' tech stack — Python, TensorFlow, LangChain, Crew.AI on standard cloud infrastructure — is the bill of materials for a thousand AI wrappers, not the foundation of a monopoly. The "Large Visual Memory Model" terminology dresses up what is functionally a RAG pipeline over sports footage. GPT-4o and Gemini already process video multimodally. When I ask "what important truth does Snapp Stats know that few agree with?", the dossier returns silence. "AI can extract insights from sports video" is not a secret — it is the operating thesis of WSC Sports ($136M raised), the strategic direction of Genius Sports (GeniusIQ), and the explicit partnership strategy of the English Premier League with Microsoft.

The dual B2C/B2B positioning reveals the deeper structural problem. Snapp Stats simultaneously describes itself as a "personalized sports feed" for fans and a "Superhuman Sports AI Teammate" for coaches and athletes. At pre-seed with six people, pursuing two fundamentally different products for two fundamentally different buyer personas is not ambition — it is the absence of a definite plan. A founder with genuine conviction about which market to monopolize first would never split focus this way. The correct approach — which Facebook demonstrated by achieving 100% penetration at Harvard before expanding to Columbia, then Stanford, then all Ivy League schools — is to identify the smallest possible market you can dominate completely. "Athletes and colleges" is not a beachhead; it is a vague gesture toward a fragmented, heterogeneous market where every college program has different needs, different budgets, and different decision-making processes. Tsao built Caviar by starting with a handful of San Francisco restaurants. The strategic clarity that made Caviar work is absent here.

Snapp Stats triggers my cleantech pattern recognition from *Zero to One* Chapter 13 — entrepreneurs swept up by the AI wave rather than possessing a specific, non-obvious insight about sports that AI merely enables. The competitive landscape confirms this: WSC Sports, SportsVisio, VueMotion, CoachNow, Onform, and every major sports data company are converging on AI-powered video analysis from different directions. When I count more than three funded entities pursuing the same thesis, there is no secret, and the market structure guarantees that value creation gets competed away. The Wish investment is my most expensive lesson in this pattern — a company entering a market with multiple well-funded competitors and no structural moat. Wish IPO'd at $14 billion and collapsed over 90%. The mechanism was identical: no proprietary technology, no network effects, no monopoly path, just execution speed in a race where speed alone cannot create defensibility.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Alex Marshall's Pentagon AI background (building a model the US Air Force used to allocate $160 billion annually) translates into sports-specific technical moats; that the "persistent, searchable memory" across sports footage will create a compounding data advantage that general-purpose models cannot replicate; and that college athletic programs, newly flush with NIL-driven analytics demand, will adopt Snapp Stats deeply enough to create switching costs before WSC Sports or Genius Sports pivot downmarket. Each of these is plausible individually. Together, they require a chain of contingencies that looks more like indefinite optimism — hoping the future breaks your way — than the definite conviction I fund. Marshall's AI credentials are real, but Genentech and Pentagon experience does not constitute a sports domain secret. The data moat is theoretical and does not exist today. And the incumbents' "business model conflict" preventing them from serving coaches is exactly the kind of argument that dissolves the moment a $670M-revenue public company decides the segment is worth pursuing.

The power-law arithmetic seals the pass. Even the optimistic TAM estimate ($49.92 billion by 2033 across all AI in sports) is fragmented across betting data, broadcast automation, performance analytics, fan engagement, and dozens of other sub-segments. Snapp Stats' addressable slice — agentic video Q&A for coaches and athletes — is a fraction of this, and the realistic best outcome is a modest acquisition by one of the public incumbents, not independent market dominance at scale. A $200 million exit would validate Tsao's execution ability and return capital to investors, but it is structurally irrelevant to my portfolio. The power law demands that each investment could return the entire fund; Snapp Stats cannot reach that threshold in a fragmented market without monopoly structure, competing against public companies with combined revenues exceeding $1.7 billion.
