# Mochatrade -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Schwab, Fidelity, and Interactive Brokers cannot offer perpetual futures on US stocks. The instrument doesn't exist under US securities regulation. Binance and Bybit could, but they've never cared about the Indian retail trader who holds one of 200M+ demat accounts and wants leveraged NVDA exposure without a US brokerage account. Mochatrade sits in a structural gap where regulated incumbents are agents of their own compliance framework and crypto incumbents are agents of their existing crypto-native user base. Neither can move here without violating their principal's interests. This is the exact market structure that allows startups to exist -- the principal-agent problem weaponized as a distribution wedge. CoinList exploited the same gap: securities lawyers were gatekeepers to token launches, and no incumbent had the incentive to remove them. Mochatrade is attempting the same removal -- US brokerage accounts as gatekeepers to leveraged US equity exposure for 200M+ Indian traders.

Utkarsh Sinha's specific knowledge is real but directional. He built Martian Wallet from an Aptos hackathon to 2M+ installs and a Pontem acquisition -- that's demonstrated expertise in scaling self-custodial crypto products to emerging-market users. Parth Maheshwari co-built the same product at the technical layer. The knowledge that matters at Mochatrade's current stage isn't derivatives infrastructure engineering -- it's the ability to get Indian retail traders to trust a self-custodial crypto product, navigate local-currency on-ramp complexity, and acquire users through crypto-native channels. Utkarsh has done this before with Martian. The infrastructure layer is increasingly commoditized (Hyperliquid's HIP-3 enables anyone to deploy equity perps), which means Mochatrade's specific knowledge advantage is distribution, not technology. That's a legitimate moat at pre-seed -- Ostium's $24M in funding from General Catalyst and Jump Crypto doesn't buy them INR on-ramp integrations or Indian crypto Twitter distribution. But it's a narrower moat than I'd like. If Utkarsh had spent five years at a derivatives desk before building this, the specific knowledge density would be categorically different.

Mochatrade's leverage architecture passes the basic test -- a perpetual futures exchange is code leverage, zero marginal cost per additional trader, and the self-custodial design eliminates the custodial operations overhead that drags on centralized exchanges. Three founders, no job postings, no evidence of a bloated roadmap. The compound interest question is where it gets interesting: every trader who joins adds liquidity, tighter spreads attract more traders, and local-currency on-ramp integrations compound as switching costs for users. But Mochatrade must reach critical liquidity mass before this flywheel spins, and they're competing against Hyperliquid's existing $55M/month revenue engine and Trade[XYZ]'s $100B+ cumulative volume on stock perps. The leverage is structurally sound but the ignition problem is severe.

Utkarsh and Parth's co-founder relationship -- IIT Kharagpur through Columbia through Martian Wallet -- is years of shared history under real conditions, not a startup weekend pairing. Utkarsh had an exit; he chose to start again in the same ecosystem, building something he and his co-founders personally wanted as Indian traders locked out of US equity leverage. That's principal behavior. Chetan Manda's connection to the other two is undocumented, and his background as Director of Product at Sprinklr is enterprise SaaS experience -- transferable, not specific to crypto or trading. The dossier notes the team's "personal" motivation: they were traders from India who wanted access to global markets with leverage. I weight revealed preference over stated motivation, and Utkarsh's choice to re-enter crypto founder mode after a successful exit is a strong reveal.

The bull case for Mochatrade is compelling on paper: India's 200M demat accounts, 62% retail-driven NSE F&O volume, CFTC exploring onshore perps legitimacy, RWA perps volume up 162% month-over-month -- and a founder who has already scaled a self-custodial crypto product to millions of users in this exact demographic. The access democratization pattern is textbook. But the bear case has teeth. SEBI data shows 91% of Indian retail F&O traders lose money, India imposes 30% crypto gains tax plus 1% TDS, and regulators have every incentive to crack down on offshore leveraged equity derivatives targeting their retail population. Ostium, dYdX, and Hyperliquid collectively have over $100M in capital or revenue behind them. Mochatrade's geographic niche is real but defensible only if Hyperliquid or Ostium deprioritize India -- and there's no structural reason they would. The condition that would flip me from Invest to Pass: evidence that any well-capitalized competitor is actively building INR on-ramp infrastructure and India-specific distribution, or a SEBI enforcement action targeting crypto-based equity derivatives.

Mochatrade earns the narrowest Invest from me, driven primarily by the access democratization pattern -- this is the same structural play as CoinList and Polymarket, removing a gatekeeper between millions of people and a financial instrument they want but cannot access -- and by Utkarsh's demonstrated ability to scale crypto distribution in exactly this market. The specific knowledge gap on derivatives infrastructure keeps this from being a conviction bet. At pre-seed valuation, the asymmetry works: I can only lose 1x, and if Mochatrade becomes the default gateway for Indian retail into leveraged US equities, the return profile is nonlinear. Angel bets are structurally convex. Small check.
