# DroneTector -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The counter-drone market is one of the most consensus investment themes in defense tech right now. Ukraine, Gatwick, Red Sea -- every LP dinner, every CNBC segment, every pitch day features someone talking about the drone threat. When every investor in the room nods along, I get nervous. The question isn't whether hostile drones are a problem -- obviously they are. The question is whether this specific company, at this stage, represents the kind of non-consensus founder-idea pairing that produces outsized returns or just a well-credentialed team surfing a wave that a dozen better-capitalized competitors are already riding.

Matthew Moore is deeply, specifically credible. He didn't read about millimeter-wave radar -- he built the 94 GHz system during his PhD and published the peer-reviewed work validating micro-Doppler signatures for drone classification. That's not "domain experience," that's "I am one of maybe twenty people on the planet who have actually done this specific thing." Thomas Doherty brings an Oxford DPhil in atomic physics and previously co-founded Quantum Fabrix, which raised £2M from Paul Graham himself. Frances de Mas adds automated reasoning expertise relevant to the signal processing challenge. The "sane" in my framework is strong here -- arguably stronger than most YC hardware companies I see. But the "insane" is missing. Building radar to detect hostile drones in 2026 is not insane. It's the most obvious application of this technology imaginable. The Doherty situation also gives me pause -- he was CEO of QFX until a new CEO was appointed in October 2025, and now he's CTO of DroneTector in the W26 batch. That timeline is tight, and residual obligations to a Paul Graham-backed company don't just evaporate.

Where DroneTector genuinely activates my framework is on the structural moat question. This is custom radar hardware. AI cannot replicate a physical millimeter-wave antenna array. Software-only counter-drone companies like Dedrone -- which Axon acquired -- rely on RF signal detection that fails against autonomous drones flying without radio emissions. DroneTector's hardware approach solves a physics problem that no amount of code can solve. This is my Craftwork pattern: the physical-world operational complexity that pure-software competitors cannot replicate, and where AI actually deepens the moat by improving the classification algorithms running on proprietary hardware. If I apply my "cherry on top" test -- would this be a good business without AI? -- the answer is yes. The radar detects drones regardless. AI just makes the classification better. That's the right structure.

The bull case is real: the specific technical approach (W-band millimeter-wave micro-Doppler) IS differentiated within the counter-drone space. Echodyne uses lower frequencies. Robin Radar uses X-band. Dedrone is software-only. DroneTector's ground-up design for nano-drone detection at 94 GHz could become the standard approach for a specific, critical detection gap that existing systems miss. If they can get through the defense procurement valley of death -- and NATO DIANA plus DASA relationships provide at least a foothold -- the proprietary micro-Doppler signature database they'd build from real-world deployments could compound into genuine data lock-in. Defense certifications and security clearances would create switching costs that no competitor can shortcut. The $729M detection sub-segment growing at 29% CAGR provides real economic tailwind.

But here's what keeps me from getting excited: this isn't a new primitive. DroneTector isn't creating a layer that other companies build on. It's a product -- a very sophisticated product, but a product. When I backed Helium, I was betting on a decentralized wireless protocol that would become infrastructure for an entire ecosystem. When I backed Aleo, I was betting on zero-knowledge proofs as a privacy primitive. DroneTector makes radar boxes. Good radar boxes, potentially the best radar boxes for this specific application, but the company doesn't create compounding network effects or become infrastructure that other businesses depend on. The narrative has real magnitude -- "we make airports and battlefields safe from drone attacks" scales to every airport and military base on earth -- but it doesn't have infinite elasticity. It's bounded by the hardware form factor and the defense procurement machine. And at pre-seed with zero revenue, three people, and competitors sitting on $195M (Echodyne) and $221M cash (DroneShield), the execution risk of surviving defense sales cycles long enough to matter is substantial. I respect the physics. I respect the founders. But this is a sane team with a sane idea in a hot market, and that's not where I make my money.
