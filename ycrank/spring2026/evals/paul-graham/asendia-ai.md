# Asendia AI -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately is the surface area. Four distinct AI agents — sourcing, SMS outreach, video interviewing, phone screening — plus fifty ATS integrations, all from a three-person team that pivoted to this product sometime between their May 2025 Product Hunt launch and their Spring 2026 YC application. I wrote once that "you can either dig a hole that's broad but shallow, or one that's narrow and deep, like a well." What staffing agencies need is a well. What this looks like is a marketing page. When I see a tiny team claiming to do everything at once, I worry they're doing what we used to call "playing house" — going through the motions of having a platform rather than obsessively solving one painful problem until users can't live without it. The SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA compliance claims from a pre-seed team founded in 2024 only deepen this concern. Either they invested heavily in compliance before finding product-market fit, which is bad prioritization, or the claims are aspirational, which is a character signal I don't like.

The founders have a genuinely good pre-existing relationship — they co-created a podcast together in 2019, well before starting Asendia. That matters. Startups do to the founder relationship what a dog does to a sock, and these two have already tested theirs. Rihab's cloud architecture stints at Google and Microsoft give her credibility in enterprise sales conversations, and Badis's engineering work at Infineon plus his prior product (FAANG AI, a B2C interview prep tool) show they can ship. But neither founder has ever worked inside a staffing agency. They haven't been recruiters. They haven't managed a desk. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise," and in this case the domain is the messy, relationship-driven, margin-squeezed world of staffing operations. Cloud architecture experience and electrical engineering degrees don't give you that. I want to see founders who have lived the problem — who noticed it because they were inside it, not because they identified a market opportunity from the outside.

The pivot from B2C to B2B is where I'd normally look for evidence of resourcefulness, because most successful startups do end up doing something different than they originally intended. The question is whether the pivot was driven by genuine learning — did they discover something unexpected about how their B2C interview tool was being used, the way the Airbnbs discovered that being on the ground in New York hunting down users revealed something about the market? Or did they simply notice that B2B has better unit economics? I see no evidence either way, but the absence of any public B2B traction signal post-pivot is telling. No named agency customers, no revenue, no LOIs, no testimonials from staffing firms (the "$6M higher revenue" claim on the website is unattributed and unverifiable). For a team that's been working on this for months, the silence is louder than any metric.

The bull case here rests on timing and structural dynamics. Paradox's acquisition by Workday in August 2025 genuinely removed the strongest independent player and created a gap. Incumbent ATS vendors like Bullhorn face real cannibalization tension in bundling AI features. If Asendia can move fast, own the standalone AI layer for staffing agencies, and lock in switching costs through those fifty ATS integrations, there's a real business here. Staffing is a $169 billion industry with agencies that are desperate for automation and technologically underserved. If the founders turn out to have the determination to go deep — to spend months inside agencies understanding workflows, to do things that don't scale like manually running candidate screens for their first ten customers — this could work. The structural opening is real.

But I keep coming back to the idea itself. "AI for recruiting" is possibly the single most consensus application of large language models. Everyone sees it. That means every competitor sees it too — hireEZ with $76M raised and $42.6M in revenue, Humanly acquiring three companies in 2025, and now Workday with Paradox's technology baked into the dominant HCM platform. The best startup ideas look like bad ideas initially. This one looks like an obviously good idea, which means the returns will be competed away unless there's something genuinely proprietary about the approach. I don't see that here. The underlying AI capabilities are commodity — LLM APIs are available to everyone. The moat argument reduces to "we'll have more ATS integrations and better agency-specific workflows," which is an execution bet, not a structural advantage. And the TAM numbers, while respectable ($656M growing at 7.2% CAGR for AI recruitment), aren't the kind of market where you'd expect a black swan outcome. This isn't a bet on something that could be as big as eBay. It's a bet on capturing a slice of a moderate market against well-funded incumbents, which is not how the math of early-stage investing works for me.
