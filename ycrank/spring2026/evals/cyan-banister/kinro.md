# Kinro -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that catches my eye here is the client roster -- WTW and The Zebra on a pre-seed company's website is the kind of signal that makes me sit up straight. WTW is one of the largest insurance brokers on the planet, and their stock dropped twelve percent when ChatGPT insurance apps were announced in February. That tells me the fear is real, the structural shift is happening now, and these incumbents are already reaching for solutions. Kinro being the company they reach for, before a Series A, before press coverage, before anyone outside the insurance industry has heard of them -- that's meaningful. It reminds me a bit of what I saw with Carta early on, where the people who needed cap table transparency most urgently were the first to adopt. The ones feeling the pain move first.

But here's where I get stuck, and it's the thing I always get stuck on: tell me your story. Not the business. Pierre-Alexandre has a PhD from the Sorbonne and led research on Gemini for financial services at DeepMind. Parth built ML infrastructure at Zoox and holds patents on robotic grippers. Corentin was the first employee at an insurance startup. These are genuinely impressive credentials -- the technical bench is deep, the domain knowledge is real through Corentin's insurance relationships. But I can't find the biographical thread that tells me why these three specific people would walk through walls for insurance distribution. Where's the lived experience? Where's the obsession that predates the opportunity? When I backed Uber, it wasn't because Travis had a good resume -- it was because I could feel years of rage about the taxi medallion racket radiating off of him. That biographical inevitability is my primary filter, and I don't see it here. What I see are talented people who correctly identified a structural shift and assembled the right skill mix to exploit it. That's smart. It's just not the signal that gets me to write a check from my own money.

The market timing, I'll grant, is genuinely interesting and has some of that non-obvious quality I look for. Eighteen months ago, "AI agents selling insurance inside ChatGPT" would have sounded like science fiction to most insurance executives. Now WTW's stock is cratering on the news. Kinro positioning as the enabler -- selling the tools to brokers and carriers rather than competing with them like Harper or Panta -- is a strategically sound bet in a regulated industry where relationships are load-bearing. That said, this is becoming consensus fast. When J.P. Morgan is writing about AI agents managing the full insurance journey and insurance broker stocks are reacting, the window where this looks "weird" is closing. Harper has $46.8 million. COVU has fifty million. FurtherAI has thirty million from a16z. The most promising companies are not popular, and this category is getting very popular very quickly.

What I do genuinely respect is the regulatory complexity Kinro is navigating. Insurance is a nightmarishly fragmented regulatory environment -- state-by-state licensing in the US, country-by-country in Europe, UDAAP compliance, carrier-specific rules on what you can and can't say about coverage. They already have SOC 2 Type II certification, which isn't trivial. Their multi-layered compliance guardrail system for factuality and prompt injection hardening addresses real risks that horizontal AI platforms won't bother to solve. My years at IronPort taught me that security and compliance infrastructure, the boring stuff nobody wants to build, is often the most durable moat. This is the dimension where Kinro most activates my pattern recognition -- it's a hard space that deters lazy competitors, and the regulatory fragmentation creates exactly the kind of accumulated integration work that compounds over time.

The bull case I want to take seriously: if AI platform distribution grows from twenty percent to fifty percent of insurance buyer traffic in the next three to five years, someone has to be the compliance-hardened infrastructure layer between those platforms and the carriers. Kinro is positioning to be that layer, and they already have enterprise relationships that would take competitors years to build. The 27% conversion rate and 23-second time-to-quote metrics, if real and sustained, represent transformative improvements over legacy distribution. If Corentin's insurance relationships give them a distribution advantage that compounds faster than better-funded competitors can replicate their compliance infrastructure, this could become the essential middleware for a multi-trillion dollar industry. That's a real outcome worth imagining. But for me personally, it's an outcome I can imagine analytically rather than feel in my gut -- and I've learned the difference matters.

Three talented founders, a real and urgent market need, genuine regulatory moat-building, and enterprise clients that most pre-seed companies would kill for. But this company doesn't set off the sensors I trust most. I don't feel the biographical inevitability. I don't feel the weirdness. I feel a well-constructed business targeting a well-identified opportunity -- and those have just never been the bets where I add the most value or where my conviction runs deepest. I'd tell these founders they're building something real, and I'd introduce them to investors whose pattern recognition lights up for exactly this kind of play. Mine doesn't, and I've learned to honor that.
