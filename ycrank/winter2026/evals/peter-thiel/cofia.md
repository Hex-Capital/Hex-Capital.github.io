# Cofia -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cofia is not the product or the founders -- it is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything I need to know. Bardeen raised $22-25 million and launched its "Work Intelligence Platform" in May 2025 with the explicit thesis that AI agents should "learn how work actually happens, then execute and improve it." That is, word for word, Cofia's thesis. Meanwhile, Zapier sits at $310 million in annual revenue with over 100,000 customers and has been investing in AI capabilities. n8n just raised $180 million at a $2.5 billion valuation. Workato has $441 million in funding. This is not a market where a secret is hiding -- this is a market where every intelligent person agrees on the direction, and a dozen well-resourced players are racing each other toward it. The mimetic convergence is textbook: when every automation company simultaneously announces "AI-driven proactive automation," you are watching firms imitate each other into mutual value destruction. There is no secret here. There is consensus.

The implied contrarian thesis would need to be something like: "Existing automation platforms are structurally incapable of building proactive automation because their business models depend on user-initiated configuration." This is the innovator's dilemma argument, and it has some theoretical merit -- Zapier's per-zap pricing is misaligned with a world where automations generate themselves. But Bardeen already exploited this insight a year before Cofia entered YC. When your "secret" has already been discovered, funded to the tune of $25 million, and launched publicly with backing from Dropbox and HubSpot, it is no longer a secret. It is a strategy that multiple competitors are executing simultaneously. I ask myself the standard question -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- and I cannot construct an answer on Cofia's behalf that would survive scrutiny. "Automation should be proactive" is a truth that everyone agrees with, which means it is a convention, not a secret, and conventions do not create monopolies.

The founders are competent professionals -- Moses Wayne reaching Senior Staff at Duolingo is a genuinely impressive engineering credential, and Paola Martínez's retention work at Brilliant.org shows product instincts. But competence is not conviction. I see no evidence of a definite vision for how the world should work, no public articulation of why this company must exist, no refusal of easier paths. The website contains nothing -- literally analytics tracking code and nothing else. Compare this to Zuckerberg, who had 100,000 users on 20 campuses and could describe exactly how real-identity networking would expand from Harvard to every university to every human. Or to Palmer Luckey, who was building VR headsets in his garage before anyone would fund him. What I look for in founders is not a Stanford degree and a senior title at a growth-stage company -- it is the kind of intellectual independence that makes someone willing to be wrong in a way that everyone else finds uncomfortable. Nothing in this dossier suggests that level of conviction.

The bull case, which I take seriously, rests on three pillars. First, the innovator's dilemma genuinely threatens incumbents: if proactive automation eliminates manual configuration, Zapier's engagement model breaks, creating an opening. Second, the personalization data -- if Cofia can observe enough user behavior, the switching costs become real, because a competitor would need to re-learn each user's workflows. Third, the timing around LLM cost reduction is legitimate: continuous observation and inference were economically infeasible two years ago. If you believe that Bardeen will stumble, that Zapier cannot cannibalize itself, and that Cofia's implementation will be meaningfully superior despite having no visible technical differentiation, then you can construct a path to a defensible business. But that path requires believing three independent things that are uncertain, and the base rate for the smallest, latest entrant in a crowded market winning the category is very low. The power law demands that I invest in companies where the upside, if correct, is extreme. Even in the best case, Cofia becomes one of several players in a competitive automation market -- which is precisely the outcome my framework identifies as structurally incapable of producing venture-scale returns.

The technology itself relies on broadly available LLM capabilities. Observing user behavior across SaaS tools and generating automation suggestions is an application layer, not a proprietary technology. There is no 10x breakthrough here -- no physics, no novel algorithm, no capability that did not exist before. It is an integration play built on commodity infrastructure. Any well-resourced team with access to the same foundation models could replicate the approach, and several already are. The absence of any identified proprietary technology, combined with the absence of any public GitHub presence from either founder, reinforces that this is a product concept, not a technological discontinuity.

I pass. The market has no secret, the thesis has no monopoly path, and the competition is already funded and shipping. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. Cofia is entering a race that was already crowded before it started.
