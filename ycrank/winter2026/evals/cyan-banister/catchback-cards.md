# CatchBack Cards -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about CatchBack Cards: this is a consensus market wrapped in crypto branding. Trading cards are hot — Pokémon TCG pulled in $1.55 billion last fiscal year, Whatnot just raised at an $11.5 billion valuation, and every investor in Silicon Valley knows the collectibles market is growing. When I say the most promising companies "are not popular" and "are difficult to assess," I mean it literally. CatchBack is the opposite — it's operating in a category that every VC understands immediately, competing against incumbents with billions in capitalization. Fanatics already runs a mystery box program called Under Wraps with official league licensing. Whatnot could add asynchronous pack features tomorrow. The fact that this is legible and appealing to most investors is precisely what makes me cautious.

When I think about the founders, I want to hear the story that connects a person to this problem at the level of identity, not just interest. Joseph Zhang has impressive credentials — Gates Scholar, ISEF finalist, AWS, McKinsey, Nexus — and he clearly loves Pokémon cards enough to quit his job to flip them. I respect the leap of quitting McKinsey-caliber work to pursue something you care about. But "I had a side hustle flipping cards and turned it into a company" is a different biography than the kind I gravitate toward. There's no adversity narrative that forges an unbreakable bond between this founder and this problem. Compare that to Contraline, where the founder spent years in academic research on a problem nobody else would touch, or to my own experience building Zivity after being told no fifty-plus times. I'm not seeing the biographical depth that tells me this person will persist through the inevitable crises that come when Fanatics or Whatnot decides to eat their lunch. And Sophia Kim — the other half of this two-person company — is essentially invisible. No public background, no LinkedIn profile I can find, no domain expertise documented anywhere. In a team of two, that's not a minor gap. After HQ Trivia, I'm extremely sensitive to what I can't see in co-founder dynamics.

The strongest bull case here is real, and I want to be honest about it. The $13K MRR with only 700 users is genuinely impressive early traction — roughly $18 per user per month suggests people who are actually spending, not just signing up. The cryptographic verification of mystery pack randomness does map, in a narrow way, to something I care about: making invisible systems visible. Mystery packs have always been trust-based, and CatchBack is essentially building what Affirm did to hidden credit card fees — bringing transparency to a process that has historically relied on seller honesty. If I squint, I can see the Carta parallel: opaque system made transparent through technology. The Steph Curry collaboration, if it materializes, could be a meaningful distribution unlock. And the open-source verification code is a genuine technical differentiator that builds real trust. If the founders could articulate this as a transparency revolution in collectibles rather than a gamified consumer app with crypto tags, the story becomes more interesting.

But I keep coming back to the structural concerns. The mystery pack format with "customized odds" and "risk levels" where users "digitally rip open packs" is essentially gamification of randomized outcomes with real money — that's gambling-adjacent territory with regulatory exposure the dossier doesn't address. The crypto/Web3/NFT tags and Nexus Layer 1 partnership feel like they could narrow the addressable audience rather than expand it — consumer sentiment toward NFTs has cratered since 2022, and mainstream collectors may be actively deterred. The buyback guarantee requires CatchBack to hold inventory and capital to honor offers, creating capital intensity at a stage where they have neither the balance sheet nor the margins to sustain it. And the card-backed loans and peer lending features mentioned on their website introduce financial regulatory complexity that a two-person pre-seed team is not equipped to navigate.

This isn't my kind of bet. The market is consensus, the founder story lacks the biographical depth I need to feel conviction, the product doesn't create meaningful economic access for underserved populations, and the competitive moat — cryptographic verification — is technically replicable by any well-funded incumbent. I'd want to see a founder who's been through something that makes this problem feel life-or-death to them, in a space that other investors think is insane, building something that expands economic opportunity rather than gamifying consumer spending. CatchBack has solid early traction and a smart technical feature, but it doesn't give me that jolt of energy I need to write a check from my own money.
