# Squid -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The most striking thing about Squid isn't the product or the market -- it's the customer continuity. Conor and George worked at National Grid and Octopus Energy. They left and built VoltQuant, which served National Grid and Octopus Energy. VoltQuant wound down and they built Squid, whose "Trusted by" section features... National Grid and Octopus Energy. And not just as logos on a website -- National Grid DSO launched a public-facing FlexPortal "powered by Squid" in February 2026. For a two-person pre-seed company, having your software deployed as the backbone of a public utility product is genuinely remarkable. These founders clearly built something real inside those organizations -- the kind of deep institutional trust that follows you from one company to the next. That's not nothing. That's actually quite rare.

But when I try to apply my usual first question -- tell me your story, not the business -- I come up empty. The dossier gives me BCG, Cambridge MPhil, AWS, Head of Technology at Octopus Energy. These are accomplished professionals with impressive CVs. What I don't see is the biographical thread that tells me why these two people are willing to grind through utility procurement cycles for the next decade of their lives. Where's the personal stake? Where's the hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this problem? I backed Contraline because the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception when nobody cared. I wrote my first check into SpaceX because Elon's intensity about becoming multiplanetary was clearly existential for him. With Squid, I see domain expertise and career logic, which is valuable but doesn't activate the same signal in me. Second-time founders who pivot from a prior venture into an adjacent product -- that's execution, not obsession.

The honest assessment of the "weird factor" here is that there isn't one. Grid planning software for utilities is a thoroughly rational, well-understood need. The electrification wave is consensus. The competitors are funded. Envelio was acquired by E.ON. Camus Energy raised $25 million. GE Vernova bundles planning tools into enterprise suites. Nobody looks at Squid and says "that's insane" -- they say "that makes sense." And the most promising companies in my experience are the ones where people say "that's insane." Browser-native, versioned, collaborative -- these are good product instincts, not contrarian ones. This is Figma-for-grid-planning, and while Figma turned out to be a great company, the pitch was never weird. It was obviously better.

Where Squid genuinely resonates with something I care about is the transparency angle. Grid planning decisions that affect millions of people -- where substations go, which neighborhoods get capacity upgrades, who gets connected and who waits -- are currently buried in spreadsheets, PDFs, and email chains. That's an invisible power structure. "Built so everyone can understand the grid" is exactly the kind of language that makes me lean in. I see echoes of what Carta did for cap tables or what Flock Safety did for neighborhood safety data: taking decisions that were opaque and making them legible. If Squid becomes the system of record for how utilities plan their networks, that's a meaningful shift in who can see and influence infrastructure decisions. The National Grid DSO FlexPortal -- making flexibility market data publicly accessible rather than locked in internal spreadsheets -- is a concrete manifestation of this pattern.

I also want to acknowledge what's genuinely strong here: the regulatory navigation. A two-person team with SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications already in hand is not typical. These founders understand from the inside what it takes to sell software to utilities -- the compliance hurdles, the procurement cycles, the organizational dynamics. That's the kind of complexity that scares off most early-stage founders and creates real moats for those who can push through it. After my years at IronPort selling security infrastructure into enterprises, I have deep respect for founders who don't flinch at these barriers. If I were investing purely on "can this team navigate a hard space," I'd be more excited.

The bull case that would make this a great investment is: the electrification transition is a multi-decade structural shift, grid planning is the bottleneck, incumbents are too bundled and slow to serve modern collaborative workflows, and Squid's existing deployment at National Grid DSO proves the product works at real utility scale. If they can expand beyond their personal network to other UK DNOs and then into European and US markets, the versioned network model becomes deeply embedded -- the kind of data lock-in that makes switching nearly impossible. That's a plausible path to a very valuable infrastructure company. For an investor who weights market timing and execution capability above founder biography and anti-consensus positioning, this would be a compelling bet. But that's not how I invest. I take extraordinary risk on extraordinary people solving problems that look impossible, and Squid looks like a solid company solving a well-understood problem competently. That's a different kind of bet than I make with my own money.
