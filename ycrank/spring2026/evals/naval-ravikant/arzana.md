# Arzana -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Marshall Kools earned $10,000 last year. That's the first number I noticed in Arzana's dossier, and it tells me more than any TAM slide. A 24-year-old Stanford wrestler living on $10K while maintaining an office in Appleton, Wisconsin — his hometown — to sell AI automation to the manufacturers he grew up around is not agent behavior. Agents optimize for salary. Principals optimize for ownership. William Alexander, 21, keeps an office in Okoboji, Iowa, where he worked at a manufacturing sourcing company as a kid. Arzana has three offices — San Francisco, Okoboji, Appleton — and five people. These founders didn't discover manufacturing through market research or a Y Combinator brainstorm. They went home. The principal signal here is unusually strong for pre-seed: Arzana's founders are building for their own communities, not performing "passion" for investors.

William Alexander's manufacturing background is real but thin. Growing up around a sourcing company in Iowa gives you the language, the empathy, the ability to walk onto a factory floor and not seem like a tourist — and that matters enormously when your buyers are manufacturing executives who distrust 21-year-olds from San Francisco. But specific knowledge, in my framework, means the founder's expertise IS the company's engine. Ev Williams at Twitter had years building Blogger; Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity had years at Google Brain. William has a Stanford CS degree and childhood proximity to manufacturing. That's enough to identify the problem authentically — and Arzana's 8 named customers, including Zeiss, prove the identification was correct — but it's not the kind of non-replicable knowledge stack that prevents a Conexiom engineer or an Epicor product manager from building the same thing. The specific knowledge here is relational and cultural, not technical. A competitor can't replicate the Iowa relationships, but they can replicate the product.

Arzana's leverage architecture concerns me most. The forward-deployed model — engineers onsite at customer facilities before configuration — is labor leverage, the form I rank lowest. Every new customer requires weeks of engineering time from a 5-person team. With 8 named customers, Arzana is already consuming most of its capacity on deployment rather than product development. The Conexiom comparison is instructive: Conexiom raised $170M and generates $28.7M in revenue processing $100B+ in B2B transactions with "touchless" automation. Arzana differentiates with broader scope — quoting, estimating, order entry, dashboard — but that breadth requires deeper per-customer integration, which means more onsite time, which means less leverage. The steady-state product has genuine AI leverage: once configured, Arzana's agents process quotes and orders at zero marginal cost. But the acquisition funnel is gated by human bodies. Palantir built a large business with this model, but Palantir required thousands of forward-deployed engineers and decades of capital — the structural opposite of what I look for.

Arzana's most interesting structural advantage is one the dossier implies but doesn't state: the principal-agent problem inside ERP vendors. SAP, Oracle, Epicor — they own the integration points and the customer relationships to build exactly what Arzana is building. But their revenue comes from license fees and professional services. Automating front-office workflows with AI cannibalizes their own implementation partners and reduces the complexity that justifies their pricing. They're agents of their P&L, unable to ship the product their customers need. Arzana, with no legacy revenue, can move where Epicor's product team cannot — the same structural dynamic that lets Perplexity challenge Google. But Perplexity's advantage compounds through a data flywheel at massive scale; Arzana's per-customer AI models, trained on individual historical job costs, create switching costs but not network effects. Each deployment is an island. The compound interest test fails at the portfolio level, even if it works within each account.

Eight named customers including Zeiss at pre-seed with five people — I want to acknowledge that this is unusual traction. The New York Times featured Arzana in February 2026, which is distribution leverage most pre-seed companies never access. The customer testimonial from Milltown Paper's CEO — "one click instead of 15 minutes" — describes genuine value creation, not vaporware. An advocate would argue that Arzana is the AngelList of manufacturing automation: taking capabilities locked behind Esker's enterprise pricing (€43.8M quarterly revenue, 3,000+ customers) and Conexiom's $170M war chest and making them accessible to job shops and mid-market manufacturers. That's my access democratization pattern. The bull case is that the forward-deployed phase is temporary — a necessary investment in understanding each ERP system's integration patterns — and the product compounds into self-serve deployment as Arzana accumulates integration templates across Epicor, Infor, JobBoss, and the other 10+ systems they support. If Arzana can productize deployment to under one week with no onsite engineers, I'd reconsider.

The bear case is simpler and, I think, more likely correct. The underlying technology — LLMs reading emails and PDFs, extracting structured data, writing to ERP APIs — is commoditizing in real time. Conexiom has already launched an AI platform. Paperless Parts has AI-powered quoting. DigiFabster shipped an AI Quote Agent. Arzana's differentiation is breadth and manufacturing specialization, but breadth requires more integration work per customer, not less. The forward-deployed model creates customer loyalty but prevents the kind of explosive scaling that produces power-law returns. This is a company that could build a solid $20-30M ARR business serving Midwest manufacturers — and I respect that outcome — but it's not structured for the nonlinear upside that makes angel bets rational. The one verifiable condition that would flip me: if Arzana demonstrates self-serve deployment for at least one ERP system (say, QuickBooks) with zero onsite engineering time and shows 3x month-over-month customer growth on that channel, the leverage architecture transforms entirely, and I'm in.

Arzana earns its score on principal authenticity — the $10K salary, the hometown offices, the manufactured-in-the-Midwest DNA — and loses it on leverage architecture and specific knowledge depth. The founders are real, the problem is real, the traction is real, but the scaling mechanism is wrong for my framework. Five people deploying onsite to eight customers is a consulting ratio, not a software ratio. In the AI era, I'm looking for companies where the ratio is five people serving eight thousand customers. Arzana isn't there yet, and the forward-deployed model suggests it may not get there without a fundamental architectural shift.
