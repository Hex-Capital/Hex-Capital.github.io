# Sila -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, and it tells a story. Glue raised $20M in October 2025 with David Sacks as co-founder, positioning as "agentic team chat." Dust.tt has $7.3M in ARR. Slack is integrating Agentforce. Microsoft Teams has Copilot. Notion is adding agents. Every large incumbent and at least two well-funded startups are converging on the same thesis: AI agents belong inside team communication. When I see five or more well-funded teams racing toward the same idea simultaneously, I know I'm looking at a consensus category. And consensus ideas have already been priced. The returns in startup investing come from being right about something most people think is wrong, or from spotting a problem hiding in plain sight. "AI will transform team communication" is hiding from nobody. It's on the cover of every tech publication.

The deeper problem is the origin of the idea. How did these founders find this? Mith Patel and Carl Huang are recent Cornell CS graduates who built an AI chatbot for Cornell classrooms and co-founded saiera, an AI course marketplace. Then they jumped to AI team communication. There's no story in this dossier about either founder drowning in Slack notifications at a previous job, or personally experiencing the communication overload they describe. The thesis page talks about "nearly 60% of knowledge workers feeling overwhelmed" -- that's a statistic, not a lived experience. When Drew Houston forgot his USB drive on the bus, that was organic. When the Airbnb founders couldn't make rent, that was organic. When two college students cite a survey about knowledge worker overwhelm, that's a brainstorming session that identified a large market. The problem is real. But the connection between these specific founders and this specific problem looks manufactured.

There's also no meaningful schlep here. Building a messaging platform with AI agents is an engineering challenge, but it's a fun engineering challenge -- the kind smart hackers enjoy. Compare it to Stripe, where the schlep was dealing with banks, PCI compliance, fraud detection, money transmission licenses across fifty states. The regulatory and institutional friction was so painful that every programmer who knew payments were broken unconsciously looked away. What's the equivalent schlep in building another chat product? There isn't one. The barriers are product quality and distribution, not the kind of tedious institutional complexity that naturally deters competitors. That's why there are already five or six teams building this simultaneously.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Slack and Teams are genuinely constrained by legacy architecture -- Slack's entire business model is per-seat pricing, and making AI agents first-class participants could cannibalize that. Teams is bundled with Office 365 in ways that make fundamental redesign politically impossible inside Microsoft. A ground-up rebuild could produce something meaningfully better, the way Slack itself was better than HipChat. These founders are technical -- they've shipped AI products, they studied CS at Cornell, they have a working co-founder relationship across multiple projects. If they nail the product and get distribution through YC, network effects could compound fast. Young, technical co-founders who've already built together are not a bad starting point. But "not a bad starting point" isn't enough when you're entering a market where David Sacks has $20M and a head start, and where every incumbent is moving in the same direction. You need either a dramatically better product insight or a distribution advantage or a schlep moat. I don't see any of the three.

The founders are the strongest element here. Both are CS graduates who build things themselves. They shipped an AI chatbot across sixteen Cornell classes -- that's a real product used by real students, not a demo. Their co-founder relationship predates Sila by at least two projects, which matters. I've seen too many teams where co-founders met at a networking event and fell apart within a year. But technical ability and co-founder stability aren't sufficient when the idea itself doesn't pass my basic tests. The LinkedIn profiles still listing saiera rather than Sila is a small thing, but it nags at me -- it suggests the transition isn't complete, or that full commitment to this particular problem hasn't crystallized yet.

I'm passing. Not because the founders are bad -- they're young, technical, and have built together before, which puts them ahead of most applicants. But the idea has all the hallmarks of what I've described as the "made up" failure mode: smart people who decided to start a startup and then went looking for a category rather than discovering a problem they couldn't stop thinking about. When I combine that with a hot, crowded market where well-funded competitors have identical positioning, I don't see what makes this bet distinctive. The team communication space will produce a winner, but the winner will likely be whoever has the deepest organic understanding of how teams actually work -- and that understanding usually comes from years inside the problem, not from building a thesis page about knowledge worker overwhelm statistics.
