# ProjectX -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Three of four original co-founders are gone. In July 2024, the Business Standard lists Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham. By Spring 2026, the YC page shows Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder -- only the CEO survived. At pre-seed, the founding team IS the company. When three-quarters of it evaporates before the product has revenue, the principal-agent problem isn't external -- it's inside the building. I've seen this pattern before. Sometimes it means the surviving founder has the conviction to rebuild around better people. Sometimes it means the original vision was never shared deeply enough to hold people together. The dossier doesn't tell me which. But it's the first thing I'd ask about in a meeting.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Rounak Adhikary has a BSc in Interaction Design and founded a tech consulting firm at nineteen. That's hustle, not GPU virtualization expertise. Bishal Karmakar writes Golang and Rust -- relevant tools, but writing systems code and having deep knowledge of GPU memory management, low-latency video encoding pipelines, and container orchestration at the kernel level are different things. When Aravind Srinivas started Perplexity, he'd spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact neural architectures his product would use. If Srinivas disappeared, you'd lose irreplaceable knowledge. If these founders disappeared, a competent systems team from NVIDIA or AWS could rebuild the product from the architecture description alone. The specific knowledge here is being acquired on the job, which means competitors with deeper starting expertise can move faster.

The leverage audit reveals a structural problem. This looks like code leverage -- software streaming apps to browsers -- but underneath, every user session burns real GPU cycles. That's not zero marginal cost. That's capital leverage dressed in software clothing. Shadow PC raised $105-169M and still charges $38-55/month because the underlying economics demand it. ProjectX has $204K in total funding competing in a sector where CoreWeave has raised billions. The "per-app" model is more efficient than a full desktop VM, yes, but efficiency improvements to a fundamentally capital-intensive architecture don't transform it into permissionless leverage. Compare this to Notion, where each additional user costs essentially nothing. Here, each additional user requires dedicated GPU compute. The scaling curve bends the wrong way.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. Seventy-five percent of Indian students lack access to quality computing hardware. That's a real access gap -- structural gatekeeping by economics, not by incumbents -- and removing it maps directly to the pattern behind my best investments. AngelList gave everyone access to startup investing. CoinList gave everyone access to token launches. Infinity could give every student in India access to Blender, Unreal Engine, and GPU-accelerated tools through a browser tab. The $2.4M in signed LOIs from Indian institutions suggests genuine institutional demand. The IIT Bombay pilot is a credible beachhead. If the founding team has genuinely solved sub-3-second cold starts for GPU apps in browser -- and that's a real technical achievement if true -- they may be building specific knowledge through shipping that no amount of prior employment could provide. Young founders sometimes create specific knowledge rather than importing it. The question is whether they can build it fast enough before hyperscalers notice this market segment.

But that question has a difficult answer. Google acquired Cameyo for app virtualization in May 2024. AWS, Azure, and GCP all have GPU infrastructure and browser streaming capabilities. Unlike the Perplexity-versus-Google dynamic, where Google's ad revenue model structurally prevents them from cannibalizing search, hyperscalers face no such principal-agent constraint in adding per-app GPU streaming. It would be a feature addition to their existing compute offerings, not a business model disruption. The structural protection that made my Perplexity bet compelling -- the incumbent's inability to act against its own revenue model -- is absent here. Ninety-five companies are already tracked in the cloud GPU platforms sector. That's not a contrarian bet. That's a crowded market with well-capitalized incumbents who can replicate the product layer once it's proven.

The access democratization signal is genuine and it's the dimension that would keep me in the conversation longest. But specific knowledge is my first filter for a reason. Innovation without a durable advantage accrues to the customer, not the company. These founders are building something real, in a market that matters, for people who need it. I respect the ambition and the persistence through co-founder turnover. But I need to see either irreplaceable technical depth or a leverage architecture that compounds -- and I see neither. Pass.
