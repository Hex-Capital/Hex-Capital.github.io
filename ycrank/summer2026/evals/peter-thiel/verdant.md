# Verdant -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Verdant is a govtech SaaS where the highest-scoring dimension is its regulated customer base — municipal permitting departments — and the lowest is founder lineage, where co-founder Aidan Ng is a Georgetown Government undergraduate and the second co-founder, Jason Yi, cannot be verified in public records. That pairing names the company: an undergraduate team selling into the one category I've defended for fifteen years, arriving two years late to a contrarian secret that GovWell has already arbitraged with a $25M Series A from Insight Partners and 130+ municipal customers across 34 states. Palantir worked because Silicon Valley refused to touch government for a decade and we showed up alone. Verdant is showing up to a crowded room.

**Palantir (2003)** is the obvious analog on the regulated-sector axis and the load-bearing comparison, because both Palantir and Verdant target a customer base — government — where procurement friction is itself the moat. The shared mechanism: jurisdictional integration depth compounds into switching costs that pure-software competitors cannot replicate from outside the relationship. Where Verdant diverges from Palantir is the entry-point asymmetry: Palantir entered with Karp and Cohen and a CIA-adjacent thesis nobody else would underwrite, while Verdant enters with two undergrads competing for the same RFP slot against Accela's 2,200 agencies, Tyler/EnerGov's ERP-bundled footprint, OpenGov's 2,000+ entities, and GovWell's 130-customer head start. The dossier shows Verdant naming Accela and Tyler as "intake and tracking only — no automation," which is a real incumbent failure mode, but the same sentence has been spoken by GovWell to Insight Partners eighteen months earlier. The secret is no longer hidden. A secret that an A-round competitor has already monetized is a feature, not a moat.

**Verdant's founder lineage** is where the Pass Example pattern triggers cleanly. The failure mode I named in that example — "first-time founders with no prior shipped-to-scale signal, no Olympiad, no acquired side project, no senior IC role at a known company" — describes Verdant's team almost verbatim. Aidan Ng's public footprint is a Georgetown Government major and an affiliation with the Global Lab; Jason Yi resolves to a LinkedIn URL whose headline cannot even be retrieved. There is no PayPal Mafia node, no Stripe-alum, no Anduril-alum, no Moskovitz-from-Facebook override of the kind that carried Asana past my structural-moat objection. The "backgrounds in public service" line on verdantapp.com is unverified in search results. Without operator pedigree, the contrarian-secret and monopoly-trajectory gates have to carry the entire weight of conviction — and they don't.

**Verdant's contrarian secret** compounds backwards over 18 months rather than forwards. The mechanism: AI-document-parsing for permitting was contrarian in 2024, consensus in 2025, and table-stakes by the time Verdant's S26 batch demos in late 2026. GovWell's May 2026 Series A is the timestamp on the secret expiring. Every additional foundation-model release narrows Verdant's differentiation from "AI-native review" to "another AI permitting tool," which is exactly the "AI for X" framing I pass on by default. The dossier surfaces zero proprietary dataset, zero patent, zero jurisdiction-specific corpus already trained — only a Calendly demo link.

**Verdant's monopoly trajectory** would require one counterfactual to flip a tier: a signed contract with a top-50 US city in the next two quarters, with the kind of integration depth that creates a reference account incumbents cannot dislodge. Absent that, the permitting market structure — fragmented across 19,000 municipalities with 5-vendor RFP shootouts (City of Astoria listed Accela, OpenGov, SmartGov, Cloudpermit, PSDcitywide) — produces a long-tail SaaS outcome with no pricing-power asymptote. Verdant gets the regulated-customer tailwind but not the regulated-customer monopoly, because monopoly requires being first or being structurally unreplicable, and Verdant is neither.

**Verdant's definite plan** maps onto a single Founders Fund anchor: **SpaceX (2008)** had a 15-year reusability roadmap Musk would not pivot from. Verdant's public surface — a website, a Calendly link, a "modular platform configurable to jurisdictions" — is the indefinite-optimism mode Zero to One was written against. No multi-year technical milestones, no architectural commitment, no named city it will own by end of 2027. This is iteration-mode by default.

My 36-month forecast on Verdant: dead or acqui-hired by GovWell, OpenGov, or Tyler for under $10M, with the modal outcome being a quiet wind-down inside 24 months as the second YC check fails to close against a competitor with $34.5M of dry powder and reference customers in 34 states. The mechanism is straightforward — Verdant has no capital advantage, no pedigree advantage, no secret advantage, and no integration-depth advantage against GovWell, and municipal procurement cycles reward incumbency, not iteration.

The strongest bull case against my pass invokes the **Lyft (2010)** exception: permitting is fragmented enough across 19,000 jurisdictions that two or three AI-native winners can coexist, and Verdant could ride Accela/Tyler renewal churn to $3-5M ARR serving small cities GovWell ignores. The bear rebuttal that wins: Lyft had driver-side network effects creating duopoly stability, and Verdant has no analogous compounding mechanism — permitting software is a per-jurisdiction sale with no cross-jurisdiction network, so the fragmentation argument produces a small services business, not a power-law shape. The single condition that would flip my verdict: Verdant signing three named municipal contracts inside six months OR adding a senior co-founder with shipped govtech-at-scale pedigree (Palantir alum, Anduril alum, prior city CTO). Until then, this is the indefinite-optimism check a consensus-AI fund writes, not the one I do.
