# Asendia AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact in this dossier is not about Asendia AI -- it is the competitive landscape table. Paradox raised $253 million. hireEZ raised $76 million and generates $43 million in revenue. Humanly raised $24 million and acquired three companies in a single year. Fetcher raised $40 million. And now a three-person team with pre-seed funding proposes to enter this same arena with an "agentic recruitment platform." This is a Girardian tableau -- multiple companies converging on identical capabilities, imitating each other's feature sets with increasing precision, burning capital to differentiate products that grow more alike with every release cycle. Sourcing, outreach, screening, interviewing -- every player advertises the same four capabilities in slightly different configurations. When I ask "what is the secret here?", the answer is silence. "AI can automate recruiting" is not a secret. It is the most consensus thesis in enterprise software today. Every VC I know has funded at least one company with this exact pitch. The absence of a contrarian insight is not a gap in the dossier -- it is a structural feature of the company's positioning.

The pivot from B2C to B2B is diagnostic. The founders built "FAANG AI," a consumer interview coaching product, launched it twice on Product Hunt, achieved respectable upvotes, and then abandoned it to build an enterprise platform for staffing agencies. This is the lean startup methodology in action -- test a hypothesis, measure the response, iterate toward a better market. It is precisely the approach I consider "code for unplanned." A founder with definite conviction does not build a consumer product, discover the market is difficult, and then reorient toward staffing agencies because the B2B economics look more attractive. A founder with a genuine vision for how recruitment transforms would have started with that vision. The pivot does not demonstrate adaptability -- it demonstrates the absence of a thesis strong enough to withstand initial resistance.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Paradox's absorption into Workday genuinely removes the strongest independent player and creates a structural opening. Staffing agencies are a specific, underserved vertical -- they operate differently from internal HR teams, with higher volume, faster cycles, and deeper dependence on candidate throughput. A platform that owns the AI layer for staffing agencies using Bullhorn could theoretically build workflow integration moats that compound with usage data. The 50+ ATS integrations already built suggest real engineering execution. If the founders could dominate staffing agencies the way early Palantir dominated CIA analytical workflows -- becoming so embedded in daily operations that switching costs become prohibitive -- the resulting position could be defensible. But this analogy breaks down immediately: Palantir's integration moat was built on years of custom deployment within classified environments where no competitor could replicate the institutional relationship. ATS API integrations are standardized interfaces that any funded competitor can replicate in months. The moat here is shallow.

The technology question compounds the problem. Every capability Asendia advertises -- LLM-based interviewing, candidate database search, SMS outreach, phone screening -- is available through foundation model APIs that improve monthly. The dossier's own "commoditization risk" section acknowledges this directly. When the underlying technology is a commodity, execution speed is the only differentiator, and execution speed is not a moat -- it is a temporary lead. Stripe succeeded not because payment APIs were novel, but because the developer experience was an order of magnitude simpler than anything else, creating a switching-cost moat through millions of code integrations. I see no analogous mechanism here. A staffing agency can swap one AI screening tool for another in a week.

The founders have credible enterprise backgrounds -- Google Cloud, Microsoft, Infineon, TU Munich -- but this is precisely the profile I worry about in competitive markets. These are competent operators, not obsessive visionaries. Neither founder has direct staffing agency experience. Neither has articulated a view of the world that anyone would argue against. The company exists because AI tools have become capable enough to automate recruitment workflows, and the founders recognized the opportunity. That is pattern recognition, not a secret. Every failed company is the same: they failed to escape competition. Asendia AI is entering a market defined by competition, armed with technology that is becoming a commodity, led by founders whose strategy is adaptive rather than definite. This is a clear pass.
