# Clam (formerly Baseframe) -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking feature of this market isn't the technology -- it's the competitive landscape. In 2025, every major standalone AI security startup was acquired by a cybersecurity incumbent: Lakera by Check Point for ~$300M, Protect AI by Palo Alto Networks for ~$500M, CalypsoAI by F5 for ~$180M, Prompt Security by SentinelOne. That's the entire independent field, absorbed in a single year. This looks superficially like the structural vacancy pattern I exploited with my Anduril investment, where Google's Project Maven withdrawal left no credible AI-for-defense startup. But there's a critical difference: with Anduril, the incumbent *left* the market. Here, the incumbents *bought* their way in. Check Point didn't abandon AI security -- they spent $300M to own it. The vacancy isn't a market gap, it's an integration window. And integration windows close.

The acquisition prices themselves are the most informative data point in this dossier, and it's one most pre-seed investors would overlook. Lakera at $300M, Protect AI at $500M, CalypsoAI at $180M -- these are feature-acquisition prices, not platform-company prices. When an entire category of startups gets acquired in the $180M-$500M range, the market is telling you something: this is a capability that belongs inside a larger platform, not a standalone business. Compare that to how infrastructure categories that produce independent multi-billion-dollar companies behave -- Stripe didn't get acquired for $500M because payments infrastructure was a platform, not a feature. The question Clam has to answer is whether AI agent security is more like payments (a standalone platform opportunity) or more like WAF (a feature that lives inside Cloudflare or Palo Alto).

The structural moment for AI agent security is real -- I won't dispute that. Agents that execute code, call APIs, and browse autonomously create a genuinely new attack surface that traditional application security doesn't address. That's a capability threshold being crossed right now. But this is a consensus observation, not a non-obvious one. Every CISO I've talked to in the last year knows AI agents need securing. Every major cybersecurity vendor has an AI security strategy. This isn't PagerDuty, where operations alerting seemed boring and investors dismissed it -- this is a market where $1B+ in acquisition capital flowed in 2025 alone. When I look for asymmetric returns, I look for markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors. AI agent security triggers the opposite reaction: everyone wants in.

The bull case I take seriously is the network-level architecture. Clam's "Semantic Firewall" operates as a proxy that intercepts all agent traffic -- that's structurally different from Lakera's API-layer approach or CalypsoAI's cognitive-layer intervention. If you sit in the network path of every agent interaction, you see every request, every response, every credential. That's a data position that compounds over time and a natural distribution channel for additional products: observability, compliance auditing, cost management, access control. The credential injection mechanism -- keeping secrets out of the AI's context entirely by injecting them at the network level -- is a genuinely clever architectural choice that creates real switching costs once deployed. If I could be convinced this is a platform, not a feature, the product-to-distribution trajectory from network proxy to agent infrastructure layer is the path I'd map.

What holds me back is the combination of pivot recency and market consensus. The team pivoted from BaseFrame (hardware design validation) to Clam during the W26 batch itself. That's not inherently disqualifying -- many strong companies pivoted early. But it means they chose this market weeks ago, not from years of operating in it. Anshul's HappyRobot experience is relevant -- he saw AI agent security challenges firsthand at a production logistics AI company serving enterprises like DHL. That's real domain exposure. But there's a difference between experiencing a problem as a user and having the deep conviction to build the defining solution for it. The founders are technically capable -- shipping two products during a single YC batch shows velocity -- but the single-miracle test is unclear. Is their miracle achieving detection accuracy that beats open-source alternatives? Getting enterprise adoption before incumbents finish integrating their acquisitions? Building framework-agnostic coverage beyond OpenClaw? When I can't identify the one miracle in one sentence, that's usually because the founders haven't either.

I'd want to revisit this in six months. If the incumbent integrations stumble (as they often do), if Clam has expanded beyond OpenClaw to multiple agent frameworks, and if they've signed enterprise design partners who chose them over their existing security vendor's AI offering -- that's a different conversation. Right now, the structural moment is real but consensus, the competitive vacancy is temporary not structural, and the acquisition price data suggests a feature market. Competent team, real problem, wrong shape for my portfolio.
