# ProjectX -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Ninety-five companies. That is how many cloud GPU platforms Tracxn tracks as of 2025, with 41 funded and $20.8 billion raised collectively. When I see a number like that, I do not see opportunity -- I see a Girardian catastrophe in progress. Dozens of companies imitating each other, fighting over the same GPU compute customers, differentiating on basis points of pricing and marginal UX improvements. ProjectX enters this landscape with $204,000 in total funding and the thesis that per-app browser streaming is meaningfully different from full desktop VMs. This is not a contrarian secret about the world. This is an architectural preference within a consensus market. Google acquired Cameyo for app virtualization in May 2024. Shadow PC, Paperspace, Workspot, and every hyperscaler on earth are already streaming GPU workloads. The insight that "students and professionals need cloud-based access to GPU-intensive applications" is something every investor in the room already believes. When everyone agrees, there is no secret, and when there is no secret, there is no monopoly.

The per-app streaming distinction -- individual applications in browser tabs with sub-3-second cold starts rather than a full persistent VM session -- is technically interesting but does not constitute a zero-to-one discontinuity. It is a better version of something that already exists. Shadow gives you a full Windows desktop for $38-55 per month. ProjectX gives you individual apps in browser tabs. This is a UX improvement, perhaps a 2-3x improvement in convenience and cost efficiency for certain use cases. It is not an order of magnitude leap that renders the prior approach obsolete. Any well-funded competitor -- certainly any hyperscaler -- could build per-app streaming if the market demanded it. AWS, Azure, and GCP already possess the GPU infrastructure, the streaming expertise, and the distribution to replicate this architecture within a product cycle. The technology is competent systems engineering, not a proprietary breakthrough that compounds over time.

The founding team dynamics concern me independently of the market structure. Between the July 2024 press coverage and the Spring 2026 YC application, three of four original co-founders departed -- Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham are gone, replaced by Karmakar and Majumder. At pre-seed, the founding team is essentially the entire company. When three co-founders leave before the company has meaningful revenue, it suggests the vision was not magnetic enough to hold people through the difficult early period. Compare this with any company I have backed at the earliest stage: the founding team's commitment was the strongest signal, not the technology or the market. Zuckerberg's inner circle stayed because the vision was irresistible. Here the opposite happened. Rounak Adhikary has entrepreneurial energy -- founding Woldix at 19, navigating the Indian accelerator circuit, earning a spot at Stanford ASES -- but his background in Interaction Design and his accelerator-hopping pattern suggest a founder who is optimizing for credentialing rather than executing a definite plan for how computing must work.

The strongest bull case rests on the India education beachhead and the $2.4 million in signed LOIs. If I squint, I can see a version of the Facebook playbook: dominate Indian technical universities (IIT Bombay pilot), where 75% of students lack access to adequate computing hardware, and expand concentrically from there. The LOIs suggest genuine institutional demand, and the India market is large enough that a dominant position there could fund global expansion. If the per-app streaming architecture genuinely delivers sub-3-second cold starts at a fraction of Shadow's cost, and if Indian institutions adopt it as standard infrastructure before hyperscalers build localized alternatives, ProjectX could own a specific niche. This is the one dimension where the company shows real strategic thinking -- a concentrated initial market with expansion potential. But even here, the structural defensibility is thin. There are no network effects in application streaming. Switching costs are minimal -- an institution could migrate to a hyperscaler's equivalent offering in a semester. And $204,000 in funding against competitors with hundreds of millions means any infrastructure scaling challenge becomes existential.

The fundamental problem is that this company is entering a market where value will be competed away. The GPU cloud infrastructure market is experiencing exactly the kind of mimetic escalation that destroys profits for everyone. CoreWeave has raised over $5 billion. Hyperscalers are adding GPU streaming capabilities as features, not as standalone products. In this environment, a small team from West Bengal with $204K is not positioned to achieve monopoly -- they are positioned to be crushed by the same competitive dynamics that make the market look attractive from the outside. Large markets with many players and no clear path to structural dominance are precisely the investments I avoid. The market is real. The problem is real. But the solution does not escape competition, and that is the only thing that matters.
