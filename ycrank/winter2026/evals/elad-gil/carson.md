# Carson -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Carson is the tagline: "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." When a pre-seed startup defines itself relative to products backed by Anthropic and OpenAI -- the two organizations with the deepest AI research talent and tens of billions in combined capital -- that's not bold positioning. It's a structural vulnerability. Anthropic adding enterprise security controls to Cowork is a quarter of engineering work for them, not a multi-year journey. OpenClaw's creator just joined OpenAI. The feature gaps Carson is targeting are exactly the kind of gaps that well-resourced platforms close as they mature. This is the "market-ending moves" dynamic I've written about -- when an incumbent can absorb your differentiator as a feature, you need something deeper than the feature itself.

The market question is where my conviction breaks down. AI agents for enterprise sales automation is not a non-obvious market -- it's the single most consensus category in venture right now. Clay is at $100M ARR with a $3.1B valuation. Artisan raised $46M. 11x raised $230M+ (even with its problems). Salesforce is building Agentforce. Every fund I talk to is looking at AI agent companies. The TAM slide cites a MarketsandMarkets report projecting the AI agents market at $52.62B by 2030 -- that's exactly the kind of static, generic market sizing that tells me nothing about whether Carson specifically can capture value. There's no structural "why now" unique to Carson. The dossier's timing thesis is essentially "Cowork and OpenClaw launched with gaps" -- but a feature gap in a competitor's product is not a structural inflection. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed boring and unnecessary to most investors. When I looked at Gusto, payroll seemed solved. Those were genuinely non-obvious markets where the adoption curve was inflecting underneath investor indifference. Enterprise AI agents in 2026 is the opposite of that pattern -- it's where every dollar is already flowing.

The strongest bull case here runs through the team, particularly Milan Bhandari. A CTO who already co-founded a YC company (Bolto, S23), raised $5.6M, and scaled past $1M in revenue -- then comes back for a second venture -- is a meaningful signal at pre-seed. That's not a first-time founder learning to ship; that's someone who has already closed the build-sell-iterate loop once. The Palantir thread across Menon and Bhandari is also relevant -- Palantir is one of the few companies that genuinely understands how to sell security-first software to enterprises that care about access controls and audit trails. If the bull case plays out, it looks like this: the team's Palantir DNA lets them build genuinely differentiated enterprise security infrastructure for AI agents (SOC 2, fine-grained authorization, audit trails), creating procurement lock-in that neither open-source agents nor consumer-grade platforms can match. The dynamic UI generation angle -- creating task-specific interfaces rather than operating through chat -- is genuinely different from most AI agent products. If that UX paradigm proves meaningfully better for sales workflows, Carson could become the enterprise-grade layer that sits between foundation model capabilities and sales team adoption, with expanding switching costs as companies embed custom automations. For this to work, though, the team needs to outrun both the AI labs adding enterprise features and the well-funded competitors (Clay, Artisan) adding agent capabilities to their existing distribution. That's a race against multiple fronts simultaneously.

What concerns me beyond the competitive dynamics is the product-to-distribution trajectory. Carson builds task-specific interfaces for sales workflows -- prospect research, outreach, slide generation. That's useful. But can it become a distribution channel for additional products? The path from "sales workflow tool" to "enterprise platform" requires the kind of integration depth that creates customer relationships you can layer products onto. Stripe started with a developer API that became the connective tissue for an entire financial services platform. I don't see the equivalent structural hook in Carson. Dynamic UI generation is a product design choice, not a distribution moat. If you stripped the AI layer away, you'd have a workflow builder with integrations -- which is closer to "software-aware" than "software-driven" in the way I think about it.

The operational clarity also gives me pause. Carson needs to simultaneously build an AI agent platform, achieve enterprise security certifications, generate useful dynamic interfaces, win enterprise sales against companies with 100x their capital, and avoid being absorbed by the foundation model labs. That's not a single-miracle company -- it's a multi-front execution challenge where several things need to go right in sequence. The best pre-seed companies I've backed could tell me their one hard problem in a single sentence. Carson's hard problem is somewhere between "enterprise trust" and "product differentiation" and "distribution against incumbents," and those are three different battles.

I'm passing. The team has real credentials -- Bhandari's prior founding experience and the collective Palantir background are above average for pre-seed. But the market is consensus-hot, the competitive field is deeply capitalized, and the positioning against Anthropic and OpenAI's own products is a structural disadvantage, not a strength. The non-obvious markets are where the asymmetric returns live, and enterprise AI agents in 2026 is about as obvious as markets get.
