# snag -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Snag's founders have been building together across three consecutive ventures — Knock Knock City (acquired by LuggageHero), OneRoof ($4.6M from General Catalyst), and now snag. That's the most distinctive signal in the dossier, and it cuts both ways. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas clearly have persistence, a prior exit, and the capacity to pivot when something isn't working. The OneRoof-to-snag pivot in late 2025 shows they recognized the neighbor community app wasn't the right product and redirected toward a real pain point. But three ventures in the same narrow domain — urban living, housing adjacency, student convenience — also reveals a ceiling on ambition. These founders are iterating within a category, not reimagining one. The departed CTO (Cem Ozer, now at Sovereign Labs) and the absence of any visible engineering leadership in a team of four designers raises a structural question I can't ignore: who builds the technology moat?

Let me start where I always start. If snag fully succeeds — becomes the dominant platform for flexible housing across every major U.S. city — what changes? Students find sublets faster. The search friction that currently lives in Facebook groups and Instagram DMs gets organized into a clean marketplace. That's genuinely useful. But it's a redistribution of existing activity, not a creation of new supply or a structural cost reduction. The 30,000 students using snag in NYC were already finding sublets before snag existed — they just did it less efficiently. Nobody's rent goes down. No new housing gets built. The world with a successful snag looks approximately like the world today, but with a better app for a specific transaction type. Compare that to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means limitless clean energy, or Impossible Foods, where success means protein without animal agriculture. Snag's consequence ceiling is a well-run marketplace in a bounded segment — exactly the outcome that doesn't justify my involvement.

The "AI-native" positioning deserves scrutiny. Snag's proprietary model converts social media posts into structured listings. The dossier itself states this capability "is replicable by any team with ML capability." That's not a technology wedge — that's a feature built on commodity LLM infrastructure. Any competent engineering team with API access could replicate this in weeks. The real defensibility is network density: 3,000 listings and 30,000 users in NYC creating a two-sided liquidity advantage. But network density in a single city, for a seasonal product (student sublets peak in summer), with no pricing power (sublet prices set by individuals, not the platform) — that's an execution moat, not a technology moat. I prefer technology risk to market risk. Snag has pure market and execution risk with no underlying technical discontinuity. LeaseBreak already has 100K+ registered users in NYC doing something similar without any AI at all, and Blueground raised $352M at a $750M valuation for a different approach to the same need. The competitive landscape tells me this market structure rewards operational excellence, not breakthrough insight.

I should address the bull case honestly. The $6.5M in booking requests in 30 days with 40% month-over-month growth is real signal. A 4.7/5 App Store rating across 184 reviews suggests genuine user love. The "why now" thesis is coherent: NYC Local Law 18 restricted Airbnb-style stays under 30 days, pushing demand toward 1-3 month sublets; GenZ workforce patterns favor flexibility over 12-month leases; LLM maturity enables cheap listing generation. If snag achieves liquidity in 20 cities and expands beyond students to digital nomads, contract workers, and relocating professionals, the booking volume could scale dramatically. Selin's Forbes 30 Under 30 recognition and prior exit demonstrate she can execute in consumer markets. For an investor looking for a 5-10x return on a well-run vertical marketplace, snag is a reasonable bet. But I don't invest for 5-10x. I either want to lose my money or make 50 times my money, and snag's architecture is designed for moderate, execution-dependent returns.

The DoorDash exception is the relevant test case — I backed a marketplace with no novel technology because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people was enormous, and Tony Xu demonstrated extraordinary learning velocity. Two conditions for the override, both required. Snag meets neither. The consequence magnitude of organizing sublet listings for college students in a handful of cities does not approach "transforming how hundreds of millions live." And while Selin Sonmez is a capable repeat founder, three ventures within the same narrow proptech vertical suggests deep domain commitment, not the outsider irreverence that drew me to a teenager building weapons systems at Mach Industries or a self-taught New Zealander building rocket engines. Snag is a competent team building a useful product in a real market. For most investors, that's enough. For me, it's not — I need the consequence of success to justify the attention, and a better sublet marketplace doesn't clear that bar.
