# Visibl Semiconductors -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Visibl is a structural feature of the problem that most people would walk right past. Cross-workstream coordination in chip design is broken, and everyone in the industry knows it. But here's why nobody has fixed it: the solution has to be vendor-neutral. Synopsys isn't going to build a coordination layer that orchestrates Cadence tools, and Cadence isn't going to build one that orchestrates Synopsys tools. Their entire business strategy is capturing the full design flow within their own ecosystem. So the coordination gap persists -- not because it's hard to see, but because the incentive structure of the incumbents makes it structurally impossible for them to address. That's a genuine instance of what I'd call institutional schlep blindness. The schlep isn't just technical complexity; it's the political and business-model complexity of sitting between competitors' tools while handling proprietary chip IP that requires on-premises deployment. Most AI startups would unconsciously filter this out as too ugly to touch.

The organic discovery question is where I get cautiously encouraged but not fully convinced. Jordon Kashanchi worked silicon design at Microsoft Azure Maia, Arm, and Intel -- three different chip companies, which likely means three different EDA tool chains and three different experiences of the coordination mess. The YC launch post says they "repeatedly [saw] coordination bottlenecks delay integration and tapeout." That's the kind of statement that could be genuine lived frustration or post-hoc positioning. The fact that Jordon has an MS in Integrated Circuits from UT Austin and actually wrote RTL at these companies -- not managed it, wrote it -- lends credibility. He would have personally experienced nightly regressions breaking across workstreams, the spreadsheet tracking, the late-discovered integration failures. That's organic. What gives me pause is Bryce Neil's path to this problem. His background is Deloitte's OmniaAI consulting practice -- enterprise AI consulting, not chip design. I don't see the moment where he personally hit this wall. And the co-founder relationship is opaque: "no shared university or concurrent employer identified." I want to know how they found each other and why Bryce chose this specific problem over the dozens of enterprise coordination problems he might have encountered at Deloitte.

The competitive landscape is what keeps this from being a conviction bet for me. ChipAgents has raised $74 million, is deployed at 50 semiconductor companies, and has an advisory board including former CEOs and CTOs of Mentor Graphics, Synopsys, and Cadence. That's not just money -- that's the industry relationships you need to get through the door at semiconductor companies, which have some of the longest and most security-paranoid procurement processes of any industry. Silimate, a YC S23 company in adjacent territory, is already generating revenue with enterprise customers. Visibl is two people with a free download and a demo request form. Now, Visibl could argue they're solving a different problem -- coordination versus verification productivity -- and that's a fair distinction. But semiconductor design teams don't buy tools based on taxonomy. They buy based on who their VP of Engineering already trusts, and ChipAgents is three years ahead on building those relationships.

The bull case is real, and I want to state it clearly. If the coordination layer genuinely sits across vendors' tools in a way that no incumbent can replicate, and if the switching costs compound as design context accumulates over multiple tapeout cycles, then the first company to get this right in a few key accounts could build a position that's very hard to dislodge. Semiconductor design teams don't switch tools easily -- the qualification overhead alone is months. And the timing argument has substance: advanced process nodes (3nm, 2nm) are genuinely increasing team sizes and coordination overhead, which means the pain is getting worse, not better. If Jordon's firsthand experience translates into a product that a few design teams love -- genuinely love, not just evaluate -- the company could establish itself before incumbents figure out how to solve the vendor-neutrality problem. That's a real path to something valuable.

On the technical founders dimension, this team is stronger than average for pre-seed. Bryce has Verilog repos on GitHub, including a CPU design project. Jordon's semiconductor engineering credentials are legitimate -- published in PNAS and ACM, worked on actual production silicon at Microsoft's custom AI chip program. They've built a working product that's available for download with an authentication system. These are builders, not slide-makers. But building the product and selling it into semiconductor enterprises are very different challenges, and the team's collective social reach is thin -- 236 and 58 Twitter followers respectively, no evidence of industry network beyond their former employers.

Where I land: the problem is real and the schlep is genuine, but this feels like a company that picked up on a real structural gap while also riding the "AI agents for X" wave that every pre-seed startup is surfing right now. The CTO's domain experience is the strongest signal. The CEO's path to the problem, the co-founder origin story, and the complete absence of traction evidence are the weakest. I'd want to see even one design team using this in production before I'd write a check. At pre-seed I don't need revenue, but I need someone pulling the product toward them -- a design lead who says "we can't go back to the spreadsheet." That signal isn't here yet.
