# ProjectX -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a founding team that has almost entirely turned over before the company has raised a real round. The July 2024 press lists four co-founders: Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, Kuncham. The YC Spring 2026 page lists three: Adhikary, Karmakar, Majumder -- only Rounak carries over. I've written about this explicitly: co-founder conflict is one of exactly three things that kill pre-product-market-fit companies, alongside not finding PMF and running out of money. Losing three of four co-founders at a stage where the company has raised $204K is not a restructuring -- it's a near-death event. Whatever happened, this team has already used one of its lives, and I need to understand whether what remains is stronger or whether the instability is ongoing. The addition of Sourya Majumder (who scaled consulting to ~$120K MRR) and Bishal Karmakar (systems engineer with Golang/Rust/C++ depth) is a reasonable rebuild, but the fact pattern makes me cautious about organizational durability under the stress that comes next.

The market question is where this falls apart for my framework. Cloud GPU infrastructure is not a non-obvious market -- it's one of the most consensus-hot categories in tech right now. Ninety-five tracked companies, $20.8B raised collectively, with CoreWeave alone holding $5B+ in capital. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody wanted to fund operations alerting. When I invested in Gusto, payroll seemed boring. This is the opposite pattern: every investor in Silicon Valley is trying to put money into GPU compute. ProjectX's specific angle -- per-app browser streaming instead of full VM sessions -- is architecturally interesting, but it's a product differentiation within a crowded category, not a structural inflection opening a new market. The "why now" factors cited in the dossier are all marked [Inferred]: GPU cost curves declining, WebGPU maturing, AI workload demand growing. These are real trends, but they benefit every player in the space equally. There's no narrow window that ProjectX uniquely occupies.

The India education GTM compounds my concern. The dossier frames the problem as "75% of Indian students lack access to high-quality computing devices," and the traction centers on an IIT Bombay pilot and Indian institutional LOIs. This is structurally adjacent to edtech -- a category I've never invested in because the person who benefits from the product (students) is not the person who pays for it (institutions with constrained budgets). Indian educational institutions are price-sensitive buyers with long procurement cycles. The Rs 20 Cr+ (~$2.4M) in signed LOIs from mid-2024 is a real signal of demand, but LOIs are not revenue, and nearly two years later there's no public revenue data. If those LOIs had converted, I'd expect to see it. Enterprise HPC and creative professional verticals are a more compelling long-term market, but the current traction points toward the harder sell.

The strongest bull case would be a Webvan-to-Instacart pattern: previous attempts at cloud desktops (Shadow, OnLive, Paperspace) failed or plateaued because they used the wrong architecture -- persistent VMs that were expensive and slow to spin up. If WebGPU and modern streaming codecs have genuinely crossed a fidelity threshold in the last 18 months, per-app browser streaming with sub-3-second cold starts could be the architecturally correct approach that only became feasible recently. The team has shipped a public beta and attracted 30,000+ community members, which shows they can build. If they could demonstrate that per-app economics are 5-10x better than VM-based competitors and lock in enterprise workflows before hyperscalers replicate the approach, this could be a real platform company. But that's asking me to believe in multiple things simultaneously: that the architecture is right, that the economics work at scale, that they can outrun Google (which acquired Cameyo for exactly this capability in May 2024), and that they can do it all on $204K in a capital-intensive infrastructure business where Shadow raised $100M+. That's not one miracle -- it's three or four compounding.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is theoretically present -- a cloud OS that becomes the default way people access GPU-intensive applications could distribute additional services through the same interface. But today, ProjectX is a delivery mechanism for other companies' software: Blender, Unreal Engine, DaVinci Resolve. They don't own the applications flowing through their platform, which means the distribution value accrues to the app makers, not to ProjectX. Compare this to Stripe, where the payment API created a developer relationship that Stripe owned and expanded. Here, the user relationship is with Blender or Unreal -- ProjectX is the pipe, and pipes get commoditized. Without a strategy to own the customer relationship beyond infrastructure delivery, the upside is structurally capped.

I'm passing. The market is consensus, the capital requirements are mismatched with the team's resources, the co-founder churn is a serious organizational risk, and the GTM is pointed at a segment with broken payment dynamics. The technical architecture is genuinely interesting, and the team has demonstrated real building ability -- but interesting architecture in a crowded, capital-intensive market where Google is actively acquiring competitors is not the kind of bet where I see asymmetric returns.
