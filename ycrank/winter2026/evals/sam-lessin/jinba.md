# Jinba -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Jinba is built at the exact intersection of everything I've been publicly screaming is a bad investment for the last two years. An AI-native SaaS workflow tool with tiered subscription pricing competing against n8n at $2.5 billion, Workato at $5.7 billion, and Microsoft Power Automate with Copilot baked into the largest enterprise installed base on earth. I wrote "the SaaS era is over" in September 2024. I said on CNBC in February that "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore." And here's a pre-seed company whose entire thesis is: we are a software company with a software moat. The natural-language-to-workflow trick is the value proposition. Strip away the LLM layer and you have another drag-and-drop builder competing with Zapier. The AI isn't the cherry on top -- the AI IS the sundae, the bowl, and the spoon. That's a feature waiting to get absorbed, not a company.

The founders are credible, and I want to be precise about that because my framework depends on separating the person from the idea. Matsumori has a real Ph.D. in deep learning from Keio, published at ICCV, and has prior startup experience. Norisugi brings McKinsey discipline and an INSEAD MBA. That's the "sane" half of my equation, and it's genuinely present. But the idea isn't insane -- it's the most consensus play in enterprise software right now. Every pitch deck I see in 2026 has some version of "use AI to automate X workflow." When your competitive landscape table includes five well-funded companies doing variations of the same thing and Microsoft is building the same feature into its dominant platform, you're not pursuing a non-consensus idea. You're sprinting in a crowded lane. Sane person, very sane idea -- in my framework, that's the configuration with the worst risk-adjusted returns because the upside is already priced into the competitive dynamics.

The bull case here rests on the 40,000 enterprise users at Fortune Global 500 financial institutions. If that number is real and represents active users on the current product iteration, that's genuinely notable traction for a company with $500K in funding. Embedded workflows in regulated banking and insurance create real switching costs -- enterprises aren't going to rebuild and re-validate audit-logged processes casually. The on-prem deployment capability is a meaningful differentiator against cloud-only competitors in regulated industries, and the Japan-first beachhead (Suntory, the Keio network) gives them a market where n8n and Workato have weaker distribution. If someone told me Jinba had $5M ARR from Japanese megabanks running mission-critical compliance workflows on-prem, I'd reconsider the structural moat question entirely. But the dossier flags what I'd flag: this user number is self-reported, the company pivoted from a data analytics tool called Jinbaflow, and there's no third-party verification that these 40,000 users are active on the current product. Birchbox had impressive user numbers too. The question was never whether people signed up -- it was whether the moat held when competitors arrived.

The structural moat problem is the killer. I look for businesses that get stronger as AI gets cheaper. Jinba gets weaker. As foundation models improve and become more accessible, the technical barrier to building "describe a workflow in natural language and we deploy it" drops toward zero. n8n is already building AI-native workflow capabilities into an open-source platform with $40M ARR. Microsoft will integrate this into Power Automate with Copilot and distribute it to every enterprise on the planet through existing contracts. The dossier itself states the quiet part out loud: "the barrier to entry for the underlying technology is low." When the company's own competitive analysis concedes that the core technology is replicable, you're betting entirely on distribution and compliance certifications as the moat. Those are real but thin -- any well-funded competitor can get SOC II certified and offer on-prem deployment. That's a feature checklist, not a structural advantage.

There's no protocol position here. Jinba builds on OpenAI, Azure, Llama, and Bedrock -- other people's primitives. It ships workflows as APIs, webhooks, MCP servers, and Slack bots -- other people's distribution. The MCP deployment option is interesting, but MCP is Anthropic's protocol, not Jinba's. When I backed Solana or Aleo or Helium, I was betting on new foundational layers that other applications would build on top of, creating compounding network effects. Jinba is an application on applications on applications. Nothing compounds. No one else's product gets better because Jinba exists. The narrative can theoretically expand to "automate any enterprise workflow anywhere" -- that's a big market -- but it's a narrative that n8n, Workato, and Microsoft all tell with more credibility, more funding, and more distribution. The infinity story test requires that each milestone makes the next narrative expansion more credible. Here, each milestone makes it more likely that a larger competitor notices and absorbs the opportunity.

I pass. The founders deserve credit for shipping a product with claimed enterprise traction on a thin funding base, and the regulated-industry focus shows strategic awareness. But this company fails every structural test in my framework: it's a SaaS business in a market I believe SaaS can't defend, an AI-first product in a category where I think incumbents capture the value, and an application-layer play with no path to becoming infrastructure. The best version of Jinba is an acqui-hire into a larger enterprise platform -- and that's not the kind of outcome I'm writing checks for.
