# Experiential Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Silen Naihin co-authored "Testing Language Model Agents Safely in the Wild" (arXiv:2311.10538) and helped build the AutoGPT benchmark before he ever incorporated Experiential Labs — that one fact is the most interesting sentence in the entire dossier, because it means he personally lived the agent-eval problem before deciding to sell a tool for it. Kion Fallah was a Staff Research Scientist at Waabi doing self-driving simulation. Put those two biographies next to the company description — "world models for AI agents, automatically generate evals from telemetry traces" — and you get a team whose product is the literal compression of their two prior jobs. That is organic in the Dropbox-USB-stick sense. The problem is that Experiential Labs has not done the thing I look at next: there is no cereal-box equivalent in the dossier. The artifacts are research repos (21 stars, 54 stars, 8 stars) of the kind any two competent PhDs would have lying around.

Drew Houston's MIT-hacker demo video for **Dropbox** (2007) sits at the top of my organic-idea ladder because Houston was personally storing things on USB sticks and forgetting them, and the product was the thing he wished existed. Experiential Labs maps onto this rung more tightly than most pre-seed teams I see — Naihin's NeurIPS paper is, in substance, a paper about why agent eval is broken, written before he started a company to fix it, which is the cleanest version of "live in the future and build what's missing" I can ask for in this batch. Where Experiential Labs diverges from the Dropbox rung is the artifact-to-company gap: Houston shipped a thing strangers used; Naihin shipped a paper and a 21-star harness repo. The intellectual provenance is there. The product evidence is not yet.

Brian Armstrong's appetite for the **Coinbase** (2012) compliance grind is what "schlep" actually means to me — unpleasant work that other smart founders unconsciously route around. Experiential Labs is doing technically hard work (self-driving-grade simulation, the dossier-claimed 99.7% reconstruction fidelity) but technical difficulty is not what "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) named. The schlep at Coinbase was banking partners and state licenses; the schlep at **DoorDash** (2013) was driver ops; the schlep at Experiential Labs is GPU bills and academic-style benchmarking, which is the schlep every AI infra YC company in S26 is also signing up for. No moat from unpleasantness here — just from talent concentration, and talent concentration in 2026 is being outbid by **World Labs** ($1.23B raised) and **AMI Labs** ($1.03B seed).

Patrick Collison won the Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010), and that is the kind of hacker genealogy I overweight. Experiential Labs has a real version of this: Fallah's ResearchGate publications in sparse coding and manifold learning, Naihin's contributions to a 150K-star repo (AutoGPT) before age 25. Over 18 months this dimension compounds two ways — through recruiting (other ex-Waabi people will take Fallah's calls) and through the credibility needed to sell simulation infra to skeptical ML engineers. It does not compound into distribution, which is what they actually need against **LangSmith** and **Braintrust**, both of whom already sit inside the eval CI pipeline.

Co-founder configuration is where Experiential Labs starts to wobble. The dossier explicitly flags that Naihin is concurrently listed as Co-founder/CEO of Endflow (YC W24, procurement AI) — meaning he is wearing two CEO hats six months in, with no public confirmation the Endflow role has wound down. To flip this from a 5/10 to a 7-8/10 I would need one of two things: a public statement that Endflow has been shut down or handed off, or evidence Fallah and Naihin overlapped at Georgia Tech, Waabi, or AutoGPT before incorporating. Neither is in the dossier. The **Reddit** (2005) Huffman/Ohanian dorm-roommate pattern is exactly the long-relationship signal that is missing here.

Thirty-six months from now I expect Experiential Labs to be acquihired by **LangSmith**, **Braintrust**, or **Arize** for sub-$50M, with Fallah staying on as a simulation tech lead and Naihin leaving within a year to return to Endflow or start something new. The mechanism: agent-eval-as-a-wedge is real but narrow, the eval incumbents own the CI workflow, and the well-capitalized world-model labs ($1B+ each) will not let a two-person team build a moat in general simulation. Acquihire is the modal outcome for a team this technically credentialed without a pre-funding revenue stunt.

The strongest case against my forecast is that Naihin's AutoGPT pedigree is a Schwartz-at-Reddit-grade tribal marker for the agent-eng community, and that as agent telemetry volumes explode in 2026-2027, automated eval generation becomes the natural insertion point that **LangSmith** structurally cannot build because they don't have a Waabi simulation researcher on the team. Under this case Experiential Labs is the **Stripe** (2010) of agent infra — boring wedge, hacker founders, schlep nobody else wants. The dossier evidence that dismantles this for me is the combination of (a) Naihin's still-active Endflow CEO listing — a founder running two YC companies in parallel is not in "Founder Mode" (September 2024), they are in dilution mode — and (b) the absence of a single named pilot customer or LOI, against competitors with revenue we cannot see but capital we can. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest: a signed paid pilot with a named agent-platform customer (Sierra, Adept, Decagon, any of them) before Demo Day, or a public Endflow wind-down. Either one and I write the check.
