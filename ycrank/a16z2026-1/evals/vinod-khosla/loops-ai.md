# Loops AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Hakan Baş co-founded Peak Games and sold it to Zynga for $1.8 billion. That's a real credential — one of maybe fifty people alive who've built a Turkish tech company to a billion-dollar exit. And yet here he is, attached as co-founder to a company building AI chatbots for e-commerce stores. That gap between founder pedigree and company ambition is the central puzzle of Loops AI. When I see a founder with a billion-dollar exit working on something, I want the ambition to match the résumé. Baş has the credibility to attempt something that makes the world say "that's impossible." Instead, Loops AI is doing what Gorgias does at $80M+ ARR, what Rep AI does at $3.5M ARR, what Tidio does with $26.8M raised — and what OpenAI and Google just launched natively in ChatGPT and Search. The talent is real. The target is small.

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if Loops AI fully succeeds? E-commerce brands get better AI-powered product search, slightly higher conversion rates, and cheaper customer support. That is a useful business. It is not a changed world. Nobody's life is materially different because their online shopping chatbot got 15% smarter. Compare this to Impossible Foods — if that works, billions of people eat protein without animal agriculture, and the environmental math of the planet changes. If Loops AI works, Tudors and Paulmark sell a few more shirts. The consequence magnitude is bounded by the nature of the problem: optimizing an existing commercial process, not replacing an expensive necessity with something radically cheaper. This is redistribution of existing e-commerce spend, not creation of new demand.

The technical approach compounds the problem. Semantic search, virtual try-on, LLM-powered chatbots — these are capabilities available through existing APIs and open-source models. The dossier itself flags that "core capabilities are technically reproducible," and the timing evidence confirms it: OpenAI launched agentic checkout in ChatGPT, Google launched agentic commerce in Search and Gemini. When the platform providers build your product natively into their distribution channels, your wrapper has a shelf life. Loops AI claims 99% accuracy on common queries, but accuracy on common queries is exactly what commodity LLMs deliver out of the box. I need to see a 10x discontinuity — a step-function improvement that couldn't exist without this specific team's technical insight. Combining four existing capabilities (search, try-on, chatbot, product enrichment) into one dashboard is integration work, not invention. That's the business Salesforce is built for.

The $500K ARR with claimed 2x growth in 45 days deserves attention — if true, that's product-market fit velocity that matters. Fifty brands across Türkiye, MENA, and Europe including P&G Fairy and Radisson Collection shows the team can sell. Zero churn across 50+ customers would be exceptional. But self-reported metrics without third-party verification are just claims, and the geographic concentration in Turkish and MENA markets creates a different competitive reality than the U.S., where Gorgias has 12,000+ merchants and Rep AI claims 22% conversion lift across 500+ Shopify stores. The strongest bull case for Loops AI is that Baş's network and credibility accelerate U.S. enterprise sales, the multi-modal stack (search plus try-on plus sales agent) creates enough integration depth to resist platform commoditization, and the team rides the agentic commerce wave to scale before OpenAI and Google close the window. For this to work, Loops AI would need to own enough proprietary interaction data across enough brands to build a compounding advantage that pure LLM providers can't replicate — a data flywheel, not a feature set. I see no evidence that flywheel exists yet at 50 brands.

I checked my known exceptions. DoorDash violated my "non-linear technical approach" criterion but the consequence magnitude was planetary — transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people. Loops AI doesn't have that consequence magnitude. Tony Xu was an immigrant outsider with visible learning velocity; Loops AI's founding story — Ari Nazir and İlker Zorluo?lu meeting in Bali over shared frustration with e-commerce support — doesn't carry the same signal. Baş's presence as co-founder rather than CEO limits how much his exit credential transfers to the company's learning rate. Four co-founders with backgrounds spanning finance, marketing, gaming, and engineering offers decent gene-pool diversity, but no evidence in the dossier shows rapid adaptation, pivots from new information, or the kind of intellectual honesty about what surprised them that separates learning machines from experienced operators.

I don't invest in better chatbots for online stores. The consequence of success is a moderately profitable SaaS company in a category that OpenAI, Google, and Gorgias are actively consolidating. Baş's $1.8B exit is the most compelling signal in this dossier, but it's insufficient to override low consequence magnitude and a commoditizing technical landscape. Consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach are the two dimensions that locked this verdict — both scored well below my thresholds for deployment of capital.
