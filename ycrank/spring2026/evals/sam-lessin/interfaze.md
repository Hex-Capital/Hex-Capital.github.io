# Interfaze -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: the company's primary distribution strategy is OpenAI API compatibility. They literally designed the product so developers can swap in Interfaze using the same SDK they use for OpenAI. That's being sold as a feature, but it's actually the company's obituary written in advance. If your moat is "easy to switch to," then by definition you're also "easy to switch away from." You've built a product whose core value proposition is frictionless replacement — and that cuts both directions with a vengeance. When OpenAI ships better multimodal capabilities next quarter, or Anthropic improves structured output reliability, or Google drops pricing on Gemini's vision features, every developer who adopted Interfaze through that compatible endpoint can leave in a single line of code.

Let me be direct about the mispricing question, because that's always where I start: there is no mispricing here. Zero. This is the most consensus category in venture right now — AI developer infrastructure. Half the companies at every demo day are pitching some variation of "we orchestrate AI models better than using them directly." Firecrawl already has 50,000 GitHub stars and $14.5 million in funding for just the scraping vertical. Apify is doing $13 million in revenue. The space is crowded, well-capitalized, and every sophisticated investor in Silicon Valley is actively looking at AI infrastructure deals. When I look for asymmetric risk/reward, I need the market to be wrong about the category. Here, the market is enthusiastically right — which means the upside is already in the price and probably then some. You're not getting Solana at four cents. You're getting a consensus bet at consensus pricing.

The primitive test is where this really falls apart for me. Routing developer tasks to specialized small models instead of one big LLM is an engineering optimization. It's a sensible architectural choice! But it's not a new primitive. Nobody is going to describe a future behavior by saying "the Interfaze of X." Compare this to Venmo, which created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavioral category that literally became a verb. Or Patreon, which created direct creator monetization as a pattern. Interfaze is "better AI API" — it competes on execution within an existing market. The individual components — OCR, speech-to-text, web scraping, classification — all exist independently as mature products. The claim is that bundling them behind a single routing layer is valuable. Maybe. But bundling is a feature, not a category.

Now, the bull case — what would have to be true for this to work? You'd need to believe that monolithic LLMs will structurally never become reliable at deterministic tasks, that the routing-to-specialized-models architecture is permanently superior, and that frontier labs with hundreds of billions in capital won't replicate this approach. You'd also need to believe that developer lock-in accrues to the orchestration layer despite API compatibility making switching trivial. The founder has some credibility here — Khemlani built Stayr to $3M ARR and exited, which shows he can execute and sell. The CTO has real ML research credentials with a peer-reviewed IEEE paper. These are competent people. But competent people building a sensible product in a hot category is exactly what I don't invest in. I want sane people building insane things. "Let's route deterministic tasks to specialized small models" is the kind of idea that makes every engineer nod thoughtfully. Nothing about it makes you say "what the hell, that can't possibly work." The sanity-to-insanity ratio is inverted — it's all sanity, no insanity.

The AI kill test is brutal here. Every time I see a pure software API play competing against the literal companies building foundation models, I think about Adobe. "Every time you get pitched Adobe for AI, you're like, this is perfect for Adobe." OpenAI is perfect for this. Anthropic is perfect for this. Google is perfect for this. They're all adding multimodal capabilities, structured output modes, and deterministic task performance to their core models. Interfaze is betting it can stay ahead of the most well-resourced AI labs in history on their own turf. That's not contrarian — that's just a losing race. There are no physical assets here, no protocol-level network effects, no regulatory moats, no creator-audience relationships. It's software on top of other people's models. The switching costs are negative — they designed it that way.

One detail I want to flag: the JigsawStack-to-Interfaze rebrand, with emails still on jigsawstack.com and GitHub repos still under the old name, signals a company that's still searching. The 10 million API requests in 9 months of beta sounds like a number until you realize there's no revenue disclosure attached to it. At their stated pricing, 10M requests could mean almost anything depending on token volume per request. The absence of any revenue figure after 9+ months of a consumption-priced product is its own kind of data point. Capital discipline on the team side looks fine — five people, lean — but the business model requires competing on price against companies with functionally unlimited compute budgets. That's not discipline; that's a structural disadvantage dressed up as a strategy.
