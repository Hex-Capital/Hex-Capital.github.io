# Advocate -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Advocate's core technology — converting voice recordings into CMS billing codes using speech-to-text AI and LLM-based code mapping — is assembled entirely from commodity components. Whisper-class ASR is open source. GPT-class language models are available to every developer on Earth. The CPT code database is public. Abridge has raised over $200 million to do voice-to-clinical-documentation; DeepScribe and Suki occupy the same space. Adding care coordination billing codes to an existing voice-to-code pipeline is a product sprint, not a company. When CodaMetrix — already sitting on $109 million and 200+ hospital deployments — decides that skilled nursing facility billing is worth automating, Advocate has no technological barrier to hide behind. This is the architectural profile of a feature, not a monopoly.

The claimed "secret" — that care coordination staff perform reimbursable work under CPT 99490 and related codes but fail to capture it — is known to every healthcare CFO, every RCM consultant, and every CMS billing specialist in the country. It appeared in McKinsey healthcare reports before Andrew Baran left McKinsey. The fact that this revenue is left on the table is not a hidden truth; it is a known operational failure caused by documentation burden. Advocate's insight is a market segmentation observation — smaller facilities are underserved by enterprise RCM platforms like Waystar ($1.1 billion in 2025 revenue) — dressed in the language of discovery. A real secret makes knowledgeable people uncomfortable or dismissive. This one makes them nod. When everyone agrees the money is there, the question becomes why no one has picked it up, and the answer — fragmented facilities, low ASP per account, expensive direct sales — is the same answer that will constrain Advocate.

The structural parallel that concerns me most is Wish. Wish identified an "underserved" segment of e-commerce — discount shoppers ignored by Amazon — and built a business on the go-to-market observation that the segment existed, without any proprietary technology, network effects, or switching costs. Advocate identifies an "underserved" segment of healthcare billing — care coordination at post-acute facilities ignored by Waystar and CodaMetrix — with the same absence of structural defense. The dossier itself acknowledges the commoditization risk explicitly: "Established AI scribe companies could extend their products into care coordination coding." The channel mismatch argument — that incumbents focus on hospitals, not SNFs — is precisely the kind of reasoning that works until the incumbent ships a downmarket SKU. Palantir survived not because the CIA was a "niche" customer, but because Palantir built proprietary software so deeply embedded in intelligence workflows that no enterprise vendor could replicate the integration. Advocate has no equivalent depth.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that enterprise RCM players will permanently ignore the care coordination billing niche at smaller facilities, that AI scribe companies will not extend their existing voice-capture pipelines into non-physician billing codes, and that Advocate can build sufficient compliance audit trail lock-in before either threat materializes. Baran's personal connection — his mother worked as a care coordinator — provides genuine domain intimacy, and Banys built healthcare data systems at Garner Health during its trajectory from Series A to a $1.35 billion valuation. These are real credentials. But the bull case still terminates at a "nice $200 million business" serving a fragmented market with low-single-digit-billion TAM — and the power law makes $200 million outcomes structurally irrelevant. The reimbursement rate dependency compounds this: Advocate's entire value proposition is anchored to current CMS payment schedules for CCM codes, which means the business model rests on political foundations rather than technological ones. I have seen this structure before. The cleantech companies did not fail because solar panels stopped working; they failed because their economics depended on government rate structures that shifted.

Advocate lacks a monopoly secret — the insight is consensus, the technology is commodity, and multiple funded competitors occupy adjacent territory with natural extension paths into this exact niche. The power-law math does not work: even optimistic penetration of a fragmented, low-ASP market segment caps the outcome well below fund-returner scale. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Return Magnitude, and Advocate fails both. Competition in healthcare billing automation is not a bug to be worked around; it is the structural condition that makes this company uninvestable under my framework, regardless of the founders' competence or the reality of the uncaptured billing opportunity.
