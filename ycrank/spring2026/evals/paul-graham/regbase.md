# Regbase -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy worked side by side at Paul, Weiss tracking regulations across 50 countries for the ESG practice, and they couldn't find a tool to do it. Aresta-Katz describes it as a "100 hour project" that had no existing solution. That's the kind of founding story I take seriously. It's not "we identified a market opportunity" — it's "we suffered through this ourselves and got fed up." Regbase's origin sits squarely in the framework I've written about for years: live in the future, then build what's missing. They lived inside a top-tier law firm's research operation, hit the wall, and built the thing that should have existed. Of the five dimensions I evaluate, this is where Regbase scores highest, and it's not close.

Ethan Shea's technical profile — Microsoft, Citadel, Staff Engineer leading agentic AI at Vercel — is directly relevant to building the ingestion and parsing infrastructure Regbase needs. The team composition is solid on paper: two people who understand the buyer and the workflow, one engineer who understands the architecture. But here's what I don't see: stories. I don't see any of the three founders having shipped a product before, even a small one. No prior startup. No side project with traction. Shea's GitHub has zero-star repos. When I backed Patrick Collison, he'd already built and sold Auctomatic as a teenager. When I backed the Airbnb founders, they'd been selling cereal boxes to keep the lights on. Resourcefulness reveals itself through specific anecdotes of creative problem-solving under constraint, and the Regbase dossier contains none. The founders may well have that quality — but a truly resourceful team usually generates notable stories even before they raise money, and the absence here is a data point.

Regbase's competitive positioning — subnational, non-English, "low media coverage" regulatory sources including content behind QR codes and obscure local government websites — passes the schlep blindness test. CUBE Global ($22M raised, 1,000+ clients) and Thomson Reuters Regulatory Intelligence (750+ regulatory bodies, 130+ countries) are both anchored in financial services and major national regulators. The structural mismatch is real: expanding to thousands of fragmented subnational sources requires a fundamentally different ingestion architecture than those incumbents were built for. This reminds me of Stripe's early insight — payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled everyone because of PCI compliance and bank integrations. Regbase is tackling a similar kind of unglamorous complexity. But the Stripe analogy breaks at a critical point: Stripe was hackers building for hackers, developer tools with superlinear network effects. Regbase is professionals building for professionals, with a human verification layer that creates trust but also limits how the business scales. When I read that Regbase combines "agentic AI research tools with human verification," I hear a services component embedded in the product, and services businesses scale linearly, not superlinearly.

"AI-powered regulatory intelligence" is the kind of idea that sounds good to everyone. Nobody hears it and says "that will never work." The TAM numbers — $2.8B in 2025, projected $7.6B by 2034 — appear in market research reports. The competitive landscape has established players with tens of millions in funding. This is not a Black Swan bet. When I look at my best investments, the pattern is that knowledgeable people's first reaction was skepticism or confusion: Airbnb ("strangers sleeping in your house?"), Coinbase ("cryptocurrency isn't real"), ClassDojo ("K-12 classroom management? VCs won't touch that"). Regbase doesn't generate that reaction. It generates nodding. Nodding is dangerous because it means the returns have already been competed away, or will be shortly. General-purpose LLMs are getting better at parsing government websites and translating content every quarter — the technical moat is moderate and eroding.

Regbase's website claims the product is "trusted by Fortune 500 companies, global consultancies, and leading law firms" — but discloses zero names, zero logos, zero revenue figures. The two customer testimonials (identifying $400M in expiring school safety grants, mapping 80 greenhouse gas disclosure requirements across 45 countries) demonstrate real work product, but the framing itself is a mild red flag. Founders who communicate with genuine clarity say "we have 4 paying customers including [name]" or "we're doing $8K MRR." Founders who write "trusted by Fortune 500 companies" are optimizing their copy for impression rather than specificity. I'm not saying the customers aren't real — at pre-seed, I don't penalize absent traction. But I notice the language choice because it tells me something about how the founders think about communication, and earnest builders tend toward directness over polish.

The strongest bull case for Regbase is that this is ClassDojo in regulatory intelligence — a company outside my typical "hackers for hackers" pattern, operating in a schlep-heavy domain with painful institutional sales cycles, where the founders' deep domain expertise creates an advantage that purely technical teams can't replicate. ClassDojo reached unicorn status despite being in a sector VCs considered unsexy. Regbase's organic problem discovery is genuinely strong, the CTO's agentic AI background at Vercel is precisely what this product needs, and the subnational coverage gap is a real structural opportunity. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Regbase has 3+ paying customers with names I can verify, combined with specific stories from the founders about creative workarounds they've used to acquire those customers or build coverage of hard sources — the kind of resourcefulness anecdotes that are conspicuously absent from everything I can see today. Without those signals, I'm betting on a well-credentialed team with genuine domain pain but no evidence of the intensity and adaptability that separate startups from consulting practices.

Regbase's organic problem discovery is the strongest signal in this evaluation — Aresta-Katz and Godoy's Paul, Weiss experience is exactly the kind of authentic origin I look for. But organic discovery alone doesn't make an investment. The two dimensions that keep me at Neutral are founder resourcefulness (14/30, no evidence in either direction, which is itself a weak negative) and idea contrarianism (7/20, this idea sounds good to everyone, which in my framework predicts competed-away returns). A company with a perfect origin story but a consensus idea and no visible evidence of the "animal quality" in its founders lands exactly where Regbase lands: worth watching, not yet worth writing a check.
