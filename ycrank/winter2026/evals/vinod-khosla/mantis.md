# Mantis -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Mantis is the gap between what the technology *could* imply and what the founder is actually pursuing. If you genuinely have physics simulations combined with large language models that understand human biology at a deep structural level, the consequence of success should be replacing clinical trials -- not making the data entry in clinical trials 20% less error-prone. Georgia Witchel has built this exact intersection of capability at Louiza Labs, simulating human biology for medical device testing. She knows the technology works. And yet she's positioned Mantis as "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data" -- an enterprise data management platform that saves pharma companies $15 million per trial in data quality costs. That's a real problem. It's also a problem whose consequence of success is a solid mid-market SaaS business, not an industry transformation. When I hear "Databricks for X," I hear a company that has already decided its ceiling.

The clinical data management system market is $3.5 billion growing to $9 billion. Fine. But I don't invest in markets -- I invest in the possibility that a market gets destroyed and replaced by something fundamentally different. If Mantis succeeds exactly as described, pharma companies still run the same clinical trials with the same basic methodology, they just have cleaner data flowing between their EDC, CTMS, and omics systems. Nobody's life changes structurally. Compare this to what happened when Juniper made Cisco's legacy architecture obsolete -- the entire routing infrastructure of the internet had to be rebuilt. Where is that magnitude of disruption here? I don't see it. The company has reduced its probability of failure by narrowing its ambition to a manageable niche, and in doing so, it has made the consequences of success relatively inconsequential.

The bull case requires you to believe something the founder hasn't articulated publicly: that the data platform is a beachhead, and the real play is using physics-enhanced simulation to eventually *replace* portions of clinical trials entirely -- running digital experiments that obviate the need for certain patient cohorts or trial phases. That would be a trillion-dollar consequence. The FDA and EMA are moving toward accepting digital twin evidence; Unlearn.AI's EMA-qualified methods prove the regulatory path exists. If Mantis could simulate trial outcomes with sufficient fidelity that Phase II trials shrink by 50% or certain Phase III arms become unnecessary, you're talking about restructuring how drugs reach patients. Georgia's technical background -- Harvey Mudd CS, Johns Hopkins computational math, UW biomedical engineering, plus direct experience building physics-based biological simulations at Louiza Labs -- is exactly the rare intersection you'd need to attempt that. And the ice climbing career (eight world records across four World Cup circuits) tells me something about her tolerance for risk and her ability to perform under conditions where failure is physical, not abstract. That combination of technical depth and athletic fearlessness is the kind of founder DNA I pay attention to.

But here's why I can't get there: the founder is not making that claim. The product positioning spans at least four different descriptions -- "digital twin company," "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," "domain-aware data platform," "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform." That's not the language of someone who has crystallized a transformational thesis. It's the language of someone searching for product-market fit, which at pre-seed is normal -- but it means the company hasn't committed to the ambitious version of itself. Worse, the competitive terrain is brutal. Unlearn.AI has $130 million and EMA qualification. Medidata's Simulants sits on 30,000 cross-sponsor trials and 9 million patient records. In clinical data, the data itself is the moat, and a three-person team starting from zero cannot outcompete incumbents who have decades of accumulated trial data. The technology combination of physics simulation plus LLMs is genuinely novel, but novelty without data access in this domain is a science project.

The Louiza Labs question also nags at me. Georgia was CTO and co-founder of a YC S25 company doing physics-based simulation of human biology -- essentially the same core technology applied to medical devices. Six months later, she's CEO of a YC W26 company doing physics-based simulation for clinical data. I need to understand what happened. Was there an amicable split? IP separation? Is Louiza Labs a competitor now? YC accepting her for both companies suggests they're comfortable, but from my seat, a founder leaving a $5M-funded company to start a closely adjacent one in the very next batch raises questions about either the founder's conviction or the relationship dynamics. Either could be fine, but neither is visible from the dossier.

I would need Georgia to walk into the room and tell me she's building something that eliminates clinical trials as we know them -- not a better data layer for the existing process. If she articulated that vision, demonstrated the rate of improvement in her simulation fidelity, and showed me that the technology trajectory would cross the accuracy threshold needed to replace human trials within five years, I'd write the check regardless of the competitive landscape. The technology foundation is there. The founder's background supports the attempt. But as currently positioned, this is an enterprise infrastructure play optimizing an existing workflow, and I don't fund optimization.
