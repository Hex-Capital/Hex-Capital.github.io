# Torus -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Marcus Lima is a repeat YC founder who put Sam Altman and Marc Benioff on the Heimdal (YC S21) cap table at a $63M valuation -- that is a real signal, and it is the single most interesting fact in the Torus dossier. It is also the only fact that pulls Torus away from being an almost-perfect match for the Pass Example I wrote into my persona file: "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], oversubscribed seed, four tier-1s already in." Torus reads as "Legora for physical engineering firms" -- a vertical AI agent wrapper priced against a consensus 2026 YC template -- attached to an operator who can actually sell into Fortune 500 EPCs. That pairing is Torus's signature: high on repeat-founder conviction, low on category-sleeve fit and on the round dynamics I need to see.

On founder conviction, Ben Silbermann/Pinterest is the HIGH rung -- I overrode price discipline there because the missionary temperament plus the operator-friend sourcing carried the check. Marcus Lima at Torus does not clear that bar, but he clears more of it than most Summer 2026 pre-seed founders will: he already ran a Heimdal-scale industrial GTM against direct-air-capture buyers, and the Oxford Energy Systems Engineering MSc gives him vocabulary with substation and pipeline procurement teams that a generalist AI founder cannot fake. Where Torus diverges from the Pinterest analogy is the absence of any public writing by Lima on why cross-document consistency in EPC workflows is a non-obvious wedge -- the dossier lists no Twitter, no GitHub, no essays, and the Rahul Thayil identity is ambiguous enough that SiliconANGLE surfaces him at Orion (Techstars '23) rather than at Torus. Repeat-founder credibility, yes; missionary artifact of asymmetric belief in this specific category, not visible.

The Pass Example is the LOW rung on category sleeve, and Torus sits on it. "AI is just more internet" is exactly how I've been reweighting the sleeve since the Joe Lonsdale podcast, and a vertical AI agent for engineering document management -- a category RTInsights already flags as receiving AI-powered classification from incumbents -- is the definitional consensus-2026 pitch. Torus's own competitor list -- AVEVA (Schneider Electric-owned), Bentley, Hexagon, AspenTech -- is a wall of public-company distribution machines that will ship 50+ consistency-check categories as a feature of their existing EDMS suites the moment the training-data question resolves. Nothing in the Torus dossier -- no proprietary corpus, no exclusive P&ID dataset, no regulatory rail -- prevents that substitution.

Round dynamics on Torus are unspecified in the dossier, which under my rules is not neutral: silence gets the absent-evidence default, because a repeat YC founder with Altman/Benioff on his prior cap table does not run an under-priced pre-seed by accident. Over an 18-month horizon, the compounding math is unforgiving -- a Fortune 500 EPC pilot cycle plus SOC 2 Type 2 completion plus procurement will burn 12-18 months before paid revenue, meaning Torus's cash needs get set by sales-cycle length, not by product cost, and the next tack in the regatta is a Series A raised into an AI-agent seed cohort that will be marking down aggressively by mid-2027. Consensus pricing on this profile is exactly the SAFE cap I cannot generate alpha from.

Capital structure would need to flip to score a tier higher: if Torus were structured as a services-attached deployment (paid pilots at $150K-$500K per Fortune 500 counterparty converting to seat licenses), booking real revenue against the burn, this reads as a capital-efficient enterprise wedge rather than a standard SaaS cap table needing five rounds to reach default-alive. The dossier gives no pricing page, no revenue signal, no LOI counterparty name -- just "working with multiple Fortune 500 companies," which is YC-page language, not evidence of a venture-shaped instrument.

Compare Torus's founder-brand posture to John Fish/Bookshelved as the single anchor: John Fish walked into the Slow Creator Fund conversation with an owned YouTube audience that made the CAC line zero and the underwriting question "how do we structure exposure to his distribution." Torus has the opposite profile -- no founder Twitter presence surfaced, no GitHub, no newsletter, LinkedIn follower counts not even retrievable -- which is fine for a B2B industrial software company but eliminates one of the two ways I underwrite pre-seed alpha. That leaves Torus's whole thesis riding on Criterion 1 (round price) and Criterion 3 (founder), and Criterion 1 has no dossier evidence supporting it.

Forecast over 36 months: Torus reaches $2-5M ARR across 4-6 Fortune 500 EPC accounts, then gets acquired by AVEVA/Schneider Electric or Bentley Systems for $40-90M as an AI-features tuck-in, well before a Series B is raisable. That outcome is consistent with the signature -- competent repeat-founder execution against a category that is not defensible as a standalone venture but is real enough as a bolt-on for an incumbent EDMS suite.

The strongest bear-to-bull objection uses my own Ro Known Exception: Ro violated capital-efficiency because 50-state prescription regulatory rails made VC capital itself the moat, and one could argue Torus has an analogous story -- safety-critical liability on pressure-rating mismatches in pipelines and substations plus on-prem sovereign deployment could constitute a real switching cost incumbents cannot cheaply clone. That objection stings because industrial safety is a genuine schlep. It fails on the specifics: Ro's rails were licensing, not just SOC 2; Torus's "sovereign deployment" is a deployment mode Bentley and AVEVA already offer, and SOC 2 Type 2 is table stakes, not a moat. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Torus discloses a signed multi-year paid contract with a named EPC counterparty (not a pilot, not "working with") AND the round is priced at or below $10M post -- either alone is insufficient; both together would move Torus off the Pass Example rung and into something I would underwrite.
