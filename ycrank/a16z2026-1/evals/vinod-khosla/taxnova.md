# Taxnova -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Taxnova is an AI tool for R&D tax advisors. Strip away the integrations and the multi-jurisdiction positioning, and the core pitch is: "We automate 75% of the data collection time in a 3-6 month compliance process." I've seen this exact structure dozens of times at YC Demo Day — AI for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for real estate agents. The TAM is quoted as $10B, which sounds large until you realize Taxnova is competing for a slice of advisory fees that already flow from companies to firms like EY and BDO. This is redistribution of existing spend, not creation of new demand. If Taxnova fully succeeds — captures the entire addressable market — companies file their R&D tax credits faster and pay less in advisory fees. Nobody's life materially changes. The software market they cite is projected at $3.28B by 2034. Even a dominant outcome is a profitable SaaS business, not a consequence that reshapes how millions of people live.

The technical approach compounds my concern. The dossier itself states what I would have concluded independently: "LLM-based document generation is becoming commoditized; the defensible layer is jurisdiction-specific tax logic and integration infrastructure." Taxnova connects read-only to Jira, GitHub, Slack, Xero, and a dozen other tools, then uses language models to generate technical narratives. That's applying existing LLM APIs to a workflow — a wrapper with domain-specific prompts, not a technical discontinuity. The 15+ integrations create some switching cost, but integrations are table stakes in 2026 B2B SaaS, not a moat. SPRX has raised $46M, Neo.Tax $13M, Boast.ai $30M — Taxnova enters this market with $1M against competitors who have multi-year head starts and established US customer bases. The "global-first" angle with UK HMRC coverage is real differentiation, but jurisdiction-specific tax logic is a feature, not a platform shift.

George Nichkov and Maria Malykh have assembled a team with genuine domain expertise — EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, IRS backgrounds, plus Revolut's Head of Tax as an advisor. The $100K ARR with named customers like incident.io and Paddle at pre-seed is solid execution, and the pilot with a Top 5 advisory firm suggests the go-to-market has legs. But here's what I notice: the team's primary selling point is domain expertise, which is the opposite of the signal I look for. Nichkov's path from Oliver Wyman consulting to Gett product management to tax software is a competent professional following a logical career arc — not a naive outsider who doesn't understand what can't be done. Maria Malykh's program management at Criteo coordinating 9 R&D teams gives her insight into how engineering teams work, which is directly relevant. These are capable founders building a sensible business. I have no evidence of rapid learning, pivoting, or first-principles reasoning that would distinguish them — no signal of what surprised them or how they adapted during building.

The strongest bull case for Taxnova: the advisor-agnostic model is structurally clever because it avoids triggering the incumbency defense that killed other fintech infrastructure plays. If advisory firms standardize on Taxnova as their workflow layer across jurisdictions, network effects could compound as each new country multiplied the switching cost. The UK HMRC inquiry rate jumping from 0.5% to 20% creates genuine urgency for audit-defensible documentation, and the $200K+ ACV enterprise pilot suggests enterprise willingness to pay. A patient investor could see Taxnova becoming the Veeva of R&D tax — vertical SaaS with regulatory moat. That's a legitimate 5-10x outcome. But I don't invest for 5-10x. I either want to lose my money or make 50 times my money, and a vertical compliance tool in a $3B software market is structurally incapable of producing those returns.

Taxnova also sits squarely in a regulatory dependency zone that I've learned to avoid the hard way. R&D tax credit rules are set by governments — UK HMRC, US IRS, EU member states. When HMRC rewrote the merged scheme in April 2024 or when Section 174 changed US treatment of R&E costs, Taxnova had to rebuild jurisdiction-specific logic. The company's value is inseparable from government policy, which means its future is partly determined by political decisions outside anyone's control. After KiOR and Range Fuels, I require that "the rationale for scaling does not depend on" regulatory stability. Taxnova's entire product is a regulatory compliance tool — its existence presupposes a specific regulatory regime.

The dimension that most definitively places Taxnova outside my framework is consequence magnitude. This is a competent team building a useful product in a real market with early traction — and none of that changes the fact that automating R&D tax compliance is an optimization of an existing process, not a transformation of how the world works. The second decisive dimension is technical approach: applying commoditizing LLM capabilities to a vertical workflow, against competitors with 10-46x more capital, is not a non-linear bet. I pass.
