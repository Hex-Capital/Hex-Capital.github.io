# Tepali -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Tepali is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything I need to know about the absence of a secret. Boulevard has raised $188 million. Zenoti is valued above a billion dollars. Vagaro has $65.9 million in revenue and a billion-dollar valuation. Mangomint just raised $60 million. AestheticsPro has been building medspa-specific clinical software for three decades. Five well-funded companies are already fighting over the same customers, the same workflows, and the same thirty thousand medspa locations. When I see this many players converging on the same market with the same thesis, I do not see opportunity -- I see mimetic competition in its purest form. Every one of these companies is imitating the others into mutual margin destruction. Tepali proposes to enter this arena with a two-person team and a YC check, claiming the differentiator is "AI-native architecture." But "AI will transform vertical SaaS" is not a secret. It is the most consensus thesis in enterprise software today. Boulevard's $80 million Series D explicitly earmarked investment in AI capabilities. The secret test fails completely here -- there is no important truth that few people agree with.

The power-law test is equally damning. The U.S. medical spa management software market is $99.8 million. The global market is $338.8 million, projected to reach $1.3 billion by 2034. Even in the most optimistic scenario -- Tepali captures a dominant share of a market that grows to $1.3 billion over a decade -- this cannot produce the kind of return that justifies the bet. I invest only in companies where success means returning the entire fund. A $1.3 billion TAM for management software in a niche vertical does not have the structural capacity for that outcome. The broader medical spa services market of $18.6 billion is irrelevant unless Tepali becomes a payments or lending platform, which would mean competing directly with Boulevard's embedded fintech model and Stripe's infrastructure -- a strategy that creates even more competitive exposure without any proprietary advantage.

The founders present a profile I find structurally unfavorable. Chrisvin Jabamani is an NYU Stern graduate who spent time in investment banking at PJT Partners before doing business operations at Rilla. Vishnu Pathmanaban studied computer science at Carnegie Mellon and built operational software at Dripos. These are competent backgrounds, but they are the backgrounds of operators, not visionaries. There is no evidence of the kind of extreme intellectual conviction or socially unconventional thinking that characterizes the founders I back. More concerning is the pivot signal: Chrisvin's personal website references founding "Lotus," a YC W26 company, with a different email domain. A rename or pivot during the batch does not signal definite optimism -- it signals the iterate-and-discover approach that I consider "code for unplanned." When Zuckerberg built Facebook, he did not test three different social networking concepts and settle on the one that got the most traction during a twelve-week accelerator program. He had a specific vision and refused to deviate from it.

The strongest bull case would require believing that the AI-native architectural advantage is structural and durable -- that incumbents literally cannot replicate it because their codebases are too deeply committed to pre-AI architectures, and that this creates a window of several years during which Tepali captures every new medspa opening. Vishnu's experience at Dripos, building all-in-one software for multi-location service businesses, is genuinely relevant domain experience, and the marketing-to-LTV attribution feature addresses a real pain point that current tools do not natively solve. If the medspa market doubles in five years and every new location adopts Tepali as its first platform, the switching costs of consolidated patient records, charting history, and marketing data could create meaningful lock-in. But this scenario requires Tepali to win a distribution race against Boulevard, Zenoti, and Vagaro -- all of whom have sales teams, brand recognition, and the ability to ship AI features on existing platforms within months. An architectural advantage in software is never permanent; it is a head start that gets compressed by incumbents with capital and customers. Boulevard's "500%+ growth" since 2022 suggests the premium segment is consolidating around existing winners, not waiting for a new entrant.

What Tepali is building is a better version of what already exists -- a more integrated, more AI-forward medspa management platform. This is definitionally a "1 to n" company. The individual components -- scheduling, CRM, charting, inventory, marketing attribution -- are well-understood software categories. Wrapping them in an AI assistant layer is a feature improvement, not a technological discontinuity. No one would have called this science fiction a generation ago. When I backed SpaceX, the secret was that reusable rockets were physically possible and would create a launch cost monopoly that no competitor could replicate. When I co-founded Palantir, the secret was that PayPal's fraud algorithms could be adapted for intelligence analysis at a scale the government could not achieve internally. There is no equivalent secret here. "Medspas need better software" is obvious. "AI makes software better" is obvious. The combination of two obvious truths does not produce a secret.
