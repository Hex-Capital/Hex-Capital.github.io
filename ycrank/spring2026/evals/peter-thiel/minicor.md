# Minicor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Minicor's dossier lists five funded competitors in a table — UiPath (public), Automation Anywhere ($6.8B raised), Skyvern, Induced AI, Browserbase — and then names three additional entrants: Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google, all shipping computer-use capabilities in their foundation models. The existence of a competitive landscape slide comparing multiple players pursuing the same thesis is the single clearest disqualifying signal in my framework. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with?" about Minicor, the answer is silence. That legacy Windows desktop applications resist API integration is not a secret — it is a complaint shared by every systems integrator and healthcare IT department in America. That AI agents can automate desktop workflows is the most consensus thesis in enterprise software today. Minicor's claimed 93–96% click accuracy versus 80–85% for raw foundation models is a quantitative margin, not a structural insight. Quantitative margins invite competition; structural secrets create monopolies. Every percentage point Anthropic and OpenAI improve their computer-use accuracy — and they will, because it is their core product roadmap — erodes Minicor's differentiation. This is the cleantech pattern applied to AI infrastructure: entrepreneurs swept into a category by its momentum rather than by possession of a specific, non-obvious truth about the world.

Faizaan Chishtie and Saheed Akinbile share a genuine prehistory — University of Ottawa Software Engineering, co-founding Software For Love — which satisfies the co-founder alignment criterion I take seriously. Faizaan's prior YC experience at Phoenix (W21) and his Homeboard venture reaching mid-five-figure ARR demonstrate an ability to ship product and find initial customers. Saheed's enterprise engineering tenure at Ford and Morgan Stanley provides relevant domain context for automotive and financial services verticals. But Minicor was born from a pivot: Laminar Run, Inc. was a low-code integration platform that encountered a legacy desktop problem and changed direction. In my framework, pivoting is diagnostic — it reveals an indefinite founder who discovers the market through iteration rather than designing the future through conviction. I find no evidence in the dossier of a bold, specific multi-year plan for what the world looks like when Minicor succeeds. The product description — "automations spoken into existence via MCP integration" — reads as feature iteration, not as a definite vision for monopolizing a category. The founders are capable; they are not, based on what I see here, building with the kind of irreversible commitment that characterizes my highest-conviction checks.

The bull case for Minicor deserves honest construction. One customer processing 25,000 patients per day is a meaningful pre-seed signal — production volume at that scale implies genuine workflow embedding, not a pilot. Six customer logos suggest organic pull across multiple accounts. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance, while not proprietary technology, cost real time and money to achieve and create a barrier that casual entrants from the browser-automation world (Skyvern at $2.7M seed, Induced AI at $2.3M) cannot trivially replicate. The B2B2B distribution insight — selling to AI companies who need legacy desktop access in their customers' environments, rather than selling to hospitals directly — is tactically shrewd and mirrors the Palantir pattern of embedding through intermediary relationships. If healthcare legacy vendors like Epic continue restricting third-party API access, Minicor's UI-automation approach becomes not merely convenient but structurally necessary. One could argue this is the Lyft exception: the AI-automation category is generating such enormous total value that even a specialized player in the desktop-native niche could produce venture-relevant returns within a $35B-and-growing RPA market. The compliance moat plus production switching costs at 25,000 patients/day could compound into genuine structural defensibility.

I find this bull case unpersuasive for one reason: the layer Minicor occupies is the exact layer that commoditizes. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are not building desktop automation as a side project — computer-use is a core capability differentiator for their foundation models. Minicor's website itself cites their accuracy against these providers as its primary value proposition, which means Minicor has defined its own moat in terms of a metric its upstream suppliers are actively optimizing. This is the structural problem the Wish investment taught me: when your value proposition can be described as "better execution in a space where larger, better-funded players are converging on the same capability," you do not have a monopoly — you have a race you will lose. The Lyft exception required a genuine duopoly with network effects; the RPA/desktop-automation market is fragmented across dozens of players with no winner-take-all dynamics. And Minicor's Palantir parallel breaks down on inspection: Palantir built proprietary analytical infrastructure with no upstream dependency on a foundation model provider who could replicate its core capability as a feature release. Minicor's self-healing orchestration layer, however sophisticated, sits atop someone else's model.

Minicor is a pure software company with no physical-world technology, no hardware component, and no atoms-level barrier to replication. The deployment targets — Windows VMs, Citrix environments — are virtual infrastructure. The compliance certifications (SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA) are process artifacts, not technical moats. In a portfolio where my highest-conviction recent bets involve nuclear fuel enrichment, X-ray lithography, autonomous defense hardware, and precision manufacturing, Minicor occupies precisely the software abstraction layer I have grown least excited about. A well-capitalized competitor — or an incumbent like UiPath with its existing customer base and professional services network — could rebuild Minicor's orchestration layer in months once the foundation models close the accuracy gap. The one verifiable condition that would change my assessment: if Minicor demonstrated that production-grade desktop automation at healthcare scale requires a durable, non-commoditizable orchestration layer — that the gap between "demo-quality" and "25,000 patients/day in production" is structurally wider than foundation model improvements can close — and validated this with binding enterprise contracts showing multi-year commitments, I would revisit. Until that evidence exists, Minicor is a competent team building in a market where competition, not monopoly, defines the structure.

The dimension most decisive for my verdict is Monopoly Secret: Minicor possesses no contrarian truth that the market disputes, and the competitive landscape — five funded competitors plus three foundation model providers — is evidence of consensus, not evidence of a hidden insight. The second decisive dimension is Physical-World Technology Moat: as a pure software wrapper around foundation model capabilities, Minicor has no replication barrier that survives the upstream providers improving their own accuracy. Capable founders building in a competitive market with a thin, commoditizing moat is precisely the structure my framework is designed to reject.
