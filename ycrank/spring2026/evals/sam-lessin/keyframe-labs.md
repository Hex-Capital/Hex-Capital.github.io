# Keyframe Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: Parth Radia spent five years at Ericsson building a 30-person team doing exactly what Keyframe does -- photorealistic digital humans, voice and video synthesis, petascale human datasets. He shipped production products in this category inside a Fortune 500. That's genuinely rare founder-domain overlap. But it also raises the question I always ask first: is the market wrong about this space? And the answer is no. The market is extremely, enthusiastically right about AI avatars. Tavus just closed $40M from CRV and Sequoia for the same product. HeyGen is doing $95M ARR. Synthesia is worth $4B. Lemon Slice pulled $10.5M at seed. Every venture investor in the country is trying to get into this category. Which means the upside is already in the price. A brilliant founder in a consensus-hot category is a bad bet at any quality level because you're paying full freight for the optionality.

The pricing argument is the only thing that gives me pause. Six cents a minute versus Tavus at thirty-two cents -- that's a real gap. If it's architecturally structural, not just "we're running on cheaper GPUs and accepting lower margins," then maybe you get a wedge where entirely new use cases open up that weren't economically viable at thirty cents. That's how AWS created categories -- not by competing with existing hosting, but by making things cheap enough that people built things that couldn't have existed before. If Keyframe at six cents enables always-on video agents for customer support at scale, that's a different market than "enterprise training avatars." But here's the problem: AI inference costs are dropping like a rock across the board. NVIDIA's next chips, open-source models getting better every quarter, Tavus with $64M to throw at inference optimization. A cost moat in AI inference is a melting ice cube. You're running to stay in place.

The primitive test is where this really falls apart for me. Real-time interactive video avatars already exist. Tavus has them. HeyGen has LiveAvatar. D-ID has them. This isn't Venmo creating a behavior that didn't exist before -- "Venmo me" was a new verb for a new action. This is "we do the same thing but cheaper and faster." That's an execution bet, not a category bet. I invest in protocols and primitives that compound, not in the fourth entrant in an established product category competing on basis points of latency and cost per minute. The underlying primitive here -- "put a face on an AI agent" -- was created by someone else. Keyframe is an application of that primitive, not the primitive itself.

And then the AI kill test, which this company fails in the most literal way possible: it IS an AI product. The entire value proposition is a model that renders video. When I say "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI, that's perfect for Adobe" -- well, Synthesia announced real-time Video Agents on their 2026 roadmap, and they have 70% of the Fortune 100 as customers already. They don't need to be better than Keyframe; they just need to be good enough and show up with existing enterprise relationships. The dossier explicitly flags this: Synthesia's entry brings "massive enterprise distribution to this segment." Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore, and Keyframe's moat is literally "our software runs cheaper." No physical assets, no protocol lock-in, no network effects that grow with usage.

The strongest bull case would require all of this to be true simultaneously: the cost advantage is architecturally durable and not replicable by well-funded competitors, the quality is genuinely superior at the latency claimed, voice agent proliferation creates a massive "add a face" API market where Keyframe becomes default infrastructure, and Synthesia's channel conflict prevents them from competing on per-minute pricing. If all four hold, you've got a company that becomes the rendering layer for the entire agent ecosystem -- infrastructure-level, not application-level. Parth's Ericsson background makes the technical bet more credible than most. His brother brings real-time perception from safety-critical drone systems at Zipline, which is complementary and serious. The siblings co-founding is actually a strong relationship signal -- deepest possible pre-existing trust. I could see a version of this where they nail the developer experience, become the Stripe of avatar APIs, and the six-cent price point creates a land-grab that higher-priced competitors can't match. But "could see a version" is not "what if they're right and everyone else is wrong." Everyone else is right that this is a good category. They're just also all already here.

Pass. Talented founder, wrong category for me. When every sophisticated investor at demo day is excited about the space, there's no asymmetry left to capture. I want the deals that make people uncomfortable, not the ones where CRV and Sequoia already wrote the $40M check to your closest competitor.
