# Moda -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Moda is that it's in a category where I can name five well-funded competitors without pausing. Braintrust has $121 million. LangChain has $260 million. Langfuse just got acquired by ClickHouse. Helicone and AgentOps exist. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space with this many funded players, my question isn't whether the market is real -- obviously it's real, that's why everyone is there. My question is whether this is a problem the founders discovered or a market they selected. Those are very different starting points.

Pranav Bedi worked at Cerebral Valley on LLM observability and agentic systems before starting Moda. That's worth something. He wasn't reading about this space in a newsletter -- he was inside it. The behavioral detection angle he's pursuing (hallucinations, tool misuse, agent laziness) versus infrastructure-level tracing (latency, cost, token counts) is a genuine distinction. I can see how someone building agentic systems daily would notice that the monitoring tools available tell you what happened mechanically but not whether the agent actually did its job correctly. That's a real observation. But it's also an observation that every engineer working with agents has made by now. This isn't schlep blindness. Nobody is unconsciously avoiding this problem because it's too painful. They're all running toward it. When Stripe tackled payments, the schlep was dealing with banks, fraud regulations, and PCI compliance -- ugly, tedious work that smart engineers instinctively recoiled from. Running ML classifiers over conversation logs to detect behavioral patterns is the opposite of a schlep. It's the kind of problem ML engineers actually enjoy solving. And enjoyable problems attract competition.

The bull case is that "agent behavioral monitoring" is a genuinely distinct category from "LLM observability," and that Moda could define it the way Stripe defined developer-friendly payments even though payment processors already existed. The distinction between monitoring infrastructure metrics and monitoring semantic behavior is real and growing -- as agents become more autonomous, the gap widens. If Moda builds the definitive taxonomy of agent failure modes from real production data, that could become a proprietary asset. The lightweight SDK integration and framework-agnostic approach are smart product choices. And having two Waterloo-trained engineers who both shipped code at Shopify means the product will get built. The 12,847 conversations analyzed, even if partly test data, means something is working. If the behavioral detection accuracy holds up (87-94% cited on the landing page) and they can get three or four serious companies dependent on their alerting workflows before the incumbents add this as a feature, they might establish enough switching costs to survive.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The underlying approach -- running LLM-based classifiers over agent conversation logs to detect behavioral patterns -- is technically reproducible." That's not a moat. That's a feature. Braintrust, sitting on $121 million, could build this in a quarter. So could Anthropic or OpenAI, directly in their dashboards. When your competitive advantage is primarily execution speed against well-capitalized incumbents, you need either a much deeper organic connection to the problem or evidence of extraordinary founder intensity. I see neither here. The founders are competent engineers with relevant backgrounds, but there's no signal of the extreme resourcefulness or determination that distinguishes the companies that survive category consolidation. No stories of creative pivots, no evidence of unscalable user acquisition, no users who would be economically harmed if Moda disappeared tomorrow.

I wrote in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies I'd seen were not working on AI. That wasn't because AI is bad -- it was because the AI space had become so consensus that the best founders were finding opportunities elsewhere, in problems that hadn't yet been crowded by capital. Moda sits squarely in the consensus zone. Two smart engineers saw a hot category, identified a reasonable wedge, and built a product. That's fine. But it's not how the best companies start. The best companies start because the founders can't stop thinking about a problem they personally can't live with, and the problem involves enough ugliness that nobody else wants to touch it. I don't see that here.
