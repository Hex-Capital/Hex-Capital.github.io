# Aurorin CAD -- Paul Graham Evaluation

This is a textbook schlep blindness play. Every programmer who has ever tried to build hardware knows CAD software is stuck in the 1980s. The kernels underneath SolidWorks, NX, and CATIA were designed when computers had a fraction of today's compute. Anyone who has waited four hours for an assembly to open knows the problem is real. Yet look at what the other AI-for-CAD startups are doing: Adam builds a plugin for Onshape. Zoo builds a text-to-CAD layer. They're all working around the kernel because rebuilding it from scratch is the kind of work your unconscious literally prevents you from seeing. It's too hard, too tedious, too deep. Michael Baron is doing the thing everyone else unconsciously filters out, and that pattern has produced some of the best companies I've funded.

The organic discovery here is genuine. Three internships at SpaceX — not one, three — working on Raptor combustion simulation, Dragon guidance and navigation, and Starshield flight software. This person was designing rocket engines and spacecraft subsystems in legacy CAD tools. He wasn't reading market reports about the $12 billion CAD market; he was sitting in front of NX or CATIA trying to do real engineering work and feeling the pain directly. Then he went to Apple and worked on GPU driver performance, which is exactly the technical foundation you'd need to build a GPU-accelerated geometry kernel. The trajectory from "frustrated engineer using broken tools" to "I'll build better tools" is the same trajectory that produced Dropbox, that produced Stripe. The idea grew from the founder's own work, not from a brainstorming session about what AI could disrupt next.

The schlep is enormous, and that's what makes this interesting rather than terrifying. SolidWorks is ten million lines of code. A parametric B-Rep kernel requires computational geometry, constraint solvers, precision arithmetic, and file format interoperability — any one of which is a multi-year project. Jared Friedman's observation that "this would have been impossible before AI coding got good" is the genuine why-now. If AI development tools compress the effort by even 5-10x, a technically exceptional founder might be able to accomplish in two years what previously took a team of fifty a decade. That's a real timing bet, and it's the kind of bet where being wrong about the timing is the main risk, not being wrong about the direction.

But I have to be honest about the solo founder problem, and it's severe. I made Drew Houston find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox, and Dropbox was orders of magnitude simpler than a CAD kernel. Baron is listed as team size one, not hiring. Building a kernel, an AI agent, and a production UI simultaneously — alone — requires either superhuman output or a bet that AI coding tools have crossed a threshold where one person genuinely can do the work of twenty. The Dropbox exception applied because Houston had a working prototype and the organic fit was undeniable. Baron has a downloadable product, which is a real signal. But the distance between "downloadable prototype" and "production CAD system that aerospace engineers trust for real work" is vast, and traversing it alone is a red flag I can't ignore.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If Baron can build a working kernel — and the downloadable product suggests he's further along than most would expect — the moat is massive. No fast-follower can replicate multi-year kernel engineering. The AI-native architecture advantage compounds: because he controls the kernel, the AI agent can operate at a depth that bolt-on approaches structurally cannot reach. If this works, it's not a feature — it's a new platform. The competitive landscape actually validates the approach: PTC paid $470 million for Onshape rather than rebuild internally, which tells you how hard kernel development is and how valuable a modern one would be. And Baron's background — SpaceX for the domain pain, Apple for the GPU expertise — is nearly the ideal combination for this specific problem. Getting three SpaceX internships apparently without a college degree is itself a signal of determination that's hard to fake.

What holds me back is the complete absence of user pull and the solo founder risk on a project of this magnitude. Thirty-seven YC Launch upvotes isn't a signal. No download numbers, no user testimonials, no community. I can't tell if mechanical engineers actually want this or if it's a technically impressive demo that doesn't yet solve real workflows. The enterprise switching costs in CAD are brutal — file migration, PLM integration, retraining, regulatory compliance in aerospace and automotive — and overcoming those requires not just a better product but a team that can handle the go-to-market schlep alongside the engineering schlep. One person cannot do both. I'd want to see Baron recruit at least one co-founder, ideally someone with enterprise sales DNA in the engineering software world, before I'd have real conviction.
