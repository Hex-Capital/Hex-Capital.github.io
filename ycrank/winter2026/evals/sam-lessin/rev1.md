# REV1 -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps off the page before I even get to the founders: DraftAid, a YC W24 company, is building the identical product — 3D CAD to 2D annotated drawing automation — and has a two-year head start with 210,000+ drawings created and $150K in paid pilot contracts. SolidWorks shipped a beta Auto-Generate Drawing command in its 2025/2026 release. Leo AI raised $9.7M for a broader mechanical engineering copilot. REV1 is entering a market where the idea is not only not contrarian — it's already being executed by multiple well-funded competitors and the largest incumbent simultaneously. When I look for "sane person, insane idea," the insane part is supposed to mean most people think you're crazy. Nobody thinks automating tedious engineering drawings is crazy. Every mechanical engineer I've ever met has complained about this. It's the most obvious automation target in hardware engineering. This is a sane person with a sane idea, and sane-sane is the least interesting quadrant in my framework.

Alex Rivero's credentials are genuinely strong — Tesla Motor Design on Cybertruck and Robotaxi, Apple contributing to iPhone 17, a mechanical engineering MS from Berkeley, and he personally created the drawings he's now trying to automate. Louis Liu solo-built Tesla's global Supercharger and Robotaxi planning platform at the software layer and has published ML research. The founder-market fit is real: a domain expert paired with an ML-capable platform engineer who overlapped at Tesla. I don't question their competence. But competence in a consensus market doesn't produce venture-scale returns — it produces a competition on execution speed where the winner is determined by who has more distribution, and in this case, that's SolidWorks (Dassault Systèmes, $56B+ market cap) adding this as a native feature to the tool every mechanical engineer already uses.

Now let me run the AI kill test, which is where this falls apart structurally. REV1 is software automating a software workflow. The moat is... GD&T annotation accuracy? That's a model capability that improves with every foundation model release. The 300 pages of GD&T rules the YC page cites as complexity? That's a finite, well-documented standard — exactly the kind of structured rule set that AI systems excel at learning. The dossier itself states "No defensibility signals found in public sources" and the inferred moats — proprietary training data, PLM integrations, accuracy advantages — are speculative. This is the precise category I've been warning about since my "SaaS era is over" essay: software companies whose moats evaporate as AI makes development cheaper. Every CAD platform will have this built in within 18 months. REV1 isn't a painting company with a tech layer — it's a tech layer with no painting company underneath.

The strongest bull case I can construct: the CAD-agnostic angle has structural logic. Companies running SolidWorks, Siemens NX, and PTC Creo simultaneously need a tool that works across all three, and no single incumbent has incentive to support competitors' formats. If REV1 could become the cross-platform drawing standard — the Figma of manufacturing documentation — that's a primitive play, not an application play. And in safety-critical verticals like aerospace and medical devices, accuracy in GD&T tolerancing is literally life-or-death, so a tool that demonstrates superior correctness could build real switching costs. If they accumulated enough proprietary training data from customer-specific annotation conventions, that could compound into something defensible. But this bull case requires REV1 to win the accuracy race against DraftAid (which has 210K+ drawings of training data already), against Leo AI (which has $9.7M and 20K+ engineers), and against SolidWorks' own AI team — all while being pre-revenue with zero disclosed traction. The narrative expansion from "drawing automation" to "cross-platform manufacturing documentation infrastructure" is plausible in theory but requires winning a knife fight in a crowded room first.

I keep coming back to Birchbox as the cautionary pattern. The pain point was real. The founders were credible. The initial wedge was clear. But the thing was trivially replicable, and when competitors showed up, there was nothing structural to defend. REV1's drawing automation is the same pattern: real pain, credible team, no structural moat. The difference is that REV1's competitors aren't even hypothetical future entrants — they're already here, already funded, and in DraftAid's case, already generating revenue in the exact same product category. This is a pass.
