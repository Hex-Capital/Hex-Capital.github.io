# Hexa -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Hexa is the exact silhouette I trained myself to recognize and decline: a labor-leverage enterprise SaaS overlay — three founders, $9K MRR from three customers, Calendly demo-book flow, SAP/NetSuite/Epicor/Dynamics integrations — selling into a market where Didero just closed a $30M Series A on Feb 12, 2026 for the same wedge. The leverage architecture is wrong before I read the founder section. "Code and media are permissionless leverage. Labor and capital require permission." Hexa's distribution requires permission from ERP procurement committees, from SAP integration certifications, from David Lieb's outbound playbook. That is the tell.

Twitter (2007) is my high rung on permissionless leverage — anyone posts, marginal cost zero, no gatekeeper between the founder's product and the next user. The shared mechanism I would need from Hexa is some channel where a manufacturer adopts the agent without a procurement signature — an open-source connector on GitHub, a Chrome extension that scrapes RFQ emails, a free tier the buyer at Acme Industrial signs up to without involving IT. The hexaagents.com site exposes only a Calendly demo flow; no Product Hunt listing, no public repos for either Ishaan Makkar or Auri Nayak, no community contribution surface. Hexa diverges from the Twitter rung at the most basic point: every dollar of the $9K MRR was earned by a human pitching another human, and the next dollar will cost the same.

Pinterest is my most expensive pass and the wrong analogy for Hexa — Pinterest passed permissionless media and I missed it on aesthetic bias. Hexa fails permissionless leverage on architecture, not on taste. The closer LOW-rung pattern is the "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity in $X TAM" template — pedigree-as-thesis with no shipped artifact. Ishaan Makkar's public surface is one LinkedIn snippet citing the "54% of manufacturers run on spreadsheets" statistic; Auri Nayak surfaces only as "Aurideep Nayak, Hexa YC X26" at a DeepMind hackathon. No GitHub for either. No prior manufacturing operator role documented. The Stack Overflow (2008) gate — Atwood and Spolsky were developers writing for developers — is exactly what is absent here. Hexa's founders may have the specific knowledge; the public dossier shows none of it, and at pre-seed the burden of proof on specific knowledge is the load-bearing filter when distribution is permissioned.

Didero's $30M Series A is the mechanism that compounds against Hexa over the next 18 months — not because Didero is better, but because in a labor-leverage category capital buys SDRs and SDRs buy the same manufacturer logos Hexa is chasing. Hexa lists eight ERP/CRM integrations (SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, Epicor, Salesforce, HubSpot, Zoho, Pipedrive) as differentiation; that is integration-as-moat, which is to say no moat — Didero ships the same connectors with 37x the engineering headcount. The $9K MRR across three customers means one churn event removes 33% of revenue. Network effects between customers? None. Each Acme Industrial deployment makes the next Acme deployment zero percent easier.

The counterfactual on founder specific knowledge: if Ishaan Makkar had spent five years at Fastenal or Grainger writing procurement systems, or if Auri Nayak had shipped an open-source EDI parser used by 200 distributors, the score flips a tier. The dossier offers neither. Manufacturing-digitization is exactly the domain where lived specific knowledge — knowing why the part-number-matching problem is hard because catalogs collide on SKU revisions — separates the founder who ships from the founder who pitches. Hexa's public footprint is consistent with the latter and silent on the former; I apply the absent-evidence default and move on.

Manufacturing and distribution is genuinely a big market with pivot room — Hexa could move from sales-side quoting to procurement-side RFQs to AP automation to supplier discovery without leaving the ICP. That is the one dimension where Hexa's placement sits closer to the Uber (2010) rung than the niche-SaaS floor. But pivot room is the safety margin, not the thesis. A big market with permissioned distribution and well-funded competition is the **Coupa** / **Zip** / **Tropic** lane — companies that became real businesses with hundreds of millions in capital and zero of them on my ledger.

My 36-month forecast: Hexa does not cross $5M ARR by mid-2029, and the modal outcome is acqui-hire by Didero, an ERP partner, or a quiet wind-down — with a non-trivial tail where Auri Nayak and Ishaan Makkar pivot the company into a narrower wedge (catalog-matching API, RFQ parsing as a service) that finds purchase as infrastructure beneath the agent layer. The mechanism is the same one that killed every undifferentiated overlay in 2014–2018 cloud-sales-software: capital-leverage competitors with the same product spec out-hire you on SDRs while ERP incumbents bundle the feature into the next release cycle.

The strongest case against this forecast: $9K MRR from three customers in six weeks at ~$3K ACV is real velocity for pre-seed, the "76% of supply chain professionals believe autonomous AI agents are ready" tailwind is the kind of narrative-arrival moment **MetaStable** (2014) taught me to fund pre-consensus, and accumulated catalog/pricing graphs across SAP and NetSuite tenants could become genuine switching cost by Series A. The Perplexity (2022) exception is the precedent — single-sided builder-product, deep founder specific knowledge, pre-consensus AI timing, three of five gates clearing emphatically. That case stings because traction velocity at pre-seed is exactly the signal I respect. It loses anyway on the founder gate: Perplexity's exception required Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD; Hexa's dossier produces no comparable specific-knowledge artifact for either Makkar or Nayak. The condition that flips my verdict: Ishaan Makkar or Auri Nayak ships an open-source connector or RFQ-parsing library that gets adopted by 50+ manufacturers outside the paying customer set within 12 months — a permissionless-distribution signal that would force me back to the table.
