# Robby -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Robby is that it's building on top of ServiceTitan -- a $9 billion public company that just announced its own AI features for 2026, including campaign recommendations and booking agents. This is the defining strategic question. Building a revenue-generation layer on someone else's platform works when there's a structural reason the platform won't build it themselves. Veeva succeeded on Salesforce because life sciences compliance required domain depth that Salesforce would never prioritize. The question for Robby is whether technician-level upselling creates similar structural tension with ServiceTitan's core positioning. The dossier's inference is interesting here: ServiceTitan optimizes for operational efficiency across the entire customer base, while Robby optimizes for revenue maximization per visit -- and aggressive upselling tools could alienate contractors who chose ServiceTitan precisely because it's a neutral operational platform. If that tension is real, Robby has room. If it's not, this is a feature waiting to be absorbed.

The market archetype, however, is exactly the kind I hunt for. Home services technician upselling is genuinely boring -- it's not AI for legal, not AI for code, not AI for any of the categories that every fund in San Francisco is fighting over. When I backed PagerDuty, operations alerting triggered the same dismissive reaction from investors that "HVAC technician talking points" would trigger today. The U.S. home services market is $650-750 billion annually, which is enormous, but Robby's actual wedge is narrower: revenue intelligence for field technicians on ServiceTitan. The "why now" has two specific components beyond generic LLM capability. First, ServiceTitan's IPO and platform maturity created a standardized data layer that third-party tools can integrate with -- this is the Salesforce AppExchange moment for home services. Second, PE consolidation is creating larger, more professionalized home services operators who think in terms of revenue per technician visit, not just scheduling efficiency. That PE angle is where Schwarzmann's Bain background becomes a potential distribution weapon -- if he has relationships with PE firms rolling up HVAC portfolios, a single PE relationship could yield dozens of locations overnight.

Nine named paying customers at pre-seed is a strong execution signal. Launching in September 2025 and having paying customers by February 2026, with specific claims like "$40K closed within the first hour of launch," shows the team can ship and sell. The EPA 608 certification pursuit and technician ride-alongs demonstrate the kind of domain immersion that creates switching costs -- once technicians depend on Robby for pre-job intelligence and automatic documentation, ripping it out is operationally painful. That said, the traction claims need scrutiny. "Six figures in additional revenue opportunities every week" is not the same as six figures in closed revenue attributed to the tool. Opportunities surfaced versus deals actually closed is a meaningful distinction, and if customers discover that conversion rates on those "opportunities" are low, the value proposition deflates.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is plausible but not yet compelling. The initial wedge -- technician upselling and documentation -- could expand into a broader revenue operations platform: training modules, quality management, customer lifecycle marketing, pricing optimization. The data flywheel is real: every technician visit generates proprietary data on equipment conditions, customer preferences, and conversion patterns that improves lead quality over time. But the ServiceTitan dependency constrains distribution independence. Robby can't become a platform if its data access depends on another platform's API permissions. Contrast this with how Stripe's developer API created an independent distribution channel -- Robby's distribution runs through ServiceTitan's pipes, which limits how far the product-to-distribution arc can extend.

The team composition maps well to the problem. Schwarzmann brings the PE-home-services pattern recognition, Jammalamadaka brings data platform engineering from founding engineer roles, and Mohideen brings product engineering depth plus an unusual credential from his time at Andreessen Horowitz. The co-founder relationship has been tested -- Mohideen and Schwarzmann previously co-founded Lifelong Health together, and all three met at HBS. The pivot history (Importrix, Lifelong Health, now Robby) is a mild concern at the speed it occurred, but the current direction has the strongest founder-market fit of the three attempts. If I'm being honest, the single-miracle test here reveals two obstacles rather than one: technician adoption at scale AND surviving ServiceTitan's AI expansion. That compounding is what keeps my conviction at neutral rather than high. The bull case requires that ServiceTitan's structural position as a neutral operational platform genuinely prevents them from building aggressive revenue-maximization tools -- and that the PE distribution channel unlocks rapid, multi-location growth before anyone else captures this niche. If both are true, this is Gusto for home services revenue -- boring, critical, and deeply embedded. But "if both are true" is two miracles, not one.
