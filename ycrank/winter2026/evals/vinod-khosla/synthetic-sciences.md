# Synthetic Sciences -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Synthetic Sciences: two 18-year-old founders with 15+ publications at ICML and NeurIPS, research stints at CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL, a prior exit, and competitive programming credentials that put them in the top fraction of a percent globally. These are genuine best athletes -- the kind of raw intellectual horsepower I look for before anything else. And they've aimed all of that firepower at building a better research workflow tool. That mismatch between founder capability and problem ambition is the central tension in this evaluation. I've seen this pattern before and it frustrates me every time: brilliant people who've de-risked their way into a market that, even if they win, produces a nice SaaS business rather than a transformation of how science operates.

Let me start where I always start -- the consequence of success. If Synthetic Sciences works perfectly, what happens? Computational researchers produce papers faster. They delegate literature reviews and experiment execution to AI agents while they sleep. The research pipeline gets 2-3x more efficient. And I stop right there and ask: does the world operate differently? Does a trillion-dollar system become obsolete? The answer is no. This is a productivity tool for a population of maybe a few hundred thousand computational researchers worldwide, priced at $50-200 per month. The TAM projections cite a $34 billion market by 2035, but that's the entire AI-for-scientific-discovery space, not the addressable market for a workflow orchestration layer. If they capture 5% of that, they build a $1.7 billion company in a decade. That's a fine outcome for most investors, but it's not why I write checks. The "Flywheel" mode hints at something more ambitious -- using product usage data to train increasingly autonomous AI scientists. If that trajectory leads to genuine autonomous discovery, the consequence magnitude explodes. But that's a research aspiration, not a product today. Today, this is an agent wrapper around existing LLMs with credential syncing and GPU orchestration.

The technology disruption question makes me uncomfortable. The dossier itself articulates the problem clearly: "the core capability -- wrapping LLM agents with research workflow tooling -- is reproducible by well-funded competitors." Edison Scientific just raised $70 million from people like Jeff Dean specifically to build autonomous AI scientists for biology and chemistry. Elicit already has 400,000 monthly researchers. Google announced its own AI Co-Scientist. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, Pradeep Sindhu had a fundamentally different architectural approach -- TCP/IP routing versus legacy switching -- that made Cisco's technology structurally obsolete at scale. I don't see the structural disruption here. Synthetic Sciences integrates existing frontier models with research toolchains. That's valuable plumbing, but it's plumbing. Any well-funded competitor can replicate credential syncing and GPU orchestration. The 92% claim on BixBench Verified is interesting but unverifiable, and the published BixBench results show frontier models at 17% -- suggesting either a very different evaluation protocol or a subset that flatters the result. I can't invest conviction capital on a benchmark I can't corroborate.

Now let me make the strongest case for investing, because these founders deserve it. The learning rate signal is extraordinary. Fifteen publications at top ML venues by age 18 is not just precocious -- it demonstrates the kind of compressed intellectual evolution I track obsessively. Bansal's trajectory from research at four world-class institutions to a prior exit to YC, all before turning 19, shows a rate of change that puts him in rare company. The pivot from InkVell (LaTeX formatting) to Synthetic Sciences (full-stack AI co-scientist) during YC could be read as exactly the kind of evolved thinking I want to see -- founders who arrived with one thesis, confronted the market, and expanded their ambition. If I were structuring this as an option-value bet at the $1 million level, I'd be betting on the founders eventually finding a problem calibrated to their ability. The question is whether Synthetic Sciences as currently conceived is that problem, or whether it's a stepping stone to something more ambitious. At their age, with their learning rate, I'd expect two or three more pivots before they find the thing that matters at scale.

The timing is favorable but consensus, which is a red flag in my framework. Everyone knows AI agents for research are coming. I prefer to invest when experts dismiss the idea and outsiders haven't crowded in. This space has $70 million flowing to Edison Scientific, $31 million to Elicit, Google's direct entry, and Sakana AI at $2.65 billion valuation. The founders aren't contrarian here -- they're competing in a market that every sophisticated investor already considers obvious. Compare this to when I wrote my check to OpenAI in 2019: AI was "laughable," no one was funding it at scale, and the trajectory was visible only if you tracked the improvement rate rather than current performance. Synthetic Sciences in 2026 is arriving at a party that started two years ago.

The team composition gives me pause. Two researchers with nearly identical backgrounds -- both Indian, both 18, both ML-focused -- building a product that targets biology workflows (protein design, genomics, pathway analysis) without deep biology expertise on the team. My gene pool engineering framework asks: have the founders identified their top risks and recruited specifically to address them? The product scope demands infrastructure engineering, enterprise sales capability, and domain expertise in computational biology. None of that is present. The homogeneity isn't disqualifying at pre-seed -- most YC companies start with two co-founders who know each other -- but it means the critical execution risks are unaddressed.

I'd pass on this as structured today, but I'd tell these founders what I genuinely believe: you are too talented for this problem. Go find something where success means the current system is destroyed, not optimized. You have the learning rate, the research depth, and the youthful irreverence to attempt something the establishment considers impossible. An AI research workflow tool isn't that. Find the problem that scares you, where the experts say it can't be done, and where the consequence of getting it right rewires how the world works. Then come back. I'll be interested.
