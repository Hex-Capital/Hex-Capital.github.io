# Protent -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape. Verkada has raised $700 million. Lumana has $64 million. Spot AI has $93 million. Actuate has nearly $24 million. These aren't hypothetical future competitors -- they're well-funded companies already building AI-powered video intelligence. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where incumbents have collectively raised close to a billion dollars, I want to understand why this particular team sees something everyone else missed. And here, the honest answer is: I'm not sure they do. The core insight -- that surveillance cameras generate more footage than humans can watch, and AI should analyze it in real time -- is not a secret. It's the thesis behind every one of those competitors. The specificity is the law enforcement angle, but that's a go-to-market choice, not a hidden problem.

There is a schlep argument to be made, though. Commercial video intelligence companies like Verkada and Spot AI sell to businesses because the sales cycles are shorter, the procurement is simpler, and you don't have to navigate CJIS compliance, FedRAMP, or city council approval. Government sales -- especially to police departments -- involves exactly the kind of tedious, painful complexity that causes smart founders to look the other direction. Every startup selling AI video analytics could pursue law enforcement, but most don't, because the procurement process is a nightmare and the political optics of "AI surveillance for police" generate hostile public commentary. The YC LinkedIn post about Protent apparently drew substantial critical commentary about dystopian surveillance. Most founders see that coming and steer toward enterprise instead. So in a narrow sense, there is a schlep here that deters competitors.

But here's my concern: this schlep might not be the productive kind. When Stripe tackled payment processing, the regulatory complexity was stable and navigable -- painful, yes, but the rules weren't changing underneath you. With law enforcement AI surveillance, the regulatory environment is actively hostile in some jurisdictions. San Francisco, Boston, Portland have passed restrictions on facial recognition and AI surveillance. This isn't a schlep you can power through with determination; it's a structural risk that could close markets entirely. That distinction matters. A schlep that scares away the lazy is an opportunity. A schlep that reflects genuine political headwinds is just... a headwind.

The organic discovery question is partially answered. Srihan Balaji deployed reinforcement-learning-optimized video intelligence at Lockheed Martin. He didn't read a TechCrunch article about AI surveillance and decide it was hot -- he was inside the defense world building these systems and recognized the gap in civilian law enforcement. That's a real connection. But it's closer to "I have a capability looking for a market" than "I stumbled into a problem I couldn't stop thinking about." The strongest organic origins produce founders who are driven by frustration with a broken system they personally experienced. Working at Lockheed on video AI and deciding police departments need it too is a reasonable inference, not a burning need. And I can't find any information about how Srihan and Abhisheik connected. No shared university, no shared employer. When cofounders meet specifically to start a company, that's one of my strongest negative signals -- though I acknowledge I may be wrong here and they could have a deeper relationship not captured in public data.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. Deployed with police departments in Atlanta, Chicago, and St. Louis metro areas -- if those are genuine working deployments, not just free pilots, that would be exceptional for a two-person pre-seed team selling into government. Getting through any door at a major metro police department is hard. Getting through three is a signal that these founders are doing something right on the ground, possibly demonstrating the kind of hands-on hustle that matters most at this stage. If those deployments are generating revenue or even signed LOIs, this evaluation changes significantly. The technical credentials are also genuine: RL video intelligence from defense plus published NLP research is exactly the right combination for building an escalation-detection system. They clearly built this themselves -- a two-person team with working deployments means nobody else built it for them.

But I keep coming back to the competitive dynamics. This is not a market where being first matters as much as being funded. Lumana AI's vision-language model approach and "any camera into a proactive sensor" positioning is nearly identical to what Protent describes. If Lumana decides to pursue law enforcement -- and $64 million gives them the runway to endure government procurement cycles -- Protent's niche evaporates. At pre-seed, you need either a problem nobody else wants to touch or a technical insight nobody else has. The problem here is one that well-funded competitors could reach by expanding their go-to-market. And the underlying technology -- computer vision, NLP, sentiment analysis -- is increasingly commoditized. Government relationships and compliance positioning are defensible, but they're grind moats, not technical moats, and the founders appear to be in their early twenties selling to police chiefs. That's a mismatch I'd need more evidence to get past.
