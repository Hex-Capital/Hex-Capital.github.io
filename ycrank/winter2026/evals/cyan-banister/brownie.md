# IncidentFox -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about IncidentFox is that this is one of the most popular categories in enterprise software right now. A December 2025 landscape survey identified at least ten dedicated AI SRE startups. Neubird has $22.5 million from Microsoft. incident.io just raised $62 million specifically to build AI agents that resolve incidents. PagerDuty won AIOps Platform of the Year. When I look at a space where every major platform company and a dozen funded startups are converging on the same thesis at the same time -- that's the opposite of where I want to be. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. AI SRE tooling in early 2026 is neither of those things. Every investor I know is excited about this category, and when that happens, the weirdness has already been priced out.

If I could sit down with Jimmy and Long, the first thing I'd ask is: tell me your story. Not the business -- how did you get here? And from what I can see in the dossier, the honest answer is: they were engineers at Roblox, Long dealt with painful on-call rotations on the database infrastructure team, and they decided to build a better tool. That's a perfectly legitimate professional origin. But it's not the kind of biographical thread I need to feel conviction. When I backed Crusoe Energy, the founders had independently arrived at an insight about waste gas and computation that seemed insane to everyone -- it came from deep in their bones, not from a frustrating quarter on the on-call rotation. Long Yi graduated in 2023 and has been working for perhaps two years. Jimmy has solid credentials from Meta FAIR and Cornell. These are talented engineers. But I'm not hearing a survival story. I'm not hearing about years of obsession with a problem nobody else cared about. I'm hearing about smart people building in a hot category, and that pattern doesn't activate my conviction.

The bull case is real and I want to give it its due. The combination of Long's SRE infrastructure experience at Roblox scale (100 million daily active users is no joke) with Jimmy's AI research background from Meta FAIR maps precisely onto the technical challenge of building an agent that understands production systems. Their open-source approach -- uncommon among AI SRE startups -- could create a genuine distribution moat if developer adoption scales, following the GitLab and PostHog playbook. The auto-discovery of integrations, where the agent learns your specific infrastructure by analyzing codebases and past incidents rather than requiring weeks of manual setup, is a smart differentiation. The 1,003 commits and 413 GitHub stars pre-pilot suggest real engineering output and early developer interest. If they become the default open-source AI SRE before the market consolidates, the outcome could be enormous. A more analytically-driven investor who weighs founder-market fit in professional terms and values execution in validated markets would likely be more excited about this than I am.

But that investor isn't me. I keep coming back to three absences. First, there's no economic access story here. IncidentFox serves well-resourced engineering teams at companies that already pay for Datadog, PagerDuty, and Kubernetes clusters. It makes efficient people more efficient. It doesn't create income for drivers or transparent credit for the underbanked or visibility into systems that affect everyday people. Second, this isn't a stigmatized or regulated space that scares other investors away -- it's developer tools infrastructure, one of the most well-trodden paths in venture. The enterprise trust barrier for autonomous remediation is a real complexity, and their credential isolation and sandboxed execution architecture shows they take it seriously, but that's table stakes engineering, not the kind of regulatory moat that kept other investors away from Contraline or Anduril. Third, the commoditization risk is severe. When the barrier to building a basic "query logs and summarize" agent is low and falling, and when your most dangerous competitor (incident.io) has $96 million and an existing customer base to layer AI onto, your differentiation window is narrow. The open-source core is a smart response to this, but it also creates its own monetization challenge -- the BSL 1.1 enterprise features convert to fully open Apache 2.0 in four years, compressing the revenue capture window.

The co-founders share a Roblox background, which gives me some comfort about alignment -- they've worked in the same organization and presumably understand each other's working styles. But I have no information about how they handle real disagreement under pressure, and after what happened with HQ Trivia, that's something I always want to understand before writing a check. No red flags here, just insufficient signal.

IncidentFox is a competent team building a well-architected product in a large and growing market. For an investor whose framework prioritizes professional founder-market fit and execution quality in validated categories, this could easily be an exciting bet. For me, it's missing the biographical conviction, the contrarian weirdness, and the human impact that I need to feel that jolt of energy. I wish them well -- the engineering is clearly strong -- but this isn't my kind of investment.
