# Parasma -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Sean Cole is the rare YC S26 founder where the category is genuinely illegible and the financing shape is genuinely wrong, and Parasma's signature is that contradiction sitting in one solo-founder LinkedIn. The Doom-on-neurons demo got Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, and Popular Science to write about human brain cells in a petri dish, which is the kind of press footprint that tells me no tier-1 fund knows how to underwrite the round -- and that's the part I like. The part I don't like is that Parasma is an algorithm layer riding on Cortical Labs' $35K CL1 hardware and the Cortical Cloud Python interface, which is a hardware-burn cap table dressed in software clothing, and the vendor can foreclose distribution any quarter it wants.

Solana at $0.05 is the HIGH rung on price-discipline -- everyone had quit crypto post-ICO crash, the seed was non-consensus by construction, and the asset was illegible to every generalist fund that still had a SaaS muscle. Parasma sits one rung below that, closer to where I priced **Aleo** at seed: biocomputing in S26 is a category two-plus tier-1 funds would visibly pass on, the YC description "writes the algorithms to turn brain cells into compute" doesn't translate into any existing VC spreadsheet, and DARPA running the O-CIRCUIT program is the kind of government R&D footprint that tells me the commercial cohort hasn't priced this yet. Where Parasma diverges from the Solana rung is that Solana owned its own protocol layer; Sean Cole is writing algorithms against someone else's Python API, which means the illegibility is real but the asset is rentable, not owned.

**Ro** is the LOW rung on capital structure -- a high-burn DTC pharma rollup I underwrote anyway because the 50-state prescription-rail regulatory schleps were the moat, and only venture capital could clear them. The Ro failure mode -- needing five rounds to reach default-alive -- is exactly the pattern Parasma's dossier reproduces without the regulatory moat to justify it. Parasma has no disclosed revenue, no LOIs, no pricing, a team size of 1, and a product that "stumbles through Doom -- badly, but they're learning" per Scientific American. There is no 50-state schlep that Cortical Labs ($11.6M raised, Series A March 2026) and FinalSpark (Series A undisclosed, 16 organoids on Neuroplatform) can't out-build natively, and the canonical Ro override -- regulatory complexity as moat -- doesn't apply when the vendor itself ships the Python interface.

Sean Cole's conviction signature -- counterfactual mode -- compounds over 18 months only if he produces a non-obvious belief about why neuron-substrate computing requires an independent algorithm layer that hardware vendors structurally cannot ship. The Twitter bio "Made some brain cells play Doom," the GitHub repo "doom-neuron," and the University of Sussex Facebook feature are visible obsession artifacts; the zmescience.com note that he had "little experience in biological computing" before the Doom project tells me the obsession is six months old, not three years old. For Parasma to score a tier higher on conviction, Sean Cole would need a published essay on the failure modes of vertically integrated wetware platforms -- and that essay does not exist in the dossier.

Founder-as-brand on Parasma is a mechanism question, not a magnitude question. Sean Cole got Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, Popular Science, Interesting Engineering, ZME Science, 80.lv, and 3DVF inside a Feb-April 2026 window off a single Doom demo, which is more earned media than 95% of YC S26 will get in their whole batch. The mechanism is "weird-thing-does-recognizable-thing" press arbitrage, and that compounds for one news cycle, not three. Unlike John Fish at Bookshelved -- where the YouTube subscriber base is an owned audience compounding monthly -- the @vytalow follower count isn't even retrievable, and there is no newsletter, Discord, or community Sean Cole has built that survives past the next demo.

Cortical Labs is the single-anchor parallel for category fit. Biocomputing is not on my explicit sleeve list -- it isn't pre-consensus crypto, isn't DTC, isn't prescription-rail health, isn't creator-economy -- but it is also not on the explicit avoid list, because "AI is just more internet" was my call on foundation-model SaaS at consensus pricing, not on biological substrate research at seed. Parasma sits adjacent to the crypto sleeve mechanically: a fundamentally disruptive computing primitive, no on-chain-equivalent economic activity yet, and a vendor-platform dynamic that mirrors the early Ethereum-vs-application-layer split. Where Parasma fails the crypto-sleeve translation is that there's no decentralized protocol -- there's one vendor in Melbourne and one in Vevey, and Parasma is downstream of both.

Parasma in 36 months is acqui-hired by Cortical Labs for $2M-$8M as an algorithm-team acquisition that lets Cortical extend natively upstream from the CL1 wetware into the training-protocol layer, with Sean Cole running an internal applied-research group rather than an independent company. The mechanism: the Cortical Cloud Python interface already exposes the surface Sean Cole's algorithms call into, so Cortical Labs faces a build-vs-buy decision on the algorithm layer by Series B, and buying the founder who got them their best press cycle is cheaper than rebuilding from scratch -- with the kicker that no third-party algorithm vendor can survive a hardware platform that wants to own its own stack.

The bear case I have to dismantle is the Solana-analogy bull case: that biocomputing in 2026 looks exactly like crypto in 2018 -- illegible, vendor-fragmented, pre-economic, and that Parasma is the algorithm-layer Aleo of wetware. The bull case stings because the Doom demo reportedly outperformed the standalone learning algorithm, which is the closest thing to "on-chain economic activity" this sleeve produces, and because Sean Cole is the named operator the press already mapped to the category. It loses to the dossier because Solana owned its base layer and Parasma rents it -- Cortical Labs sells the $35K CL1 and ships the Python API, and FinalSpark's Neuroplatform exposes 16 organoids through its own API, which means Parasma is two API-deprecation announcements away from a dead product. The single condition that flips my verdict: Sean Cole closes a multi-year exclusive co-development or royalty agreement with Cortical Labs, or restructures Parasma as a creator-style instrument on his future biocompute IP rather than as a SAFE-cap algorithm company -- either move converts the rented asset into an owned one, and I'll take that meeting.
