# Palus Finance -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check with any fintech startup is whether it's software-driven or software-aware. Palus connects to your bank via Plaid, custodies assets through Alpaca, and invests in an existing ETF (MBSF) managed by Regan Capital. Strip away those three integrations and you have a financial advisor recommending agency MBS to startups. The software makes this more accessible, but it doesn't create compounding advantages -- it's a delivery mechanism for an investment strategy that any institutional finance professional could articulate on a whiteboard. When I wrote about "smart toasters," I was describing exactly this pattern: technology bolted onto a fundamentally non-tech value proposition. The 1-1.5% yield premium over MMFs comes from the asset class selection, not from any proprietary algorithm or data flywheel. That's a financial services business with a web interface, not a technology company with financial services embedded in it.

The market structure question is where I'd want to be wrong but don't think I am. Mercury, Brex, Rho, and Arc have all built treasury products. The dossier argues there's an innovator's dilemma -- incumbents can't offer bond-portfolio yields without cannibalizing their MMF fee-sharing arrangements and complicating their banking UX. That's a real dynamic, and it's the strongest thing Palus has going for it. But innovator's dilemma protection is temporary by nature. Mercury has $650M in annualized revenue and $3.5B in valuation. If startup customers start demanding higher yields in volume, Mercury will build a "Treasury Pro" tier in a quarter rather than watch AUM walk out the door. The disincentive to cannibalize exists today; it evaporates the moment the opportunity is large enough to matter. And Palus's own addressable market is structurally constrained -- they note that Series B+ companies are often locked into specific bank relationships by venture debt covenants. So the addressable customers are early-stage startups with relatively small balances, generating $12,500/year in fees per $5M of idle cash at their 0.25% rate. The math to build a venture-scale outcome here is brutal.

Where's the structural "why now"? The dossier points to declining Fed funds rates making the MBS spread more attractive. But interest rate cycles are cyclical, not structural -- they're the opposite of a technology threshold being crossed. When I backed Harvey, the "why now" was GPT scaling laws pointing to a specific capability threshold 12-18 months out. When I backed Coinbase, the "why now" was blockchain infrastructure reaching a maturity point that enabled consumer-grade exchange products. For Palus, the "why now" is "rates are going down, so our spread looks better." That's a macro trade, not a structural inflection. And it cuts both ways: if rates stabilize or reverse, the incremental yield Palus offers becomes proportionally less compelling. Building a business whose core value proposition is a function of the yield curve is building on sand.

The strongest bull case would require believing that Palus can evolve from "yield optimization on idle cash" into a comprehensive startup treasury platform -- duration matching, cash flow forecasting, multi-asset portfolio construction, maybe eventually lending against the portfolio. The Gusto analogy: "just payroll" became the full HR platform. If Palus accumulates enough data on startup cash drawdown patterns, it could build something genuinely proprietary around cash flow prediction and portfolio optimization. The YC network gives them a warm distribution channel, and the pivot from consumer savings to startup treasury shows the founders can read market signals and adapt. Sam Lushtak's experience building investment research platforms for asset managers managing $3T+ AUM is genuine domain exposure. For this bull case to work, though, they'd need to build real software moats fast -- the kind of data-driven portfolio optimization that makes the platform meaningfully better than what a financial advisor with an ETF allocation could offer. I don't see evidence they're headed there yet.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is what keeps this firmly in pass territory for me. Stripe's payment API was a distribution channel because every developer integration created a relationship through which Atlas, Billing, and Radar could flow. What's Palus's second product? The treasury relationship is narrow -- it's a set-and-forget allocation, not a daily-use workflow that creates opportunities for expansion. You can imagine them building more financial tools, but that path leads directly into Mercury's core banking product, where Mercury has 200K+ business customers and massive distribution advantages. The expansion path is either too narrow (stay in yield optimization) or too competitive (expand into banking).

The founders shipped a live product in their YC batch, pivoted intelligently when they saw the opportunity, and Sam's institutional finance exposure is relevant. But the execution challenge here isn't building software -- it's accumulating AUM at a scale where 0.25% generates meaningful revenue, which is fundamentally a sales and trust problem in a market where Mercury and Brex have already built that trust at scale. This is a competent team building a real product that solves a real problem. But the problem it solves is marginal yield optimization in a well-served market, without a structural moment driving adoption and without a clear path from product to platform.
