# Minicor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the fundamental question I cannot get past with Minicor: if this company succeeds spectacularly, what changes about the world? Legacy desktop applications in healthcare and automotive get better connected to modern AI systems. That's useful. It might even be a good business. But it is not transformational. Minicor's entire value proposition rests on the premise that Athena, Epic, CDK Global, and SAP will continue to run as closed desktop applications with no writable APIs -- and that the right response is to build an elaborate workaround rather than to replace them. This is a company whose success *extends the life of legacy systems* rather than making them obsolete. I have spent forty years backing companies that make incumbent approaches structurally unviable -- Juniper made Cisco's legacy routing architecture obsolete, Square made traditional POS infrastructure unnecessary. Minicor makes legacy systems more comfortable to live with. That is a fundamentally different kind of ambition, and it calibrates the consequence of success to a level I find insufficient for the risk profile.

The timing argument is the strongest card in Minicor's hand, and I want to engage with it honestly. Anthropic released Computer Use in October 2024, creating a genuine infrastructure-layer opportunity. Someone needs to build the managed deployment, compliance, and orchestration layer on top of these raw model capabilities -- the VMs, the Citrix support, the SOC 2 and HIPAA certifications, the observability tooling. Minicor is early to this. The "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" claim and "thousands of workflows a week" in production are real signals for a company founded in 2024. Having SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance at the pre-seed stage is unusually disciplined execution. If you believe that every AI company selling into healthcare, finance, and supply chain will need a managed legacy-integration layer, and that Minicor can become the Twilio of that layer, there is a path to a significant infrastructure business. That is the bull case, and it is not absurd.

But I keep returning to two structural problems. First, the commoditization risk is severe. Anthropic's Computer Use API is available to everyone. UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent feature. The core capability -- AI models interacting with desktop UIs -- is not proprietary to Minicor. Their differentiation is the managed infrastructure layer: VMs, scaling, compliance, observability. That is operational excellence, not a technology moat. Operational excellence can build a business, but it does not create the kind of structural lock-in that produces 100x returns. Second, Minicor's customer segment -- AI companies selling into legacy industries -- is itself nascent and unproven. This is a bet on a bet. If AI adoption in healthcare and automotive accelerates slowly, Minicor is building infrastructure for a market that may take years to materialize at scale.

The founders show solid execution and relevant domain pain. Saheed spent three years at Ford and time at Morgan Stanley -- he understands legacy enterprise environments from the inside. Faizaan built at a YC company (Phoenix) doing system integration work. They have been building together since 2019, which I value. But through my lens, this team profile reads as competent engineers solving a problem they personally experienced, not outsiders attempting something the experts consider impossible. Nobody looks at managed desktop automation infrastructure and says "that's crazy." The industry consensus is that RPA is a real market and AI-driven automation is the next wave. When UiPath, Automation Anywhere, Microsoft, and well-funded YC companies like Automat ($19.25M raised) are all converging on the same opportunity, the contrarian signal I look for is absent. This is a company that has reduced its probability of failure by choosing a well-understood problem -- but in doing so, has limited the consequence of success to a fraction of the broader RPA market in a segment that may or may not scale.

I pass on companies like this regularly. The team works, the market exists, the technology functions, the timing is reasonable. And when I add it all up, if everything goes right, the outcome is a solid infrastructure business competing with entrenched incumbents and commoditizing model providers. Nobody's industry gets disrupted. No trillion-dollar system becomes structurally unviable. The founders are too capable to spend their careers building a better bridge to systems that should be torn down entirely.
