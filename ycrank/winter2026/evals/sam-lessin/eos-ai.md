# Eos AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about healthcare data interoperability: everybody agrees it's broken, everybody agrees it needs fixing, and everybody with a cloud platform or $100M+ in venture funding is already building a version of the solution. When I see a 2-person pre-seed team pitch "the autonomous operating system for healthcare," the first thing I notice isn't the ambition -- it's that this is maybe the single most consensus idea in all of healthtech right now. Commure has raised $820M+ and sits at a $6B valuation doing something adjacent. Redox has $98M. Google, Microsoft, and AWS all have healthcare data harmonization products. Epic controls 38% of the US hospital EHR market and has every incentive to build -- or block -- interoperability on its own terms. The sane-person-insane-idea filter exists precisely to avoid situations like this: a smart person pursuing an idea that every other smart person is also pursuing. There's no contrarian edge here. The market has already priced in the opportunity.

Arya Khokhar is clearly technically capable -- Caltech CS and Math, published research in medical imaging ML at the intersection of physics-aware neural operators, and the ONCOVATE cancer triage project piloted at Stanford Medicine. That's a real signal of technical depth, particularly in imaging. But technical depth in a research context is different from the operational credibility required to sell enterprise software to hospital systems with 12-18 month procurement cycles, navigate HIPAA compliance across federated data architectures, and build a business that can withstand competitive pressure from players with three orders of magnitude more capital. This is a first-time founder, solo-listed, with a team of two. The "sane" part of my equation requires not just intelligence but specific operator credibility for the problem being attacked -- and selling to hospital CIOs is one of the most operationally grueling enterprise sales motions that exists.

My AI kill test runs in a particularly uncomfortable direction for Eos. Normally I ask whether AI commoditizes a business's moat. Here, AI *is* the business. The entire value proposition -- VERA for imaging harmonization, LUCIA for clinical text normalization, the federated index, the predictive modeling layer -- is software and ML. This is precisely the category I've been arguing is structurally disadvantaged: software moats dissolving as AI makes development cheaper and faster. If foundation models get good enough at handling messy, multi-format clinical data natively (and they're getting better rapidly), the need for a specialized harmonization layer shrinks. If Epic decides to ship AI-native interoperability features to its installed base, the third-party market contracts overnight. The "cherry on top" test I apply -- would this be a great business if AI didn't exist? -- produces a clear no. Without AI, there's no Eos. That's the opposite of what I want to fund.

The strongest bull case, and I want to be honest about it: healthcare data fragmentation is genuinely a structural problem that incumbents are incentivized *not* to solve completely, because interoperability undermines EHR lock-in. An independent federated data layer that becomes the standard index could achieve a protocol-layer position -- the infrastructure that every clinical AI application depends on. If Eos became to healthcare data what FHIR was supposed to be but actually works in practice, with proprietary harmonization models that compound in accuracy as they see more institutional data variations, that's a real infinity story. The regulatory tailwinds from CMS interoperability mandates create genuine urgency on the buyer side. And the imaging harmonization specialization (cross-scanner, cross-modality distribution alignment) is genuinely technically demanding work that generic cloud offerings don't do well. If I squint, there's a world where this team's deep technical understanding of medical imaging ML gives them a wedge into a protocol-layer position that broader competitors can't match because they lack the domain-specific expertise. But that world requires this specific 2-person team to outexecute $6B-valued competitors and trillion-dollar cloud providers -- and I see no evidence of the distribution strategy, enterprise relationships, or operational infrastructure to make that plausible at this stage.

The metrics on the website -- 3x administrative productivity, 37% revenue recovery, 18% model performance improvement -- are presented without attribution to named customers, which means I can't treat them as evidence of a revenue cushion. The Stanford Medicine pilot was for ONCOVATE, the precursor project, not the current Eos platform. There's no verified revenue, no named paying customer, and no indication of pipeline beyond the Stanford relationship. For a pre-seed company that's expected, but combined with the consensus market positioning and competitive landscape, it means the entire investment thesis rests on narrative -- and the narrative is shared by too many well-capitalized players for a seed bet on a 2-person team to carry a differentiated risk-return profile.

I keep coming back to the structural question that dominates my framework: is this company building a new primitive, or an application? The federated data index *could* be a primitive. But nothing in the execution evidence -- no developer ecosystem, no API adoption by third parties, no evidence of other AI companies building on Eos's infrastructure -- suggests it's being built or sold as one. It's being sold as an enterprise product to hospitals. That's an application, and applications in crowded markets with well-funded incumbents are exactly the bets I avoid.
