# Sazabi -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Datadog bills separately for logs, metrics, and traces. Three revenue lines. Three product teams. Three reasons why their 47,000+ customers can't be served by a startup that ingests only logs. Except Sherwood Callaway's core thesis — "logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events and traces are basically correlated events" — implies those three revenue lines are an architectural accident, not a necessity. If Callaway is right, Datadog faces the exact structural trap that Google faces against Perplexity: the incumbent's agents are optimizing a P&L that depends on the old paradigm persisting. Sazabi's logs-only approach, if it works, is the kind of contrarian bet where you get paid for being right first. The question is whether the specific knowledge behind this bet is deep enough to be non-replicable, or whether it's an insight any competent infrastructure engineer could independently reach.

Callaway started Brex's Infrastructure and Observability teams as employee approximately #70. That's genuine specific knowledge — he built production observability at a hyper-growth fintech where downtime meant failed financial transactions. He then spent three years building LLM-based voice agents at Opkit (YC S21, acquired by 11x in September 2024) and rebuilt an agentic AI system at 11x. Sazabi sits at the intersection of these two knowledge stacks — observability infrastructure and AI-agent architecture — which is a rare and non-obvious combination. But I have to be precise: Callaway's observability-specific experience is one team at one company, roughly two years deep. Compare Sazabi's knowledge density to Perplexity, where Aravind Srinivas spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact systems that power AI search. Callaway's knowledge is genuine but shallower. He identified the logs-only insight through lived experience, which matters. But the insight itself — that AI can derive metrics and traces from logs — is architecturally reproducible. The dossier confirms this directly: "Any team with LLM infrastructure expertise could attempt a similar product." Dash0 already ships AI agents for observability with $155M in funding and 600+ customers. Sazabi's specific knowledge creates a head start, not a permanent moat.

Sazabi's leverage architecture is where the thesis gets interesting. Pure software, AI-native from the ground up, zero marginal cost per additional user once the platform is built. Autonomous alerts that require no manual setup eliminate a category of human labor. Conversational natural-language querying replaces specialized query languages. Sazabi integrates with coding agents — Claude Code, Codex, Cursor — positioning itself as infrastructure for the AI-agent workflow era. This is genuine permissionless leverage, and the principal-agent problem at Datadog provides structural cover: collapsing to logs-only pricing would cannibalize Datadog's existing per-signal revenue model. The parallel to Google's ad-revenue trap against Perplexity is real. But Perplexity was attacking a consumer market where distribution follows quality. Sazabi is attacking B2B infrastructure where enterprise procurement, compliance certifications, and existing integrations create switching friction that favors incumbents. Sazabi's SOC 2 Type II and ISO certifications are legitimately hard to obtain at pre-seed and signal seriousness, but they're table stakes in enterprise observability, not a moat.

Callaway and Justin Ko were roommates who co-founded Opkit together after working at both Crunchbase and Brex. That co-founder relationship — years of shared history across multiple companies — is exactly the principal signal I look for. Callaway being an a16z scout means he has options; he's choosing Sazabi over a comfortable career in the scout-to-VC pipeline. The revealed preference of returning to observability after gaining AI expertise at Opkit and 11x suggests this is a problem he can't let go of. But Sazabi has 10 people pre-launch with no disclosed revenue, no customer counts, and a full platform launch scheduled for end of 2026. Ten people at zero revenue is the opposite of the shrinking company thesis. Midjourney generates $500M with 163 employees; Cursor hits $500M ARR with fewer than 50. Sazabi has 10 people generating nothing. That ratio needs to invert dramatically and quickly for this to validate the leverage architecture I'm evaluating.

Sazabi's democratization angle is moderate but real — making production observability accessible to early-stage and growth-stage teams that "lack specialized observability expertise." Previously, meaningful observability required dedicated SRE teams or expensive Datadog contracts configured by specialists. Sazabi's autonomous alerts and natural-language interface lower the expertise barrier, fitting the tools-for-builders pattern that runs through my portfolio from Stack Overflow to Notion to Cursor. The angel backing from leaders at Vercel, Graphite, LangChain, Replit, and Browserbase creates a developer-community distribution network that could drive bottom-up adoption. Village Global and Agent Fund as institutional investors provide some co-investor signal, though not at the conviction level of, say, Founders Fund or Jeff Dean co-investing in a domain bet.

The bear case is straightforward: Sazabi is pre-product in a market where Dash0 raised $110M at a $1B valuation in March 2026 and already has 600+ customers with AI-native agents. Datadog ships AI features on a $2.7B ARR base. The logs-only-plus-AI approach is an architectural choice, not a proprietary technology. If the insight is correct, better-funded competitors can replicate it faster than Sazabi can build distribution. The logs-only thesis also bets against the OpenTelemetry standard that the entire CNCF ecosystem has adopted — if OTel becomes the industry default, Sazabi faces adoption friction with teams already instrumented. And 10 people burning pre-seed capital with a late-2026 launch timeline is a lot of runway consumed before product-market fit is tested. The bull case is that the Datadog principal-agent trap is structural and permanent, that the logs-only insight is correct and creates a genuinely simpler product that wins bottom-up with developer teams, and that Callaway's intersection of observability + AI-agent knowledge produces a product no incumbent can replicate without cannibalizing themselves. If Sazabi ships by Q3 2026 — ahead of the stated end-of-year target — and shows organic adoption from 50+ engineering teams without a sales team, I'd move from lean-invest to high-conviction. The combination of specific knowledge, structural leverage against incumbents, and authentic principal commitment is enough for me to write a small check, but the commoditization risk and team-to-value ratio prevent me from leading or doubling down until Sazabi proves the logs-only thesis in production.
