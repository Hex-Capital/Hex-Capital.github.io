# Tasklet -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$175M valuation. Series A from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and both Collison brothers. $5M ARR with 1,200% growth since January. On paper, Tasklet looks like a rocketship — and that's exactly my problem. When every sophisticated investor in Silicon Valley is already in the deal, what am I buying? The upside is in the price. At $175M, even if Tasklet becomes a billion-dollar company — which would be a legitimately great outcome — I'm looking at under 6x on my capital. I need 100x asymmetry, and Tasklet has zero asymmetry left. The consensus isn't wrong about Tasklet being a good product. The consensus is already reflected in the check I'd have to write.

Andrew Lee built Firebase through YC S11 to Google acquisition, and Firebase now powers roughly 15% of all developers. Jonny Dimond led Google Cloud Firestore. These two have worked together for over a decade across Firebase, Google, Shortwave, and now Tasklet. That's genuinely rare co-founder DNA — the kind of repeat relationship I prize. I backed Andrew Kortina from Venmo through Fin because watching someone across multiple ventures reveals character that a single pitch never can. Lee and Dimond pass that test. But here's where the sane/insane ratio breaks: the founder is clearly sane, and the idea is... also sane. "AI agent that automates knowledge work" is the single most consensus pitch in tech right now. Half the YC batch is some version of this. When I get excited, it's because a very reasonable person is doing something fucking crazy. Tasklet is a very reasonable team doing something very reasonable. That's boring to me.

Lee told The New Stack, "what if the workflow goes away? And you just let the agent reason about what to do" — and I'll grant there's a kernel of genuine primitive creation in that framing. Zapier's $310M revenue business is built on explicit workflow construction. Destroying the workflow abstraction entirely is a real paradigm shift. But Tasklet isn't creating that primitive. OpenAI Operator is. Google Gemini is. Anthropic's agent capabilities are. The primitive — natural language task execution — lives at the foundation model layer, not the application layer. Tasklet is an application built on someone else's primitive, which is exactly the wrong layer to invest in. I invest in protocols, not dapps. I invest in primitives, not wrappers. Tasklet is structurally a wrapper, however sophisticated its context engineering might be.

The AI kill test is where Tasklet fails most decisively. Lee literally built Firebase at Google. Google has Workspace with hundreds of millions of users, Gemini models getting better every quarter, and every incentive to build exactly what Tasklet is building directly into Gmail, Docs, and Sheets. Microsoft has Copilot embedded across M365. The dossier itself acknowledges "foundation model providers could integrate agent capabilities directly" and that "the core technology (LLM-based agent with tool integrations) is reproducible." Tasklet's CEO admitted "it is super expensive to run" — those Ubuntu VMs and browser automation instances are compute-intensive with no physical moat. Compare this to Metropolis, where I seeded an AI-powered parking company now valued at ~$5B: the parking infrastructure is a physical asset no LLM can replicate. Tasklet's integration layer through Pipedream is a dependency, not a moat. When Google ships native agent capabilities in Workspace — and they will — Tasklet's thousands of integrations become a commodity.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. An advocate would argue: Firebase started as "just another backend service" in a crowded developer tools market and became critical infrastructure. Lee has demonstrated the ability to build platform layers that achieve ubiquity. The $5M ARR with 1,200% growth proves product-market fit, not just hype. The Slack exception in my framework says I'll pay consensus prices when a company is genuinely creating a new behavioral primitive — and "the workflow goes away" could be exactly that kind of primitive. Shortwave's pivot to Tasklet shows pattern recognition, not trend-chasing: they saw agent demand emerge organically from their own user base. And the team includes people from Firebase, Google, Amazon, and OpenAI — deep infrastructure talent executing at the application layer could create defensibility through sheer engineering quality that competitors can't match. This is a strong bull case. But it breaks on one fact: Firebase was a developer primitive — a real-time database with protocol-level lock-in. Tasklet is an end-user application competing for attention in a category where every incumbent has superior distribution and is actively building. The Slack exception required that the company was creating the primitive; Tasklet is consuming a primitive created by foundation model providers. The condition that would flip me: if Tasklet demonstrated that its context engineering or agent runtime had become a protocol layer that OTHER agent companies built on top of — if it became infrastructure rather than application — I'd revisit immediately.

Tasklet's capital discipline is middling — $5M ARR provides a revenue base, but 11 open positions at $125K–$350K salaries and compute-intensive infrastructure suggest burn is substantial. The $20M Series A buys runway, but Lee's admission about costs combined with the competitive pressure from free or bundled agent capabilities from Google and Microsoft means Tasklet may face a squeeze: needing to spend aggressively to grow while margins compress as model costs decline unevenly across the stack. This isn't a company that could survive if it never raised again — it's on the venture treadmill that I've said publicly is dying.

My verdict on Tasklet comes down to two dimensions. First, consensus mispricing: at $175M with tier-one investors already in, there is zero asymmetry — the market has priced this exactly where the excitement warrants, which means the expected value of my marginal dollar is terrible. Second, structural durability: Tasklet fails the AI kill test as cleanly as any company I've seen. Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI can all replicate this with their existing distribution within 18 months. Great founders, real traction, wrong layer, wrong price. Pass.
