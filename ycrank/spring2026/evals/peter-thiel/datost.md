# Datost -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most illuminating fact about Datost is not what the company does, but who else is doing it. Julius AI has two million users and $10 million from Bessemer. Defog.ai has open-sourced text-to-SQL. Narrative BI is generating revenue. Equals raised $16 million from Andreessen Horowitz. Microsoft is shipping Copilot inside Power BI. Google is shipping AI into Looker. And Salesforce -- which owns both Slack and Tableau -- has every structural incentive to build exactly this product as a native integration. When I look at a market and see this many funded players converging on the same thesis, the diagnosis is straightforward: there is no secret here. "Use large language models to make data analysis accessible to non-technical people" is not a contrarian insight. It is the consensus view of every BI product manager, every AI startup founder, and every enterprise software executive in Silicon Valley. The mimetic dynamics are already in motion -- companies imitating each other toward mutual margin destruction.

I ask my standard question: what important truth do Datost's founders believe that most intelligent people would actively argue against? The implied answer seems to be that data analysis should happen inside Slack rather than in dashboards or spreadsheets. But this is a UX preference, not a secret about the world. Slack bots for analytics have existed for years -- Statsbot was doing precisely this before LLMs made it easier. The "own computer" framing -- a sandboxed execution environment -- is an engineering implementation choice, not a discontinuous technology. Any competent team can spin up a sandboxed compute layer. The underlying capability -- natural language to SQL to results -- is available to anyone with an API key to a frontier model. When your core technology is a commodity capability wrapped in a distribution channel owned by someone else, you have neither a secret nor a path to monopoly.

The platform dependency deserves particular emphasis because it is not merely a risk factor -- it is structurally fatal to the monopoly thesis. Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. The incentive to build a native AI data analyst that connects Slack conversations to Tableau data sources is so obvious that the only question is when, not whether. Building your entire distribution on a platform whose owner has both the capability and the commercial incentive to replicate your product is the opposite of last-mover thinking. You are not studying the endgame; you are ignoring it. Palantir succeeded in government because no one inside government had the engineering talent or culture to build what Palantir built. Salesforce has thousands of engineers and already owns every piece of the stack Datost depends on.

The strongest bull case would require several things to be simultaneously true: that Salesforce remains organizationally unable to unify Slack and Tableau for multiple years, that Datost achieves enterprise trust (SOC 2, audit trails, data governance) fast enough to land meaningful accounts before that window closes, and that the multi-source integration depth -- docs, Slack history, databases, code -- creates genuine switching costs that a native Salesforce product cannot replicate. If all three hold, the Slack-native viral distribution could work: one person asks a data question in a channel, the whole team sees the answer, adoption spreads bottom-up. There is a version of this story where Datost becomes the default AI analyst for Slack-native organizations before incumbents close the gap. But this requires winning a race against a company that owns the track, and doing so with a two-person team, no visible traction, and no demonstrated enterprise credibility. The probability structure does not support the bet.

The founders are 21-year-old software engineers who previously worked together at Traba. They can clearly write code. But I see no evidence of the kind of definite conviction that distinguishes transformative founders from competent builders. No articulated vision for how the world changes if they succeed. No indication they have thought deeply about why this matters beyond the product level. The LinkedIn headline still reads "Software Engineer Intern." The GitHub organization has one repository with zero stars. I am not penalizing youth -- Zuckerberg was 19, Buterin was 19 -- but both of those founders had already demonstrated that they would hold an unconventional vision against enormous social pressure. I see no such signal here. What I see is two capable young engineers building a sensible product in a category that every other capable young engineer is also building in. That is the definition of competition, and I know how that story ends.
