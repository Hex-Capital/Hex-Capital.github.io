# Kaaro -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Both founders of Kaaro are described as "second-generation railroaders" -- and in an industry as insular as North American freight rail, that fact carries more weight than any pitch deck. Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran were the first two engineers at Toma (YC W24), where they built AI voice agents for auto dealerships and helped scale from roughly 20 to hundreds of customers before Toma raised a $17M Series A led by a16z. Now they're running the identical playbook -- AI agents automating phone calls, emails, and tribal knowledge workflows -- in a new vertical where they have familial access to the customer base. I've backed founders who previously operated at companies during hypergrowth and then applied that scaling DNA to a new domain. The Toma pedigree is thin (founding engineers, not executives managing a scaling org), but the pattern transfer is direct and the domain access is genuine. Most 19-year-old dropouts pitching railroad software would be dead on arrival at CPKC. These two are "working with" CPKC and in late-stage conversations with CN, Canada's two Class I railroads, because they grew up in this world.

The market structure timing question is where Kaaro gets analytically interesting. "AI agents for vertical X" is one of the most consensus categories in 2026 -- every second YC company is doing some version of this. But railroads? Nobody is building AI workflow agents for railroads. The structural catalyst here mirrors what I saw with Harvey in legal tech: a capability threshold crossing (LLMs can now handle unstructured communication and exception-handling workflows that prior automation couldn't touch) combined with institutional openness that's genuinely new. CPKC signed a multi-year AI deal with Duos Technologies in May 2024; CN partnered with Google Cloud for digital twin infrastructure. Three years ago, Class I railroads weren't taking meetings with AI startups. The CPKC-Kansas City Southern merger completing in April 2023 -- creating the first single-line Canada-US-Mexico railroad -- added operational complexity that created fresh demand for workflow unification tools. This maps to my "boring but critical" pattern: Gusto built a defensible payroll business because ambitious founders chased flashier markets and left an operationally essential category underserved. Railroad operational workflow software is about as unglamorous as it gets, which means Kaaro faces almost no prestige-seeking competition. Wabtec RailConnect ($25B parent company) is the incumbent TMS, but it's a deterministic transaction system being modernized from green-screen terminals to web interfaces -- not an AI agent layer. Optym does locomotive scheduling optimization with operations research, not communication workflow automation. The competitive vacuum is real.

Where my enthusiasm cools is on infrastructure-layer positioning and market ceiling. Kaaro is building "AI coworkers for rail" -- an application layer that ingests data from existing railroad systems and automates the communication workflows around them. That's valuable, but it's not a platform that other businesses build on top of. Stripe became the payments API; Kaaro becomes a workflow tool. The switching costs from embedded railroad-specific AI training are real (FRA regulatory knowledge, interchange rules, customer-specific exception handling), and these compound over time in the same way Gusto's payroll regulatory integration creates lock-in. But the platform potential is limited. North America has 7 Class I railroads and roughly 600 short-line operators. Even with high per-customer ACV -- and railroad IT budgets can support enterprise pricing -- the structural concentration of this market creates both customer concentration risk and a ceiling question. Kaaro's disclosed pipeline is three entities: CPKC, CN, and Gulf & Ohio Railways (a holding company for four short-line railroads operating 200 miles of track with 30 locomotives). Loss of the CPKC relationship alone would be a severe setback at this stage.

The single-miracle test is moderately favorable. The technology miracle is happening exogenously -- foundation model capabilities are improving on their own curve, and Kaaro's founders have already proven at Toma that they can build AI agents that handle phone and communication workflows at scale. The remaining miracle is primarily distribution: can a two-person pre-seed company close contracts with Class I railroads that typically run 6-18 month procurement cycles? The family connections ("second-generation railroaders") and industry memberships (CARS, Canadian Railway Club, NAMRC) de-risk this somewhat, but converting "in late conversations" with CN to a signed contract while burning pre-seed capital is a genuine execution challenge. There's also an integration miracle lurking: Wabtec RailConnect and other legacy TMS systems have limited APIs, and building reliable data ingestion from on-premise railroad infrastructure is nontrivial. I'd call this 1.5 miracles -- above my single-miracle ideal but not the compounding three-miracle problem that triggers an automatic pass.

The strongest bull case for Kaaro: if you take the Gusto analogy seriously, this could become the default operational intelligence layer for North American railroads, starting with short-lines (where deployment friction is lower and IT budgets don't support Wabtec-class TMS deployments) and expanding upward to Class I operators as the product matures. FRA regulatory complexity and railroad-specific domain knowledge (interchange rules, car tracking conventions, billing exception patterns) create genuine barriers that would slow any horizontal AI agent company trying to enter this vertical. At pre-seed valuation ($5M post-money on the a16z Speedrun terms), even a $200M outcome delivers a 40x. The question is whether railroad workflow automation can become a venture-scale business or whether it caps at a very good but relatively small SaaS company. No revenue data, no pricing information, and no confirmed contracts make it impossible to assess margin architecture at this stage.

Kaaro scores strongest on founder-market fit in a genuinely underserved vertical -- the railroad family background combined with Toma AI-agent experience is a rare combination that money can't replicate. It scores weakest on infrastructure-layer positioning (workflow application, not platform) and on the market ceiling question inherent to a structurally concentrated customer base. The nonobvious market timing is real but partial: the specific vertical is nonobvious, but the approach (AI agents for manual workflows) is deeply consensus. I'd want to see a signed contract with CPKC or CN, evidence that the product handles FRA-regulated workflows with appropriate compliance guardrails, and a credible path to $500M+ SAM before writing a check.
