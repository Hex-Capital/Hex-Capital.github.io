# arc-prize-foundation -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with ARC Prize Foundation: I can't invest in it. Not because the people aren't brilliant -- they clearly are -- but because this is a nonprofit. I write checks from my own money, and a nonprofit has no equity structure that generates a return. That's not a philosophical objection, it's a structural one. And when I dig beneath that surface, what I find doesn't change my mind.

When I sit down with founders, I always start the same way -- tell me your story, not the business. François Chollet's story is extraordinary in one sense: he built Keras, which 63.9K GitHub stars tells you reshaped how millions of people interact with deep learning, and he walked away from a Senior Staff position at Google to pursue this vision that current AI scaling approaches won't reach genuine intelligence. Mike Knoop built Zapier from a YC S12 company into something worth $5 billion and profitable since 2014. These are elite credentials. But they're accomplishment stories, not survival stories. I'm listening for the specific hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this exact problem -- the thing that means this founder will keep going when the money runs out and everyone tells them they're wrong. I don't hear that here. I hear two very successful people who became intellectually fascinated with a hard problem, which is admirable but different from what I look for when I'm writing a check.

The contrarian case is real but narrower than it first appears. Chollet's thesis -- that scaling large language models alone won't reach AGI, and that we need formal benchmarks to measure genuine reasoning -- is genuinely unpopular within the AI research community. He's pushing back against the dominant paradigm, and that's the kind of intellectual courage I respect. But here's the thing: AGI itself is the most consensus category in technology right now. Every fund in the Valley is trying to get exposure to AGI-adjacent companies. Coatue, Factorial Capital, and Quiet Capital gave Ndea -- the for-profit entity these same co-founders run -- $43 million. That's not "nobody wants to fund this because it sounds insane." That's a very well-capitalized, very mainstream bet. When I backed SpaceX, private space was a punchline. When I backed Crusoe, mining Bitcoin on gas flares made people question my judgment. ARC Prize exists at the absolute center of the AI conversation, not at its margins.

The dual-entity structure genuinely concerns me, and this is where I'd push hardest if I were in the room. Chollet and Knoop run both ARC Prize Foundation -- the nonprofit that creates the benchmark -- and Ndea, a for-profit AI research lab whose "program synthesis approach to AGI" is directly informed by results from that benchmark. Ndea is also listed as a donor to ARC Prize. So the people who create the test also run a company that benefits commercially from insights the test produces. After HQ Trivia, I learned to look very carefully at structural dynamics that create misaligned incentives, even when the people involved have the best intentions. This isn't a co-founder conflict exactly, but it's the kind of governance ambiguity that can quietly corrode trust and mission over time.

If I'm being honest about the bull case: ARC Prize is doing something genuinely important for the AI ecosystem by creating transparent, rigorous measurement of intelligence that doesn't let labs grade their own homework. That's the "making invisible systems visible" pattern I've been drawn to in investments like Carta and Flock Safety. If ARC-AGI becomes the definitive benchmark for measuring progress toward genuine AI reasoning -- which it's well-positioned to do given Chollet's 617K-follower platform and deep credibility -- it could shape the direction of an entire field. And there's something beautiful about a nonprofit anchoring that measurement, keeping it independent from the commercial interests of the labs. But "important for the ecosystem" and "belongs in my portfolio" are different questions. I invest to find magically weird outsiders building companies that create economic access for real people, and this is an elite research organization benchmarking AI capabilities for other elite research organizations.
