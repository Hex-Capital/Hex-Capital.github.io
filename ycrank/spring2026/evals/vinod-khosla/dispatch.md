# Dispatch -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Seven funded competitors building reentry vehicles. Varda at $329 million raised and five successful missions. Inversion with $125 million in combined VC and government contracts. Space Forge backed by NATO's Innovation Fund. And Dispatch -- two engineers, a heat shield tested in the Mojave, and about $500K. When I see a market with this many well-capitalized players already racing, the question I ask is not "who wins?" but "has the window for contrarian entry already closed?" The best investments I've made -- Juniper against Cisco, OpenAI before AI was fashionable, Impossible Foods before plant-based meat was a category -- shared one feature: when I wrote the check, the experts dismissed the entire endeavor. Nobody is dismissing space manufacturing anymore. Varda proved it works. The consensus has formed. That's a problem for a new entrant.

The consequence of success here is real but bounded. If Dispatch fully succeeds -- autonomous space stations, a fleet of refurbishable reentry vehicles, lights-out manufacturing in orbit -- you're building infrastructure for a new industrial paradigm. That matters. But the in-space manufacturing market is projected at $5 to $23 billion by the mid-2030s. Compare that to animal agriculture ($1.7 trillion), global energy ($10 trillion), or knowledge work (effectively infinite TAM for AI). Even at the optimistic end, successful space manufacturing logistics is a solid multi-billion-dollar market, not a system-level transformation of how civilization operates. And the near-term positioning -- reentry-as-a-service, the "picks and shovels" play -- further narrows the ambition. Being the FedEx of orbital cargo is a defensible business if you get there first, but it's not the kind of bet where I say the problem is too important not to work on. Varda made the higher-ambition choice: own the manufacturing AND the return. Dispatch chose the capital-efficient path, which is the kind of risk reduction that makes the consequence of success more moderate.

The founders concern me precisely because they're insiders. Payton Case came from the CTO's office at Astranis and the thermal group on Lunar Gateway. Andrew Mello led avionics at Astranis and did hardware at Zoox and Apple. These are excellent credentials for building a reentry vehicle. They are also exactly the profile I'm wary of -- aerospace engineers building the thing aerospace engineers would build, in the way aerospace engineers would build it. My best investments have come from people who didn't know what the industry considered impossible. Peter Beck built Rocket Lab from New Zealand without an aerospace pedigree. Pat Brown was a biochemistry professor who'd never set foot in a food processing plant. The outsider advantage is not naivety for its own sake -- it's freedom from the mental constraints that industry experience installs. Case and Mello know what's hard about reentry vehicles, which means they also know what the industry considers the "right" way to build them. The 100x cheaper heat shield claim -- built in an apartment -- is the one signal that cuts against this concern. If that cost structure is real and extends beyond a single component to system-level economics, it suggests a first-principles approach that defies industry assumptions. But one unverified claim about one component is not enough evidence to override the broader pattern.

Let me steelman the investment. If the 100x heat shield economics hold at production scale, Dispatch becomes the lowest-cost reentry provider in the market. The picks-and-shovels positioning means they serve every space manufacturer, not just one vertical -- as the market grows from $1 billion to $20 billion, they're the logistics layer underneath all of it. Varda's success actually helps Dispatch by validating demand and creating more companies that need downmass but won't build their own capsules. The two-person team with Astranis overlap means tight execution with minimal coordination overhead, and they've already demonstrated scrappiness by testing a full-scale heat shield at pre-seed budget. That's a legitimate path to a valuable company. What keeps me from leaning in is that none of these signals point to something transformational enough to justify the capital intensity ahead. This company needs $50 to $100 million to reach first orbital flight, based on what competitors have spent. The path from $500K to first revenue has multiple existential gates -- each fundraise, each test, each government certification -- and at each gate, they're competing against players with 100x to 600x more capital. Capital efficiency is an advantage, but only if it persists through the full development cycle, not just through the heat shield phase.

The timing trajectory is mixed. On one hand, the in-space manufacturing trend is clearly accelerating -- Varda's operational cadence, declining launch costs, growing interest from pharma and semiconductor companies. On the other, the wave of investment into reentry vehicles means Dispatch is arriving when the category is already legible to the market. I look for companies positioned ahead of exponential curves before mainstream awareness -- OpenAI in 2019, not OpenAI in 2023. Reentry vehicles in 2026 feel more like the latter. The technology is proven, the market thesis is accepted, and the competition is funded. That's not where I find my best returns.

I see competent founders attacking a genuine market with capital-efficient thinking and a provocative cost claim. In most investors' frameworks, that's interesting. In mine, it doesn't clear the bar. The consequence of success is a good space logistics business, not an industry transformation. The founders are insiders, not outsiders. The category is crowded, not contrarian. The technology is competitive, not paradigm-breaking. Dispatch may well succeed as a company -- but that's a different question from whether it represents the kind of asymmetric, consequence-maximizing bet I optimize for.
