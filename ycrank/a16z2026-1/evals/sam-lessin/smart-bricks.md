# Smart Bricks -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Smart Bricks self-reports a ~$12M revenue run rate on a $5M pre-seed raise for a company founded in July 2024. If that number is real platform revenue — not gross transaction value, not listings volume, not some creative aggregation — it would be one of the fastest pre-seed-to-revenue trajectories I've ever seen. But the dossier finds zero independent verification of that figure, and the definition is unspecified. That's the entire bull case and the entire problem wrapped in one datapoint. A company claiming $12M run rate should be drowning in press coverage of its metrics. Instead, TechCrunch covers the funding round and nobody touches the revenue claim. I've been around long enough to know what unverified self-reported revenue at pre-seed usually means.

Here's what I actually care about: is the market wrong about Smart Bricks? Absolutely not. AI-native real estate investing is about as consensus-hot as it gets in 2026. The company is backed by a16z Speedrun, Techstars, and 500 Global — that's not one consensus program, it's three. The angel cap table reads like a who's-who of exactly the investors you'd expect to fund this: people from OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Airbnb, Blackstone. Every sophisticated investor in the room is nodding along. When everyone is nodding, the upside is already in the price. I look for deals where people think I'm wrong — where there's genuine discomfort in the category. "AI makes real estate investing better" makes zero people uncomfortable. There's no asymmetry to exploit here.

The AI kill test is where Smart Bricks falls apart for me. Mohamed Mohamed worked at Blackstone. Blackstone manages over a trillion dollars in assets, with real estate as a core vertical. If ingesting a million data feeds and analyzing 1,000 variables per property to find the top 0.1% of deals is valuable — and it probably is — Blackstone will build this internally by Tuesday. So will CBRE, CoStar, and Zillow. The dossier's own commoditization risk section concedes that "LLM-based property analysis is technically reproducible" and that EliseAI, sitting on $392M in funding and $100M+ ARR, could expand into adjacent investment use cases. Compare this to Metropolis, where we seeded AI-powered parking at the physical infrastructure layer — you can't replicate parking garages with a language model. Smart Bricks analyzes physical assets from a software layer, which is categorically different from owning or operating them. The moat claim rests on "1M+ proprietary and public data feeds," but how much of that is genuinely proprietary versus public data scraped at scale? The word "proprietary" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that press release.

Mohamed has a legitimately strong resume — Blackstone, Goldman, McKinsey AI practice, BCG, Forbes 30 Under 30. That's a sane person. But the idea is also sane. Everyone thinks AI should transform real estate investing. The calibration is off — I want a sane person pitching something that makes smart people say "that's never going to work." Building an AI platform for real estate analysis makes every smart person say "obviously, someone should do that." Obvious ideas attract obvious competition. The sole-founder structure also gives me pause for a company calling itself an "applied AI lab" with 20 employees and no named technical co-founder in any public source. Who's actually building the stack that Smart Bricks claims to have built from scratch?

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: the $12M revenue run rate is real platform fees, the cross-border data integration across Dubai-London-NYC-Miami creates a moat that single-geography incumbents can't replicate, and Smart Bricks can outrun EliseAI ($392M), Cherre (~$120M), and every major brokerage building AI tools internally. If all three are true, you have a legitimately fast-growing company attacking a massive market with a data compound advantage. But "if all three things I can't verify are true" is not an investment thesis — it's wishful thinking. The company also exhibits the platform-vs-product ambiguity the dossier flags: simultaneously an "applied AI lab" building infrastructure and a consumer-facing platform advertising golden visa programs. Those are different businesses with different unit economics, and trying to be both at pre-seed with 20 people across three continents smells like unfocused capital deployment, not disciplined survival-mode building.

Smart Bricks triggers four of my explicit red flags: the AI-as-product positioning, the "better version of existing tools" framing, consensus excitement among VCs, and pure software without physical moats or protocol-level lock-in. None of my known exceptions apply — this isn't creating a new behavioral primitive like Slack's real-time messaging, and there's no new social behavior being born like Clubhouse's synchronous audio. The decisive dimensions are Consensus Mispricing (fully priced, no asymmetry) and Structural Durability (fails the AI kill test against incumbents like Blackstone and EliseAI). Pass.
