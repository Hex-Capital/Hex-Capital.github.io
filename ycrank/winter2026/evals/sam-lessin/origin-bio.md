# Origin -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Origin: two talented CS undergrads from UIUC are racing Google DeepMind, Asimov ($209M raised), Dyno ($100M+), and Tessera ($300M+) to own the regulatory DNA layer of gene therapy — and AI for gene therapy is the most consensus category in biotech right now. When I say I look for a sane person with an insane idea, the emphasis is on *insane idea*. Designing regulatory DNA with AI is not insane. It is, in fact, exactly what every computational biology lab, every DeepMind research team, and every well-funded genomics startup is trying to do. Nature Genetics published open-source alternatives in 2025. AlphaGenome is a direct benchmark. The idea has already been de-risked by the market, which means the non-consensus premium has been arbitraged away. You're buying narrative at market price, and the market has already priced in "AI will transform gene therapy."

On the founder credibility side — Yash won an OpenCV competition and spent time at NVIDIA; Malhar published in Nature Scientific Reports in high school and worked at Automorphic (YC S23). These are impressive young engineers. But the "sane" in my framework requires that the founder's specific background makes the crazy idea suddenly plausible. Brian Armstrong was an Airbnb engineer who understood consumer financial products when he proposed building a regulated crypto exchange. The Mysten Labs team literally built Meta's blockchain infrastructure before launching Sui. Here, the founders have strong ML credentials but the biological domain expertise is entirely borrowed through an advisory board — Kellis at MIT/Broad, Paulk at UCSF, Bashir at UIUC. Great advisors, but advisors don't build wet-lab pipelines or navigate IND filings. The founder-problem specificity isn't tight enough.

This company also fails my most fundamental screen: AI IS the product. Remove the AI and there is nothing. No physical operations, no installed infrastructure, no existing customer relationships, no revenue. The Axis model was trained on publicly available ENCODE V4 Registry data using standard transformer architecture — the model itself is architecturally reproducible by anyone with compute. Origin claims they're building "the largest proprietary dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences across diverse cell-states" through wet-lab validation, and if realized, that would be genuinely defensible. A wet-lab-validated dataset is a physical-world asset that software alone can't replicate. But with a four-person team and no disclosed lab infrastructure, this is an aspiration, not a moat. It's the thing I'd need to see actually built before getting excited. As it stands, the company is a transformer model trained on public data claiming it will eventually have proprietary data — which is the pitch of approximately every AI startup I've seen in the last two years.

The strongest bull case here — and I want to be honest about it — is the primitive layer positioning. Origin occupies a genuinely distinct niche in the gene therapy stack. Dyno does capsids (the delivery vehicle). Tessera does gene writing (inserting sequences). Asimov does genetic circuits (manufacturing). Nobody specifically owns the regulatory element design layer — the switches that control when and where therapeutic genes express. If Origin becomes the standard source of regulatory DNA designs that pharma companies license across their therapeutic pipelines, that's a protocol-layer position in a $100B+ market. The "regulatory element design API" that every gene therapy program plugs into — that story has real infinity optionality. And the advisory board could open doors to exactly the pharma partnerships needed to get there. What would have to be true: the proprietary dataset gets built at scale before DeepMind or Asimov replicates the approach, the regulatory elements actually work in vivo (massive translation risk), and pharma companies decide to license this component rather than build internally. That's three simultaneous bets that all need to land, which is too many degrees of freedom for my comfort at pre-seed.

There's no revenue, no pricing, and a free API — which is fine for pre-seed biotech, but it means the entire investment thesis rests on narrative momentum and the forward-looking dataset claim. The $15M funding figure from an unconfirmed source, if real, suggests someone with more biotech conviction than me has already bought in. I respect that. But from where I sit, this is a talented team in a consensus category, building an AI-first product on public data, competing against entities with hundred-times their resources, with an aspirational moat that doesn't yet exist. The Birchbox lesson echoes: narrative magnitude without present structural moat is exactly how you end up with a company that's too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound.
