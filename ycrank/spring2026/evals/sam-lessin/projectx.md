# ProjectX -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The number that jumps off the page is 95 -- as in 95 tracked companies in the GPU cloud infrastructure space, collectively absorbing $20.8 billion in capital. That's not a market waiting for a new entrant. That's a market that has already priced in the thesis. When I see a six-person team from West Bengal with $204K in total funding trying to compete in a capital-intensive infrastructure category where CoreWeave alone has raised north of $5 billion and Google just acquired Cameyo to do exactly what ProjectX describes, my first question isn't "Is this team special?" -- it's "Has the window already closed before they walked up to it?" Cloud GPU streaming is not an insane idea. It's a consensus idea with a consensus TAM slide. The fact that Fortune Business Insights projects the GPUaaS market at $49.84 billion by 2032 is precisely why every hyperscaler and their second cousin is building in this space. My framework specifically selects for ideas that provoke skepticism. "Streaming GPU apps in browser tabs" provokes nodding, not skepticism. That's a problem.

On the founder-idea pairing: Rounak Adhikary is 23 or 24, with a BSc in Interaction Design from Lovely Professional University, and he founded a consulting company at 19 that funded early development. That's scrappy and admirable. Bishal Karmakar has genuine systems programming chops in Golang, Rust, and C++ -- languages that matter for this kind of infrastructure work. But "sane person, insane idea" requires the "sane" part to be domain-credible for the specific challenge. When Anatoly Yakovenko pitched Solana, the "sane" signal was years of systems engineering at Qualcomm building the kind of high-throughput architecture that a new Layer 1 blockchain would demand. For ProjectX, the specific challenge is GPU virtualization, low-latency streaming, and container orchestration at scale -- competing against teams staffed by ex-NVIDIA and ex-Google engineers with hundred-million-dollar infrastructure budgets. A consulting background and accelerator pedigree don't generate the same credibility signal for this particular fight. And the founding team restructuring -- three of four original cofounders replaced between 2024 and 2026 -- is not a death sentence but it's a yellow flag at pre-seed. Speed-dating co-founder dynamics are fragile under real pressure.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart for me. ProjectX's entire defensibility is software execution: sub-3-second cold starts, per-app compute isolation, browser-native streaming. There are no physical assets, no proprietary data, no protocol position, no regulatory lock-in. This is the exact category I've been arguing is dissolving. "Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore" -- and ProjectX hasn't even built the moat yet. Google acquires Cameyo in May 2024 and suddenly has app virtualization expertise inside the largest cloud infrastructure on earth. AWS adds a "stream any app" button to their console and ProjectX's differentiation is a feature comparison chart, not a structural advantage. My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be good if you removed the software? The answer is no -- there is no business without the software. The software IS the product. Compare to Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews and operational logistics that software optimizes but cannot replace. ProjectX has no equivalent physical-world anchor.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. The $2.4 million in signed LOIs from Indian institutions is a real demand signal -- not revenue, but evidence that people want what this team is building. India's compute access gap is massive and underserved: 75% of students lacking high-quality computing devices is a structural problem that won't be solved by shipping them all RTX 4090s. The per-app browser-tab model is genuinely different from Shadow's persistent VM desktop or Paperspace's developer-focused GPU instances. If ProjectX executes the UX layer brilliantly and India's institutional market converts those LOIs into recurring revenue, there's a version of this that becomes the access layer for GPU compute in emerging markets -- a "compute URL" primitive. The WebGPU timing angle is real. And young founders from non-obvious geographies building for markets they intimately understand is exactly the kind of bet I respect in principle. If the founding team stabilizes and they can find a capital-efficient way to serve the Indian education market before hyperscalers care about that segment, there's a path. But that path requires competing on distribution in a geography where both price sensitivity and institutional procurement cycles are brutal, while somehow avoiding the attention of Google, Microsoft, and AWS -- all of whom are aggressively courting the Indian education market.

Ultimately, I can't get past the structural positioning. This is an application layer built on hyperscaler infrastructure, competing in a market with nearly a hundred funded companies, where the next Google feature launch could eliminate the differentiation. There's no protocol position, no new primitive that others would build on. It's a nicer interface to GPU compute -- and "nicer interface" is the most fragile possible moat in a world where AI is making software development radically cheaper every quarter. The narrative ceiling is high in theory -- universal compute access is an infinity story -- but it's not proprietary to ProjectX. Every cloud GPU company tells the same story. I need either a founder whose credibility makes me believe they can out-execute billion-dollar competitors, or a structural position that makes the competition irrelevant. I see neither here.
