# Walter -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Walter is a learning-rate story trapped inside a capital-light category — Nikolas Keller, a CDTM dropout, has pivoted from customer-support automation into reading purchase orders for SAP, and that is the most interesting sentence in the entire dossier. The success-state of Walter is "packaging plants enter purchase order data 14 minutes faster." That sentence is the deal.

The OpenAI (2019) check is the right mirror to hold up to Walter, because both pitches lead with "AI employee" framing. The mechanism that made OpenAI a $50M first VC check for me was that the success-state collapsed the entire knowledge-work cost curve — $15T of U.S. GDP per the Fortune (2026-03-06) frame. Walter's own success-state, per walter.one, is "order management 15 min → 1 min" and "30+ hours/week saved in procurement." That is a productivity tool. The Outlook Business test — "make a great company and the money takes care of itself" — does not survive contact with a dossier whose only published outcome metric is minutes-per-document. No incumbent regime collapses if Walter wins; SAP still ships invoices, the packaging plant still runs, the procurement officer is reassigned. This is the sales-call-summarizer pattern from my pass example, dressed in a manufacturing apron.

Range Fuels (~2007) failed because the capital plan understated what the science required — the inverse failure mode applies to Walter on the capital-risk axis. Walter requires no science miracle. The dossier names SAP, Oracle NetSuite, and Microsoft Dynamics 365 as the integration surface; it names Rossum ($100M Series A, General Catalyst 2022), Hyperscience ($300M+ raised), Workist, and Nanonets ($29M Series B, Accel March 2024) as the field already in the trench. The 270-language claim and "90%+ accuracy after 10 documents" are real engineering, but they are commodity LLM application engineering at YC Spring 2026 — not a falsifiable physics or biology bet that consensus VCs decline to underwrite. Any Tier-1 generalist can fund Walter on Monday morning. That is the diagnostic, not the compliment: I am over-resourced for this deal.

On founder defiance of expert consensus — the dimension that registered Physical Intelligence (2024) for me through Sergey Levine — I read Nikolas Keller's profile as the inverse pattern. The strongest founder evidence in the dossier is a personal site listing fine-dining tenure at Zeitlang and Le 1051 plus a CDTM life-paths tool, and a co-founder Lukas Postulka whose identity the researcher could not even confirm against the Walter entity. The 1986 McKinsey forecast of <1M U.S. cellphones by 2000 (actual: 109M) is the prior I run founders against — the question is whether Keller has publicly contradicted manufacturing-operations orthodoxy in a way that makes him uniquely positioned to redraw it. The dossier surfaces no such contradiction; it surfaces a young generalist who is learning the SAP back office on his customers' time.

The Slash (2024, $100M Series C) exception is exactly where Walter wants to sit on the learning-rate axis, and it is the strongest single signal in the dossier. The LinkedIn snippet documenting that first customers hired Walter for "customer support and software development" automation before Keller narrowed to manufacturing back office is a real pivot with a real reason — the kind of update-on-evidence pattern I rewarded with $100M into Slash after the Kanye West pivot story. Walter has not earned the Slash check, but the *shape* of Keller's update — kill the horizontal AI-employee story, pick a vertical with budget — is the right cognitive move. Over 18 months this dimension compounds if Keller pivots once more with clarity, or evaporates if the manufacturing "9-figure customers" turn out to be one PoC and a handshake.

On required-capital profile: a single open Full-Stack/Data Engineer role at $125K–$200K with up to 1.00% equity, demo-booking as the sole CTA, and SOC 2 still "in progress" describe a $2M seed round, not a $115M Commonwealth Fusion (2019) Series A. To flip Walter's capital-profile score even one tier, the roadmap would have to require Walter to ship its own foundation model trained on manufacturing-document corpora at >$50M of compute — and the dossier shows the opposite, a wrapper plus ERP connectors plus per-customer fine-tuning. That is healthy SaaS economics. It is not my edge.

My 36-month forecast for Walter: $3–8M ARR in manufacturing document automation, then acquired by Rossum, Hyperscience, or an ERP-adjacent mid-tier (Sage, Epicor) for $30–80M — or a second pivot back toward horizontal "AI employee" positioning where Walter collides directly with UiPath and Salesforce Agentforce and loses on distribution. The mechanism producing this forecast is the high/low signature: a founder with genuine learning rate operating in a category where the ceiling on consequence is "document extraction at packaging plants." Learning rate without consequence magnitude produces a competent acquisition, not a defining company.

The strongest case against my pass is the Slash precedent itself: YC pedigree, documented pivot, young hands-on founder selling demos personally — I overrode the consequence-magnitude prior once and wrote a nine-figure check. The bull argument is that Keller is doing the Slash move two years earlier and I should be paying for optionality on the third pivot, not pricing the current vertical. The dossier dismantles this for me on one fact: Slash's pivot landed in consumer business banking with compounding distribution mechanics, while Walter's pivot landed in enterprise SAP back office where the only published GTM is "30-minute demo with the CEO via Cal.com." There is no compounding distribution surface; there is manual enterprise sales against Rossum's $100M war chest. One verifiable condition would flip my verdict: Walter discloses three named Fortune 500 packaging or defense logos at >$500K ACV each within twelve months AND extends from data entry into procurement *decision-making*, at which point the "$15 trillion of U.S. GDP is labor" frame activates and I revisit.
