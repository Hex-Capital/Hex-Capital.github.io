# qomplement -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most revealing thing about this company is what it used to be. In May 2025, qomplement described itself as "the only OS agent that controls your computer — like having the smartest AI built right into your operating system." That's a big vision. A general-purpose computer-using agent could transform how humans interact with machines entirely. By March 2026, the company had narrowed to "AI agents for document filling" — populating PDF forms and spreadsheet templates. This is the trajectory I warn founders about constantly: you had a vision that could matter at scale, and you reduced your probability of failure by shrinking the ambition until the consequences of success became inconsequential. If qomplement succeeds completely, enterprises fill out forms faster. That's not a transformation. That's a feature.

Let me be direct about consequence magnitude. One transport client automated 10,000 invoices per month and saved $15,000. That's meaningful to that client, but it's not a disruption — it's an efficiency gain on an existing workflow. The intelligent document processing market is real ($12 billion by 2030), but qomplement is targeting a subset of a subset: not extraction, but filling. They've carved out a niche so specific that even total dominance of it doesn't change how any industry fundamentally operates. I tell founders: you're too smart to spend your career on a problem this small. Go find something that would change the world if it worked. Document filling doesn't pass that test.

The competitive picture confirms this is a feature, not a company. Nanonets has $100 million in revenue and is building full back-office AI agents. Rossum has $104 million raised. Reducto has $108 million. Any one of these extraction vendors could add a "fill" step as a quarter's product initiative. The local-first architecture is clever — data staying on-device matters in regulated industries — but it's a deployment choice, not a structural moat. Microsoft's Power Platform already offers form-filling. When your entire product is one feature extension away from being subsumed by five well-funded competitors, the technology isn't disrupting incumbent systems — it's waiting to be absorbed by them.

The bull case would require me to believe three things simultaneously. First, that document filling is a wedge into a much larger enterprise workflow automation platform — that qomplement starts with forms and expands to orchestrate entire document-heavy business processes. Second, that the local-first architecture creates a genuine lock-in advantage in Latin American and regulated markets where cloud data residency is a hard constraint, not a preference. Third, that these young founders — Kerim has relevant experience from Contalink (YC S21) and understands Latin American enterprise pain — will demonstrate the kind of rapid trajectory expansion that turns a narrow tool into a platform. If all three proved true, the $60K/month AWS infrastructure investment suggests real technical work is happening, and the four-person team has shipped a product with database connectors, workflow builders, and multiple fill modes. That's execution. But execution on a small problem compounds into a small outcome. The wedge-to-platform story requires evidence of platform ambition I don't see — the pivot went the wrong direction, from platform to wedge.

The founders are young engineers from Tec de Monterrey, and Kerim's Contalink experience gives him genuine exposure to document workflows in Latin American enterprise. That's relevant domain knowledge. But I don't see the contrarian courage or outsider audacity that characterizes my best investments. Nobody told these founders their idea was crazy — because it isn't crazy. It's sensible. It's practical. And that's precisely the problem. When experts and incumbents validate your approach rather than dismissing it, you're probably building something incremental enough to be non-threatening. My best bets were in founders pursuing what the establishment declared impossible. Document filling is what the establishment considers obvious.

This is a pass. The team can build software, the pain point is real, and the Latin American enterprise angle is underserved. But I invest for the possibility of transformational outcomes — the money either expires or you build something that changes an industry. Document filling, executed perfectly, produces a modest business in a crowded market adjacent to well-funded competitors. The consequence of success doesn't justify the opportunity cost of these founders' careers, let alone my capital.
