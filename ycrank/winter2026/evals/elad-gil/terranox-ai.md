# Terranox AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most interesting question here is whether Terranox triggers my Anduril exception -- a capital-intensive, non-software business where a structural market vacuum justifies overriding my usual preference for asset-light, software-driven models. When I led Anduril's $450M Series D, the conditions were specific: Google's Project Maven cancellation created a defense AI vacancy with no credible startup competitor, Palmer Luckey had deep defense relationships, and the company already had contracts and deployed systems. With Terranox, you can see the structural analogy the founders are reaching for -- well-funded AI mineral exploration companies (KoBold at $2.96B valuation, Earth AI, GeologicAI) have deliberately avoided uranium, leaving a competitive vacancy. But the analogy breaks down on every dimension except the vacancy itself. There's no deployed system, no validated AI model, no mineral claims, no exploration results, and no uranium industry relationships. A vacancy without the capacity to fill it isn't an opportunity -- it's just empty space.

The structural moment in uranium is real, and I want to be clear about that. AI data center power demand is creating a genuine supply-demand imbalance -- long-term contract prices at $86/lb, persistent under-delivery from global producers, 15 new reactors expected online in 2026. This is not a fabricated "why now." When I track adoption curves, the convergence of AI compute demand and decarbonization goals points to sustained nuclear expansion, which means sustained uranium demand. The question I keep returning to is whether this structural moment is best captured by a 2-person pre-seed startup or by an established exploration company that adds AI capability. Cameco has $2.6B in revenue and decades of geological data. KoBold has over $1B raised and proven AI exploration infrastructure across five continents. If uranium economics become attractive enough, KoBold retraining its models on uranium geological data is a quarter's worth of work, not a fundamental capability rebuild. The competitive vacancy exists because the economics haven't yet justified entry, not because entry is structurally blocked.

This is fundamentally a multi-miracle business, which directly conflicts with my investment framework. Miracle one: the AI actually identifies viable uranium deposits better than decades of geological expertise. This is unproven -- "trained on 70+ years of exploration outcomes" is a claim with no validation metrics, no discovery success rate, no comparison to baseline methods. Miracle two: securing mineral claims in North America ahead of larger, better-capitalized competitors. Miracle three: raising the tens of millions required for physical exploration drilling -- orders of magnitude beyond pre-seed funding. Miracle four: navigating NRC or CNSC regulatory approval for uranium exploration, which can take years. Miracle five: converting discovery into economic extraction. Each step is a low-probability event, and they compound. When I see a plan that requires sequentially overcoming five distinct obstacles, that's not a startup I fund -- that's a hope stack.

The product-to-distribution trajectory here is essentially nonexistent, which in my framework caps the upside structurally. Stripe's payment API became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Figma's design tool became the channel for FigJam. What does Terranox distribute through a uranium deposit? Nothing. If the AI works and they find uranium, they sell uranium. There's no customer relationship to expand, no integration to deepen, no platform to build on. This is a commodity discovery business with a technology input -- the very definition of software-aware rather than software-driven. The AI doesn't fundamentally change what the business is. Remove the AI and you still have a uranium exploration company; it just drills in different locations.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the AI provides a genuine 10x improvement in discovery rates (possible but unproven), that the uranium supply deficit persists for the 7-15 years required to move from exploration to production (plausible but uncertain), and that no better-capitalized competitor enters uranium exploration in that timeframe (unlikely given rising prices). If all three hold, the value of discovered deposits could be enormous -- uranium at $86/lb with rising demand creates real economic value for anyone holding proven reserves. The founders' backgrounds are directionally relevant -- Checlair's geophysics PhD and Lipton's 8 years of AI/ML at Borealis AI is a reasonable combination for this problem. Their 10+ year working relationship reduces co-founder risk. But directionally relevant credentials and a real market moment aren't sufficient when the business model requires this much sequential execution against this much capital intensity with this little current evidence. I need to see that the company is building something that compounds through software -- and a mining company that uses machine learning to pick drill sites doesn't meet that bar.
