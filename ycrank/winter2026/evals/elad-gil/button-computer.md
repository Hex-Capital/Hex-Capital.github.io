# Button Computer -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The wearable AI hardware category has destroyed more venture capital than almost any other segment in the last three years. Humane raised $230M and sold to HP for $116M. Rabbit R1 shipped to brutal reviews. Limitless and Bee both got acqui-hired rather than scaling independently. When I see a graveyard this fresh, I ask two questions: Did the previous companies fail because the thesis was wrong, or because the timing was wrong? And has something structural changed that makes the current attempt different? With Harvey, I could point to the GPT scaling curve and say "model fidelity for legal work is 12-18 months away." With Button, the voice AI latency improvement is real -- GPT-4o and similar models crossed a conversational threshold in 2024-2025. But that capability improvement accrues primarily to the phone already in your pocket, not to a separate $179 clip-on device. The structural moment exists for voice AI. I'm not convinced it exists for a dedicated voice AI hardware form factor.

Here's what concerns me most: this looks like a "software-aware" business rather than a "software-driven" one. Button is commodity hardware -- dual-mic array, Bluetooth, speaker, a button -- wrapped around third-party LLM APIs. The company doesn't control the AI layer that provides the core value. Strip away the software and you have a Bluetooth speaker clip. The BYOK option on the website actually makes this explicit -- the AI is interchangeable. When the competitive moat depends on industrial design quality and firmware latency optimization rather than something that compounds, you're in consumer electronics margin territory, not software infrastructure territory. Plaud NotePin has proven that AI wearable hardware can sell -- 1M+ units, $250M annualized revenue -- but Plaud sells transcription, a tool with clear daily utility for professionals. Conversational AI wearables compete with a gesture every iPhone user already knows: holding the side button and talking.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this breaks down most clearly under my framework. A $179 hardware device with an optional $7.99/month subscription has a structurally limited ceiling. Compare to what makes my highest-conviction investments work: Stripe's payment API became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury. Figma's design tool became the channel for FigJam and developer handoff. Button's initial product creates a hardware sale and maybe a subscription, but there's no obvious second act -- no multi-product vision, no distribution channel being built, no platform dynamics. The device doesn't become more valuable as more people use it. It doesn't create a relationship that enables additional product distribution. At best, Button becomes a consumer electronics brand that ships new hardware versions annually, which is a fine business but not the kind of compounding trajectory I invest in.

The strongest bull case: these founders are real. Both worked on Vision Pro at Apple -- Burgoyne was one of the first engineers on the project and co-invented the gaze-and-tap interface for visionOS. That's genuine expertise in novel hardware interaction paradigms, directly relevant to building a voice-first wearable. Burgoyne's Skyglass shows execution velocity -- $700K raised, Techstars NYC, 95K downloads, $6K MRR. The "iPod Shuffle for AI" positioning is strategically sharp -- radically simpler and cheaper than Humane's failed approach, with the phone handling connectivity rather than trying to replace it. If the pattern here is Webvan-to-Instacart -- correct thesis, simpler execution, better timing -- then Button could capture the conversational AI wearable segment that Plaud's passive recording approach doesn't serve. For this to work, two things would need to be true: that people want conversational AI access frequently enough to wear a dedicated device (not just occasionally enough to use their phone), and that Apple doesn't ship a competing accessory. If both are true, these founders might be the right team to build it.

But my framework requires exactly one miracle, and Button needs at least two: proving that the conversational AI wearable form factor has product-market fit (Humane's $230M didn't find it), AND executing hardware manufacturing at scale at a $179 price point with margins that work. Hardware manufacturing is a grind -- FCC certification, supply chain management, quality assurance, returns handling. These founders have Apple-quality design intuition but haven't shipped physical products to consumers at scale. That's compounding low-probability events, which is exactly the pattern I avoid. Add the looming Apple AI wearable threat -- Apple is the platform owner for Button's iPhone-only launch -- and the risk profile steepens further. When your most important distribution partner is also your most dangerous potential competitor, you're in a structurally vulnerable position.
