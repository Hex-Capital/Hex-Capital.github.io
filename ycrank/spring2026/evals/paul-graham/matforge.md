# matforge -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about Matforge is something that almost never happens at pre-seed: the CEO has already had work product adopted by the customers he plans to sell to. Akash Ramdas discovered materials during his Stanford PhD that ended up on Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. That's not a pitch deck claim about a future relationship. That's the kind of credential you can't manufacture. When I think about what "domain expertise" means in the context of "the component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise," this is the extreme case. Most founders at this stage are trying to get their first meeting with a potential customer. This founder's research is already inside the planning documents of the two largest logic foundries on the planet.

The founder pairing here is close to what you'd design in a lab. Akash brings the semiconductor materials knowledge -- a Stanford PhD screening 15,000+ materials, published results, validated by Intel and TSMC. Advaith brings production agentic AI systems -- he was a founding applied scientist at Persona AI, built autonomous agents, went through the Luma Labs acquisition, did foundation model work at CMU. They've known each other since their IIT Madras days in 2015, co-authored a book together in 2019. "The relationship between the founders has to be strong. They must genuinely like one another, and work well together. Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock." A seven-year relationship that includes co-authoring together suggests genuine compatibility, not a co-founder matching event.

This is a textbook case of schlep blindness working in the founders' favor. Semiconductor materials discovery is one of those problems that sits unsolved for a decade because "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." The sales cycles are brutal -- 12 to 24 months with fewer than ten major customers worldwide. You need to bridge computational prediction to physical synthesis, which MIT Technology Review says nobody has fully cracked. You're selling to procurement organizations at Intel and TSMC that move at geological speed. Most AI founders would rather build another developer tool. "It's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site." The willingness to take on the schlep, combined with the domain expertise to actually navigate it, is the positive signal.

The competitive landscape deserves honest examination because it's the strongest objection. Periodic Labs raised $300M. Lila Sciences has $550M. CuspAI has $154M with Samsung Ventures backing. Matforge has maybe $500K from YC. That's a 600x capital disadvantage against the best-funded competitor. But here's what I've learned: "fundraising is not merely a useless metric, but positively misleading." Every one of those mega-funded companies is going broad -- multiple verticals, general-purpose platforms. Matforge is digging a well instead of a trench. "You can either dig a hole that's broad but shallow, or one that's narrow and deep." And the narrow expertise required for semiconductor fab-compatible process integration can't be bought with a $300M check. CuspAI lists semiconductors as a focus area but doesn't have a founder whose materials are on TSMC's roadmap. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors" -- especially when the startup has domain depth the competitors lack.

The bull case for Matforge is that it sits at a genuine inflection point. AI chip power consumption doubling annually means the materials bottleneck at sub-3nm nodes is getting worse every quarter. The semiconductor industry needs new interconnect metals, thermal interface materials, and dielectrics, and the traditional 10-year discovery cycle can't keep pace. If Matforge can compress that to months -- even for a subset of material candidates -- the value creation is enormous against a $67-81B materials market. The SAM question matters: are they a software platform worth hundreds of millions, or a material IP licensor worth tens of billions? The answer depends on execution, but the ceiling is real. This has the structure I described in "Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas" -- it's terrifying because you wonder if any team could pull it off, but the payoff if they do is asymmetric in exactly the way that matters for power-law returns.

What gives me pause is the prediction-to-synthesis gap. There's been no "ChatGPT moment" in materials discovery because the bottleneck isn't computation -- it's physical validation. Matforge's AI-first approach needs to bridge that gap, and there's no evidence yet of lab partnerships or synthesis capabilities. The concentrated customer base also constrains growth dynamics: you can't grow 7% per week when your total addressable buyer count fits in a conference room. And Akash is the sole repository of the domain expertise that makes this company special -- the classic single-point-of-failure risk. These are real concerns, but they're the kind of concerns you have about any pre-seed company attacking a genuinely hard problem. The founders haven't yet shown me the cockroach quality -- the cereal box moment that proves they'll survive anything. But the credentials and the domain depth are strong enough that I want to bet on them showing it.
