# HEVN, inc -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Peter Volnov shipped HERE Wallet to 600,000 users and HOT Wallet to 30 million before he showed up at YC pitching HEVN, inc as "Brex for international companies." That pairing — real consumer shipping chops on one end, a borrowed-framework category pitch on the other — is the whole story of HEVN, inc. The founders can build. The question is whether they wandered into a fintech category by analogy rather than by frustration, and whether 30M crypto-wallet users translate into anything Airwallex's $1.2B ARR machine can't already do better.

Founder resourcefulness is where HEVN, inc actually rates. Airbnb sits at the top of this ladder for me — Brian and Joe selling $40 cereal boxes to fund the company is the canonical signal. Stripe is the mid-rung, Patrick Collison grinding through bank integrations as a teenager. The bottom of the ladder is the McKinsey-deck team that waited for a seed round before writing code. HEVN, inc sits between Stripe and the consultant team, closer to Stripe: Volnov, Nikita Briuzgin, and Andrei Zhevlakov shipped HOT Wallet to 30 million users (Chainwire, Nov 2024) before HEVN, inc existed. That's not a deck. That's three people who've already done the unglamorous work of getting strangers to use their software, and that experience is mostly transferable even when the surface-area changes from crypto to USD ad-spend cards.

Idea-that-repels is where HEVN, inc rates worst, and it's the dimension I weight most heavily for an unknown founder. Stripe is the high rung — payments compliance was a schlep nobody wanted; Patrick had to convince me payments was even a real opportunity. ClassDojo is the mid rung, K-12 procurement nobody else would touch. The bottom is the "B2B workflow automation" pitch from my pass example. HEVN, inc sits closer to the bottom than the middle: the pitch is literally "Brex for international companies," which is the most consensus-coded framing a fintech founder could pick in 2026, with Airwallex at $1.2B ARR (Sacra, March 2026), Mercury at $650M ARR with an OCC charter pending, and Brex being acquired by Capital One for $5.15B. When everyone agrees the space is good, the returns are already gone. The non-EIN FDIC piece is the one thing on the page that someone might say "that won't work" about, and the website itself marks it "soon."

Organic problem discovery is the next-weakest. Dropbox is the high rung — Drew forgot his USB drive. Octopart is mid — physics grad students who couldn't find capacitors. The low rung is the pass-example team that "identified an opportunity" through interviews. HEVN, inc places between Octopart and the low rung, and only because Volnov and Briuzgin actually are non-US founders running international companies, so there's a plausible lived-frustration story behind HEVN, inc — but the dossier contains no such story. No moment-of-frustration narrative, no "we kept getting denied by Mercury so we built this." Without the artifact, I'm using the absent-evidence default.

Founder learning rate sits between Patrick Collison's Auctomatic-to-Stripe arc (mid rung) and a static one-year-no-iteration team (low rung). HEVN, inc's pivot from crypto wallets to regulated USD banking is a real pivot in domain — but the dossier flags that HOT Labs is still operating as of late 2024, which raises the bandwidth-split question I always ask repeat-builder founders: are you actually running HEVN, inc, or are you running two things badly. Earnest builder identity, against the Replit mid-rung and the Watsi high-rung: I see no mission orientation in the HEVN, inc materials, and the "Brex for international companies" framing is exactly the kind of borrowed-frame signal I called out in "Writes and Write-Nots." A founder who can think clearly doesn't reach for the analog-comparison shorthand on their homepage.

My 36-month forecast: HEVN, inc reaches $4–12M ARR as a niche tool for non-US founders running USD ad spend, gets stuck in Airwallex's gravity well before crossing $25M, and either pivots into a stablecoin-payout-specific product where the founders' crypto-rails experience actually matters, or gets aqui-hired by a payments incumbent for $20–60M. The mechanism: repeat-builder founders generally don't fail; they execute their way to a real-but-bounded outcome in a category that has a billion-dollar incumbent already.

The strongest case against my pass is the HOT Wallet distribution argument: 30 million wallet users is a distribution asset no other "Brex for international" competitor has, and a 0.5%-conversion of that base into business-banking customers is 150,000 SMB accounts — Mercury-scale. That's the bull mechanism, and it would matter if the conversion were real. It isn't. Crypto-wallet retail users and non-US-incorporated SMBs running Google Ads spend are nearly disjoint populations; the buying motion, the KYB pipeline, and the trust model are all different. The hard part of HEVN, inc is the sponsor-bank stack and the no-EIN KYB pipeline, and 30M TON wallet users do not help with either. The condition that flips my verdict: HEVN, inc lands a sponsor bank that delivers genuine FDIC-insured no-EIN USD accounts in production (not "soon"), and shows me three months of weekly account-opening growth at 7%+ from a customer segment that is verifiably not the HOT Wallet base. Then HEVN, inc has the schlep moat its pitch implies, and I'd reconsider.
