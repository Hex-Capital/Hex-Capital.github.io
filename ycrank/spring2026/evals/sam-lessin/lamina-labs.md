# Lamina Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with Lamina Labs in one sentence: this is a software tool whose entire moat is software, built on top of LLM capabilities the founders don't control, in a category that every AI lab on the planet is already circling. It's the textbook case of what I mean when I say the SaaS era is over. The deterministic rendering pipeline sounds impressive technically, but strip away the language and what you have is an LLM wrapper that outputs Manim-style animations instead of text. Manim itself is open source with 35,800 GitHub stars. The hard part isn't the rendering -- it's the text-to-scene-description conversion, and that layer is borrowed wholesale from foundation models that will improve whether or not Lamina Labs exists.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't hit my filter the way it needs to. Sudip and Rohan are clearly smart -- MIT CS/Math and Caltech Physics with olympiad medals and a published astrophysics paper is a strong technical duo for building a mathematically precise animation engine. That's the "sane" half, and it's genuinely there. But the idea isn't insane. "Use AI to make educational content creation faster" is one of the most consensus ideas in technology right now. Knowlify came through YC a batch ago doing substantially the same thing and already raised $3 million. Vyond has $35 million in ARR doing template-based animation for corporate training. Steve.AI has five million users. When I describe the Coinbase investment, the key isn't just that Brian Armstrong was credible -- it's that building a regulated crypto exchange in 2012 made people think you were out of your mind. Nobody thinks making educational videos faster is crazy. They think it's obvious. And obvious means the returns are already priced in across the entire category.

Run the AI kill test and this company fails it cleanly. Would this business be good if AI didn't exist? No -- the entire product is "type a prompt, get an animation in 8 seconds." Remove the AI and you have... Manim, which already exists for free. The AI isn't a cherry on top of a fundamentally strong business; the AI IS the business. That's the exact category I've been publicly arguing is uninvestable. When OpenAI or Google adds structured mathematical visualization to their model outputs -- and this is a when, not an if -- the standalone tool becomes a feature. The Birchbox pattern applies: you can build real traction in a category with no structural defense, raise money on the narrative, and end up in the worst possible venture outcome where the company is too alive to write off but too fragile to compound.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously: these two have genuinely exceptional technical credentials for this specific problem, and they're attacking a real gap. The STEM precision angle is a legitimate differentiator -- most AI video tools produce hallucinated garbage that's useless for teaching calculus or organic chemistry. If Lamina's deterministic pipeline truly produces frame-accurate mathematical visualizations, and they ship fast enough to become the default tool before the foundation models catch up, there's a window to build a real user base. EdTech AI spending is growing at 42% CAGR. Every professor, every Khan Academy-style creator, every corporate trainer teaching technical content needs this. If Lamina becomes the verb for "make an explainer video" -- the Canva of STEM animation -- the business could be meaningful. But this bull case requires winning a race against the most well-funded AI labs in history, and it requires that winning the race produces durable lock-in rather than a temporary lead. I don't see what creates that lock-in. There's no network effect, no proprietary data that compounds with usage, no physical infrastructure, no protocol position. The product gets better as the underlying LLMs get better, but so does every competing approach.

The primitive-versus-application test seals it for me. Lamina is an application built on someone else's infrastructure. It doesn't create a new layer that other products integrate with. Compare this to how Venmo created a new payment primitive that Braintree, PayPal, and an entire social payments ecosystem built around. Or how Aleo established zero-knowledge proofs as infrastructure for privacy across the entire blockchain ecosystem. Lamina makes videos. Good videos, potentially very precise videos, but videos. The ceiling is the ceiling of a tool company, and tool companies in categories where software is the entire product are exactly what's being destroyed right now.

Both founders being undergraduates on leave adds a minor concern -- not because young founders can't build great companies, but because managing a YC startup alongside potential academic obligations introduces competing demands at the exact moment when singular focus matters most. The lack of any revenue signal, user counts, or even waitlist metrics means there's no downside cushion to offset the structural concerns. This is pure narrative with no floor underneath it.
