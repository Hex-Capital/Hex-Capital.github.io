# Benchspan -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Benchspan: this is two smart engineers who built agent benchmarking infrastructure at Microsoft and high-throughput systems at Bloomberg, looked around at the hottest market in tech, and decided to start a company in it. When I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is résumé, not biography. Avi was on GitHub Copilot building exactly this kind of evaluation infrastructure. Ritesh was architecting trading systems at Bloomberg. They met at Georgia Tech. They started a prediction markets company called Oddpool, pivoted mid-batch to AI benchmarking, and Avi's GitHub still says "Co-founder @Oddpool." I don't doubt their competence — Avi might be one of a few hundred people who've built agent eval infrastructure at true scale. But competence and forge are different things. Nobody's life story made them incapable of ignoring the problem of slow benchmark runs. This problem didn't find these founders through lived experience; they found it through employment, then pivoted to it when the market shifted. That reads to me like career optimization, not mission inevitability.

The space itself is about as far from "magically weird" as you can get in 2026. AI developer tooling is consensus incarnate — every partner at every fund in San Francisco wants to write this check. Braintrust just raised $80 million. Galileo raised $45 million with 834% revenue growth. LangChain sits on $260 million. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "we help AI teams run benchmarks faster." When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I wrote the SpaceX check, rockets were exploding and people thought I was out of my mind. The courage required to pitch "run your agent benchmarks in the cloud" at a YC Demo Day in 2026 is approximately zero. This is a space where investors compete for allocation, which is the opposite of the signal I look for.

I always look for the hidden system — the opaque structure that extracts value from people who can't see it. When I backed Affirm, the invisible system was credit card interest compounding in ways consumers couldn't understand. When I backed Ownwell, it was property tax assessments that nobody knew how to challenge. Benchspan's customer is an AI engineering team at a well-funded company that's burning money on benchmark runs. That's not an invisible tax on the powerless — that's an efficiency tool for the already-powerful. There's nothing wrong with that, but it doesn't activate the part of my framework that produces my highest-conviction bets.

Let me give the bull case its due, because I try to be honest about what I might be missing. If agent development becomes as ubiquitous as web development, and if benchmarking becomes as essential as CI/CD testing, Benchspan could be the GitHub Actions of agent evaluation. Avi's domain experience is genuinely rare — he didn't just read about agent benchmarking, he built the infrastructure at one of the largest deployments in the world. The pivot from Oddpool actually shows an interesting thread: PredictionMarketBench was a benchmarking framework for trading agents, so they've been circling evaluation infrastructure across multiple domains. If they nail developer experience (the "37 lines of code" onboarding story is compelling), they could grow bottom-up inside AI teams faster than the bloated, well-funded observability platforms can slim down to serve the pure benchmarking use case. And historical result data does create real switching friction over time. For an investor who weights market timing and technical founder-market fit above everything else, this could be a strong bet.

But that's not my framework. The execution layer — Docker containers running benchmarks in parallel — isn't technically defensible. The dossier itself notes that open-source alternatives exist and a well-resourced competitor could replicate it. My worst investment failures shared a common trait: they were products that could be features of larger platforms. When Braintrust or LangSmith decides to add parallel cloud benchmark execution, which is an incremental infrastructure investment for companies already doing eval and tracing, Benchspan's narrow positioning becomes a liability rather than an advantage. I learned this lesson with products that burned bright on initial adoption and then got absorbed. Without network effects, proprietary data, or regulatory moats, the first-mover advantage is temporal, not structural. The benchmark catalog breadth is real but replicable. This isn't infrastructure that compounds like Flock Safety's camera network or Flexport's logistics graph — it's a well-executed tool in a category that larger, richer companies are already circling.

I wish them well. Avi clearly understands this problem domain at a level most founders don't. But I need the biographical forge, the contrarian discomfort, and the invisible system beneath the surface to write a check from my own money. What I see here is a capable team building a reasonable product in the most consensus market in technology, competing against hundreds of millions in funded competitors, with an execution layer that doesn't compound structurally. This isn't a Long Journey bet.
