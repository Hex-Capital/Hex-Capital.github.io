# Prototyping.io -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about AI-native manufacturing: everybody's doing it. RMFG raised $4.5M. Jiga raised $19.1M. Toolpath raised $10M. Korso's in YC. Xometry is a public company doing $687M in revenue and already has an AI quoting engine processing millions of parts. Protolabs' CNC business grew 25% year-over-year last quarter. When I look at this landscape, I don't see an opportunity the market is mispricing -- I see a category where every LP deck in San Francisco has a slide that says "AI + manufacturing = reshoring opportunity." The upside is already in the price. There is no gap between market consensus and reality here. The smart money is already crowding in, which means even if Prototyping.io executes perfectly, the return profile is capped by the competition for the same thesis.

Strip away the manufacturing vocabulary and run the AI kill test. Prototyping.io doesn't own factories. They don't operate physical infrastructure. They ingest CAD files and run AI-powered DFM analysis. That's a software layer. That's a feature. Xometry can -- and probably will -- ship deeper DFM analysis inside their existing instant quoting flow. They've already acquired Thomasnet for $300M to strengthen their data position. Compare this to Metropolis, which I seeded -- that company operates actual parking garages. The AI amplifies the business, but the physical asset base is the moat. Nobody's replicating 10,000 parking facilities with a model. But replicating "upload your CAD file and get manufacturability feedback"? That's a product feature for any platform with an existing supplier network and enough training data. When I hear "AI-native" applied to what is fundamentally a marketplace intermediary, I hear "we built the feature before the incumbent did" -- and that's a timing advantage, not a structural one.

The co-founders are clearly smart. Revanth's IIT Bhubaneswar silver medal and Schlumberger manufacturing experience give him real domain credibility. Prerit published at ACM CoNEXT and IEEE ICDCS, built distributed systems at Microsoft and Arista -- he can ship. But the idea isn't insane. It's sensible. "AI-powered DFM analysis integrated into the ordering flow" is exactly what every mechanical engineer would nod along to at a demo day. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "yeah, that makes sense, a bunch of people are building that." Sane founders, sane idea -- which in my framework is the boring quadrant. And there's a specific red flag: Prerit's LinkedIn says he "recently joined forces" with Revanth. No shared employer. No shared university. No shared history I can find. My best returns come from founder relationships forged over years -- Kortina and I go back to college, I watched Raj through three companies before writing the Solana check. Synthetic co-founder pairings correlate with synthetic commitment, and I have no signal here to override that prior.

Now, the bull case, because I want to be honest about what would need to be true. If Prototyping.io builds a genuine buyer-side experience that hardware engineers love -- think "Stripe for custom parts" where the DFM feedback becomes so deeply embedded in the design workflow that engineers can't imagine going back to uploading to three different portals -- then the switching costs compound. If they build CAD plugins, PLM integrations, and institutional memory of each customer's design patterns, the platform becomes sticky in a way that a simple marketplace isn't. The $1.5B online CNC market growing to $3.2B by 2033 is real. Reshoring is real. And Revanth genuinely understands manufacturing tolerances and material properties in a way that most software founders don't. If I squint hard enough, I can see a world where the buyer-side DFM intelligence creates a wedge that neither Xometry (sell-side marketplace incentives) nor Protolabs (factory-asset rigidity) can easily replicate because their business models create tension with giving buyers the kind of pre-order analysis that might reduce order complexity. That's a real structural argument. But it requires everything to go right against entrenched competition, and I don't see any evidence -- no revenue, no customers, no LOIs, no testimonials -- that they've even begun to prove the wedge works.

This is a competent team building something incremental in a consensus-hot category. That's the definition of a bet where the risk is not in the price -- it's hidden behind the enthusiasm. Pass.
