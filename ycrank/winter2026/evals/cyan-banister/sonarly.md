# Sonarly -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Sonarly is that it's operating in one of the most popular investment categories of the last two years. AI-powered developer tools -- particularly anything with "AI agent that writes code" in the description -- is the exact kind of consensus that makes me instinctively pull back. Every fund I talk to is looking at AI dev tools. Every accelerator is full of them. When I think about the companies that have made my career -- SpaceX when people thought private rockets were science fiction, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when the idea of mining Bitcoin on gas flares made people laugh -- they share a common trait: other investors thought the founders were out of their minds. Nobody thinks AI-powered alert triage is crazy. Sentry, a public company, already ships Autofix with a reported 95% root cause accuracy. Datadog has Bits AI tested across 2,000+ customer environments. This isn't an unpopular space where a pocket of dreamers are seeing something others can't. This is the most popular space in enterprise software right now, and the incumbents are already here.

When I try to get to the founders' story -- and that's always where I start -- I find something that's common but not compelling in the way I need it to be. Dimittri and Alexandre met at CY Tech in France, built MEORIA together (a chatbot helping French high school students choose universities), and got overwhelmed by production alerts while running it as a two-person team. They experienced a real frustration. I believe that. But when I ask myself "is this person's biography inseparable from this problem?" -- the way Travis Kalanick's fury at the taxi medallion system was inseparable from Uber, or the way the Contraline founder's years of reproductive biology research was inseparable from male contraception -- I don't find that thread here. Having too many alerts on a small app is something every developer has experienced. It's a relatable annoyance, not a biographical crucible that forged an irreversible commitment to production reliability infrastructure.

The structural insight about cross-platform aggregation is the strongest intellectual element of this pitch, and I want to be honest about what would have to be true for this to be a great investment. If Sonarly is right that Sentry will never integrate with Datadog's telemetry, and Datadog will never ingest Sentry's errors -- because each company's business model depends on platform lock-in -- then there's a genuine opening for a monitoring-agnostic layer. Every serious engineering team uses multiple observability tools. If Sonarly can get fix quality from the current 30% mergeable PR rate (per that Hacker News commenter) up to 80%+, and build enough architectural context through their "living map" approach that switching becomes painful, they could become essential infrastructure. The market is enormous and growing. That's the bull case, and it's not irrational. But it requires execution that would be extraordinary for two first-time infrastructure founders whose prior experience was a consumer edtech chatbot -- and the moat is thin because the core stack (LLMs + monitoring APIs + MCP) is available to anyone.

What I don't see at all is economic access. This product makes engineers at well-funded companies more productive. That's valuable, but it doesn't create income for people who lack it, doesn't make an opaque system transparent for underserved populations, and doesn't open doors that were closed. When I backed Uber, I could see immediately that it would create economic opportunity for drivers. When I backed Affirm, I could see transparent credit replacing hidden fees. Sonarly solves a real problem for a narrow technical audience, but it doesn't resonate with the part of my thesis about why capitalism should exist -- to create positive-sum economic outcomes for people at the margins.

The one genuinely positive signal is the co-founder relationship. They went to school together, built a company together, and moved across an ocean to San Francisco together. That's a tested partnership. After HQ Trivia taught me that even perfect timing and the right product can't survive co-founder conflict, I take this seriously. These two have already navigated the stress of building and presumably shutting down or stepping away from MEORIA, and they chose to do it again together. That matters. But a strong co-founder relationship in a consensus category, without biographical depth or a non-obvious insight, isn't enough for me to write a check from my own money.
