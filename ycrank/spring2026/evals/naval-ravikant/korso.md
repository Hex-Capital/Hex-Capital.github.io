# Korso -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, because it tells me everything about the timing. Paperless Parts has $30M and an established customer base. Uptool raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins -- four of the most sophisticated enterprise firms in the world -- to solve the same problem. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all funded and building AI quoting for manufacturers. When I see five-plus funded competitors converging on the same thesis simultaneously, that is the market telling me this is consensus. Everyone read the same memo: LLMs can now extract structured data from unstructured manufacturing documents, only 35% of procurement teams use AI for RFQs, the TAM is enormous. That's not a secret. That's a slide deck everyone has. The asymmetric upside I look for comes from being right when most people think you're wrong. Here, everyone thinks you're right -- which means the pricing already reflects the optimism, and the race goes to whoever has the deepest domain knowledge and the most capital to deploy against enterprise sales cycles.

Which brings me to specific knowledge. What do these founders know about manufacturing quoting that can't be taught to any competent engineering team in six months? Martin Pan spent time at GM as an automations engineer in 2025 -- relevant but brief, the kind of exposure you get as a new grad rotation, not the kind of pattern recognition you develop from ten years on the shop floor watching quotes get botched and orders get lost. Daichi Hiraoka has strong ML research chops and a Rockwell Automation internship -- useful for building the technical system, but not for understanding why a particular manufacturer prices aluminum extrusions differently for a repeat customer versus a new one. Alex Liu dropped out and reads research papers -- I respect the intellectual hunger, but there's no evidence of manufacturing-domain obsession. None of these founders have run a quoting operation, managed supplier relationships at scale, or built the ERP systems they're trying to replace. The knowledge they bring -- ML, engineering, general technical ability -- is precisely the kind of knowledge that can be trained. When Uptool's team is backed by Khosla with $6M and presumably has its own domain experts, Korso's specific knowledge deficit becomes a structural disadvantage, not a timing issue.

The leverage architecture is where I'd normally find something to get excited about, but this is a workflow automation tool -- it makes an existing process faster, not a qualitatively new capability. One person with Korso still does quoting; they just do it quicker. Compare that to what I look for: Notion didn't speed up document management, it created a new relationship between an individual and their organizational capacity. Replit didn't optimize existing coding workflows, it made programming accessible to people who never would have coded. Korso takes the quote-to-order pipeline and compresses the time. That's valuable, but it's efficiency, not leverage. The product doesn't give manufacturers a new capability they didn't have before -- it gives them the same capability at lower cost. Software businesses built on efficiency gains rather than capability creation tend to compete on price, which is exactly the wrong dynamic when five competitors are in the same space.

The bull case would require believing that Korso's WhatsApp-plus-email multi-channel ingestion, combined with the RAG assistant's accumulation of per-customer pricing history, creates a compounding data moat that the better-funded competitors can't replicate. There's a version of this that works: if manufacturers in emerging markets -- where WhatsApp is the primary business communication channel -- adopt Korso first, and the accumulated pricing data makes the system irreplaceable, you get a Stack Overflow-style knowledge graph effect at the manufacturer level. The "why now" is real. LLMs genuinely crossed a threshold for parsing manufacturing documents. And the market is genuinely underserved -- most manufacturers still run on spreadsheets and email. But for this to work, Korso needs to win the land grab against better-capitalized teams, with founders whose manufacturing knowledge is measured in months rather than years, selling to enterprise buyers who take 6-12 months to make purchasing decisions. That's a lot of simultaneous breakthroughs required from a team that hasn't demonstrated the domain depth to outmaneuver competitors who arrived at the same insight with more resources.

I look for founders who know something that can't be taught, building something that creates leverage that didn't exist before, at a time when most people think they're wrong. Korso's founders are smart engineers with acquirable knowledge, building an efficiency tool in a category where multiple well-funded teams already agree on the thesis. The technology is commodity LLM/RAG -- the dossier itself notes this. The potential data moat is real but shared with every competitor and doesn't exist at launch. This is a pass for me. Not because the market isn't real -- it is -- but because nothing about this particular team, at this particular moment, gives them an unfair advantage in a race that five other teams are already running.
