# Agentic Fabriq -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about this company isn't the founders or the technology -- it's the competitive landscape. When I backed Juniper Networks, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I invested in Impossible Foods, the food industry thought plant-based meat was a joke. The signal I look for is experts dismissing the problem. Here, the opposite is happening: a former Okta executive has already raised $12M to build essentially the same thing (Arcade.dev), OASIS Security has $75M, Composio has $29M, Opti has $20M, and SailPoint -- a public company -- has already shipped Agent Identity Security as a product. Nobody is saying agent identity management is impossible. Everyone is building it. When the incumbents and well-funded startups are racing toward the same thesis, you're not skating to where the puck will be -- you're arriving at a crowded rink.

Let me start where I always start: the consequence of success. If Agentic Fabriq succeeds perfectly and becomes "Okta for Agents" -- what happens? Enterprises have better-managed AI agent permissions. That's a real business. Okta itself is worth over $15 billion. But better-managed enterprise permissions does not change how any industry fundamentally operates. No physical system is transformed. No trillion-dollar incumbent approach becomes obsolete. The world just has slightly more secure AI deployments. Compare that to what I fund with conviction: Impossible Foods would reclaim billions of acres of arable land, Commonwealth Fusion Systems would make fossil fuels irrelevant, OpenAI would create machine intelligence. "Okta for Agents" is infrastructure plumbing for a trend that's already consensus. The framing itself -- "Okta for Agents" -- reveals the ceiling. You're explicitly analogizing to an existing company in an existing category. I invest in things that don't have analogies yet because the category hasn't been invented.

The technology question reinforces this concern. The dossier states explicitly that the underlying components -- OAuth flows, RBAC, token management -- are "well-understood" and that "the barrier to building a functionally similar product is moderate." This is not a technology breakthrough; it's a product abstraction layer on top of existing primitives. There's no deep technical moat here, no proprietary algorithm, no novel scientific insight. The value lies in product packaging and go-to-market execution. And on execution, two MIT dropouts with AI research backgrounds but zero enterprise sales experience are going to sell security governance software to enterprise CISOs -- against a former Okta executive with $12M and against SailPoint's existing customer relationships? That's not an outsider-versus-incumbent dynamic I find compelling. That's a capital and credibility disadvantage.

The strongest bull case is timing. Agent adoption is genuinely on an exponential curve -- NHIs grew 44% year-over-year, 90% of agents are over-permissioned, and the agentic AI market is projected at 43.8% CAGR through 2034. These are real numbers suggesting a real problem that will intensify. If Paulina and Matthew can build the most developer-friendly, agent-native identity layer before incumbents figure out the architecture -- and if legacy IAM systems truly can't be retrofitted for real-time, per-action agent authorization -- then there's a structural wedge. Okta and SailPoint were built for human identity lifecycles. Agents operate at machine speed with dynamic scoping requirements that might demand fundamental re-architecture. If that architectural gap is real and persistent, a purpose-built startup could win despite the funding gap. For this to work, agent adoption would need to explode faster than incumbents can adapt, and these founders would need to demonstrate the kind of enterprise sales velocity that their backgrounds don't currently suggest. It's possible. But it requires the market AND the execution AND the competitive dynamics to all break their way simultaneously -- which is exactly the multi-variable dependency pattern that makes me cautious.

Paulina and Matthew are clearly smart -- MIT AI research at Kavli, Haystack, CSAIL, and FutureTech is legitimate technical training. Dropping out at 19 takes some courage. But I see no evidence of contrarian thinking. Building identity management for AI agents in 2025-2026 is the consensus play in enterprise security. Every VC I know is looking at this space. There's nothing here that the experts are dismissing, nothing that makes people look at these founders like they're crazy. And that absence of expert rejection is, paradoxically, my biggest concern. The best investments I've made were in things that made smart people uncomfortable. "Okta for Agents" makes smart people nod approvingly -- which usually means the consequences of success, while profitable, are not transformational enough to justify the risk of being the smallest, least-funded team in a competitive sprint.
