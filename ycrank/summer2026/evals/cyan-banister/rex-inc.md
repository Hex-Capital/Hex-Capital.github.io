# Rex -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Merlin Kafka and Lewis Blackwood spent four years at Sequence building a16z-backed B2B finance ops together, and now they have walked out the door to build Rex on the exact problem they watched their own customers fail to automate -- which is the part of this dossier that actually loads, and the part where the rest of the dossier quietly unloads it. Rex is a domain-veteran team building an invisible enterprise back-office product I cannot ride, scan, or photograph in my own life, which is the inverse of my sourcing method. The signature pairing -- high founder-lived-experience, low physical verifiability -- tells me what Rex is: a credible AR-agent company built by operators who saw the manual labor in person, sold to a buyer (the Fortune 100 controller) I will never bump into at The Lobby.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage before he ever pitched Anduril, and that adjacent-product-to-scale precedent is the high rung I score founder origin against. Rex is not Luckey-grade, but Merlin as Sequence's CPO and Lewis as Engineering Lead spent four years zero-to-Series-A on a B2B finance ops platform with the same buyer persona, and their claim that they "saw firsthand that at-scale, O2C still runs on manual labour" is autobiographical, not analytical -- this is the John Hanke / Keyhole-into-Niantic shape, just smaller. Where Rex diverges from the Luckey rung is the absence of an obvious "moment" -- there is no Wi-Fi-name, no roughneck-on-the-well-pad story I can quote; it is operator pattern recognition rather than a singular personal collision with the problem. That puts Rex above the consensus-AI-agent-darling I would pass on slide three, but a clear notch below the founders my flagship checks were written for.

HQ Trivia collapsed because Yusupov and Kroll could not name the tiebreaker when the product cooled, and that is the failure mechanism I screen every two-founder pre-seed against. Rex does not look like HQ on this axis -- Merlin is named CEO, Lewis is CTO, the roles are delineated, and four prior years of shared trench work at Sequence is the kind of co-founder forging that survives the first real disagreement. No red flag here, just an absent operating agreement disclosure which is stage-normal.

The "magically weird" filter is where Rex actively fails me. AI agents for order-to-cash is not the gas-flare-Bitcoin-rig of 2018; it is the most consensus pre-seed thesis of the Summer 2026 batch, sitting in a category where HighRadius ($3.1B), BlackLine (public), Versapay, Tesorio, and Qurrent are already swinging, and where the Vercel "#1 of 2,500" badge is itself a consensus-deselection signal. Eighteen months from now the agent-orchestration layer over a frontier LLM compounds for whoever owns the proprietary exception data first -- Rex's >$500M AR under F100 management is a real head-start on that flywheel -- but the same eighteen months let every well-funded peer ship the same wrapper, so the dimension does not compound, it races.

Rex's $60B-outsourced-O2C framing is software arbitraging labor, not hardware or regulated-frontier risk, which puts it well below the Crusoe / Anduril / Vigil Labs sleeve where my real edge sits. For the capital-intensity score to flip a tier upward, Rex would need to be doing something I do not see in the dossier -- owning a regulated money-movement license, building a proprietary ledger primitive into the ERP layer, or taking balance-sheet risk on the receivables it manages. None of that is here; outcome-based pricing is clever commercial design, not a regulatory moat.

Postmates I bet on because the delivery happened in front of me on a San Francisco sidewalk -- Rex I cannot bet on the same way, because a normal human cannot install, ride, or observe an AR-agent triaging a NetSuite inbox at a Fortune 100. The dashboard screenshot showing "342 customers / $18.4M" may be illustrative, the F100 logos are undisclosed, and the only tactile artifact in the dossier is a WorkOS SSO integration. This is the developer-tools-abstraction failure mode for my method, and it is structural, not solvable by a better demo.

Thirty-six month forecast: Rex hits $6-12M ARR on a handful of pre-IPO and one or two F100 logos, gets a strategic acquisition offer in the $80-200M range from HighRadius, Workday, or NetSuite, and either takes it or collides directly with Sequence's $20M-Series-A revenue-ops product where shared a16z gravity makes the boardroom awkward. The mechanism is straightforward -- operator-grade founders execute, the agent layer commoditizes, and the exit is a strategic absorption rather than a category-defining outcome, which is exactly the shape the high-founder / low-weirdness signature predicts.

The bear-case-of-my-pass is that Merlin and Lewis are precisely the lived-experience operators my thesis privileges, the Sequence-to-Rex arc is the Keyhole-to-Niantic arc one scale down, and >$500M in F100 AR managed pre-seed is the kind of traction signal that retrospectively justifies a check even when the category is consensus. I hear it, and the steel-man stings -- but the Sequence adjacency is the part that actually wins the argument against itself: the founders left their own a16z-backed company to build a product that overlaps Sequence's December 2025 "AI agents for billing and invoice automation" Series A, which is either a quiet non-compete fracture or a competitive collision the dossier refuses to name. I would flip to Invest the day Rex publishes a signed F100 logo and a clean disclosure on the Sequence relationship -- until then, this is a Neutral with a polite "rebuttal, not a hard no."
