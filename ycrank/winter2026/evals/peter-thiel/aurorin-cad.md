# Aurorin CAD -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

There is a paradox at the center of Aurorin's thesis that most investors will fail to notice. The company's "why now" — articulated explicitly by Jared Friedman — is that AI coding tools have crossed a threshold where a single person can plausibly build what previously required hundreds of engineers over decades. SolidWorks is ten million lines of code; AI makes it possible for Baron to attempt a ground-up rebuild. But if that premise is correct, it undermines the durability of the very moat Baron is constructing. If AI compresses the timeline for building a parametric B-Rep kernel from a decade to a year, then the custom kernel is no longer a multi-year barrier to replication — it is a barrier that any well-funded competitor can also compress. The secret consumes itself. This is the structural problem I cannot get past: the enabling condition for the company's existence is also the enabling condition for its competitors' existence.

Set that aside and ask the prior question: what is the secret here? There are two layers. The surface-level insight — "CAD software is ancient and needs modernization" — is pure consensus. Every engineer knows SolidWorks is painful. Adam, Zoo, Shapr3D, and a dozen others have all identified the same problem. This is not a secret; it is a complaint. The deeper thesis is more interesting: that AI-layer approaches are structurally doomed because they inherit the limitations of 1980s geometric kernels, and only a ground-up rebuild can deliver the order-of-magnitude improvement that makes switching costs worth paying. This is genuinely contrarian — it rejects the approach that every other AI-for-CAD startup is pursuing. But it is a thesis about architectural strategy, not about the world. The strongest secrets reveal something about reality that most experts deny. "Reusable rockets are physically possible" was SpaceX's secret. "Real-identity social networking creates an unbreakable network effect" was Facebook's. "You must rebuild the kernel" is a product architecture decision, not a truth about the world that rearranges what is possible. It is closer to a correct engineering judgment than to a genuine secret.

The founder's profile is intriguing and partially legible to me. Three SpaceX internships — Raptor combustion simulation, Dragon GNC, Starshield flight software — is not decoration. SpaceX selects for and produces a specific kind of engineer: one who builds from first principles, questions inherited assumptions, and operates at the boundary of what established institutions consider feasible. The Apple GPU driver work is directly relevant to building a hardware-accelerated geometric kernel. Baron apparently has no university degree, which I find more interesting than most investors would — I have paid people $100,000 to drop out of college, and the ones who succeeded (Buterin, Field) shared a quality of conviction that made formal credentials irrelevant. But Buterin had already published the Ethereum whitepaper before receiving the fellowship. Field had already built a working design tool. What has Baron built? The claim is extraordinary — parts created in seconds versus twenty minutes in SolidWorks — but no public benchmark, no user testimonial, and no evidence of kernel completeness exists beyond the YC launch page. The vision is definite, not indefinite; this is clearly not a lean-startup iterate-and-pivot operation. But definite vision without demonstrated execution is aspiration, not conviction. Conviction is what remains after reality pushes back, and I see no evidence of Baron having been tested.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously: if Baron has genuinely built a working parametric kernel with GPU acceleration and integrated AI capabilities, this is a zero-to-one technology in a twelve-billion-dollar market where every incumbent faces cannibalization risk. PTC paid $470 million for Onshape — a cloud-native CAD tool that still runs on Parasolid, a legacy kernel. An AI-native platform with a modern kernel that delivers 10x speed improvements could be worth multiples of that. The incumbent dynamics are genuinely favorable: Dassault, Siemens, and PTC have massive VAR channel dependencies and maintenance-revenue streams that create structural disincentives to rebuild. This is the pattern that makes disruption possible — the thing Clayton Christensen got right. And the SpaceX analog is real: a small team with first-principles thinking rebuilt rocket technology that the entire aerospace industry had declared impossible to rebuild. If Baron internalized that methodology during three tours at SpaceX, the pattern could translate. In this scenario, the solo-founder constraint resolves because a working prototype attracts the kind of engineers who want to build something genuinely new, and the YC network provides access to early customers in hardware companies.

But I keep returning to the competitive structure. The AI-for-CAD space already has multiple funded players — Adam with $4.1 million and 10 million social media impressions, Zoo with $5.12 million and an open-source approach, Shapr3D with modern UX on Parasolid. None are building a custom kernel, which differentiates Aurorin architecturally. But the mimetic convergence around "AI for CAD" means that user attention and engineering talent are being distributed across multiple entrants simultaneously. When I look at a market and see a half-dozen startups all attacking the same problem with slightly different approaches, I see a category that is already competitive — and the returns in competitive categories trend toward mediocrity. Aurorin's full-stack approach is the most ambitious, but ambition and monopoly are not the same thing. The absence of any beachhead strategy compounds my concern: the product is a free download for everyone, targeting generic "hardware companies" and "design engineers." Facebook started at Harvard. PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Where is Aurorin's Harvard? The SpaceX background suggests aerospace prototyping as a natural wedge, but it is not articulated, and that omission suggests the founder has not yet thought carefully enough about the sequence of market dominance.

I respect the technical ambition and the architectural bet. Building a full CAD kernel from scratch is the kind of hard technology work I prefer to fund over another SaaS dashboard. But a solo founder with internship experience — however impressive — tackling one of the most complex software engineering challenges in existence, with no visible traction, no team, no revenue, and a competitive landscape that is already crowding, does not clear my threshold for deploying personal capital. The secret is half-formed: the kernel-rebuild insight is genuinely contrarian, but it lacks the self-reinforcing quality that the strongest secrets possess. I would want to see the product demonstrated on a non-trivial assembly, evidence that the AI agent can handle the edge cases that make CAD kernels so difficult (filleting, shelling, boolean operations on complex geometry), and a specific plan for which narrow segment of engineers Baron intends to monopolize first. Without those signals, this is a promising but unproven bet in an increasingly contested space.
