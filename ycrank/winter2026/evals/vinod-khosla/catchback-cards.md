# CatchBack Cards -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: a company that has found a real consumer pain point -- opaque mystery packs, fragmented card markets, trust deficits -- and built a competent solution. The $13K MRR at 700 users is genuine early revenue, which I respect. But my first question isn't "does it work?" It's "if this works perfectly, does the world change?" And the answer is no. If CatchBack Cards achieves total victory, collectors have a more trustworthy way to buy mystery packs. That's a business. It might even be a good business. But it's not a problem whose consequence of success justifies the kind of bet I make. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. No trillion-dollar system collapses. No incumbent industry becomes structurally obsolete. The trading card market is real -- $13 billion, growing -- but CatchBack isn't trying to reinvent what a trading card is or what collecting means. They're adding a trust layer and a better UX to an existing format that Fanatics, Whatnot, and eBay already serve at massive scale.

The crypto and blockchain framing actually works against this company in my evaluation, and I think it reveals a strategic confusion. The Nexus Layer 1 partnership, the NFT tags, the "onchain collecting" podcast appearance -- these signal a bet on Web3 infrastructure at a moment when consumer sentiment toward crypto has cratered since 2022. Meanwhile, the actual product -- physical cards shipped to your door, Venmo buybacks, a $1 marketplace -- is a straightforward consumer commerce platform. The cryptographic randomness verification is a clever feature, genuinely useful for trust, but you don't need a blockchain partnership to generate verifiable random numbers. The technology here is a feature bolted onto a marketplace, not a breakthrough that makes the incumbent approach economically unviable. Whatnot could add cryptographic pack verification in a quarter. Fanatics already runs "Under Wraps" mystery boxes with official league licensing that CatchBack can't match. When your differentiation is a feature that a $11.5 billion competitor could replicate as a sprint, you don't have disruption -- you have a head start measured in months.

Joseph Zhang has the credentials of someone who should be working on a harder problem. Gates Scholar, ISEF finalist, AWS engineer, McKinsey consultant, blockchain infrastructure experience at Nexus -- that's a profile capable of attacking something that genuinely terrifies the establishment. Instead, he quit his job to sell Pokémon cards. I don't doubt his passion -- the personal collecting experience is authentic. But passion for a hobby is not the same as the "irreverence, foolish confidence, and naivety" I look for in founders attempting what experts declare impossible. Nobody declared this impossible. Nobody is threatened by it. The Steph Curry collaboration is nice distribution, but it's a marketing partnership, not evidence of contrarian thinking. And Sophia Kim is essentially invisible -- no documented background, no public presence. In a two-person company, I need to understand both founders, and here I can only evaluate half the team.

The strongest bull case would be this: CatchBack becomes the trust infrastructure for all collectibles commerce, not just mystery packs. The cryptographic verification layer expands to authenticate any transaction. The card-backed loans and peer lending become a fintech play for alternative assets -- turning trading cards into a financial instrument class with CatchBack as the rails. That's a bigger vision. If the lending features scale, you're looking at something closer to what Alt is building ($106M raised, $325M valuation) but with a consumer-first flywheel. The $13K MRR proves people will pay, and if that's growing aggressively week-over-week, the trajectory matters. But even this bull case describes a successful vertical marketplace with financial services attached -- not the kind of systemic transformation where success means the existing approach becomes obsolete. Square didn't make payment terminals 20% better; it enabled millions of businesses that couldn't accept cards at all. That's the difference between a feature improvement and an economic paradigm shift.

I pass on companies like this regularly. The founders are smart, the market is real, the product works. And none of that matters if the consequence of full success is capturing a few points of share in a $13 billion market that will continue operating essentially the same way regardless of CatchBack's existence. These founders are too capable for a problem this contained. Go find something where the experts tell you it's impossible.
