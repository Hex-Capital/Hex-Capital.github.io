# TakeCareOS -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Ragav Sachdeva is an Oxford VGG PhD candidate under Andrew Zisserman building demo-gated B2B software for licensed home-care agencies — that single sentence captures TakeCareOS's analytical signature in my portfolio: high on AI specific knowledge, structurally backwards on the leverage axis I read first. TakeCareOS is an AI researcher selling permissioned enterprise software into NDIS- and HIPAA-adjacent compliance plumbing, which is the inverse of the Twitter/Stack Overflow/Replit pattern I keep funding. Six agencies signed and 200+ employees onboarded is real pull, but the *shape* of the pull is coordinator-replacement sold via founder-led demos, not code or media compounding without gatekeepers.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung of the leverage ladder — media-native, anyone can post, marginal cost of replication is zero, and the platform charges nothing at the door. TakeCareOS diverges from that mechanism at every point of contact: takecareos.com gates a 60-minute demo behind a "Talk to us" CTA, the closed customers are licensed agencies that must pass state Medicaid or NDIS compliance plumbing, and the billing surface integrates Xero, QuickBooks, and MYOB — all of which are permissioned enterprise integrations rather than permissionless distribution surfaces. The TakeCareOS go-to-market is direct sales by a solo founder, which is labor leverage requiring permission from each individual agency buyer, not the code or media leverage that drove the **Twitter** (2007) and **Stack Overflow** (2008) outcomes. This is the dimension where TakeCareOS is structurally furthest from anything I have written a check into.

Pinterest is the LOW rung I want to invoke on network effects, because Pinterest at least had a real network compounding — and I still passed and was wrong. TakeCareOS does not even have that problem in the bull direction: it is single-sided B2B SaaS where each home-care agency on the platform is economically independent of the next, so the Nth agency does not make the (N-1) prior agencies more valuable. The Pinterest miss was a pattern-bias failure on a network that was compounding; the TakeCareOS profile is the cleaner pass case where no network exists to misread. The 200+ employees number is a seat-count inside six unrelated agencies, not a graph, and the AlayaCare / HHAeXchange incumbents have not lost share because of network gravity — they have lost share, where they have, because of workflow ergonomics. That is a feature war, not the asymmetric-user-value mechanic that underwrites my 10,000x upside math.

Ragav Sachdeva's specific knowledge cuts on two axes and lands split. **Perplexity** (2022) is the single anchor I want here: Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley ML PhD in retrieval was non-replicable AI specific knowledge applied to an AI-native product, and TakeCareOS's founder has the analogous AI credential — Oxford VGG, Zisserman supervision, NVIDIA PhD intern from May 2025, ML engineer at Shaped (YC). What TakeCareOS does *not* have is any documented home-care, NDIS, HIPAA, or EVV operations history in the public record, and the chosen vertical is one where the specific knowledge that compounds is regulatory-workflow knowledge held by ex-coordinators and ex-agency-owners, not by computer-vision PhDs. The founder-product-market fit is real on the AI substrate and absent on the domain substrate, and in regulated care the domain leg is the load-bearing one.

The home-healthcare software market at USD 9.27B by 2031 (Mordor Intelligence) is not small in absolute terms, but the pivot mechanics matter more than the headline TAM — and 18 months of building EVV plumbing, NDIS compliance documents, and state-Medicaid billing integration is a sunk investment that does not transfer to an adjacent pivot if the first idea does not work. If TakeCareOS discovers in month nine that AI-agent coordination cannot clear care-documentation audit thresholds, the company cannot redirect that compliance investment into a horizontal agent platform without writing off most of the surface area. This is the Venture Hacks "niche markets don't work because the first idea never works" failure mode in its purest form, sharpened by the geographic ambiguity between MYOB (Australia/NZ) and the San Francisco YC posting.

The Buffett trio reads clean on TakeCareOS — no contested exits, no self-promotion about ethics, no partner-litigation pattern, and the academic record under Zisserman is exactly the depth-rather-than-promotion signal I want. What is missing is the second human: TakeCareOS is a team of one going into a regulated category where "Picking a co-founder is your most important decision" (Venture Hacks) means the most important decision is still pending. The 531 X followers and 35 GitHub followers tell me Ragav has not been compounding media leverage on the founder identity for years the way Amjad Masad did before **Replit** — he is a researcher who started a company, not a builder-in-public who accumulated audience first.

Thirty-six month forecast: TakeCareOS reaches $300K–$1.5M ARR confined to Australia/NZ disability-care agencies via the MYOB-integrated NDIS wedge and is either acquired by **ShiftCare** or AlayaCare in a sub-$25M tuck-in, or pivots out of vertical care software entirely into a horizontal "AI agents for regulated workflows" platform that competes with Sierra and Decagon. The mechanism is that the AI specific knowledge is real enough to ship a coordinator-replacement demo, but the leverage architecture is wrong enough that direct-sales velocity caps at the rate a solo founder can run agency demos before headcount has to scale into a labor-leverage SaaS company.

The strongest bear-case-of-my-pass is the **Perplexity** (2022) precedent: I invested into a single-sided product, the Known Exception logic I wrote into my own framework says when criterion #1 and criterion #3 and category-timing all clear at top quartile I waive criterion #2, and TakeCareOS arguably hits AI-cycle timing with LLM tool-use crossing usability threshold in 2024–2025. The dismantling is mechanical: the Perplexity exception required criterion #1 to clear at *top quartile* via free consumer web distribution and API permissionless reach, and TakeCareOS's criterion #1 fails at *bottom quartile* via demo-gated agency sales — the exception does not extend to a B2B vertical that has flipped the leverage architecture from permissionless to permissioned. I would flip my verdict on one specific verifiable condition: TakeCareOS open-sources an agent framework for care-coordination workflows that accumulates organic GitHub adoption (1,000+ stars, inbound contributors not solicited) and at least three of the six pilot agencies arrived via inbound rather than founder outreach — that would be the **Stack Overflow** (2008) developer-pull signature, retrofitted into healthcare, and it is the version of TakeCareOS I would write a check into.
