# Standout -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what stopped me before I even got to the founders: Placement. Founders Fund — my old firm — backed the exact same thesis in 2019. "Talent agent for tech." ISA pricing, candidate-side loyalty, the whole pitch. It didn't scale. It got quietly absorbed into Exec. And now Clera is running a near-identical model to Standout with 60,000 users and a head start. When I see the same structural thesis attempted repeatedly without producing an enduring company, that's not market validation — that's a warning about the shape of the problem itself. The recruiting system is genuinely misaligned against candidates, but the reason nobody has fixed it from the candidate side isn't that nobody tried. It's that the person with the checkbook is the employer, and the moment you monetize through employer placement fees, you've become a recruiter with a different pitch deck. The Hollywood agent analogy breaks down because talent agents in entertainment take a percentage of the talent's earnings — a model that tech recruiting has tried (Placement's ISA) and that collapsed under its own unit economics.

When I ask "tell me their story," what I get here is two capable operators who spotted a gap. Aftalion was a VC at Alven, then COO at Zealy where he helped scale to 1.5 million monthly users — genuinely impressive consumer-growth muscle. De La Chapelle engineered at Dropbox, Samsara, and Chime, and built a job board that reached 100K MAU. They clearly know how to build and ship products. But knowing how to build products is not the same as being forged by a problem. Nothing in either biography suggests that the recruiting system broke them personally, that they were invisible to the warm-intro network, that they experienced the powerlessness of navigating opaque hiring systems from the outside. This reads as two talented people who saw a market inefficiency from a position of strength — VC networks, elite engineering roles, French tech ecosystem connections — and decided to solve it. That's career optimization, not biographical inevitability. I've learned to distinguish between the two because the founders who were forged by their problem keep building through the valley of death, and the ones who identified a market gap pivot when the unit economics get hard.

The strongest bull case is real and I want to name it clearly: if Standout can build a curated roster of genuinely elite talent — and the "8 Best Operators" list featuring engineers from OpenAI's Sora team and Anthropic signals early quality — they could create a brand-driven network effect that employers pay premium access to reach. The Hollywood agent model works when the agent controls scarce, differentiated supply. If Standout becomes the place where the best 500 startup operators are represented, employers will pay whatever it takes. The combination of Aftalion's consumer-growth skills and de La Chapelle's domain experience in job platforms is directly relevant. And the AI timing is genuinely new — LLMs make it possible to run deep discovery conversations and nuanced matching at a scale that human talent agents never could. The 35% stat (users reaching first interviews within four days) is a compelling early signal if it holds. What would have to be true for this to be a great investment: they'd need to win the supply-side curation war definitively against Clera, build a talent brand so strong that being "Standout-represented" becomes a credential, and find a monetization model that survives the structural tension between candidate loyalty and employer payment.

There is a real invisible system here — I want to give that its due. Recruiters represent employers while telling candidates they're there to help. The warm-intro network that determines who gets startup jobs is invisible to anyone outside it. Information about which companies are actually good to work for, which roles are real versus performative postings, which founders treat their teams well — all of that is opaque and gatekept. That resonates with me. But the opacity is moderate, not extreme. Candidates broadly understand that recruiters serve employers. The access gap is real but it's not the kind of deep, extractive, hidden structure that animates my strongest convictions — it's not the taxi medallion cartel or credit card opacity where ordinary people are being systematically exploited without knowing it. It's more like an inconvenience concentrated among people who already have valuable skills and relatively strong bargaining positions.

The defensibility question is where this falls apart for me. The dossier itself notes that the AI-agent-on-WhatsApp mechanism is technically reproducible, and Clera's existence proves it. WhatsApp platform dependency is a structural risk — one API pricing change from Meta and your delivery channel is repriced or restricted. The candidate data from discovery calls is a nascent moat, but it's slow to compound and any competitor doing similar intake conversations builds the same thing. When I look at my biggest wins — Flexport's logistics network, Carta's cap table switching costs, Flock Safety's camera infrastructure — each new customer made the product structurally harder to displace. I don't see that compounding mechanism here. A well-funded competitor could rebuild the candidate experience in a quarter. The moat, if it exists, will be pure brand — and brand moats in recruiting are notoriously fragile.

I wish the founders well. The problem is real, the timing around AI capabilities is legitimate, and there's genuine competence on this team. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. There's no biographical forge driving the founders through the inevitable near-death moments, no contrarian space that makes investors squirm, no deep infrastructure being built beneath a weird surface. It's a smart product in a competitive market with unclear monetization and a predecessor from my own portfolio that didn't make it.
