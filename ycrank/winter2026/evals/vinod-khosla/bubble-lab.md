# Bubble Lab -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with the question I always start with: if Bubble Lab succeeds beyond anyone's expectations, what happens? Operations teams at mid-market companies save ten hours a week on CRM updates and Jira tickets. Their own customer testimonial quantifies the ceiling — Dyna Robotics reports "saving our Ops team 10+ hours per week." That's the dream case. Nobody's food system changes. No trillion-dollar industry becomes obsolete. No physical reality transforms. The world gets marginally more efficient Slack channels. I tell founders like this — and I mean it kindly — you are too talented to spend your careers making Jira tickets file themselves.

This is the archetype of what I avoid: smart engineers who have narrowed their ambition until the probability of failure is low and the consequence of success is negligible. An AI agent that lives in Slack and connects to 25 integrations is a perfectly reasonable product. It's also a product that n8n (with $253M raised and $40M ARR), Gumloop ($24.5M), Dust ($21.5M), and Salesforce itself are all building simultaneously. The dossier states it plainly: "The core capability — an LLM-powered agent that calls APIs on behalf of users — is technically reproducible." When the core technology is reproducible, you're not investing in technology disruption. You're investing in execution speed in a market where your competitors have a hundred times your capital. That's a race I don't fund.

The platform dependency here is not a risk factor to be mitigated — it's a structural contradiction at the heart of the business. Bubble Lab's entire value proposition is Slack-native. Salesforce owns Slack and has explicitly announced Agentforce 360 to make Slack an "agentic OS." When I backed Juniper against Cisco, we were building our own routers — hardware that Cisco couldn't simply absorb. Bubble Lab is building a feature inside Salesforce's living room and hoping Salesforce doesn't notice. The dossier's inference that Salesforce might leave pricing space for a freemium agent is hopeful thinking. Salesforce's incentive is to make AI the reason enterprises upgrade to Business+ and Enterprise+ plans, which means eventually covering exactly the use cases Bubble Lab serves. The open-source engine provides an escape hatch, but 1.1k GitHub stars on an Apache 2.0 repo doesn't create defensibility — it creates a blueprint for self-hosting.

The strongest bull case I can construct: AI agents in the workplace are on a genuine exponential curve (46.3% CAGR for the agent market), and the team that nails conversational UX with long-term memory inside the tool people already live in — Slack — could achieve viral product-led growth fast enough to build switching costs before Salesforce's native AI matures. The 4,000 users and named customers at pre-seed are a real signal of product-market resonance. The founders showed pivot instincts moving from consumer (gymii.ai) to B2B, and Selina Li's quant background at JPMorgan suggests comfort with operations-heavy environments. If Pearl's personalization layer — learning user preferences and workflows over months — creates genuine stickiness, the freemium-to-enterprise path at $30-$100/month could compound. For this to work, Bubble Lab would need to become the de facto ops agent for thousands of Slack workspaces before the market consolidates. It's possible. But even in that best case, you're looking at a solid SaaS business, not an industry transformation. And I don't write checks for solid SaaS businesses.

The founders demonstrate competence — Cornell Tech, a prior startup that won awards, the ability to ship a product with 25+ integrations and SOC 2 certification as a two-person team. But nothing about this company is contrarian. Building an AI agent for workflow automation in 2025-2026 is the most consensus bet in technology. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, people thought AI was laughable. When I backed Impossible Foods, people thought plant-based meat was a joke. Nobody laughs at "AI agent for ops teams" — which means the outsider premium isn't there, and the space is already crowded with better-funded competitors who arrived at the same insight independently. The team is also homogeneous for the problem they're solving: two recent CS graduates targeting BizOps and RevOps teams, with no documented experience in those roles and no GTM hire for a B2B product. The gene pool doesn't address the venture's most critical risk — enterprise sales and ops credibility.

Pass. The consequence of success doesn't justify the attempt by my framework. This is a capable team building an unremarkable product in a crowded market on top of a platform whose owner is building the same thing. The money would be better deployed on a problem that terrifies people and matters.
