# Eos AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Eos AI: the competitive landscape tells you everything about the consequence of success. Commure has raised $820 million and is valued at $6 billion. Redox has raised $98 million and generated $181 million in revenue last year. Google, Microsoft, and AWS all have healthcare data harmonization products. Epic controls 38% of US hospital EHRs and is expanding its own interoperability stack. When I see a market where every major incumbent and a half-dozen well-capitalized startups are already converging, I know something important: the experts and insiders all agree this problem needs solving. And when the experts agree, the magnitude of disruption available to a new entrant shrinks dramatically. My best investments — Juniper, Impossible Foods, OpenAI — were in spaces where the established players actively dismissed the approach. Nobody is dismissing healthcare data harmonization. They're all building it.

The TAM here tells the story the founders probably don't want told. Healthcare data integration: $1 billion today, growing to $3 billion by 2030. Even if Eos captures a meaningful share of that, the consequence of success is a solid infrastructure business, not an industry transformation. The founders might argue that the real consequence is enabling clinical AI at scale — that harmonized data is the prerequisite for the AI revolution in healthcare. I've heard this argument many times. It's the picks-and-shovels thesis, and it has a fundamental problem: picks-and-shovels companies capture a fraction of the value they enable. If AI transforms healthcare into a $10 trillion opportunity, the data plumbing layer captures maybe 1-2% of that value, and splits it with a dozen competitors. The world-changing outcome accrues to whoever builds the clinical AI models on top, not whoever cleaned the data underneath. Eos is building a better pipe, not a new water source.

Arya Khokhar has exactly the profile you'd expect for this company: Caltech CS and Math, Stanford research, published work on physics-aware neural operators for medical imaging, a prior cancer triage AI project piloted at Stanford Medicine. It's a strong résumé. But it's a predictable one. This is an insider building what insiders agree needs building. The ONCOVATE project — an AI tool for cancer patient triage — evolved into a data harmonization platform, which is the natural, logical progression someone with this background would follow. I don't see the contrarian leap. I don't see someone attempting what the established community considers foolish. Where's the naivety combined with persistence that I look for? Where's the thesis that makes people look at you funny? "Healthcare data should be harmonized and ML-ready" makes nobody look at you funny. Everybody nods.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If clinical AI is about to cross a capability threshold — and I believe it is — then the bottleneck shifts from model quality to data quality. The company that solves federated data harmonization with clinical-grade fidelity across imaging, EHR text, labs, and billing becomes the foundation layer for every AI application in healthcare. The VERA engine for imaging harmonization and LUCIA for clinical text normalization represent domain-specific technical depth that generic cloud providers haven't matched. The federated architecture — keeping data in place while building a unified index — is architecturally elegant for healthcare's regulatory constraints. And interoperability mandates like HTI-1 and TEFCA are creating a regulatory tailwind that didn't exist two years ago. If Arya can build this into the standard infrastructure layer before Epic or Google closes the gap, the switching costs become formidable. That's a real scenario. But it requires winning an enterprise sales war against companies with hundreds of millions in capital, with a two-person team and no visible revenue. The timing is good but not contrarian — it's consensus timing, which means the window is contested by everyone who sees the same opportunity.

The technical approach — federated indexing, imaging distribution alignment, clinical text normalization — is genuinely sophisticated. The 18% model performance improvement claim through imaging harmonization, if validated, would be meaningful. But I keep coming back to the distinction between disruptive and incremental. Does Eos make the existing healthcare system obsolete, or does it make it work better? It clearly does the latter. The hospitals still use Epic. The radiologists still read scans. The billing systems still process claims. Everything continues as before, just with cleaner data flowing between systems. That's a 3x productivity improvement and a 37% revenue recovery — their own claimed metrics describe an optimization, not a transformation. Compare this to what I look for: Impossible Foods didn't make cattle ranching more efficient, it aimed to replace it. Juniper didn't help Cisco's routers interoperate, it made them architecturally obsolete. Eos makes Epic's data more accessible. That's a feature Epic could ship, not a paradigm it needs to fear.

I'll pass on this one. The problem is real, the founder is technically capable, and the timing is reasonable. But the consequence of success is a well-run infrastructure business in a contested market — not a transformation that justifies my tolerance for 90% failure rates. This team is too talented to spend their careers making hospital IT systems talk to each other 30% better. The magnitude of the outcome, even in the best case, doesn't match the magnitude of the competition they'll face getting there.
