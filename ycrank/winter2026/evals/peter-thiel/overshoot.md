# Overshoot -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells the story before anything else. Fireworks AI has raised $331 million. Together AI has raised $554 million. Groq has its own inference hardware. Roboflow has $63.6 million and a developer community. Clarifai has $101 million and enterprise relationships. Into this arena step two cousins with a pre-seed check and the thesis that video inference is different enough from text inference to justify a standalone platform. I count at least five well-funded players who could add video-specific optimizations the moment the market justifies the engineering investment. This is not a Blue Ocean -- it is a Girardian contest of imitation, where multiple companies converge on the same insight (multimodal AI needs optimized serving) and will destroy each other's margins through competitive mimicry. The fundamental structure of this market -- serving open-source models that anyone can access, competing on latency benchmarks that shift quarterly -- is hostile to monopoly formation.

What is the secret here? The implicit claim is that video and image modalities are so fundamentally different from text that they require a specialized inference platform, and that general-purpose providers will never adequately serve this need. But this is not a truth that most intelligent people reject -- it is an optimization thesis that every infrastructure engineer in the AI space would nod along with. The distinction between modalities is real, but it is an engineering observation, not a contrarian insight about how the world works. When Stripe built developer-first payments, the secret was not "payments integration is hard" -- everyone knew that. The secret was that by making it trivially easy, you could become the default financial infrastructure layer and lock in developers through compounding integrations. Overshoot's "3 lines of code" echoes the Stripe developer experience playbook, but without Stripe's structural moats: money transmission licenses, bank relationships, regulatory compliance layers that took years to build. Overshoot's underlying models are open-source commodities. Its optimization layer, however sophisticated, is replicable by any team with GPU infrastructure expertise -- and several such teams already exist with hundred-million-dollar war chests.

The founders deserve serious consideration independent of the market structure. Zakaria spent seven years at Uber building surge pricing -- systems that demand sub-second latency at massive scale -- then wrote GPU kernels for inference engines at Meta. Younes built a computer vision platform from scratch at Cosmonio before Intel acquired the company. These are not "men in suits" -- they are engineers who have built the exact type of system they are now productizing. The cousin relationship and complementary expertise (Zakaria on latency and inference, Younes on CV platform engineering) creates genuine founder-market fit. If I were evaluating the founders in isolation, I would see a pair capable of building something significant. But founder quality cannot overcome market structure. The most talented team in a competitive market with no path to monopoly will generate returns that look nothing like the power law.

The bull case requires believing that Overshoot can become the Stripe of video AI before incumbents wake up -- that developer mindshare, once captured, creates sufficient switching costs to sustain the business. If every robotics company, every security camera system, every AR application defaults to Overshoot's API the way web companies default to Stripe, the 300 developers today could become 300,000, and the ecosystem effects (documentation, community packages, integration partners) could create meaningful stickiness. The "why now" argument has merit: VLMs have reached a capability threshold in 2024-2025 that makes real-time video understanding newly feasible, and the first platform to nail the developer experience during this window could establish itself before the general-purpose providers prioritize this niche. But this argument assumes that a two-person team with pre-seed funding can build and defend a developer ecosystem against companies with 100x their capital. The window of opportunity for specialization typically closes once the market grows large enough to attract serious attention from incumbents -- and the computer vision AI market is projected to reach $330 billion by 2034, which means incumbents will invest heavily.

The sub-200ms latency claim -- described as "10x faster than existing inference platforms" -- represents a real engineering achievement if accurate, but it is a quantitative improvement along an existing dimension, not a qualitative discontinuity. A 10x speed improvement is meaningful for developers, but latency benchmarks are a moving target. What is 10x faster today may be 2x faster in eighteen months as competitors optimize their stacks. SpaceX achieved 10x cost reduction in rocket launches through reusable hardware -- a physical engineering breakthrough that competitors could not replicate for over a decade. Overshoot's latency advantage is a software optimization that any well-resourced team can pursue. The durability of this advantage is measured in quarters, not decades.

I see competent founders building a useful product in a growing market -- and nothing about that sentence describes a company I would invest in. The absence of a genuine secret, the commoditization of the underlying models, the swarm of well-funded competitors, and the lack of any structural monopoly mechanism all point in the same direction. This is a "1 to n" company: taking existing VLMs, serving them faster for a specific modality, and hoping to capture developers before incumbents optimize. The world does not need another inference API competing on benchmarks. It needs companies built on truths that the consensus rejects. I do not see that truth here.
