# Remy AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately about Remy AI: this is a two-person team walking into a space where Nimble has raised $221 million, Covariant has $222 million, Dexterity is valued at $1.65 billion, and Physical Intelligence has crossed a billion in funding. Warehouse robotics in 2026 is not an unpopular idea -- it's one of the most actively funded categories in all of deep tech. Every major VC in the Valley has a warehouse robotics bet already placed. When I look at my best investments, the defining characteristic was that nobody else wanted to fund them. SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe -- these were companies that made other investors uncomfortable. Remy AI is entering a category that makes other investors very comfortable, and that's precisely what gives me pause.

I always want to hear the founder's story before I hear the business. Tell me how you got here -- not the pitch, the biography. And when I look at Oscar and Ben, I see two genuinely talented people from Oxford. Oscar advised Fortune 500 logistics companies at BCG on the exact problem Remy is solving. Ben has a CVPR 2025 publication from the Visual Geometry Group on 3D reconstruction -- directly relevant technical work. These are strong credentials. But credentials and lived experience are different things. The founders who stick with me, the ones I write my biggest checks for, are the ones whose commitment to their problem was forged through something personal and difficult. I backed Contraline because the founder had spent years in academic isolation pursuing male contraception when everyone told him it was impossible. When I invested in Uber, I'd spent years talking with my husband about the medallion system and watching how it failed real people. I'm looking for that biographical thread connecting the founder to the problem in a way that will keep them going when everything falls apart, and I don't see it here. Oscar identified a smart market gap through consulting analysis. Ben has exceptional research skills. But who on this team has loaded a truck at 4 AM, or watched a warehouse floor struggle with turnover so painful it threatened the business? The connection here is intellectual, not embodied.

The strongest argument for Remy is the brownfield angle -- targeting the 80% of warehouses with zero automation because existing solutions require facility redesign and million-dollar capital commitments. That's a genuine insight about market structure, and it echoes a pattern I've seen work before. Affirm didn't compete with premium credit cards -- it made transparent credit available to people who couldn't get it before. If Remy can genuinely deploy robots into existing workstations in days at 50% lower cost, they're not competing with Nimble and Dexterity so much as serving a completely different customer base. The claim of a data-efficient training pipeline that learns new tasks with less data than standard VLA fine-tuning is interesting -- if real, each deployment enriches the model, creating the kind of compounding advantage that could matter. Ben's CVPR publication suggests the technical capability to build something differentiated. For this to work as a great investment, you'd need to believe that the VLA space won't fully commoditize in the next 18 months, that the brownfield hardware form factor creates real switching costs, and that two people from Oxford can out-execute teams with hundred-million-dollar war chests by being faster and scrappier in the low end of the market. It's a credible innovator's-dilemma thesis. I just don't feel the jolt.

There's also a tension in the economic access dimension that I notice through my lens. When I backed Uber, it created income for drivers -- people who previously couldn't participate in the transportation economy now could. Postmates did the same for delivery workers. Remy AI automates the work that warehouse laborers currently do. The economic access runs toward warehouse operators who can now afford automation, not toward workers. In a sector where churn hovers near 100% and the work breaks bodies, there's a legitimate argument that automation is humane. But it's not the same positive-sum economic story I gravitate toward. The people whose lives this changes most directly are losing their jobs, not gaining new ones.

On co-founder dynamics, I know both Oscar and Ben attended Oxford, which gives them shared context and presumably a pre-existing relationship. But I have no visibility into how long they've worked together, how they resolve disagreements, or whether they've been through a real crisis as partners. After HQ Trivia, I probe these dynamics hard. A shared alma mater is a starting point, not proof of resilience. Nothing in the public record tells me whether this partnership has been stress-tested.

The honest assessment is that Remy AI is a well-constructed pre-seed thesis in a hot market, built by credentialed founders who identified the opportunity through professional analysis rather than personal necessity. It's the kind of company that will attract institutional interest because the market sizing is impressive and the technical credentials are legitimate. But it doesn't match how I invest. I'm looking for the magically weird, for the founders who were told no fifty times and kept building anyway, for the ideas that make most investors squirm. Warehouse robotics at a lower price point is a sensible bet, not a weird one. I'd wish Oscar and Ben well, follow their progress, and perhaps revisit if they hit an inflection point that reveals something I'm not seeing today.
