# Cofia -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out about Cofia isn't the founders or the product -- it's that this company is walking into a fight that's already happening. Bardeen shipped a "Work Intelligence Platform" in May 2025 with the exact same pitch: AI agents that learn how work actually happens and then automate it. They have $22-25 million in funding and distribution partnerships with Dropbox and HubSpot. And behind Bardeen sits the real gorilla -- Zapier, doing $310 million in revenue with over 100,000 customers, actively investing in AI features. When I look at the companies I've backed that turned into something extraordinary, they shared a characteristic: they were not popular, they were difficult to assess. Cofia is entering the most popular category in SaaS right now -- AI-powered productivity automation -- with a value proposition that a funded competitor has already taken to market. That's the opposite of the signal I trust.

I always start with the founder's story. Tell me how you got here -- not the business, the person. And here I'm reading two impressive resumes but I'm not finding the thread. Paola has Stanford, the Mayfield Fellowship, d.school, product management at Brilliant.org where she led retention. Moses rose to Senior Staff Software Engineer at Duolingo, which is a genuinely hard rank to achieve -- that's a person who can build serious technical systems. These are strong credentials. But credentials aren't what I evaluate. I'm looking for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem. Why workflow automation? What happened in their lives that made them see this particular pain so clearly that they couldn't do anything else? The dossier shows two people who came from education-tech companies and pivoted to enterprise automation tooling. That pattern -- smart people chasing a hot market without a biographical anchor to the problem -- is one of the things I've learned to be cautious about.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The "zero-config" automation paradigm -- where the system watches you work and surfaces automations you never knew you needed -- is a genuinely different UX model from Zapier's "build your own Zap" approach. If Cofia nails this, they could be the product that brings automation to the 95% of knowledge workers who never built a Zap because the setup cost was too high. Moses's depth as a Senior Staff engineer at a company like Duolingo means the technical execution capability is real. Paola's retention background at Brilliant.org is directly relevant -- a product that proactively suggests automations lives or dies on whether those suggestions feel useful rather than intrusive. And there's a credible innovator's dilemma argument: Zapier's entire business model is built around users manually configuring automations with usage-based pricing. Proactive automation that eliminates that manual step could cannibalize their core engagement loop. That's the kind of structural advantage that sometimes lets small teams outmaneuver incumbents. If I could hear these founders tell me a story about a moment when they viscerally felt the pain of repetitive work -- if there were a personal origin that made this feel inevitable rather than opportunistic -- this would be a different conversation.

But I can't get past several things. The website at cofia.ai has no product content -- just analytics tracking code. The two founders have no documented shared history: different universities, different employers, different cities of origin. How did they find each other, and how do they fight? After HQ Trivia, I never invest without understanding co-founder dynamics, and here I have nothing to work with. The company doesn't create economic access for underserved people or make an invisible system transparent -- it saves time for knowledge workers who already have good jobs using SaaS tools. That's a real business, but it's not the kind of company that gets me out of bed. There's no regulatory complexity creating a moat, no stigma deterring other investors, no weirdness that makes people uncomfortable. This is a well-executed entry into a crowded, consensus category by two credentialed operators who don't appear to have a personal stake in this specific problem. I wish them well, but this isn't my kind of bet.
