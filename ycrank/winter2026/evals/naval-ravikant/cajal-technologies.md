# Cajal -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see is a gap between the domain and the founders' documented knowledge. Formal verification at scale -- autonomous proof discovery in Lean, dependent type theory, proof automation for applied mathematics -- requires a depth of mathematical understanding that takes years of immersion to develop. It's the kind of knowledge you can't acquire through adjacent expertise. Luke Johnston's background is computational neuroscience and meta-reinforcement learning at Cambridge's CBL. Pedro Nobre's is general computer science from Spanish universities and a hackathon win. Neither has a public track record in formal methods, theorem proving, or contributions to Lean, Coq, or Isabelle. The dossier says it plainly: "neither founder has documented prior experience specifically in formal verification or theorem proving from public sources." When I invested in Stack Overflow, it was built by developers who had lived inside the problem of developer knowledge-sharing for years. When I backed Replit, Amjad had been building coding tools obsessively. Here, I'm being asked to believe that two early-career researchers pivoted into one of the most mathematically demanding subfields in computer science and can out-execute Harmonic AI, which has $295M and is working in the same proof assistant ecosystem.

The bull case deserves a real hearing. If you reframe the problem, the specific knowledge argument shifts: maybe the bottleneck in formal verification isn't mathematical maturity but search architecture. Lean's type-checking kernel handles correctness; what you need is a better way to explore the proof space, and that's fundamentally a reinforcement learning problem. Luke's meta-RL work could be precisely the non-obvious skill that makes autonomous proof agents work -- not knowing the math, but knowing how to build agents that navigate mathematical structure efficiently. If this framing is correct, then the specific knowledge is in the RL architecture, not in the mathematics, and Cajal's Cambridge ML pedigree becomes a genuine asset rather than an adjacent credential. Additionally, the applied focus -- quantum computing, finance -- is a real niche. Harmonic is chasing olympiad gold medals, which is impressive for benchmarks but arguably orthogonal to commercial value. Certora is locked into smart contracts. Nobody is productizing formal verification for the applied sciences that actually generate enterprise revenue. If Cajal owns that wedge, the market could be theirs to define.

The leverage architecture is where this company gets interesting despite the founder concerns. If Tau works as described, it's a genuine new form of leverage: one multi-agent system autonomously discovering and formalizing proofs that previously required small teams of expert mathematicians working for years. That's code leverage applied to mathematical reasoning itself -- the compression ratio is enormous. The ancillary products (verified training datasets, RL environments for proof assistants) are a picks-and-shovels play for AI labs building mathematical reasoning capabilities. This maps directly onto my infrastructure preference: sell the tools that everyone building in this space needs, regardless of which approach to AI-math wins. One engineer's work on verified corpora serves every frontier lab training on formal mathematics. That's the kind of leverage ratio I look for.

But leverage architecture without defensible specific knowledge is a blueprint, not a moat. The timing here reads as warm consensus rather than genuine contrarianism. AI for mathematical reasoning is a category that Harmonic just raised $295M into at a $1.45B valuation. DeepMind has been publishing on this. LeanDojo from Caltech/NVIDIA/MIT provides open-source infrastructure that lowers the barrier for any well-resourced team to enter. Two years ago, this would have been contrarian. Today, it's an emerging category with serious capital flowing in. The applied-science angle provides some differentiation, but differentiation is not contrarianism -- it's positioning within a consensus trend. And the Entrepreneurs First pipeline origin raises a question I always ask: are these founders obsessed with formal verification, or did they identify it as a promising category during a residency designed to match people with startup ideas? The distinction between genuine domain obsession and pattern-matched opportunity-seeking matters enormously at this stage, because when Harmonic starts expanding into applied verticals -- and with $295M, they will -- the only thing that protects you is knowledge so deep it can't be replicated by a larger team with more resources.

The compounding potential is real in theory. Each proof discovered and verified by Tau adds to a proprietary corpus that makes the system better at discovering the next proof. Over time, this creates a data flywheel that's hard to replicate -- you can't shortcut verified mathematical knowledge. But Lean is open source, and the academic community is actively building competing tooling. The barrier is engineering effort, which is substantial but not insurmountable for a $295M-funded competitor. The technology compounds, but the moat around it has gaps.

I keep coming back to the central asymmetry: the vision is genuinely compelling, but the founders haven't yet demonstrated they possess the non-transferable knowledge this particular vision demands. A great leverage architecture built on borrowed expertise is fragile. I'd want to see evidence that Luke or Pedro has spent meaningful time inside Lean, has contributed to proof automation, or has formalized non-trivial mathematical results before I'd write a check. Without that evidence, this reads as a smart bet on a trend rather than a founder-problem match that only these specific people could execute.
