# Korso -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Paperless Parts has raised $45-51 million. Leverage AI has raised $14.9 million from 26 investors. Turian in Berlin has 17 employees building agentic AI for procurement. Epicor has already shipped Prism, a native AI agent for RFQ automation inside its own ERP. And now Korso arrives with the thesis that "the great inefficiency of modern manufacturing is not on the shop floor, it's in the office." I ask the canonical question: what important truth do very few people agree with Korso on? The answer, evidently, is one that at least four funded competitors and a major incumbent already share. This is not a secret. This is a consensus dressed in the language of insight. When the dossier itself reports that 80% of CPOs name AI a top priority, you are not discovering a hidden truth — you are joining a parade.

Korso's competitive landscape is the most damning exhibit in this dossier, and it maps precisely to the cleantech pattern I warned about in *Zero to One*: entrepreneurs swept up by a category-level wave rather than anchored to a specific non-obvious thesis about the world. The core capability — LLM-based document parsing, email triage, ERP API integration — uses generally available AI infrastructure. Korso differentiates on "long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing," which is an architectural choice, not a proprietary technology. Any competent engineering team with access to frontier models can build stateful workflow orchestration. The question is not whether Korso can build the product — it is whether building the product creates a monopoly. When I backed Palantir, the secret was that intelligence agencies needed commercial software for analytical workflows, a thesis the intelligence community actively rejected and no startup was pursuing. Korso operates in a market where every enterprise software company is pursuing the identical thesis simultaneously.

Martin Pan's General Motors experience gives Korso some domain credibility, and the shared prehistory between Pan and Daichi Hiraoka at Penn Electric Racing is a modest positive — co-founders who have built under pressure together before starting a company are better positioned than strangers from an accelerator matching service. But the founding team has no prior startup experience, no enterprise sales background, and no evidence of the kind of irreversible commitment that signals definite conviction. The dossier describes Korso's founders "traveling across Asia, talking directly to clients and fine-tuning products" — this is the language of customer discovery, not the language of a founder who knows exactly what the world looks like when they succeed. Contrast this with Palmer Luckey walking into Founders Fund having already built and sold Oculus, proposing to make the Pentagon's acquisition model obsolete with autonomous defense systems. Korso's team is competent but indefinite — they are searching for product-market fit in a crowded category rather than executing a bold plan that only makes sense if their specific vision is correct.

The bull case deserves articulation before I dismiss it. Korso's ERP-agnostic positioning addresses a real structural gap: SAP, Epicor, and Oracle are incentivized to add AI as a feature upsell within their platforms, not to build a standalone autonomous layer that reduces the need for their seat-based licenses. A manufacturer running three different ERPs across four plants cannot rely on Epicor Prism. Korso could become the integration layer across fragmented manufacturing IT environments, accumulating per-account switching costs as it digests pricing history, supplier patterns, and tribal knowledge. This is the Palantir argument: embed so deeply that displacement becomes structurally impossible. If Korso executed this across thousands of manufacturers, the compounding data moat could generate genuine monopoly dynamics. But this argument fails on three counts. First, it applies equally to every competitor in this space — Turian and Leverage AI can make the same integration-depth claim. Second, manufacturing is so fragmented that each customer deployment requires bespoke integration engineering, creating consulting economics rather than software economics. Third, no customer, no revenue, no LOI appears anywhere in this dossier — Korso has not even begun to prove that manufacturers will trust an AI agent to autonomously execute purchase orders and customer communications in production environments where errors have physical consequences.

Korso scores near zero on my atoms-over-bits criterion despite operating in the manufacturing sector. The company touches no physical process — it is an office automation layer wrapping existing ERP systems with LLM-based document parsing. We wanted flying cars, and Korso is offering a smarter email triage for the people who order the parts for the cars. There is no hardware component, no physical-world manipulation, no regulatory barrier to replication. A well-funded competitor — or an incumbent with distribution — could rebuild Korso's functionality in months. The TAM cited ($34 billion growing to $155 billion) is the entire "AI in manufacturing" market, not a serviceable segment Korso could monopolize. Power-law returns require winner-take-all dynamics, and manufacturing operations software has never exhibited them — the market is structurally fragmented by geography, ERP vendor, manufacturing type, and regulatory environment.

The single verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: Korso signs an anchor customer — ideally a manufacturer with 500+ employees — on a multi-year contract where the AI agent autonomously processes more than 50% of that customer's RFQ volume in production, with measurable cost reduction documented. That would demonstrate something no competitor has shown: that manufacturers will actually trust autonomous agents for critical procurement workflows, and that Korso's integration architecture works in the real world of messy ERP environments. Until that evidence exists, Korso is a software wrapper on generally available AI infrastructure, competing in a crowded market with no secret, no monopoly structure, and no physical-world moat. The Monopoly Secret dimension and Physical-World Technology Moat dimension are jointly decisive — Korso fails both, and no other criterion can rescue an investment that begins without a genuine contrarian insight.
