# Korso -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Korso is the canonical 2026 YC pitch from my Pass Example, but with one genuinely interesting wrinkle: Martin Pan ran automations at GM before this, which is the closest thing in the dossier to a real Wizard-of-Oz prior on the manufacturing floor. The signature is high-on-domain-fit, zero-on-distribution -- three engineers in San Francisco selling agentic middleware across SAP, NetSuite, Plex, SYSPRO, Acumatica, Dynamics, and Sage at a moment when Lumari (also YC), Turian ($3.78M, Berlin), Smartbase, and qomplement are pitching essentially the same wedge into the same buyer. Korso is an execution-team building a commodity-priced agent in a crowded sleeve, and the financing structure on offer is the standard SAFE cap table I cannot make money at.

Solana at $0.05 in 2018 is what the top rung of my price-discipline ladder looks like -- a category every consensus fund had quit, with no competitive process around the round. Korso sits at the opposite pole: "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], three engineers, Spring 2026 batch" is almost word-for-word the pitch I wrote into my Pass Example before I'd ever heard of Korso. The dossier's own competitive map -- Lumari already YC-backed on RFQ/PO agents, Turian raising $3.78M from Cherry and Visionaries on the same wedge, AWS Bedrock AgentCore shipping procurement agents -- is the consensus tell. There is no disclosed pricing on the Korso round and no named pass from a tier-1, but the absence of any contrarian signal in a category this crowded is itself the signal; "AI is just more internet" (Lonsdale #113) re-weights this entire sleeve away from seed pricing.

The Pass Example founder has no public writing on why their take on agents is non-obvious, and Korso replicates that exactly -- no Twitter for Daichi Hiraoka, no Twitter for Alex Liu, no Twitter for Martin Pan, no GitHub for any of the three, no essays, no opinionated thread on why long-running ERP agents fail. The pattern in the dossier where Korso's homepage dashboard references fictional "Northbridge Metals" and "Blue Harbor Components" -- a product mockup posing as a customer screenshot -- is the inverse of the Wizard-of-Oz signal I look for; the Fin template was running a fully manual service before the marketing layer, not the other way around. Korso is shipping the marketing layer before evidence of the manual service.

Capital structure: an agent layer that has to integrate seven ERPs and pass IT-security review at mid-market manufacturers running SAP and Oracle will need five rounds to reach default-alive, and the Korso team of three has no disclosed enterprise-sales hire to compress that arc. Over 18 months the compounding goes the wrong way -- each ERP integration is a new schlep, each pilot is a new procurement review, and the regatta tack count between today and a Series A buyer's frame keeps growing while Lumari and Turian eat the same logos. Teamshares this is not.

Founder-conviction counterfactual: if Martin Pan had published a post-GM essay on why production-floor automations engineers keep failing the same way against SAP exception flows, or if Daichi Hiraoka had spent two years moonlighting on RFQ triage for a single Penn Electric Racing sponsor before YC, Korso flips from a 7 to a 14 on Criterion 3 and I take the meeting. The GM line item gets Korso above the default for founder-market fit -- Pan has actually been on the floor -- but absent any artifact of asymmetric conviction on the category itself, this reads as opportunity-response, not mission.

Category, single-anchor parallel: Ro is my Known Exception for capital-heavy regulated-rail businesses, and the case Korso would have to make is that cross-ERP orchestration in manufacturing is structurally analogous -- that only venture capital can clear the 7-ERP integration matrix the way only VC could clear 50-state prescription licensing. The dossier doesn't support the analogy. Ro's moat was regulatory; Korso's stated moat is "verification layers, scoped tools, dry-run validation, audit trails" -- architecture features every competitor will also build. Manufacturing ERPs are fragmented, but fragmentation alone has never been a moat; SAP Ariba and Coupa already sit on the buyer-side, and Microsoft's Dynamics agentic roadmap will reach the mid-market before Korso reaches Series A.

36-month forecast: Korso is acqui-hired into a Plex/Acumatica/SYSPRO ecosystem partner or absorbed in a Lumari-vs-Turian consolidation at sub-$2M ARR -- the team lands, the cap table doesn't return capital, and the agent layer becomes a feature inside someone else's ERP-adjacent suite. The mechanism is straightforward: a three-engineer team selling enterprise integration in a six-vendor field cannot out-distribute the YC-backed direct competitor that started the same quarter, and the SAFE I'd buy today caps out before the consolidation event clears.

The strongest bull case is the one I have to take seriously: Martin Pan's GM automations role is the Wizard-of-Oz prior I claim to look for, the seven-ERP integration list is precisely the kind of "illegible asset" most SaaS-trained funds will misprice, and SAP/Oracle channel conflict between selling agent layers and protecting implementation-partner revenue is real -- Sublime Security looked similarly crowded before it broke out of the SOC SaaS pack. That case loses on two facts in the dossier: the fictional-company mockups on korsoai.com are evidence the team is leading with marketing instead of manual operations, and Lumari being another YC company on the same wedge collapses any "category fund passed on this" claim Korso could make. The verifiable condition that flips this for me is a named manufacturer pilot -- one real mid-market SAP or NetSuite shop running Korso on a live RFQ queue with a logo I can call -- combined with a Korso-authored teardown of why Lumari's and Turian's architecture fails on multi-week checkpointed workflows. Until then, I pass and watch the next tack.
