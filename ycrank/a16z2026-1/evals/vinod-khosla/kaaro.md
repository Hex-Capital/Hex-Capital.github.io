# Kaaro -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with what's genuinely interesting about Kaaro: the founder archetype. Sai Surisetti dropped out of Waterloo at 18 to become the first engineer at Toma, an a16z-backed company that built AI voice agents for car dealerships and scaled from twenty customers to hundreds. Gautham Venkateshwaran was founding engineer number two, leading infrastructure and DMS integrations at that same company. They're 19 and 20 years old, they've already lived through one zero-to-scale AI agent deployment in a manual, relationship-driven industry, and now they're running the same playbook in their family's industry — both are described as "second-generation railroaders." When I backed the teenage dropout at Mach Industries building weapons to compete with Lockheed Martin, the signal was the same: irreverence, velocity, and a willingness to enter a calcified industry without permission. Kaaro's founders have that energy. The question is whether they've aimed it at a problem with sufficient consequence.

Freight rail moves roughly 40% of US freight ton-miles, and the operational layer — billing, car tracking, crew coordination, incident reporting — runs on phone calls, email, and what Kaaro's website calls "tribal knowledge." That's a real problem. CPKC just completed the merger creating the first single-line railroad spanning Canada, the US, and Mexico, which almost certainly generated new operational complexity. Getting CPKC engaged as a two-person pre-seed company is a strong early signal in an industry where vendor trust takes years to build. But when I apply my primary filter — "if Kaaro fully succeeds, does the world visibly change?" — the answer is that railroads run more efficiently. Revenue leaks less. Paperwork moves faster. That matters to railroad operators, but it does not materially change how millions of people live. This is the structural problem: Kaaro fits the exact pattern I see constantly at YC Demo Day — "AI agents for [underserved vertical], large TAM, we automate X% of the workflow." The framing sounds different when you say "railroads" instead of "accountants," but the consequence architecture is identical. A moderately profitable vertical SaaS company, not a changed world.

The technical approach reinforces my concern. Kaaro appears to be an AI overlay that ingests data from existing railroad management systems — Wabtec RailConnect, Railinc, proprietary Class I tools — and wraps communication workflows in agent interfaces. The dossier describes real-time car tracking, idle alerts, shipment notifications, and contextual decision support. These are useful features built on existing LLM capabilities applied to a domain, not a step-function technical breakthrough. The founders' Toma experience proves they can execute this type of integration — CDK Global, Reynolds & Reynolds, PBS, Dealervault — but what they demonstrated at Toma was operational competence in connecting AI agents to legacy systems, not a novel technical insight that creates a 10x discontinuity. Wabtec, a $25 billion public company, is actively modernizing RailConnect from "green screens" to web interfaces. If they decide to add AI capabilities natively, Kaaro's overlay becomes a feature inside the incumbent's platform. The moat here is relationships and domain knowledge, not technology.

The strongest bull case: Kaaro captures the tribal knowledge of an aging railroad workforce before it retires, becoming the institutional memory of North American freight rail. Every railroad that deploys Kaaro's agents feeds data back into a system that gets smarter about operational patterns, billing exceptions, and contact networks — creating a compounding data advantage that no incumbent TMS has. The CPKC and CN engagements, if they convert, would give Kaaro access to the two Canadian Class I railroads and their combined $35 billion+ in annual revenue, establishing a beachhead that American Class I railroads would eventually follow. In this scenario, Kaaro evolves from workflow automation tool to the AI operating layer for freight rail. For this to work, the sales cycle has to compress (Class I procurement typically runs 6-18 months), Wabtec has to remain slow to add AI natively, and the founders' domain access through their railroader families has to translate into signed contracts, not just "late conversations." These are market execution risks, not technology risks — exactly the risk profile I avoid.

I checked my known exceptions and none apply. DoorDash's override was planetary-scale consequence magnitude — transforming food access for hundreds of millions. Kaaro's consequence, even at full success, is a better-run railroad industry. Oscar Health's override was radical affordability of healthcare, a basic human need. Kaaro is selling enterprise efficiency to railroad operators, not making transportation radically cheaper for consumers. The founders earn real respect — the Toma experience, the family railroad connections, the CPKC engagement at pre-seed — but consequence magnitude is the top of my hierarchy, and when the answer to "what if it works?" is "railroads have better software," every other criterion becomes secondary in the other direction. The two dimensions that lock this verdict: consequence magnitude (automating railroad workflows is operationally useful but not world-changing) and non-linear technical approach (AI agent overlay on existing systems using current LLM capabilities, not a technical discontinuity).
