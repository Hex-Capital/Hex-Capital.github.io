# Stage -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: two brilliant Stanford engineers who could be working on something that matters at scale, and instead they're building a nicer way to look at code diffs. Stage restructures pull request changes into "structured chapters" so reviewers can follow the narrative. It's a real problem — AI-generated code is flooding review queues with unfamiliar changes, and GitHub's line-by-line diff UI was designed for an era when humans wrote every line. I understand the pain. But the consequence of Stage succeeding completely — every engineering team adopts structured chapters — is a useful B2B SaaS business in a narrow slice of the $6-7 billion developer tools market. Graphite, the closest comparable, sold to Cursor for roughly $290 million. That's a fine outcome for some investors. It's not what I write checks for. If I'm investing my own money, I need the possibility that the world operates differently if this works. Stage's world looks the same, just with better-organized diffs.

The dossier itself articulates my concern more precisely than I could: "The 'structured chapters' concept is a UX innovation, not a deep technical moat. Any well-resourced competitor could implement a similar organizational layer." This is the kind of sentence that makes me close the folder. When the technology can be described as a UX reorganization, and the company's own competitive analysis shows Haystack from YC S24 pursuing an analogous approach with a different visual metaphor, and GitHub could ship a chapters feature in a single quarter if it wanted to — where is the disruption? Where is the technology that makes the incumbent approach economically unviable? Square didn't just build a nicer cash register UI; it made traditional POS infrastructure obsolete by turning a phone into a payment terminal. Stage is building a nicer cash register UI.

The bull case deserves honest engagement, because the timing signal is genuinely interesting. AI coding tools are growing at nearly 28% CAGR. Eighty-four percent of developers are adopting AI assistants. The volume of machine-written code that humans must review is about to explode — this is a real inflection point. If Stage evolved beyond chapter organization into the definitive "human comprehension layer" for AI-generated code — the system that lets humans maintain meaningful oversight of machine-produced software — the consequence magnitude would be transformational. That's a civilization-scale problem: how do humans stay in the loop as AI writes most of the code? But nothing in the dossier suggests Stage is aiming there. The product description is about organizing diffs, not about solving the fundamental human-AI collaboration problem in software development. Dean Stratakos built an in-house coding agent at Five Rings and has an MS in AI from Stanford — he has the technical depth to attack that harder problem. The fact that he's building a diff viewer instead tells me the ambition hasn't been calibrated to the scale of the opportunity.

On founders: Charles and Dean are clearly talented — Stanford CS, Five Rings (one of the most selective quant firms), Apple, Citadel. But this is the profile of industry insiders executing a safe playbook, not outsiders attempting something experts consider impossible. They experienced code review friction at Five Rings and built a tool to fix it. That's the "5% improvement from established experience" pattern I'm wary of, not the "irreverence, foolish confidence, and naivety" that produces transformational companies. The team is also homogeneous — same school, same employer, same technical background. No go-to-market thinking, no design rethinking, no one from outside the engineering bubble asking whether code review itself is the right abstraction in an AI-native workflow. Compare this to Peter Beck at Rocket Lab — a New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree who built orbital rockets because he didn't know what was supposed to be impossible. Stage's founders know exactly what's possible, which is why they're building something incremental.

The competitive dynamics compound the problem. CodeRabbit has $88 million in funding and $15 million in ARR for AI-automated review. Greptile raised $29 million with Benchmark leading. Anthropic just launched a multi-agent code review tool for Claude Enterprise. Cursor acquired Graphite and will integrate review workflow into the dominant AI editor. Stage is entering a space where well-funded incumbents are converging from every direction — and its differentiation is a UX concept that any of them could replicate. When the established players are *already building* in your space rather than dismissing it, that's the opposite of the signal I look for. My best investments were in companies that incumbents mocked. Nobody's mocking code review tools — everyone's building them.

I pass. These founders should be working on something harder and more consequential. The problem of human comprehension of AI-generated code is real and important — but the solution isn't organizing diffs into chapters. It's something none of us have imagined yet, and it probably comes from someone who doesn't think "code review" is a fixed category.
