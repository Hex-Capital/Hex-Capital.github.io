# Caretta -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did the founders find this problem? And with Caretta, I can't find the answer. The dossier tells me three CS graduates from TU Delft built a Discord bot, then built something called VoysAI, then pivoted to real-time AI for sales calls. What I don't see anywhere is a founder who was actually on a sales call, fumbling a technical question, feeling that specific pain of losing deal momentum because they couldn't recall a pricing detail or counter an objection in the moment. The problem statement reads like market analysis — "Gong does post-call, nobody does real-time well for B2B sales, there's a gap" — and that's exactly the kind of idea generation I've warned about. Smart people looking at a market map and identifying whitespace is not the same as a founder who can't stop thinking about a problem because they live with it every day.

The schlep test here is more nuanced than it first appears, and unfortunately it cuts against Caretta. Real-time AI during live sales calls is genuinely hard engineering — low-latency transcription, fast retrieval, context-aware answer generation. But it's the wrong kind of hard. The schlep that protects Stripe is the ugliness of banking regulations, fraud detection, and compliance — things that make engineers' eyes glaze over. The "schlep" in real-time sales AI is building a fast RAG pipeline with good speech-to-text. That's a solvable engineering challenge, and more importantly, it's one that multiple well-funded teams are already solving. Salesken has $41M. Balto has $57M. Cresta has $276M. Gong just raised $250M in October 2025. This problem is not hiding in plain sight. It's visible, funded, and actively being attacked from multiple directions. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space with this much capital already deployed, my concern isn't that the problem is too hard — it's that the structural advantage of being first to see it simply doesn't exist.

The strongest case for Caretta is that these are genuine hacker-founders who build things. Three technical co-founders from a good CS program, with demonstrated experience shipping real-time communication products. Omar Elamin's 78-commit Discord bot that reached 30K users shows someone who actually writes code and ships it to real users. They've iterated from VoysAI to the current product, which shows willingness to adapt. The GitHub repo wrapping NVIDIA's Parakeet model for transcription tells me they're making real technical decisions, not just assembling a no-code stack. If I squint, the data flywheel story is interesting — each customer's knowledge base improves with every call, creating genuine switching costs over time. And there's a version of the innovator's dilemma argument where Gong, optimized for post-call analytics dashboards that revenue leaders use for forecasting, can't easily cannibalize that value proposition to serve in-call assistance to individual reps. If Caretta could get deep inside a few sales teams and accumulate enough organizational knowledge to become indispensable, the moat could build from the customer side.

But that bull case requires something I don't see evidence of: actual users pulling the product toward them. No revenue signals. No customer stories. The website returned a 429 error. The GitHub repos have one star total. There's no waitlist, no Product Hunt launch, no organic community forming around this. At pre-seed I don't demand much — even a handful of sales reps who tried the product and refused to do calls without it would be a meaningful signal. The complete absence of any user pull data, combined with the lack of organic problem discovery, makes this look like a technically capable team building a product in search of confirmed demand.

The VoysAI-to-Caretta pivot is worth noting. It could indicate healthy iteration — founders who built something, learned from it, and adapted. But without knowing what VoysAI was, what they learned, and why they pivoted specifically to B2B sales calls, I can't read this as a positive signal. It could equally indicate founders exploring the voice AI space looking for a market that responds, which is exactly the "brainstorming for ideas" pattern that worries me. The founders who build the best companies usually can't explain why they're working on their specific problem without telling you a story from their own life. Here, the most natural explanation for "why B2B sales calls?" appears to be "because Gong proved the market is large and nobody's doing real-time yet."

I'd want to be wrong about this. If Kayra, Omar, or Pavlos has a backstory I'm missing — if one of them spent years watching a parent struggle in technical sales, or personally sat through calls where deals died because the right information wasn't at hand — that would meaningfully change my assessment. Technical founders with genuine domain pain in a high-schlep space is a combination I actively seek. But based on what's in front of me, this is a capable technical team that identified a market gap through analysis rather than experience, entering a competitive space without the structural protection that schlep blindness provides. I'm passing.
