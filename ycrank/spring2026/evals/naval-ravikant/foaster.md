# Foaster -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the self-description: "AI-native consulting firm." That word -- firm -- tells me everything about the leverage architecture before I read another line. A firm scales with headcount. A platform scales with code. Foaster has a human consultant scoping each engagement, reviewing every output, challenging the AI's reasoning, and delivering final recommendations. That's not leverage -- that's a slightly faster version of what McKinsey already does. You've replaced the junior analyst's interview notepad with an LLM, but preserved the entire human delivery chain that makes consulting a linear business. When I invested in AngelList, the thesis was that one engineer's code could process a thousand fund formations. When I invested in Notion, one product served millions of knowledge workers without proportional headcount. Foaster needs more human consultants to serve more clients. That's the structural problem, and it's not a timing issue -- it's encoded in the business model.

On specific knowledge: Combes built Repondia, which allegedly handled 60,000+ AI phone calls for restaurant groups. That's real operational knowledge about making conversational AI work at scale -- the failure modes, the edge cases, the moments where an AI agent needs to recover gracefully. The Werewolf benchmark is a sharper signal than it first appears: designing experiments to probe LLM social intelligence requires understanding both the models and the dynamics of multi-agent conversation. OpenAI's president shared it. xAI's co-founder reached out. These are not trivial credentialing events for two 23-year-old students. But here's the gap: their specific knowledge is in AI agent interaction design, not in organizational consulting. They understand how to build systems that conduct conversations. They don't have non-transferable knowledge about why a VP of Operations at a Fortune 500 company reveals certain things in an interview and hides others, or how a partner at McKinsey structures an engagement to create dependency. Consulting is a trust business wrapped in an analysis business, and that trust layer requires knowledge these founders haven't had the opportunity to accumulate.

The bull case deserves honest examination. The innovator's dilemma is real here -- McKinsey cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper product without destroying its own economics. That structural barrier has fueled great companies before. The operational map as a persistent asset is genuinely interesting: if Foaster interviews every employee once and maintains that map, follow-on engagements become closer to software than services. That's where the leverage could eventually emerge -- not in the first engagement, but in the second and third, where the map is already built and the AI reasons on existing data. If that flywheel works, the business transitions from consulting to platform without the client noticing. And the timing -- LLMs reaching conversational competence sufficient for nuanced organizational interviews -- is arguably a real capability threshold crossed in the last twelve months.

But "AI will replace consulting" is the most consensus AI narrative I've heard in a year. Everyone with a pitch deck has a slide about disrupting professional services. Distyl AI has $202 million. Aily Labs has $101 million. PromptQL has $136 million. This is not a contrarian bet -- it's a crowded, hot category where the pricing already reflects optimism. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was literally illegal. When I backed Bitcoin, sophisticated people called it a Ponzi scheme. Nobody is dismissing AI consulting automation -- they're funding it aggressively. The Foaster founders aren't positioned against a gloomy crowd; they're positioned inside a euphoric one, with a fraction of the capital their competitors wield. And the core technology -- LLM-based interviewing -- runs on generally available foundation models. Any of those well-funded competitors could add an interview layer in a quarter. The compounding moat would need to come from accumulated methodology and proprietary organizational data, but at zero disclosed traction, that moat doesn't exist yet.

Two talented builders, early twenties, strong AI agent expertise, YC backing -- in a different structure, I'd be more interested. If they were building the infrastructure layer that consulting firms use rather than being a consulting firm themselves, the leverage math would be different. A platform that enables any consultant to deploy AI interviews and operational mapping -- that's picks-and-shovels. What they're building is the mine itself, and mines require labor proportional to output. The specific knowledge in AI agent design is real but insufficient without consulting domain depth. The leverage architecture is structurally capped by the services model. And the timing, while real in terms of technology capability, is consensus rather than contrarian. I'd pass, but I'd watch whether they pivot from firm to platform -- that's the version of this company I'd want to fund.
