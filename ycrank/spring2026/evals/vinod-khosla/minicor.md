# Minicor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Minicor is the commoditization clock ticking underneath it. Anthropic shipped Computer Use in public beta in October 2024. Any team with an API key can now point an AI agent at a Windows desktop and have it click buttons. When the model provider is handing the core capability to every developer on earth, what exactly is the moat? Minicor's answer is the managed layer — VMs, compliance certifications, observability, self-healing orchestration. That's a real answer. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA are non-trivial to obtain. But it's an infrastructure-operations answer, not a technology answer. And infrastructure businesses that depend on someone else's model are in a fundamentally precarious position. UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent feature. When a $13 billion incumbent with thousands of enterprise accounts starts doing your differentiation, you're in a race you probably lose.

Let me ask my first question: if Minicor fully succeeds — becomes the standard plumbing layer connecting AI to every legacy desktop system in healthcare, automotive, supply chain, and finance — what happens to the world? The honest answer is that integration friction goes away and AI companies can deploy into legacy industries faster. That's useful. But the world-changing consequence belongs to those AI companies, not to Minicor. This is like investing in the company that builds the adapter plugs for electric vehicles rather than investing in the company that builds the battery technology. The adapter plug company can be a fine business, but it doesn't change the shape of the future. I want bets where success means the world visibly shifts. Minicor's success means enterprise plumbing works better. That's a moderate consequence — real revenue, real customers, but not the kind of outcome that justifies binary risk-taking.

The technical approach reinforces this concern. Minicor claims 93–96% click accuracy versus 80–85% for competitors. That's better, not different. A 10-percentage-point accuracy improvement is the definition of incremental. At 25,000 patients per day, even 96% accuracy produces a thousand errors daily in healthcare contexts — contexts where errors carry regulatory and patient-safety weight. The deterministic-code-with-AI-recovery architecture is a sound engineering decision, and I respect the pragmatism of using AI for edge cases rather than the entire control flow. But this is optimization, not disruption. There is no fundamental technical insight here that produces a 10x discontinuity. Nobody is going to look at this in ten years and say "they invented something."

The bull case deserves serious engagement. Legacy desktop systems with no writable APIs are a genuine prison for AI companies trying to deploy into healthcare and finance. If every AI healthcare startup needs to connect to Epic, Athena, and Cerner, and Minicor is the only reliable way to do it at scale, that's a picks-and-shovels play with real pricing power. The traction signal — 25,000 patients per day at production architecture scale, thousands of workflows weekly — is impressive for a pre-seed company founded in 2024. The founders have complementary backgrounds: Saheed Akinbile is a Nigerian immigrant who worked inside legacy enterprise environments at Ford and Morgan Stanley, which gives him firsthand knowledge of the pain. The co-founders have worked together since 2019, built a nonprofit together during the pandemic, and have the kind of forged-in-the-trenches relationship that survives hard times. If the managed infrastructure layer creates enough switching costs before UiPath catches up, and if the AI-company customer segment grows as fast as I think AI will penetrate legacy industries, Minicor could be a $500M-$1B outcome. That's a real business. It's just not the kind of bet I make.

I don't invest for $500M outcomes. I invest for outcomes where the money either evaporates or returns 50 times. Minicor is designed to be a competent, compliant, well-run infrastructure company in a competitive and rapidly commoditizing market. The founders are capable but not the kind of irreverent outsiders attempting something the world says is impossible — they're experienced engineers building better plumbing. Nothing about this provokes the reaction "that can't be done." Everything about it provokes the reaction "that's a crowded market." When I size a bet, I need the consequence of success to justify accepting a high probability of failure. Here, the probability of some success is moderate, but the consequence of success is bounded. That's exactly the profile I avoid — the 3-5x return that consumes the same attention as a 50x bet. Pass.
