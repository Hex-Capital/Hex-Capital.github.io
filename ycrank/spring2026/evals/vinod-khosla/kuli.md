# Kuli -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$50K per month in agency fees saved for enterprise marketing teams. That's the headline consequence if Kuli fully succeeds. I need to be honest: when the biggest outcome I can project is that brand managers at Disney and Supercell spend fewer hours reviewing TikTok videos, I know this isn't my kind of bet. The influencer marketing platform TAM is growing at 33-37% CAGR toward $50B — real money, no question — but the consequence of Kuli winning that market is a more efficient advertising industry, not a changed world. Nobody wakes up in a different reality because influencer campaigns launched 4x faster. I regularly pass on exactly this archetype: AI applied to a specific enterprise vertical, automating 40% of a workflow, generating a 3-5x return. Kuli fits that pattern precisely.

Kuli's core technology — multimodal video analysis using frame-by-frame AI processing — sounds impressive until you examine the substrate. The dossier's own defensibility section concedes that "large AI model providers (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic) offer multimodal video understanding APIs that could lower the barrier for new entrants" and that the technical complexity is "replicable by well-funded teams." Kuli is building an application layer on top of commodity APIs, not a proprietary technology. Compare this to Rocket Lab, which engineered electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a physical-world breakthrough that couldn't be replicated by plugging into someone else's infrastructure. Kuli's "world's largest analysed social media content database without storing any video" is a data accumulation play, not a technology discontinuity. When CreatorIQ, sitting on $80.8M in funding and $70.7M in revenue with 300 enterprise customers, decides to plug GPT-4V into their existing CRM workflows, Kuli's technical differentiation evaporates. Incumbent feature absorption isn't a theoretical risk here — it's the most likely outcome.

Michael Hodara brings an EPFL MSc in Robotics and direct experience managing $20M+ in annual marketing spend at Aircall. Jonathan Hassan brings six years of KPMG M&A advisory. These are competent, relevant backgrounds — and that's exactly what concerns me. Hodara experienced the pain point firsthand and built the logical solution. This is domain expertise producing a domain-specific tool, not a first-principles outsider reimagining an industry. I don't see evidence of rapid learning or surprising pivots in the dossier — I see a capable engineer who noticed a problem at his last job and built software to fix it. When I backed the Mach Industries founder, a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems against Lockheed Martin, the signal was irreverence and learning velocity, not relevant work experience. Kuli's founding story reads as sensible career progression, not the kind of naive ambition that produces outlier outcomes.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Disney, Supercell, and Havas as named users at pre-seed with a three-person team is genuinely notable traction. Microsoft selected Kuli among the "top 15 most innovative Gen AI startups in France" for their GenAI Studio program. The 4.8/5 rating on Influencer Marketing Hub suggests real product quality. If I apply the DoorDash exception — where I overrode the technical novelty requirement because consequence magnitude was planetary-scale — could it apply here? No. DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. Kuli optimizes workflows for thousands of marketing professionals. The consequence magnitude gap is orders of magnitude too large for the exception to trigger. Strong enterprise traction at pre-seed is a signal of good execution, but execution advantage without a technology wedge produces a moderately profitable company, not a portfolio-defining return. I either want to lose my money or make 50 times my money — Kuli is engineered for the middle, which is the one outcome I can't use.

Kuli's path to radical affordability is real but narrow: replacing $50K/month agency fees with SaaS pricing genuinely reduces costs for enterprise marketing teams. But this isn't making a basic human need affordable — it's making advertising cheaper. When I backed Impossible Foods, the end-state was feeding billions of people sustainably at lower cost than animal agriculture. When I look at Kuli, the end-state is that Procter & Gamble's influencer marketing team runs leaner. These are categorically different consequences. The ambition level is correspondingly modest — no informed observer would say "analyzing social media videos with AI can't be done." The approach is sensible, the market is real, and the founders are qualified. For most investors, that's enough. For me, it's a pass. The one condition that would flip me: if Kuli demonstrated a proprietary model architecture for video understanding that produced results no commodity API could match — a genuine technology moat, not an application layer — and if that technology generalized beyond marketing to brand safety, content moderation, or media intelligence at scale affecting millions, I'd reassess the consequence magnitude entirely.
