# Hub.xyz -- Paul Graham Evaluation

500,000 contributors, 321,000 Twitter followers, 10 million replies to AI agent prompts, and zero disclosed revenue or named enterprise customers. Hub.xyz has all the metrics of a thriving community and none of the metrics of a business. I've seen this pattern before — it's what happens when you incentivize participation with future tokens rather than current value. The 500K contributors across 150+ countries aren't a data moat; they're an airdrop farming operation. People accumulating IQ Points convertible to $HUB tokens at a future Token Generation Event are not the same as users who desperately need your product in its crappiest form. The question I always ask — who wants this so much they'll use it even when it's a terrible version one made by a two-person startup they've never heard of? — has no answer here, because Hub.xyz's contributors aren't using a product. They're speculating on a token.

Armin Kiani and Tim Sprecher connected through Q42, a venture fund where Armin is an LP and Tim was an advisor. That's not a founding story — it's a networking story. When I evaluate founders, I'm looking for the animal quality, the person who takes their work a little too seriously and crosses from professional into obsessive. Neither founder has a public GitHub profile. Neither has a documented prior exit or shipped product. Tim started his first company at 18, which shows initiative, but there's no story of what happened with it — no narrative of creative problem-solving under constraint. Armin describes himself as "Making AI training sexy with Hub.xyz. Early crypto, long agentic & robotics." That's a Twitter bio, not the language of someone who has been personally tormented by the problem of AI training data scarcity. Compare this to Drew Houston, who built Dropbox because he literally kept forgetting his USB drive, or the Collison brothers, who built Stripe because they'd personally struggled with payment integration as developers. Hub.xyz's founders appear to have identified a market opportunity and layered crypto incentives on top. That's exactly backwards from how the best startup ideas emerge.

Hub.xyz's idea — a crypto-incentivized network collecting AI training data — sounds good. That's the problem. DePIN plus AI is a trending narrative in crypto circles. "API for real-world training data" is the kind of thing that gets nods in any AI-adjacent conversation. Nobody's first reaction to Hub.xyz is "that will never work" — the reaction is "oh, that's hot right now." When I funded Airbnb, investors literally told the founders the idea was insane — strangers sleeping in strangers' houses. That universal rejection was the signal that Airbnb was in the sweet spot between "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Hub.xyz sits in the opposite quadrant: it seems like a good idea to almost everyone in crypto and AI. The consensus enthusiasm has already competed away the return premium. And the competitors confirm it — Scale AI ($1.6B+ raised, ~$2B revenue), Surge AI ($1.4B revenue bootstrapped), Toloka ($72M raised, Bezos-backed), Labelbox ($189M raised). Hub.xyz has $1.7M in funding against this field. The market validates that data labeling is valuable; it also validates that the opportunity is visible to everyone.

The bull case an advocate would make: Scale AI's 49% acquisition by Meta in June 2025 caused OpenAI and Google to cut ties, fragmenting the incumbent market and creating a genuine opening for alternative data providers. Hub.xyz's 500K contributors in 150+ countries could collect rare audio, language samples, and video that don't exist in Common Crawl — the kind of multimodal diversity that frontier AI labs increasingly need. The $3.2B market growing to $16.3B by 2033 at 22.6% CAGR (Grand View Research) is real demand, not narrative. Tim Sprecher was named one of "30 Global Founders to Watch" by Tracxn. SwissBorg led a real seed round. And crypto-incentivized contributor networks could genuinely solve the geographic and linguistic coverage problem that centralized annotation workforces can't. I've made exceptions before — ClassDojo succeeded in education despite violating my "hackers building for hackers" pattern, because the schlep was real and the execution was exceptional. But Hub.xyz doesn't parallel ClassDojo's exception: the schlep in AI training data is quality control, expert annotation, and enterprise trust-building. Hub.xyz's crypto-token approach doesn't tackle that schlep — it tries to route around it with passive bandwidth sharing and Twitter reply farming. That's not schlep blindness; it's schlep avoidance.

Hub.xyz's learning rate is opaque from this dossier. The roadmap projected ">7M active nodes by Q4 2025," but no data confirms whether Hub.xyz hit that target. The website returned HTTP 403 at the time of research — a company whose primary interface with potential customers is inaccessible doesn't inspire confidence in iteration speed. One active job posting for a "Founding Full Stack AI Engineer" at $70K-$100K in London suggests Hub.xyz is still building its core technical team at the pre-seed stage, which is normal, but also means the AI engineering capability that would differentiate Hub.xyz's data pipeline from commodity bandwidth collection doesn't yet exist in-house. The Léonie Nguyen situation — listed as co-founder on Tracxn but angel backer on SwissBorg's page, while simultaneously serving as CEO of Meey Group — creates due diligence friction that a more carefully run company wouldn't generate.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Hub.xyz names three enterprise AI customers paying recurring revenue for datasets that demonstrably couldn't be assembled through existing providers — proving the contributor network produces commercially unique data, not just volume. Until that evidence exists, Hub.xyz looks like a crypto community with an AI narrative, not an AI data company with a crypto distribution channel. The distinction matters enormously. The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery — where the founders show no evidence of having personally experienced the AI training data problem — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — where the DePIN-meets-AI positioning generates consensus enthusiasm rather than the productive skepticism that signals genuine alpha. Hub.xyz is a pass.
