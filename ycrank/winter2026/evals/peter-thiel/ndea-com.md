# Ndea -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

François Chollet built Keras, the framework that powers much of modern deep learning. He then created ARC-AGI, the benchmark that most rigorously exposes what deep learning cannot do. Now he has left Google to start a company premised on the thesis that the entire paradigm he helped enable -- scaling large language models -- will not produce genuine intelligence. The architect of the cathedral is telling you the foundation is wrong. That is an extraordinarily rare signal. Most founders claim contrarian insight they don't actually possess; Chollet earned his contrarian thesis by spending a decade inside the machine and understanding its limits from within. When I co-founded Palantir, it was because the anti-fraud work at PayPal revealed a truth about intelligence analysis that the intelligence community couldn't see from inside its own institutions. Chollet's trajectory has the same structure: the person who built the tools is the one who understands what the tools cannot do.

The secret here passes my first test. "LLMs will not achieve AGI; program synthesis -- searching discrete program spaces rather than training continuous neural networks -- is the fundamentally different paradigm required." This is not a repackaged consensus. The AI industry has deployed over a hundred billion dollars on the opposite thesis. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI are all mimetic competitors locked in the same scaling race -- each imitating the other's architecture, each raising larger rounds to train larger models. The Girardian dynamics are textbook: the more they resemble each other, the more they compete, the more they destroy each other's margins. Ndea is the one lab that has explicitly escaped this mimetic trap by pursuing a different paradigm entirely. The question is not whether the secret is genuinely contrarian -- it is -- but whether the secret, if correct, creates a monopoly.

And this is where my enthusiasm encounters structural problems. Ndea is a research lab with no product, no revenue model, no defined customer, and no commercialization timeline. The stated mission -- "a factory for rapid scientific advancement" capable of "inventing and commercializing N ideas" across drugs, energy, robotics, and space -- is precisely the kind of unbounded aspiration that substitutes grandeur for specificity. I distinguish sharply between definite optimism and indefinite optimism. Definite optimism means you have a concrete plan for a specific future. Indefinite optimism means you believe things will be great but cannot articulate how. "We will achieve AGI through program synthesis and then commercialize scientific discoveries across every domain" sounds bold, but it is operationally vague. Musk at SpaceX had a specific plan for reusable first stages. Zuckerberg at Facebook had a specific product with measurable network density at Harvard. Chollet has a research direction and a benchmark score that moved from 33% to 55.5% -- still thirty points below the human target. The technical vision is definite; the business vision is not.

The capital asymmetry is the most dangerous structural fact. Ndea has raised $43 million. Google DeepMind operates inside a company spending $100 billion on AI. If program synthesis demonstrates breakthrough results, DeepMind already has formal reasoning expertise and could redirect a fraction of its budget that would dwarf Ndea's entire war chest. The history of AI research is not kind to small labs with open breakthroughs -- transformers, attention mechanisms, diffusion models were all democratized rapidly. Chollet's own Keras is open-source. The culture of AI research runs toward openness, which structurally undermines proprietary technology moats. Ndea's defensibility rests primarily on talent density and tacit knowledge -- real advantages, but fragile ones against organizations that can offer ten times the compensation and ten thousand times the compute.

The strongest bull case is this: if LLM scaling genuinely hits a wall -- and there are increasing signals of diminishing returns -- then every dollar of the hundreds of billions invested in transformer scaling becomes a stranded asset, and the small team that spent years mastering the alternative paradigm suddenly holds the keys. The "deep learning circa 2012" comparison would mean Ndea is positioned at the inflection point of the next exponential curve in AI, and their head start in program synthesis would be analogous to the early deep learning labs that captured a decade of advantage. Chollet's benchmark ownership also creates a subtle structural advantage: the person who defines what AGI means has enormous influence over what counts as progress. Knoop's operational track record at Zapier (millions of users, YC S12, still on the board) provides the complement that pure research labs often lack. If I believed the AGI timeline were short and the commercialization path would become obvious upon breakthrough, this would be a strong invest. The power-law upside of "the company that actually achieves AGI" is essentially unbounded.

But I do not invest in unbounded upside with undefined pathways. A company without a specific monopoly strategy is a company that will either produce a research contribution captured by larger players or spend a decade burning capital without commercial traction. There is no beachhead market -- Ndea targets everything simultaneously. There are no network effects, no switching costs, no economies of scale in their current position. The moat is one extraordinary researcher and fifteen talented people working on a paradigm that remains unproven at scale. That is a foundation for a great research institute, not necessarily for a great company. I respect the secret deeply; I am unconvinced it has been embedded in a structure that creates a monopoly.
