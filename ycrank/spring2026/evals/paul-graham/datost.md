# Datost -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The problem Datost is working on -- "non-technical people can't query their own data" -- is the exact opposite of what I look for when I talk about schlep blindness. Schlep blindness describes problems that are obvious but that people unconsciously avoid because the work is so painful. This problem isn't avoided. It's the most worked-on problem in enterprise software. Tableau, Looker, Power BI, Metabase, Mode, Redash, Equals, and a dozen AI-native startups are all attacking it right now. When I see a space this crowded, I ask: why would these two founders in particular win? And the answer I'm looking for is some combination of organic insight and willingness to do ugly work that competitors won't touch. I can't find either here.

The origin of the idea is the first thing I look at, and the dossier gives me nothing to work with. Both founders are 21-year-old software engineers who worked together at Traba, a staffing technology company. There's no story about one of them being a frustrated data analyst buried under ad-hoc requests from marketing. There's no story about personally watching a decision go wrong because a team couldn't access their own metrics. The tagline is "Democratize data" -- which is fine, but it's also a phrase that could have been generated by feeding "enterprise AI startup ideas" into ChatGPT. The best startup ideas come from founders who can't not work on the problem. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, Dropbox was inevitable for him personally. I see no evidence that Datost is inevitable for these founders specifically.

The Slack-native angle deserves a fair hearing because it represents a genuine product insight. Meeting people where they already work rather than making them switch to a dashboard is the right instinct. But this has been tried before -- Statsbot and similar Slack analytics bots came and went. And there's a structural problem that's hard to ignore: Salesforce owns both Slack and Tableau. They have every commercial incentive to build a native AI analyst that bridges the two products, and they have the distribution, the data connections, and the enterprise trust to do it. Building on a platform where the platform owner has the most natural competitive advantage is a bet that requires extraordinary execution speed. Two 21-year-olds with no enterprise sales experience facing a potential Salesforce integration feature is not a bet I'd take with my own money.

Here's what would have to be true for this to work: the "AI agent with its own computer" execution environment would need to be meaningfully better than anything incumbents can build -- not just SQL generation, but genuinely autonomous analysis across databases, docs, Slack history, and code. If Datost could build something that understands an organization's entire data context so deeply that it replaces not just the SQL query but the data analyst's judgment, that would be a real product. And if they could achieve that reliability before Salesforce ships their version, the switching costs from accumulated organizational context could create a real moat. The Slack distribution channel, combined with LLM-powered analysis that actually works on messy real-world data, could create viral adoption within organizations. That's a genuine upside scenario. But it requires the LLM execution to be dramatically better than what any well-resourced competitor can achieve with the same underlying models -- and I see no evidence these founders have a technical edge that would enable that.

On the positive side, both founders can code. They've shipped a product, even if the GitHub repo has zero stars. Jason Wang has a computer engineering degree from University of Toronto. They're young, they're technical, and they got into YC. That's not nothing. But the gap between "two young engineers who can build a Slack bot" and "a team that can sell enterprise data access to security-conscious organizations" is enormous. Enterprise data trust is one of the hardest things to earn in software, requiring SOC 2 compliance, security reviews, and months-long procurement cycles. This is a type of schlep, but not one where being a 21-year-old hacker is an advantage.

I keep coming back to the same question: twenty well-funded companies are working on AI-powered data analysis right now. What do these founders know that all of them don't? I can't find the answer.
