# Alchemize -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Alchemize is a regulated-category swing wrapped in a B2B abstraction with no founder biography that touches the dock -- Samuel Fu's LinkedIn headline reads "Building AI-enabled customs brokerage," which is the headline of someone who picked this problem after reading about it, not someone who lived inside a broker's back office watching entries get hand-keyed. The dimensional signature is "unsexy regulated category, no tactile artifact, no lived founder" -- the regulated swing is real (sub-15% broker exam pass rate per Mordor Intelligence 2026, $3T in US imports flowing through this pipe) but Alchemize is missing the two ingredients I weight hardest: a founder *of* the place, and a thing I can physically observe.

Founder origin is where Alchemize loses me. On my ladder the HIGH rung is Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) -- shipped Oculus from his parents' garage before pivoting to defense, his biography put him in the room. The MID rung is Hassabis at DeepMind (~2013), pedigreed but a chess-prodigy-neuroscientist-game-designer polymath whose cross-domain compounding I let override the outsider preference. The LOW rung is my own Pass example -- two ex-FAANG PMs, Stanford CS, who discovered the problem in a memo. Alchemize sits a hair above the LOW rung: Samuel Fu is Columbia with an unconfirmed Netflix mutual-connection signal, Robert Hou's background was not even retrievable from LinkedIn (fetch status 999). Neither founder shows a prior role at Expeditors, Livingston, a freight forwarder, CBP, or a licensed brokerage. The dossier surfaces zero "I watched my dad's import shipment sit for three weeks in Long Beach" moment, which is exactly the moment I am looking for in a customs-brokerage founder.

Physical-world verifiability is the other floor. The HIGH rung is Flock Safety (~2018) -- I literally read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café before I knew what the company did, and a year later the cameras were on neighborhood poles. The MID rung is Anduril (2017) -- real hardware, but I can't fly a drone home from a demo. The LOW rung is the dev-tools-API-abstraction Pass. Alchemize lands on the LOW rung: there is no tryalchemize.com product I can install, no app I can hand a friend, no shipment I can ride along on. A B2B customs entry filed through CBP ACE is the definition of an artifact I cannot observe with my own eyes, and the dossier confirms no Product Hunt, no HN Launch, no public pricing page -- the typical bread crumbs that let me trail a normal human into the product are absent.

"Magically weird" -- Alchemize is the opposite. Crusoe (~2018) on the HIGH rung was three uninvestable things stapled together; Niantic (~2015) on the MID rung was an AR-walking-game most VCs dismissed; the LOW rung is the consensus YC AI darling with six funds bidding. Alchemize pattern-matches to LOW: "AI-native [legacy vertical] brokerage" was a YC pitch template by 2025, Flexport shipped an AI Customs Suite in October 2025 claiming a 0.2% auditor error rate, KlearNow.AI is sitting on $69M, and Amari AI ($4.5M, First Round + Pear) is selling the same capability *to* the brokers Alchemize wants to replace. Four credible entrants in eighteen months is not "find them before it becomes consensus" -- it is the consensus.

Single-CEO authority gets the absent-evidence default. The HIGH rung is Musk at SpaceX (2008); the MID rung is John Hanke at Niantic (~2015), unambiguous solo operator; the LOW rung is HQ Trivia (2017) Yusupov/Kroll whose unresolved tiebreaker contributed to the 2020-21 collapse. Alchemize is two co-founders with no public designation of who breaks ties on Fu vs. Hou disagreements -- I take the silence as "they haven't had the conversation yet," which lands at the persona default. Regulated swing partially redeems Alchemize: the customs-broker license is a real gating function (sub-15% pass rate, aging 43+ workforce), and the category sits in my "too unsexy" lane next to Vigil Labs (August 2025) -- but the dossier does not disclose which founder, if either, actually holds a broker license, and without that license Alchemize literally cannot file an entry with CBP.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Alchemize either gets acqui-hired by KlearNow.AI or a mid-tier broker for sub-$25M after burning eighteen months trying to win Power-of-Attorney filings away from incumbent relationships, or pivots to the Amari AI model and sells the same automation *to* existing licensed brokers once the seat-of-record fight proves harder than the deck implied. The mechanism: AI capability commoditizes (Flexport already shipped, $73M+ of competing capital is deployed across KlearNow, Amari, Altana), so durable margin lives in customer relationships and licenses, both of which a two-person team without documented customs domain history will struggle to compound faster than the incumbents now mobilizing.

The strongest bull case borrows my DeepMind exception -- if Fu and Hou are polymath generalists, the regulated category compounds in their favor as they accumulate proprietary HTS classification data per cleared entry, and tariff volatility (the techbuzz.ai "trade policy chaos" framing) is a real demand catalyst that didn't exist in 2022. Hassabis didn't grow up in oilfields either, and that bet worked. The bull case stings -- but Hassabis was a publicly visible cross-domain prodigy with a track record I could name in three domains by 2013, and Alchemize's dossier surfaces neither a polymath signal nor a domain signal for either Fu or Hou; one Columbia line and one un-retrievable LinkedIn does not clear the DeepMind bar. The single verifiable condition that flips Alchemize for me: documented evidence that either founder holds a licensed customs broker credential or spent two-plus years inside a freight forwarder, import operation, or CBP role -- the autobiographical contact with the problem that I haven't seen yet.
