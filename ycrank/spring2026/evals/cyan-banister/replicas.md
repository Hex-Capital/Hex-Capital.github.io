# Replicas -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$570 million in disclosed funding poured into autonomous coding agents in 2025-2026 alone — Cognition's $400M at a $10.2B valuation, Factory's $150M at $1.5B, OpenHands' $23.8M with 60,000 GitHub stars. When I look at a space where that much capital is already chasing the same thesis, my instinct is to walk, not run. Replicas is building background coding agents for engineering teams, running Claude Code and Codex in sandboxed VMs triggered from Slack, Linear, and GitHub. The product works. The timing is real. But this is one of the most consensus-attractive categories in tech right now — 51% of developers use AI tools daily, 90% of Fortune 100 companies deploy Copilot. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "AI that writes code." Every serious investor in Silicon Valley is actively looking for this deal. That's the opposite of what I look for. My biggest wins came from spaces that made people nervous — rockets blowing up, surveillance cameras, male birth control. Replicas lives in a space where the room nods along approvingly, and that nod is the danger signal.

Connor Loi is a University of Waterloo CS student who interned at Helicone, a YC W23 LLM observability company, for roughly eight months, where he built their AI Gateway (573 GitHub stars) and maintained the platform at scale. He won a hackathon prize at CalHacks 11.0. He built a Rust PR stacking tool with 8 stars. These are real technical credentials for a pre-seed founder. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — the dossier gives me a career trajectory, not a biographical forge. There's no evidence of a life experience that made Replicas inevitable for Connor specifically. No survival under constraint, no personal encounter with the problem that predates the company, no evidence he'd be building this without funding. "I was at an AI observability startup and saw an opportunity in coding agents" is market pattern-matching, not mission inevitability. Replicas could have been founded by any of a thousand capable Waterloo engineers who noticed the same gap. The absence of that story isn't just a missing data point for me — it's the data.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Replicas has six named customers — Mintlify, Helicone, Moda, Dart, Sorce, Bluma — which is real traction for a solo founder at pre-seed. The "30% of PRs" claim, if verifiable, suggests genuine product stickiness. The $30/seat Developer tier undercuts Devin's original $500/month by an order of magnitude, creating accessible bottom-up adoption. The multi-platform trigger surface — Slack, Linear, GitHub, API — differentiates from GitHub Copilot's walled garden, and teams that build automation workflows around Replicas-specific hooks and Environments configurations face real switching friction. The RTFKT exception in my framework acknowledges that cultural timing at a narrow window can substitute for infrastructure moats. Could Replicas be at such a window — where the orchestration layer winner gets locked in before GitHub expands its integration surface? The answer is: the window favors distribution, and GitHub already has 90% of Fortune 100. RTFKT succeeded because Nike was an identifiable acquirer in a genuinely novel category. Replicas competes in a category where the incumbent owns the platform.

Replicas runs third-party models — Claude Code and Codex — with no proprietary model, no proprietary data, and no patents. The dossier states plainly: "The core workflow — send a task description to an LLM coding agent running in a sandboxed container, output a PR — is architecturally reproducible." Lee Jacobs, my partner at Long Journey, said it directly: "Not some B.S. SaaS company or AI wrapper." I'm not calling Replicas B.S. — the execution is real, the customers are real. But when I run the ten-year question — where does this technology end up? — I can't see what Replicas becomes that Anthropic, OpenAI, GitHub, or Linear can't replicate in a quarter by adding a "run agent" button to their existing products. Crusoe Energy looked like a crypto gimmick on the surface but was secretly building energy infrastructure — portable data centers that could go anywhere there was stranded power. The weird surface hid something infrastructural underneath. Replicas looks like a developer tool on the surface, and underneath... it's a developer tool. There's no hidden infrastructure layer waiting to emerge.

Engineering backlogs are a real problem, but they're a capacity constraint, not an opaque system exploiting information asymmetry. Developers know exactly what's in their Jira. They know what tickets cost them time. Nobody is being extracted from by a hidden structure — they're just busy. Compare Replicas to Affirm, which attacked credit card opacity, or Ownwell, which exposed the property tax system, or Carta, which demystified cap table ownership. Those companies revealed something hidden and shifted power from gatekeepers to users. Replicas makes already-visible work go faster for already-well-compensated professionals. That's valuable, but it's optimization of a transparent market — exactly the pattern my portfolio conspicuously lacks.

One verifiable condition would flip me: if Connor Loi has a biographical story I can't see in the dossier — something that makes coding agent infrastructure deeply personal rather than commercially opportunistic — and if Replicas' Environments feature is evolving toward a proprietary runtime layer that creates genuine infrastructure lock-in beyond what GitHub or Linear can replicate, I'd reassess. The dimensions that locked this verdict are Contrarian Conviction and Invisible System. Replicas scores near the bottom of both: it operates in the most consensus-funded category in current tech (developer AI tools, $570M+ deployed by competitors) and targets an already-transparent market with no hidden structure to disrupt. The founder's biographical forge is absent from the record, and the defensibility picture — third-party models, reproducible architecture, GitHub as an incumbent with massive distribution — maps directly to patterns I've learned to avoid. I wish Connor well. He ships fast and has real customers. But this isn't a Long Journey bet.
