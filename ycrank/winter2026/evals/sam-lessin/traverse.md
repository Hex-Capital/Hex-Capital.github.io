# Traverse -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Traverse is that they pivoted from Clice AI -- an "AI communication OS for loan officers" -- to RL environments for non-deterministic work during the YC W26 batch itself. Both YC pages are still live with the same founders. I have a visceral reaction to mid-batch pivots toward whatever category is currently attracting the most lab spend. It's the clearest signal I know that founders are navigating by funding consensus rather than building from conviction. When I backed Solana at five cents a token, Anatoly Yakovenko wasn't chasing what was hot -- he'd spent years at Qualcomm thinking about proof-of-history and had a structural thesis about blockchain throughput that almost nobody agreed with. That's a sane person with an insane idea. Two young CS undergrads from Waterloo who were building mortgage broker tools three months ago and are now building RL environments because labs are writing seven-figure checks for them? That's a sane team chasing a very sane opportunity. The combination produces exactly the outcome I try to avoid: buying narrative at market price.

Run this through my core filter and the structural problems compound. RL environments for AI labs is one of the most consensus-validated markets in all of tech right now. Mercor just raised $350 million at a $10 billion valuation doing this. Surge AI bootstrapped to $1.2 billion in revenue. Applied Compute raised $100 million with an ex-OpenAI team. The fact that Traverse's CTO Zachary Yu previously worked at Mercor is both the strongest and weakest part of the story -- he has insider knowledge of how labs consume these products, but he's also a former employee of a company with three hundred and fifty million dollars that explicitly lists healthcare and law among its target domains. I keep coming back to the question I always ask: if every VC wants in, the premium has already been arbitraged away. Every VC wants into RL infrastructure right now. This isn't a market where non-consensus conviction gets you an asymmetric entry price.

The deeper issue -- and this is where my framework gets really uncomfortable with Traverse -- is that this business fails my "cherry on top" test so completely it almost wraps around. Would this business exist without AI? Obviously not. The entire value proposition is selling training infrastructure to AI labs. This is not a painting company that uses software to schedule crews more efficiently. This is not a parking infrastructure business with installed hardware. This is pure AI-for-AI -- software products sold to software companies to make their software better. My thesis says the investable AI play is businesses that would be great pre-AI but where AI non-obviously accelerates them. Traverse is the inverse: a business that exists only because of AI and that gets commoditized the moment a better model reduces the need for specialized RL environments or an incumbent with actual domain experts in medicine and law decides to build this capability in-house.

The bull case deserves real engagement. If formalizing subjective verification in medicine, law, and business strategy is genuinely a different kind of hard -- requiring not just crowd-sourced annotators but deeply integrated ML-plus-domain teams that take years to assemble -- then maybe Traverse could carve out a defensible niche that the crowd-sourcing giants can't replicate with their existing workforce models. Maybe Zachary's Mercor experience means he knows exactly where their approach breaks down in non-deterministic domains. And the TAM growth is real: Anthropic's RL environment spend growing three to five times into 2026 means the pie is expanding fast enough that specialized providers can thrive alongside generalists. If all of that is true, this could be a $100M+ outcome as the go-to provider for the hardest verification challenges. But the structural problem remains: the founders are computer science students, not doctors or lawyers. The "deep domain expertise" they claim is necessary for non-deterministic verification is not expertise they possess. They're building the scaffolding to combine ML understanding with domain knowledge, but they don't have the domain knowledge themselves and haven't demonstrated they can recruit or retain the specialists who do. Mercor can hire any domain expert on Earth with their war chest.

The buyer concentration issue is the final nail. Traverse is selling to maybe five organizations globally. That's not building a protocol or a primitive. That's being a vendor. Venmo created a new payment primitive that millions of people and businesses built workflows around. Helium created a decentralized wireless protocol that hotspot operators and applications integrated with. Traverse sells datasets to Anthropic and OpenAI. There are no network effects, no compounding dynamics, no scenario where Traverse becomes infrastructure that other companies build on. The ceiling isn't infinite -- it's capped by the procurement budgets of a handful of frontier labs. When I'm evaluating whether something has "infinity optionality," I need to see a credible path to becoming a layer that compounds. I don't see it here.
