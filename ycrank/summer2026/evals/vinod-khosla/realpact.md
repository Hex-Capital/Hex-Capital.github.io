# RealPact -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

RealPact is a workflow-automation SaaS dressed in voice-agent language, sold by two Dartmouth undergrads into four New Hampshire brokerages -- and the highest thing it has going for it (founders who shipped CourseMe to 90% of Dartmouth before graduating) is paired with the lowest thing in my framework (a success-state where Four Seasons Sotheby's saves twenty minutes per transaction). That high/low pairing is the analytical signature. Capable young builders, optimization play. I have a name for this pattern and I pass on it in thirty seconds.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the consequence anchor on my ladder, and I bring it up not to be unfair to RealPact but to be honest about the question I actually ask first. The shared mechanism on the consequence dimension is "what world-state exists if this company hits maximum ambition," and the OpenAI answer was a civilization with synthetic cognition. The RealPact answer, read straight off realpact.ai's own description, is that Deed Agent, Tax & Vision Agent, Property Agent, and Transaction Agent collectively eliminate the re-keying step between a county registry and a purchase contract at a $2.1B?$11B transaction-management software market. No incumbent regime collapses -- Dotloop, currently inside Zillow Group with roughly 50% share per Paperless Pipeline 2026, keeps existing -- it just gets faster paperwork. This is the exact "30% better" inversion of the **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) pattern I flag as a red flag.

**Range Fuels** (~2007) is the low rung on my technical/capital-risk ladder, and the failure mechanism there was that the science miracles were real but the capital plan understated burn -- the company died because the underlying physics was hard. RealPact's dossier reveals the inverse pathology, which is just as disqualifying for my edge: there is no technical miracle to underwrite. Parsing scanned tax assessments with vision models and auto-filling contracts with an LLM is something Dotloop, SkySlope (400,000+ professionals per skyslope.com), Brokermint, DocuSign, or any YC S27 team can ship in two quarters given commodity model availability. The dossier itself concedes this under "Commoditization risk." When the technical risk is low enough that any Tier-1 generalist can fund the deal, I am over-resourced and the firm has no edge -- I leave it to Bessemer.

Founder-defies-expert-consensus is where mechanism analysis matters more than ladder placement, because the compounding question over eighteen months is whether Ranvir Deshmukh and Erik Peterson develop a contrarian view of brokerage workflow that the Dotloop product team would not. The dossier gives no evidence of a "too important not to do" framing, no public position contradicting the brokerage-tech orthodoxy, no instigator stance. CourseMe was a sympathetic execution play at a university, not a defied-expert moment. Over eighteen months this dimension compounds negatively for RealPact because the incumbent will ship the same feature set with distribution RealPact cannot match, and the founders have no thesis-level reason they would see the next move before Dotloop does.

Learning rate is the counterfactual dimension: what would have to be true for RealPact to flip a tier here? The CourseMe acquisition is a real prior-shipped-product signal -- not nothing, especially for two undergraduates -- but to clear the **Slash** (2024) bar that justified my $100M Series C I would need a documented pivot inside RealPact itself with stated reasoning, or evidence that Deshmukh and Peterson absorbed a market signal and changed direction. The dossier shows linear execution from idea to four NH customers in one geography. Absent a pivot moment, this scores at the mid-default, not the override level that rescued Slash from its consequence-magnitude problem.

Required-capital profile is the single-anchor parallel to **DoorDash** (2013), and the comparison cuts against RealPact. DoorDash was capital-light too, and I funded it -- the override was a consumer-commerce flow reshape with network effects on both sides of the marketplace, validated by a $72B IPO. RealPact's $500K from YC plus LeapYear Fund angels and a $20K Magnuson prize describes a company that will run on $3?5M to $1M ARR with zero network effects on either side -- per-brokerage SaaS in one state. I am over-resourced for this deal at every check size Khosla writes, which by itself is not disqualifying but combined with the consequence floor leaves no reason for me specifically to be on the cap table.

Thirty-six month forecast: RealPact reaches $1?3M ARR across roughly fifty NH and adjacent-state brokerages, then is acqui-hired by Dotloop, SkySlope, or a regional MLS consortium for under $25M -- or shuts down after Dotloop ships the same voice-agent feature set on top of its existing fifty-percent distribution footprint. The mechanism producing this is the one Paperless Pipeline's own competitor analysis already exposes: public-records scraping plus LLM autofill is feature work, not company work, and the incumbent owns the channel.

The strongest case against this forecast borrows from my **DoorDash** (2013) exception: capital-light SMB software with strong execution can compound into category leadership when the incumbent is sleepy, and Zillow Group has not historically been a fast-shipping AI organization. Deshmukh and Peterson hit 90% penetration at Dartmouth with CourseMe -- that is real shipping velocity, and Four Seasons Sotheby's International Realty is a non-trivial logo for a pre-seed. The bull mechanism is that voice-first agentic workflows become the brokerage default before Dotloop's roadmap reacts, and RealPact compounds switching costs through accumulated compliance audit trails. This stings because I have seen it work once. It loses against the dossier on two counts: DoorDash's override was network-effect consumer-commerce reshape with a TAM measured in restaurant-meal-occasions, not a $2?11B transaction-management SaaS slice; and RealPact's "SignPact, Inc." footer with "RealPact" branding hints at an unstated prior pivot or rename that the dossier cannot characterize, which is exactly the opaque-founder-history signal I treat as soft negative. Flip condition: a signed enterprise contract at >$1M ACV with one of Compass, Keller Williams, RE/MAX, or Anywhere Real Estate within twelve months. Until that lands, this is a pass.
