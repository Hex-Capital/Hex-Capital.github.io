# Laurence -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about Laurence that jumped out at me immediately: the team is legitimately world-class for this exact problem. A Jump Trading quant research lead who oversaw billions in live trading volume, paired with a Google/Meta ML engineer who built the Data Science Agent and moved new user retention by 18%. If you told me "these two people are starting a company," I'd lean forward. The "sane person" half of my filter is clearly satisfied — maybe as clearly as any pre-seed team I've seen this batch. But the idea? "Apply reinforcement learning to Amazon ad bidding" is not insane. It's the opposite of insane. It's what every technically sophisticated person looking at the Amazon ads market would propose. Pacvue is doing it. Quartile is managing $2B+ in ad spend doing some version of it. Teikametrics raised $65 million doing it. This is a sane person with a sane idea, and sane-plus-sane is where venture returns go to die — because the opportunity is already priced in, and you're betting on execution in a crowded field rather than on structural insight that nobody else sees.

The structural moat question is where Laurence really falls apart for me. The entire value proposition IS the algorithm. Strip away the ML, the reinforcement learning, the hourly bid recalculation — and you have nothing. No physical operations, no regulatory position, no protocol that others build on, no hard asset. This is the precise category I keep warning about: software as a business model, not software as a business tool. The dossier itself acknowledges the commoditization risk plainly — "the reinforcement learning approach to ad bidding is technically reproducible by well-funded competitors." I believe that statement completely. And worse than competitors replicating it, Amazon itself could build this feature into its native ad console tomorrow. You're building your entire business on one platform's API, optimizing that platform's auction mechanics, and hoping the platform doesn't decide to internalize your value prop. That's not a moat — that's a lease.

The quant trading analogy the founders use is clever framing, but it actually reveals the structural weakness when you think about it carefully. In real quantitative trading, the moat is physical: colocation, proprietary hardware, speed-of-light advantages, massive capital deployment, regulatory licenses. Jump Trading's edge isn't just "better algorithms" — it's billions in deployed capital, microsecond latency infrastructure, and decades of accumulated market microstructure knowledge. In Amazon ad bidding, there's no colocation advantage. There's no capital moat. The data is Amazon's data, accessible to anyone with API access. What Laurence is really building is a better brain on top of a platform they don't control, using techniques that are well-documented in the academic literature. That's a feature, not a company — and it's exactly the kind of feature that either gets absorbed by an incumbent or replicated by the next smart team out of a quant shop.

I want to be honest about the bull case because the traction is real: $10M in managed ad spend, growth from $2.5M at application time, 15-20% advertising profit improvements, 40% ACoS reduction, named clients. That's not a story — that's a working product generating measurable value. If you believe the data flywheel compounds meaningfully (more managed spend produces better models produces better results), and if you believe the quant trading market structure analogy holds (a few firms dominate and the winner's edge compounds), then Laurence could grow into a significant business in the $60B+ Amazon advertising market. The advisory relationship with Aaron Apple, who built and exited a top-75 Amazon 3P seller and ran an eight-figure PPC consultancy, gives genuine channel credibility. If this team executes at the level their backgrounds suggest they can, and if the RL approach delivers durably superior results to rule-based competitors, there's a real path to capturing meaningful share of a massive market. For another investor — one who believes in software moats and execution advantages — this is probably a strong bet.

But I'm not that investor. My Birchbox lesson haunts me here: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome. "We're the Two Sigma of Amazon advertising" is a great narrative that could expand to all retail media, all digital advertising, all programmatic buying. But if the moat is just "our models are currently better," that advantage is temporary by definition. Every model improvement gets arbitraged away by the next team with the same techniques and more data. This is an application sitting on someone else's platform, in a market with well-funded incumbents, where the AI capability IS the product rather than a cherry on top of something structurally defensible. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech company whose only layer is tech.
