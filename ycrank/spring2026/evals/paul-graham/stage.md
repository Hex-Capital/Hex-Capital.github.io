# Stage -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Stage isn't the founders or the product — it's the competitive landscape. CodeRabbit has raised $88 million. Greptile has raised $29 million. Graphite was acquired for reportedly over $290 million. Haystack, one YC batch ahead, is solving the exact same problem with a different visual metaphor. When I look at a space this crowded, the first thing I ask is: why is it this crowded? And the answer is that "code review is broken" is an idea that sounds good to everyone. Every engineer nods when you describe the problem. That immediate, universal agreement is precisely the signal I worry about. The best startup ideas provoke confusion or dismissal, not knowing nods. When the entire venture ecosystem has already poured hundreds of millions into variations of your thesis, the contrarian return premium has been competed away. Stage is entering a consensus market — and that makes the bar for the founders and the insight dramatically higher.

The founders have the right raw ingredients. Both Stanford CS, both from Five Rings, both varsity athletes. Dean Stratakos in particular has a genuine origin story: he led the AI initiative at a quant trading firm, built an in-house coding agent, and experienced firsthand how AI-generated code overwhelms human reviewers. That's real organic discovery — he lived in the future of AI-assisted development and found a missing piece. It's not Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's in the right family. The question I can't answer from this dossier is whether Dean and Charles have the animal quality — the intensity that goes beyond professional competence into obsession. Their credentials are impressive but frictionless: top school, top internships, competitive firm, startup. I don't see the cereal boxes. I don't see the story where they did something unreasonable to make this work. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes almost involuntarily, and the absence here is mildly informative.

The specific bet Stage is making — that human comprehension of diffs matters more than automated bug detection — is the most interesting element. Most capital in this space is flowing to AI reviewers: bots that scan your code and flag issues. Stage is arguing that the bottleneck isn't finding bugs but understanding intent. That's a genuinely different thesis from CodeRabbit or Greptile, and it might be right. AI-generated code is getting better at not having obvious bugs, but it's not getting better at being legible to the human who has to approve the merge. "Structured chapters" — organizing a diff into a narrative — addresses the comprehension problem rather than the detection problem. This is the contrarian edge, small as it is. But it's a UX innovation, not a technical moat. Any reasonably talented team could implement chapter-based diff organization. GitHub could add it as a feature toggle. Cursor, which now owns Graphite, could build it into their editor. The insight is real; the defensibility is not.

The bull case would go something like this: Graphite's absorption into Cursor leaves a vacuum for independent code review tooling. The comprehension crisis is real and getting worse every week as AI coding tools improve. Stage's "chapters" concept is deceptively deep — it's not just a UI rearrangement but a fundamentally different mental model for review, like the difference between reading a list of changed files and reading a story about what changed. If they execute well enough to become habitual for even a few hundred teams, the switching costs create a wedge. Dean's firsthand experience building a coding agent gives him product intuition that someone approaching this from market analysis wouldn't have. And this is developer tools — my favorite category. The pattern is there: hackers who experienced the problem building for other hackers who have the same problem.

But the bull case requires several things to go right simultaneously, and the evidence for extraordinary execution isn't yet present. They need to beat Haystack, which has a one-batch head start on an overlapping thesis. They need to grow fast enough to matter before GitHub or Cursor adds structured diffs as a native feature. They need to do this as a two-person team with no publicly visible traction, users, or revenue signal. The name "Stage" is a discoverability disaster — try searching for it and you'll get everything from theatrical lighting to Belkin products. These aren't fatal problems, but they compound, and I see nothing in the dossier suggesting the founders have the creative workaround instinct that would let them navigate this minefield with unusual agility. I want to see the founders in a room. I want to hear their stories. From the dossier alone, this reads as competent engineers in a crowded, consensus space — and that combination produces mediocre returns far more often than exceptional ones.
