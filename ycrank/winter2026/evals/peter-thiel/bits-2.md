# Bits -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

One hundred and forty-three startups. That is how many OpenClaw wrapper companies exist, collectively generating approximately $320,000 in monthly revenue — roughly $2,200 per company. SimpleClaw reached $30K MRR in two weeks and was listed for sale within a month. Multiple wrapper founders are simultaneously exiting their companies on marketplace sites. Industry observers describe a "90-day wrapper window." This is not a market. This is a Girardian catastrophe — the purest expression of mimetic rivalry I have encountered in years. Every founder is staring at every other founder, building the same product for the same users, and collectively grinding the entire category to dust. When I say competition destroys value, this is the textbook illustration. The food delivery wars burned billions before consolidating into low-margin oligopoly. The OpenClaw wrapper market is doing the same thing at micro-scale, and Bits is one of 143 participants.

The first question I ask is always: what is the secret? What does this company know about the world that most intelligent people actively disagree with? The implied thesis here is that OpenClaw is too dangerous for casual users and needs a managed, security-hardened host. But this is not a secret — it is a consensus observation. The project's own maintainer said publicly on Discord that it is "too dangerous for casual users." The ClawHavoc supply-chain attack was covered in every security publication. Emergent, with $100 million in the bank and 104 employees, already offers managed OpenClaw hosting. NanoClaw offers security-first containerization as free, open-source software. DigitalOcean and Hostinger offer one-click deployment. When every actor in the ecosystem — open-source contributors, venture-backed platforms, cloud providers, and 143 wrapper startups — all see the same problem and all build the same solution, there is no secret. There is only competition, and competition is structurally incapable of producing monopoly returns.

The founders are technically competent. Robbie Thompson has a Stanford Math/CS degree and traded bonds at Jane Street. Bailey Wickham has genuine systems engineering depth — a Markov-chain fuzzer in Go, a DNS client in Rust, a container implementation in C, and Arctic Code Vault Contributor status on GitHub. Both worked together at Console building AI agents, which is directly relevant experience. These are not "men in suits" — they are engineers. But competence is not conviction, and relevant experience is not a definite vision. Nothing in the public record suggests a specific, non-consensus theory about what the AI agent infrastructure landscape will look like in ten years. The product description — "opinionated, batteries-included distribution" — is a feature set, not a worldview. I want founders who can articulate exactly what the future looks like if they succeed and why that future is structurally inevitable. Here I see two capable engineers who identified a real but obvious problem and built a competent but undifferentiated solution.

The strongest bull case is this: OpenClaw's explosive growth (250,000 GitHub stars in 60 days) represents a genuine platform shift, the ClawHavoc attack created urgent enterprise demand for security-hardened deployment, and a YC-backed team with direct agent-building experience at Console could become the trusted name in secure AI agent hosting — a kind of Heroku for autonomous agents. If OpenClaw becomes the default agent framework and Bits captures the enterprise security segment, the company could expand concentrically from "secure OpenClaw hosting" into broader AI agent infrastructure, security tooling, and compliance. For this to work, three things would need to be true simultaneously: OpenClaw would need to win as the dominant agent framework despite its creator leaving for OpenAI, Bits would need to establish security-brand dominance over Emergent's $100 million war chest and NanoClaw's open-source alternative, and the wrapper market would need to consolidate around one or two winners rather than fragmenting indefinitely. Each of these conditions is uncertain; requiring all three simultaneously is a compound improbability.

The technology itself is not discontinuous. Klaus is a configuration and deployment layer on top of an open-source codebase — pre-installed integrations, permission controls, malware scanning. This is useful but represents no order-of-magnitude improvement over what a competent DevOps engineer can achieve with Docker and existing security tools. The 430,000-line OpenClaw codebase, with its known architectural weaknesses including plaintext credential storage and no skill sandboxing, flows through to every Klaus instance. Bits's security is a layer of paint on a house with structural issues in the foundation. When the upstream project undergoes governance changes — its creator is joining OpenAI, the codebase is transferring to a foundation, it has been forcibly rebranded twice due to trademark complaints — Bits inherits every tremor. Platform dependency without proprietary technology is the inverse of a monopoly position. It is subordination.

I pass. The absence of a secret is not a minor gap — it is the fundamental structural problem. A company built on a consensus observation in a market with 143 competitors, near-zero barriers to entry, a well-funded incumbent with 50x their resources, and a free open-source alternative addressing the same security concerns has no path to monopoly. The power law demands that every investment have the potential to return the entire fund. Even in the most optimistic scenario, Bits becomes a modest managed-hosting business in a fragmented market — a outcome that is commercially fine but structurally incapable of generating the returns that justify the risk.
