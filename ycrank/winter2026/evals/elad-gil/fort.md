# Fort -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most important data point in this dossier isn't about Fort -- it's about Whoop. In 2021, Whoop acquired Push, which was the closest existing product to what Fort is building: a wrist-worn device that tracks barbell velocity and rep-by-rep effort. Whoop had the technology, the team, and $454M in capital. They discontinued the standalone hardware and integrated only a simplified muscular load estimate into their platform. When an informed market participant with full access to the technology and the resources to execute chooses to downgrade a product category rather than build it out, that's a signal about the standalone market's viability. Most investors would read Whoop's retreat as "market gap." I read it as "the company closest to this problem, with the most relevant acquisition, concluded the standalone product wasn't worth pursuing."

The structural moment question is where Fort breaks down for me. What has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this possible now? The dossier points to Whoop's 122% YoY increase in strength workouts and the longevity movement driven by Attia and Huberman. That's a real behavioral shift -- more people are lifting weights and caring about progressive overload. But it's not a technology inflection. The IMU sensors, optical heart rate monitors, and ML-based exercise classification needed for automatic rep detection existed when Push Band shipped years ago. RepOne exists today. There's no GPT-2-to-GPT-3 moment here, no cost curve crossing a threshold, no capability jump unlocking something previously impossible. When the "why now" is purely behavioral demand growing, incumbents capture that demand by adding features -- which is exactly what Whoop is doing with its February 2026 muscular load update, and what Garmin and Apple will do if the market proves real. Compare this to Harvey, where the "why now" was a specific model fidelity threshold that foundation labs were delivering on a known timeline. Fort's "why now" is "lifting is trendy," which is true but doesn't structurally favor a new entrant over a well-resourced incumbent.

The product-to-distribution path is theoretically present but practically constrained by the hardware business model. Fort charges $289-349 for hardware plus $80/year for software. That's a hardware company with a software layer, not a software company with hardware distribution. Stripe's payment API created a developer relationship that distributed Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through the same integration point. Fort's wearable creates a consumer relationship that could theoretically distribute coaching, programming, or nutrition products -- but hardware-based distribution channels are fundamentally harder to expand than software ones. The switching costs from accumulated training data are real, but they're the same kind of switching costs every fitness app claims and few actually achieve. I don't see evidence the founders have articulated a multi-product vision that turns the device into a platform.

The strongest bull case: this is the PagerDuty pattern. Strength tracking looks niche and boring to most investors, but every person who lifts weights needs it, the existing solutions are embarrassingly bad (manual logging), and the market is growing at triple digits. The Tesla hardware team has shipped physical products at enormous scale, and the magnetic multi-placement form factor is a genuine innovation that competitors would need to replicate. If Fort nails accuracy, the per-rep kinematics dataset compounds into a moat that enables personalized coaching no one else can match. But this bull case requires at least three sequential miracles: (1) achieve validated accuracy for wrist-based velocity tracking across 50+ exercises -- a challenge significant enough that Push Band, with dedicated hardware, never fully solved; (2) manufacture and ship consumer electronics at quality and scale as a three-person team with no consumer hardware experience; and (3) build brand awareness and distribution to compete with Whoop's $260M revenue engine and the marketing budgets of Garmin, Apple, and Oura. Three miracles compounding low-probability events is exactly the pattern I avoid. Tesla battery cell design and Semi truck autonomy architecture are impressive credentials, but they're in a different domain than consumer wearable electronics -- the manufacturing tolerances, supply chains, form factor constraints, and go-to-market are fundamentally different.

The commoditization risk is the structural problem I keep returning to. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The core technology (IMU-based exercise detection from wrist motion) is not patented in a way that prevents replication." If Fort proves the market exists and the technology works, Apple adds it to Apple Watch in a software update. Garmin adds it as a feature. Whoop re-activates the Push technology they already own. Fort would need to have built such deep brand loyalty and data moats in the 18-24 months before incumbents respond that users won't switch -- and that timeline is extremely compressed for a hardware product that hasn't shipped yet and won't until Q3 2026. This is closer to "software-aware" than "software-driven" in the way I think about it -- the hardware is the business, and the software enhances it rather than defining the competitive structure.
