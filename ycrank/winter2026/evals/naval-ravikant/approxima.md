# Approxima -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most striking feature of Approxima is not what the founders are building — it's what they pivoted away from, and when. They entered YC W26 as SideKit, a mobile app SDK. Mid-batch, they became Approxima, an AI agent for autonomous code maintenance. The pivot happened at the precise moment that AI coding agents became the most consensus category in technology — Cognition's Devin reached $73M ARR and a $10.2B valuation, Factory raised at $300M, and the foundation model providers themselves started shipping autonomous coding agents as native features. Pivoting into this market isn't contrarian. It's running toward the loudest noise. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities. When I backed Bitcoin through MetaStable, it was dismissed as digital tulips. Those were bets against gloomy crowds. Approxima is a bet alongside the most exuberant crowd in technology.

The specific knowledge question here is straightforward, and the answer is unfavorable. What do these founders know about software maintenance automation that can't be taught to any strong engineer in six months? Both are 2025 University of Waterloo CS graduates. Selvaraj interned at Databricks and Microsoft — respectable, but those are rotational internships, not years of deep immersion in how codebases decay, how bugs propagate through distributed systems, or what patterns of software failure look like at scale. Pronev has competitive programming background, which signals algorithmic ability but not domain-specific insight into production software health. Their prior product was an iOS/React Native SDK — unrelated technology, unrelated customer, unrelated problem space. The pivot itself is the clearest evidence that they don't carry accumulated non-transferable knowledge into this domain. They're smart generalists entering a new field. That's exactly the profile my framework filters against.

The leverage architecture compounds the concern. Self-healing software agents built on foundation model APIs don't create a new form of leverage — they redistribute existing leverage. The LLMs do the heavy lifting. Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, OpenAI Codex — the providers of the underlying intelligence are shipping their own autonomous coding capabilities directly to developers. Building on top of someone else's leverage layer is the opposite of what I look for. When I backed Alchemy, it was infrastructure that every blockchain application needed — a platform layer, not an application layer. Approxima sits at the application layer of someone else's infrastructure, which means its value proposition erodes every time the foundation models improve their own developer tooling. One API update from Anthropic or OpenAI could obsolete the product.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that "self-healing" — proactive, autonomous detection and resolution without human initiation — is a genuinely distinct category from general-purpose coding agents; that foundation model providers won't build this natively (even though monitoring and fixing is a simpler task than novel code generation); and that two recent graduates without domain-specific knowledge will build better maintenance automation than teams backed by hundreds of millions in capital. Sweep AI, the closest prior analog from YC S23, attempted this exact positioning and pivoted away toward IDE tooling — which is either evidence that the niche is inhospitable or evidence that the opportunity remains open. If Approxima develops a genuine data flywheel from customer codebases — learning how specific architectures break and accumulating proprietary repair patterns — that could create switching costs the model providers won't replicate. But there's no evidence this flywheel exists yet, and the website wasn't even accessible at time of research. The product appears to be in its earliest stages of development.

I apply the intelligence-energy-integrity filter with limited data. No red flags on integrity. The founders are clearly smart — Waterloo CS, competitive programming, strong internships. But the energy signal from the pivot reads as reactive rather than obsessive. The best founders I've backed were working on their specific problem long before it became fashionable. These founders were building a mobile SDK three months ago. That pattern — jumping to whatever's hottest — is what I mean when I warn against founders who are more passionate about founding than about the specific problem they're solving. I don't see the decade-long obsession that would make me want to learn from them over a ten-year relationship.

This is a pass. Two talented engineers entering the most crowded, most consensus, most heavily-capitalized market in technology, without specific knowledge that would give them an edge, building on leverage they don't own, at a moment when everyone agrees AI coding agents are the future. The pricing of this category already reflects optimism. The asymmetric upside I look for — being right when most smart people think you're wrong — simply isn't present.
