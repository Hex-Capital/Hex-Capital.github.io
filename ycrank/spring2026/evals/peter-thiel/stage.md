# Stage -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know before I even evaluate the founders. CodeRabbit has raised $88 million and generates $15 million in annual recurring revenue. Greptile has raised $29 million. Haystack -- a YC S24 company pursuing what the dossier itself describes as "a nearly identical problem" with a different visual metaphor -- has a one-batch head start. Graphite raised $81 million before being acquired by Cursor for over $290 million. Cubic is another YC-backed entrant. And now Anthropic has launched its own multi-agent code review tool for enterprise customers. This is not a market with a secret hiding in it. This is a market where every participant sees the same obvious truth -- AI-generated code is creating review burden -- and is racing to build slightly different interfaces to address it. The thesis is consensus. When I count five funded competitors and two incumbents all pursuing the same insight, I am looking at the purest form of mimetic competition: companies imitating each other's diagnosis while differentiating on surface features. "Chapters" versus "infinite canvas" versus "AI bot comments" -- these are differences in metaphor, not differences in understanding.

What is the secret here? I cannot find one. The claim that AI-assisted coding creates review complexity is not something most smart people disagree with -- it is something every developer tools investor and every engineering leader already believes. The YC application itself frames the problem in language indistinguishable from CodeRabbit's pitch or Greptile's blog posts. A genuine secret would be a contrarian claim about how code review should fundamentally work that most experts would actively argue against. "Organize diffs into narrative chapters" is a reasonable product design choice, but it is not a truth that the world resists hearing. It is an incremental UX improvement that any product team at GitHub could ship as a feature release.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that "structured chapters" will become the default paradigm for how humans review code, that this paradigm shift creates meaningful switching costs, and that Stage can establish this standard before GitHub, Cursor (which now owns Graphite's team and technology), or any of the better-funded competitors do. I take the timing argument seriously -- Graphite's acquisition did remove the closest independent workflow competitor, and Dean Stratakos's experience building an in-house coding agent at Five Rings gives him genuine lived understanding of the AI-generated code problem. These founders are not tourists. But lived experience with a problem is Paul Graham's framework for evaluating startups, not mine. The question I ask is not "did you experience this pain?" but "what do you know about the world that others deny?" And I cannot identify what Stage knows that CodeRabbit, Greptile, Haystack, and GitHub's own product team do not.

The technology dimension confirms my concern. The dossier explicitly characterizes "structured chapters" as "a UX innovation, not a deep technical moat." This is the definition of a 1-to-n improvement -- taking an existing workflow (diff review) and reorganizing its presentation. It is not making something previously impossible suddenly possible. It is not an order of magnitude improvement in cost, speed, or capability. A well-resourced competitor could replicate the chapter-based organizational layer within months. There is no proprietary algorithm, no novel data pipeline, no hardware advantage. Dean's AI expertise from Stanford is real, but the product as described is fundamentally a frontend reorganization of information that already exists in the diff, not a new computational capability.

The Graphite acquisition is instructive in a way that should give Stage's founders pause rather than encouragement. Graphite's outcome -- acquisition for roughly $290 million after raising $81 million -- is a fine result for its investors but a terrible illustration of power-law dynamics. No fund is returned by a $290 million exit of a company that raised $81 million. The code review workflow category appears to produce companies that become features of larger platforms, not independent monopolies that dominate for decades. Graphite became a feature of Cursor. If Stage succeeds on its current trajectory, the most likely outcome is a similar absorption -- a useful capability folded into someone else's platform. That is not what I invest for. I invest for companies where the outcome, if positive, is so extreme that it alone justifies the entire portfolio. A code review UX tool, even a successful one, does not have that shape.

The founders are technically capable Stanford CS graduates with relevant quant-trading backgrounds and direct experience with the problem they are solving. I do not question their intelligence or their engineering ability. But intelligence applied to a consensus problem in a crowded market produces competition, not monopoly. Two smart people building a better diff viewer are competing with five other smart teams building better diff viewers. The endgame is margin compression, not market dominance.
