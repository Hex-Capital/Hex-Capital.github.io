# Auxos -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three people who fenced together on Team USA and have known each other for fifteen years is the strongest thing in the Auxos dossier, and the conspicuous absence of any pre-funding stunt is the weakest -- that pairing tells me Auxos is a tight, technically credentialed trio who haven't yet had to prove they're animals, attacking a category (digital twins for customer research) where Aaru just raised at a $1B headline and Simile pulled $100M while Ashton Daniel was presumably still inside BCG running panels for Fortune 500s.

The cereal-box pattern from **Airbnb** (2009) is what I look for first, and Auxos does not have a cereal-box. Chesky and Gebbia raised $30K by selling $40 boxes of Obama O's when no investor would touch them; Drew Houston shipped the **Dropbox** (2007) demo video to Hacker News before the seed check; Brian Armstrong hand-built an Android Bitcoin wallet to overturn his **Coinbase** (2012) rejection. The Auxos dossier offers me a YC launch tweet, a Cal.com booking link to Ashton Daniel, and example marketing-page numerics ("847 packaging tests") explicitly labeled illustrative. There is no rejected-then-rebuilt artifact, no unscalable revenue improvisation, no founder anecdote of grinding pre-YC. Animals leave tracks. I am not seeing tracks for Auxos -- I am seeing a Columbia fencing champion, a Duke engineer, and a Penn engineer with very good resumes.

The bright spot for Auxos is dimension two, and it cuts the way I want it to cut. Ashton Daniel "ran surveys, panels, and interviews" at BCG -- that is the exact workflow Auxos is trying to compress into minutes. This isn't an MBA spotting a $56B SAM from the ESOMAR report; this is someone who personally suffered the weeks-to-recruit-and-moderate pain. Kerry Lu built ad-simulation infrastructure modeling "40% of US e-commerce traffic" at Amazon Ads, which is adjacent to simulating ICP populations. Jerry Wu cites Instagram A/B latency as the founding inspiration. This reads closer to the Collisons hacking commerce at **Auctomatic** (2007) before **Stripe** (2010) than to the AI-wrapper MBAs from my pass example -- Auxos founders lived inside the problem before they named it.

Schlep test on Auxos is genuinely interesting. The differentiator versus Aaru (fully synthetic populations, TechCrunch Dec 2025) is that Auxos "sources and interviews real people" per ICP. Recruiting humans is exactly the schlep Auxos itself describes as broken in the incumbent business ("weeks to recruit, moderate"), and Aaru has explicitly routed around it by going purely synthetic. That is schlep blindness in the competitive set, and Auxos is running into the schlep instead of past it -- the **Coinbase** (2012) posture, in miniature. The unresolved question is whether per-twin recruitment cost amortizes across enough queries to undercut Qualtrics and Dynata panels, and the dossier doesn't answer it.

Hacker genealogy is thinner than the resumes suggest. Three CS degrees from Columbia, Duke, and Penn -- credential-by-credential, not hacker-by-output. The one real artifact I can point to is Kerry Lu's prior work at Vorticity (YC S19) on "the fastest seismic wave simulator in the world," which is the kind of shipped technical thing the Collisons' Young Scientist Lisp prize represents in the **Stripe** (2010) story. But no GitHub trails for any of the three Auxos founders, no open-source footprint, no Hacker News presence pre-startup. For this dimension to flip a full tier, I'd want to see Kerry's seismic simulator code public, or one of the three with a non-trivial OSS project predating Auxos. Absent that, I'm rating on Vorticity alone.

Cofounder configuration is where Auxos clearly clears the bar I set in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010). Three founders who fenced together on Team USA for fifteen years is structurally closer to the Collison brothers at **Stripe** (2010) than to a hackathon-paired duo. Athletic-trio chemistry is a real signal -- it survives the fights that kill most startups. I don't need the YC interview to surface this one; Auxos has the rare profile where the team risk is actually below median.

Thirty-six months out, my forecast for Auxos is acquisition by Aaru or a Qualtrics-class incumbent in the $30-80M range, not a Stripe outcome -- because the capital asymmetry against Aaru ($50M+ at $1B headline) and Simile ($100M Series A) will force Auxos into either a category-defining real-person-fidelity moat that the dossier hasn't yet evidenced, or a tuck-in. The mechanism is straightforward: Auxos's differentiator (one real human per twin) is also its cost driver, and without a pre-funding animal moment proving these three will out-grind the funded incumbents, the default trajectory in a capital-saturated category is consolidation.

The bull case that stings: Aaru is the **Kiko** (2005) of this category -- fully synthetic populations will hit a fidelity ceiling that real-person-anchored twins won't, and Auxos's fifteen-year trio with a CEO who actually ran the panels is the Collison-grade founder-market fit I claim to underwrite. Maybe Ashton is the one BCG escapee who turns into Patrick Collison. Maybe the recruit-once-query-forever unit economics work and Auxos becomes the default research substrate. I want to believe it. But "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) warns me that the best ideas look dumb on first read, and Auxos doesn't look dumb -- it looks reasonable, well-resourced in talent, and competitively crowded. The reasonable-looking pattern is where I lose money in the other direction. The condition that flips my verdict: Ashton or Kerry ships a named, paying Fortune 500 customer with a published fidelity benchmark against real respondents within six months. That would be the cereal-box moment for Auxos. Until then, I'm at Neutral.
