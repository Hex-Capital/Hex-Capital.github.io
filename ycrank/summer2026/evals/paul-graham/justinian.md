# Justinian -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Justinian is a lobbying firm with a real schlep and no hacker in the room -- Parth Badhwar has the Biden-Harris White House and Harris for President lines on his LinkedIn, and he is currently trying to hire a Technical Staff person at 0.50%-3.00% equity to build the "AI-native" half of "AI-native government affairs firm." That inversion -- the domain half staffed, the technical half advertised -- is the whole story of Justinian as I read it.

Coinbase in 2012 is the shared mechanism I want to test Justinian against on the schlep dimension: Armstrong walked toward bitcoin compliance because every other applicant in his batch was routing around it, and the grind itself became the moat. Justinian is doing something structurally similar -- Lobbying Disclosure Act registration, state-by-state lobbyist registration, services-margin economics that FiscalNote's software-plus-analyst blend already admits is unpleasant, and a Forbes-cited $3.13M Anthropic / $2.99M OpenAI 2025 federal lobbying market that says the willingness to pay is arriving. Where Justinian diverges from the Coinbase rung is that Armstrong was the compliance appetite; Badhwar is the political-network appetite, which is a different and more common animal in Washington, and the schlep here compounds into relationships more than into software the way Coinbase's did into custody infrastructure.

The reverse pattern -- an MBA-flavored two-person team pitching an AI wrapper with no shipped artifact -- is where I lose money, and Justinian's hacker-genealogy row reads uncomfortably close to it. Badhwar's background is BA Political Science at GWU, Deloitte, campaign, White House, then Head of GTM at Clipbook; there is no GitHub trail on the dossier, no open-source footprint, no competitive programming, no Patrick-Collison-Young-Scientist-Lisp-Prize equivalent, and the second team member at Justinian is not publicly named. When a company selling "AI agents that draft briefs and map stakeholders" is still recruiting the person who will build the agents, the "AI-native" adjective is doing load-bearing marketing work rather than describing an artifact. Vulcan Technologies literally shipped an AI agent named "Justinian" at vulcan.ai/agents/justinian before this Justinian shipped anything I can find, which is what happens when the software layer is aspirational rather than built.

On animal resourcefulness pre-funding, the dossier is silent in the way that matters. There is no cereal-box moment at Justinian -- no signed Anthropic pilot, no waitlist, no revenue, no scraped-together first client that Badhwar personally lobbied for before the check. What the dossier gives me is a Cal.com demo link on the founder's personal calendar and a Twitter account with 38 followers joined February 2025, which is the top-of-funnel of somebody who has not yet started. Compounded over 18 months, this dimension either flips hard -- Badhwar's Rolodex converts three named AI-lab clients on retainer in the first two quarters -- or it stays exactly here, and Justinian becomes a boutique services firm running on one person's network, which is a fine career and a bad venture bet.

On organic-idea fit, what would have to be true for Justinian to score a tier higher is that Badhwar himself was on the buyer side of this problem: a founder who tried to hire a GA firm for a startup and got quoted $30K/month and built Justinian because he couldn't afford it. That is not what the dossier shows. Badhwar was inside government -- White House, campaign -- which is the supplier side of policy, not the "early-stage company priced out of lobbying" side that Justinian's website claims as the wedge. It is adjacent domain knowledge, real and rare in a YC batch, but it is not the Drew-Houston-forgot-his-USB-stick pattern where the founder was the customer first.

The cofounder configuration at Justinian parallels early Airbnb only in count -- two people listed -- and diverges everywhere else that matters. Chesky and Gebbia and Blecharczyk had lived and roomed and built together; the second person at Justinian is not publicly named, and the dossier surfaces no prior working relationship, which means the in-person interview is doing all the work of assessing whether these two can actually run a services firm together under compliance strain. I would need the interview to flip me here.

My 36-month forecast for Justinian: a 5-10 person boutique government-affairs firm doing $1M-$2M in retainer revenue to a handful of AI-lab and Series A clients, wrapped in an internal LLM tool that never becomes the product, with no venture-scale outcome and a plausible acquihire into a larger GA firm or into FiscalNote for the client list. The mechanism is straightforward -- lobbying is a relationships business, Badhwar's relationships are real, and services-margin gravity plus the naming collision with Vulcan's "Justinian" agent will pull Justinian toward being a firm rather than a platform.

The strongest bull case against that forecast is the Loopt exception -- I funded Sam Altman before mobile-location infrastructure existed because his force-of-personality animal test result overrode weak product-market-fit timing, and Justinian could be the same trade if Badhwar's political network converts fast. The bear response is that the Loopt override was a person I sat across from and read; the dossier's proxy for that -- 38 Twitter followers, a personal Cal.com demo link, no press, no waitlist -- is the opposite of an Altman signal. What would flip my verdict on Justinian is a single concrete artifact before Demo Day: a named OpenAI or Anthropic retainer contract disclosed on Justinian's site, or a shipped policy-monitoring tool that a non-client can use and I can inspect. Absent that, this is a pass -- the founders are not animals on the record, the hacker seat is empty, and the schlep alone does not carry the check.
