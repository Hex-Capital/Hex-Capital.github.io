# Asendia AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

I regularly see YC companies building AI tools for specific enterprise workflows, and the pitch is always structurally identical: large fragmented market, incumbents are slow, we automate 40-70% of the manual work. Asendia AI is this archetype applied to staffing agencies. The consequence of full success is that staffing agencies become more profitable. Recruiters save five hours a day. Fill rates improve. That's a perfectly fine business outcome — and it's exactly the kind of outcome I have no interest in funding. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The world doesn't look different. A hundred million people don't gain access to something they couldn't afford before. The stated TAM is $656 million growing to $1.2 billion — a market so small it barely registers against the problems I care about. Even if Asendia captures a dominant share, the consequence is a moderately successful SaaS company. I want to either lose my money or make fifty times my money, and the ceiling here is structurally capped.

The technical approach compounds my disinterest. The dossier itself flags the core problem: "the underlying AI capabilities (LLM-based interviewing, candidate matching) are increasingly available via foundation model APIs." Four specialized AI agents — sourcing, SMS outreach, video interviewing, phone screening — all built on commodity LLM infrastructure. This is an integration and workflow layer, not a technology company. There is no proprietary model, no novel architecture, no data advantage that compounds over time. The "moat depends on execution speed, agency-specific workflow design, and ATS integration depth rather than proprietary AI." That's the definition of an execution play without a technology wedge. When I backed Impossible Foods, the technical insight was synthesizing heme protein — a genuine biochemistry breakthrough. When I backed Rocket Lab, the Rutherford engine used 3D-printed combustion chambers and electric turbopumps. What's the technical discontinuity here? Calling four LLM API endpoints instead of one? Fifty ATS integrations are plumbing, not a moat.

The pivot from B2C to B2B is the most telling signal about the founders. They built FAANG AI — a consumer interview coaching product — got Product Hunt traction, then pivoted to selling to staffing agencies. I could read this generously as learning velocity: they discovered the economic buyer is the agency, not the candidate. That's a real insight. But I could also read it as searching — no product-market fit in B2C, so they're trying B2B with no public evidence of any customer traction post-pivot. Zero B2B revenue signal. Zero named customers. Zero LOIs. The corporate backgrounds (Google, Microsoft, Infineon, Siemens) are solid but are exactly the kind of resume-driven profile I discount. These are competent professionals following a playbook, not outsiders with irreverent conviction attacking an impossible problem.

The strongest bull case deserves real engagement. Paradox was acquired by Workday after raising $250M+, proving that AI recruiting platforms can reach significant scale. That acquisition removed the dominant independent player and created a structural gap. If Asendia can become the next standalone AI layer for staffing agencies, there's a real business — maybe a $500M-$1B outcome. The 50+ ATS integrations suggest real engineering effort, and the compliance certifications (SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA) create modest barriers. But here's what makes this a clear pass for me: the company is selling tools to staffing agencies — the very intermediaries that make hiring expensive. The most consequential version of this company would replace staffing agencies entirely by offering AI recruiting direct to employers at near-zero marginal cost. That would be interesting. That would collapse a 15-25% fee structure. Instead, they're reinforcing the existing cost structure by making the intermediary slightly faster. They've chosen the safe customer and the incremental value proposition. I don't fund companies designed to generate 3-5x returns. I fund companies where success means the world is different.
