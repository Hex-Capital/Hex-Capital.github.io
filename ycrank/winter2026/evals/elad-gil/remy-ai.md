# Remy AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing I see, and it's hard to look past. Nimble has $221 million. Covariant has $222 million. Physical Intelligence has over a billion. Dexterity sits at a $1.65 billion valuation with FedEx and UPS as customers. Pickle Robot has $113 million and UPS is ordering 400 units. This is a two-person team with YC funding entering a market where the existing players have collectively raised over $1.5 billion and are already in production warehouses. The warehouse robotics market isn't "too boring" or "too niche" -- it's one of the most heavily capitalized categories in robotics right now. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody else was funding operations alerting. That's what made it structurally interesting. Remy AI is entering a market where the venture community has already placed enormous bets.

The "why now" story doesn't resolve this concern. Yes, VLA models crossed practical performance thresholds in 2024 with Physical Intelligence's π0 and Covariant's RFM-1. Yes, warehouse labor costs climbed 7-9% year-over-year with vacancy rates above 8%. But these are exactly the same catalysts every warehouse robotics startup cites. The structural moment driving this market has been legible to the entire venture ecosystem for at least two years. When I backed Harvey, the structural moment -- GPT scaling curves predicting legal-grade fidelity -- was visible to me but not to most investors. Here, the inflection is consensus. That doesn't mean nobody wins, but it means the market isn't underpriced by the venture community, which compresses the return profile for an angel check.

The brownfield angle is the one piece of the thesis that activates my framework. Targeting the 80% of warehouses with zero automation -- particularly mid-market 3PLs that can't justify $1M+ capital-intensive systems -- maps to the pattern I've seen work with Gusto and Samsara. A massive underserved segment that incumbents won't touch because per-deal revenue is too low. The "deploy in days by taking photos of the customer's environment" claim, if real, would represent a genuine distribution innovation. But here's where my "software-driven versus software-aware" filter applies: this is fundamentally a hardware business. The AI makes the robots better, but the margin structure is physical -- manufacturing, shipping, maintenance, field service. You can't iterate on hardware the way Stripe iterates on API features. And the company's core technical differentiation -- a training pipeline that requires less data than standard VLA fine-tunes -- faces direct commoditization risk from open-source VLA models and much better-funded competitors building foundation models for robotics. When Alibaba releases an open-source VLA and Physical Intelligence is spending hundreds of millions on robotic foundation models, a two-person team's proprietary training pipeline is a fragile moat.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: (1) the brownfield deployment model is genuinely different from what Covariant, Nimble, and others are building; (2) the data-efficiency advantage in their training pipeline persists despite rapid VLA commoditization; and (3) the team can scale from two people to production deployments in competitive time. That's my problem -- this plan requires multiple miracles. Building reliable custom hardware for demanding warehouse environments is one miracle. Maintaining a training pipeline advantage against billion-dollar competitors is a second. Achieving "days not months" deployment speed is a third. Each is individually difficult; compounding them makes the probability very low. Compare this to Harvey's structure, where the single miracle -- model fidelity -- was being delivered by foundation model labs, freeing Harvey to focus entirely on workflow and distribution. Here, Remy needs to solve the AI, the hardware, and the deployment model all at once.

The founders have real credentials. Ben Kaye's CVPR 2025 publication from Oxford's Visual Geometry Group on 3D reconstruction is directly relevant, and Oscar Brisset's BCG experience advising Fortune 500 logistics companies on warehouse automation gives the CEO genuine domain knowledge. That founder-market fit is meaningful. But I don't see evidence of shipped product, rapid iteration, or any traction signal -- no customers, no pilots, no LOIs, nothing. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of traction, but I do need to see some evidence that the team has moved from research to building. The dossier shows academic credentials and consulting experience, not execution velocity.

I'm passing. The market is real and large, but it's consensus-hot with extraordinary capital concentration among incumbents. The brownfield insight is interesting but the path requires multiple sequential breakthroughs -- hardware, AI, and deployment -- against competitors with 100x the capital and existing production deployments. The technical moat faces rapid erosion from VLA commoditization. This is a company I'd want to revisit in 12 months if they've demonstrated production deployments and their data-efficiency claims hold up under independent validation, but today the risk profile doesn't match my framework for deploying personal capital.
