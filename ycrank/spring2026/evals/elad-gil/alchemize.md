# Alchemize -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Alchemize is the rare pre-seed where the *catalyst* paragraph writes itself — Trump-era tariffs in 2025 broke the customs broker workflow visibly enough that the Washington Post wrote a piece about it in November — and yet the *founder* paragraph collapses into "Columbia graduate" and a LinkedIn profile that can't be disambiguated from three other Robert Hous. That pairing — real re-opening, illegible runners — is the entire signature of Alchemize and the entire reason this doesn't pencil for me at consensus pre-seed terms.

On the catalyst axis, Alchemize is genuinely in **Anduril** (2022) territory in *shape* if not in *team*. Anduril worked because Ukraine made Pentagon software inadequacy a 24-month named event, and the customs equivalent is real here: NCBFAA (June 2025) and the Washington Post (Nov 2025) both document that "vague executive orders, overlapping tariffs and reduced access to federal guidance" cratered broker capacity and spiked importer demand. That is a specific, dated, regulatory step-function — exactly the entry pattern I look for. Where Alchemize diverges from the Anduril analogy is that Trae Stephens had already built Palantir's forward-deployed muscle before he stood up the defense-software thesis; Alchemize's dossier names no customs, trade-compliance, or logistics operating experience for either Samuel Fu or Robert Hou. The catalyst opened the window; the dossier doesn't tell me these two are the ones who walk through it.

On revenue velocity, the **Lyft** Series C pass is the wrong template — the right one is the AI-company-silent-on-growth profile I've been calling out since the Tim Ferriss episode in April: "if you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken." Alchemize's dossier reports no revenue, no customer count, no Product Hunt launch, no press. In a normal pre-seed cycle I'd default that to 8/25 and move on. Here the default has to come down because the *direct competitor* Amari AI emerged from stealth in February 2026 with $4.5M from First Round and Pear, 30+ customers, and $15B in goods cleared — and TechCrunch wrote it up. That's the "just working" disclosure pattern. Alchemize's silence isn't pre-seed stage-default silence; it's silence ten months into a footrace where the lead runner is already publishing splits.

The index test is where Alchemize's framing breaks for me. **Stripe** (2010) was an index on e-commerce because every merchant who came online had to touch the payments rail; that's distribution that compounds automatically. Alchemize is not the rail — Alchemize is *a broker on the rail*, and the rail is occupied by KlearNow (2,000+ customers, $66M raised), Flexport Customs (whose 2025 AI auditor is already reporting 0.2% error rate across 100% of entries), Caspian ($5.4M, explicitly CBP-licensed), Amari, and Digicust. To win, Alchemize has to take share *against* named, funded incumbents inside a 4.95% CAGR market — that is the inverse of an index position. The category grows 5% a year; Alchemize has to grow by capturing it from people already there.

Counterfactually, the founder-shipping dimension would flip a tier if the dossier surfaced even one named shipped artifact — a side-project SaaS, a prior YC company, a GitHub with a parser for HTS codes, anything. The dossier instead reports "No public repos found" for both founders, no Twitter for either, and search results so ambiguous the researcher couldn't pin Robert Hou against the Confido (YC S21), Permira, or Cognition same-name profiles. **Perplexity** (2022) went into my book because Aravind pinged me cold and shipped a built version of whatever we'd discussed the following week. The Alchemize dossier gives me zero signal in either direction on shipping rhythm — and in pre-seed, where shipping is the only legible signal, an absence of evidence functions as evidence of absence.

The single-miracle test is the one dimension where Alchemize actually scores well. The bet decomposes cleanly: LLMs are now accurate enough on structured-document tasks like HTS classification and entry filing — that miracle has happened — and everything else is GTM and CBP licensure. This is the same shape as **Decagon** (2023) in customer support or **Abridge** (2023) in medical scribing, both of which I funded. The catch is the licensure: BusinessWire's July 2025 Caspian writeup makes a point of "rare CBP-approved tech company and licensed customs broker" being a structural barrier, and Alchemize's broker-license status is undisclosed in the dossier. If they don't have it, the miracle isn't one — it's two.

My 36-month forecast for Alchemize: acqui-hired into a freight forwarder (most likely Flexport or a mid-market 3PL) at sub-$1M ARR by mid-2028, with Amari and Caspian having absorbed the standalone AI-customs-brokerage wedge that Alchemize is targeting. The mechanism: catalyst-opened markets attract three-to-five funded entrants within 12 months, and the one with the earliest disclosed customer compounding wins the category before the late entrants can build broker licensure and a sales motion. Amari is that company; Alchemize is the fourth-mover with a two-person team and no disclosed wedge.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **Brex** (2017) template: a YC company in a regulated, incumbent-heavy market where the operationally-disciplined founders simply out-executed the field, and where the wedge looked crowded at seed and obvious at Series B. Andrew Miklas (PagerDuty co-founder) is Alchemize's YC partner, which is non-trivial signal on operating coaching. The bear-side rebuttal: Brex's Henrique and Pedro had already shipped and sold Pagar.me in Brazil — they walked in with a legible prior exit — and the Alchemize dossier surfaces no comparable artifact for either Samuel Fu or Robert Hou. The verifiable condition that would flip me to invest: Alchemize discloses CBP broker licensure *and* a named customer count within striking distance of Amari's 30+ before YC demo day. Absent that, the catalyst is real but Alchemize isn't the runner I'd back to capture it.
