# Standout -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most informative data point in this dossier isn't about Standout -- it's about Placement. In 2019, Founders Fund backed a company with the identical thesis: "Hollywood agent for tech talent." Placement had ISA-based candidate-side monetization and strong backing. It didn't scale. It got absorbed into Exec. When the closest historical analog with better funding failed to build a sustainable business, the burden of proof shifts: what's structurally different now? The dossier's answer is "AI is better," and I don't think that's sufficient, because the failure mode for Placement wasn't a technology problem. It was a unit economics and monetization structure problem. AI reduces the cost of matching candidates to roles, but it doesn't solve the fundamental question of who pays and why they pay a premium for this channel over the dozen alternatives they already use.

Market structure timing is where I start, and the timing case here is thin. The dossier notes 43% of organizations already use AI for HR and recruiting, up from 26% the prior year. That's not a nonobvious shift -- that's consensus. Everyone in the recruiting space is bolting AI onto their workflow. When a market has that level of AI adoption visibility, the window for a new entrant isn't structural -- it's competitive. Compare this to when I looked at Harvey: legal AI had been a graveyard because accuracy requirements were absolute, and GPT-4 crossed a specific precision threshold. In recruiting, the accuracy bar has always been lower. A recruiter sending seven decent matches out of ten was already functional. LLMs make that cheaper, not newly possible. There's no stuck market cracking open here -- there's cost optimization in an already-functioning market, which is a fundamentally different bet.

The infrastructure-layer test fails clearly. Standout is a consumer-facing application built on WhatsApp -- someone else's platform -- using LLMs from foundation model providers -- someone else's technology layer. There's no API that other businesses build on, no developer tool, no platform play. The "data moat from candidate conversations" is theoretically interesting but practically nascent, and it sits one Terms of Service change away from evaporating if Meta adjusts WhatsApp Business API policies. I've seen companies get crushed by platform dependency -- building your core interaction layer on a channel you don't control introduces risk that compounds rather than diminishes as you scale. The companies I back in this pattern are the ones building the infrastructure layer itself, not the application on top.

The strongest bull case requires two things to be true simultaneously. First, that the "candidate agent" model genuinely shifts the locus of power in recruiting from employer to candidate, creating a new category worth billions. Second, that Standout wins that category against Clera (which already has 60,000+ users and 600+ startup connections running the identical model) and against every incumbent that bolts AI-agent features onto their existing employer-side platform. That's at least two miracles -- a business model miracle and a competitive-execution miracle -- before you even get to the question of whether the AI matching is differentiated enough to generate placement premiums. I've seen great founders get crushed by markets that require compounding breakthroughs. The fact that Mercor ($450M+ ARR, employer-facing) and Turing ($300M ARR) have already captured enormous scale on the employer side while no candidate-facing model has achieved comparable traction is a market signal I take seriously.

The founders are the strongest part of this picture. De La Chapelle's engineering tenure at Samsara and Dropbox means he's operated inside the kind of scaling organizations I know well, and his prior job board reaching 100K MAU demonstrates direct domain relevance. Aftalion's track record at Zealy -- COO of a product that hit 1.5M MAU and $3M ARR within twelve months -- shows real consumer-growth instincts. The combination of consumer-scaling expertise and recruiting-domain experience is directly relevant to this problem. But I've been doing this long enough to know that strong operators in a structurally unfavorable market tend to produce outcomes that are worse than mediocre operators in a structurally favorable one. The market structure concerns here dominate my assessment regardless of founder quality.

One more thing: the margin architecture has a fundamental retention problem. Recruiting is transactional. When Standout successfully places a candidate, that candidate leaves the platform. There's no recurring revenue, no negative churn, no expansion revenue from existing users. The lifetime value of a candidate is one placement fee (presumably 15% like Clera). To grow, you need a continuous supply of new candidates, which means your acquisition cost never compounds downward the way it does with infrastructure companies that embed in workflows. This is the opposite of the "high margin, negative churn" businesses I look for -- it's a business that needs to constantly refill the top of the funnel because success means your customer leaves.
