# ClaimGlide -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Prior authorization is one of the great schlep-blindness problems in American healthcare. Every doctor in private practice knows the process is broken. Every office manager has spent hours on hold with insurers, filling out payer-specific forms, chasing approvals that should have arrived days ago. The AMA has been documenting this pain for years. And yet, when you look at the funded prior authorization companies -- Cohere Health at $200M, Basys.ai backed by Mayo Clinic, Foundation Health at $26M -- almost all of them serve the payer side. They're helping insurers process requests faster, not helping providers submit them. The provider side for small private practices sits there, an obvious and painful problem that most startups have walked right past. That's genuinely the kind of gap that schlep blindness creates. The question is whether this particular founder found it the right way.

I can't tell how Nami Lindquist arrived at prior authorization. That's the problem. The prior company was Sync Labs, an AI platform for senior care facilities -- a different corner of healthcare entirely. It's possible that working with senior care facilities exposed Lindquist to the prior auth nightmare firsthand; those facilities deal with Medicare and Medicaid authorizations constantly. But that connection isn't documented anywhere. What I see instead is a smart technical person from Penn's M&T program who previously built one healthcare AI product, looked at the landscape, noticed the CMS-0057-F rule creating standardized payer APIs in January 2026, and decided to build for a large market with regulatory tailwinds. That's a plausible origin story, but it's also exactly the kind of idea that emerges from market analysis rather than lived frustration. When I ask "would this founder have built this product anyway, even without deciding to start a startup?" -- I can't answer yes with any confidence.

The strongest argument for ClaimGlide is competitive positioning. The provider-submission side for private practices is a genuinely underserved niche. CoverMyMeds is an incumbent focused on pharmacy prior auth. Cohere and Basys serve payers. Foundation Health targets larger systems with pharmacy operations. Nobody with serious funding is doing exactly what ClaimGlide claims to do -- end-to-end medical prior auth automation specifically for small private practices. If Lindquist can build real EMR integrations and accumulate payer-specific approval language that improves over time, the switching costs could be meaningful. The CMS rule creates a structural opening because standardized APIs make programmatic submission technically feasible in a way it wasn't before. And private practices, unlike hospitals, don't have the administrative staff to handle this themselves -- they need the automation more than anyone. If this founder turns out to have deep conviction about this problem and can recruit a co-founder with billing domain expertise, this could work. The schlep itself would become the moat.

But that's a lot of "if." The solo founder structure is my biggest concern, and it's structural, not cosmetic. Building and maintaining live integrations with eClinicalWorks, athenahealth, NextGen, and ModMed -- each with distinct APIs, authentication schemes, and data formats -- while simultaneously navigating payer-specific submission rules, HIPAA compliance, and customer support for medical practices that expect phone-call-level service -- that's an enormous surface area for one person. I've seen what happens when startups require this kind of integration depth. You need at least two people, and ideally one of them has lived inside the problem domain. Stripe had two brothers, and they still found the banking and regulatory schlep almost overwhelming. ClaimGlide has one founder with no documented experience in revenue cycle management or medical billing. I made Drew Houston find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox, and that was for a product with far simpler integration requirements.

Lindquist shows real signals of capability. The Penn M&T program is selective. SpaceX hires good engineers. Sync Labs won the President's Innovation Prize and raised $400K+ in non-equity funding while conducting seven pilot studies -- that demonstrates both building ability and the kind of resourceful fundraising that suggests real drive. Getting into YC at this stage is itself a signal. But I don't see evidence of the specific crucible moments that reveal relentless resourcefulness -- the cereal-box moments, the creative workarounds when everything falls apart. The breadth of activity (published book, Udemy course, two startups in different domains) could signal versatility or could signal someone searching for the right thing. At pre-seed I can't distinguish these.

The absence of any traction signal is notable even for pre-seed. No revenue, no users mentioned, no waitlist, no testimonial from even one practice. The website lists EMR integrations, which suggests some technical work has been done, but I don't know whether those integrations are live or aspirational. Zero traction doesn't condemn a pre-seed company, but combined with the unclear organic origin and solo founder status, it means I'm being asked to bet entirely on the problem and the founder's raw ability. The problem is real. The founder is smart. But I don't feel the gravitational pull of someone who discovered something they can't walk away from.
