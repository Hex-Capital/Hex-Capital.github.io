# Beyond Reach Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What grabbed me first about Beyond Reach Labs isn't the technology -- it's the structural echo of SpaceX circa 2002. A capital-intensive space infrastructure bet built on physics that sounds absurd to most people, founded by someone who spent years doing the foundational research everyone else ignored, paired with someone who actually built and shipped flight hardware. Solar arrays that launch the size of a dining table and grow to a football field in orbit using metamaterial geometry? That's the kind of sentence that makes most investors' eyes glaze over. Which is exactly why I'm still reading. The most promising companies are not popular, and they are genuinely difficult to assess. This one checks both boxes. And the $175M in LOIs at pre-seed -- even understanding that LOIs in space are often aspirational -- tells me that the people who actually need this technology already know what it means.

Here's what I don't have, though, and it matters to me: I don't know these founders' survival stories. Pele Collins grew up in South Africa and ended up leading parachute engineering at SpaceX for seven years -- the parachutes that brought NASA astronauts home safely for the first time since Apollo. That's extraordinary domain credibility. Mitchell Fogelson spent his PhD years at Carnegie Mellon inventing the core technology under NASA funding, running zero-gravity flight experiments, publishing the foundational papers. These are deeply committed people who have poured years into this specific problem. But I can't tell you what hardship forged that commitment. I don't have the biographical thread that connects their personal pain to this particular mission. Maybe it exists and the dossier doesn't capture it -- I'd want to sit across from them and ask my question before anything else. Without that story, I'm evaluating domain expertise rather than founder destiny, and those are different things in my framework.

What I do see clearly is my own pattern of backing infrastructure at technological inflection points -- the same pattern that drew me to SpaceX, to Crusoe Energy, to Anduril. Starship is about to crater launch costs, which makes power-hungry orbital applications economically viable for the first time. Orbital data centers, commercial space stations, lunar outposts -- all of these need orders of magnitude more power than current arrays deliver. Beyond Reach Labs is building the power infrastructure for that inflection, and their approach isn't incremental improvement on existing roll-out or fan-fold designs. It's a fundamentally different structural paradigm. Patented. Tested in microgravity. That's real technology risk, but not insurmountable technology risk -- which is exactly where I like to invest.

The co-founder dynamic here is reassuring in ways that matter to me deeply after HQ Trivia. Collins and Fogelson have known each other for thirteen years, since freshman year at Penn. They went through undergrad and the GRASP Lab robotics program together before diverging -- one into SpaceX flight hardware production, the other into PhD research on the exact technology they're now commercializing. That's a relationship that's been stress-tested across different life phases. The co-CEO structure does make me pause -- I always want to understand who breaks ties -- but the complementary skill split is clean: one builds and ships hardware to space agencies, the other invented the IP and holds the patents. That's not a 50/50 power struggle waiting to happen; it's a natural division of labor between the person who knows how to get something qualified and flown and the person who created what's being flown.

Where this company doesn't activate my framework is on economic access. I look for investments that create real opportunity for people who currently lack it -- the way Uber created income for drivers, or Affirm made credit transparent. Space solar arrays, however transformative for the orbital economy, don't directly touch the lives of underserved populations. More power in orbit could eventually enable global connectivity or climate monitoring, but those are downstream effects several steps removed. This isn't a dealbreaker for me -- SpaceX didn't have a direct poverty-alleviation story either -- but it means one of my core criteria simply doesn't engage.

The bull case for this company is genuinely compelling: if Starship delivers on cost reduction, the demand for orbital power will be enormous and real. Beyond Reach Labs has the only patented metamaterial-based deployment architecture, invented by one of the two founders, with NASA validation and zero-g testing already complete. The advisory board includes a former AFRL Chief Scientist who spent twenty-five years in space vehicles -- that's not decorative, that's a direct channel into the defense procurement system. If the Q2 2027 demo flight succeeds, flight heritage unlocks the LOI pipeline, and this becomes the power infrastructure standard for the next generation of space platforms. Crusoe Energy started as something equally weird and is now a $3 billion company on the Stargate project. Infrastructure bets at inflection points can compound in ways that are hard to model from the outside. I'd want to write a check here -- but I'd want to hear their stories first, face to face, before I could feel the conviction that moves me from interested to committed.
