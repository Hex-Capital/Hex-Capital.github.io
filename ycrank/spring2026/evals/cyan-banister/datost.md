# Datost -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Datost: this is one of the most consensus ideas I've seen in a while. "Put an AI data analyst in Slack" is the kind of thing that gets built at every hackathon in San Francisco right now. There are at least five funded companies doing versions of this — Scoop Analytics is literally in the same Slack-native positioning with a $3.5 million head start, Julius AI has two million users doing conversational data analysis, and the incumbents (Salesforce owns Slack, Microsoft owns both Power BI and Teams) have every incentive and capability to build this natively. When I think about the investments that have defined my career — SpaceX when everyone said private rockets were insane, Crusoe Energy when people laughed at mining Bitcoin on gas flares, Contraline when male contraception was too weird for most VCs — they all shared a quality of being genuinely unpopular. Datost is the opposite. Everyone agrees AI should make data accessible. The question is just who wins, and nothing in this dossier tells me why these two founders are the ones.

And that brings me to the founders, which is where I always start. Tell me your story — not the business. But I can't find the story here. Maceo is 21, went to Beacon School in New York, did a certificate at Baruch College, worked as a software engineer at a few startups. Jason Wang studied computer engineering at U of Toronto, worked at Confluent on data governance, then at Traba alongside Maceo. These are perfectly fine engineering backgrounds. But I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this specific problem — the hardship that forged the commitment, the lived experience that means they'll keep going when the company hits a wall at 2 AM and every rational person would quit. I don't hear it. I don't see the biographical reason why democratizing data through Slack is a calling rather than a career choice. At 21, Maceo has impressive hustle — founding a non-profit, shipping code at multiple companies — but hustle and grit born from adversity are different things.

Let me be fair about what would make this work, because I've been wrong before about young founders who surprise everyone. The bull case is genuinely interesting: data democratization IS a form of making invisible systems visible. Right now, if you're an operations manager or a finance lead at a mid-size company, your own company's data is locked behind a SQL skills barrier. You have to file a ticket with the data team, wait three days, and hope they understood your question. That's a real form of information asymmetry — people who can't code are second-class citizens in their own organizations. If Datost's agent truly works reliably across messy, undocumented database schemas while also connecting to docs and codebases, and if it spreads virally through Slack channels the way good bots do, there's a version of this that becomes infrastructure. Jason's Confluent experience on data governance gives him real exposure to how messy enterprise data environments actually are. And both founders working together at Traba means they've seen each other operate professionally — that's not nothing when it comes to co-founder dynamics.

But the bull case requires you to believe that a two-person pre-seed team with no visible traction, no revenue, no moat signals, and no public product differentiation will outrun Scoop Analytics (already funded, same positioning), Julius AI (two million users, $11 million raised), and the inevitable moment when Salesforce ships an Einstein-powered analytics agent directly into Slack. The core technical capability — LLM-powered natural language to SQL — is reproducible by any competent team with API access. The "broader connectivity" to codebases and data lakes is a feature, not a moat. And the enterprise data security trust barrier is brutal for a pre-seed startup — getting SOC 2 compliance, handling GDPR, convincing security teams to give a Slack bot read access to production databases and proprietary code — that's a mountain of work that favors established players, not scrappy two-person teams. I know what navigating hard security spaces looks like from my years at IronPort, and this team hasn't shown me they're equipped for that fight yet.

The co-founder dynamic is the one bright spot I can assess. Maceo and Jason worked together at Traba before starting Datost, which means they chose each other deliberately rather than meeting at a co-founder matching event. After what happened with HQ Trivia — a company that failed entirely because of founder conflicts despite having the right product at the right time — I always probe this dimension hard. A shared professional history is a real positive signal. But it's untested under the kind of pressure that breaks teams: running out of money, losing a key customer, pivoting when your thesis is wrong. I'd need to sit across from them and ask how they fight before I'd have real confidence here.

I respect what these founders are building, and I respect the energy it takes to go through YC at 21. But my portfolio is built on companies that are genuinely unpopular and difficult to assess, led by founders whose life stories explain why they're the only people on earth who should be solving this particular problem. Datost is a popular idea in a crowded market, built by capable engineers without a visible biographical connection to the problem. That's a pass for me.
