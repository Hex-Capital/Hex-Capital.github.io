# Thirdbrain Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Thirdbrain Labs is building post-training infrastructure in a space where Scale AI ($29B valuation, ~$2B revenue), Together AI ($3.3B valuation, 450K+ users), Labelbox ($100M+ ARR), and Snorkel AI ($1.3B valuation) are all expanding aggressively -- and where Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, and LLaMA-Factory are freely available. Scale AI launched Scale Labs in March 2026 specifically targeting post-training evaluation. Together AI expanded fine-tuning to 100B+ parameter models in September 2025. AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI offer managed fine-tuning natively. When I see a two-person startup entering a market where four billion-dollar-plus incumbents are converging on the same capability from different directions, my first question is not "can they compete?" -- it is "what specific knowledge do these founders possess that makes competition irrelevant?" The answer here does not survive scrutiny.

David Huang's ML background -- Stanford XCS224N certificate, Coursera certifications from 2015-2017, and a top-1% finish in Two Sigma's 2018 Halite AI Challenge -- signals genuine capability and sustained interest. But capability is not specific knowledge. These are credentials that thousands of engineers share. Compare Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, who spent years inside Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact retrieval and generation systems he then productized. Huang's background is self-directed learning, not years embedded in the fine-tuning infrastructure problem at a frontier lab. Margaret Zhang brings NYU Stern, a VC fellowship at INVC, a partnerships role at Spice AI, and a co-founding stint at Coalition Systems (defense AI). This is a business-development profile oriented toward fundraising and sales -- relevant to GTM, but it does not constitute the non-replicable technical insight that would make Thirdbrain Labs' "expert decision capture" workflow defensible against incumbents with 1,000x the engineering resources. If I asked "could you replace these founders with two other competent operators and retain the company's core insight?" -- the honest answer is probably yes.

The leverage architecture concerns me more than the competition itself. Thirdbrain Labs sits on top of other people's base models, providing a UX layer over increasingly commoditized fine-tuning compute. This is renting someone else's leverage, not creating your own. The moment OpenAI or Google ships a "customize this model with your expert feedback" button -- which is a product decision, not a research breakthrough -- the wrapper evaporates. Notion, one of my best seed bets, created its own permissionless leverage: a workspace that compounds in value as teams build more content inside it, with switching costs that grow every month. Thirdbrain Labs' "expert decision capture" could theoretically create similar lock-in if each client's feedback loops generate model improvements embedded in production workflows. But that flywheel is entirely hypothetical -- no customers, no production deployments, no data on whether expert decisions actually compound into durable model differentiation.

The principal-agent diagnostic raises a flag I cannot resolve from public data. Zhang co-founded Coalition Systems, a defense AI company backed by a16z Speedrun, where she reportedly traveled to Kyiv to meet with Ukraine Ministry of Defence operators. What happened to Coalition Systems? Whether Thirdbrain Labs represents a pivot, a parallel venture, or a clean departure matters. A founder who moves from defense AI coordination software to LLM fine-tuning infrastructure within a short window looks more like someone searching for the right company to found than someone who cannot stop building this specific thing. That is agent behavior, not principal behavior. I would need to hear Zhang explain why post-training infrastructure kept her up at night before Coalition Systems existed -- and I suspect that conversation would reveal that the market opportunity, not personal obsession, drove this founding.

The strongest bull case: the "expert decision capture" framing is genuinely differentiated from standard RLHF workflows, the timing is right because open-source base models (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek) created millions of potential fine-tuners who lack ML expertise, and the 2-person team can iterate faster than incumbents whose sales motions target Fortune 500 procurement cycles. If Thirdbrain Labs builds the interface that makes domain experts -- radiologists, manufacturing engineers, legal analysts -- feel like they are teaching a model rather than labeling data, that UX insight could be the wedge. Labelbox and Scale AI are structurally unable to serve small domain-expert teams because their cost structures require six-figure contracts. The democratization angle is real: giving non-ML teams access to model customization that currently requires hiring ML engineers removes a genuine gatekeeper. For that reason alone, Thirdbrain Labs scores above zero on my access-democratization filter.

The decisive dimensions are specific knowledge and leverage architecture, and both point the same direction. Thirdbrain Labs' founders identified a market opportunity through proximity to AI infrastructure -- not through years of obsessive engagement with the post-training problem that produced non-replicable insight. The product architecture rents leverage from foundation model providers rather than creating its own. In a market filling with billion-dollar incumbents and free open-source alternatives, the absence of founder-specific-knowledge moat is not a gap that execution can close. Escape competition through authenticity -- and I do not see the authenticity here that would make competition irrelevant.
