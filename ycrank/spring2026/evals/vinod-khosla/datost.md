# Datost -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is a company building an AI data analyst inside Slack, and the parent company of Slack -- Salesforce -- also owns Tableau, the dominant BI platform. I want you to sit with that for a moment. Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack and $15.7 billion for Tableau. They have every engineering team, every enterprise relationship, every data connector, and every incentive to build exactly this product. They haven't unified Slack and Tableau into a single AI analyst yet -- but when they do, it won't be because they couldn't, it'll be because the product roadmap hadn't prioritized it. Building your entire company on the assumption that a $300 billion platform owner won't ship a feature that connects two products they already own is not contrarian courage. It's a timing bet dressed up as a company.

Let me step back to my first question: if Datost succeeds completely -- every Slack workspace has an AI data analyst, every non-technical employee can query databases conversationally -- what actually changes in the world? People get answers to data questions faster. Data analysts spend less time on ad-hoc requests. This is a genuine productivity improvement, and I don't dismiss the market -- BI is a $34 billion category. But the consequence of success is a better workflow tool. No industry becomes structurally obsolete. The databases still exist, the data teams still exist, the dashboards still exist. Datost makes the front door wider; it doesn't rebuild the house. I have passed on dozens of companies that make enterprise workflows 30% more efficient. The people building them are often smart and capable, and the businesses can work. But the consequences of their success are, by my measure, not worth the opportunity cost of a brilliant team's careers.

The technology risk assessment here is sobering. The dossier states it plainly: "The core capability (LLM + SQL generation + execution) is reproducible. OpenAI, Anthropic, and open-source models can all generate SQL from natural language. Any Slack bot developer could build a basic version." The claimed differentiator -- "the first AI data analyst that has its own computer" -- is a sandboxed execution environment. That is competent engineering, not a technology moat. Microsoft has Copilot in Power BI. Google has AI in Looker. Julius AI already has 2 million users and $10 million in funding doing substantially the same thing in a different interface. When your differentiation is the channel you deliver through (Slack) rather than a capability nobody else can replicate, you are one platform update away from irrelevance.

The strongest bull case I can construct: data democratization genuinely is a massive unsolved problem, and meeting users inside Slack -- where they already work -- could create viral distribution that's extremely difficult to replicate through dashboards. If the "own computer" execution layer achieves meaningfully higher reliability than prompt-to-SQL competitors, and if the multi-source context integration (docs, Slack history, databases, code) compounds into something no single query can replicate, you could imagine Datost becoming the default way non-technical teams interact with data. Square started as a plastic dongle and became a financial infrastructure company. Maybe Datost starts as a Slack bot and becomes the universal data access layer. For that to happen, three things would need to be true simultaneously: Salesforce would need to fumble the Slack-Tableau integration for at least three years, the multi-source context would need to become a genuine technical moat that improves with scale, and the founders would need to navigate enterprise sales cycles -- SOC 2 compliance, security reviews, data governance -- as a two-person team of 21-year-olds with no enterprise sales experience. That is a lot of simultaneous requirements, and my portfolio taught me through KiOR that stacking multiple low-probability conditions is how you lose $160 million.

The founders are two 21-year-old software engineers who previously worked together at Traba, a staffing technology company. There is no evidence of contrarian thinking, no signal of an outsider attacking a problem from first principles, no indication that they are attempting something the industry considers impossible. Building an AI analytics bot in Slack in 2026 is consensus -- it is the kind of idea that every hackathon team at a data company has prototyped. The team is homogeneous: same age, same employer, same engineering background, no business or enterprise sales capability visible. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, everyone said a New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree couldn't build a launch vehicle. When I backed Pat Brown, a biochemist had no business making hamburgers. The signal I look for is experts telling you it cannot be done. Nobody is telling these founders that an AI Slack bot for data queries cannot be done -- half the industry is already doing it.

The timing wave is real: LLM capabilities for code generation and reasoning have improved dramatically, and the enabling technology is genuinely better than it was two years ago. But this is a wave that every incumbent and well-funded startup is riding simultaneously. Datost is not ahead of the curve -- they are arriving at the exact moment when Microsoft, Salesforce, Google, and a half-dozen funded competitors are all shipping the same category of product. Being early to a trend before it becomes consensus is how I made 160x on OpenAI. Arriving at the same time as every large platform company is how you get absorbed.
