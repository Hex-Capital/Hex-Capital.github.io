# Advocate -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Advocate is the commoditization shadow. The core engine — speech-to-text plus LLM-based CPT code mapping — runs on widely available components. Abridge, DeepScribe, and Suki already capture clinical voice data and are extending into coding workflows. CodaMetrix has raised $109M for AI medical coding. Adding care coordination billing codes to any of these existing pipelines is a product sprint, not a company. When the technology is rented from foundation model providers and the domain data (CPT codes, CMS documentation requirements) is public, the defensibility question becomes: what does Advocate know that these incumbents cannot learn in six months? I don't see a clear answer.

Specific knowledge density is where I always start, and here it's thin. Andrew Baran grew up watching his mother work as a care coordinator — that's proximity, not expertise. His career arc is McKinsey due diligence, then healthcare VC at Radian Capital, then founding. He observed the problem from the investor's chair. Justinas Banys built data systems at Garner Health, a care navigation unicorn, which is the strongest signal — he was engineering inside an adjacent domain. But adjacent isn't identical. Neither founder spent years inside care coordination billing workflows, fighting with CMS audit requirements, learning which voice-documented activities map to which modifiers on CPT 99490 versus 99491. The product isn't an extension of knowledge that took years to accumulate — it's an application of generally available AI capabilities to a well-defined but reproducible problem. Compare this to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, who had built the actual systems at Google Brain and OpenAI. There's no equivalent depth here.

The leverage architecture has a split personality. On the product side, it's clean — voice notes in, compliant billing packets out, zero marginal cost per facility added. AI does the coding work that would otherwise require a human medical coder. That's genuine permissionless leverage. But the distribution side is fragmented: skilled nursing facilities, home health agencies, behavioral health centers — small accounts, local decision-makers, relationship-driven sales. Waystar generates $1.1B in revenue selling to hospital CFOs through enterprise sales. Advocate will be selling to facility administrators through direct outreach. Each new customer requires a human conversation. This is the structural tension I watch for: code leverage in the product, labor leverage in the GTM. The product scales; the company may not scale the same way.

The bull case deserves honest examination. CMS expanded reimbursable care coordination codes — CPT 99490 alone is ~$62/month per patient — and most post-acute facilities don't capture this revenue because the documentation burden exceeds their administrative capacity. That's real money left on the table, and Advocate's "no new hires, no workflow changes, pure margin" pitch is the right sell. The incumbents — Waystar, CodaMetrix, Sully.ai — focus on physician encounter coding at health systems, not facility-level care coordination billing. The channel mismatch (facility administrators versus hospital CFOs) and low per-account ASP may genuinely discourage them from building a dedicated solution. If Advocate can demonstrate fast ROI at a handful of facilities and compound a data flywheel of voice-note-to-billing-outcome pairs, the switching costs accumulate — facilities become dependent on the audit trail. Baran's personal connection through his mother and Banys's healthcare data background at Garner suggest genuine care about the problem space, even if it falls short of non-replicable expertise.

The principal authenticity question is ambiguous. Baran's mother connection is real — growing up inside a care coordinator's household plants seeds that market research cannot replicate. But McKinsey to VC to startup is the canonical agent trajectory, and the Leilo co-founding claim discrepancy (public sources identify different individuals as Leilo founders) introduces a credibility uncertainty I can't resolve from the dossier. Banys staying in healthcare data from Analysis Group through Garner Health suggests sustained interest, not opportunism. I'd want to spend an hour with these founders to determine whether they're principals who would be building this without venture funding or agents who identified a fundable niche. The dossier doesn't resolve this.

Advocate fits a pattern I've backed before — removing a procedural gatekeeper between people and revenue they're entitled to, similar to how AngelList removed the gatekeeper between founders and angel capital. But AngelList was a platform with compounding network effects. Advocate is a tool. Tools generate revenue; platforms generate leverage. A $62/month CPT code times thousands of patients across hundreds of facilities is a real business — but it's a vertical SaaS business with linear scaling characteristics, not a platform that gets more valuable with each additional user. The question I always ask — could this be a billion-dollar company with five people? — has an honest answer of probably not. The fragmented facility market will eventually require a sales organization that grows roughly in proportion to revenue.

The decisive dimensions are specific knowledge and leverage architecture, and on both, Advocate lands in the middle. The founders have relevant proximity to the problem but not the non-replicable expertise that makes a company structurally irreplaceable. The product has code leverage but the market demands labor leverage to distribute. The access democratization signal is genuine — unlocking billing revenue from behind a documentation gatekeeper — but without deeper specific knowledge or a platform architecture that compounds, this reads as a useful vertical AI tool in a niche that well-funded incumbents could address as a feature extension. I pass.
