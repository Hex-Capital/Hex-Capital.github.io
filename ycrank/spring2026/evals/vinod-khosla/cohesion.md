# Cohesion -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Cohesion is building AI agents that help hedge fund analysts monitor earnings calls, podcasts, and X/Twitter posts more efficiently. I read that sentence, and my immediate reaction is: so what? I regularly see YC companies deploying AI copilots for narrow enterprise verticals — AI for recruiters, AI for accountants, AI for real estate agents. Cohesion is AI for equity analysts. If Cohesion fully succeeds — captures every fundamental equity fund on the planet — a few thousand professionals at a few hundred wealthy institutions save some hours each week. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The consequence of success is a moderately profitable SaaS company serving one of the most privileged professional classes on Earth. That's fine for investors who calculate IRR on spreadsheets. I don't make IRR calculations. I either believe or I don't, and what I need to believe is that success means the world is different. Cohesion's success means hedge fund analysts go home earlier.

AlphaSense has $500M+ ARR and a $4B valuation built on exactly this workflow — search and retrieval across filings, transcripts, and expert calls. Cohesion's differentiation is "autonomous agents versus search-initiated queries." That's a UX improvement, not a technology discontinuity. The dossier itself identifies the core problem: "Open-source projects like the ai-hedge-fund repo on GitHub demonstrate that basic investment research agents can be assembled from commodity LLM APIs." When your technology approach can be replicated from open-source components, you don't have a 10x advantage — you have a feature that AlphaSense can ship in a quarter. Hebbia raised $130M at a $700M valuation doing adjacent document analysis. Cohesion is entering a market where well-capitalized incumbents are actively acquiring capabilities (AlphaSense bought Tegus for $930M, Sentieo, and Carousel) and where the underlying technology — LLM agents parsing public data — has no proprietary barrier. This is the definition of a wrapper, not a wedge.

Devon Krapcho spent 5+ years as a fundamental equity analyst at Long Path Partners covering software companies. Matt Munns built AI at T. Rowe Price. Matthew McBrien built AWS Lambda infrastructure at Amazon. The team is competent and complementary — buy-side analyst, ML engineer, infrastructure engineer — and that's actually the problem. Krapcho is the quintessential domain expert building the tool he wished he had. I've said many times: when I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I pick better thinking. Where is the evidence that Cohesion's founders reason from first principles, that they've pivoted based on new information, that their rate of learning over the YC batch produced surprises? The dossier has none. No GitHub repos of consequence. No public writing demonstrating intellectual trajectory. No signal of the outsider irreverence I look for — Krapcho is an insider building for insiders. Compare this to Peter Beck building rocket engines in a New Zealand garage, or the Mach Industries founder — a 19-year-old dropout competing with Lockheed Martin. Cohesion's founders are experienced professionals following a logical career path. That's respectable, but it's not the signal I invest on.

The bull case, stated as strongly as I can: Cohesion is already live with 10+ fundamental equity funds managing combined $10Bn+ AUM at pre-seed. That's genuine traction — real users with real money on the line, not toy engagement. The earnings-season trial timing shows GTM sophistication. And the "why now" is legitimate — GPT-4-class models crossed a quality threshold for parsing nuanced financial commentary. Could this be a DoorDash-style exception, where I override my technical novelty requirement because the team is exceptional and the consequence magnitude is large? No. DoorDash's consequence was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people. Cohesion's consequence is making wealthy institutions slightly more efficient. The DoorDash exception demands planetary-scale impact from an extraordinary founder. Cohesion has neither. The Oscar Health exception requires a strong affordability thesis in a calcified industry. Cohesion has no affordability thesis — hedge funds are not underserved populations lacking access to research tools. They have Bloomberg terminals, AlphaSense subscriptions, and six-figure analyst salaries. Cohesion redistributes existing research spend; it creates no new demand.

Cohesion's path to radical affordability is nonexistent. My deepest investments — Impossible Foods making protein cheaper than animal agriculture, Curai Health delivering AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost, Commonwealth Fusion Systems producing limitless energy — converge on making expensive necessities free. Cohesion makes an expensive luxury marginally more efficient for the wealthiest buyers in the economy. When I project Cohesion forward, the endgame is not "equity research becomes free for everyone" — it's "hedge funds pay $50K/year instead of $200K/year for an analyst seat." That's value capture, not value creation. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Cohesion demonstrated a technical approach that could replace the analyst entirely — not assist, but eliminate — and applied that to democratizing investment intelligence for retail investors or pension funds who currently cannot afford professional research. That would change the consequence magnitude from "tool for the wealthy" to "investment intelligence as a public good." Nothing in the dossier suggests Cohesion is aiming there.

Cohesion is a well-executed pre-seed company that fails my framework on the two dimensions that matter most: consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. The consequence of success is a useful B2B SaaS business serving a narrow professional class. The technology is commodity LLM agents applied to public data sources with no proprietary barrier. Both dimensions score well below my thresholds. The traction — 10+ funds, $10Bn+ combined AUM — demonstrates product-market fit for a workflow tool, not evidence of a company that would make me write an apology letter to my LPs for betting on something audacious.
