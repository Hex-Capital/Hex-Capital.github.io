# CellType -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting tension in CellType is that its founder's greatest achievement is also its most dangerous vulnerability. David van Dijk invented Cell2Sentence -- the insight that single-cell gene expression data can be converted into ranked gene lists and processed natively by large language models. That insight is genuinely non-obvious. It required someone who had spent years immersed in both computational biology and modern ML to see that the same transformer architectures processing human language could learn the grammar of cellular behavior. Then he published it at ICML, open-sourced it under Apache 2.0, and co-developed the 27B parameter version with the same Google DeepMind that funds Isomorphic Labs with $600M. The founder's credibility creates the company's moat. The founder's openness creates the company's attack surface. Every interesting investment has a paradox at its center, and this is CellType's.

The specific knowledge here is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed. Van Dijk didn't pivot into AI drug discovery from an adjacent field -- he's been at the intersection of machine learning and biology for over a decade. 11,589 citations. Published in Cell, Nature, ICML, NeurIPS. NSF CAREER Award. He built the van Dijk Lab at Yale specifically to develop these computational biology methods. The Cell2Sentence framework didn't emerge from a hackathon or a weekend insight -- it emerged from years of thinking about how to represent biological state in formats that learning systems can process. That's knowledge that can't be taught. You could hand someone van Dijk's papers and his code, and they'd still be years behind his intuition about what biological signals matter and how to encode them. Vrkic complements this well -- CERN engineering background, foundation model training at a biotech, co-developer of C2S. Both founders are genuine technologists building the core product themselves. The professor-researcher dynamic is natural and durable.

The leverage architecture is what activates my investment thesis. If CellType's computational simulations can reliably predict drug behavior at the cellular level, one GPU hour replaces months of wet-lab screening. They've already screened 4,000+ compounds computationally and identified a validated cancer treatment signal. The platform model -- pharma companies plug in, CellType's models screen and predict, each partnership generates proprietary data that improves the models -- is the kind of infrastructure play I systematically prefer. This isn't "Salesforce for pharma." This is a new form of leverage: biological reasoning as a service. One small team's models serving the entire pharmaceutical R&D apparatus. The Senhwa MOU and claimed Top 10 pharma engagement at pre-seed, with 100% inbound deal flow, suggest the leverage is already being recognized by the market. But I won't romanticize it -- drug discovery has irreducible physical constraints. Computational predictions still need wet-lab validation. The leverage ratio is high but not infinite, and the downstream capital requirements for taking discoveries to clinic are massive.

The contrarian question is nuanced. AI drug discovery as a category is consensus -- billions deployed across Recursion, Insilico, Isomorphic, Atomwise. That's not where the non-consensus bet lies. The contrarian positioning is in the *approach*: treating biology as a language modeling problem rather than a structure prediction or molecular dynamics problem. Every major competitor works at the molecular or protein level. CellType operates at the cellular transcriptomics level, modeling entire cellular programs rather than individual molecular interactions. That's a genuinely different paradigm. If it's right, it captures information that structure-based approaches miss entirely -- the emergent behavior of cells, not just the shape of individual proteins. The timing makes sense too: single-cell sequencing data (Human Cell Atlas and related projects) reached sufficient scale to train foundation models only recently, and transformer architectures demonstrated their ability to learn from biological sequences only in the last few years. The approach wasn't feasible before ~2023.

The defensibility question keeps me honest. The core technology is Apache 2.0 open-source. Google DeepMind co-developed the scaled version and could build a competing product tomorrow with vastly more resources. This is the structural risk. But I've seen this pattern before -- the originator of a technology maintains an informational lead that open-sourcing doesn't eliminate. Van Dijk knows where Cell2Sentence breaks and where it can be extended. He knows what the next architectural innovation looks like before it's published. The proprietary value isn't in the open-source code; it's in the agentic orchestration layer, the unpublished model improvements, and critically, the proprietary validation data that accumulates from each pharma partnership. Every Senhwa-like deal generates data that makes the models harder to replicate. If CellType executes the pharma partnership flywheel, the compounding effect could become formidable. The question is whether they can accumulate enough proprietary data before Google or a well-funded competitor catches up using their own published methods.

Van Dijk turning down Google to build this independently is the integrity signal I weight most. He could have stayed at Yale with tenure-track security and published papers. He could have joined Google and had unlimited compute. Instead he chose the hardest path -- building a company from the science he invented, with a two-person team, against competitors with hundreds of millions in funding. That's not the behavior of someone optimizing for comfort or quick acquisition. That's someone who believes the agentic pipeline he's building on top of Cell2Sentence is the right way to deploy this technology, and that a startup is the right vehicle. I'd want to work with this person for a decade.

I'd write a check. The specific knowledge is exceptional, the leverage architecture is sound, and the approach is non-consensus enough within its category to offer asymmetric upside. The Google-shaped risk is real but manageable -- Google builds research tools, not focused drug discovery pipelines. The two-person team needs to scale carefully, particularly adding pharma commercial expertise. But the best companies I've backed started with a founder who knew something nobody else knew and built a new form of leverage from that knowledge. Van Dijk knows how to make language models reason about biology. If that knowledge compounds through proprietary data and pharma partnerships, this is the kind of infrastructure bet that defines a portfolio.
