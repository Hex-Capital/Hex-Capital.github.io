# Nine Fives -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Nine Fives is building driverless, ethernet-first RF test instruments -- programmable attenuators and switches that work out of the box via REST API, browser UI, or SCPI -- against incumbents like Keysight ($5.4B revenue) and the former National Instruments (acquired by Emerson for $8.2B). The incumbent business model conflict here is real and worth examining carefully: Keysight and NI sell full test system stacks where proprietary drivers and control software generate high-margin recurring revenue. Nine Fives' "zero-install, API-first" approach directly cannibalizes that software ecosystem, which means incumbents face a genuine innovator's dilemma in responding. I've seen this dynamic work -- it's structurally similar to how legacy defense contractors couldn't adopt Anduril's software-defined approach without undermining their cost-plus billing model. But there's a critical difference: Anduril entered during a generational structural shift (bipartisan defense modernization consensus) that was nonobvious to most Silicon Valley investors. The structural shift Nine Fives is riding -- DevOps-style automation moving into hardware test environments, headless Linux CI/CD pipelines replacing GPIB setups -- is real but directionally obvious to anyone working in the space. Mini-Circuits already offers Ethernet connectivity on their RCDAT series. Adaura added Ethernet to USB attenuators. Nine Fives executes the vision more cleanly, but cleaner execution of an obvious trend is not the same as identifying a nonobvious market window.

Noah Levy's background as a Senior RF Engineer on SpaceX satellite hardware and Andrew Kurtz's experience on Falcon 9 test automation and payloads give Nine Fives genuine founder-market fit -- these are engineers who lived the pain of legacy RF test workflows in one of the most demanding aerospace environments on the planet. Both are MIT and Cornell educated, both worked at a company (SpaceX) that represents exactly the kind of scaling culture I respect. The fact that Nine Fives already has two shipping products (POE-ATTEN-6G and POE-SWITCH-6G) with published datasheets and reference manuals at pre-seed is meaningful execution signal. But neither founder has managed teams at scale, led a company through hypergrowth, or demonstrated the organizational decision-making capability I look for when evaluating whether a team can navigate the 1-to-100 transition. They're strong individual contributors from an elite engineering environment, which is a solid starting point -- not a red flag, but not the operator-scaler profile that gives me high conviction on execution through scaling.

Nine Fives maps to my "boring but critical infrastructure" pattern at first glance -- RF test equipment is unglamorous, the payers are clear (aerospace, defense, and telecom engineers with real budgets), and prestige-seeking founders aren't flooding the category. That pattern produced Gusto, Samsara, and Checkr in my portfolio. But those companies share something Nine Fives lacks: a software-first or software-heavy margin structure. Gusto is SaaS with 60%+ gross margins and negative net revenue churn from payroll tax complexity. Samsara layers software subscriptions on top of hardware sensors, creating recurring revenue that scales with fleet size. Nine Fives, based on available evidence, sells individual hardware instruments in the $500-$5,000 range (competitor pricing benchmark) with no visible SaaS layer, usage-based pricing, or expansion revenue mechanism. Hardware without software leverage means sub-50% gross margins, lumpy revenue, and capital-intensive inventory scaling. At pre-seed funding levels (~$500K), the capital constraints of RF instrument manufacturing -- component procurement, calibration equipment, RF testing infrastructure -- are significant.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Nine Fives falls shortest against my framework. Nine Fives builds instruments that sit ON a test bench alongside Keysight oscilloscopes and spectrum analyzers. The REST API creates integration surface area, and test automation scripts calling Nine Fives endpoints do generate moderate switching costs that compound with deployment scale. But Nine Fives is not building a platform that other businesses build on top of. Compare with Stripe: Stripe didn't just process payments, it became the payments API that an entire generation of internet businesses integrated into their core stack. Nine Fives' REST API is an interface to a hardware product, not a platform layer that enables a new category of applications. The compounding network effects and switching costs that define infrastructure-layer positioning are absent here.

The bull case an advocate would make: Nine Fives is positioned to become "the Stripe of RF test infrastructure" -- start with two instruments, prove the networked-first architecture, then expand to a full platform of 20+ modern test instruments that collectively replace the entire legacy bench. The incumbent business model conflict prevents Keysight and NI from responding without cannibalizing software revenue. The $4-8B TAM in RF test equipment is large enough for venture returns if Nine Fives captures even a modest share. Government funding for 5G testbeds in Europe and Japan (Fortune Business Insights, Research Nester) is creating immediate demand. And the founders' SpaceX pedigree in RF and test automation is exactly the domain expertise needed. This is the Anduril pattern: a boring, critical, defense-adjacent market where incumbents can't innovate. The bull case is coherent, but it breaks down on two specifics. First, the Anduril exception required a generational structural shift -- bipartisan defense procurement reform -- that overwhelmed multi-miracle risk. Nine Fives has an incremental modernization trend, not a structural discontinuity. Second, the "expand from 2 to 20+ instruments" path requires significant capital investment in hardware R&D, manufacturing, and calibration infrastructure that $500K doesn't support, and the 6 GHz frequency ceiling (versus competitors operating at 26.5-40 GHz) limits addressable demand in the fastest-growing mmWave segments. One verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Nine Fives is building a software orchestration layer on top of the instrument hardware -- a test bench management platform with recurring SaaS revenue -- that transforms the margin structure from hardware-sale to software-platform economics.

Nine Fives earns respect for real domain expertise, shipping products, and a genuine incumbent business model conflict, but doesn't activate my core investment criteria strongly enough to write a check. The market timing is directionally right but not nonobvious. The positioning is product-level, not infrastructure-layer. The margin architecture is hardware-native without visible software leverage. I'd watch this company -- if they build a networked instrument orchestration platform with SaaS pricing, the infrastructure-layer score changes materially. Today, this is a competent hardware startup in a real but well-understood market, and that puts it below my threshold.
