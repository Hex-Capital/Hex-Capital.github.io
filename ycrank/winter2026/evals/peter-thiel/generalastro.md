# General Astronautics -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything you need to know about the absence of a secret. Varda has raised $329 million to build vertically integrated pharmaceutical spacecraft. GITAI has $129 million and builds robotic arms for space with existing NASA and JAXA contracts. Redwire is a public company doing $304 million in annual revenue with operational manufacturing hardware already aboard the ISS. Space Forge has $51 million for returnable satellite platforms. And now two recent graduates propose to build general-purpose robots for orbital laboratories -- a market segment whose total addressable customer base consists of perhaps five entities on Earth. When I look at this landscape, I see something revealing: the well-funded, experienced operators in this space all chose vertical integration. Varda builds its own spacecraft because Delian Asparouhov and Will Bruey understood that in a market with a handful of buyers, you need to own the entire value chain. General Astronautics is making the opposite bet -- that horizontal, platform-agnostic robotic infrastructure is the right layer to occupy -- but I see no evidence this is a deliberate strategic insight rather than the only option available to a team without the capital to build spacecraft.

The secret question yields thin results. The implied thesis is that the bottleneck to microgravity manufacturing is labor cost, not science or launch economics, and that general-purpose robotic automation resolves this bottleneck. But who disagrees with this? Nobody at NASA, Axiom, or any space station operator would argue that $130,000-per-hour astronaut time is an efficient way to pipette reagents. The insight that automation reduces labor cost is not a secret -- it is a truism dressed up in a novel environment. A genuine secret would be something like "microgravity manufacturing will generate trillion-dollar industries, and every vertical player will fail because the physics of zero-g manipulation requires a dedicated robotics platform they cannot build internally." That would be contrarian. But nothing in the dossier suggests the founders have articulated anything at this level of specificity or provocation. The thesis reads more like a sensible engineering observation than a truth that smart people reject.

The timing dependency is what concerns me most from a structural standpoint. This company's market literally does not exist yet in any meaningful commercial sense. The ISS is the only operational orbital laboratory, and it is scheduled for deorbiting around 2030. Commercial stations from Axiom, Vast, and Blue Origin are under development but face their own technical, regulatory, and funding risks -- risks entirely outside General Astronautics' control. A two-person pre-seed team must survive long enough for its market to materialize, then compete against GITAI (which could pivot from satellite servicing to lab automation with a single strategic decision and $129 million in the bank) and against the station operators themselves, who may choose to develop proprietary automation rather than depend on a startup. This is not a market where you can create demand through product excellence; the demand is gated by infrastructure that other companies must build first.

The strongest bull case requires believing several things simultaneously: that commercial space stations will launch roughly on schedule, that microgravity manufacturing will prove economically viable at scale, that station operators will buy rather than build their own automation, and that General Astronautics' early specialization in zero-g fluid handling and manipulation will create an engineering moat that neither GITAI nor terrestrial lab automation companies like Opentrons can replicate. If all of this is true, General Astronautics becomes the embedded automation layer across every orbital facility -- the robotic operating system for space manufacturing. The founders' credentials are legitimate for this: Schork's SpaceX experience with Starlink hardware reliability, his published soft robotics research, and Zhou's training in CMU's robotics program cover the mechanical and autonomy axes of the problem. The technical challenge of microgravity fluid handling is genuinely hard, and whoever solves it first accumulates operational knowledge that compounds. But "if five things all go right" is not the foundation of a power-law investment. It is the foundation of a complicated bet with too many external dependencies.

What I do not see here is the thing that makes me write a check: a founder with a definite vision that bends the world to their will rather than waiting for the world to arrive at the right configuration. Zuckerberg did not wait for social networking to become consensus -- he created the conditions for it, starting at Harvard. Musk did not wait for the launch market to mature -- he built the rockets. General Astronautics is building robotic systems for facilities that do not yet exist, operated by companies that have not yet decided whether they want third-party automation. The founders are technically capable, but I see no evidence of the kind of intellectual framework or singular conviction that would make me believe they can shape this market rather than react to it. This is a reasonable engineering project, not a definite vision of the future.
