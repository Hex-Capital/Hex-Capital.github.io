# Atrisa (formerly Refortifai) -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Atrisa is a single-sided enterprise tool sitting inside a real and growing market with no network mechanics underneath it -- a profile defined by its strongest dimension being TAM and its weakest being the compounding-user-value mechanism that underwrites the 10,000x math. The dossier opens with a YC pivot from Refortifai (runtime security for AI agents) to Atrisa (analog circuit design agents) *inside the same Spring 2026 batch*, and that single fact tells me more about the leverage architecture than the product description does -- Atrisa's distribution will run through enterprise sales motions into semiconductor design teams whose budgets are already controlled by Synopsys (~31%), Cadence (~30%), and Siemens (~13%), per SemiAnalysis. That is capital-and-labor leverage requiring permission from gatekeepers I cannot route around.

Stack Overflow (2008) is the load-bearing analogy on my permissionless-leverage ladder -- Atwood and Spolsky shipped a free product to the developer community and the developers themselves did the distribution. Atrisa does not have that mechanism available. The buyer of analog EDA tooling is not the practicing analog engineer at 2 a.m. publishing a circuit; it is the VP of Engineering at a chip company evaluating seat licenses against a Cadence enterprise agreement. Atrisa's competitor set confirms the shape: Quilter raised a $10M Series A from Benchmark, Astrus raised $13.3M including a Khosla check, and ChipStack ran straight into Cadence's arms in February 2026 rather than scale independently. Code is being written, but code is not the leverage -- the leverage is procurement cycles inside ~70%-share incumbents.

Perplexity is my known exception on the network dimension -- a single-sided AI tool that I funded anyway because criteria #1 (permissionless API/product distribution), #3 (Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD), and pre-consensus AI timing all cleared at top quartile. Atrisa does not earn that waiver. Each customer of Atrisa is economically independent of every other customer; the Nth analog design team does not make the (N-1) team's experience better unless Atrisa builds a shared data layer customers will never agree to. The Perplexity exception required three gates passing emphatically; Atrisa fails #1 outright, so the waiver does not apply, and what remains is a per-seat tool whose upside is bounded by license revenue rather than by network effects.

Atman Kar's RTL Digital Design Engineer role at Texas Instruments is the real founder-market-fit signal in the Atrisa dossier, and I want to weigh it honestly -- RTL is digital, not analog, but it is genuine silicon-design tribal knowledge rather than the "ex-FAANG PM identified opportunity" pattern I flag as a red flag. Over 18 months that adjacent-but-not-direct knowledge compounds only if Kar personally absorbs the analog tacit knowledge Viks Newsletter explicitly says LLMs struggle with, and the other two co-founders -- Sayan Mitra (whose identity is unresolved against the UIUC ECE professor of the same name) and Rithik Jain (no analog credentials) -- do not backstop that gap.

The EDA market dimension is where Atrisa scores best, and the counterfactual is instructive: for Atrisa's TAM dimension to flip a tier up, the dossier would need to show that the agent-layer is a *platform* spanning analog plus digital plus verification plus layout rather than a single sub-vertical inside EDA. MarketsandMarkets puts EDA tools at $20.78B in 2026 growing 8.1% to $30.67B by 2031 and the AI EDA segment at $4.27B growing 24.4% to $15.85B by 2032 -- those are real numbers, but Atrisa's stated wedge is *analog circuit design specifically*, which is one slice of one segment. That is pivot-room inside EDA, not the platform-shaped market Twitter or Uber had.

On the founder trio, the dossier's most discomfiting fact is the mid-batch pivot from runtime security for AI agents to analog circuit design agents -- this is not the Odeo-to-Twitter pivot where the founders carried domain conviction into the new product; this is a complete category jump from AI infrastructure security to semiconductor design tooling, which reads as the "memo founder" pattern responding to where YC interest is hottest rather than to a personally-discovered problem. Integrity does not get flagged negative here -- no public scar tissue -- but my 10-year-partner test wants to see what Sayan Mitra, Atman Kar, and Rithik Jain were obsessed with *before* YC selection, and the public record cannot yet answer that.

My 36-month forecast for Atrisa is acqui-hire by Cadence or Synopsys at $20-60M total consideration, mirroring the ChipStack pattern Cadence ran in February 2026 -- the leverage architecture flows that way mechanically. The incumbents own the seats, the incumbents own the workflow, and once Atrisa shows enough agentic-analog promise to threaten a Synopsys account, the cheapest defensive move is to buy Atrisa for what amounts to a Cadence rounding error. That outcome is fine for the founders and structurally bad for my asymmetric-math thesis: 1x downside, ~3-10x upside is not the bet I'm at the table for.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-pass is that AI EDA is compounding at 24.4% CAGR, Atman Kar has real silicon background, and Cadence's willingness to pay for ChipStack at modest progress means even a half-working Atrisa exits at a multiple of seed -- so the *expected value* is fine even if the *upside geometry* is wrong. I dismantle it with my own rule from Knowledge Project #18: "on the downside you can only lose one X. On the upside, you can make 10,000 X" -- a $60M acqui-hire on a seed check is a fine outcome inside Vinod Khosla's portfolio construction, not mine. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict on Atrisa: the founders publish an open-source analog-agent benchmark that practicing analog engineers adopt unprompted -- if Atrisa demonstrates Stack-Overflow-style developer pull rather than enterprise-sales pull, the leverage architecture has flipped and I reopen the file.
