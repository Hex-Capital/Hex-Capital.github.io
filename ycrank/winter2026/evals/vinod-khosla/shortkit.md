# shortkit -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the question I always start with: if ShortKit succeeds beyond the founders' wildest ambitions -- captures every consumer app, every publisher, every e-commerce platform that wants a swipeable video feed -- what changes? The answer is that engineering teams save six months of build time and ShortKit becomes a solid infrastructure business. Nobody's food system changes. No trillion-dollar industry becomes obsolete. No physical reality transforms. The consequence of maximum success is a well-run developer tools company -- Twilio for short-form video, maybe. That is a fine outcome for many investors. It is not the kind of consequence that justifies the effort in my framework.

Michael Seleman spent six years building YouTube Shorts infrastructure at Google. He is, by every measure, the expert. And that is precisely what gives me pause. I have said repeatedly that experience creates a set of biases that prevent you from inventing whole new markets. Seleman is not reimagining video or attacking a problem the industry considers impossible -- he is repackaging what he already built at the dominant platform into a purchasable SDK. This is the industry insider executing the industry's known playbook. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never work in telecom, that was the signal to invest in Juniper. When everyone agrees that short-form video is the dominant format and every app should have it -- that is the opposite signal. There is no expert dismissing this. There is no contrarian thesis. The consensus has already formed.

The strongest bull case deserves honest engagement. If short-form video becomes as universal as payments -- every app needs it, nobody wants to build it -- then ShortKit could become the default infrastructure layer and capture real value at scale. Stripe made online payments trivially easy for developers and became worth $50 billion. Could ShortKit do the same for video feeds? The timing elements are real: AV1 hardware decoding is now standard, non-social apps are desperate for video engagement, and nobody owns this infrastructure layer cleanly yet. Seleman's insider knowledge is actually the right background for an infrastructure product -- you need someone who has operated at YouTube scale to understand what breaks at millions of concurrent streams. And Bhammar's BusRight experience ($7M raised, 700% revenue growth in 2022, customers in 23 states) demonstrates he can execute a B2B sales motion. If I squint, this is a well-timed, well-matched team attacking a gap in the market that could grow faster than expected. But the "Stripe for X" analogy breaks at a fundamental level: payments are a universal requirement for every internet business, while short-form video feeds are a discretionary UX pattern. Not every app needs a TikTok feed. The addressable market for this SDK is materially narrower than the $48 billion short video market headline suggests.

The technology is competent engineering, not disruptive technology. HLS transcoding, CDN delivery, adaptive bitrate, ML-driven pre-fetching -- these are individually commoditized capabilities assembled into a specialized package. The 2-3x byte reduction through device-aware codec selection is applying existing advances (AV1, HEVC) to a specific use case, not inventing a new capability. Mux, with $173.9 million in funding and $46.1 million in revenue, could build a short-form SDK as a product extension. BytePlus already has the entire TikTok stack commercialized. The commoditization risk the dossier identifies is real and structural: ShortKit's moat is integration convenience, not technology that makes the incumbent approach uneconomic. Nobody looks at ShortKit and concludes that building video infrastructure the old way is now structurally obsolete.

I pass on companies like this regularly. The team is capable, the market is real, the technology works. And the consequence of success is a modest infrastructure business in a segment where well-funded incumbents can enter whenever the market proves large enough to justify their attention. ShortKit has de-risked intelligently -- chosen a proven format, targeted known demand, built on existing technology -- and in doing so has bounded its upside to exactly the range I find inconsequential. The founders are too capable to spend their careers making it slightly easier for apps to add video feeds. The problem is real but small. I would tell them: find the version of this that terrifies you and matters at civilization scale.
