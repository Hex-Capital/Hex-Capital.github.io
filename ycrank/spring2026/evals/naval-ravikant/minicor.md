# Minicor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting question about Minicor isn't whether legacy desktop automation is a real problem -- it obviously is -- but whether managed infrastructure on top of Anthropic's Computer Use API is a thick enough layer to own. This is the Alchemy question applied to a different substrate: when the protocol layer commoditizes (blockchain nodes then, computer-use models now), does the infrastructure layer above it accrue durable value? Alchemy worked because every blockchain developer needed the same reliability, caching, and observability primitives, and building those from scratch was painful enough that even well-funded teams chose to buy. Minicor is making the same bet -- that the gap between "Claude can click buttons on a screen" and "production-grade automation running 25,000 patient workflows a day inside a SOC 2 / HIPAA boundary" is wide enough to build a business in. The architectural choice to store automations as deterministic code and invoke AI only for self-healing recovery is the right instinct. It means the product degrades gracefully rather than catastrophically. But the question is whether this layer is genuinely difficult technology that compounds, or operational complexity that a well-funded competitor replicates in six months.

On specific knowledge, I'm underwhelmed. Saheed spent three years at Ford and some time at Morgan Stanley -- that's exposure to legacy enterprise environments, not obsessive, non-transferable understanding of how desktop automation should work at the systems level. Faizaan was an engineer at Phoenix, a YC company, and ran a non-profit. They've both felt the pain of legacy integrations, but so has every software engineer who's spent a year inside a Fortune 500 company. When I backed the Notion founders, Ivan Zhao had spent years thinking about the relationship between tools and thought as a personal intellectual project -- that knowledge couldn't have been acquired through any career path. Here, the founders' domain insight is the kind that a smart generalist acquires through normal employment. That's not disqualifying at pre-seed, but it means the moat has to come from execution and infrastructure depth, not from founder-knowledge barriers.

The leverage architecture is where this gets more interesting, and it's what keeps me from a quick pass. Minicor is positioning itself as infrastructure for AI companies -- not selling to enterprises directly, but giving AI startups the connective tissue they need to ship products in healthcare, automotive, and supply chain. That's leverage on leverage: one Minicor integration unlocks an entire legacy system for every AI company that needs it. The MCP interface is a smart distribution choice -- it makes Minicor composable with coding agents, which means adoption can be developer-led rather than enterprise-sales-driven. This is the picks-and-shovels instinct I respect. The compliance certifications (SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA) at pre-seed stage are surprisingly serious -- those represent real cost and discipline, and they function as a barrier that most startups won't bother to cross. At 25,000 patients per day of claimed scale, they're not vaporware.

The timing, though, isn't contrarian -- it's consensus. Everyone building AI agents in 2026 understands that legacy system integration is a bottleneck. Automat raised $19.25 million. UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent. Microsoft is investing in Power Automate. The smart crowd isn't dismissing this category; they're piling into it. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. "AI agents need to talk to legacy desktop apps" is a slide in every enterprise AI pitch deck right now. You don't get paid for being right at the same time as everyone else. The 93-96% click accuracy claim is a real signal if it holds in production, but accuracy improvements in AI-driven UI interaction are happening across the entire ecosystem, not just at Minicor.

The bull case I'd want to believe: Minicor becomes the default integration layer between AI companies and legacy systems, the way Twilio became the default integration layer between software companies and telecom. The compliance certifications, production-hardened orchestration, and accumulated workflow data across dozens of legacy applications create switching costs that compound. The customer segment (AI companies needing legacy access) grows explosively as AI penetrates regulated industries. In that scenario, Minicor's position as managed infrastructure -- not the model, not the application, but the reliable plumbing between them -- is exactly the kind of platform-layer investment I favor. The founders' willingness to invest in SOC 2 and HIPAA before they have meaningful revenue suggests they're building for the long game, not a quick flip. If the team can execute fast enough to accumulate workflow-specific training data across EHRs, DMS systems, and supply chain platforms before incumbents or better-credentialed teams catch up, the data advantage could compound into something real.

But execution speed against incumbents with distribution is the central risk, and the founders' specific knowledge doesn't give me confidence they'll win that race. The technology layer is getting thinner as model capabilities improve. The customer segment is nascent and uncertain. I'd want to see a founder who spent five years inside Epic or Cerner, someone whose understanding of how these systems break and heal couldn't be replicated by reading documentation. What I see instead is competent engineers who identified a real infrastructure gap. That's necessary but not sufficient for the kind of asymmetric outcome I invest for.
