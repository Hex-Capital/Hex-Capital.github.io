# Robby -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: three Harvard MBAs have built a well-functioning upselling tool for HVAC technicians, and it works. Nine paying customers. Claims of $40K closed within the first hour of a launch. $135K additional revenue per technician annually. These are real numbers in a real market. And that's precisely the problem — this team has done everything right by the conventional playbook, and in doing so, they've built something whose consequences of success are, frankly, a nice business. If Robby captures every home services company in America, the world gets plumbers who are better at recommending thermostat upgrades. I struggle to get out of bed for that.

The consequence magnitude here tells the whole story. The U.S. home services market is $650-750 billion, but Robby isn't disrupting it — they're adding a revenue optimization layer on top of it. Nobody becomes obsolete. No structural change occurs. ServiceTitan still runs scheduling and dispatch. Technicians still do the same work. The only difference is they sell more stuff per visit. Compare this to what gets me excited: when Juniper was building TCP/IP routers, the entire architecture of how networks operated was at stake. When Square gave every coffee shop a payment terminal, the merchant account system became structurally unnecessary. Robby doesn't make anything unnecessary — it makes everything marginally more profitable. That's a feature, not a transformation.

The team's composition reinforces my concern. Joseph Schwarzmann's Bain background in PE-backed home services is exactly the kind of industry insider knowledge I'm skeptical of — he knows the playbook, the margins, the growth levers. That's why they've built something so commercially sensible and so fundamentally incremental. Vineet's founding engineer experience at Cloud Health Systems and Feroze's stint at a16z give them execution chops and pattern recognition. But pattern recognition from Bain and a16z tells you what *has* worked, not what could work if you ignored every constraint the industry takes for granted. The rapid pivot trajectory — customs brokerage AI, health tech, then home services upselling in under a year — doesn't signal a team pursuing a problem they believe is too important to abandon. It signals talented people searching for the most attractive market opportunity. That's rational behavior, but it's not the kind of conviction that produces the investments I remember twenty years later.

The technology itself has a commoditization problem the dossier makes explicit: "The core technology (combining CRM data with third-party data to generate leads and talking points) is technically replicable." ServiceTitan has already announced Atlas Campaign Recommendations and an SMS Booking Agent for 2026. Arch is doing overlapping lead generation. When the incumbent platform controls the data layer you depend on and is building toward your feature set, you're not disrupting them — you're showing them what to build next. The defensibility argument rests on a data flywheel from technician visits, but that data is about which customers might buy a maintenance plan, not something that creates a structural moat.

The strongest bull case requires believing that Robby is the Square analogue for home services — that a simple revenue tool becomes the wedge into an entire financial and operational platform for the trades. If they capture the real-time intelligence layer for every technician visit, they could theoretically expand into pricing optimization, workforce allocation, demand prediction, and eventually become the operational brain that ServiceTitan's scheduling-and-invoicing platform never was. The early traction — nine named customers paying real money within months of launch — is genuinely impressive execution and suggests the team can build and sell. In a world where ServiceTitan stays focused on operations and cedes the intelligence layer, Robby could become a substantial vertical SaaS company worth hundreds of millions. But "substantial vertical SaaS company" is exactly the outcome I'm describing when I say the consequences of success are insufficient to justify the opportunity cost of three talented people's careers. That ceiling isn't what I invest for.

The timing is defensible but consensus — everyone can see that LLMs enable real-time field assistance, and ServiceTitan's platform creates a standardized integration surface. This isn't building before the technology is ready or arriving years before the market. It's arriving exactly when everyone else sees the same opportunity. The EPA 608 certification pursuit and months riding along with technicians are admirable discipline, but domain immersion in a problem that doesn't demand civilization-scale ambition doesn't change the nature of the problem itself.
