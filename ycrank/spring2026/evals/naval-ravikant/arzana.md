# Arzana -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Arzana sells AI agents that read RFQs and POs and write them into Epicor, JobBoss, NetSuite, SAP, Salesforce, and HubSpot — and the moment I see ten ERP integration logos and a "<4 months to go-live" claim on arzana.com, the leverage question is already answered before I get to the founders. This is labor and capital leverage, both of which require permission from incumbents (nav.al/product-media). The Stanford pair, William Alexander and Marshall Kools, are clean operators paying themselves $10K/year per the NYT — but the architecture they have chosen routes every dollar of growth through professional-services implementation cycles and trade-show booths at MD&M. That is the opposite of the bet I take.

Twitter (2007) is my HIGH rung on permissionless leverage — anyone posts, the platform charges nothing, marginal cost of replication is zero. The mechanism that mattered was that distribution did not require asking Epicor or SAP for anything. Arzana's mechanism is the inverse: arzana.com lists ten-plus ERP/CRM integrations as the *product*, and the NYT-cited GTM is the CEO walking MD&M trade-show floors. That is the **Pinterest** anti-pattern in reverse — Pinterest passed leverage and I missed it on taste; Arzana fails leverage and the dossier itself confirms it with the "<4 months to go-live" implementation clock. Every new logo on the wall (Zeiss, Tecton, Koike) is a permissioned deployment, not a permissionless propagation. I do not size up here.

Uber (2010) is the marketplace mechanism I underwrite — the Nth driver makes the (N-1) riders more valuable, and that asymmetry is the whole reason I tolerate 1x downside. Arzana is single-sided enterprise SaaS: Zeiss's deployment does not make Tecton's deployment more accurate, and Milltown Paper's job-cost data does not flow into Iowa Mold's quoting engine. The "proprietary training data" future-moat in the dossier is marked [Inferred] and would require Arzana to build a cross-customer learning loop the founders have not described. Without that, each customer on arzana.com is economically independent of the next — and the 10,000x upside math the Knowledge Project framework requires (#18) simply is not in the architecture.

William Alexander grew up working for a manufacturing sourcing firm in Okoboji, Iowa — that is genuine lived problem ownership and the closest Arzana gets to my specific-knowledge gate (Almanack, 2020). The mechanism that should compound over 18 months is the Iowa-sourcing instinct producing parts-matching heuristics that a Stanford CS clone could not replicate. The failure mode the NYT itself surfaces is that "neither founder had a tech background prior to Arzana," and the product they have to ship is a multi-ERP integration platform claiming 99.9% accuracy across heterogeneous PDFs. Specific knowledge in *the problem* without specific knowledge in *the construction medium* is half a moat — Ivan Zhao at Notion (2013) had both because he had been rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence. Arzana has the first half.

Manufacturing back-office is platform-shaped enough that I do not call it a niche — pivoting from quoting to order-entry to supplier-coordination is plausible inside the original runway, and the YC-batch density (Korso on RFQ follow-up, Smartbase on order entry, Tiny on full ERP replacement) confirms the category is broad enough that three teams chose three different slices. The counterfactual that would flip Arzana's score a tier on this dimension is a credible adjacency outside manufacturing — accounting firms, logistics brokers, distributor back-offices — that the founders can name. The dossier names none, which keeps Arzana mid-rung: real market, pivot-room is theoretical not articulated.

The Buffett trio on Alexander and Kools reads clean — Stanford credentials, $10K/year self-pay, D1 wrestling energy, no contested prior exits, no self-promoted ethics talk that would trip the "if someone says they're honest" filter (Knowledge Project #18). I would take a meeting and I would not regret the hour. That clears the integrity leg without theatrics, which is what I want. But clean-Stanford-founders is the most replicable input on this list, and the trio passing is necessary, not sufficient — it is the gate every check has to clear, not the reason to write one.

My 36-month forecast on Arzana is acqui-hire by NetSuite, Oracle, or QAD in the $30-80M range as the incumbent ERPs (gurussolutions.com, top10erp.org, 2026) absorb the overlay vendors whose agents they could have built in-house — OR loss of the YC-P26 dogfight to Korso or Smartbase as the category compresses. The mechanism is the one the dossier itself names: incumbent ERP encroachment is identified as Key Risk #2, and the overlay architecture has no permissionless distribution to outrun the incumbents' channel advantage. Arzana reaches $3-8M ARR before that resolution; it does not reach a billion.

The bull case that should sting is the **Perplexity** exception — single-sided, no network, and I funded it anyway because permissionless distribution, specific knowledge, and category timing all cleared at top quartile. Could Arzana be the manufacturing-AI analogue? No: Perplexity ships free at arzana.com-equivalent and distributes through code-native API and pure product virality; Arzana ships through MD&M booths and four-month ERP implementations. Perplexity's Aravind Srinivas brought a Berkeley retrieval PhD; Arzana's founders, per the NYT, brought no tech background. Two of the three gates that bought Perplexity its waiver are absent in Arzana. The one verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is a public, self-serve, code-native distribution channel — an open-source agent SDK or a free arzana.com signup that bypasses the implementation cycle — and arzana.com today shows the opposite.
