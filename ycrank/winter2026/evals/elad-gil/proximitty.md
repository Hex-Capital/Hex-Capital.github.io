# Proximitty -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The platform risk here is the first thing I need to resolve before anything else. Proximitty's core technical differentiation -- computer-vision-based browser automation that operates across existing loan management systems without API integration -- is being commoditized by the foundation model providers who made it possible. Anthropic shipped "computer use" in October 2024. OpenAI has Operator. Google is working on similar capabilities. When the infrastructure layer you're building on is actively being turned into a platform feature by companies with orders of magnitude more resources, you need to be very clear about where your durable value sits. The question isn't whether Proximitty can build browser agents today -- it's whether the browser automation itself is the moat, or whether it's the lending-specific workflow, compliance engine, and borrower data that sit on top. If it's the former, this company has a shelf life. If it's the latter, cheaper browser automation actually helps them by reducing their build cost while the domain layer remains defensible.

The market itself fits a pattern I've seen work repeatedly. Loan servicing and collections is genuinely boring -- nobody at a dinner party gets excited about skip tracing and right-party contact rates. That's exactly the archetype where I've found asymmetric returns. PagerDuty seemed like niche alerting software. Gusto seemed like "just payroll." Checkr seemed like a background check utility. Every lending institution in the world needs to service loans and collect payments, and the current workflow is fragmented across five or six systems with human agents doing repetitive, compliance-sensitive tasks. The structural moment is real: LLMs with tool-use capabilities crossed the production threshold for nuanced financial conversations in 2024-2025, and CFPB Regulation F created both compliance complexity that favors automated monitoring and explicit regulatory cover for AI-mediated outreach. What gives me pause is that this market isn't non-obvious anymore. Five funded competitors -- Prodigal ($14.2M), TrueAccord ($78M), Skit.ai ($47.6M), LoanPro ($100M), Peach Finance ($35.8M) -- are already attacking variations of AI-powered loan servicing and collections. The "why now" is clear, but so is the competitive intensity.

The no-API deployment model is the most interesting architectural insight. In banking, the typical enterprise sales cycle runs 6-18 months largely because of integration work -- security reviews of API connections, data mapping, infrastructure provisioning. If you can deploy on top of existing browser-based tools using computer vision, you can dramatically compress that timeline. That's not just a technical decision -- it's a distribution strategy. Faster deployment means faster data accumulation means better borrower-level risk models. The product-to-distribution trajectory is also structurally sound: start with collections automation (highest pain, most measurable ROI), build the unified data layer, then expand into full loan lifecycle management -- origination, servicing, portfolio analytics. That's how payroll becomes an HR platform, how a payment API becomes Stripe Atlas and Treasury. But I see no evidence the founders have articulated this expansion explicitly, which means I'm projecting the market structure onto their strategy rather than evaluating their strategic clarity.

The founding team has credentials that matter for this specific problem. Ho scaling a fintech from $75M to $200M ARR at Taptap Send -- even in a growth role, not as founder -- means he's seen the operational mechanics of a high-growth lending-adjacent business from the inside. His McKinsey credit risk advisory background gives him the vocabulary and network to sell into banks. Zhang's combination is rarer: enterprise security infrastructure at Bloomberg scale (300k+ terminals) plus leading an open-source AI agent tooling platform (ACI.dev, 4.7k GitHub stars). That's the exact intersection of enterprise reliability and AI agent architecture this product requires. The claim of serving "publicly traded fintechs and banks globally" is a strong signal if true -- navigating enterprise procurement at a bank with a two-person team suggests the product delivers measurable value quickly enough to shortcut normal cycles.

Where my conviction drops is on the single-miracle test. I count at least two miracles this company needs: first, making browser automation reliable enough for regulated financial workflows where a UI change on a third-party loan management portal could break automated collections mid-conversation; second, building a compliance monitoring layer that actually works in real-time across FDCPA, CFPB, TCPA, and state-level regulations -- that's not a checkbox, it's a continuous engineering challenge with legal liability attached. A compliance failure by an AI agent calling a borrower could expose both Proximitty and its banking client to regulatory action. These aren't sequential miracles -- they're concurrent and ongoing. The strongest bull case would require that browser automation rapidly stabilizes as foundation model providers improve the underlying capability (reducing Proximitty's maintenance burden), and that the compliance layer becomes the actual moat once it's built -- hard to replicate, deeply embedded, and expanding in value as regulations multiply. If both resolve favorably, this is a company that looks like Gusto circa 2012 -- boring, critical, regulatory-moated, with compounding data advantages and a clear multi-product expansion path inside every lending institution.

I'd want to see evidence that the browser automation handles UI changes gracefully at scale before getting to Invest conviction. The team is strong, the market is real, the timing is defensible -- but the architectural fragility of the core deployment model and the competitive density in AI collections keep me at a watchful Neutral. If they can show six months of reliable production usage across multiple lending platforms with minimal breakage, and if the compliance layer has withstood a real regulatory review, the picture changes substantially.
