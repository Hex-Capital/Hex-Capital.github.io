# 10x Science -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about this company is that it named itself after a publicly traded competitor. "10x Science" will be perpetually confused with 10x Genomics (NASDAQ: TXG), a company with roughly $600 million in annual revenue and entrenched brand recognition in life sciences. This is not merely a marketing inconvenience -- it is a mimetic signal. When a company defines itself in relation to an existing category leader, even unconsciously through naming, it reveals something about the founders' orientation. They are positioning within a known landscape rather than defining a new one. The name is a tell.

What is the secret here? The stated thesis is that AI-powered drug discovery is flooding the pipeline with candidates faster than the downstream characterization process can handle, and that legacy mass spectrometry software is too slow and manual to keep pace. This is not a secret. It is a widely observed bottleneck that anyone attending a proteomics conference in 2025 could articulate. The deeper structural insight -- that instrument vendors like Thermo Fisher, SCIEX, and Waters who acquired the independent software players (Genedata into Danaher in 2024, Protein Metrics into Dotmatics in 2021) will never build truly vendor-neutral AI-native software because it would undermine their hardware lock-in -- is more interesting. This creates a genuine structural opening for an independent platform. But "incumbents are too conflicted to innovate" is a common observation in enterprise software, not a contrarian truth that most intelligent people would reject. It is the standard playbook of every vertical SaaS startup that positions against bundled incumbent solutions. There is no Girardian insight here, no truth that inverts conventional understanding. The company sees a real problem -- but so does everyone else in biopharma analytics.

The team is the strongest element, and I want to be precise about why. David Roberts trained directly under Carolyn Bertozzi -- the 2022 Nobel laureate in Chemistry -- on exactly the top-down mass spectrometry and glycoproteomics techniques the software automates. Thirty-seven publications. Damon Runyon Fellowship. Andrew Reiter built mass spectrometry methods at the Broad Institute's proteomics platform under Steven Carr, one of the most respected names in the field. Vishnu Tejus was the second employee at Nooks, a YC-backed company that raised a $43 million Series B, and led CMU's XPRIZE-finalist RoboTutor project. This is a genuinely rare combination of wet-lab domain expertise and production-grade ML engineering. The founder-problem alignment is the tightest I have seen in biotech analytical software -- Roberts is not adjacent to the problem, he is the problem's foremost practitioner building the tool he wishes existed. But exceptional domain expertise does not by itself constitute a secret or create a monopoly path. Stripe's founders did not merely understand payments -- they had a vision for rebuilding financial infrastructure from the API layer up. What is 10x Science's equivalent architectural vision? "AI-native protein characterization" describes a product approach, not a theory about how the world changes.

Two of three founders appear to maintain active academic positions at Stanford -- Roberts as a postdoc, Reiter as a Ph.D. student. The YC page says they are not currently hiring. This is an anti-conviction signal that concerns me. When I backed Zuckerberg, he had already dropped out of Harvard. When Elon Musk started SpaceX, he was all in -- three consecutive rocket failures and he was prepared to spend his last dollar on the fourth. Academic hedging is the opposite of definite optimism. It says: if this doesn't work, I still have a career. Founders who keep their options open are telling you something about their own probability assessment. Maybe they will fully commit after the YC batch. But the signal right now is equivocal.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to take it seriously -- runs like this: the consolidation of analytical software into hardware conglomerates has created a structural vacuum analogous to what existed in enterprise data analysis before Palantir. Incumbent vendors are incentivized to keep customers locked into proprietary instrument ecosystems. A vendor-neutral, AI-native platform that reduces characterization time from weeks to minutes -- and saves $150,000 per team per month -- could become the de facto standard across all instrument platforms. As more biopharma companies run analyses through the platform, proprietary training data accumulates, creating a data moat that compounds over time and becomes impossible to replicate. If every major biopharma company standardizes on 10x Science for protein characterization, switching costs would eventually rival the incumbents'. This is a plausible path to dominance in a specific niche. But the niche is the problem. The software-only portion of a $5 billion protein characterization market is perhaps $500 million to $1 billion. Even monopolizing that segment produces a valuable company, but not one that returns the fund. The power law demands that I invest only in companies where success produces extreme outcomes. A monopoly on analytical chemistry software for biotherapeutics is a good business, not a civilization-defining company. It does not pass the test: if this succeeds beyond all expectations, does it reshape how the world works?

This is a competent team with genuine domain expertise attacking a real problem in a structurally interesting market. In a different framework -- one that values founder-market fit and bottleneck identification above all else -- this would score well. But my framework asks different questions. Where is the secret that most smart people reject? Where is the monopoly that compounds into something enormous? Where is the founder conviction that burns every bridge? I see a 1-to-n improvement -- a better, faster version of existing analytical software -- not a 0-to-1 creation. The technology may be 10x faster, but it does not make a previously impossible thing possible. It makes a slow thing fast. That is valuable, but it is not the kind of discontinuity I invest in.
