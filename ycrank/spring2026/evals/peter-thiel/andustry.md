# Andustry -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know about whether a secret exists. Matchory has raised $8.86 million for AI supplier discovery. LightSource Labs has raised $33 million for AI-powered procurement and processed over a billion dollars in spend. Cavela raised $8.6 million for AI sourcing. MESH Works has $884 million in RFQs flowing through its platform. Xometry, a public company with $686 million in revenue, already owns Thomasnet. When I see five or six funded players converging on the same thesis -- "industrial procurement is manual and AI can automate it" -- I do not see a market opportunity. I see a Girardian catastrophe in formation. These companies are imitating each other into mutual destruction, competing for the same suppliers, the same buyers, and the same LLM-powered workflow automation. The dossier itself notes that "AI adoption in procurement is expected to reach 86% by end of 2026." When 86% of the market agrees with your thesis, you do not have a secret. You have a consensus dressed in startup clothing.

The question I always ask is: what important truth does this company see that most intelligent people actively reject? Andustry's implicit thesis -- that mid-size industrial machinery procurement can be automated through AI agents -- is not something anyone disagrees with. It is the water supply of procurement tech in 2026. Every venture-backed competitor shares this belief. The differentiation claim is that Andustry automates the "full discovery-to-quote workflow" while competitors handle only pieces. But full-workflow automation is a product scope decision, not a secret. LightSource or Matchory could expand their scope in a single product cycle. The dossier's own risk assessment states it plainly: "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." When your core technology is acknowledged as reproducible by the people writing your investment memo, the monopoly question answers itself.

The founding team has credentials but not the profile that signals the kind of conviction I look for at pre-seed. Johann Sturken comes from McKinsey's Aerospace & Defense practice and has a mechanical engineering background from RWTH Aachen and TUM -- competent, certainly. His family background in industrial goods trading provides genuine domain texture. But a consultant who has spent the last four years at McKinsey and Airbus building strategy decks is precisely the "man in a suit" archetype that has historically correlated with my worst investments. I recall the cleantech companies of 2008 -- led by polished professionals who understood the market analytically but lacked the obsessive, contrarian conviction that distinguishes founders who create new categories from those who optimize existing ones. Het Dave's background in transforming SaaS businesses into AI-native products at Aries Global is relevant but derivative -- applying AI to existing workflows is the definition of 1-to-n thinking.

To be fair, I should articulate the strongest bull case. EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume for a two-person pre-seed team is not nothing -- it demonstrates that the product functions and that real buyers are willing to transact through it. If the 500K+ supplier database turns out to be genuinely proprietary -- not aggregated from public directories but built through direct verification with data quality that compounds over time -- that becomes a real asset. The specific focus on off-the-shelf industrial machinery, rather than custom manufacturing (Xometry) or horizontal enterprise procurement (LightSource), could represent a niche narrow enough to dominate. And Johann's family background in industrial trading could provide the kind of insider trust network that accelerates early sales in a relationship-driven industry. If this team could monopolize German mid-size industrial machinery procurement and expand concentrically across European markets, the two-sided marketplace dynamics could eventually create real switching costs. That is the path to something interesting -- but it requires the supplier data to be proprietary, the niche to hold, and the team to resist the temptation to go broad before going deep. The 100+ countries and 500K+ suppliers already advertised on the website suggest they are targeting breadth from day one, which is the opposite of monopoly thinking.

The technology does not represent a discontinuity. Reducing quoting time from "weeks" to "less than one week" is perhaps a 3-4x improvement -- meaningful, but well below the 10x threshold that creates genuine proprietary advantage. The underlying capability is LLM-based text generation and data matching, which every competitor has access to and which improves for everyone simultaneously as foundation models advance. There is no rocket equation here, no physical constraint that creates compounding returns to learning. The barrier to entry is supplier data quality and network density, which are legitimate but take years to compound -- and Andustry is starting this race behind multiple better-funded competitors. This is a business that could become modestly successful. But the power law demands that I ask: can this return the fund? A marketplace for industrial procurement in a competitive landscape with commoditizing technology and long enterprise sales cycles does not have the structural characteristics of a power-law outcome.
