# Zolvo -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that caught my attention about Zolvo isn't the idea — it's the apprenticeship. Rodriguez and Montes were employee number one at Domu, a YC S24 company, and they weren't just along for the ride. Rodriguez closed the first million in revenue and landed Chubb as a client. Montes built voice AI infrastructure handling 50,000 to 100,000 daily calls. Then they left to start their own company, and the Domu founders put their own money into it. That last detail matters more than it might seem. The people who watched these two work every day, who saw how they handled the daily crises of an early-stage startup, decided to back them with real money. It's not the same as the repeat-founder signal I got from Patrick Collison going from Auctomatic to Stripe, but it's a cousin of it. They graduated from someone else's company having done founder-level work, and the "professors" endorsed them.

Commercial lending servicing is a genuine schlep. I mean that as a compliment. When I talk about schlep blindness — the way your unconscious won't even let you see problems that involve painful, unglamorous work — this is a textbook case. Nobody walks into a hackathon and says "I want to automate reconciliation and verification for mid-market commercial lenders managing $200M in assets." The entire space makes founders' eyes glaze over: regulated, compliance-heavy, legacy systems, long sales cycles, customers who still operate on spreadsheets. And yet the numbers are enormous — $20 to $30 billion annually in US loan servicing labor, and the AI competitors have all clustered around consumer and auto lending, leaving commercial servicing essentially untouched. When most AI startups are chasing the same flashy verticals, choosing to serve the part of lending that "looks like 2005" is the kind of deliberate unglamour I find promising. The fact that Salient raised $70M and hit $25M ARR in adjacent consumer lending actually helps Zolvo's case: it proves the market is ready for AI-driven servicing while simultaneously demonstrating that the big players are looking in a different direction.

What I can't see clearly, and it bothers me, is whether this was an organic discovery or a market analysis decision. Did Rodriguez encounter commercial lending pain points while selling fintech at Domu? Did she have a specific moment of frustration watching lenders fumble through reconciliation? Or did she and Montes sit down after leaving Domu, survey the fintech landscape, and identify an underserved niche? The dossier doesn't tell me, and the absence of an origin story is itself mildly informative — founders who stumbled into a problem through lived experience almost always have a compelling narrative they can't stop telling. The problem description on their YC page reads like market positioning ("service their loans like it's 2005"), not personal frustration. I'd want to hear their story before drawing conclusions, but the signal is weaker than I'd like.

The bull case here is real, and I want to be honest about it. If Rodriguez can sell to commercial lenders the way she sold at Domu — closing enterprise deals in a regulated environment at age 23 — and if Montes can build AI workflows with the same production quality he demonstrated at scale, this team could establish themselves in a schlep-heavy niche before anyone else bothers to compete. The $6,000 in revenue within four weeks of launch is small but meaningful: it means someone is already paying. Mid-market commercial lenders with $10M to $600M in assets are underserved by incumbents like nCino (who focus on origination for large banks) and ignored by AI startups chasing consumer lending. The switching costs, once you're integrated into a lender's servicing workflow, create moderate lock-in. And Rodriguez relocating to San Francisco with $3,000 has the flavor of the scrappy determination I associate with founders who find a way through when blocked.

The bear case is equally real. The technical defensibility is thin — they're assembling commercial LLMs (OpenAI, Gemini, Claude) into workflows, not building proprietary models. That means the barrier is domain knowledge and customer relationships, not technology. Salient, with $70M and a16z's backing, could pivot into commercial lending if they see the opportunity. More fundamentally, I don't see evidence that Montes or Rodriguez have deep commercial lending domain expertise. They have fintech sales experience and AI engineering chops, but the specific knowledge of how a $200M commercial lender's back office actually works — the regulatory edge cases, the reconciliation nightmares, the audit requirements — that domain depth isn't demonstrated yet. They may acquire it quickly, but it's a gap. The SOC 2 certification is still in process, and enterprise lenders will likely require SOC 2 Type II at minimum, adding months to sales cycles.

I'd put Zolvo in the category of "promising team in a real schlep niche, but the evidence isn't yet strong enough to get me excited." The Domu apprenticeship is a positive signal. The schlep factor is genuine. The early revenue is encouraging. But I'm missing the stories — the specific anecdotes of creative problem-solving under pressure that I use to diagnose the "relentlessly resourceful" quality. And I'm missing the organic discovery narrative that would tell me they understand this problem at a cellular level rather than a spreadsheet level. They're above average for a pre-seed team, but I'd need to sit across from them for ten minutes before I'd know if they have the animal quality.
