# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is real and I want to be precise about what kind it is. Amit trained multimodal LLMs on Meta's LLaMA team, then built video assistants for Ray-Ban smart glasses at Reality Labs. Robert spent four years engineering the orchestration and low-latency inference that made those glasses work. Between them, they have 298 citations across CVPR, NeurIPS, and ICASSP, plus DARPA-funded research. They didn't study real-time conversational AI in a classroom -- they built one of the most prominent implementations of it at scale. That's non-transferable knowledge about how to make machines behave in social contexts at latencies humans can tolerate. When I backed Stack Overflow, the founders were developers who understood developer knowledge infrastructure from the inside. Amit and Robert understand real-time multimodal inference from the inside. The knowledge is genuine.

But I distinguish between knowing how to build something and knowing something the market doesn't know. Amit and Robert's specific knowledge is about execution -- they know how to build low-latency, socially-aware AI better than almost anyone outside a major lab. What they're building it *for*, however, is a meeting assistant for sales teams. That's not a non-obvious insight. Everyone in AI right now agrees that meetings need better AI. Otter.ai just crossed $100M ARR. Fireflies hit a $1B valuation. Read.ai raised $81M. This is a hot, consensus category with well-capitalized incumbents who have millions of users and will inevitably move toward real-time participation. The founders are bringing extraordinary tools to a fight everyone already showed up to. Their edge is execution speed, not market insight -- and execution edges compress faster than insight edges.

The leverage architecture concerns me more than the competition does. This is an application-layer product. Each meeting is an inference job, each customer is a SaaS seat. There are no network effects in the dossier -- one sales team using Fern doesn't make it more valuable for another sales team. Compare this to the investments I gravitate toward: Replit creates code leverage where every user-built repl enriches the platform. Notion creates a compound document model where templates and integrations compound value. Fern, as described, is a tool that makes individual meetings better. That's valuable, but it's productivity improvement, not leverage creation. The company isn't building a new form of leverage that didn't exist before -- it's applying AI to an existing workflow. And "starting with sales" suggests a vertical application wedge, not a platform play. I want to see infrastructure, not applications, because applications get absorbed by the platforms they sit on top of.

The commoditization vector here is structural, not speculative. OpenAI and Google are already building real-time conversational AI natively -- ChatGPT Voice and Gemini Live are the foundation model providers' own implementations of exactly the capability Fern is commercializing. The differentiation is "social awareness" -- knowing when to speak versus stay silent. But that's a feature of conversational AI, not a separate product category. When GPT-5 or Gemini 3 natively understands conversational turn-taking, what's left? Fern's durability depends on accumulating proprietary training data from meeting interactions to keep their social awareness models ahead of the foundation providers, but there's no evidence of that flywheel yet, and the privacy barriers to collecting meeting data in enterprise environments are real -- especially in "Shadow Mode" where the AI is invisible.

The strongest bull case: if social awareness in conversations turns out to be a genuinely hard problem that foundation models don't solve incidentally -- if understanding multi-party social dynamics requires specialized models that can't be absorbed into general-purpose AI -- then these founders are uniquely positioned to own that layer. The Meta smart glasses work gave them insight into a problem most AI teams haven't deeply studied: not what to say, but when to say it. If that problem is deep enough to sustain a moat, this becomes an infrastructure play for conversational AI rather than a meeting tool. The meeting assistant would be the wedge; the real product would be a social awareness model that any voice AI needs. That's the version of this company I would fund -- but it's not the version described in the dossier. What's described is a meeting assistant with two modes targeting sales teams.

I respect the founders and would take the meeting. Amit's publication record and their shared Meta Reality Labs background represent the kind of technical depth I look for. But my portfolio tells a consistent story: infrastructure over applications, new leverage over better tools, contrarian timing over hot markets. Fern is an application in a consensus market, built by exceptional engineers. The specific knowledge is about how to build, not about what the market misunderstands. That's the difference between a strong team and a strong bet.
