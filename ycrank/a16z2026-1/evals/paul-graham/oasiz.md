# Oasiz -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Abel Dagne has already done the hardest thing in consumer mobile: he built apps that hit 500,000 downloads and reached #1 on the App Store social charts. Most pre-seed founders I meet have shipped nothing. Abel has shipped something that hundreds of thousands of people actually used. When I backed Patrick Collison's first company Auctomatic — which sold for $5 million and was frankly unremarkable as a product — what I was really investing in was the demonstrated fact that a teenager could ship and sell real software. Abel's prior track record carries that same signal. The question with Oasiz is whether this is the organic next step of a builder who deeply understands consumer distribution, or a calculated bet placed at the intersection of three trendy vectors. The dossier doesn't resolve that question, and the absence of a clear origin story — a specific moment of frustration that created Oasiz — is the gap that worries me most.

Jonathan Dinh's top-0.01% ranking in competitive mobile gaming is the kind of credential that most investors would scan past and forget. I wouldn't. It means he has thousands of hours of lived experience inside the product category Oasiz is targeting. He's not building for a customer segment he needs to study through interviews; he IS the customer. That said, being an elite consumer of games is a different skill from building the infrastructure that distributes them. Jonathan's Berkeley AI research (ROAR lab) and Amazon search optimization work provide the technical backbone, and the team of four reportedly includes people from Google, Tesla, Nintendo, Amazon, and Superhuman. For a pre-seed company, that's a dense concentration of talent. But I notice neither founder has public GitHub repositories, and the company tagline — "the interactive social distribution platform for software" — is the kind of abstracted, buzzword-adjacent phrasing that signals marketing instinct rather than the direct, specific language I associate with founders who can't stop thinking about their actual problem.

When I discovered Replit on Hacker News in 2017, what struck me was that Amjad Masad was building something deeply personal — an IDE that made programming accessible because he was obsessed with programming being accessible. The idea was slightly weird. Plenty of smart people thought online IDEs were toys. That skepticism was the moat. Oasiz occupies the opposite position: "AI-generated games in a TikTok-style feed" is the kind of pitch that gets immediate nods from everyone in the room. Rosebud AI raised $15 million from a16z and Khosla for adjacent AI game creation. CodeWisp and Playabl.ai are both YC-backed and building overlapping tools. When I can name four well-funded competitors before I finish reading the dossier, the Black Swan test fails. The returns on consensus-good ideas have already been competed away before anyone cuts a check. Oasiz's differentiation — bundling creation with social distribution in a single mobile app — is real, but it's a product design choice, not a structural moat. Any of those competitors could add a feed.

The iteration velocity is the brightest signal in the traction data. Oasiz shipped v1.0 on February 13, 2026 and reached v1.4 by approximately April 6 — four versions in under two months, including the introduction of a virtual currency system called Jemz in v1.3. That's the kind of rapid experimentation I use as a proxy for learning rate: the founders are clearly talking to users and adapting. The 230,000 plays and 6.5 million organic TikTok views show the viral mechanics have some life. But 21 App Store ratings after two months of availability suggests a severe conversion gap between TikTok viewership and retained app usage — 6.5 million views producing only 21 ratings implies that whatever Oasiz is doing on TikTok generates curiosity, not commitment. The commoditization risk on the AI generation layer — built on generally available LLMs — means the creation tool itself won't hold as a moat. Everything hinges on whether the social graph and content library reach escape velocity before Roblox, with its 380 million monthly active users, adds AI-assisted casual creation to its existing ecosystem.

The strongest bull case for Oasiz requires two things to be true simultaneously: first, that Abel's consumer distribution instincts — demonstrated by his prior #1 App Store ranking — transfer to this new category and allow Oasiz to build a social graph faster than Rosebud, CodeWisp, or Playabl.ai can bolt one on; second, that the unconfirmed Atari IP partnership materializes and gives Oasiz a catalog advantage no AI-generation-only competitor can match. If those two things happen, the comparison shifts from "one of several AI game tools" to "the platform where recognizable IP lives in a social context," which is genuinely defensible. The 5,000 playable experiences built in closed beta show early content generation velocity that could compound into a library moat. But I've seen many consumer social startups generate early content and attention without converting either into a durable network effect. Abel Dagne is a founder I'd want to track across multiple companies — the prior shipping track record is real and rare. Oasiz as an idea, though, sits in the zone where everyone immediately agrees it's good, which in my experience means the expected returns have already been priced in by the market's enthusiasm. The founding team deserves a higher score than the idea they're currently pursuing.
