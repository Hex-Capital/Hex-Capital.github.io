# Seeing Systems -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Defense drones are the consensus bet of 2026. VC funding in defense tech hit $49.1 billion last year, up from $27.2 billion the year before. European defense venture grew 132% year-over-year. Five funded competitors in the dossier alone are valued at a combined $37+ billion. When every LP dinner conversation includes the phrase "Ukraine proved drones work," the asymmetric upside has already been arbitraged away. I invested in Uber when ride-sharing was illegal and Bitcoin when it was dismissed as nerd money. Those bets paid because the crowd was gloomy and wrong. Here, the crowd is euphoric and mostly right -- which means the returns accrue to the incumbents with capital advantages, not to a two-person pre-seed team from Guernsey competing against Anduril at $30.5 billion and Shield AI at $5.6 billion.

The specific knowledge question is where I spend the most time, and the answer here is mixed. Alexander Le Maitre built EOD training hardware for military users and led projects with national security implications -- that's genuine hands-on knowledge of what military operators need and how procurement works. It's not academic. But it's adjacent knowledge, not deep domain immersion in drone warfare specifically. Matthew's work at the Cambridge Mobile Robotics Lab on autonomous systems and context-aware drones is directly relevant technically, and Jane Street gives him the engineering rigor to ship reliable systems. Together they cover the full hardware-software stack, which is necessary. But necessary isn't sufficient. The founders I back at my highest conviction are people who've been circling a specific problem for years, who can articulate non-obvious insights that surprise me about their own domain. What does this team know about the evolution of electronic warfare, swarm coordination, or modular military systems architecture that couldn't be learned by two other smart Cambridge engineers in a year? The EOD hardware experience suggests real proximity to the military user, but it doesn't reach the level of non-transferable knowledge that makes me write a check on the founder alone.

The leverage architecture concerns me most. This is a hardware business with Forward Deployed Engineers embedded in each military unit. That model -- borrowed from Palantir -- creates tight customer relationships, but it's the definition of unleveraged. Each new customer requires proportional human effort. The hardware itself requires manufacturing, supply chain management, and quality control that scale linearly with volume. The only potential leverage mechanism is Aerie AI, the swarm coordination software -- if it becomes the coordination layer for multi-platform drone operations, that's genuine code leverage where one software system multiplies the capability of many operators. But Aerie AI is listed as "in development." Investing in a hardware company on the promise of future software leverage is investing in a hypothesis, not a mechanism. I backed Replit because the leverage was already visible in the product -- one person writing code that runs for millions of users. Here, the leverage I'd need to see doesn't exist yet.

The strongest bull case deserves honest engagement. Defense procurement lock-in is real -- once you're qualified and integrated into a force's logistics pipeline, switching costs are enormous. The fact that this two-person team already has relationships with UK Royal Marine Commandos and four NATO forces is remarkable for pre-seed. Stark, the closest competitor in expendable combat drones with $100 million raised, failed all four strikes in UK military trials. If Seeing Systems' modularity architecture becomes the standard that NATO adopts for its attritable drone programs, the defense procurement moat could be extraordinary -- more durable than any software moat because military qualification processes take years and governments avoid re-qualifying vendors. The fibre-optic guided Banshee variant is a genuine first-principles engineering response to the jamming problem -- solving an information theory challenge at the physical layer rather than the software layer. That's the kind of physics-first thinking that drew me to Extropic. If this team executes on modularity as a platform standard and Aerie AI as a coordination layer, the outcome could be infrastructure-grade -- the picks-and-shovels play for NATO's drone buildout. But that "if" carries a lot of weight against $37 billion in funded competition.

The technology compounding story has potential but faces a structural headwind: FPV strike drone hardware is commoditizing. Ukraine produces four million drones annually. Basic FPV designs are widely available. The moat has to come from the modularity architecture, the swarm software, and the fibre-optic variant -- all of which are harder to replicate but not impossible for competitors with hundred-million-dollar war chests. I look for technology that gets measurably harder to compete with over time. Here, the compounding comes from operational deployment data feeding back into Aerie AI, and from defense procurement qualification creating institutional lock-in. Both are plausible but unproven. The company is shipping prototypes, not production units, to its military partners.

Two capable brothers building hardware for a real, urgent military need. The market timing is correct but consensus. The specific knowledge is real but not extraordinary. The leverage architecture is currently inverted -- more consulting than platform. I'd want to see Aerie AI working in production and evidence that modularity creates genuine platform lock-in before investing. This is a company I'd track, not fund.
