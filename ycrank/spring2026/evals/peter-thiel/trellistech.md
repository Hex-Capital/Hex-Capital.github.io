# Trellis -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

"Software is being commoditized" — Trellis's own words from its YC company page. A company that announces the destruction of software moats as its founding thesis and then proceeds to build a software company has not discovered a secret. It has described a problem and then positioned itself on the wrong side of it. Trellis claims it reached feature parity with competitors in two weeks. If that claim is true, it is the strongest possible argument against investing in Trellis — because it means any well-funded competitor can reach feature parity with Trellis in two weeks. The company has articulated the terms of its own obsolescence and mistaken them for a value proposition.

Guesty has raised $409 million. Hostaway has raised $543 million and achieved unicorn status. Boom has raised $12.7 million with an AI-first positioning nearly identical to Trellis's. Conduit — the YC W24 company where Trellis co-founder Lodovico Benvenuti previously built AI guest messaging agents — operates AI across 77,000+ properties. When I look at the competitive landscape slide for Trellis, I see exactly the red flag my framework was built to detect: multiple well-funded startups pursuing the same thesis simultaneously. "AI agents for short-term rental operations" is not a secret anyone disagrees with — it is the consensus of every investor, operator, and vendor in the STR ecosystem. Every player in this market is building agentic AI capabilities. The question "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" has no satisfying answer here. Trellis's insight — that STR operators need unified AI orchestration across their operational stack rather than point solutions — is directionally reasonable but structurally identical to what Boom, Conduit, and the incumbents are all pursuing. A truth that four competitors share is not a secret. It is a competitive market.

The strongest case an advocate would construct centers on Lodovico Benvenuti's rare dual qualification: he scaled a property management company to approximately $2 million in revenue and 150+ listings while also building AI agents at Conduit. That combination of operator intuition and technical execution is genuinely uncommon. Jan Sahagun's STR Tech Report — a media platform targeting the exact buyer persona Trellis needs to reach — is a distribution asset most pre-seed companies would kill for. A fair advocate would argue this is the Palantir pattern: embed deeply into operational workflows, create switching costs through 200+ integrations, and accumulate cross-operational data that compounds into a network effect no competitor can replicate. The advocate would note that incumbents face the classic innovator's dilemma — Guesty and Hostaway cannot cannibalize their per-listing SaaS revenue to rebuild AI-native from scratch. I take this argument seriously enough to identify the exception test: does the Lyft exception apply, where category-level value creation is so enormous that even the number-two player produces fund-relevant returns? It does not. Vacation rental software is an $8.6 billion market growing at 5.1% CAGR — this is not a category generating hundreds of billions in new value where positional competition still pays. The ceiling is too low and the dynamics too fragmented for power-law returns.

Trellis is operating one property management company's operations "with 1 person" — the sole traction signal in the dossier. The 84.5% AI automation rate from a dashboard screenshot is a claim, not validated traction. Both founders' career paths reveal a pattern that concerns me more than their individual credentials impress me: Lodovico moved from his own PM company to Conduit (a direct competitor) to founding Trellis; Jan moved from Guestway to Boom (another direct competitor) to Trellis. This is not the shared prehistory of co-founders who survived a high-intensity experience together and emerged with a definite vision — it is two people who worked at different STR vendors and decided to build their own. The relationship was forged within the industry trend, not before it. The dossier notes their paths "likely crossed through the small STR tech vendor ecosystem" but identifies no specific shared institution or crucible. Trellis's roadmap reads as iterative discovery — "onboarding design partners," building integrations, accumulating data — rather than the bold, irreversible commitment that signals definite optimism.

Jan Sahagun's distribution architecture is the single dimension where Trellis outperforms its stage. STR Tech Report provides organic access to professional STR operators — the exact persona Trellis targets — without paid acquisition costs. This is the kind of embedded channel that most founders neglect entirely. If Trellis were attacking a market with monopoly structure, this distribution edge would be a meaningful accelerant. But superior distribution into a competitive market with no structural defensibility produces a "nice small business," not a monopoly. Trellis has no atoms-based moat, no government customer pathway, no physical-world technology barrier, no proprietary dataset that cannot be assembled by a competitor with more capital. It is a pure software layer built on third-party foundation models, integrating with third-party tools, in a market where the company itself argues software features can be replicated in two weeks.

The condition that would flip me to invest: Trellis signs an exclusive, multi-year platform contract with a top-five STR property management aggregator — not a design partner, but a customer that commits operationally in a way that creates the kind of embedded switching costs Palantir achieved with the intelligence community. That would transform the thesis from "better software in a competitive market" to "operational infrastructure with monopoly-grade lock-in." Without that, Trellis is the pattern I pass on most frequently: founders swept up by the AI trend rather than by hard analysis of what secret they possess that the market disputes. Competition is not escaped by working harder in a crowded field. It is escaped by finding a truth no one else sees. Trellis has not shown me that truth. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret — where the evidence actively contradicts the existence of a contrarian insight, given four funded competitors pursuing identical theses — and Power Law Return Magnitude, where the $8.6 billion software TAM and fragmented market structure impose a ceiling incompatible with fund-returner outcomes.
