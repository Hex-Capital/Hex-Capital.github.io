# Pulsent -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Toplyne raised $17.5M from Tiger Global and Sequoia to identify PLG conversion signals for B2B SaaS companies, then shut down and returned capital to investors after failing to find product-market fit. Pulsent is attacking the same category -- PLG conversion and retention -- with the added claim that AI agents can now autonomously execute outreach, not just surface signals for human reps. Before I evaluate whether Pulsent's "AI agents that watch sessions" approach is meaningfully different, I need to understand why Toplyne failed. The answer isn't that the technology was bad. Toplyne failed because PLG companies were reluctant payers for conversion optimization tooling -- the signal-to-action gap was wide, but the budget line item didn't exist in most orgs. Pulsent's pitch is that autonomous execution closes that gap. Maybe. But the underlying market structure question -- is there a motivated buyer with budget authority? -- remains unanswered by this dossier. Three named customer testimonials (Pletor, Leadbay/YC F25, Basalt) are all early-stage B2B SaaS co-founders, not the mid-market or enterprise buyers who would validate a real budget category. Pulsent's "hundreds of millions of events daily" claim is eye-catching, but the YC description may predate the current product form given the Bluco-to-Swish-to-Pulsent pivot sequence.

Pulsent's "why now" argument -- advances in visual AI in December 2025 enabling agents to watch user sessions at "human or better level" -- is a real capability shift but not a nonobvious one. Compare this to Harvey: legal tech had been a graveyard for a decade because the precision requirements exceeded AI capability. A specific GPT threshold crossed, unlocking a stuck market. Customer engagement is not stuck. Intercom generates $343M in revenue. Pendo exceeds $300M ARR. Intercom's Fin AI agent is approaching $100M ARR on its own (Sacra, 2024). These incumbents are actively shipping AI features -- Intercom added predictive engagement scores in 2026 that highlight expansion-ready accounts (Demandbase, 2026). Pulsent is entering a market where consensus already recognizes the AI opportunity and well-capitalized incumbents are executing on it. That's the opposite of a nonobvious window. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed. When I backed Perplexity, AI search was considered a failed concept. Pulsent's category -- AI-powered customer engagement -- is on the cover of every SaaS newsletter.

Pulsent's architecture fails the infrastructure-layer test decisively. Pulsent depends on PostHog, Mixpanel, Amplitude, Snowflake, BigQuery, Stripe, Salesforce, and Attio for data ingestion, and on Intercom, Loops, and Customer.io for message delivery. Pulsent is an application layer sitting between two infrastructure layers it doesn't control. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies built on -- that's infrastructure positioning. Pulsent is a consumer of other companies' APIs, not a provider of APIs that others build on. Each new Pulsent customer requires multi-tool configuration across 8+ platforms, creating onboarding friction without the corresponding switching-cost moat. API changes, rate limits, or competing native integrations from PostHog or Intercom could disrupt Pulsent's pipeline at any time. This is the exact red flag I watch for: a startup building an application on a contested infrastructure layer where the platform vendors have every incentive and capability to replicate the functionality.

Pulsent requires at least three independent breakthroughs to reach product-market fit: the AI needs to generate outbound messages that convert measurably better than template-based or human-crafted approaches; the multi-platform integration layer needs to work reliably across 8+ data sources simultaneously; and PLG companies need to trust an autonomous agent to contact their users across email, LinkedIn, and WhatsApp without brand damage. Toplyne only needed the first and third of these and still failed. Pulsent adds the integration complexity miracle on top. My "single miracle" framework exists precisely for this pattern -- compounding small odds means you're very likely to fail regardless of how talented the team is.

Arseniy Shishaev's three years at Datadog (2021-2024) building tools for an internal data platform managing "hundreds of millions of data assets using complex recursive queries" is the strongest signal in this dossier. That experience maps directly to Pulsent's event-processing core and gives me some confidence in the technical architecture. Nicolò Magnante's claim of "scaling two startups to millions in ARR" is unverifiable from the dossier -- his prior startup Bluco won a €25K grant and had named clients including Porsche and Trenord, but the ARR claim lacks sourcing. Neither founder has operated at a company during hypergrowth in a leadership capacity. The pivot from Bluco (AI recruitment) to Swish to Pulsent in early 2026 signals market learning but also means Pulsent's current product maturity is measured in months.

The strongest bull case for Pulsent is that multimodal AI has crossed a specific capability threshold that makes truly autonomous observe-and-act loops feasible for the first time, creating a new category that didn't exist when Toplyne failed. The data unification layer creates real switching costs once configured, and Arseniy's Datadog background provides the technical foundation to build it. If Pulsent can prove that autonomous AI-driven outreach converts at 2-3x human-crafted messages in a repeatable way across 10+ paying mid-market customers, the category objection dissolves. I checked my Known Exceptions -- the Anduril pattern (overwhelming market structure overriding multi-miracle concerns) doesn't apply because customer engagement lacks a generational structural opening. The BioAge pattern (deep personal domain expertise overriding sector avoidance) doesn't apply because neither founder brings deep customer success domain expertise. None of the exception patterns activate here. The condition that would flip me to Invest: Pulsent demonstrates $30K+ MRR from 5+ non-YC customers with measured conversion lift above 50% versus their prior approach, proving both buyer willingness to pay and product efficacy in the PLG conversion category where Toplyne could not.

Pulsent's score is anchored by two dimensions. Nonobvious Market Timing scores low because the AI-powered customer engagement market is consensus-obvious with $600M+ in incumbent revenue and a direct category failure precedent in Toplyne. Infrastructure-Layer Positioning scores low because Pulsent is architecturally an application layer dependent on 8+ third-party APIs, the inverse of the picks-and-shovels positioning I look for in my highest-conviction investments.
