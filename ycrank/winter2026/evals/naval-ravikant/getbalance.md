# Balance -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape table. Pilot: $325M raised. Digits: $100M. Finally: $305M. Puzzle: $66.5M. And Haven, a pre-seed company with the same "AI + human accountant" model, already at $1M+ revenue. When I count $700M+ in combined competitive funding pursuing AI accounting from slightly different angles, this is not a contrarian bet — it's a consensus trade. Everyone agrees accounting is ripe for AI disruption. That agreement is the problem. You get paid for being right first, not for being right alongside a hundred other well-capitalized teams. The most expensive investments I've ever made were the ones where the thesis was obviously correct and therefore already priced in.

The specific knowledge question is where I start any evaluation, and here it's instructive but not convincing. Mathias was CFO at Female Invest and ran a small accountancy — he's felt the pain of accounting from both sides of the desk. That's genuine operational familiarity, not something fabricated. But I'd distinguish between having experienced a problem and possessing non-transferable insight about its solution. Thousands of former CFOs and small-firm accountants understand that reconciliation is tedious and error-prone. The question is whether Mathias knows something about how AI transforms this workflow that a smart generalist couldn't learn in six months. Nothing in the dossier suggests that kind of depth. Gus brings solid AI engineering credentials — Imperial, Oxford NLP research, production ML at Orbital Witness — but his specific knowledge is in building AI systems generally, not in the peculiarities of financial data. The team composition is sensible: accounting domain, AI engineering, operations. But "sensible" isn't the same as "irreplaceable."

The leverage architecture is what concerns me most. Balance positions as an "AI accountancy" — it replaces your accountant rather than selling you software. That sounds like a bold move until you trace the implications. Every client requires human accountant review and sign-off. Every new jurisdiction (they're operating in Denmark, US, and UK simultaneously) requires locally qualified accounting professionals who understand that jurisdiction's tax code, GAAP variant, and regulatory framework. This is not code as leverage — it's labor augmented by code. The marginal cost of serving the next client is lower than a traditional firm's, but it's not zero. Contrast with Notion, where one engineer's work compounds across millions of users, or with Stack Overflow, where every answered question makes the platform more valuable for every other developer. Balance's model looks more like a tech-enabled services business — the economics of a consulting firm with better tooling, not the economics of a platform. AI efficiency gains can improve margins, but they don't eliminate the linear relationship between revenue growth and headcount growth. That's a structural ceiling on returns.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The $650B+ accounting services market is genuinely enormous, and the structural conflict facing incumbent firms — their billable-hours model makes AI adoption self-destructive — is real and creates a meaningful window. If Balance can drive the AI-to-human ratio to 50:1 or 100:1 over time, the services component becomes negligible and the leverage architecture emerges retroactively. The conversational interface via Slack and WhatsApp is a genuinely lower-friction entry point than forcing SMB owners into a dashboard they don't understand. And there's a plausible data flywheel: processing thousands of clients' books across multiple jurisdictions could build categorization models and tax-rule engines that no single-market competitor replicates. If all of that compounds over five years, this could look like infrastructure for SMB financial operations rather than a staffing play. The reason I still pass is that every step in that chain — the AI ratio improvement, the data flywheel, the multi-jurisdiction encoding — is available to any of the five competitors with more capital and more customers already in the pipeline. The enabling technology is LLM-based document understanding, which is a capability, not a moat.

What I look for at the earliest stage is a founder who knows something that can't be taught, building a system where code creates nonlinear output, at a moment when smart people think they're wrong. Balance has a competent team with relevant experience in a massive market — and none of those three conditions. The accounting domain knowledge is real but replicable. The model augments labor rather than replacing it with leverage. And the timing is consensus, not contrarian. I'd want to see either a genuinely novel technical approach to accounting automation that competitors haven't conceived, or a founder with the kind of decade-deep obsession with financial systems architecture that produces insights no one else has. Instead, this looks like a well-executed entry into a category that's already crowded with well-funded players running similar playbooks.
