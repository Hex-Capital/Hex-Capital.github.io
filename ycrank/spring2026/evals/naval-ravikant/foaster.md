# Foaster -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural contradiction at the center of Foaster is what drew my attention first, not the founders. They're building with the most leveraged tool available — AI agents — but deploying it inside the least leveraged business model in existence: consulting. "A human consultant scopes the engagement, reviews outputs, challenges reasoning, and delivers final recommendations." That sentence describes labor leverage with an AI accelerant, not permissionless leverage. Every new client engagement requires fresh human time. Notion serves its millionth user for the same marginal cost as its hundredth. Foaster's hundredth engagement requires roughly the same human scoping and delivery as its tenth. The AI makes the human faster, but the human is still the bottleneck. This is the difference between owning leverage and renting it.

The specific knowledge question is where the bet gets interesting but ultimately unresolved. Combes built Repondia — AI agents handling 60,000+ customer calls for French restaurant groups. That's directly relevant experience in conversational AI at scale, and it maps precisely to Foaster's core mechanism. The Werewolf benchmark on LLM social intelligence, shared by OpenAI's president, shows both founders understand how to evaluate and design agent interactions. But here's what I keep asking myself: their specific knowledge is in building AI conversational agents, not in understanding how organizations actually function. The deepest insight in consulting isn't conducting interviews — it's knowing which questions to ask, recognizing organizational dysfunction patterns, and delivering recommendations that executives trust enough to act on. A McKinsey engagement partner who learned to build AI agents would have more relevant specific knowledge for this exact company than two 23-year-old engineering students who learned to build AI agents and pointed them at consulting. The knowledge is adjacent to the problem, not fused with it.

The bull case deserves serious attention because there's a real innovator's dilemma here. McKinsey's entire economic model is the leverage structure — partners billing out junior analysts at multiples of their cost. Offering a product that's "3–5x more cost-effective" would cannibalize the core margin engine. McKinsey's consultants have the specific knowledge to build this, but their incentive structure makes them agents of their own P&L, structurally unable to destroy what feeds them. Two principals with no legacy revenue to protect can move where those agents cannot. If the AI interview methodology genuinely produces superior data — comprehensive coverage of every employee versus a sample of 15 interviews — the output quality improvement could be real, not just a cost play. And if the operational map transitions from a per-engagement deliverable to a persistent organizational intelligence layer that companies subscribe to, the leverage architecture completely transforms. That's the path from consulting to SaaS, from labor leverage to code leverage. But that transition is hypothetical, and most companies that start as services stay as services.

The compound interest test reveals the deeper problem. Within a single client, follow-on analyses benefit from the existing operational map — that's genuine switching cost. But across the client portfolio, nothing compounds. Client A's organizational map doesn't make Client B's engagement better. There's no data flywheel. Perplexity gets smarter with every query. Stack Overflow gets more valuable with every answer. Foaster starts from scratch with each new organization. The data asset is siloed by design — enterprise clients would never allow their operational maps to train a general model. Without cross-client compounding, you have a very good tool, not a platform.

On founder authenticity: both are builders. Dabadie grew a 400K-user community at eleven — that's a kid who builds things because he can't help it. Combes shipped Repondia before Foaster. These are principals, not agents playing founder. But they appear to have identified AI consulting as a market opportunity rather than arriving at it through years of obsession with how organizations operate. The Werewolf benchmark led them to understand AI agent interactions; consulting was where they pointed that capability. I invest in people who would be working on the problem even without funding. I don't see evidence that either founder was losing sleep over the inefficiencies of management consulting before they saw the AI application.

The competitive landscape reinforces my concern about commoditization. Distyl AI has $202M. Skan.ai has $57M and $22M in revenue. PromptQL has $136M. Any of them could add an interview layer. The core technology — LLM-based conversational agents, data synthesis, recommendation generation — sits on foundation models anyone can access. The barrier isn't the technology; it's the accumulated methodology. But methodology without deep domain-specific knowledge is replicable. When the foundation model providers ship native agent frameworks that make organizational interviews a template, the methodology advantage erodes. Innovation without a moat becomes consumer surplus — the value flows to the client and eventually to the model provider.

Two talented young builders with real AI agent experience, attacking a market with genuine structural vulnerability. But the architecture is wrong for how I think about leverage, and the specific knowledge points at the tool rather than the domain. If they pivot from consulting engagements to a persistent SaaS platform — an always-on organizational intelligence layer — I'd want a second look. Right now, they're building a faster horse for an industry that needs a car.
