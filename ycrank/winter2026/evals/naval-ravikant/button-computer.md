# Button Computer -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The leverage architecture is what kills this for me before I even get to the founders. Button sells a physical object at $179 per unit. Each device must be manufactured, packaged, shipped, and supported. The intelligence layer -- the actual AI response -- belongs to OpenAI or Anthropic or whoever's API they're calling. The subscription is optional; users can bring their own key. So you have a hardware company with linear unit economics, no owned intelligence, and a porous recurring revenue layer. This is the opposite of every infrastructure bet I've ever made. Replit scales because one engineer's work serves millions of coders. Stack Overflow compounds because every question-answer pair makes the platform harder to replicate. Button compounds nothing. The device you sell to customer one thousand is identical to the device you sell to customer one. The AI gets better only because someone else's AI gets better. You're building a peripheral for someone else's technology.

The founders have real credentials -- I don't dismiss that. Burgoyne co-invented the gaze-and-tap interaction for visionOS. That's genuine specific knowledge about how humans want to interface with computing devices. You can't teach that in a classroom. Nolet was a Staff SWE on Vision Pro. They've both shipped production software at Apple and both have prior startup experience -- Burgoyne's Skyglass hit 95K downloads and $6K MRR, which shows he can move from corporate engineering to scrappy product building. But here's the disconnect: their deep expertise is in spatial computing interaction paradigms -- complex, multi-modal, visual-first interfaces. Button's entire interaction model is "press button, speak, listen." The UX is intentionally trivial. That's the pitch -- "iPod Shuffle for AI." So the founders' most formidable knowledge is precisely the knowledge that this product doesn't require. It's like hiring someone who designed the space shuttle to build a paper airplane. The paper airplane might fly beautifully, but you didn't need that specific knowledge to build it.

The strongest bull case is contrarian timing. After Humane burned through $230M and sold to HP at a 50% loss, after Rabbit R1 was mocked, after Meta absorbed Limitless and Amazon swallowed Bee -- the consensus among smart people is that AI wearables are a dead category. "The form factor doesn't work." If that consensus is wrong, and the category failed because of overambition rather than absent demand, then a $179 phone companion that does one thing simply could be the right form factor at the right price. Plaud NotePin proves there's a real market -- 1M+ units, $250M annualized revenue -- for a cheap AI accessory that clips to your shirt. Button is the conversational version of what Plaud proved for transcription. If you believe LLM voice latency continues dropping and consumer willingness to talk to AI keeps rising, the window is open. The acquisitions cleared the competitive field, and Apple's rumored wearable is vaporware until it ships. For this to be a great investment, Button needs to ship on time, nail the audio quality and latency so the experience feels magical at first use, and build brand before Apple arrives. It's a race to establish the category before the platform owner shows up.

But I keep returning to the structural problem. "iPod Shuffle for AI" is a revealing metaphor because it gets the analogy exactly backward. The iPod Shuffle worked because Apple controlled the entire vertical: hardware, iTunes software, content licensing, and distribution. Button controls a Bluetooth speaker with a button. The operating system belongs to Apple. The phone connection belongs to Apple. The AI belongs to OpenAI or Anthropic. Button sits at the thinnest possible layer of the stack -- a commodity hardware shell around an API call. When Apple ships its own AI wearable, integrated with Siri and Apple Intelligence and the health sensors and the notification system, what does Button's $179 clip-on have that can't be replicated in a software update to AirPods? The commoditization risk isn't hypothetical; it's structural. The dual-mic array, Bluetooth chip, speaker, and button are all off-the-shelf components. Any competent hardware team can build this in months.

I respect the founders' willingness to leave Apple and build something together -- Burgoyne calling Nolet "my best friend" signals the kind of relationship that endures through hard stretches. No integrity red flags. And the design philosophy of press-to-talk rather than always-listening reflects genuine user empathy. But when I run my full framework -- specific knowledge that doesn't map to the core challenge, no leverage, commodity technology that doesn't compound, and a category where the platform owner is circling -- this isn't my kind of bet. I want to invest in things that create new forms of leverage for individuals. Button doesn't create leverage. It's a convenient interface to leverage that someone else built and owns.
