# Payna -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a market-ending move already in progress. Brico has $21.6M in funding, 600% year-over-year revenue growth, Marqeta, BitGo, and Better Mortgage as customers, and Forbes Top 50 FinTech recognition -- all in the exact same licensing automation category Payna is entering. When I map competitive dynamics, I look for whether a category leader has already achieved compounding distribution advantages that make displacement structurally difficult. Brico has enterprise relationships, regulatory data coverage built over 18+ months, and the trust signals that matter in compliance software. Payna has placeholder "0%" metrics on its website and a product that appears to be roughly two months old. This is not the pattern I saw with Coinbase entering an empty regulatory infrastructure space or PagerDuty entering a market no one was building for. This is entering a market where the structural opportunity has been identified, funded, and is being captured by a well-resourced competitor.

The "why now" is real but not unique to Payna. The GENIUS Act expanding stablecoin regulation, rising compliance costs, and growing state-level VASP requirements do create genuine demand for licensing automation. I take regulatory complexity seriously as a market driver -- my investments in Coinbase and my experience founding Color Health taught me that compliance burden creates real willingness to pay. But the structural moment here favors the category, not this specific company. The RegTech market growing at 17-20% CAGR is a consensus observation that Brico, Regology, and others have already acted on. A non-obvious market is one where the inflection point hasn't been widely recognized. When a competitor has already raised a $13.5M Series A from Flourish Ventures and is being profiled in American Banker, the market has been discovered. The "too boring" archetype works when you're early to the boring market -- Gusto entered payroll before anyone realized it was a platform opportunity. Here, the platform opportunity has been identified and is being built.

The founder domain gap concerns me significantly. Building compliance software that tracks regulatory requirements across 50+ jurisdictions and generates legally accurate BSA/AML policies is not a design problem or a generic software problem -- it requires deep regulatory domain expertise. Aaron Chen's background is in crypto product design at Axal and hackathon projects. He Song's background is entirely unknown. Neither founder has documented experience in compliance, legal operations, or financial licensing. This matters because compliance software errors have direct legal consequences for customers, which means enterprise buyers in this space apply extreme scrutiny to vendor credibility. The company needs multiple things to go right simultaneously: build accurate jurisdiction-specific compliance logic without domain expertise, compete with an entrenched funded competitor, and earn trust from enterprises in a high-stakes purchasing decision. That's not one miracle -- that's three sequential breakthroughs compounding against each other.

The strongest bull case would be this: Brico validates the market, the regulatory surface area is expanding fast enough to support multiple winners, and Payna's "compliance operating system" framing -- covering lending, mortgage, insurance, and debt collection licensing alongside crypto -- positions it as a broader platform than Brico's MTL-focused workflow. If the team could build comprehensive multi-vertical licensing automation while Brico stays narrow, there's a path to differentiation. The YC network gives access to crypto and fintech companies that need exactly this tool. And the combination of AI document generation with cryptographically timestamped compliance records could create genuine switching costs once customers are onboarded. If Payna could execute quickly enough to carve out even one licensing vertical before Brico expands into it, the product-to-distribution trajectory from licensing into renewals, audit management, and ongoing compliance monitoring is real. But this bull case requires assuming the team can build domain-specific compliance logic they don't yet have expertise in, faster than a competitor with 10x their resources and an 18-month head start. I don't see what structural advantage makes that likely.

The recent pivot from Paena (on-chain earned wage access, built at ETHGlobal Buenos Aires in November 2025) to B2B compliance infrastructure in January 2026 also triggers my pattern recognition for end-of-cycle repositioning. When founders move quickly from a consumer DeFi product to enterprise compliance software -- two fundamentally different business models requiring different skills, sales motions, and domain knowledge -- it suggests they're searching for a fundable idea rather than attacking a problem they understand deeply. Compare this to how Stripe's founders spent years frustrated by payment integration before building the product. The best compliance infrastructure companies I've seen are built by people who lived inside the pain. This team lived inside crypto and pivoted to compliance tooling, which is a very different starting point.
