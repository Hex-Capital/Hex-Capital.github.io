# Mercury -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Mercury orchestrates other people's agents. That's the fundamental architectural fact here, and it determines everything else. The homepage shows Claude Code, Devin, and gstack operating under Mercury's coordination layer — three products built by Anthropic, Cognition, and another provider, each of which has every incentive to own the orchestration experience themselves. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown had synthesized the heme protein — the proprietary science *was* the company. Nobody could disintermediate Impossible because nobody else had the molecule. Mercury doesn't own the agents, doesn't own the models, doesn't control the underlying AI capability. The orchestration layer is a feature these agent providers will ship, not a durable product. OpenAI already released an Agents SDK with multi-agent orchestration. Anthropic will do the same. Mercury is renting a position in the value chain that the landlords are about to reclaim.

The consequence magnitude test kills this for me before I examine anything else. If Mercury fully succeeds — every non-technical user can deploy multi-agent AI teams through a no-code interface — what changes? Knowledge workers manage their AI tools somewhat more efficiently. The "20+ browser tabs and 9+ disconnected tools" problem goes away. That's a convenience improvement, not a transformation of how humans live. The agents themselves — Claude Code writing software, Devin debugging systems — those are the transformative technologies. Mercury is a management dashboard sitting on top of the revolution, not the revolution itself. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, success meant limitless clean energy. When I backed OpenAI, success meant artificial general intelligence. If Mercury succeeds, the world gets a better toolbar. I need my bets to change the world or lose my money — a moderately profitable SaaS orchestration tool is the worst possible outcome in my framework.

Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma both spent years as Palantir Forward Deployed Engineers — a role I respect because FDEs bridge technical complexity and operational reality. Stefano's Stanford dual in Religious Studies and Electrical Engineering signals intellectual range, which I notice. The dogfooding — running Mercury's own sales pipeline through a 5-agent system on their platform — shows they're building something they use, which is always a positive signal. But Palantir FDEs are domain experts in exactly this problem space of orchestrating complex systems for non-technical users. My stated preference is for outsiders who don't understand what can't be done. These founders understand precisely what can and can't be done, which means they'll build something competent rather than something audacious. The third co-founder "Jayson" — no last name, no background, no public presence — on a five-person team where co-founders are the gene pool, this opacity is a diligence problem I can't ignore.

The competitive landscape confirms this is a consensus opportunity, not a contrarian one. Lindy has raised roughly $50M and generates $5.1M in revenue. Relevance AI closed a $24M Series B. CrewAI has $24.5M and 150 enterprise beta customers. Dust is at $7.3M ARR. When every informed observer agrees a market exists and well-funded companies are already pursuing it, my interest drops — the return profile of consensus bets doesn't generate the asymmetric payoffs I need. Mercury's no-code multi-agent angle is a positioning distinction, not a technology moat. Adding multi-agent coordination to Lindy's existing no-code builder with an established user base is an incremental feature, while Mercury must build everything from scratch and acquire users against entrenched competitors with 50-100x more capital.

The bull case deserves examination. If heterogeneous AI agents proliferate — Claude Code for coding, Devin for engineering, specialized agents for sales, research, legal — and no single provider wins the whole stack, then a neutral orchestration layer becomes structurally necessary. Kubernetes won container orchestration despite Docker's attempt to own it, precisely because users needed a vendor-neutral coordination standard. If the multi-agent ecosystem fragments rather than consolidates, Mercury's position improves dramatically. The Palantir FDE background is genuinely the right training for this problem — years of making complex systems legible to non-technical operators. And the "why now" is real: twelve months ago, there weren't enough capable agents to need orchestration. But this bull case requires Mercury to have a proprietary technical advantage in orchestration — a novel memory system, a breakthrough in agent routing, something that makes their coordination qualitatively better, not just wrapped in a nicer interface. The dossier shows no evidence of such an advantage. "No defensibility signals found in public sources" is the exact sentence. Without a technical wedge, Mercury is competing on UI speed against better-funded competitors who can ship the same interface.

Mercury scores a Pass driven by two dimensions: consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. The consequence of success is a better workflow tool, not a changed world — that alone pushes below my threshold. The absence of any proprietary technology or technical discontinuity — orchestrating third-party agents through their existing APIs via a no-code interface — is the definition of the wrapper pattern I systematically avoid. Competent founders in a real market, building an incremental product in a crowded space. I'd rather lose my money on something that could change everything.
