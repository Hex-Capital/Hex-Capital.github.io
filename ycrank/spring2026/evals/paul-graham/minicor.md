# Minicor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Minicor is what it reveals about the current AI automation landscape: dozens of well-funded startups are building browser agents (Browserbase raised $40M, Browser Use raised $17M, Skyvern raised $2.7M), and not one of them touches Windows desktop applications or Citrix environments. That's not a coincidence. That's schlep blindness in action. Dealing with Windows VMs, Citrix session management, HIPAA compliance, and SOC 2 Type II certification is genuinely ugly work. It's the automation equivalent of dealing with banks and fraud at Stripe -- everyone can see the problem, nobody wants to wade into it. Minicor waded in. That impulse is worth examining closely.

The question I can't answer from the dossier is the one that matters most to me: how did they find this problem? The founders previously built Laminar, a low-code integration platform. There's a plausible organic story here -- you're doing system integrations, you keep smashing into legacy desktop applications that have no APIs, and eventually you realize the real bottleneck isn't integration logic but the desktop itself. That would be genuine organic discovery through adjacent work. But there's an equally plausible manufactured story -- Anthropic ships computer use in late 2024, you see the demos, and you pivot to build AI desktop agents because the technology suddenly exists and the market sounds large. The dossier contains no founder narrative distinguishing between these two paths. The pivot from Laminar to Minicor is recent enough that the sign-in portal still resolves to app.laminar.run. Without hearing the founders describe the specific moment they understood this problem, I'm left with a signal I can't fully read.

What I can read clearly is the schlep commitment. Getting SOC 2 Type II certified and HIPAA compliant as a four-person pre-seed company is not something you do casually. That's months of documentation, auditing, and process work that most founders at this stage would dismiss as premature. The fact that they did it tells me either they have customers who demanded it (strong signal) or they understood from day one that compliance is the moat in healthcare automation (also a strong signal, though a different one). The claim of "25,000 patients/day" in production deployment, if accurate, would place this company well ahead of where most pre-seed startups sit. But it's self-reported on their own website, and the dossier found no named customers or external validation. The enterprise logos are on the website but unnamed. I want to believe this is real pull, but I've learned to weight what users do over what founders claim.

The founding team fits a pattern I like. Both founders are engineers -- Faizaan at Microsoft, Saheed at Ford working on Android Auto, with additional experience at Morgan Stanley and Kinaxis. They attended the same university, co-founded a non-profit together, and clearly have a working relationship that predates the startup. Faizaan's GitHub shows genuine hacker instincts -- building a vector database (vemcache) and distributed LLM system (p2pllm) are projects that come from curiosity, not from resume optimization. Saheed's Android Auto work at Ford means he's dealt with complex platform UI integration before. These are builders, not presenters. The team composition is solid.

The bull case for Minicor is genuinely compelling: the schlep creates a natural moat that browser-only competitors cannot cross without rebuilding their entire infrastructure. Healthcare organizations are locked into legacy EHR systems for years, possibly decades, creating sustained demand for automation around immovable desktop software. The compliance certifications are real switching costs. If the 25K patients/day number is real production usage -- not aspirational capacity -- then they've already found product-market fit in a segment where customers pay enterprise prices and don't churn easily. This could be the company that does for desktop automation what Stripe did for payments: takes the obvious-but-ugly problem and builds the infrastructure layer everyone else avoided.

What holds me back is the convergence of two concerns. First, the "computer use" category is getting hot fast. When I said the most impressive YC companies aren't working on AI, I was warning about exactly this -- categories where the idea is consensus and well-funded competitors are abundant. Minicor's desktop focus differentiates them from browser agents, but UiPath ($6B market cap) and Automation Anywhere ($10B valuation) are not asleep; they'll bolt AI onto their existing enterprise relationships. Second, the foundation model dependency is structural. If Anthropic or OpenAI ships a production-grade desktop automation product, Minicor's core technology advantage compresses to the deployment and compliance layer. That layer is real, but it's infrastructure rather than intelligence -- and infrastructure businesses at this stage need to move extremely fast before the platform providers catch up. The recent pivot adds uncertainty about whether the founders have the deep domain conviction needed to outrun both incumbents and platform providers simultaneously.
