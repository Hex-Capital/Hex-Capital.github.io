# Ruma Care -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Christina Huang has been taking biologics for seven years. She has personally had her medications denied by insurers. This is the detail that stops me. When a founder has spent seven years navigating the system they're now trying to fix, that's not market research. That's not a brainstorming session. That's the kind of organic problem discovery you can't manufacture. And then her co-founder Meng Fei Shen went and actually worked as a medical assistant at a rheumatology clinic -- sat in the chair, made the faxes, navigated the portals, did the paperwork. Together they've experienced this problem from both sides: the patient waiting months for an approval that determines whether they get treated, and the staff member drowning in 70+ insurance portals trying to make that approval happen. I've written that the best startup ideas come from founders who stumble into problems through lived experience. This is that.

The schlep here is enormous, and it's the right kind of enormous. Prior authorization for biologics involves extracting submission criteria from dozens of insurance plans, each with different rules for each medication-diagnosis combination, filling out different forms for each, and then navigating denials and appeals. Seventy-plus online portals. Faxes in 2026. Phone calls to insurance companies. This is exactly the kind of ugly, tedious regulatory complexity that makes smart founders unconsciously look away. It's the same dynamic that kept Stripe hidden in plain sight for a decade -- every programmer knew payments were broken, but nobody wanted to deal with the banks and the fraud rules and the compliance burden. Here, every rheumatologist knows the PA system is broken, but nobody wants to build a product that requires mapping the rules of every insurer for every biologic for every diagnosis. That's the schlep, and the fact that it deters competitors is the moat.

The competitive landscape actually strengthens rather than weakens my conviction. Cohere Health raised $200M but chose to serve payers, not providers -- they're on the opposite side of the transaction. Waystar has a billion in revenue but offers PA as one feature among hundreds in a broad RCM platform. When large well-funded companies are in the space but positioned differently, that tells me the niche is real enough to attract capital but specific enough that nobody is building exactly this. The biologics buy-and-bill segment is where the economic stakes are highest: clinics purchasing $80-150K drugs upfront and absorbing the cost when denied. That's not a nice-to-have automation. That's existential for the clinic.

The team composition is right for this problem. Huang brings ML engineering from Apple and healthcare integration experience from Medallion, where she worked with UnitedHealthcare -- she's built in this domain before. Shen scaled Uber One to 13M+ members and led AI integrations for 100K+ suppliers at Walmart. They met at Yale, which means the co-founder relationship predates the company. The Formulary Navigator -- a free tool that lets providers instantly check coverage rules -- is a smart wedge that reminds me of the "Collison installation" mentality. You give people something useful for free, you become part of their workflow, and then you sell them the full platform. The Biologics Access Symposium with Stanford Medicine shows they're building credibility in their domain rather than just pitching VCs. These are founders who are going to their users, not waiting for users to come to them.

The gaps are real but appropriate for pre-seed. No revenue data. No user counts. The claimed metrics -- 50% denial rate reduction, 20x staff time reduction -- come from the YC page without independent verification. And the CMS FHIR mandate arriving in 2027 could lower technical barriers for new entrants. But two things give me confidence: first, the regulatory mandate is more tailwind than threat, because standardized APIs help a focused startup more than they help a broad platform trying to add a feature. Second, a two-person team burning very little cash while already live in pilot clinics in California and Nevada has a plausible path to ramen profitability given the high value of what they're automating. If preventing even one biologic denial saves a clinic $80-150K, the ROI on this product is immediate and obvious.

This is the kind of company that fits my framework almost by design: an organic problem discovered through personal suffering, a regulatory schlep that deters competitors, technical founders who have already built and deployed a working product, and a niche narrow enough to dominate before expanding into adjacent specialties. The main thing I'd want to see next is evidence that the pilot clinics are pulling the product forward -- renewals, referrals, expanding usage -- rather than passively participating. But the foundation is strong enough that I'd write a check.
