# arc-prize-foundation -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

I learned a very expensive lesson about nonprofit-for-profit hybrid structures with OpenAI. I put $50 million into an organization where I later admitted I "erred when my firm invested without discussing or considering that the nonprofit board really oversaw the for-profit enterprise." ARC Prize Foundation presents the same structural architecture -- a nonprofit benchmark organization whose co-founders simultaneously run Ndea, a for-profit AGI lab that raised $43 million from Coatue and already appears as a donor to the nonprofit. Chollet and Knoop are straddling both entities, and the insights from ARC-AGI -- the benchmark they control -- flow directly into the commercial entity they operate. I made that mistake once at a much larger scale and got lucky because OpenAI's trajectory overwhelmed the governance problem. But the lesson wasn't "governance doesn't matter." The lesson was "never again ignore governance complexity just because the technology trajectory is exciting." Here, the governance questions are baked in from day one, and unlike OpenAI in 2019, the entire AI field is now watching.

Let me address consequence of success directly: if ARC Prize succeeds, the world gets a better yardstick for measuring progress toward AGI. That matters. Defining what intelligence means shapes the entire field's direction. But a measuring stick is not the thing it measures. The benchmark doesn't disrupt a trillion-dollar industry. It doesn't make an incumbent system obsolete. It doesn't replace the way anything works. The civilization-scale consequence -- actual AGI -- lives inside Ndea, not ARC Prize Foundation. And I'm not being asked to invest in Ndea. I'm evaluating a nonprofit that runs annual competitions with $1 million prize pools, funded by donations from AI labs and individuals. My entire portfolio construction depends on "either the money expires or you build a very successful company." A nonprofit, by definition, cannot produce the transformational financial outcome that justifies the risk of total loss. Even my most mission-driven investments -- Impossible Foods, Commonwealth Fusion Systems -- were structured as for-profit entities where saving the planet and generating a hundred-fold return were the same event. ARC Prize separates those two outcomes across two entities, and I'm looking at the one without the return.

The bull case is worth engaging seriously. François Chollet holds one of the most genuinely contrarian positions in all of AI: that current LLM scaling approaches will not achieve general intelligence. While the entire industry -- OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta -- is spending tens of billions on the thesis that scale is all you need, Chollet is arguing they're measuring the wrong thing entirely. His ARC-AGI benchmark explicitly tests for novel problem-solving that memorization-based systems cannot fake. If he's right, he's the person who defined the goalpost that actually matters, and every AI lab will orient around his framework. That's the kind of contrarian courage I look for -- not just disagreeing with consensus, but building the infrastructure that forces the industry to confront whether the consensus is wrong. Knoop walking away from a $5 billion company he co-founded to pursue AGI measurement is a signal of conviction I respect. And Greg Kamradt created the Needle In A Haystack benchmark that became industry standard for evaluating context windows -- proving this team knows how to build evaluation tools the field adopts.

But here's where my framework pushes back on the founder profile: Chollet isn't an outsider. He's the creator of Keras, one of the most widely used deep learning frameworks in history, with 63,900 GitHub stars and 71,000 citations. He spent years as a Senior Staff Engineer at Google. He is the definition of an AI insider. Yes, his thesis is contrarian, but his identity is not. The most transformative companies in my portfolio were built by people who didn't know what the industry considered impossible -- a biochemist making hamburgers, a New Zealander building rockets, a Xerox PARC scientist challenging Cisco. Chollet knows exactly what the AI industry considers impossible; he's been arguing about it publicly for years. That's a different thing than the naive outsider confidence that produces genuine breakthroughs. His contrarianism might be exactly right, but it's expert contrarianism -- and expert contrarians often build important critique rather than category-defining companies.

The timing is perfect -- I'll give them that. The question of "what is AGI and how do we measure it" has become the most consequential question in technology precisely as AI capabilities are advancing exponentially. ARC Prize is positioned at the exact nexus of that question. If I were evaluating Ndea as a standalone commercial entity -- a program synthesis approach to AGI informed by Chollet's benchmark framework, with $43 million in funding and the same founding team -- I'd be leaning much harder in. The rate of change in AI capability makes the timing right. But the entity in front of me is a nonprofit benchmark organization, and my framework cannot reconcile "no path to financial returns" with "write a check from my own money." Some problems are too important not to work on. This one is too important not to invest in -- but through Ndea, not through the foundation.
