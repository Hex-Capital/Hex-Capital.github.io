# Baud -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Baud is the shape of company I almost always pass on and occasionally regret: a credentialed hardware pair attacking a Stripe-grade schlep (ASIC tape-out against NVIDIA) without the cereal-box moment that would tell me Sarang Zambare and Eric Taylor are animals rather than excellent industrial chip engineers. The dossier's headline number — a 50M-parameter model running 1000+ tokens/sec on FPGA emulation, plus a claimed "100x more efficient at the same process node" — is a real artifact, not a deck slide, and that matters. But the pattern that activates my checkbook is a founder who shipped a thing because they had to, and Baud reads more like two senior IC engineers who spotted a wedge than two people who couldn't sleep until the wedge existed.

Coinbase (2012) is the ladder rung Baud rhymes with on the schlep dimension: Brian Armstrong walked toward bitcoin compliance because every other YC applicant was unconsciously routing around it, and the regulatory grind itself became the moat. Baud's schlep — Eric Taylor's three-plus tape-outs of NVIDIA-class silicon, the multi-year RTL and foundry grind, the PyTorch compiler surface that has to mirror CUDA — is exactly the kind of work software-only YC founders flinch from, which is the right side of "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012). Where Baud diverges from the Coinbase rung is that this particular schlep is no longer contrarian: Etched is at a $5B valuation, MatX raised $500M, Taalas raised $219M, and Dealroom counted $8.3B into AI chip startups in 2026 alone. The schlep is real, but the crowd has arrived, which means the schlep stops being a moat and starts being a capital race.

The pattern that scares me on the lowest dimension — co-founder configuration — is the Kiko (2005) cautionary inverse: Justin Kan and Emmett Shear worked together long enough that I re-funded them as Twitch (2007) on relationship strength alone. Baud's dossier discloses no shared prior employer or university between Zambare (IIT Bombay → Berkeley → Caper → Peloton) and Taylor (NVIDIA → NXP → Arteris → Enfabrica), the third seat is literally an open job posting for "Founding ASIC Architect / RTL & FPGA Lead," and team-size-3 with two named founders means the chemistry I'd usually test in a YC interview is the only thing I have. Not a red flag — but not the Huffman/Ohanian dorm-room signal either.

On founder-pain organicness, Baud is mechanism-adjacent rather than mechanism-organic: Zambare shipped Peloton Guide vision models to 100,000+ devices on Qualcomm silicon, which means he has held the GPU-and-memory-bandwidth bottleneck in his own hands, and Taylor has spent ten-plus years staring at the same matmul-dominated floorplans. That's stronger than "we saw a market"; it's weaker than Drew Houston forgetting his USB stick. Over 18 months, this kind of adjacency compounds well if the compiler ergonomics get personal — if Zambare ports his old Peloton workload onto the Baud toolchain as the canonical demo — and rots fast if Baud writes its first reference kernels for a customer's workload instead of its own.

On animal resourcefulness, the counterfactual I keep running is: what would have to be true in this dossier for Baud to flip from neutral to a check? The FPGA demo at 1000+ tokens/sec is a real pre-funding artifact and that's why I'm not at 12/35 default, but to clear the bar I'd need to see something like a public bring-up video on Hacker News with a reproducible benchmark, or Taylor and Zambare having self-funded a small test tape-out before the YC check, or a signed LOI from a named frontier-model lab pulled in via cold outreach. None of those are in the dossier. What is there — patents, NVIDIA pedigree, tape-out count — reads as resume rather than stunt.

On hacker genealogy, the single-anchor parallel is Stripe (2010): Patrick Collison won the Young Scientist prize for Lisp work at sixteen, and that public hacker artifact predated the company. Baud's founders have four patents (Zambare) and two patents plus a publication (Taylor) and zero discoverable GitHub presence or HN footprint. Hardware people leave different tracks than web hackers, and tape-outs are the genuine hacker artifact in this domain, so I don't penalize them as hard as I would two MBAs. But the absence of any open-source compiler work, any public RTL repo, any conference talk on the arithmetic representation they're claiming — that's a tell that the work has lived inside corporate walls, not in the open.

My 36-month forecast: Baud raises a $25–60M Series A in the next 12 months on the FPGA demo plus Taylor's NVIDIA pedigree, fails to reach a competitive 5nm tape-out before the cash gets tight against MatX's $600M war chest, and gets acquihired by Meta's MTIA group or Amazon's Annapurna team for sub-$300M between months 24 and 36. The mechanism is capital intensity colliding with hyperscaler in-house silicon: the buyer set for merchant frontier-training ASICs is roughly six companies, three of which (Google TPU, Meta MTIA, AWS Trainium) are building competing internal chips, and the acquihire path is the natural exit when the cap table can't match the foundry bill.

The bull case that stings: Etched took a narrower bet (transformer inference only) and got to $5B, and Baud's claim — a *lossless* arithmetic representation that eliminates multiplications while preserving PyTorch generality — is structurally bigger if even one-fifth of the 100x claim survives silicon. The "Frighteningly Ambitious" essay (March 2012) tells me to lean in here, and the Stripe known exception tells me that a credentialed pair with prior shipping experience can override my YC-process-completion preference. What dismantles the bull for me is the spend implication: this is a category where you need $200M+ to be in the room, and PG-thesis companies that need $200M+ to be in the room have historically not been my best checks. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest is a public, third-party-benchmarked silicon (not FPGA) demonstration of >5x perf-per-watt on a real PyTorch training workload from a named AI lab within 18 months — at that point the schlep has produced an asset, and I'd write the follow-on at whatever the markup is.
