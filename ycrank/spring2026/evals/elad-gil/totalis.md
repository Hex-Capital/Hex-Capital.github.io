# Totalis -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the first thing I notice, and they're unfavorable. Totalis wants to be the neutral cross-platform derivative layer for prediction markets -- a position analogous to what Stripe became for payments. But there's a critical structural difference: Stripe built infrastructure that merchants needed and that banks couldn't easily replicate because banks had no incentive to serve developers directly. Totalis depends on exchange APIs controlled by Kalshi, Polymarket, and others -- exchanges that are actively building competing parlay infrastructure themselves. Kalshi already has RFQ-based parlays accounting for over 20% of its weekly sports volume. Polymarket acquired QCX, a derivatives platform. Robinhood licensed Kalshi's RFQ system for Custom Combos. This isn't a market where the infrastructure layer is unoccupied -- the incumbents are filling it, and they're structurally incentivized to keep it proprietary because parlays drive volume retention on their own exchanges. This triggers one of my clearest red flags: building an application on a contested infrastructure layer where the platform owners have both the capability and incentive to compete directly.

On market timing: the prediction market explosion is real -- 17x monthly volume growth from early 2025 to January 2026, Kalshi at $22B valuation, CFTC moving to formalize regulation. But this is the opposite of nonobvious. It's one of the most visible structural shifts in fintech right now. Every sophisticated investor can read the TRM Labs data. The slightly less obvious angle -- that cross-platform derivatives infrastructure is needed -- becomes less compelling when you note that Kalshi has Builder Codes for third-party developers, Polymarket is acquiring derivatives capability, and Robinhood is already licensing parlay technology. The derivative layer thesis isn't contrarian; it's the natural next step that incumbents are already executing on. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was genuinely dismissed by mainstream investors. Prediction market derivatives infrastructure is not being dismissed -- it's being built by $22B companies with $1.5B in annualized revenue.

The single-miracle test is where this really breaks down for me. I can count at least three independent challenges that each need to resolve favorably: exchanges need to maintain open API access rather than restricting it to protect their own parlay products; market makers need to provide liquidity through Totalis rather than directly through the exchanges where they already operate; and the CFTC's ongoing rulemaking on event contracts needs to not classify a cross-platform derivative layer as requiring its own DCM or swap execution facility registration. Washington state's AG suing Kalshi for illegal gambling adds another regulatory vector. When I see compounding independent risks like this, the math gets very unfavorable very quickly. Each challenge might have reasonable odds individually, but multiplied together they erode expected value rapidly.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: prediction markets are following the trajectory of traditional financial markets, where the derivatives layer eventually dwarfs the spot market. The global derivatives market is $12.4 trillion versus spot. If prediction markets reach even a fraction of traditional market structure, the derivatives layer is the bigger prize. The cross-platform angle is genuinely valuable -- traders want to combine a Polymarket position on an election outcome with a Kalshi position on an economic indicator, and no single exchange can credibly offer this because they'd need to admit their competitors' products are worth combining with. Exchange neutrality is a position exchanges structurally cannot occupy. If Totalis achieves critical mass in cross-platform parlay liquidity, network effects compound: more market makers improve pricing, which attracts more traders, which attracts more market makers. That's the Coinbase-at-seed version of this company. What would have to be true is that exchanges remain fragmented long enough for a neutral layer to establish itself, regulation permits it, and the founders can bootstrap market maker liquidity without the resources of a $22B exchange. That's possible, but it requires the market to evolve in a very specific way.

On the founders: Eric Liu and Pravesh Mansharamani have genuine domain depth in exactly the right area. Pravesh built EigenBets, worked at Nethermind and SquidRouter, and the pair co-built Goddid.Money -- a Polymarket-related tool -- at ETHGlobal. Eleven-plus hackathon wins totaling $55K+ demonstrates rapid prototyping ability. They know this stack. But hackathon wins aren't operating experience, and building a financial infrastructure company that sits between exchanges and market makers requires navigating exchange business development relationships, regulatory compliance, and institutional trust that goes well beyond shipping code quickly. These are student founders with strong technical credentials and zero evidence of the organizational scaling capability I look for. At pre-seed, I give founders benefit of the doubt on execution -- but the market structure challenges here are severe enough that even experienced operators would struggle.

I'm passing. The market is real but obvious, the infrastructure position is structurally fragile because it depends on exchange cooperation that exchanges are incentivized to withhold, and the company needs multiple simultaneous breakthroughs. The founders are technically sharp in the domain but the challenges ahead are business development and regulatory, not engineering. This is a bet I'd need to see significantly more evidence on -- specifically, signed agreements with at least one major exchange guaranteeing API access for parlay construction, and some indication that the regulatory path doesn't require its own exchange registration -- before I'd revisit.
