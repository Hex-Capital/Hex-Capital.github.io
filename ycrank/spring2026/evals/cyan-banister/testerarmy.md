# TesterArmy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me before anything else: at least six companies have raised north of $200 million combined in AI-powered testing in just the last two years. QA Wolf took $56 million. Momentic — also YC — raised $18 million and already has 2,600 users. Checkly, $32 million. Mabl, $76 million. Functionize, $41 million. When I see that many funded players in a space, with VCs falling over each other to write checks, I know this is the opposite of the kind of bet I make. My best investments have come from spaces where the room goes quiet when I describe them — rockets that blow up, surveillance cameras that civil liberties groups hate, male birth control that makes VCs squirm. AI-powered QA makes nobody uncomfortable. It's the most obvious application of LLMs plus browser automation you can imagine. Every developer tools investor on Sand Hill Road is nodding along to this thesis right now. That consensus signal is loud, and it's telling me to move on.

When I ask "tell me your story" — not the business, the story — I get three talented young Polish developers who worked together at Callstack, a React Native consultancy. Szymon started coding at 14 and has been professionally shipping code since he was essentially a kid. Oskar has over a hundred merged PRs to React Native Core and 8,200+ stars across his open-source projects. That's real technical credibility, not manufactured. I respect what these founders have built in their young careers. But when I look for the biographical forge — that specific life experience that made this person incapable of ignoring *this* problem — I don't find it. They're front-end developers who experienced the annoyance of testing, the way every developer who has ever shipped code has experienced it. "I was building React Native apps and testing was painful" is a market observation, not a crucible. The problem didn't find them in some visceral, unavoidable way. They found it by looking at the market.

There's no invisible system here. This is what separates TesterArmy from the kind of company that lights up my pattern recognition. When I backed Affirm, there was a hidden tax — credit card companies were extracting value from people through deliberate opacity around interest rates. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessment was an opaque system that punished people who didn't know how to fight back. Software testing is annoying and expensive, but it's transparently so. Every engineering manager knows exactly what QA costs them. There's no information asymmetry being exploited, no gatekeepers hiding value from people who can't see it. The pain is real, but it's visible pain in a well-understood market. I need to see an extractive structure being dismantled, and I don't see one.

The strongest bull case I can construct: if the zero-config agent actually works with high reliability and low false positives, and if "vibe coding" genuinely creates a tsunami of AI-generated code that needs automated QA, then the first product to nail bottoms-up GitHub distribution could capture a generation of developers. And underneath the testing surface, these founders might be building a general-purpose web understanding agent — if your AI truly learns to navigate any web application autonomously, that's infrastructure that goes far beyond QA. But this is my imagination doing work the dossier doesn't support. There's no evidence the founders think about it this way, and even if they did, the barrier to building a basic version is low with Playwright and commodity LLMs available to everyone. Momentic already has users and momentum. The defensibility question haunts this one — GitHub itself could ship native AI testing through Copilot and make the entire category an afterthought.

These founders have real talent and genuine open-source standing. In a different space — something stigmatized, something structurally opaque, something that made investors shift in their chairs — I'd want to lean in and learn more about who they are beneath the resume. But talented people in a crowded, consensus-attractive market with no hidden system to disrupt and no biographical inevitability connecting them to the problem? That's not a Long Journey bet. I wish them well, genuinely. They may build a solid business. But this isn't where my conviction lives.
