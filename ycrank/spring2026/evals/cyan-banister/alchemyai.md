# Alchemy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Alchemy's core capability — using LLMs to orchestrate open-source computer vision models for microscopy analysis — sits directly on top of generally available foundation models and free tools like CellProfiler, Cellpose, and napari. When Lee Jacobs and I talk about what we won't fund, we use a phrase: "not some B.S. SaaS company or AI wrapper." Alchemy needs to prove it isn't one. The dossier itself flags the risk plainly: the technical challenge "relies on prompt engineering and model orchestration rather than proprietary model architectures." A well-resourced incumbent like Leica, which already offers Aivia with 22 built-in applications and 20 pre-trained deep learning models, could integrate a natural-language interface in a product cycle. PathAI has $490M in funding and FDA approval. Proscia has $130M and 400% year-over-year case growth. Alchemy's moat today is speed of execution against organizations that haven't prioritized this specific workflow yet — and speed without structure is borrowed time.

Phillip Baek holds a BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin, which gives him domain familiarity with life science workflows, but familiarity is not the forge I look for. He also has an M.Ed. in Psychological Counseling from Columbia and was heading to Boston Scientific as an EP Mapping Specialist. Liam McBride brings strong engineering credentials — MS in Computer Science from UIUC — and built a shared living app called BoBo that raised $20K in non-dilutive funding. When I ask my first question — "tell me their story, not the business" — what I hear is two capable young engineers who spotted a market gap, not founders whose lives made them incapable of ignoring this problem. Neither has published in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis. No Google Scholar results, no PubMed results. Alchemy's credibility with research lab PIs — the people who sign off on institutional licenses — will depend on early validation, and right now that validation is a single UC Santa Cruz PhD student endorsement on LinkedIn. I've backed founders with thinner resumes who were forged in something that burned so hot they couldn't walk away. I don't see that heat here.

Alchemy operates in AI-powered life sciences, which in 2026 is about as consensus-attractive as a space gets. Nobody shifts in their chair when they hear "AI for microscopy analysis." There is no stigma, no regulatory frontier, no political toxicity. Compare this to when I wrote my check into Anduril — defense tech was actively shunned by Silicon Valley engineers who wouldn't even take the interview. Or Contraline, where male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable in the pitch meeting. Alchemy's pitch provokes nodding, not discomfort, and that nod is precisely the signal that tells me every AI-focused fund in the YC batch is looking at similar companies. The 98% time reduction claim is impressive if verified, and the PhD student who went from three hours of H. pylori analysis to under ten minutes is a real data point. But impressive workflow acceleration in a consensus category is a different animal from contrarian conviction in a hard space.

The bull case an advocate would make: Alchemy is genuinely early to a specific application — natural-language-to-pipeline orchestration for research microscopy — that wasn't technically feasible before 2024. Enterprise incumbents (PathAI, Proscia, Aivia) are oriented toward clinical diagnostics and large pharma, leaving individual researchers and academic lab PIs underserved and stuck with free-but-painful open-source tools. Alchemy's self-service, no-code interface could drive bottom-up adoption the way Carta crept into startups before enterprise vendors noticed. If researchers build reproducible pipelines on Alchemy and cite them in publications, switching costs emerge organically. The model library — each user contributing trained models that benefit the next — could create a network effect that compounds with adoption. This is real. But the Carta parallel breaks down at the structural level: Carta disrupted an opaque system where cap table information was hidden, inaccessible, and used as leverage against founders and employees. Alchemy makes a transparent process faster. Researchers understand exactly what CellProfiler and ImageJ do — they just wish it took less time. There's no invisible tax here, no information asymmetry being exposed, no gatekeeper extracting value from people who don't know any better. The hidden system beneath the business, which is what I look for in everything from Uber's attack on the taxi medallion cartel to Ownwell's attack on property tax opacity, is simply absent from Alchemy's thesis.

Alchemy's potential for real-world impact is genuine but modest. Making microscopy analysis accessible to researchers who can't code does expand participation in biological research, and accelerating drug discovery workflows has downstream benefits for patients. That UC Santa Cruz PhD student getting hours back every week is meaningful to that person's career. But this isn't Wonderschool expanding childcare access or Serve Robotics bringing delivery to underserved neighborhoods — Alchemy serves researchers who are already inside institutions, already doing this work, already privileged with lab access and grant funding. The impact cascades are real but they don't structurally change who can participate in the system.

One verifiable condition would flip me: if Baek or McBride has a personal story I'm not seeing in this dossier — a family member's diagnosis that required microscopy analysis that failed, a research experience where a critical finding was missed because the manual analysis was too slow, something that connects their biography to this problem at a level deeper than "I studied biomedical engineering" — that changes my read on the forge. Combined with evidence of a proprietary model library with meaningful researcher contributions creating genuine network effects (not just a roadmap slide), Alchemy could move from Pass to Neutral. But the absence of biographical depth, the consensus nature of AI + life sciences, the lack of an invisible system to disrupt, and the thin moat against both open-source alternatives and well-funded incumbents all point in the same direction. Alchemy is a competent team building a useful product in a real market, and I wish them well. It's not a Long Journey bet.
