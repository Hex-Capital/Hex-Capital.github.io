# Aurorin CAD -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural bet here is worth isolating before anything else: a solo founder is attempting to build a complete parametric CAD kernel from scratch — something that took Dassault thousands of engineers and decades to create with SolidWorks. The thesis is that AI-assisted coding has compressed that timeline by orders of magnitude, making a task that was previously institutional now possible for an individual. If that thesis is correct, this is not just a CAD company — it's an early proof that AI development tools have shifted the boundary of what a single person with specific knowledge can build. That's a leverage thesis I care about deeply. If it's wrong, this is a solo founder drowning in an ocean of computational geometry.

Michael Baron's specific knowledge sits in an interesting but incomplete zone. Three SpaceX internships — Raptor combustion simulation, Dragon GNC, Starshield — means he's been on the user side of demanding CAD workflows in the most performance-critical engineering environment on earth. He knows what it feels like when SolidWorks takes four hours to open your assembly. The Apple GPU driver optimization work is directly relevant to building a GPU-accelerated geometric kernel. The no-degree path — going from high school straight into SpaceX — signals the kind of obsessive autodidact who follows curiosity rather than credentials, which is a positive marker in my framework. But there's a gap: I don't see evidence of deep computational geometry expertise — NURBS mathematics, boundary representation algorithms, constraint solver design. These are among the hardest problems in software engineering, and they're not the kind of thing where close-enough works. A kernel that's 98% geometrically correct is useless for production mechanical engineering. Baron has adjacent technical knowledge and genuine user-side pain, but the core kernel-building specific knowledge is unproven.

The leverage architecture, if it works, is among the best I've seen at pre-seed. "A part that takes an experienced SolidWorks user 20 minutes can be created in seconds" — that's not incremental improvement, that's a qualitative shift in what one mechanical engineer can produce. This maps directly to my thesis: tools that multiply individual output are persistently undervalued because the market prices them against the incumbents rather than against the new category of work they enable. More importantly, controlling the kernel rather than bolting AI onto Parasolid is the infrastructure play. It's the difference between Alchemy building the blockchain API layer versus building another DeFi app on Ethereum. Adam and Zoo are building at the application layer on top of existing geometric engines. Aurorin is building the engine. Every limitation in those legacy kernels — constraints on what the AI agent can modify, what operations can be parallelized on GPU, how deeply the conversational interface can reach into the geometry — becomes Aurorin's structural advantage. This is a picks-and-shovels decision that reveals genuine understanding of where the bottleneck lives.

The contrarian timing is specific and testable, which is what I want. Jared Friedman stated it explicitly: "This would have been impossible before AI coding got good." Most smart engineers would dismiss a solo founder building a CAD kernel as delusional — and until recently, they'd be right. The bet is that the same AI tools transforming software development have crossed a threshold where the complexity barrier that protected incumbents has collapsed. If that's true, every complex infrastructure category — not just CAD — is suddenly attackable by small teams. Baron is making this bet with his own time and career, which I respect. The competitors confirm the contrarian nature: Adam and Zoo both chose the easier path of building on existing kernels, implicitly accepting the conventional wisdom that kernel development is too hard. Aurorin is the only one saying the conventional wisdom is newly wrong.

The strongest bull case: if Baron can demonstrate a working kernel with sufficient geometric precision for even early-stage prototyping workflows, this becomes one of the most defensible positions in AI-for-engineering. A custom kernel that works is a multi-year barrier. It compounds as AI capabilities improve — because Aurorin can integrate AI at the kernel level, not the plugin level, every advance in LLM reasoning translates directly into deeper design capabilities. The TAM is $12B+ and growing. Incumbents face classic innovator's dilemma — Dassault can't rebuild SolidWorks from scratch without cannibalizing their maintenance-fee revenue and VAR channel. PTC paid $470M for Onshape rather than attempt this internally. If Baron is the right founder, this is a decade-long compounding bet.

The bear case is the solo founder risk at this specific scope. Building a CAD kernel isn't writing a web application — it's computational geometry, numerical precision, constraint solving, and file format interoperability, all of which require specialized expertise that AI coding tools are least equipped to generate. No confirmed GitHub presence for someone building the most complex software possible is a yellow flag. No traction beyond a free download and 37 YC launch upvotes. And the enterprise switching costs in CAD are brutal — retraining, PLM integration, regulatory compliance — which means even a superior product faces years of sales cycle friction. I'd need to meet Baron, see a demo, and assess whether the kernel handles real engineering geometry before writing a check. The thesis is sound. The question is whether this specific founder has the depth to execute it.
