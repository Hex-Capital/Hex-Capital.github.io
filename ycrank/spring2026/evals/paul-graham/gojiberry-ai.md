# Gojiberry AI -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing I can't get past is the TrustMRR listing. On March 9, 2026 — one week after a #1 Product Hunt launch and while actively participating in YC S26 — this company was listed for sale at $11.15 million. I don't know the intent behind this. Maybe it's a signal to acquirers, maybe it's testing valuation, maybe it's nothing. But when I think about the Airbnb founders selling cereal boxes at a loss to keep the lights on, or Patrick Collison grabbing people's laptops to install Stripe on the spot, what I'm seeing is the opposite of that energy. Those founders could not have been bought out at that stage because they were building something they couldn't stop building. Listing for sale during YC doesn't tell me these founders are cockroaches. It tells me they might be flippers. And when I'm investing my own money at the earliest stage, "the earlier you pick startups, the more you're picking the founders" — so this matters enormously.

Which is frustrating, because the execution here is genuinely impressive. $175K MRR, 1,673 active subscriptions, 75% profit margin, <5% churn, reached on roughly $380/month in distribution spend. These three have all had prior exits — Pierre-Eliott and Roman built and sold CoCo AI together, Dylan built Edusign to $3.3M in revenue and 600K+ users. They pivoted cleanly from a CRM assistant with zero paying users to this intent-signal product that found immediate traction. That pivot speed — "most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended" — is the mark of founders who listen to the market rather than fall in love with their own idea. And Roman's 12-channel distribution machine producing those numbers is something I'd normally celebrate. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off," and these founders are clearly making it take off.

But here's where my framework diverges from a conventional analysis. I wrote that "whenever you hear anybody talk about 'growth hacks,' just mentally translate it in your mind to 'bullshit.'" Five thousand cold emails a day, 800 LinkedIn DMs a week, Reddit posts, YouTube videos — this is a distribution operation, not a product that's hitting a nerve so hard it pulls users in. Compare that to Stripe, where the product was so obviously needed by developers that the main bottleneck was just getting them to type in their API keys. Gojiberry's traction is real, but I want to understand what fraction comes from the product being genuinely better versus the founders running an outbound machine that would generate traction for almost any reasonably useful tool. When growth comes from tactics rather than organic pull, it raises the question of what happens when those tactics stop or competitors copy them — and in this market, competitors are numerous and well-funded.

The idea itself doesn't have the shape of a black swan. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." LinkedIn intent signals for SMB sales teams? Everyone can see that's useful. Apollo just acquired Pocus to add signal-based selling to their platform. Clay hit $100M ARR building flexible signal-to-action workflows. Lemlist, Instantly, HeyReach — they've all reached $10M+ ARR in adjacent categories. This is a market where a $99/mo tool can absolutely build a nice business, and these founders are proving it. But "nice business" is not what generates the returns that matter in a power-law portfolio. The enterprise pricing floor creates a real gap that protects Gojiberry's SMB niche, and <5% churn suggests real product value. But LinkedIn signal detection and automated messaging are technically reproducible — the memo says so directly — and the platform dependency on LinkedIn is severe. One policy change could disable core functionality overnight.

The strongest bull case: these are three repeat founders who've proven they can build, sell, and ship software. They've found real product-market fit in an underserved segment. The data flywheel from 1,000+ customers creates compounding advantages in lead scoring accuracy. If they maintain 44% MoM growth, they hit $1M MRR by summer. The $99/mo price point is structurally inaccessible to enterprise incumbents — this is a "schlep blindness" opportunity where 6sense and Demandbase literally cannot serve these customers without destroying their own unit economics. And maybe the sale listing was just savvy optionality, not lack of commitment. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that SMB sales intelligence is a winner-take-most market at the bottom of the funnel, that the data flywheel becomes genuinely proprietary, and that these founders, once inside YC, will catch the ambition to build something much larger than a $10M ARR tool. That's possible. I just don't see enough evidence that it's probable.

What keeps me in the middle is the tension between exceptional execution and uncertain ambition. "When founders are both formidable and earnest, they're as close to unstoppable as you get." These founders are formidable — three exits, rapid iteration, real traction. But I need the earnestness too. I need to believe they're building this because they can't not build it, because "a genuine interest in something is a very powerful motivator — for some people, the most powerful motivator of all." The sale listing, the growth-hack distribution playbook, the pattern of building-and-flipping across CoCo AI and now possibly Gojiberry — it all suggests founders who are excellent at the game of startups rather than obsessed with the problem of helping small sales teams win. They're resourceful. I'm not convinced they're relentlessly resourceful about this specific thing.
