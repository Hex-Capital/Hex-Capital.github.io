# Adialante -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is real and it's deep. Torres and Jenkins didn't read about MRI physics and decide to start a company -- they invented a new physics modality during years of graduate research at UMN's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research, which is arguably the best MRI research lab on the planet. FREE technology -- eliminating gradient coils entirely from an MRI system -- is the kind of breakthrough that emerges from obsessive curiosity at the frontier of a field, not from surveying a market and identifying a gap. You cannot hire someone off LinkedIn to do this. You cannot read a textbook and replicate it. The team includes an ISMRM Rabi Award winner, which is the premier recognition for young MRI researchers globally. Five PhDs spanning MRI physics, image reconstruction, and computational physics. This isn't domain expertise dressed up as specific knowledge -- it's the genuine article. Torres's personal mission, rooted in growing up on the south side of Chicago and wanting to bring diagnostic imaging to communities that lack it, adds the kind of authentic founder-problem connection that can't be manufactured.

But here's where my framework creates tension: the leverage architecture is inverted. The physics innovation is pure leverage -- one breakthrough in how magnetic resonance works, applicable to every scan forever. That's code-like leverage at the physics layer. Yet the business model they've chosen -- mobile imaging clinics on wheels -- is the opposite of leverage. Every new market requires trucks, drivers, MRI technicians, scheduling systems, multi-jurisdictional regulatory compliance, fleet maintenance. This scales linearly with physical assets and headcount. It's closer to a logistics company with proprietary hardware than a technology platform. When I invested in Uber, the leverage was that the app turned every existing car into a taxi without Uber owning any cars. Adialante is proposing to own and operate the vehicles. The natural leverage play would be licensing FREE technology to imaging companies, or selling compact MRI units to clinics at a price point that makes capital purchase feasible. Instead, they've chosen the hardest operational path -- and nothing in their academic backgrounds suggests they know how to run a mobile healthcare fleet.

The contrarian positioning is mixed. Five years ago, "MRI should be cheaper and portable" was a genuinely non-consensus bet. In 2026, it's practically a category -- Hyperfine is public with $20M+ revenue guidance, Promaxo has FDA clearance and 69 installed prostate MRI systems with $63-81M raised, Chipiron just raised $17M for quantum-based MRI, Siemens launched the Free.Max. Smart money is pouring into low-cost MRI. The specific physics approach (FREE) is contrarian within the MRI physics community -- eliminating gradient coils is a bold claim that most MRI engineers would question. And targeting prostate rather than neuro is differentiated positioning. But the category itself has moved from contrarian to consensus, which means the pricing on deals in this space already reflects optimism. Promaxo is the direct threat: same use case, FDA-cleared, 69+ systems deployed, and backed with 25-30x Adialante's capital.

The strongest bull case is that this resembles my Extropic bet -- a physics-first breakthrough that, if it works, creates a qualitatively new capability that compounds at the hardware layer. If FREE technology delivers 1/10th the cost and size of conventional MRI with clinically useful image quality, and they achieve FDA clearance, then the mobile model is just a beachhead. The real business becomes selling or licensing compact MRI units that fit in any clinic, anywhere in the world. FDA clearance itself becomes a multi-year moat. And the per-scan model, while operationally heavy, eliminates adoption friction entirely -- zero CapEx means every urology practice becomes a potential customer overnight. The global prostate cancer imaging market alone is $2.4B. If this physics works, the founders' specific knowledge is irreplaceable, and the technology compounds as they refine reconstruction algorithms with each scan. That's a legitimate path to an outsized outcome.

What holds me back is the academic-to-commercial transition combined with the operational complexity of the business model. I've seen brilliant scientists build extraordinary technology and fail because they didn't understand distribution, operations, or how to navigate the FDA. No one on this team has visible startup experience. The $2.43M raised to date is thin compared to Promaxo's $63-81M and Hyperfine's $90M+. And the mobile operations layer adds a second hard problem on top of the already-hard physics problem. When I look at my deep-science hardware bets -- Extropic, Atomic Semi -- those are pure technology plays where the leverage is in the chip, not in operating a fleet. Adialante is asking me to believe they can simultaneously solve novel MRI physics, navigate FDA clearance, and build a mobile healthcare logistics operation. That's three bets, not one. The specific knowledge earns my respect, but the leverage architecture gives me pause. I'd want to see either a pivot toward licensing/hardware sales, or the addition of an operator with mobile healthcare experience, before writing a check.
