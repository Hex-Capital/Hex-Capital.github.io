# Benchspan -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to: AI agent benchmarking in spring 2026 is one of the most crowded, well-capitalized corners of the developer tools market. Braintrust just closed $80 million in February. LangChain has $260 million and 1,000 customers. Galileo posted 834% revenue growth. When I look at a space and see that much capital already deployed, that much consensus already formed, my instinct is to walk the other direction. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Benchspan is neither of those things -- it's a sensible product in a space where every serious investor is already writing checks. The "weirdness" that I need to feel in my bones just isn't here. Nobody hears "cloud platform for running AI benchmarks faster" and says "that's insane." They nod. And when everyone nods, the price is wrong and the differentiation has to come from somewhere I can't yet see.

If I could sit across from Avi and Ritesh, the first thing I'd ask is: tell me how you got here. Not the Microsoft pedigree or the Bloomberg throughput numbers -- I want the thread that connects who you are to why this problem keeps you up at night. And looking at what's available, I see strong professional credentials but no biographical thread that would make me believe these founders are solving something personal. Avi built agent evaluation infrastructure at Microsoft for GitHub Copilot, which is genuinely relevant domain experience -- probably the most precisely relevant background you could design for this exact company. Ritesh architected 100K+ TPS trading systems at Bloomberg. These are talented engineers. But career-derived expertise is different from the kind of forged-in-hardship conviction I look for. The founders I back tend to have been told "no" by the world long before they heard it from investors. I don't see that signal here, and at the earliest stages, it's the signal I trust most.

The pivot from Oddpool gives me pause in a specific way. They entered YC building a prediction markets data layer, then shifted to AI agent benchmarking mid-batch. I'll grant that there's a connecting thread -- PredictionMarketBench was literally a benchmarking framework for trading agents, so generalizing that into horizontal agent benchmarking has organic logic. But I've seen the pattern of founders pivoting toward whatever category is hottest during their batch, and prediction markets to AI infrastructure is exactly the trajectory that pattern would predict. The strongest founders I've backed were working on their problem before anyone else cared about it. Crusoe Energy was mining Bitcoin on gas flares when people thought it was lunacy. These founders were in prediction markets six months ago and are now in the hottest sector in tech.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because it's real. If you believe -- and reasonable people do -- that every AI team will need standardized, reproducible benchmarking infrastructure the way every software team eventually needed CI/CD, then Benchspan is positioned at an infrastructure inflection point. Avi saw the pain firsthand at Microsoft. The "37 lines of code" onboarding story is exactly the kind of developer-friendly wedge that wins bottom-up adoption. If benchmark fragmentation continues (SWE-bench Verified, Terminal-Bench, MATH, GPQA, and counting), the orchestration layer becomes increasingly valuable. And the big cloud providers have a genuine channel conflict here -- they profit from the wasted compute that Benchspan claims to eliminate. That structural dynamic is the most interesting thing about the business. If I were an investor who optimized for market timing and professional founder-market fit, this would be compelling. But that's not how I invest.

Through my lens, what's missing is everything I look for. There's no economic access story -- this is a tool for well-funded AI teams, not a product that creates opportunity for people who lack it. There's no regulatory or stigma barrier creating a moat that deters other investors -- the Docker execution layer is replicable and open-source alternatives already exist. There's no founder survival narrative connecting personal hardship to this specific problem. And there's no anti-consensus quality that would make most investors uncomfortable. This is a competent team with relevant experience building a reasonable product in an overcrowded market. It's exactly the kind of deal where I'd say "I understand why other people like this, and that's exactly why it's not for me."
