# Bubble Lab -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Salesforce owns Slack. Salesforce is building Agentforce 360, explicitly positioning Slack as an "agentic OS" for enterprises. Bubble Lab's entire product is an AI agent that lives inside Slack. This is the structural fact that dominates everything else in the dossier. Building your core product on a platform whose owner has publicly announced they're coming to occupy exactly your use case isn't bold -- it's building on a shrinking island. The Agentforce roadmap doesn't need to be perfectly executed to destroy Bubble Lab's positioning; it just needs to be good enough that enterprise buyers pause before adding a third-party agent to a messaging platform that already ships one natively. My Birchbox experience taught me that narrative without structural defensibility produces the worst venture outcome. Here the structural problem isn't just the absence of a moat -- it's the active presence of a platform owner building the competing moat around you.

The sane-person-insane-idea test fails at the idea level. "AI agent that automates operations workflows" is possibly the single most consensus startup concept in 2026. n8n has $253M in funding. Gumloop raised $24.5M. Dust raised $21.5M from Sequoia. Microsoft is building Copilot agents. Google has Gemini in Workspace. Every major platform company and hundreds of startups are telling the exact same story. When I backed Solana, building another Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter was genuinely insane -- most VCs thought the category was dead. Bubble Lab isn't doing something most investors think is crazy; it's doing something most investors think is obvious. That's the difference between non-consensus and consensus dressed up in new vocabulary. The founders -- Selina Li and Zach Zhong, both Cornell Tech CS '25 -- are clearly capable engineers. But capable engineers building the most consensus product in AI doesn't produce the founder-idea tension that generates outsized returns.

My "AI kill test" is unusually straightforward here: if AI didn't exist, Bubble Lab doesn't exist. There is no business underneath the AI layer. The entire product is an LLM calling APIs on behalf of users. This is precisely the category I've been publicly arguing against for two years. I want businesses where AI is the cherry on top -- where the company would be compelling pre-AI and AI makes it exceptional. Craftwork has painting crews and operational logistics; AI optimizes scheduling but doesn't constitute the moat. Bubble Lab has no analog. The software IS the business, built on someone else's LLM capabilities, delivered through someone else's messaging platform, at SaaS price points I've declared structurally indefensible. The "long-term memory" feature -- Pearl learning user preferences -- is the closest thing to a switching cost, but any competent agent platform will ship equivalent personalization within a cycle.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Salesforce will remain too Salesforce-ecosystem-focused to build a tool-agnostic operations agent, that Bubble Lab's Slack-native distribution creates viral adoption faster than well-funded competitors can replicate, and that the open-source workflow engine (1.1k GitHub stars) evolves into an ecosystem standard. If all three prove true, the "ops super-employee" narrative has real expansion potential -- from Slack automation to comprehensive business operations infrastructure. The 4,000 users and named customer testimonials (Dyna Robotics reporting 10+ hours per week saved) suggest genuine product utility. And SOC 2 certification at this stage shows enterprise seriousness that most pre-seed companies lack. But even if I grant all of this, I'm looking at an application-layer company whose core technology -- LLM-powered API orchestration -- is being built by every player with more capital, more distribution, and more data. The open-source engine doesn't create a protocol position; Apache 2.0 licensing means anyone can fork it, which is exactly why open-sourcing your execution layer before you have lock-in is a double-edged play that cuts deeper on the wrong side.

The co-founders met at Cornell Tech and pivoted from a consumer nutrition app to B2B operations automation. Neither has documented experience in BizOps or RevOps -- the exact buyer persona they're targeting. The domain shift from gymii.ai to enterprise operations is non-trivial, and the absence of lived operational experience makes me question whether they understand the workflow complexity of their target customers or are building based on what the tooling looks like from the outside. The pricing tiers ($29.99 Pro, $99.99 Scale) are the subscription SaaS model I've been publicly arguing is structurally eroding. Every dollar of revenue here faces the same compression dynamics that are hollowing out the entire SaaS category -- AI makes these capabilities cheaper faster than any individual company can capture value through monthly subscriptions.

I pass. This is a competent team in a crowded consensus market building an application layer on a platform whose owner is actively constructing the competing product. The AI isn't the cherry on top -- it's the whole sundae, and sundaes melt when the weather changes.
