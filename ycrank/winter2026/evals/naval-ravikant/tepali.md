# Tepali -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The Lotus-to-Tepali rename during the batch tells you something structural about the founding team's relationship to this problem. Vishnu built all-in-one software for coffee shops at Dripos. Chrisvin watched AI transform field service at Rilla. They looked across the landscape of fragmented multi-location service verticals and identified medspas as the next analogous opportunity. That's good pattern recognition -- it's the kind of analytical skill you learn at Carnegie Mellon or NYU Stern. But pattern recognition across verticals is the definition of transferable, teachable knowledge. The question I always start with is: what does this founder know that a smart generalist couldn't acquire in six months of customer interviews? I don't see the answer. Neither founder has worked in a medspa, managed aesthetic treatments, navigated HIPAA from the clinical side, or spent years obsessing over why injection mapping workflows are broken. They arrived at medspas through horizontal analysis, not vertical obsession. Dripos-to-Tepali is a career arc that says "I build vertical SaaS for service businesses" -- and that's a skill, not specific knowledge about aesthetics medicine.

The leverage architecture confirms the concern. Tepali consolidates six known software categories -- scheduling, CRM, charting, inventory, payroll, marketing -- into one platform. That's integration, not invention. A medspa owner using Tepali gets a cleaner dashboard and fewer logins, but they don't gain a fundamentally new capability that didn't exist before. Compare this to what Notion did for individual knowledge workers: Notion didn't just merge five tools, it created a new primitive -- the composable block -- that let people build workflows that weren't previously possible. Tepali is assembling existing capabilities into a single pane of glass. That's valuable as a product. It's not leverage in the structural sense -- it doesn't allow one person to do what ten previously did, or enable a new type of actor to enter a market. Standard SaaS economics apply: monthly per-location fees, linear relationship between revenue and customer count, incremental improvements through feature addition.

The competitive landscape is the hardest problem here, and it's not contrarian at all. Boulevard just raised $80M explicitly to invest in AI capabilities. Zenoti serves 30,000 businesses. Vagaro has $65.9M in revenue. AestheticsPro has three decades of medspa-specific clinical depth. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal and every smart person thought it was a terrible idea. When I invested in Twitter, microblogging seemed trivial to the entire industry. Where's the non-consensus element in "medspas need better software with AI"? Everyone agrees. Everyone is building it. The incumbents have the customer relationships, the data, the payment flows, and now the capital to integrate AI layers into their existing platforms. Tepali's "AI-native architecture" advantage is real but time-limited -- Boulevard's codebase was built before GPT-4, but $80M buys a lot of re-architecture.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. New medspas are opening at roughly 18% annually -- thousands of greenfield locations making first-time software decisions with no switching cost barrier. Vishnu has directly relevant engineering experience building exactly this kind of multi-module platform at Dripos, which means faster time to parity on core features. The marketing-to-LTV attribution feature is a genuine gap in the market -- connecting ad spend on Meta and Google to patient lifetime value within a single system is something no incumbent natively offers, and it addresses the exact pain point that drives medspa owner frustration. If Tepali could nail this single feature as an acquisition wedge, land greenfield locations during the industry's expansion phase, and layer in embedded payments, the $99.8M software TAM becomes a floor, not a ceiling. The services market underneath is $18.6 billion. There's a version of this story where Tepali is the Shopify of medspas -- not the most technologically novel company, but the right product at the right time for a wave of new business formation.

But I don't fund that version of the story at this stage. The founders' knowledge is horizontal, not vertical. The leverage is integrative, not generative. The timing is consensus, not contrarian. The technology doesn't compound in a way that creates distance from competitors -- every feature Tepali builds, Boulevard or Zenoti can build with greater resources and existing distribution. This is a competent team executing a recognizable playbook in a crowded, well-funded category. That can be a good business. It's not the asymmetric bet I look for.
