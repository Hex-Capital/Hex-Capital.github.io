# Ditto Biosciences -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Nature has been running the world's longest drug discovery program for 500 million years, and nobody in pharma thought to read the results. That's the core insight at Ditto Bio, and it's the kind of insight that makes me lean forward. Parasites -- worms, ticks, viruses -- have evolved exquisitely precise proteins to manipulate the human immune system, because their survival depends on it. Every parasite that successfully colonizes a human host is carrying molecular proof that its immunomodulatory proteins work against human targets. GLP-1 agonists came from Gila monster venom and became a $100 billion drug class. Aspirin came from willow bark. Penicillin from mold. Ditto is asking: what happens when you don't stumble on these molecules one at a time, but systematically mine the entire parasite kingdom computationally? With over 98% of viral and parasite proteins uncharacterized, they're exploring a chemical space that the pharmaceutical industry -- with its $700 million-funded competitors like Odyssey Therapeutics -- hasn't even looked at.

The consequence of success here is substantial. Autoimmune disease treatments are a $125 billion market built on a crude paradigm: broad immunosuppression. You carpet-bomb the immune system and hope the collateral damage is manageable. If parasites have evolved proteins that achieve precision immunomodulation -- selectively tuning specific immune pathways rather than suppressing everything -- you're not improving the existing therapeutic approach, you're replacing its logic entirely. That's a meaningful distinction. But I'll be honest about the limits of the consequence: this is still a drug that goes through the FDA, gets prescribed by doctors, and moves through pharmaceutical distribution. The system persists; only the molecules change. It's not restructuring how medicine is delivered or who has access. The consequence magnitude is real but bounded by the pharmaceutical framework in which it must operate.

What draws me in is the contrarian positioning. Coronado Biosciences tried parasite-derived therapy a decade ago with live pig whipworms and failed spectacularly in Phase 2. That failure poisoned the well. Most pharma companies and investors now hear "parasites" and think "proven not to work." But Coronado's failure was a failure of approach, not concept -- using live organisms instead of isolating the specific active proteins. Ditto's founders understood this distinction because they're parasitologists and evolutionary biologists, not pharma executives recycling the industry playbook. They're outsiders to pharmaceutical development bringing deep knowledge from fields that pharma R&D organizations don't house. That pattern -- domain scientists entering a commercial space that dismissed their domain -- is one I've seen produce extraordinary outcomes. The three founders worked together for three years at Arcadia Science, co-authoring publications on exactly the kind of cross-kingdom protein analysis their company now depends on. This isn't a team assembled for a pitch deck; it's a team that formed around the science before the company existed.

The timing trajectory is favorable. AI-driven protein analysis has crossed a capability threshold in the last two to three years -- AlphaFold, large language models for protein structure, computational screening at million-protein scale. Five years ago, systematically mining parasite genomes against human immune targets was computationally impractical. Now it's feasible. Ditto reports analyzing over a million proteins and achieving 1-2 nM binding affinities on early candidates. That's not clinical proof, but it's evidence that the computational pipeline is producing hits, not noise. The highest single-year jump in AI-originated IND filings happened in 2025. The curve is accelerating, and Ditto is building at the leading edge rather than chasing a consensus trend.

My hesitation is structural, not scientific. Drug discovery doesn't fit my preferred option-value architecture. I like investments where either the money expires or you build a transformational company -- binary outcomes on compressed timescales. Biotech at the pre-clinical stage is a ten-year, hundred-million-dollar journey through preclinical validation, IND filing, Phase 1 through 3 trials, and FDA approval. A million-dollar angel check barely funds the first set of in vivo experiments. The company will need to raise substantial follow-on capital from investors who understand pharma timelines, and it will compete for that capital against companies like Odyssey ($700M raised) and Apogee (public, NASDAQ-listed) that are already in or near clinical stages. The immunogenicity challenge -- foreign proteins triggering immune responses that neutralize the drug -- is a fundamental technical risk that the tissue biobank is designed to address but hasn't yet resolved. These aren't disqualifying, but they mean the path from here to a consequential outcome requires everything to go right over a very long timeline.

The bull case that would make this extraordinary: if the parasite-derived protein platform produces even one drug that reaches market, it validates an entire new discovery paradigm. The database of a million-plus analyzed proteins becomes the most valuable proprietary asset in immunology. Every pharma company developing autoimmune therapies becomes a potential partner or acquirer -- not for a single molecule, but for the platform. The analogy isn't one drug; it's the creation of an entirely new source code for immunomodulation. That's a category-creation bet, and those are the ones I live for. The team is deliberately composed to address the key risks -- parasitology expertise, protein engineering capability, and high-throughput screening experience from Illumina. If I were making this bet, I'd be betting on the platform becoming the definitive map of evolution's pharmacy -- not on any single drug candidate.
