# Harbor -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Clinical trial electronic data capture is exactly the kind of market most investors dismiss without investigating -- boring, deeply regulated, dominated by incumbents who were acquired for billions. Medidata sold to Dassault for $5.8 billion in 2019 running what is essentially a data entry system designed in the 1990s. That acquisition price tells you the market matters. What it doesn't tell you is that data management still consumes 30% of clinical trial budgets and research coordinators still spend hours per subject per day on manual transcription. The structural moment here is that document extraction models crossed a reliability threshold in the last 18 months that makes "AI extraction with human review" viable for regulated clinical data for the first time. Two years ago, no LLM could reliably parse a clinical trial protocol and generate compliant case report forms. Today Harbor does it in ten minutes versus eight weeks. That capability gap closing is the "why now" -- and it maps to the same pattern I tracked with Harvey in legal AI. The difference is that clinical trials have even higher regulatory barriers to entry, which means the moat for whoever gets there first is deeper.

The incumbent cannibalization dynamic here is textbook. Medidata and Veeva make money on complexity -- per-subject pricing, per-site fees, 8-12 week professional services engagements to build trial databases. Harbor automates the database build in days and charges a flat monthly rate. Medidata can't easily match that without gutting their own revenue model. When Veeva announces "AI Agents" for 2026, they're talking about bolting features onto a legacy architecture to protect existing contracts, not rebuilding their business model. This is the same structural opening Stripe exploited against PayPal -- the incumbent's business model is the moat for the disruptor, not the other way around. Harbor's free academic tier is smart distribution thinking: universities run thousands of clinical studies, coordinators learn Harbor, then carry that preference to commercial sponsors and CROs. That's a funnel, not charity.

What I find most compelling is the product-to-distribution trajectory. The EDC is the gravitational center of a clinical trial -- every sponsor, CRO, site coordinator, and monitor interacts with it daily. Once Harbor is the system of record for trial data, the expansion surface is enormous: clinical trial management, electronic trial master files, safety reporting, randomization, eCOA. The company already shows multi-product instinct with three AI modules at launch (Build, Capture, Monitor), and their enterprise tier mentions CTMS/eTMF integrations. This isn't a single-feature tool; it's a wedge into a platform that touches every workflow in clinical development. Gusto started with "just payroll" and became the HR operating system for small businesses. Harbor is positioning to do the same for clinical trials -- start with "just EDC" and expand into the full trial operations stack.

The founder-market fit is genuine, not performative. Albert Cai spent four years at Biolinq doing the actual work -- taking a medical device from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization. He was the person sitting in front of legacy EDC systems, entering data manually, dealing with 10-week setup timelines. That's not consulting experience or market research -- that's operational scar tissue, which is what I look for in regulated markets. I had the same experience at Color Health -- you can't build compliant clinical software by reading the regulations; you have to have lived the workflows. Nathan Leung as employee #1 at a YC startup (Jupiter S19) plus Google and Ramp internships shows he can build production systems from scratch. The fact that they already have 5 live clinical studies, 6 named customers, and a testimonial citing a 1,600-subject trial configured in one week -- that's strong execution velocity for a two-person pre-seed team.

The single miracle here is getting AI extraction accuracy to the point where pharma sponsors and FDA auditors trust it. Critically, like Harvey, the hardest part of this miracle is being delivered by foundation model labs improving base capabilities -- Harbor's job is to build the domain-specific layer, the compliance infrastructure, and the customer trust. They've already done the regulatory groundwork (21 CFR Part 11, HIPAA, GDPR with immutable audit trails), and the confidence-scoring monitoring feature shows they understand that human-in-the-loop review is the bridge to full trust. The risk is real -- one extraction error in a pivotal trial could damage credibility with the entire market. But the product architecture suggests they've internalized this, not ignored it.

The main risk I'm weighing is incumbent response speed. Veeva's AI Agents announcement for 2026 and Medidata's AI roadmap mean Harbor has a window, not permanent advantage. But regulated markets move slowly -- enterprise pharma procurement cycles are 6-18 months, existing multi-year contracts create inertia, and incumbents face the cannibalization constraint I described. The window is probably 3-4 years, which is enough to build a meaningful installed base if the team executes. The two-person team for a regulated product is a legitimate constraint, but they're hiring and YC capital addresses the immediate scaling need. This is the kind of boring, regulated, infrastructure-layer company that my portfolio has been built on -- and it's positioned at a clear technology inflection point.
