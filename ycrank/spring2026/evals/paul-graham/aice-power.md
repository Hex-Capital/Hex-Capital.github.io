# AICE Power -- Paul Graham Evaluation

AICE Power is a two-person team that, on May 16, 2026, walked away from a six-figure ARR energy-sensor business -- a real business with real paying customers -- to chase submarine defense drones into a pool where Anduril just raised $4B at a $60B valuation and Saronic just closed $1.75B at $9.25B. The dimensional signature is brutal in its symmetry: AICE Power is highest on schlep (defense procurement, ITAR, subsea hardware is unambiguously unpleasant work) and lowest on organic-idea fit (Gr?goire Chomette is aerospace/NASA and R?mi Bouteiller is wearables-embedded; neither has touched a hull, a sonar, or a Navy buyer in their public record). What I am looking at is the inverse of the pattern I keep funding -- a hot market that smart founders are running toward, attacked by founders who have no personal grievance with the problem.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the obvious ladder rung for the schlep axis: Brian Armstrong leaned into a compliance grind nobody else in his batch wanted, and the unpleasantness became the moat. AICE Power genuinely sits in that zone of work -- subsea hardware crossing a France?U.S. manufacturing border, ITAR exposure, multi-year DoD procurement cycles like the Feb 2024 DIU contract that won't demo until 2026. But the load-bearing word in "Schlep Blindness" was *blindness* -- the unconscious avoidance. AICE Power is chasing a schlep that two decacorns and the entire Pentagon Replicator apparatus have already located on the map. Anduril's $1.7B Ghost Shark and Saronic's surface vessels mean the schlep is no longer hidden; it is the consensus trade in defense tech this cycle. That changes what the unpleasantness is worth.

**Dropbox** (2007) is where I keep landing on the organic-pain axis -- Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT, built the thing for himself, then shot a demo video the night before. The failure mechanism on the LOW rung is the MBA two-hander pitching a market they read about in a McKinsey deck. Does AICE Power look like Houston, or look like the deck-readers? Chomette's MIT AeroAstro thesis was deep-learning on jet-engine signals; Bouteiller built a wearable predicting hot flashes at Amira Health. Nothing in either founder's public record suggests they have personally suffered from undersea mine threats or coastal infrastructure sabotage. They had a product they were personally connected to -- energy sensors at six-figure ARR -- and they killed it on May 16. That is the inverse of the Houston signal.

On animal resourcefulness, the prior product is the only thing AICE Power has to offer. *Mechanism analysis:* six figures in ARR with paying customers, shipped within roughly a year, is non-trivial evidence Bouteiller and Chomette can ship and sell. That hustle compounds -- the team that hit $100K+ in sensors can probably hit a working subsea prototype in 18 months. But the canonical animal moment in my notebook is Armstrong building the Android wallet *after* I rejected him, to argue for a problem he already loved. AICE Power's pivot runs the other direction: they abandoned the artifact they had already shipped to chase a different problem. That is not a retreat, but it is not Armstrong, either.

On hacker genealogy, *counterfactual:* what would have to be true for AICE Power to clear the rung where Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize sits? A GitHub footprint with real stars, an open-source subsea control stack, a Hacker News presence pre-pivot. The dossier reports "No public repos found" for Chomette and "no top repo with notable star count found" for Bouteiller -- both have strong academic credentials (MIT AeroAstro MSc, ENS Paris-Saclay) but the tribal markers I over-index on are conspicuously absent. Credentialed technical, not tribal-hacker technical. The Collison comparison breaks here.

*Single-anchor parallel* on cofounder configuration: **Stripe** (2010) was two brothers who had already shipped **Auctomatic** (2007) together and sold it for $5M before they were 21. AICE Power is a two-French-founder pair where the dossier explicitly says "no public data found on a shared prior employer or university." Two is the right number per "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010), but the long-relationship pattern that made the Collisons readable is absent. The YC interview will surface chemistry; the dossier cannot.

My 36-month forecast for AICE Power: either a quiet pivot again -- most likely to maritime-data software or subsea sensor analytics -- or one small DIU/SBIR Phase II award under $2M that stalls below $1M ARR, with Anduril or Saronic absorbing the technical talent in an acquihire by 2029. The mechanism: defense procurement cycles are longer than a 2-person pre-seed team can typically fund, the founders have no naval-buyer relationships to compress that cycle, and the two best-capitalized competitors raised more money in a single 2026 round than AICE Power will see across its lifetime.

The strongest case against the pass is the *animal* read on the pivot itself: killing a six-figure ARR business is not the move of soft founders, and **Airbnb** (2009) is the Known Exception where I funded a team after rejecting their idea because their willingness to do irrational things -- sell cereal boxes at $40 -- overrode my idea-quality skepticism. A bull might argue AICE Power abandoning paying customers to chase submarine drones is the same signature of conviction. It stings because it is partly right. But Brian Chesky did not kill a working business to chase Airbnb; he leaned harder into the one nobody believed in, while Bouteiller and Chomette walked away from the one customers were paying for to chase the one a16z is already paying Anduril for. That is the opposite vector. The flip condition is concrete: if AICE Power posts a working multi-vehicle subsea swarm demo on the public internet within six months, or signs a named DIU/Navy contract before Demo Day, I will reread this evaluation. Until then, pass.
