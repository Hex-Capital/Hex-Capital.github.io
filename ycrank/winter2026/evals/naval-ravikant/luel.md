# Luel -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The leverage architecture here is the first thing that catches my eye -- and not in a good way. Luel pays contributors $4.50 per video clip and $0.80 per audio recording, then sells the aggregated, rights-cleared dataset to AI labs at a margin. That's a take-rate business where revenue scales with human labor hours on the supply side. Every new dataset specification requires recruiting contributors, managing their submissions, running QA, handling payouts across jurisdictions. This is closer to what I'd call "renting out other people's time" than creating genuine leverage. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every answer posted became permanently useful to every future developer who searched that question -- that's compounding. Luel's datasets are bespoke deliverables. The data gets consumed by a model training run and then you need to sell the next batch. There's no compound interest in the product itself.

The specific knowledge question is more interesting. William Namgyal has personally overseen the collection of 200K+ hours of multimodal data for top-100 AI labs. That's not something you learn from a textbook or a product management bootcamp. He understands contributor management, cross-modal QA failure modes, and the operational friction of getting a South Asian patient-doctor conversation recorded, rights-cleared, and formatted for model training. That knowledge is real and earned. But I'd distinguish between operational specific knowledge -- understanding how the sausage gets made -- and the kind of deep, physics-first or protocol-level insight that creates category-defining companies. Namgyal knows how to run this operation well. The question is whether that operational edge persists when Scale AI, Defined.ai, or a new entrant decides to build the same pipeline with 10x his capital.

The timing story has a surface appeal that dissolves under examination. Yes, the EU AI Act creates regulatory pressure for rights-cleared data. Yes, Meta's acquisition of Scale AI's controlling stake theoretically opens a gap for independent providers. These are real dynamics. But they're also obvious dynamics -- every data company in the space is making exactly this pitch. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, the consensus was that cryptocurrency was worthless. When I backed Uber, the consensus was that ride-sharing was illegal and would stay that way. The consensus on AI training data in 2026 is that it's incredibly valuable and demand is skyrocketing. That's not a contrarian bet. That's surfing the wave everyone already sees. The 3M+ contributor claim, if accurate, is an execution achievement, not a contrarian insight.

The strongest bull case would require believing that the contributor network constitutes a durable, self-reinforcing moat -- that getting to 3M contributors first creates a supply-side density advantage that makes Luel the default for any frontier lab needing custom multimodal data within days rather than weeks. If the network compounds -- if contributor reliability scores, domain coverage, and geographic diversity create a matching engine that no new entrant can replicate -- then this becomes infrastructure rather than services. That would look more like the picks-and-shovels play I tend to favor. And the regulatory tailwind is real: if rights-clearance becomes table stakes for model training, whoever has the best-documented, most auditable contributor pipeline wins. The problem is that I see no evidence of technology that makes this matching fundamentally better over time. The QA runs on Google Vertex AI -- a third-party service. The marketplace mechanics are standard. Defined.ai grew its marketplace partner datasets by 1,200% in 2025, suggesting the supply-side moat isn't as deep as the 3M number implies.

Both founders dropped out of Berkeley to pursue this, which signals conviction. No integrity red flags surface in the dossier. But I notice the website lists logos for xAI, Meta, DoorDash, and Apple under "Backing & Investors" -- and the dossier can't confirm whether these are paying customers, investors, or aspirational associations. That ambiguity bothers me. Not as a fraud signal, but as a clarity-of-communication signal. The best founders I've backed are precise about what they've achieved and what they haven't. Conflating customer logos with investor logos is the kind of thing that erodes trust incrementally.

I'd pass. The founders have real domain experience, the market is genuine, and the execution appears competent. But this is a consensus bet in a hot category, built on a labor-intensive marketplace model without a clear leverage mechanism or compounding technology. I'm looking for companies where one engineer's work serves millions, where the product gets better without proportional human input, where the timing is wrong according to smart people. Luel is a well-executed services marketplace in a market everyone agrees is growing. That's a fine business -- it's just not the kind of asymmetric, leverage-driven bet I write checks for.
