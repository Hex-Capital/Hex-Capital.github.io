# Autumn AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the mismatch between the founders' capabilities and the problem they've chosen. Vishnu has published graph neural network research and contributed to LMCache. Shiv presented at NeurIPS and did an AI residency at SandboxAQ — an Alphabet spinout doing serious post-quantum cryptography and AI work. These are research-grade ML minds. And they're building a sales prospecting signal aggregator. That's like hiring a physicist to sort mail — the mail gets sorted, but you're not using the physics. The technology underneath Autumn AI is fundamentally web scraping plus NLP extraction plus filtering. Any competent engineering team with access to the same LLM APIs could build a comparable product in months. The founders' genuine technical depth — which is real — doesn't create specific knowledge *about this particular problem*. It creates general capability applied to a specific vertical.

Vishnu's time building sourcing technology at Sierra Ventures is the strongest domain signal in the dossier. He saw firsthand how VCs piece together signals to find investment targets — that's adjacent to how sales teams piece together signals to find prospects. But it's a tour of duty, not a decade of obsession. He didn't run a sales floor. He didn't build and scale a GTM motion. He didn't spend years in the trenches understanding why existing intent data fails at the workflow level. When I backed companies like Stack Overflow, the founders had been living inside developer knowledge problems for years — they knew things about how developers learn that no outsider could reverse-engineer. Here, the founders identified a real problem from an adjacent vantage point, but the insight — "sales reps waste time toggling between tabs to research prospects" — is observable by anyone who's spent a week shadowing a BDR. That's not non-transferable knowledge.

The leverage architecture concerns me more than the specific knowledge gap. Autumn delivers a filtered feed of buying signals per customer's custom ICP definition. Each customer configures their own signals, their own filters, their own alert preferences. That's useful, but it's linear SaaS — one customer's configuration doesn't make the product better for the next customer. Compare this to the compounding dynamics I look for: Stack Overflow's knowledge graph got richer with every question. Replit's platform got more valuable with every user-created project. Notion's template ecosystem created self-reinforcing adoption loops. Autumn AI has no such flywheel in its current architecture. The dossier speculates about a "data flyback loop where user engagement data improves signal relevance over time," but that's inference, not evidence. Without network effects or compounding data, this is a feature competing in an ecosystem of well-funded platforms.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Autumn nails signal quality and workflow integration, they could build meaningful switching costs — sales teams that configure their ICP criteria and build outbound workflows around Autumn's alerts won't switch easily. There's also a genuine structural insight buried in the approach: ZoomInfo, 6sense, and Demandbase are economically dependent on selling proprietary data access. Building a product that monitors *publicly available* signals creates a structural conflict for incumbents — they can't easily say "stop paying us for data, just watch LinkedIn for free." That's a real wedge. And the founders' ML research depth could eventually produce signal extraction models that outperform simpler approaches, especially for nuanced intent signals that require reasoning beyond keyword matching. If I squint, I can see a version where the team's research capability creates compounding accuracy advantages over time. But "if I squint" is not how I invest my own money.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. "AI for sales intelligence" is one of the most funded categories in 2025-2026. The dossier itself lists five well-capitalized competitors. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I invested in Bitcoin, it was considered nerd money. The best returns come from being non-consensus and right. Autumn AI is consensus — every pitch deck in this category cites the same LLM capability threshold, the same $4-5B TAM, the same "incumbents are slow" narrative. That doesn't mean they'll fail, but it means the market has already priced the opportunity. The asymmetric upside I require isn't present when twenty companies are running the same race.

The co-founder relationship is a bright spot — meeting on the first day at Columbia and spending four years together before founding a company suggests genuine compatibility, not a co-founder dating service match. Their shared research orientation and intellectual seriousness signal integrity and long-term thinking. But relationship quality without specific knowledge is necessary but not sufficient. I need founders who know things that can't be taught, building leverage that didn't previously exist, at a time when others think they're wrong. Autumn AI has smart, technically capable founders building a reproducible application in a hot market. That's a pass.
