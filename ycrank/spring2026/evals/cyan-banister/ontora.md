# Ontora -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ontora is what's missing: a story. When I look at the three founders, I see impressive credentials -- CODE University, Stanford, ETH Zürich, HSG -- but I don't see the biographical thread that tells me why *these* people are building *this* company. Maximilian Arnold built an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data during a two-year stint as an AI Product Engineer at Porsche. That's technically relevant work, and I respect the craft. But it's a corporate job, not a calling. Leon Iwanowitsch comes from one of Europe's premier business schools and organized hackathons. And David Korn -- one of three co-founders -- has essentially no confirmed public identity. I can't find a LinkedIn, a Twitter, a GitHub, nothing. When I ask my foundational question -- "tell me your story, not the business" -- I'm hearing silence where I need to hear fire. Nobody on this team appears to have been the employee whose voice was ignored, the worker whose process knowledge was dismissed by McKinsey consultants who interviewed ten percent of the staff and billed millions. They're building from the consultant's perch, not from the factory floor.

This matters because the company's framing reveals whose problem they're solving: "We help managers regain control of their organisations." That language tells me everything. This isn't about giving voice to the invisible worker -- it's about reinforcing the authority of people who already hold organizational power. Compare that to what drew me to Uber, where drivers gained economic independence, or Affirm, where borrowers finally saw transparent credit terms, or Carta, where employees could actually understand their equity. Those companies made invisible systems visible *for the people who needed that visibility most*. Ontora makes organizational processes visible for management. The direction of transparency matters deeply to me, and this direction doesn't create economic access for the people who lack it.

The competitive positioning also concerns me. "AI agents for enterprise" is one of the most crowded, consensus-driven categories in tech right now. Everyone from well-funded startups to the consulting giants themselves are racing to automate organizational knowledge work. Celonis sits on $1.77 billion in funding and is already expanding from structured process mining toward AI-powered process intelligence. Worktrace AI just raised $9.3 million with an ex-OpenAI PM at the helm. This is the opposite of unpopular and difficult to assess -- it's one of the most obvious plays in the current AI cycle. When I backed SpaceX, people thought Elon was out of his mind. When I invested in Crusoe, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was lunacy. Nobody is going to tell these founders that applying AI to management consulting is crazy. They'll get polite nods and competitive term sheets from investors who invest on consensus. That's not where I operate.

To be fair, the bull case deserves honest engagement. If Maximilian's Porsche experience means he's already built and deployed exactly this kind of system inside a Fortune 500 company and seen it work, that's real founder-market fit at the technical level. The Vertiv testimonial -- "what used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" -- is a genuinely compelling data point if it holds up under scrutiny. The consulting industry really does face structural incentive misalignment: McKinsey can't cannibalize its own billable hours. And the timing is legitimate -- LLMs crossed the quality threshold for open-ended conversational interviews sometime in 2024-2025, making this approach technically viable for the first time. If this team can execute and lock in enterprise contracts before Celonis pivots or the Big Three build their own tools, there's a real business here. But "there's a real business here" is different from the question I ask myself, which is whether I feel that jolt of intuitive energy, that sense that I'm looking at something others can't yet see. I don't feel it. I see a competent team building a reasonable product in a hot market. That's not my kind of bet.

The co-founder dynamics add a layer of concern. Three founders with no visible shared history -- no overlapping university, no shared employer, no public evidence of a relationship that's been stress-tested. After what I learned from HQ Trivia, I need to understand how co-founders argue and resolve disagreements before I invest. I can't even assess one-third of this founding team because David Korn is essentially a ghost in public records. That's not necessarily disqualifying -- some great founders are private -- but it means I have even less signal to work with on the dimension I now consider essential.
