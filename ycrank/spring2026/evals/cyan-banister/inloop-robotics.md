# InLoop Robotics -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$2.26 billion flowed into warehouse and industrial robotics startups in Q1 2026 alone. That number tells me everything about where InLoop Robotics sits on the consensus spectrum. When I wrote my check into Anduril, defense tech was so politically toxic in Silicon Valley that founders at dinner parties would change the subject. When I backed SpaceX, rockets were blowing up and every rational person told me I was lighting money on fire. InLoop Robotics is entering a space where Nimble has raised $221M at a $1B valuation, Dexterity has raised $300M at $1.65B, and venture capital is competing to fund warehouse automation. Nobody shifts uncomfortably when you pitch robots picking items off shelves — they reach for their checkbook. That's a problem for me, because I've learned that my best returns come from the places where other investors won't go, not from the places they're already crowding into.

Zakariea Sharfeddine did ML at Bosch and BMW. Pasha Rizali co-founded RoboTUM and ESRA, and his LinkedIn headline reads "Aspiring Robotics Engineer." Stepan Feduniak was doing robot learning research at 18. Martin Mohammed taught himself to code at 12 and interned at IBM at 16 doing reinforcement learning. These are smart, technically capable young builders from the TUM ecosystem — I respect the credentials. But when I ask my question, the one that surprises other founders because other investors apparently don't ask it — "tell me your story, not the business" — I don't hear a biographical crucible. Nobody on the InLoop Robotics team worked the night shift in a warehouse. Nobody's family ran a fulfillment operation that collapsed under labor costs. Nobody stumbled into this problem because life put them inside it. They formed through an academic robotics community and identified a market opportunity. That's how most startups begin, and most startups don't become defining companies. The biographical connection I look for — the one where the problem found the founder, where they'd be working on this without a dollar of funding — I can't see it here.

InLoop Robotics deploys "imperfect policies" with a confidence-aware Safety Module that routes uncertain tasks to remote human teleoperators, generating labeled training data with every intervention. That's a genuinely clever engineering inversion — most robotics companies chase full autonomy before deployment, and InLoop ships first and learns in production. I like the instinct. But the architecture itself is not proprietary. Nimble Robotics uses human-in-the-loop supervision at enterprise scale with FedEx as a strategic investor. The data flywheel InLoop Robotics describes on its YC page — failures becoming training signal, autonomy rates rising over time — is real as a mechanism, but it's the same mechanism a competitor with 400x more capital is already running with actual named customers. When the architectural pattern is known and the moat depends on accumulated data volume, capital becomes the proxy for defensibility, and InLoop Robotics has $500K against Nimble's $221M and Dexterity's $300M.

The warehouse labor shortage is real — 2.1 million unfilled jobs projected by 2030 — but it's also the most visible, most-discussed, most-funded pain point in industrial operations right now. When I look at my strongest investments, I see a pattern: I backed companies that attacked hidden structures, opaque systems extracting value from people who didn't understand how the extraction worked. Uber exposed the taxi medallion cartel. Affirm made credit card terms transparent. Ownwell automated property tax appeals against a system designed to be too complex for ordinary homeowners to challenge. InLoop Robotics is solving a labor supply problem in a market where costs, options, and alternatives are well understood by buyers. Warehouse operators know what they pay for labor. They know what automation costs. There's no information asymmetry to disrupt, no invisible tax to eliminate. The value proposition is straightforward efficiency — legitimate, but not the kind of structural power shift that anchors my highest-conviction bets.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Covariant's acqui-hire by Amazon in August 2024 removed a major independent player from the warehouse AI market, creating a genuine gap. InLoop Robotics' RaaS model — zero upfront capital, flat monthly subscription, deployment in hours — removes the procurement friction that has historically slowed warehouse automation adoption. The team has the right technical stack: ML policy training, robotic manipulation, cloud infrastructure for teleoperator routing. Martin Mohammed brings prior YC experience through Eggi and relevant computer vision work. And foundation model advances in 2023-2025 may genuinely enable a 4-person team to build what would have required 40 people three years ago. If InLoop Robotics can demonstrate a faster learning rate per dollar — if fewer deployments with smarter architecture generate better training data than brute-force scaling — the capital disadvantage becomes less deterministic. I checked my Known Exceptions: RTFKT succeeded in a narrow timing window with no structural moat, and Superplastic expanded my definition of "magically weird" beyond hard spaces. But neither exception maps here. InLoop Robotics isn't occupying a culturally bizarre category boundary — it's a well-understood robotics company in a well-funded market. The timing window created by Covariant's exit is real, but it's being filled by multiple well-capitalized entrants, not uniquely by InLoop.

Serve Robotics is the portfolio thread that comes closest — autonomous robots in logistics, spinning from my Postmates investment, eventually IPO'ing on NASDAQ. But Serve had an operational heritage from Postmates' real-world delivery network, accumulated data from millions of deliveries, and a founder team that had already navigated the valley of death at scale. InLoop Robotics has no named customers, no disclosed deployment count, no autonomy rate metrics, and a team whose visible experience is academic and corporate, not operational. The dossier notes "zero robot breakdowns reported among deployed units" on InLoop Robotics' website, implying at least one deployment, but without customer names or operational data, I can't evaluate whether the data flywheel has even begun spinning. One verifiable condition that would flip me toward Invest: if InLoop Robotics showed me a named customer deployment with measured autonomy improvement over time — concrete evidence that fewer human interventions are needed per 1,000 picks each month — that would demonstrate the learning rate advantage that could overcome the capital gap. Until then, talented young engineers in a consensus market with a replicable architecture and no biographical stake in the problem isn't a bet I can make with my own money.
