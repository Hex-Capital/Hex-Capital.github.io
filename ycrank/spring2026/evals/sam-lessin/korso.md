# Korso -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

This is the exact pitch I use as my canonical pass example when people ask me what I mean by "AI is sustaining, not disruptive." AI-powered quoting for manufacturers. RAG over pricing history. Natural-language queries on business documents. It's a perfectly fine product concept — and it's a terrible venture bet. You know who can automate quote-to-order workflows for manufacturers? SAP. Oracle. Microsoft Dynamics. Every legacy ERP vendor already sitting inside these factories is staring at their roadmap right now thinking "we should add AI quoting this quarter." That's not a hypothetical — that's a Tuesday planning meeting. Uptool already raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, AND Kleiner Perkins. Paperless Parts has $30M and Series B distribution. The market hasn't dismissed this category — the market is enthusiastically funding it. When four of the most sophisticated seed firms on the planet are already backing your direct competitor, the price reflects the upside. There's no asymmetry left for me.

The primitive test is where this really falls apart. What new behavior does Korso create? Manufacturers already do quoting. They already respond to RFQs. They already track purchase orders. Korso makes the existing workflow faster and less error-prone — and that's great, genuinely useful software — but it's not a new verb. Nobody is going to say "Korso me a quote" the way people say "Venmo me." This is automation of an existing process, not creation of a new category. "Better quoting" is a feature that will eventually live inside every ERP platform, not a standalone company that defines an industry. Compare this to what Teamshares did — employee-owned small business rollups as a venture-backable asset class. That was a genuinely new structure that didn't exist before. Korso is doing something that Paperless Parts has been doing since 2017, just with newer LLMs bolted on.

The founders are engineering undergrads from Penn. Martin Pan has a stint at GM, which gives some manufacturing floor credibility. Daichi's ML research on radiation therapy treatment planning shows technical chops. But I need to be honest about what I'm seeing: I haven't watched these people through multiple ventures. I haven't seen how they respond when things go sideways. The idea they're pursuing isn't insane — it's the most sensible, obvious application of LLMs to a manual industry workflow that a smart engineering student would identify. Sensible people building sensible things is the definition of what I find uninteresting at the seed stage. I want the person where I say "what you're doing sounds completely unhinged" and they calmly explain why the world is about to reorganize around their vision. That tension doesn't exist here.

Let me steelman this: the bull case is that small manufacturers — the shops running on spreadsheets and WhatsApp, not SAP — are an underserved segment where no incumbent has distribution. The WhatsApp ingestion angle is actually smart; it shows the team understands that global manufacturing doesn't run on Salesforce. If Korso could build deep enough workflow integration with enough manufacturers, the accumulated pricing history becomes a genuine switching-cost moat over time. And only 35% of procurement teams use AI for RFQs, so the adoption window is legitimately early. In a world where this team executes flawlessly, gets to a few hundred SMB manufacturers before the funded competitors do, and builds a data flywheel that makes their quotes more accurate than anyone else's — sure, this could be a solid $200-300M outcome. But that's a market-share capture story, not a category-creation story, and I don't get excited about market-share capture in a space where five competitors are already racing.

The structural durability problem is fatal for my framework. This is pure SaaS built on broadly available LLM/RAG technology. The dossier itself says the data moat "does not exist at launch." There are no physical assets, no protocol-level lock-in, no regulatory moat, no network effects. The AI kill test here isn't even close — this is the platonic ideal of what incumbents will absorb. Pass.
