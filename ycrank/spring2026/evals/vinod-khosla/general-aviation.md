# General Aviation -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

General Aviation is a consequence-respectable bet ruined by an expert-continuity founder profile -- Ben Frank wants to replace WWII-era radar/VHF for "every aircraft, drone, rocket, and ground vehicle" (generalaviation.com), which is the right shape of ambition, but he is an MIT-aero, Boeing-then-Rotabull-then-Rotor pilot/mechanic whose pattern recognition lives entirely inside the regime he claims to be replacing. The highest rung and the lowest rung on my ladder pull in opposite directions, and the middle three rungs do not save the deal.

On Consequence Magnitude, the **OpenAI** (2019) HIGH rung is civilizational cognitive-labor redirect; the **DoorDash** (2013) MID rung is consumer-logistics reshape; the GPT-4-sales-call-summarizer from my Pass Example is the LOW rung at 20-30% workflow lift. General Aviation sits between OpenAI and DoorDash, closer to DoorDash. The "$50B annual global ATC + $300B annual aviation fuel" (generalaviation.com/memo) plus the extension to drones and rockets is the only reason I am writing this evaluation rather than passing in 30 seconds -- the success-state where every aircraft, drone, and ground vehicle is a node on a coordinated global airspace network is genuinely civilizational, because it unlocks autonomous flight at scale, not just cheaper ATC. But the dossier headlines "Fix ATC" and "50% lower ATC costs," which is a Commonwealth Fusion-shaped problem framed as a cost-reduction play. That framing is exactly the inversion my Outlook Business essay warned against -- lead with consequence, not with margin.

On Founder Defies Documented Expert Consensus, **Physical Intelligence** (2024) with Finn and Levine is the HIGH rung -- academics shipping into industry against received robotics wisdom. **Glean's** Arvind Jain ex-Google-search is the MID rung Known Exception where I tolerated the domain-expert profile because capital scale and consequence were both large. The Pass Example "8 years at Salesforce now building a Salesforce feature" is the LOW rung. Ben Frank pins between Glean and the Pass Example, closer to the Pass Example. Rotabull aviation parts marketplace, Seer Aerospace fleet analytics (acquired by Aviation Week), CCO at Rotor Technologies, pilot, A&P mechanic -- this is a decade of aviation continuity, not a defiance of aviation orthodoxy. The Glean exception required Fortune-500-scale consequence and a $100M-scale check; General Aviation has neither yet, so I cannot extend the exception.

On Technical/Capital Risk That Repels Consensus VCs, **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) and its $115M Series A is the HIGH rung; **Cognition** (2024) at $175M with stack-deep training is the MID rung; the GPT-4 wrapper is the LOW rung. General Aviation sits between Cognition and the LOW rung. There is no satellite constellation being built -- Aireon already raised ~$470M and Iridium just bought the remaining 61% for $366.7M (FlightGlobal, May 2026), so the capital-hostile constellation risk is taken. What is left for General Aviation is DO-178/DO-254 avionics assurance and FAA TSO certification on top of Starlink or Iridium, which is hard regulatory work but not a science miracle. This is the dimension where consensus VCs do not flinch.

On Founder Learning Rate, **Slash** (2024) with its documented Kanye pivot is the HIGH rung that justified my $100M Series C exception; DoorDash's linear execution is the MID rung; the inert single-plan founder is the LOW rung. Ben Frank pins between Slash and DoorDash. Three aviation companies in roughly ten years with one acquisition (Seer ? Aviation Week) is real iteration, but the dossier contains no public "I was wrong about X" moment for General Aviation -- it reads as career accumulation within one domain, not the documented thesis-update that earned Slash its check.

On Required-Capital Profile, **OpenAI** at $50M first check and **Genesis AI** at $105M co-led seed are the HIGH rung; DoorDash capital-light is the MID rung; the Pass Example at $5-10M-to-ARR is the LOW rung. General Aviation pins between DoorDash and the Pass Example. YC pre-seed, solo founder, team size 0, Hiring: False (YC company_data) -- the roadmap as written is a software/protocol layer atop existing constellations, which is replicable and capital-light. I am over-resourced for this deal at its current shape.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation lands at $1-3M ARR selling aircraft-connectivity and flight-data services to Part 135 operators and fractional fleets, or gets absorbed as an application layer under Peraton's FAA prime-integrator umbrella for a sub-$50M acquisition -- the Aireon-shaped outcome at a tenth of Aireon's scale. The mechanism is that Ben Frank's true edge is his operator Rolodex from Rotor (~200 helicopter operators per FlyingMag) and Rotabull, not a defiant architecture; he will find product-market fit in the niche he can actually reach, and the ATC-replacement headline will quietly become a connectivity-and-routing SaaS.

The bull case stings: DOT publicly announced a new ATC build in May 2025 (CNN), the OBBB allocated FAA tech-upgrade funding, Iridium consolidated Aireon at $366.7M with ~$20M/year recurring fees as the comp, and Frank is one of maybe fifty people on earth with the aviation-B2B sales DNA plus airframe credibility to walk into NAV CANADA or NATS and be taken seriously -- the Glean Known Exception is literally built for this profile. I dismantle it on two facts: Aireon needed ~$470M and an exclusive Iridium NEXT hosted-payload deal to become Aireon, and General Aviation has zero disclosed constellation partnership, zero certification progress, and a team size of 0 against a Verizon $2.4B FENS incumbent (CNN, Feb 27, 2025) and SpaceX/Starlink already operating inside FAA trials at 17 sites (avitrader.com, Mar 5, 2025). The 1986 McKinsey forecast of <1M U.S. cellphones by 2000 (actual 109M) reminds me that incumbents extrapolate the past -- but in regulated procurement, incumbents also win the next contract while the extrapolators are right. One condition flips my verdict: a signed MOU or paid POC with a named ANSP (FAA, NAV CANADA, NATS, ENAV) or an exclusive hosted-payload deal with a constellation operator -- either pulls General Aviation up the Commonwealth Fusion ladder and I take a second meeting.
