# Ontora -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my immediate problem with Ontora: "AI replaces management consultants" might be the single most consensus AI application thesis circulating in Silicon Valley right now. Every pitch competition I've attended in the last eighteen months has featured some variant of "we use LLMs to automate expensive knowledge work." This isn't an insane idea — it's the most obvious idea. When I apply my founding filter to Maximilian Arnold, I see a competent person pursuing a thoroughly conventional opportunity. He built CoAnalyst at Porsche, an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data — that's directly relevant experience and establishes baseline credibility. Stanford, ETH Zürich, the Roche challenge win — fine credentials. But credibility in service of a consensus thesis doesn't produce venture-scale returns. It produces a competitive bloodbath. My first investment in Venmo worked because peer-to-peer mobile payments in 2009 seemed unnecessary and weird to almost everyone. Ontora doesn't have that quality of wrongness that makes everyone else walk away.

The structural moat problem here is severe and goes to the core of what I've been writing about for two years. The dossier states it plainly: "The underlying technology (LLM-based interviewing, RAG over enterprise documents) uses broadly available foundation models." This is software as the business, not software as a tool for a business. There are no painting crews, no parking infrastructure, no hardware installations, no regulatory moats. Strip away the AI layer and there is literally nothing left — no operations, no physical assets, no protocol. That's the exact opposite of what I look for. When I backed Craftwork, the insight was that software makes the painting business better but the moat is operational — you can't download a crew of skilled painters. Ontora's moat is... prompt engineering and enterprise integrations? Celonis has $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in revenue and is already expanding from structured process mining into AI-powered process intelligence. When they decide unstructured conversational data matters — and they will — they have the enterprise relationships, the installed base, and the capital to build or acquire whatever Ontora builds. McKinsey itself has been deploying proprietary AI tools for internal consulting workflows. The incumbents with distribution will absorb this functionality as a feature, which is precisely what I've been arguing happens to AI-first startups.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The consulting market's incentive misalignment is structurally real — McKinsey cannot cut its own billable hours without destroying its revenue model. If Ontora can establish itself as the category-defining platform for unstructured organizational intelligence before the incumbents figure out their cannibalization math, there's a window. The Vertiv testimonial from Julien Kang — a verified Strategy Manager at a public company — suggests the product actually works and delivers value. "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" is a compelling proof point. If aggregated cross-company organizational data compounds into proprietary benchmarks, that could eventually create a data moat that individual consulting firms can't replicate. And the market is genuinely enormous — even a tiny slice of $1 trillion in consulting spend is a massive business. For this to work, Ontora needs to move faster than both the incumbents and the dozens of well-funded startups converging on the same thesis, and they need to do it as a three-person team with zero GTM hires and no visible sales infrastructure. That's a timing arbitrage bet, not a structural moat bet — and I'm a structural moat investor.

There's no primitive or protocol play here. Ontora is an application built on someone else's foundation models, integrating with someone else's enterprise tools, generating reports that don't become infrastructure for an ecosystem. Compare this to Aleo building zero-knowledge privacy as a cryptographic layer that other applications build on, or Helium creating decentralized wireless infrastructure. Those companies created new primitives with compounding network effects. Ontora creates consulting reports. Each deployment doesn't make the platform more valuable for the next customer in any structural way — the "[Inferred]" data moat from cross-company benchmarks is speculative and unproven, and the dossier correctly flags it as such. The third co-founder, David Korn, has no confirmed public background whatsoever, which is an additional concern — I want to know who I'm backing, and a founder-shaped gap in the dossier doesn't inspire confidence. The co-founder relationships show no visible pre-existing bonds across the team, which is another pattern I watch for. This is a pass — competent people in a real market, but structurally positioned in the exact category of AI-first SaaS that I believe will get compressed by commoditization and absorbed by incumbents with distribution.
