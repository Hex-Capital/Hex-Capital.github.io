# Coasty -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Coasty is what happens when two competent engineers ship a benchmark number against the same product Anthropic and OpenAI release as a feature on a Tuesday. Prateek Jannu and Nitish Kovuru are running 82% on OSWorld against Claude's 72% and Operator's 38%, and the entire defensibility story sits on top of that delta. The high dimension here is the named-incumbent failure mode against UiPath, Blue Prism, and Automation Anywhere ? script-based RPA breaks when UIs change, and Coasty reads the screen instead. The low dimension is the structural moat. That pairing tells you what Coasty actually is: a thin contrarian wedge against a legacy category whose real competitor is not the legacy category.

Stripe (2010) is the load-bearing analogy on the contrarian-secret axis ? Patrick Collison named a specific developer-experience failure mode inside an incumbent (PayPal/Authorize.Net) that the incumbent could not fix without rewriting itself. Coasty does the same maneuver against UiPath: scripted click-recording is structurally brittle, and a vision-based agent in a sandboxed VM is the architecturally different thing the incumbent cannot ship without cannibalizing its installed base. Where Coasty diverges from the Stripe rung is that Stripe's incumbent was not also being attacked by Anthropic and OpenAI shipping `/computer-use` endpoints on the same week. The Coasty blog itself names Claude Computer Use and OpenAI Operator on the same leaderboard ? the secret terminates at UiPath but the war is being fought against frontier labs, and the dossier does not name a failure mode inside Anthropic that Coasty exploits. That is the structural weakness of the secret, not its absence.

The "AI for X" pass pattern fails because the moat terminates at "we have a better model," and any competitor closes the gap on the next release cycle. Coasty's stated moat is 82% on OSWorld ? a public benchmark with 369 tasks that any team with GPU budget can target, and one Coasty post itself flags "82% Real, 73% Exploited," which means even the founders concede measurement is contested. Browserbase has raised $67.5M, Kernel $22M Series A, Browser Use ~$17M, all funded ahead of Coasty's pre-seed, all adjacent. The Coasty pricing page ? $19/$50/$99 tiers ? is a PLG developer ladder, not a regulated-customer integration moat. There is no Palantir-style switching-cost story here; there is a benchmark number that compresses every time Anthropic ships a model.

On monopoly trajectory, the mechanism over 18 months runs against Coasty. Vision-language model capability is improving inside the frontier labs faster than Coasty can fork their lead, and the moat hypothesis ? "trajectory data from 100+ step sessions" compounding into proprietary corpus ? requires session volume Coasty does not disclose and that Anthropic collects at three orders of magnitude more scale through Claude enterprise. The 10-percentage-point OSWorld lead is a feature, not a company. One year of pricing power, not ten.

On the definite-plan dimension, the counterfactual to flip a tier would be a named 2-3 year architectural commitment ? a specific bet on something Anthropic structurally will not build, like on-premises sandboxed deployment for Fortune 500 SCIFs, or a vertical-specific RPA migration program with named Fortune 500 logos. The Humans in the Loop interview gestures at Fortune 500 hesitance about agents touching internal systems, which is exactly the regulated-customer wedge Palantir (2003) built fifteen years on. Coasty does not commit to it. Without that commitment, the plan reads as benchmark-chasing plus PLG pricing, which is the iteration-mode default.

Quora (2010) is the single-anchor parallel on founder lineage ? Adam D'Angelo's Facebook CTO pedigree was the override that carried a weaker monopoly thesis. Prateek Jannu does not have that signal. Microsoft ML researcher and Purdue is real technical credentialing, and Lead MLE at TruGen AI is shipped work, but neither equals shipped-to-scale operator status at a Thiel-orbit company. Nitish Kovuru's documented origin story ? an agent that deleted his production database, producing the sandboxed-VM thesis ? is the kind of biographical specific I weight, but it is one anecdote, not a track record. No PayPal Mafia, no Olympiad, no prior exit. The lineage carries weight but does not override.

The 36-month forecast: Coasty is acquihired for under $30M by a mid-tier RPA incumbent or browser-infra player (UiPath, Browserbase, Kernel) chasing a vision-agent capability, or dies when Anthropic ships Claude 5 Computer Use at 88% and the benchmark delta inverts. The mechanism is straightforward ? Coasty's defensibility is a public-benchmark number against opponents whose underlying capability is improving on a faster cadence than Coasty's two-person team can match, and the pricing tiers cannot fund the GPU spend required to stay ahead.

The strongest bull case: vision-based agents operating sandboxed desktop sessions generate a class of trajectory data ? error states, mid-task self-corrections, legacy-application UI traversals ? that Anthropic and OpenAI do not collect because they ship APIs, not operated sessions. If Coasty captures 10,000 Fortune 500 desktop trajectories on bespoke legacy systems (SAP, mainframe terminals, custom ERPs), that corpus is genuinely not reproducible by frontier labs and compounds into the proprietary-data moat the dossier gestures at. The Asana (2010) exception is the precedent ? pure SaaS, no atoms-heavy moat, carried by operator pedigree into a category the consensus dismissed. The dismantling: Coasty's founders are not Moskovitz. No enterprise logos, no LOIs, no SOC 2, no named Fortune 500 customer in the dossier ? just an interview citing Fortune 500 "hesitance." The proprietary-corpus thesis requires customer volume Coasty has not demonstrated it can win against incumbents with thousands of enterprise relationships. The verdict flips if Coasty announces three named Fortune 500 paid pilots with on-prem sandboxed deployment by month 12 ? a regulated-customer wedge with switching cost, not a $99/mo PLG tier.

Pass.
