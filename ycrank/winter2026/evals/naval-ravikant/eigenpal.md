# EigenPal -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that every smart person in enterprise software agrees this is a good idea. AI document processing. Template-free OCR. Workflow automation. There are at least five well-funded companies doing variations of this -- Rossum at $114M raised with $45M in revenue, Hyperscience at $300M raised, Nanonets at $29M, plus Google, AWS, and Azure all offering document AI APIs bundled into existing cloud contracts. When I see a category where the consensus is already "this is the future," the asymmetric upside has been priced away. Nobody is dismissing AI-powered document processing as impossible or silly. The contrarian bet here would be betting *against* standalone IDP companies -- arguing that this capability gets absorbed into the cloud platforms. EigenPal is swimming with the current, not against it.

On specific knowledge: Blaszyk's years at Elastic give him genuine enterprise search and data infrastructure experience, and his open-source docx-js-editor (443 stars) demonstrates he can build document tooling. That's real. But this is adjacent knowledge, not the kind of obsessive, non-transferable understanding I look for. He didn't spend a decade inside a bank watching document processing fail. He didn't build three previous document AI tools that each taught him something the next competitor hasn't learned. Novak's background is more concerning from a specificity standpoint -- McKinsey analyst, Accenture intern, then a different B2B AI startup (Assetario, which appears still active). That's a career optimizer's path through enterprise tech, not a domain obsessive's. The McKinsey and Accenture logos on EigenPal's website raise an uncomfortable question: are these customers, or are they Novak's former employers being listed ambiguously? No case studies, no press releases, no named testimonials verify the relationship.

The leverage architecture is the weakest element from my framework. Enterprise document processing for banks requires direct sales, long implementation cycles, compliance reviews, and on-premises deployment work. Each new $1B-revenue bank customer likely requires significant human effort to onboard. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where each developer question-and-answer pair made the platform more valuable for every subsequent visitor with zero marginal cost. Or Replit, where one improvement to the IDE serves millions simultaneously. EigenPal's model is software, so it has baseline leverage over a services business, but "enterprise SaaS with on-prem deployment for regulated industries" is one of the least leveraged forms of software. You're essentially building a consulting-adjacent product that requires white-glove treatment for each customer.

The bull case: if the evaluation-first workflow approach genuinely solves the trust problem that prevents enterprises from deploying AI in regulated document processing, EigenPal could become the standard interface between enterprise compliance requirements and AI capabilities. The two European bank relationships, if real and deepening, could generate the kind of proprietary training data and workflow templates that create compounding switching costs. Banks talk to other banks. A beachhead in European banking regulation -- where GDPR makes compliance harder -- could create a moat that Rossum and Hyperscience, with their more American-centric go-to-market, haven't built. SOC II Type 2 certification at pre-seed is genuinely impressive if verified, and suggests the founders understand that in regulated industries, compliance *is* the product. If I believed the founders had spent years inside banking compliance and understood something non-obvious about why existing IDP solutions fail in that specific context, this would be a different evaluation.

But I don't see that evidence. What I see is two technically competent Imperial College graduates building a better mousetrap in a category where Rossum has a seven-year head start and $45M in revenue, where cloud providers are bundling the core capability for free, and where the underlying technology -- LLM-based document extraction -- is commoditizing in real time. The "agentic workflow builder" and "natural language configuration" are features, not moats. Any well-funded competitor can add an evaluation layer and a Grafana integration. The founders don't appear to possess knowledge that would take a competitor years to acquire. This is the kind of company where execution quality determines outcomes in a tight competitive race -- and in a tight race, the team with $114M in the bank usually wins. I'm passing.
