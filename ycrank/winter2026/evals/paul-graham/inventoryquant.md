# InventoryQuant -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sander Schulhoff is that he's a genuinely exceptional AI researcher building something deliberately boring. Best Theme Paper at EMNLP 2023. Published at NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR. Created LearnPrompting.org before ChatGPT launched and grew it to 3M+ users. Organized HackAPrompt with sponsorship from OpenAI and HuggingFace, collecting 600K+ adversarial prompts. This is someone who could be doing almost anything in AI right now -- building another developer copilot, launching an AI agent platform, riding the hottest trend -- and instead he's automating the transcription of damaged household items for insurance adjusters. That's interesting. Insurance contents inventorying is exactly the kind of work that makes smart people's eyes glaze over, which means it might be the kind of problem where schlep blindness creates real opportunity.

But here's what concerns me. When I look at how Schulhoff found this problem, there's no organic backstory. His entire documented background -- BS in CS at Maryland, NLP research, deep reinforcement learning, prompt engineering education -- has zero connection to insurance. The dossier marks it explicitly: domain expertise likely comes from customer discovery rather than prior industry experience. No insurance advisors identified. No prior industry roles. This looks like a technically brilliant person who went looking for a vertical to apply AI to, found insurance contents claims, and decided the schlep was big enough to build a business around. That's better than chasing the AI copilot trend, but it's fundamentally different from Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive or the Airbnb founders needing to make rent. The problem wasn't discovered through lived experience -- it was selected from analysis. I've seen many companies born this way, and the issue isn't that they can't work. It's that when the inevitable hard slog of selling into insurance companies begins -- the long sales cycles, the compliance requirements, the relationship dynamics that no amount of technical excellence prepares you for -- founders without organic connection to the problem are more likely to pivot away to something shinier.

The solo founder issue compounds this. Insurance is a relationship industry. Verisk's XactContents is used by 22 of the top 25 U.S. property insurers. You're not just building software -- you're navigating an ecosystem where the incumbent has decades of embedded trust. I required Drew Houston to find a co-founder, and Dropbox was a consumer product where the technical product could speak for itself. Insurance B2B sales with a single founder who has no industry relationships is a harder path by an order of magnitude. The ideal configuration here would be Schulhoff paired with someone who has spent fifteen years as a public adjuster and knows every pain point in the XactContents workflow from the inside. That person doesn't appear to exist on this team.

Now, the bull case. The schlep here is genuinely real. Transcribing damaged items from walkthroughs, looking up replacement prices one by one, formatting reports for XactContents -- this is exactly the kind of tedious, unglamorous work that most founders filter out unconsciously. And Schulhoff isn't just technically competent; he ships products that people use. LearnPrompting.org reaching 3M+ users is not an academic exercise -- that's a real product with real distribution. The GGG testimonial matters too: their CFO says the product has been "running smoothly for the last two months and has already saved us entire days worth of time." That's not hypothetical demand. An enterprise customer whose general counsel is willing to put his name on a testimonial after two months of actual use is a real signal. The XactContents export format shows smart thinking about ecosystem integration -- you plug into the incumbent workflow rather than trying to replace it. If Schulhoff can stack up five or ten more customers like GGG and find a domain co-founder along the way, this could become a real business.

But the commodity risk troubles me. The core pipeline -- speech-to-text via something like Whisper, structured extraction via an LLM, retail price lookup -- is built from generally available components. Verisk has already announced XactAI integrating across their ecosystem. Tractable has $185M in funding. When Stripe built payment infrastructure, the technical challenge involved building relationships with banks, handling fraud detection, managing compliance across jurisdictions -- layers of accumulated complexity that couldn't be replicated by stitching together APIs. Here, a well-resourced team could build a competitive pipeline in months. The potential moat is in the pricing data that accumulates from processed claims, but that moat doesn't exist yet, and it requires significant scale to become meaningful. Schulhoff's technical edge is real but it's in AI research broadly, not in proprietary technology specific to this problem.

What I keep coming back to is this: Sander Schulhoff is the kind of founder I'd want to fund -- technical, prolific, clearly capable of building things people use. If he'd come to me with a problem he'd discovered organically, something that grew from his own work in AI education or prompt engineering, I'd be much more excited. Instead he's applied his talent to a domain he picked rather than one he lived in. That's not fatal -- some good companies start this way -- but it means the margin for error is thinner. He'll need to develop the domain intuition on the fly that other founders bring from day one. In insurance, where the sales cycles are long and the relationships are everything, that learning curve matters.
