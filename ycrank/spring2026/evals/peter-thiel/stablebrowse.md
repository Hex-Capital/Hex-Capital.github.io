# StableBrowse -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The dossier names five funded competitors pursuing the same thesis — Browserbase at $67.5 million, Browser Use at $17 million, Kernel at $22 million, Airtop at $38.8 million, TinyFish at $47 million — totaling over $180 million deployed into "browser infrastructure for AI agents" before StableBrowse has shipped a public product. When five well-capitalized companies occupy adjacent positions in the same emerging category, you are not looking at a secret. You are looking at a consensus. The existence of a competitive landscape slide with five entries is itself the diagnosis: this is a market, not a monopoly. All failed companies are the same — they failed to escape competition — and StableBrowse is entering a space already defined by it.

The claimed insight is that AI agents should not use browsers built for human visual consumption — strip the rendering pipeline and output semantic data instead. Grant that this is technically correct. Is it a secret? A secret must be an important truth that most informed people actively deny. But no one in this space denies that rendering pixels for machines is wasteful. Browserbase, Kernel, and Browser Use are all building toward the same conclusion through different architectural layers. StableBrowse's specific bet — removing the rendering stack entirely at the engine level — is a more aggressive engineering choice, but it is a choice about implementation depth, not a contrarian truth about the world. The question "how should browsers serve AI agents?" is consensus. The answer "strip the renderer" is an engineering optimization that any well-funded competitor with browser expertise could pursue once it proves viable. Browserbase, sitting on $67.5 million and 1,000 customers, could build a rendering-free mode as a product extension and distribute it to their existing base overnight. When your secret can be replicated by an incumbent adding a feature flag, you do not have a secret.

The strongest bull case requires believing that removing the rendering pipeline is not an optimization but a discontinuity — that StableBrowse is building the car while everyone else optimizes horses. If the claimed 85MB RAM, sub-80ms cold start, and 10x cost reduction hold at production scale with full web compatibility, this could represent the last mover in browser infrastructure — the approach that makes Chromium-based agent browsing structurally obsolete. In that scenario, CDP compatibility functions as a Trojan horse: developers drop in StableBrowse as a replacement, discover the performance is categorically superior, and never switch back. The infrastructure layer captures disproportionate value as the agentic AI market scales to $100 billion+. I acknowledge this possibility. But the bull case demands that four first-time founders with no demonstrated browser engineering expertise solve what is arguably the hardest problem in the stack — maintaining web compatibility across the full diversity of modern SPAs, Shadow DOM, iframes, and WebSocket-driven UIs without a rendering engine — before any of five well-funded competitors with head starts and existing customers converge on the same approach. That is a bet on execution speed in a competitive market, which is precisely the kind of bet my framework rejects.

The founders present the weakest signal in the dossier. No confirmed shared prehistory. No prior exits. No visible open-source contributions or browser engineering credentials. Somansh Shah graduated Purdue in 2025; the others are largely unverifiable through public data. Building a custom browser engine that correctly handles the modern web is among the most demanding engineering challenges in software — the kind of project where credentialed teams from Google Chrome, Mozilla, or Servo would struggle. The dossier provides no evidence that this team possesses that depth. The absence of any public GitHub repositories, any developer community, any social presence for what is ostensibly a developer infrastructure tool compounds the concern. For a company whose entire strategy is bottom-up developer adoption, being invisible to developers is not a neutral signal.

This is a pure software company building a commodity layer — no physical-world moat, no government customer pathway, no network effects beyond ecosystem integrations that any competitor can also build. The category itself has the structure I associate with value destruction: multiple players, converging approaches, open-source alternatives gaining traction, and incumbents with the resources to absorb any technical innovation that proves out. I wanted to find the contrarian case — a young team with a genuine architectural insight that the market dismisses. But the market is not dismissing this thesis; it is flooding it with capital. When $180 million is already deployed against your macro thesis before you have launched, you are not a contrarian. You are late to a consensus.
