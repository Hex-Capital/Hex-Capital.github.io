# Datost -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me before anything else: Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack, and they already own Tableau. Datost is building an AI data analyst inside Slack to replace the need for Tableau-style dashboards. That's building your entire company on top of someone else's platform when that someone else has every incentive in the world to build exactly what you're building — and they own both sides of the equation. I've seen founders navigate platform risk before, but this isn't ordinary platform risk. This is like opening a restaurant inside someone's house and hoping they never learn to cook. The structural incentive for Salesforce to unify Slack and Tableau into a native AI analyst is so obvious that it's probably already on a product roadmap somewhere in San Francisco.

I don't know these founders' stories, and that's the problem. My whole process starts with "tell me your story — not the business." Maceo and Jason are 21, they both worked at Traba as software engineers, and they're building an AI data analyst. But why? What happened in their lives that made them feel the pain of data bottlenecks so deeply that they had to start a company? There's a non-profit called Lighthouse Startups that Maceo co-founded, which tells me something about initiative, but I can't see the biographical thread connecting these specific people to this specific problem. When I backed the Contraline founder, what convinced me was years of dedicated research that only made sense in the context of his whole life. With Datost, I see capable young engineers solving a problem they probably observed at work — which is fine, but it's a very different signal than someone whose personal history made this problem impossible to ignore.

The honest truth is this idea doesn't give me any jolt at all. "AI data analyst" in 2026 is about as consensus as you can get. Julius AI already has two million users. Defog.ai is doing open-source text-to-SQL. Microsoft has Copilot in Power BI. Google has Looker AI. Every major platform is shipping some version of this. The companies I get excited about are the ones that other investors think are crazy — SpaceX when everyone said private rockets were a pipe dream, Crusoe when people laughed at Bitcoin mining on gas flares. An AI bot that answers data questions in Slack? Nobody's laughing at that. Everyone's building it. And when everyone's building something, the price is wrong and the weirdness has been arbitraged away before you even write the check.

Let me be fair about the bull case, because I try to be honest with myself. The "first AI data analyst that has its own computer" framing — an autonomous execution environment rather than just SQL generation — could be a real technical edge if they nail it. The Slack-native form factor is a genuine distribution insight; meeting people where they already work is powerful, and I know that from watching Uber succeed partly because you didn't have to go stand on a corner and wave your arm. Two twenty-one-year-olds with production engineering experience, already in YC, willing to bet on themselves this early — I respect that hunger. If the Salesforce internal politics between the Slack team and the Tableau team create enough friction that they can't ship a unified product for three years, and if Datost can build deep organizational context that becomes genuinely sticky, there's a path to something real. The data context accumulation — learning an organization's terminology, schemas, tribal knowledge — could create real switching costs over time. But that "if" is doing a lot of heavy lifting against Microsoft, Google, and Salesforce all converging on the same idea simultaneously.

The "democratize data" tagline resonates with something I care about deeply — making invisible systems visible, giving access to people who've been locked out. But when I look at who actually benefits from Datost, it's knowledge workers at companies that already have Slack workspaces and databases. These are not underserved populations. This isn't Uber creating income for drivers who had no other option, or Affirm giving transparent credit to people shut out by hidden fees. It's making life more convenient for people who already have good jobs at data-driven companies. That's useful, but it's not the kind of impact that keeps me up at night imagining what's possible.

I'm passing on this one. The founders may be wonderful — I genuinely can't tell from what's available, and I'd love to hear their stories over coffee. But the combination of a deeply consensus idea, existential platform dependency, well-funded competitors already at scale, and no biographical signal connecting these founders to this problem in a way that would survive the inevitable crises ahead — that's not my kind of bet. The best investments I've made looked bad to most people at the time. This one looks fine to everyone, and that's exactly what worries me.
