# Taiga -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Nanda Guntupalli and Adam Wax both grew up watching their physician parents lose money to denied claims and billing errors. That's the most interesting thing about Taiga, and the reason I can't dismiss it quickly. When two founders can point to a specific kitchen-table frustration they've observed since childhood -- not a market report, not a TAM slide, but watching a parent stay late wrestling with payer denials -- that's as organic as problem discovery gets. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive and built Dropbox. These two watched their parents bleed revenue to administrative overhead and built a billing agency. The origin structure is right. The question is whether everything surrounding that origin is also right, and here the picture gets complicated.

Taiga enters a market where Candid Health has raised $99.5M and grew revenue roughly 250% in 2024, AKASA has raised $205M targeting large health systems, and even within YC's own recent batches, LunaBill already reports $764K in contracted ARR from AI voice agents for insurance follow-up. AI-powered medical billing is not a bad idea that happens to be good -- it's an idea that sounds good to everyone. When 62% of all digital health venture funding goes to AI-enabled startups, the contrarian signal has inverted. Taiga is building in a category where no one says "that will never work." They say "oh yeah, someone should definitely do that." In my framework, universal head-nodding is one of the most reliable negative signals. The best opportunities hide behind an initial reaction of confusion or dismissal. Taiga's pitch provokes neither.

Adam Wax won "best overall" at the Lehigh Valley Collegiate Hackathon and built an AI study assistant using AWS Bedrock and Textract. Nanda Guntupalli studied CS at Penn. Both are technically capable. But when I look for the "animal" quality -- the founder who has shipped something people actually use, who has a story about hacking together revenue before anyone would fund them, who built the first version over a weekend because they couldn't stop thinking about the problem -- I find a near-empty evidence trail. Taiga's GitHub organization contains three repositories, all forks or profile repos, no original code. No public users, no revenue figures, no customer names. No Twitter presence for either founder. The hackathon wins tell me Adam can build under a deadline, which is worth something, but hackathons are structured competitions with clear rules. Startups are unstructured problems with no rules. The gap between "won a hackathon" and "sold cereal boxes to fund the company when every VC said no" is the gap between competence and the relentless resourcefulness I need to see.

Taiga's positioning as a full-service agency rather than a SaaS tool is the one element that cuts slightly against consensus. Most AI billing startups sell software; Taiga says "upload your visit data and we handle everything." That's smarter for small practices that don't want another dashboard -- they want the problem to disappear. But it creates a structural tension with superlinear returns. An agency model, even an AI-augmented one, scales closer to linearly: more clients means more claims to manage, more payer relationships to maintain, more edge cases to handle. Stripe built payment infrastructure that scales superlinearly because every new merchant makes the network more valuable. Taiga's billing agency adds clients one practice at a time. The percentage-of-collections model is standard for billing agencies (typically 4-10%), and while AI reduces the human labor component, the fundamental unit economics still track client count rather than compounding network effects. I don't see the "how well you've done determines how well you do" dynamic that defines a startup versus a service business. The denial-prediction model could theoretically improve with volume -- more claims data creating better payer-specific predictions -- but that data flywheel is explicitly labeled a "potential future moat, not a current one" in the dossier, and the underlying LLM capabilities are accessible to any well-funded competitor.

The strongest bull case for Taiga runs like this: the incumbents (Waystar at ~$8B valuation, R1 RCM) have cost structures built around enterprise contracts with hospital systems, making small independent practices unprofitable under their model. Taiga's AI-first approach could crack the unit economics for serving the 200,000+ physician-owned practices that incumbents can't reach. The founders' organic discovery is genuine, their co-founder relationship dates to 3rd grade (the strongest possible "have they discussed what happens if one leaves" signal), and both can build the product themselves. YC accepted them, Nicolas Dessaigne is partnering with them, and sometimes the right pair of young builders in a massive market is all you need. The ClassDojo parallel beckons -- a non-developer-tool, unsexy problem domain where schlep (HIPAA compliance, payer rules, EHR integrations) creates a natural moat. But ClassDojo's founders had demonstrable product traction and exceptional execution metrics when I invested. Taiga has seven EHR integrations listed on a website and zero public evidence of a single paying customer. The schlep-blindness advantage that would have protected Taiga three years ago has been competed away by over $300M in venture funding flowing to direct competitors. When the schlep becomes visible to everyone, it stops functioning as a moat.

Taiga's founders show genuine earnestness -- building something to solve their families' actual problem rather than chasing a trend, choosing medical billing over sexier AI applications, maintaining a childhood friendship that suggests authentic partnership. That registers. But earnestness without demonstrated resourcefulness is necessary but not sufficient. The one verifiable condition that would flip me toward Invest: show me three to five small practices using Taiga today, with specific denial-rate reductions or collection-rate improvements, acquired through founder-led hustle rather than paid channels. If Nanda and Adam walked into their parents' practices, implemented Taiga, proved it works, and used those results to sign up neighboring practices -- that would be the resourcefulness story I'm missing. Right now Taiga has strong organic discovery in a consensus-attractive market with no visible traction, no demonstrated resourcefulness, and a business model that trends toward linear scaling. The organic problem discovery is the one dimension that genuinely stands out, but it can't carry the full weight alone.
