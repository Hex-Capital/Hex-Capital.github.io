# Cofia -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that Bardeen already shipped this product. In May 2025 they launched what they call a "Work Intelligence Platform" -- AI agents that "learn how work actually happens, then execute and improve it." That's essentially Cofia's pitch, word for word. Bardeen has $22-25M in funding, backing from Dropbox and HubSpot, and a multi-year head start. When a well-funded competitor has already launched the thing you're building, you need an extraordinary reason to believe you'll win anyway. DoorDash won despite skepticism because Tony Xu was personally delivering food -- the founders had an execution intensity that couldn't be replicated. Here I see a website that returns only analytics tracking code. No product. No users. No visible prototype. That's not a schlep being navigated. That's a race being lost.

The origin question matters even more in a crowded market. How did Paola and Moses find this problem? Paola was a product manager focused on retention at Brilliant.org. Moses was a Senior Staff Engineer at Duolingo building monetization infrastructure. Neither of them worked at an automation company. Neither has a visible history with workflow tools. There's no "I kept copying data between Slack and Salesforce fifty times a day and finally snapped" moment. The idea -- AI that watches how you work and proactively builds automations -- reads like what you'd generate if you asked "what's the next evolution of Zapier?" during a brainstorming weekend. That doesn't mean the idea is wrong. It means the motivation might be. Earnest founders work on problems because they can't stop thinking about them. When the connection between the founders and the problem is invisible, I worry the problem was selected rather than discovered.

The schlep dynamics here are actually inverted from what I look for. With Stripe, the schlep -- dealing with banks, fraud, compliance -- was a barrier that kept smart people out of the market. That's the good kind of schlep. With Cofia, the biggest schlep is building integrations across hundreds of SaaS tools and getting users to grant the deep access permissions needed for behavioral observation. But the incumbents have already climbed that hill. Zapier has 100,000+ customers and presumably thousands of integrations. Microsoft Power Automate is embedded in the enterprise stack. These companies could add a "suggested automations" feature in a quarter. The schlep here doesn't protect Cofia from competition; it protects incumbents from Cofia.

There's also a co-founder signal I can't ignore. No shared employer or university overlap between Paola and Moses -- Stanford and Duke, Brilliant.org and Duolingo. The dossier doesn't tell me how they met or how long they've known each other. I've seen this pattern enough times to call it what it is: a risk. In one YC batch, nine companies added co-founders they didn't already know well, and all nine imploded within a year. That doesn't mean Paola and Moses don't have a strong relationship -- maybe they're close friends through other channels. But the absence of any visible shared history is a data point.

The bull case deserves a paragraph because the individual founder credentials are genuinely strong. Moses reaching Senior Staff Engineer at Duolingo -- a $9B+ public company with serious engineering culture -- means he can build. That's a real signal. And Paola's focus on user motivation and retention at Brilliant.org is actually relevant to a product that needs to understand behavioral patterns. If the paradigm shift from user-initiated to proactive automation is real, and if incumbents truly can't cannibalize their configuration-driven business models fast enough, then a small team that nails the AI observation layer could create something with real switching costs. The personalization data moat -- where a competitor would have to re-learn each user's workflows from scratch -- is a legitimate defensibility thesis if adoption happens first. For this investment to work, you'd need to believe the team can ship faster than Bardeen, build integrations faster than seems plausible with two people, and that the observation-first approach is technically differentiated enough that Zapier can't clone it. That's a lot of "ifs," but the individual elements aren't crazy.

What I keep coming back to, though, is that this idea has no organic backstory, sits in a market where a funded competitor already ships the same value proposition, and the website has no product on it. The founders are capable people. But capable people building manufactured ideas in competitive markets is exactly the pattern that produces forgettable companies. The automation market is real, the timing around LLM capabilities is real, but "real market + good timing + no organic founder-problem connection" is how you get a company that raises money and then slowly loses to someone who cares more about the problem.
