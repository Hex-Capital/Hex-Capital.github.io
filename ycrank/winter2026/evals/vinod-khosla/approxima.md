# Approxima -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Approxima is not what the company is building -- it's where they chose to pivot. Mid-batch, they abandoned SideKit (a mobile app SDK) and landed on "self-healing software" using AI agents. They pivoted directly into the single most consensus category in technology today. Cognition has $73M ARR and a $10.2B valuation. Factory AI has Sequoia and NVIDIA backing at $300M. Anthropic and OpenAI are shipping coding agents natively -- Claude Code, Codex -- that do precisely what Approxima describes. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, the food industry laughed. The signal I look for is experts dismissing the attempt. Here, the opposite is happening: every major platform company and well-capitalized startup is validating this exact approach. When incumbents endorse your category, it usually means the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening -- or worse, that they're already building it themselves.

Let me start where I always start: the consequence of success. If Approxima fully succeeds -- if their agents autonomously find and fix every routine bug -- what changes? Software engineers spend less time on maintenance and more on features. That is a productivity improvement inside an existing system. It does not replace how software is built. It does not make an industry structurally obsolete. It does not reclaim billions of acres of land or eliminate a trillion-dollar supply chain. The ceiling here, even in the wildest success scenario, is a useful enterprise tool in a crowded market. I have spent forty years telling founders: you are too talented to spend your careers on problems this small. Automating bug fixes is a feature, not a transformation. The agentic AI TAM numbers look impressive on paper ($200B by 2034), but Approxima's slice of that -- autonomous maintenance for codebases -- is not a new category. It is a feature that every IDE, every cloud platform, and every foundation model provider will ship as table stakes.

The technology question compounds the problem. There is no proprietary technology visible. Their GitHub organization contains only SideKit-era repositories -- an iOS SDK with 7 stars, a React Native SDK with 1 star. The website returned a connection error. No patents, no open-source projects, no evidence of a working Approxima product. The core capability they're building on -- LLMs understanding and modifying code -- is entirely borrowed from foundation model providers. This is the KiOR lesson applied to software: when your technology achievement depends on someone else's underlying platform, and that platform provider decides to compete with you directly, your position evaporates. Except KiOR at least had proprietary catalytic chemistry. What is Approxima's proprietary layer? I see no evidence of one.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: Sweep AI occupied this exact niche and pivoted away, leaving a gap. The maintenance burden on engineering teams is genuinely enormous -- most developer time goes to keeping existing systems alive, not building new ones. If Approxima builds deep CI/CD integrations and accumulates proprietary data from customer codebases, they could develop a flywheel that generic coding agents cannot replicate. The mid-batch pivot itself could signal founder learning rate -- they recognized SideKit was not working and moved toward a larger problem. Perhaps Selvaraj's Databricks and Microsoft internships gave him a visceral understanding of maintenance burden at enterprise scale. I take this seriously. But the counter is fatal: the positioning gap may exist precisely because this is not a viable standalone category. Sweep AI did not pivot away from a gold mine -- they pivoted away because the wrapper value was collapsing as models improved. And pivoting into the hottest category in tech is not contrarian courage. It is trend-following dressed up as adaptability.

Two University of Waterloo CS graduates, freshly minted in 2025, entering a market where their competitors have raised a combined $700 million. No visible product, no customers, no press, no working website. The team is homogeneous -- two CS students with similar backgrounds, no AI research depth, no enterprise sales experience, no one who has built and scaled infrastructure at the level this problem demands. I evaluate founders on their rate of learning and willingness to attempt what experts consider impossible. I see no evidence of either here. What I see is two smart young engineers who picked the most obvious application of current AI capabilities. That is precisely the move I warn against: reducing the probability of failure by choosing a well-validated market, to the point where the consequence of success -- even if everything works -- is a modest business in a field with a dozen better-funded competitors.
