# ZeroSettle -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

ZeroSettle's existence is entirely downstream of a court ruling. The company was born because Judge Gonzalez Rogers held Apple in contempt in April 2025, and its value proposition endures only as long as that ruling remains intact and Apple is barred from imposing compensating fees. This is not a company built on a secret about how the world works -- it is a company built on a judicial event that every iOS developer, every payments company, and every tech journalist immediately understood. When Epic sued Apple, the entire industry watched. When the injunction was enforced, Stripe published its own iOS alternative billing guide within weeks. Paddle cited the opening as a growth driver. Neon Pay raised $14 million from Thrive and a16z to chase the same opportunity in gaming. There is no information asymmetry here, no hidden truth. "Developers want to keep more of their revenue" is not a contrarian insight -- it is the most consensus observation in mobile software. The question I always ask -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- finds no answer in this company's thesis. Everyone agrees. That is precisely the problem.

The competitive structure confirms the diagnosis. When a regulatory door opens and a dozen well-funded players rush through simultaneously, you are looking at a Red Ocean, not a monopoly opportunity. RevenueCat has $119 million in funding and 70,000+ apps on its platform. Paddle has $293 million and explicitly identified this market as a growth vector. Stripe -- the literal payment rail underneath ZeroSettle -- already provides guidance for the same use case and could build a turnkey migration product with trivially more effort than ZeroSettle itself expended. The dossier acknowledges the core technology is "replicable." A web checkout SDK, entitlement syncing, and "Switch & Save" campaigns are not proprietary technology -- they are product features that any competent engineering team can implement. ZeroSettle's entire technical stack is a wrapper around Stripe's infrastructure. When your value proposition can be absorbed by your own payment processor, you do not have a moat; you have a feature request.

The strongest argument for ZeroSettle is the structural conflict it exploits among incumbents. RevenueCat cannot easily offer alternative billing bypass without jeopardizing its relationship with Apple -- its business depends on deep StoreKit API access that Apple could restrict. Apple itself obviously will not build a tool to cannibalize $27 billion in annual commissions. This creates a genuine opening for a new entrant. Both founders are former Apple systems engineers with insider knowledge of App Review, StoreKit, and Apple's enforcement mechanisms. That domain expertise is real and relevant. If ZeroSettle could establish itself as the default Merchant of Record for direct billing before Stripe or Paddle builds a comparable turnkey product, the switching costs of migrated subscribers would create some defensibility. The MoR relationship means that once a developer's users are billing through ZeroSettle, moving them back to Apple IAP or to a different MoR involves subscriber disruption -- real friction, not trivial.

But this bull case collapses under scrutiny of the endgame. The December 2025 Ninth Circuit ruling remanded the question of whether Apple can charge any fee on linked-out purchases. If Apple is eventually permitted to impose even a 10-12% commission on alternative payment transactions -- a very plausible outcome given Apple's legal resources and lobbying power -- ZeroSettle's value proposition shrinks dramatically. A developer currently paying 30% to Apple would instead pay 10-12% to Apple plus 5% plus $0.50 to ZeroSettle, saving perhaps 12-13 percentage points rather than 25. At those margins, many developers would conclude the savings do not justify the risk of Apple's institutional hostility -- the chilling effect on App Review alone would deter adoption. The company's entire future hangs on a regulatory variable outside its control. I avoid businesses whose destiny is determined by judges rather than founders. A company should be able to design its future through its own technology and vision, not wait for a court to decide whether its business model is permitted to exist.

The founders are competent engineers with relevant Apple experience, but nothing in the dossier suggests the kind of definite, world-shaping vision that distinguishes great founders from good ones. There is no articulated theory of how mobile commerce fundamentally changes, no plan for what the world looks like in ten years if ZeroSettle succeeds. The ambition is calibrated to a regulatory window: help developers save on commissions while the law permits it. This is a services business dressed in SDK clothing -- useful, perhaps profitable in the near term, but not the kind of company that returns a fund. The power law demands investments where success means creating a new category that dominates for decades. ZeroSettle, at its most optimistic, captures a fraction of a commission spread that may itself be legislated out of existence.
