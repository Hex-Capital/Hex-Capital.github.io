# Interfaze -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The architecture tells you exactly what kind of bet this is. Interfaze routes deterministic subtasks to specialized small models and passes everything else to a "user-selected LLM." That last phrase is the tell. When your product requires the customer to bring someone else's foundation model to do the heavy reasoning, you're building an orchestration layer on top of rented leverage. The proprietary value lives in the perception modules — the OCR, STT, classification DNNs — and in the routing logic that connects them. But the moment you're charging $1.50 per million input tokens while OpenAI and Anthropic control the model your system forwards context to, you're in a structurally squeezed position. You capture value on the routing, they capture value on the reasoning. That's not permissionless leverage — it's intermediary leverage, and intermediaries get compressed.

Specific knowledge. Khurdula brings legitimate ML research credentials — Purdue M.Sc., peer-reviewed IEEE paper on the exact architecture, GitHub repos in Hindi OCR and X-ray classification. That's domain competence, not domain obsession. Compare to Srinivas at Perplexity, who spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the systems that became his company's foundation. Khurdula's research is in the right neighborhood but at an early-career depth that many ML graduates share. Khemlani is the more puzzling signal — prior exit at Stayr, a proptech company in Singapore, growing from $1M to $3M ARR before exit. That demonstrates execution ability, but proptech-to-AI-infrastructure is a domain jump, not a domain deepening. If I remove the founder, does the core insight survive? A competent ML team could rebuild the routing architecture from the IEEE paper. That's the test Interfaze doesn't pass cleanly.

The principal-agent diagnostic gives me mixed readings. The JigsawStack-to-Interfaze rename, the email domain still pointing to jigsawstack.com, the GitHub org carrying the old name — these are surface-level brand hygiene issues, but they pattern-match to a company still searching for its identity rather than executing from deep conviction. Khemlani has been building since July 2024, roughly two years of sustained effort, which earns some credibility. But the co-founders have no shared history before JigsawStack — no common employer, no overlapping university, no prior collaboration. I've said before that co-founders without pre-existing trust create internal principal-agent problems before the company launches. That's not fatal, but it's a signal I track.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If AI agents proliferate — and every trend says they will — those agents need reliable perception and action primitives. You don't want your agent calling GPT-4 to do OCR when a specialized model does it faster, cheaper, and deterministically. Interfaze could become the perception layer for the agent ecosystem, the way Twilio became the communication layer for software. The MCP server, the n8n integration, the OpenAI-compatible endpoints — these are smart distribution bets that position for agent interoperability. Ten million API requests in beta shows developers are finding the product useful. And the published IEEE paper, whatever its limitations, is more technical substance than most YC pre-seed companies produce. If the small-model routing architecture proves structurally superior for deterministic tasks as foundation models get more expensive, the cost advantage could compound.

But the compound interest test is where this breaks down. OpenAI API compatibility means near-zero switching costs — easy in, easy out. Every request processed generates some training signal, but the data flywheel is thin because the individual perception tasks are well-understood problems with abundant training data. The competitive landscape is brutal: Firecrawl has 50K GitHub stars and $14.5M in funding for scraping alone, Apify does $13.3M in revenue, and frontier model providers are adding multimodal capabilities that overlap with Interfaze's entire task set. When OpenAI ships native OCR that's good enough for 90% of use cases, the orchestration layer loses its reason to exist. The individual components — OCR models, STT models, scraping engines — are available as open-source or commercial point solutions. Integration is real work, but it's not the kind of compounding advantage that creates power-law returns. Innovation without a durable moat becomes consumer surplus.

Five people building a multi-model AI system is the right shape for a team in my framework, but maintaining a zoo of specialized DNNs across OCR, STT, classification, and scraping requires ongoing ML engineering that scales with the number of modalities, not with the number of users. This isn't Notion, where a small team builds one product that millions use unchanged. Each new task type requires new model training, new benchmarks, new maintenance. The team-to-value leverage is decent but not extraordinary. I pass — not because the founders aren't competent, but because the architecture rents more leverage than it creates, and the specific knowledge required to build this is learnable, not non-replicable.
