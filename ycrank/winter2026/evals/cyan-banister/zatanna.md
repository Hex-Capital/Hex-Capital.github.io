# Zatanna -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me is the pivot trail. Google's cache shows this company as "a lawtech company focused on jury trials," then "Adaptive Healthcare Admin Agents," and now "Request Based Scraping Infrastructure." Three entirely unrelated domains. When I sit down with a founder, I want to hear the story of how they arrived at this problem -- the biographical thread that makes this person and this company inseparable. Jury trials, healthcare admin, and web scraping have nothing in common except that they're all markets someone might chase. This is exactly the pattern that makes me pull back: founders pivoting toward whatever feels hot rather than grinding on a problem they were born to solve. Travis Kalanick spent years fuming about taxi medallions before Uber existed. The Crusoe team had independently arrived at an insight about wasted gas that nobody else believed in. That kind of biographical inevitability is what I need to feel, and I don't feel it here.

I want to be fair to Alex Blackwell, because his anti-bot bypass work at Pikkit is real domain expertise -- he's not a tourist in this technical space. And the protocol-level scraping approach is genuinely differentiated. Bypassing headless browsers to replicate HTTP requests at the protocol level is clever engineering, and the performance claims (sub-500ms, <1 MB memory) are meaningful if they hold up. But here's my problem: scraping infrastructure in 2026 is not unpopular. It's not difficult to assess. It's not the kind of bet that makes other investors uncomfortable. Firecrawl just raised $14.5 million. Bright Data is doing $300 million in annual revenue. This is a consensus category being pulled by AI and LLM demand, and every developer tools investor in San Francisco has a scraping deal on their desk right now. The most promising companies I've backed -- SpaceX, Contraline, Crusoe -- were the ones nobody else wanted to fund because they sounded absurd. Zatanna sounds reasonable, and reasonable makes me nervous.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it honestly. If the AI data extraction wave continues accelerating, protocol-level scraping could become the performance tier that every serious data pipeline needs. The custom integration library could compound into a genuine moat -- each reverse-engineered target site represents non-trivial work that a competitor would have to replicate one by one. Alex's specific technical background means the team can actually build this, not just talk about it. And sometimes pivoting isn't weakness -- sometimes it's intellectual honesty about what's working. If this team has found genuine product-market fit with their third iteration and can land a few enterprise contracts quickly, the consumption-based revenue model could scale nicely. A world exists where this becomes the performance infrastructure layer beneath the browser-based scraping platforms, the way CDNs sit beneath web applications.

But that world doesn't need me. It needs a developer tools investor who evaluates on technical architecture and go-to-market efficiency. I invest in people solving problems that emerged from their own scars, in ideas that other investors think are crazy, in companies that create economic access for people who don't have it. Zatanna is a B2B developer tool for making web scraping faster. It doesn't make an invisible system visible. It doesn't create economic opportunity for underserved people. It doesn't operate in a stigmatized or regulated space where complexity becomes a moat -- the legal and ToS risks here are liabilities, not strategic advantages, and I don't see evidence the team is navigating them with the kind of sophistication that would turn risk into defensibility. The three founders come from three different universities with no shared history I can identify, and while that's not automatically a red flag, combined with multiple pivots it raises the question of whether this team has been stress-tested together in the way I've learned matters most. This is a pass for me -- not because it's a bad company, but because nothing about it triggers the signal I've learned to trust.
