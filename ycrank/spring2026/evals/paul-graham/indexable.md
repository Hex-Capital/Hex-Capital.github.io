# Indexable -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft game engine that processes 1,000 players in 455 microseconds. He's targeting the Guinness World Record for largest multiplayer PvP battle -- 170,000 players in a single world. Then he went to xAI. Now he's building a custom virtual machine monitor from scratch, with a modified Linux 6.19 kernel, a content-addressable storage engine, and a custom Nix fork rewritten in Rust. This is someone who would rather build a hypervisor than use one. That matters, because the entire reason nobody else has achieved sub-30ms full-state VM forking is that it requires exactly this kind of person -- someone who looks at KVM and thinks "I'll build my own VMM on top of this" instead of reaching for Firecracker like everyone else. The technical founder signal here is about as strong as it gets at pre-seed. When I talk about the "animal test" -- whether you'd describe someone as an animal at what they do -- Gazelka passes it on systems engineering alone.

The schlep in this company is genuinely impressive. Building a custom VMM, modifying a Linux kernel, writing a Nix fork from scratch in Rust, implementing a content-addressable storage engine with tiered caching and cross-tenant deduplication -- this is years of deep systems work that most teams wouldn't even consider attempting. E2B raised $43.8 million and built on Firecracker, which is a sensible engineering decision that also means they architecturally cannot add full-state forking without rebuilding their core. Daytona raised $31 million and used Docker containers, which gives them forking but at 3x slower speeds and weaker isolation. The schlep here functions like a structural moat: it's not that competitors don't want sub-30ms forking, it's that getting there requires the kind of ground-up systems rebuild that funded companies with existing customers will resist until it's too late. This is the dynamic I've described with Stripe -- payment processing was painful for a decade, and the reason nobody fixed it was because the fix required dealing with banks and regulators and fraud systems, not because the problem was invisible.

Where I hesitate is the origin story. Gazelka was at xAI, which plausibly exposed him to the limitations of existing sandbox infrastructure for AI agents. His Hyperion project already involved state management and snapshotting at massive scale. The technical path from "snapshot game world state for 170K players" to "fork VM state for AI agents in 26 milliseconds" is natural. But I can't point to the specific moment of organic discovery -- the equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive or the Airbnb founders renting air mattresses because they couldn't make rent. The circumstantial evidence is solid: systems engineer with exactly the right skills encounters exactly the right problem domain at xAI. But I can also construct a less favorable narrative: brilliant hacker sees the AI infrastructure wave, looks at what he's uniquely good at building, and matches capability to market. That's not a bad way to start a company -- but it's different from a problem you discovered because you were living inside it. Without hearing the story in his own words, I can't distinguish between the two.

The competitive landscape deserves honest assessment. E2B has 88% of Fortune 100, implied $12M+ ARR, and 500 million sandbox sessions. That's not a projection -- that's a market that already exists. Daytona offers fork-and-snapshot with $31M behind it. Modal Labs is valued above $1 billion. Google open-sourced Agent Sandbox for Kubernetes. This is not a market hiding in plain sight waiting for someone to notice it. It's a market with well-funded incumbents and growing competition. The bull case is that Indexable's performance advantage (26ms vs. 90-300ms) isn't incremental -- it's qualitative. Sub-30ms forking enables workflows that are impossible at 200ms: real-time branching of agent execution, parallel path exploration, instant rollback. If those workflows become the standard way agents operate, Indexable's architecture becomes the only one that works. That's the scenario where this is a generational company -- where the custom VMM isn't just faster, it's the only way to deliver the product that AI agent developers actually need. If Gazelka can get a handful of serious agent teams building on ix and showing that fork-based workflows produce meaningfully better agent outputs, the incumbents' architectural debt becomes a liability rather than a moat.

But there's nothing yet. No users, no revenue, no waitlist numbers, no testimonials, no public repos even. The product exists -- the website claims specific performance numbers -- but there's zero evidence that anyone outside the team has used it. The solo founder concern is real: the YC page lists only Gazelka, the team is three people, and the visible skillset is purely technical. For developer infrastructure, commercial traction eventually requires someone who can sell to engineering leaders, and I don't see that person yet. I made Drew Houston find a co-founder as a condition of acceptance, and the same logic applies here. The technology is exceptional. The question is whether there's a company around it, or just an extraordinarily talented engineer building something impressive in isolation.

This is a company I'd watch closely rather than invest in today. The technical moat is real and the schlep is enormous -- those are strong signals. But the absence of any organic origin narrative, combined with zero user evidence and a competitive field where the leading company already has Fortune 100 penetration, leaves me wanting more before writing a check. If Gazelka comes back in six months with five agent teams that tried E2B's architecture and hit a wall that only sub-30ms forking could solve, I'd invest immediately. The technology is there. The founder has the skills. What's missing is the evidence that this specific technical advantage translates into something developers can't live without.
