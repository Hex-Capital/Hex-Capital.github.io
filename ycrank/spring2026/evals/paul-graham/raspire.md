# RASPIRE -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Kareem Selim and Hassan Mostafa spent years patching mobile apps for Egyptian banks before they built RASPIRE, and that single biographical fact pins the company for me: this is a domain-veteran pair attacking a problem they personally got tired of, with apps already protecting 20M+ end users -- but with no GitHub footprint, no open-source ledger, no hacker tribal markers I can verify from outside. The signature is organic-pain-high, hacker-genealogy-low. That's a specific kind of bet -- competent operators who lived the schlep, not Lisp-prize kids who built a clever artifact in their bedroom -- and it scores well on most of my filters while leaving the one filter I cannot easily replace.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the right HIGH rung for RASPIRE on the schlep dimension, and the comparison is load-bearing: Brian Armstrong took the compliance grind nobody else in his batch would touch, and the grind became the moat. RASPIRE is selling mobile RASP into regulated banking, fintech, insurance, and government -- the buyers who require security questionnaires, on-prem options, FIPS conversations, and 9-month procurement cycles. Where RASPIRE diverges from the Armstrong pattern is that the schlep is not unclaimed: Appdome has already raised $39.6M doing post-compilation binary fusion with explicit "no-code" marketing, and Zimperium has raised $60.1M on the broader MAPS suite. Armstrong's schlep in 2012 had no Appdome sitting on it. RASPIRE's does.

**The MBA-team-with-glossy-deck** failure pattern from my Pass Example is the LOW rung on hacker genealogy, and the failure mechanism is that you cannot verify technical taste from credentials alone -- you need shipped artifacts. RASPIRE's dossier shows zero public GitHub for either Selim or Mostafa, no published security research, no CVE disclosures, no open-source tool with a star count. For security founders specifically, this is louder than it would be for, say, a marketplace team -- security reputation in the wild is built through Defcon talks, exploit writeups, and open instrumentation libraries. Selim being a "CyberTalents alumnus speaker" and Mostafa being ex-Cyshield are real but credential-shaped signals. The pattern is not the MBA failure mode -- they ship -- but it is the adjacent failure mode where I cannot independently audit the craft because the craft is private. That gap matters more here than in most categories.

Animal resourcefulness compounds well over 18 months for RASPIRE: the team has already converted itself from a Cairo cybersecurity startup into a Black Hat MEA exhibitor with a Sadq partnership tied to Saudi Vision 2030, with 500 Global, Sanabil 500 MENA Seed, and Plus VC on the cap table before YC. 20M+ protected end users is not a stage-default number for pre-seed -- it is shipped revenue with named enterprise counterparties implied. That is not cereal boxes, but it is the same biological category: builders who manufactured their own runway through unglamorous enterprise sales in a market where nobody from Sand Hill was going to write them a check first.

For the organic-idea dimension to flip a tier on RASPIRE, the dossier would need to weaken the founder-pain claim -- and it does not. Selim and Mostafa identified the post-deployment protection gap from inside the bank-and-fintech app-security workflow they personally ran. This is the **Dropbox** (2007) shape: Drew Houston forgot his USB stick; Selim and Mostafa kept handing off compiled binaries that lost their defenses the moment they left the build server. The counterfactual where this scores lower requires evidence the founders backed into the category from a market scan, and the public record points the other way.

**Stripe** (2010) is the single-anchor parallel for RASPIRE's co-founder configuration -- two operators who worked the same problem together for years before incorporating, which is the relationship shape I trust most. RASPIRE lists three on the team page but the dossier surfaces only Selim and Mostafa with bios, with their pre-RASPIRE collaboration "inferred" from shared bank/fintech work in Cairo. The Collison-brothers comparison is structurally right but weaker in evidence -- I cannot name the shared employer or the duration with confidence the way I can with Patrick and John.

My 36-month forecast for RASPIRE: $3-8M ARR concentrated in MENA banking and fintech, acquired by Appdome or Zimperium in the $40-80M range as a regional tuck-in for MENA expansion. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature -- organic pain plus operator competence gets you to real revenue in a category; missing hacker-genealogy and a leader (Appdome) already executing the same "no-code" pitch caps the ceiling below fund-returning. This is a good company, not the No. 1 in a batch, and "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) tells me to be honest about which one I'm looking at.

The strongest bull case against my forecast borrows from **Coinbase** (2012): mobile RASP for regulated banks under Saudi Vision 2030 and parallel MENA open-banking mandates is exactly the regulatory-grey-zone schlep I keep telling people I want to fund, and RASPIRE has already done the unglamorous enterprise legwork that Appdome's US-centric sales motion has not optimized for the region. The bear-case rebuttal is in the dossier: Appdome is not absent from MENA, the AI-driven autonomous response that RASPIRE positions on is "[Inferred]" with no detection-accuracy figures, and the moat thesis depends on telemetry volume that has not been quantified. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: RASPIRE lands a tier-1 US or European bank logo within 12 months of YC, or publishes detection-rate benchmarks against Appdome that survive third-party scrutiny. Without one of those, the Appdome-acquisition outcome is the base case, and I'd write a small check on the founders, not a conviction check on the company.
