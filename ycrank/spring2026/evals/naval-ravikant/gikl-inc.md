# Gikl, Inc -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founders of ScienceSwarm have built some of the most consequential AI systems of the last decade — Vajda directed text-to-image and text-to-video generation at Meta, Yamamoto worked in the Core Llama group on pre-training and inference optimization. That's not relevant experience. That's world-class specific knowledge in AI model building. But here's the problem: the company they're building doesn't productize that specific knowledge. It uses it as a commodity input. ScienceSwarm's core challenge isn't "can we build great AI models?" — it's "can we design incentives that make non-expert humans produce verifiable scientific contributions through a community platform?" That's a community design problem, an incentive alignment problem, a verification architecture problem. None of those map to directing generative media research at Meta. Their specific knowledge is the engine of someone else's company. For this company, it's fuel.

Yamamoto's physics PhD and publications in PNAS and Physical Review Letters — including co-authorship with a Fields Medalist — give him genuine insider knowledge of how academic science works and where it breaks. That's a real signal. He's felt the friction of peer review, the bottleneck of literature surveys, the absurdity of 12 years of training before you can contribute. But the platform spans six domains, and his deep scientific credibility covers one. The gap between "I understand why science is slow" and "I can build a system that makes non-experts scientifically productive" is enormous. Understanding a disease doesn't make you a pharmaceutical manufacturer.

The leverage audit is where this investment thesis fractures. The tagline says "coding assistants turned millions into developers — we're doing the same for science." But coding assistants are pure permissionless leverage. You write code, you run it, the compiler tells you if it works. Zero marginal cost per verification. Science doesn't have a compiler. Scientific claims require expert verification — that's the entire reason peer review exists. If every contribution from a non-expert needs a human expert to validate it, you've built a labor-leverage platform with an AI coat of paint. The dossier notes the product is "built for AI agents," which suggests they might envision AI doing the verification. But if AI can reliably verify novel scientific claims, you don't need the community — you have an autonomous AI scientist, and now you're competing with FutureHouse ($70M) and Sakana AI ($379M, $2.65B valuation) with two people and YC funding. Either path leads to a structural disadvantage.

The bull case activates my access democratization pattern hard — I know this pattern, I've lived it. AngelList gave everyone access to angel investing. CoinList opened token launches. ScienceSwarm aims to give 7.9 billion people access to scientific contribution currently restricted to 8 million researchers. If it works, it's one of the most profound democratization plays imaginable. The Polymath Project proved that crowdsourced mathematics can solve hard problems. Foldit proved gamified citizen science can contribute to protein folding. The "why now" is real: AI has crossed the capability threshold where it can compress months of domain onboarding into hours. If these founders can design the right incentive and verification architecture — and their combined AI depth means they can iterate on the AI verification layer faster than almost anyone — the network effects would be extraordinary. Every solved problem attracts more contributors. Every contribution generates training data that makes the AI better. That flywheel, if it spins, compounds indefinitely. And the 9-year co-founder relationship forged at Meta is the kind of battle-tested trust that survives the inevitable pivots.

But I keep returning to the structural question: what is this company's leverage type? A community platform's leverage is its network, which is real but requires continuous investment in community health — moderation, quality control, expert engagement. Stack Overflow built that, but it required years and significant operational effort, and its value eventually became partially captured by AI training (which is ironic given the context here). The underlying AI capabilities — literature synthesis, hypothesis generation — are available through foundation model APIs today and will only become more commoditized. Google has already deployed AI Co-Scientist at 17 DOE National Labs. The company's defensibility would need to come from community density and accumulated contribution data, but with zero traction signals — no users, no revenue, no community metrics — there's no evidence the cold-start problem is being solved or that the contribution data has begun accumulating.

These are exceptional people building in a space I care about deeply. The co-founder relationship is genuine, the career sacrifice is real, and the democratization vision is exactly the kind of structural access expansion I look for. But specific knowledge that isn't productized is just a impressive resume. And a democratization platform without a clear leverage architecture for the verification step is a vision, not yet a company. I'd want to see evidence that non-expert contributions, mediated by AI, can pass expert scrutiny at meaningful rates before writing a check. Show me that the compiler for science actually works.
