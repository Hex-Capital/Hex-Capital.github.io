# AICE -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Grégoire Chomette spent two years assessing asteroid risk at NASA and wrote his MIT AeroAstro thesis on numerical methods, and now — two weeks after pivoting AICE away from energy sensors — he is building swarms of 600m-rated submarine drones for navies. AICE's signature in my portfolio is the inverse of Crusoe: it sits high on the frontier-hardware axis that I love and low on the founder-lived-it axis that I love more, while landing inside the single hottest defense category of the last fourteen months. That is the wrong direction on the two ladders that actually decide my checks.

Anduril (2017) is the load-bearing analogue for AICE on lived experience, and that comparison is where AICE breaks. Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from a parents'-garage workshop before he turned to defense; the biography preceded the product, and the social license to walk onto a DoD test range came with it. AICE's two-founder dossier shows Chomette doing asteroid-risk autonomy at NASA from 2019 to 2021 and Rémi Bouteiller building an embedded sensor platform and an airplay-esp32 repo with 282 stars on GitHub — credible engineering, but neither founder has a single documented hour inside a navy, a shipyard, a sonar lab, or an ITAR submission. Subsea defense is not asteroid autonomy with the y-axis flipped; the problem of mine countermeasure inside a contested EEZ is not something you reach by analogy from low-Earth-orbit debris modeling. AICE discovered submarines in a memo two weeks ago, and the dossier reads exactly like that.

The HQ Trivia (2017) failure mechanism was tactile virality masking a co-founder authority vacuum that detonated under stress — Yusupov and Kroll could not name the tiebreaker when the product hit turbulence. AICE does not present that exact failure mode: Chomette is named CEO and Bouteiller is CTO, so the titles delineate. But AICE also presents none of the documented operating-agreement scaffolding — no founder vesting disclosure, no exit-conflict protocol, no advisor board, no named investor beyond YC — and the pair has shared the cap table across one prior product (the 30%-energy-bill sensor) and one pivot, with no public record of how they resolved the call to abandon the previous company. A two-person team navigating defense procurement, a foreign-manufacturing supply chain in Paris, and a category pivot inside their first YC quarter will face a tiebreaker moment within twelve months, and AICE has not yet shown me they have run that drill.

The non-consensus dimension is where AICE fails the mechanism test most cleanly: my "magically weird" filter compounds over 18 months precisely because the category is unfashionable when the check is written, which buys the founder time to build before the bidding war begins. AICE enters in May 2026 against Anduril at a $60B valuation with Dive-LD already shipping, Helsing in talks for $1.2B at $18B with the SG-1 Fathom doing 90-day surveillance, and Saronic at $9.25B after a $1.75B Series D — three multi-billion-dollar rounds inside fourteen months in adjacent maritime autonomy. That is not Crusoe (~2018) burning gas flares while everyone laughed; that is the room where everyone is already buying. The compounding runs against AICE: each incumbent dollar funds another ITAR specialist, another classified contract, another Dive Technologies-style acquisition that compresses the small-form-factor swarm window AICE claims as its wedge.

AICE earns its strongest score on the frontier-hardware axis, and the counterfactual is what would have to be true to flip it higher: a named DGA or French Navy LOI, an ITAR/EAR registration filed, or a tested prototype at the 600m rating cited on aicepower.com. The dossier shows hardware manufactured in France, swarm coordination as the architectural bet, and a sector — undersea grey-zone conflict — where the moat genuinely compounds with classified contracts and export certifications. That is real. It is the only dimension where AICE belongs in the same conversation as Anduril (2017) at the seed.

Flock Safety (~2018) is my single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability, and AICE does not survive it. I sourced Flock by reading a founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café — the artifact was already in the world, observable, photographable, deployable on a residential pole within 48 hours of the conversation. AICE has no MVP visible to a normal human: no Product Hunt, no press, no app store, no shipped unit, no demonstration video, no named customer pilot. Two weeks past pivot, the company is pre-prototype in a category where the prototype is a pressure-rated underwater vehicle, not a notebook of CAD files.

My 36-month forecast on AICE: either a sub-$20M acqui-hire by a European defense prime (Naval Group, Thales, or Helsing post-Blue Ocean) absorbing the two engineers for swarm-coordination IP, or a second pivot back into adjacent industrial autonomy — AICE does not reach $5M of independent ARR or close a Series A above $40M. The mechanism is straightforward: capital-intensive frontier hardware without a founder who lived the problem requires either a customer who teaches you the domain or a prior-product trajectory you can lean on, and AICE has neither at the moment the well-funded incumbents are sweeping the procurement officers.

The strongest case against my pass is the DeepMind exception — the polymath override that let me write a check into Hassabis despite the elite-PhD pedigree pattern I usually screen against. Chomette is MIT AeroAstro plus NASA autonomy plus energy consulting plus a Google Scholar profile, and "AI is the age of the polymath" is something I have said on the record. Pair that with France launching AI signals analysis for underwater acoustics warfare in May 2024 and the European rearmament wave funding Helsing's $1.2B round, and the bull case is that AICE is the right two polymaths in the right capital city with the right hardware partner, and the consensus around the incumbents is exactly what creates the procurement budget AICE will catch downstream. It is a real case. It loses to the dossier evidence anyway: Hassabis arrived at DeepMind with a decade of chess-prodigy, neuroscience-PhD, and game-design compounding *already inside the problem* of general intelligence; AICE's founders arrived at submarines two weeks ago via an energy-sensor pivot. The polymath exception requires the polymathy to have already touched the domain. The verdict flips if AICE publishes a signed contract or formal partnership with the DGA, the French Navy, or a tier-one European defense prime within six months — a rebuttal, not a hard no.
