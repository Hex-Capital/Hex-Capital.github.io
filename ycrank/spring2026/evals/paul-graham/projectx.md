# ProjectX -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The single most striking fact about ProjectX is not the technology or the market — it's that three out of four original co-founders departed between the 2024 launch and the 2026 YC application. "The relationship between the founders has to be strong. They must genuinely like one another, and work well together. Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." This sock didn't just get pulled apart. It was shredded and replaced with an entirely different sock. The original team was Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham. The YC team is Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder. Only Rounak survived. At pre-seed, before the real stress has even started, this is a deeply uncomfortable signal. It doesn't automatically kill the company — but I need to see extraordinary evidence elsewhere to override it.

There's a "playing house" pattern here that concerns me. Rounak has been through Draper University, IIM Bangalore Launchpad, Stanford ASES, exhibited at GITEX, won Tiger Launch at Princeton, won Eureka at IIT Bombay among 17,000 startups. That is a lot of time on the accelerator and competition circuit for a company that still reports no public revenue. "A characteristic mistake of young founders is to go through the motions of starting a startup. [...] We saw this so often we made up a name for it — playing house." The $2.4M in signed LOIs from July 2024 is the right kind of signal — but it's now almost two years old. If those LOIs had converted, we'd see revenue figures. We don't. The gap between "30,000+ community" and 116 Discord members tells a similar story: the vanity metrics and the substantive metrics aren't tracking together. "They are like sharks. Sharks are too stupid to fool. You can't wave a red flag at a shark and fool it — it's like meat or no meat." Where's the meat?

The strongest bull case is this: sub-3-second cold starts for GPU-accelerated applications streamed in browser tabs is a genuine schlep. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." This is the kind of problem that sat unsolved precisely because it's intimidating — the GPU virtualization, the low-latency streaming protocol, the container orchestration, all running at browser-native speeds. If this team has actually built it, the technical achievement is real. And Rounak's survival through a complete team rebuild, scraping together $204K from a Google Cloud grant and seed round, getting into YC from Kalyani, West Bengal — that's a cockroach signal. "You just won't die." That counts for something. Starting with IIT Bombay as a wedge and expanding from Indian educational institutions to global enterprise is the right shape — "the way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that the technical moat is real, that the new founding team is the right one, and that the LOIs will finally convert post-YC with credibility and capital behind them.

But here's what keeps me from conviction: this idea doesn't look like a bad idea. It looks like an obviously great idea. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." Cloud GPU streaming for everyone? Browser-native professional apps? Of course that's the future. The problem is that everyone else thinks so too — 95 companies tracked in cloud GPU platforms, $20.8B raised collectively. Shadow has $105-169M. CoreWeave has billions. Google acquired Cameyo specifically for app virtualization. When your idea is consensus-good and capital-intensive, you're not in a startup race — you're in an arms race. And $204K in total funding against that competitive set is not "relentlessly resourceful," it's just undercapitalized. "When a startup grows fast, it's usually because the product hits a nerve" — not because of more infrastructure spending. But infrastructure is precisely what this business requires. The CEO's background in interaction design rather than systems engineering adds another layer of concern about technical founder-market fit, though the CTO's Golang/Rust/C++ profile is the right stack for this problem.

What I want to see and don't: evidence that anyone loves this product. Not LOIs, not community counts, not awards — actual usage data, retention, the kind of organic pull where "if you pay a ridiculous amount of attention to your tiny initial set of customers, ideally you'll kick off exponential growth by word of mouth." The IIT Bombay pilot is the closest thing, but there's no reported outcome from it. At the point where you've had a public beta running since June 2024, nearly two years should produce some usage signal if the product genuinely hits a nerve. Its absence is informative.
