# HyperProbe -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Shailendra Singh spent ten years and $2.13M building HyperTest into an API-testing SaaS with Nykaa and Groww logos, and then rebranded it into HyperProbe to apply to YC S26 with a coding-agent runtime-debugging pitch. That single fact is the whole picture for me. HyperProbe is not a hacker who scratched his own itch and shipped a demo to Hacker News; HyperProbe is a professional dev-tools operator who executed a category pivot into an adjacent problem the moment Cursor and Claude Code became the sexy end of the market. The schlep is real -- non-blocking probes across Node/TS/Java/Scala/Kotlin at sub-1% CPU is genuine engineering work, and Rookout took seven years and ~$28M to reach a $33.9M Dynatrace exit doing something narrower. But the founder signature does not match the schlep.

**Coinbase** (2012) is where I want to place HyperProbe on the schlep dimension, and where the analogy breaks. Armstrong went into bitcoin compliance because nobody else in his batch wanted to touch it -- the grind was the moat. HyperProbe's multi-runtime instrumentation with in-memory PII redaction is the same shape of unpleasant work: no software-only YC applicant wants to write JVM agents in Scala and Kotlin runtimes. The dossier's <1% CPU overhead claim (unbenchmarked externally) and the five-runtime target list are exactly the sort of thing that scares the wrapper crowd away. But Armstrong entered a market where the incumbents were paralyzed by regulation. HyperProbe is entering one where Lightrun already raised $70M Series B in April 2025 for this exact primitive and Dynatrace already owns Rookout. The schlep is real; the competitive vacuum around it is not.

The MBA-team AI-wrapper pass I described earlier fails because there is no hacker in the room -- no shipped open source, no GitHub trail, no competitive-programming prize. HyperProbe's founder page is closer to that pattern than I would like. Shailendra Singh is IIT Bombay 2009 Material Science, ex-VP Products at OYO Rooms, ex-VP Sales at Langoor -- product and business roles, not a hacker genealogy. No public GitHub was found. Karan Raina co-founded the prior HyperTest but his role in HyperProbe is not disclosed. When Patrick Collison walked into **Stripe** (2010) he brought Ireland's Young Scientist Lisp prize with him. HyperProbe's founder brings a P&L from a unicorn hotel chain. That is not the same signal.

On the organic-idea axis, HyperProbe compounds badly over 18 months. The mechanism is this: an idea born from watching Cursor adoption charts rather than from personally hitting a wall with a print-statement-and-redeploy loop tends to lose focus the first time a customer asks for an adjacent feature. HyperProbe's positioning is that "coding agents lack live runtime evidence" -- true, but the founder narrative does not include Shailendra himself being the backend engineer who suffered this. The dossier reads like a spotted opportunity, not a lived one. **Dropbox** (2007) was Drew Houston forgetting his USB stick; HyperProbe is Shailendra reading the AI dev-tools tape.

Counterfactual on animal resourcefulness: for HyperProbe to jump a tier here, I would need to see Shailendra do something the market did not expect -- rewriting the HyperTest customer base (Urbancompany, Nykaa, Groww) into HyperProbe design partners inside 90 days, or shipping a public benchmark that Lightrun cannot match, or personally onboarding twenty backend teams in a month. What the dossier shows instead is two testimonials (CheQ Digital Tech Lead, Housing.com SDE) and a Calendly link. Two testimonials from India-based mid-market engineers is not the cereal-box moment; it is the modal YC S26 traction slide.

The **Stripe** (2010) parallel on cofounder configuration cuts against HyperProbe cleanly. The Collisons were brothers with a decade of shared hacking and a prior $5M Auctomatic exit at ages 19 and 17. HyperProbe's YC page names only Shailendra. Team size 8, no named cofounder in the public materials, prior cofounder Karan Raina absent from the HyperProbe listing. This is the solo-founder shape I explicitly avoid per "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010), and none of the mitigations that let me override that avoidance -- deep hacker credentials, prior YC alum vouching, an animal-test interview moment -- are present in the dossier.

36-month forecast: HyperProbe gets acquired by Dynatrace, Datadog, or a mid-tier APM vendor for $20-60M inside 36 months, echoing Rookout's $33.9M Dynatrace outcome from July 2023, or stalls out and shuts down as Lightrun's Series B war chest and Datadog Bits AI SRE close the agent-native gap. The mechanism producing this: repeat B2B dev-tools founder who executes competently in an occupied lane produces a competent modest exit, not a Stripe. The signature -- high schlep, low hacker genealogy, solo-listed founder -- historically resolves to acquihire-adjacent outcomes, and Shailendra's rebrand-for-YC move already reads acquihire-coded to me the way **Kiko** (2005)'s eBay sale did.

The strongest bull case, which I take seriously, is the **Stripe** (2010) known exception: I funded the Collisons even though they had already done YC once with **Auctomatic** (2007), because their prior outcome was real. Shailendra is a repeat founder with paying B2B dev-tools customers at HyperTest -- Nykaa, Groww, Urbancompany. He is not a first-timer flailing. The bull case says: Cursor and Claude Code adoption is a real tailwind, HyperProbe is early to the "agent injects probes" primitive, and a competent repeat dev-tools founder with two paying customers at YC application time is exactly the profile I should back. The problem: Auctomatic sold for $5M when the Collisons were 19 and 17 and Patrick had won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work. HyperTest raised $2.13M over roughly a decade and did not exit -- that is not the Auctomatic pattern, that is the professional-entrepreneur pattern, and rebranding the same company into a new YC batch is closer to the acquihire-coded framing I flag as a red flag. I would flip to Invest if Shailendra publishes a public benchmark showing HyperProbe's runtime overhead beats Rookout and Lightrun on a named workload, or if a hacker cofounder with JVM/V8 instrumentation credentials is added to the YC listing. Neither is present. Pass.
