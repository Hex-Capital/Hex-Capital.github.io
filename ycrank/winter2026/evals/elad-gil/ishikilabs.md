# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Fern is the competitive topology. AI meeting assistants are one of the most consensus-hot categories in enterprise software right now -- Otter.ai is at $100M ARR, Fireflies.ai hit a $1B valuation, Read.ai raised $81M, and Fathom is growing 90x over two years. This is not a market that "appears too small, too boring, or too niche to most investors." It's the opposite: it's a market where every investor can size the TAM, every accelerator has three companies attacking it, and every platform owner -- Microsoft with Copilot for Teams, Google with Gemini in Meet, Zoom with its native AI -- is building the feature internally. When I look at my best investments, they were in markets that triggered dismissive reactions. Nobody dismissed this market. The $2.5-3.7B TAM slide is exactly the kind of static market sizing I warn founders about -- not because it's wrong, but because it's consensus and therefore already priced in.

Fern's differentiation is the shift from passive recording to active participation -- an AI that joins meetings, decides when to speak, and executes tasks in real time. That's a genuine paradigm shift from the transcription-first architecture of Otter.ai and Fireflies.ai. But here's the structural problem: this differentiation sits in a squeeze between two commoditization forces. From below, every incumbent meeting assistant can add real-time features to their existing 20M+ user bases -- they'll iterate faster than Fern can build a customer base. From above, OpenAI's advanced voice mode and Google's Gemini Live are already demonstrating conversational awareness at the foundation model layer. The "social awareness" capability that Fern frames as its moat -- knowing when to speak versus stay silent -- is exactly the kind of capability that foundation model providers are actively building into their core products. When ChatGPT Voice already handles conversational turn-taking, the gap between "general conversational awareness" and "meeting-specific social awareness" narrows rapidly.

The founder-market fit is genuinely exceptional and is the strongest signal in this dossier. Amit and Robert built the exact product at Meta -- a real-time, multimodal AI assistant for Ray-Ban smart glasses that had to understand conversational context, operate at low latency, and decide when to engage. Amit brings deep ML research credentials (PhD, 298 citations, CVPR/NeurIPS publications, LLaMA team), and Robert built the orchestration and low-latency inference systems underneath it. They've worked together at Meta's Reality Labs, so the co-founder relationship has been tested. This is the kind of technical founding team that can actually build the hard thing. If I were evaluating founder quality alone, this would score very high.

But my framework evaluates the market's structural moment before the team, because I've seen great founders repeatedly get taken out by unfavorable market dynamics. The product-to-distribution trajectory concerns me most. Fern is "starting with sales" -- a reasonable wedge -- but what's the second product you push through the meeting integration? Follow-up email drafting, CRM updates, coaching dashboards? Those are features that Salesforce, HubSpot, and Gong can add to their existing distribution channels. I don't see how Fern's meeting presence becomes the kind of distribution channel that Stripe's payment API became. The meeting relationship is thin -- it doesn't create the deep integration and switching costs that enable platform expansion. A meeting bot can be swapped out; a payment infrastructure layer cannot.

The bull case requires believing three things: first, that "social awareness" in meetings is a durable technical moat that won't be absorbed by foundation models or replicated by well-funded incumbents within 18 months; second, that enterprise platforms (Zoom, Teams, Meet) won't restrict third-party AI participants as they build native AI features; and third, that the meeting integration becomes a wedge for something larger -- a real-time enterprise copilot that extends beyond meetings into broader workflow. If all three hold, Fern could capture the transition from passive meeting tools to active AI participants, and the founders' Meta experience gives them a genuine head start on the hardest technical problems. The timing would be analogous to building ahead of a capability curve -- except here the founders themselves are delivering the capability, not waiting for external labs.

My concern is that this requires multiple miracles executed sequentially: build the socially-aware AI (partially de-risked by founder experience), navigate enterprise privacy and trust barriers for an AI that listens to everything (distinct from passive transcription), maintain integration access as platforms build competing features, and compete with incumbents who have 10-100x the user base. That's three to four low-probability events compounding. The technical miracle may be the easiest one, and the founders are well-equipped for it -- but the platform gatekeeping and commoditization dynamics are structural obstacles that technical excellence alone doesn't solve.
