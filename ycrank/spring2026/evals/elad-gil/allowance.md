# Allowance -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Allowance is a correct-catalyst, wrong-wedge pre-seed: Dasmer Singh has read the agent-commerce re-opening accurately (Amazon Bedrock AgentCore went live in March 2026 enabling agent purchases across "more than a billion items on Amazon and Shopify"), but Allowance has positioned itself as a consumer iPhone approval app one layer downstream of where Stripe-for-Agents, Visa Intelligent Commerce, and Crossmint's $23.6M Series A are already building the rails. The signature is a real market with a thin position inside it.

**Anduril** (2022) is the catalyst-ladder rung that matters here. Anduril worked because Ukraine opened a Pentagon software window in a specific 24-month range and the founders were positioned to be the workflow layer the catalyst funneled through. Allowance has the catalyst half of that template -- Visa Intelligent Commerce + Skyfire KYAPay shipped December 2025, Bedrock AgentCore shipped March 2026, "Stripe for Agents" is mid-launch (odaily.news, 2026) -- this is not a steady-state TAM pitch, this is a named 12-month re-opening. Where Allowance diverges from the **Anduril** template is the wedge: Anduril was the software layer Lockheed eventually integrates against, while Allowance is a consumer iPhone wallet that sits parallel to, not inside, the agent-payment primitives Stripe and Visa are wiring into the agents themselves. The catalyst is real; the position is not load-bearing on it.

**Harvey** (2023) is the dimension Allowance fails outright. Harvey's signature was "$3B ? $5B ? $8B in months" (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) -- that's what "just working" looks like in dollars, and the absence of any equivalent disclosure is what I flagged in *AI Market Clarity* as the tell that something is structurally broken in an AI company at this point in the cycle. Allowance's dossier says "early public beta" with no install counts, no MoM, no enterprise logos, no Product Hunt rank, no revenue signal -- and the comparison companies (Crossmint, Skyfire, Ramp) are running "hundreds of real-world agent-initiated transactions" through pilots that Allowance is not named in. Silence on growth in an AI pre-seed in 2026 is itself the signal; Harvey founders disclosed velocity at this stage because velocity was the only thing that mattered.

Dasmer Singh's shipping resume is the most interesting line in the dossier and the reason Allowance doesn't fall to a Pass on founder grounds. He launched the Venmo Debit Card, ran Cash App Families, was Sr PM on Petal Card, and has personally shipped iOS artifacts (EmojiKit, Paste-Emoji on GitHub). That is a decade of shipping the exact product surface Allowance requires -- consumer payment instruments wrapped in iPhone UX. Over 18 months this compounds asymmetrically: a single founder who has personally launched a debit card at Venmo can move through BIN-sponsor negotiations with Lithic or Marqeta faster than a generalist team, and the iOS-engineer-then-PM arc means Dasmer can ship the consumer surface himself while the founding engineer (still unfilled per useallowance.com) handles backend. The shipping-between-meetings rhythm that flagged **Perplexity** (2022) is not visible in the dossier yet, but the shipped-at-scale-before half of that criterion is the strongest single fact in this file.

The index-test is where Allowance breaks against my **Stripe** (2010) template. To flip Index-Distribution one tier up, Allowance would need to be the approval layer every consumer agent transaction routes through by default -- not a discretionary app a user downloads, but a wallet OpenAI, Anthropic, or Apple integrates against natively. Nothing in the dossier shows a signed distribution agreement with Claude or Codex despite their being named on useallowance.com; without that, Allowance is a consumer app fighting for installs in a category where Cash App (Dasmer's former employer), Apple Pay, and Privacy.com/Lithic already own the wallet surface. The counterfactual that flips this is concrete: a default-integration deal with one major agent platform.

Single-miracle test compared to **Decagon** (2023): Decagon assumed one validated capability (LLM accuracy at customer-support tasks) and the rest was GTM execution. Allowance's miracle inventory is messier -- consumers must adopt a separate approval app *and* an issuer-sponsor relationship must be secured (undisclosed per useallowance.com) *and* the standards war between MPP, ACP, AP2, and x402 must not be settled in a way that bypasses third-party wallets. That's closer to one-and-a-half miracles than three, which is why Allowance is not a "science project" pass -- but it is not the clean single-miracle structure that made the **Decagon** check easy.

My 36-month forecast: Allowance does not become a standalone breakout. Most likely outcome is an acqui-hire by Lithic, Ramp, or Cash App in the $5M-$25M range for Dasmer and whatever issuer relationships Allowance has assembled by then; second-most-likely is a pivot to a B2B developer API to compete directly with Crossmint, at which point Allowance is fighting Ribbit-backed capital from behind. The mechanism producing this is the gap between catalyst (real) and position (downstream of platform-controlled rails) -- the same gap that made **Lyft** Series C a winner-take-all-but-not-by-you pass for me.

The bull case I have to steel-man: Dasmer literally launched the Venmo Debit Card, and consumer payment wallets historically *do* emerge as standalone apps that platforms refuse to build (Venmo, Cash App, Privacy.com all routed around incumbent issuer UX). Stripe sells to developers, Visa sells to merchants -- neither will ship the consumer-side approval flow because it's not their customer, which leaves a structural gap exactly where Allowance is standing. That argument stings because it's the **Stripe** (2010) shape -- payments looked done, the gap was in the developer experience nobody owned. The reason I still pass: Stripe in 2010 had revenue velocity within months of launch; Allowance has "early public beta" and no disclosed numbers, and in this cycle silence is the signal. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed default-integration with Claude, Codex, or an Apple-level platform partner *and* MoM transaction growth disclosed in the next 90 days. Absent both, Allowance is a founder I'd want to back on his next company, not this one.
