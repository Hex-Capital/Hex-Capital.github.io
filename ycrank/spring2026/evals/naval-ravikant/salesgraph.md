# Salesgraph -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Gong at $300M+ ARR and Clari-Salesloft post-merger with $520M raised are not sleeping. Salesgraph is building an aggregation layer on top of their APIs — and on top of ZoomInfo, Clearbit, 6sense, Salesforce, HubSpot, Slack, and LinkedIn. That's 10+ third-party integrations maintained by two people. I need to be direct: aggregating other companies' leverage is not creating leverage. Every API change, rate limit adjustment, or access restriction from any of those providers degrades Salesgraph's core product. The dossier states it plainly — "any well-resourced sales tech company with API access to the same tools could replicate the technical approach." When I look at my highest-conviction investments, the founder's specific knowledge created something that didn't exist before. Salesgraph stitches together things that already exist. The stitching has value. But stitching is labor, not leverage.

Ricardo Nunez and Ruhan Ponnada were among the first ten employees at Mintlify, where they co-built a business case generator that reportedly helped close enterprise deals at higher ACV. That's the specific knowledge claim. I take it seriously — they lived inside the problem, they built a prototype that worked, they left to productize it. But Mintlify is a developer documentation platform, not an enterprise sales organization. Building an internal sales tool at a dev tools startup gives you operational awareness of the problem. It does not give you the kind of non-replicable expertise that takes a competitor years to accumulate. Compare Salesgraph's founders to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity — years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact technical foundations that power AI search. The knowledge depth differential is an order of magnitude. Salesgraph's founders identified a workflow pain point and built a tool. That's competent product instinct. It is not specific knowledge productized.

Salesgraph's leverage architecture concerns me most. The product synthesizes CRM records, call transcripts, enrichment data, and Slack threads into pre-call briefs and three-page business cases. The underlying capabilities — API data aggregation, LLM-based summarization, document generation — are generally available. This maps uncomfortably close to the AI wrapper archetype I consistently pass on: founders integrating foundation model APIs with a clean workflow, showing real user value, but with zero proprietary technology underneath. Salesgraph is more sophisticated than a pure wrapper — the 10+ integration depth and business case generation involve genuine engineering. But sophistication of execution is not a moat. Gong could ship a "deal context" feature leveraging its own call data plus Salesforce integration in a quarter. The structural argument that Gong cannot integrate Clari's data because they compete has some merit — a neutral aggregation layer exploits a principal-agent gap between incumbents. But Clari already merged with Salesloft in December 2025. Consolidation is collapsing exactly the structural gap Salesgraph needs to exist.

Ruhan Ponnada holds a BS in Computer Science from Georgia Tech and served as PM at Microsoft Mesh before joining Mintlify as Founding Solutions Engineer. Ricardo Nunez's GitHub shows 28 public repos with modest engagement — 53 stars on a React Server Components resource. Both are technical, both can build. The principal authenticity signal is mixed: they built the prototype at Mintlify before founding Salesgraph, which shows real conviction around the problem. But Ruhan previously built Mabbu, an auto-diagramming tool for developers — his gravity is toward developer tooling, not sales obsession. I look for founders whose identity is fused with the problem, who would work on this without venture funding. Salesgraph's founders seem drawn to the opportunity more than consumed by the domain. Two technical co-founders shipping product without hiring is the right team structure — my framework rewards that. But maintaining 10+ API integrations scales linearly with tool count, not logarithmically. The two-person team is lean by design but constrained by the architecture they've chosen.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Salesgraph occupies a genuinely unserved gap — no incumbent synthesizes context across the full sales stack into buyer-ready business cases. Gong owns call analytics, Clari owns forecasting, Aligned owns deal rooms, but none aggregates across all three plus enrichment plus CRM plus communications. The Mintlify prototype reportedly contributed to higher ACV enterprise closes — that's real commercial validation, not a demo. The timing is right: LLMs now make cross-source synthesis feasible where prior NLP could not, and Gartner projects 95% of seller research workflows will begin with AI by 2027. Two technical YC-backed founders attacking a $4.85B sales intelligence market with a working prototype is not nothing. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Salesgraph's cross-tool synthesis produces measurably better deal outcomes — win rate improvements, ACV increases, cycle compression — that single-vendor tools cannot match. If the aggregation layer generates unique intelligence that no individual tool possesses, the leverage shifts from rented to owned. Show me five enterprise customers whose close rates improved 20%+ and I reconsider.

Salesgraph scores below my threshold because the two dimensions I weight most heavily — specific knowledge and permissionless leverage — both point toward fragility. The founders' Mintlify experience is relevant but transferable; any competent engineer who has sold B2B software could identify and build this. The leverage is structurally rented from the platforms Salesgraph integrates with, and the consolidation wave in sales tech (Clari-Salesloft merger, Gong's expansion) is actively narrowing the gap Salesgraph occupies. Salesgraph is not a bad company. It solves a real problem with a working prototype and a lean team. But it fails the test I apply to every investment: if these founders disappeared, would the company's core insight survive? Yes — because the insight is obvious to anyone who has run an enterprise sales process, and the technology to execute it is available to everyone.
