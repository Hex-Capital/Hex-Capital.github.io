# Zolvo -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing about commercial loan servicing AI right now: everybody wants in. Salient raises $70M and hits $25M ARR with zero churn in two years. Eighty-three percent of lenders are planning to increase their GenAI budgets in 2026. nCino is public at half a billion in revenue. LoanPro has $100M. When I look at a space and the room is already packed with well-capitalized players racing toward the same thesis, I know I'm not looking at something that makes investors shift in their chairs — I'm looking at consensus. And consensus is where I lose interest, because the best ideas I've ever funded were the ones that made people uncomfortable, not the ones that made them nod along.

Isabela Rodriguez relocated to San Francisco with three thousand dollars. That detail caught my eye because I know what it means to arrive somewhere with nothing and will yourself into existence. She's twenty-four, already closed a million dollars in revenue at Domu, landed Chubb as a client. Tony Montes is twenty-two with publications at ACL and EMNLP and built voice AI infrastructure handling fifty to a hundred thousand daily calls. These are not lightweight founders — they ship, they sell, they've been through YC once already and have their Domu founders backing them with their own money. That's all real. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is: "We were successful at a fintech company and saw that commercial lenders were still using spreadsheets." That's pattern recognition from a professional vantage point. It's not the same as a founder who lived inside a broken system and couldn't walk away from it. The problem didn't find them through biography; they found the problem through work. There's nothing wrong with that, but it's a different kind of signal than the one I weight most heavily.

The deeper issue for my framework is what Zolvo is actually disrupting. When I backed Affirm, the invisible system was credit card opacity — hidden fees and compounding interest that extracted value from consumers who didn't understand the math. When I backed Ownwell, it was the property tax system that punished homeowners who couldn't navigate byzantine appeals processes. Those companies made something transparent for people who had no power. Zolvo makes loan servicing workflows more efficient for commercial lenders managing ten to six hundred million in assets. The beneficiary is a financial institution's back office, not a borrower trapped in an opaque system. I'm not saying operational efficiency isn't valuable — it clearly is, and lenders will pay for it. But it doesn't trigger the part of my brain that lights up when I see a hidden tax being exposed or a gatekeeper being disintermediated. This is enterprise process automation dressed in fintech clothing.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If these founders execute well — and their Domu track record suggests they can — they could own the commercial lending servicing niche before Salient expands from consumer and auto loans. Commercial lending has genuinely different workflows, compliance requirements, and relationship dynamics than consumer lending. Mid-market lenders between ten and six hundred million in AUM are underserved by the big platforms that focus on top-tier banks. If Zolvo embeds deeply into those workflows and accumulates proprietary data on commercial servicing patterns, switching costs could create a real moat over time. Six thousand dollars in revenue within four weeks and a billion in pipeline AUM shows they can get meetings and close early deals. The market timing with GenAI adoption in lending is undeniable. If everything breaks right, this could be a strong vertical SaaS outcome — maybe a hundred million dollar company. But a hundred million dollar company is not what I'm looking for. I'm looking for something that reshapes a category or reveals a structure nobody else can see.

The commoditization risk is what keeps me on the sidelines. They're building on OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude — commercially available models that every competitor has access to. The dossier itself notes the technical barrier is moderate and defensibility would need to come from workflow integration and data accumulation. That's true, but Salient is two years ahead with seventy million dollars and has already proven the "AI replaces loan servicing labor" thesis. The moat Zolvo needs to build — deep workflow integration, proprietary data, regulatory compliance certifications — is the same moat every competitor is trying to build, and several have a significant head start and more capital to do it with. I've been burned by companies that relied on timing and execution speed without structural defensibility. This space has the feel of a race where the starting gun already fired and Zolvo is lacing up.

I wish them well — genuinely. Rodriguez and Montes are resourceful, technically capable, and clearly driven. In a different framework they might score highly. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. There's no biographical inevitability, no contrarian discomfort, no invisible system being revealed to the powerless, and no magically weird surface hiding infrastructure underneath. It's a competent team entering a hot market at speed. That can work. It's just not how I invest.
