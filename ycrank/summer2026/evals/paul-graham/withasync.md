# Async -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Async is a schlep dressed as SaaS, built by two founders who never lived the schlep -- Ben Smith and Connor Park are shipping "audit ? scope ? deploy ? maintain" custom agent engagements into law, healthcare, and real estate firms without a single line in either bio suggesting they've sat inside one. That pairing -- highest on Schlep Zone, lowest on Organic Founder Pain -- is Async's signature in my portfolio, and it is the exact inverse of the pattern I keep re-underwriting.

On the schlep axis, **Coinbase** (2012) is the anchor: Armstrong took bitcoin compliance work no other S12 applicant would touch, and the grind became the moat. Async has picked a real schlep -- per-firm custom builds against Word, Outlook, SharePoint, and CRMs, across three regulated verticals, with SOC 2 Type I already stapled on -- and the healthcare branch drags HIPAA scope behind it that Harvey and Spellbook route around. The mechanism that made **Coinbase** (2012) work was Armstrong wanting to live inside that grind for a decade; Async's dossier gives me the grind but not the appetite, and a two-person team spanning law/healthcare/real-estate is going to feel the audit-and-maintain cost per firm before the compounding kicks in. This is genuine "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) territory, but only if the founders find it beautiful, which the dossier does not tell me.

The **Dropbox** (2007) failure mechanism -- founders who did NOT lose a USB stick at MIT don't build Dropbox -- runs directly through Async's problem. Ben Smith dropped out of Michigan at 20 and worked at Merit; Connor Park studied CS at Michigan; neither has documented time inside a law firm, a clinic, or a brokerage. Compare Async to my Pass Example almost line-for-line: no founder has personally suffered the problem. The pivot trail confirms it -- nonprofit fundraising agents ? Skope outcome-based billing ? Skope AI Agents for Law Firms ? Async AI Agents for Service Businesses, all inside twelve months. That is not "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) organic discovery; that is scanning verticals for one that will hold still. Adriena Clifton at Clifton Law PLLC is one boutique testimonial, not a founder's own pain.

Animal resourcefulness -- the 35-point axis -- compounds or fails over the next 18 months on whether Ben Smith and Connor Park treat these pivots as batting practice or as flinching. Three product changes inside YC S25 could read like Armstrong hand-building his wallet after **Coinbase** (2012) rejection: relentless iteration until something bites. It could also read as the opposite -- a team that runs when the current vertical gets hard, which is what "Async" instead of "Skope Law" looks like from outside. Async gives me one testimonial and 55 Launch HN points on a since-abandoned Skope pitch. No cereal-box stunt, no side-channel revenue, no unscalable manual operation named in the dossier. Animals leave tracks; I'm looking at faint ones.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual: to move Async from a Pass tier to an Invest tier on this axis alone, Connor Park's GitHub would need to look like Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp win before **Stripe** (2010), or Aaron Swartz's RSS co-authorship before **Reddit** (2005). What Async actually has is @connorpark24 at 18 followers, 11 repos, 72 total stars, top-pinned repo being his personal site. That is a competent CS grad, not a hacker with a tribal marker. Ben Smith's GitHub is not found at all. The dossier is silent where I most want noise.

Async's co-founder configuration -- **Reddit** (2005) is the single-anchor parallel here -- has the Michigan-shared-school pattern that Huffman and Ohanian had at UVA, but the dossier does not name a multi-year building relationship pre-Skope, and Connor lists NYC while the company sits in San Francisco. It's a pair, which clears the solo-founder red flag, and they've now survived three pivots together, which is real data. I stay near the default; nothing here to lean on, nothing to flinch from.

My 36-month forecast on Async: another pivot or wound down by mid-2028, most likely acquihired into a legal-AI consultancy or a mid-tier Harvey/Spellbook competitor for under $10M, with an outside case at $1?3M ARR as a boutique AI-services shop billing hourly deployments. The mechanism is the signature: high schlep + low organic fit is where founders discover mid-deployment that they don't love the work, and the discovery arrives right when Harvey ($11B post-money) and Legora ($5.55B) ship the same Word add-in for a fraction of the audit cost.

The strongest case against my pass is the **Twitch** (2007) exception: Kan and Shear pivoted hard off **Kiko** (2005), and I re-funded them because the pivots WERE the animal signal. Async has done three pivots inside a single batch, which is either the same pattern in fast-forward or its opposite. The reason it fails as a **Twitch** (2007) analogy is that Kan and Shear failed one startup completely and came back with a different thesis; Ben Smith and Connor Park are re-labeling inside one YC cycle without abandoning the underlying "AI agents for [vertical]" wrapper. That is search, not conviction. The condition that flips my verdict: if Async posts a second and third named customer -- not testimonial, signed and paying, in a vertical where one of the founders actually spent operator time -- inside the next six months, I re-read this dossier. Until then, pass.
