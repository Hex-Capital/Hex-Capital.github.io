# Insurf -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Insurf is what my **Pass Example** looks like when it puts on a healthcare costume -- "Inveto reads the chart from the EHR and turns a denial into a source-cited, physician-attested appeal packet in minutes" is a 20-30% efficiency play against a $35B administrative spend that nobody is collapsing, only recovering inside. Highest dimension is consequence (the prior-auth dollar pool is at least real and named in Health Affairs Scholar 2024); lowest is technical/capital risk (a two-person team in San Francisco, no disclosed proprietary stack, racing five named competitors already shipping). That pairing is the signature: Insurf is a consensus AI-appeals entrant dressed in moat-language, not a science bet, and that resolves before I read the founder line.

Start with the **OpenAI** (2019) rung on consequence and the analogy collapses immediately. The $50M I wrote into OpenAI was a bet that the success-state -- machine intelligence at or beyond human range -- eliminates a category of cognition rather than improving its throughput. Insurf's stated success-state is "recovering revenue practices lose today," explicitly framed on insurf.io as restoring a margin that already exists inside the prior-auth regime. The civilizational variable does not move when Insurf wins; the same $35B pool simply changes hands from payer-denial-leverage to provider-appeal-leverage, possibly compressed by what Stateline (Nov 2025) already documents as payer counter-AI. The mid-rung **DoorDash** (2013) at least restructured how consumer goods physically reach households; Insurf restructures who wins a paperwork arbitrage, and the **Impossible Foods** (2011) pattern of displacing animal protein production is the consequence shape Insurf would need to imitate and does not. The dossier names no incumbent regime that disappears -- prior auth itself remains intact, only its appeal-rate inflects.

Start with the **Pass Example** -- "we use GPT-4 to summarize sales calls and integrate with HubSpot" -- and trace the failure mechanism into Insurf. That pattern fails on technical/capital risk because the underlying capability is commodity LLM ingestion plus workflow plumbing, which means any Tier-1 generalist can fund it and five other founders are already shipping it. The Stateline (Nov 2025) and PYMNTS (2026) coverage of Counterforce Health (free, 70% reported success), Claimable ($10M raised, Mark Cuban-backed, ~75% reversal), Fight Health Insurance, PenguinAI, and Aegis Health is the empirical proof that the appeal-generation step is non-miraculous. Insurf's stated differentiation -- a "coverage-decision graph" -- is unmeasured per the dossier's own [Inferred] tag, with no disclosed pilot count, no claim volume, no graph density. **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) repelled consensus VCs because the tokamak physics required $115M to even attempt; Insurf repels nobody, and the absence of repulsion is the diagnosis.

Chang Lu and Bryan Chung -- LinkedIn URLs listed on the YC page, both returning status 999, no GitHub, no prior exits, no advisors disclosed, no domain credentials retrievable. The dossier's own founder-market-fit line reads "cannot be assessed from available data." Mechanism analysis on this dimension over the next 18 months: in a five-competitor field where Claimable already has $10M and Tennr has $162M raised at a $605M valuation, the founder credential gap compounds against Insurf because GTM into specialty practices runs through trust signals -- HIPAA BAA posture, physician-attestation workflow accuracy, named clinical advisors -- and Insurf has zero of these in public sources. My TED Talk skepticism is about experts who extrapolate the past; here I do not even have an expert to dismiss, which is worse, because the **Physical Intelligence** (2024) Levine/Finn pattern requires a public intellectual identity that the dossier confirms is absent.

Counterfactual on learning rate: for Insurf's 7/15 default to flip to a 12+, the dossier would need a documented prior pivot -- a previous company sold by Chang Lu or Bryan Chung, a public thesis update from a prior attempt at RCM automation, a "we were wrong about X" moment of the kind that justified my $100M Series C into **Slash** (2024) after the Kanye pivot story. None exists in the Insurf dossier; founder histories are entirely opaque, and "no public data found" appears five times across the founder section. That absence is not stage-default -- two-person YC Summer 2026 teams routinely have at least a Twitter trail or a GitHub. The silence reads as low public footprint, not as in-progress narrative.

Single-anchor parallel on capital profile: against **Genesis AI** (2025), where Eclipse and I co-led $105M because the foundation-model-for-robots roadmap needed every dollar to fund GPU training and simulator infrastructure. Insurf's roadmap on insurf.io is a 90-day Access Recovery pilot booked through a Calendly link -- contingency-fee RCM economics that reach $1M ARR on perhaps $3-5M of capital. I am over-resourced for Insurf by an order of magnitude, which is the precise pattern that disqualifies a deal from my check size, not qualifies it. A consensus generalist healthcare fund -- Bessemer, Andreessen's Bio+Health -- is the natural buyer of this allocation, not me.

Forecast, 36 months: Insurf reaches under $2M ARR, fails to differentiate from Claimable's $10M-funded patient-side product and Tennr's $605M provider-document franchise, and is either quietly wound down or acquihired by an RCM incumbent (Waystar, R1, or Tennr itself) for a sub-$15M outcome that returns roughly 1-2x to YC and zero meaningful capital to a hypothetical Khosla check. The mechanism producing this is the one I scored highest and lowest on simultaneously -- a real $35B pool attracts many entrants, and Insurf has no science miracle, no founder signal, and no disclosed data asset with which to win the resulting commoditization race.

Strongest opposing case rides the **Slash** (2024) Known Exception: YC-backed, consequence magnitude below my normal bar, justified by founder learning rate and documented pivot. The bull would argue Insurf could pivot mid-batch -- Surely, the plan-cost-pricing second product, is a more interesting wedge into broker/employer purchasing than Inveto is into appeals, and the coverage-decision graph could in principle compound into a Glean-shaped information-asymmetry play on payer behavior. The bear evidence wins: Slash had a publicly told pivot story before my Series C; Insurf has no pivot history because it has no history, two founders with status-999 LinkedIns and no GitHub do not get the **Glean** override that ex-Google-search Arvind Jain earned, and Surely is contingent on Inveto generating claim-resolution volume that the dossier explicitly notes is undemonstrated. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Insurf publishes within twelve months a coverage-decision graph of >250,000 resolved claims with measurable predictive lift on payer plan pricing, plus a named clinical co-founder or medical advisor with HIPAA-compliant attestation workflow -- absent that, this is the 30-second pass.
