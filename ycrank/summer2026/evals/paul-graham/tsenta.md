# Tsenta -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta personally applied to 1,500 jobs each as international students before building Tsenta, and that is the only fact in the dossier that matters for my framework. Tsenta is the rare consumer tool where the organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) reads almost too cleanly -- two people who lived inside the problem, built it for themselves, shipped a Chrome extension, an Android app, an iMessage bot, and an MCP integration before pitching -- but the hacker-genealogy column is empty (no public GitHub for either founder) and the category already contains Jobright at $7.7M with 520K users. The signature is: a real organic-pain bet whose founders' technical lineage is invisible and whose schlep moat is contested by funded incumbents.

Dropbox in 2007 is the right comparison for Tsenta's strongest dimension. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT and built Dropbox for himself; Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta applied to 1,500 jobs apiece on F-1 status and built Tsenta for themselves. The mechanism is identical -- founders who hit the problem in their own workflow have a permanent taste advantage over anyone arriving via competitive analysis. Where Tsenta diverges from Dropbox is that Houston's pain ("I lost my files") was universal and unowned, while Tsenta's pain ("ATS forms are tedious") is currently being attacked by Simplify ($4.35M, Craft Ventures), Jobright ($7.7M, claims 520K users), Sonara, LazyApply, and JobCopilot. The organic-idea filter triggers; the "few others realize are worth doing" half of the same filter does not.

The MBA AI-wrapper team I described in my pass example is the rung Tsenta's hacker-genealogy column most resembles -- not because the founders are MBAs (they are not, they are Rose-Hulman CS), but because the dossier returns "no public repos found" for both Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta and no Twitter for either. Hackers leave GitHub trails; the absence of one in 2026 for two CS seniors building an LLM-driven ATS-integration product is itself the signal. I've over-indexed on hacker tribal markers since Viaweb, and Tsenta is exactly the kind of company where the missing markers would normally make me wait one batch to see whether the technical work holds up under load -- 353 Chrome installs (June 2026) is not enough surface to know.

On schlep blindness, Tsenta's 19-ATS coverage claim across Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, and Ashby is real grind work -- file uploads, free-text screening questions, anti-bot detection, per-platform schema drift -- and that is the kind of unpleasant labor Coinbase's compliance moat compounded out of. The mechanism over 18 months is: each new ATS shipped is another month other entrants must spend to match, and proprietary success-rate data on field-matching accumulates. The problem is that this schlep is contested rather than avoided -- Jobright is already running it with seven times the capital, which inverts the "Schlep Blindness" thesis (January 2012). When the schlep has five funded competitors, it isn't a moat, it's a feature race.

Counterfactually, Tsenta's animal-resourcefulness score would flip a tier upward if the dossier surfaced a stunt comparable to Airbnb's cereal boxes -- $40 Obama O's at $4 cost -- rather than the diligent-but-expected breadth of shipping Chrome + Android + iMessage + MCP. The 3,000 personal applications across the two founders is closer to a workflow audit than to a stunt; it proves the pain is real, not that Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta will improvise revenue when blocked. Tsenta's 353 installs and pricing tiers from $19/mo to $99/mo are competent, not feral, and the dossier surfaces no moment where the founders did something I would not have predicted.

Reddit's Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates at UVA; Tsenta's Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta are Rose-Hulman classmates who built from their dorm during summer internships. That is the right kind of long-relationship pair I want -- not solo, not hackathon-paired strangers, not three-plus founders with no working history. The cofounder column is the one column where Tsenta is simply correct against the "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) checklist.

My 36-month forecast for Tsenta is plateau at $300K-$1M ARR followed by acquisition by an incumbent careers platform (Indeed, LinkedIn, ZipRecruiter) or by Jobright as a tuck-in, OR a forced pivot into a B2B white-label "apply API" for university career centers once consumer LTV cannot service the ATS-engineering treadmill. The mechanism producing that forecast is the same mechanism that capped Sonara at ~500 users in 2023 -- consumer auto-apply has a ceiling because the unit economics of $19/mo against an integration surface that breaks weekly across 19 platforms forces either premium pricing (which Jobright undercuts) or B2B repositioning (which is a different company).

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that Tsenta is a Wufoo-style "do things that don't scale" consumer-tool play, where Agnay Srivastava and Pulkit Gupta's organic-pain origin compounds into a default career-services API the way the Collisons' commerce hacking compounded into Stripe -- and that I am pattern-matching on a crowded-category surface that "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) explicitly warns me against. The Airbnb known exception applies here: I rejected that idea inside my head too, and the founders overrode me. What stings about the bull case is that the founders did exactly what the organic-idea filter asks for, and I am leaning away anyway. The dossier evidence that wins the argument is the Jobright comparison: 520K users on $7.7M of Series A capital means the customer-acquisition surface is already saturating against a better-funded entrant, while Tsenta is at 353 Chrome installs. The verdict flips to Invest if Tsenta shows me a stunt-grade pre-funding revenue moment -- not the $500K YC check, but founder hustle of the cereal-box class -- or if the 353 installs cross 25,000 paid users within a year, which would mean the founders found a distribution improvisation I cannot currently see.
