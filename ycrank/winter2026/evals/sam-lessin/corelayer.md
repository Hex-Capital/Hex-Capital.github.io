# Corelayer -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Corelayer is the platonic ideal of a company I pass on. Two credible Goldman Sachs data infrastructure engineers building an AI-powered debugging tool for production systems -- and every single well-funded incumbent in observability and incident management is building the exact same thing simultaneously. incident.io raised $62M in April 2025 explicitly to "build AI agents that resolve incidents." Datadog sits at a $30B+ market cap with enormous R&D budgets and existing customer relationships with every engineering team that Corelayer wants to sell to. PagerDuty owns the on-call workflow. When the idea you're pursuing is the one that every competitor with distribution is already racing toward, you're not investing in non-consensus -- you're buying consensus at seed-stage prices and hoping execution alone produces venture returns. It almost never does.

The founders pass the "sane" half of my filter. Mitch Radhuber and Shipra Jha built data infrastructure together at Goldman Sachs, debugging systems processing hundreds of billions of rows daily. That's exactly the domain experience you want for this problem. Princeton astrophysics plus Oracle cloud infrastructure rounds out the technical credibility nicely. The pre-existing co-founder relationship at Goldman is real -- they're not speed-dating co-founders who met at a hackathon. But sane-person-sane-idea is my least interesting configuration. The returns in venture come from the "insane" premium -- the gap between what you see and what the market dismisses. There is no dismissal gap here. Every VC in the market understands that AI debugging is valuable. The question is whether a two-person pre-seed startup captures that value or whether Datadog ships a feature.

Here's where my framework is most damning: the business fails the "cherry on top" test in the most ironic way possible. The AI IS the product. Strip away the AI layer and there is literally nothing left -- no physical operations, no proprietary data asset, no protocol, no installed infrastructure. This is precisely the category I've been publicly arguing is uninvestable. As foundation models improve, the debugging capability that Corelayer offers becomes easier for every incumbent to replicate as a feature. The better AI gets, the more commoditized Corelayer's core value proposition becomes. Contrast this with Craftwork, where AI optimizes dispatch and scheduling but the moat is painting crews and operational execution that no software can replace. Corelayer has no painting crews. It has software, and software moats are dissolving.

The strongest bull case centers on the regulated-industry wedge: financial services firms genuinely cannot send production data to cloud-hosted AI tools, and Corelayer's confidential compute and on-prem deployment options address a real compliance constraint. Datadog and PagerDuty are architecturally built as cloud SaaS -- restructuring for on-prem deployment runs against their entire margin structure and business model. If Corelayer establishes itself as the trusted AI debugging layer inside Goldman, JPMorgan, and their peers, the compliance overhead and security review process creates genuine switching costs. The SOC 2 Type I certification, while table stakes in isolation, signals that the founders understand enterprise procurement reality. This is the one angle that keeps this from being a reflexive pass -- the regulated-industry deployment constraint is closer to a physical-world barrier than a software moat. But even this bull case has a ceiling problem: "AI debugging for regulated financial services" is a niche within a niche, and the narrative doesn't expand into anything approaching an infinity story. You're capped at being a very good tool for a specific buyer in a specific industry, which is a fine business but not venture-scale.

The protocol-layer test seals the verdict. Corelayer builds on top of everyone else's infrastructure -- AWS, GCP, Snowflake, Airflow, dbt, Datadog. It creates no new primitive that other companies integrate with. It doesn't establish a standard or a layer that generates compounding network effects. Each customer deployment is independent. Compare this to Venmo, which created a new payment primitive that became infrastructure for how a generation moves money, or Helium, which established a decentralized wireless protocol. Corelayer is an application on existing platforms, which means its upside is bounded by the value those platforms choose to leave on the table -- and they're actively choosing to capture it themselves.

I like these founders, and in a different market structure I might feel differently. But the structural logic here is broken for my framework: a consensus idea, an AI-native value proposition that commoditizes as AI improves, application-layer positioning with no protocol dynamics, and well-funded incumbents converging on the same capability from positions of existing distribution. This is a feature looking for an acquisition, not a company building toward escape velocity.
