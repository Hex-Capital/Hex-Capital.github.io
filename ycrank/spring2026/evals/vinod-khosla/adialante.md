# Adialante -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

MRI has been fundamentally the same machine for forty years. Forty years. The entire computing stack was reinvented three times in that period, but the dominant cancer-detection imaging modality still requires a $2M superconducting magnet, a shielded room, a specialized operator, and a building to put it in. Over a million men per year need prostate MRI before biopsy, and almost no urology clinic can offer it. That's not a market inefficiency — that's a broken system holding a basic human need hostage behind infrastructure costs. If Adialante's FREE technology works — eliminating gradient coils entirely, shrinking the system to 1/10th the size and cost — you don't improve MRI access by 20%. You create MRI access where none existed. The consequence isn't a better radiology department; it's cancer caught in stage I instead of stage III for millions of people who currently have no path to an MRI scanner. That consequence is worth betting on.

The physics here is real and non-incremental. Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes isn't a marketing name for a slightly cheaper magnet — it's a fundamentally different encoding scheme that eliminates an entire subsystem (B0 gradient coils) that accounts for roughly a third of conventional MRI hardware, cost, and power consumption. Published in peer-reviewed journals. Patent-pending. And they have Sai Abitha Srinivas on the team — the ISMRM I.I. Rabi Young Investigator Award winner from Vanderbilt, which is the most prestigious recognition for early-career MRI researchers in the world. When I see that caliber of talent choosing to join a four-person startup instead of running a lab, I take it as a signal about the technology's validity that no due diligence report can replicate. This isn't a wrapper around someone else's API. This is novel physics with a path to 10x cost disruption — exactly the kind of discontinuity I look for.

What I find most compelling is the business model insight layered on top of the physics. A cheaper MRI machine is interesting. A per-scan mobile service with zero capital expenditure for clinics is a different category entirely. Siemens and GE sell $1-3M systems with lucrative service contracts — they are structurally incapable of offering a $200-per-scan mobile unit without destroying their core business. This is textbook innovator's dilemma. Promaxo has 69 systems installed, which sounds impressive until you realize there are over 10,000 urology practices in the U.S. alone that will never spend $500K+ on in-office MRI hardware. The per-scan mobile model doesn't compete with Promaxo for the same 69 customers — it opens the other 9,900. That's the Rocket Lab insight applied to healthcare: you're not fighting for share of existing launches, you're enabling a new class of customer that couldn't previously participate.

The concerns are real and I won't pretend otherwise. Promaxo is FDA-cleared and shipping. Hyperfine is public with $13.6M in revenue. Adialante has no revenue, no deployed units, no FDA submission. The founding team is entirely academic — PhDs from UMN's CMRR who invented this technology in a university lab. No visible startup experience, no commercial track record, no evidence of operational capability for running a mobile fleet. The mobile imaging model requires fleet management, multi-state regulatory compliance, staffing, and logistics that are completely orthogonal to MRI physics research. I've funded plenty of academic spinouts that built extraordinary technology but couldn't build a company around it. That's the risk here.

But here's what would have to be true for this to be exceptional: the FREE technology delivers on the 10x cost and size reduction, FDA clearance comes through a 510(k) predicate pathway in 18-24 months rather than a de novo 3-5 year slog, and the team hires an operator with mobile healthcare experience as their first non-technical executive. If those three things happen, this company doesn't compete with Promaxo — it obsoletes Promaxo's model entirely. A mobile MRI service at 1/10th the cost becomes the default screening pathway for prostate cancer, and then extends to breast, liver, and every other cancer where early MRI detection saves lives. The TAM isn't $2.4B in prostate imaging — it's the entire unserved population that should be getting screened but can't. That's the consequence explosion I'm evaluating. Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago and frames this as bringing MRI to underserved communities — that's not a pitch deck talking point, that's personal conviction driving a company toward radical affordability. Making cancer screening accessible to people who currently can't afford it is exactly the "making the expensive free" pattern that drives my highest-conviction bets.

I'd write a small check — this is a $1M option on whether the physics translates to a commercial product and whether this team can learn to build a company as fast as they learned to build a scanner. The technology is genuinely novel, the consequence magnitude is significant, and the affordability thesis is as clean as anything I've seen in healthcare diagnostics. The gap is execution risk and the FDA timeline, both of which are addressable with the right hires and a bit of luck. I'm not calculating IRR. I'm asking: does a world where MRI costs $200 per scan and arrives at your clinic in a van look materially different from today? It does.
