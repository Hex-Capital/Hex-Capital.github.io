# Sazabi -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Sherwood Callaway started Brex's observability team as employee number seventy. That's not a line on a résumé — it's a diagnostic. When someone builds the monitoring infrastructure at a hyper-growth fintech from scratch, they accumulate scar tissue about what's broken in observability that no amount of customer interviews can replicate. Sazabi's core thesis — that logs are the only telemetry signal you actually need, and that metrics and traces are just derived views of log events — is the kind of claim that could only come from someone who spent years wiring up all three pillars and realized two of them were redundant. Callaway told SiliconANGLE that "logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events and traces are basically correlated events." That's not market positioning. That's a technical insight born from the specific frustration of maintaining three separate instrumentation pipelines at Brex and discovering they were three views of the same underlying data. The parallel I keep coming back to is Stripe: Patrick Collison experienced the pain of integrating payment systems firsthand, then built the radically simplified version that incumbents couldn't copy because their business models depended on the complexity. Sazabi's bet is structurally identical — Datadog bills separately for logs, metrics, and traces, which means collapsing to logs-only would cannibalize their own revenue model. That's the kind of moat you get for free when your contrarian thesis happens to be right.

Callaway's trajectory gives me more signal than most founders at this stage. Sazabi is his second YC company — Opkit (S21) was a healthcare voice AI startup he co-founded with Justin Ko, his roommate and former colleague at both Crunchbase and Brex. Opkit raised a few million, got acquired by 11x in September 2024, and Callaway spent eight months there rebuilding their AI sales agent using agentic patterns before leaving to start Sazabi. That sequence — building observability at Brex, building an LLM-based voice agent at Opkit, building an agentic AI system at 11x, then returning to build observability for the AI-agent era — is the most organic problem discovery arc I could ask for. He didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's a good AI startup idea." He lived inside both observability infrastructure and AI agent development, then built the tool that sits at their intersection. The manifesto's opening line — "Modern observability is bloated" — reads like someone who finally got fed up enough to fix it. Sazabi's explicit positioning around monitoring AI-generated code and integrating with Claude Code, Codex, and Cursor came from Callaway actually building agentic systems, not from reading trend reports about the agentic future.

The logs-only architectural bet is where Sazabi gets genuinely contrarian, and I mean contrarian in the specific way I care about — the Venn diagram where "seems like a bad idea" overlaps with "is a good idea." The entire CNCF ecosystem is standardizing on OpenTelemetry, which accepts all three signal types. Dash0, which just raised $110 million at a billion-dollar valuation, is OpenTelemetry-native. SigNoz is OpenTelemetry-native. Sazabi rejects this framework entirely. Most observability engineers would hear "we only ingest logs" and say that's obviously wrong — you need structured metrics for dashboards and distributed traces for latency debugging. That's exactly the reaction I look for. But I want to be precise about what is and isn't contrarian here: "AI-native observability" is consensus — Datadog, Dash0, everyone is shipping AI features. The contrarian part is specifically the simplification thesis, and it's narrower than it first appears. Sazabi has attracted backing from leaders at Anthropic, Vercel, Replit, LangChain, and Browserbase — these are sophisticated technical investors who validated the approach. An idea that makes most observability engineers uncomfortable but that AI infrastructure leaders endorse is in the right zone, though not as far out on the "repels everyone" spectrum as Airbnb renting strangers' air mattresses.

Ko's role at Sazabi is the thing that bothers me. He co-founded Opkit with Callaway, they were roommates, they worked together at three companies — and yet the YC page lists only Callaway as founder, with Ko as an engineer. I've written a lot about why co-founder relationships matter and why solo founders face steeper odds. Sazabi isn't technically solo-founded if Ko is there building alongside Callaway, but the structural ambiguity — why isn't Ko listed as co-founder? — is a soft flag. Either there's a good organizational reason I can't see from the dossier, or the relationship dynamics shifted between Opkit and Sazabi in a way that might matter later. A team of ten with SOC 2 Type II certifications before launch is unusual for a pre-seed YC company and suggests Callaway knows what enterprise buyers need, but it also means Sazabi is burning real money before any revenue while Dash0 already has 600+ customers and Datadog has 47,000.

The bear case is straightforward and I take it seriously: Sazabi is pre-product, with full platform launch not until end of 2026, competing against Dash0's $155 million war chest and Datadog's entrenched position. The logs-only thesis could simply be wrong — maybe you do need native metrics and traces, and LLM-derived approximations will always be too lossy for production debugging at scale. OpenTelemetry adoption creates network effects that Sazabi swims against. And every observability incumbent is adding AI features on top of existing customer bases, which means Sazabi's AI differentiation has a shelf life measured in quarters, not years. If by mid-2027 Sazabi doesn't have a few hundred paying customers who demonstrably prefer logs-only over the three-pillars approach, the thesis was wrong and the window closed. That's the specific evidence that would flip me to Pass.

What keeps me on the Invest side is the combination that matters most in my framework: a founder who organically discovered the problem through years of direct experience, building in a developer-tools category that creates natural schlep barriers, with a technical thesis contrarian enough to repel the consensus but grounded in genuine architectural insight rather than intellectual posturing. Callaway isn't a teenage prodigy — he's thirty-one with a History degree and a Dev Bootcamp credential, which is not my typical profile for backing. But the Stripe pattern holds: someone who lived inside the pain of existing infrastructure, understood at a structural level why it was overcomplicated, and is now building the simplified replacement that incumbents can't copy without destroying their own revenue model. Sazabi's organic problem discovery is the strongest dimension here — Callaway's career from Brex observability through Opkit's LLM agents to Sazabi's AI-native monitoring is as clean an example of "live in the future, then build what's missing" as I've seen this batch.
