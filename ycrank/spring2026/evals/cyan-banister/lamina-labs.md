# Lamina Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what catches my eye first: two young men from Nepal — one from Humla, which is about as remote and resource-starved as any place on earth, the other from Butwal with a string of olympiad medals — find their way to MIT and Caltech, and now they're building deterministic animation infrastructure. The journey from Humla to MIT at nineteen is the kind of biographical signal that makes me sit up. I know what it takes to get from nowhere to somewhere when every system is stacked against you. That's a real forge. But then I ask my question — the one that surprises people because they expect me to ask about the business — "Tell me your story. Why *this* problem?" And the dossier goes quiet. I see the resourcefulness, I see the credentials, I see two brilliant young people who clearly earned their way to the table through sheer determination. What I don't see is the moment where educational animation became personal and inevitable for them. Did they grow up unable to access visual STEM content? Did they watch teachers in Nepal struggle without the tools to explain concepts visually? Maybe — and if so, that story needs to be front and center, because right now it reads like two technically gifted founders who spotted a gap in the AI video market. That's market identification, not biographical inevitability.

When I run my invisible system test, I can see the outline of something. The tools that create educational animations — Manim, After Effects, Remotion — they gatekeep who gets to be an effective STEM communicator. You need to code Python or have motion graphics expertise. That's a form of information asymmetry: the people who understand the math deeply aren't always the ones who can make it visual. But this is a soft gatekeeping — it's inefficiency and skill mismatch, not the kind of extractive, opaque structure that gets my blood moving. Nobody's getting exploited by Manim being hard to use. Compare that to the taxi medallion cartel that Uber cracked open, or the credit card opacity that Affirm exposed. Those systems were actively extracting value from people who couldn't see the mechanism. A difficult animation tool is a friction problem, not a structural injustice. I'm drawn to companies that shift power from gatekeepers to users, and while Lamina moves in that direction, the power dynamic it's disrupting isn't sharp enough to anchor a thesis.

The space itself doesn't make me uncomfortable, and that's actually the problem for how I evaluate. EdTech is consensus-positive. AI-generated content is consensus-positive. "Help teachers make better videos" is the kind of thing that gets applause at a dinner party, not the kind that makes sophisticated VCs shift in their seats. My biggest returns came from rockets blowing up on launch pads, defense tech that Silicon Valley wouldn't touch, male birth control that made boardrooms squirm. The deterministic-versus-probabilistic angle is technically interesting — choosing mathematical precision over the hallucination-prone generative approach is a genuine technical bet — but it's not the kind of contrarianism that signals a founder operating where others refuse to go. Nobody's going to pass on this because it's too uncomfortable. They'll pass because they're worried about commoditization, which is a completely different kind of risk.

Now, the bull case — and I want to be honest about it because there's something here worth examining. If these founders have genuinely built a rendering engine that converts natural language to mathematically verified STEM animations in eight seconds, that's not a wrapper around an LLM. That's purpose-built infrastructure. The ten-year question reveals something: if you can generate any precise technical visualization from a text description instantly, you're building the visual layer for all of human knowledge. Every textbook, every lecture, every technical document gets instant precise illustrations. That's not an animation tool — that's knowledge infrastructure. And the combination of Sudip's systems engineering and Rohan's deep physics (published astrophysics researcher, olympiad medalist in physics, math, *and* computing) maps directly onto the core technical challenge. These are the right two people to build a mathematically precise rendering engine. If they execute, the moat is the rendering pipeline itself — not the LLM layer, which is commoditized, but the structured scene generation and deterministic rendering that sits beneath it. The fact that Synthesia and Runway are architecturally incompatible with this approach (their diffusion-based models fundamentally can't guarantee mathematical correctness) gives Lamina structural space to build.

But I keep coming back to the commoditization question, and it's where my conviction stalls. Manim has 35,000 GitHub stars and is free. An ambitious weekend project wrapping Manim with Claude or GPT-4 could approximate a significant chunk of Lamina's value proposition. OpenAI or Anthropic adding structured visual output modalities to their models could disintermediate the text-understanding layer entirely. The rendering itself isn't the hard part — it's the translation from natural language to structured scene description, and that's exactly where foundation model providers are investing billions. Two undergrad founders on academic leave, no users, no revenue, waitlist stage, competing against that gravitational pull — it's a race I'm not confident a two-person team wins, no matter how talented. My worst failures — HQ Trivia, GameCrush — were situations where the product was real but the structural moat wasn't. I learned that lesson with scar tissue.

I wish these founders well. The journey from Nepal to YC is itself proof of a forge that most people never survive. But I need the biographical connection to *this* specific problem, I need a harder space that scares other investors away, and I need more structural defensibility than a rendering pipeline that sits downstream of rapidly commoditizing LLM capabilities. This isn't a Long Journey bet — not because the founders lack talent, but because the space is too consensus and the moat is too thin for where I place my conviction.
