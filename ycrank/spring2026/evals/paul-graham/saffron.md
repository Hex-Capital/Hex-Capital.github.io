# Saffron -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Here's what bothers me about Saffron: the problem they're solving is one that literally every engineer on Hacker News has been talking about for the last year. "AI changed how we code, but interviews haven't caught up" is not a discovery -- it's a trend observation. When I ask myself how these founders found this problem, the answer that comes back is: they're smart CS students at MIT, Stanford, and Harvey Mudd who noticed the most obvious thing happening in software engineering right now. That's exactly the kind of idea that gets generated in a brainstorming session about what to build in the age of AI. The problem isn't that they're wrong -- they're right. The problem is that being right about something this visible isn't worth much. When the idea is consensus, the upside has already been priced.

The Jane Street connection is the strongest organic signal here. Two of the three founders have Jane Street experience, and Jane Street runs one of the most rigorous technical evaluation processes in the industry. If they'd come to me and said "we spent three years at Jane Street watching the hiring process break down as candidates started using AI tools, and we tried to fix it internally and couldn't," that would be a different conversation. But the timeline suggests these are very early-career roles -- Jane Street Academy, Morgan Stanley Finance Academy -- more like exposure than immersion. There's a difference between having visited a problem and having lived inside it for years. Stripe worked because the Collisons had been building web apps and banging their heads against payment processing for years before they started the company. The pain was in their bones.

The competitive dynamics here concern me more than they would in most categories. HackerRank is doing $221 million in revenue. CodeSignal has $90 million in funding. Both already offer configurable AI tools in their assessments. For Saffron's thesis to work, adding AI interaction tracking and code attribution has to require a fundamental rebuild of these platforms -- not just a feature sprint. But a browser-based coding environment with session replay is not architecturally alien to companies that already run millions of coding assessments. This isn't Stripe vs. the banks, where the incumbents were structurally incapable of building good developer tools. HackerRank's engineers could probably ship a "Claude Code mode" in a quarter if they decided to. The schlep here is technical complexity, not the kind of regulatory and operational ugliness that structurally deters incumbents. Technical complexity is exactly what well-funded competitors are good at overcoming.

Now, the bull case. If AI-native coding becomes so fundamentally different from traditional coding that the entire assessment paradigm shifts -- if evaluating someone's ability to prompt, iterate, and verify AI output is as different from evaluating algorithmic problem-solving as driving is from riding a horse -- then the incumbents' content libraries become dead weight and Saffron's clean-sheet design is a genuine advantage. The per-line code attribution between human-written and AI-generated code is a technically novel feature that doesn't exist anywhere else, and the data moat from capturing granular human-AI interaction patterns across thousands of assessments could become extremely valuable. This team clearly has the technical chops to build it -- Choji's NeurIPS and ICML publications are directly relevant to the multi-agent scoring system, and Yao has shipped products to 50,000+ users before. Three technical co-founders from MIT, Stanford, and Harvey Mudd, two with Jane Street backgrounds -- this is a strong builder team. If I believed the problem was organic rather than manufactured, I'd be much more interested.

But I keep coming back to the origin. The best startups are built by people who couldn't not build them -- who were so frustrated by a problem in their own lives that the company was almost an involuntary response. I don't see that here. I see a well-constructed thesis by smart people who identified a market gap. That's a fine basis for a venture-backed company, but it's not the signal that makes me write a personal check. The Anthropic platform dependency adds another layer of risk -- building your core product experience around Claude Code means your cost structure and feature set are controlled by someone else's roadmap. And the name collision with at least four other companies called "Saffron" is the kind of thing that seems trivial but compounds into a real discoverability problem over time.

The team is technically impressive, and the timing is genuinely right. But timing that's obvious to everyone provides no edge. I'd want to see these founders do something that surprises me -- go door-to-door at engineering orgs installing their product the way the Collisons installed Stripe, or discover something unexpected about how engineers actually use AI that contradicts the conventional wisdom. Right now this feels like a well-reasoned bet on a trend, not an organic discovery of a hidden problem. I'm passing.
