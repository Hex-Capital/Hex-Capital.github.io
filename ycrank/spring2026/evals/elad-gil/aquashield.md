# AquaShield -- Elad Gil Evaluation

AquaShield is a technically-contained workflow product in a steady-state insurance-and-maintenance category with no named recent catalyst -- the inverse of my **Anduril** (2022) entry template, where AquaShield's strongest axis (a reasonably isolated single-miracle architecture around GNN localization) is paired with its weakest (zero specific market re-opening in the last 24 months). The pin: Paul Beckers and Marguerite Benoist are running clamp-on ultrasonic sensors plus Transformer + GNN models against a problem ("Europe's largest real estate company... lost > $100M to water damage") that has existed every year since buildings had pipes.

**Catalyst** is where AquaShield is lowest, and this is the dimension I lead with for a reason. **Anduril** (2022) sits at the high rung -- Ukraine in a 24-month window, defense procurement specifically re-opened, "arrows in the back" reversing. **Brex** (2017) sits mid-rung -- fintech infrastructure maturity made corporate cards re-buildable, but no single catalyzing event. **Lyft** Series C sits low -- no re-opening, just a market with declared dynamics. AquaShield places near the **Lyft** rung: the dossier's "why now" section is explicitly marked "[Inferred]" on both the transformer-maturity claim and the European insurance-pressure claim, with "No analyst or company-stated 'why now' catalyst was located in public sources." Water damage in commercial real estate is a steady-state market, and steady-state is where great teams die ("market first and the strength of the team second," *First Round Review*).

**Single-miracle architecture** is where AquaShield is highest, and this is genuinely the saving grace. **Stripe** (2010) sits at the high rung -- one miracle, developer-friendly payments. **Decagon** (2023) sits mid -- LLM accuracy plus enterprise GTM, two coupled bets. A fusion startup sits low -- multi-independent breakthroughs compounding to near-zero. AquaShield places between **Stripe** and **Decagon**: the EPFL article specifies "Transformer-based time-series models" and "Graph Neural Networks" on off-the-shelf clamp-on ultrasonic sensors -- no new physics, no regulatory novelty, the bet is GNN localization accuracy. The dossier flags that "Localization performance is unproven at portfolio scale," so the miracle is real and unresolved, but it is one miracle, not three.

**Revenue velocity** at AquaShield is closer to my "false signal" rung than my "just working" rung. **Harvey** (2023) is the high rung -- "$3B → $5B → $8B in months" (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). **Decagon** (2023) is mid-rung -- compounding enterprise logos. A generic pre-revenue AI startup with no disclosed curve is low ("something's fundamentally broken," Tim Ferriss #863). AquaShield pins near the low rung: the only quantitative claim is a self-reported "70% reduction" of >$100M at one anchor account, no ARR disclosed, no pricing page, a "pilot program intake form" as the entry point. MIT Facilities is a pilot, the Germany deployment is a pilot -- this is pilot tourism shape, not buying-behavior shape.

**Founder shipping rhythm** -- **Perplexity** (2022) is the high rung where Aravind shipped between meetings; **Brex** (2017) is mid-rung with operationally-disciplined founders who'd shipped fintech; ex-FAANG-with-no-artifacts is the low rung. AquaShield pins between mid and low: Paul Beckers is a "previous robotics researcher at MIT," Marguerite Benoist is a "Harvard Master Thesis Student" per the LinkedIn snippet -- research credentials, not shipped products. No GitHub, no named prior artifacts, no independent shipping history outside academia.

**Index position** -- **Stripe** (2010) and **Coinbase** (2013) sit high (index on e-commerce; basket of every crypto). **Decagon** sits mid (workflow path of customer support). A thin vertical app fighting WINT and Phyn on features sits low. AquaShield pins near low: real estate water monitoring is not a compounding category in the way e-commerce or crypto were -- buildings are a fixed installed base, and the competitive set already includes **WINT** at $35M raised with Insight Partners and a multi-year enterprise head start. AquaShield must take share from WINT, not ride a curve WINT is also riding.

In 36 months I expect AquaShield to be acquired by a property-insurance carrier (HSB, FM Global) or facilities-management incumbent (JLL, CBRE) for $20-60M after reaching $1-4M ARR concentrated in 2-4 European real-estate portfolio accounts -- the mechanism is that GNN localization will probably work well enough to be useful, the European anchor will convert, but the absence of a category catalyst caps the velocity curve below the threshold where a growth fund pays up. This is **Phyn**'s exit shape (~$25M to an investor group in 2025), not **Harvey**'s shape.

The strongest opposing case is the **Color Genomics** (2015) precedent -- workflow infrastructure on top of a hard physical layer, where the "catalyst" was not public/famous but was real (commodity sequencing economics). One could argue AquaShield's catalyst is European climate-disclosure and insurance-loss pressure on real estate portfolios post-2024, and that GNN-from-sparse-sensors genuinely matured in the last 18 months. The bear-killer that wins: Color is a Known Exception specifically because I co-founded and operated it -- I architected the workflow layer myself -- and the dossier explicitly says the insurance-pressure thesis is "not confirmed by a named analyst source." I won't grant Color-treatment to a deal where I'm a passive check and the catalyst is inferred rather than named. The one verifiable condition that flips my verdict: AquaShield disclosing >$1M ARR across 3+ named portfolio customers within 90 days -- that would convert the "70% reduction" claim from anchor-account folklore into a velocity curve, and I would re-engage at the next round.
