# Zolvo -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Zolvo: this is one of the most popular categories in venture right now. AI automating financial services back offices -- every investor in San Francisco understands this pitch in the first thirty seconds. Salient raised $70M and hit $25M ARR in about two years doing adjacent work in auto and consumer loans. Eighty-three percent of lenders say they're increasing their GenAI budgets in 2026. The market timing is undeniably real. But in my experience, when a category is this legible, when the "why now" is this obvious, the returns compress. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Zolvo is neither unpopular nor difficult to assess -- it's a clean, logical thesis that any VC would nod along with. That doesn't make it a bad company. It makes it a bad fit for how I invest.

Now, Isabela Rodriguez's story does pull at something in me. A twenty-four-year-old Colombian woman who packed up and moved to San Francisco with three thousand dollars to build a fintech company -- that takes real nerve. I know what it feels like to be somewhere with nothing and bet everything on yourself. She'd already closed Domu's first million in revenue and brought in Chubb as a client, so this isn't someone operating on pure faith -- she has receipts. Tony Montes is twenty-two with publications at ACL and EMNLP and built voice AI infrastructure processing fifty to a hundred thousand daily calls at Domu. These are genuinely impressive young people. But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- "tell me how you got to where you are, and why this particular problem" -- I can't find the biographical thread that connects them to commercial loan servicing specifically. They worked at a fintech company, they saw an adjacent market opportunity, they're executing on it well. That's smart. But it's not the same as a founder who spent years inside the dysfunction and can't sleep until it's fixed. The connection feels professional, not personal. And at the earliest stage, where there's no data to lean on, I need that personal connection to form conviction.

The economic access angle is where I feel the flattest about this. When I backed Uber, I saw a company that was going to create income for hundreds of thousands of drivers who were locked out of the medallion system. Affirm made credit transparent for people who were being gouged by hidden fees. What Zolvo does is reduce operational costs for mid-market commercial lenders -- institutions managing $200M in assets. That's valuable to those lenders, absolutely. But who downstream benefits? The company isn't making lending more transparent for borrowers, isn't opening up credit access to underserved communities, isn't surfacing an invisible system for people who lack power. It's an efficiency play for financial institutions. That's a fine business. It's not the kind of impact that gets me out of bed.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If you believe that commercial lending servicing is a distinct enough vertical that Salient won't simply expand into it with their $70M war chest, and that workflow integration plus customer data accumulation can create a moat despite using off-the-shelf AI models, and that Rodriguez and Montes can navigate bank procurement cycles and regulatory compliance at ages twenty-four and twenty-two -- then you have a company with $6,000 in revenue within four weeks, a billion dollars in pipeline AUM, a complementary founding team with a proven working relationship from Domu, Tom Blomfield providing fintech mentorship, and a market growing at nearly ten percent annually. Those are above-average pre-seed signals, and I could see a disciplined growth investor getting excited here. The co-founder dynamic is actually one of the better signals in this dossier -- they attended the same university, worked different functions at the same YC company, their former bosses invested in them, and the CEO/CTO split is clean with no role ambiguity. That matters to me after watching HQ Trivia implode over exactly this kind of structural question.

But I keep coming back to the same issue: this is a consensus bet built on commodity technology in a hot market. The AI models are OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude -- all commercially available. The competitive landscape includes multiple well-funded companies. The differentiation is market segmentation (commercial vs. consumer lending), which is smart positioning but not a durable moat. I invest when I feel that jolt of energy about something nobody else sees yet. What I feel here is respect for the founders' hustle but not the conviction that only I would write this check. Plenty of investors will fund Zolvo -- and that's actually part of why it's not for me.
