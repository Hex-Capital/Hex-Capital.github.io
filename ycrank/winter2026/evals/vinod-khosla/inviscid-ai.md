# Inviscid AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Inviscid AI is the three case studies on their website: HVAC vent optimization, coastal infrastructure, and storm surge forecasting. Three different verticals, three different customer bases, three different sales channels — from a two-person team. When Pat Brown came to me with Impossible Foods, he had one person and one problem: animal agriculture is destroying the planet, and he was going to replace it. That singular obsession is the signal. When a founder spreads across three verticals, it tells me they've fallen in love with a technology — physics-informed neural networks for CFD — and are searching for a problem worthy of it. The technology-in-search-of-a-problem approach is fundamentally different from the problem-too-important-not-to-work-on approach. And in my experience, the latter produces the outsized outcomes.

Let me address consequence magnitude directly. If Inviscid AI fully succeeds — every commercial building, every data center, every industrial facility running their real-time physics simulation — what changes? Buildings run their HVAC systems 20-40% more efficiently. That matters. Buildings consume roughly 40% of US energy, and HVAC is a major share. But this is optimization of an existing system, not its replacement. The HVAC industry still exists. The building management systems still exist. The cooling towers, the chillers, the ductwork — all unchanged. Inviscid AI makes the existing infrastructure waste less energy. Compare that to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means fossil fuel power generation becomes economically obsolete, or to Juniper Networks, where success meant Cisco's entire routing architecture became structurally uncompetitive. Making an existing system run 30% more efficiently is valuable. It is not transformational. The consequence of success here is a good business, not a reordered industry.

The competitive landscape is what concerns me most, though. This is not a case of outsiders attacking a problem that incumbents dismiss as impossible — which is exactly the signal I invest behind. PassiveLogic has raised $125 million for physics-based building digital twins. Trane Technologies paid $103 million to acquire BrainBox AI in January 2025. EkkoSense is deployed at 300+ data center sites. Cadence — an $80 billion public company — already offers CFD digital twins for data centers. And NVIDIA is building Modulus, an open framework for physics-informed neural networks that could commoditize the exact inference layer Inviscid AI is building. When incumbents and well-funded competitors are actively building in your space, you've arrived after the category was created. My best investments — OpenAI in 2019, food tech in 2010, cleantech in 2004 — were made before the category had a name, when experts mocked the approach. Nobody is mocking AI-driven building optimization in 2026. The category is consensus.

The strongest bull case: data center cooling is becoming the binding constraint on AI compute scaling, growing at 16.4% CAGR. If Inviscid AI could achieve what traditional CFD vendors cannot — real-time, physics-accurate airflow optimization that runs autonomously in production — they could become critical infrastructure for the AI buildout. The 1,000x speedup claim, if it holds at production scale with 95%+ accuracy, is a genuine qualitative shift from batch simulation to real-time control. A focused data center play could be very valuable, and Trane's acquisition of BrainBox AI shows M&A appetite in this space. What would make this a great investment is evidence that the founders have identified data centers as their singular, civilization-scale problem — that they're building the system without which the next generation of AI infrastructure cannot be cooled — and that their physics-informed approach creates accuracy advantages that purely data-driven competitors like BrainBox AI and EkkoSense cannot match. That story could work. But the dossier doesn't tell that story. It tells a story of a technology applied across three unrelated verticals by a team whose backgrounds, while technically competent, don't signal the kind of domain conviction or contrarian courage I look for.

On the founders: Ziming Qiu is a 5th-year PhD student at NYU with published ML research — a conventional path for an academic-to-startup spin-out. Kabir Jain describes himself as someone who "likes physics and AI." I have no evidence of prior exits, rapid learning rate, or the kind of foolish confidence that characterized Peter Beck when he decided to build rockets from New Zealand with no aerospace pedigree. A PhD student applying their research to a commercial problem is the most common startup origin story in deep tech — it's the industry playbook, not a contrarian act. And the physics-informed neural network methodology they're building on is actively published by multiple research groups, which means the technical moat is narrow. No patents, one forked repository on GitHub with zero stars. The gene pool is homogeneous: two technical founders with no go-to-market capability for what is clearly an enterprise sales motion requiring relationships with facilities managers and data center operators.

I pass. The problem is real but the consequence of success is optimization, not transformation. The technology is capable but not proprietary, built on published academic methods in a field where NVIDIA is actively commoditizing the toolchain. The competitive landscape is well-funded and validated — this is a 2-person team entering a category where a $125 million startup and an $80 billion public company are already building. The founders show competence but not the outsider conviction or singular problem obsession that my best investments share. This feels like a company that has reduced its risk of failure by picking a proven category — and in doing so, has ensured the consequences of success will be bounded.
