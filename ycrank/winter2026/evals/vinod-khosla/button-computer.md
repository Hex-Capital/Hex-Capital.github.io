# Button Computer -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The graveyard here is what strikes me first. Humane raised $230 million and sold to HP for less than half that. Rabbit R1 became a punchline. Meta swallowed Limitless, Amazon swallowed Bee. And now two ex-Apple Vision Pro engineers walk into this same field with a $179 clip-on device and call it "an iPod Shuffle for AI." I respect the courage, but the analogy tells me everything about the ambition. The iPod Shuffle was a nice accessory. It was not a civilization-scale bet. Nobody's life was fundamentally different because of an iPod Shuffle.

Let me start where I always start: if Button succeeds beyond anyone's expectations, what changes? People clip a small device to their shirt and get spoken answers to questions without pulling out their phone. That is a convenience improvement to a task you can already do with the device in your pocket. Compare this to the problems I think are worth dedicating a career to -- eliminating animal agriculture, achieving fusion energy, replacing fossil fuels, making cancer detection routine. Button's consequence of success is a consumer electronics business. Maybe a few hundred million in annual revenue at peak. The world doesn't operate differently. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The founders have looked at Humane's catastrophic overreach and course-corrected into something safer and more modest -- but that's precisely the move I warn against. They've reduced the probability of failure to the point where the consequences of success are, in my view, not worth the best years of these clearly talented engineers' careers.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Voice AI interaction may be an inflection point as significant as the smartphone itself. LLM voice latency has dropped dramatically in 2024-2025 -- GPT-4o and Claude now support real-time conversation -- making a conversational wearable technically feasible for the first time. The competitive landscape has cleared: first-gen failures created consumer lessons, and the major acqui-hires removed independent players. There's a real window before Apple ships its own wearable. If habitual voice-AI interaction becomes as fundamental as habitual smartphone use, the simple, well-designed device that catalyzes that behavioral shift could be the Square of voice AI -- Square wasn't just a card reader, it enabled a transformation in who could participate in electronic commerce. The founders' experience co-inventing the Vision Pro gaze-and-tap interface gives them rare expertise in designing novel hardware interaction paradigms. Burgoyne's Skyglass reaching 95K downloads and $6K MRR demonstrates he can ship product and find users. If Button nails the industrial design and firmware latency, it could define the category the way iPod defined MP3 players. That's the best version of the story.

But I don't buy it, because the phone already does this. Square enabled something previously impossible -- a street vendor accepting credit cards without a $2,000 POS terminal and a merchant account. Button enables something already possible but slightly less convenient. You can talk to ChatGPT, Siri, or Google Assistant right now on the phone in your pocket. The friction being removed is reaching into your pocket and tapping a button on a screen versus tapping a button on your collar. That's a feature, not a revolution. And the technology underneath -- commodity dual-mic array, Bluetooth, speaker, wrapping third-party LLM APIs -- has no structural moat. The dossier itself acknowledges "multiple startups and hardware manufacturers could build a similar device." Apple could ship this as a software update to AirPods.

The founders are competent engineers executing from inside their expertise, which is the opposite of what I look for. Both came from Apple Vision Pro. Both have hardware interaction design backgrounds. They're applying what they know to an adjacent product -- that's the industry playbook, not the outsider irreverence that created Juniper or Impossible Foods. I look for the biochemistry professor who doesn't know what the food industry considers impossible, or the New Zealander who doesn't know why aerospace veterans say private orbital launch can't be done cheaply. These are Apple hardware engineers building Apple-quality hardware. That's a fine skill set, but it predicts a well-executed incremental product, not a category-defining disruption. The team also lacks manufacturing and supply chain expertise, which for a hardware company shipping at $179 is a critical gap they'll need to fill before they can deliver on pre-orders by end of 2026.

The timing dimension is genuinely favorable -- I'll give them that. Voice AI capabilities are on an exponential curve, consumer awareness of conversational AI is broadening, and the first-generation failures have created a "lessons learned" opening. This is the one aspect that activates my rate-of-change framework. But favorable timing on an exponential curve matters most when the startup has a technology or structural advantage that lets it ride that curve uniquely. Button doesn't. It's a thin integration layer on top of the same LLM APIs available to everyone. When the technology curve is this accessible, timing advantage evaporates quickly -- you need something proprietary underneath, and I don't see it here.
