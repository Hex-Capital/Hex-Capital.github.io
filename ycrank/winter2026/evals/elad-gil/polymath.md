# Polymath -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive field here is the first thing I see, and it tells me this is not a non-obvious market. Mechanize has Nat Friedman, Daniel Gross, Patrick Collison, and Jeff Dean behind it and is already working with Anthropic. Prime Intellect raised $70.4M in December 2025. Scale AI is building Agent-RLVR. Idler, a fellow YC company, has already closed a multimillion-dollar contract with a frontier lab. When TechCrunch is writing features about your market and Wing VC is publishing consolidation timelines predicting 3-5 winners by 2030, the non-obvious window has closed. PagerDuty worked for me because operations alerting seemed boring and triggered dismissive reactions from other investors. Gusto worked because payroll seemed too simple. RL environments for frontier AI labs in early 2026 is a market where every sophisticated investor is already writing checks. The structural inflection is real -- Anthropic reportedly planning $1B+ in RL environment spend, agentic AI requiring complex training environments, MCP standardizing tool integration -- but the inflection is consensus. Consensus inflections compress returns, and a 2-person pre-seed team competing for those returns against this field faces a resource gap that's hard to characterize as anything other than severe.

The buyer concentration amplifies the problem. The entire RL environment market is essentially 4-5 frontier AI labs. This isn't infrastructure that every company needs -- it's a specialized supplier relationship with a handful of buyers who have enormous bargaining power. Compare this to Checkr, which sells background checks to every company that hires people, or Samsara, which serves every company with a fleet. Those businesses have thousands of potential customers, which creates distribution optionality and resilience. When your total addressable customer count is single digits, losing one deal or failing to win one contract is existential. And the competitors with the best shot at those contracts -- Mechanize (already working with Anthropic), Scale AI (existing lab relationships at massive scale) -- have distribution advantages that are structural, not superficial.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Polymath's generative approach to environment creation represents a genuine innovator's dilemma for Scale AI and Surge AI, whose labor-intensive models can't easily cannibalize their contractor workforces. If automated environment generation from text descriptions actually works at quality -- replacing what Scale does with thousands of contractors -- this is the kind of software-driven disruption I look for. Scale AI built a $1.2B revenue business on human annotation; a fully automated approach would have fundamentally different margin structure. The Harvey parallel partially holds: build the workflow and positioning ahead of the capability curve, capture the market when the technology matures. If Polymath's world generation models produce environments 10x cheaper and 10x faster than manual construction, the resource gap against competitors becomes irrelevant because those competitors are structurally unable to match the economics. That's a real case. But it's a case built entirely on an unproven technical claim. The shipped product is Horizon-SWE, which is a benchmark -- a single handcrafted environment, not a demonstrated automated environment generator. The gap between building one benchmark and automating diverse environment generation at scale is the entire company.

That gap points to what I think is a two-miracle problem. Miracle one: build world generation models that produce environments matching or exceeding human-constructed quality at scale. Miracle two: win contracts against much better-funded competitors who already have relationships with the 4-5 buyers that matter. Neither miracle is trivial, and they compound. When Harvey's single miracle was model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy, that miracle was being delivered by the foundation model labs -- Harvey just had to build workflow and distribution around it. Polymath's technical miracle is entirely internal, and they have to solve it while simultaneously out-competing teams with 100x their resources for a customer base they could count on one hand. I need to see exactly one miracle, and I see at least two.

The founders bring relevant backgrounds -- Dylan Ma's post-training experience at Hume AI and Naren Yenuganti's infrastructure work at Amazon and Plaid -- and they shipped Horizon-SWE quickly, which shows execution capability for a two-person team. But the backgrounds are solid-not-exceptional for this market. Multiple competing teams have people who literally built the frontier models these environments are designed to train. The product-to-distribution trajectory is also thin: a benchmark is a credibility signal, not a channel. There's no visible path from "benchmark publisher" to "platform that distributes multiple products through an enterprise relationship" -- which is the trajectory I need to see for generational outcomes.

The technology cycle timing is the strongest dimension here. The moment is genuinely right -- model capabilities have crossed the threshold where complex RL environments create training value, MCP standardization reduces integration cost, and lab budgets are inflecting upward. If this were a non-obvious market with a defensible approach and a single clear miracle, I'd be leaning in. But a well-timed entry into a consensus-hot, competitively crowded, buyer-concentrated market with an unproven core technical claim doesn't match how I deploy capital.
