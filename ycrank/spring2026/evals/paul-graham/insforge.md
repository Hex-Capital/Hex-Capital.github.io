# InsForge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hang Huang is a Yale MBA and former Amazon PM, and InsForge is a backend platform pitched at AI coding agents -- that pairing tells me what kind of company InsForge is before I read another word. The dossier shows 10.7k GitHub stars, 4,147 commits, and four separate Show HN posts -- a real shipping cadence sitting in a real infrastructure schlep against Supabase's $5B valuation -- but nowhere in the dossier does anyone say "I was using Claude Code and kept hitting this exact wall." InsForge is a spotted-the-gap company, not a scratched-my-own-itch company, and that asymmetry is the whole evaluation.

Coinbase (2012) is the right anchor for InsForge's strongest dimension. Brian Armstrong didn't pick bitcoin compliance because it was sexy; he picked it because it was a grind everyone else's batch flinched from, and the grind itself became the moat. InsForge is genuinely in that zone -- Postgres plus pgvector plus auth plus S3-compatible storage plus edge functions plus a model gateway, all unified behind an MCP server, is the kind of integration schlep that a clever solo hacker building an AI wrapper would not touch. Where InsForge diverges from the Coinbase template is that the schlep is not regulatory or offline -- it's pure backend plumbing, which means Supabase ($544M raised, already shipping its own MCP server per the sealos.io comparison) can compress InsForge's interface advantage on a six-month roadmap without needing a compliance team or a delivery fleet. The schlep is real but it's a software schlep, and software schleps get commoditized faster than regulatory ones.

The AI-wrapper MBA team from my own pass example is the failure pattern I have to test InsForge against on the organic-idea axis, because Hang's bio leads with "Yale MBA, ex-Amazon PM" the way that hypothetical pitch led with credentials. That pattern fails because nobody on the team has personally suffered the problem -- they spotted it on a slide. The InsForge dossier shows the same silence: no founder anecdote about Tony losing a weekend stitching Supabase to Claude Code, no "we built this for ourselves first and then noticed others wanted it." The "Why now" line is explicitly marked [Inferred]. Drew Houston's USB stick at MIT is a one-sentence story everyone repeats; InsForge does not have that sentence, and after enough applications you learn that the absence of the sentence is almost always the absence of the experience.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism that matters is whether shipping volume compounds into a stunt or just into output. Four Show HN posts in a year, 4,147 commits, Apache 2.0, 2,000+ self-reported hosted databases, and 1,036 Discord members is animal-tier *output* -- it is not Brian Armstrong sending a working Android wallet after a rejection letter, but it is not nothing either. The 10.7k stars on InsForge tell me Tony Chang in particular is grinding; the worry is that grinding-as-volume against an incumbent with 30x the capital is a different animal test than grinding-as-stunt against zero capital.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is clean: if Hang Huang were a second technical co-founder with a shipped artifact instead of a Yale MBA, InsForge would jump a tier on this dimension and probably on the previous one too. Tony Chang alone passes -- Databricks networking infrastructure, Meta Ads ML Infra, "5x Hackathon Winner," and the 4,147 commits on the InsForge repo are the real hacker trail. Hang is the manager-mode half of the pair, and "Founder Mode" (September 2024) is exactly the essay I wrote because that half is usually where companies break.

Reddit (2005) is the single anchor for the co-founder dimension, and InsForge doesn't reach it. Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates -- the chemistry was load-bearing before the idea existed. The InsForge dossier explicitly notes "No public data on co-founder history (no shared employer or school surfaced)" for Hang and Tony, which reads as a pair that met to start the company rather than a pair that started the company because they were already a pair. Two founders is better than one, so InsForge clears the solo red flag, but it doesn't clear the long-relationship bar.

My 36-month forecast for InsForge: acqui-hire or strategic acquisition by Supabase, Databricks, or Vercel in the $30M-$80M range, with Tony Chang retained and Hang Huang exiting -- the open-source distribution and the MCP-layer codebase get absorbed once Supabase's own MCP server reaches parity and InsForge's interface advantage compresses. This is the Kiko (2005) shape, not the Twitch (2007) shape, because the schlep here is software-compressible rather than regulation-protected.

The bull case that stings: InsForge's 10.7k stars in a few months is a curve that looks a lot like early Supabase, and if the MCP protocol becomes the default agent-to-backend interface the way HTTP became the default human-to-server interface, then the company that owns the agent-native primitives at the moment the protocol hardens is sitting on Stripe-shaped leverage, not Kiko-shaped optionality. Tony's Databricks networking-infra background is exactly the resume that lets you build the boring distributed-systems substrate that makes a developer platform actually work at scale, and "10.7k stars + Apache 2.0 + self-hostable" is a distribution moat that doesn't care about Supabase's $544M. What dismantles this for me is the founder-pain absence plus the self-published benchmark (1.6x faster, 30% fewer tokens, 1.7x accuracy) with no third-party reproduction -- the bull case requires me to trust that InsForge is a category-defining bet, and the dossier is asking me to trust it on a credentialed pair's word rather than on a stunt. The condition that flips my verdict: Tony Chang personally shipping a piece of MCP infrastructure outside InsForge that the broader agent-tooling community adopts as a standard, the way Aaron Swartz co-authored RSS before Reddit. That would convert InsForge from a credentialed gap-spotter into a hacker-genealogy bet, and I'd write the check.
