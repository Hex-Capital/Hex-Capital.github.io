# Corvera -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Corvera is that the CEO built a CPG brand across 5,000+ retail locations in six countries before deciding to build software for CPG operations. That's the right direction of founder-market fit — an operator who discovered the pain, not an engineer who discovered the market. When I invested in companies like Gusto, the pattern was similar: someone who'd lived inside the problem built the infrastructure they wished existed. Christopher Kong ran Better Nature's supply chain and knows exactly which spreadsheets break at scale. That matters.

The structural question, though, is whether "AI agents for CPG supply chain" represents a non-obvious market at an inflection point — or a consensus bet dressed in boring-market clothing. The underlying market is genuinely boring: order processing, inventory tracking, purchase order generation. Most investors would rather fund another coding tool. That part I like. But the "AI agent" framing is the most crowded positioning in tech right now. When I map the competitive landscape, I see Crisp with $127M+ raised launching their own "AI Agent Studio" in 2025, Settle with $506M targeting adjacent CPG financial operations, and Unioncrate backed by Founders Fund doing supply chain planning since 2016. The market is real and growing — CPG software at $12.5B heading to $25B by 2033 — but the inflection isn't hidden. When Harvey was building legal AI in 2022, nobody else was funding that category because model fidelity wasn't there yet. CPG operations automation has capital flowing in from multiple directions. The structural gap — sub-$500M brands underserved by SAP/Oracle/NetSuite — is genuine, but it's not the kind of gap that only this team can see.

Where Corvera potentially differentiates is in the autonomous execution layer versus the analytics and planning layers that funded competitors occupy. Crisp aggregates retail data. Settle automates AP. Unioncrate does supply chain planning. None of them are actually processing orders, generating purchase orders, and managing inventory without human intervention. That's a meaningfully different product — and a harder product to build. The reliability bar for autonomous order processing in CPG is extremely high. One wrong quantity on a PO to a major retailer can damage a brand relationship. This sets up what I'd frame as a dual-miracle problem: you need near-perfect document parsing accuracy across heterogeneous formats (emails, PDFs, EDI from hundreds of retail buyers) AND you need to build enough brand trust that operators hand over control of their most critical workflows. Those are two distinct obstacles, and the second one compounds the difficulty of the first — any parsing error in the early days destroys trust that takes months to rebuild.

The product-to-distribution potential is structurally sound. If Corvera becomes the system of record for a brand's order-to-cash and procurement workflows, they're sitting on the most operationally sensitive data a CPG company generates. That's a Gusto-like wedge: payroll became the distribution channel for HR, benefits, compliance, and financial wellness. Similarly, order processing and inventory could become the distribution channel for financial operations, retail analytics, supply chain financing, and compliance. The 15 design partners represent early integration depth. But I don't see evidence that the founders have articulated this multi-product trajectory yet — and at pre-seed with four people split between the UK and US, the operational stretch is real.

The bull case I'd want to believe: Kong's CPG network and operator credibility create a founder-driven distribution moat in a market where trust is the primary buying criterion. CPG founders buy from people who've been in their shoes. If Corvera can convert even half of those 15 design partners to paid customers and demonstrate autonomous order processing reliability above 99%, the word-of-mouth within the CPG founder community could create a Deel-like network referral dynamic — which is interesting given that Alex Bouaziz is an angel investor here. The "boring but critical" infrastructure pattern, combined with a genuine operator-founder, has produced some of my best returns. If this is Gusto for CPG operations, the outcome is large.

But I keep coming back to the timing thesis. When I invested in Harvey, the structural moment was specific: GPT scaling curves told me legal-grade model fidelity was 12-18 months away, and almost nobody else was tracking that curve. Corvera's "why now" — LLM capabilities improved and CPG AI adoption jumped to 71% — is real but shared by every AI-for-operations startup pitching today. The specific technology threshold that makes autonomous CPG order processing possible now versus two years ago isn't crisp enough. Document parsing has been improving incrementally for a decade. The jump from "pretty good" to "reliable enough for autonomous execution" may be happening now, but it's not the dramatic capability threshold crossing that creates asymmetric opportunity. This feels more like a competent team riding a broad technology wave than a team positioned at a specific structural moment that others haven't mapped.
