# Unifold -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most analytically interesting thing about Unifold isn't the product -- it's the competitive dynamics. The founders built Streambird, a wallet-as-a-service infrastructure platform. MoonPay acquired it. Now they're building Unifold, which sits directly adjacent to what MoonPay could extend into using the founders' own prior work. MoonPay has $757M raised, a $3-5B valuation, and the engineering talent that stayed behind after the acquisition. This is the kind of competitive setup where you need a clear answer to "why can't the incumbent just do this?" -- and the answer here is nuanced. MoonPay's business model is optimized around fiat-to-crypto conversion margins. Pivoting to on-chain deposit orchestration would mean building a different technical stack while potentially cannibalizing their existing revenue. That's a real structural tension. But "the incumbent's incentives are misaligned" is a timing argument, not a permanent moat. MoonPay can resolve that tension whenever the market gets large enough to matter.

On the structural moment: multi-chain fragmentation is genuinely compounding. Every new L2 that launches -- Base, Blast, MegaETH -- makes the deposit problem worse. Stablecoin volumes hit $1 trillion per month in mid-2025. Prediction markets exploded as a consumer category. YC is accepting stablecoin payments. These are real catalysts. But crypto infrastructure is not a non-obvious market. Every venture firm has a crypto thesis. The on-chain-to-on-chain niche is a meaningful distinction from fiat on-ramps, but it's not the kind of market that triggers the pattern I look for -- where the category appears too boring, too niche, or too weird for most investors to engage. Crypto deposit infrastructure is well within the Overton window of VC attention. When I backed PagerDuty, no one cared about operations alerting. When I backed Coinbase, crypto was genuinely unfamiliar to institutional investors. On-chain deposit routing in 2026 is neither boring nor unfamiliar -- it's a well-understood problem in a sector that's received tens of billions in venture funding.

The strongest dimension here is the team. These founders built and exited the exact adjacent infrastructure, onboarded 30M+ users through products like Phantom and Polymarket, and have a multi-year working relationship tested through an acquisition cycle. Timothy Chung and Quang Huynh co-founded Streambird together and both worked at Polymarket and Instabase -- that's the kind of repeated co-founder stress-testing that de-risks the interpersonal dimension. Hau Chu brings deep chain-level engineering from Solana Labs and MoonPay. They already have named early adopters (Alpha Arcade, Lofty.ai), ecosystem partnerships (Algorand, MegaETH), and multi-platform SDKs shipped during a private beta. This is execution velocity consistent with a team that knows the problem space cold. If I were evaluating founders alone, I'd lean Invest.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this gets interesting. A deposit API that handles cross-chain routing, gas abstraction, and compliance checks has a plausible path to becoming a platform -- the same way Stripe's payment integration became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Unifold could expand into settlement, analytics, compliance-as-a-service, or treasury management for crypto-native applications. The developer-first approach with sub-10-line integrations mirrors the Stripe playbook deliberately. But the low integration effort cuts both ways: what takes 10 lines to add takes 10 lines to replace. Stripe's distribution moat deepened because payment infrastructure touches accounting, tax reporting, subscription management -- creating layered switching costs. On-chain deposits don't yet create that same depth of integration. The compliance checks with Coinbase and Gemini are a start, but the switching cost story needs more layers.

The bull case I take seriously: if on-chain consumer apps become a mainstream category -- not just prediction markets but gaming, social, commerce -- then every single one needs deposit infrastructure, and the team that ships first with the most chain coverage and best developer experience wins a category. The founders' prior work at Polymarket gives them direct insight into what deposit flows actually kill conversion. The "why now" could be that 2026 is the year on-chain consumer apps cross the usability threshold, and Unifold is positioned the way Stripe was in 2011 when e-commerce was crossing its threshold. If that's the trajectory, this team is as well-positioned as anyone to capture it. What holds me back is that the miracle test isn't clean. The business needs to simultaneously prove the on-chain-to-on-chain category is large enough to sustain a standalone infrastructure company, fend off MoonPay and Stripe (which acquired Privy in 2025), and build enough chain coverage to create switching costs before better-funded competitors replicate the API. That's closer to two miracles than one.
