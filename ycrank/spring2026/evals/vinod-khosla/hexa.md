# Hexa -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Hexa is the exact pattern my Pass Example was written about -- a YC pre-seed selling GPT-on-top-of-ERP to industrial distributors, where the success-state at maximum ambition is that quote-to-cash cycles at mid-market manufacturers get 30% faster. Ishaan Makkar's framing thesis -- "54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets" -- is an efficiency observation, not a civilizational one, and that is the entire signature: a competent workflow-automation wedge with no consequence ladder to climb. The highest-relative dimension is founder learning rate by default (we have no evidence either way); the lowest is technical/capital risk repelling consensus VCs, because Didero just took $30M from Chemistry, Headline, and M12 to do an overlapping wedge -- consensus VCs are not repelled, they are already there.

On consequence magnitude, the HIGH rung is **Impossible Foods** (2011), where the success-state was the disappearance of animal-protein production -- a named incumbent regime collapsing, with a $15T-scale consequence variable. Hexa's success-state, derived from hexaagents.com's own copy, is that emails-and-PDFs stop being copy-pasted into SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, and Epicor by clerks. That is a labor-efficiency improvement inside one functional persona at distributors, not the collapse of an incumbent system; the ERPs themselves persist and in fact get *stickier* underneath Hexa. The dossier offers no labor-cost figure, no industry-displacement frame, no "$X trillion of GDP" anchor of the kind I demand when I cite my Fortune 2026-03-06 frame. The gap from Impossible Foods is not narrow -- it is categorical.

On technical and capital risk, the LOW rung is the **GPT-4 sales-call summarizer** from my Pass Example -- the pattern where any Tier-1 generalist can write the check because there is no science gating the product. Hexa's stack as disclosed -- ingest emails/PDFs/transcripts, match parts to catalogs, post into eight named ERPs/CRMs (SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, Epicor, Salesforce, HubSpot, Zoho, Pipedrive) -- is the same shape. The proof that this dimension is failing is not internal to Hexa; it is external: Didero already raised a $30M Series A on Feb 12, 2026 targeting the identical ICP ("manufacturers and distributors who need to source raw materials," TechCrunch), and Traza took $2.1M for AI workers in procurement quoting. When the consensus market funds three companies into the same wedge inside twelve months, the capital-risk moat I look for is definitionally absent.

Founder defies expert consensus -- counterfactual mode. For Hexa's score to flip a tier here, the dossier would need to show that Ishaan Makkar or Auri Nayak spent years inside SAP's channel economics and then publicly broke with the incumbent orthodoxy on what agentic overlays could do -- the Arvind Jain shape that let me make an exception for **Glean**. What we have instead is a LinkedIn snippet citing a spreadsheet statistic and a Cactus x DeepMind hackathon listing for Aurideep Nayak. No prior employer, no exit, no domain-employment evidence -- the dossier explicitly flags "founder-background opacity" as a risk. Absent evidence of defiance, I apply my default and assume conformity.

Founder learning rate -- mechanism analysis. The reason I weight this dimension is that 18-month learning rate compounds: a founder who pivoted once will pivot again when Didero's $30M war chest forces the wedge to shift. Hexa's founders show no documented pivot, no public thesis update, no prior company sale -- not because they are rigid, but because the company is six weeks into revenue and there is nothing to pivot *from* yet. This is the dimension where I do not penalize aggressively; I apply my mid-default and watch what happens when the Didero pressure arrives. If Hexa's third customer churns and Makkar publishes a thesis update naming a narrower vertical, that is the **Slash** (2024) shape and I re-engage. Silence today is not disqualifying; silence in twelve months is.

Required-capital profile -- single-anchor parallel. Compare Hexa to **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019), where I led a $115M Series A because the SPARC tokamak roadmap demanded that capital depth or it would not run. Hexa's roadmap, as exposed by $9K MRR across three customers and a Calendly-only GTM, is the inverse -- a company that needs $3-5M to reach $1M ARR and then a Series A on multiples. I am over-resourced for this deal by an order of magnitude; any seed fund writes this check more efficiently than Khosla Ventures does, and the deal does not get the kind of concentrated conviction I write when the capital is the moat.

My 36-month forecast for Hexa: acquired by Epicor, Acumatica, or a mid-market ERP integrator for $20-50M as a feature tuck-in, OR dead, after Didero's $30M Series A and the inevitable native-agent release from NetSuite collapse the third-party overlay's pricing power below the $3K/customer/month implied by current MRR. The mechanism is straightforward -- when consensus capital fills a category at 37x your war chest, the third-party overlay either gets bought as a feature or starves; and when the ERP incumbents ship native agents (Tenable shipping its own "Hexa AI" engine in March 2026 is the indicative pattern, even at a different product), the substitution path closes from above.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **DoorDash** (2013) exception -- a capital-light consumer-logistics bet that violated my "science risk" rhetoric and returned a $72B IPO because the founder ambition and consumer-commerce reshaping frame overrode the dimension gaps. Could Hexa be the DoorDash of industrial distribution? The dossier evidence kills it: DoorDash had Tony Xu's documented founder ambition and a multi-sided network effect that compounded with every restaurant added. Hexa has no founder-ambition signal in the dossier, no network effect (each ERP integration is a one-to-one workflow graph that does not compound across customers), and the comparable competitor Didero is not absent -- it is funded at 37x scale with the same ICP. The one verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Hexa demonstrates a proprietary catalog/pricing graph spanning >50 distributors that competitors cannot replicate without re-integrating every ERP from scratch, AND Makkar publicly commits to a labor-displacement frame with named GDP anchors. Until then, this is the 30-second pass.
