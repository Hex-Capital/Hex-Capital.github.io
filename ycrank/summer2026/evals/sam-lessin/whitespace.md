# Whitespace -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Whitespace is a two-person London team whose founders "grew up in families running wholesale distribution businesses" pitching a "horizontal multi-agent OS" against Pepper's $128M and Choco's $301M war chests -- which makes Whitespace an operator-credentialed-but-invisible-online pre-seed where the only thing I can genuinely underwrite is a domestic anecdote, and the rest of the dossier is the canonical YC AI-agent template I named in my Pass Example almost verbatim. Alex Tung and Leon Yao have a real seat at the family dinner table; they also have zero Twitter, zero GitHub, zero press, and a homepage whose three "customer" logos (Mercer & Co., Halton Foods, Brightway Retail) the dossier flags as demo data. That pairing -- live domain context, dead public surface -- is the signature, and it tells me what kind of bet Whitespace actually is: a quiet acqui-hire candidate for Epicor or Infor, not a regatta I can sail.

On founder conviction the load-bearing rung is **Drop.io** in 2007 -- I was bootstrapping a file-sharing primitive against free incumbents because I personally would not be talked out of the take, and the public artifacts of that obsession existed before any check arrived. Whitespace's founders share the "lived inside the problem" mechanism through the family-distribution anecdote, which is the single dossier specific that lifts them off the floor of this dimension; what they do not share is the Drop.io evidence trail. Alex Tung's last role was Chief of Staff / Head of GTM at Ankar AI -- a useful B2B sales motion, not a category obsession -- and Leon Yao's identity isn't even definitively confirmed against InstaDeep in the dossier. There is no opinionated essay, no founder thread, no "what if they're right" non-obvious claim I can quote back at them. The family backstory is real, but it is doing the work of a sentence, not the work of three years of public writing.

On founder-as-brand the failure rung is the lifestyle-vlogger-with-no-business-system the Slow Creator Fund was built to avoid -- distribution exists nominally but isn't ownable into a cap table. Whitespace doesn't even reach that rung: there is no Twitter account for either founder, no GitHub, no Product Hunt, no LinkedIn follower count retrievable, no Discord, no newsletter. Whitespace will acquire every customer through outbound or YC demo-day handshake, which means every dollar of growth has a CAC line attached and none of it compounds outside the round. The Mogilko deal worked because the asset was the person and the person owned the audience for free; Whitespace has neither half of that, and the absence is rock-bottom on the axis where the entire Slow Creator Fund thesis lives.

The round dynamics are where Whitespace fails the Pass Example test almost line-for-line. Over 18 months the YC S26 batch will be priced into the same consensus AI-agent SAFE band as S25 and W26; "AI Operating System for Wholesale Distributors" is the deck title I wrote myself as the pattern to deselect on, and there is no signal in the dossier -- no named tier-1 pass, no unusual instrument, no sub-batch-median price -- to tell me Whitespace is the exception. The compounding direction here is wrong: each tack ahead will be priced against Pepper's Series C and Choco's $1.2B mark, not against a two-person London seed.

For capital structure, the counterfactual that would flip Whitespace one tier is concrete: the founders show me a revenue-share instrument tied to a distributor's recovered margin (the 1% claim on the homepage), or a holdco shape that lets Whitespace acquire a small distributor outright and run the AI internally à la **Teamshares**. Neither is on offer. What is on offer is usage-based SaaS pricing tied to "workflows automated" against an undisclosed dollar figure, the default cap table, and ERP-integration burn that for a two-person team will demand a seed extension before any reliable connector count exists. Standard SaaS shape, standard dilution path, standard five-rounds-to-default-alive trajectory.

Category-wise, Whitespace lives in the exact sleeve I publicly de-rate. **Sublime Security** worked because enterprise email-security was pre-consensus and the late-stage SOC SaaS crowd had stopped underwriting seed; Whitespace's wholesale-distribution-AI sleeve is the opposite -- Proton.ai raised $24M in 2022, Pepper raised $50M Series C in February 2026, Choco has been a unicorn since 2022. "AI is just more internet," and Whitespace is the most internet-shaped version of that bet: horizontal AI agents over a vertical with three funded incumbents already shipping the same workflow surface.

My 36-month forecast: Whitespace gets acqui-hired by Epicor, Infor, or NetSuite for $15-40M as a connector + agent team, or stalls below $2M ARR after a seed extension while Pepper and Choco expand horizontally into Whitespace's mid-market UK distributors. The mechanism is straightforward -- two founders, no public surface, no pricing edge, no instrument edge, against vertical incumbents with two-to-three orders of magnitude more capital -- and the family-business domain context is exactly the kind of credential an ERP M&A team will pay a modest premium for and a venture round cannot underwrite to a fund-returner.

The strongest bull case sounds like this: Whitespace's "grew up in distribution families" detail is the rare founder-market-fit anchor I tell myself I should care about, wholesale distribution is precisely the unsexy "lifestyle business" category Sand Hill dismisses, and ERP integration depth is the kind of switching-cost moat that compounds quietly over five years into a Squarespace-grade outcome -- exactly the "billion dollar company that raised very little" archetype I cite on the SafeGraph podcast. The case stings because it is structurally my own framework pointed back at me. It loses on two facts the dossier won't let me ignore: Pepper and Choco between them have raised $429M against this exact buyer persona, and the round on offer is a YC AI-agent SAFE, not the bootstrapped-into-venture-moment shape that makes Squarespace-style outcomes investable for me. No Known Exception applies -- Whitespace isn't Robinhood-style accidental access, isn't Pinterest-style operator-friend sourcing, isn't Ro-style regulatory moat. The single condition that would flip Whitespace to Invest: Alex Tung publishes an opinionated long-form piece naming the specific reason agents fail inside Epicor and Infor today, and the next round prices at a visible discount to S26 median. Until then, Whitespace is a pass.
