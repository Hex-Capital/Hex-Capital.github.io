# Alt-X -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the entire story before I even get to the founder. Endex has $14M from OpenAI's Startup Fund. Shortcut has $78M+ from Sequoia at a $300M+ valuation. Datarails has $175M raised and is approaching $100M in revenue. Apers is targeting the identical real estate underwriting niche. And Alt-X is a two-person pre-seed company whose differentiation is "we do the same Excel AI thing but specifically for alternative assets." When the market is this hot and this well-capitalized, the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged away. You're not buying non-consensus conviction -- you're buying a ticket to a knife fight where the other fighters brought machine guns. This is a sane person with an aggressively sane idea, which is the configuration my framework specifically screens out.

Ryan Samadi is genuinely credible -- Stanford CS with an AI track, Citadel Global Equities, BofA fixed income, PE at IIFG, and CIO of Stanford's student hedge fund. He's lived inside the pain point. That's the "sane" part working beautifully. But the idea of using AI to automate financial modeling in Excel is the single most consensus application of AI in fintech right now. Every VC I talk to wants to fund this exact category. When I described Brian Armstrong as "sane person, insane idea," the insane part was that almost nobody in Silicon Valley took Bitcoin seriously in 2012. Here, everyone takes Excel automation seriously in 2026. The non-consensus element is completely absent.

Now let me run the test that matters most to me: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? The answer is catastrophically clear -- this business IS software, and specifically software built on top of foundation models that are improving generically. Alt-X's "moat" is domain-specific extraction models for alternative-asset KPIs. But GPT-5 or whatever comes next will extract cap rates and NOI from CIMs without domain-specific training. Microsoft Copilot is already expanding inside Excel. The entire value proposition is a feature waiting to be absorbed -- either by a foundation model provider, by Microsoft itself, or by one of the better-funded competitors who can add alternative-asset templates in a quarter. A $30/month SaaS subscription is the business model I've spent two years publicly arguing is dead. This company is the embodiment of what I mean when I say software is a business tool, not a business model.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Alt-X builds deeply specialized extraction pipelines for alternative-asset underwriting -- the kind of domain specificity that handles the messy reality of CIMs from different sponsors, non-standard RE proformas, bespoke PE waterfall structures -- there's a world where general-purpose Excel AI tools can't match the accuracy. Family offices and mid-market PE firms are genuinely underserved by Bloomberg-tier products, and the pain point is viscerally real. If Samadi's Citadel-trained eye for what investment committees actually need translates into product decisions that competitors miss, Alt-X could carve out a defensible niche. And at $30/month entry with upsell tiers for teams, bottom-up analyst adoption could work if the product genuinely saves 30 hours per deal. The Datarails trajectory -- $175M raised, $550M valuation, approaching $100M revenue -- proves there's a large market for Excel-native financial tools. But even in this optimistic scenario, the outcome is a nice vertical SaaS business in a competitive market, not a venture-scale return. There's no protocol layer here, no new primitive being created, no compounding network effect. It's a tool on someone else's platform.

This is an application stacked on top of two platforms it doesn't control -- Microsoft Excel and foundation models from OpenAI/Anthropic/Google. That's the opposite of the protocol-layer position I look for. Venmo created a new payment primitive. Solana created a new consensus layer. Even Craftwork, which is a painting company, owns the physical operations that software can't replicate. Alt-X owns nothing structural. Every layer of the stack -- the LLMs, the Excel runtime, the financial data -- belongs to someone else. The narrative could theoretically expand from "alternative asset underwriting" to "all institutional financial modeling," but Shortcut already claimed that narrative at $300M+ and has $78M to execute against it. The infinity story is foreclosed by competitors who got there first with vastly more capital. I don't see a path where my money produces differentiated returns here. Pass.
