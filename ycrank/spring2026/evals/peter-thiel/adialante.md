# Adialante -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

MRI technology has been frozen for forty years. Siemens, GE, and Philips have maintained an oligopoly built on multi-million-dollar systems requiring specialized rooms, liquid helium cooling, and dedicated operators — a paradigm so calcified that the word "innovation" in this space has meant marginally wider bores and faster pulse sequences, not architectural reinvention. When a category has been structurally unchanged since the 1980s, and the incumbents cannot disrupt themselves without destroying their capital equipment sales and service contract annuities, the conditions for a genuine technological discontinuity exist. Adialante's FREE technology — eliminating B0 gradient coils entirely through a novel frequency-modulated encoding approach — represents exactly the kind of physics-level rethinking that makes the old paradigm structurally obsolete rather than incrementally worse. If this works at clinical imaging quality, it is not a cheaper MRI. It is a different machine built on different physics, and the distinction matters enormously.

But here is where my framework becomes unforgiving: the secret must create a *monopoly*, not merely a correct insight deployed into a competitive market. And the competitive landscape around low-cost MRI is precisely what I warn against. Promaxo has FDA clearance, 69 installed systems, and $63-81 million in funding — already building the clinical evidence base for in-office prostate MRI. Hyperfine is publicly traded with $13.6 million in annual revenue and shipping units. Chipiron raised $17 million for its own alternative MRI physics. Synaptive has $35 million in revenue and 100+ installations. Five well-funded companies pursuing the same macro thesis — "democratize MRI access" — is the textbook condition under which there is no secret at the market level, regardless of how clever the underlying physics might be. The existence of a competitive landscape slide comparing these players is itself the clearest signal that this market has already attracted the consensus. Adialante's technical approach is genuinely distinct from its competitors, but technical distinctness does not automatically produce monopoly. Multiple different roads can lead to the same commodity destination.

The strongest bull case requires separating the technical secret from the market thesis. The market thesis — cheap MRI for everyone — is consensus. But the *technical* secret — that gradient-free encoding via FREE can achieve clinical-quality cancer screening at 1/10th the size and cost — is not consensus, and the MRI physics establishment has not validated it at production scale. If FREE produces images sufficient for prostate cancer screening while eliminating a third of the conventional hardware stack, then Adialante possesses a proprietary technology moat that Promaxo's miniaturized-but-architecturally-conventional approach cannot match. Add the per-scan mobile deployment model — which creates zero capital risk for urology clinics and builds an operational moat through fleet management, scheduling infrastructure, and multi-site regulatory compliance — and you have a potential path to monopolizing the *access layer* of cancer screening MRI rather than competing in the hardware market. This is the Palantir pattern applied to medical imaging: embed so deeply in clinical workflows through operational integration that displacement requires rebuilding the entire service layer, not just the machine. The ISMRM Rabi Award winner on the team, the NSF SBIR Phase II validation, the peer-reviewed publications — these are real signals of physics credibility. If the FDA clears this device, the regulatory barrier becomes a time-based moat that compounds annually.

What gives me pause beyond the competitive landscape is the gap between the team's technical depth and their commercial readiness. Torres and Jenkins invented the core technology during their graduate research at UMN's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research — they *are* the technology, which is valuable. But the transition from academic laboratory to commercial medical device company operating mobile imaging fleets across multiple states is not a technical challenge; it is an operational and commercial challenge of a fundamentally different character. There is no evidence of prior startup experience, healthcare operations expertise, or distribution sophistication on this team. The "Get Early Access" sign-up on the website is not a distribution strategy — it is the absence of one. I see a team that has solved a hard physics problem and assumed the commercial problem would follow. That assumption has killed more technically brilliant companies than bad physics ever has. "If you've invented something new but you haven't invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business" — and I see no evidence that selling has received anything close to the attention that inventing has.

The atoms-level innovation here is real, and it activates my deepest conviction about where technological progress actually matters. We wanted flying cars and instead got social media; Adialante is building a new kind of machine that could bring cancer detection to communities that have never had access to MRI. That mission-technology alignment, grounded in genuine physics rather than software abstraction, is rare. But a genuine secret about the physical world, deployed into a market where five other companies are also deploying their own secrets about cheaper imaging, does not automatically produce monopoly. The power-law calculation is further constrained by medical device economics — even the most successful MRI company is unlikely to produce the 100x return that my framework demands. I would watch this company through FDA clearance and first commercial deployments. If the images are clinically equivalent and the mobile model achieves organic pull from urology practices — the equivalent of eBay sellers adding PayPal logos unprompted — then the monopoly thesis strengthens considerably. Today, the physics is compelling but the path from physics breakthrough to market monopoly remains undrawn.
