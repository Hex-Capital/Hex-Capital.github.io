# Ndea -- Elad Gil Evaluation

François Chollet is one of maybe five people on Earth with the credibility to start this company. He created the benchmark that defines AGI progress, built one of the most widely used deep learning frameworks, and spent nine years inside Google studying exactly why current AI systems fail at generalization. Mike Knoop scaled Zapier to millions of users and knows how to build a company. The founder credentials here are extraordinary — genuinely in the top fraction of a percent of founding teams I've seen. And that's precisely what makes this evaluation harder, because my framework starts with market structure, not founder pedigree. I've watched great founders get crushed by a market that isn't ready or doesn't exist. The question for Ndea is whether the market is real, and whether it's real now.

The structural moment Ndea is riding is the ARC-AGI 2024 competition result: combining deep learning with program synthesis pushed scores from 33% to 55.5% on a benchmark that specifically measures the kind of fluid reasoning current LLMs can't do. That's a genuine capability inflection — it's empirical evidence that program synthesis works, not just theoretical argument. Ndea's own framing is revealing, though: they describe program synthesis in 2025 as being where "deep learning was circa 2012." In 2012, deep learning had immediate commercial applications — image recognition, speech processing — that could generate revenue within 12-18 months. When I backed Harvey, the miracle was model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy, and the foundation model labs were delivering that miracle on a visible timeline. Program synthesis in 2025 has benchmark scores on an academic test. The "market" is AGI itself, which makes TAM analysis meaningless — you can't size a market that doesn't have products yet. This isn't "too boring" or "too niche," which are the non-obvious market archetypes where I've found the best returns. It's "too far from commercial reality," which is a different and more dangerous category.

My single-miracle framework breaks hard against this company. Ndea needs: (1) program synthesis to scale from constrained benchmarks to real-world AGI-level capability — completely unproven, (2) translation of fundamental research breakthroughs into commercially viable products — no pricing model, no GTM strategy, no product defined, and (3) defense of that position against frontier labs with 100-1000x more capital who can redirect resources the moment program synthesis proves viable. That's three sequential low-probability events. When I ask "what's the one miracle?", the answer has to be "program synthesis achieves AGI" — but even if that miracle happens, the path from breakthrough to revenue requires additional miracles that have nothing to do with research capability. OpenAI spent years as a research lab before finding a commercial model. DeepMind required Google's balance sheet for a decade. The history of pure AI research labs monetizing their work is not encouraging for a $43M company.

The distribution dimension is where I see the starkest absence. There is no product. No pricing page. No defined customer segment. No articulated go-to-market strategy. The stated mission — "a factory for rapid scientific advancement" — is aspirational, not operational. Chollet's 617K Twitter followers and the company's podcast are awareness channels for recruiting, not product distribution. Compare this to how Stripe thought about distribution from day one — the developer-first API was a distribution strategy embedded in the product architecture itself. Or how Harvey built enterprise workflow tools alongside the AI research, positioning for distribution the moment model fidelity arrived. Ndea has explicitly chosen pure research first, commercialization later. That's a legitimate choice, but it's not the pattern I invest behind. The founders who build generational companies think about distribution from their earliest days, even when the product is still forming.

The strongest bull case is the Airbnb exception: sometimes the market doesn't exist yet because the founder is creating it. If Chollet is right that program synthesis is the architectural path to AGI and transformer scaling hits diminishing returns, then Ndea is positioned at the single most consequential inflection point in technology history. The $43M from Coatue, Factorial, and Quiet Capital reflects real institutional conviction. The team of 15 assembled in under a year suggests genuine momentum. And the structural vacancy argument has merit — every major lab has committed billions to transformer architectures, creating organizational inertia that makes pivoting to program synthesis costly and politically difficult. A focused startup with the right team could outrun them on this specific paradigm. If program synthesis is where deep learning was in 2012, then a $43M bet here is the equivalent of backing Hinton's lab before AlexNet. The returns in that scenario are essentially unbounded.

But I'm not the right investor for this bet. My framework evaluates what structural change makes a market possible now — not in five years, not after multiple research breakthroughs, but now. The technology cycle positioning here looks more like 2015 VR/AR than 2012 deep learning: a real capability improvement that's years away from commercial viability. The absence of any distribution thinking, the multi-miracle requirement, the undefined revenue model, and the capital asymmetry with frontier labs all point in the same direction. This is a Vinod Khosla bet — "how big is the consequence if this works?" — not an Elad Gil bet, which requires evidence that the adoption curve is inflecting into a commercial opportunity today. Exceptional founders, but the market's structural moment hasn't arrived.
