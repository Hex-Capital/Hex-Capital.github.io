# Clara -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps off the page here is that George Favvas already built a telehealth company — Circle Medical, YC S15 — to a $100M revenue run rate with profitability. That is an extraordinarily rare credential at any stage, let alone pre-seed. When I look at the earliest rounds of companies that became huge, the single strongest signal was always the founders. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." Favvas isn't just a good person — he's someone who has already navigated the entire treacherous obstacle course of healthcare delivery once: the state-by-state licensing, the insurance billing, the clinical hiring, the regulatory compliance. And now he's back with two co-founders he worked alongside at Circle Medical, plus a designer who shipped product to 40M users at Careem and worked on Instagram. The co-founder relationship here is battle-tested in exactly the domain they're entering. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." These three have already been through the sock-pulling machine together.

What makes Clara interesting to me — and what I think most investors will underweight — is the schlep. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." I wrote about this in the context of Stripe: payments processing sat there unsolved for a decade because the schlep of dealing with banks and compliance was so intimidating that smart people's minds just slid off it. Healthcare primary care delivery is arguably the biggest schlep in the entire economy. State licensing. Insurance credentialing. Clinical liability. HIPAA. Medical records integration across 150,000 facilities. Any sane person would build a SaaS tool instead. The fact that this team has already built the operational infrastructure to do all of this at Circle Medical — and is now proposing to rebuild it with AI at the center rather than human clinicians — means they're attacking from a position that would take a new entrant years to reach. That operational knowledge is the moat, not the AI model itself.

The idea itself has the right shape. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." An "AI primary care doctor" sounds dangerous, possibly illegal, certainly scary to most people. That's exactly the reaction Facebook got — "a site for college students to waste time? It seemed the perfect bad idea." The regulatory tension in Clara's positioning — marketing as an "AI doctor" while disclaiming it's "not medical advice" — is going to make cautious investors run. But the insight underneath is sound: there aren't enough primary care physicians, the shortage is getting worse (86,000 by 2036), and LLMs have crossed a threshold where they can do the data synthesis and clinical reasoning that consumes most of a 15-minute visit. The human clinician becomes a reviewer rather than the bottleneck. This inverts the model in a way that incumbents like Teladoc literally cannot adopt without cannibalizing their per-visit economics. Carbon Health's bankruptcy in February 2026 is a signal that the old capital-intensive model is breaking.

Now let me be honest about what worries me. The dual commitment question is real. Favvas is listed as CEO of Circle Medical — a WELL Health subsidiary doing $100M+ in revenue — and CEO of Clara. I don't know the details of his transition, but "starting a startup is too hard for one person," and it's certainly too hard if your attention is divided. If he's fully committed to Clara, this concern evaporates. If he's straddling both, it's a serious problem. I'd want to look him in the eye and ask. The competitive landscape is also formidable: K Health at $441M raised, Hippocratic AI at $404M and a $3.5B valuation. But I've learned over and over that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." K Health is human-first with AI triage — structurally different. Hippocratic AI sells B2B to health systems — different customer, different motion. Neither is doing what Clara is doing, which is the full DTC AI-first primary care stack.

The absence of any public traction data doesn't particularly concern me at this stage. They have two founding engineer job postings — they're building. At pre-seed with a Spring 2026 YC batch, I wouldn't expect metrics yet. What I'd expect is exactly what I see: a team that has done this before, attacking the same problem with a fundamentally better architecture enabled by a technology shift. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." If Clara can get a few hundred patients who love the experience — who feel like they finally have a doctor who knows their full history and is available when they need one — the word-of-mouth flywheel in healthcare is extraordinarily powerful. "If you pay a ridiculous amount of attention to your tiny initial set of customers, ideally you'll kick off exponential growth by word of mouth."

The bull case is straightforward: this is a repeat founder with deep domain expertise, a proven co-founder team, attacking an enormous market with structural tailwinds, using an approach that incumbents cannot copy without self-harm, at the exact moment when the enabling technology has matured. If Favvas is fully committed and the regulatory environment doesn't shut down AI-first care models entirely, this has the profile of a company that could define the category. The combination of operational healthcare expertise and AI-native architecture is genuinely rare. I'd want the meeting. I'd want to understand the Circle Medical transition. But on paper, this team passes every founder test I know how to run.
