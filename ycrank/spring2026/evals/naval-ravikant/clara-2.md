# Clara -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

George Favvas already built this company once. Circle Medical was the manual version -- human clinicians delivering telehealth primary care. He scaled it to $100M revenue with profitability. That's not a resume credential. That's a founder who spent a decade accumulating specific knowledge about every operational bottleneck in healthcare delivery: state licensing, insurance billing, EHR integration, clinician workflows, patient retention. He hit the structural ceiling -- you cannot scale a clinician-dependent model without proportional headcount -- and now he's rebuilding with AI as the primary reasoning layer. This is the pattern I find most compelling in founder-specific knowledge: someone who built the manual version, understood why it breaks, and returned to rebuild it with the correct leverage architecture. The three co-founders all worked together at Circle Medical. This isn't a team assembled for a pitch -- it's a team that has already shipped the hard version of this problem together.

The leverage audit reveals a genuinely interesting structure, but also its main limitation. Clara inverts the telehealth model: AI performs clinical reasoning and data synthesis, human clinicians review and approve. This exploits the exact principal-agent dynamic I look for. Teladoc, K Health, and every hospital system are agents of their per-visit revenue model -- an AI-first approach that reduces billable clinician encounters would cannibalize their core economics. They cannot adopt Clara's architecture without destroying what pays their employees. That structural misalignment is a real moat, arguably more durable than any technical advantage. But the human-in-the-loop requirement means this is not pure permissionless leverage. Every prescription, every lab order needs a licensed clinician's sign-off. The question is the multiplier: if AI makes each clinician 15-20x more productive rather than 2-3x, the economics shift from "slightly better telehealth" to "structurally different business." The records integration from 150,000+ facilities creates a compounding data advantage per patient -- switching costs increase with longitudinal history, which is the compound interest test in action.

The principal authenticity question has a genuine crack. The dossier notes Favvas is listed as CEO of both Circle Medical and Clara. Circle Medical is a WELL Health subsidiary doing $100M+ revenue. If he's still operationally running both, that's the one thing I screen hardest for -- "I almost won't invest in someone if they aren't fully into it." A part-time founder is an agent. But the counter-signal is strong: he brought his clinical lead and his product designer with him. People don't leave a profitable $100M company to join a pre-seed startup unless the principal is all in. The team composition itself is the authenticity signal -- Swift brings the clinical knowledge of what the AI needs to replicate, Ahmed brings consumer product design from Careem and Instagram. Each person's specific knowledge maps to a core function. If any one of them left, that function would degrade.

The bull case that would make this a portfolio-defining investment: AI clinical reasoning has crossed the threshold where one nurse practitioner reviewing AI-drafted care plans can handle 10x the patient load of a traditional telehealth visit. Clara becomes the operating system for primary care -- continuous, longitudinal, available to anyone with a phone. Primary care access in America is structurally gatekept by physician scarcity (86,000 projected shortage by 2036), and Clara removes that gatekeeper the way AngelList removed the gatekeeper between founders and capital. If the AI leverage ratio is high enough, this is a platform that democratizes access to something hundreds of millions of people want but cannot currently get. Carbon Health's bankruptcy this February signals the asset-heavy model is dying -- the timing for an AI-native replacement is now.

The risk I weight most heavily isn't the well-funded competitors -- K Health's $441M and Hippocratic AI's $404M make them agents of their existing architectures. The real risk is that the AI reasoning layer is commodity. Clara is likely calling the same foundation models every other health AI startup uses. The differentiation is entirely in the clinical operations wrapper -- licensing, prescribing authority, insurance billing, records integration. That's specific knowledge productized, which is defensible. But it's not zero-marginal-cost defensibility. It's operational knowledge that requires ongoing human infrastructure to maintain. Nine people at pre-seed, with clinical staff in the mix, tells me this will need 50+ employees well before $50M revenue. That's not a Cursor-shaped company. It's a better-leveraged healthcare company, which is a different category of return.

Specific knowledge density is high. Leverage architecture is real but structurally capped by human review requirements. The access democratization pattern is one of the strongest I've seen in this batch -- primary care is genuinely gatekept, and AI-first delivery could unlock it for millions. I'd invest, but with clear eyes that this is a leverage-constrained healthcare company, not a pure software play. The compound interest on the patient data flywheel and the operational knowledge moat are what make the bet asymmetric.
