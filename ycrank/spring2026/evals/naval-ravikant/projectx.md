# ProjectX -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founding team restructured. Three of four original co-founders -- Das, Banerjee, Kuncham -- are gone between the 2024 launch and the 2026 YC application. At pre-seed, losing seventy-five percent of your founding team isn't a personnel adjustment. It's information. "Play long-term games with long-term people" is the principle I apply most ruthlessly, and a company that has already cycled through its co-founder roster before raising a real round raises the question: what happened? The replacement team may be stronger. But the pattern itself signals something about alignment, vision disputes, or founder-problem commitment that I can't ignore. When I invested in Twitter, Ev Williams had been circling the same problem for a decade. When three of four people building your company leave within two years, I want to understand what conviction held and what broke.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Rounak Adhikary has a BSc in Interaction Design from Lovely Professional University, founded a consulting company at nineteen, and has accumulated an impressive set of accelerator stamps -- Draper, IIM Bangalore, Stanford ASES. That's entrepreneurial energy, not domain knowledge. GPU virtualization, low-latency streaming protocols, container orchestration with sub-3-second cold starts -- this is deep systems engineering at the kernel and driver level. Where did the founders accumulate non-teachable knowledge about this? The CTO, Bishal Karmakar, has the right language stack -- Rust, Go, C++ -- but general systems programming competence is exactly the kind of skill that can be taught and trained. The team acknowledges the gap implicitly by recruiting from NVIDIA and CISCO, but those are hires, not founders. When I backed Replit, Amjad Masad had spent years at Facebook and Mozilla building developer tools -- he understood the problem space at a level no outsider could replicate. Here, I see a team that identified a real problem and is working hard to solve it, but I don't see the obsessive, lived-in understanding of GPU compute architecture that would make them the inevitable team to build this.

The leverage architecture is theoretically interesting but practically constrained. Per-app GPU streaming in browser tabs -- one platform, millions of users, no local hardware -- sounds like software leverage. But underneath, you're purchasing and operating GPU infrastructure for every active session. That's not code-as-leverage; that's a cloud provider with a better abstraction layer. Shadow PC raised $105-169M and still charges $38-55/month because the unit economics of streaming GPU compute are fundamentally capital-intensive. The ninety-five companies tracked in cloud GPU platforms, collectively raising $20.8B, aren't all wrong about the capital requirements. This is closer to Opendoor -- where algorithmic pricing tried to create technological leverage in an inherently capital-heavy business -- than to Notion, where one engineer's work serves millions at near-zero marginal cost. I made the Opendoor exception because the founding team had extraordinary specific knowledge. Here, neither the leverage structure nor the founder profile reaches that bar.

The bull case deserves engagement. Rs 20 Cr+ (~$2.4M) in signed LOIs and a pilot with IIT Bombay from a team with $204K in funding is remarkable hustle. If they've genuinely achieved sub-3-second cold starts for GPU-accelerated applications in browser tabs, that's non-trivial systems engineering regardless of credentials. The insight that hyperscalers won't build per-app streaming because it cannibalizes their higher-margin VM business is structurally sound -- the same logic that made Uber possible despite every taxi company having cars. And India's compute-access gap for seventy-five percent of students is a genuine, massive, underserved market that a team from West Bengal understands viscerally. If the technology works, the distribution in Indian education alone could build a meaningful business. What would have to be true: the CTO has quietly accumulated deep GPU streaming expertise not visible in public profiles, the founding team restructuring was addition-by-subtraction that strengthened alignment, and the per-app abstraction creates enough switching costs that hyperscaler competition is slow to materialize. That's a plausible universe -- but it requires me to infer specific knowledge rather than observe it.

The timing isn't contrarian enough. Cloud streaming has been attempted repeatedly -- OnLive, Google Stadia, GeForce Now. The smart critique isn't "this can't work" but "the economics don't work at scale." Per-app streaming is a genuine architectural twist, but the underlying bet -- that GPU cost curves will decline fast enough to make streaming profitable -- is the same bet ninety-five funded competitors are making. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, the consensus was "this is a toy." When I invested in Uber, the consensus was "this is illegal." Here, the consensus is "cloud GPU is the future" -- the disagreement is only about who captures the value. That's a competitive execution race, not a contrarian positioning advantage. You don't get paid a thousand-to-one for being right about something everyone already believes.

I pass. The founders have energy and ambition, but I can't identify the non-transferable knowledge that makes this team uniquely suited to solve GPU streaming at the systems level. The capital intensity works against my leverage framework. The founding team instability is a concern I weight heavily. And the market is hot, not contrarian. The LOIs and the IIT Bombay pilot are real signals of hustle, but hustle without specific knowledge is a race that better-capitalized teams usually win.
