# RoboDock -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about RoboDock is that both founders were already building charging systems for autonomous machines before they started a company about it. Zinny Weli designed the autonomous charging infrastructure for Zipline's drone fleet and built the charging system for Amazon's Astro robot. She also built EV battery swap stations for motorcycle taxi drivers in Rwanda. Celine Wang integrated actuator and sensor systems for autonomous semi-trucks at Plus, then did field robotics in rural Indonesia and wildlife reserves in China. These aren't people who read a McKinsey report about autonomous vehicles and thought "depots seem like a gap." They were literally inside the charging problem — multiple times, across drones, home robots, trucks, and motorcycles — and they saw the same pattern every time: the vehicle can drive itself, but nobody's automated what happens when it stops. That's how organic problem discovery works. You don't find the idea; the idea finds you because you keep bumping into it.

The schlep here is real and ugly. Robotic charging in a depot involves high-voltage electrical systems, physical connector manipulation with centimeter-level precision, thermal sensing for safety, computer vision in dirty industrial environments, fleet orchestration software, and eventually safety certification. It's the kind of problem where you need to understand mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, computer vision, and field robotics simultaneously — and then you have to physically install and maintain hardware at every location. Most founders who think about autonomous vehicles think about the driving. Nobody wants to think about the depot. It's the backstage work. This is the pattern I called schlep blindness: the problem is obvious once you name it — of course autonomous vehicles need autonomous depots — but the multi-disciplinary complexity makes most people unconsciously look away.

The concerning signal is Rocsys. They've raised $42.7 million, launched a commercial product in April 2025, and have been iterating on robotic EV charging for years. In hardware, head starts compound differently than in software — each deployed system generates physical-world data and operational lessons you can't replicate in simulation. RoboDock's argument is that Rocsys does only charging, while RoboDock does charging plus inspection as a full depot automation platform. That's a reasonable differentiation, but it's still a narrow niche with a well-funded incumbent. The other risk is structural: the largest potential customers — Waymo (Alphabet), Tesla, Zoox (Amazon) — are all divisions of companies with massive robotics capabilities and the resources to build depot automation in-house. Tesla already has a "Snake Charger." If the top three customers build internally, the addressable market shrinks to mid-tier fleet operators who may not have enough depots to justify the economics.

The bull case is that RoboDock is positioned like a picks-and-shovels play for the AV gold rush — every autonomous fleet needs depot infrastructure regardless of who wins the driving problem. If the AV market really does grow from $200 billion to $3 trillion, manual depots become an absurd bottleneck. The fleet-agnostic angle matters because the future likely involves mixed-brand depots managed by third parties, not every OEM running its own facility. And these founders are as technically qualified for this specific problem as any two people I could imagine — Stanford robotics, Zipline, Amazon Lab126, Plus. If RoboDock gets a single major depot pilot running and shows that their combined charging-plus-inspection system meaningfully outperforms human crews, the unit economics ($1.2M recovered per depot annually) could drive rapid enterprise adoption. The timing angle is also strong: Waymo just expanded to multiple cities, Tesla's robotaxi is launching, and these fleets are hitting the scale where manual depot operations become the binding constraint.

What worries me most is that I see no evidence anyone is actually using this yet. No named customers, no pilots, no LOIs, no revenue — just modeled projections on a website. The problem is well-articulated, the founders are well-qualified, but "make something people want" requires evidence of wanting. Eleven YC Launch upvotes and one German press article don't tell me whether any fleet operator has looked at this and said "yes, we need this now." At pre-seed I don't expect much, but I'd feel better seeing even a single handshake agreement with a depot operator. The co-founder relationship is solid — they've known each other seven years from Stanford, which avoids the "met last month to start a company" failure mode. And the team is lean, which is right for this stage. But hardware companies burn cash faster than software companies, and a two-person team deploying physical robotic systems across multiple depots is going to need capital and bodies quickly.

This is a company with genuinely strong organic problem discovery and a massive schlep, attached to founders with precisely the right technical background. The gap is that I can't yet see evidence of user pull, and the competitive landscape — Rocsys ahead, OEMs potentially building in-house — creates execution risk that's higher than I'd like. I'd want to talk to these founders and understand whether any depot operator has shown real purchase intent. The pieces are all in the right place; I just don't have the signal yet that someone on the other side is reaching for this product.
