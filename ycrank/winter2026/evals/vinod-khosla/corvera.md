# Corvera -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with the question I always start with: if Corvera succeeds completely — dominates its market, becomes the operating system for every CPG brand under $500M — what changes? Brands process orders faster. Inventory gets rebalanced automatically. Purchase orders go out without a human checking a spreadsheet. That is a good business. It is not a transformation of how the world works. The $78 billion in "wasted operational time" that Corvera cites is real, but eliminating waste inside an existing system is fundamentally different from replacing the system. Nobody's building a new market here. Nobody's making an incumbent approach obsolete. They're making the current approach run more smoothly. I've seen hundreds of B2B SaaS companies pitch me on making some enterprise workflow 30% more efficient, and my answer is always the same: the consequence of success is inconsequential relative to the talent deployed.

The strongest signal in this dossier is Christopher Kong's founder-market fit — and paradoxically, it's also my biggest concern. Kong ran Better Nature across 5,000+ retail locations in six countries. He lived the operational pain of scaling a CPG brand. He knows exactly which workflows break, which spreadsheets multiply, which emails get lost. That's extraordinary domain expertise. But my entire investment framework says that extraordinary domain expertise is often the enemy of transformational thinking. Insiders know what's broken. They build tools to fix what's broken. Outsiders don't know what's "impossible," so they build something that replaces the broken system entirely. Kong built exactly the product an insider would build: automate the order-to-cash workflow he struggled with at Better Nature. An outsider might have looked at the same industry and asked: why do CPG brands manage supply chains at all? Why not restructure the relationship between production and demand from first principles?

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Kong's network gets him 15 design partners and 50+ pipeline brands before the product is mature — that's real pull, not manufactured demand. The team composition is genuinely well-assembled: a CPG operator, a Google AI/data lead who built multi-agent systems, a Princeton-trained product leader. Breeuwer's experience building a multi-agent workflow at Google that improved brand compliance review efficiency by 90% maps directly to the technical architecture required. If vertical SaaS for CPG operations can produce a $5-10 billion outcome — and companies like Veeva in pharma suggest it can — then Corvera has the right team, the right timing, and the right initial traction to pursue it. The agentic AI moment is real: LLMs crossed the reliability threshold for document parsing and multi-step workflow automation in 2024-2025, and CPG AI adoption leaped from 42% to 71% in a single year. That's a genuine inflection.

But the technology itself is the problem. Order processing, inventory tracking, demand forecasting — these are well-understood operational problems that the dossier itself acknowledges "rely on generally available LLM capabilities." Crisp has $127 million and just launched an AI Agent Studio. Unioncrate has been in CPG supply chain AI since 2016, backed by Founders Fund. Cin7 processes $20 billion in sales orders. The AI agent layer is the differentiation, but it's a differentiation built on capabilities that every competitor can access. This isn't Juniper building TCP/IP routers when Cisco's CTO said they'd never do it. This is applying 2025's generally available AI to a known problem that well-funded competitors are already attacking. When incumbents and funded competitors validate your approach rather than dismissing it, you're building incrementally, not disruptively.

The timing is good but consensus. Everyone in 2025-2026 is building "agentic" workflows. CPG brands are already adopting AI at 71%. Corvera isn't skating to where the puck will be — the puck is already there, and so are a dozen other players. Compare this to OpenAI in 2019, when I tracked the rate of improvement in AI models before most VCs noticed the curve. Or Impossible Foods in 2010, when I invested in plant-based meat before the category existed. Those were bets placed before the trend became consensus. Corvera is riding a wave that's already breaking.

I respect the team's execution capability and Kong's authentic understanding of the customer. This will likely be a real company that generates real revenue. But I don't invest in real companies that generate real revenue — I invest in companies that, if successful, make the world operate differently. Corvera makes CPG operations smoother. That's important to the brands it serves. It's not important enough for me.
