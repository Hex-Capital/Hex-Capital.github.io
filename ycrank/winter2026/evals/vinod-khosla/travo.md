# Travo -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: four Stanford CS undergrads have chosen to build a better data product for RV park investors. That sentence captures both the strength and the fundamental problem. The strength is technical talent applied to an underserved segment. The problem is that the ambition has been calibrated to a market so small, so manageable, and so safe that even spectacular success yields an inconsequential outcome. If Travo builds the definitive RV park data platform -- captures every customer in the space, achieves perfect data accuracy -- what happens? A few hundred specialized investors get better comps at a lower price than CoStar. The world doesn't change. The real estate industry doesn't restructure. Nobody's life is different. They've found a corner where failure is unlikely and success doesn't matter much.

The bull case requires a wedge theory: start with RV parks, prove that AI-driven data collection is structurally cheaper than CoStar's approach, then expand asset class by asset class until you're a $3 billion revenue competitor. CoStar charges $30,000-$100,000 per year and underserves dozens of niche property types -- the innovator's dilemma is theoretically real. If Travo's AI methodology produces institutional-grade data at one-tenth the cost of manual collection, the expansion path from campgrounds to self-storage to manufactured housing to eventually mainstream CRE becomes plausible. That's a company worth building. But the dossier shows no evidence this wedge theory is articulated, no evidence the AI methodology has been validated against established data sources, and no evidence the team has mapped the expansion path with any specificity. The niche focus reads less like a deliberate beachhead strategy and more like a way to avoid competing with CoStar directly -- which is precisely the risk-reduction behavior that concerns me most.

The technology itself doesn't excite me. AI-driven data scraping and aggregation is 2024's consensus, not 2026's frontier. Every proptech startup in this YC batch and the last three could make the same claim. The dossier acknowledges this: "AI-driven real estate data collection is not inherently defensible as a technology -- any well-funded data company could build similar scraping and analysis pipelines." Where's the 10x structural advantage? CoStar has $3.2 billion in revenue and will integrate LLMs into their data collection within 18 months if they haven't already. CompStak has 30,000 contributing brokers. Cherre raised $130 million for data integration. Travo's differentiation is depth in a niche nobody else bothers to serve -- that's a feature of market size, not technology superiority. I compare this unfavorably to how Square disrupted payment processing: Square didn't just make POS systems cheaper for small merchants, it reimagined what a payment could be. Travo is making CoStar cheaper for a segment CoStar ignores.

On the team: all four founders are Stanford CS undergrads with strong technical credentials -- ML research, satellite code, Roblox engineering, a prior education exit. That's impressive for a dorm room. But the team is entirely homogeneous in background and entirely absent of the domain expertise this venture demands. Building "the most accurate and comprehensive real estate data set" for professional investors requires understanding how real estate valuations actually work, what data sources brokers trust, how zoning and ownership records vary by jurisdiction, and what "institutional-grade" means when a PE firm is deploying $50 million into a campground portfolio. The team has none of this. More troubling, there's no signal they've recruited for it -- no real estate advisor, no industry veteran on the team, no evidence of the gene pool engineering that would address this critical risk. When I backed Pat Brown at Impossible Foods, he was an outsider to the food industry, but he was a world-class biochemist attacking the core scientific problem. These founders are outsiders to real estate without a compensating depth advantage in the core challenge of the business, which is data quality and domain trust.

The timing trajectory is the one dimension where Travo gets partial credit. LLMs have genuinely transformed the economics of data collection from fragmented, unstructured sources, and institutional capital is flowing into alternative real estate asset classes that historically lacked good data infrastructure. That's a real moment. But riding an enabling technology wave is different from being ahead of a curve. Hundreds of startups are applying LLMs to various data aggregation problems right now. Travo isn't skating to where the puck will be -- they're skating to where the puck already is, alongside a crowd.

I pass. The consequence of success is a modestly useful SaaS data tool for a small investor community. The technology is table stakes. The team is talented but unequipped for the domain challenge and shows no contrarian spark -- no willingness to attempt something experts consider impossible. This is a reasonable small business, not a transformational bet. These founders are too smart to spend their twenties making RV park comps slightly more accurate. I'd tell them to find a problem whose consequence of success keeps them up at night.
