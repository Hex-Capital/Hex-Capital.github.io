# Confluence Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Two kids with no funding beat a $232 million company at its own game. That's the signal that stops me cold. Imbue has raised a quarter billion dollars, runs a 10,000 H100 cluster, and achieved 95.1% on ARC-AGI-2. Confluence Labs, two founders and a YC check, hit 97.9%. When resource-starved outsiders outperform well-capitalized incumbents on a capability benchmark, I pay attention -- that asymmetry is exactly what I saw when Pradeep Sindhu at Xerox PARC was building router architectures that Cisco's entire engineering organization said were impossible. The question is whether this benchmark achievement is a Juniper moment -- an early technical demonstration of a paradigm that will reshape an industry -- or whether it's a science fair trophy that doesn't translate to anything that matters economically.

Let me start where I always start: if Confluence Labs succeeds, does it matter? The claim is foundation models that learn from minimal data -- models that can accelerate drug design, materials science, and physics research in domains where a single experiment costs thousands to millions of dollars. If that works, you're attacking the fundamental bottleneck of scientific R&D. Drug discovery costs $2.6 billion per approved compound, and most of that cost is the brute-force search through chemical space with inadequate data. Materials science is still running experiments that take months when the design space is effectively infinite. The consequence of success here is genuinely transformational -- you'd change how humanity does science. That puts this in the top tier of problem magnitude. But I've learned the hard way, at $160 million of my own money with KiOR, that the distance between "the technology demonstrates something impressive" and "the technology creates economic impact at scale" can be infinite. ARC-AGI-2 is abstract grid transformations. Drug design is molecular dynamics, protein-ligand binding, ADMET properties. The leap from one to the other is not engineering -- it's an unsolved scientific question. The company's language about "hypothesis generation" and "data-efficient modeling" for scientific domains is aspiration, not demonstration.

The rate-of-change signal here is genuinely interesting. ARC-AGI-2 scores went from GPT-5.2's 54% to 97.9% in roughly three months. That's an exponential improvement curve in program synthesis for few-shot reasoning -- exactly the kind of trajectory I tracked when I wrote my OpenAI check in 2019. The underlying capabilities these founders are leveraging (LLM code generation, multi-agent architectures, consensus voting) are all on steep improvement curves. They're building at the intersection of two accelerating forces: AI's improving ability to write code that describes transformations, and the rising cost of physical experiments in scientific domains. That positioning is favorable. But here's what concerns me -- when ARC-AGI-2 scores jump 44 points in three months for everyone, the technology is being commoditized in real time. And Confluence open-sourced their solver under MIT license. They gave away their calling card. Future defensibility has to come from domain-specific applications they haven't built yet.

The bull case is strong enough to articulate clearly. If these founders have cracked something fundamental about how to structure problems so that LLMs can learn from minimal examples -- if the ARC-AGI-2 result reveals a general-purpose insight about learning efficiency rather than a benchmark-specific trick -- then this is a category-creation company. The AI-for-science market is $4.8 billion today headed to $35 billion. The big labs (OpenAI, DeepMind, Anthropic) are structurally misaligned with this problem -- their entire business model is built on scaling with massive data, not learning from sparse data. That structural misalignment is exactly what creates opportunity for a startup. A young, hungry team with the right technical insight could build domain-specific moats through customer partnerships before the large labs pivot. Paul Graham writing a personal check adds signal -- he's selective and sees these companies up close. Niranjan reportedly built a prior startup to $5M ARR, which at his age would demonstrate exactly the kind of learning rate I look for. If I could verify that prior exit and see evidence that the program synthesis approach transfers to even one scientific domain, this would move from interesting to investable.

What holds me back is the gap between what's been demonstrated and what's being promised. The company has one public credential: a benchmark score. The benchmark tests abstract pattern recognition on colored grids. The target customers are pharmaceutical companies and materials science labs. I've seen too many AI companies build impressive demos that don't survive contact with messy real-world data. The competitive landscape is brutal -- Isomorphic Labs has $600 million and AlphaFold's lineage, Imbue has $232 million and is closing fast on the benchmark. A two-person team with no visible domain expertise in pharma or materials science is going to need extraordinary learning velocity to close that gap. The brand confusion with Atlassian's Confluence is a minor but telling detail -- it suggests the founders haven't yet thought carefully about the commercial surface of this company, which is fine at this stage but consistent with a team that's more benchmark-oriented than business-oriented.

I'd take the meeting. The outsider signal is real -- two young founders with minimal resources outperforming a quarter-billion-dollar competitor is the kind of asymmetric result that precedes category creation. But I need to see two things before writing a check: evidence that the learning-efficiency approach transfers to at least one scientific domain beyond abstract grids, and evidence that these founders are thinking about the economic impact of the technology rather than the elegance of the benchmark. The magnitude of the problem they're pointing at is large enough to justify the attempt. The question is whether they're actually building toward that problem or whether they've built an impressive solution in search of one.
