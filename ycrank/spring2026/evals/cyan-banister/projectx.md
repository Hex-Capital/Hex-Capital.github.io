# ProjectX -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that caught my eye wasn't the product — it was the ghost roster. Between July 2024 and the YC Spring 2026 listing, three of the four original co-founders disappeared. Subhrakamal Das, Ronit Banerjee, Suraj Kuncham — all gone. Replaced by Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder. Only Rounak Adhikary survived the transition. I've lived through this movie before. HQ Trivia had the right product at the right time and it was destroyed by founder dynamics. When I see that level of turnover at pre-seed, before a company has even faced real adversity, I need to understand what happened. The dossier doesn't tell me. That silence is loud. It could mean Rounak is the kind of leader who upgrades ruthlessly when co-founders aren't performing — which would actually be a strength. Or it could mean he's difficult to work with, or the original team lost faith in the vision. Without hearing the story directly from him, I'm carrying this as an open wound in the evaluation.

When I ask my first question — tell me the story, not the business — I get fragments but not a forge. Rounak is from Kalyani, West Bengal. He started a consulting company at nineteen. He won competitions, got into accelerators, hustled his way from a small Indian town to Stanford ASES and YC. That's real resourcefulness, and I recognize the energy of someone who builds because standing still isn't an option. But I don't hear why *this* problem — why GPU access, why cloud streaming, why the specific pain of watching talented students in India locked out of professional tools because they can't afford the hardware. The "75% of Indian students lack access to high-quality computing devices" stat is in the dossier, and I suspect Rounak lived that reality growing up in West Bengal. If he did, that's the story I need to hear him tell. Right now it reads like a sharp young founder who identified a large market, not someone whose biography made this problem unavoidable.

The impact dimension is where this company genuinely moves me. When I think about what it means for a student in Kalyani — or Madanpur, where Bishal is from — to open a browser tab and suddenly have access to Blender or Unreal Engine on hardware they could never afford, that's the kind of access expansion I've spent my career investing in. Serve Robotics expanded who could receive a delivery. Wonderschool expanded who could find childcare. Infinity could expand who gets to become a 3D artist, a game developer, an AI engineer. The structural barrier being addressed — expensive hardware as a gatekeeper to professional capability — is real and under-attacked. It's not quite an "invisible system" in the way I usually think about it; everyone knows computers cost money. But the *second-order* gatekeeping is hidden: the students who never discover they could be brilliant engineers because they never got to touch the tools. That's the version of this story that would get me leaning forward.

Here's what would have to be true for this to be a great investment: the per-app browser streaming approach at sub-3-second cold starts is genuinely hard to replicate, even by hyperscalers whose business models are built around selling you a whole VM. If ProjectX can deliver GPU-accelerated professional applications through a URL at a fraction of the cost of a local workstation or a Shadow PC subscription, and if the $2.4M in signed LOIs converts into real enterprise and education contracts, then the India beachhead strategy could work beautifully — they'd build density in a market where Western competitors aren't competing, develop their streaming infrastructure with lower-cost talent, and expand globally from a position of operational efficiency. The technical complexity is real, and the WebGPU timing window is genuinely now. That's a credible bull case.

But I keep running into walls. Cloud GPU infrastructure is the opposite of a contrarian space — 95 companies tracked, $20.8B raised collectively, hyperscalers circling. Google already acquired Cameyo for app virtualization. This isn't making anyone shift uncomfortably in their chairs; it's making them reach for their checkbooks in a crowded market. The capital mismatch is severe: $204K in total funding against competitors with nine-figure war chests. The per-app streaming differentiator is clever but not structurally defensible — no patents, no network effects, no data moats. A well-resourced team at Google or AWS could build this in a product cycle if the market signal is strong enough. And if I'm being honest with myself, the founding team restructuring at this early stage, combined with the absence of any biographical narrative connecting these specific founders to this specific problem, means I can't build the conviction I need to write a check with my own money. The impact case is compelling, but impact without a moat is a gift to the acquirer or the fast follower, not to the early investor.
