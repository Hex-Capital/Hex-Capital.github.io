# Framewise Health -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Tane Kim dropped out of Brown's BS/MD program after observing 500+ patient interactions and is now selling AI-generated multilingual discharge videos to hospitals via FHIR integrations with Epic, Cerner, athena, ModMed, eClinicalWorks, and NextGen -- and that one sentence captures Framewise Health's entire signature for me: a domain-immersed clinical founder pointed at a six-EHR enterprise SaaS sales cycle with no owned audience, no unusual instrument, and no price discount on a 2026 YC AI-healthcare deck. Framewise Health is a Memora-shaped business with a video wrapper, and the regatta tacks between here and a Series A look exactly like the ones I refuse to underwrite at consensus seed prices.

Tane Kim's 500+ documented patient interactions and Brown BS/MD dropout arc is the dimension where Framewise Health rates highest, and the load-bearing analogy on this axis is my own **Drop.io** founding -- bootstrapping a file-sharing primitive against free incumbents on the basis that you'd lived inside the user problem before anyone funded you to. Framewise Health's Tane Kim has that clinical-immersion shape: he watched the actual discharge-instruction failure mode across a dozen-plus institutions before naming a company after it, and David Cui contributing Brown computer-vision research plus a Freya S25 voice-AI engineering tour is a real technical complement rather than a hired CTO. Where Framewise Health diverges from the Drop.io rung is that I cannot find a single piece of public writing by Tane Kim on patient-engagement failure modes, multilingual discharge non-comprehension, or why Memora's text-only approach is structurally wrong -- "Writing is thinking" and the absence of writing on this category by the founder closest to it is the gap that keeps Framewise Health off the high rung rather than on it.

The canonical 2026 YC seed deck I described in my pass example -- AI agent for vertical X, four tier-1 VCs in, no price discount -- is the failure mode that drags Framewise Health's lowest dimension, founder-as-brand, into the basement. That rung's mechanism is dependence on paid enterprise acquisition with zero owned channel cushioning the cap table, and Framewise Health fits it precisely: a January 2026-created @FramewiseHealth X account with no retrievable follower count, a LinkedIn company page with no retrievable follower count, two founders whose handles @tanekimm and @sourdoggy8 produce no public footprint, and a stated GTM of "hospitals, specialty groups, medical devices, pharma" that is literally the most expensive distribution surface in American software. The Slow Creator Fund thesis doesn't reach Framewise Health -- there is no creator here, no newsletter, no Discord, no 10K free-acquired users, nothing the $60M vehicle was built to underwrite.

On capital structure, the mechanism that worries me about Framewise Health over 18 months is the burn compounding of a six-EHR enterprise integration footprint colliding with hospital sales cycles measured in quarters per logo: shipping live FHIR connectors to Epic and Oracle/Cerner is genuine engineering cost, HIPAA and SOC 2 already burned legal and audit dollars, and the dossier discloses zero signed pilots, zero logos, zero LOIs to amortize that against. The Ro known exception almost rescues Framewise Health here -- regulatory complexity as moat justifying capital intensity -- but Ro cleared 50-state prescription rails as the moat itself, whereas Framewise Health's clinician-review workflow is a liability mitigation, not an asset, and the FDA software-as-medical-device posture is undeclared in public sources. This is a structure that needs five rounds to reach default-alive, not a venture moment.

For Framewise Health's pricing dimension to flip a tier upward, the counterfactual would need to be: a tier-1 healthcare-IT specialist (a16z Bio, GV) visibly passed on the round, the $500K came in below batch median on a discounted SAFE, and at least one EHR vendor was the publicly named pass. None of that is in the dossier -- what is in the dossier is Aaron Epstein as YC partner, $500K standard YC investment, and a "AI-native patient engagement" tagline that could have been on twenty Spring 2026 demo-day slides. The provocation writes itself: AI-native patient engagement is just more SaaS-on-LLM with a healthcare label, and the round is consensus-priced for a category two tier-1s would have written into without thinking.

The single-anchor parallel on category sleeve fit is **Truepill**, which sits in my unstated healthcare-DTC / prescription-rail pattern alongside Ro, Hinge Health, PillPack, and Livongo. Framewise Health is adjacent to that sleeve but not in it -- Truepill owned actual prescription infrastructure with regulated pharmacy operations, while Framewise Health owns a per-patient video generation pipeline with clinician review. The Memora Health acquisition by Commure on December 20 2024 is the structural fact that defines Framewise Health's sleeve placement: the patient-communication incumbent now sits inside a hospital-software consolidator with a checkbook, which makes Framewise Health a feature waiting to be bundled rather than a standalone "venture moment." Crypto this is not, DTC this is not, and "AI is just more internet" applies cleanly.

Framewise Health in 36 months: $1-3M ARR from 5-15 hospital pilots, acquired by Commure or athenahealth in the $20-50M range before a Series B, with the founders rolling into a patient-engagement product line. That forecast follows directly from the signature -- domain-credible founders shipping into a consolidating incumbent landscape with no owned distribution -- and the Commure-Memora deal of December 2024 is the precise comp telegraphing the buyer.

The strongest bull case against my pass: Tane Kim's Brown BS/MD dropout plus 500+ patient observations is a genuine missionary-temperament signal, the six live FHIR integrations are real plumbing that took quarters to build, multilingual discharge video at 75+ languages is a 10x improvement over paper handouts that even a hostile incumbent will struggle to match in one product sprint, and the Ro precedent says I have overridden capital-efficiency objections before when healthcare regulatory complexity carried the moat. That case stings because Tane Kim's clinical immersion is the most thesis-aligned fact in the dossier and I am genuinely undervaluing it. The reason it doesn't win: Ro's override required the regulatory rail to BE the moat, and Framewise Health's clinician-review workflow is a defensive liability shield, not an offensive licensing schlep no one else will clear. I'd flip my verdict on one verifiable condition -- a signed paid pilot with a named integrated delivery network (Kaiser, Intermountain, HCA) disclosed before the next round, at which point the asset becomes legible enough to re-underwrite.
