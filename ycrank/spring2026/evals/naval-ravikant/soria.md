# Soria -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Adam Ron spent his career as VP of Equity Research at Bank of America covering healthcare facilities and managed care, and now Soria sells a healthcare sector model back to that exact desk -- so the founder-market fit is overwhelming and the leverage architecture is exactly the kind I don't fund. Soria is a domain-expert wedge into a capital-and-labor-leverage business: per-seat enterprise SaaS into Bulge Bracket Banks and hedge funds, sold through the seller's own analyst Rolodex. That pairing -- specific knowledge at the top, permissioned distribution at the bottom -- is Soria's signature, and it tells me what kind of company this is before I score a single dimension.

Bloomberg Terminal at ~$24K per seat across 3,430+ customer companies is the closest analog for what Soria is structurally building, and that is not a compliment in my framework. Bloomberg won on a closed, permissioned data-and-distribution architecture that requires a salesforce, an installed terminal, and a procurement cycle -- the inverse of what I fund. Twitter (2007) and Stack Overflow (2008) entered my ledger because anyone could post, anyone could answer, the marginal cost of replication was zero, and the founders did not need to dial a managing director to get the next user. Soria's distribution is a sell-side healthcare analyst on a call with another sell-side healthcare analyst -- Adam Ron's BofA network is a real asset, but it is labor leverage that requires permission from a procurement team at Morgan Stanley, not a permissionless code-or-media channel. The 125+ aggregated data sources and the MCP server are real engineering, but they are *features* of the product, not the *distribution* of the product. Soria is selling a permissioned product through a permissioned channel.

Perplexity is my Known Exception that says single-sided AI products can clear the bar when specific knowledge plus distribution plus category timing all hit top quartile -- and Soria explicitly fails that override. Aravind Srinivas brought a Berkeley retrieval PhD into an API-distributable consumer product in a pre-consensus AI moment in 2022. Soria has the specific knowledge leg (Ron is genuinely the buyer), but the distribution is enterprise sales, not API, and the timing is post-consensus: Hebbia is already at $13M ARR with $700M valuation and a16z behind it, Daloopa took $13M in July 2025 to do exactly the structured-data extraction adjacent to Soria's sector models, and Rogo is in the same fairway. The Perplexity override does not extend to Soria because two of the three required legs (permissionless distribution, pre-consensus category) are absent.

Specific knowledge is where Soria scores best, and Adam Ron is the reason. He covered healthcare facilities and managed care at BofA, holds a Series 7, and is now building the tool he wished he had -- the lived-problem-ownership pattern that Ivan Zhao at Notion (2013) and Jeff Atwood at Stack Overflow (2008) embodied. Cameron Spiller at Virginia Tech CompE with Pinwheel and Findigs behind him is a credible data-platform engineer, not a memo co-founder. If Soria fails, it will not be because Adam Ron does not understand the buyer; it will be because the buyer is structurally the wrong buyer for me.

Asymmetric-payoff math is where Soria breaks against my portfolio. The downside is still one X -- that part is fixed. The upside on a healthcare-vertical Bloomberg analog is bounded by the shape of the buyer universe: a few hundred healthcare desks at banks and hedge funds, even at Bloomberg-level $24K/seat economics, caps the realistic outcome at a mid-nine-figure ARR business and a strategic acquisition by AlphaSense, FactSet, or S&P. That is a fine private-equity outcome. It is not the 1,000-10,000x geometry I fund Twitter (2007) and Uber (2010) to chase. Soria's "and beyond" expansion to additional sectors is the pivot-room story -- each new vertical is a new from-scratch data-sourcing and sales motion, not a network effect compounding the prior verticals. Pivot-room exists in theory; in practice each pivot rebuilds, it does not compound.

Network effects are absent in a way Soria's dossier does not try to disguise. Each customer of Soria is economically independent of the next -- the 1,001st hedge fund analyst using the healthcare sector model does not make the 1,000th analyst's experience better. The DBOS-based pipeline and the 125+ data sources improve as Soria invests labor and capital; they do not improve as users join. That is the structural definition of a single-sided SaaS, and it is the architecture my 273-check ledger has the fewest entries from.

36-month forecast: Soria reaches $4-9M ARR across 15-30 healthcare desks, then gets acquired by AlphaSense, FactSet, or S&P Global for $80-200M as the strategic absorbs the vertical data pack -- or, less likely, Hebbia ships a healthcare data module first and Soria stalls at sub-$3M ARR. Adam Ron's network gets the first 10 logos; the structural ceiling and Hebbia's $13M ARR head start cap the rest. This is a respectable PE outcome for the founders and a 3-8x return for the seed investors who priced it right. It is not my shape.

The strongest bull case is Anchorage Digital -- the Known Exception where I funded a permissioned business because the permission itself became the moat in a pre-consensus crypto cycle. Soria's bull argument would run: healthcare data licensing is gnarly enough that aggregating 125+ sources is itself a permission moat, and Adam Ron's BofA-trained understanding of which data actually matters is the specific knowledge incumbents lack. Three reasons that bull case loses here. First, Anchorage rode pre-consensus crypto in 2019-2021; Soria is entering AI-finance data infrastructure in 2026 after Hebbia ($13M ARR, $700M val), Daloopa ($13M July 2025), and Rogo have already drawn the category map. Second, Anchorage's permission moat was a federal OCC charter -- Soria's "moat" is data partnerships that any acquirer with a checkbook can replicate inside 18 months. Third, I served as advisor to Anchorage; Soria's pre-seed gives me no such surface. The verdict flips if Soria ships a permissionless distribution surface -- the MCP server going viral with independent analyst usage outside the banks, or an open-data tier that pulls in non-paying healthcare researchers and creates pull-through demand. Until then, Soria is a fine company in a category I do not fund.
