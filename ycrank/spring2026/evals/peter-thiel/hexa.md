# Hexa -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Hexa is the 2026 archetype of indefinite optimism dressed in agentic-AI vocabulary -- a three-person team, Ishaan Makkar and Aurideep Nayak, selling an "OS for manufacturers and distributors" at $9K MRR across three customers, against a Didero that closed a $30M Series A six weeks before Hexa surfaced. The analytical signature is a thin secret paired with a near-absent monopoly trajectory: Hexa identified a real workflow ("54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets") but neither the founders nor the dossier name an incumbent that structurally cannot ship the thing, and a competitor with 37x the capital is shipping it.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing comparison on the contrarian-secret axis because Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent failure mode -- PayPal's batch-processor architecture and its merchant-not-developer orientation -- that the consensus had declared a closed category. Hexa's equivalent reads, on the dossier, as "manufacturers use spreadsheets and email," which is a market observation, not a secret. The Stripe-shaped move would have required Hexa to name *why* SAP and Oracle NetSuite structurally cannot ship native agents on top of their own ERPs -- channel conflict with SI partners is the obvious candidate -- and the dossier surfaces it only as Phase-3 inference, not founder thesis. The Hexa pitch terminates in breadth-of-integration (SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, Epicor, Salesforce, HubSpot, Zoho, Pipedrive) which is the language of horizontal overlay, not of a secret. This is closer to the Pass Example archetype than to Stripe; the difference between 8/35 and 11/35 is that the spreadsheet stat is at least a real wedge, not a TAM slide.

The Pass Example's failure mechanism is that the moat hypothesis terminates at "we have a better model," and any competitor closes the gap in nine months. Hexa exhibits the same mechanism with a different surface: the moat hypothesis terminates at *breadth of ERP integrations*, and Didero ($30M, Feb 2026), Traza ($2.1M), plus the incumbents themselves, are all building the same integration surface area. The dossier itself flags "Well-funded competitors are shipping overlapping agentic procurement products" and "low technical barrier to category entry." Hexa's $9K MRR split across three customers gives Didero, with ~37x the capital and an explicit ICP overlap on "manufacturers and distributors who need to source raw materials," a 12-month window to outrun Hexa on integrations, partner deals, and AE headcount. There is no pricing-power story here -- no government certification depth, no network density that compounds with scale, no integration moat that a $30M competitor cannot replicate. Monopoly trajectory: structurally absent.

On *definite plan vs indefinite iteration*, the 18-month compounding mechanism cuts against Hexa. A definite-plan founder commits to a specific architectural bet that gets harder to copy as it deepens -- SpaceX's reusability roadmap is the canonical case. Hexa's roadmap, as revealed by the breadth of surface area on hexaagents.com (sales, procurement, finance, customer service, eight ERP/CRM integrations), is the opposite shape: it broadens horizontally rather than deepening vertically. In an indefinite-iteration mode, the founder will follow the next three customers wherever they pull, and at three customers the pull is undefined. Default applies, marginally.

On *founder lineage*, the counterfactual that would flip the score a tier is concrete: if Ishaan Makkar had shipped procurement software at SAP Ariba or Coupa, or if Aurideep Nayak had been an early IC at Ramp or Brex, Hexa moves from a 4/15 to a 9/15 and the Asana override becomes live -- Moskovitz's Facebook pedigree carried Asana across a missing-moat gap, and a similar override could carry Hexa. The dossier surfaces neither. Makkar appears as "Hexa (YC P26)" on LinkedIn with no prior employer indexed; Nayak appears once at a DeepMind hackathon. No Olympiad, no prior YC outcome, no senior IC role at a known company. Absent-evidence default holds.

On *atoms-heavy / regulated-sector moat*, the single-anchor parallel is **Palantir** (2003), where the moat was not the software but the 15-year integration depth into Three-Letter-Agency workflows that no competitor would invest the schlep to replicate. Hexa sells to industrial distributors -- a real customer base, but not a regulated one; the switching cost is ERP-integration data, not certification depth or cleared-personnel scarcity. Pure software, commercial buyers, commoditizing category. The dossier offers no regulatory anchor, no hardware capex, no certification moat. Default low.

Hexa's 36-month forecast: either shuts down below $30K MRR by Q4 2028 after Didero consolidates the procurement-AI category, or gets acqui-hired by an ERP incumbent (Epicor or NetSuite the most probable) for under $15M in a talent-and-integrations deal. The mechanism is straightforward power-law math run in reverse -- a horizontally-positioned overlay in a category where a 37x-capitalized competitor is already shipping cannot reach the monopoly equilibrium that a fund-returner requires; the floor scenarios are the modal outcomes.

The strongest bull case is the **Asana** (2010) exception -- operator pedigree overriding a missing structural moat -- combined with the **Lyft** (2010) override of stable duopoly equilibrium in a category with network effects. Pressed: Hexa shipped $9K MRR in six weeks, which is real customer pull, and the ERP-integration data graph could compound into switching costs that look retrospectively like a network effect on the catalog/pricing side. If procurement-AI is a duopoly category and Didero is Uber, Hexa could be Lyft. This stings only until the dossier evidence is checked: Asana required Moskovitz, and Hexa has no Moskovitz; Lyft required driver-side network effects in a consumer mobility category, and B2B procurement workflow has no equivalent two-sided density -- catalog data is buyable, not networked. The single verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is Ishaan Makkar surfacing a prior shipped-to-scale role at a manufacturing-software incumbent (SAP, Epicor, Coupa, Ariba) or Hexa landing a named Tier-1 industrial distributor logo at >$20K MRR in the next two quarters. Absent that, this is the indefinite-optimism check a rival fund will write into the consensus wave. Pass.
