# Cedar -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Greg Gunn and Beier Cai have been working together for over a decade — Hootsuite from five employees to 1,500, then Commit.dev (the engineer-matching network, $6M seed from iNovia), and now Cedar. That's three companies. When I backed Raj on Solana, I'd watched him through three companies before writing the check. The repeat-relationship signal is the single most reliable predictor in my portfolio, and Cedar's cofounders hit it cleanly. Cai wrote Hootsuite's first line of code; Gunn ran its enterprise product line through $200M+ ARR. These are not incubator-matched cofounders fumbling through their first sales call. And $2.5M to $3M in contracted revenue with four people, before a public launch, is the kind of capital efficiency that makes me sit forward. That ratio — revenue per headcount, while still in stealth — is genuinely exceptional and suggests real enterprise pull, not manufactured demo-day metrics.

Here's what keeps me from getting actually excited: AI recruiting is one of the hottest consensus categories in venture right now. Over $208M poured into AI recruiting in the first eleven months of 2025. Perfect raised a $23M seed. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce. Every GP at every fund is nodding along to "AI agents replace recruiters." Cedar is swimming in a crowded, well-funded, consensus-exciting pool — and my entire framework is built on finding mispriced bets where the market is wrong. The market isn't wrong about AI recruiting being a big category. The market has aggressively bid it up. When I look at Cedar's positioning, I see a well-priced asset in a consensus category, not a contrarian opportunity where the risk-reward asymmetry is extreme. The "private relationship graph" angle is a real differentiator versus Perfect or hireEZ scraping public profiles, but it's a product differentiator within a consensus market — not the category-level mispricing that gets me to write a check.

The primitive test is where Cedar gets interesting but doesn't quite close. The tagline says "Killing LinkedIn with Agents," and if the actual thesis is "we're building a competing professional graph to LinkedIn, constructed from private relationship data no one else has, and recruiting is just the wedge" — that's a genuine new primitive. A private professional relationship graph that compounds with every 15-minute employee call and every new customer is structurally different from LinkedIn's self-reported, public-by-default data. Venmo didn't compete with banks on features; it created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavior. If Cedar is creating "private trust-graph as a platform" — not "better referral hiring" — that's a primitive. But the dossier describes the execution as referral optimization, not graph platform. The product is "surface high-signal candidates and build a proprietary relationship graph." That's an improved recruiting workflow with a data moat, not a new category that spawns its own vocabulary. Nobody is going to say "Cedar me" the way they "Venmo" each other.

The sanity-to-insanity ratio is miscalibrated for my framework — but in the wrong direction. Gunn and Cai are clearly sane: decade-plus working relationship, Hootsuite hypergrowth pedigree, they've built and shipped enterprise products before, and their advisor bench (Laszlo Bock from Google, Tiffany Fenster from Stripe recruiting, Brendan Browne from LinkedIn talent acquisition) is surgical for this exact problem. The issue is that "AI agents improve enterprise referral hiring" is also sane. It's a sensible idea pitched by sensible people. I want a very reasonable person doing something fucking crazy, and "we'll conduct structured phone calls with employees to map their networks" is a good product insight, not an insane thesis that makes smart people uncomfortable. Compare this to when I invested $1.7M in Marina Mogilko for 5% of her earnings for 30 years — people thought I was a lunatic. Cedar doesn't make anyone think I'm a lunatic.

On structural durability, Cedar has a real argument: the accumulated private relationship data from employee conversations is genuinely proprietary and can't be scraped or replicated by an LLM training on public data. That's better than most AI startups, which are pure wrapper plays. But the technology itself — conversational AI that extracts relationship information — is commodity infrastructure built on the same LLMs everyone else uses. The moat is the data, not the tech. And LinkedIn is the elephant: the dossier argues LinkedIn faces a business-model conflict in mapping employee networks because it would cannibalize Recruiter per-seat revenue. I've heard this "incumbent won't cannibalize itself" argument a hundred times, and it's true right up until the moment the incumbent decides the new revenue stream is bigger than the old one. LinkedIn already has 900M+ professional profiles and every HR department on the planet as a customer. If Cedar proves the private-graph model works, LinkedIn copies it in eighteen months. The Commit.dev → Cedar evolution suggests the founders have iterated toward this insight across years, which I respect, but the structural defense is thinner than something like Metropolis, where the physical parking infrastructure literally cannot be replicated by software.

The strongest bull case: Cedar might actually be building the anti-LinkedIn — a professional graph based on private trust relationships rather than public self-presentation, with recruiting as just the entry wedge. If that's what's happening, and the $2.5-3M in contracted revenue proves enterprise willingness to pay for this data layer, then the TAM isn't $656M in AI recruitment — it's some fraction of LinkedIn's $15B+ revenue built on a fundamentally different data primitive. The advisory bench (Bock, Fenster, Browne — all recruited from the exact institutions Cedar needs to penetrate) suggests the founders understand this is an enterprise relationship sale, not a product-led growth play. And the Commit.dev experience means they've already made the obvious mistakes in talent marketplaces and are running a refined playbook. If all of that is true, my consensus-mispricing score is too low because the market is pricing Cedar as an AI recruiting tool when it's actually a new-graph-infrastructure company. But I need to see evidence of that thesis, not aspirational taglines.

Capital discipline is Cedar's best dimension by my framework. Four people, stealth mode, $2.5-3M in contracted revenue, no public marketing, no splashy launch. This is a company that's making money before it's raising money — the exact behavior I look for. "You never want to raise money when you need money," and Cedar appears to be raising from a position of revenue strength, not desperation. If the revenue figures are real (they're self-reported and unverified, with an unexplained discrepancy between $2.5M and $3M), this is one of the most capital-efficient pre-seed companies I've seen. The decision to stay in stealth while generating enterprise revenue is a maturity signal — these aren't first-time founders chasing TechCrunch headlines.

The decisive dimensions: Cedar fails my consensus-mispricing test (AI recruiting is a hot, well-funded category where the upside is already priced in) and my primitive-creation test (improved referral hiring is incremental, not category-creating). The founders and capital discipline are strong — Gunn and Cai's decade-plus partnership is the exact repeat-relationship pattern that produced my best returns — but sane founders with a sane idea in a consensus market is a bet where the price already reflects the quality. I'd need to see evidence that Cedar is genuinely building a competing professional graph, not just a better recruiting tool, before this moves from neutral to conviction.
