# 10x Science -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most analytically interesting thing about 10x Science isn't the AI layer — it's the market structure underneath it. In August 2024, Danaher acquired Genedata. In 2021, Dotmatics absorbed Protein Metrics. The two leading independent software platforms for biopharma mass spectrometry analysis have been swallowed by instrument conglomerates in the last few years. This is a pattern I've seen repeatedly: when focused software companies get acquired by hardware-first conglomerates, innovation velocity drops, the product becomes a bundled attachment to instrument sales, and a structural opening appears for a new entrant. The channel conflict is real — Thermo Fisher and Danaher can't build truly vendor-neutral, AI-native analytical software without undermining their hardware lock-in business model. That's the structural moment worth paying attention to.

This fits squarely into the "boring but critical" market archetype that I've consistently found underpriced by other investors. Protein characterization software sounds like the most niche thing imaginable — most AI investors are chasing agents and consumer applications, not mass spectrometry data curation for antibody development teams. But every biotherapeutic candidate requires molecular-level characterization before advancing through development. The dossier cites FDA data that 74% of drug rejections reference quality control deficiencies. If AI drug discovery platforms like Recursion and Absci are genuinely accelerating pipeline volume (and billions in funding suggest the market believes they are), the characterization bottleneck intensifies. The question is magnitude — is the AI-driven pipeline acceleration creating a 2x or 10x increase in downstream characterization demand? That determines whether this is a nice efficiency tool or a structural necessity. The software-only SAM within a $5B protein characterization market (which includes instruments and reagents) is probably $500M-$1B today, which limits the ceiling unless the company can expand into adjacent analytical workflows.

The founder-market fit here is unusually tight, even by my standards. David Roberts has 37+ publications specifically in top-down mass spectrometry — the exact technique being automated — earned as a postdoc in Carolyn Bertozzi's lab at Stanford. Bertozzi won the 2022 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Andrew Reiter built mass spectrometry methods at the Broad Institute's proteomics platform. That's deep, specific domain credibility. Then Vishnu Tejus brings production-grade engineering: founding engineer at Nooks (raised $43M Series B from Kleiner Perkins), a 2x YC founder, and CMU XPRIZE finalist. The intersection of Nobel-lab proteomics expertise with proven startup engineering talent is genuinely hard to replicate. However, two of three founders appear to still hold active academic positions at Stanford — postdoc and PhD student, respectively — and the company is not currently hiring. That's an execution velocity concern I'd want to resolve before writing a check.

Where I get less excited is product-to-distribution trajectory. The initial product — AI-automated peptide mapping and protein characterization — solves a real workflow problem, but can it become a distribution channel for additional products? In regulated biopharma, validated platforms do create switching-cost moats, and once embedded, a characterization platform could theoretically expand into adjacent analytical capabilities (stability testing, formulation analysis, release assays). That's the Gusto pattern: payroll seems boring and narrow until it becomes the distribution channel for benefits, compliance, and HR management. But I see no evidence the founders are thinking about this expansion yet, and the biopharma validation cycle makes each new product extension expensive and slow.

The bull case is compelling enough that I want to name it clearly: if AI drug discovery pipelines continue accelerating — and the funding trends strongly suggest they will — then protein characterization becomes one of the most critical bottlenecks in the biopharma value chain. A vendor-neutral, AI-native platform built by founders with Nobel-lab domain expertise could capture this bottleneck during the exact window when incumbents have been absorbed into slow-moving conglomerates. The regulatory validation requirements that make this hard are the same requirements that protect the moat once achieved — the Color Health pattern I know well from personal experience. If the team goes full-time, ships fast, achieves FDA 21 CFR Part 11 compliance early, and lands two or three top-20 pharma customers, this could compound into a deeply embedded infrastructure layer. That pathway is real. But the current evidence — "onboarding customers," academic founders, no revenue signal, narrow initial product scope — leaves me in wait-and-see territory rather than write-a-check territory.

The single-miracle test produces a mixed result. The core miracle is clear: can their AI match or exceed domain expert accuracy in interpreting complex mass spectrometry data for regulated biopharma use? The team's publication record suggests they understand exactly what "good enough" means in this domain. But unlike Harvey, where the critical miracle (model fidelity) was being delivered by OpenAI's scaling investments, 10x Science must deliver their own accuracy miracle AND navigate enterprise biopharma sales AND achieve regulatory compliance. That's closer to 1.5 miracles than the clean single-miracle profile I prefer. Each additional required breakthrough compounds the risk, even when the individual probabilities are reasonable.
