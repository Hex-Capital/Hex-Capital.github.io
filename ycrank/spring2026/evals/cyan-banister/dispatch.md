# Dispatch -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what caught me first: a two-person team says they built a full-scale reentry heat shield in an apartment for a hundred times less than anyone else, then hauled it to the Mojave Desert and blasted it with rocket exhaust to simulate the forces of atmospheric reentry. That sentence has a specific frequency I recognize — the same frequency as a guy building rockets in a warehouse in Hawthorne when everyone said private space was a billionaire's vanity project. The resourcefulness is genuine. You don't fake a ground test at Mach 20+ conditions. But here's where my pattern recognition gets complicated: when I wrote my SpaceX check, there were no other well-funded companies trying to do what Elon was doing. The space was empty because it was considered insane. Dispatch is entering a field with seven-plus funded competitors, Varda already at five successful reentry missions and $329M raised, and Inversion sitting on $125M including a SpaceWERX contract. The cost structure thesis might be right, but the loneliness of conviction — the part where everyone else thinks you're crazy — that's not here. The room is full.

When I sit down with founders, I don't open with the business. I open with "tell me your story." With Payton and Andrew, what I get is strong but not forged: Purdue aerospace engineering, Northeastern electrical engineering, both at Astranis building geostationary microsatellites, one did thermal work on the Lunar Gateway, the other led avionics. These are sharp, credentialed engineers who saw a gap in the market from inside the spacecraft industry. I respect that — it's legitimate founder-market fit at the technical level. But I can't find the moment where this problem found them rather than the other way around. Nobody was homeless and couldn't get a package delivered from orbit. This reads like two talented engineers who reasoned their way to an opportunity, which is exactly what it probably is. The apartment heat shield gives me a flicker of something more primal — that survival resourcefulness, building with almost nothing — but one ground test isn't a biographical crucible. It's a proof of concept.

The "picks and shovels" positioning is smart, and it's the part of this that I find most structurally interesting. Varda owns the whole stack — the manufacturing payload, the capsule, the bus. Dispatch says: we just bring your stuff back. That's a logistics layer, and logistics layers at infrastructure inflection points are exactly the kind of bet that has made me money. Flexport was a logistics layer over opaque freight forwarding. But — and this matters to how I see the world — freight forwarding was an extractive, opaque system that exploited information asymmetry against importers who didn't understand what they were paying for. What is the invisible system in space reentry? There isn't one. This is a nascent industrial capability serving sophisticated customers — pharma companies, semiconductor manufacturers, DoD programs — who understand exactly what they're buying. There's no hidden tax being eliminated, no gatekeeping being dismantled, no power shifting from institutions to people. It's industrial infrastructure for industrial customers. That's fine. It's just not where my deepest conviction lives.

The bull case I have to honestly confront: if the 100x cost claim holds at production scale, this could be the SpaceX of downmass. SpaceX's entire thesis was that launch was overpriced by orders of magnitude because of institutional inertia and cost-plus contracting. Dispatch might be saying the same thing about reentry vehicles. Refurbishable vehicles driving per-mission costs down over time is exactly the playbook that made SpaceX dominant. And the inflection point is real — Varda proved the physics works, launch costs keep falling, and the in-space manufacturing market has credible CAGR projections. If you believe that manufacturing in microgravity becomes a real industrial category in the next decade, then someone needs to build the trucking network that brings product back to Earth, and it probably shouldn't be the same company doing the manufacturing. That logic is clean. What I can't resolve is whether a pre-seed company with ~$500K survives the capital gauntlet when the path to first orbital flight typically costs $50-100M and several of the seven competitors have raised nine figures. Resourcefulness can take you far — I've seen it — but orbital mechanics doesn't care how scrappy you are.

The ten-year question lands somewhere genuinely interesting: autonomous space stations doing lights-out manufacturing, serviced by a fleet of reentry vehicles shuttling product back to Earth. That's not a logistics company — that's an orbital industrial infrastructure company. The vision has scale. But vision without biographical inevitability and without a hidden system to disrupt leaves me in a place I know well: respecting the team, understanding the thesis, and keeping my checkbook closed. This connects to my SpaceX thread, yes, but threads need more than thematic rhyme. They need the kind of founders who would be building this even if no investor ever said yes.
