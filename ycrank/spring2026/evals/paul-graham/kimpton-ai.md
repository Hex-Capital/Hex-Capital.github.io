# Kimpton AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Kimpton AI is what happens when a hedge-fund team productizes their internal tooling — strong on team chemistry, weak on the hacker-genealogy and schlep-as-moat axes I lean on. Jack Zumwalt and Mauricio Ortiz met at a Goldman intern event around 2018, joined the bank together in February 2020, then ran Level III Capital for four years before turning their internal stack into Kimpton AI. That is the load-bearing fact in the dossier. Everything else — the FactSet integration, the 21,000-ticker coverage, the `mandate.md` framing — is downstream of whether I think these are the right people pointed at the right problem.

The Collisons at **Stripe** (2010) are the obvious portfolio anchor for the organic-idea axis: two brothers who had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007), building the API they themselves wanted. Kimpton AI's founders share the mechanism — operators productizing the tool they couldn't buy — and that is the strongest thing in the dossier. But the analogy strains at one specific seam: Level III Capital was a *systematic digital-asset* fund, and Kimpton AI is selling to *discretionary equity* PMs at hedge funds, family offices, and RIAs. The Collisons sold payments to people running businesses like the one they had just run. Zumwalt and Ortiz are selling earnings-prep and 13F workflows to a user they were not. The pain is adjacent, not personal — close enough to score well, not close enough to score like Drew Houston's USB stick at **Dropbox** (2007).

The pass example I keep coming back to — the polished AI-wrapper team with the $50B TAM slide and no shipped artifact — is the failure mechanism to test Kimpton AI against on hacker genealogy. That team failed because credentials substituted for code. Kimpton AI's dossier reports "No GitHub repos found" for Zumwalt, Ortiz, *and* Del Bosque. No Twitter. No open-source footprint. No hacker community trace. Goldman Sachs Marcus DevOps and "algorithms to detect cyberattackers" are real engineering credentials, but they are *institutional* engineering, not hacker-genealogy. Patrick Collison won the Young Scientist prize at 16 with Lisp; Aaron Swartz co-authored the RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005). I bet on people who left tracks in public hacker culture before they pitched. Kimpton AI's three engineers built internally inside a hedge fund and shipped nothing the open internet can find.

On schlep zone — mechanism analysis over 18 months — Kimpton AI is an LLM agent layered on FactSet, Tiingo, Massive, Plaid, and Polymarket. That stack is replicable by any team with data licensing budget, which is exactly why Rogo at $750M post-money, Hebbia at $159M raised, and AlphaSense at ~$4B already exist. **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance grind became Brian Armstrong's moat because it compounded; nobody wanted to do it for three years, so by year four he was alone in the room. Kimpton AI's "Trade Proposals" framing flirts with SEC investment-advice exposure, but the dossier shows them deflecting it with "humans make the decisions." Routing around the schlep is the opposite of the **Coinbase** (2012) play. The mandate-file switching-cost story is real but takes 18+ months to compound; meanwhile Rogo and AlphaSense have the distribution.

Counterfactual on animal resourcefulness: what would have to be true for Kimpton AI to clear the 25/35 threshold I use for Houston-tier signal? A demo on Hacker News with a public sandbox where any PM can drop a `mandate.md` and get a trade proposal back. A free tier that ate a competitor's seat at a named fund. Some cereal-box equivalent — a stunt I would not have thought of. Instead the dossier reports the website "routes to discovery calls with the CEO" and the company is "not publicly hiring." That is the founder-led enterprise-sales motion, which is fine for a Series A pitch and inert as an animal-test signal at pre-seed. Running a $10M fund for four years is resourceful in the institutional sense — it is not the post-rejection wallet-prototype move Armstrong made at **Coinbase** (2012).

Single-anchor parallel on cofounder configuration: **Twitch** (2007)'s Kan/Shear/Vogt trio worked together through **Kiko** (2005)'s failure before regrouping. Kimpton AI's three-person team has been working together since 2020 — Goldman, then Level III Capital, now Kimpton — which is the genuinely strong axis in the dossier. This is the dimension where the dossier evidence pins them near the top of the ladder without caveat. If I were writing the check on team chemistry alone, I would write it.

My 36-month forecast for Kimpton AI: acquired by FactSet, AlphaSense, or Rogo for $20–60M as a vertical feature for buy-side PMs, with no standalone exit and roughly $1–4M ARR at acquisition. The mechanism is the signature pairing — strong team, no schlep moat, hacker genealogy absent. Competent operator-founders with thin technical differentiation in markets where Microsoft already bought Fintool ($440K revenue) get absorbed, not compounded. The mandate-file switching cost is real but feature-shaped, not company-shaped.

The bull case that stings: Kimpton AI's three founders ran the user's job for four years and are now building for that user, which is the **Stripe** (2010) pattern at the level of mechanism — operators going vertical against horizontal incumbents (Rogo's "chat for finance professionals" is exactly the horizontal frame the Collisons beat at payments). If `mandate.md` becomes the standard PM-onboarding artifact the way Stripe's API became the developer-onboarding artifact, the switching cost is generational and Kimpton AI wins the vertical that AlphaSense is too broad to defend. What dismantles it: the Collisons shipped raw code to Hacker News and let developers self-serve; Kimpton AI ships discovery calls to a CEO and has no public artifact a PM can touch without an intro. Stripe leaked into developer culture organically. Kimpton AI is doing enterprise sales. The flip condition I would actually wait for: a public, free `mandate.md` sandbox that gets pulled into PM Twitter (or whatever PMs use) within 90 days, plus three named hedge fund customers at >$50K ACV. Absent that, I pass and watch.
