# Arzana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What caught me first wasn't the product or the market -- it was the NYT headline: "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype." A 21-year-old from Okoboji, Iowa (population roughly 1,500) who taught himself discus from YouTube to reach state ranking and ran a Neapolitan pizza operation as a teenager, paired with a 24-year-old D1 wrestler from Neenah, Wisconsin who paid himself $10,000 last year. Neither has a traditional engineering background. Marshall took "one coding class." And yet they've somehow built a platform they claim serves Schneider National and Zeiss, and they got Tom Blomfield to back them into YC. That pattern -- scrappy, self-taught kids from small-town America doing something that shouldn't work on paper -- is the kind of thing that makes me lean forward. William's quote, "There is no Plan B, because that assumes you will fail," sounds like something Travis would have said in 2010. The resourcefulness is unmistakable.

But here's where I get stuck: I always ask founders, "How did you get to where you are?" -- and I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem. William is a "manufacturing nerd from Iowa." Marshall grew up in the Fox Valley, which is genuine manufacturing country. They have a field engineer stationed in Okoboji. These are real signals that they understand the customer, the culture, the way a manufacturing office actually works -- and that matters enormously because Silicon Valley founders cannot sell to a plant manager in Wisconsin the way these two can. But understanding the culture is different from having lived the pain. Neither founder worked in a manufacturing office grinding through RFQs at 2am. The connection is geographic and cultural, not forged through the kind of adversity that makes someone incapable of quitting. I've learned that the founders who persist through the worst crises are the ones solving a problem that personally wounded them.

The business itself -- AI agents automating manufacturing front-office workflows -- is solving a real problem. Manufacturing admin is genuinely invisible infrastructure. Nobody in tech thinks about the person in Neenah manually entering purchase orders into Epicor from email PDFs. That invisibility pattern resonates with me -- it's the same pattern I saw in Carta making cap tables transparent, or Affirm exposing hidden credit card fees. A 5-person office replacing 30 is a transformative productivity claim, and if true, it could be the difference between a small manufacturer surviving or dying against overseas competition. That's meaningful economic impact for communities that rarely get any benefit from the AI wave. And the multi-ERP integration approach (10+ systems) creates real switching costs -- once you're wired into someone's Epicor instance running their quoting and order entry, they aren't ripping you out.

Here's the bull case I genuinely wrestled with: if you believe that manufacturing front-office automation is a massive, overlooked category -- and it might be -- then these two Midwestern founders with boots-on-the-ground distribution in Iowa and Wisconsin could build something that no San Francisco AI startup can replicate. The customer logos, if real, are extraordinary for a pre-seed company. The bundled "Office Execution System" versus point solutions is a smart strategic bet -- Conexiom does order automation, Paperless Parts does quoting, but nobody bundles the whole front office. And these kids are young enough and hungry enough to grind through the 1-3 month implementation cycles that enterprise manufacturing sales require. The field engineer in Okoboji is exactly the kind of unglamorous distribution insight that wins in industrial markets.

But I can't get past two things. First, "AI agents for business process automation" is one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. This isn't the kind of thing other investors are dismissing as crazy -- Endeavor AI just raised $7M from Craft Ventures for nearly the same market. Conexiom has $170M behind it. Rossum has $111M. The manufacturing vertical gives it specificity, but specificity isn't weirdness. I'm drawn to companies that other investors think are insane, and nobody thinks automating manufacturing workflows with AI is insane in 2026. Second, the engineering risk with a non-technical founding team and a single lead engineer supporting integrations across 10+ ERPs is significant. When your Epicor integration breaks at 6am and a customer's order flow stops, you need deep technical capacity, not just domain empathy. The entire technical moat rests on Joe Politi's shoulders, and that's a fragile foundation for a company selling reliability to manufacturers.

I respect what these founders are building and I genuinely like who they appear to be -- resourceful, committed, grounded in the real economy rather than tech-world abstractions. But for me to write a check, I need either the kind of biographical conviction that makes the founder unstoppable, or the kind of anti-consensus weirdness that means I'm seeing something nobody else sees. Arzana has traces of both but not enough of either. This is a competent team in a real market doing reasonable work, and there's a version of this story where they build something important for American manufacturing. But it's not my bet to make.
