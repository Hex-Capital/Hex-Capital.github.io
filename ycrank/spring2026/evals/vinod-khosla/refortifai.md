# RefortifAI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Software DRM has been broken in every domain it has ever been deployed. Music, movies, games, enterprise software — every protection scheme in history has been cracked by determined adversaries with physical access to the hardware. Denuvo, the state of the art in game anti-cheat, typically falls within days of a major release. RefortifAI is building DRM for AI model weights, framing it as "anti-cheat for AI Models." I respect the technical ambition of weight obfuscation that preserves inference fidelity — the academic literature calls this an open problem. But the historical record of software-only protection systems is unambiguous: they delay extraction, they do not prevent it. And unlike games, where the economic value of protection decays rapidly after launch week, model weights retain value indefinitely. One successful crack and your weights are in the wild forever. The security model needed here is fundamentally different from anything that has worked before, and I see no evidence of a novel cryptographic or architectural insight that breaks from this historical pattern.

Let me ask my first question: if RefortifAI fully succeeds — if they build perfect, unbreakable DRM for AI model weights — does the world change? The honest answer is no. AI model providers get a new deployment option. Companies that currently restrict to API-only access can now ship models to on-premise and edge environments. That's a useful expansion of a business model. It is not a transformation of how millions of people live. The beneficiaries are AI companies' revenue protection departments. The end user — the person whose healthcare, education, or energy access might improve — is three abstraction layers removed from this product. Compare this to what I care about: Commonwealth Fusion Systems makes energy limitless. Impossible Foods feeds billions without animal agriculture. OpenAI reshapes civilization. RefortifAI lets model vendors license weights more carefully. These are not the same category of consequence.

What concerns me more is that this company is architecturally designed to *maintain* pricing power, not to reduce costs. The entire value proposition is helping model providers prevent unauthorized redistribution — keeping models expensive and controlled. This is the opposite of my deepest investment instinct, which is making the expensive radically affordable. Every one of my highest-conviction bets converges on collapsing cost structures: protein without cows, diagnosis without surgery, energy without fossil fuels. RefortifAI converges on preserving cost structures. It's a tool for incumbents to protect margins, not a tool for insurgents to democratize access. That's a legitimate business, but it's someone else's bet, not mine.

The bull case deserves engagement. The AI model distribution market is genuinely underserved. There's an awkward gap between API-only deployment (OpenAI's model) and fully open weights (Meta's Llama). Model providers who want local inference capability without IP exposure have no good solution today. Palo Alto Networks paid $500M+ for Protect AI. F5 bought CalypsoAI. Enterprise buyers are clearly spending on AI security infrastructure. The team has real systems depth — a CTO who builds operating systems in Assembly and did RTL design at Texas Instruments and Tenstorrent, a COO who studied ROP gadgets and attack vectors under Somesh Jha at Wisconsin. The drop-in replacement for vLLM and SGLang is a smart wedge — low-friction adoption for an audience already standardized on those engines. If the weight obfuscation actually works at production latency without accuracy degradation, this could become essential plumbing for every model provider doing on-premise distribution. That's a credible $500M-$1B outcome. For many investors, that's an excellent seed bet. For me, it's a 3-5x return, and I don't invest for 3-5x returns. I want to lose my money or make 50 times my money. This company is designed to generate the moderate outcome I explicitly filter against.

The founders are technically credentialed for this problem — the combination of hardware-level understanding, AI engineering, and security research is precisely what you'd want. But I have no signal on learning velocity, no evidence of pivots or adaptation, no indication of first-principles reasoning versus domain expertise execution. They've assembled the right resume stack for the problem. That tells me they're competent. It doesn't tell me they're the kind of unreasonable people who build unreasonable companies. I look for founders who are attempting something the world considers impossible. Building DRM for AI models is hard engineering. It is not impossible engineering. Nobody laughs when you describe this company. When I backed a teenager building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin, people laughed. That's the signal I look for, and it's absent here.

This is a well-scoped B2B security company with a real technical challenge and a clear market. I pass — not because it's a bad company, but because the consequence of success is a profitable middleware business, not a changed world. Some problems are too important not to work on. This problem is important to AI companies' legal departments. It is not important to humanity.
