# Corvera -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Corvera: "AI agents for CPG brands" is possibly the most consensus investment thesis I could imagine in early 2026. Every fund I know is hunting for vertical AI agents. CPG AI adoption leapt from 42% to 71% in a single year. Crisp just closed north of $127 million. Unioncrate has been in this exact space since 2016. The "agentic AI" framing is the hottest label in tech right now — not difficult to assess, not unpopular, not the kind of thing that makes people uncomfortable. When I think about the companies that have worked best for me — SpaceX when private rockets were a punchline, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded certifiably insane — the common thread is that other investors couldn't even categorize what they were looking at. Corvera is the opposite of that. Every sophisticated investor immediately understands this pitch, and that's not a compliment in my framework.

Now, if I sat down with Christopher Kong and said "tell me your story," I think I'd hear something real. He built Better Nature — a tempeh brand — and scaled it to 5,000+ retail locations across six countries. That's not a slide deck claim, that's years of wrestling with purchase orders, warehouse logistics, stockouts, and all the operational chaos that comes with growing a physical products business. He left that company to build the tool he wished he'd had. That biographical thread from operator to builder is exactly the kind of founder-market fit I respect. I can feel the authenticity in someone who's lived inside the problem. But — and this matters to me — an Oxford biochemistry MSc, Forbes 30 Under 30, a CTO from Google, a CPO from Princeton who taught at Eton College... these are accomplished, credentialed people. They're not outsiders. I'm not hearing the survival story, the adversity that forges the kind of irrational persistence you need when everything falls apart. Maybe it's there and I just can't see it from the dossier. But from what I can see, this team reads more like smart operators making a rational career bet than misfits burning to solve something nobody else will touch.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Kong's CPG network is a genuine distribution asset — 15 design partners at pre-seed is strong evidence that the problem is real and that this team can get in the door. The mid-market CPG segment ($5M-$500M revenue) genuinely is underserved: SAP and Oracle are too heavy, spreadsheets are too fragile, and nobody has nailed autonomous execution — actually processing orders and generating POs — rather than just showing dashboards. If Corvera can achieve the reliability bar required for end-to-end autonomous order processing (and that's a serious "if" — one wrong quantity shipped to a major retailer damages a relationship that took years to build), they could become deeply embedded in their customers' operations in ways that create real switching costs. The combination of an operator-CEO who knows what CPG brands actually need with a CTO who built multi-agent systems at Google is well-matched to the technical challenge. For a different investor — one who values execution quality in large addressable markets — this could be a strong bet.

But that investor isn't me. Corvera doesn't create economic access for underserved populations — it helps businesses with $5M+ in revenue run their operations more efficiently. It doesn't make an invisible system visible the way Carta exposed cap table opacity or Affirm exposed hidden credit card fees. It's not operating in a regulated or stigmatized category where complexity deters competitors and creates natural moats. The supply chain space is operationally complex, sure, but it's not the kind of hard space — defense, psychedelic therapy, male contraception — where I've found the most durable advantages. And the commoditization risk is real: the core workflows are well-understood, the AI capabilities are generally available, and well-funded competitors like Crisp are explicitly building AI agent products for CPG. Fifteen design partners is a good start, but I haven't seen evidence of any converting to paying customers, and the gap between "design partner" and "trusting an AI to autonomously process your orders" is enormous in CPG, where every mistake has direct revenue consequences.

On the co-founder dynamics, I have limited signal. Three co-founders with no shared prior employer or university that I can identify, plus a rebrand from "Sarah AI" to "Corvera" that could mean healthy iteration or could mean unresolved strategic disagreements early on. I'd want to understand how they found each other and how they fight before writing a check. That said, absent red flags, I won't penalize what I simply can't see.

This is a competent team building a sensible product in a real market. In many ways, that's the problem — from where I sit, the best companies don't look sensible. They look absurd until suddenly they don't. Corvera looks exactly like what a smart investor would design in a lab: strong founder-market fit, growing TAM, AI tailwinds, YC stamp. But it doesn't make my heart race. There's no jolt. It's a well-constructed consensus bet, and I've learned — sometimes the hard way — that consensus bets at seed stage tend to attract competition faster than they can build moats.
