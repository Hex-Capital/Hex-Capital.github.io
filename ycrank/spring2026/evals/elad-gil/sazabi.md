# Sazabi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The observability market for AI-generated code is one of the most obvious bets in enterprise infrastructure right now. That's the problem. LangSmith is at $1.25B. Langfuse got acquired by ClickHouse in January. Arize closed a $70M Series C. Laminar came through YC two batches ago. And Datadog -- a $50B+ company with $2.1B in annual revenue -- is layering AI observability features onto its existing platform with the advantage of installed base and existing budgets. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was considered fringe. When I wrote the Perplexity seed check, "AI search" was a failed category that serious investors avoided. Sazabi is entering a market where the structural shift -- AI agents writing production code -- is not only recognized but is already being aggressively monetized by well-capitalized players. The window opened, and a half-dozen companies climbed through it before Sazabi hung out its waitlist sign. That's not a nonobvious market. That's a crowded one.

The one thing that gives me pause is the "logs are all you need" thesis -- the claim that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces from logs alone, giving you "three pillars for the price of one." If that's technically true at production scale, it's not just a feature; it's a structural challenge to Datadog's entire pricing architecture. Datadog bills separately for logs, metrics, traces, and APM. A startup that collapses all of that into a single log ingestion layer with AI reconstruction on top isn't building a better dashboard -- it's attacking the incumbent's business model at the foundation. The analogue would be something like Stripe collapsing payment processing complexity into a single API call. That's an infrastructure-layer insight, and it's the kind of thing I look for. But here's where the single-miracle test gets tough: this technical thesis is ambitious and entirely unvalidated publicly. Reconstructing high-cardinality metrics and distributed traces from log streams at production scale, with the accuracy and latency that operations teams require, would be a significant engineering achievement. The miracle is plausible given where foundation models are heading, but it's a real miracle, not a trivial execution gap.

On infrastructure positioning, observability is inherently an infrastructure category, and Sazabi is showing some of the right signals. The SOC 2 Type I & II, ISO 27017, ISO 27001, and GDPR certifications for a 10-person pre-seed company represent a meaningful resource commitment toward enterprise readiness. The "Perfect Memory" feature that accumulates organizational incident data could create compounding switching costs over time. And the inferred potential for an agent telemetry schema to become a de facto standard is interesting -- if Sazabi defines how coding agent decisions get logged, and that schema gains adoption, that's a platform play. But none of this is proven. There are no public customers, no integrations at scale, no evidence of API-first extensibility. The infrastructure-layer potential is architectural theory, not demonstrated reality.

Callaway's founder-market fit is the strongest signal in the dossier. He built the Infrastructure and Observability teams at Brex -- he literally built the thing he's now productizing. He founded Opkit, took it through YC, and sold it to 11x in 2024. Then he ran engineering at 11x for Alice, described as one of the largest agentic systems in production, which means he has firsthand operational experience with exactly the failure modes Sazabi claims to detect. The angel investor roster -- Anthropic, Vercel, Brex, LangChain, Replit leadership -- reads like a who's who of the AI developer tooling ecosystem, which is both a distribution signal and a credibility signal. But I note the single-founder structure for a 10-person team with no named co-founder or technical leadership. I've written before that every company needs a final decision-maker, so a single CEO isn't inherently a red flag. But in an infrastructure category this competitive, key-person concentration matters. If Callaway gets hit by a bus, what survives?

The bull case requires believing three things: (1) that "logs are all you need" is technically achievable at production scale, which would give Sazabi a genuinely differentiated architectural position; (2) that Datadog's business model -- built on separate billing for logs, metrics, traces, and APM -- creates enough structural inertia that it can't respond quickly; and (3) that the AI coding agent adoption curve continues accelerating, expanding the addressable market for agent-specific telemetry beyond what today's LLM tracing tools (LangSmith, Langfuse) cover. If all three are true, Sazabi isn't just another AI observability startup -- it's the company that restructures how the entire observability stack works in an AI-native world. That's a compelling upside scenario. But "if all three are true" is the operative phrase. And in a market this crowded, with this much capital already deployed, execution speed becomes table stakes rather than a differentiator. Datadog ships features fast. LangSmith has LangChain's installed base. Langfuse now has ClickHouse's data infrastructure behind it.

I keep coming back to the timing question. Not whether the market is real -- it clearly is. But whether the market is still nonobvious enough for a pre-seed entrant to build a defensible position before the well-capitalized incumbents consolidate it. My read is that the window for differentiation in AI observability narrowed significantly in 2025, and Sazabi is arriving at the party just as the music is getting loud. The "logs are all you need" thesis could be a legitimate second-order insight that reopens a differentiated lane, but it needs to be proven, and it needs to be proven fast. I'd watch this company closely -- Callaway has the right background and the right backers -- but at this stage, the crowding and the unvalidated technical thesis keep me from writing a check.
