# shortkit -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

ShortKit's co-founder spent six years building YouTube Shorts infrastructure at Google. This is the most salient fact in the dossier, and it cuts both ways. On one hand, it is genuinely rare domain expertise -- there are perhaps a few hundred engineers on Earth who have operated short-form video infrastructure at that scale. On the other hand, it frames what this company actually is: a productization of institutional knowledge from an incumbent. Seleman is not discovering a secret about the world; he is packaging what Google already knows and selling it as an SDK. The entire playbook -- codec optimization, adaptive bitrate transcoding, ML-driven buffering, feed mechanics -- is drawn from systems that already exist inside YouTube and TikTok. This is definitionally a "1 to n" company: taking what has been built at massive scale and replicating it for a broader market. That can be a perfectly reasonable business. It is not a zero-to-one investment.

What is the secret here? The implicit thesis is that every consumer app and digital publisher will want a TikTok-style short-form video feed, and they will buy infrastructure rather than build it. But this is not a truth that few people agree with -- it is perhaps the single most consensus view in consumer technology today. Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat, Pinterest, LinkedIn, and Netflix have all added short-form vertical video. The format's dominance is so widely acknowledged that it has become a cliché. ShortKit's downstream customers are themselves engaged in mimetic imitation -- copying TikTok's format for their own platforms. And the Girardian dynamics extend upward: ShortKit is one of several companies (BytePlus, Firework, Mux extending its capabilities) racing to serve these imitators. A company whose entire addressable market consists of other companies copying a dominant platform is building on borrowed consensus, not a proprietary insight about the future.

The competitive structure is particularly concerning. BytePlus is a ByteDance subsidiary that has literally commercialized TikTok's internal video stack -- the very technology that defined the short-form format. BytePlus possesses deeper engineering knowledge, broader product surface area, and the implicit backing of the company that invented the category. ShortKit's dossier acknowledges that BytePlus "already possesses TikTok's internal video stack and has commercialized it," then hand-waves this threat by noting BytePlus "targets large enterprises" and "carries geopolitical risk." These are real friction points, but they are circumstantial advantages that could evaporate with a single strategic shift or regulatory change. Meanwhile, Mux has raised $174 million, generates $46 million in annual revenue with 4,900 customers, and would need only to extend its existing platform -- not build from scratch -- to add short-form-specific features. When your competitive moat depends on an incumbent deciding a market segment isn't worth pursuing, you don't have a moat.

The strongest bull case would be a Stripe analogy: Stripe won payments not by discovering a secret about payments but by building a developer experience that was an order of magnitude better than anything available. Could ShortKit do the same for short-form video infrastructure? The dossier claims 2-3x byte reduction through device-aware transcoding and ML-driven pre-fetching. But 2-3x is precisely the kind of incremental improvement I consider insufficient. Proprietary technology must be at least ten times better than its closest substitute to create real monopolistic advantage. A 2-3x improvement invites fast followers; a 10x improvement makes the old way structurally obsolete. Furthermore, the Stripe comparison breaks down on timing and competition: when Stripe launched, there was no well-funded competitor offering a developer-first payments API. ShortKit launches into a market where BytePlus, Mux, and potentially Cloudflare are all capable of offering comparable products. The developer experience advantage, even if real, operates within a competitive landscape that Stripe never faced.

I should note the founders are competent. Seleman's YouTube Shorts tenure is genuine domain expertise, and Bhammar's experience co-founding BusRight -- which raised $7 million and grew revenue 700% in 2022 across 23 states -- demonstrates real operating capability. This is a team that can build and sell a product. But competence is necessary, not sufficient. I do not see evidence of the kind of definite optimism that characterizes my strongest investments -- a specific, bold vision of how the world changes, articulated with conviction that resists social pressure. The company page describes infrastructure features, not a thesis about the future. "We make enterprise-scale short-form video really simple" is an operational description, not a vision. Compare this to what drew me to invest in Palantir or SpaceX: founders who could articulate precisely what the world would look like in ten years if they succeeded, and why that future mattered beyond commercial returns.

This is a services infrastructure company entering a competitive market with incremental technology improvements, serving customers engaged in mimetic imitation of TikTok. No secret is visible. The technology is explicitly 2-3x better, not 10x. The monopoly path is obscured by well-funded competitors with structural advantages. The founders are capable but not visionary in the way my framework requires. I pass.
