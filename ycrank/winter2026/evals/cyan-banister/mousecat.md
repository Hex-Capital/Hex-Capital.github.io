# MouseCat -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that strikes me first about MouseCat: it was the second highest-rated company at the YC W26 product showcase. And I know that sounds like a compliment, but for how I invest, it's actually a caution signal. When something is popular with a room full of sophisticated investors, the price discovery has already happened. The best investments I've made — SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe Energy — were things people thought were crazy. Nobody was voting them "most likely to succeed" at a showcase. AI agents for fraud detection is exactly the kind of opportunity that every venture capitalist in the Valley wants to write a check into right now. "AI" plus "fintech" plus "fraud" is the triple crown of consensus investing, and consensus-based investing is very different from conviction-based investing.

When I sit down with founders, my first question is always: tell me your story, not the business. I want to understand the thread that connects this human being to this specific problem. Nicholas Aldridge spent six and a half years as a principal engineer at AWS building Bedrock — that's genuinely impressive infrastructure work, and being a core maintainer of MCP puts him at the center of how AI agents will communicate. Joseph McAllister built risk models at Coinbase and had a prior exit with Roo Storage. These are accomplished engineers with relevant professional experience. But professional experience isn't the same as the kind of biographical stake I look for. The founders I gravitate toward are solving problems that were forged in their own lives — Travis Kalanick's visceral frustration with the taxi medallion racket, the Crusoe team arriving independently at the insight about wasted gas because they couldn't stop thinking about the physics of it. I don't see that biographical thread here. I see two talented people from elite institutions who identified a market gap through their careers. That's rational and it might work, but it doesn't give me the conviction that they'll be the ones still standing after the inevitable crisis that every startup faces.

The competitive landscape genuinely concerns me. Sardine has $145 million and is growing ARR 130% year-over-year. Feedzai has $347 million and processes 70 billion events annually. DataVisor has nearly $100 million and patented unsupervised ML technology. These aren't sleepy incumbents — they're actively building AI-powered investigation features with existing customer relationships and data moats that took years to accumulate. MouseCat's positioning as a "toolkit that augments existing workflows" is smart product strategy, but there's no structural barrier — no regulatory moat, no patent, no network effect — that prevents these well-funded players from shipping similar AI investigation agents as features within their established platforms. The MCP maintainer role is a genuinely distinctive credential, but here's the irony: maintaining an open protocol standard means you're helping commoditize the very infrastructure layer your product depends on. Open standards lift all boats, including the boats with $347 million in the hull.

Let me be fair about the bull case, because it deserves real consideration. If I'm wrong about this company, here's why: the "investigation-to-production" loop they're closing is a genuine workflow gap that exists because incumbent fraud platforms were architected before LLMs were capable enough to automate multi-step reasoning. Aldridge literally built the agent infrastructure at AWS that this entire category runs on — he's not just using the tools, he helped create them. McAllister built the exact risk models at Coinbase that MouseCat aims to automate. That's not generic founder-market fit; that's two people who collectively built both sides of the equation at the companies that defined the space. If they can land a few mid-market fintech customers before Demo Day and create real switching costs through on-premise deployment and integration density, they could establish a beachhead that incumbents struggle to dislodge because the big players' sales motions are oriented around wholesale platform replacement, not lightweight toolkit adoption. The timing — with AI-enabled fraud accelerating faster than manual teams can handle — creates genuine urgency. And McAllister's prior exit demonstrates he can actually ship and sell, not just build.

But even granting all of that, this doesn't match how I invest. I'm looking for the misfits and free-thinking people building things that seem ridiculous to everyone else. MouseCat is building something that seems smart and obvious to everyone. The founders are credentialed insiders from the institutions that defined this space, not outsiders who arrived at a non-obvious insight through their own unusual path. The company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations — it serves enterprise risk teams at financial institutions. There's no "weird factor," no stigma that scares other investors away, no moment where I feel that jolt of intuitive energy about a future that only a few dreamers can see. This is a well-executed entry into a hot market by qualified founders, and I genuinely hope it works. But it's not my bet to make.
