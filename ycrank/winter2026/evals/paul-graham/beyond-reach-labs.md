# Beyond Reach Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately is Fogelson's PhD. He didn't decide to start a space company and then go looking for a problem. He spent years at Carnegie Mellon, funded by NASA, working on the mathematics of how structures can change geometry -- mechanical metamaterials. The deployable solar array concept didn't come from a brainstorming session or a market report. It grew out of years of research, published papers, zero-gravity experiments, and a genuine obsession with making structures do things they weren't supposed to do. That's the organic test passing cleanly. And then his co-founder Collins spent seven years at SpaceX building parachute systems for Dragon -- which are, in essence, deployable structures that have to work perfectly or astronauts die. These two didn't meet at a startup weekend. They met as freshmen at Penn in 2013. Thirteen years. That co-founder relationship alone puts them in rare territory.

The schlep here is extraordinary. Space hardware qualification is the kind of painful, tedious, multi-year regulatory obstacle that makes smart software engineers unconsciously look away. You need to survive vibration testing, thermal cycling, atomic oxygen erosion, radiation degradation -- and then convince risk-averse government procurement officers to trust your hardware with their $500M satellite. Redwire and Northrop Grumman have decades of flight heritage. Every satellite buyer's default is to use what already worked. Breaking into that market requires not just better technology but the willingness to grind through years of testing, certification, and relationship-building. This is exactly the kind of problem I described in "Schlep Blindness" -- not hidden because it's contrarian, but hidden because the work required to solve it is so ugly that founders unconsciously filter it out.

What makes me lean forward rather than away is that these founders are the right people for precisely this schlep. Collins has already navigated the design-qualify-fly cycle at SpaceX. He knows what it takes to get human-rated hardware from prototype to orbit and back. Fogelson holds the patents and conducted the zero-g testing with NASA's money. They're not two MBAs who read about the space power market in a McKinsey report -- they're two mechanical engineers with robotics degrees from Penn's GRASP Lab who converged on this problem from complementary angles. The $175M in LOIs suggests they can sell, too, though I weight LOIs cautiously. Non-binding letters from unnamed counterparties in the space industry are common and often don't convert. Still, the fact that they've generated that kind of interest at the pre-flight-heritage stage says something about the sales conversations they're having.

The honest concern is capital intensity and timeline. This is not a company that can become ramen profitable by next quarter. They need to build flight hardware, get it on a rocket, deploy it in orbit, and accumulate operational data -- all before they can compete for real contracts. That demo flight is targeted for late Q2 2027. Between now and then, they'll need significantly more capital than their ~$200K in grants plus YC. The "multiple miracles" problem applies partially: the technology needs to work in orbit AND customers need to convert LOIs AND manufacturing needs to scale. But I've made exceptions for this pattern before. My largest personal check went to Boom Supersonic -- a company that needs all of those same things and more. When the ambition is genuinely frightening and the founders are genuinely the right people, I override my usual preference for capital-efficient software businesses. The question is whether the magnitude of the opportunity justifies the magnitude of the bet.

The bull case is substantial. If deployable power in orbit really does need to scale from 50 MW to anything like 500 GW, whoever solves the structural deployment problem owns a critical piece of space infrastructure. The current architectures -- roll-out, fan-fold, rigid panel -- all hit the same length-stiffness-mass wall. A genuinely new structural paradigm, protected by patents and backed by published science, could become the standard. Once you have flight heritage, switching costs in aerospace are enormous. The competitive moat gets deeper with every successful mission. And the timing is right -- Starship and New Glenn are about to make power-hungry space applications economically viable for the first time. This is the kind of company where if they execute, they don't just succeed, they become a category-defining infrastructure provider.

I'm investing. The organic origin is genuine -- years of PhD research, not a trend-chase. The schlep is massive enough to keep competition structurally thin. The founders have been friends for thirteen years and bring complementary expertise that maps precisely to the problem. The risks are real -- capital intensity, long timelines, flight heritage gap -- but these are execution risks, not idea risks. The idea is sound. The question is whether these specific founders can push through the qualification gauntlet. Given Collins's SpaceX pedigree and Fogelson's NASA-funded research, they're as well-positioned as any two-person team could be.
