# Bylaw -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Bylaw is three University of Waterloo first-years shipping a developer SDK into the most-chased YC category of the season, and Galileo released Agent Control as Apache-2.0 in March 2026 with AWS and CrewAI as launch partners -- which means Bylaw's highest-scoring axis is a faint founder-adjacency signal from a BitGo internship, and its lowest is the "magically weird" filter, which inverts hard when the category is consensus. That pairing tells you exactly what kind of company this is: a credentialed-cohort technical sleeve in a lane where the moat will accrue to whoever has distribution, not whoever shipped the cleanest SDK.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling I described in my pass example -- ex-FAANG PMs, Stanford CS, the room is already bidding -- is the LOW rung on the "magically weird" ladder, and Bylaw sits one notch above it only because the three founders aren't FAANG PMs. The mechanism that killed that hypothetical is the same one operating here: Galileo at $68M raised and 834% YoY revenue growth shipped open-source Agent Control in March 2026, Lakera was acquired by Check Point for ~$300M in November 2025, and Prompt Security is sitting on $23M with an enterprise GenAI gateway. Bylaw is not finding them before it becomes consensus; Bylaw IS the consensus, three months late to the public OSS release of a substitute. The "deterministic policy engine, LLM compiles not decides" framing on usebylaw.com is a sentence I could have heard from four other YC S26 booths, which is precisely the Yahoo Finance March 2025 anti-pattern -- the round will be priced as if the outcome is settled.

HQ Trivia's failure mechanism was unresolved co-founder authority -- Yusupov and Kroll, no tiebreaker, the company eats itself when the product slows -- and I lived that lesson. Bylaw lists three co-founders who met in their first year of CS at Waterloo, with no named CEO anywhere in the dossier and no documented operating agreement. That is the GameCrush ~2011 setup, not the SpaceX one. The mitigating note is that the trio appears to be a single Waterloo cohort that has presumably stress-tested each other through internships at Meta, Optiver, BitGo -- but cohort affinity is not governance, and I've watched cohort affinity dissolve on the first product disagreement. Default applies, with no upward signal to lift it.

On founder lived experience -- dimension one -- the Anduril-Luckey 2017 template is a founder who built Oculus in a garage before pivoting to defense. Bylaw's three founders interned at BitGo and Optiver, environments where unverified-data writes carry real financial blast radius, which is a thread worth pulling. But an internship is not ownership; Mazin Al-Ani's LinkedIn says "cs @ uw, swe @ optiver," not "I watched a write-error blow up a custody operation and I'm fixing it." The origin reads analytical-adjacent rather than lived-autobiographical, which is the difference between an 11-of-32 default and a 20-of-32 conviction check.

Capital intensity and unsexy-regulated swing -- dimension four -- has a real mechanism here that could compound over 18 months: the EU AI Act high-risk obligations apply August 2, 2026, and a signed audit log for write-enabled agents is the kind of artifact that turns into regulatory infrastructure if enforcement bites. That's the Vigil Labs (August 2025) shape of bet, where the moat compounds in domains other investors won't enter. But Bylaw is a software SDK, not hardware or licensed health, and the regulated-tailwind story works only if customers fear August 2026 enough to pay for runtime verification rather than accept Galileo's free Apache-2.0 alternative. The mechanism is real; the differentiation against free is not yet proven.

Physical verifiability -- dimension five -- is where the counterfactual is starkest. Niantic worked because I had been playing Ingress since 2012 and could feel the pull in my own thumbs. Bylaw is a developer SDK that emits "allow/restrict/review/block" decisions on a critical path I cannot install on my phone, photograph at a Four Seasons café, or hand to a friend. For Bylaw to flip a tier on this axis it would need a public demo agent doing live evidence reconciliation that a non-developer could observe in 48 hours, and the website has no such artifact -- no pricing page, no Product Hunt launch, no logos. Defaults below midpoint because nothing tactile has shipped, and that's where Bylaw lands.

Forecast, 36 months: Bylaw either shuts down or is acqui-hired by Langfuse, Braintrust, or LangSmith for under $20M, with Galileo's Agent Control absorbing the deterministic-policy use case as a free OSS primitive and foundation-model vendors shipping native evidence-verification gates by late 2027. The mechanism is platform-vendor absorption -- Bylaw's own integration list (LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI, Braintrust) is a roster of the entities most likely to commoditize the layer, because evidence-gate is a feature of an observability platform, not a standalone company.

The strongest objection is that EU AI Act enforcement starting August 2, 2026 creates a forcing function specifically for signed, deterministic, runtime audit logs that Galileo's eval-flavored Agent Control doesn't cleanly satisfy, and that the trio's BitGo exposure means they actually know what a high-stakes write path feels like -- the way Hassabis at DeepMind violated my pedigree screen but won on polymath compounding. That argument stings because regulated-finance and crypto-custody really are domains where "the tool was permitted but the facts disagreed" is a live incident, not a hypothetical. I dismantle it on two specifics: first, no founder in the dossier names a write-error incident they personally owned -- the BitGo line is a team-aggregate internship credit, not a Palmer Luckey origin story -- and second, the dossier shows no design partners, no logos, no pilots, which is what regulatory-tailwind plays do have at pre-seed when the tailwind is real. The verdict flips if within six months Bylaw names two regulated-industry design partners (a custody bank, a healthcare payer, a defense contractor) signed to the deterministic-policy engine specifically, OR one founder publishes a written incident report of a write-error they owned in production. Until then, this is the pass example, almost line for line.
