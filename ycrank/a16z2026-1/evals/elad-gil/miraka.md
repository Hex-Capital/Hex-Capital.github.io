# Miraka -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Miraka's most striking feature is the $300K ARR with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments from a three-person team that just graduated from Princeton. In healthcare, where sales cycles often stretch six to twelve months, selling into NewYork-Presbyterian and reaching that revenue milestone at pre-seed is legitimately uncommon. Nolan Abeyta, Kazuo Nakamura, and Jesse Abeyta all built Overwatch Health together -- a cardiac rehab platform -- before founding Miraka, which means these founders aren't tourists in cardiac care. They've been grinding on the same problem for four-plus years. The "youngest team to sell into a top-5 heart hospital" claim maps to the NewYork-Presbyterian partnership, which is verifiable and meaningful. I take early traction in healthcare seriously because it's hard-won: you can't growth-hack your way into a hospital system's clinical workflow.

The market structure timing, though, is where my framework gets skeptical. Remote patient monitoring for cardiac patients is not a nonobvious market. Cadence has raised $144M at a $1B valuation. Biofourmis raised $465M before its CopilotIQ acquisition. HRS has pulled in $85M+. When I backed Harvey in 2022, legal AI had been a graveyard for a decade and I was reading a technology threshold crossing (GPT capability) that most investors hadn't recognized. Miraka's market has no equivalent structural catalyst. The CMS 2026 fee schedule lowering RPM billing thresholds is a genuine tailwind, but it benefits Cadence and HRS equally -- it's a rising-tide regulatory change, not a window that favors new entrants over well-capitalized incumbents. Wearable proliferation has been ongoing for years. The "why now" for Miraka is more about business model innovation (being the care team vs. selling tools) than about an exogenous shift cracking the market open.

Miraka's positioning sits at the opposite end of my infrastructure-layer spectrum. The company delivers clinical services directly to patients -- it's the care team, not the API that care teams build on. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments layer that thousands of companies integrated into core workflows, creating compounding switching costs. Miraka creates switching costs through clinical outcomes integration with hospital workflows, but these are linear, relationship-based switching costs, not the multiplicative, API-dependency switching costs I look for. More concretely: Miraka must recruit, credential, and retain clinical staff to grow. Every new patient requires incremental human capital. Jesse Abeyta's Amazon AI background and the predictive risk models could improve clinician-to-patient ratios over time, but the fundamental unit economics remain services-driven. This matters because I've seen this pattern repeatedly -- technology-enabled services companies grow impressively until they hit the staffing wall, and then growth flattens or margins compress.

The single-miracle test produces a mixed result. Miraka's core clinical model appears to work -- the >90% readmission reduction and 94% patient capture rate (versus 10% industry average), if validated, represent extraordinary outcomes. That's the good news: the product-market fit miracle may have already occurred. But scaling from NewYork-Presbyterian to fifty hospital systems requires simultaneously solving clinical workforce recruitment at scale, maintaining outcome quality as the clinician base grows, and navigating annual CMS billing rule changes that directly determine unit economics. The CMS reimbursement dependency is particularly concerning -- one unfavorable annual rule change could restructure the entire revenue model. That's closer to two miracles (scale clinical ops + maintain regulatory favorability) than one.

The strongest bull case: Miraka has identified that the 10% capture rate isn't a technology problem but an operational model problem. Every well-funded competitor -- Cadence, Biofourmis, HRS -- sells monitoring tools to health systems that lack the staff to use them. Miraka's insight is that you have to BE the care team to reach the other 90% of eligible patients. If that's correct, the $14B RPM market has been structurally mispriced because SaaS tools were solving the wrong problem, and Miraka's vertically integrated model is the actual unlock. The 94% capture rate would be evidence of this thesis. For me to invest, I'd need third-party validation of the clinical outcomes (not self-reported), visibility into per-patient unit economics including clinical staffing costs, and evidence that the staffing model can scale without margin degradation. I'd also want to see the path from fee-for-service billing to outcome-based contracts, which would move Miraka's margin architecture from services-grade to something closer to software-grade.

Miraka scores highest on founder-market fit -- four years of domain iteration from Overwatch Health to Miraka, a live hospital partnership, and real revenue -- but lowest on the two dimensions I weight most heavily: nonobvious market timing and infrastructure-layer positioning. The RPM market is well-understood and well-funded, and Miraka builds at the application/services layer rather than the infrastructure layer. These are the two dimensions that, in my framework, separate investments I make from investments I respect but pass on.
