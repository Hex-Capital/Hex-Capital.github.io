# matforge -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Akash Ramdas spent years at Stanford screening 15,000+ semiconductor interconnect materials through first-principles calculations. His discovered compounds ended up on Intel and TSMC roadmaps — not because he pitched them, but because the materials worked. That's the clearest specific knowledge signal I've seen in this batch. He didn't identify "AI for semiconductor materials" as a market opportunity and go looking for domain expertise. He *is* the domain expertise, and the company is the productization of knowledge that took years of obsessive work to accumulate. If you replaced Akash with another competent technical founder, you'd have a generic AI-for-materials wrapper competing against companies with $300M+ in funding. With him, you have something those companies can't replicate by hiring.

The principal-agent dynamics here are structurally beautiful. The incumbent materials suppliers — Entegris, Shin-Etsu, BASF Electronic Materials — profit from selling existing materials at scale. Discovering replacements for their own products is economic suicide. They're agents of their current P&L. The foundries — Intel, TSMC — have internal R&D but are capacity-constrained and have historically outsourced exploratory materials research to universities. They're agents of their production schedules, not principals in discovery. Matforge is the principal with no legacy revenue to protect, attacking an industry where every incumbent has structural incentives *not* to innovate on materials. That's the same dynamic that made Perplexity compelling against Google — but here the agents are locked in by physical supply chains, not just ad revenue.

The leverage architecture is real but impure. AI agents screening thousands of candidate materials computationally is genuine code leverage — you're replacing years of PhD researchers running DFT simulations with software that runs while you sleep. Two people building what previously required large research teams is exactly the shrinking company thesis playing out. But materials science has a last mile that software doesn't: atoms. MIT Technology Review flagged the prediction-to-synthesis gap as the fundamental bottleneck. You can predict a material computationally, but someone still has to make it in a lab and prove it works at fab scale. This isn't zero marginal cost all the way down. The leverage is massive in the discovery phase — compressing 10 years to months — but the validation phase requires physical infrastructure, whether through partnerships with university labs or eventually building their own. That physical bottleneck caps the leverage below what a pure software company achieves.

The co-founder pairing is well-constructed. Akash brings the semiconductor materials knowledge; Advaith brings production-grade agentic AI systems experience from Persona AI (acquired by Luma Labs), where he built autonomous agents for real-world applications. They've known each other since IIT Madras in 2015, co-authored a book together, and both chose technical paths — not MBA-to-startup pipelines. That's a relationship built on shared work over seven years, not a co-founder matching event. The fact that both are builders — one in materials science, one in AI systems — means neither is dependent on hired engineers to ship. And I'll note that Akash's prior stint at ePlane Company means he's been in the deep-tech startup environment before; this isn't someone stepping out of academia cold.

The bear case is the $1B+ wall of capital in AI-for-materials. Periodic Labs raised $300M from a16z. Lila Sciences has $550M. CuspAI has $154M with Samsung Ventures as a backer. These companies could pivot to semiconductors tomorrow. But capital is labor leverage — it lets you hire people. Specific knowledge is permissionless leverage — it can't be bought. CuspAI already lists semiconductors as a focus area, but they don't have a founder whose materials are on TSMC's roadmap. The concentrated customer base is the other risk — fewer than ten major foundries globally, twelve-to-twenty-four month sales cycles. A two-person pre-seed company selling to Intel faces an organizational mismatch that specific knowledge alone may not bridge. If the Intel/TSMC roadmap adoption turns out to be academic citations rather than commercial relationships, the warm entry point evaporates.

I'm investing. The specific knowledge density is the kind of signal that's almost impossible to fake at this stage — years of PhD work producing materials that the industry's two largest foundries plan to use. The principal-agent structure of the market favors a small, knowledge-dense attacker over capital-rich generalists. The leverage isn't pure software, and the customer concentration creates execution risk, but the core formula — irreplaceable specific knowledge productized through AI leverage by a principal with no legacy to protect — is the structure that produces outlier returns in deep tech. The physical synthesis bottleneck is real, but Akash has done synthesis. He knows what's makeable. That's the specific knowledge gap that $300M of competitor funding doesn't close.
