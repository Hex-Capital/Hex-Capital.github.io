# ProjectX -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Two of ProjectX's three original co-founders — Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das, listed on Crunchbase as founding the company in 2022 — are gone from the YC page, replaced by Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder. I list co-founder disputes as one of the top mistakes that kill startups, and losing two-thirds of your founding team before you've shipped a product people use is not a personnel adjustment. It's a structural event. Maybe Rounak Adhikary made the right call upgrading his team — Bishal's Go/Rust/Kubernetes depth is directly relevant to building a cloud OS, and that matters. But the absence of any explanation in the dossier means I'm left pattern-matching against the many companies I've seen where early co-founder departures signal unresolved equity complications or fundamental disagreements about direction. ProjectX needs its current team to be the right team, and I have no stories to evaluate that claim against.

Rounak Adhikary founded Woldix, a web development agency, at 19 and used its revenue to fund early ProjectX development. That's a genuine resourcefulness signal — bootstrapping from a services business to fund the product you actually want to build. I've backed founders who did versions of this. But the signal is mild compared to what I look for at the top of the scale. ProjectX has also collected roughly $276K in non-dilutive funding: $204K from Google Cloud, approximately $60K in Microsoft and AWS credits, and $12K from Indian government ministries. Winning Tiger Launch at Princeton and the WTC Innovation Award among 17,000 startups at IIT Bombay shows Rounak can compete and present. But competitions reward pitching; markets reward products. I want to hear the story about the time something went wrong and how they hacked around it. ProjectX's dossier gives me institutional achievements — grants, awards, accelerator acceptances — when what I need is evidence of the animal quality, the slightly-too-intense obsessiveness that makes founders improvise solutions nobody expected.

ProjectX's origin story is conspicuously absent from the dossier. When I invested $15K in Stripe for 2%, I already knew Patrick Collison because I'd funded Auctomatic. But more importantly, Stripe's genesis was viscerally organic — two developers who'd personally experienced the absurdity of accepting payments online and decided to fix it. ProjectX frames its problem as "every conventional computer restricts users to a single-focus model." That's a market observation, not a personal frustration. Nobody in the dossier describes the specific moment they were running Windows and Linux workloads side by side and thought "this is broken." The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. When the origin story reads like a category analysis rather than a lived experience, I worry the founders are seeing what their market research revealed rather than what their own work demanded. ProjectX is building infrastructure for power users — but are they themselves the power users who couldn't live without it?

ProjectX's per-app GPU isolation architecture does pass my schlep blindness test. Building a cloud OS where every application runs in its own isolated virtual computer with dedicated GPU, sub-second cold starts, and cross-OS support — Windows and Linux in the same session — is genuinely painful engineering that most founders would avoid. Microsoft would have to cannibalize Azure Virtual Desktop's per-desktop model to replicate it; Amazon WorkSpaces is structurally committed to single-OS workspaces. Neverinstall ($1.21M raised) and Vagon ($542K) are adjacent but neither offers per-app isolation or multi-OS execution. The schlep here is real, and it creates at least a temporary moat. But ProjectX announced its public beta in June 2024 — nearly two years ago — and the website returned a minimal loading page at time of research. No user counts, no customer logos, no revenue signals, zero job postings on the YC page. Four years of work from a 23-year-old founder with sustained commitment is admirable, but four years without visible product iteration or user traction is also a data point about learning rate. I'd expect a team with Bishal's systems engineering depth (29 GitHub repos spanning Go, Rust, C++, Docker, Kubernetes) to have shipped something people are using, even if imperfectly.

The bull case for ProjectX is real and I want to take it seriously. Young founder self-funds from his own agency revenue, assembles a systems-capable CTO, tackles a genuine schlep in cloud infrastructure that incumbents can't easily replicate without self-cannibalization, wins competitive validation from Princeton and IIT Bombay, secures hyperscaler backing from Google Cloud, and gets into YC. The AI agent workflow angle — human and AI agent working side by side in the same session — is timely and could be the wedge that makes per-app isolation feel necessary rather than novel. If ProjectX can demonstrate that AI agents need their own isolated compute environments running alongside human workflows, they could own a category that doesn't exist yet. The Boom Supersonic exception shows I'll override normal heuristics for genuinely frightening ambition, and "reinvent the computer" qualifies as ambitious. But Boom's override required missionary conviction at a level that was unmistakable — Blake Scholl's obsession with supersonic flight was visible in every conversation. ProjectX's dossier gives me awards and grants, not obsession. The condition that would flip me to Invest: show me a cohort of users — even 50 — who use ProjectX daily and can't go back to their old setup. That's the signal I trust. A small number of people wanting it a large amount.

The name "ProjectX" itself is a minor but telling detail — it's the placeholder name you give a project before you know what it is. Combined with the tagline "the computer that does everything at once," it suggests a team still searching for the precise language that would make this idea click, which correlates with still searching for the precise product that would make users stay. ProjectX has the raw ingredients — young, committed CEO in Rounak, technically capable CTO in Bishal, a genuine infrastructure schlep to solve — but the two dimensions that most determine my verdict are the weakest: no organic problem discovery narrative, and no evidence of product iteration or user love after four years and a public beta. Those gaps, combined with the co-founder turnover, keep this in mixed-signal territory where I can see the potential but can't convince myself to write a check from my own money.
