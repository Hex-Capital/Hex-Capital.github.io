# Cascade -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Cascade is an AI application-layer startup. Full stop. Every time someone pitches me "we used LLMs to automate [workflow] for [vertical]," I run the same test: can the incumbent with distribution add this as a feature in 18 months? AutogenAI has $65.3M in the bank and a Salesforce Ventures relationship. Procore is the dominant construction management platform and already owns the customer relationship Cascade is trying to build. Hannia Zia's own Head of Growth was the #2 sales rep at Procore -- which tells you exactly who the incumbent is. When your best hire's Rolodex is the incumbent's customer base, you're borrowing distribution, not building it. Cascade is selling proposal automation and RFP discovery into the AEC vertical. That's not a new primitive. That's a feature set that Procore's product team is probably already whiteboarding.

The mispricing test fails here in a way that's almost instructive. "AI for B2B vertical" is the single most consensus-crowded category in venture capital right now. Cascade has four funded competitors visible in the dossier alone -- AutogenAI, Joist AI ($4M), Workorb AI ($2.6M), Inventive.ai ($4M from YC S23) -- and that's just what surfaced in a quick search. a16z Speedrun is itself a factory-farm system, exactly the conveyor belt I've said is dying. There's no contrarian thesis here. Nobody at a demo day is going to hear "AI proposal writing for construction" and think "that's fucking crazy." They're going to think "that makes obvious sense," and that's precisely the problem. When every sophisticated investor nods along, the upside is already in the price.

Compare Cascade to Metropolis, which I seeded early and is now valued around $5B. Both apply AI to unglamorous physical-world industries. But the structural difference is everything: Metropolis operates physical parking infrastructure. The software amplifies the business, but no LLM can replicate a parking garage. Cascade has no physical asset. It scrapes government RFP portals -- portals that could change format, restrict access, or get consolidated without warning -- and generates proposals using the same foundational models available to everyone. The Davis-Bacon compliance and Deltek/Ajera ERP integrations provide real switching costs, and the "data layer for all construction projects" positioning hints at something more durable. But hints aren't moats. Accumulated proposal history and firm capability profiles take years to compound into genuine defensibility, and Cascade is three months old.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. $183k cARR from zero in three months is exceptional velocity at any stage. Fifteen companies signed, eleven in the last month, and the first three clients doubling their spend -- that's not trial-and-bounce behavior, that's product-market fit forming. Hannia Zia scaling Google Pay from zero to 100 million users in India demonstrates she can execute at scale in markets that are operationally brutal. Joana Ferreira's ML engineering background from Google Search and Amazon is legitimate technical depth, and the fact that they worked together at UnlikelyAI "for many years" before starting Cascade means the co-founder relationship isn't synthetic. Construction firms that built JFK, LaGuardia, Four Seasons, and nuclear reactors are not small accounts playing with free trials. If Cascade can become the system of record for AEC project pursuit before Procore wakes up, the vertical data flywheel could get ahead of horizontal competitors who lack construction-domain context. That's a real scenario.

But "what if they execute faster than the incumbent" is a race condition, not a structural advantage. And race conditions are exactly the kind of bet I avoid. Hannia and Joana are clearly competent -- Yale CS, Google, Forbes 30 Under 30, real ML depth -- but competent founders executing a sensible plan in a validated market is the definition of boring in my framework. I need the sane-person-insane-idea ratio, and nothing about "AI automates RFP workflows for construction" triggers the insanity detector. This is a well-executed version of exactly what you'd expect smart people to build in 2026. The construction industry's slowness to adopt software is a real market gap, but recognizing that gap is no longer contrarian -- it's a thesis you can read in any construction-tech investor's deck. Cascade is a good company in a competitive category at consensus pricing. That's not my kind of bet.
