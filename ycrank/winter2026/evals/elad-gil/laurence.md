# Laurence -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Laurence is that it's essentially a quantitative trading desk pointed at Amazon's ad marketplace. Leo Gierhake led research at Jump Trading overseeing billions in live volume, and the team frames ad bidding explicitly as a stochastic optimization problem -- continuous decision-making under uncertainty with profit constraints. That's not marketing language. That's the actual technical transfer: the same mathematics that displaced human traders in financial markets applied to a $60 billion ad marketplace where the incumbents are still running rule-based or shallow-ML systems. When I see a team with genuine quantitative finance depth entering a market dominated by what are essentially "1980s trading pit" approaches, the pattern recognition fires. The early results support it -- $10M in managed ad spend at pre-seed, 40% ACoS reductions, 15-20% profit increases for clients. Those aren't vanity metrics; that's measurable ROI on live capital.

But here's my structural problem: what's the inflection point? When I invested in Harvey, the "why now" was precise -- GPT-3 to GPT-4 scaling laws meant legal-grade model fidelity was 12-18 months away, not five years. I could map the capability curve and see the moment approaching. With Laurence, the "why now" is softer. Amazon's ad complexity has grown, RL infrastructure has matured, keyword counts have exploded -- but these are gradual trends, not a threshold crossing. The reinforcement learning techniques Jump Trading uses have been available for years. The market has been large and growing for years. Pacvue has $50.8M in revenue. Quartile manages $2B+ in annual ad spend across 5,300 brands. This is not an underserved market waiting for its first real solution -- it's a served market where the bet is that RL is categorically better than what exists. That's a defensible technical thesis, but it's a different kind of bet than catching a market at its structural moment.

The single-miracle test actually works well here. The one thing that has to be true: RL-based bid optimization must deliver measurably and durably superior ROI compared to rule-based and simpler ML approaches. The team is organized around proving exactly this, and the early client results (2x to 5.78x ROAS improvement, 50% sales growth) suggest they're making progress. The miracle isn't being delivered by an external lab -- it's their own technical capability, which gives them more control than Harvey had waiting for OpenAI. But it also means the miracle IS technically reproducible. If Pacvue or Quartile hires three quant traders and rebuilds their bidding engine over 18 months, the moat narrows considerably. The switching cost from audit trails and optimization history provides some stickiness, but it's not structural like ITAR compliance or CLIA regulation.

Where this falls short in my framework is the product-to-distribution trajectory. Stripe started with payments and became a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury. What does Laurence distribute through Amazon PPC optimization? You could imagine expansion to Walmart or Instacart ad marketplaces, or moving into Amazon pricing, inventory, and catalog optimization -- but each of those is essentially a new product requiring new model development, not distribution through an existing channel. Worse, the company is building entirely on Amazon's platform, which means Amazon controls the distribution infrastructure. That's the opposite of becoming a distribution channel yourself. If Amazon decides to offer native RL-based optimization as a feature of its ad console -- which is well within their technical capability -- Laurence faces an existential threat from its own platform.

The strongest bull case: this is my "boring but critical" pattern hiding in plain sight. Amazon PPC management is deeply unsexy infrastructure that every CPG brand needs, exactly like payroll or operations alerting. The quant finance transfer is a genuine technical arbitrage that the e-commerce-native incumbents will be slow to replicate because they lack the talent and the institutional knowledge. The $2.5M to $10M managed spend growth curve suggests product-market fit is emerging, and in a market where optimization quality is directly measurable in dollars, superior performance creates a self-reinforcing data flywheel. If Laurence can prove 40% ACoS reductions consistently across diverse brand categories, the switching argument becomes purely economic -- and economic arguments scale. The "boring" market with provable ROI is how PagerDuty and Gusto became category leaders. That's the version of this company I'd be excited about. But PagerDuty didn't face five funded competitors with $50M+ in revenue when it started. That competitive density changes the math.

I respect the execution -- $10M in managed ad spend with named clients and measurable results at pre-seed is impressive velocity, and the founder-market fit between Jump Trading quant research and ad bid optimization is tighter than most teams I see. But the market doesn't have the structural inflection my framework requires for high conviction. This is a better-mousetrap bet in a competitive market, not a non-obvious market at a tipping point.
