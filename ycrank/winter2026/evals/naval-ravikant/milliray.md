# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Matthew Moore didn't read about drone detection and decide to found a company. He spent years in a St Andrews lab building 94 GHz Doppler radar systems with his own hands, publishing peer-reviewed work on the micro-Doppler signatures that spinning propeller blades leave in millimeter-wave returns. His PhD thesis IS the product. That's the kind of specific knowledge I look for -- knowledge that emerged from genuine obsession with a narrow physics problem, not from scanning market reports for a hot category. You can't teach someone to design a 94 GHz radar system in a classroom. You can't even teach it in most RF engineering labs. The number of people on Earth who have actually built and tested hardware at this exact frequency for this exact application is probably in the low dozens. When I combine Moore's radar physics with Doherty's atomic/laser physics from Oxford and Frances de Mas's automated reasoning work, the team has a rare complementarity -- sensor hardware, precision optics, and classification algorithms, each grounded in deep doctoral-level work. On the specific knowledge axis alone, this is among the strongest founder-domain fits I've seen in a pre-seed company.

The problem is leverage -- or rather, the absence of it. Milliray is building physical radar hardware and selling it to defense ministries through multi-year procurement cycles. Each unit must be manufactured. Each customer requires integration, qualification, and relationship management through byzantine acquisition processes. A 3-person team with a deployment-ready prototype is not creating a new form of leverage for individuals or building a platform where one engineer's work serves millions. This is the opposite of code-as-leverage. It's closer to renting out specialized knowledge by the unit. I backed Extropic because thermodynamic computing chips, if they work, create computational leverage at the hardware layer that compounds -- each chip enables exponentially more computation. Milliray's radar units are excellent instruments, but each one detects drones in one location. The scaling function is linear: more coverage requires proportionally more hardware and more sales effort. Defense hardware businesses can become very large -- DroneShield just crossed AUD 216M in revenue -- but the path there requires a sales and manufacturing apparatus that looks nothing like the leverage-first companies I prefer.

The timing is also less contrarian than it appears. In 2010, when I backed Uber, ride-sharing was illegal and investors thought Travis was crazy. In 2014, Bitcoin was dismissed as nerd money. Counter-drone detection in 2026 is the opposite of contrarian -- it's front-page consensus. Ukraine demonstrated 1,600 drone attacks per month. Over a billion dollars in counter-drone contracts closed in December 2025 alone. DroneShield grew revenue 276% year-over-year. Every defense minister in NATO is demanding counter-drone capability. The specific technical approach -- 94 GHz millimeter-wave vs. lower-frequency radar -- carries a degree of non-consensus, since most competitors operate at different bands. But "we use a better frequency" is an engineering insight, not a paradigm challenge. Smart defense analysts are not dismissing the need for better drone detection; they're actively funding it. The pricing in this market already reflects optimism.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If Milliray's 94 GHz radar genuinely detects nano drones that evade everything Robin Radar, Blighter, and DroneShield field -- targets that are invisible to existing systems -- then this is not incremental but categorical. A radar that sees what nothing else can see has physics-based differentiation that no amount of funding can quickly replicate. NATO DIANA and DASA provide institutional on-ramps that solve the cold-start distribution problem in defense. European sovereign tech preferences create a structural tailwind for a UK-based company. And if the micro-Doppler classification algorithms improve with each deployment's data, there's a nascent compounding dynamic in the software layer. The company that becomes the standard detection layer for the smallest, hardest-to-see drones could be extraordinarily valuable as drone warfare scales. This is the Anduril analogy -- capturing defense value by applying modern engineering where legacy primes won't bother. If I were evaluating this purely on founder-domain knowledge and the physics of the opportunity, I'd be much more excited.

Two additional concerns temper my conviction. The CTO, Thomas Doherty, appears simultaneously listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, an ion trap quantum computing venture. In a 3-person startup where every founder's full bandwidth is existential, divided attention at the technical leadership level is a structural risk, not a scheduling inconvenience. And the dual branding -- Milliray vs. DroneTector, with redirecting domains and mismatched email addresses -- signals early organizational ambiguity that defense procurement officers notice. These are fixable problems, but they reveal something about the team's current operational focus.

The net assessment: exceptional specific knowledge trapped in a leverage architecture that doesn't match my thesis. Moore knows something that almost nobody else on the planet knows, and he's built the hardware to prove it. But I invest in leverage mechanics, not just knowledge. A company that requires me to ask "how many units can they manufacture and ship?" rather than "how many users does each engineer serve?" sits outside the framework that generated my best returns. I respect the team and the technology. This is a genuine deep-science bet in a real and growing market. But it's not my kind of bet.
