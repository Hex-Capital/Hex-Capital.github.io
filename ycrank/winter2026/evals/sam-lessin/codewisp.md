# CodeWisp -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my fundamental problem with CodeWisp: this is a sane person with a sane idea. Elvin Fu's credentials are genuinely impressive -- 20 million students taught game development, top-20 on Scratch out of 135 million users, built two game engines from scratch, AWS experience. That's the sane part, and it's strong. But "use AI to let anyone make games without coding" is not an insane idea. It's the single most obvious application of large language models to a consumer creative workflow. Every AI coding platform on the planet is converging on exactly this use case. The 2025 Vibe Coding Game Jam attracted over a thousand submissions -- this category is already a movement, not a contrarian bet. When I backed Brian Armstrong, almost everyone thought building a regulated crypto exchange was crazy. When I look at CodeWisp, I see an idea that every VC in San Francisco would nod along to enthusiastically. That's a problem, because if the idea isn't insane, the returns are already priced in.

The AI kill test is where this falls apart completely. Strip away the LLM layer and ask: what is this business? It's a game publishing community with educational courses. That's fine, but it's not a killer business on its own -- it's a feature set that only matters because the AI generation capability makes it magical. The AI isn't the cherry on top; the AI IS the sundae. This is precisely the category I've been publicly arguing against for two years. CodeWisp depends on third-party LLM APIs with no evidence of proprietary model training. When OpenAI or Anthropic ships the next model that's 3x better at code generation, every competitor gets that upgrade simultaneously. Rosebud AI already has $2.7 million in 2024 revenue and $9.2 million raised. Replit has $222 million in funding and a massive existing user base. The competitive moat here is 1,513 published games -- that's not a defensible position, that's a weekend for Roblox's creator tools team.

The bull case I have to genuinely engage with: Elvin Fu's distribution is a real, non-replicable asset. Eighty-four thousand YouTube subscribers and reach to twenty million students represents organic access to exactly the audience that would adopt this product. If CodeWisp becomes the default on-ramp from Scratch to real game creation -- the way Scratch itself became infrastructure for teaching kids to code -- there's a version of this story that looks like a new educational primitive. The Scratch-to-CodeWisp pipeline could compound the way protocol layers do, where every kid who learns on the platform creates content that attracts the next cohort. Fu's eleven years in the Scratch ecosystem gives him credibility that a random AI startup founder couldn't fabricate. If this were structured as investing in Elvin Fu the creator-educator -- more like our Creator Fund model, where you're buying equity in the person's entire future output across education, content, and tools -- I'd be significantly more interested than I am in the company as currently constituted.

But that's not what this is. This is an application-layer AI company built on someone else's models, competing against better-funded startups in a consensus category, with no visible revenue and no pricing model. The community network effects are embryonic -- top game has 1,355 views, which suggests the flywheel isn't spinning yet. The narrative ceiling of "anyone can make games" is big, but the narrative belongs to the entire category, not to CodeWisp specifically. When the narrative belongs to the category rather than to your company, you're in a footrace, and footraces favor the better-capitalized runner. Rosebud, Replit, and eventually the incumbents (Roblox, Epic) will all pursue this same vision with more resources. Fu's audience is a head start, not a structural advantage -- audiences can be built, and the bigger platforms have their own creator ecosystems already.

I keep coming back to Birchbox when I look at this. A credible founder with genuine domain expertise, an early community that feels like a network effect, and a product category that's about to get very crowded. The subscription box model was trivially replicable; so is LLM-powered game generation. Birchbox's data never compounded into lock-in; CodeWisp's 1,513 games won't either if the switching cost to a better-funded competitor's platform is zero. I respect what Fu has built in the game education space, but I'm not putting my own money into a company whose entire moat thesis depends on AI capabilities that every competitor will have access to within months.
