# Booko -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Booko is the competitive structure around Mindbody, and it tells me something important about how this idea was likely conceived. Mindbody launched its own dynamic pricing feature and chose to monetize it at 20% plus 3.5% per transaction -- a fee so punitive that it practically begs someone to build a cheaper alternative. That fee structure is like a neon sign advertising an opportunity. And that's precisely my concern. This looks like an idea that was *found* by studying market inefficiencies rather than *discovered* through lived frustration. When the Collisons built Stripe, they didn't start by analyzing PayPal's fee schedule. They started by hating the experience of integrating payments into their own projects. I see no equivalent origin story for Booko. Neither founder's background connects visibly to fitness studios, medspas, or booking systems. One may have studied mechanical engineering at Columbia. There is no public trace of either founder having operated, worked at, or been a regular customer of the kinds of businesses they're building for.

The underlying insight, though, is real. An empty 5pm yoga class is gone forever -- perishable inventory with zero salvage value. Airlines figured out revenue management in the 1980s. Hotels followed. But millions of small service businesses still set their prices once and leave money on the table every single day. That's genuinely a problem hiding in plain sight, and the reason nobody has solved it for yoga studios is partly schlep blindness: the work of integrating with fragmented booking platforms, training per-client ML models, getting SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance, and selling to merchants who think in haircuts rather than yield curves. That schlep is real, even if it's not Stripe-level regulatory pain. The question is whether these founders stumbled into this problem because they couldn't stop thinking about it, or whether they identified it through the startup idea machine. Everything in the dossier points toward the latter.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If Booko's claimed numbers are real -- 20% revenue uplift, fill rates jumping from 67% to 89%, results in two weeks -- that's the kind of value proposition where merchants do your marketing for you. A salon owner who's making an extra $4,000 a week tells every salon owner she knows. That kind of economic impact can create organic pull even when the founders' original motivation was analytical rather than organic. And the fact that two people built per-client ML models, achieved compliance certifications, and integrated with multiple booking platforms shows technical execution that's nontrivial. SOC 2 with two founders is not easy. Someone on this team can build. The structural position is also interesting: Mindbody validated the concept but priced itself into a corner, creating exactly the kind of opening where a lean startup can undercut an incumbent's business model. If Booko can establish itself as the pricing layer before Mindbody fixes its fee structure, the switching costs from calibrated ML models could create real stickiness.

But I keep returning to the founder question, and at pre-seed the founder question is almost everything. I have no background on either person. No GitHub repos. No confirmed LinkedIn profiles. No prior startup experience. No domain expertise in dynamic pricing, ML, wellness, or booking systems that I can verify. YC accepted them, which is a signal -- we don't accept teams we don't believe in. But when I'm writing my own check, I need to be able to picture these specific people grinding through the next five years of merchant sales calls, booking platform API changes, and competitive pressure from Mindbody's $500M revenue machine. I can't picture what I can't see.

The market risk compounds the founder uncertainty. Booko depends on Mindbody's API for distribution, and Mindbody has every incentive to restrict third-party access to its pricing layer. This is the classic platform dependency problem -- building your house on someone else's land. Booko integrates with Mariana Tek too, which helps, but the wellness booking market is dominated by a handful of platforms, and any of them could decide to build or acquire this capability. The per-client ML model creates moderate switching costs once embedded, but it's not the kind of deep moat that protects you when a $1.9B incumbent decides you're eating its lunch.

This is a pass for me. The problem is real and somewhat hidden in plain sight, which I give them credit for. The execution on compliance and integrations with two people is impressive. But I can't invest my own money in founders I know nothing about, solving a problem they have no visible personal connection to, while sitting atop a platform that could cut off their oxygen at any time. The idea feels manufactured -- competent, logical, defensible in a pitch, but lacking the organic fire that makes founders push through the years of painful execution ahead.
