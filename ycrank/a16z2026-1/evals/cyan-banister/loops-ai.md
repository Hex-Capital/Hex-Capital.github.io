# Loops AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Hakan Baş co-founded Peak Games and sold it to Zynga for $1.8 billion. That's a real exit, a rare credential, and it tells me this team has at least one person who has been through the fire of building, scaling, and exiting a consumer technology company. When I see a repeat founder with that track record attached to a pre-seed company, my instincts lean forward. But then I look at the structure: four co-founders, Hakan listed as "Co-Founder" without a CEO or CTO title, the CEO is Ari Nazir whose path runs through HSBC, the White House, and crypto funds, and the founding story is that Ari and İlker met in Bali and shared a frustration with e-commerce customer support. I've been burned by this exact configuration. HQ Trivia was the right product at the right time, and it was destroyed by co-founder dynamics when there wasn't a clear hand on the wheel. Four co-founders with unclear power delineation — where the strongest operator isn't visibly steering — sets off the alarm I installed after watching that company die.

When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," what comes back from Loops AI is a career pivot, not a biographical crucible. Ari's background is finance and crypto. İlker's is marketing and metaverse consulting. Yusuf's technical profile is thin in public sources. Hakan has the relevant e-commerce DNA through Lidyana.com, his fashion marketplace, but his role in Loops AI's day-to-day isn't clear from anything I can find. The founding story — frustration while living in Bali — is an observation, not a forge. Compare this to the founders I've backed who were broken open by the problem they're solving: people who lived inside the dysfunction, who couldn't walk away from it. The biographical connection between this team and e-commerce AI feels assembled rather than inevitable.

The space itself is where Loops AI loses me most decisively. AI-powered commerce is the definition of consensus in 2026. OpenAI launched agentic checkout in ChatGPT. Google launched agentic commerce in Search and Gemini. Gartner forecasts 75% of online shopping interactions will involve AI-driven conversation by next year. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley — VCs wouldn't touch it. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made the room squirm. Loops AI makes nobody uncomfortable. Every major venture firm is chasing AI commerce. That's not where I find my best bets. The $500K ARR across 50+ brands in Turkey, MENA, and Europe is genuine traction — $10K average ACV with named customers like P&G Fairy, Radisson Collection, and Mövenpick shows real enterprise adoption — but traction in a consensus category against Gorgias ($114M raised, 12,000+ merchants, actively shipping AI Agent 2.0) and platform-native moves from OpenAI and Google doesn't give me the asymmetric upside I need.

I keep looking for the invisible system — the opaque, extractive structure that Loops AI makes transparent — and I can't find it. E-commerce brands know their return rates are 19.3%. They know customer support is slow. They know product discovery is broken. This pain is visible and well-understood, which is exactly why Gorgias, Rep AI, Tidio, and Phia all exist to address it. When Affirm attacked credit cards, the opacity *was* the product — consumers genuinely couldn't understand what they were paying. When Ownwell attacked property taxes, homeowners didn't even know they were overpaying. Loops AI is solving a real operational problem, but it's an efficiency play in a transparent market, not a revelation of hidden extraction. That distinction matters to how I invest.

The strongest case for Loops AI is that Hakan Baş knows how to build and exit companies, the $500K ARR with 2x growth in 45 days shows a product that works, and the Turkey/MENA beachhead could be a wedge before US competitors pay attention to that geography. If the multi-modal stack — semantic search, virtual try-on, image search, sales agents — compounds into a platform that's genuinely hard to replicate, and if the team can navigate the US market transition, this could become a meaningful business. But "could become a meaningful business" isn't the same as "could become a defining company." For this to be a Long Journey bet, I'd need to believe that the AI commerce application layer won't be commoditized by the platforms building the foundation models underneath it — and every signal in the market points the other direction. The core capabilities are technically reproducible, the data advantage across 50+ brands is dwarfed by incumbents, and the defensibility thesis depends on outrunning companies with orders of magnitude more capital.

Two dimensions locked this for me. The consensus nature of AI commerce — a space every investor is chasing, where the largest platform companies are building native solutions — scores against everything I look for in contrarian conviction. And the founding story, where the strongest founder credential (Hakan's $1.8B exit) sits in an undefined role while the CEO's biographical connection to the problem is a Bali conversation, doesn't pass my forge test. Loops AI is a competent team with real traction in a market that doesn't need me to be the first check.
