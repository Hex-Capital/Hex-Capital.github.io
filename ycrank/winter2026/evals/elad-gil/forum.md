# Forum -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Forum's plan requires at least three sequential miracles, and that's the core problem. To succeed, they need to: (1) convince the CFTC to approve a novel contract category -- attention indices -- that has no regulatory precedent, (2) build index methodology robust enough that it can't be gamed by bot traffic or coordinated social manipulation, and (3) bootstrap liquidity on a two-sided trading platform from zero while operating only with virtual currency until miracle #1 is achieved. Each of these alone is a significant challenge. Together, they compound into exactly the kind of multi-miracle plan I consistently pass on. "If you have more than one miracle, you have compounding small odds" -- and Forum has at least three.

The market positioning concerns me more than the concept itself. Prediction markets are the consensus hottest category in fintech right now -- Kalshi at $11 billion, Polymarket at $11.6 billion implied valuation, Opinion raising $20 million, Noise raising $7.1 million from Paradigm. This is the opposite of a non-obvious market. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto exchange infrastructure was something most investors found too unfamiliar and too speculative. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed boring and niche. Forum is entering a category that every fintech investor already has a thesis on and multiple funded bets in. The "attention" sub-niche adds novelty, but "novel sub-niche of a consensus hot market" is structurally different from "non-obvious market at an inflection point." The prediction market TAM slides write themselves -- that's precisely the kind of obvious sizing that should make you pause, not lean in.

The regulatory positioning creates a paradox I've seen before. Forum claims "first regulated exchange" as their primary differentiator, but regulation is simultaneously their biggest bottleneck. Compare this to Coinbase: Coinbase pursued compliance in an existing market (cryptocurrency trading) where regulatory frameworks were emerging. Forum needs the CFTC to decide that attention indices constitute a legitimate contract category -- that's not navigating existing regulation, it's convincing regulators to create new regulation for an unproven asset class. Meanwhile, Noise sidesteps the entire regulatory bottleneck by going on-chain with $7.1 million from Paradigm and already has 1,300+ beta users on testnet. The crypto-native competitor can reach market faster precisely because they avoid the obstacle Forum has built its entire thesis around overcoming. I normally appreciate regulatory complexity as a moat -- Color Health, Anduril, Coinbase all benefited from it -- but only when the regulation already exists and the company navigates it better than others. Forum needs regulation that doesn't yet exist to validate a category that hasn't been proven.

The strongest bull case: attention is genuinely becoming quantifiable infrastructure. The IAB and MRC released standardized Attention Measurement Guidelines in November 2025. Brands spend hundreds of billions annually on advertising based on attention proxies. If you squint, there's a world where attention indices become the foundational data layer for a massive hedging market -- studios hedging franchise relevance, brands hedging cultural positioning, labels hedging artist trajectories. Owen Botkin's Balyasny background gives him real exchange design intuition that most prediction market founders lack. And the behavioral shift toward trading everything (meme stocks, crypto, sports betting) among younger demographics creates genuine demand-side tailwinds. If Forum gets CFTC approval first, the compliance moat would be formidable. This is a bet where the upside scenario is genuinely large -- the question is whether the path there requires too many things to go right simultaneously.

On the product-to-distribution dimension, I see a narrow path. If Forum's attention indices gain credibility, the indices themselves -- not the exchange -- could become the distribution channel. Licensing attention data to ad buyers, studios, and brands would create an enterprise revenue stream independent of trading volume. That's a legitimate product-to-distribution trajectory: wedge with a trading platform, expand into data infrastructure. But the dossier gives no evidence the founders have articulated this multi-product vision. The inferred business model is transaction fees -- single-product thinking for what could be a platform play.

Botkin's Balyasny experience is the strongest founder signal. Trading long/short equities at a multi-manager hedge fund means he understands market microstructure, risk management, and exchange mechanics at a professional level. Thomas's NASA and GEICO engineering background is solid if unspectacular. They've known each other since high school debate, which reduces co-founder conflict risk. They've shipped a live demo app, which shows some execution velocity. But a two-person team pursuing simultaneous regulatory approval, data pipeline construction, and exchange platform development is stretched extremely thin for the scope of what they're attempting.
