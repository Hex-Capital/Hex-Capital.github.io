# Cajal -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cajal is the competitive asymmetry. Harmonic AI raised $295 million at a $1.45 billion valuation in November 2025, pursuing a directly adjacent vision -- AI-driven mathematical reasoning with formal verification in Lean 4. Cajal's differentiation is applied science domains (quantum computing, finance) versus Harmonic's pure math focus. That's a real distinction today, but it's a product positioning choice, not a structural moat. If Harmonic decides applied domains are interesting -- which they will, because that's where the revenue lives -- Cajal is a two-person team defending against a company with roughly 500x their capital. I've seen this pattern enough times to know that domain focus can work against larger competitors (Certora carved out smart contracts against much bigger firms), but only when the domain creates genuine switching costs or data advantages that compound faster than the incumbent can replicate. I don't see that compounding mechanism clearly here.

The structural moment is real but shared. Lean 4 reaching production maturity, LLM mathematical reasoning crossing capability thresholds, foundation model labs investing heavily in reasoning -- these are genuine inflection points. Two years ago, autonomous proof discovery at any useful scale was impractical. Today it's becoming possible. That's the pattern I look for. But unlike Harvey, where the structural moment was building ahead of a capability curve into a $900 billion legal market with millions of potential enterprise seats, Cajal is building into a market where the number of organizations that need formally verified proofs in quantum computing and quantitative finance is probably in the low hundreds globally. The cited TAM of $103 billion for "software verification services" is a misdirection -- Cajal's actual addressable market is a fraction of a fraction of that number. When I invested in PagerDuty, it seemed niche but actually every company running software needed it. Here, I can't see the mechanism by which formal verification of applied mathematics expands from "quantum computing researchers" to a market that supports venture-scale returns.

The most interesting part of the business is actually the ancillary products -- verified training datasets for Lean 4, Coq, and Isabelle, plus RL environments with sub-millisecond proof assistant bindings. If frontier AI labs are racing to improve mathematical reasoning (and they are -- this is a key capability frontier for GPT successors), they need exactly this: high-quality verified training data and training environments. This could be Cajal's real wedge -- not "autonomous proof discovery" but "data infrastructure for AI reasoning." That's a much larger market, and it has the product-to-distribution characteristic I look for: a dataset relationship with an AI lab becomes the channel for benchmarks, then RL environments, then custom verification services. But I don't see evidence the founders have recognized this as their primary opportunity. The positioning is "scaling formal verification to accelerate scientific discovery," which is the academic framing, not the commercial one.

The bull case I have to take seriously: this team could be building the Scale AI of mathematical reasoning data. If reasoning capability becomes the primary differentiator for next-generation AI systems -- which many signals suggest it will -- then verified mathematical training data becomes infrastructure-grade. The Cambridge affiliation provides access to the right academic talent. The compounding data moat (each verified proof adds to the corpus) is real. And the market could be much larger than it appears if you measure it by "what frontier AI labs will pay for reasoning training data" rather than "how many companies need formal verification." If that's the actual trajectory, this is the kind of non-obvious market I typically get excited about -- one that looks like a niche academic tool but is actually positioned at the infrastructure layer of the next AI capability wave. For this to work, the founders would need to recognize this pivot opportunity, execute the data business faster than LeanDojo and similar open-source projects can commoditize it, and do so before Harmonic captures the same relationships with frontier labs. That's not impossible, but it requires strategic clarity I don't see evidence of yet.

The founder domain gap concerns me. Neither Luke Johnston (computational neuroscience, meta-RL) nor Pedro Nobre (CS, hackathons, AI applications) has documented expertise in formal verification, dependent type theory, or theorem proving. Building a multi-agent proof discovery system in Lean requires deep expertise in exactly these areas. The Cambridge affiliation might provide access to that knowledge, but at a two-person company, the founders ARE the company. This isn't a case where you can hire around the gap at this stage. When I invested in Color Health, I had spent years in cancer genomics research -- the founder-market fit was visceral, not adjacent. Here, the founders' backgrounds are adjacent (ML, neuroscience, CS) rather than direct. That's not disqualifying, but it means the single miracle isn't just "can we build a system that discovers proofs" -- it's "can we learn enough formal verification to build the system AND find customers AND compete with Harmonic." That's at least two miracles, possibly three.

I'm passing. The timing is right, the market is genuinely non-obvious, and there's a latent infrastructure play in the data products that could be significant. But the resource asymmetry with Harmonic, the narrow initial market without a clear expansion mechanism, and the founder domain gap add up to a bet that requires too many things to go right simultaneously. If the founders pivot to explicitly targeting "reasoning data infrastructure for AI labs" as their primary business, demonstrate formal verification expertise through shipped work, and show early traction with even one frontier lab, I'd want to look again.
