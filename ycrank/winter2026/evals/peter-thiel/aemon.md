# Aemon -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most important fact about Aemon is that Google DeepMind already built the thing they are building. AlphaEvolve demonstrated in May 2025 that LLM-powered evolutionary coding agents can solve hard optimization problems and make genuine mathematical discoveries. Within months, an open-source implementation appeared on GitHub. Now Aemon proposes to sell this capability to enterprise R&D teams. The question I always ask first -- "What is the secret?" -- has a troubling answer here: there is no secret. The core insight that evolutionary approaches combined with large language models can explore solution spaces better than humans is now published, demonstrated, and replicated. It is consensus dressed in startup clothing. When DeepMind publishes a paper, and within a year open-source projects and multiple well-funded startups converge on the same approach, the mimetic cascade is already underway. Everyone can see this opportunity. That is precisely why it is not an opportunity in the way I define the term.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the founders have real substance. Twin brothers, both publishing at ICLR and EMNLP before age 20, with international math and robotics medals -- this is the kind of raw intellectual horsepower I take seriously. The circle packing result is the most interesting data point in the entire dossier: beating DeepMind's AlphaEvolve on n=26 with under ten dollars of compute, if true, suggests algorithmic ingenuity rather than brute-force GPU expenditure. There is a version of this story where Aemon becomes what Palantir was to PayPal's anti-fraud algorithms -- taking an internal capability proven at one institution and productizing it for a market that the original institution has no interest in serving. DeepMind is a research lab inside Alphabet; selling enterprise optimization tools to R&D teams at manufacturing companies or financial firms is genuinely peripheral to their mission. This structural gap between DeepMind's capability and DeepMind's commercial incentives is a real opening, and a team smart enough to beat their benchmark on a shoestring budget might be the right team to exploit it.

But the Palantir analogy ultimately fails under scrutiny. When we co-founded Palantir in 2003, the idea that commercial software could transform intelligence analysis was genuinely contrarian -- the intelligence community relied on manual processes, and no one in Silicon Valley was building for three-letter agencies. The secret was hidden in plain sight but actively rejected by both communities. Aemon has no analogous secret. The idea that AI can automate R&D optimization is the defining consensus thesis of 2025-2026. Lila Sciences raised $550 million. Periodic Labs raised $300 million. Sakana AI is valued at $2.6 billion. Cognition Labs has $900 million and $155 million in ARR. When five companies with a combined several billion dollars in funding are building adjacent tools, the market structure is competitive, not monopolistic. The founders are pointing at a $3.1 trillion TAM on day one rather than identifying a specific, concentrated beachhead they can completely own -- a classic sign of competitive thinking masquerading as ambition.

The technology question is whether this is zero-to-one or one-to-n. AlphaEvolve was the zero-to-one moment. Aemon is attempting to take that insight and build a product around it -- that is one-to-n work. Valuable, perhaps, but not what generates power-law returns. The compute efficiency claim is intriguing and might represent genuine algorithmic innovation, but the legal retrieval case study -- improving Recall@10 from 0.540 to 0.912 -- is the kind of incremental optimization metric that describes a useful tool, not a category-defining product. Where is the 10x discontinuity that makes the old way of doing things structurally obsolete? A research engineer who can solve a circle packing problem is impressive; an enterprise product that transforms how R&D is conducted across industries requires a fundamentally different kind of insight about workflows, integration, and organizational behavior that I see no evidence of here.

The durability picture concerns me most. No network effects. No switching costs. No economies of scale yet. The potential data flywheel -- accumulating domain-specific solution patterns across customer engagements -- is explicitly described as "unproven." The open-source OpenEvolve project already replicates the core approach. As LLM capabilities continue to improve, the barrier to building evolutionary code generation systems drops further. In two years, any competent team with API access to frontier models could build a comparable system. The endgame here is not defensible, and I always study the endgame before everything else. A company whose primary moat is "we productized it first" in a space where the underlying technology is open and replicable is not a last mover -- it is a first mover waiting to be overtaken.

I respect these founders. Publishing at top venues before twenty, dropping out to build, beating DeepMind on their own benchmark -- these are signals of genuine capability. But capability in service of a consensus thesis in a competitive market does not create monopoly returns. The right outcome for these founders might be extraordinary, but the structure of this market -- multiple well-funded players, open-source alternatives, no visible path to monopoly -- makes this a pass.
