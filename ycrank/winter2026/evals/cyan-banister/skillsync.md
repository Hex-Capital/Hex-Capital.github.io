# Skillsync -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that catches my eye here isn't the product or the team -- it's the pivot. Six months ago, Skillsync launched on Product Hunt as a tool to "discover hidden talent in your codebase" -- an internal team skill-mapping play. Now it's "find anyone in open source," an external recruiting platform. That kind of rapid repositioning can signal either dangerous indecision or healthy iteration, but at this stage the honest read is that these founders haven't yet found the version of this problem that locks them in. And when I look at where they've landed, I see Prog.AI already sitting there with 60M+ developer profiles, a million in pre-seed capital, and a founder who already sold a company to Dun & Bradstreet. That's the competitive reality. AI-powered developer sourcing is not a lonely frontier -- it's a category with half a dozen funded players, which immediately tells me this is consensus territory. Nobody's uncomfortable funding recruiting tools. Nobody's calling this crazy. And in my experience, the deals where everybody nods along are exactly the ones where the returns disappoint.

I want to know these founders' stories -- not the business, but how they got here. What I can see from the dossier is two technically sharp engineers from Juspay who helped grow the Hyperswitch open-source project from zero to 20,000+ GitHub stars. That's genuinely impressive execution, and it gives them an authentic window into how open-source talent operates. Nishant's Rust chops are real -- 76% of his code in Rust, which takes serious discipline. Aaditya brings the management and HR psychology lens from XLRI. These are capable, credentialed people. But where's the survival story? Where's the hardship that makes this problem personal and non-negotiable? Growing an open-source project and noticing that recruiters can't find good developers is an observation, not a calling. I've backed founders who were solving problems that had shaped their entire lives -- Travis couldn't let go of the taxi medallion racket, the Crusoe team couldn't stop thinking about wasted gas flares. When I look at Skillsync, I see smart builders who spotted a market gap, and that's a perfectly fine reason to start a company, but it doesn't give me the signal I need to write a first check.

There's a thread here that does tug at something in my pattern -- the idea of making invisible people visible. Flock Safety made neighborhood safety data visible. Carta made equity ownership visible. Skillsync wants to make the quiet, brilliant engineer who contributes beautiful code to open-source repos visible to the companies that need them. That resonates with how I think about the world. But the economic access question is inverted. The primary beneficiary is the company doing the hiring, not the developer. The developer didn't ask to be profiled. They didn't sign up. In fact, many skilled engineers deliberately avoid LinkedIn and traditional recruiting precisely because they don't want to be sourced -- and now we're building tools to find them anyway. Unlike Uber, where the driver actively chose to create income, or Postmates, where the delivery worker signed up for economic opportunity, Skillsync extracts value from developers' public contributions without their participation. That's a meaningful philosophical distinction for me.

The bull case here requires you to believe that the LLM-powered code analysis creates a knowledge graph so deep and so accurate that it becomes the definitive map of who knows what in open source. If Skillsync can build that graph faster and better than Prog.AI, and if GitHub doesn't build it themselves (Microsoft owns both GitHub and LinkedIn -- connecting those two databases is an obvious product move), then maybe this team's authentic open-source experience gives them the edge. The "Cursor-like" natural language search interface is a genuinely better UX vision than traditional filter-based recruiting tools. And "hundreds of talent teams" at $499/month, if real, would be meaningful pre-seed revenue. But those are a lot of ifs, and none of them require the kind of extraordinary conviction I need to overcome the structural risks -- platform dependency on a Microsoft-owned property, a direct competitor with a head start, and a limited addressable pool restricted to engineers with public GitHub activity.

I respect what these founders built at Juspay, and the co-founder dynamic -- two people who shipped real open-source infrastructure together -- is clean and credible. But this is a well-understood problem space with well-funded incumbents, no regulatory moat, no weird factor, and no biographical thread that tells me these specific founders will persist through the inevitable crisis when GitHub changes their API terms or when LinkedIn Recruiter ships a "code analysis" tab. I'd want to meet them and hear their stories directly before fully closing the door, but based on what's in front of me, this doesn't pattern-match to where I write checks.
