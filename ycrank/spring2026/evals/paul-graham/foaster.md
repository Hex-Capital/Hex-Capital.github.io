# Foaster -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The single most important fact about Foaster is one I can't verify. Alexandre Combes claims his prior venture Repondia handled 60,000+ AI customer calls for restaurant groups in France. If true, this is extraordinary — it means he's already built and scaled the exact core mechanism Foaster depends on: AI agents conducting real conversations with real people at real scale. That's not a prototype, that's operational proof. But Repondia has no web presence, no press, no Crunchbase profile, nothing. The claim just floats there, unsourced. And this matters enormously, because at pre-seed, "the earlier you pick startups, the more you're picking the founders." If the 60K number is real, Combes is a founder who's already done the hard thing. If it's embellished, that's a different conversation entirely. I'd need to verify this in a meeting before it could carry weight.

What I find more interesting about these founders is the pattern underneath the credentials. Dabadie built a 245K-follower Instagram account around Rafael Nadal at age eleven. At eighteen, he was recruited as an agent for Nadal's Academy. That's not résumé optimization — that's a kid who saw something he was obsessed with and figured out how to turn it into influence and then a real role. It's the same instinct that made the Airbnb founders sell $40 cereal boxes. It tells you someone is resourceful in the bone-deep way that matters. The Werewolf Benchmark paper — probing LLM social intelligence through a game that requires deception, persuasion, and reading others — got shared by OpenAI's president and drew contact from xAI's co-founder. That's a genuine signal that these two understand how AI agents behave in socially complex situations, which is precisely what you need if your product is AI agents interviewing employees about sensitive organizational dynamics. The co-founder relationship looks solid: same school, co-authored research together, complementary skills. Startups do to the relationship between founders what a dog does to a sock, and these two have already stress-tested theirs.

The schlep angle here is real and worth naming. Management consulting is one of those problems that sat unsolved because "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." It's intimidating — you're selling to C-suite executives, navigating enterprise procurement, handling sensitive organizational data, competing against McKinsey's brand. Most hackers would rather build another developer tool. The fact that two technical founders from ENSAE Paris are willing to wade into this schlep is structurally interesting for the same reason Stripe was interesting: payments infrastructure was a painful, boring problem that hackers avoided, and that avoidance was the moat. But — and this is a critical distinction — Stripe's schlep was primarily technical. Foaster's schlep is primarily relational. Enterprise consulting is a trust business. Two twenty-three-year-olds who haven't finished their degrees face a credibility gap that no amount of technical sophistication can close quickly. The human-consultant-in-the-loop model is smart precisely because it acknowledges this, but it also limits how fast they can scale without hiring experienced consultants.

The market is genuinely large and the timing argument holds: LLMs just crossed the threshold where AI agents can conduct nuanced, open-ended interviews rather than just structured Q&A. The innovator's dilemma for incumbents is textbook — McKinsey can't offer 3-5x cheaper consulting without cannibalizing its own leverage model. But here's my concern: this idea doesn't look bad. It looks obvious. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." AI-powered consulting looks like a good idea to everyone right now, which is why Distyl AI has raised $202M, Aily Labs has $101M, and PromptQL has $136M+. When an idea attracts that much capital that quickly, a two-person pre-seed team needs something extraordinary to survive — either a dramatically different wedge or traction so strong it speaks for itself. Foaster has neither in the public record. The per-client operational map creates some switching costs, but the core technology — LLM-based interviewing — is available to anyone with an API key. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors," yes, but that aphorism applies when you have product-market fit and users who love you. Without any verifiable traction, the competitive convergence risk is more than theoretical.

The bull case for Foaster is this: if Combes really did build a system that handled 60K AI calls, and if the Werewolf research translates into genuinely superior interview methodology, then Foaster has a shot at being the Stripe of consulting — the team that solved the schlep everyone else avoided. The data moat from accumulated operational maps could compound over time, creating the kind of switching costs that make enterprise software sticky. If they land even two or three mid-market engagements through the YC network and deliver results that make clients say "I could never go back to traditional consulting," that's the beginning of exponential word-of-mouth growth. The "why now" is real, the market is enormous, and these founders have genuine intellectual depth in AI agent behavior. But I need to see evidence that they can actually sell to enterprises, that the product works in the wild, and that Repondia was real. Right now I'm evaluating a promising hypothesis, not a demonstrated capability.
