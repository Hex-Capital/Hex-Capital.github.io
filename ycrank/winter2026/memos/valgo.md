## The Idea

**Problem:** Insurers cannot price coverage for autonomous systems (autonomous trucks, robots, self-driving vehicles) because the historical claims data that underpins traditional actuarial models does not exist. Conventional auto insurance draws from over 30 billion claims records (YC company page), but autonomous systems have nearly zero. Without a credible loss estimate, insurers either refuse coverage or price it speculatively, creating a bottleneck for commercial deployment of autonomous systems. The customer segments are both (a) autonomy companies needing insurance to operate commercially and (b) insurers needing risk quantification tools to underwrite these new categories.

**Approach:** Valgo builds probabilistic models of routes, tasks, and environments from the bottom up, then ingests aggregate simulation statistics from autonomy companies via API. The platform transforms simulation data—already being generated by autonomy developers for testing and validation purposes—into simulated loss estimates that insurers can use to price coverage (YC company page, valgo.dev). The mechanism is essentially: autonomy company plugs in simulation data → Valgo's probabilistic risk model processes it → output is an actuarially usable loss estimate for the insurer. The company's earlier-stage positioning, per LinkedIn and ZoomInfo, also describes "algorithmic safety validation tools for autonomy" that efficiently find rare and realistic failure events in simulation at reduced compute cost.

**Differentiation:** Koop Technologies, the closest direct competitor, also bridges AV data and insurance underwriting but operates as an insurance MGA (Managing General Agent), writing policies directly via Lloyd's of London and its own Singularity Platform (Koop blog, 2025). Valgo positions as a risk quantification infrastructure layer—a data/model provider to insurers rather than an insurance carrier itself. Avinew focuses on usage-based insurance with telematics discounts for ADAS engagement (VentureBeat, 2019) rather than bottom-up probabilistic modeling. Hyperexponential provides general insurance pricing software ($91M raised, processes $45B in premiums annually) but is not specific to autonomous systems risk (hyperexponential.com). Valgo's differentiation rests on its academic foundation in safety validation algorithms and its simulation-to-loss-estimate pipeline specifically designed for the no-historical-data problem.

## Defensibility

Valgo's founders authored the textbook on the subject—"Algorithms for Validation" (Kochenderfer, Katz, Corso, Moss; MIT Press, forthcoming)—and teach the corresponding Stanford course (AA228V: Validation of Safety-Critical Systems) (algorithmsbook.com/validation). Robert Moss spent 7 years at MIT Lincoln Laboratory on the team that designed the FAA-certified ACAS Xa collision avoidance system (robert-moss.com). This combination of published academic authority and real-world certification experience in safety-critical systems represents a technical complexity moat—few teams globally have equivalent depth in both the algorithmic validation and insurance application domains.

If Valgo accumulates proprietary simulation-to-loss datasets across multiple autonomy companies, a data advantage could develop: each new customer's data improves the platform's risk models, making estimates more accurate and creating switching costs for both insurers (who rely on model continuity) and autonomy companies (who are integrated via API).

**Market structure:** Traditional insurers face a structural data gap—their actuarial models are built on historical claims, which do not exist for autonomous systems. Building bottom-up probabilistic simulation models requires AI safety and validation expertise that is outside the core competency of insurance underwriting teams. For autonomy companies (e.g., Waymo, Aurora), building insurance-grade risk quantification is not a core business function. [Inferred]: The structural barrier is that neither side (insurer nor autonomy company) has the incentive or expertise to build this in-house, creating space for a specialized intermediary.

**Commoditization risk:** Teams with combined depth in probabilistic safety validation, simulation-based testing, and insurance actuarial science are rare. However, large reinsurers (Swiss Re, Munich Re) have innovation labs and could develop internal capabilities. Koop Technologies already operates in a similar space with $7M in funding and a Lloyd's partnership (Koop press releases, 2021–2025). Academic labs (including the founders' own Stanford SISL lab) could produce open-source tooling that competes on the validation side.

## Key Risks

**Dual-sided market adoption risk:** Valgo must simultaneously convince autonomy companies to share proprietary simulation data via API *and* convince insurers to trust simulation-derived loss estimates over traditional actuarial methods. Neither side has established norms for this workflow. If autonomy companies resist sharing data (competitive sensitivity), or if insurers remain skeptical of simulation-based risk models (regulatory conservatism), the platform faces a chicken-and-egg problem.

**Regulatory uncertainty for simulation-based underwriting:** Insurance regulation varies by state and country, and regulators may not yet accept simulation-derived loss estimates as a basis for rate filing. If state insurance commissioners require traditional actuarial backing, Valgo's output may not be directly usable for policy pricing without additional validation layers.

**Koop Technologies incumbency:** Koop Technologies has been operating in the AV insurance space since at least 2021 with $7M in funding, a Lloyd's of London partnership, and an established data-sharing framework (ISF) with autonomy companies (Koop press releases, 2021–2025). As a vertically integrated MGA, Koop controls the end-to-end workflow from data ingestion to policy issuance, which could limit Valgo's addressable market if autonomy companies prefer a single provider.

**Narrow initial market window:** The addressable market depends on the pace of commercial autonomous vehicle and robotics deployment. Delays in AV commercialization (regulatory setbacks, technical challenges, public safety incidents) could extend the timeline before insurers face urgent demand for autonomous-specific underwriting tools, limiting near-term revenue potential.

**Brand disambiguation:** Multiple unrelated entities share the "Valgo" name, including Valgo SA (a French industrial decontamination company), Valgo Mayoreo (Mexico), and Valgo Machinery (India). This creates potential confusion in search results and brand recognition.
