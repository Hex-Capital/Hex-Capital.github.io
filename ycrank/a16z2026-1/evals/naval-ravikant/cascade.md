# Cascade -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Cascade went from zero to $183k cARR in three months and signed 15 AEC firms including builders of JFK Airport and Four Seasons hotels. That velocity is real. But I keep returning to the question I ask at the start of every evaluation: if Hannia Zia and Joana Ferreira disappeared tomorrow, would Cascade's core insight survive? The answer is yes — because the insight isn't theirs. Any competent team scanning government RFP portals and wrapping LLMs around proposal generation could arrive at this product. Hannia's specific knowledge is payments at scale (Google Pay, 0 to 100M users). Joana's is ML infrastructure (Google Search, Amazon). Both are formidable execution credentials, but they're transferable skills applied to a market identified through analysis, not years of accumulated domain obsession. "Grew up in a family of carpenters" is a biographical detail, not specific knowledge about how AEC firms navigate procurement. The person on this team with actual AEC-specific knowledge — the Head of Growth who was ranked #2 at Procore and drove $3M ARR — is a hire, not a founder. When your deepest domain expertise lives in an employee rather than a principal, you have a fragile knowledge architecture.

The leverage audit exposes a structural tension. Cascade's AI components — project discovery, proposal drafting, spec analysis — have genuine zero-marginal-cost characteristics per query. But the business wrapping those components is enterprise vertical SaaS requiring direct sales, ERP integrations with Deltek Vantagepoint, Ajera, Costpoint, and OpenAsset, plus compliance work like Davis-Bacon certified payroll reports. Each new AEC client needs onboarding, integration engineering, and relationship management. This is not Notion scaling to millions through word-of-mouth templates. It is Procore's model with an AI layer — valuable, yes, but structurally bound to proportional headcount growth. The proposal-writing feature reduces client effort from four weeks to days, which is genuine leverage for the customer. But Cascade itself requires labor leverage to deliver that code leverage to each new firm. That asymmetry caps the company's team-to-value ratio in ways that my highest-conviction investments avoid.

The access democratization angle is where Cascade genuinely activates my framework. Smaller AEC firms that couldn't afford dedicated business development teams to monitor 20+ government portals now get those opportunities surfaced in five minutes. This rhymes with the AngelList pattern — removing a structural gatekeeper between people and something they want. The difference: when I built AngelList, my specific knowledge from years as a founder was the reason the platform worked for its users. I understood what angel investors and startups actually needed because I had lived on both sides of the table. Cascade's founders are building the access layer without having lived inside the AEC procurement process themselves. The democratization pattern is present, but the specific knowledge powering it is thin.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. First-three-clients doubling their spend is a compound interest signal — firms are finding and winning enough incremental projects through Cascade to justify expanding usage. If that pattern holds across the next 50 clients, the data flywheel starts compounding: each firm's proposal history, capability profiles, and win/loss data trains Cascade's matching engine, creating switching costs that horizontal competitors like AutogenAI ($65.3M raised) cannot replicate without equivalent AEC client penetration. The vertical integration — discovery plus proposals plus compliance plus ERP sync — creates bundled switching costs that point solutions (Joist AI, $4M seed; Workorb AI, $2.6M) cannot match individually. And the Procore Head of Growth gives Cascade distribution leverage that most pre-seed companies spend years trying to build. If AEC adoption velocity continues at 11 companies per month, Cascade could achieve enough data density to build a genuine moat before AutogenAI decides to verticalize.

But I've seen this pattern before. Wish had extraordinary early traction in discount e-commerce — a real market, real growth, real users. What it lacked was specific knowledge creating defensibility. The market rewarded execution temporarily, then punished the absence of a non-replicable advantage permanently. Cascade's moat today rests on domain-specific integrations and accumulated client data — real but buildable by any well-funded competitor willing to invest the engineering hours. AutogenAI with $65.3M could add AEC templates, Deltek integrations, and Davis-Bacon compliance in a quarter. Procore, already embedded in every large AEC firm's workflow, could build native RFP tools as a natural product extension. The co-founder relationship is genuine — years together at UnlikelyAI — which avoids my "startup weekend co-founders" red flag. But the domain selection reads as smart market analysis, not principal-level obsession. Hannia's pitch deck likely leads with TAM ($11.78B construction software market) rather than a problem she couldn't stop solving.

Cascade is a well-executed vertical AI play by competent operators who identified an underserved market. The traction is impressive, the access democratization is real, and the co-founder bond is authentic. But my framework requires specific knowledge that is the company, not merely applied to the company — and I don't find it here. The leverage architecture demands sales headcount to scale, the domain expertise sits in a hire rather than a founder, and the competitive moat is integrations rather than insight. This is a company I'd expect to build a solid $50M ARR business. It is not structured for the power-law returns that justify angel bets with my own money. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation: specific knowledge productization, where neither founder possesses non-replicable AEC procurement expertise, and permissionless leverage architecture, where enterprise sales and ERP integration work bind growth to headcount.
