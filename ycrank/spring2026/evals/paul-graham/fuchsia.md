# fuchsia -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Saphira AI graduated YC S24 with the exact positioning "Vanta for hardware," has scaled to a team of 6, and is actively hiring a Founding Account Executive — meaning they're already in GTM mode. Fuchsia entered YC over a year later with functionally the same pitch, the same regulatory targets (UL, CE, ISO), and a listed team size of 1. When I see a company arrive in a space where YC has already funded a direct competitor with a meaningful head start, the question isn't whether the market is real — clearly it is — but whether these founders are so much better that the gap evaporates. And the honest answer is: I don't know, because I have almost nothing to evaluate. No stories of creative problem-solving, no evidence of a shipped prototype before YC, no user love, no growth metrics. The dossier gives me credentials but not character. Credentials tell me Tony Gao and Jack Yin are smart; only stories would tell me they're resourceful.

Tony Gao's robotics background — perception research, humanoid deployments — is the strongest signal fuchsia has, because it means he almost certainly lived through the hardware certification pain himself. Anyone who's tried to get a robot through FCC and UL testing knows the months of consultant fees and cross-referencing standards documents. This is the organic discovery path I trust: you encounter a problem in your own work, and the frustration is so acute that you can't help building a solution. But I'm inferring this narrative because the dossier doesn't actually provide it. There's no specific moment of frustration described, no story of "I spent four months on compliance for our humanoid and realized the whole process was insane." The plausibility is there, but the evidence isn't. Jack Yin's background at D.E. Shaw, Citadel Securities, and Five Rings tells me he can write code and think quantitatively — but quant trading has nothing to do with hardware compliance, and fuchsia needs at least one founder who has the regulatory domain expertise to know when the AI is hallucinating a requirement versus citing a real one.

The schlep blindness case for fuchsia is real on paper. Hardware compliance is genuinely ugly work — dozens of independent standards bodies, thousands of individual codes, different update schedules, different geographies. Most founders' unconscious filters screen out this kind of problem because it's regulatory, boring, and requires reading thousands of pages of dense technical standards. That's exactly the dynamic that created the opening for Stripe in payments. But the parallel breaks down in a critical way: when Patrick Collison tackled payments, no other YC company was already a year ahead of him doing the same thing. The whole point of schlep blindness is that the problem repels everyone — but fuchsia's problem has attracted Saphira AI, Reglyr (launched April 2026), Compliance & Risks (188 employees, ~$29.8M revenue), and UL Solutions' own software platform ($102M quarterly in software and advisory). "Vanta for hardware" is a pitch that makes everyone nod. That's usually the tell that the returns have been competed away.

Yale dropouts in quantitative disciplines — Math & Physics for Gao, CS & Math for Yin — is a founder profile I've historically leaned into. The Bindwell founders were 18 and 19. Patrick Collison was a teenager. I bet on learning velocity over experience, and young technical talent often has the highest slope. But even with Bindwell, I had direct interaction and could observe how they processed advice — I told them to stop selling tools and own the molecules, and they pivoted immediately. With fuchsia, I have no comparable signal. The name change from Noetic to Fuchsia shows some adaptability, but renaming your company to share a name with Google's well-known open-source operating system (fuchsia.dev dominates every search result) raises a different question about judgment. A founder with the obsessive attention to detail I look for would have caught that collision.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Tony Gao's hands-on robotics experience represents genuine organic problem discovery in a schlep domain, Jack Yin brings elite engineering talent from the most selective quantitative firms in the world, they're the young-technical-founder archetype that produced Stripe and Bindwell, the $36B compliance software market is growing at 12.67% CAGR, and UL Solutions' structural conflict — building tools that help customers bypass UL's own consulting — creates a moat against the largest incumbent. The lab-matching feature could generate network effects that pure software competitors can't replicate. A believer would say this is Stripe circa 2010: two young builders tackling regulated infrastructure that everyone else avoids, with the YC network providing initial distribution into hardware startups. I find this case plausible but unconvincing, because the Stripe comparison requires founders of Collison-caliber quality, and I have zero stories, zero shipped artifacts, and zero user evidence to support that assessment. The one condition that would flip me: if I sat down with Tony and Jack for ten minutes and heard them describe, with specificity, creative workarounds they've already deployed — customers they've closed through unscalable effort, regulatory documents they've personally parsed to build their knowledge base, a prototype that's already being used by a hardware team going through certification — I could see this moving to Invest. The problem is real. The question is whether these are the right founders, and right now fuchsia is a blank page where that answer should be.
