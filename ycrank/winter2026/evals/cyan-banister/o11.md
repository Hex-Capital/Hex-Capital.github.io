# o11 -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about o11 is the positioning: "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works." That's their actual Product Hunt tagline. They're building an add-in that lives inside Microsoft's own platform, competing directly against Microsoft's own product, and advertising that fact as a selling point. This is the opposite of finding a pocket of dreamers and weirdos before consensus forms. AI productivity tools are the single most consensus investment category in technology right now. Gamma just hit $100M ARR at a $2.1B valuation. Microsoft is pouring billions into Copilot. Every VC in the valley wants a piece of this market. When I think about the investments that have defined my career — SpaceX when private space was considered delusional, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded insane — they all shared a quality of making other investors uncomfortable. Nothing about "make PowerPoint slides faster with AI" makes anyone uncomfortable. It makes everyone nod their heads. That's the wrong signal for me.

When I think about the founders, I want to understand the biographical thread. How did Aryah and Ajay end up here? Aryah is 19, a Morehead-Cain Scholar — which is genuinely impressive, that's one of the most selective merit awards in the country — and he's started several things already (ERA-0, CitySprout, KlubSpace). Ajay led AI at Cal AI, which hit $40M ARR and 10M users before getting acquired by MyFitnessPal. That's a real credential. But when I listen for the story that connects these founders to *this* specific problem, I hear silence. Neither has worked in banking, consulting, or law. They report that "bankers, lawyers, and consultants told us they were tired of chatbots that couldn't do real work" — so the problem came from market research, not from lived experience. Compare that to how I found Uber: years of conversations with my husband about the taxi medallion racket, then my own driver Roger showing me the inefficiency from the inside. That biographical inseparability between founder and problem is what I look for first, and it's absent here. These are talented builders who identified an opportunity — but talented builders chasing hot markets is the most common pattern in Silicon Valley, and it's not how I invest.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Ajay's Cal AI track record is the strongest signal in this entire dossier — he has proven he can build an AI product that reaches massive scale, and the MyFitnessPal acquisition validates that he can build something worth buying. At 19, both founders have execution velocity that most people don't develop until their thirties. The enterprise logos — Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Mayo Clinic — suggest they can get in the door with serious institutions, even if I suspect those may be individual users on the free tier rather than enterprise contracts. The in-app embedding strategy is genuinely clever: instead of asking people to switch to a new tool (like Gamma does), you meet them inside the app they already use. If Microsoft continues to price Copilot at $30/user/month and leaves vertical gaps for finance and legal workflows, o11 could build real switching costs through template customization and data integration depth. The "unlimited context" claim — processing thousands of documents — could be a meaningful technical differentiator if it's real. If you believe that Microsoft will always be too horizontal to serve finance-specific needs well, and that o11 can lock in enterprise customers before the window closes, this could be a real company.

But here's what I keep coming back to: this is a company building on someone else's platform, competing against that platform's own product, in the single most popular investment category in technology. Microsoft controls the add-in APIs. Microsoft controls AppSource distribution. Microsoft could change policies, restrict access, or simply improve Copilot's finance features — and o11's entire business evaporates. I've seen this movie before with companies built on top of platforms that eventually consumed them. The founders are shipping fast, but they're shipping toward the strongest incumbent in enterprise software. And the problem they're solving — making document workflows faster for knowledge workers at banks and consulting firms — doesn't create economic access for anyone who lacks it. It makes affluent professionals slightly more efficient. That's a real business, but it's not the kind of investment that gets me out of bed. I'm looking for companies that change who gets to participate in the economy, that make invisible systems visible, that operate in spaces others are afraid to touch. o11 is a well-executed product in a crowded market serving people who are already well-served. I wish them well, but this isn't my kind of bet.
