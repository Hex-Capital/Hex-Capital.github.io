# PerfectBit, Inc. -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Péter Vajda spent eleven years running Meta's media generation -- Emu, Movie Gen -- and Seiji Yamamoto spent nine on Core Llama pre- and post-training, which means PerfectBit, Inc. is one of the rare AI training data pitches where the founders actually ate the noisy-web-data dinner every night for a decade. So the dimensional signature for PerfectBit, Inc. is "lived-experience high, physical-verifiability near zero" -- two foundation-model insiders pointing a verifier pipeline at a problem they personally hit at production scale, wrapped in a SHA-256-batched B2B abstraction I will never observe in the wild. That pairing is what I'm pricing.

Anduril is the HIGH rung on lived experience for me -- Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus to a billion-dollar exit *before* turning the same hardware muscle onto defense, which is the template where the founder's prior product is the entry pass to the new problem. PerfectBit, Inc. is genuinely on this ladder: Vajda and Yamamoto shipped Llama and Emu, which means they were the buyer of Scale AI / Surge AI data and watched the ~10% annotation error rate (AOL/TechBuzz 2025) corrupt their own training runs. Where PerfectBit, Inc. diverges from the Luckey pattern is that Luckey ran the Oculus garage on his own conviction, whereas Vajda and Yamamoto are surfacing from inside Meta Superintelligence Labs -- pedigreed insiders, not outsider-trajectory operators. The lived problem is real; the autobiography is corporate, not garage. That's the Hassabis-DeepMind shape, not the Luckey-Anduril shape, and I weight it accordingly.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling I passed on is the LOW rung for physical verifiability -- pure software abstraction I couldn't install, photograph, or hand to a friend -- and the failure mechanism was that I had no sourcing edge: nothing in my own environment would have surfaced the company before it became consensus. PerfectBit, Inc. inherits that exact failure mode. A SHA-256-tracked dataset shipped to a foundation-model lab is not a Niantic Ingress portal I stumble across on a walk or a Flock Safety camera I notice in a Four Seasons parking lot. There is no version of me running into PerfectBit, Inc.'s product in my own life; the pilot program lives behind a "contact us" form. This is the dimension where my whole sourcing method goes dark, and the dossier confirms it -- zero job postings, no Product Hunt, no Discord, no public artifact a normal human can touch.

The "magically weird" dimension breaks against PerfectBit, Inc. on a compounding mechanism: AI training data is, by June 2026, the loudest category in the YC orbit -- Scale AI just took $14B from Meta, Surge AI is reportedly raising up to $1B, Micro1 priced at $500M last September, and Garry Tan is the assigned partner, which is the *opposite* of the "two credible VCs already passed on aesthetic grounds" tell that hit me on Crusoe. Eighteen months from now the round PerfectBit, Inc. raises will be benchmarked off Surge AI's revenue multiple, and that means I'm paying a consensus price for a contrarian *technical* approach inside a consensus *market*. The verifier-grounded angle is genuinely odd; the category is not. The weirdness lives one layer too deep for the price.

On single unambiguous CEO authority -- to flip PerfectBit, Inc. from a default-low score to a tier-up here, the dossier would need to name one of Vajda or Yamamoto as CEO with majority operating control, and ideally a documented tiebreaker protocol. It names neither. Two ex-Meta directors of roughly comparable seniority co-founding a company is the exact shape of the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll setup before the feud, and the GameCrush activist-investor lesson is what I trace it to. The override would be obvious tactile virality the way HQ Trivia overrode it, but PerfectBit, Inc. has no tactile artifact to override anything with. Counterfactual fails.

Mindbloom is the single anchor on capital-intensity / unsexy-regulated -- ketamine therapy is the kind of category Surge or Scale literally cannot pivot into because the regulatory and aesthetic moat compounds against incumbents. PerfectBit, Inc. has no such moat. Verifier pipelines integrating formal-proof systems and physics simulators are technically sophisticated, but Scale AI at ~$870M revenue and Surge at >$1B can hire the same PhDs and fund the same integrations, and Meta's own TBD Labs researchers can build the pipeline internally. The moat is the founders' taste, not the regulatory or capital-intensity geometry of the domain. That's a software bet dressed as infrastructure.

My 36-month forecast for PerfectBit, Inc. is acqui-hire to a foundation-model lab -- most likely Meta Superintelligence Labs, where Vajda and Yamamoto already have a decade of relationships -- in the $80-180M range, with the verifier library absorbed into an internal data team rather than scaled as standalone infrastructure. The mechanism is straightforward: the buyers ARE the competitors, the founders are the asset, and the most rational exit for both sides closes that loop before Series A dilution makes it expensive.

The strongest case against my pass is the DeepMind exception, and it stings. Hassabis, Suleyman, and Legg were also pedigreed insiders, not outsider-trajectory founders, and I wrote that check in 2013 because the polymath cross-domain compounding overrode the bias against credentials. Yamamoto has PNAS and Physical Review Letters publications co-authored with a Fields Medalist *and* shipped Llama pre/post-training, full-duplex speech, and computer vision -- that is exactly the polymath signature. Vajda ran Emu and Movie Gen, a different surface of the same model stack. If "the age of the polymath" is the active thesis, PerfectBit, Inc. has a credible claim on the override. What dismantles it for me is the price: DeepMind in ~2013 was not the most-bid category in venture; PerfectBit, Inc. in Spring 2026 is being placed into the hottest market with the YC president on the cap table, which means I will pay consensus-level pricing for the polymath bet. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a named foundation-model lab customer -- OpenAI, Anthropic, or DeepMind itself -- signing a paid pilot before Demo Day, at which point the buyer-is-competitor risk inverts into validation and I would chase the round.
