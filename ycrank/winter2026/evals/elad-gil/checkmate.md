# Checkmate -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the most interesting thing about this company -- and also its biggest vulnerability. AlphaSense just spent $930 million acquiring Tegus, then launched its own AI Interviewer built on that proprietary transcript library. That's a $4 billion incumbent with $500 million ARR and distribution to 6,500+ enterprises making its move. In most cases, I'd call that a "market-ending move" and pass immediately. But look at the structural constraint: AlphaSense's AI Interviewer only works within its own expert ecosystem. It can't touch GLG calls. It can't touch AlphaSights calls. It can't touch direct expert relationships. Checkmate sits in the gap -- network-agnostic, integrating with calendar invites from any source. That's a genuinely different positioning. Meanwhile, the traditional expert networks (GLG, AlphaSights, Third Bridge) face a classic cannibalization problem: automating the interview undermines the perceived value of their human intermediation, which is their entire revenue model. So you have incumbents who either can't build this (cannibalization) or won't build it agnostically (ecosystem lock-in). That structural gap is real, but it's also narrower than it appears -- InsightAgent is already in the same gap, and AlphaSense could expand beyond Tegus over time.

The market itself fits my "boring but critical" archetype. Expert network calls are the unglamorous plumbing of institutional investment research -- nobody at a dinner party gets excited about channel checks with former executives. But every hedge fund, every PE firm, every consulting firm runs on this workflow. The $3-4 billion expert network market understates the real opportunity, which includes the analyst labor cost currently absorbed by each call: scheduling, attending, note-taking, synthesis, compliance review. When I frame it as "AI replacing the analyst labor layer in primary research," the addressable value expands significantly. The structural moment is genuine -- LLM voice agents crossed a usability threshold for multi-turn adaptive conversation in 2024-2025, making this technically feasible for the first time. Two years ago, the voice quality and contextual follow-up capability simply weren't there for a conversation that hedge fund analysts would trust. That's a specific technology threshold, not a vague "AI is improving" story.

Where I get nervous is the miracle count. Checkmate needs institutional investors to trust an early-stage startup's AI agent with unsupervised expert calls -- in an industry where compliance violations can trigger SEC investigations. That's miracle one. They also need experts on the other end of the call to consent to and engage meaningfully with a non-human interviewer -- an unproven behavioral shift at scale. That's miracle two. The voice AI quality itself is being delivered by foundation model labs, which is favorable (similar to how Harvey outsourced the model fidelity miracle to OpenAI). But the trust and behavioral adoption obstacles are both squarely Checkmate's responsibility, and either one failing independently kills the business. Two distinct miracles with separate probability distributions is where my framework starts flashing warnings.

The founders bring relevant credentials: Garde's eight years as a Google PM across AI products plus building Monzo US from zero to one gives him enterprise-sales muscle and compliance instincts. Sha's NLP background at Google, prior exit with Onyx (300K+ users, acquired by CureFit), and Forbes 30 Under 30 signal both technical depth and execution capability. The fact that they've gotten a working product into pilots with a "$10B+ L/S Equity Hedge Fund" and "MBB PE Consulting" firms -- even if anonymous -- shows real execution velocity for a pre-seed company selling to the most risk-averse buyers in tech. The a16z Speedrun participation adds a credibility signal. This is a team that could plausibly navigate the compliance trust barrier; I just don't know if they can simultaneously solve expert acceptance.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has structural potential that's worth noting. If Checkmate owns the AI interview layer across all expert networks, every call generates proprietary transcript data. That corpus enables cross-call pattern recognition, industry-specific interview protocols, and eventually a proprietary intelligence layer that sits on top of GLG, AlphaSights, and Guidepoint simultaneously. The initial product could become a distribution channel for research synthesis, competitive intelligence dashboards, and even expert sourcing -- making Checkmate the aggregation layer for primary research. That's a real platform trajectory, similar to how Stripe's payment API became the distribution channel for Atlas and Treasury. But the founders haven't publicly articulated this multi-product vision, and right now it's a single-capability product. The bull case is that they're building the "Stripe for expert research" -- a network-agnostic infrastructure layer that every institutional investor integrates into their research workflow, creating switching costs through compliance integration and data accumulation, then distributing additional intelligence products through that relationship. If expert acceptance proves high and the compliance moat hardens, this could be a category-defining company in a boring, high-value market that most investors dismiss.

I'm landing at neutral. The market archetype is right, the founders are credible, and the structural gap between incumbents is real. But the dual-miracle problem -- institutional trust AND expert acceptance -- combined with AlphaSense's distribution advantage and the moderate commoditization risk of LLM voice agents keeps me from writing a check. I'd want to see evidence that experts actually engage productively with the AI interviewer at scale, and that the compliance infrastructure creates real retention, before this moves into my conviction range.
