# tday.com -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

tday.com is a single-sided B2B SaaS aimed at software companies' launch-marketing workflow, scoring highest on market shape and lowest on network compounding — which is the analytical signature of a YC marketing tool, not the leverage architecture I write checks against. Cyrus Kelly is 19, the team is three, and the round is the standard $500K YC ticket; none of that is disqualifying. What is disqualifying is that tday.com's distribution mechanic — a `/book` sales-call CTA on the homepage, hand-picked logos like Relay and Drift on display — is labor-leverage in a code-leverage costume.

Twitter (2007) is the high rung on permissionless leverage because the marginal cost of replication was zero and the platform charged nothing for reach. tday.com shares the *production* primitive — it reads a GitHub repo and auto-regenerates creative on UI change, which is genuinely code-as-leverage on the supply side — but the *distribution* primitive is the opposite: a sales-assisted onboarding flow into software-company buyers. AdCreative.ai and Omneky already ship the same value prop into the same buyer with paid acquisition and outbound, and tday.com's homepage gives me no reason to believe it routes around that motion. The architecture is half-permissionless and the half that matters for compounding — getting to the next customer without permission — is the half that's missing.

The low rung on the network dimension is the generic single-sided SaaS where the Nth customer makes the (N-1) prior customers no more valuable, and tday.com sits squarely on that rung. Relay, Drift, Slate, Forge, and Inkwell each consume tday.com independently; one software company's launch videos do not improve another software company's launch videos. The Perplexity exception is the only door open here — single-sided builder-product can clear if criteria #1 and #3 both pass at top quartile — and tday.com clears neither. Aravind brought a Berkeley retrieval PhD to Perplexity; Cyrus Kelly brought an Adelaide BMaCS and a Chief Student Entrepreneur title to tday.com. Different specific-knowledge gate.

On specific knowledge, the mechanism analysis is what concerns me over 18 months: the founders' edge is a claim of "fully deterministic" code-based design output (Adelaide University, 2026), and that claim either becomes a reproducible technical artifact in the next two product cycles or it does not. Kelly and Shrestha already pivoted from communities.one to markup.one to tday.com — that is iteration *within* the same broad marketing-AI category without yet finding the wedge, and the third founder McDonough is unsourced in every public record I can find. Compare to Ivan Zhao at Notion (2013), who had been rebuilding personal productivity tooling since adolescence before the seed; tday.com's founders have shipped one prior software product together and are 18 months into the current attempt.

On market with pivot room, the counterfactual: tday.com scores in tier-flip range only if I see explicit evidence that the founders intend to expand beyond "marketing creative for software companies shipping product updates" into the broader creator-tools or performance-ads platforms — and the dossier shows the opposite, with the homepage scoped tightly to launch content and the `/book` flow pointed at software-company buyers. The market is real and the pivot vectors exist (Canva-adjacent, Arcade-adjacent, Omneky-adjacent), but Arcade already sits on the launch-video vector with $11.5M from Greylock and a head start.

On the founder trio, the single-anchor parallel is Jeff Atwood at Stack Overflow (2008) — developers writing for developers, with shipped artifacts (Coding Horror) that let me run the integrity check from public behavior. tday.com gives me none of that: no verified Twitter, no verified LinkedIn, no GitHub for any of the three founders. That's not a red flag — quiet founders shouldn't be punished — but it leaves me at the absent-evidence default, and the default doesn't pull a check.

Forecast, 36 months: tday.com lands between $400K and $2M ARR and either gets acqui-hired by Canva, Adobe, or Arcade for a small multiple of capital raised, or pivots into a narrower vertical (Shopify-merchant launch content, for instance) to survive. The mechanism is the absence of permissionless distribution colliding with five funded competitors — Arcade, AdCreative.ai, Omneky, Pencil/Brandtech, Lapis — all chasing the same dollar with more capital and more brand.

The strongest bull case is the Perplexity-style override: if tday.com's git-triggered architecture turns the codebase into a uniquely structured ground-truth feed for product launches, and the performance-data loop becomes a proprietary dataset for creative ranking, the single-sided objection gets waived the way it got waived for Aravind. The bear case wins because the specific-knowledge gate doesn't clear at Perplexity-tier — Kelly is a 19-year-old generalist, not a domain PhD, and the third founder has no published artifact at all. The condition that flips my verdict: tday.com open-sources its deterministic design pipeline, organic GitHub stars compound, and the founders ship measurable performance-lift data versus AdCreative.ai inside the next 12 months. Until then, this is a pass on leverage architecture, not on the people.
