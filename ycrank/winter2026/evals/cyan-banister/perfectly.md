# Perfectly -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Perfectly is the competitive landscape -- and not in a good way. Juicebox has $36 million and $10 million in ARR. Paraform has $25 million and 40x revenue growth. A company literally called "Perfect" raised $23 million for AI recruiting. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce. Dover has been in YC and building for years. AI recruiting is one of the most crowded, most consensus categories I can think of right now. Every investor I know is looking at deals in this space. When I say the most promising companies are not popular and are difficult to assess, I mean precisely the opposite of this. Perfectly is entering a market where the consensus has already formed, the capital has already flowed, and the winners are already scaling. That's the single hardest signal for me to overcome.

When I try to apply my usual framework and ask "tell me your story," I come up empty. Three ML engineers from TikTok who built recommendation systems decided to apply that expertise to recruiting. Victor sat through 800+ agency-sourced interviews and got frustrated. I understand that pain -- I've hired enough people to know how broken recruiting agencies are. But professional frustration is not the same as biographical conviction. I don't hear the story of someone who was failed by the system in a way that forged an unbreakable commitment to fixing it. Compare that to when I met Travis and could feel his gravitas about the taxi medallion racket, or when the Contraline founder had spent years in the academic wilderness pursuing male contraception because nobody else would. These founders are technically strong and the frustration is real, but I'm not sensing the deeper thread that tells me they'll persist through the inevitable crisis that's coming -- and in this competitive market, the crisis will come fast.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The TikTok recommendation systems background is genuinely strong founder-market fit on the technical side -- candidate-to-job matching IS a recommendation problem, and these are people who built those systems at massive scale. The fully automated, no-human-in-the-loop approach is actually a bold claim that the entire recruiting intermediary is obsolete. That has some contrarian energy to it, even within a consensus market. And success-based pricing -- you only pay when someone gets placed -- is a smart GTM move that removes all buyer risk. If the matching quality is truly superior because of their rec-sys expertise, the data flywheel from reusable candidate screens could compound into something defensible. The customer who dropped both Paraform and Juicebox after two weeks is a real signal. For this to work spectacularly, they'd need the AI screening to be so good that placement rates blow away human recruiters, creating word-of-mouth among hiring managers that makes the competitive funding gap irrelevant. That's possible but it requires the product to be extraordinary, not just good.

What I notice through my own lens is that this company doesn't create economic access in the way I care about. Uber gave drivers income. Postmates gave delivery workers opportunity. Affirm gave underserved borrowers transparent credit. Perfectly serves startups hiring technical talent -- already the most sought-after workers in the economy -- and saves companies money on recruiting fees. That's valuable, but it's efficiency optimization for people who are already well-served. There's an argument that AI screening could reduce bias compared to human recruiters, making hiring more fair for non-traditional candidates, but the dossier doesn't suggest the team is thinking about it that way. They're building a better mousetrap for YC companies, not making an invisible system visible or opening doors for people who've been locked out.

On the regulatory front, AI hiring is becoming one of the most scrutinized categories in tech -- the EU AI Act flags it as high-risk, and New York already requires bias audits. I normally get excited when founders are deliberately building in regulated spaces because the complexity creates a moat. But here, the regulation reads as liability rather than advantage. There's no evidence the team has regulatory expertise or is thinking about compliance as a differentiator. And the brand collision with Perfect ($23M, same market) is the kind of messy distraction that drains early-stage energy. The shared TikTok background among all three co-founders gives me moderate comfort on team alignment -- and the shared surname between Victor and Gary Luo makes me curious whether there's a family connection, which I tend to view positively. But I don't have enough signal on how they navigate disagreements to feel confident there.

This is a competent team in a hot market, and I wish them well. But competent teams in consensus markets are the opposite of what gets me out of bed. I need the weirdness, the biographical thread, the sense that everyone else is wrong and these founders know something nobody can see yet. I don't feel that here.
