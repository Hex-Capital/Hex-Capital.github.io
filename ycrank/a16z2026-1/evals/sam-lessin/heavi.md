# Heavi -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari scaled WayCool Foods to $250M+ ARR running agricultural supply chains in India, then went and got his ASE T8 diesel mechanic certification. That's the kind of biographical detail that makes me sit up. A Harvard MBA who already built a supply chain marketplace at massive scale, choosing to go get his hands dirty -- literally -- certifying on heavy-duty truck repair before starting his next company. His father ran Ashok Leyland, one of India's biggest commercial vehicle manufacturers. The domain credibility here is real and deep. Paired with Holkesvik, who built NCX's satellite-imagery marketplace to 15,000 members as CTO, this is a team with demonstrated marketplace-building chops. The founder-market fit is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed.

But here's where my framework diverges from a generic "great founders, real problem" assessment. The mispricing question for Heavi is complicated. On one hand, heavy-duty truck parts procurement is genuinely unsexy territory -- nobody at demo day is salivating over diesel mechanics losing institutional knowledge. That fragmented, blue-collar, low-tech customer base creates natural investor squeamishness, and squeamishness creates mispricing. On the other hand, Heavi frames itself as an "AI workforce" building "AI-powered" procurement agents, which is the single most consensus-hot framing in venture right now. Every time I hear "AI agent that searches multiple sources and handles the full workflow," I hear a pitch that fifteen other companies are making in fifteen other verticals. The unglamorous vertical buys some mispricing; the AI-agent framing gives it all back.

The primitive test is where Heavi loses me. "Find Parts 75% Faster" is an efficiency claim on an existing workflow, not a new behavior. Parts procurement exists. Catalog search exists. Vendor outreach exists. Heavi makes it faster with AI. That's a feature improvement, not a primitive creation. Compare this to Venmo, which created a behavior that literally didn't exist -- peer-to-peer mobile payments as a social act. Or Metropolis, which created a new category of AI-managed physical infrastructure. Heavi isn't creating a new verb. Nobody's going to say "Heavi me that part." They're going to say "we use Heavi to find parts faster." That's the language of a tool, not a primitive. The "digitizing retiring institutional knowledge" angle is interesting conceptually, but the product as described is a procurement search agent, not a knowledge platform.

The AI kill test is the crux. Fullbay has 5,000+ shops on its platform, $19.4M in 2024 revenue, and just acquired Pitstop for AI capabilities in March 2026. Every time I see an incumbent with distribution acquiring AI capability in the same quarter a startup launches, I think about Adobe. Fullbay already owns the shop management workflow -- invoicing, scheduling, parts ordering. Adding an AI procurement layer to that existing relationship is exactly the kind of sustaining innovation that benefits the incumbent. The dossier notes the parts data itself is not proprietary. Heavi's "domain complexity barrier" -- thousands of OEM part numbers and cross-references -- is real, but it's a data problem, not a structural moat. FleetPride, TruckPro, or any large distributor with access to the same catalogs could build a similar agent. Where Metropolis controls physical parking infrastructure that no software company can replicate, Heavi is a pure software layer searching other people's inventories.

The bull case I have to honestly confront: Dasari's combination of marketplace scaling experience and genuine domain expertise is rare. The 67,000 diesel technician shortage by 2026 is a structural workforce crisis, not a cyclical one. The $100K in contracted revenue from 5 design partners with reported 40% margin improvement is meaningful signal at pre-seed -- these shops are paying real money because the pain is real. If Heavi executes fast enough to build a proprietary dataset of pricing, availability, and vendor reliability across the fragmented heavy-duty ecosystem before Fullbay bolts on similar functionality, the data flywheel could create genuine switching costs. And the "marketplace" tag suggests Heavi may evolve toward owning the transaction -- taking a cut of parts purchases -- which would be a stronger structural position than pure SaaS. If the founders can navigate from procurement tool to transaction platform, this starts looking more like financial plumbing than an AI wrapper.

The co-founder relationship gives me pause -- no shared prior employer or university between Dasari and Holkesvik, likely connected through SF's startup network. My best returns come from founders I've watched across cycles together, not from people who found each other through networking. Holkesvik's NCX trajectory also warrants scrutiny: raised $74.4M, then Verra declined to certify the core product, followed by 40% layoffs. That's not disqualifying -- plenty of good operators have been through hard pivots -- but it's a "sane person" data point that cuts both ways. The two decisive dimensions for me are Primitive Creation and Structural Durability. Heavi is making an existing workflow faster, not creating a new category, and the AI capability sits in a layer where incumbents with distribution can replicate it. Dasari is an exceptional founder in a real market with real pain, but that alone doesn't make it my kind of bet.
