# Sidekick -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

400+ workers across 19 locations, built by a solo founder — that's real usage, and I don't dismiss it. Justin So got Sidekick into the hands of people actually wearing gloves on factory floors, not just into a demo environment. The SMS delivery choice is the smartest thing about this company: in a world where every AI startup is building another dashboard or app, Sidekick recognized that its actual users don't have company email, might not have smartphones, and can't stop operating a machine to open an app. That's a genuine product insight. But a product insight is not a thesis. When I look at Sidekick, I see a competent tool in a crowded space — Yourco with $8M, TeamSense with ~$9M, Beekeeper with $100M+ — and I need to explain why this isn't my kind of bet despite the real traction.

Justin So's origin story is that his father owns a manufacturing facility and asked him for a tool to help with worker onboarding and knowledge management. I always ask founders to tell me their story before the business, and this one is honest — a father's request is a genuine starting point, not a manufactured narrative. But it's his father's problem, not his. Justin studied CS at UPenn and worked in finance at Arm. He didn't work the factory floor. He didn't experience being the deskless worker who couldn't read the manual because it was in a language he didn't speak, or who got injured because the safety procedure was locked in a binder three buildings away. The biographical forge I look for — the kind I recognized in the Crusoe founders who were standing in North Dakota oil fields watching energy literally burn, or in the Uber driver who handed me his card because the product had already changed his life — requires the problem to have found the founder through lived experience. Sidekick's origin is one degree removed: familial proximity, not biographical inevitability. Justin might be a talented builder, but when I ask whether he'd be working on this even without funding, the honest answer is I don't know.

Sidekick's space — AI-powered knowledge retrieval for enterprise workers — is about as consensus as it gets in 2026. Every LLM company pitches RAG over company documents. The SMS channel is a distribution insight, but distribution insights don't make investors shift uncomfortably in their chairs. Nobody at a dinner party would raise an eyebrow when you say "we help factory workers get answers via text message." Compare that to when I told people I was writing a check to a company mining Bitcoin with waste gas flares, or backing a male birth control company, or funding defense AI when Silicon Valley thought it was politically radioactive. Sidekick is solving a real problem, but it's solving it in a way that every other investor would nod along with. The competitive landscape confirms this: Yourco raised a $6M Series A in March 2026, TeamSense is growing, and Beekeeper has over $100M deployed in the adjacent space. When well-funded competitors are already in the neighborhood, I'm not the first check — I'm joining a consensus.

Here's where an advocate would push back on me, and I want to be fair about it: Sidekick's escalation-learning loop — where unanswered questions get routed to managers, resolved, and fed back into the knowledge base — could create a genuine per-customer data flywheel over time. Each deployment gets smarter. The switching costs from accumulated institutional knowledge encoded in Sidekick's system are real. And the "why now" timing is legitimate — multimodal LLMs that can process photos of equipment and voice memos just hit production quality, and 83% of frontline workers report missing critical information. An advocate would argue that Sidekick is doing for frontline knowledge what Affirm did for lending terms: making something opaque visible. But here's where the parallel breaks down. Affirm exposed a system that was designed to extract value through opacity — credit card companies profit from consumer confusion about rates. Sidekick's target isn't an extractive system; it's an inefficient one. The factory that doesn't have a good training process isn't profiting from keeping workers uninformed — it's losing money from it. Sidekick is a productivity tool that aligns incentives between management and workers, which is valuable, but it doesn't shift structural power from gatekeepers to the powerless the way my strongest investments do. The knowledge base still belongs to the company. The worker gets better answers, not more power.

Sidekick's defensibility concerns me most. The dossier states plainly: "The core technology (LLM + RAG over company documents delivered via SMS) is reproducible with widely available components." Yourco and TeamSense already own SMS infrastructure and frontline worker customer bases — adding AI-powered Q&A is an incremental feature extension for them, not a new product. Beekeeper, with $100M+ in funding, could add an SMS channel tomorrow. Sidekick's moat today is execution speed and the learning loop, but those are time-limited advantages, not structural ones. My biggest failures — HQ Trivia, GameCrush, IRL — all shared a pattern: no infrastructure-grade defensibility beneath an attractive surface. The per-customer data accumulation is valuable but siloed; it doesn't create network effects across deployments. Each new customer doesn't make Sidekick structurally harder to displace the way each new Flock Safety camera strengthens the network.

Sidekick's strongest dimension is genuine real-world impact — 12+ languages, no app download, no login, works on basic phones for workers who may lack technical literacy or data plans. If Sidekick scales, it could expand information access for exactly the workers who are most excluded by app-based solutions. That matters to me. But impact without defensibility is a donation, not an investment. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: if Justin So had spent years working on factory floors himself and could demonstrate that Sidekick's escalation-learning loop had generated a proprietary knowledge graph spanning multiple manufacturing verticals — something that created cross-customer intelligence, not just per-customer memory. That would be infrastructure hiding beneath a simple SMS surface, the pattern I saw in Crusoe when everyone else saw a crypto gimmick. Right now, I see a capable solo founder building a useful tool in a consensus market without the biographical forge, contrarian conviction, or structural moat that define a Long Journey bet.
