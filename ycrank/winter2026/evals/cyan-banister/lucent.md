# Lucent -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me is the pivot. Lucent raised $1.3 million in 36 hours — genuinely impressive velocity — but that money was raised for a completely different company. The original pitch was "behavioural datasets for browser agents," which frankly was the more interesting and contrarian idea. Training data for browser agents was at least approaching something that could be a fundamental infrastructure layer. The current product — AI that watches session replays to find bugs — is a move toward the obvious, not away from it. Every developer tool company is adding an AI layer right now. Every session replay vendor is thinking about automated analysis. This isn't the kind of unpopular, hard-to-assess opportunity I get excited about. It's the opposite: it's consensus wrapped in a YC badge.

If I could sit down with Alisa Wu, my first question would be the one I always ask: tell me your story. Not the product, not the market — how did you get here? And from what I can see in the dossier, I'd hear the story of a clearly talented 22-year-old who's already had two exits and moved from Sydney to San Francisco to build something. That's admirable. But I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this specific problem, and I can't find it. She built an edtech tutoring app, she was a founding engineer at an ad platform, she interned at Atlassian. These are the résumé markers of a capable generalist, not someone who's been keeping herself up at night for years because nobody watches their session replays. When I backed the Contraline founder, he'd spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund him — the problem was in his bones. I don't get that signal here. The description of ChatGPT as her "Chief Technology Officer" is resourceful and candid, but for a product that requires sophisticated AI analysis of visual and DOM data, it makes me wonder about the technical depth underneath.

Here's where I'd push back on myself, because I've been wrong before when I dismissed young founders for lacking the narrative I wanted to hear. The bull case is real: Alisa Wu ships fast. She built and exited Stella AI in nine months. She raised pre-seed capital in 36 hours. If speed of execution is the moat — get to customers, build the data flywheel, become the default AI analysis layer before FullStory or Sentry wake up — then she might be exactly the right founder. The insight that thousands of session recordings go unwatched is genuinely true, and the team that solves it first could build a meaningful business. If she can get embedded with 50 engineering teams in the next six months and build enough proprietary understanding of bug patterns across diverse applications, the incumbents might find it easier to acquire her than to build it themselves. That's a plausible $100M+ outcome.

But I keep coming back to my core filters and none of them are lighting up. This isn't weird — it's a logical application of broadly available AI to an existing workflow. It doesn't create economic access for underserved populations; it saves engineering teams time. There's no regulatory moat, no stigmatized category, nothing that makes other investors uncomfortable enough to pass. The competitive landscape is full of well-funded incumbents who could ship this as a feature update — Sentry at $3 billion and $100 million in revenue has both the data and the engineering muscle to build exactly this. And the brand name "Lucent" is shared with roughly a dozen other companies, which is the kind of practical problem that signals the founder hasn't yet thought deeply about long-term positioning. I respect the hustle and the early exits, but this doesn't match the pattern of companies I write checks into. The best investments I've made looked ridiculous to everyone else at the time. This one looks reasonable to everyone, and that's exactly the wrong signal for me.
