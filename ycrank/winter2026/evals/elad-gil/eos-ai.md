# Eos AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Healthcare data interoperability is one of the most consensus problems in health tech -- and has been for two decades. Every investor who's spent a week in digital health knows that EHRs don't talk to each other, imaging systems use different formats, and clinical data is fragmented across silos. This is not a non-obvious market. It's the opposite: a market so obviously broken that it has attracted billions in capital -- Commure at $820M+ raised and a $6B valuation, Redox with $181M in 2024 revenue, Datavant with $83M raised, Health Gorilla with $80M, plus Microsoft, Google, and AWS all building healthcare data harmonization products. When I look at a market where a startup would be my investment, I need to see something most investors are missing. Here, they're not missing anything -- they see exactly this problem and are already funding it aggressively. The "AI-native" repositioning adds a lens, but the underlying market is well-mapped.

The strongest bull case requires reframing the market entirely. If you squint past "healthcare data integration" and see "infrastructure for clinical AI deployment," there's a more interesting structural moment. CMS interoperability mandates (TEFCA, HTI-1) are forcing health systems to open their data, and the simultaneous explosion of clinical AI models -- radiology AI, pathology AI, clinical decision support -- creates sudden demand for ML-ready datasets that existing interoperability tools were never designed to produce. Redox routes data between apps; Datavant links de-identified records for research. Neither preprocesses imaging data for ML training or normalizes clinical text into structured representations optimized for downstream models. That gap is real, and it's been enabled by specific technology thresholds: LLM capabilities reaching clinical NLP fidelity (enabling LUCIA-style text harmonization) and domain adaptation techniques maturing for cross-scanner medical imaging (enabling VERA-style distribution alignment). Two years ago, you couldn't build this. For this bull case to hold, Eos would need to be the Stripe of clinical AI infrastructure -- the integration layer that every AI application vendor plugs into. That's a compelling vision, and the federated architecture (keeping data in place while building a centralized index) avoids the failed "centralize everything" approaches that have repeatedly died in healthcare.

The problem is this company needs more than one miracle. First, the imaging and text harmonization engines (VERA and LUCIA) must achieve clinical-grade fidelity -- this is a hard technical challenge involving domain-specific ML across heterogeneous scanner manufacturers, imaging modalities, and clinical documentation styles. Second, health systems must adopt despite the political economy of EHR vendor lock-in -- Epic controls roughly 38% of the US hospital EHR market and has a structural incentive to resist vendor-neutral data layers that reduce their lock-in advantage. Epic doesn't need to outbuild Eos; it just needs to not cooperate. Third, a 2-person team must navigate 12-18 month enterprise healthcare procurement cycles while simultaneously building product. These are three sequential obstacles, and that's the kind of compounding low-probability plan I avoid.

Arya Khokhar brings relevant technical depth -- published ML research in medical imaging from Caltech, Stanford research experience, and the ONCOVATE cancer triage pilot with Stanford Medicine. That Stanford pilot is a meaningful signal at pre-seed; getting any product in front of a top-tier health system demonstrates some ability to navigate clinical environments. The website claims deployment metrics (3x administrative productivity, 37% revenue recovery, 18% model performance improvement), which, if real, suggest actual engagement with health system data. But these are unverified, unattributed to named customers, and could reflect small-scale or controlled experiments. There's no evidence of prior startup founding, rapid shipping velocity, or the kind of weekly iteration cadence I look for. This is a first-time founder with academic credentials -- not a red flag, but not the execution signal that would override my market concerns.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is the most interesting dimension here. The federated data layer, if it becomes the canonical harmonization infrastructure for a health system, creates a natural distribution channel for every AI application that needs clinical data. Start with imaging harmonization (VERA), expand to clinical text (LUCIA), then distribute predictive models and workflow automation through the same integration. That's structurally similar to how Stripe started with payments and distributed Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through the developer relationship. The architecture enables platform expansion, and the founders appear to think about this -- the product already spans data indexing, harmonization, prediction, and workflow automation. But this is theoretical potential at a stage where the company hasn't demonstrated the initial wedge product working at scale, and the competitive moat against Commure ($6B, 130+ health systems already) building this same multi-product expansion from a larger installed base is unclear.

I'm passing on this one. The market is too consensus, the competitive landscape too well-capitalized, and the plan requires multiple sequential breakthroughs. The structural moment at the intersection of regulatory mandates and clinical AI proliferation is real, but it's visible to every health tech investor and every well-funded competitor already working this space. I need to see a market that other investors are dismissing -- not one where a $6B competitor already exists. The founder has relevant technical credentials and the Stanford pilot is a positive signal, but a 2-person team entering a market with $1B+ in aggregate competitor funding needs an extraordinary structural advantage I don't see here.
