# InLoop Robotics -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural paradox here stops me cold. InLoop's entire thesis is that robots should ship imperfect and use human teleoperators to close the gap. But human teleoperators are labor leverage — the exact form of leverage I penalize most heavily. The company's differentiation is literally its dependence on human workers. Yes, the data flywheel promises to eliminate that dependence over time, but "we need labor leverage now so we can achieve code leverage later" is a promissory note, not an architecture. Every deployed robot that calls for help is simultaneously generating training data AND consuming teleoperator wages. The question is whether the flywheel spins fast enough to escape the labor trap before the unit economics collapse — and before Nimble ($221M raised) or Dexterity ($291M raised) achieve full autonomy from the opposite direction and render the bridge unnecessary.

On specific knowledge: I see student-age founders from TUM Munich with genuine robotics talent — Sharfeddine's BEHAVIOR Challenge win is real, his BMW and Bosch stints show industrial exposure, and the RoboTUM connection between him and Rizali is an organic co-founder bond, not a startup weekend artifact. But academic robotics competition wins and internships are not the same as years of non-replicable expertise that IS the company. If these founders vanished, any competent robotics team could reconstruct the human-in-the-loop concept. Sharfeddine himself forked Physical Intelligence's open-source openpi framework — which means the tools to build a competing system are publicly available. The insight isn't proprietary. Compare this to Perplexity, where Srinivas's years inside Google Brain and OpenAI constituted knowledge no competitor could download from GitHub. Here, the concept is in the air. It's a known approach in the robotics community. What would make this defensible is thousands of hours of real-world intervention data from diverse warehouse environments — but at pre-seed with "multiple robots" deployed and no named customers, that data moat is aspirational.

The leverage audit reveals a company structurally misaligned with my framework. Hardware manufacturing requires capital leverage. Field deployment requires human technicians. Teleoperators are pure labor leverage. The AI model training loop is the one permissionless component, but it's layered on top of three labor- and capital-intensive layers. This is not a company that reaches meaningful revenue with fifteen people. Growth requires manufacturing more robots, hiring more teleoperators, deploying more field teams. Formic, which uses third-party hardware and still raised $52M+, demonstrates how capital-hungry even the lighter-touch version of RaaS becomes. The shrinking-company thesis — Midjourney at $500M revenue with 163 people, Cursor at $500M ARR with under 50 — does not apply to a company that must physically build, ship, install, maintain, and remotely operate robots in warehouses.

Now, the bull case deserves honest consideration. If the human-in-the-loop flywheel works as designed, InLoop has a legitimate structural advantage over competitors chasing full autonomy in the lab: real-world edge case data from production environments. Nimble and Dexterity train in controlled settings and deploy when ready; InLoop deploys immediately and trains on real chaos. That's a faster path to robust manipulation policies if — and this is the critical if — the intervention-to-autonomy ratio improves at exponential rather than linear rates. The RaaS model genuinely democratizes warehouse automation for operators priced out of $100K+ CapEx solutions, and that access expansion pattern is one I systematically back. If the team can demonstrate that intervention frequency drops by 50% per quarter as the model trains on real deployments, the story becomes compelling. The zero-CapEx entry point could create a distribution advantage that well-funded competitors, optimized for enterprise contracts, cannot replicate in the SMB segment.

But I keep returning to the denominator problem. Nimble has $221M and FedEx as a strategic customer. Dexterity has $291M and UPS contracts. Pickle Robot has $87M+ and 30+ robot orders. InLoop has four people, no named customers, and a concept that requires more humans to scale before AI can replace them. The gap is not merely financial — it's in deployed hours of real-world data, which is the very resource InLoop claims as its moat. Well-funded competitors accumulating deployment data in FedEx facilities may close the human-in-the-loop advantage before InLoop can open it. And one of three co-founders has no retrievable public credentials whatsoever — a diligence gap that in a hardware company, where trust in execution capability is everything, I cannot dismiss.

I pass. The founders show genuine passion for robotics, the co-founder relationship is organic, and the democratization angle is real. But the leverage architecture is inverted — labor-dependent by design — the specific knowledge is academic and replicable, and the competitive landscape features incumbents with 50x the capital pursuing convergent solutions. Innovation without a defensible leverage architecture is a race where the better-funded team wins.
