# Origin -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Origin is who they're picking a fight with. Their flagship model Axis claims to outperform Google DeepMind's AlphaGenome by 6.7% on regulatory element binding prediction. Two CS undergrads from UIUC, with a four-person team, built something they say beats what might be the most well-resourced AI research organization on the planet. That's either the kind of audacity I love or a warning sign that your competitive moat has a very short half-life. When I backed SpaceX, Elon wasn't benchmarking against a side project from a trillion-dollar company's research lab -- he was building capability that didn't exist. Here, the core model is trained on publicly available ENCODE data using standard transformer architectures. DeepMind could ship an update tomorrow that subsumes this niche entirely. The proprietary wet-lab dataset is where real defensibility would come from, but they haven't disclosed its scale, and validating millions of synthetic regulatory sequences with four people is a claim I'd need to see operating infrastructure behind before I believed it.

My first question for any founder is always: tell me your story, not the business. How did you get here? And with Origin, the dossier gives me academic credentials -- impressive ones, genuinely. Malhar published in Nature Scientific Reports while still in high school. Yash won first prize in an OpenCV competition and worked at NVIDIA. These are clearly talented people. But I'm not hearing the biographical thread that connects them to the suffering caused by unsafe gene therapies. I don't see someone who watched a family member endure a toxic CAR-T infusion, or who spent years in a wet lab obsessing over why regulatory elements fail in vivo. This reads like two brilliant ML engineers who identified an interesting technical application in genomics. That's intellectual ambition, and I respect it, but it's not the same as the founder whose biography and mission are inseparable. The founders I write the biggest checks for are the ones where the problem chose them before they chose the problem.

The niche itself -- AI-designed regulatory DNA elements for gene expression control -- is genuinely distinct from the adjacent competitors. Dyno does capsids. Asimov does genetic circuits. Tessera does gene writing. Origin focuses on the switches that control when and where therapeutic genes turn on. That's a real layer of the gene therapy stack that nobody else owns exclusively, and it's the kind of narrow, hard-to-assess problem that usually doesn't attract consensus capital. I like that. It's not "magically weird" in the way Crusoe Energy mining Bitcoin on gas flares was weird -- nobody's going to laugh you out of the room for saying "AI for regulatory DNA" -- but it is obscure enough that most investors won't know how to evaluate it, which creates opportunity for someone willing to develop conviction. The advisory board -- Manolis Kellis at MIT/Broad, Nicole Paulk at UCSF, Rashid Bashir at UIUC -- is strong enough to partially compensate for the founders' computational-rather-than-biological backgrounds.

Let me steel-man the bull case, because it deserves one. If Origin actually builds the largest proprietary dataset of experimentally validated synthetic regulatory sequences -- and if that dataset creates a compounding flywheel where each experimental cycle improves the model, which generates better sequences, which attract more pharma partners -- then they could own a critical chokepoint in the gene therapy supply chain. Cell and gene therapies are a $13-26 billion market growing at 18-24% CAGR. Every one of those therapies needs regulatory elements to control gene expression, and right now the design process is slow and imprecise. If Origin becomes the company that pharma calls when they need a promoter or enhancer that turns on only in the right cell type, that's a defensible, high-margin position. The timing is right -- multiple gene therapies approved in 2023-2025, ENCODE V4 data available, transformer architectures mature enough for the task. And the regulatory complexity of biotech does create a natural moat against casual entrants, which is a pattern I've seen work in my portfolio.

But I keep coming back to the gap between where they are and where they'd need to be. No revenue. No disclosed pharma partnerships. A free API for researchers. Four people trying to bridge computational biology and wet-lab validation simultaneously. The invisible-systems pattern I've seen in Carta and Flock Safety is present in a loose sense -- regulatory DNA elements are literally invisible control mechanisms that Origin is trying to make designable -- but the beneficiaries are pharmaceutical companies, not underserved populations locked out of economic access. This is infrastructure for an industry that charges patients a million dollars per treatment. That's important work, but it doesn't create the kind of positive-sum economic outcomes I gravitate toward with companies like Uber or Affirm or Postmates. And without a biographical story that gives me conviction these specific founders will persist through the inevitable multi-year slog of biotech validation, I can't find the jolt of energy I need to write a check from my own money.
