# Amboras -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Amboras is "powered by Anthropic's Claude AI." That single line from the Extruct profile tells me most of what I need to know about the leverage architecture. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas wasn't renting OpenAI's infrastructure and calling it a product — he was building retrieval-augmented generation systems from his years of specific knowledge at Google Brain. Amboras is calling an API. The store generation, the A/B testing, the SEO optimization — these are Claude prompts with e-commerce context windows. Innovation without a moat is consumer surplus. The value flows to Anthropic and eventually to merchants who can get the same functionality from Shopify Magic, Wix's AI builder, or the next well-funded team that reads the same API documentation. Amboras faces the structural problem I see in every AI wrapper: the permissionless leverage belongs to someone else.

Amin and Imad Mokadem ran an e-commerce brand to $400K+/month revenue. Imad built board games at ETH Zurich and sold them on Shopify. They "spent considerable time and money working with developers" to manage their store. This is genuine principal behavior — brothers who lived inside the problem, felt the friction personally, and built EcomCoder as a Shopify plugin before pivoting to Amboras as a standalone platform. The progression from selling board games to building a plugin to building a platform is the organic trajectory of a principal, not an agent scanning YC application trends. I give the Mokadem brothers high marks on authenticity. But specific knowledge and principal commitment are different tests. Running a successful Shopify store is real experience — and it's experience shared by tens of thousands of six-figure merchants worldwide. Compare this to Ev Williams at Twitter: his Blogger-era knowledge about public communication was held by perhaps a dozen people on earth. Amin's CS degree from ETH Zurich is strong but not AI-research-depth. Amboras has founder-market fit; it does not have founder-specific-knowledge that would take a competitor years to acquire.

Shopify's 2025 Renaissance update already added AI-native features including Shopify Magic and agent-led commerce. Shopify has 4.8M+ merchants and the engineering resources to ship an "autopilot" mode that replicates Amboras's entire value proposition within months. The Perplexity-vs-Google analogy doesn't hold here. Google's principal-agent problem is structural — their search team literally cannot cannibalize $200B in ad revenue. Shopify's principal-agent problem is weaker: their app ecosystem generates revenue, but Shopify has repeatedly shown willingness to absorb plugin functionality into core product when it improves merchant retention. Amboras is positioning as "the Shopify of the AI era," but Shopify is also positioning as the Shopify of the AI era. The Mokadem brothers are principals facing an incumbent that behaves more like a principal than most large companies do.

The bull case an advocate would make: Amboras has 1,000+ stores on the platform with a claimed 3.9% average conversion rate and 2-day average time to first sale. A 4-person team serving 1,000+ merchants is genuine team-to-value leverage. The brothers' co-founder bond is the deepest trust structure possible — no "find a co-founder" event here. Abde Aboulas as CRO brings Rapha and Carhartt scaling experience. And Shopify's app ecosystem dependency creates a real channel conflict that prevents full AI integration — every feature Shopify automates natively is revenue taken from its 8,000+ app partners. The access democratization is real: Amboras gives a solo merchant in Mumbai the same conversion optimization previously requiring a $5,000/month agency. I have to take this seriously. But the traction claims are unverified — testimonials from Milan, London, Amsterdam, Mumbai, Barcelona, and Rome for a San Francisco pre-seed startup is an unusual geographic spread that deserves scrutiny. And the pivot from EcomCoder (97 Product Hunt upvotes, #14 day rank) to Amboras is recent enough that the current platform's product-market fit remains untested by time.

My Wish investment taught me that e-commerce leverage without deep specific knowledge is structurally fragile. Wish had marketplace network effects, zero-marginal-cost digital matching, and massive scale — and it collapsed 95% from its IPO peak because there was no founder-specific-knowledge moat underneath the leverage. Amboras has better principal authenticity than Wish did, but the same core vulnerability: the technology layer (AI store optimization) is commoditizable, and the domain knowledge (running a Shopify store) is widespread. The condition that would flip me to Invest is evidence of a proprietary data or model advantage — if Amboras showed that its optimization engine trained on 10,000+ stores produces measurably better conversion than any competitor using the same foundation model, that's a compounding data flywheel worth backing. At 1,000 unverified stores, that flywheel hasn't started spinning.

Amboras scores highest on principal authenticity — the Mokadem brothers are genuine principals building from lived pain — and lowest on specific knowledge productization and permissionless leverage architecture, my two highest-weighted criteria. The founders' e-commerce experience is real but replicable. The AI leverage is rented from Anthropic. The incumbent response from Shopify is already underway. This is a competent team in a real market, but the structural elements that produce outlier returns — non-replicable knowledge, owned leverage, compounding advantages — are absent or unproven.
