# Human Archive -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Human Archive is a structural market problem that should keep these founders up at night. Their best potential customers -- Physical Intelligence ($1.1B raised), Generalist AI (Nvidia-backed), Figure AI -- are the exact companies with the strongest incentive to never buy from a third-party data provider. Generalist AI already collects 270,000 hours of manipulation data and adds 10,000 hours per week internally because, for a robotics foundation model company, proprietary training data *is* the competitive advantage. Selling data externally is selling your moat. This means Human Archive is building a supply business for a demand side that is structurally motivated to vertically integrate. That's not a death sentence -- there were moments when semiconductor companies said they'd never outsource fabrication, and TSMC proved them wrong -- but it means the consequence of success here is bounded in ways the TAM numbers don't capture.

Let me ask my first question: if Human Archive fully succeeds, what changes? They become the dominant third-party supplier of multimodal robotics training data. A $30 billion market by 2035, if the projections hold. That's a real business -- potentially a very large one. But nobody's life changes because a data vendor captured market share. The robots that train on the data might change the world. The data supply chain that feeds them is infrastructure, not transformation. I funded Impossible Foods because eliminating animal agriculture was worth the risk of total loss. I funded Commonwealth Fusion because fusion energy would restructure civilization. A data collection operation for robotics labs, no matter how large, doesn't reach that threshold. The consequence of success is a profitable company in an important supply chain, not an industry made obsolete or a physical reality reshaped.

The competitive dynamics compound this concern. Scale AI entered robotics data collection in September 2025 -- before Human Archive even started YC. Scale has $2 billion in revenue, existing relationships with the very frontier labs Human Archive targets, and a contractor army that can scale data collection across geographies without designing custom hardware. Human Archive's counter-argument -- that custom sensor hardware produces qualitatively superior multimodal data -- is plausible but unproven in this dossier. I've seen this movie before: the specialized startup claims quality differentiation against the generalist incumbent, and sometimes it works (Juniper vs. Cisco's legacy architecture) and sometimes the incumbent's scale advantage overwhelms the quality delta. The difference is that Juniper had a fundamental architectural advantage -- TCP/IP routing that made Cisco's approach structurally obsolete. Human Archive's custom hardware captures the same modalities (video, force-feedback, teleoperation) that any well-funded competitor could capture with off-the-shelf sensors and operational scale. That's not structural disruption; it's operational excellence. Operational excellence is a fine business strategy, but it's not what I invest in.

Now, the bull case -- because this team deserves a genuine hearing. Four founders who dropped out of Stanford and Berkeley and relocated to Asia to build a data collection operation are demonstrating the kind of reckless commitment I respect. The timing is exceptional: billions are flowing into robotics foundation models right now, Goldman projects 50,000-100,000 humanoid robot shipments in 2026, and the gap between available training data and what these models need is real and widening. If Human Archive moves fast enough to build the definitive multimodal dataset -- genuinely the world's largest, with modality alignment that simulation and contractor-recorded video cannot match -- that dataset becomes a compounding asset. Every additional hour of annotated, aligned data makes the next hour more valuable because it improves the coverage and diversity of the training distribution. If the robotics model companies discover that the *quality* of real-world data matters far more than quantity, and that Human Archive's custom hardware captures subtleties that smartphone-wielding contractors miss, then Scale AI's entry becomes irrelevant and the vertical integration threat fades because building this capability in-house is harder than it looks. The team composition -- ML researcher, mechanical engineer, product person, operations lead -- maps directly to the key risks. For this to be a great investment, what has to be true is: (1) multimodal data quality is a genuine moat, not a marketing claim, (2) model companies find it more efficient to outsource data than build in-house, and (3) Human Archive executes faster than Scale AI's robotics division. That's three simultaneous conditions, and my framework flags that as a plan with too many dependencies that all need to break favorably.

What I don't see here is what I saw in my best bets: a technology that makes an existing system economically unviable. Human Archive doesn't displace anything -- it adds to the supply chain. The founders aren't attacking a problem the consensus considers impossible; they're following capital flows into robotics AI, which is entirely consensus as of 2025. Every major VC is funding robotics now. Scale AI validated the exact data collection opportunity six months before Human Archive entered YC. When incumbents and established players are already moving in the direction you're headed, the outsider advantage I prize disappears. These founders are smart, committed, and well-positioned in a real market. But they're building a service business dressed as a data moat, in a market where the largest players are structurally inclined to build rather than buy.
