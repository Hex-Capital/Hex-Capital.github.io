# Kita -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is clear and it's one I recognize. Vision-language models crossed a document processing capability threshold in 2024-2025 that fundamentally changes what's possible with messy, multilingual, real-world financial documents. Before GPT-4V and its equivalents, extracting structured data from a Filipino e-wallet screenshot or an Indonesian payslip required bespoke OCR pipelines that broke constantly -- Ocrolus solved this for US documents by throwing human verification at the problem, which works when you're processing US W-2s but doesn't scale across dozens of emerging market document formats. The VLM capability jump is analogous to what I saw with GPT-3 to GPT-4 when evaluating legal AI: a technology threshold that transforms a market from "impossible" to "inevitable" within a compressed window. The additional structural factor is that open banking APIs don't exist in these markets and won't for years -- meaning document-based underwriting isn't going away. Kita is building infrastructure for a workflow that will persist, using technology that just became viable. That's a real "why now."

This company maps onto one of my highest-conviction patterns: "boring but critical" enterprise infrastructure in a market most investors would dismiss as too niche. Document processing for emerging market lenders -- I can hear the pitch falling flat in Sand Hill Road conference rooms. "The Philippines alt lending market is $1.64 billion? Pass." But that's exactly the kind of static TAM analysis I warn against. You have to look at the adoption curve: digital lending across Southeast Asia, Latin America, and Africa is growing rapidly, each market requires the same core infrastructure (document-to-signal conversion for underwriting), and the incumbent serving this function in the US (Ocrolus, now at $252M revenue) has zero structural incentive to localize for Filipino e-wallet records or Mexican payslips. When I invested in PagerDuty, the objection was "operations alerting is niche." When I invested in Checkr, the objection was "background checks are boring." The market here is bigger than any single country suggests, and the per-country regulatory fragmentation that scares off most investors is actually a moat -- it's compliance-as-barrier, which is a pattern I'm comfortable with from building Color Health through CLIA regulations.

The founder-market fit is unusually precise. Carmel Limcaoco is from Manila and brings the local network that apparently generated a six-figure paid pilot the week the prototype shipped -- if true, that's exceptional execution velocity and suggests the pain point is genuine and acute. Rhea Malhotra won the Firestone Medal, Stanford CS's highest thesis honor, for work in computer vision and robotics -- the exact technical domain this product requires. The combination of "knows the market firsthand" and "world-class in the underlying technology" is rare at any stage. The prototype-to-pilot speed is the kind of signal I weight heavily: builders who can ship fast and convert demand immediately.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I see both promise and uncertainty. If Kita becomes the document processing layer for emerging market lenders, the integration creates a natural channel for distributing additional products: fraud scoring, credit signals tied to repayment outcomes, eventually full underwriting recommendations. The data flywheel they describe -- linking document-level signals to actual loan repayment -- is the mechanism that could transform a document processing tool into lending intelligence infrastructure. That's the difference between a feature and a platform. But this flywheel is entirely prospective. They need sufficient loan outcome data across markets to create a meaningful advantage, and at two people serving multiple countries, the operational challenge of building this across fragmented regulatory environments is real. The single-miracle test gets a bit murky here: the core miracle is VLM fidelity for messy emerging-market documents, which is partially being delivered exogenously by foundation model labs. But there's a second challenge around distribution across fragmented geographies that introduces multi-miracle risk -- you need both the technology AND market access across multiple regulatory regimes simultaneously.

The bear case I take seriously is commoditization. VLM technology is becoming table stakes. A well-funded emerging market lender could potentially build document processing in-house using off-the-shelf vision models. Kita's defense has to be the lending-specific data flywheel and hyperlocalization, not the underlying VLM architecture. If those compounding advantages don't materialize quickly, this becomes a thin layer on top of commoditized AI -- a smart toaster. The bull case is that this is the Harvey pattern for emerging market lending: build the workflow infrastructure during the capability inflection, accumulate domain-specific data that generic models can't replicate, and become the embedded intelligence layer that lenders can't rip out. If Kita executes on the data flywheel across even three or four markets, the switching costs and localization advantages create a durable position that neither US incumbents nor generic VLM providers would find worth attacking.

I'm at the edge of my conviction threshold. The structural moment is genuine, the founder-market fit is strong, and the "boring infrastructure in a niche market" pattern has been among my most reliable. But the market fragmentation risk, unverified traction claims, and commoditization exposure keep me from full conviction. This is a company I'd want to have a conversation with -- specifically about how they think about the data flywheel timeline and whether they've identified their single miracle clearly enough to avoid spreading thin across too many markets too early.
