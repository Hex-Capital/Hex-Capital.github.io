# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The AI meeting assistant market is a Girardian nightmare. Four well-funded companies -- Otter.ai at $100M ARR, Fireflies.ai valued at over $1 billion, Read.ai with $81M raised, Fathom growing 90x in two years -- are all competing for the same customers, the same meeting platforms, and the same enterprise budgets. And behind them stand the platform owners themselves: Microsoft is building Copilot directly into Teams, Google is embedding Gemini into Meet, and OpenAI's real-time voice capabilities could be integrated into any conferencing system tomorrow. When I see a landscape this crowded, with this many well-capitalized players converging on the same thesis, I know one thing with certainty: there is no secret here. The insight that "AI should participate in meetings rather than just transcribe them" is the next item on every product roadmap in this category. It is a consensus observation dressed in contrarian language.

The founders' framing of "social awareness" -- the ability to understand conversational context, decide when to speak versus stay silent, and distinguish who is being addressed -- is positioned as their differentiating secret. But this is not a truth that most intelligent people reject. It is precisely what every foundation model lab in the world is investing billions to achieve. GPT-4o already demonstrates real-time conversational turn-taking. Gemini Live handles multimodal interaction at sub-second latency. The capability Fern claims as its moat is the stated research agenda of organizations with three orders of magnitude more capital and talent. Building a feature that sits on top of capabilities being commoditized by the infrastructure providers is not a path to monopoly -- it is a race you structurally cannot win.

I want to take the bull case seriously, because the founder-market fit here is genuinely noteworthy. Amit Yadav and Robert Xu built exactly this kind of system at Meta -- real-time, multimodal AI assistants for Ray-Ban smart glasses. Yadav has a PhD from Purdue, 298 citations, and publications at CVPR and NeurIPS. Xu spent four years building low-latency inference and orchestration systems for the same product. These are not dilettantes. They have the technical depth to build what they are describing, and their prior collaboration at Meta provides the kind of documented co-founder relationship I take seriously. If this were a genuinely novel problem space -- if "social awareness" in AI represented a 0-to-1 discontinuity rather than a feature being pursued by every major lab -- these founders would be exactly the team to back. But having the right team for the wrong market structure does not create monopoly profits. The best crew cannot make a ship profitable on a route where a dozen competitors are undercutting each other's fares.

The bull case would require believing that "social awareness" is not just hard but structurally impossible for foundation model providers to commoditize -- that the fine-tuning required for conversational turn-taking in professional contexts constitutes a durable proprietary advantage that compounds over time. If Fern could accumulate millions of hours of meeting interaction data, fine-tune models for domain-specific conversational dynamics (sales calls, board meetings, medical consultations), and build workflow integrations deep enough to create meaningful switching costs, the company could potentially create defensibility through accumulated advantage rather than initial breakthrough. The "starting with sales" wedge is reasonable -- sales teams have high willingness to pay, clear ROI metrics, and intense enough pain around meeting follow-up to tolerate a new tool. If Fern monopolized the sales meeting AI category before expanding concentrically, that would echo the small-market-first strategy I look for. But I see no evidence that this is the founders' definite plan rather than a generic go-to-market assumption, and the sales meeting AI sub-segment is itself becoming crowded.

The structural problem is fatal. This company faces a triple threat that no amount of execution excellence can overcome: incumbents with large installed bases who can add real-time features (Otter.ai's 25 million users make feature additions trivially distributable), platform owners who control bot access and can degrade third-party participants (Zoom, Teams, and Meet all have incentives to build native AI rather than enable competitors), and foundation model providers whose core roadmaps include exactly the capabilities Fern is building. A startup can survive one of these threats through superior execution. It cannot survive all three simultaneously. The market will not produce monopoly returns -- it will produce the same margin-destroying competition I have seen in every crowded software category. Competition is not just for losers; it is the structural condition that guarantees losing.
