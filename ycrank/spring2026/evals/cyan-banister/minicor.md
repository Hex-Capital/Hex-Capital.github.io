# Minicor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

There's something underneath Minicor that caught my attention before I even got to the founders. Legacy software vendors — Epic, CDK Global, Cerner — are deliberately restricting third-party API access to their systems. Not because they can't build APIs. Because lock-in is the business model. Healthcare providers are trapped. AI companies that could genuinely improve patient outcomes can't get their technology into hospitals because the EHR vendor won't open the door. That's a toll booth on innovation, and the people paying the hidden tax are patients who never see the better care they could have received and clinicians drowning in manual workflows. When I see an opaque, extractive structure like that — gatekeepers profiting from information asymmetry — something in me pays attention. It's the same structural pattern I saw with Affirm attacking credit card opacity or Carta making cap tables legible. Minicor is saying: we'll route around the gatekeeper entirely, through the UI, because they've sealed every other entrance.

When I ask "tell me their story," though, I get a story that's competent rather than inevitable. Faizaan and Saheed met in a UX class at the University of Ottawa, built a non-profit together during the pandemic, and came to this problem through their engineering careers — Faizaan at Phoenix connecting systems, Saheed at Ford and Morgan Stanley swimming in legacy enterprise infrastructure. Saheed's journey from Lagos to Ottawa to Wall Street to Ford shows real resourcefulness. The non-profit background tells me they care about building things that matter, not just things that pay. But this is "I saw this problem at my job and knew I could build something better" — which is legitimate domain expertise, and I respect it. It's not the same as a founder who can't sleep because this problem haunted them before they ever wrote a line of code. I don't sense biographical inevitability here. I sense smart engineers who identified a real gap. That distinction matters in my framework because it tells me something about what happens when the company hits its near-death moment — and every company does.

The positioning is genuinely clever, and I want to give credit for that. They're not selling RPA to enterprises, which would put them directly in UiPath's crosshairs. They're selling legacy system access as infrastructure to AI companies — a different customer, a different sales motion, a different competitive dynamic. The MCP interface, the API-first approach, the managed VMs — this is plumbing for the AI-into-legacy-industries wave. And the compliance work is real: SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA at pre-seed is not a checkbox exercise, it's months of work that signals these founders understand the actual barriers to enterprise adoption. Twenty-five thousand patients a day running through their architecture is a meaningful production signal for a company this young.

But here's where my conviction stalls. When I run the ten-year question — where does this technology end up in a decade? — I keep bumping into a ceiling. Anthropic released Computer Use in public beta in late 2024. The core capability that Minicor is building on top of is available to anyone with an API key. UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent. Automat, a YC peer, has raised $19 million. The moat Minicor is building is real — compliance, managed infrastructure, recovery patterns across specific legacy applications — but it's an operational moat, not a structural one. It doesn't compound the way Flock Safety's camera network compounds or the way Flexport's logistics graph compounds. A well-funded competitor with the same Anthropic API access, the same willingness to pursue SOC 2 and HIPAA, and a larger sales team could reach parity in a year. The deterministic-code-with-AI-for-recovery architecture is smart engineering, but it's an approach, not a proprietary asset.

If I steel-man the bull case: legacy enterprise integration is one of those problems that looks simple from the outside and is brutally hard in practice. Every EHR has different UI quirks, update cycles, and edge cases. If Minicor accumulates proprietary recovery patterns across dozens of legacy applications — a database of how these specific systems break and how to heal them — that data could become genuinely defensible. And the timing may be narrowly perfect: AI companies are flooding into healthcare and automotive right now, they all hit the same legacy wall, and Minicor is already there with production scale and compliance certifications. If this team can execute through the commoditization wave and emerge as the trusted infrastructure layer, the market pull could be enormous. The invisible-system disruption is real — vendor lock-in in healthcare is one of the most entrenched gatekeeping structures in American industry.

I land on this as a company I'd watch closely but wouldn't write a check into today. The invisible system being disrupted is one of the strongest I've seen in this batch — vendor API lock-in is genuinely extractive and blocking real innovation. But I need more from the founders' biographical story to believe they'll endure the valley that's coming when incumbents converge and commoditization pressure intensifies. And I need to see evidence that the operational moat is compounding into something structural before I can call this an infrastructure bet rather than a well-executed wrapper. Smart founders, real problem, legitimate production traction — but not enough of the signals that make me reach for my checkbook at this stage.
