# Seeing Systems -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Seeing Systems is that Matthew and Alexander Le Maitre are brothers. Two brothers from Guernsey -- this tiny island in the English Channel, population sixty-some thousand -- who decided to build military drone systems together. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder conflict will kill even a perfectly timed product, I started probing how co-founders fight and whether they've survived real disagreements. Brothers have a different substrate. They've been arguing since childhood. They know each other's patterns, their triggers, the way they recover. I've said before that I have a preference for family-run businesses, and here's exactly why: Matthew and Alexander chose each other not because of a co-founder matching event or a shared MBA program, but because they grew up together and decided the other person was the one they wanted in the trenches with. The skill split is clean too -- Matthew handles the software and AI side with his Cambridge robotics research and Jane Street rigor, while Alexander is self-taught in embedded electronics and PCB design, having already built EOD training hardware for military users. That complementarity built on a lifetime of trust is rare and valuable.

But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- tell me their story, not the business -- I struggle to find the biographical thread that explains why *these* founders and *this* problem. Alexander's self-taught hardware path resonates with me personally; I know what it means to teach yourself technical skills without the credentialing system's permission. And his prior EOD work shows genuine familiarity with military operators and their needs, which matters enormously. But Matthew's trajectory -- Cambridge, Jane Street -- is an elite pipeline, not an outsider journey. I don't see evidence of the kind of personal hardship or lived experience that typically forges the obsessive commitment I look for. Why defense drones? Why not stay at Jane Street? There may be a powerful answer, but it's not visible to me, and that biographical opacity makes it harder for me to develop the conviction I need to write a check from my own money.

The competitive landscape gives me real pause, and it's the dimension a generic analyst might underweight. Defense tech broadly has become exactly the kind of consensus category I try to avoid. VC funding in the sector jumped to $49 billion in 2025 -- a near-doubling from the prior year. Everyone and their cousin is now building defense drones. Anduril sits at $30.5 billion, Shield AI at $5.6 billion, Stark just raised $100 million and landed a 2.4 billion euro German contract. When I backed Anduril through Founders Fund, Palmer Luckey's vision for defense autonomy was genuinely unpopular -- people thought he was a controversial figure building toys for the military. That anti-consensus quality is what made it interesting. Today, defense drones are front-page news. The weirdness has been priced out of the category. Now, within that space, "two self-funded brothers from a Channel Island building modular expendable drones" is still non-obvious -- they're not the Stanford-pedigreed, well-connected defense founders that VCs are chasing. And Stark's failure in UK military trials in October 2025 creates a genuine opening. But I'm honest with myself: the sector tailwinds that make this investable are the same tailwinds that make it consensus.

Where this company genuinely impresses me is in navigating hard spaces. They already have the UK Royal Marine Commandos, multiple other UK military units, and four NATO forces as customers or partners -- at pre-seed, with two people and $500,000. That's not a pitch deck claim; they've documented operations with names (Operation Kull, Operation Osprey) and are shipping prototypes to Ukraine for iterative feedback. Navigating defense procurement across multiple NATO jurisdictions, managing ITAR-adjacent export controls, embedding Forward Deployed Engineers with military units -- this is the kind of regulatory and institutional complexity that deters most early-stage founders and that I've learned to see as a competitive moat. My IronPort years taught me that complexity others avoid is often where the real defensibility lives. The fiber-optic guided variant addressing electronic warfare jamming is a technically differentiated approach to a real battlefield problem. This isn't vaporware; they're building real hardware for real operators in real conflict environments.

The honest bull case is compelling enough that I want to name it explicitly: if the Le Maitre brothers can convert their existing military relationships into actual procurement contracts, their modularity architecture could become a platform standard for NATO's expendable drone programs. Stark failed UK trials. The large defense primes are structurally incentivized to build expensive, long-lifecycle platforms -- the cheap, modular, expendable paradigm cannibalizes their business models. Two brothers with deep military rapport, complementary skills, and a fiber-optic jamming solution could capture a wedge of NATO's rapidly expanding drone budgets that the incumbents can't or won't serve. If I were meeting them in person and Matthew told me a story about a personal experience that anchored his commitment to this problem -- something that made it clear he'd never go back to Jane Street -- I'd lean in hard. But from what I can see, this is a competent team in a real market with real traction that lacks the biographical imperative and anti-consensus weirdness that define my best investments. The category has gotten too popular for me to feel the contrarian energy I need, and the economic access dimension -- building systems that create opportunity for underserved populations -- simply isn't present. Military drones serve national security, which matters, but it doesn't map to the Uber-for-drivers, Affirm-for-credit-access pattern that runs through my portfolio.
