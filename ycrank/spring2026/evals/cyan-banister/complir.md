# Complir -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Gustav Bang, Marc Kaa Brejner, and Tine Køhnel have built the cleanest three-seat governance structure I have seen out of Copenhagen this batch -- CEO, CTO, CPO, no role overlap -- and pointed it at the most consensus-shaped wedge possible: "Vanta for physical products." That pairing is Complir's signature. The execution layer is competent and the framing is borrowed, which is exactly the profile that makes me wince before slide three, because I am never playing the game I seem to be playing and Complir is openly playing the game it appears to be playing.

The "Vanta for physical products" tag is the load-bearing problem. The consensus YC AI-agent darling I pass on opens with a TAM slide and a four-logo team page; Complir opens with EU-Startups naming it one of the "10 most promising Danish startups to watch in 2026," press in ArcticStartup, TechFundingNews, Vestbee, Øresund Startups, and The SaaS News all in a single December 2025 window, and a €1.7M round at a €6.8M post led by Vendep with Plug and Play tagging along. That is a fund-of-funds-approved seed, not a misfit's round -- the opposite of the Crusoe pattern where credible VCs had already passed on aesthetic grounds before I wrote the check. Magically weird means the deck makes a sophisticated person frown; Complir's deck makes them nod.

Flock Safety I sourced from a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café, because the product was sitting on a pole outside the building I could photograph; Niantic I played for three years before I sat outside John Hanke's door. Complir's tactile artifact exists -- a normal human can walk into a Flying Tiger Copenhagen store and pick up a label, a warning sticker, a declaration of conformity that Complir's agents auto-generated across 44 markets -- but the artifact is downstream of the buyer, not the buyer's experience itself. The 109,886 SKUs "live monitored" is a dashboard number, not a product I install. That is closer to the developer-tools abstraction floor than the Flock pole, and the dossier offers no founder anecdote where Bang, Brejner, or Køhnel physically walked a Flying Tiger warehouse pre-product.

Founder-market fit is where I want to lean in and cannot quite. Gustav Bang ran Denmark for Legora, which is legal AI -- adjacent to regulatory document generation, but adjacent is not lived. Brejner built large-scale systems for Danish authorities, which means he can ship to a regulator, not that he ever sat inside a retailer's compliance team at 11pm trying to translate a CPSC warning into Finnish. The company was "born out of a concrete problem at Flying Tiger Copenhagen," but the dossier does not place any of the three founders inside Flying Tiger as employees; the more honest read is that Flying Tiger was a design partner Bang found, not a job he survived. For this score to flip a tier, I would need to learn that one of the three founders personally owned the compliance function at a high-SKU European retailer before 2024 -- a Palmer Luckey-shipped-Oculus-first equivalent. Without it, the founder-lived-experience axis sits below the midpoint by default and stays there.

GPSR enforcement on 13 December 2024 is the kind of regulatory catalyst that compounds 18 months out -- "Responsible Person" liability under 2023/988 is exactly the unsexy-regulated lane that produced my Vigil Labs check in August 2025 and Mindbloom before it. Complir is in the right country at the right post-GPSR moment with a former Coop Group CEO and a GS1 board member on the cap table, which is real European retail-distribution muscle. The capital-intensity story is thinner -- this is software, not a hardware moat -- but regulated-output liability across 40+ markets is the closest a SaaS company can get to my unsexy filter without touching atoms. Kraen Oestergaard Nielsen as an angel is the kind of physical-world introduction signal I respect; I just wish he was the founder.

Anduril (2017) is the cleanest single-CEO comparison on the governance axis, and Complir actually clears it on titles: three co-founders, three distinct functional surfaces, no 50/50 trap visible in the dossier. The dossier says nothing about equity split, vesting, or tiebreaker protocol, which is the HQ Trivia (2017) silence pattern I learned the hard way -- Yusupov and Kroll looked clean on paper too -- but with three founders and asymmetric domain depth (engineering vs. design vs. commercial), the natural tiebreaker is the CEO title Bang already holds. I score this above default because the structural geometry is right; I do not score it at ceiling because the dossier never names the document.

My 36-month forecast: Complir reaches €4-9M ARR by mid-2029 on the European retailer wedge, gets acquired by Assent or a PLM incumbent like Trace One in the €40-90M range, and does not return a venture-scale outcome to my fund. The mechanism is that the "Vanta for X" shape compresses the upside ceiling -- the category goes to the first credible player with European retail distribution, which Complir might be, but Assent has $350M in the bank and can ship an AI document-generation module without rebuilding the supply-chain relationships it spent a decade building. Complir's 45% MoM growth is the early curve of a real category, not a defensible one.

The bear case I just made is what I believe, so let me steel-man the bull. Vanta itself was "Vanta for SOC2" and became a $2.5B company because the regulation arrived, the buyers were forced, and the first credible workflow won; GPSR is structurally analogous, the buyer is a CFO under enforcement pressure, and Complir already has Matas, COOP, Flying Tiger Copenhagen, Konges Sløjd, and Stronger as named logos before YC even started. Former Coop CEO Kraen Oestergaard Nielsen on the cap table is the European retail mafia, and the polymath thesis (DeepMind exception) might apply to a CTO who built systems for Danish authorities now applying agents to regulator-mapped output generation. The dismantle: Vanta won a brand-new category from zero against no incumbents; Complir enters with Assent ($350M) and Source Intelligence ($17.5M) already holding the manufacturer relationship and Reglyr advertising the same AI-first multi-domain pitch in the same blog post Complir is cited in. First in a new category is Vanta; fourth in an existing one is an acquisition. The single verifiable condition that flips my pass: Gustav Bang publishes that he personally ran compliance ops inside a high-SKU European retailer before founding Complir -- the autobiographical contact with the problem that turns this from a competent execution team into the founder I actually back.
