# Remy AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Remy AI is the competitive landscape, and it tells a story about how this idea was found. Nimble has $221M. Covariant has $222M. Dexterity sits at a $1.65B valuation. Physical Intelligence has raised over a billion dollars. Pickle Robot has $113M and UPS is buying 400 of their units. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight. This is a problem that hundreds of millions of dollars of venture capital have already identified. When I ask "how did the founders find this problem?" and the answer is that one of them was a BCG consultant advising Fortune 500 logistics companies on warehouse automation, that tells me this idea was assembled from market analysis, not discovered through lived experience. Oscar Brisset didn't run a warehouse and rage at the absence of automation. He saw the gap from the outside, through consulting decks and client meetings. That's a fundamentally different motivation than Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive.

There is, however, a genuinely interesting insight buried in the approach. The brownfield angle -- slotting robots into existing workstations without requiring warehouse redesign -- targets the 80% of facilities that have zero automation. The incumbents sell million-dollar systems with month-long integrations, and their business models depend on those fat deals. A low-cost retrofit that deploys in days targets facilities that incumbents are structurally reluctant to serve because the per-deal revenue is too small. That's the right shape for disruption. It's similar to how Stripe went after individual developers while payment incumbents sold to enterprises. The problem is that Stripe could scale through code. Remy has to scale through physical robots, which means manufacturing, safety certification, field maintenance, and all the capital intensity that entails. The schlep is real, but it's the kind of schlep that requires money, not just determination, and their competitors have orders of magnitude more of it.

Ben Kaye is the strongest signal here. A CVPR 2025 publication on 3D shape and pose reconstruction from Oxford's Visual Geometry Group, working under Andrea Vedaldi, is not filler on a resume. That's publishing at the top venue in precisely the domain that warehouse manipulation requires. His GitHub shows robotics projects including quadcopter control. He has a patent-pending medical device. This is a genuine hacker-builder in the relevant technical domain. If I were evaluating only on the CTO's technical depth, I'd be more excited. But the company's core technical claim -- a proprietary training pipeline that learns from less data than standard VLA fine-tunes -- is unverified and faces a commoditization wave. Alibaba released an open-source VLA. Physical Intelligence published their general-purpose model. The window during which data-efficient fine-tuning is a moat may be measured in months, not years.

The bull case would require believing three things simultaneously. First, that the brownfield market segment is large enough and distinct enough that the $1B+ funded competitors genuinely won't pursue it -- that Nimble and Covariant will stay upmarket the way big payment processors stayed away from individual developers. Second, that Remy's hardware form factor for brownfield retrofits creates durable switching costs even if the AI model layer commoditizes. Third, that a two-person team with $500K can get to production deployment and prove unit economics before running out of money in a category where hardware iteration cycles are long and expensive. Each of these is plausible individually. All three together starts requiring multiple miracles. And I've written about how dangerous that is -- each additional dependency multiplies the risk.

The absence of any traction signal compounds the concern. No users, no revenue, no waitlist, no LOIs, no customer logos. The website returned only a title when researchers tried to access it. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I look for any evidence that someone outside the founding team wants this to exist. A warehouse operator who said "yes, I'd try that" would be something. Total silence means we're evaluating the idea in a vacuum, and the idea itself, while containing a smart insight about brownfield deployment, was found through consulting rather than organic necessity. Oscar knows the market from PowerPoint; he doesn't know it from the loading dock at 4 AM.

I respect what these founders are attempting. The technical depth is real, the brownfield insight is sharp, and the team composition -- ML researcher plus industry-knowledgeable operator -- is well matched. But I keep coming back to the origin. The most compelling warehouse robotics company I could imagine would be founded by someone who spent years managing a mid-size 3PL, who personally dealt with 100% labor churn, who tried to buy automation and couldn't justify the cost, and who then recruited a technical co-founder to build what they wished existed. That company would pass my organic test. Remy AI passes the technical competence test but not the organic test, and in a space this competitive, the motivation gap matters because the years ahead will be brutally hard.
