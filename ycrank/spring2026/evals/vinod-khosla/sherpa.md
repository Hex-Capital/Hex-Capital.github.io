# Sherpa -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most revealing line in this entire dossier is the commoditization risk assessment: "LLM-powered variant generation is technically reproducible. Any team with access to foundation models and a JavaScript injection layer could build a comparable product." That is not a risk factor — that is the company description. Sherpa is taking existing large language models, generating marketing copy variants, injecting them into web pages via JavaScript, and measuring which one converts better. There is no proprietary science here. There is no novel technical architecture. There is a workflow automation built on commodity infrastructure. I have seen this pattern hundreds of times across every vertical you can name — AI for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for real estate agents — and now AI for conversion rate optimization. The pitch structure is identical every time: large TAM, manual workflow today, we automate 40% of it. My response is always the same: a 40% efficiency gain in a business process nobody outside that industry thinks about is not a bet worth making.

Let me start where I always start. If Sherpa fully succeeds — if it becomes the dominant platform for automated conversion rate optimization — what happens to the world? Marketing teams at SaaS companies get slightly better at turning website visitors into customers. Headline copy improves. Button colors change. Conversion rates tick up 15-20%. That is a useful product. It is not a consequential outcome. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No expensive necessity becomes free. No new market is created. The CRO market grows from $10 billion to $24 billion, and Sherpa captures a piece of that growth. This is redistribution of existing marketing spend — one of my explicit filters for companies I pass on. The consequence of success here is a moderately profitable SaaS business, which is fine for investors who calculate IRR on spreadsheets. I don't use spreadsheets.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The shift from manual A/B testing to autonomous experimentation is real, and the timing is defensible — LLMs crossed a quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes automated copy generation viable for the first time. There is a genuine business model conflict for Optimizely and VWO, whose revenue partly depends on the complexity of manual testing workflows. If Sherpa moves fast enough, the data flywheel from accumulated test results across customers could create a meaningful advantage — knowing which variant patterns work across industries and page types before a new customer even starts testing. The founders have relevant experience from Lindy, an AI agent platform, which means they understand the agent workflow paradigm. Norbu Sonam shipped Microsoft Copilot's image generation to general availability, which is real engineering at scale. If I believed the CRO category could expand from an optimization tool into something that fundamentally restructures how businesses interact with customers online — if the endgame is not "better A/B testing" but "AI that autonomously runs your entire growth engine" — the consequence magnitude would look different. But the company as described is not aiming for that. It is aiming to automate conversion testing.

The founder profile does not activate my pattern matching. Both are early in their careers — Kinnan graduated in 2024, Sonam recently from RPI. Neither has a prior exit. Neither displays the kind of irreverent outsider energy I look for — the Peter Beck building rocket engines in a New Zealand garage, the Mach Industries dropout competing with Lockheed Martin. These are competent young operators who left a well-regarded AI company to build a SaaS product in a known market. That is a perfectly rational career decision but it does not signal the improbable ambition that justifies binary bets. The only named customer is Lindy — their former employer. That is a network favor, not market validation. I need to see evidence that strangers are paying for this product before I believe in the value proposition.

The fundamental problem is that this company is designed to produce 3-5x returns. It could become a $500 million ARR business — maybe even a billion — and that outcome would be a success by any conventional measure. But I am not a conventional investor. I want to lose my money or make 50 times my money. A better A/B testing tool, even one powered by AI agents, does not have the return profile that justifies the opportunity cost of my attention. Some problems are too important not to work on. Conversion rate optimization is not one of them.
