# Stage -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that hits me. CodeRabbit has $88M in funding and $15M in ARR. Greptile raised $29M. Graphite was acquired for over $290M. Haystack -- a YC company from just two batches ago -- is attacking the exact same problem with a different visual metaphor. And now two guys from a quant trading firm want to build a standalone code review platform that competes with GitHub's native diff UI, arguably one of the most entrenched developer workflows in existence. I wrote once that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors," and I still believe that. But there's a difference between competitors killing you and trying to wedge into a space where multiple well-funded companies have already staked claims on adjacent territory, and the incumbent platform has little reason to make room for you. The question isn't whether competitors will outrun Stage -- it's whether the gap between GitHub's "good enough" review flow and Stage's structured chapters is wide enough that engineering teams will actually switch.

The founders are clearly smart. Stanford CS, both of them. Five Rings is genuinely selective -- it's the kind of place that filters hard for raw intellectual horsepower. Dean's MS in AI and his experience building an in-house coding agent at Five Rings is the strongest signal in this entire dossier. That's "live in the future and build what seems interesting" -- he was doing AI-assisted development inside a quant firm, watching the review bottleneck form in real time, and he noticed a problem rather than going looking for a startup idea. Both were varsity athletes at Stanford, which is a decent proxy for the kind of discipline I look for. And the co-founder relationship has the right foundation: same school, same employer, direct professional overlap. But I keep looking for the thing that tells me these founders are animals -- the cockroach signal, the thing that makes you say "they just won't die." When the Airbnb founders were selling novelty cereal boxes to fund their company, that told me something that no Stanford degree ever could. I see no equivalent signal here. No scrappy traction, no evidence of doing things that don't scale, no prototype with ten obsessive users, no waitlist, nothing. Their GitHub profiles are modest. There's nothing that separates them, behaviorally, from hundreds of other credentialed Stanford CS graduates who walk into a YC interview each batch.

The idea itself sits in an uncomfortable middle zone. It's not a bad idea that looks good -- the kind that kills you slowly because everyone validates it but nobody desperately needs it. But it's also not a good idea that looks bad -- the kind I've learned to pay attention to after missing too many of those early on. "Structured chapters for code diffs" sounds sensible, even clever. Everyone who reviews PRs would nod and say "yes, that would be nice." But "nice" is dangerous at this stage. I wrote that you want to dig a well -- narrow and deep -- not a broad shallow hole. Is there a segment of developers for whom the current diff experience is genuinely broken, not just annoying? The AI-generated code angle provides a plausible "why now": if most of the code in a PR was written by an AI, the reviewer has no mental model of the changes, and a flat diff is nearly useless. That's a real insight. But I'm not seeing evidence that Stage has found the users for whom this is a burning problem and built something they can't live without.

Here's the bull case, and I want to give it its due. The AI code generation wave is not slowing down. When the majority of code shipping to production is AI-authored, the review step becomes the single most critical point of human judgment in the entire development workflow. GitHub's diff UI was designed for a world where humans wrote code and understood it before submitting it for review. That world is ending. Stage could be perfectly positioned at this inflection point -- the way Stripe was positioned at the moment when every company suddenly needed to accept payments online. Dean's AI background means he could build chapter structuring that actually understands code semantics, not just file boundaries. Graphite's exit cleared the field. If Stage nails the UX and gets adopted by a handful of teams at fast-moving AI-native companies, the "chapters" metaphor could spread virally because it's inherently collaborative -- one person using Stage on a PR makes the entire team see the value. That's a real path to something meaningful.

But I keep returning to what I don't see. No traction. No distribution signals. No evidence of anyone using this product or even waiting to use it. And the defensibility picture is bleak -- "structured chapters" is a UX concept, not a technical moat. It's the kind of thing a product designer at GitHub could spec in a week, even if building it into their platform would take longer. Haystack is already one batch ahead pursuing the same fundamental insight with a different metaphor. The name "Stage" is going to be an SEO nightmare -- try searching for it and you'll drown in noise from unrelated products. These aren't fatal problems individually, but together they paint a picture of a company that has a reasonable thesis and capable founders but hasn't yet demonstrated anything that would make me reach for my checkbook. I've written that "good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." These seem like good people. But at this stage, I need something more than credentials and a plausible thesis. I need to see the determination, the resourcefulness, the evidence that these founders will make this company take off rather than waiting for it to happen.
