# 6thSense -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

6thSense is a frontier-hardware-adjacent data infrastructure play -- eight synchronized modalities, tactile gloves, calibrated capture rigs -- staffed by four founders whose biographies I cannot verify in a single public search. That's the signature: the SpaceX-shaped opportunity without a Musk-shaped human attached to it. James Baek, Ronak Agarwal, Alex Hyungwoo Noh, Matt Wulff -- four names on a YC page, and not one confirmable LinkedIn, not one GitHub I can point to, not one prior robotics paper or tactile-sensing patent. I am being asked to underwrite the most founder-dependent stage of a company's life with no founder to underwrite.

Palmer Luckey at **Anduril** (2017) is the high rung of the lived-experience ladder -- he shipped Oculus from a parents'-garage VR rig before he ever touched defense, and the trajectory was the whole bet. The shared mechanism is that the founder had *built the adjacent thing before* and could not be replaced by a quarter of a polymath generalist's time. 6thSense's dossier on this axis is the inverse: the YC page lists four founders, and the diligence trail returns "identity match not confirmed" for three of them and "no public data found" for the CEO. Maybe these are quietly extraordinary outsiders -- the **HandUp** (~2014) exception lives in my book precisely for that case -- but the dossier offers zero biographical hook, no homelessness-mirroring origin, no oilfield-roughneck context, no academic robotics trail. The absence is itself the signal.

The "consensus YC AI-agent darling" from my own pass example is the low rung of the magically-weird ladder, and 6thSense sits one notch above it -- not because the company is weird, but because Human Archive already exists. **Human Archive** raised $8.2M in May 2026 from Wing, NVP, YC itself, and angels from OpenAI/Nvidia/Google for what the dossier describes as the same tactile + egocentric data thesis, with >1,000 headsets already deployed. **Physical Intelligence** is reportedly raising $1B at an $11B valuation to build the foundation model. **AGIBOT** is open-sourcing the G2 multi-modal dataset for free. This is not Crusoe burning flares in North Dakota while three VCs pass on aesthetic grounds -- this is the most-chased physical-AI sub-category of the moment, with a direct YC-backed competitor that closed an institutional round thirteen months ago. The thesis is to find them before consensus, and consensus is already wearing the t-shirt.

James Baek carries the "Co-Founder & CEO" title on the YC page, which mechanically clears the bar that **HQ Trivia** (2017) failed -- Yusupov and Kroll never resolved who broke a tie, and the company died of governance before it died of TikTok. The 6thSense version is partial credit: a CEO is named, but the cap table is a 4-way founder split with no documented operating agreement, no vesting protocol, no tiebreaker clause surfaced. The 18-month compounding question is whether Baek can fire a co-founder when the inevitable disagreement arrives about whether to sell datasets, build foundation models, or pivot into hardware -- and at four founders with no clear equity gradient, the answer historically is "no, the company stalls."

6thSense passes the unsexy-frontier filter more cleanly than any other dimension on the card. Tactile sensor calibration, eight-modality synchronization, hardware capture rigs sold to robotics labs -- this is not an AI-app SaaS wrapper. The category sits adjacent to **Crusoe** (~2018) in the sense that the moat, if there is one, compounds in a physical-world domain that pure-software founders cannot casually enter. What would have to be true to flip this dimension into a top-quartile score is a defensible hardware artifact -- a proprietary sensor, an IP filing, an exclusive supply agreement with a glove manufacturer. The dossier offers none of that; it offers "integrated multimodal capture stack" as a phrase. The category is right; the specific moat is unargued.

6thSense's product is tangible in principle and inaccessible in practice -- closer to **Niantic** at the Ingress-developer-kit stage than to **Flock Safety** cameras you can photograph in a parking lot. I can't install the capture stack at the Four Seasons. I can't watch a friend strap on the glove at a dinner. The buyer is "robot learning teams," and the artifact lives inside their labs, which means my entire diligence on whether the product works depends on customer testimony I do not have -- the dossier reports zero named customers, zero LOIs, zero hours-of-data-collected. The physical-verifiability dimension is the one where my method most explicitly breaks down on B2B infrastructure, and 6thSense does not give me the workaround.

36-month forecast: 6thSense gets acquihired by Physical Intelligence or Genesis AI in the $5-25M range for the calibration IP and four engineers, or shuts down after Human Archive's $8.2M war chest consolidates the dataset-services niche. The mechanism is the dimensional signature itself -- a capital-intensive frontier category attracts the right kind of customer (vertically integrated labs), and that same customer profile is the one that builds rather than buys, leaving 6thSense's four-founder unverified team in a knife fight with a better-capitalized YC-backed peer for a buyer set that may not exist.

The bull case that stings: the founders are quietly excellent outsiders whose absence from LinkedIn is the **HandUp** pattern -- immigrants, dropouts, builders who don't perform online, mirrors of my own Tucson trajectory. Cyan-2014 would have written the HandUp check on exactly that profile. The dismantling evidence is that HandUp's founder told me her story; the 6thSense dossier surfaces no story at all, just four names and a product page. The verifiable condition that flips this to invest: a 30-minute call where Baek walks me through which specific contact-rich manipulation task he personally tried to teach a robot, failed at, and built this stack to solve -- a lived moment, not a market memo. Until that call happens, I pass.
