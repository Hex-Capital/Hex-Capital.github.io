# Kuli -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

CreatorIQ has $80.8 million in funding and $70.7 million in annual revenue. GRIN raised $145 million at a $910 million valuation. Upfluence generates $35 million in revenue. Aspire has $26.4 million in funding. Favikon has ten thousand users. Kuli enters a market with five funded competitors and calls its approach differentiated because it analyzes video content instead of metadata. I have a phrase for markets with five funded competitors pursuing overlapping theses: competitive. And competition is the one structural condition from which no amount of execution can rescue you. The dossier itself contains the competitive landscape table — the very artifact I identify as a red flag, because a company that needs to explain how it differs from five similar players has already conceded that it occupies shared territory rather than monopolized ground.

Kuli's claimed secret — that influencer marketing platforms should analyze actual video content rather than surface-level metrics like follower counts — fails the most basic test: would a knowledgeable person disagree with it? No one disputes that understanding what creators actually say and show is more valuable than counting their followers. This is not a hidden truth; it is the obvious next step that every platform in the space will take as multimodal AI APIs from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic become commodity infrastructure. The dossier itself flags this: "Large AI model providers offer multimodal video understanding APIs that could lower the barrier for new entrants or enable incumbents to add video analysis features." When the dossier's own risk section articulates why your moat is temporary, you do not have a moat. Kuli's position is structurally identical to every company I describe in my standard pass framework: an "AI-powered" version of an existing software category where the enabling technology is available to everyone and the contrarian insight is consensus dressed in new vocabulary.

Michael Hodara's background — EPFL robotics MSc, AI lead at Aircall managing $20 million in annual marketing spend — represents genuine domain experience, and I acknowledge this is not a trivial credential. He lived the problem he is solving. Jonathan Hassan's six years of M&A advisory at KPMG provides enterprise sales capability. The bull case would note that Disney, Supercell, and Havas as named users at pre-seed is impressive traction, that Kuli's "proprietary content index" built "without storing any video" could compound into a data advantage, and that the 37% CAGR market growth provides tailwind. A reasonable advocate would argue that Kuli is doing to CreatorIQ what Palantir did to legacy analytics — building a fundamentally different architecture that incumbents cannot replicate without rearchitecting their entire stack. But the Palantir analogy collapses on inspection. Palantir's moat was not its software alone — it was operational embedding within CIA and military intelligence workflows where switching costs approached infinity and the customer was the U.S. government. Kuli's customers are marketing departments at consumer brands, where switching costs are low, procurement cycles are quarterly, and the buyer's loyalty extends precisely as far as the next budget review. Kuli lacks every structural element that made Palantir's position unassailable.

Kuli's power-law potential is constrained by the market's fundamental structure. Influencer marketing software is not a winner-take-all category — it is fragmented across segments (enterprise, mid-market, SMB, DTC, agency), geographies, and platform specializations. CreatorIQ serves 300 enterprise customers and generates $70.7 million in revenue without dominating the category. GRIN raised $145 million and occupies a distinct DTC niche. Kuli becoming a $10 billion company would require consolidating a market that has resisted consolidation for a decade, against incumbents with established relationships and the resources to add video analysis as a feature. The Wish parallel is instructive: Wish entered a large, growing market (e-commerce) with a differentiated wedge (extreme discount, mobile-first) and still collapsed because no structural monopoly existed. Kuli's wedge — multimodal video analysis — is more technically interesting than Wish's price arbitrage, but equally susceptible to incumbent absorption. The dossier notes that CreatorIQ already "uses AI for database curation," meaning the feature migration has already begun.

Kuli's distribution represents its strongest relative dimension. Landing Disney and Havas as enterprise users with a three-person team demonstrates that Hodara and Hassan can sell. The Microsoft GenAI Studio selection and VivaTech 2025 showcase on Microsoft's stand provide credibility infrastructure uncommon at pre-seed. Hassan's KPMG enterprise advisory background maps well to the founder-led sales motion Kuli requires. But distribution excellence without monopoly structure produces a "nice business," not a power-law outcome — and I do not invest in nice businesses. Kuli has zero physical-world technology moat: it is a pure software layer consuming commodity APIs, analyzing content on platforms it does not control (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube), subject to API access changes at any time. The TikTok regulatory uncertainty in the U.S. alone represents a platform dependency risk that no amount of product quality can mitigate.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: if Kuli demonstrated that its accumulated content index creates a genuine data flywheel — where each additional analysis makes the platform measurably more accurate in ways that a competitor starting from scratch cannot replicate within twelve months — and if that flywheel produced measurable switching costs visible in retention data, I would reconsider. But the dossier provides no evidence this flywheel exists today, and the commoditization of multimodal AI works against it compounding faster than competitors can build their own. Kuli is a competent team in a real market building a feature that will become table stakes. The monopoly secret is absent — Kuli's insight is correct but consensus, and correctness without monopoly structure is not investable. The power-law magnitude is structurally capped by market fragmentation. These two dimensions, scoring well below their ranges, place Kuli firmly in pass territory.
