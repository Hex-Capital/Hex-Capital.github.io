# RefortifAI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing about DRM: it has been broken in every domain it has ever been applied to. Music, movies, games, ebooks — the history of software-only digital rights management is a graveyard of systems that were "unbreakable" until someone with a debugger and a weekend proved otherwise. RefortifAI is proposing to do for AI model weights what no one has durably accomplished for any other digital asset: prevent extraction through software obfuscation alone. The "anti-cheat for AI models" framing is honest about the lineage — game anti-cheat is perhaps the most successful DRM-adjacent category, and it's still an endless arms race where Denuvo gets cracked within weeks. That's the terrain these founders are choosing to build on. The question isn't whether they can build it — three people with hardware design, AI engineering, and security research backgrounds probably can build a first version — it's whether they can keep it ahead of adversaries with physical access to the hardware running their runtime. That's not a product problem; it's a physics problem dressed up as an engineering one.

When I ask "tell me their story," I get resumes. Sayan was at KLA doing AI engineering. Atman designed RTL at Texas Instruments and interned at Tenstorrent. Rithik studied security under Somesh Jha at Wisconsin. These are real credentials, and the technical triangle maps well to the problem — you want someone who understands silicon, someone who understands inference, and someone who understands how systems get broken. But that's founder-market fit, not biographical forge. Nobody here was building a startup because their model got stolen and it destroyed something they cared about. Nobody was driven to this because they lived through the consequence of the problem they're solving. They're friends who saw a gap in the market. I've seen that story a hundred times. Sometimes it works, but it's not the story that makes me write first checks into companies where rockets are blowing up on launch pads.

What strikes me most through my own lens is that this company runs exactly backwards from the pattern that drives my deepest conviction. My strongest bets — Affirm, Carta, Ownwell, Uber — take opaque systems that extract value from people who don't understand them and rip the curtain back. RefortifAI does the opposite: it makes model weights harder to see, harder to access, harder to understand. It reinforces the information asymmetry in favor of the already-powerful party — the AI model provider — against their customers and deployers. That's not inherently wrong; IP protection is a legitimate business. But it doesn't activate anything in me. When I was fifteen and navigating systems designed to keep me ignorant, DRM for corporations wouldn't have been the tool I needed. The companies I back tend to put power in the hands of people who had none. This one keeps power where it already sits.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. AI model distribution is becoming real. Companies are fine-tuning and licensing models for on-premise and edge deployment, and the only current protection is legal agreements that are unenforceable across borders and API-only delivery that constrains use cases. Palo Alto Networks paid $500M+ for Protect AI, F5 acquired CalypsoAI — enterprise appetite for AI security is proven and growing. If RefortifAI's drop-in replacement for vLLM and SGLang actually works without meaningful latency overhead, the low-friction GTM could be powerful. And the specific niche — weight-level DRM rather than supply-chain scanning or adversarial attack detection — is genuinely unclaimed by the funded competitors. If what you believe is that AI model licensing becomes a dominant distribution pattern over the next decade, someone will own this chokepoint, and the current team has the right technical DNA to build the first credible product. I can see a version of this story where they become the critical infrastructure layer between every model provider and every on-premise deployment. That's a real outcome.

But "I can see a version" isn't how I invest. AI security is a consensus-hot category right now — VCs are competing for deals in this space, not avoiding it. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "AI model protection." There's nothing magically weird here, nothing that provokes the discomfort I've learned to interpret as signal. The technical risk is real and the arms race dynamic means the moat is always eroding — every security improvement invites a new attack vector, and a three-person team maintaining parity across NVIDIA, AMD, and evolving inference engines is a brutal operational challenge. The founders are capable engineers solving a real problem in a crowded-adjacent market, and I wish them well. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. There's no biographical inevitability, no invisible system being exposed, and no contrarian conviction required to write this check.
