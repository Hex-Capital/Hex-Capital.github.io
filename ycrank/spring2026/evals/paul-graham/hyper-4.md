# Hyper -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hyper is a fast-iterating two-person team building in the most crowded consensus wedge of 2026 — the "memory layer for AI tools" — and that pairing of high learning rate with near-zero idea contrarianism is the analytical signature here. Shalin Shah and Kanyes Thaker pivoted from an iOS voice notetaker on Product Hunt in March 2026 (96 upvotes, rank #18) to a "self-driving company brain" by the May YC page, which tells me they iterate. It also tells me they are searching, not discovering. Hyper is the kind of company where I would want the 10-minute working session to see what these two are actually like, because the dossier alone reads like two strong engineers wandering through a category that everyone is wandering through.

On Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — Hyper's weakest dimension — the ladder runs from Stripe at the top (Collison brothers tackling PCI compliance and bank integration plumbing nobody wanted to touch), through Replit in the middle (online IDEs sounded toy-like for years before anyone took them seriously), down to the consultant B2B-workflow-automation pass at the bottom, where everyone agrees the idea is good and that agreement is the tell. Hyper sits one rung above the bottom. Glean at $200M ARR up from $100M nine months earlier (Fortune, Dec 2025), Dust at $7.3M ARR with $21.5M raised, Notion AI shipping external connectors, plus per-tool native memory shipping inside Cursor and Claude Code and ChatGPT — every serious investor and every incumbent agrees this category is hot. "Shared memory for humans and AI" is exactly the kind of tagline that would appear in a TechCrunch trend piece without modification. The Hyper bet is not that the category is wrong; it is that two engineers from a robot-vacuum company will out-execute Glean's $610M war chest on the same wedge. That is not Black Swan farming. That is fighting in the consensus.

On Founder Learning Rate — Hyper's strongest dimension — the ladder runs from Sam Altman at Loopt (the slope that made me champion him for YC president even when his current work was unremarkable), through Alexis and Steve at Reddit (pivoting cleanly from cell-phone food ordering to link aggregation in days), down to founders who have been working a year on the same flat thing. Hyper sits between Reddit and Altman: a March 2026 iOS voice product to a May 2026 cross-tool team brain is real movement inside a single batch, and the willingness to publicly relaunch under the same name in two months suggests the right kind of stubborn — attached to the goal of building something teams would use, flexible about what that thing is. But I cannot tell yet whether Hyper's pivot was driven by user signal or by the founders chasing a hotter narrative. Reddit's pivot was triggered by me handing them an idea at dinner; I have no equivalent forcing function in Hyper's story.

Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint sits below Replit on the ladder and well above the consultant pass. Shalin Shah led autonomy at Matic Robots and Kanyes Thaker did perception and motion planning there, plus an MLEng stint at Snorkel — these are real builders, not deck-makers, and the speed of the March-to-May relaunch is itself a small resourcefulness signal. But Hyper has no Airbnb-cereal-box equivalent in the dossier: no early revenue, no manual concierge of the product, no story of getting their first ten teams through unscalable hustle. Default-plus, not high.

Organic Problem Discovery is Hyper's second-weakest dimension and pulls hard against Dropbox at the top of that ladder — Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, then built sync. Hyper's founders spent the last several years building sensor-fusion stacks for a home-robotics company. The product they are now building is for "AI-first teams generating context across Notion, Claude Code, Cursor, email, and LinkedIn DMs." That is not their lived workflow at Matic; that is a workflow they observed and decided to serve. The dossier contains no origin frustration, no specific moment, no early users who are people exactly like them. This is the "live in McKinsey, build what the framework suggests" failure mode, except in San Francisco rather than midtown. Earnest Builder Identity lands near the Watsi-to-buzzword ladder's middle-low: the language ("self-driving company brain," "silently learns") is on-trend rather than direct, and there is no Drew-Houston-style obsession visible in the public record.

My 36-month forecast: Hyper does not exist as an independent company at meaningful ARR. Either Hyper pivots once more — most likely into a narrower developer-tool-specific memory layer wrapped tightly around Cursor or Claude Code, where the founders' systems instincts compound — and reaches roughly $300K-$1M ARR before being acqui-hired by a mid-tier AI tooling player, or Hyper gets squeezed flat between Glean's enterprise descent and per-tool native memory and shuts down quietly. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: high learning rate plus consensus market means the team will keep moving, but each move lands them adjacent to a better-capitalized incumbent rather than into open water.

The strongest case against my forecast is Bindwell — two teenagers I personally backed for AI pesticide discovery after advising them to pivot from selling tools to owning molecules. Shah and Thaker have a structurally similar story: technical depth in adjacent perception/sensor-fusion work, a product still searching for its real wedge, willingness to relaunch publicly, and a small team that can move fast. If their next pivot is into something I would describe as a schlep — say, the regulatory and security plumbing of getting passive memory into a SOC-2-bound enterprise, where Glean's enterprise-sales motion is too heavy and per-tool memory is too thin — Hyper could become a real company. What dismantles this for me is that Bindwell's pivot was into a domain that actively repelled other founders; Hyper's most recent pivot was deeper into the most attractive narrative in AI infrastructure, which is the opposite vector. A verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Hyper announces in the next six months a wedge that knowledgeable people react to with "huh, that sounds painful and narrow" rather than "oh, like Glean but lighter" — pair that with a handful of teams using the crappy v1 obsessively, and I'd want the working session.
