# Oximy -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Oximy is the commoditization surface. The core sensor is a fork of mitmproxy -- an open-source network proxy. CrowdStrike Falcon already monitors network connections from enterprise endpoints. Zscaler already proxies traffic. For either of them, adding an AI tool detection layer -- flagging domains associated with ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, and the rest -- is a feature sprint, not a new product. Oximy's differentiation lives in the taxonomy (3,500+ AI tools), the governance workflow, and the spend attribution logic. Those are product execution advantages, not structural moats. When I apply the "software-driven vs. software-aware" test, I'm looking for whether removing the technology collapses the business. Here, the technology layer is thin enough that a well-resourced incumbent could replicate the core detection capability in weeks. The value is in the product design and go-to-market, which is valid at pre-seed -- but it means the entire bet is on execution speed against companies with agents already deployed on millions of endpoints.

The structural moment is real but consensus. Every CISO I talk to knows that employees are adopting AI tools outside IT procurement. The EU AI Act implementation timeline is creating compliance pressure. CFOs are demanding AI spend attribution. None of this is hidden or non-obvious -- it's the dominant narrative in enterprise security right now. When I look at my best investments, they were in markets that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors. PagerDuty's operations alerting seemed boring. Coinbase's crypto exchange seemed speculative. This market triggers the opposite reaction: universal agreement that AI governance is important and growing. The TAM projections ($2.5B to $68.2B by 2035) are exactly the kind of analyst-driven extrapolation that I find unreliable precisely because it maps a consensus trajectory. Nudge Security has already raised $30M with 200 customers and 3x ARR growth targeting the same buyer persona. Grip has $66M. The fact that multiple well-funded companies are attacking overlapping problems confirms the market is real -- but it also confirms there's no information asymmetry to exploit.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. SaaS management incumbents (Productiv, Zylo, Torii) built their discovery mechanisms around SSO logs and email receipts -- architectures that structurally miss unsanctioned AI tool usage through browser and desktop applications. Oximy's bet that you need a system-level agent to capture the full picture of shadow AI could be the non-obvious insight within an otherwise obvious market. If that bet is correct, there's a window where purpose-built AI governance tools outperform repurposed SaaS management platforms. The "processing millions of requests daily for enterprises in financial services and healthcare" claim, if accurate, suggests real enterprise deployment at pre-seed -- which would be a strong signal. And the multi-product architecture (Oversight, Pulse, Spend) plus Oximy Lens as a $49/month lead-gen wedge shows the kind of distribution thinking I look for. If Oximy can reach 100 enterprise deployments before CrowdStrike or Zscaler add this as a feature, the switching costs of ripping out a governance workflow embedded in compliance processes could create a real moat.

But the plan requires too many things to go right simultaneously. They need to overcome enterprise agent deployment friction (security teams resist adding another endpoint agent, especially one proxying network traffic). They need to build governance workflows sticky enough to create switching costs before Nudge Security's 200-customer base expands into the same features. They need to outrun the inevitable feature additions from CrowdStrike, Zscaler, and Netskope -- companies that already have the endpoint presence Oximy has to build from scratch. And they need to do all of this with a solo founder and a team of five. That's at least three sequential obstacles, each with meaningfully low probability. My single-miracle framework exists precisely to filter for this pattern -- compounding low-probability events kill startups regardless of market quality.

Naman Ambavi's execution velocity is the strongest signal here. Building Easocare to $223K revenue, shipping a COVID tracker to 10M+ users in four months, achieving SOC 2 Type I certification at pre-seed -- these are real accomplishments for a founder in his mid-twenties. The prior investor's specific citation of "execution ability and product velocity" as the investment rationale is the kind of builder-evaluating-builder signal I weight heavily. The Google Code-In and CodeJam results confirm technical depth. But enterprise sales at scale -- managing procurement cycles at financial services and healthcare organizations while simultaneously building product and hiring across engineering and GTM -- is a fundamentally different challenge from shipping consumer-facing products quickly. Solo-founder execution risk compounds with the already-challenging competitive dynamics.

This is a competent team attacking a real market at the right time. But the market is consensus rather than non-obvious, the technical moat is thin, the competitive landscape is crowded with better-funded players, and the path to success requires overcoming multiple simultaneous obstacles. If I'm deploying capital in enterprise AI infrastructure, I'm looking for companies where the structural insight creates an asymmetric information advantage -- where most investors dismiss the market or misjudge the timing. Here, everybody sees it.
