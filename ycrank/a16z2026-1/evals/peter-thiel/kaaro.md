# Kaaro -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Kaaro's most striking feature is not the idea but the distribution signal: a two-person pre-seed company is already working with CPKC — Canada's largest railroad, a $70B+ enterprise created by the 2023 CP-KCS merger — and claims late-stage conversations with CN. Freight rail is among the most insular industries in North America. New vendors do not walk in the front door. Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran are described as "second-generation railroaders," and this family provenance is doing real structural work — it is the reason CPKC takes a call from two twenty-year-olds with no product revenue. In my experience, distribution advantages rooted in relational trust within closed institutional ecosystems are among the hardest to replicate. Palantir's early access to the CIA was not a product advantage; it was a relationship advantage that became a product advantage once the software was operationally embedded. Kaaro's railroad heritage functions similarly: access first, embedding second, switching costs third.

The monopoly secret, however, is thinner than the distribution channel deserves. Kaaro's implicit thesis is that railroad operational workflows — the phone calls, emails, and tribal knowledge surrounding billing, car tracking, and crew coordination — constitute an automation opportunity that incumbent TMS vendors like Wabtec's RailConnect cannot address because their business models depend on the manual data entry those systems are designed to support. This is a real structural observation about incumbent cannibalization risk, and it correctly identifies the gap between what Wabtec automates (deterministic operations and billing) and what remains manual (the communication layer around those systems). But it is not a secret that makes knowledgeable people uncomfortable. Every railroad executive already knows their operational workflows are manual and inefficient. The insight that "LLMs can now handle the unstructured communication that prior automation couldn't" is the consensus thesis of a thousand AI-agent startups applied to a specific vertical. A genuine secret would be something the railroad industry itself actively disputes — Kaaro's thesis is something the industry has been waiting for someone to build.

The Toma pedigree strengthens the founder conviction dimension more than the idea dimension. Both founders were founding engineers at Toma (YC W24, a16z-backed), where they built AI voice agents for auto dealerships and scaled from roughly twenty to hundreds of customers. The playbook transfer is direct: Toma automated phone and communication workflows in a manual, relationship-driven industry; Kaaro applies the identical pattern to freight rail. This is not lean-startup iteration — it is a specific plan drawn from demonstrated execution in an analogous domain. The shared prehistory passes my co-founder test decisively: Surisetti and Venkateshwaran have known each other over a decade, share VEX Robotics backgrounds, and survived the intensity of being Toma's first two engineers together. The irreversible commitment — both dropped out of Canadian universities to build this — signals genuine conviction. What I lack is a bold, specific multi-year vision for what Kaaro becomes at scale. "AI coworkers for rail" is a product description, not a definite plan for the future of railroad operations.

The power-law question is where Kaaro structurally fails my framework. North America has seven Class I railroads and approximately 600 short-lines. Even at full penetration — every railroad paying meaningful SaaS fees for AI workflow agents — the addressable market for this specific product category is bounded. The railway AI market is $2.55 billion growing at 18.4% CAGR, but Kaaro's serviceable segment is a fraction of that. Optym, with twenty years of Class I relationships and train scheduling optimization, generates approximately $25 million in annual revenue. Kaaro could become a very successful vertical SaaS company — perhaps a $200-500 million outcome — but the market structure does not support the $10 billion trajectory that power-law investing demands. This is not a criticism of the business; it is a statement about category ceiling. A monopoly in a small market is still small. The Palantir comparison illuminates the gap: Palantir started with intelligence agencies but its data integration platform was horizontally extensible to every large enterprise on earth. Kaaro's railroad-specific AI agents do not obviously generalize beyond rail without becoming a different company competing in the crowded horizontal AI-agent market.

The strongest bull case requires believing that Kaaro's railroad beachhead is the entry point for monopolizing operational AI across all freight logistics — that railroad workflows are the small market Kaaro monopolizes before expanding concentrically into trucking, maritime, and intermodal. If the AI agents accumulate proprietary operational data that creates compounding intelligence advantages, and if the CPKC engagement converts to a reference customer that pulls CN and the five American Class I railroads in sequence, the market ceiling lifts substantially. The Toma precedent — scaling from a first customer to hundreds in auto dealerships — suggests the founders understand how to expand within a vertical. And the incumbent displacement dynamic is real: Wabtec's green-screen-to-web modernization cycle creates a window where Kaaro's AI-first approach could leapfrog the legacy architecture entirely, the way Palantir's modern data platform made the intelligence community's legacy systems obsolete. For this bull case to work, Kaaro must convert CPKC from "working with" to a signed, revenue-generating contract within twelve months, and the product must demonstrate measurable operational value (revenue recovered from idle cars, hours saved in billing) that creates internal champions at the railroad.

Kaaro lands in the neutral zone — genuinely impressive distribution in a closed industry, a real if non-contrarian insight about workflow automation, founders with directly transferable execution history from Toma, but a market structure that caps the return magnitude well below power-law territory. The two dimensions most decisive for my assessment: Distribution scores near the top of its range because the CPKC and CN engagement at pre-seed, combined with second-generation railroader access, represents a distribution advantage most vertical AI startups would spend years failing to build. Power Law Return Magnitude scores near the bottom because seven Class I railroads and 600 short-lines do not constitute the kind of expanding market where a single winner captures value that compounds without ceiling. I would watch Kaaro with interest — particularly the CPKC conversion — but my own capital follows the power law, and this market's structure does not accommodate it.
