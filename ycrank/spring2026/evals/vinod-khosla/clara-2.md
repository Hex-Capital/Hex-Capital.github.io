# Clara -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

George Favvas built a telehealth company to $100M in revenue and profitability — and walked away from it. That's the signal I start with. Not because operational success impresses me on its own, but because of what it implies: he saw from inside Circle Medical that the human-clinician bottleneck is the fundamental constraint, not a scaling problem. You don't abandon a profitable business to build a slightly better version of it. You abandon it because you believe the architecture is wrong. The question is whether Clara represents a genuine discontinuity or just the same playbook with an LLM bolted on.

The consequence magnitude here is real. If an AI can handle 80-90% of primary care reasoning — reviewing medical history, synthesizing lab data, drafting treatment plans — and a human clinician spends two minutes reviewing instead of fifteen minutes producing, you don't just make healthcare more efficient. You make primary care accessible to the 100 million Americans who struggle to see a doctor at all. The U.S. is heading toward an 86,000 physician shortage by 2036. That's not a market opportunity — it's a crisis. When I backed Curai Health, it was on exactly this thesis: AI primary care at marginal cost, healthcare as a basic need liberated from its cost structure. Clara's model, if it works at scale, collapses the cost of a primary care visit from $200+ to potentially $10-20. That's not a 2x improvement — it's a structural transformation of how a basic human need gets delivered. Carbon Health just went bankrupt trying to do primary care with physical clinics and human labor. The asset-light, AI-first model isn't just better — the alternative is dying.

But here's where I get uncomfortable. The technical approach is not non-linear. Clara isn't building a novel AI architecture or a proprietary clinical reasoning engine. It's applying existing LLM capabilities to medical records and wrapping that in clinical operations — licensing, billing, prescribing authority. That's an operational moat, not a technology moat. K Health has $441M and 413 employees doing something structurally similar. Hippocratic AI has $404M and a $3.5B valuation building healthcare AI agents. When I look at Clara, I see an execution race against well-capitalized competitors, not a technology breakthrough that creates an unfair advantage. I prefer technology risk to market risk. This is market and execution risk. The 150,000-facility records integration is operationally impressive but not proprietary in a way that prevents Google Health or Amazon from replicating it with their own healthcare data partnerships.

The bull case I have to take seriously: Favvas has the one thing the AI startups don't — he's already built the clinical operations machine once. Licensing across fifty states, insurance billing, regulatory compliance, clinical team management — this is the graveyard where pure-tech healthcare companies go to die. Hippocratic AI sells to health systems; they don't operate a clinical practice. K Health still runs human-first with AI triage. Clara is attempting the inversion — AI-first, human-verified — and the team that can execute it is the team that already ran the human-first version and understands exactly where the bottleneck sits. The Circle Medical trio (Favvas, Swift, Ahmed) isn't a team that needs to learn healthcare operations from scratch. Swift is a practicing nurse practitioner who knows what clinical workflows actually look like. Ahmed designed for 40 million users at Careem. They're starting with the operational DNA that takes most health-tech companies five years and $50M to develop. If timing is right — and LLM capabilities crossing the clinical reasoning threshold in 2024-2025 suggests it might be — this team could move faster than both the incumbents who can't cannibalize their fee-for-service models and the AI startups who can't navigate clinical operations.

What holds me back is this: everyone agrees AI will transform primary care. When every expert in the field agrees something will work, I get less interested — the return profile of consensus bets doesn't justify my framework. I want to bet on things the world thinks are impossible or foolish. Nobody thinks AI-first primary care is impossible. They think it's inevitable. The fight is over who captures the value, not whether the value exists. That's a market share battle, and I don't win market share battles — I win by backing discontinuous technology that creates entirely new categories. Clara isn't creating a new category; it's racing to be the best execution of a category everyone sees coming. Favvas is a domain expert applying known techniques to a market he understands deeply. That's the profile of a good operator building a solid company. It's not the profile of a naive outsider who doesn't know what can't be done. I respect the founder. I respect the consequence. But I'd rather lose my money on something that could return 50x than make a reasonable 5x on a competitive execution race.
