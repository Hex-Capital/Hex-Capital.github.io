# Cohesion -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Devon Krapcho spent five-plus years as a fundamental equity analyst at Long Path Partners covering software companies, and when I hear that, my first instinct is to ask: what happened to you that made this problem find you? The dossier gives me a clean professional trajectory — Notre Dame BBA, DBO Partners investment banking, then Long Path. Cohesion's co-founders Matt Munns (ML engineering at T. Rowe Price) and Matthew McBrien (AWS Lambda infrastructure) bring genuine technical depth to the problem. But the founding story reads as "I was at my hedge fund desk and saw a workflow gap," which is career observation, not biographical inevitability. None of the three co-founders share overlapping employers or universities, and there's no public record of how they came together. Cohesion was born from professional frustration, not from a life experience that left these founders unable to walk away from the problem. I've seen this pattern many times — credentialed, competent people who spotted a market opportunity. That's different from someone who was forged for it.

Cohesion operates in what might be the hottest consensus space in venture right now: AI tooling for finance. YC explicitly listed "AI-Native Hedge Funds" in their Spring 2026 Requests for Startups. AlphaSense has raised $650M at a $4B valuation and runs north of $500M ARR. Hebbia pulled in $130M at a $700M valuation with $13M in profitable revenue. Over 35% of new hedge fund launches in 2025 branded themselves as AI-enhanced. Cohesion's own YC batchmate Standard Signal is building in the same category. When I look at a space where every sophisticated investor is eagerly competing for deals, where the incumbents are well-capitalized and actively acquiring (AlphaSense bought Tegus for $930M), and where the category itself is an explicit YC request — that's the opposite of "magically weird." Nobody shifts in their chair hearing this pitch. Cohesion is swimming in warm, crowded water.

Cohesion's target customers are hedge fund analysts at funds managing a combined $10Bn-plus in AUM. These are among the most well-resourced, information-advantaged professionals on the planet. They already have Bloomberg terminals, AlphaSense subscriptions, and teams of junior analysts. When I think about the invisible systems I've spent my career attacking — the taxi medallion cartel that Uber exposed, the credit card opacity that Affirm made transparent, the property tax labyrinth that Ownwell navigates for ordinary homeowners — Cohesion sits on the wrong side of that equation. Cohesion isn't making a hidden, extractive structure visible to people who are being harmed by it. Cohesion is making already-powerful people marginally more efficient at extracting alpha. I don't doubt the workflow pain is real. But the structural thesis that drives my deepest convictions — that technology should shift power from gatekeepers to the people locked out — points away from this company, not toward it.

The commoditization question is what concerns me most about Cohesion's defensibility. The dossier explicitly flags that the core technology — LLM agents monitoring public data sources — has low technical barriers, and points to the open-source ai-hedge-fund repository on GitHub as evidence that basic investment research agents can be assembled from commodity APIs. Cohesion's potential moats are switching costs from analyst-tuned coverage universes and proprietary signal-to-source mappings, but neither exists meaningfully at pre-seed scale. Compare this to Affirm, where every new merchant integration deepened network effects, or Flock Safety, where every camera installed expanded a surveillance mesh that no competitor could replicate without physically deploying hardware. Cohesion's "moat" today is domain expertise and UX polish — real advantages for winning early customers, but not structural barriers that compound. AlphaSense, with its $500M-plus ARR and deep enterprise relationships, could add autonomous agent features without cannibalizing its core search product. Cohesion is building on a layer that the incumbents can absorb and the open-source community can approximate.

The strongest bull case for Cohesion is the traction: live with 10-plus funds managing combined $10Bn-plus AUM at pre-seed stage is genuinely impressive execution, and the earnings-season trial timing shows sophisticated GTM instinct. Devon Krapcho is the target user persona — he lived this workflow for five years. The team composition covers buy-side domain (Krapcho), institutional ML (Munns at T. Rowe Price, a $1.5T AUM manager), and infrastructure scale (McBrien building AWS Lambda). If Cohesion can build proprietary signal-ranking models trained on how top analysts actually use non-traditional data — data that accumulates only through deployment at real funds — the commodity LLM layer becomes a substrate for something defensible. That's the path. But it requires Cohesion to survive long enough against AlphaSense's expansion and against the dozens of teams building identical agent architectures in the hottest AI subcategory in venture. The condition that would flip me: evidence that Cohesion has built or is building a proprietary data asset — not public podcast transcripts and Twitter feeds processed through commodity LLMs, but fund-specific behavioral data about which signals actually predicted returns — combined with a founder story that reveals why Devon Krapcho would keep building this through Cohesion's inevitable near-death moment even when a senior analyst seat at a hedge fund pays seven figures.

Cohesion is a competent team in a real market solving a real problem, and the early fund adoption validates that the product works. But this is not a Long Journey bet. The biographical forge is professional, not personal. The space is consensus, not contrarian. The system being served is already transparent and well-resourced, not hidden and extractive. The moat is thin against both incumbents and open-source replication. Cohesion scores lowest on contrarian conviction — a 7/25 driven by YC's own explicit request for this category, $780M-plus in competitive funding, and over 35% of new funds self-identifying as AI-native — and on invisible system disruption, where serving $10Bn-AUM hedge funds inverts the structural thesis that produced my strongest returns.
