# Asendia AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

This is the pitch I get three times a week. Four specialized AI agents for the recruiting funnel, 50+ ATS integrations, claims about hours saved and fill rates improved. Every time I hear it, I think about the same thing: Workday just paid north of $250 million to acquire Paradox — a company doing essentially this — and folded it into their platform. That's not a signal that the market is open. That's a signal that the incumbents have decided this is their feature to build. When the biggest HCM platform in the world acquires your closest comp and absorbs it into their stack, the correct read is not "gap in the market." The correct read is "the market has decided where this value accrues, and it's not to a standalone layer."

The mispricing test here is simple and it goes the wrong direction. AI + recruiting is not a category where the market is wrong — it's a category where the market is enthusiastically, consensually, aggressively right. Half of every YC batch for the last two years has some version of "AI agents for [workflow]." Recruiting is one of the most popular target workflows. There are dozens of funded competitors already. hireEZ has $76 million raised and $42 million in revenue doing sourcing alone. When every sophisticated investor in the room is excited about the same category, the upside is already in the price. I'm not finding mispriced asymmetry here — I'm finding a crowded auction.

The primitive test is equally clear: Asendia is not creating a new behavior. AI-assisted recruiting exists. Chatbot screening exists. Automated sourcing exists. Video interviewing exists. What they're doing is bundling four existing capabilities into one platform. That's a product strategy, not a primitive. Nobody is going to say "we got Asendia'd" the way people say "Venmo me." The verb already exists — it's called recruiting. They're automating steps within it. That's incremental, and incremental in a category where every incumbent is simultaneously automating the same steps is a race to commoditization, not a race to category creation.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: staffing agencies are a specific, underserved vertical with gnarly operational workflows that generalist platforms won't bother to optimize for. Paradox's absorption into Workday actually helps because Workday serves enterprises, not staffing agencies — so the agency-specific niche is genuinely open. The founders have real enterprise credibility from Google and Microsoft, and the CTO built a prior recruiting AI product, so they understand the domain. If they can embed deeply enough into agency workflows through those 50+ ATS integrations, they build switching costs that matter. I'll grant all of that — and it still describes a modest, execution-dependent SaaS business competing against well-funded players in a non-contrarian category. It's the kind of business that might work and might even be profitable, but it's not mispriced, it's not creating a new primitive, and it doesn't survive the kill test when Bullhorn decides to ship their own AI agents.

The founder profile is competent but not calibrated in the way I look for. Rihab has cloud architecture chops from Google and Microsoft. Badis has six years at Infineon and a TUM engineering degree. They co-founded a podcast together in 2019, so the relationship is real — not synthetic. All good. But the idea isn't insane. It's extremely sensible. A sane person pitching a sane idea is just... fine. There's no ratio to get excited about. And the pivot from a B2C job seeker tool to a B2B staffing platform — with zero visible B2B traction post-pivot — introduces execution risk without the compensating upside of a contrarian bet. The compliance claims (SOC 2, HIPAA) from a three-person team also raise an eyebrow; either those certifications aren't fully in place, or significant resources were spent on compliance instead of product-market fit, and at pre-seed that's a concerning allocation.

This is a pure software play with no physical moats, no protocol-level lock-in, and no network effects that compound. The AI capabilities are API calls to foundation models that improve every quarter for everyone simultaneously. When I say "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore," this is exactly the topology I mean. Pass.
