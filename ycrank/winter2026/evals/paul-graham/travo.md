# Travo -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did the founders find this problem? With Travo, the answer is conspicuously absent. Four Stanford CS undergrads are building "the most accurate and comprehensive real estate data set" for RV parks and campgrounds. None of them have documented experience in commercial real estate. None have worked at a brokerage, managed a property, or invested in an RV park. The dossier is explicit about this gap, and it's the kind of gap that matters. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he kept forgetting his USB drive. When the Collisons built Stripe, they'd been fighting payment APIs for years. When I look at Travo, I see a team that identified a hole in CoStar's coverage through market analysis and decided to fill it. That's not the worst origin for a startup idea, but it's not the best one either. It's the kind of idea you get when you sit down and try to think of startup ideas -- which is precisely the method I've warned against.

The schlep here is real, and that's worth noting. Building accurate, comprehensive data for niche real estate asset classes is genuinely tedious work. You're dealing with fragmented county records, inconsistent zoning classifications, properties that don't appear in any standard database. Most smart people would unconsciously avoid this problem, which is exactly what schlep blindness predicts. But here's what troubles me: the founders are approaching this schlep from the outside. They noticed the schlep exists and decided it was a business opportunity. That's different from living inside the schlep and being unable to tolerate it anymore. Stripe worked because the Collisons were programmers who personally experienced the pain of integrating payments -- the schlep was something they'd been suffering through, not something they identified as a market gap. When outsiders identify a schlep, they often underestimate what it actually takes to navigate it. Real estate data is particularly treacherous because accuracy matters enormously -- people make million-dollar investment decisions based on comps -- and the founders have no intuition for what "accurate" even means in this domain.

The strongest bull case is that sometimes brilliant technical outsiders can automate what insiders do manually, and that AI-driven data collection really could make it economically viable to cover niche asset classes that CoStar ignores. The innovator's dilemma framing against CoStar is legitimate: a $3.2 billion company has no incentive to build comprehensive RV park data. If Travo nails this niche and expands to adjacent property types -- self-storage, mobile home parks, marinas -- it could become a real platform. Clarence Chen has a prior exit from an education company, which shows he can build and sell something. Ashwin Sriram led backend engineering at an edtech startup with millions of users and worked at Roblox, which is real engineering at scale. The team is technically capable. These are not people who need to hire engineers -- they can build. If they happened to discover this problem organically, through a family member who invests in RV parks or a summer job that exposed them to the data gap, this would be a much more interesting bet. But that story isn't in the dossier, and its absence is itself informative.

What concerns me most is the sales challenge. Selling an institutional-grade data product to real estate PE firms and experienced brokers when you're twenty years old and have never worked in real estate is exceptionally hard. This isn't a developer tool where the product speaks for itself and you can do Collison installations. Real estate professionals are relationship-driven, skeptical of new data sources, and accustomed to paying CoStar's $30K-$100K because they trust it. Earning that trust requires either a dataset so obviously superior that it sells itself, or credibility that comes from industry experience. Travo has neither yet. The niche focus on RV parks also creates a ceiling problem -- the number of institutional buyers who specifically need RV park data is small enough that even dominant market share might not support a venture-scale outcome without rapid expansion into categories where the incumbents are much stronger.

I'm passing on this one. The team is technically strong, and the market gap is real, but the idea has the fingerprints of a brainstorming session rather than lived experience. Smart people identifying an underserved niche through competitive analysis is a common pattern, and it usually produces companies that can raise money but struggle through the years of domain-specific execution required to build something people actually depend on. I'd want to see a founder who can tell me about the specific moment they realized RV park data was broken -- not from a market map, but from trying to use it.
