# Palus Finance -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Palus is what it isn't: it's not a technology company. It's a distribution wrapper around someone else's investment strategy. The core product is the MBSF ETF, managed by Regan Capital — the founders didn't design the floating-rate agency MBS approach, they didn't build the custodial infrastructure (Alpaca does that), and the connectivity layer is Plaid. What the founders built is a Plaid integration that routes startup cash into an existing ETF. That's an afternoon of engineering, not difficult technology compounding over time. The entire value chain is assembled from off-the-shelf components. When I back companies like Alchemy or Stack Overflow, the technology itself is the moat — it gets harder to replicate with each passing month. Here, a well-resourced competitor replicates the full stack in a quarter.

The specific knowledge question is where I look first, and the answer is mixed. Sam Lushtak built investment research platforms for asset managers with $3T+ in AUM — he's been adjacent to institutional fixed-income. That's relevant exposure. But "built tools for people who manage bonds" is not the same as "managed a bond portfolio" or "ran a treasury desk." It's consultant-depth knowledge, not operator-depth knowledge. The pivot from consumer savings to startup treasury is authentic — they felt the pain of parking YC funding in a 3.5% MMF and asked "why can't we do better?" That's a real itch. But recognizing an arbitrage that exists in plain sight on any Bloomberg terminal isn't the kind of non-transferable insight I mean by specific knowledge. Any smart generalist who spends two weeks studying institutional treasury practices would land on the same observation: startups overpay for liquidity they don't need. The MBS strategy is textbook.

The leverage architecture is my deepest concern. This is an AUM-fee business — 0.25% annually on assets under management. Revenue scales linearly with assets gathered. There are no network effects, no protocol dynamics, no marketplace flywheel. To reach $10M in annual revenue, Palus needs $4 billion in AUM. The entire U.S. startup treasury market is perhaps tens of billions — they'd need to capture a dominant share of a niche market just to become a mid-sized business. Compare this to AngelList, which created a new form of capital leverage where one engineer's work enabled thousands of funding transactions, or Replit, where code written once serves millions. Palus is asking "how do we gather more assets?" at every growth inflection. That's the structure of an asset management firm, not a technology company. Asset management firms can be excellent businesses, but they don't produce the 1000x asymmetric outcomes I optimize for.

The bull case is worth engaging honestly. The innovator's dilemma angle is the strongest thing in this dossier. Mercury earns revenue from MMF fee-sharing arrangements, and switching to a lower-margin bond strategy would cannibalize their existing business model. Brex and Rho face the same structural conflict. This gives Palus genuine breathing room — the incumbents are disincentivized from copying the exact move. If Palus uses the treasury wedge to accumulate data on startup cash drawdown patterns and builds that into an intelligent duration-matching system that automatically allocates across liquidity tiers, the data advantage could compound. The company that best understands when startups actually need their money — not when they think they need it — could build a genuinely defensible position. And in a declining-rate environment, the 1-1.5% spread over MMFs becomes proportionally more valuable, making the value proposition sharpen as rates fall. If the founders demonstrate the long-term vision to evolve from a treasury yield optimizer into the operating system for startup cash management, this could become something. But that's a different company than the one described in this dossier.

The timing isn't contrarian enough. The startup treasury space already has four funded competitors — Mercury, Arc, Rho, and Meow — all doing variations of "better yield on idle startup cash." Palus's differentiation is the underlying instrument (MBS vs. MMF), not a fundamentally different vision of what treasury management should be. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, people thought I was wrong. When I invested in Uber at $4M, ride-sharing was illegal. Here, nobody would argue that startups shouldn't earn more on their cash — the disagreement is only about whether the 1-2 day liquidity trade-off is acceptable. That's a product feature debate, not a contrarian bet on a new category.

Two Caltech founders with clean backgrounds, no red flags, and the pragmatism to pivot when their original idea didn't fit — I like that. But liking the founders isn't sufficient when the business structure doesn't produce leverage. I'd tell Sam and Michael: you've identified a real inefficiency, and you'll probably build a profitable small business. But the question I keep returning to is whether any amount of execution can turn a 0.25% AUM fee business into a venture-scale outcome without fundamentally reinventing what the company does. I don't see it.
