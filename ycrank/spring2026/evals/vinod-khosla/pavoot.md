# Pavoot -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Pavoot is the company from my pass example, with an ETH accent. Two ETH Zürich computer scientists -- Gohar Tamrazyan and Ana Yoon Faria de Lima -- have built an AI agent that automates event sourcing, in-event capture, and CRM-synced follow-ups for marketing teams. The dimensional signature is unambiguous: the highest dimension is technical capability of the founders (ETH CS, ETH AI Center origin, 20+ scientific olympiad medals between them), and the lowest is consequence magnitude. That pairing describes a competent technical team building a productivity tool. I have seen this shape a hundred times and I almost always pass.

Consequence magnitude is where Pavoot lives or dies in my framework, and Pavoot dies here. The HIGH rung is OpenAI -- I wrote a $50M check and an apology letter to my LPs because if AGI works, civilization reorders. The MID rung is DoorDash -- not a technical breakthrough, but feeding hundreds of millions of people changed food access at planetary scale. The LOW rung is the AI-for-accountants/AI-for-recruiters pitch I get every week, where success means a profitable SaaS company and nobody's life changes. Pavoot sits squarely on the LOW rung. The dossier itself frames the problem as marketing teams that "spend millions on events" and want better pipeline conversion. If Pavoot fully succeeds, B2B marketers close more deals from conferences. That is not a world I am trying to build with my own capital.

Non-linear technical approach is the second nail. Rocket Lab built the Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers -- a step-function cost reduction that did not exist in the world before. That is the HIGH rung. The MID rung is DoorDash, where the technology was unremarkable but the execution mattered. The LOW rung is the LLM-wrapper company whose stack is OpenAI APIs plus a Salesforce SDK. Pavoot sits at the LOW rung: the dossier explicitly notes the "underlying components (LLM-based personalization, CRM sync, photo recognition) are widely available via OpenAI/Anthropic APIs and existing CRM SDKs," and competitors could replicate within 6-12 months. There is no Rutherford engine here. There is a workflow.

Improbable ambition level: Commonwealth Fusion Systems is the HIGH rung -- commercial fusion, the answer is "that can't be done." Rocket Lab is MID -- dedicated small-sat launch, audacious but conceivable. The LOW rung is the AI copilot for a single vertical workflow. Pavoot's stated goal -- "AI that turns events into pipeline" -- provokes no incredulity from anyone in the event-tech category. Vendelux raised $16.4M doing adjacent work; Grip raised $14.5M; Cvent and Bizzabo are entrenched. The world already believes this is buildable. Pavoot lands near the LOW rung.

Founder-as-learning-machine is where Pavoot earns its strongest mark, and even here it is mid at best. The HIGH rung is the 19-year-old MIT dropout at Mach Industries taking on Lockheed without the burden of knowing what couldn't be done. The MID rung is Peter Beck, a self-taught New Zealand engineer who built rocket engines in his garage. The LOW rung is the domain expert with rehearsed answers. Pavoot's founders are technically credible -- ETH CS, ETH AI Center course origin, Gohar a 6-time Swiss chess champion which signals the kind of competitive intensity I respect. The product scope shift from media-hub to pipeline-agent mid-batch could be read as healthy learning velocity, or as drift; the dossier does not give me enough to tell. Neither founder shows enterprise marketing or RevOps leadership, and selling six-figure ACVs to CMOs requires founder-led selling muscle that ETH CS curricula do not teach. I place Pavoot between MID and LOW, closer to MID on raw athleticism. Path to radical affordability -- where Impossible Foods anchors the HIGH rung by making protein cheaper than animal agriculture, and Curai sits at MID with AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost -- offers Pavoot nothing. A demo-led enterprise SaaS sold to marketing ops teams does not collapse the cost of a basic human need; it redistributes a slice of existing event-marketing budget.

My 36-month forecast: Pavoot reaches $1-3M ARR with 10-25 mid-market logos, then gets acqui-hired by HubSpot, Cvent, or Bizzabo for $15-40M as a feature tuck-in once Salesforce Einstein or HubSpot Breeze ships overlapping native event-pipeline automation. The mechanism is straightforward -- the technical components are commoditized, the buyer relationship belongs to the CRM incumbent, and a two-person ETH team without enterprise GTM cannot out-sell Vendelux's three-year head start before the platforms close the window.

The strongest case against my pass invokes the DoorDash exception: I funded an execution-only company with no novel technology because the consequence magnitude was planetary and Tony Xu was a high-velocity immigrant outsider. Could Pavoot be that? No. DoorDash's consequence was hundreds of millions of people getting food access; Pavoot's consequence is marketing teams getting better pipeline attribution. The exception requires planetary consequence to override the technical-novelty miss, and Pavoot does not clear that bar by an order of magnitude. The Oscar exception requires a calcified industry where radical affordability of a basic need justifies regulatory risk; event marketing is not healthcare. The OpenAI exception requires civilizational consequence; this is not that. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Pavoot reaches $500K+ ARR within 12 months with three named Fortune 500 logos AND demonstrates a proprietary attendee-graph data asset that compounds across customers. Without both, this is a pass.
