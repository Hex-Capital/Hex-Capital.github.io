# Travo -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check with any CRE data startup is whether the founders have ever actually been on the other side of a property transaction — underwriting a deal, managing a portfolio, arguing with a county assessor about parcel data. Travo has four Stanford CS undergrads or recent grads, none of whom have documented experience in commercial real estate, RV parks, or property data at all. This matters more than usual here because the product promise is "the most accurate and comprehensive dataset" for niche asset classes. Building institutional-grade property data isn't primarily an AI problem — it's a domain knowledge problem. You need to know which county recorder offices have digitized records and which haven't, which zoning codes matter for RV park entitlements, how campground revenue is actually calculated (seasonal vs. annual sites, utility pass-throughs, ancillary income). PE firms deploying millions of dollars into these assets will identify data quality issues within minutes. This is the kind of market where a smart toaster is still a toaster — bolting AI onto a fundamentally domain-intensive data business doesn't make the domain expertise optional.

The structural moment question is where I spend the most time, and here the "why now" is frustratingly generic. The dossier infers two catalysts: AI scraping got better, and institutional capital is flowing into alternative asset classes. The first applies to every data aggregation startup in existence right now — it's not a thesis, it's a technology wave. The second is more interesting: there is real institutional capital entering RV parks and outdoor hospitality post-pandemic, and these buyers genuinely lack the CoStar-quality data they're accustomed to in mainstream CRE. That's a real gap. But I need to see evidence that this gap is large enough to build a venture-scale business, and no SAM estimate exists for niche real estate data. My rough math: there are maybe a few hundred institutional buyers focused on RV parks and campgrounds. Even at $10K/year per seat, you're looking at single-digit millions in total addressable revenue for the initial wedge. Compare that to PagerDuty, where "boring alerting" served every company running software — the hidden scale was massive. Here, I'm not sure the hidden scale exists.

The bull case I take seriously: this is a classic innovator's dilemma play against CoStar. At $3.2 billion in revenue focused on office, retail, multifamily, and industrial, CoStar has no incentive to invest in comprehensive RV park data coverage. If Travo nails the niche, they expand to self-storage, mobile home parks, marinas, and other alternative asset classes that are collectively a meaningful market — and by the time CoStar notices, Travo has the dataset and customer relationships in these segments. The land-and-expand from niche to mainstream CRE data follows the Stripe pattern: enter through a narrow wedge, build trust, then distribute additional products through the same customer integration. Clarence Chen's prior education company exit shows he can build and ship. Ashwin Sriram's backend experience at scale (edtech with millions of users, Roblox) is real engineering credibility. If the team can recruit a domain expert as a fifth hire or key advisor, and if alternative asset classes are genuinely institutionalizing at scale, the pieces could come together.

But I keep coming back to the miracle count. To win here, Travo needs to: (1) build accurate data without domain expertise, (2) sell to sophisticated institutional buyers who are 20-30 years older and expect industry fluency, (3) expand beyond RV parks before the initial TAM ceiling constrains growth, and (4) maintain data quality at scale against potential competitive responses. That's at least three miracles, and compounding low-probability events is exactly what I avoid. The founding team is technically strong — I don't doubt their ability to build AI pipelines — but the gap between "can scrape and structure data" and "institutional investors trust this for seven-figure deployment decisions" is enormous and requires domain credibility this team hasn't demonstrated.

The technology cycle positioning is the weakest dimension. AI-driven data collection isn't ahead of a capability curve — it's the most consensus application of current LLM technology. There's no equivalent of the Harvey pattern where the team was building before model fidelity reached the required threshold. The tools to do what Travo is doing exist today and are available to every competitor, including the incumbents. Cherre raised $30M in September 2024 for CRE data integration; Built AI raised $6M for AI-native CRE workflows. This isn't a structural vacancy — it's an active market with funded players, and the incumbents have multi-year data moats. I don't see the asymmetric positioning that gets me excited.
