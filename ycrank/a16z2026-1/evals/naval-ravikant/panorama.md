# Panorama -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Panorama's founding team has real credentials in the wrong domains. Jingwei Hao progressed from Senior Engineer to Staff Engineer to Tech Lead Manager at Lyft, led infrastructure migration across 20+ engineering teams at Cash App, and contributed to Envoy Mobile. Jaclyn Lunger earned a PhD in ML for materials science at MIT and was the founding scientist at Lila Sciences, which subsequently raised $350M at a $1.3B+ valuation with Nvidia backing. These are accomplished builders. But the question I always ask is: if these founders disappeared, would Panorama's core insight survive? Hao's specific knowledge is mobile infrastructure and platform engineering. Lunger's specific knowledge is computational materials science. Neither possesses years of obsessive, non-replicable expertise in workplace behavior pattern recognition or workflow automation. They experienced coordination waste at large companies — so did ten thousand other senior engineers at Lyft, Cash App, and Google. Relevant experience is not specific knowledge. Specific knowledge would be: someone who spent five years building internal automation tools, noticed patterns no one else documented, and finally couldn't resist productizing what they'd learned. That founder doesn't appear to be here.

The leverage architecture looks clean on the surface — a "TikTok-like recommendation engine" for workflows is a compelling frame, and software recommendation engines carry zero marginal cost per additional suggestion once built. But Panorama's actual mechanics introduce friction that degrades the leverage. The product requires 1-2 weeks of workplace data observation before generating meaningful recommendations, creating a cold-start dependency that breaks the instant-value expectation of modern SaaS. Each customer requires integrations with their specific tool stack — Slack, Notion, Linear — which means engineering work per ecosystem, not pure permissionless scaling. Compare this to Notion, where a new user gets value in minutes and tells three colleagues the same day. Panorama's delayed time-to-value compresses virality and extends sales cycles, which means the business will likely need salespeople to bridge the trust gap during that observation period. That's labor leverage creeping in where code leverage should dominate.

The incumbent dynamic here is structurally different from Perplexity versus Google, and this distinction matters. Perplexity could attack Google because Google's ad revenue model made its own search team agents of their P&L — they couldn't cannibalize their core business. Microsoft, Google, and Slack face no such constraint with workflow automation. Copilot Studio, AgentSpace, and Slack's own AI features all enhance their existing products rather than cannibalizing them. These platforms already have native access to the workplace data Panorama must access through APIs — Panorama is building on rented land. ServiceNow's $2.85B acquisition of Moveworks signals that enterprise incumbents are actively absorbing AI workflow automation into their stacks. Panorama's differentiation — proactive observation versus manual configuration — is a product design philosophy, not a structural moat. Zapier at $310M revenue and n8n at $40M ARR haven't added observational features yet, but the absence of a structural barrier means they could ship similar functionality in a quarter.

The bull case I have to honestly consider: Hao's experience managing cross-team migrations at Cash App and mobile observability at Lyft gave him a visceral understanding of how engineering teams waste time on repetitive coordination. Lunger's demonstrated ability to build production ML systems at Lila Sciences — a company that went from founding to $1.3B valuation — suggests she can build a genuinely sophisticated recommendation engine, not a GPT wrapper. The team of 4-6 people already has a shipped product, SOC2 Type I certification, CASA Tier 3 security rating, and a Product Hunt #2 finish with 363 upvotes. That's meaningful output from a small team at pre-seed. If Panorama's recommendation quality becomes dramatically better than anything Zapier or Microsoft ships — if the engine genuinely surfaces automations that surprise users — the data flywheel could compound into real switching costs. For this to work, Lunger's ML chops would need to produce recommendation quality so far ahead of what generalist AI teams at incumbents can build that enterprise buyers choose a startup over their existing vendor's native feature. Possible, but it requires Panorama's ML to be a genuine breakthrough, not an incremental application.

The co-founder relationship gives me pause without triggering a hard red flag. No shared employer or university overlap is visible between Hao and Lunger — Hao's background is Lyft/Cash App/Google infrastructure, Lunger's is MIT/Lila Sciences materials science. This isn't a "met at a startup weekend" situation, but it's not the deep pre-existing trust that reduces internal principal-agent friction either. On principal authenticity more broadly, there's no evidence either founder was working on workflow automation before deciding to start Panorama. The Product Hunt launch copy and the "plain English into powerful workflows" positioning read competent but not obsessive — I don't hear a founder who couldn't stop building this. The name collision with Panorama Education (YC S13, 15M+ students) and AWS Panorama is a practical nuisance that compounds the brand-building challenge.

Panorama lands in neutral territory for me. The decisive dimensions are Specific Knowledge Productization — where the founders' impressive but domain-transferred expertise scores near my absent-evidence default of 12/30 — and Permissionless Leverage Architecture, where the cold-start dependency and incumbent platform access undermine what should be Panorama's core scaling advantage. Competent team, real market, but no signal that this couldn't be built by any strong engineering team that identified the same opportunity through market analysis. Innovation without a moat becomes consumer surplus — and I don't yet see the moat.
