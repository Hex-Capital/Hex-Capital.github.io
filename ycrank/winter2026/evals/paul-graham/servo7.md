# Servo7 -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Servo7 is that Standard Bots already exists. $63 million raised, $12 million in ARR, Fortune 500 customers, and fundamentally the same pitch: robot arms that learn from human demonstration instead of requiring months of custom programming. When a pre-seed company enters a space where a well-funded competitor has already proven the concept works and is scaling, the question shifts from "is this a real problem?" to "why will these specific founders win despite arriving later and with 126x less capital?" The problem of painful industrial robot deployment is genuine, but it's the opposite of schlep-blind. Everyone in robotics sees it. Multiple well-funded teams are already grinding through it.

How did Becking and van Leuven find this problem? The dossier doesn't contain the kind of organic discovery story that excites me -- no moment where they were personally stuck trying to deploy a robot in a warehouse and thought "this is insane, there has to be a better way." Instead, the origin appears to be capability-forward: two TU Delft roboticists with backgrounds in military drones and autonomous defense systems looking for an industrial application of their skills. That's not the worst origin -- it's better than brainstorming over coffee -- but it's the difference between Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive every day and an engineer deciding that cloud storage sounds like a good market. The former builds something because they can't not build it. The latter builds something because the market seems attractive. When I see a team whose path to the problem runs through "we're good at robotics, where should we apply it?" rather than "we lived inside this broken workflow for years," my conviction drops.

The strongest case for Servo7 is the founders themselves. These are genuine builder-hackers. Becking has 16 GitHub repos, a Boeing deep reinforcement learning project, and was team captain of Delft Hyperloop -- which means he's led a complex hardware engineering effort under real constraints. Van Leuven built and deployed military drone software, which is about as high-stakes as robotics deployment gets. Their third team member wrote a master's thesis on surgical robotic navigation. This is not a team that will outsource their core technology or struggle with the engineering. They've forked LeRobot and are building on it, which is the right pragmatic choice for a small team -- use the open-source foundation, build proprietary value on top. If I were evaluating purely on technical founder quality, this team would score well. They are clearly animals at building robots.

But technical ability alone doesn't create a moat, and the competitive dynamics here concern me. The AI foundation is literally open-source -- Hugging Face's LeRobot with 28K stars. Anyone can fork it. Servo7's differentiator is supposed to be wheeled humanoids that navigate existing environments, versus the stationary arms that Standard Bots and others build. That's an interesting form factor bet, and if it's the right one, it could matter enormously. But "wheeled humanoid for industrial work" is a dramatically harder engineering challenge than a robot arm, and they're attempting it with $500K. Hardware companies that try to do too much with too little capital don't run out of ideas -- they run out of runway. The path to default alive for a company building custom wheeled humanoid robots on half a million dollars is not one I can see clearly.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The wheeled humanoid form factor would need to be the decisive insight -- that warehouse work fundamentally requires mobile robots, not stationary arms, and that everyone building arms is building the wrong thing. If that's true, then Servo7's military deployment experience (navigating unstructured environments, operating around humans) becomes the exact right background, and their European starting point becomes an advantage in a market with acute labor shortages and strong automation tailwinds. They'd need to prove this with a single warehouse deployment so compelling that the customer refuses to give the robot back. That would be the signal -- not TAM slides, but a warehouse operator whose throughput jumped enough that they called asking for more units before Servo7 even asked for a reorder. I don't see evidence of that yet, and "working with warehouses" without named customers or quantified results is the kind of claim that could mean anything from "we have a paying pilot" to "we had a meeting."

The team is real. The technical chops are real. But the problem is consensus, the competition is well-funded, and the organic founder-problem connection is weak. I'd want to see a deployment that generated genuine pull -- a customer who found them rather than the other way around -- before writing a check with my own money.
