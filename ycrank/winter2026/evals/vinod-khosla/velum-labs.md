# Velum Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see in this dossier is a retreat. Velum Labs started with homomorphic encryption for content-level access control — a genuinely hard, genuinely contrarian technical problem. Their GitHub is full of forks of Microsoft SEAL, GPU-accelerated HE tools, a privacy-preserving inference stack. That is the kind of technology that, if it worked at enterprise scale, could have been structurally disruptive: cryptographic guarantees that no monitoring layer can replicate. Then they walked away from it. The flagship "Firewall" repo has 1 star. Now they're building LLM-powered data quality tooling — using NER and relationship extraction techniques available to every ML engineer on the planet — to compete in a market where Monte Carlo has $236 million and Atlan has $206 million. They reduced their probability of failure. And in doing so, they reduced the consequence of success to a point I find hard to get excited about.

Let me be precise about consequence magnitude. If Velum succeeds perfectly — becomes the dominant "operating system for data quality" — what changes? Enterprise data teams stop writing manual validation rules. Data contracts auto-generate. The data observability market is $3 billion growing to maybe $6 billion. That is a real business. But nobody's life changes. No trillion-dollar industry gets displaced. No physical system transforms. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence was reclaiming half the world's arable land. When I backed OpenAI, the consequence was augmenting human cognition itself. The consequence of better automated data contracts is... data engineers file fewer Jira tickets. The "ontology engine for enterprise AI" framing tries to elevate this — if you're THE semantic layer between all enterprise data and all enterprise AI, that's foundational infrastructure. But three different product framings in the pre-launch stage (homomorphic firewall, data quality OS, ontology engine) tells me this positioning is marketing exploration, not deep conviction about a specific problem the founders believe is too important not to work on.

The bull case deserves engagement. LLMs crossed a capability threshold around 2023 that makes automatic ontology derivation from live data traffic technically feasible for the first time. Enterprise AI adoption is accelerating exponentially. If data quality becomes the binding constraint on enterprise AI — and there's a credible argument it will — then the company that solves "trustworthy data at the semantic level, across any stack, automatically" could become genuinely foundational. The 50+ pre-built enterprise extractors are real integration surface area. The SOC 2 Type II certification shows enterprise seriousness. Alen Rubilar-Muñoz's technical depth — topological neural networks, CalHacks overall winner, 80 GitHub repos including a pinned "ontology" implementation — suggests real capability in the core technical domain. If these two founders converge on the right product and the enterprise AI wave creates urgent demand for exactly this capability, the timing could be excellent. That's the case. But it requires the technology AND the market AND the right product direction AND enterprise sales execution to all work simultaneously with a two-person team and $500K. When I see that many simultaneous low-probability requirements, I ask whether the founder has identified which risk to eliminate first — and the pivot history suggests they haven't.

The technology itself does not disrupt the incumbent approach — it iterates on it. Monte Carlo monitors for anomalies; Velum derives ontologies. That's a product differentiation, not a structural disruption that makes the existing way of doing things economically unviable. The core components — zero-shot NER, LLM relationship extraction, schema mapping — are broadly available. Databricks is building Unity Catalog. Snowflake has Horizon. A well-resourced platform company can add ontology features as a native capability, and when they do, the standalone tool becomes a feature rather than a company. The earlier homomorphic encryption approach had genuine technical moat; the current approach has integration moat, which is real but fragile.

On the team: two quantitative founders — physics/math and CS/math — with strong academic and technical credentials but no visible enterprise sales experience, targeting hospitals, banks, and government agencies. These are the three longest procurement cycles in enterprise software. I spend more of my calendar on recruiting than anything else because I know that the founding team must address the venture's critical risks. The critical risk here is not "can we build the ML pipeline" — Alen clearly can. The critical risk is "can a two-person team of recent graduates close six-figure enterprise contracts in regulated verticals?" The team composition doesn't address the company's most obvious existential risk.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a real market. And that, in my framework, is precisely the problem. I look for founders attempting things the consensus considers impossible. I look for consequences of success that justify tolerating 90% failure probability. What I see here is a team that retreated from the hard bet and landed on a competitive, well-understood market opportunity. The money might not expire — they might build a real business. But the outcome I optimize for is not "a real business." It is a transformation worth the attempt. I don't see that here.
