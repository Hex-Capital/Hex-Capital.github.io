# Ressl AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything you need to know before you examine anything else. Netic has raised $43 million at a $450 million valuation building AI agents for home services. Avoca has raised $48 million doing voice AI for the same customer base. WorkHero raised $5 million targeting back-office automation for HVAC contractors specifically. ServiceTitan, the $8.3 billion public incumbent, has launched its own AI division, Titan Intelligence. And now Ressl AI enters with $750K in total funding, a 4-person team, and the thesis that trades businesses need AI agents for back-office work. This is not a market with a secret hiding in plain sight -- this is a market where the thesis is so consensus that half a dozen well-funded companies are already fighting over it. When I see five startups and an incumbent converging on the same insight in the same sector, I see a Girardian dynamic playing out in real time: companies imitating each other into mutual destruction, each one convinced their particular flavor of AI-for-trades is the differentiated one. The overlay-versus-platform distinction, or the back-office-versus-revenue focus, are tactical variations, not secrets. Nobody in this space possesses a truth that the others would actively reject.

The pivot history deepens my concern. The founders describe their origin story in terms I find structurally revealing: "We started as an AI consulting firm talking to businesses across every industry. After a month, the pattern was obvious." This is the lean startup methodology stated without irony. They iterated across industries until they found the most receptive customer segment, then built for it. I understand why YC finds this appealing -- it demonstrates responsiveness to market signal. But in my framework, a founder who discovers their market through broad customer development is a founder without a definite plan. The Salesforce product -- "Devin for Salesforce" -- was announced as recently as December 2024, and Salesforce-related content appears through mid-2025. The current trades product is months old. When I backed Zuckerberg, he had been building the same thing since his dorm room and refused a billion-dollar acquisition offer to keep building it. When Musk started SpaceX, he didn't survey industries first and conclude that rockets had the most friction. Definite optimists know what they are building before they start.

The strongest bull case deserves honest examination. $440K in revenue with four people is genuine capital efficiency -- if that revenue is attributable to the current trades product and not residual Salesforce consulting. The PE roll-up distribution channel is structurally interesting: a single relationship with a portfolio company aggregator could yield dozens of deployments, which would be an unusually efficient go-to-market for a pre-seed company. And the "agent overlay on existing software" positioning has a real adoption advantage over rip-and-replace platforms like ServiceTitan -- businesses that resist digital transformation might accept an AI assistant that sits on top of QuickBooks and CCCOne without requiring them to change anything. If Ressl could dominate collision repair specifically -- a vertical with standardized software (CCCOne), complex insurance coordination workflows, and enough specificity that generalist AI companies won't bother -- there might be a beachhead strategy here. But the company's own materials describe targeting HVAC, plumbing, electrical, collision repair, and roofing simultaneously. That is not a beachhead. That is a broad assault on a massive market from day one, which is precisely the pattern I have warned against for twenty years.

The technology does not clear my threshold for discontinuity. Ressl is applying general-purpose large language models to vertical business workflows -- reading from one system, reasoning about the task, writing to another. This is competent engineering, and it may produce a useful product, but it is a "1 to n" application of existing AI capabilities, not a "0 to 1" creation. The multi-agent architecture is a design pattern available to any competent AI team, not proprietary technology. As LLM capabilities continue to improve and agentic frameworks become more accessible, the barrier to entry for building exactly this kind of product falls every quarter. A company whose moat depends on integration work with legacy software -- connecting to CCCOne, parsing insurance forms, interfacing with QuickBooks -- is building on a foundation that can be replicated by any competitor willing to do the same integration labor.

I see no path to monopoly here. No network effects -- one plumber using Ressl does not make it more valuable for the next plumber. No proprietary technology -- the AI capabilities are general-purpose. No economies of scale that compound -- each new vertical or customer segment requires its own integration work. The only potential defensibility would come from accumulated workflow data over time, but this requires years of deployment at scale, and better-funded competitors are accumulating similar data simultaneously. Notably, Founders Fund has already invested in Netic's Series B -- this is a market where my own fund has placed its bet, and the bet was not on Ressl's approach. When the structural dynamics of a market ensure that multiple players will compete on similar capabilities without any path to dominance, the outcome is margin compression, not monopoly profits.
