# Zibra Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a deep-distributed-systems team — Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer, both ex-Anyscale Ray Core, Policzer also ex-LinkedIn Venice — pointing their specific knowledge at the wealthiest, least-gatekept buyers on Earth: quant hedge funds running backtests. The dimensional signature is a high specific-knowledge floor paired with the lowest possible access-democratization ceiling. Zibra Labs is picks-and-shovels for Bridgewater and Two Sigma, and that framing tells me almost everything about the shape of the outcome.

On specific knowledge, my ladder runs Perplexity at the top — Srinivas couldn't stop building AI search because his Google Brain and OpenAI years had already built it inside his head — through Notion in the middle, down to the AI-wrapper MBAs at the bottom who can be replaced by anyone with an API key. Zibra Labs sits between Notion and Perplexity, but on the Notion side. Building Ray Core at Anyscale and Venice at LinkedIn is genuine, non-trainable distributed-systems knowledge — 50,000-node clusters with <50ms dispatch overhead is not something you hire your way into. But Rabbani and Policzer have zero documented quant-domain knowledge. The specific knowledge productized is general HPC scheduling, not quant backtesting. The company would survive without them only if a competing distsys team showed up; that's not nothing, but it's not Perplexity.

On access democratization — my lowest dimension for Zibra Labs — the ladder runs CoinList at the top (retail investors getting token launches once locked behind securities lawyers), Stack Overflow in the middle (developer knowledge unlocked at scale), and Clearview at the bottom (the structural opposite, which I held my nose and rationalized via leverage). Zibra Labs sits between Stack Overflow and Clearview, much closer to Clearview. Selling multi-cloud spot HPC to hedge funds running "hundreds of signals per day" is not removing a gatekeeper between people and something they want — it's compressing the cost structure of firms who already have the most expensive compute on the planet. Citadel does not need democratizing.

Permissionless leverage: my ladder is Twitter (pure media, hundreds of millions per node of headcount), Uber in the middle (marketplace but ops-heavy), Poshmark at the bottom (marketplace operations teams). Zibra Labs sits between Uber and Twitter on paper — HPC infra is code leverage, zero marginal cost per backtest scheduled — but the AWS Batch + Coiled reference architecture published by AWS itself, and Anyscale (their alma mater) adjacent in the same lane, mean Zibra is renting leverage they don't structurally own. The Ray framework underneath is open source at 42.4k stars; the scheduler is where the moat would live, and 6.4M parallel tasks at <50ms is unverified by any third party.

Principal authenticity: Lavingia coding Gumroad alone after the Series A failure is the high rung; Kalanick at Uber is the middle, intense but not lived-inside; the AI-wrapper MBAs picking for fundability are the bottom. Zibra Labs sits between the wrappers and Kalanick. Two Anyscale alumni building essentially a vertical Anyscale for the most fundable customer segment in software is principal behavior on the technology and agent behavior on the market choice. They didn't escape from a quant fund because they couldn't stand the backtest queue — they identified an opportunity adjacent to their employer's wedge.

Team-to-value leverage is genuinely strong: two founders, zero job postings, the Midjourney/Cursor archetype I keep citing. Sits between Notion-seed and Midjourney. This dimension alone won't carry the check.

My 36-month forecast for Zibra Labs: acquired by Anyscale or a hyperscaler (AWS most likely) for $30-80M as a quant-vertical bolt-on, or pivoted to general AI training/inference compute when the quant TAM proves narrower than the AI training market the same engine could serve. The mechanism is straightforward — the technical work is real and acquirable, the customer wedge is too narrow to support a standalone outlier, and the founders' Anyscale relationships make the strategic acquisition path the path of least resistance.

The strongest objection: Bridgewater and Two Sigma already run Ray, the founders have warm Anyscale-network intros into exactly the buyers who write $5M-$50M infra checks without blinking, and Tako proved Druckenmiller-grade Wall Street co-investors will rally around financial infra plays Naval-style investors usually overlook. A single Citadel contract could be $10M ARR. The bear-killing version of Zibra Labs is a 15-person, $100M ARR private compute business that never needs to raise again. Real case. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway: zero named quant customers, zero benchmarks, and a direct competitor (Coiled, $26M raised) plus AWS itself publishing the same reference architecture. The principal-agent moat that drove my Perplexity check doesn't exist here — Anyscale isn't an agent of legacy revenue protecting an ad business; it's a peer that can ship the same product. The condition that would flip my verdict: a signed >$1M ARR contract with a tier-1 quant fund (Citadel, Jane Street, Two Sigma, Jump) within 12 months. Until then, I pass.
