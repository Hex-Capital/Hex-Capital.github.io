# Clawvisor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for $70M in 2023 and then spent time as a YC Visiting Group Partner — which is the high end of credentialed solo-operator I would normally lean into, except Clawvisor is pointed at "authorization layer for AI agents," which is the most consensus-shaped sleeve in the Spring 2026 batch. The signature here is a high-conviction operator pointed at a low-conviction category, and that pairing in my book reads as a competent acqui-hire setup, not a venture moment. Clawvisor is the founder I would back doing almost anything other than what Clawvisor is doing.

The canonical pass case in my deck is the YC AI-agent seed at $30M post with four tier-1s already circling — and Clawvisor sits one rung above that because Eric Levine is materially more operator than the typical batchmate, not because the round is differently priced. Arcade.dev took $12M in March 2025, Composio is at $29M total with $1M+ ARR and 200+ paying customers as of July 2025, and Permit.io closed $8M Series A in February 2024 — that's roughly $55M of consensus capital already deployed across direct competitors before Clawvisor's seed even prints. The "authorization gateway for agents" frame is exactly the legible-to-every-VC pitch I treat as near-automatic deselection; the dossier shows no evidence of pricing discipline, no named tier-1 pass, no strategic ambiguity. Solana at $0.05 worked because crypto funds had quit; Clawvisor is launching into a category every security and infra fund is actively bidding.

The low rung on category sleeve is the pass example I wrote myself — pure AI infrastructure at consensus pricing, "AI is just more internet" (Lonsdale #113, 2024) — and Clawvisor is precisely shaped to that mold. Clawvisor ships 14 SaaS adapters for Gmail, Slack, GitHub, Notion, Linear, Stripe, which is the same surface area Composio already covers with 500+ integrations and Nango covers with an open-source vault. The bet that "purpose-based verification" becomes the standalone gateway primitive rather than a checkbox feature inside Arcade or Okta or Auth0 is a bet I cannot make at seed pricing in 2026, because the upstream platforms (Google, Slack, Anthropic, OpenAI) are the parties most likely to ship native agent-permission scopes and collapse the middleware. This is not pre-consensus crypto; it is post-consensus AI middleware.

Clawvisor's capital structure compounds the problem over an 18-month window: a single-engineer team selling a developer gateway against 25+ headcount competitors with $12-29M war chests will be back in market for a Series A inside 12 months at a price set by Arcade's and Composio's growth curves, not Clawvisor's. The structure is a standard SaaS cap table that needs five tacks to reach default-alive, and there is no revenue-share, royalty, holdco, or employee-equity instrument to underwrite anything other than the equity sliver. Teamshares this is not.

On founder conviction, Eric Levine has the domain résumé — Airbnb Trust & Safety engineering 2012-2017, Berbix CEO through a $70M exit, then a stint reading every YC application as a Visiting Group Partner — and that is real, the kind of "ran the problem manually for years before automating" prior I genuinely weight. What is missing is the public artifact of an opinionated non-obvious take: 419 Twitter followers, no essays on agent authorization, no thread explaining why Arcade and Composio have it wrong. The founder-market fit is there; the missionary signal that would let me predict how he tacks when the first product thesis breaks is not yet visible in the dossier. He scores on credential, not on conviction.

Counterfactual on founder-as-brand: for Clawvisor to flip into the John Fish or Marina Mogilko bucket where the founder's distribution is the asset, Eric Levine would need a security-developer newsletter, a podcast, a 50K-follower technical audience that pulled the first 100 design-partner customers in for free — and Clawvisor has none of that. 202 GitHub stars on the open-source core is a polite developer signal, not a distribution moat; Composio's 100K developer community is two and a half orders of magnitude larger and was the actual lever behind their Series A. Clawvisor will buy distribution at sales@clawvisor.com prices, which is exactly the consumption pattern that forces five rounds.

My 36-month forecast: Clawvisor gets acqui-hired into Okta, CrowdStrike, Snyk, or — more likely — Arcade.dev or Composio for $25-80M in a Berbix-pattern repeat by mid-2029, having reached under $2M ARR and never raised a true Series A. The mechanism is straightforward — Eric Levine is exactly the operator who knows how to run that exit cleanly, he has done it once, and the category will consolidate into either the agent-tooling layer (Arcade/Composio) or the identity incumbents (Okta/Auth0) within 24 months. That outcome returns 3-5x on a seed check at YC pricing, which is fine for an index fund and uninteresting for me.

Strongest bull case: Sublime Security is the Known Exception that says I do write pre-consensus enterprise security seeds, and a Berbix-caliber identity operator building agent-auth before Okta ships native scopes is exactly that move — Clawvisor as the Sublime of agent authorization, bought into before the next consolidation wave. The objection stings until I look at the funding clock: Sublime was genuinely pre-consensus when I wrote it; Clawvisor is launching after $55M+ has already been deployed into adjacent funded competitors and the YC partner pool is actively shopping the category. The Sublime analogy requires the category to be unwritten, and Clawvisor's category is being written aggressively right now by better-funded teams. Verifiable flip condition: if Clawvisor closes this round below $8M post with a named tier-1 having explicitly passed AND Eric Levine ships a public essay reframing agent authorization as a non-obvious primitive Arcade and Composio are structurally unable to build, I revisit. Absent both, I pass and stay close to Eric for the next thing.
