# Button Computer -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Button Computer isn't the founders or the product — it's the competitive graveyard they're walking into. Humane raised $230 million and sold to HP for less than half. Rabbit R1 was panned. Limitless got swallowed by Meta. Bee got absorbed by Amazon. Every first-generation AI wearable startup has either died or been acqui-hired. And now two ex-Apple Vision Pro engineers are saying "everyone overcomplicated this — it's just a button and a speaker." That's an interesting observation, and they may even be right. But "make it simpler and cheaper after the expensive version fails" isn't the kind of non-obvious insight that gives me that jolt of energy. It's the most logical conclusion anyone could draw from watching an entire category implode. When I backed SpaceX, nobody was drawing logical conclusions about private space — it was considered delusional. When Crusoe Energy wanted to mine Bitcoin on gas flares, people thought the founders had lost their minds. Button Computer is a sensible product idea being built by capable people. That's not the same thing as a magically weird company that nobody else can see yet.

I always want to hear the founder's story first — not the business, the person. What I see here are two genuinely talented engineers with elite Apple Vision Pro credentials. Ryan Burgoyne co-invented the gaze-and-tap interaction paradigm for visionOS, holds three patents, graduated summa cum laude from Arizona State, and shipped Skyglass to 95,000 downloads with real revenue. Chris Nolet was a Staff Software Engineer on Vision Pro and previously founded App.io through 500 Startups. These are impressive resumes. But when I ask myself the question I always ask — "why is THIS person solving THIS problem?" — I hear a professional answer, not a personal one. They built hardware interfaces at Apple and now they want to build a different kind of hardware interface. That's a career progression, not a biographical wound. I'm not hearing about the moment that made this problem impossible to walk away from. Compare that to the Contraline founder who spent years in academic research on male contraception when everyone thought it was a dead end, or to Travis Kalanick who had been grinding against the taxi medallion system for years before Uber. The biographical thread matters to me because it's the thing that keeps a founder going when everything falls apart — and in hardware, everything will fall apart at least once.

The category dynamics concern me specifically. Apple is reportedly developing its own AI wearable. Button is an iPhone-only accessory at launch, which means they're building on a platform whose owner is coming for their exact use case. I've seen this pattern before — it's one thing to build in a space where big companies are afraid to go because of regulation or stigma. Contraline operated in reproductive health, Anduril in defense tech, Mindbloom in psychedelic therapy. Those are spaces where institutional inertia and cultural discomfort create genuine moats that protect small companies. Consumer electronics is the opposite — it's the space where Apple, Google, and Amazon are most aggressive and most capable. There's no regulatory complexity here creating a barrier. There's no stigma deterring incumbents. If Apple ships a $99 AI wearable that works seamlessly with Siri and the iPhone ecosystem, Button's $179 device with a Bluetooth tether becomes redundant overnight. The founders' Apple experience cuts both ways — they know how to build great interactions, but they also know better than anyone how thoroughly Apple dominates its own platform.

Here's what I'd need to believe for Button to be a great investment: that the "iPod Shuffle for AI" analogy is exactly right, that simplicity is the killer feature, and that Apple will move slowly enough to let Button build a loyal user base before the platform owner arrives. I'd also need to believe that the industrial design and firmware optimization create a user experience so noticeably better than anything else that people develop real attachment to the product — like early iPod owners did. The Apple Vision Pro pedigree gives some credibility to this: if anyone can nail the moment-of-interaction between a human and a small device, it's engineers who literally designed how people interact with spatial computing. And Burgoyne has proven he can ship consumer products that people actually download. But even in that optimistic scenario, I don't see the economic access story that gets me excited. This is a convenience device for people who already have iPhones and already use AI. It doesn't create income for an underserved population, doesn't make an opaque system transparent, doesn't unlock opportunity where it didn't exist before. When I backed Uber, I saw a tool that would create earnings for hundreds of thousands of drivers. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit access replacing predatory hidden fees. Button makes AI slightly more convenient for people who are already well-served.

The one dimension where Button genuinely shines is co-founder alignment. These two worked together at Apple, stayed close friends after Burgoyne moved to Colorado, and both bring complementary startup experience — Nolet's prior founding, Burgoyne's revenue-generating consumer product. Burgoyne calling Nolet "my best friend" and choosing to build with him after years of maintained friendship is the kind of stress-tested relationship I trust. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I take co-founder dynamics seriously, and this team has the foundation to weather hard times together. That matters. But it's not enough on its own to override the absence of the signals I weight most heavily.
