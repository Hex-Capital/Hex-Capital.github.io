# Superset -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's my problem with Superset in one sentence: the company it most reminds me of is not any of my portfolio companies — it's the generic YC AI workflow company I described when Sam Altman asked me what I pass on. "AI for developers managing their AI agents." The pitch is a convenience layer. A better tmux. A nicer way to juggle terminal sessions. If Superset captures every developer who runs multiple CLI coding agents in parallel, the world gets... slightly more organized software engineers. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic human need becomes affordable. No industry gets disrupted. The consequence ceiling is a profitable developer tools SaaS business, and I don't write checks for that outcome.

The commoditization risk here is not a risk — it's a certainty. The dossier itself states it plainly: "Anthropic has already shipped native multi-agent orchestration features for Claude Code." When the platform you depend on absorbs your feature, you're finished. Git worktree isolation, centralized monitoring, diff viewing — these are features, not products. Cursor has $1.2 billion in ARR and every incentive to add multi-agent orchestration to retain its developer base. VS Code ships to tens of millions of developers and GitHub Copilot is already deeply integrated. The "agent-agnostic" positioning sounds strategically clever until you realize it means Superset has no proprietary technology, no data advantage, and no switching cost beyond muscle memory. The barrier to entry, as the dossier notes, is "UX polish and ecosystem integration, not deep technical complexity." That's a damning admission. UX polish is a temporary advantage. It's not a moat — it's a head start.

Let me give the bull case genuine consideration, because the founders deserve it. Three ex-YC CTOs who have shipped products with real traction — Kiet Ho built Onlook to 25,000 GitHub stars, which is not trivial. They clearly know how to build developer tools that developers actually adopt. The 8,000 stars in five months and the #1 Product Hunt launch demonstrate real pull. The bull case is that we're at the beginning of an "agentic development" paradigm shift analogous to the containerization wave, and Superset becomes the Docker of AI agent orchestration — a convenience layer that becomes infrastructure. If every developer runs 5-10 concurrent AI agents by 2028, the orchestration layer becomes critical, and the company that owns the workflow owns the developer relationship. In that scenario, Superset's agent-agnostic positioning lets it ride every wave simultaneously. But Docker had deep technical innovation — cgroups, namespaces, a fundamental rearchitecting of how software is packaged and deployed. Superset is managing git worktrees and terminal processes. There is no comparable technical depth here, and Docker itself struggled to capture the value it created — Kubernetes ate its lunch. Orchestration layers that lack deep technical differentiation tend to get absorbed or outflanked.

The founders are capable builders, but this is the wrong problem for capable builders. They're applying real talent to a 2-3x improvement in developer workflow management. I'd rather see this team tackle something where their ability to ship fast and build community creates a step-function change — where the consequence of success is worth the risk of failure. A company designed to generate moderate SaaS returns is, in my framework, a misallocation of founder talent. If you're going to take the existential risk of starting a company, the payoff should be worth the pain. A better way to manage terminal sessions is not that payoff. I'm not calculating IRR here — I'm asking whether the world visibly changes if this works. It doesn't.
