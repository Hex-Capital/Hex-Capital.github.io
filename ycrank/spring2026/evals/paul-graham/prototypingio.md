# Prototyping.io -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The website promises ±0.0001" tolerances across six manufacturing processes with part sizes up to 34 feet and no minimum order quantity. That is a staggering specification sheet for two people with a waitlist and zero shipped parts. I keep thinking about what I wrote regarding young founders going through the motions of starting a startup — we saw this so often at YC we made up a name for it: "playing house." I'm not saying that's what's happening here, but the gap between the polished website and the total absence of any evidence that manufacturing is actually occurring is the single most important thing I need to understand about this company, and the dossier gives me nothing to close that gap.

The founders are competent. Revanth's mechanical engineering credentials are genuine — IIT Bhubaneswar silver medal, UT Austin aerospace research, Schlumberger manufacturing experience. He's lived inside DFM problems. Prerit brings real AI systems capability from Microsoft and published distributed systems research at ACM CoNEXT and IEEE ICDCS. The domain-plus-technical pairing is textbook complementary. But I wrote once that you can recognize genuinely smart people by their ability to say things like "I don't know," and the analog for founders is whether they show you what they've actually done rather than what they plan to do. There's no evidence here of the cockroach quality — no cereal boxes moment, no story of hunting down their first ten customers in machine shops. And the founders "recently joined forces" with no shared history. Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock. An untested partnership entering a market this brutal gives me pause.

The deeper problem is that this idea doesn't look bad. I've written extensively about how the best startup ideas look initially like bad ideas — Facebook sounded like a site for college students to waste time. AI-powered DFM analysis for manufacturing sounds... obviously useful. Everyone can see it. That's precisely why RMFG has shipped 100,000+ parts, Jiga has NASA and Siemens as customers, Toolpath raised $10M, Korso is in YC alongside them, and both Xometry and Protolabs are layering AI onto their existing platforms. When an idea is consensus, the question shifts entirely to execution and timing advantages, and I see neither here. The "why now" argument — that LLM capabilities crossed a threshold — applies equally to every competitor in this cohort.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Manufacturing is a $402 billion global market. The structural tension in Xometry's business model is real: they profit from order volume, so deep DFM analysis that reduces complexity and rework actually conflicts with their revenue incentive. An AI-native platform without this conflict could genuinely serve engineers better. Revanth has the domain credibility to build trust with hardware teams in a way that pure-software founders cannot. And the schlep here is enormous — supplier vetting, quality control across processes, logistics — which means most founders' unconscious minds won't even let them see this opportunity. If these founders are actually doing the schlep, actually on the ground building a vetted supplier network and delivering parts that meet those tolerance specs, this could compound into something meaningful. But I need evidence of that. What I see instead is a "Request Early Access" button.

I keep returning to what separates the best YC companies at this stage: the founders make the startup take off. They don't wait for users to come to them. The Airbnbs were physically in New York, photographing apartments, understanding their users at an almost uncomfortable level of intimacy. The Collisons would say "give me your laptop" and install Stripe on the spot. I see no analog here. The gated early access model might be prudent supply-chain management, or it might be a way to avoid confronting whether engineers actually want this specific product from this specific team. Without any traction signal — not revenue, not user counts, not testimonials, not even a community channel — I cannot distinguish between the two.

The market is real, the founders are credentialed, and the schlep is the kind that keeps lesser teams away. But a consensus idea in a crowded cohort with zero distinguishing traction and an untested founder relationship is not a bet I can make with my own money based on what's in front of me. I'd want to sit across from these founders for seven minutes and understand whether they're the kind of people who are actually on the ground making parts, or the kind who built a beautiful website and are waiting for the market to come to them. The dossier doesn't let me make that distinction, and at this stage, that distinction is everything.
