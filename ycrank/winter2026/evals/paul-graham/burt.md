# Burt -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Burt is the ghost of a previous company hiding in plain sight. The YC page title still reads "Easily fine tune and deploy specialized models." The current website says "AI teammates for logistics." These are two completely different companies. That transition tells a story: two engineers built a general-purpose model fine-tuning tool, it didn't get traction, and they went looking for a vertical to apply their skills to. They landed on freight brokerage. This is the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" — sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than noticing them organically. The question is not whether logistics back-office work is painful. It obviously is. The question is how Bobby and Kurt found it. And the answer appears to be: they went shopping for a problem.

This matters because the schlep in freight brokerage is genuinely enormous — chasing proof-of-delivery documents, validating invoices against carrier contracts, tracking shipments across dozens of carriers with different systems. It's exactly the kind of ugly, tedious complexity that causes most smart engineers to unconsciously look away. But here's the thing: someone already looked. HappyRobot, a YC S23 company, has raised $62M, reports eight-figure revenue, and has signed DHL, Werner, and Schneider. The schlep blindness window on this problem closed roughly two years ago. When I talk about problems hiding in plain sight, I mean problems where the first serious attempt hasn't happened yet. This isn't that. This is two talented engineers arriving after the land rush with no domain-specific advantage to differentiate their approach.

Neither founder has any documented connection to freight, logistics, or supply chain. Bobby's background is CS at UCI, founding engineer at a YC company, engineer at Replo building coding agents. Kurt is EECS at Berkeley, also an engineer at Replo. Their shared experience at Replo is the co-founder relationship, which is fine — colleagues who worked together is a legitimate basis for partnership. But when I look at what made Stripe work, the Collisons didn't just have technical ability — they'd been processing payments themselves and knew where the bodies were buried in the banking stack. When I look at DoorDash, Tony Xu personally delivered food. These founders built LLM tooling at Replo and then chose logistics. That's a meaningful distinction. In an industry where sales are relationship-driven and trust is built on demonstrating that you understand a broker's specific workflow, showing up as "two AI engineers from outside the industry" is a real handicap.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. HappyRobot targets enterprise — DHL, Werner, Schneider. The mid-market and SMB segment of freight brokerage is fragmented, with thousands of small brokerages still running on email and spreadsheets. If Burt could own that segment with a simpler, more configurable product, there's a real business there. The founders' LLM and agent-building experience from Replo is directly relevant to the technical challenge. And sometimes outsiders see things insiders can't — they're not anchored to "how things have always been done." If Bobby and Kurt spent six months embedded with three or four small brokerages, learned the workflows deeply, and built something those brokers genuinely couldn't live without, the domain expertise gap could close. The problem is that right now, none of that has happened yet. There's no traction — no revenue, no named customers, no waitlist, not even a Product Hunt launch. Three hundred LinkedIn followers and a Calendly link. At pre-seed, thin evidence is expected, but zero evidence combined with a recent pivot and no domain background is a constellation of signals that all point the same direction.

The one genuinely strong dimension here is technical ability. Both founders are real engineers who build things. Kurt has 46 public repos and 176 stars on GitHub. Bobby was a founding engineer at Pirros. They have hands-on experience building coding agents and LLM tooling, which is directly applicable to building AI agents for logistics tasks. If I were evaluating them purely as a technical team working on a problem they discovered organically, I'd be more interested. But technical skill is necessary, not sufficient. The history of YC is full of brilliant engineers who built impressive technology for problems they didn't deeply understand. The ones who won — Stripe, Dropbox, Airbnb — had founders whose understanding of the problem went bone-deep because they'd lived inside it.

I'm passing on this one. Two strong engineers who picked a vertical by market analysis rather than personal pain, entering a space where a well-funded YC alum has a two-year head start and eight-figure revenue. The schlep is real, but the organic discovery is absent. The team can build, but they haven't yet demonstrated they understand what to build for people who move freight for a living.
