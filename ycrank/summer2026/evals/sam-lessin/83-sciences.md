# 83 Sciences -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

83 Sciences is a technically-credentialed materials-AI seed sitting inside the loudest consensus sleeve of the 2026 batch, and that pairing — Eric Riesel's JACS 2024 Crystalyze paper as the high signal, "AI-native materials discovery" as the low one — is the analytical signature. Real domain founder, wrong sleeve, wrong moment on the pricing curve, and CuspAI reportedly closing $400M at a $2.6B valuation is doing the pricing work for the whole category whether 83 Sciences likes it or not.

Take the pass I ran on the canonical 2026 YC AI-agent pitch — $30M post, four tier-1s in, no non-obvious belief. 83 Sciences sits one rung above that on Criterion 1 only because the dossier discloses no round terms at all, which under my absent-evidence default gets 9/30, and I'm actually going below that because the adjacent-category heat is screaming: when CuspAI is being marked at $2.6B and Citrine has absorbed $81.3M, every generalist seed fund with an "AI-for-hard-tech" bucket is going to bid the S26 materials-AI team into a consensus SAFE cap. The mechanism I bought Solana on at $0.05 was the opposite — people had quit the category. Here people are stampeding into it. 83 Sciences doesn't disclose a discount, doesn't disclose a passed-on tier-1, doesn't disclose a structural weirdness in the round; silence at this batch-moment reads as consensus pricing, and that's the whole game.

Recall why the generic SaaS pitch scores at the floor on capital structure — five rounds to default-alive, standard SAFE, no instrument creativity. 83 Sciences is not that, but it's not Teamshares or the Mogilko structure either. Materials-AI competing against a $130M-raised CuspAI is a genuine "you can't bootstrap this" venture moment in the Ro sense — regulatory-adjacent schleps (IP-sensitive lab data agreements) are the moat and the moat requires capital. That earns partial credit. What it does NOT earn is a non-standard instrument: no revenue-share against pilot contracts, no royalty on downstream materials IP co-developed with lab partners, no holdco wrapping the "novel collaboration operating models" language the YC description gestures at. The instrument that would actually price 83 Sciences correctly — equity plus a carry on jointly-developed materials IP with partner labs — is not on the table, and without it this is a standard hard-tech SAFE that will need five tacks against a competitor with an eight-figure head start.

Riesel is the one dossier fact that actually loads. Lead author on Crystalyze in JACS September 2024, MIT News wrote it up, the model is live at crystalyze.org — this is a founder who has been paid and unpaid on this exact problem for years, publishes opinionated technical work by name, and has the missionary-temperament artifact I look for. That's a real 13/20, not a default. The compounding question over 18 months is whether Riesel is the one making the sales calls to Sila and LG Chem or whether Naccarella and Yang (both BCG-shaped operators) run the commercial motion while Riesel stays in the model — the former compounds the founder-brand asset, the latter starves it.

On distribution, 83 Sciences has none of what John Fish had on entry to Bookshelved. No Twitter accounts across any of the three founders, no GitHub, no newsletter, no niche subreddit or Discord. The Crystalyze paper is a credential, not a community. For the score to flip a tier here, Riesel would need to be running a materials-ML Substack or a lab-notebook-in-public thread with 5K+ researchers subscribed — the artifact that would make the "unpublished data" pitch inbound instead of outbound. Absent it, this is enterprise BD into R&D labs with 6-9 month cycles, and the founder-as-brand default of 5/15 is generous; I'm pulling it to 4.

The sleeve question is where I'm hardest. My revealed portfolio is pre-consensus crypto, DTC consumer, prescription-rail health, and creator-holdco — 83 Sciences is none of those. It is exactly the "AI is just more internet" category I re-weight away from at seed, dressed in a hard-tech wrapper that doesn't change the underlying pricing dynamics. Compare to Aleo on the crypto sleeve — I underwrote that when the category was in retreat; here I'd be underwriting materials-AI when CuspAI is setting the tape at $2.6B. Different tape, different alpha, and the alpha isn't mine.

My 36-month forecast on 83 Sciences: acqui-hire by CuspAI or Citrine Informatics for Riesel's modeling talent at a $30-60M outcome, or grinding to $300-600K ARR across two or three chemicals/battery pilots with a bridge round at flat terms — not dead, not a breakout, not returning a Slow-sized fund. The mechanism is the CuspAI capital differential: their $130M raised plus reported $400M closing means they can pay for the same lab-access relationships 83 Sciences would need 24 months to negotiate, and pre-seed runway does not survive that clock.

The bull case that stings: materials informatics is genuinely a "venture moment" in the Ro sense — the 50-state-prescription-rail analogue is the IP-sensitive multi-corporate data-sharing agreement, and the first team to lock those in gets a proprietary corpus that CuspAI's compute-and-simulate approach can't replicate. Riesel's JACS credentials plus Naccarella's Sila-alum network is exactly the trust-collateral needed to sign those deals, and the fact that the dossier discloses zero LOIs is a pre-seed timing artifact, not a signal of failure. Ro was capital-inefficient too, and I wrote the check anyway. My rebuttal: Ro's regulatory schlep was legible to downstream Series A buyers — 50 states is a countable thing to underwrite. 83 Sciences' "unpublished data corpus" is illegible in the exact way that makes the legibility-translation problem hard, not the good kind of illegible where I'm the only bid. The verifiable flip condition: 83 Sciences discloses two signed exclusive multi-year data-partnership agreements with named tier-1 corporates (Sila, LG Chem, BASF, Merck) before the seed closes — I'll re-underwrite that day. Absent that, pass.
