# Haladir -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Haladir isn't the technology -- it's the trajectory. Their LinkedIn page previously described them as "an AI-powered mainframe modernization suite" for COBOL migration. Then it became "AI-led software development correctness." Now it's "Operational Superintelligence" with reinforcement learning for operations research. That kind of repositioning at pre-seed isn't unusual -- plenty of great companies pivot early. But the pattern here doesn't read like founders who stumbled onto a deeper truth while pursuing their original obsession. It reads like smart people scanning the landscape for the hottest framing. And "Operational Superintelligence" -- that's not the kind of name that makes other investors squirm. That's the kind of name that makes them salivate. When I invested in Crusoe, nobody wanted to touch "Bitcoin mining on gas flares." When I backed Contraline, VCs literally didn't want to talk about male contraception. The deals that have defined my portfolio were the ones where the name alone made people uncomfortable. This name doesn't do that. It's riding exactly the wave that every AI company in YC is riding right now.

My first question for any founder is always: tell me your story, not the business. And here I'm looking at the dossier and I genuinely can't find the story. Four current or recent university students -- CMU, Princeton, UVA, UVA Wise. One of them is described as a freshman. Jibran Hutchins built a phone-to-Wii-controller project at a hackathon and publishes a Substack about civic liberalism. These are clearly bright, capable people. But where is the biographical thread that ties them to operations research? To logistics? To supply chain? When I backed Uber, it was after years of conversations with Scott about how broken the taxi medallion system was -- I felt it in my bones because I'd experienced bad taxis and talked to drivers. When I championed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone cared. I need to see that the founder IS the problem, not that they found a clever research angle. Here, I see talented students who wrote a strong paper at an interesting intersection, but I can't identify who among them has ever struggled with a supply chain, run a logistics operation, or sat in front of a solver trying to schedule crews. The ConstraintBench paper is genuinely impressive research -- 10 OR domains, solver-verified rewards, clear demonstration that frontier LLMs fail at constrained optimization. That's real. But a paper is not the same as a founder who can't sleep because this specific problem haunts them.

The bull case deserves honest engagement, because there's real substance here. The technical insight -- that operations research problems have natural verifiability through existing solvers, creating a bridge for RL training that nobody has built -- is legitimately non-obvious. LLMs achieving only 30.5% joint feasibility-and-optimality on constrained optimization is a real gap. The big AI labs are focused on math and code reasoning, not OR formalization. Harmonic raised $295M for math proofs, Code Metal raised $161.5M for code translation verification, but nobody is occupying this specific intersection. If Haladir can own the formalization layer between business constraints and RL reward signals, that could become genuine infrastructure -- the kind of picks-and-shovels play that compounds as more companies try to deploy AI in operations. The investors see something: Susa Ventures, SV Angel, and Joshua Browder all wrote checks. Young founders without industry experience have built transformative companies before. If these four can translate research depth into a product that enterprise buyers actually need, the technical moat from interdisciplinary expertise (OR + formal methods + RL) could be real.

But I keep coming back to what I don't feel. There's no jolt here for me. The company doesn't create economic access for people who lack it -- better optimization algorithms for enterprise supply chains is valuable, sure, but it doesn't put money in a driver's pocket or make a hidden fee structure transparent. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to the people most affected by it. It makes an already-visible system slightly more automated for the companies that already run it. And the competitive moat question nags at me: RLVR methodology is well-documented in public research, and the moment operations research becomes a priority training domain for OpenAI or DeepSeek, a four-person team of recent graduates is going to face a resource asymmetry that no benchmark paper can overcome. The specialized knowledge barrier is real but not insurmountable -- it's the kind of thing a well-funded lab acquires by hiring three OR professors.

I respect the intellectual ambition, and I wouldn't be surprised if one or more of these founders builds something important over the course of their careers. But this isn't my kind of bet. I need the founder's biography and the company's mission to be inseparable. I need the idea to be so weird that it makes other investors uncomfortable, not excited. I need to see a path to real-world impact for people who currently lack access. Haladir is smart research in a hot space -- and that's precisely the kind of consensus-adjacent opportunity I've trained myself to pass on.
