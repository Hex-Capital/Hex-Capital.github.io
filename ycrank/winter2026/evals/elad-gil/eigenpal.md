# EigenPal -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about EigenPal is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything about the market structure. Rossum has raised $114.5M and generates $44.9M in revenue. Hyperscience has raised roughly $300M. Nanonets has $29M. Google, AWS, and Azure all offer document extraction APIs bundled into cloud contracts that enterprises already pay for. This is not a non-obvious market. Intelligent document processing is a well-mapped category with identified winners, established enterprise relationships, and hundreds of millions in deployed capital. When I look for the "too boring, too niche, too unfamiliar" archetype that signals asymmetric returns, I see the opposite -- a market that every enterprise AI investor has already sized, funded, and staffed.

The "why now" thesis is that LLMs crossed a capability threshold enabling template-free document extraction. That's true -- but the structural problem is that this capability improvement accrues to every player simultaneously. Rossum can integrate GPT-4 into its existing product with its existing $44.9M revenue base. AWS Textract can improve its APIs overnight. The technology shift doesn't create a structural vacancy the way Google's Project Maven cancellation created a defense AI vacuum for Anduril. It creates a capability uplift that benefits incumbents with distribution at least as much as it benefits new entrants. When the rising tide lifts all boats equally, there's no timing arbitrage for the startup.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. European banking with on-premises deployment requirements is a genuine wedge -- regulated financial institutions often can't use cloud-hosted document processing, and compliance certifications (SOC II Type 2, GDPR) combined with on-prem readiness could create real switching costs with these specific customers. The evaluation-first workflow testing approach is smart product design for risk-averse enterprises. If the two European bank relationships are deep paid contracts (not pilots), and if banking becomes a beachhead that generates proprietary training data and workflow templates that compound, EigenPal could build a defensible niche within the broader IDP market. Rossum's $44.9M revenue against a $2.3B market means less than 2% penetration -- plenty of room for a focused competitor. But this bull case requires the enterprise relationships to be real and deep, which the dossier cannot verify.

The product-to-distribution trajectory concerns me. Document extraction is a feature that sits inside a workflow, not a platform that becomes a distribution channel for additional products. Stripe's API created a developer relationship that naturally extended to Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. EigenPal's document processing creates a procurement relationship with operations teams -- useful, but it doesn't obviously become a channel for distributing other products. The agentic workflow builder gestures toward platform ambitions, but at this stage it reads as a product feature rather than a distribution wedge. I don't see the multi-product expansion path that separates a good company from a generational one.

The founding team has relevant credentials -- Blaszyk's Elastic and Yelp engineering background maps well to enterprise search and document processing, and the docx-js-editor open-source project (443 GitHub stars, 130-point Show HN) demonstrates real document engineering capability and shipping velocity. Novak's McKinsey and Accenture consulting exposure provides enterprise sales context. But Novak's prior company Assetario appears still active, which raises a commitment clarity question. And the unverified enterprise logos (McKinsey, Elastic, Accenture, MIT listed without case studies or press releases) make me cautious -- at a pre-seed stage, the difference between "they used our free trial" and "they signed a six-figure contract" is the difference between signal and noise.

The core issue is that EigenPal requires multiple things to go right simultaneously: build a technically superior product against competitors with 10-100x more capital, win enterprise deals against established sales organizations, avoid commoditization by cloud providers who can bundle document AI into existing contracts, and convert pilot relationships into sticky paid deployments. That's not a single-miracle company -- it's a company that needs to outcompete well-funded incumbents across multiple dimensions at once. The single miracle I'd want to see is a structural advantage that compounds, and I don't see one that the incumbents can't replicate.
