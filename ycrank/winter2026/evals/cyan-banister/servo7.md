# Servo7 -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me before anything else: industrial robotics AI is one of the most competed, most consensus investment categories on the planet right now. Over two billion dollars poured into warehouse and industrial automation startups in Q1 2026 alone. Standard Bots has $63 million and $12 million in ARR pursuing demonstration-based robot programming. Formic has $52 million plus a $100 million debt facility. Sunrise Robotics raised $8.5 million to do rapid-deployment robots in European manufacturing — Servo7's exact geography and thesis. When I see a space where every fund in town is writing checks, that's when I instinctively pull back. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Servo7 is operating in a space that is extremely popular and relatively straightforward to assess, which means the weirdness premium — the thing I hunt for — has already been competed away. The founders are entering a gunfight with $500K against opponents carrying tens of millions, and they're not bringing a fundamentally different weapon.

If I were sitting across from Pieter and Jasper, I'd ask them what I ask everyone: tell me your story. Not the business — how did you get here? And what I'd hear is two TU Delft engineers with strong technical backgrounds in autonomous systems, hyperloop engineering, military drone software, and deep reinforcement learning. That's impressive on paper. But it doesn't tell me why warehouses. Why palletizing and kitting? Where's the biographical thread that makes this problem personal and inescapable? When I backed Uber, it was after years of my husband and me raging about the taxi medallion racket — we'd lived the frustration. When I found Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on a problem that was deeply his. With Servo7, I see talented engineers who chose a hot market, and that pattern — technically gifted people chasing what's fundable rather than what's personal — is one of my clearest pass signals. I need to feel that the founder would be working on this problem even if it were unfundable, and nothing in the dossier gives me that.

The strongest argument for this company is the deployment experience. Jasper built and deployed military drone software. Pieter worked on autonomous defense systems. Willem's thesis was on surgical robotic navigation. These aren't people who've only written code in labs — they've put autonomous systems into regulated, high-stakes environments where failure has real consequences. That's the kind of hard-space navigation that I've always valued, from IronPort through Anduril. If they can translate that military-grade deployment discipline into rapid warehouse rollouts, the operational playbook could be genuinely differentiated from competitors who've only worked in controlled settings. The wheeled humanoid form factor also adds something Standard Bots doesn't have — a robot that can navigate existing floor plans without infrastructure modification. If I squint hard enough, I can see a version of this story where the combination of European manufacturing relationships, military deployment rigor, and the humanoid form factor creates a wedge that the American competitors can't easily replicate. But "if I squint hard enough" isn't conviction — it's rationalization.

The defensibility question troubles me. Their AI stack is built on Hugging Face's LeRobot, an open-source framework with 28,000 stars that any competitor can fork. The moat has to come from proprietary training data accumulated through real deployments, but they're at the earliest stage with minimal deployments, while Standard Bots already has Fortune 500 customers generating exactly that kind of compounding data advantage. Hardware is capital-intensive, and $500K is barely enough to iterate on a single robot design, let alone build the manufacturing pipeline needed to compete. I also notice that this company doesn't fit my pattern of backing things that make invisible systems visible or create economic access for people who lack it — warehouse automation primarily displaces human labor rather than creating new economic opportunity. That's not a moral judgment, but it means this company doesn't activate the part of my investing instinct that gets excited about Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm giving people transparent credit.

The co-founder dynamic has the right shape — university friends with complementary skills, one training the AI and the other building the hardware. That division is clean and sensible. But I've learned the hard way that complementary skills aren't the same as tested conflict resolution. I'd want to understand how Pieter and Jasper have disagreed and recovered before I could score this dimension with any confidence. The shared TU Delft network is a modest positive — at least they come from the same world and probably share assumptions about rigor and process. But without evidence of surviving a real crisis together, I'm neutral here rather than encouraged.

I'm passing on this one. Not because the founders aren't capable — they clearly are — but because nothing about Servo7 triggers the signals I've learned to trust. It's popular, not unpopular. It's easy to assess, not difficult. The founders are chasing a market, not a mission born from their own experience. And they're dramatically outgunned on capital by competitors pursuing the same thesis. The company that eventually wins in adaptive industrial robotics might look a lot like this, but at this stage, with this level of differentiation and this funding gap, I don't have the conviction to write a check from my own money.
