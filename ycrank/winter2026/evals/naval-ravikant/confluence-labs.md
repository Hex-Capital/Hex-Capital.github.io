# Confluence Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting tension in this company is that two founders with no resources outperformed teams backed by hundreds of millions of dollars on a hard reasoning benchmark -- then gave the implementation away for free. The 97.9% ARC-AGI-2 score, beating Imbue's 95.1% despite Imbue sitting on $232M and 10,000 H100s, is a genuine signal of raw technical intelligence. But open-sourcing the solver under MIT license means the demonstrated capability is now a public good, not a private asset. When I invested in Stack Overflow, the knowledge graph compounded with each question and answer, becoming more valuable precisely because it accumulated proprietary signal. Confluence Labs took their most impressive artifact and made it infinitely replicable. Either they understand something about defensibility that I don't see in the dossier -- that the real moat is in domain-specific applications they haven't built yet -- or they've confused credibility signaling with strategy.

The specific knowledge question is where this gets complicated. Neither founder has spent years inside drug design, materials science, or physics research -- the domains they claim to serve. Niranjan's RSI selection at MIT marks him as genuinely gifted in scientific reasoning, and a prior startup reportedly at $5M ARR shows execution. Brent's self-taught path through AI tooling -- steering vectors, prompt engineering, pair programming agents -- gives him hands-on fluency with LLM behavior that most ML PhDs lack. Their specific knowledge is real, but it lives in the AI systems layer, not the scientific application layer. They know how to make models learn efficiently from sparse data. They don't know which experiments a materials scientist should run next. That gap between the technology and the domain is where companies either build moats through deep customer partnerships or discover that domain expertise can't be acquired at API speed. Alchemy succeeded as blockchain infrastructure without building DeFi apps, but Alchemy's customers were developers who spoke the same language. Confluence's customers will be scientists who speak an entirely different one.

The leverage architecture, if it translates, is genuinely exciting. Reducing experimental requirements from thousands of samples to dozens is a new form of leverage for researchers -- one scientist with this tool produces output that previously required a lab burning through millions in experiment costs. That's the Notion pattern applied to R&D: individual leverage through tools that compress effort. The program synthesis approach scales as software, not as services. But "if it translates" is carrying enormous weight in that sentence. ARC-AGI-2 tasks are abstract grid transformations. Predicting molecular binding affinities or simulating novel material properties under stress is a categorically different problem. There's no public evidence yet that the approach generalizes beyond the benchmark, and in the absence of that evidence, I'm evaluating a technology demonstration, not a product.

The contrarian angle is in the method, not the market. AI for scientific discovery is consensus hot -- $4.8B market, 21.9% CAGR, Isomorphic Labs raising $600M, Recursion partnering with Bayer for $1.5B. But within that consensus market, Confluence is betting that sample efficiency beats scale. Everyone else is pouring compute and data at the problem. These two are arguing that the right program synthesis architecture extracts more signal from fewer examples than brute-force scaling. That's a genuinely contrarian technical thesis -- it bets against the scaling laws that the entire AI industry has organized around. If they're right, it's an asymmetric bet: the cost of being wrong is a small angel check, but the upside of a sample-efficient foundation model for science is a new infrastructure category. The timing is defensible too -- LLM capabilities have recently crossed a threshold where program synthesis approaches became practically viable, creating a window that didn't exist eighteen months ago.

The bull case I'd need to believe: these founders' specific knowledge in AI reasoning systems is the *right* specific knowledge for this problem, and domain expertise in target verticals can be acquired through customer partnerships rather than founder biography. The ARC-AGI-2 result is not just a benchmark trophy but a genuine proof-of-concept for learning from minimal examples. The open-sourcing is strategic -- the solver is the demo, the domain-specific applications are the product. Paul Graham saw something in these founders that justifies personal backing. If even one scientific vertical validates the approach -- say, materials science, where datasets are small and experiment costs are high -- the leverage ratio is enormous and the compounding begins. That's a plausible path. But plausible paths aren't what I write checks for at this stage. I write checks when the founder knows something about the problem that I can't learn by reading for an afternoon. Here, what the founders know is how to make LLMs reason efficiently. What they don't yet know -- and what would change my calculus -- is which specific scientific domain represents their wedge, and why their approach works there in a way no one else has demonstrated.

I'm a soft pass at current information. The technical intelligence is undeniable. The leverage potential is real. But the gap between a benchmark result and a product for data-sparse scientific domains is wide, the defensibility is undermined by the open-sourcing, and the domain-specific knowledge needed to close customer partnerships isn't present on the founding team yet. If they come back in six months with a working deployment in one scientific vertical and proprietary data accumulating, the calculus changes entirely.
