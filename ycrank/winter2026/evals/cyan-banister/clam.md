# Clam (formerly Baseframe) -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: every major standalone AI security company got acquired in 2025. Lakera, Protect AI, CalypsoAI, Prompt Security — all scooped up by Check Point, Palo Alto, F5, and SentinelOne for a combined total north of a billion dollars. And now two Berkeley engineers pivot mid-batch from hardware design validation into exactly this space. I understand the strategic logic — incumbents absorbing startups often fumble the integration, creating a window for a nimble independent player — but this pattern sets off my alarm bells. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. AI agent security in early 2026 is neither of those things. It's one of the most validated, consensus-approved enterprise categories I can think of right now. When four acquisitions have already told the market "this is worth hundreds of millions," you're not finding a pocket of dreamers and weirdos. You're arriving at a party that's already crowded.

The founders are technically sharp. Anshul was the first hire at HappyRobot, which hit a $500M valuation, and he built AI evals and observability there — so he's seen the security gaps in production AI agents firsthand. Vaibhav did agent orchestration at Augment Code and data infrastructure at Sigma Computing. They took a Computer Security course together at Berkeley. This is solid professional founder-market fit. But when I sit with my core question — tell me your story, not the business — there's no thread here that connects these founders to this problem through something they lived, something that kept them up at night long before it was a market category. They spotted an opportunity during their YC batch and pivoted to it. That's smart. But it's not the biographical fire that I need to feel when I'm writing my own check. The founders I back are usually solving a problem that grew out of something deeply personal — a pain they couldn't walk away from. Here I see career exposure, not conviction forged through adversity.

The "Semantic Firewall" architecture is sensible — intercepting traffic at the network level rather than trying to constrain the AI model through prompts is a cleaner security boundary. The credential injection approach, where the AI agent never sees API keys directly, is a smart design choice. But these are engineering decisions that a capable security team at any well-funded company could replicate. PII detection, prompt injection scanning, malicious code analysis — open-source tools like LLM Guard already cover pieces of this. The moat isn't deep yet, and at pre-seed I need to believe the founders have a unique capacity to build one that others can't. With Contraline, the regulatory pathway through FDA approval was itself the moat. With Anduril, the defense procurement labyrinth protected the position. Here, the competitive barrier is execution speed — and execution speed is exactly what incumbents with massive sales teams and existing customer relationships have.

Let me be honest about where I could be wrong. The bull case is genuinely interesting: when Check Point absorbs Lakera, the product may get diluted into a platform bundle that enterprise buyers don't love. There's a real window, maybe 12-24 months, where a focused independent tool could capture developers building with OpenClaw and similar frameworks. If Clam nails the developer experience — "Run OpenClaw Securely in Minutes" — and builds a bottoms-up adoption motion while incumbents are busy integrating, they could own a wedge. Anshul's time at HappyRobot means he knows enterprise AI deployment pain points intimately. And the co-founder relationship — roommates, shared coursework, already navigated one pivot together — is a solid foundation. If they build proprietary detection models from the traffic flowing through their firewall, that data advantage could compound. But I'd need to see them articulate why they are uniquely positioned beyond timing, and I'd need to feel that this is a problem they'd be solving even if the market hadn't just been validated by a billion dollars in acquisitions. Right now, I don't feel that.

This company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations. It doesn't make an invisible system visible for regular people. It's enterprise security tooling sold to companies deploying AI agents — important work, but it doesn't carry the kind of real-world impact that draws me in. When I backed Uber, it wasn't just a logistics play — it was income for drivers. When I backed Affirm, it was transparent credit replacing hidden fees. Clam is a B2B security product for technical teams. There's nothing wrong with that, but it doesn't activate the part of my framework that asks whether this company makes the world more accessible.

I respect the founders' technical depth and I understand the timing thesis. But this sits squarely in the category of companies I pass on: a hot market, consensus validation, no biographical thread connecting the founders to the problem through lived experience, and a pivot that looks like it's chasing what's popular rather than what's personal. I'd tell these founders to come back and tell me the story of why this problem won't let them sleep — not the market dynamics, not the acquisition wave, but the thing inside them that makes this their fight.
