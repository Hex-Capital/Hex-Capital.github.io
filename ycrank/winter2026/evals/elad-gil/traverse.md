# Traverse -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Traverse isn't what they're building -- it's who they're building against. Mercor is at $10B valuation with $450-500M in annualized run rate and already lists healthcare and law among its RL environment target domains. Surge AI bootstrapped to $1.2B in revenue and is seeking up to $1B in new capital. Applied Compute raised $100M from ex-OpenAI researchers. This is not PagerDuty circa 2010, where operations alerting seemed too boring for serious capital to chase. RL environments is one of the most consensus-hot infrastructure categories in AI right now. When a market has multiple billion-dollar incumbents actively expanding into your claimed niche before you've shipped a product, the structural moment isn't in your favor -- it's already been captured. My best investments are in markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors. This market triggers the opposite reaction.

The founders pivoted into this space during the YC W26 batch, away from Clice AI -- an "AI communication OS for loan officers." That's a complete domain change: from fintech workflow automation to AI training infrastructure. When I see founders entering whatever sector is currently getting funded without deep prior domain conviction, it pattern-matches to the late-cycle trend-chasing I describe in my "End of Cycle" framework. Now, Zachary Yu's stint as a backend engineer at Mercor partially offsets this concern -- he has direct exposure to how frontier labs consume RL environments, and presumably saw firsthand where Mercor's crowd-sourcing model breaks down. That's a real signal. But one engineer's observation at a previous employer isn't the same as the deep domain expertise Traverse claims is required to build verification systems for medicine and law. If your core thesis is that "taste is hard to formalize" in non-deterministic domains, you need people who have spent years developing that taste -- doctors, lawyers, domain scientists -- not two CS undergrads from Waterloo, however talented.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If non-deterministic verification truly requires a fundamentally different technical architecture from coding RL -- if it can't be solved by Mercor hiring domain experts and plugging them into their existing annotation pipeline -- then Traverse is positioned at a genuine frontier. The analogy would be Harvey in 2022: building before the capability threshold crossed, ready to capture market share once model fidelity made it viable. The "why now" for this sub-segment is that labs have saturated coding RL and need the next capability frontier. If Traverse develops a proprietary methodology for encoding subjective judgment into reward models, that methodology becomes the moat. For this to work, two things must be true simultaneously: the verification problem must be solvable at the quality level labs require, and it must be solvable through a software-driven approach that doesn't reduce to "hire expensive domain experts" -- because if it does, the incumbents with hundreds of millions in capital win on pure economics.

That's the problem: this plan requires multiple sequential breakthroughs. First, solve a verification methodology the company itself describes as "largely unsolved." Second, demonstrate quality to a buyer set of literally three to five frontier labs where procurement decisions are high-stakes and relationship-driven. Third, out-execute incumbents who are already expanding into your target domains with 100x your resources. When I apply the single-miracle framework, the path to success here compounds at least two low-probability events -- the technical feasibility of non-deterministic verification AND winning enterprise sales against entrenched, massively funded competitors. That's too many miracles.

The buyer concentration issue compounds everything. The addressable customer set for RL environments is Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and a thin tail of enterprises. This can never become a distribution channel for additional products -- there's no platform play when your entire market is five procurement teams. Compare this to Gusto, which seems "boring" but serves every employer in America and uses that payroll relationship to distribute HR, benefits, and compliance products. Or Stripe, where a payment API became the channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Traverse's ceiling, even in the success case, is a specialized data vendor to a handful of labs -- a good business perhaps, but not the kind of company that becomes a distribution platform with compounding returns. The RL environments space broadly has the structural characteristics of a services business with concentrated buyers, not infrastructure with network effects or distribution moats.

Zachary Yu's side projects do show genuine shipping velocity -- 25K users in 24 hours for vibeornot.com, 10K+ users for tunesform.com. These are real execution signals. And both founders are clearly technically capable. But execution velocity on consumer side projects doesn't transfer directly to a domain where you need deep expertise in medicine and law to build credible verification systems. The technology cycle positioning is correct in the broadest sense -- RL is indeed the primary scaling axis for LLM capability right now, and lab spending on environments is growing 3-5x. But being right about the macro cycle while entering a specific sub-segment with no traction, no domain experts, and billion-dollar competitors already claiming your niche isn't enough. The market's structural moment has already been identified and is being aggressively captured by better-resourced teams.
