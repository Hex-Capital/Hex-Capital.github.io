# RunAnywhere -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about RunAnywhere: it is a software abstraction layer sitting on top of primitives controlled by Google, Meta, Apple, and Microsoft. The founders are building middleware. Middleware gets squeezed -- the layers above want to go direct, the layers below want to expand upward. I lived this exact dynamic at Facebook, where we routinely absorbed the functionality of third-party developer tools once we understood the pattern well enough. The entire value proposition here is software wrapping other people's software, monetized via a SaaS control plane. I've been saying for two years that the SaaS era is over, that software moats are dissolving faster than founders realize. RunAnywhere is building precisely the kind of business I think AI is about to destroy -- not because AI will compete with it directly, but because the underlying runtimes (CoreML, ONNX, ExecuTorch) will get easier to use natively, and the abstraction layer becomes unnecessary. When the thing you're abstracting over gets simpler, your abstraction is the first thing that dies.

The sane-person-insane-idea calibration fails on the idea side. On-device AI deployment is one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. Apple dedicated half its WWDC to on-device intelligence. Qualcomm acquired Edge Impulse. Every hardware company is shipping NPUs. EdgeRunner raised $17.5M, Nexa AI raised $16.5M. When every major platform company is investing billions and multiple startups are well-funded in your exact space, you are not pursuing an insane idea -- you are pursuing the idea that every investor already thinks is obvious. The founders themselves are competent -- Sanchit shipped SDKs at Intuit across millions of devices, Shubham built distributed systems at AWS, they've known each other for 6-7 years from RIT. That's genuine operational background and relational depth. The "sane" part works. But sane person plus sane idea is exactly the wrong combination in my framework. You're buying consensus at market price.

The strongest bull case requires me to take seriously the business model conflict argument: Google, Meta, and Microsoft monetize cloud inference, so building excellent on-device tools that route computation away from their clouds cannibalizes their own revenue. That's a real structural insight, and it's the kind of disruption theory logic that has produced great outcomes historically. If RunAnywhere became the Docker of on-device AI -- the default abstraction that every developer reaches for -- the community effects (10.2K GitHub stars, multi-platform support across Swift, Kotlin, React Native, Flutter) could compound into genuine lock-in. The control plane adds operational stickiness at enterprise scale. For this to work, though, you need the abstraction layer to remain necessary as the underlying runtimes mature, and you need the open-source community to translate into paying enterprise customers. Docker is the cautionary tale: it became the most important abstraction in infrastructure, struggled to monetize, and got outflanked by Kubernetes -- which was built by Google. The same companies whose business model conflict supposedly protects RunAnywhere are precisely the companies capable of building the orchestration layer that makes the abstraction obsolete.

My "cherry on top" test is the clearest disqualifier. Would this business be good if AI didn't exist? Obviously not -- the entire product is an AI deployment tool. Strip away AI and there is literally nothing here. That's a feature, not a business. I want companies where AI is the non-obvious accelerant on an already-good business -- Craftwork's painting operations get more efficient with AI scheduling, but the crews and the trucks and the customer relationships exist independent of any model. RunAnywhere has zero value in a world where on-device AI doesn't proliferate, zero value if cloud inference costs drop enough to make edge deployment unnecessary, and zero value if the next generation of runtimes ships with built-in fleet management. That's three existential dependencies on external trends the founders don't control. The narrative can theoretically expand -- "default infrastructure for all edge AI" is a big story -- but it's a story built entirely on software, entirely dependent on AI trends, with an open-source license (Apache 2.0) that means anyone can fork the core product. Applied to YC five times shows admirable persistence, but persistence alone doesn't create structural moats.
