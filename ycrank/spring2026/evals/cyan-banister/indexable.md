# Indexable -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Indexable: it's playing in one of the most popular investment categories on the planet right now. AI agent infrastructure is the deal that every VC in San Francisco wants to be in. E2B just raised $43.8 million. Daytona closed $31 million in February. Modal is valued north of a billion dollars. When I see that kind of consensus energy flooding into a space, every instinct I've developed over 130-plus investments tells me to slow down. The most promising companies are the ones nobody wants to fund. This is the opposite — everybody wants to fund AI agent sandboxes. That doesn't mean the company is bad. It means the weirdness has already been priced out of the category.

What I wish I could do is sit down with Andrew Gazelka and ask him: tell me your story, not the business. How did you end up here? Because from what I can see publicly, this reads as a brilliant systems engineer who identified a technical gap and went after it. That's admirable. But it's not the signal I'm tuned to. I look for the founder whose life experience made this problem personal and inescapable — the way Travis couldn't stop talking about the taxi medallion racket, or the way the Crusoe founders had independently arrived at an insight rooted in real physics and real waste. Andrew's Hyperion project — building a Minecraft engine targeting 170,000 concurrent players to break a Guinness World Record — is genuinely the kind of obsessive, seemingly pointless technical quest that reveals deep capability. Processing 1,000 players in 455 microseconds on 12 cores is not normal. That level of systems mastery is rare and directly transferable to building custom VMMs with sub-30-millisecond forking. But technical talent and biographical compulsion are different things, and I weight the latter more heavily at the earliest stages because it's what carries a founder through the years when everything is falling apart.

The bull case here is real and I want to be honest about it. The technical moat is genuinely deep — a custom virtual machine monitor built on KVM, with a content-addressable storage engine, a customized Linux 6.19 kernel, and a forked version of Nix written in Rust. This is not something someone replicates over a weekend. E2B would have to fundamentally rebuild their Firecracker-based architecture to match 26-millisecond full-state forking, which is the kind of structural constraint that creates durable advantage. If agentic AI workflows shift from ephemeral single-turn execution to persistent branching exploration — and that shift seems plausible — then Indexable is building the right primitive at the right time, and the incumbents are architecturally constrained from following. I backed SpaceX when building rockets privately seemed insane, and there's an echo here of betting on deep infrastructure at a technological inflection point. The founder-market fit from a pure skills perspective is exceptional.

But here's where the analogy breaks down. When I wrote my first angel check into SpaceX, private space was dismissed as lunacy. That's what gave us the price and the position. Indexable is entering a space where the biggest names in venture are already writing large checks to competitors with real traction — E2B reportedly has seven-figure monthly revenue and 88% of the Fortune 100. Andrew is bringing a knife to a gunfight in terms of funding and distribution, armed with a faster knife. Faster forking matters, but infrastructure markets often consolidate around "good enough" performance combined with ecosystem lock-in and developer familiarity. The 26ms versus 200ms advantage is technically meaningful but commercially uncertain — developers tolerate 200ms latencies all the time if the SDK is well-documented and the community is active. And the brand confusion with "indexable" as a common programming term, plus index.dev being a separate company, creates real friction for the bottom-up developer adoption motion they need.

The dimension where Indexable is weakest for my framework is economic access and real-world impact. This is infrastructure for companies building AI agents — it serves well-resourced technology teams, not underserved populations. I don't see the Uber dynamic of creating income for drivers, or the Affirm dynamic of bringing transparency to opaque credit systems. That doesn't make it a bad company. It just means it doesn't activate the part of my thesis where I get most excited. The companies I've championed hardest have always had that thread of making invisible systems visible or creating economic opportunity where none existed.

I respect what Andrew is building technically, and I could see this being a strong investment for someone whose framework weights technical moat and market timing above founder narrative and anti-consensus positioning. For me, a consensus-category infrastructure play from a technically gifted founder without a visible biographical thread to the problem doesn't generate the felt sense of conviction I need to write a check from my own money. If I sat down with Andrew and heard a story that changed my understanding of why he's the one person who has to solve this — the way the Contraline founder's years of lonely academic work told me everything I needed to know — I'd reconsider. But on what's visible today, this is a pass.
