# Mireye -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Mireye is a single-sided geospatial API wrapped in MCP, parked inside a platform-shaped GeoAI market ($37.13B in 2025 climbing to $62.88B by 2030 at 11.1% CAGR per MarketsandMarkets) -- the market is real, the compounding is not. Ansh Chokshi and Shashwat Kapoor are shipping infrastructure middleware on top of public US federal datasets, and the analytical signature here is the inverse of what I fund: market-room is the strongest leg, network compounding is conspicuously absent. That pairing tells me exactly what Mireye is -- an API arbitrage on Anthropic's late-2024 MCP standard, not a network whose Nth user makes the (N-1)th user more valuable.

**Stack Overflow** (2008) is the leverage-architecture rung I keep returning to: Jeff Atwood and Joel Spolsky shipped a developer-pull product on permissionless code, and the dev community itself was the distribution. Mireye runs the same channel theory -- self-serve API plus MCP server listings into Claude and Cursor -- but the mechanism diverges where it matters most. Stack Overflow's distribution compounded on user-generated answers; Mireye's distribution compounds on normalizing USGS, NOAA, and Census layers any ETL-competent team can ingest. The dossier names Google Maps Platform, Mapbox, and CARTO all shipping MCP servers in 2025-2026, which means Mireye is permissionless in posture but competing inside a channel the incumbents already occupy with brand and data breadth. That's leverage-architecture-as-posture, not leverage-architecture-as-moat.

The marketplace-and-network rung is where Mireye breaks hardest. The failure mechanism I watch for is single-sided economic independence -- where customer N's existence does not raise customer N+1's value -- and that is precisely Mireye's shape: a stateless API returning cited geospatial answers for any US coordinate. There is no supply side getting denser, no developer ecosystem whose contributions accrue to other developers, no two-sided pricing dynamic. Mireye is closer to a data utility than to **AngelList** (2010) or **OpenSea** (~2018), and the asymmetric-payoff math I run -- one X downside, 10,000 X upside -- requires network shape to deliver the upper bound. Without it, Mireye caps at a strategic acquisition multiple, not a category-defining outcome.

On specific knowledge, the mechanism compounds over 18 months only if the founders accumulate something un-trainable -- proprietary indexing logic, citation-quality benchmarks, integration depth that a Stanford-CS clone team cannot replicate inside a quarter. Chokshi's Seismic AI/ML lead role and Kapoor's six years of data infrastructure at Tools for Humanity and Lyra Health are credible data-engineering pedigree, but neither carries the geospatial-specific immersion that Aravind Srinivas brought to **Perplexity** (2022) via a Berkeley retrieval PhD. The Poker Pit $250K ARR pre-acquisition exit at 21 tells me Chokshi can ship and sell -- it does not tell me Mireye has discovered something about terrain, land cover, or risk data that Mapbox engineers haven't already shipped.

Counterfactual on market size and pivot-room: Mireye's $37B-$62B GeoAI TAM is platform-shaped enough that I can sketch at least two adjacent vectors -- vertical climate-risk underwriting for insurance, or property-intelligence APIs for real estate -- where Chokshi's data-infra background plus federal-dataset normalization becomes a defensible wedge. For this dimension to flip to a higher tier, the dossier would need a named pivot the founders have publicly considered or one signed LOI from an insurance or reinsurance counterparty; absent that, Mireye gets credit for the room without credit for the choreography.

Single-anchor parallel on founder trio: I compare Mireye's team to **Notion** (2013), where Ivan Zhao's clean integrity reads and shipped artifacts gated my check before product-market fit existed. Mireye reads neutral on the integrity leg -- Chokshi's Poker Pit acquisition appears to have closed cleanly per the YC snippet, no contested-exit pattern, no self-promotion about ethics in the public surface, no 50/50 unresolved-CEO red flag. The Buffett trio passes without enthusiasm, which is the most common state for two-founder pre-seed teams and not where checks get written or refused.

The 36-month forecast: Mireye gets acqui-hired by Mapbox or a vertical buyer (an insurance-underwriting platform like CAPE Analytics or a climate-risk firm) at sub-$30M, OR pivots from horizontal agent-grounding infrastructure into a vertical risk-data product where the federal-dataset normalization layer becomes a wedge against incumbents who don't bother with US-only depth. The mechanism producing this is the dimensional signature itself: real market plus no network compounding plus public-domain underlying data is the canonical profile of a strategic acqui-hire, not a category winner. Modal outcome is sub-$5M ARR stall and a soft landing in 2028-2029.

The strongest objection runs through **Perplexity** as a Known Exception: single-sided API products can win if leverage architecture, specific knowledge, and category timing all clear top quartile. Mireye has the MCP-timing call (Anthropic shipped the standard late 2024, mainstream mapping vendors adopted it in 2025-2026, a focused team can target the standardized surface before it ossifies) and arguably has code-leverage distribution. The bear-case dismantle: the Perplexity override required Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD as the load-bearing specific-knowledge gate, and Mireye's founders do not carry the geospatial-domain equivalent -- they carry general data-infra pedigree, which Mapbox already has at scale. The exception does not apply. The condition that flips my verdict is concrete: Mireye ships a named LOI or paid integration with a top-five US insurance or reinsurance carrier within 12 months, OR posts 10,000+ MCP-server installs with documented repeat-usage, before any Series A discussion. Either signal would tell me the agent-native geospatial wedge is real and not a posture; absent either, Mireye is a neutral pass.
