# Pulsent -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Toplyne raised $17.5M from Tiger Global and Sequoia to do almost exactly what Pulsent is doing — identify PLG conversion signals in B2B SaaS and route them to sales teams — then shut down and returned the capital to investors after failing to find product-market fit. That's not ancient history; it's a fresh data point about this specific category. Pulsent's twist is adding autonomous execution on top of the signal identification, which is a real architectural difference, but the underlying question is the same one Toplyne couldn't answer: do B2B SaaS companies actually want a third-party tool sitting between their analytics stack and their customer communication channels? The integration complexity is brutal — PostHog, Mixpanel, Amplitude, Snowflake, BigQuery, Stripe, Salesforce, Attio, Intercom, Loops, Customer.io — and every new customer requires configuring data pipelines across multiple tools before Pulsent can deliver any value. That's not schlep blindness in the way I mean it. Pulsent isn't tackling an unglamorous problem that repels other founders. It's tackling a popular problem — "AI agents for sales" — that has attracted enormous capital and attention across the industry. Intercom's Fin AI agent is approaching $100M ARR. Pendo surpassed $200M ARR and is adding AI features. Nobody hears "AI agents that watch and convert your users" and says "that will never work." They say "oh cool, who else is doing that?" — which is the tell.

Nicolò Magnante's background is where my framework runs into friction. Ex-Bain, ex-BCG, HEC Paris Startup Launchpad. I've written about this pattern explicitly — I see it in almost every batch. Two former consultants identify a market gap through competitive analysis, describe it in terms of TAM and competitive positioning, and build a polished narrative around it. Pulsent's problem framing reads exactly like a consulting slide: "analytics platforms observe but do not act; engagement platforms act but lack behavioral context." That's a 2x2 matrix, not a personal frustration. Nothing in the dossier suggests Nicolò was running a SaaS product, personally watching user sessions to prevent churn at 3am, and built Pulsent because he couldn't stand doing it manually. He and Arseniy were running Bluco, an AI recruitment company that won a €25K AXA grant and had clients including Porsche, then pivoted to Pulsent in early 2026. The pivot itself isn't damning — I gave the Reddit founders their idea after rejecting their original pitch — but with Alexis and Steve, I could see the animal quality in person, the speed at which they absorbed feedback. Here, the origin story reads like market research, not organic discovery.

Arseniy Shishaev is the stronger signal. Three years at Datadog (2021-2024) building tools for internal data pipelines managing "hundreds of millions of data assets using complex recursive queries" — that maps directly to Pulsent's event-processing core. The 42 engineering school in Paris is a genuine hacker credential; it self-selects for people who love building. If Pulsent's "hundreds of millions of events daily" claim is accurate, Arseniy's Datadog experience is doing real work. But his GitHub shows three forked repos and one follower, which is thin for a CTO claiming to build infrastructure at scale. When I found Replit on Hacker News in 2017, Amjad Masad's public building was part of the signal — he was shipping in the open, obsessively, for anyone to see. Arseniy's public presence suggests competence, not obsession.

Pulsent has paying customers — three named testimonials from Pletor, Leadbay (YC F25), and Basalt — but no revenue figures disclosed, which at pre-seed usually means the numbers are small enough that disclosing them would hurt more than help. The YC description claims "strong demand from PLG B2B Clients" without quantification. The co-founders have worked together for 3+ years across Bluco and now Pulsent, which is a real positive — co-founder stability through a pivot reduces one of the top startup killers I've identified. And Nicolò claims to have "scaled two startups to millions in ARR," though the dossier can only partially verify this through Bluco's grant win and named enterprise clients. The learning rate evidence is mixed: they pivoted from recruitment AI to user engagement AI, which shows flexibility, but the pivot is so recent that the current product form is barely established. The name change from "Swish" to "Pulsent" suggests they're still iterating on positioning, which could be either a sign of learning or a sign of searching.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Arseniy's Datadog infrastructure experience creates a genuine technical advantage in building the data unification layer that makes Pulsent work. The co-founders have been battle-tested together across two companies over three years. They already have paying customers at pre-seed. The "why now" argument — multimodal LLMs capable of interpreting session recordings becoming viable in late 2025 — creates a legitimate technological discontinuity that incumbents like Intercom and Pendo aren't positioned to exploit because they'd need to rebuild their data architectures. And Pulsent's autonomous execution differentiates it from Toplyne, which only identified signals for human reps. I've checked my Known Exceptions: Pulsent doesn't fit the Boom pattern (not frighteningly ambitious), the Watsi pattern (not pure mission), or the ClassDojo pattern (not an unsexy domain that creates natural moats through schlep). None of them apply here.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Nicolò could tell me a story about personally doing customer success for a SaaS product — not advising one as a consultant, but actually watching user sessions, sending personalized outreach at 2am, and discovering that the manual version of Pulsent was so effective that customers begged him to automate it. That would be Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive — organic discovery through lived frustration. Right now, Pulsent reads like a company built from a market map, not from a midnight frustration. The consulting-origin pattern, the consensus-attractive category, and the Toplyne precedent combine to form a coherent picture: a competent team pursuing an idea that sounds good to everyone in a category that has already claimed one well-funded casualty. The founders may prove me wrong — the pivot from Bluco shows they have the flexibility half of the "relentlessly resourceful" equation — but I need to see the relentlessness, and the dossier gives me credentials where I want stories.
