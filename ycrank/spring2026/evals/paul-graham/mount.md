# Mount -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Mount is the rarest configuration in my notebook: outsiders correctly diagnosing a schlep that incumbents are actively fleeing, paired with zero personal anchor to the pain they propose to underwrite. Fabian Amherd and John Bachmann are pitching an AI-agent insurance carrier from San Francisco while Berkshire, Chubb, and Travelers have just gotten 80%+ of their requests approved to *exclude* AI damages from general liability. That gap is real. The question is whether two Swiss operators who have never written a policy can be the ones who fill it.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the high rung on schlep-zone fit, and Mount sits right under it. Brian Armstrong walked into a regulatory grind nobody at YC wanted to touch -- bitcoin compliance, state-by-state money transmitter licensing, banking partners who wouldn't return calls. Mount is hunting an isomorphic schlep: rebuilding underwriting, claims adjudication, and capacity sourcing for a peril that Munich Re's aiSure program caps at $15M after *eight years* of data. The 80%+ exclusion-approval rate at PYMNTS is the same regulator-shaped vacuum Armstrong walked into. Where Mount diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) rung is the asymmetry of who is doing the running -- Armstrong personally pulled the licensing applications; Mount markets itself as a "carrier" on the YC page with no disclosed Lloyd's syndicate, no admitted-lines paper, no named reinsurer. Armilla AI sits on Chaucer's Vanguard structure; Testudo runs through Atrium/QBE via Lloyd's Lab. Mount has the word "carrier" and nothing behind it yet.

The Pass Example -- a two-MBA team pitching an AI wrapper they had never personally needed -- is the failure mechanism I want to test against Mount on organic-idea fit. That mechanism is *founders did competitive analysis instead of suffering the problem*. Trace it through Mount's dossier: Amherd built CNNs for industrial robotics at MESH; Bachmann ran a five-magazine media business in Switzerland. Neither has shipped an AI agent into production at an enterprise that subsequently faced a prompt-injection claim. Neither has filed a cyber policy and been told it was "silent or ambiguous" on autonomous workflow errors. The Mount pitch reads exactly the way the Pass Example reads -- a market gap identified from the outside, not a tool the founders built because they kept losing money to it. **Dropbox** (2007)'s Drew Houston forgot his USB stick. Mount's founders forgot nothing; they read the same Risk & Insurance article I did.

On animal resourcefulness, the dossier hands me real pre-funding artifacts, and I want to weight them honestly. Bachmann founded Horizonn at 18 and pushed it to $400K annual revenue across five magazines -- in Switzerland, in print, in 2020s media economics. Amherd ran a web development company for 3.5 years starting at age 16. Neither is a cereal-box stunt, but both are teenage operator track records of the kind that compound. Mount loses points only because these animal moments predate the AI thesis by years and were not deployed *to rescue this specific company* the way Armstrong's Android wallet rescued **Coinbase** (2012) post-rejection.

The hacker-genealogy dimension compounds badly over 18 months for Mount. ETH Zürich CS plus MESH robotics is credentialed technical depth, and the red-teaming product Mount describes -- scanning AI deployments for prompt injection vectors -- requires exactly Amherd's skill set. But there is no GitHub footprint, no open-source contribution, no Hacker News presence pre-dating the company. The mechanism that worries me: red-teaming products live or die by reputation in a security community that vets you on prior published artifacts. AIUC raised $15M from Nat Friedman partly because Friedman knows that community. Mount entering it cold, against AIUC's war chest, means the technical-credibility flywheel starts spinning later or not at all.

Co-founder configuration is a counterfactual exercise: what would have to be true to flip Mount from neutral to strong on team chemistry? The dossier gives me two Swiss founders, inferred network overlap, no shared prior employer, no documented years of building together. **Airbnb** (2009)'s trio roomed together; **Reddit** (2005)'s Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates; **Stripe** (2010) was brothers. To flip Mount to the top rung I would need to learn Amherd and Bachmann had been collaborating since secondary school in Switzerland -- plausible from the geography, unstated in the dossier. I default to neutral, leaning slightly negative because the YC interview is where I would normally resolve this and I do not have that signal.

My 36-month forecast on Mount: capacity-constrained as a self-styled carrier with no Lloyd's or A-rated reinsurer disclosed, outgunned on the demand side by AIUC's $15M and Klaimee's CEO who already scaled an insurance product from $5M to $60M ARR at SafetyWing, Mount pivots within 18 months into a pure red-teaming/risk-scoring SaaS for AI deployments at sub-$2M ARR and is acquihired by an insurtech platform or one of Klaimee/AIUC for under $25M by Q2 2029. The mechanism is the carrier-versus-MGA capital gap: you cannot underwrite without paper, and paper requires a relationship Mount has not shown.

The opposing case is the **Airbnb** (2009) exception, and it stings. I funded a team whose idea I had mentally rejected because the cereal-box stunt revealed founders who would not stop. Bachmann's $400K magazine business at 18 is structurally the same kind of revealed-preference data -- a teenager who shipped revenue against the grain of his market. Mount could be the Pass I regret. What dismantles it: the cereal boxes were *for Airbnb*, deployed when the company was already drowning, and they convinced me the founders would solve the next problem too. Mount's animal moments predate the AI thesis and tell me Bachmann and Amherd are operators, not that they will personally walk into Hartford and convince a reinsurance underwriter to back a peril Munich Re caps at $15M after eight years. I flip from Pass to Invest if Mount discloses a named A-rated capacity provider plus one paying enterprise pilot within 90 days of this evaluation. Absent that, the schlep is real but the wrong team is running at it.
