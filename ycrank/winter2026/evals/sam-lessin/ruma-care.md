# Ruma Care -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: the CTO has been taking biologics for seven years and has personally navigated the insurance denial gauntlet. That's the kind of founder-market fit you can't fake. And Shen didn't just read about rheumatology workflows — he went and worked as a medical assistant in a rheumatology clinic. These are sane, operationally rigorous people. I'm not questioning the founders. I'm questioning the idea, because "automate prior authorizations with AI" is one of the most consensus plays in healthcare AI right now. Cohere Health has $200M. Develop Health just raised $17.6M. Waystar is a public company doing a billion in revenue with PA as a feature. When I look at Ruma, I see sane people with a sane idea — and sane ideas in crowded consensus categories don't produce venture-scale returns because the upside is already priced into the market.

The biologics niche is clever positioning, and I'll grant that the buy-and-bill economics create genuine urgency — when a provider is eating $80-150K on a denied infusion drug, they'll pay real money for authorization success. The reported 50% denial rate reduction and 20x staff time savings are significant if they hold up at scale. But here's where my framework gets sharp: the moat is entirely software and data. The "denial pattern learning" argument is a data flywheel pitch — I've heard this story hundreds of times across healthcare AI, fintech AI, legal AI. The data compounds, sure, but the question is whether that compounding creates genuine lock-in or just a temporary lead that a well-funded competitor erases in eighteen months. And the CMS FHIR mandate requiring standardized PA APIs by January 2027 is a perfect illustration of why this moat is fragile. That regulation looks like a tailwind for Ruma today, but structurally it commoditizes the exact integration layer they're building on. When every payer has to expose a standardized API, the technical complexity that currently creates Ruma's advantage evaporates. Epic could build this natively. Oracle Health could build this natively. The regulatory "why now" is simultaneously the regulatory "why not for long."

This fails my "cherry on top" test completely. If AI didn't exist, this business doesn't exist. The entire value proposition IS the AI — extracting payer rules, auto-filling forms, learning denial patterns. There's no physical infrastructure, no operational complexity that software amplifies but can't replace, no protocol position that others build on. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews showing up at houses — AI makes the scheduling better, but no amount of software replicates the labor. Ruma is the inverse: software IS the business, and that's the category I've been writing essays about for two years. "Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore." A startup whose entire defensibility is an AI model trained on denial patterns is building exactly the kind of moat that dissolves fastest as foundation models get cheaper and more capable.

The bull case I'll engage with honestly: the biologics-specific formulary intelligence — mapping every medication-diagnosis-insurance combination to approval criteria — is genuinely hard domain work that broad RCM platforms won't prioritize. The Formulary Navigator as a free lead-gen tool is smart distribution. The founder team has both the technical chops (Apple ML, Uber scale) and the visceral domain understanding (patient experience, clinical immersion) to execute well. If Ruma can build a sufficiently deep data moat in the biologics niche before the FHIR mandate levels the playing field, and if they can expand from PA automation into a broader biologics access platform — copay enrollment, patient support programs, outcomes tracking — there's a path to becoming genuinely essential infrastructure for infusion clinics. The $60B in unaccessed care is a real problem. But "real problem with a software solution in a consensus category" describes most of the companies I pass on. The structural position — application layer on EHR infrastructure, SaaS business model, AI as the core value proposition — triggers too many of my anti-patterns simultaneously.

I'd need to see something structurally different to get excited here. If they were building a new data standard for biologics formulary information that payers and providers both integrated against — a protocol-layer play rather than an application — that would change the calculus entirely. Or if they were vertically integrating into specialty pharmacy operations, creating physical-world complexity that software amplifies but can't replace. As it stands, this is competent founders building a good product in a crowded, consensus category with a software moat that I believe AI will erode rather than strengthen. Pass.
