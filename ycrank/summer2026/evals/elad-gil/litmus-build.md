# Litmus -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Litmus is a clean-architecture, no-growth-signal AI wrapper -- one plausible miracle (LLM-graded, repo-context assessments), $60K ARR, and no month-over-month curve to point at. That's the signature: the *single-miracle* dimension is the highest thing on the page and *revenue velocity* is the lowest, which is exactly the pattern I described on Tim Ferriss #863 when I said "if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken." Shaivi Rau and Elena Zhao have built a real product with ATS integrations into Ashby, Greenhouse, and Lever, but the dossier does not show the curve that makes me pay up.

Perplexity (2022) is the load-bearing comparison for how I score founder shipping rhythm, and it's why Litmus doesn't score lower on that axis than it does. Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and shipped a thing we'd discussed a week later -- that's the template. Litmus's evidence is thinner but not empty: two Columbia seniors, no GitHub trail, no named side projects, but a two-person team that has stood up native integrations with three ATSes (Ashby, Greenhouse, Lever) and processed 3,000+ assessments while still in a YC batch. That is shipping, just not the specific "artifact between meetings" cadence that made Perplexity a cold-inbound conviction check. Litmus lands mid-ladder on execution rhythm because the artifacts exist but the iteration signature I filter on doesn't.

Lyft Series C is the analog on the axis where Litmus is weakest -- revenue velocity -- and the mechanism there was that I couldn't see a curve that inverted the obvious #1's economics. Litmus's $60K ARR with no MoM disclosed, one named client (Neo Scholars), and no pricing page is the pre-seed version of that same silence. In an AI batch cycle where real growth is trivial to disclose -- Harvey went $3B → $5B → $8B in months (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) -- the absence of a disclosed curve for Litmus is itself the signal. It's not that $60K is bad; it's that the dossier's silence on trajectory is what a "false signal vs. just working" filter is designed to catch.

The market re-opening catalyst for Litmus is genuine but softer than my usual triggers -- Claude Code / Copilot CLI adoption in 2025-2026 made 2018-era assessment platforms structurally obsolete, and even CodeSignal's own blog concedes assessments "still look like 2018." Over 18 months that catalyst compounds if candidates keep adopting AI coding tools faster than HackerRank and CodeSignal can retool their shared-library businesses; it decays if the incumbents ship repo-context generation as an SKU, which is a two-quarter engineering project for either of them. The catalyst is real -- it's just not defense-post-Ukraine real, and Litmus is not the only startup that will notice.

For the index-position dimension, the counterfactual is what would flip Litmus into a real distribution wedge: exclusive default-status inside Ashby or Greenhouse, the way Stripe (2010) became the default inside developer stacks. Today Litmus is *integrated* into Ashby, Greenhouse, and Lever -- not embedded as the default assessment layer, and not compounding automatically as hiring volume grows across those ATSes. Without that exclusivity or a revenue-share embed, Litmus has to win against HackerRank (G2 4.6, 553 reviews) and CodeSignal (~$87M raised, G2 4.5, 1,435 reviews) on features and calibration data, which is a share fight, not an index position. Litmus's 3,000 assessments is a rounding error against those review-count denominators.

Compare Litmus's single-miracle profile to Decagon (2023) on this axis alone: Decagon assumed LLMs were good enough to run a customer-support workflow and spent the rest of its execution budget on GTM and integrations. Litmus is making the equivalent assumption -- that LLMs are good enough to *grade* end-to-end feature work objectively -- and the dossier's Key Risks section flags exactly that assumption as unvalidated at scale, with no accuracy benchmark published. It's one miracle, cleanly isolated, which is why I don't score this dimension harshly; but it's a miracle where the evidence of validation is 3,000 graded submissions and one named client, not a published accuracy number a hiring manager can defend to their VP Eng.

My 36-month forecast for Litmus: $400K-$1.2M ARR by mid-2029, no Series A from a top-tier growth fund, and either an acqui-hire into Ashby or Greenhouse at low-nine-figures at best, or a pivot into AI-native engineer sourcing / interview-agent tooling once HackerRank or CodeSignal ships their own repo-context generation SKU. The mechanism is the commoditization risk the dossier already names -- Litmus's generation layer is a thin wrapper over foundation models, and the only durable piece is per-account grading calibration, which incumbents can replicate the moment they see churn to Litmus.

The strongest opposing case is that Litmus's ATS integrations with Ashby, Greenhouse, and Lever are the actual moat, not the generation layer -- the same way I initially underestimated Stripe (2010) because I was looking at payments and missing developer-channel embedding. If Litmus becomes the default assessment layer inside Ashby's fast-growing modern-ATS install base, the grading calibration compounds per-account, incumbents can't rip out an embedded workflow without customer revolt, and Litmus rides Ashby's growth as an index position on modern-engineering-hiring. That case stings because it maps to a pattern I've paid for before. It loses on today's evidence: Litmus is *integrated with* three ATSes, not defaulted into any of them, and $60K ARR after building those integrations means the embed isn't converting into pull-through yet. Verifiable flip condition: if within 12 months Litmus discloses an exclusive default-assessment partnership with Ashby or Greenhouse *and* $1M+ ARR growing >20% MoM, I re-underwrite at Series A.
