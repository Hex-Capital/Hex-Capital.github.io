# Vector Legal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The ghost of Atrium is the first thing I see here, and I can't look away from it. Justin Kan raised $75.5 million trying to build exactly this -- a hybrid AI law firm plus software platform for startups -- and it collapsed in 2020 because the services business and the technology business pulled in opposite directions. The bull case for Vector Legal is that LLMs have changed the equation entirely, that what was impossible with 2018-era NLP is now achievable with GPT-4 class models. And that's probably true on the technology axis. But Atrium's failure wasn't fundamentally about AI capability -- it was about the organizational physics of running a law firm and a software company simultaneously, about the tension between billable humans and scalable software, about whether the services margin ever converges with the software margin. The two-entity structure here (P.C. plus PBC) is clever and shows legal sophistication, but cleverness in corporate structure doesn't resolve the operational contradiction that killed Atrium.

When I evaluate founders, I want to hear the story before the business plan. And what I find here are two very accomplished professionals -- Mitch Duncombe with a teaching career that pivoted into Berkeley Law, then Fenwick, then three years at YC Legal; Keenan Venuti with Harvard, Adobe, Accenture, and a Staff AI Engineer role at Ironclad building exactly the kind of contract intelligence tools Vector Legal needs. On paper, the founder-market fit is almost too perfect. Duncombe has literally processed the legal workflows of thousands of YC startups. Venuti built AI for the leading contract management platform. But here's what I'm not hearing: the personal story of adversity that makes this founder-problem connection unbreakable. The teaching-to-law-to-startup arc is interesting, but it's a career progression, not a survival story. Nobody here was kept awake at night by this problem for years before starting the company. When the inevitable crisis hits -- and with a hybrid model, it will -- what keeps these founders in the ring? Professional interest in a well-scoped opportunity is different from the biographical inevitability I look for. Travis Kalanick had a gravitas about the taxi medallion system that came from years of obsession. The Crusoe team had independently arrived at an insight about waste gas that was inseparable from who they were. I don't feel that here.

The other thing I notice -- and this is where I part ways with how most investors would evaluate this -- is that AI-powered legal services for startups is about as consensus as it gets right now. Harvey just raised $300 million. Legal tech AI is growing at 33% CAGR. Every VC in the Valley is looking at this category. When I backed SpaceX, the entire world thought private space was insane. When I wrote the check for Uber, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities. The most promising companies are unpopular and difficult to assess. Vector Legal is popular and easy to understand. A YC legal counsel starting an AI law firm for YC startups -- I can explain that to anyone in ten seconds. That's precisely the kind of obvious, well-constructed thesis that makes me nervous, because the best investments I've made looked terrible to everyone else at first.

Now, I want to be honest about where this company does resonate with patterns I care about. Making quality legal counsel accessible to early-stage startups who currently either overpay BigLaw rates or go without representation -- that's the same kind of economic access play I saw in Affirm making transparent credit available, or Carta making cap table equity visible instead of buried in opaque spreadsheets. The regulatory moat of being a licensed law firm is real and hard to replicate -- pure software companies literally cannot offer legal advice, which means Vector Legal occupies a structural position that tech-only competitors can't touch without getting their own bar admissions and malpractice insurance. That combination of making a complex system transparent while operating behind a regulatory barrier is genuinely interesting, and it's the piece of this company that I'd want to explore further.

But the strongest version of this bull case still requires me to believe that the hybrid services-plus-software model works at scale this time, that the founders' professional credentials substitute for the biographical grit I usually require, and that being in a hot category doesn't mean the weirdness has been arbitraged away. I can see a scenario where Duncombe's YC network gives them unbeatable distribution, where Venuti's Ironclad experience means the AI layer is genuinely superior, and where the timing window is exactly right. If that's all true, this could be a meaningful company. But I'm not the right investor for a bet that requires all those things to align. I take extraordinary risk on people whose personal stories tell me they'll persist through anything -- and on ideas so weird that nobody else will fund them. Vector Legal is a well-reasoned company built by competent professionals in a popular category, and I think plenty of investors will want in. That's exactly why I don't feel the jolt.
