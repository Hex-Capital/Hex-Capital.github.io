# BioStack Platforms -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Tempus has $532M in annual revenue and the world's largest clinical and molecular data library. Recursion sits on 50+ petabytes of proprietary biological data. Insitro has raised $700M+ and has milestone-based pharma deals with Lilly and BMS. BioStack Platforms is a two-person pre-seed company with no disclosed data partnerships, no disclosed revenue, and no disclosed institutional agreements — trying to be the horizontal data supplier in a market where the vertically integrated incumbents already own the raw material. That's the core tension, and it's not a pricing anomaly the market is missing. It's a structural disadvantage the market has correctly identified.

BioStack Platforms sits squarely in the AI infrastructure category — "training data for healthcare AI models" — which is about as consensus-hot as it gets in Spring 2026. Scale AI is valued at $7.3B+ doing exactly this across industries. Every sophisticated LP and VC I talk to is excited about AI picks-and-shovels plays. When I say "find cool shit that others do not believe," I mean the opposite of this. BioStack Platforms is building in a category where ten other VCs at demo day are nodding along. The risk isn't baked into the price — the enthusiasm is baked into the price. And when I look at the TAM numbers cited — $423M in 2024 for AI training datasets in healthcare, projected $1.47B by 2030 — those are the kind of numbers that make every generalist VC lean forward, which means the asymmetry I'm looking for simply isn't there.

Sanat Mishra's cancer genomics research across Stanford, Yale, CMU, and Max-Planck is real domain credibility — he's not a tourist in healthcare. Parth Patwa's 1,823 Google Scholar citations and Amazon/AWS engineering experience round out a technically credible team. But here's the problem with my sane-insane test: both the people and the idea are reasonable. "Build better data pipelines for healthcare AI" is sensible, incremental, validated by market size reports. Nobody hearing this pitch thinks "that's fucking crazy" — they think "that makes sense." And that's exactly when I get nervous, because sensible ideas in hot categories get priced at premiums that eat the returns. The patent filing (US App. 63/875,328) on automated biomedical research using LLM agents shows genuine technical thinking, but a provisional patent application is aspiration, not moat.

The bull case an advocate would make: Tempus and Recursion are vertically integrated — they consume their own data internally for diagnostics and drug discovery. They're structurally disincentivized from becoming horizontal data suppliers because licensing data externally would cannibalize their competitive advantage and require renegotiating hospital data use agreements. BioStack Platforms could be the neutral aggregator precisely because it doesn't compete downstream. Mishra understands clinical data workflows from inside research institutions, and the RL-environment framing is genuinely differentiated from static dataset labeling. The "why now" is real: post-training and reinforcement learning have become the primary performance levers for foundation models, and nobody has built the healthcare-specific training environment layer yet. That's a reasonable argument. But it's the same argument every horizontal platform makes before the vertically integrated players decide to license — Flatiron Health was going to be the neutral oncology data platform until Roche acquired them for $1.9B and locked the data up. BioStack Platforms has no disclosed hospital partnerships, no disclosed CRO relationships, no disclosed revenue signals of any kind. The entire value proposition depends on data access they haven't demonstrated they have.

BioStack Platforms fails my AI kill test in a specific way: it's not that AI commoditizes their product, it's that AI incumbents already possess the underlying asset. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the parking infrastructure was physical — no algorithm could replicate control of 4,000+ parking facilities. BioStack Platforms has no physical asset, no protocol-level network effect, no data that exists today behind their firewall. Their "proprietary clinical and preclinical data pipelines" are described on a website but unsubstantiated by named partnerships. Recursion's 50+ petabytes are real. Tempus's $532M revenue proves their data access is real. BioStack Platforms is asking me to believe they'll build data moats from scratch against incumbents who've spent billions building theirs. Pure software, no lock-in, no physical presence — this is exactly the kind of company where software moats are "learning they aren't moats anymore."

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if BioStack Platforms showed me signed data access agreements with two or more named hospital systems or CROs, with exclusive or preferential terms for RL-environment structuring — that would be physical-world data access that changes the structural durability picture entirely. Without that, this is a pitch deck, not a data company. The two most decisive dimensions are Consensus Mispricing (this is a consensus-hot AI infrastructure play, not a contrarian bet) and Structural Durability (no disclosed data assets, no physical moat, pure software competing against billion-dollar incumbents who already own the data). Pass.
