# Sherpa -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sherpa is that no one would ever say "that will never work." AI-powered conversion rate optimization? Every marketing VP in Silicon Valley is nodding along. Optimizely, VWO, Mutiny — billion-dollar incumbents already own the CRO category, and every one of them is bolting LLM-powered variant generation onto their platforms right now. When I hear "AI agent that automatically writes headlines and runs A/B tests," I don't hear the uncomfortable silence that preceded Airbnb or Coinbase. I hear polite agreement from a room full of people who can already name four competitors doing the same thing. The way to find good startup ideas is to look for the ones that sound wrong. This one sounds right. That's the problem.

The founders both came from Lindy, an AI agent platform, and their only named customer is — Lindy. I want to be fair here: working at an AI agent company gave them proximity to the tools and techniques for building autonomous workflows, and Sonam shipped real product at Microsoft, including the image generation features in Copilot that made it into a Super Bowl ad. That's not nothing. But proximity to agent technology is different from organic discovery of a painful problem. Neither founder is a marketer who stayed up at night cursing at Optimizely dashboards. They observed marketers' frustration from the partnerships and engineering side of a different company, then decided to build a tool for those marketers. When someone tells me they "identified an opportunity in CRO" after working at an AI platform, what I hear is a solution looking for the most obvious problem it could attach to. The best startup ideas come from founders who lived inside the problem so deeply they couldn't not build the solution. I don't see that here.

The competitive dynamics are what concern me most. Mutiny has raised $72.6M and already does AI-powered website optimization for B2B marketers. Optimizely has $400M in ARR and every incentive to ship an automated variant generation feature. The "why now" for Sherpa is that LLMs can generate plausible marketing copy — but that same "why now" applies equally to every incumbent in the space. This is the opposite of schlep blindness. CRO is a category that attracts founders precisely because it's well-understood, has clear buyer personas, and connects to measurable ROI. Nobody's unconscious is filtering this idea out. Stripe worked because Patrick Collison was willing to wade into the regulatory swamp of payment processing — a problem that made other hackers recoil. There's no equivalent repulsion mechanism protecting Sherpa's territory. Any team with API access to a foundation model and a JavaScript snippet can reproduce the core capability.

The strongest bull case would be this: the incumbents are structurally unable to fully automate CRO because their business models depend on professional services, agency partnerships, and enterprise contracts that reward complexity. A two-person startup with no legacy customers could move faster and make the whole manual testing workflow obsolete. If Sherpa's data flywheel from the claimed 1M+ visits compounds into genuinely superior variant selection — if they learn which headline patterns convert across industries faster than anyone else — that could create a real moat. And Sonam's experience shipping AI features at Microsoft scale means the technical execution could be strong. But I keep coming back to the fact that Mutiny already has distribution, data, and $72M to fund exactly this kind of feature expansion. The moat theory requires Sherpa to accumulate data faster than well-funded competitors who already have thousands of customers generating that same data. That's a bet I wouldn't take with my own money.

Both founders are early in their careers — Kinnan graduated in 2024, Sonam from RPI around the same time. I've backed young founders before; age isn't the issue. The issue is that I don't see the stories. When I evaluate determination, I ask founders to tell me about times things went wrong and what they did. This dossier has no such stories. Finquire, Kinnan's prior company, appears to have evaporated without a trace. There's no narrative of scrappy workarounds, no evidence of the team doing things that don't scale to acquire their first customers outside their existing employer's network. The 1M+ visits number is unverifiable, and the only social proof is a testimonial from the place they used to work. These aren't red flags exactly — they're the absence of the green flags I look for. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes the way sparks fly from a grinding wheel. The silence here is itself informative.
