# Hex Security -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing I see, and it tells me almost everything I need to know. XBOW has raised $117M and already has enterprise bank customers and a top HackerOne ranking. Novee has $51.5M and a proprietary offensive security model that outperforms frontier LLMs by 55% on exploitation benchmarks. Aikido is at $84.5M and a $1B valuation with $13M ARR growing 5x year-over-year. Then there are YC peers Casco and MindFort building the same thing, plus open-source tools like PentestAgent and PentAGI commoditizing the base capability. This is not a non-obvious market at a structural inflection -- this is a consensus-hot market where the entire venture industry recognized the AI-meets-pentesting thesis simultaneously. When I see five well-funded competitors, two YC peers, and multiple open-source projects all converging on the same insight at the same time, that's the opposite of the pattern I look for. PagerDuty faced funding resistance because operations alerting seemed boring. Nobody thinks autonomous pentesting is boring right now.

The "why now" is real -- LLMs and RL crossed the capability threshold for multi-step attack chain reasoning in 2024-2025, and the chronic cybersecurity talent shortage creates genuine pull for automation. But this timing thesis is universally recognized. XBOW's $75M Series B closed in June 2025. Novee raised its Series A in January 2026. Every investor who follows AI and security has mapped this inflection point. Compare this to Harvey's situation in 2022, when legal AI looked like a science project to most investors because GPT-3 wasn't good enough yet -- Harvey was building ahead of the capability curve before the market validated the thesis. Hex Security isn't building ahead of anything. They're entering a market where the structural moment has been widely identified, capital has already flooded in, and the early movers have compounding advantages in data, customers, and model performance.

The product-to-distribution trajectory concerns me almost as much as the competitive dynamics. Finding vulnerabilities is a point solution. The dossier shows no evidence the founders have thought about how autonomous pentesting becomes a distribution channel for additional security products -- remediation, compliance automation, continuous monitoring, developer security workflows. Stripe's developer API was a distribution strategy from day one, not just a payment product. I see no analogous distribution thinking here. Their initial GTM of testing against YC-batch peers is a fine proof-of-concept motion, but it's not a scalable distribution wedge. And when XBOW already has large bank and enterprise customers, the enterprise sales channel is going to be extremely difficult to crack with $500K against their $117M.

The strongest bull case I can construct: penetration testing is a $2B market today because it's manual and expensive, but automated pentesting could expand the addressable market 5-10x by making continuous testing affordable for the millions of companies that currently can't justify annual engagements. That would make this look like PagerDuty -- "boring but critical" infrastructure that every software-running company needs. Ahmad Khan's decade of professional pentesting experience at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM gives genuine domain credibility, and the team demonstrating real critical vulnerabilities across dozens of YC companies shows a working product, not vaporware. If Hex Security's RL-trained agents develop a compounding data advantage through deployment breadth, they could potentially differentiate on accuracy and coverage. For this to work, you'd need to believe that the market will support multiple winners at scale (like cybersecurity broadly has), that Hex Security's RL approach creates technical differentiation that compounds faster than XBOW's deployment lead, and that the founders can execute an enterprise sales motion that overcomes a 200x funding disadvantage. That's three things that need to go right -- which is two more miracles than I'm comfortable with.

The co-founder dynamic also flags a concern. No shared prior employer, no shared university, no public evidence of a pre-existing working relationship. The three founders come from UC Berkeley, UT Austin, and Georgia Tech -- three different schools, three different graduation years (Khan graduated 2013, Huzaifa 2025, Prama's year unclear). I've watched enough pre-PMF startups die from co-founder conflict to treat untested working relationships as real risk, especially when the company will face the stress of competing against opponents with 100x more capital. This isn't disqualifying on its own, but layered on top of the competitive and market concerns, it adds to the risk profile.

The execution signals are the one genuinely positive note. Finding SQL injection exposing billions of records and building a proof-of-concept worm across real production environments at YC companies is a tangible demonstration -- not a pitch deck, but shipped capability that works. Khan's security background is directly relevant, not adjacent. Yudhistira's experience building autonomous coding agents at Codegen maps well to building autonomous pentesting agents. But execution velocity alone doesn't solve the structural problem: this team is building a competitive product in a consensus market against entrenched, well-capitalized competitors with no clear distribution advantage. That's a race I don't want to fund at pre-seed odds.
