# 9 Mothers -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about 9 Mothers isn't the product or the market — it's the CTO's biography. Roman Khomenko has a master's from Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics, served in the Police of Ukraine, and has spent the last decade building production ML systems. Kharkiv has been one of the most heavily drone-attacked cities on earth since 2022. A Ukrainian AI engineer with a degree in radioelectronics building counter-drone systems — that's not someone who "identified a market opportunity." That's someone who watched the problem land on his doorstep. I can't confirm from the dossier that this is the origin story, but the convergence of his education, his nationality, and this product is too specific to be accidental. If I'm right, this is one of the more authentic organic discovery signals I've seen at this stage.

The founder team has a structure I like in one respect and worry about in another. Smith and Khomenko worked together for eight years at Rainforest QA — Smith as CTO, Khomenko as lead AI data scientist. That's not a co-founder relationship formed at a networking event; that's a relationship forged under the specific pressures of shipping production software together for the better part of a decade. Smith is a second-time YC founder who built a real company, raised $42 million, and ran it for ten years. He can clearly build and manage engineering organizations. But here's what nags at me: Smith's entire career is Ruby, AWS, DevOps, CI/CD. Khomenko's is data science and ML pipelines. Pyzh's is cybersecurity consulting. None of them have built hardware, navigated defense procurement, held a security clearance, or worked inside the military-industrial ecosystem. They're software people building an autonomous weapon system. The schlep here isn't just technical complexity — it's ITAR compliance, DoD certification, contracting officer relationships, and the sheer physical reality of manufacturing something that has to work when someone is trying to kill you with a drone. I keep asking myself: do these founders understand how different this world is from deploying a QA testing platform?

The schlep-blindness argument cuts both ways on this one. Defense hardware genuinely repels most software founders — the procurement cycles, the regulatory burden, the classification headaches. That's a real moat for whoever makes it through. And the acoustic AI approach is clever: passive detection that doesn't emit signals the way radar does, potentially cheaper and harder to jam. If EDDA works at one-tenth the cost of Anduril's systems in a form factor a soldier can carry, the military will buy it regardless of who has the existing relationships. The Ukraine war has made this market real and urgent in a way that's undeniable — the $20B Army framework contract to Anduril in March 2026 isn't a promise, it's procurement infrastructure being built right now. The timing is as good as it gets.

But I have to be honest about what I don't see. There are no traction signals — no contracts, no LOIs, no field tests, no evidence that EDDA works at all. There are fourteen job postings at $150K-$250K salaries, which tells me they're building a team but also burning significant cash for a pre-seed company. Where's the working prototype? When I backed the AppJet team, they impressed me by building the product during the YC process. The strongest signal a hardware company can send at this stage is a demo that works in ugly conditions. I don't see that here. What I see is a hiring plan that looks like it belongs to a Series A company, not a company that should be in a garage getting the first unit to detect its first drone.

The competitive landscape is the elephant. Anduril is at $60 billion and $2 billion in revenue. DroneShield did $216 million in FY2025, up 276% year-over-year. Epirus has raised over $550 million. These aren't paper companies — they have deployed systems, government relationships, and the institutional trust that comes from years of navigating the defense bureaucracy. The argument that 9 Mothers can carve out a niche at the low end — portable, cheap, autonomous — is plausible. It's the same argument that worked for Stripe against the banks: the incumbent is too big and too slow to care about the small use case. But Stripe was competing against banks that moved at geological speed. Anduril moves fast. They have Lattice, they have the contracts, and they're specifically building counter-drone systems. The gap that 9 Mothers needs to exploit has to be real and durable, and I can't tell from this dossier whether it is.

The bull case is real enough that I wouldn't dismiss this company. If Khomenko's Ukrainian experience is the genuine origin, if the acoustic AI approach works as a passive alternative to radar, if they can build a system cheap enough that the DoD can buy thousands instead of dozens, and if they can navigate procurement before running out of money — this could be significant. The counter-drone market is growing at 26% CAGR and the need is existential. A startup that solves this at the right price point doesn't need to beat Anduril; it needs to serve the use cases Anduril is too expensive for. But that's a lot of "ifs," and I don't have enough evidence of the founders' ability to execute in this specific domain — which is radically different from anything they've done before — to write a check from my own money today.
