# Sellara -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The team composition at Sellara tells me almost everything I need to know about what kind of company this is. Charles-André Jolly covered 180 institutional clients at Citi across credit derivatives. Spencer Secord spent six years on Citi's trading desks and — this is the detail that matters — actually built workflow automations there, collaborating with IT and middle office to integrate them into core risk systems. Then they recruited Ahmad Roumie, an ETH Zurich robotics MSc who was CTO of BabylonAI, a YC W23 nutrition tracking startup, to be their technical co-founder. This is a specific pattern I see constantly: domain experts from a non-tech industry identify a problem, then recruit an engineer to build the solution. It can work. But it's structurally different from what happened at Stripe, where Patrick and John Collison were simultaneously the developers who felt the payment integration pain AND the builders who wrote the fix. At Sellara, the people who understand the problem aren't writing the code, and the person writing the code just pivoted from tracking what people eat to automating credit derivatives reconciliation. That split demands more communication overhead and slower iteration than teams where the builder is also the user.

Secord's history is the strongest founder signal in this dossier. He didn't just observe manual workflows on the trading desk — he started automating them, which is about as close to "live in the future, then build what's missing" as you get from someone inside a bank. That's genuine organic discovery. Jolly's contribution is different: 180 institutional client relationships is a distribution asset, not a product insight. Distribution matters — Airbnb would have died without the founders' relentless early hustle on Craigslist — but distribution without deep product intuition produces companies that sell well and iterate slowly. Roumie's IEEE publications and 17 GitHub repos confirm he can build, but his domain pivot from nutrition to institutional trading is steep. The specific requirements of financial data pipelines, FIX protocols, and regulatory constraints aren't things you pick up from a master's thesis on visual odometry.

The SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed are the one signal that genuinely surprised me. These take months and real money to obtain. Most startups don't bother until a Series A customer demands it. Either Sellara has institutional prospects far enough into procurement that compliance certifications became blocking requirements — which would be meaningful traction evidence not captured elsewhere in the dossier — or the team prioritized enterprise readiness over product iteration speed. Both interpretations are informative; the first is bullish, the second is concerning. I can't tell which from the available evidence.

My core problem with Sellara is that "AI agents for financial services" is the definition of a consensus idea right now. Beam AI already has $4.5M in revenue doing adjacent BFSI automation in the same city. Blueflame raised a $5M Series A for AI in investment workflows. Forrester reports one financial firm already running 60 agentic AI agents with plans for 200 more. 44% of finance teams projected to use agentic AI in 2026. When I read Sellara's claim that their AI "outperforms leading LLMs by 60%+ and runs 30x faster," with no published benchmark or third-party validation, I hear marketing language where I want to hear honest specificity. The founders who impress me most say "I don't know" when they don't know and cite real numbers when they do. An unsubstantiated 60% claim is the kind of thing that works in a sales deck for institutional buyers but raises questions about whether the underlying technical advantage is fine-tuned models on proprietary financial data — defensible — or prompt engineering on GPT-4 — evaporating with the next model release.

The strongest bull case for Sellara is that operational workflow automation in regulated finance is a genuine schlep. Booking, allocations, reconciliations, compliance — this is the unglamorous plumbing that most AI founders avoid because it requires navigating SR 11-7 model risk frameworks, six-to-twelve-month vendor onboarding cycles, and integration with Bloomberg terminals. That regulatory moat is real, and Sellara's team is better positioned to cross it than almost any outside team would be. If Jolly can convert even nine of his 180 former institutional relationships into customers, that's potentially $1M+ in enterprise ARR from founder-led sales alone. The "deploy in weeks, not quarters" positioning against incumbents like FIS and Broadridge attacks a genuine pain point — the implementation timeline for legacy vendors is a known frustration. And Secord's hands-on experience building automations inside Citi's risk systems means at least one founder understands both the problem and the organizational politics of getting AI tools approved.

But this configuration — domain experts from finance building AI for finance with a recruited technical co-founder — produces a very specific kind of company. It tends to produce good businesses, not great startups. The growth pattern is linear with the sales team's capacity to close enterprise deals, not superlinear with the product's ability to spread. Each institutional deployment requires custom configuration, compliance review, and integration work. Sellara's own website describes a three-step process — identify workflow, configure and validate, deploy with monitoring — that sounds like professional services with software attached. When I ask whether "how well you do depends on how well you've done," the compounding dynamic I look for, I don't see it. Doubling effort roughly doubles output. That's a consultancy growth curve, not a startup growth curve. The question Sellara hasn't answered — at least not in any public evidence — is whether the AI agents get meaningfully better with each deployment, creating a data flywheel, or whether each new client is essentially a fresh integration project.
