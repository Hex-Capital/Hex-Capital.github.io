# Voxel Energy -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Voxel Energy is the competitive landscape, and it tells me nearly everything I need to know. Exowatt has raised $140 million with Sam Altman and a16z backing it. Crusoe has raised over $2 billion at a $10 billion valuation. Enchanted Rock has a gigawatt deployed and a contract with Microsoft. The Washington Post is writing trend pieces about off-grid data center power. McKinsey has published the TAM projections. When I ask my question -- what important truth do very few people agree with you on? -- the answer here would be something like "data centers should go off-grid because the utility interconnection queue is a bottleneck." But that is not a secret. That is a headline. Every sophisticated energy investor, every hyperscaler procurement team, and every infrastructure fund in the country has already reached this conclusion. A thesis that Sam Altman, Andreessen Horowitz, and the Washington Post all endorse is, by definition, consensus. And consensus markets are mimetic traps -- multiple well-capitalized players imitating each other's positioning, competing for the same hyperscaler customers, eventually destroying margins through rivalry. This is the food delivery problem wearing a hard hat.

The more specific claim -- that second-life EV batteries combined with DC-native architecture constitute a superior approach to off-grid data center power -- is a differentiated tactic, not a contrarian truth. Solar panels, lithium-ion batteries, and power electronics are commodity components. Assembling them in a novel configuration is engineering, and sometimes good engineering, but it is not a zero-to-one innovation. The DC-native architecture reducing conversion losses from 30% to approximately 4% is a real efficiency gain, but efficiency gains within an existing paradigm are textbook 1-to-n improvements. Compare this to General Matter, where I joined the board: HALEU enrichment is a capability that structurally does not exist in the private sector, backed by $900 million in DOE contracts that create regulatory moats no competitor can replicate quickly. Voxel's approach -- solar plus batteries plus backup generation -- could be assembled by Fluence, Tesla Energy, any large EPC firm, or frankly by Crusoe or Exowatt themselves if the second-life battery thesis proves compelling. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "Solar + battery microgrid systems are not inherently proprietary."

The strongest bull case here centers on time as the binding constraint. If Voxel can deploy powered data center capacity in months rather than five years, that is a genuine order-of-magnitude improvement on the dimension that hyperscalers care most about right now. The team has legitimate hardware DNA -- Pfeiffer built an actual electric vehicle company and earned Forbes 30 Under 30 for it, Spencer managed Tesla Autopilot projects, and Schmidt has a decade of data center construction management. These are engineers, not consultants. The confluence of first-generation EV battery retirement, continued solar cost decline, and explosive AI compute demand creates a genuine window. If Voxel moves fast enough to lock up battery supply and land positions, and if the DC-native architecture provides a durable cost advantage, they could build a meaningful infrastructure business. The Spotify exception in my own portfolio is relevant -- when supply-side positioning creates structural advantage, the small-market-first strategy can be overridden. But Spotify's moat came from exclusive licensing agreements with major labels that took years to negotiate and were nearly impossible to replicate. Voxel's battery supply is not exclusive -- second-life batteries are a growing commodity market projected to reach 330-350 GWh by 2030, and any well-funded competitor can amass inventory.

What troubles me most deeply is the absence of a monopoly path. I see no network effects -- each site operates independently. I see no proprietary technology that compounds over time -- the learning curve on battery management is real but ultimately transferable. I see no economies of scale unique to Voxel -- larger competitors with more capital can procure batteries and land more aggressively. And I see no brand power in a market where customers are sophisticated enterprises making decisions on cost-per-megawatt, not consumer sentiment. The endgame here is not a monopoly; it is one of several off-grid data center power providers competing on price and deployment speed in an increasingly crowded market. The founders may execute well and build a profitable business, but my framework requires that a successful outcome return the fund -- and a commodity infrastructure provider in a multi-player market, no matter how well-timed, does not produce power-law returns. The company is well-constructed for the moment, but moments pass, and durability requires structural advantages that I do not see here.
