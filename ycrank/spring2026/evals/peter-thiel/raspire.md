# RASPIRE -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

RASPIRE is a competent regional security vendor in a commoditized category whose strongest dimension is the regulated buyer list — banks, fintechs, insurance, and Saudi government via the Sadq partnership at Black Hat MEA 2025 — and whose weakest dimension is the absence of any contrarian secret that Appdome has not already publicly claimed. The dossier reads as Kareem Selim and Hassan Mostafa shipping a credible MENA-flavored RASP product, not as a thesis. That pairing — regulated-customer access plus consensus-shaped technical positioning — is the analytical signature here, and it is the wrong half of the **Anduril** (2017) shape.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy for the contrarian-secret dimension because Patrick Collison named PayPal's developer-experience failure mode at a moment when consensus said payments was "won." The shared mechanism would have to be: RASPIRE names a structural reason Appdome cannot ship what RASPIRE ships. The dossier does the opposite. Appdome's own marketing page, cited in the dossier, already describes "no-code" post-compilation binary fusion with a 400+ defense library; RASPIRE's differentiation collapses to "no-code plus AI-driven autonomous response," which is the 2026 YC garnish, not a secret. There is no named architectural reason Appdome ($39.6M raised) or Zimperium ($60.1M raised) cannot bolt the same AI layer onto their existing distribution within nine months. RASPIRE is not contradicting consensus; RASPIRE is restating it in Arabic and English.

The pass example in my own file is the failure mechanism for RASPIRE's monopoly dimension: "AI-powered platform for vertical X" where the moat hypothesis terminates at "we have a better model." G2 catalogues 25+ vendors shipping anti-debug, anti-hook, anti-tamper, and API attestation as commodity features. RASPIRE's dossier offers no detection-accuracy figures, no telemetry volume, no integration depth metric — the AI data moat is asserted, not quantified. The pattern is present and undisguised: a commoditized primitive plus an unproven AI accumulation story is exactly the indefinite-optimism shape Zero to One was written against.

On founder lineage, the relevant counterfactual is what would have to be true to push Selim and Mostafa above the Asana/Moskovitz override threshold. Moskovitz had shipped **Facebook** (2004) to scale; that single fact carried Asana past a missing structural moat. RASPIRE's founders spent years securing bank and fintech apps and ex-Cyshield work in Egypt, and the apps they protect reportedly reach 20M+ end users — real domain depth, real operator signal, but no prior exit, no PayPal-Mafia adjacency, no Olympiad-tier credential. The lineage is sufficient to be taken seriously as practitioners; it is not sufficient to override a missing secret.

On the definite-plan dimension, the 18-month compounding question is whether RASPIRE's roadmap is a commitment or a posture. SpaceX in 2008 had a named reusability timeline founders refused to pivot from. RASPIRE's dossier surfaces no multi-year technical milestone, no architectural commitment, no protected-app SLA target. "AI-driven autonomous response" is the kind of phrase that survives every pivot, which is the diagnostic for indefinite optimism. Eighteen months of iterating against Appdome's roadmap is exactly how regional security vendors get re-priced as acquisition fodder.

The single-anchor parallel for the atoms-heavy and regulated-sector dimension is **Palantir** (2003), which I weighted not for software novelty but for the integration depth and trust moat that government and intelligence buyers compound over a decade. RASPIRE's customer mix — banks, fintech, insurance, government, plus the Sadq partnership tied to Saudi Vision 2030 — has the right shape. The dissimilarity is decisive: Palantir built proprietary deployment depth inside customers that took years to rip out; RASPIRE's "no-code post-compilation" pitch is engineered to minimize integration, which is the opposite of switching-cost accumulation. The regulated-customer base is real and is the reason this is not a bottom-quartile score; the moat the regulation creates is shallow because the product is designed to be removable.

The 36-month forecast: RASPIRE reaches $3–8M ARR concentrated in MENA banking and Saudi government accounts, fails to displace Appdome or Zimperium in a single US Tier-1 financial institution, and is acquired by Appdome or a MENA-regional security holding company for $40–80M, returning a clean multiple to 500 Global and Sanabil but no power-law shape. The mechanism is straightforward — commodity primitives plus regional distribution equals trade sale, not monopoly.

The strongest bull case is the Anduril shape inverted: that the AI-driven autonomous response trained on live telemetry from 20M+ protected end users compounds into a detection data moat that incumbents with smaller live-attack corpora cannot replicate, and that Vision 2030 procurement gives RASPIRE a privileged certification depth in the Gulf that becomes the wedge into US defense-adjacent fintech. This stings because telemetry-as-moat is real in security — CrowdStrike built one — and the regulated-customer thesis I run on Palantir and Anduril would, taken seriously, force me to take RASPIRE's Saudi position seriously too. It does not win because the 20M-user figure is delivered without detection metrics, attack-volume disclosure, or any evidence the AI layer is more than positioning, and because Appdome's distribution into the same buyers is already at $39.6M of capitalization. The verdict flips if RASPIRE signs a named US Tier-1 bank or US federal-adjacent agency at >$1M ACV within twelve months — that single fact would re-rate the lineage and monopoly scores and force a second look.

Pass.
