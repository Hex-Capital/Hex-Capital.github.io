# Carson -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Carson's tagline tells me everything I need to know about its ambition: "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." The founding aspiration of this company is to be a slightly better version of two existing products. When Pradeep Sindhu and I spent six months incubating Juniper, his pitch wasn't "faster than Cisco's routers" — it was that TCP/IP would replace the entire telecom switching architecture. When Pat Brown walked into my office, he didn't say "better than beef" — he said animal agriculture consumes half the planet's arable land, and he was going to make it obsolete at the molecular level. Carson is defining itself as a relative improvement within an existing product category, and that positioning tells me the founders have calibrated their ambition to the wrong scale entirely.

The consequence of success here is a well-run B2B SaaS company that helps sales teams send better outreach emails and generate branded slide decks with tighter security controls. If Carson executes flawlessly, sales reps at mid-market companies work somewhat more efficiently. No industry becomes structurally obsolete. No trillion-dollar system gets displaced. The world doesn't operate differently. This is the archetype I warn founders about — you've reduced the probability of failure by choosing a proven market with known customer needs and a clear competitive landscape, and in doing so, you've made the consequences of success entirely inconsequential. Enterprise AI sales automation is a real market, yes, but it's a crowded features war where Clay already has $100M in ARR, Artisan has raised $46M, Salesforce is building Agentforce, and both Anthropic and OpenAI have their own agent products with functionally unlimited engineering resources behind them. Carson's differentiation — dynamic UI generation and enterprise security controls — is a product design choice, not a technology moat. The dossier itself states this plainly: these components "are individually reproducible by well-funded startups or platform incumbents."

The founding team is competent in exactly the way that concerns me. Three Harvard CS graduates who worked together at Palantir and Flowcode, executing the enterprise software playbook they learned at enterprise software companies. This is a team of insiders building for insiders. Milan Bhandari's prior founding experience at Bolto — YC S23, $5.6M raised, $1M+ revenue — demonstrates execution ability, which I respect. But Bolto was an AI-powered HR/payroll platform, and now Carson is an AI-powered sales automation platform. The pattern is competent SaaS construction, not first-principles rethinking of how an industry operates. I don't see the "irreverence, foolish confidence, and naivety" that I look for in founders attempting something the consensus considers impossible. Nobody considers enterprise AI sales automation impossible — half of Silicon Valley is building it right now.

The strongest bull case: enterprise AI agent adoption is on a genuine exponential curve, and the security gap between consumer-grade agents and enterprise requirements is real. If Carson nails the enterprise security layer and becomes the trusted governance bridge between powerful AI models and security-conscious organizations, they could ride that wave as the AI agent market grows sevenfold over five years. Bhandari has proven he can ship product and find revenue. The team's Palantir pedigree means they understand enterprise procurement dynamics viscerally. And OpenClaw's creator joining OpenAI creates real uncertainty about that competitor's development trajectory. I can see a world where Carson builds a $50-100M revenue business. But that world looks like a Salesforce acquisition, not a category-defining company — and seeking acquisition rather than transformation is precisely the outcome I don't fund.

The timing argument superficially appeals to my rate-of-change framework, but it fails on closer inspection. The AI agent trend is not pre-consensus — it is peak consensus. Gartner is projecting 40% enterprise agent adoption by 2026. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was "laughable" and I was the first VC check. When I backed cleantech in 2004, most investors were running away. Carson is entering a market where hundreds of millions of venture dollars have already poured in, where the technology primitives (LLM orchestration, function calling, RAG pipelines) are commoditizing in real time, and where the two most capitalized AI companies on Earth are building competing products. This is not skating to where the puck will be — this is arriving at the rink after the game started.

These are four smart founders who should be working on something that scares them and matters. The sales automation problem they're solving exists, but it's not too important not to work on — it's too small to justify their talent.
