# Prototyping.io -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is what jumps out first, and it's not encouraging. When I see a market where two public companies (Xometry at $687M revenue, Protolabs at $533M) are actively adding AI capabilities, AND a cohort of well-funded AI-native startups -- RMFG ($4.5M seed, 100K+ parts shipped), Jiga ($19.1M raised, NASA and Siemens as customers), Toolpath ($10M seed), and Korso in the same YC batch -- are all attacking the same structural opening simultaneously, I have to ask the fundamental question: what's nonobvious here? The answer is nothing. The AI-enables-manufacturing-intelligence thesis is consensus. Every investor who's looked at industrial automation in 2024-2025 has seen this pitch. The reshoring tailwind is on the cover of every macro newsletter. When a structural shift is this widely recognized and this many funded teams are pursuing it, the timing advantage has already been captured by earlier movers. This is the opposite of the Coinbase situation where crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors -- here, the market is crowded precisely because everyone agrees the opportunity is real.

The infrastructure-layer test is where I'd need to see something compelling to override the timing concern, and I don't. Prototyping.io is building a buyer-facing platform: upload CAD files, get DFM feedback, order parts. That's an application sitting on top of a supplier network, not an infrastructure layer that other businesses integrate into their workflows. Compare this to how Stripe became the payments API -- developers built Stripe into their applications, creating compounding switching costs that made ripping it out progressively harder. Prototyping.io's DFM analysis is a feature in an ordering flow, not a platform primitive. The interesting pivot would be if they built DFM-as-a-service -- an API that CAD software, PLM systems, and other manufacturing platforms integrate for real-time manufacturability feedback. That would be an infrastructure play. But the current positioning is "better marketplace with DFM analysis upfront," which puts them in direct competition with Xometry's Instant Quoting Engine and its 10,000+ supplier network, fighting on feature differentiation rather than structural positioning.

The single-miracle test is unfavorable. I count at least three independent challenges that all need to go right. First, the AI/DFM technology must be materially better than what Xometry and the other AI-native startups are building -- with a two-person team against Xometry's engineering organization and RMFG's head start on actual parts shipped. Second, they need to build and vet a supplier network capable of delivering the tolerances they advertise (±0.0001" across six manufacturing processes, parts up to 34 feet) -- supply-side execution in manufacturing is an operational miracle in itself, completely independent of the software challenge. Third, they need to win distribution in a market where Jiga already has enterprise logos and RMFG has shipped 100K+ parts. Three miracles, compounding odds. I've seen this pattern before and it doesn't end well.

The strongest bull case would be this: the DFM-first approach genuinely shifts the value proposition from "procurement marketplace" to "design optimization platform," embedding Prototyping.io earlier in the engineering workflow where switching costs are higher and willingness to pay is driven by time-to-market rather than per-part margin. If they can get DFM analysis integrated into the design iteration loop -- before procurement even starts -- they could build a different category than Xometry. The founder pairing covers the right bases: Revanth's mechanical engineering depth (IIT BBS silver medalist, UT Austin aerospace research, Schlumberger manufacturing experience) provides real DFM domain expertise, and Prerit's AI systems background (Microsoft AI, published distributed systems research) provides the technical infrastructure capability. The $400B global machining market means even a narrow wedge represents enormous value. For this to work, the DFM analysis would need to be so good that engineers start their design process with it -- making Prototyping.io the beginning of the workflow, not the end. That's a plausible future, but it requires the technology to be genuinely superior while simultaneously building the supplier network to fulfill orders, and I don't see evidence that either miracle is underway.

The founders have solid domain-technical complementarity but no scaling experience. Neither has operated at a company during hypergrowth, and the "recently joined forces" language suggests this is a new partnership without the kind of shared operational history I look for. At pre-seed I don't penalize this heavily -- strong markets can carry execution gaps -- but the market here isn't strong enough in my framework to compensate. Good engineers tackling a real problem, but in a market where the structural shift is fully priced in and better-capitalized teams are already executing.
