# Alchemize -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Alchemize is a regulated-category wedge without a founder who lived the regulation -- Samuel Fu (Columbia) and Robert Hou are pitching AI-native customs brokerage in a $5.17B US market (datainsightsmarket.com, 2025) where the moat is downstream of CBP licensure, and neither founder's public biography touches a freight forwarder, a bonded warehouse, or a single Harmonized Tariff Schedule line. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but I do need the founder to have already been playing the game -- and Alchemize hasn't given me that yet.

Anduril (2017) is the ladder rung that matters here. Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus to scale before he ever touched a defense contract, which meant the regulated lane he walked into was already the *second* thing he'd built, not the first. Alchemize's founders have no analogous prior artifact -- no customs software they shipped at a forwarder, no time inside CBP, no operator stint at Flexport. The dossier surfaces "no verified prior employer or education for this specific Robert Hou" and "no public evidence of customs, trade compliance, or logistics operating experience for either founder" (WebSearch, May 2026). That is the inverse of the Anduril-Luckey pattern. The Amari AI team, by contrast, includes a former senior LinkedIn engineer plus an operator co-founder (TechCrunch, Feb 2026) -- not a Luckey, but at least a biography. Alchemize is reading to me right now as a memo, not a moment.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling is the rung Alchemize sits on for the magically-weird axis, and that is where the analysis stings most. The pattern I named in my pass example is "six top funds chasing, deck priced as if the outcome is already certain" -- Alchemize is not that, but its category is: Amari AI closed $4.5M co-led by First Round and Pear in February 2026, Caspian closed $5.4M from Primary in July 2025, KlearNow has $66M and 2,000+ customers, Flexport launched an AI customs auditor in 2025 reporting 0.2% error rate. By the time Alchemize walks across the Demo Day stage in Spring 2026, "AI-native customs brokerage" is the consensus YC-adjacent wedge of the prior eighteen months. The thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus (Yahoo Finance, March 2025). This one became consensus before Alchemize incorporated.

Customs brokerage is genuinely unsexy and genuinely regulated, and that is the one dimension where Alchemize earns real credit -- the dossier flags that Caspian markets itself as "a rare CBP-approved tech company and licensed customs broker" (BusinessWire, Jul 2025), which tells me the license itself is the gating moat. The mechanism that compounds over 18 months is whoever gets the broker license and signed importer relationships first; the mechanism that fails over 18 months is software-without-license that has to revenue-share with a third-party licensed broker and watches its margin collapse. Alchemize's broker-license status is not disclosed publicly. That silence is not neutral -- in a regulated category, undisclosed licensure reads as "not yet," and "not yet" in 2026 means losing the race to Caspian, who has already filed.

For the single-CEO axis, the counterfactual: a two-person team with no named CEO and no public governance disclosure would have to show me, within 90 days, a documented operating agreement and a clear tiebreaker for me to move this score up a tier. The dossier names "Samuel Fu (Co-Founder)" and "Robert Hou (Co-Founder)" -- two co-founder titles, no CEO designation, no equity split disclosed. The HQ Trivia (2017) Yusupov/Kroll lesson is exactly this shape pre-feud. I would want to know who signs the CBP-38 before I write a check.

For physical verifiability, the single-anchor parallel is Flock Safety -- I knew Flock was real when I read the founder's Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons café, because the camera was a *thing* I could photograph. Alchemize is an entry-filing pipeline that sits behind ACE and an importer's TMS. I cannot ride it, scan it, or hand it to a friend within 48 hours. Even Amari's "30+ customers, $15B goods cleared" is a number, not an artifact -- and Alchemize doesn't have that number either.

My 36-month forecast on Alchemize: acqui-hired into Flexport or a mid-tier licensed broker for under $15M, or wound down, with Amari AI and Caspian splitting the licensed-broker-plus-AI lane that Alchemize entered late and underfunded. The mechanism is straightforward -- a Spring 2026 pre-seed two-person team with no disclosed CBP license enters a category where two competitors already have $4.5M and $5.4M of seed capital and a 3-10 month head start, and the incumbent (Flexport) is bundling AI customs into existing accounts at a 0.2% error rate.

The bull case stings in exactly one place: the Trump-era tariff regime in 2025 produced "vague executive orders, overlapping tariffs and reduced access to federal guidance" (Washington Post, Nov 2025), the NCBFAA confirmed importers are turning to brokers at higher cost (Jun 2025), and Andrew Miklas as YC partner is not a random pairing -- PagerDuty was a B2B-infra wedge that looked saturated and wasn't. A 2-person team can move faster than KlearNow's $66M-burdened roadmap. Fine. But Caspian is already CBP-licensed and Amari already cleared $15B in goods before Alchemize's batch began -- the catalyst is real and Alchemize is not the team capturing it. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Alchemize publishes its CBP broker license number and shows 50+ paying importer accounts within 12 months of Demo Day. Until then, I pass.
