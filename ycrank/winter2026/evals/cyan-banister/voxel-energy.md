# Voxel Energy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Voxel Energy is how much it reminds me of Crusoe -- and not in the way the founders probably want. When we backed Crusoe, mining Bitcoin on flared gas in North Dakota was genuinely insane to other investors. Nobody wanted that deal. The insight was grounded in real physics and real waste, but the pitch sounded like a fever dream. Today, off-grid data center power is on the front page of the Washington Post. Exowatt has $140 million from a16z and Sam Altman. Crusoe itself is a $10 billion company. The window where this category was unpopular and difficult to assess has closed. Voxel is entering a space where consensus has already formed, and that fundamentally changes the nature of the bet. I'm not saying the company can't win -- I'm saying the risk-reward profile has shifted. When you're competing against players with $140 million to $2 billion in funding, being right isn't enough. You have to be right in a way nobody else can replicate, and you need capital to execute before they do.

The second-life EV battery angle is the one piece that genuinely interests me. Taking batteries that have "graduated" from cars -- still holding 70-80% of their capacity -- and putting them to work powering AI compute has a certain poetic quality I respond to. It echoes what I love about Crusoe's origin: finding energy in waste, making an invisible resource productive. There's a circular-economy story here that's beautiful in principle. And the supply-side timing is compelling -- first-generation EV batteries are aging out right as data center power demand is exploding, creating a brief window where cheap storage meets desperate demand. If Voxel can lock up supply relationships with OEMs and recyclers before larger players move in, that could become a real procurement moat. But "could become" carries a lot of weight at pre-seed against competitors who already have billions.

What I can't find -- and this matters more to me than anything else -- is the founders' personal stories. I know Casey Spencer was at Tesla Autopilot and started three hardware companies. I know Max Pfeiffer made Forbes 30 Under 30 and built electric trucks at Maxwell Vehicles. I know Evan Schmidt managed data center construction projects for a decade. These are strong professional credentials. The domain expertise across the three of them -- power electronics, EV batteries, physical infrastructure -- is precisely what this problem demands. But when I evaluate founders, I start with a question no credential can answer: tell me your story, not the business. What happened in your life that made this the problem you'd die on a hill for? The dossier gives me polished résumés where I'm listening for scars. That doesn't mean the stories aren't there -- it means I can't hear them from here, and that silence is the biggest gap in my assessment.

The bull case deserves honest engagement because the underlying technical bet is strong. The DC-native microgrid architecture claiming 4% conversion loss versus the industry-standard 30% is not a marketing number -- that's real physics, and if it holds at scale, it translates directly into unit economics that could undercut every grid-connected competitor. The "5,400% overhead capacity on load rating" for AI training surges suggests they've thought deeply about the specific power profile of GPU clusters, not just generic data center loads. If the team can prove that second-life batteries can reliably deliver 99.999% uptime -- and the Sift partnership for predictive monitoring suggests they understand this is the critical engineering challenge -- then Voxel could win on pure economics even against far better-capitalized competitors. Cheaper batteries, less conversion loss, no utility interconnection queue. That's a compelling stack. The question is whether they can prove it fast enough.

The co-founder dynamic is an open question. Casey and Max share a Tesla background, which gives them a common professional language, and Evan brings the construction execution expertise they'd otherwise lack. Three co-founders who cover hardware, power systems, and physical infrastructure is actually the right configuration for this specific problem. But I don't know how they met, how long they've worked together, or what happens when things go sideways -- and in infrastructure companies, things always go sideways. I learned the hard way with HQ Trivia that perfect product-market fit cannot survive founder conflict. Three people building power plants under regulatory uncertainty will face high-stress decisions, and I have no signal on how they navigate disagreement.

This is a competent team building a real solution to a massive problem with a differentiated technical approach. In a different framework, that might be enough. But my framework starts with biographical conviction and anti-consensus positioning, and Voxel doesn't light up either signal strongly enough for me. The founders have the right résumés but I haven't heard the right stories. The market is enormous but it's no longer the kind of bet that makes other investors uncomfortable -- it's the kind of bet that makes them competitive. I'd want to sit down with Casey and Max and hear about the moments that broke them before I'd write a check from my own money.
