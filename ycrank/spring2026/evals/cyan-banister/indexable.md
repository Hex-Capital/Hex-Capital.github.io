# Indexable -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what stops me cold with Indexable: this is one of the most crowded categories in infrastructure right now. E2B has $32 million and claims 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. Daytona raised $31 million and crossed a million in ARR within two months of relaunching. Modal is at $50 million ARR with over a hundred million in funding. Cloudflare, Vercel, and Fly.io all shipped sandbox products in the last few months. When I think about the companies that have worked best for me -- SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy, Crusoe when mining crypto on gas flares sounded like a joke -- what they shared was that nobody else wanted to fund them because the category itself was dismissed. AI agent sandboxes are the opposite of dismissed. This is a category where consensus has already formed so thoroughly that half a dozen startups and three hyperscalers are fighting over it. That's the signal I trust least.

I went looking for Andrew Gazelka's story and I found an impressive engineer -- xAI, Speechify, a Rust-based Minecraft engine handling ten thousand concurrent players that got a thousand GitHub stars, a prior acquisition with BountyBot. But I couldn't find the biographical thread I need. When I backed the Contraline founder, what grabbed me was years of solitary academic research in a category everyone else thought was too weird to touch. When I wrote the check for Uber, it came after years of conversations with my husband about the medallion racket -- I felt the problem before I understood the company. With Gazelka, I see a talented systems programmer who identified a market opportunity and has the rare technical chops to build a custom virtual machine monitor. That's real. But "I can build it better" is a different thing than "this problem lives inside me and I'm the only person who was going to solve it." I don't hear the second story here, and at the stage where I'm writing a check with my own money, that's the story I need to hear.

The technical work deserves respect, and I want to be honest about the bull case. A custom VMM on KVM that forks an entire running environment -- files, processes, memory, databases -- in 26 milliseconds is not trivial. The competitors using Firecracker or Docker are working at a higher abstraction layer, and there's a real argument that when AI agents need stateful forking (not just fast cold starts), the company that built from the kernel up will own the infrastructure layer. Gazelka's Hyperion project is a direct analog -- managing massive concurrent mutable state is exactly the muscle this product needs. If the agentic AI market really does grow at 44% CAGR and stateful sandbox forking becomes the bottleneck, Indexable could be to agent infrastructure what Crusoe became to AI compute: the weird low-level bet that everyone dismissed until it wasn't dismissible anymore. That's the version of this company I'd need to believe in.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. The companies I've done best with operate in spaces that other investors actively avoid -- regulated industries, stigmatized categories, things that make people uncomfortable. Sandbox infrastructure for AI agents makes people excited, not uncomfortable. Everyone wants to fund it. That means the price discovery is already happening, the differentiation window is narrowing, and a three-person team with YC-level funding is entering against competitors with ten to twenty times the capital and established customer bases. The custom VMM is a real technical moat, but E2B is open source with broad adoption and Zeroboot already demonstrates sub-millisecond VM forking as an open-source primitive. Technical advantages at this layer can erode quickly when the category is hot enough to attract serious engineering talent to every competitor. This isn't the kind of structurally hard space -- regulatory, stigmatized, or genuinely ignored -- where I've seen early-stage companies thrive despite being outgunned on capital.

I also notice that this company doesn't create economic access or make any invisible system visible to people who need it. That thread runs through my best investments -- Uber giving drivers income, Affirm making credit transparent, Flock Safety bringing public safety data to communities, Carta replacing opaque equity spreadsheets. Indexable is infrastructure for other infrastructure. It's important plumbing, but it doesn't connect to the part of my thesis about capitalism as a vehicle for lifting people up. That's not automatically disqualifying, but when I combine the consensus category, the absent founder narrative, the missing economic access dimension, and a single named founder carrying the weight of a custom kernel, a custom VMM, an RDMA transport layer, and a Nix reimplementation on a team of three -- I can't find my way to conviction here with my own money.
