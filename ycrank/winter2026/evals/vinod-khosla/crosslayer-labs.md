# Crosslayer Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me most about Crosslayer Labs: this team invented MPIC, a standard now mandated by the CA/Browser Forum that secures every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate on the planet — roughly 8 million certificates issued daily across Let's Encrypt, Google Trust Services, and Cloudflare. They didn't just contribute to Internet security, they rewrote its rules. Prateek Mittal holds the ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award, has shaped the Tor network, and sits at one of the most important intersections of security research and Internet infrastructure in the world. And what they've decided to build with all that leverage is... a monitoring dashboard for enterprise security teams. That gap between what this team has demonstrated it can do and what it's choosing to attempt is the most important signal in this entire evaluation.

Let me start where I always start: if Crosslayer Labs succeeds completely, what changes? Enterprises in healthcare, crypto, and fintech get better alerts when someone tries to hijack their DNS, spoof their BGP routes, or inject malicious JavaScript. That's valuable. It's worth protecting. But the consequence of full success is a well-run cybersecurity SaaS company capturing share in a subset of the EASM market — a market that's maybe $1.5 billion today. Their specific niche of cross-layer impersonation detection is a fraction of that. Even at maximum execution, no trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. No industry operates fundamentally differently. The Internet continues to work the same way, just with slightly better monitoring. I have a visceral reaction when I see founders this talented calibrating their ambition to a manageable niche. These are the people who could be redesigning Internet trust architecture from first principles — instead they're selling alerts to security teams that already have twelve other tools in their stack.

The expert-insider dynamic compounds my concern. My entire career has taught me that the greatest innovations rarely come from the established authorities in a field. These founders are not outsiders — they are literally the world's foremost experts on cross-layer Internet attacks. They invented the standard, published 15+ papers at the top venues, won the prizes. In my experience, that level of domain expertise creates a gravitational pull toward incremental thinking. You know exactly what's possible and what's hard, which means you aim for what's achievable rather than what's transformational. I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab precisely because he didn't know what aerospace engineers considered impossible. Pat Brown didn't know what the food industry thought couldn't be done. Crosslayer's founders know exactly what the security industry thinks, because they are the security industry. That's not always disqualifying — cybersecurity does require deep protocol knowledge — but I don't see evidence of contrarian courage here. This is the obvious commercialization of their research, not an attempt at something the consensus considers crazy.

The strongest bull case deserves a full hearing. If AI-driven attacks become exponentially more sophisticated — and I think they will — cross-layer impersonation could go from a niche attack vector to the primary mechanism for Internet-scale fraud. A team that literally wrote the defensive standard has unmatched credibility with exactly the right buyers: CAs, cloud providers, financial institutions. Their relationships from the MPIC standardization process (Google, Apple, Amazon, Cloudflare) are extraordinary distribution channels that no competitor can replicate. CrowdStrike started as a focused cybersecurity play and became a $70 billion company. The MPIC mandate creates a genuine timing tailwind — enterprises that never thought about BGP-layer attacks now know the threat exists. If you believe Internet impersonation attacks will become the defining security challenge of the next decade, this is the team. I take that seriously. But even in this bull scenario, the company's current framing — monitoring and alerting for a specific attack category — doesn't suggest the founders are thinking at the scale that bull case demands. Show me a vision to rebuild Internet trust infrastructure, and we're having a different conversation.

The technology itself has real differentiation — correlating signals across DNS, BGP, TLS, and JavaScript layers to detect attack patterns that single-layer tools miss. That's genuinely novel, and the knowledge moat is deep. But it's additive to existing security infrastructure, not a replacement. CrowdStrike and Palo Alto don't become obsolete; they just have one more capability gap relative to a specialist. And the individual components (BGP monitoring via Kentik, certificate transparency via crt.sh, JavaScript integrity checking) exist separately — the differentiation lives entirely in the correlation engine and the team's expertise. A well-funded incumbent could assemble something close enough in eighteen months. The team composition reinforces my concern: three academics from the same Princeton research group, zero evidence of go-to-market capability, zero open positions, no sales hire. The gene pool hasn't been engineered — the lab group became a company. That works for publishing papers. It doesn't work for landing six-figure enterprise contracts against CrowdStrike's sales machine.

I respect what this team has accomplished — they genuinely moved Internet security forward. But I invest for consequences of success that reshape industries, and I don't see that here. A solid cybersecurity company that helps enterprises detect a specific category of attack is a good business. It's not the kind of bet that justifies the risk of early-stage investing for me. The founders are too talented for the ambition they've scoped.
