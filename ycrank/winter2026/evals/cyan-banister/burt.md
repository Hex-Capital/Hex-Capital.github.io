# Burt -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that jumps out at me immediately is how crowded this space already is. HappyRobot -- a YC S23 company doing essentially the same thing -- has raised $62 million, reports eight-figure revenue, and counts DHL and Werner as customers. Two other companies in Burt's own YC W26 batch are targeting the same freight broker buyer. AI agents for logistics is not an unpopular idea that's difficult to assess. It's a validated, well-funded, consensus category. And that's precisely the kind of deal I've learned to walk away from. When every VC in town can already see the opportunity, the weirdness has been arbitraged out, and the price of being right goes down even if the company executes well. I look for the pocket of dreamers working on something everyone else thinks is ridiculous. This is the opposite.

When I think about the founders, I want to hear the biographical thread -- the specific lived experience that makes Bobby Zhong and Kurt Sharma the people who simply must solve this problem. I don't see it here. Both are strong engineers who met at Replo, and Bobby has the restlessness of someone still searching: Moonwish Studios, then Educado for seven months, then Pirros for a year, then Replo, and now Burt. That pattern doesn't trouble me on its own -- plenty of great founders iterate before finding their thing. What concerns me is that neither founder has documented experience in freight brokerage. Nobody worked a summer at a trucking company. Nobody grew up watching a parent run a small logistics operation. The YC page title still references "fine-tune and deploy specialized models," suggesting they pivoted into logistics recently. When I see a team jump from general-purpose AI tooling to a vertical they haven't lived inside, I worry they followed the market signal rather than their own conviction. That's the kind of founder I specifically try to avoid -- someone chasing a category because it's working for someone else, rather than solving a problem that's been eating at them for years.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. HappyRobot's success proves massive demand exists. The freight market is extraordinarily fragmented -- thousands of small brokerages running on spreadsheets and phone calls who can't afford enterprise solutions. There's a genuine mid-market wedge here: if Burt can build a configurable, self-serve product that lets a 15-person brokerage spin up AI teammates without an enterprise sales cycle, they could own a segment HappyRobot isn't prioritizing. Bobby's experience building LLM tooling and coding agents at Replo is directly applicable to the technical challenge. And the macro timing is strong -- freight downturns compress margins and force brokerages to find operational leverage. If these two are fast, focused builders who can earn domain credibility through relentless customer immersion, they could carve out real territory. But "could" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

What I keep coming back to is defensibility -- or the absence of it. There's no regulatory moat here. There's no data network effect yet. The core technology is LLM-based task automation, which any well-resourced team can replicate. When I backed Contraline, the regulatory pathway itself was the moat -- nobody else was willing to spend years navigating FDA approval for male contraception. When I backed Affirm, the financial licensing created structural barriers. Burt is building B2B SaaS on top of general-purpose AI capabilities in a space where a $500 million competitor already exists. The switching costs from TMS integrations could develop over time, but nothing prevents HappyRobot or Parade from building the same integrations with far more resources. For a company to earn my check, I need to believe that something about its approach or its founders creates an advantage that compounds -- and I don't see that compounding mechanism here.
