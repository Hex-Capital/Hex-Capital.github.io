# Prior Foundry -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Prior Foundry is that its entire technical foundation is published on GitHub. The Stanford generative agents repository is open-source. The General Social Survey, World Values Survey, and European Social Survey are public datasets. The 85% accuracy benchmark comes from a paper anyone can read (arxiv 2411.10109). When a company's core methodology is freely available to every well-resourced competitor on Earth — Palantir, McKinsey's internal AI labs, Accenture — I have to ask what's actually proprietary here. The answer, as far as I can tell, is domain-specific workflow tooling, calibration quality, and Shirin Abrishami Kashani's OECD rolodex. That's a real but fragile moat. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where the heme protein synthesis was genuine science IP that couldn't be replicated by reading a preprint. Prior Foundry's defensibility is relational and operational, not technological. That changes my calculus significantly.

The consequence question is real but bounded. If Prior Foundry fully works — if governments can simulate population responses to tax changes, migration programs, and public health interventions before enacting them — that's valuable. Preventing a bad healthcare policy that affects millions of people is consequential. The OECD partnership spanning 38+ governments gives this theoretical reach across most of the developed world. But this is fundamentally decision support, not decision replacement. Governments will still make policy through political processes. Prior Foundry makes the analysis faster and cheaper — it doesn't create a discontinuity in how societies govern themselves. The second-order effects (better policy outcomes, fewer catastrophic mistakes) could be large, but this is optimization of an existing function, not creation of a new one. That puts it in a different category than fusion energy changing the cost of everything or AI replacing entire professions.

$60K ARR in two months and over $1M in distributor pipeline is genuinely impressive early traction for a pre-seed company selling to governments — a buyer class notorious for 18-month procurement cycles. The Allegheny County pilot and OECD partnership suggest Prior Foundry found an institutional distribution shortcut: sell through intergovernmental organizations that give you simultaneous access to dozens of national governments. That's smart GTM. But my cleantech experience taught me that early institutional enthusiasm doesn't validate the core technology. KiOR had Department of Energy backing and government grants before the science-to-deployment gap destroyed hundreds of millions in capital. The gap here is analogous: 85% accuracy replicating survey responses is not the same as accurately predicting how populations respond to complex, multi-dimensional policy interventions. Prior Foundry hasn't bridged that gap, and the dossier offers no evidence it can be bridged. The option price at pre-seed is right — this is a $500K bet, not $200M — but the technology risk is underappreciated.

The founding team is highly credentialed and has obvious founder-market fit: Shirin brings OECD policy relationships, Jonne Kamphorst built the Stanford research, Keshav Sivakumar deployed government software at Palantir and co-founded Lumitics. That's a well-assembled team. But this is precisely the profile I'm less excited about — domain experts commercializing their own research. Jonne is literally the researcher productizing his own paper while starting an assistant professorship at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026. That's a red flag for commitment and a signal that this may be a "research spin-out" rather than a company built by founders who've burned the boats. When I backed the 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems at Mach Industries, the signal was irreverence and total commitment. Prior Foundry's founders are hedging — Jonne has a tenure-track position, Shirin is a PhD candidate. The rate of learning isn't visible because they're executing a pre-existing thesis, not iterating from first principles.

The strongest bull case: LLM capability improves rapidly, simulation accuracy crosses from "replicates surveys" to "predicts behavioral outcomes," and Prior Foundry's early government relationships become the distribution moat that matters more than the open-source technology. In that world, this becomes the default infrastructure for evidence-based policymaking globally — a platform every government subscribes to. The OECD channel would make this scaling path unusually efficient. If I believed the accuracy gap would close within 2-3 years through LLM improvements alone, the early institutional relationships would justify a bet. But that's a market-timing thesis, not a technology thesis, and Prior Foundry doesn't control the variable that determines its success (foundational model capability).

Prior Foundry scores well on radical affordability — replacing million-dollar consulting engagements with on-demand simulation is exactly the "make the expensive cheap" pattern I look for. But affordability is a consequence of the technology working, and the technology hasn't proven it works for the actual use case (policy outcome prediction, not survey replication). The approach is novel relative to AnyLogic's rule-based simulation, but it's an application of published open-source research, not a proprietary breakthrough. The ambition level — better policy simulation tools — is useful but not the kind of improbable bet that makes people say "that's impossible." This is a competent team building a real product for a real market. That's a fine company. It's not my kind of bet. The consequence magnitude is bounded (decision support, not decision transformation), and the technical moat is exposed. I pass.
