# Grade -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check with any company is the structural moment -- what changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this market possible now? For Grade, I can't find one. Creator payments have been a known infrastructure gap for years. Lumanu raised $20.6M to address it. Trolley raised $44.4M. Creative Juice raised $20M plus a $50M creator fund. The performance-based compensation angle Grade emphasizes -- paying per view instead of flat fees -- is a genuine product insight, but it's a feature differentiation in an already-funded category, not a structural inflection. The dossier's "why now" section is entirely marked [Inferred], which tells me even thorough research couldn't identify a concrete trigger. When I invested in Gusto, the structural moment was cloud infrastructure making modern payroll viable for the first time -- ADP and Paychex literally couldn't compete on the new architecture. Grade doesn't have an equivalent discontinuity. The creator economy has been growing at a healthy clip for half a decade; nothing in the last two years fundamentally altered the payment infrastructure requirements.

The founder execution velocity is where this deal gets interesting and where I'd want to be honest about what's working. James Heaney hit $40K MRR within two months at Pray Screen with 250,000+ downloads, then sold it. He sold his first startup at 22. Lotanna founded XPO (raised over £1M), sold two SaaS businesses, and built Creator Check. Together they've built, scaled, and exited four mobile AI apps using creators as their primary growth channel -- which means they discovered this payment pain through direct operating experience, not market research. The 120% month-over-month growth in payouts ($380K+ in 30 days) and 790+ creators paid across 190+ countries at pre-seed is real velocity. These are builders who ship. And critically, they've worked together before -- the co-founder risk that kills most pre-seed companies is substantially mitigated here.

The bull case requires believing that "performance-based pay" is not just a feature but a fundamentally different product category that incumbents can't easily absorb. If that's true, Grade has a version of what Stripe had -- an integration model so different from the existing paradigm that bolting it onto legacy architecture creates structural friction. Tipalti is built for fixed invoices; rearchitecting around variable performance-linked payouts would conflict with its core AP workflow. Lumanu positions around tax compliance and vendor consolidation, not real-time performance tracking tied to compensation. If the shift from flat-fee to performance-based creator compensation accelerates -- and there's reasonable evidence it will as brands demand measurable ROI from creator spend -- then the company purpose-built for this workflow wins. The product-to-distribution potential also exists: payments are inherently sticky, and Grade could expand into creator analytics, discovery, tax compliance, and campaign management through the same customer relationship. That's the Stripe playbook -- wedge with payments, distribute everything else.

But here's where I struggle: the core technology -- connecting performance APIs to a payment disbursement layer -- is technically reproducible. The dossier itself acknowledges this. Any company with API access to payout rails could build something similar, and the better-funded competitors have every incentive to add a performance-tracking layer once the category proves out. Grade's $500K against Lumanu's $20.6M and Trolley's $44.4M means they need to win on product velocity before incumbents react. That's a race against capital, not a structural advantage. And the moat signals are thin -- no patents, no network effects, no regulatory barrier. The switching costs from accumulated creator payment data are real but modest; companies switch payment processors regularly. This is a competitive execution bet, not a structural inflection bet. The miracle count is concerning: they need to (1) build a differentiated product, (2) acquire customers faster than better-capitalized competitors, and (3) achieve enough volume for data advantages to compound -- before Lumanu adds a performance-tracking tab.

The brand disambiguation issue is also worth noting -- "Grade" is a common English word, and a Nordic HR-tech company already occupies the primary LinkedIn listing. At scale this is solvable; at pre-seed with limited marketing dollars, it creates unnecessary friction in a demo-based sales motion.

I respect the founders' execution capabilities and domain experience. In a market with a clear structural inflection, I'd be writing a check on the strength of their velocity alone. But my framework weights the market's structural moment at the top of the hierarchy because I've seen great operators get ground down in markets where the timing thesis amounts to "growing steadily" rather than "inflecting now." Creator payment infrastructure has been growing steadily. That's not the same thing as a structural moment where the adoption curve suddenly bends.
