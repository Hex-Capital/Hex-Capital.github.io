# Nine Fives -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Two ex-SpaceX RF engineers selling driverless rack instruments to hardware manufacturers is the kind of dossier that scores high on category non-consensus and zero on owned distribution — and that pairing tells me exactly what Nine Fives is. It is a deep-domain industrial hardware play in a sleeve where the consensus YC crowd will not pattern-match, fronted by a CEO (Noah Levy, six-time SpaceX kickass award, Falcon 9 Radar Altimeter, Dragon 2 S-Band, Starship GPS/S-Band) whose entire pre-pitch artifact is a LinkedIn page. That's the signature: maximum founder-market fit, minimum founder-as-asset, wrapped around physical SKUs (PD8X, POE-ATTEN-6G, POE-SWITCH-6G, POE-PMETER-6G) capped at $1,500 ASP. Most of my partners would file Nine Fives next to **Sublime Security** on the deal memo. I don't think that's the right comp.

**Sublime Security** is the HIGH rung on my non-consensus ladder because tier-1 security funds had crowded into late-stage SOC SaaS while the email-security primitive was sitting underpriced; Nine Fives sits between **Sublime** and the MID rung because the category is genuinely something the Sand Hill consensus will dismiss — RF test equipment is the definition of "boring industrial" and I see no evidence other generalist seed funds were bidding. The "January 1997 revision of the USB HID specification" line in the YC Launch is exactly the kind of legibility-translation hook I'd use to story-broker this into a Series A buyer's frame later. Where Nine Fives diverges from **Sublime**: Sublime was pure software with infinite gross margin and a Series A path priced on ARR multiples; Nine Fives is selling $1,500 hardware units against Mini-Circuits' >400-SKU catalog up to 67 GHz and DS Instruments' $649–$749 6 GHz attenuators. The category is non-consensus in the right direction, but the asset underneath the round is not the same asset.

**John Fish / Bookshelved** is the LOW-rung failure mechanism here, inverted — Bookshelved earns its check because the creator brings owned distribution to the cap table on day one; Nine Fives brings the opposite. Noah Levy has no Twitter, no GitHub, no Substack, no opinionated essay on why the Keysight/R&S/NI driver stack is rotting. Andrew Kurtz's LinkedIn headline is literally the word "Builder." This is the "Writing is thinking. If you can't write, you can't think" filter failing not because the founders can't think — they clearly can, you don't ship Starship S-Band radios otherwise — but because there is zero pre-funding artifact I can read before the meeting, and zero owned channel that compounds without paid sales motion. Every dollar of Nine Fives revenue will come from enterprise design wins ground out one hardware-engineering team at a time.

On capital structure — the question I'm supposed to ask first — Nine Fives looks like a hardware business being force-fit into a standard YC SAFE cap table. The regatta tack count is ugly: this seed funds inventory and certification, then a Series A funds field engineers and channel build-out, then a B funds international compliance and a higher-frequency SKU line, and somewhere across those tacks the $1,500 ASP has to support enough gross margin to pay for the NineVue software layer that supposedly becomes the moat. The **Ro** Known Exception doesn't bail this out — Ro's capital intensity bought a 50-state prescription-rail moat that nobody could replicate; Nine Fives' capital intensity buys inventory and FCC filings that DS Instruments already cleared. This is structure-wrong for what I underwrite.

Founder conviction is the dimension where I will give Nine Fives more credit than the absent-evidence default would suggest. Counterfactually, for Nine Fives to flip a tier on this axis I'd need one essay from Noah Levy on why the SCPI/HID-era driver stack is structurally unfixable inside Keysight, or a public thread breaking down why TestStand compatibility is a Trojan horse rather than a courtesy. What I have instead is seven years of SpaceX RF work culminating in Starship radios — that's not nothing, that's the closest thing to a missionary-temperament proxy you can get without the writing artifact, because nobody spends a decade tuning production RF in that environment without obsession. It moves Nine Fives off the floor on this dimension but doesn't get it to the **Ben Silbermann** comp where conviction was visible and verifiable pre-check.

The category-sleeve question is where Nine Fives is most clearly outside my convicted lanes. Mechanism: my sleeves compound because the category itself has a structural mispricing — crypto was mispriced post-ICO crash, DTC was mispriced because SaaS-trained VCs couldn't underwrite physical inventory, creators were mispriced because the asset was the person. Industrial RF test equipment has no analogous mispricing; it has a real $3.99B market growing 8.74% to $8.48B by 2034 with five incumbents holding ~55% share, which is a fine business to attack but not a sleeve where I have an edge over a hardware-specialist fund that knows the FCC certification timeline cold. Nine Fives is the right deal for somebody — probably Lux or Eclipse or a defense-tech fund — and the wrong deal for the Slow LP base.

My 36-month forecast on Nine Fives: $3–8M in cumulative unit-sales revenue to 30–80 hardware engineering teams (Anduril, Skydio, Qualcomm, defense primes), followed by an acquisition by Keysight or Rohde & Schwarz in the $40–120M range as a defensive software-stack tuck-in to neutralize the driverless/REST narrative — or, more likely, a quiet plateau as a profitable ~$5M/year industrial hardware shop that nobody at Slow can mark up. Neither outcome justifies the seed entry at venture pricing on a Slow check; the upside case clears 5–10x at best and the modal case is a Type 2 lifestyle hardware company that doesn't return the fund.

The bull case that stings: this is exactly the kind of "illegible asset" I claim to specialize in pricing — boring industrial hardware with a software wedge (skill.md, NineVue, the LLM-readable API layer) that compounds into lock-in as customers version-control test racks against Nine Fives' endpoints, and Keysight literally cannot ship the same product without cannibalizing the driver-software services revenue that funds half their enterprise sales motion. That's a real disruption-from-below opening and I'm the kind of investor who should see it. What dismantles it: the software wedge is asserted, not demonstrated — there are no customer logos, no API call volumes, no Product Hunt launch, no public artifact showing skill.md being adopted by anyone outside the Nine Fives founders. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: signed purchase orders from three or more named tier-1 hardware buyers (SpaceX itself, Anduril, Skydio, Qualcomm) within the next 12 months, or a public commit history showing skill.md integrations landing in a non-Nine-Fives test-automation repo. Until one of those exists, Nine Fives is a pass for me and a fit for somebody whose LPs underwrite industrial deep tech.
