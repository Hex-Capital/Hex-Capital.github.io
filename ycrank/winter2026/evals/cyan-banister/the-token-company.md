# The Token Company -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I noticed first: this is an AI infrastructure middleware company, in the hottest category in tech, with a direct YC batchmate competitor (Compresr, four EPFL PhD researchers), and an existing open-source alternative from Microsoft with 4,400 GitHub stars. When I look for companies that are "not popular" and "difficult to assess," this is essentially the inverse. Everyone is building AI tooling right now. Every fund I know is writing checks into AI infrastructure. The compression-as-a-service concept has been published in academic literature at EMNLP, ACL, and NAACL. This isn't a pocket of dreamers and weirdos — it's a crowded market where consensus already formed. The deals I've done best on — SpaceX when private space was lunacy, Crusoe when it was "Bitcoin mining on gas flares in North Dakota" — those were deals where other investors physically recoiled. Nobody recoils at "make LLM calls cheaper and faster." They nod and ask about your moat.

Otso Veisterä is clearly a remarkable young person. Programming since age seven, national physics champion in Finland, writing Rust and CUDA code at eighteen, building ML models at Planmeca, getting into YC as one of the youngest founders in the batch. I respect that enormously. But when I sit down with a founder, I'm not asking about their awards — I'm asking how they got here, what broke them and rebuilt them, and why this specific problem keeps them up at night. The dossier tells me Otso conceived this product at a hackathon in Sweden. That's a clever technical insight, not a biographical imperative. Compare that to someone who spent years drowning in LLM costs for a product they built out of desperation, or someone who watched a family business fail because they couldn't afford the tools they needed. I don't see the thread connecting Otso's personal story to this particular problem in a way that suggests he'll be the one who persists through the brutal parts. He might — prodigies sometimes have a drive that substitutes for adversity — but it's not the signal I've learned to trust most.

The strongest bull case here is genuinely interesting, and I want to be fair to it. The claim that compression *improves* LLM output quality — not just preserves it while cutting costs — is counterintuitive and, if consistently replicable, would be a real technical insight. The 268K-vote blind arena study is a meaningful signal, not a toy benchmark. And if you believe that agentic workflows will explode prompt verbosity faster than LLM providers can cut prices, then a compression middleware layer becomes structurally necessary rather than merely cost-optimizing. The angel investors from Supercell, Wolt, and Hugging Face suggest that serious operators in the Finnish and ML ecosystems see something here. If Otso's bear-1.1 model genuinely achieves quality improvements that survive across diverse workloads — not just cherry-picked benchmarks — and if he can build a data flywheel from customer usage patterns before Compresr or LLMLingua catches up, this could become the standard compression layer for enterprise LLM deployments. That's a real business.

But I keep returning to the structural problems. LLM inference costs are dropping roughly 10x per year. The cost-savings pitch has a melting ice cube quality — even if compression helps today, the absolute dollar savings shrink every quarter. The quality-improvement angle is the real story, but then you're competing with LLM providers who have every incentive to improve their own output quality directly. If compression genuinely makes outputs better, OpenAI and Anthropic will ask "why don't we just compress internally?" — and they have the research teams, the data, and the distribution to do it overnight. The moat here is thin. I also notice the team dynamics: Rasmus's role isn't publicly defined as co-founder, there's no public GitHub activity from him, and the relationship between these two Finnish tech ecosystem participants is opaque. After HQ Trivia, I always ask how co-founders argue. I can't even confirm the co-founder structure here.

This isn't a company I'd write a check into from my own money. It's technically competent work in a consensus market, built by a talented young engineer without a biographical connection to the problem, in a space with declining unit economics and direct competition from both a YC batchmate and Microsoft Research. None of my core signals fire: it's not anti-consensus, it doesn't create economic access for underserved populations, it doesn't make an invisible system visible, and it doesn't operate in a stigmatized or regulated space that deters other investors. I wish Otso well — he has genuine technical gifts and decades ahead of him — but this particular company doesn't match how I invest.
