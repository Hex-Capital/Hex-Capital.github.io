# Superlog -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Superlog is the kind of company where the technically credible co-founder profile (Arseniy Shishaev, ex-Datadog engineer from École 42) collides with a category that every funded player on Earth is currently shipping into — Resolve AI at a $1B valuation in February 2026, Datadog Bits AI SRE, Sentry Seer, Middleware OpsAI, and YC batch-mate IncidentFox all post a fix-PR to Slack. The dimensional signature is a high on organic problem exposure paired with a near-floor on whether the idea repels anyone, and that pairing tells me almost everything I need to know about Superlog's likely trajectory.

The dimension where Superlog places highest is Organic Problem Discovery, and the ladder runs from Dropbox (Drew Houston's USB stick — pure personal frustration) at the high rung, through Replit (hackers building for hackers, which I found on Hacker News in 2017) at the mid rung, down to my pass example of consultants who "identified an opportunity" in B2B workflow automation through customer interviews. Superlog sits in an awkward split: Shishaev spent real time inside Datadog, which is the closest thing to "live in the future, then build what's missing" that this dossier offers, and that pins his half above the mid rung. But Nicolò Magnante's headline is literally "Swish | Ex BCG | Ex Bluco," and the trio Bluco (AI recruiting, 2024) → Swish (post-signup GTM agent, October 2025) → Superlog (Spring 2026 observability) is the textbook profile of founders picking ideas from a menu rather than encountering them. So Superlog lands between Replit and the consultants — closer to Replit on the engineer's side, closer to the consultants on the CEO's side.

The dimension where Superlog places lowest is Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, and that ladder runs from Stripe (PCI compliance schlep that everyone else fled) and Bindwell (teenagers doing pesticide chemistry) at the top, through Reddit at the middle, down to the workflow-automation founders whose pitch could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece. Superlog sits at the bottom rung, and the dossier pins it there with embarrassing specificity: Resolve AI raised $125M from Lightspeed in February 2026, Datadog and Sentry have shipped overlapping features natively, IncidentFox is in Superlog's own YC cohort. The "AI investigates and posts a fix PR" pattern has at least four named competitors. There is no schlep here. Nobody's unconscious is filtering this idea out. Everyone's conscious mind is racing toward it.

On Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint, the ladder runs from Airbnb's cereal boxes through Patrick Collison shipping Auctomatic before Stripe, down to founders who treat setbacks as fundraising occasions. Superlog gives me no scrappy stories — no weekend prototype, no manual customer acquisition tale, no ramen-profitable anecdote — only the published `superloglabs/skills` repo at 1 star and 0 forks. That places Superlog near the mid rung on artifact evidence (the OSS skills library is real shipped work) but below it on narrative, since Airbnb-tier founders generate notable anecdotes and Superlog has produced none.

On Founder Learning Rate, the ladder runs from Sam Altman's trajectory at Loopt through Collison's Auctomatic-to-Stripe arc, down to founders grinding on the same flat metrics for a year. Superlog's two pivots in eighteen months — recruiting to GTM-agent to observability — could be high learning velocity or could be thrashing across unrelated categories. I read this as the wrong kind of stubborn: persistent about being founders, flexible about what to build, but flexible across domains rather than within one. That's not the running-back quality. On Earnest Builder Identity, the ladder runs from Watsi through Replit down to status-driven operators, and Magnante's HEC Paris / BCG / Bain stack with the LinkedIn headline leading on consulting brands sits much closer to the polished-operator pattern than the obsessive-builder one. Shishaev's personal site `arseniy.wtf` is the only flicker of the hacker identity I look for.

My 36-month forecast for Superlog: under $2M ARR, acquihired by a mid-tier observability platform or pivoted into a narrower vertical (LiveKit or Expo-specific telemetry, riding the existing skills library), while Resolve AI consolidates the AI-SRE category and Datadog absorbs the rest into Bits. The mechanism is straightforward — Superlog's wedge (auto-installing OTel wizard plus daily re-scan) is a feature, not a company, in a market where the buyer's incumbent already ships an adjacent version of it.

The bull case stings, and I want to be honest about it. Superlog hits my Unstated Pattern dead-on: hackers building for hackers, developer tools, open-source distribution via `npx skills add superloglabs/skills`, an ex-Datadog CTO who knows where the bodies are buried, Slack-native UX. This is the demographic I have systematically overweighted across Stripe, Replit, Retool, and Wordware. If Shishaev's insider knowledge produces a genuinely superior auto-instrumentation experience, the OSS skills repo could compound into the default OTel onboarding kit the way Stripe's docs compounded into payments-default — and the two-pivot history would be re-narrated as learning velocity. What dismantles the bull case is the dossier's own competitive section: Resolve AI was founded by the OpenTelemetry co-creators themselves, which means Superlog's CTO-ex-Datadog edge is dominated by a competitor's CTO-co-created-the-standard edge, and they have $125M to deploy it. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a working repo-fingerprint dataset producing demonstrably higher PR-merge rates than Sentry Seer or Datadog Bits on a public benchmark, plus evidence that Magnante and Shishaev have stopped picking categories from menus and are now obsessed with this one specifically. Until then, Superlog is a pass.
