# Oximy -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that "AI governance for enterprises" is exactly the kind of idea that sounds good in a brainstorming session. Every CISO in America is saying they need visibility into AI tool usage. Every VC is nodding along. Nudge Security just raised $22.5M to do overlapping work. CrowdStrike already has agents on enterprise endpoints and could add AI tool detection as a Tuesday afternoon project. When a problem is this visible and this many people are already working on it, it's not schlep blindness keeping competitors away -- it's that competitors are already here. The problem Oximy is solving is real, but it's consensus-real, which is the most dangerous kind of real for a pre-seed startup. Consensus problems get priced accordingly.

That said, the origin story is not entirely manufactured. Naman Ambavi was a founding member at Induced AI, working on LLMs and generative UI at a company backed by Sam Altman. He was inside the AI tooling ecosystem and likely saw the governance gap from the builder's side. That's a semi-organic discovery -- he didn't read a Gartner report and decide to start a company, he was building AI tools and presumably encountered enterprises struggling to manage them. But the strongest organic signals come from the buyer side, not the builder side. The ideal founder here would be a former CISO who spent three years cobbling together spreadsheets and SSO logs to track AI usage and finally snapped. Ambavi's proximity is real but indirect.

The solo founder situation concerns me specifically. I've seen this pattern enough times to name it. Enterprise sales is a war on multiple fronts -- you're simultaneously building product, closing six-month procurement cycles, maintaining SOC 2 compliance, navigating privacy law in different jurisdictions, and hiring a team. That's hard enough with two founders who trust each other deeply. With one person managing a team of five while hiring across engineering, GTM, content, and partnerships, the surface area of failure grows faster than the team. The dossier shows no co-founder, and at enterprise scale, that's not just a structural weakness -- it's a specific prediction about where this company will break under load.

The technical execution is a mixed signal. Ambavi is genuinely technical -- Google Code-In finalist, CodeJam runner-up, built Easocare and a COVID tracker that hit 10M users in four months. He builds things, which matters. But the core "sensor" component is a fork of mitmproxy. That's assembly, not invention. The actual product value lives in the AI tool taxonomy (3,500+ tools) and the governance workflows on top -- those are product execution advantages, not technical moats. When your differentiation is a categorization database rather than a novel system, you're in a feature race against companies with fifty engineers.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If "millions of requests daily for enterprises in financial services and healthcare" is accurate, that's meaningful deployment at pre-seed. Financial services and healthcare are the hardest verticals to sell into -- if Oximy is actually running in production at regulated enterprises, that demonstrates a level of execution that's unusual for a W26 batch company. Ambavi's prior experience getting Easocare to SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance may have given him the playbook for navigating enterprise procurement. And the repeat investor who backed his previous startup and came back specifically citing "execution ability and product velocity" is a genuine signal -- investors who re-up on a founder after seeing them operate have information that isn't in the dossier. If Ambavi can close two or three named enterprise accounts in the next six months and build a data advantage from aggregate usage patterns, the early mover position in AI-specific governance could compound. The desktop agent approach, despite its deployment friction, captures data that agentless competitors structurally miss.

But I keep coming back to the competitive dynamics. Nudge Security has 200 customers and 3x ARR growth with $30M in funding. Endpoint security vendors already have agents deployed on every laptop in the Fortune 500. The window for a pre-seed company to establish itself in this category before incumbents absorb the use case is narrow, and narrowing. Ambavi is a capable builder operating at high velocity, but this feels like a race where the other runners started with a significant head start and longer legs. I'd want to see evidence that Oximy's desktop agent approach discovers meaningfully more shadow AI usage than Nudge's agentless approach -- that would be a real wedge. Without that proof point, I'm watching a solo founder sprint into a consensus category against well-capitalized competitors.
