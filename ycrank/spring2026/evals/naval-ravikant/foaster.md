# Foaster -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Foaster's case study is the first thing that caught my attention: two people, 280 interviews, 1,200+ processes mapped, 9 days. A McKinsey team would need ten consultants and two months to produce equivalent output for a 350-employee firm. That ratio — two founders replacing a ten-person team in one-tenth the time — is a hundred-fold productivity differential. When I see that kind of output-per-person, I lean forward. But then I ask the question I always ask: is this leverage sustainable, or is it a demo?

Raphaël Dabadie and Alexandre Combes are 23-year-old ENSAE Paris classmates. Alexandre built Répondia, where AI agents handled 60,000+ restaurant calls for major French restaurant groups. That's real. He knows how to build conversational AI agents that work in production — not toy demos, not benchmarks, but systems handling tens of thousands of real interactions. Their Werewolf benchmark got 300,000+ views on X and drew engagement from OpenAI's president, Google DeepMind, Mistral, and NVIDIA researchers. The specific knowledge here is genuine, but it's specific knowledge in *building conversational AI agents*, not in *enterprise operational transformation*. Those are different domains. When Aravind Srinivas built Perplexity, his specific knowledge from Google Brain and OpenAI was the exact knowledge required to build the product. Foaster's founders have the AI agent expertise but lack the enterprise operations expertise — they're building a product for a domain they've studied, not one they've lived inside. The Répondia pivot confirms this: French corporate registry shows Foaster Technologies shares a registration number (983585290) with Répondia, meaning they pivoted from restaurant calls to enterprise consulting. That's founders identifying a market opportunity, not founders unable to stop solving a problem that's been consuming them for years.

Foaster's leverage architecture is where my framework gets uncomfortable. The AI interview tool itself is pure code leverage — zero marginal cost per additional interview, scales to hundreds of parallel conversations. Beautiful. But look at Foaster's three product tiers: the Continuous tier adds "dedicated expert support," and the Enterprise tier adds "forward-deployed engineers and custom integrations." That's labor leverage wrapped around code leverage. Every enterprise client requires human delivery time. Foaster isn't building Notion, where each additional user costs nothing — it's building an AI-augmented consulting firm where revenue scales with headcount for the upper tiers. Notion scaled to millions of users with a small team because the product IS the delivery. Foaster's product does the diagnosis, but the prescription and implementation still require human principals on-site. The consulting business model has a structural ceiling that code-only businesses don't face.

McKinsey's partnership with Wonderful AI for enterprise AI agent deployment validates the principal-agent problem working in Foaster's favor — McKinsey can't automate its own billable hours without cannibalizing its revenue model. That's real structural advantage. The bull case writes itself: $90 billion TAM by 2035, incumbents structurally unable to self-disrupt, two founders already delivering enterprise engagements (LIPTON Teas and Infusions is a real company, not a friend's startup), and AI interview technology that captures tacit organizational knowledge that Celonis's log-based process mining ($1.77 billion raised, $771 million revenue) cannot touch. If Foaster can productize the assessment tier into a self-serve platform — where companies run the interviews themselves and receive automated dashboards — the leverage architecture flips from consulting to software. That would change everything. But I don't see evidence they're building toward that. The three-tier structure moves in the opposite direction, toward more human involvement at higher price points.

Foaster's commoditization risk is the bear case I can't dismiss. The core technology — LLM-based interviews with structured output — relies on generally available foundation models. Any consulting firm with domain expertise could build a competing interview tool in months. Celonis could add an interview layer to its process intelligence platform. The Big Four already have the client relationships that Foaster will spend years building. Foaster's accumulating operational maps per client create some switching costs, but those maps belong to the client's problem space, not to a compounding network effect. Compare this to Perplexity, where every query improves the model and every citation builds publisher trust — a genuine data flywheel. Foaster's data doesn't compound across clients in the same way. Each engagement starts relatively fresh.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Foaster is building toward a self-serve assessment platform where mid-market companies ($10M-$100M revenue) can run AI interviews and receive transformation blueprints without any human involvement from the Foaster team — and evidence of 10+ paying customers using it independently. That would transform the leverage architecture from consulting to software, validate the access democratization thesis (making McKinsey-grade analysis available to companies that could never afford it), and confirm the team-to-value ratio is structural rather than heroic. Right now, Foaster is a talented two-person team using AI to deliver better consulting faster. That's valuable, but it's not the kind of leverage architecture that produces power-law returns. The specific knowledge gap in enterprise transformation, combined with the hybrid consulting model, puts this in neutral territory — I respect the founders' AI agent expertise and the case study is impressive, but the business as described isn't the kind of bet I write checks for.
