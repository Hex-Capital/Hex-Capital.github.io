# Protent -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Protent is something that would make most investors lean in but actually makes me pull back: this is a crowded, well-capitalized market. Verkada has raised $700 million. Lumana AI just raised $40 million. Spot AI has $93 million. Actuate, Coram — the list keeps going. When I look at a space and see hundreds of millions already deployed by well-funded competitors, the contrarian signal I depend on is gone. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe — were companies nobody else wanted to fund. That lonely conviction, that willingness to look foolish, is what creates the asymmetric return. Protent is entering a market where the consensus has already formed: AI video analytics is going to be massive. They've picked a specific wedge — law enforcement instead of commercial enterprises — and that's smart positioning, but it's not an insight that makes other investors uncomfortable. It's a go-to-market choice.

The founder story here is where I struggle most. I always want to hear about how someone got to where they are — not the business plan, not the technology stack, but the human path. And what I see with Srihan Balaji and Abhisheik Sharma is strong technical pedigree — Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology, Lockheed Martin deploying RL-optimized video intelligence, Amazon AI/ML, published NLP research. These are credentials that signal capability, and I don't dismiss that. But I don't see the biographical thread that makes this problem *theirs*. Why public safety? Why policing? The best founders I've backed are solving problems that emerged from their own lived experience — problems they couldn't walk away from even if they tried. Here it feels like technically talented people identified a market opportunity that matches their skillset. That's rational, but rational and inevitable are different things, and at the earliest stage I'm betting on inevitability.

I backed Flock Safety because Garrett Langley's story and the way he was building it felt different — he was bringing public safety visibility to communities, making an invisible system visible to the people who needed it most. That's the pattern I gravitate toward even when I don't articulate it explicitly: companies that take opaque power structures and open them up. Protent sits on the other side of that equation. They're building predictive surveillance tools — "sentiment analysis," "escalation detection," real-time monitoring across thousands of camera feeds — and delivering that intelligence to police departments. The power flows from communities *to* institutions, not the other direction. There's a legitimate case that preventing violent incidents saves lives in exactly the underserved communities that experience disproportionate violence, and I take that seriously. But "sentiment analysis" applied to live surveillance feeds in public spaces also carries real risk of reinforcing the kinds of systemic biases that harm those same communities. The YC LinkedIn post generated substantial critical commentary about dystopian surveillance, and while controversy alone doesn't scare me — I've backed companies in defense, male contraception, psychedelic therapy — I need the founder to have a deeply personal relationship with that controversy. I need to feel that they've wrestled with the ethical weight of what they're building, not just its technical elegance.

The strongest bull case here is real and I want to name it honestly: these founders have already landed deployments with police departments in Atlanta, Chicago, and St. Louis metro areas at pre-seed. That's remarkable for a two-person team selling into government. Government procurement is brutal — long cycles, compliance requirements, political approvals — and the fact that they've navigated this at all suggests either unusual hustle or meaningful connections from Srihan's Lockheed Martin background. If they can convert those pilots into multi-year contracts and build the data flywheel of escalation patterns trained on real law enforcement scenarios, the switching costs become formidable. Government customers don't churn easily. The path from here to a dominant law enforcement video intelligence platform is plausible, and the TAM — even the GovTech subsegment of a $5-12 billion market — is substantial. If I were a different kind of investor, one who optimized for market timing and technical founder-market fit, this would score well.

But I'm not that investor. I'm looking for the biographical commitment that tells me this founder will still be here when the procurement cycle stalls at month eighteen, when a city council bans predictive surveillance technology, when Lumana AI decides to build the exact same law enforcement module with sixty-four million dollars behind them. I need the personal stake that makes quitting impossible. And I also need the anti-consensus signal — the feeling that I'm seeing something the market hasn't priced in yet. Here, the market has very much priced in AI video analytics. The founders are technically strong but early-career, navigating a space that demands not just ML expertise but deep relationships with government buyers, regulatory sophistication around CJIS and FedRAMP, and the political judgment to manage public backlash around surveillance. That's a lot to ask of what appears to be a team in their early twenties without evidence of the adversity-forged resilience I weight most heavily.
