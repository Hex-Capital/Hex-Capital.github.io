# Gikl, Inc -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Péter Vajda has 11,684 Google Scholar citations and an h-index of 41. That's not a line on a resume — that's a decade of genuinely impactful computer vision research, including FBNet (1,949 citations) and foundational work on visual transformers. Seiji Yamamoto has a physics PhD from Rice and was working on Llama at Meta's AGI Foundations team — one of the few AI projects in the last three years that actually mattered. These are serious researchers. And yet when I look at Gikl, Inc, I see a paradox I've encountered many times: extraordinary credentials paired with an idea that has no edges. ScienceSwarm's website returns a 403. There's no Twitter account, no Discord, no users, no revenue, no Product Hunt launch, no app store listing. The most technically accomplished founders in a batch have the least visible product. That gap is the signal I'm paying attention to.

The question I always start with is whether the founders have the animal quality — that slightly-too-intense obsessiveness where they've already built something before anyone asked them to. With Gikl, Inc, I can't find it. Vajda co-founded something called Carbon; details are "limited" per the dossier. Yamamoto's career traces a clean arc through Palantir, Salesforce, Facebook, and Meta — senior titles, increasing scope, the trajectory of a talented operator climbing a well-defined ladder. Neither founder has a story of scrappy building under constraint, of shipping a prototype over a weekend, of personally doing the unscalable thing their software would eventually automate. Maybe those stories exist and the dossier didn't capture them. But in my experience, truly resourceful founders generate notable anecdotes the way stars generate light — you can't miss them. The Airbnb founders selling cereal boxes, the Stripe brothers emailing developers and offering to install their integration on the spot — those stories surfaced because the behavior was so distinctive it became part of the company's mythology. Gikl, Inc has no such mythology yet, and the absence, at a stage where resourcefulness should be most visible, is itself information.

"AI for scientific discovery" is probably the single most consensus-exciting idea in technology right now. FutureHouse raised $70M. Edison Scientific raised $70M at a $250M valuation with Eric Schmidt's backing. Elicit has 400,000 monthly users and $31M in funding. Nobody hears "accelerate science with AI" and thinks "that will never work." Everyone nods enthusiastically. And that's exactly the problem. My entire framework for evaluating ideas — the Peter Thiel Venn diagram, the Black Swan test — is built on the insight that if everyone agrees an idea is good, the returns have been competed away. Gikl, Inc is swimming in the most crowded narrative pool in AI, positioned against competitors with 10-100x their resources, with no clear contrarian insight about why their approach is right and the well-funded incumbents are wrong. The "swarm" concept — community verification of AI-generated hypotheses — is the closest thing to a distinctive angle, but citizen-science platforms have existed for decades (Foldit, Galaxy Zoo, Zooniverse) and none has produced venture-scale returns. Gikl, Inc would need to explain what changes when you add LLMs to this model. The dossier doesn't contain that explanation.

Organic problem discovery is where Gikl, Inc loses me most decisively. Vajda is a computer vision researcher. Yamamoto is a physics PhD turned ML infrastructure leader. Neither appears to have been a working scientist banging their head against the wall because hypothesis generation was too slow, or a citizen-science enthusiast frustrated by the lack of AI tools. The framing — "Scientific research remains bottleneck-constrained by the number of trained researchers" — reads like a thesis statement composed during a brainstorming session, not a personal frustration that kept someone up at night. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he could tell you the exact moment on the bus when he realized he'd forgotten his USB drive again. When the Octopart founders built their parts search engine, they were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic components. Gikl, Inc's origin story, as far as I can reconstruct it, is: two senior Meta AI researchers decided to leave and build something ambitious with AI. That's a motivation — ambition is good — but it's ambition in search of a problem, not a problem demanding a solution.

The bull case for Gikl, Inc goes like this: Vajda and Yamamoto have deeper AI expertise than 99% of founders in any YC batch. Yamamoto's physics PhD gives genuine scientific domain grounding that most AI-for-science teams lack. They worked together at Meta, reducing co-founder relationship risk. The "community verification" model could create network effects that pure-automation approaches like Edison Scientific can't replicate. And maybe I'm wrong about the idea — maybe the Reddit pattern applies, where exceptional founders find their way to the right product through iteration, and I should bet on the people regardless. I backed the Reddit founders on exactly this logic. But the Reddit analogy breaks down in a specific way: Alexis and Steve were 22, hungry, and demonstrated instant responsiveness. They built the first version of Reddit in weeks after I suggested it. Vajda and Yamamoto are mid-career research managers who left what I'd estimate were $500K+ compensation packages at Meta. The urgency profile is different. The running back quality — looking for openings, cutting sideways when blocked — I can't evaluate it because Gikl, Inc hasn't shown me any blocks they've navigated around. The condition that would flip me to Invest: show me 50 users who are obsessively using ScienceSwarm to work on real scientific problems, in whatever crappy early form it takes, and can articulate why they can't go back to Elicit or just using ChatGPT directly. That would be evidence of making something people want, which is always the ultimate test.

Gikl, Inc scores highest on earnest builder identity — Vajda's 11,684 citations represent genuine intellectual commitment, and you don't get a physics PhD at Rice without caring about science for its own sake. But the two dimensions that most determine my verdict are organic problem discovery, where I see no evidence that this idea grew from lived frustration rather than opportunity identification, and the idea-that-repels test, where "AI for science" fails completely — it's the most broadly appealing pitch category in the current landscape. Strong founders can overcome a bad idea, but these founders haven't yet demonstrated the scrappy, obsessive builder quality that would make me bet on them regardless.
