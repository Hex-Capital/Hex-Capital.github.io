# OctaPulse -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Fish hatchery quality assurance is exactly the kind of market that makes most investors' eyes glaze over. Five minutes per fish, manual inspection, trained technicians doing repetitive visual assessment -- it sounds like a process problem from the 1990s, and that's precisely what makes it interesting to me. My best-performing investments share a common trait: the market sounded boring when I first heard about it. PagerDuty was "just alerting." Gusto was "just payroll." Samsara was "just fleet tracking." OctaPulse is "just fish inspection." The "too boring" archetype is the most reliable non-obvious market pattern in my portfolio. And OctaPulse fits it cleanly -- nobody in the venture ecosystem is sitting around thinking about broodstock phenotyping automation.

The structural moment here is real but narrower than I'd like. Edge AI hardware -- specifically the Nvidia Jetson Orin family and Luxonis OAK cameras with onboard VPUs -- crossed a performance-per-watt threshold in the last 18-24 months that enables production-grade inference in remote, power-constrained environments. Three years ago, running YOLO-based detection with TensorRT INT8 quantization on a device you could deploy in a fish hatchery with Starlink connectivity wasn't feasible at production quality. That's a genuine capability threshold, not a hand-wavy "AI is better now" argument. And the macro backdrop is relevant: aquaculture surpassing wild-catch creates industrialization pressure on a sector that's been artisanal in its production methods. But here's my concern -- unlike Harvey, where the capability gap (GPT-4 fidelity for legal work) was closing on a predictable scaling curve with massive implications, the edge AI threshold for fish inspection doesn't unlock a market that's 10x larger than it appears. The precision aquaculture technology market is under $800M today, and hatchery-stage QA is a fraction of that. The $310B global aquaculture TAM is the kind of static number I warn founders about using -- it tells you the size of the ocean, not the size of the fish you can catch.

Where this gets more compelling is the product-to-distribution trajectory, though it's more latent than articulated. Once OctaPulse's hardware and data pipeline are embedded in a hatchery's workflow -- capturing phenotypic data that feeds into selective breeding programs -- the switching costs compound. That accumulated genetic and phenotypic dataset becomes integral to the farm's breeding decisions. From that position, expanding into feeding optimization, health monitoring, and processing QA is a natural distribution play. You're already the system of record for the fish's biological profile from hatching. But I'm inferring this trajectory more than the founders are articulating it. The dossier shows a team focused on nailing the initial use case, not yet thinking about the platform expansion that would separate a $200M outcome from a $2B one.

The single-miracle test is where I start getting cautious. What's the one thing that has to work? I can identify at least three: (1) the vision and robotics system needs to generalize reliably across species -- right now it's validated on trout only, and model accuracy may degrade significantly for salmon, sea bass, or bream without substantial retraining data; (2) the hardware needs to survive harsh hatchery environments at scale without requiring heavy on-site customization per deployment; and (3) they need to sell into a conservative, internationally fragmented industry where decision-makers aren't Silicon Valley early adopters. That's compounding obstacles. Compare this to Samsara, which had a clearer single miracle -- getting industrial fleet operators to adopt IoT dashboards -- with the hardware itself being relatively standardized across deployments. OctaPulse's species generalization problem means every new customer segment potentially requires significant model retraining and hardware adaptation.

The revenue signal is genuinely strong for pre-seed -- a six-figure annual contract with Riverence, the largest trout producer in North America, is not a pilot or an LOI. That's real money from a real customer solving a real problem. Singh's hardware pedigree (ASML, Nvidia, Tesla, Toyota) gives me confidence in the robotics engineering, and the CMU Robotics Institute connection is the right institutional backing for this kind of work. But I notice that neither founder has deep aquaculture domain experience. They identified this opportunity from the outside -- Grech is a Bloomberg finance person with an MBA, Singh is a mechanical engineer. The "Future Leader" recognition from the National Fisheries Institute helps, but it's an award, not a decade of industry relationships. Selling into Chilean salmon farms and Norwegian aquaculture operations requires deep industry trust networks that take years to build.

The bull case for OctaPulse is a Samsara-like trajectory: boring industrial IoT infrastructure that becomes indispensable, with a data moat that compounds over time and a hardware-plus-software integration that's genuinely difficult to replicate. If the vision models generalize across species faster than I expect, and if the first three deployments create enough reference customer credibility to accelerate sales in the top-five aquaculture-producing countries, this could become the default hatchery automation platform globally. The M&A activity (Aquabyte acquired, Observe Technologies acquired by AKVA) validates market demand and could clear competitors from the field. A world where OctaPulse captures hatchery QA, then expands into full-lifecycle farm management with a proprietary multi-species dataset, is a world where this is a billion-dollar company. But that path requires the species generalization miracle, the hardware reliability miracle, and the international sales miracle to all resolve favorably -- and I've seen too many robotics companies stumble on exactly these compounding challenges.

I like this market archetype and the timing thesis is credible. But the multi-miracle concern, the narrow initial SAM, and the founders' limited domain depth keep me in neutral territory. This is a company I'd track closely -- if the next two deployments go smoothly on different species, my conviction would shift substantially.
