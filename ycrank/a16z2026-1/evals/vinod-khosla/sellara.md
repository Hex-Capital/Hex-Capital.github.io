# Sellara -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Sellara is the company I describe when I explain what I don't invest in. AI agents that automate booking, reconciliations, and compliance for banks and broker-dealers — this is the vertical AI copilot pitch I hear fifty times a year at Demo Day. The consequence of Sellara fully succeeding is that Citi's middle office runs with fewer people. That's a fine business outcome for someone, but nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic human need becomes affordable. No industry gets unmade. The world with a successful Sellara looks almost identical to the world without one — banks are slightly more profitable, some operations staff get redeployed. I need to either lose my money or make fifty times my money, and the consequence magnitude here caps the upside at "moderately successful enterprise SaaS." Charles-André Jolly covered 180 institutional clients at Citi, which means he can probably sell this product. But selling a product and changing the world are different activities.

The technical approach reinforces my concern. Sellara claims its AI "outperforms leading LLMs by 60%+ and runs 30x faster" — a claim with no published benchmark, no peer review, no third-party audit. Spreadsheets are fiction, and so are unvalidated performance claims. The product description — copy-and-paste interface, drag-and-drop execution, Outlook and Bloomberg integrations — reads like applied AI layered on top of commodity LLMs, not a step-function technology breakthrough. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown had synthesized heme protein — that was novel science, not a wrapper. When I look at Sellara, I see the same general-purpose AI agent frameworks (LangChain, CrewAI) that anyone can deploy, dressed in financial services compliance certifications. The SOC II and ISO 27001 at pre-seed is genuinely impressive operational execution, but certifications are table stakes for selling to banks, not a technology moat. Beam AI is already in this exact space with $4.5 million in revenue, 57 employees, and an Accenture partnership — Sellara is entering a fight where the more-resourced competitor has a multi-year head start.

The founding team is competent but structurally misaligned with how I pick founders. Jolly and Secord are Citi veterans — domain experts building tools for the domain they came from. I have said explicitly and repeatedly: "When I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I've always picked better thinking." These founders know exactly what can't be done in institutional trading operations, which means they're optimizing within the existing paradigm rather than questioning whether the paradigm should exist. Ahmad Roumie's pivot from robotics and nutrition tracking at BabylonAI (YC W23) to financial operations AI is the most interesting founder signal here — it suggests adaptability — but pivoting into a domain your co-founders already know well is not the same as the outsider naivety I look for. Peter Beck built rocket engines in a garage in New Zealand without an aerospace degree. The Mach Industries founder is a 19-year-old dropout competing with Lockheed. Sellara's founders are experienced professionals building exactly what their experience would predict. That's execution, not insight.

The strongest bull case for Sellara is distribution. Jolly's 180 institutional client relationships and Secord's six years across Citi's sales desks give them warm introductions that most pre-seed enterprise startups would kill for. Financial services sales cycles run 6-12 months; founders who already have the trust of institutional buyers can compress that timeline meaningfully. If the 60% performance claim reflects genuinely proprietary models trained on financial domain data — not prompt engineering on GPT-4 — then each deployment could create switching costs and a data flywheel that compounds over time. And the $11 billion AI trading platform market growing at 16% CAGR means there's real enterprise spend migrating toward AI-native tools. For this to be a Khosla-style bet, Sellara would need to demonstrate that its technology doesn't just automate existing workflows but eliminates entire cost centers — replacing not 40% of a process but 100% of the humans doing it, at a fraction of the cost, creating a discontinuous shift in how financial operations work. I see no evidence of that ambition in the current positioning.

The comparison that keeps surfacing is the negative space of Impossible Foods. Pat Brown didn't set out to make the meat industry 40% more efficient — he set out to eliminate animal agriculture. The consequence of success was feeding billions of people sustainably at lower cost. Sellara is making trade reconciliation faster for hedge funds. One of these is a problem too important not to work on. The other is a workflow optimization. Both can be profitable, but only one justifies the risk profile I underwrite. Sellara's ambition level — automating institutional trading operations — is consensus, not improbable. Every bank CTO in 2026 is already budgeting for AI workflow automation. When the experts agree it should be done, the return profile collapses toward competitive markets and commoditized margins.

Consequence magnitude and ambition level are the two dimensions that locked this verdict. Sellara scores low on both — not because the founders lack capability, but because the ceiling of success is a well-run enterprise software company serving thousands of institutional clients, not a force that reshapes how millions of people live. I pass.
