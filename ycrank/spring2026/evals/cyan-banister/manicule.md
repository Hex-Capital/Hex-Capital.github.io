# Manicule -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Manicule: everybody already knows documentation is broken. Every developer tool founder I've ever met will tell you their docs are a mess, they need to fix them, they just don't have time. That's not an insight — that's a dinner party complaint. The most promising companies I've backed share a characteristic that Manicule fundamentally lacks: they are not popular. They are difficult to assess. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, people told me I was throwing money away. When I backed Crusoe Energy, mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded like a fever dream. Manicule is an AI-augmented documentation agency for developer tools. Nobody is going to tell you that's a crazy idea. Nobody is going to struggle to assess it. It makes perfect, reasonable, consensus-approved sense — and that's exactly what makes me hesitate.

I always want to hear the founder's story before I hear the business pitch. With Naman and Shreyans, what I see is two competent professionals who noticed a gap while working together — Naman was freelancing on Supermemory's docs, Shreyans was a founding engineer there, and they saw that good documentation moved the needle on enterprise deals. That's a smart professional observation, and I respect the domain experience. Naman's three years running a content studio for devtools companies means he understands the craft. But I'm listening for something deeper — the biographical thread that makes this founder and this problem inseparable. The kind of story where you can feel that this person would be doing this work even if nobody paid them for it. What I hear instead is: "We saw a business opportunity and we're well-positioned to execute on it." That's legitimate, but it's not the signal that makes me write a check from my own money.

I'll give credit where it's due: the co-founder dynamic here is actually solid. These two have worked together across Duelance and Supermemory before deciding to co-found together — that's exactly the kind of pre-tested relationship I look for after what happened with HQ Trivia. They have complementary skills, a shared professional history, and they clearly know how to work through projects together. And $15K MRR with profitability at this stage is genuinely noteworthy. Five named clients, a viral case study with Supermemory driving real metrics — 30% improvement in answer success rates, enterprise deals closing. They're executing.

But let me articulate the bull case, because I want to be honest about what could make this work. If Manicule's AI tooling layer — the code verification against live APIs, the automated auditing, the draft generation from OpenAPI specs — becomes genuinely better than anything a freelancer or traditional agency can replicate, they could build a services moat that eventually flips into a product. The timing is real: thousands of AI startups are launching, all of them need docs, none of them want to hire for it. If Naman and Shreyans can use their early client engagements to build proprietary process automation that compounds with each deployment, the agency model becomes a Trojan horse for a platform. Mintlify is doing $10M ARR selling the tools — Manicule could own the execution layer on top. For this to be a great investment, though, you'd need to believe that the AI automation creates a genuine scale advantage before LLMs themselves commoditize technical writing entirely. And I don't see evidence of that product ambition in anything public. No planned SaaS pivot, no indication they're building toward a platform. Right now, this is a well-run agency, and agencies are not what I invest in.

The piece that's hardest for me is that Manicule doesn't create economic access for anyone who currently lacks it. My best investments — Uber, Postmates, Affirm, Thumbtack — opened doors for people who were locked out of economic opportunity. Manicule writes documentation for VC-backed developer tool startups. These are among the most well-resourced companies in technology. The service is valuable to its clients, but it doesn't make an invisible system visible or unlock opportunity for an underserved population. Combined with the commoditization risk — Mintlify already acquired Trieve for RAG-powered documentation, and every LLM improvement chips away at the human editorial premium — this feels like a well-executed business in a space that doesn't activate any of my core conviction signals. Naman and Shreyans clearly know how to deliver, and I wish them well, but this isn't the kind of bet that keeps me up at night with excitement.
