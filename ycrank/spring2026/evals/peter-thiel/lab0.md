# Lab0 -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Rocketlane raised $105 million, has 750+ customers, doubled revenue in the past year — and felt threatened enough by Lab0 to ban them from a conference. That exclusion from Propel on April 19, 2026 is the most analytically interesting signal in this dossier, and it cuts both ways. An incumbent gatekeeping against you proves you exist in their field of vision. But existing in an incumbent's field of vision is precisely the problem. Rocketlane launched "Nitro," an agentic execution platform. Certinia launched "Veda," AI agents for professional services. Kantata launched "Expertise Engine." ServiceNow acquired Moveworks. Four well-capitalized players shipped AI implementation agents within a six-month window. Lab0 is not entering a market built around a secret — it is entering a market where every participant has converged on the same thesis simultaneously. When I see a competitive landscape with five companies building functionally identical products, I see the cleantech pattern: entrepreneurs swept up by the AI wave rather than driven by a proprietary insight about the world. "AI will automate enterprise implementation" is not a truth that few people agree with — it is the organizing assumption of an entire competitive category.

Lab0's research output hints at something more specific. Lakshya Gupta co-authored "World of Workflows," a benchmark demonstrating that frontier LLMs suffer from "dynamics blindness" — failing to predict cascading side effects in enterprise systems. This is a real observation about a genuine technical limitation. But it is published on arXiv for anyone to read, it was conducted on ServiceNow's own platform, and it identifies a problem without demonstrating that Lab0 possesses a proprietary solution. A secret must be both non-obvious and structurally defensible. Gupta's paper makes the problem obvious to every researcher and competitor who reads it, which is the opposite of creating a monopoly. Compare this to Palantir's founding insight: that intelligence agencies needed a platform for integrating heterogeneous data sources under strict access controls — a thesis the intelligence community itself initially rejected, built on proprietary technology developed over years of classified work. Lab0's insight is an academic benchmark available to all.

Lab0's three founders — Gupta and Onkar Borade from IIT Bombay, Sujay Srivastava from IIT Madras — bring competent technical backgrounds. Srivastava previously founded Ravvio in BFSI AI; Borade has production engineering experience at Oracle and Mudrex. But I see no evidence of the kind of shared prehistory forged under extreme pressure that predicts founder durability. A university connection is the weakest form of founding bond. More critically, Lab0's stated ambition — "0-day Enterprise software Implementation" — has the cadence of definite conviction but the substance of a marketing slogan. Where is the specific, irreversible multi-year plan? What exactly does the world look like when Lab0 succeeds? Which enterprise platform do they monopolize first, and how do they expand concentrically from that beachhead? The dossier reveals no evidence of answers to these questions. The lean-startup playbook — ship agents, learn from rollouts, iterate — is precisely the indefinite thinking my framework penalizes. "Continuous learning from every rollout" is the language of discovery, not design.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Lab0 targets a $451.6 billion system integration market where the incumbents — Accenture, Deloitte, Wipro — have a structural inability to cannibalize their own billable-hours revenue model. This is the classic innovator's dilemma: consulting firms cannot automate implementations without destroying the business model that pays their consultants. If Lab0 could build a data flywheel from accumulated implementation knowledge — configuration patterns, integration failures, enterprise-specific adaptations — it could compound an advantage that late entrants cannot match. And the Rocketlane exclusion is not nothing: incumbents ban threats, not irrelevancies. The strongest version of this argument says Lab0 is the pure-play autonomous implementation company in a market where every competitor is hedging with "AI-augmented human consultants," and that the fully autonomous approach will win because half-measures always lose to complete solutions. I have made investments on structural arguments like this before. But even the best version of this case fails my monopoly test. Lab0 has no proprietary technology — it wraps the same frontier LLMs available to every competitor. It has no distribution channel — no revenue, no LOIs, no named customers, no government contract. It has no physical-world barrier. ServiceNow, which owns the workflow platform Gupta benchmarked against and just acquired Moveworks, can build Lab0's product as a feature. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: Lab0 secures an exclusive implementation automation contract with a major enterprise platform vendor — becoming the embedded implementation layer for Salesforce or ServiceNow the way Palantir became embedded in intelligence agencies. That would create switching costs approaching monopoly. Without it, Lab0 is a software wrapper in a competitive market.

Lab0 is a pure software layer with no atoms component, no hardware moat, no regulatory barrier, and no physical-world manipulation. A well-funded engineering team could replicate the product in months using the same foundation models. This is precisely the category I described when I said "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" — except now we get AI wrappers. The power-law potential exists in the abstract — automating a $451 billion services market could theoretically produce a $10 billion+ outcome — but power-law dynamics require winner-take-all structure, and this market is fragmenting across multiple AI-augmented incumbents rather than consolidating around a single autonomous winner. Lab0 might succeed and still be structurally irrelevant to fund returns because four other companies succeeded alongside it.

The dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Monopoly Secret and Distribution. Lab0 scores low on both because it enters a consensus market with no proprietary insight and no solved path to enterprise customers. The competitive landscape — Rocketlane at $105M, Certinia with Veda, Kantata with Expertise Engine, ServiceNow post-Moveworks — is not a market waiting for a secret. It is a market where the secret is already known to everyone, which means it is not a secret at all.
