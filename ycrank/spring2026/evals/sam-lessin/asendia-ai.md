# Asendia AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Asendia AI is the company I'm describing when I go on CNBC and say "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore." An agentic recruitment platform for staffing agencies — AI sourcing, AI outreach, AI video interviews, AI phone screening — all bundled into a subscription product. Strip away the "agentic" framing and this is a SaaS tool whose entire value proposition is that current AI models can do recruiting tasks. The business model is the subscription. The moat is the software. And every major HCM platform on earth — Workday, Bullhorn, SAP SuccessFactors — has both the distribution to 50,000+ agencies and the economic incentive to build exactly these features. Workday literally just bought Paradox for $253M+ to do this. When I ask my fundamental question — "Does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper?" — the answer here is unambiguously weaker. As foundation models improve, the gap between Asendia's AI agents and what any ATS vendor can build in-house narrows to zero. That's not a startup opportunity. That's a feature roadmap item for an incumbent.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't pass my primary filter. Rihab Lajmi spent a year at Google Cloud and eighteen months at Microsoft in cloud and marketing roles. Badis Zormati did six years as a product application engineer at Infineon and built a B2C interview prep tool. These are competent people — "sane" in the loose sense — but neither has staffing agency operational experience. The deep domain credibility that makes "sane person, insane idea" work requires the sane part to be specifically credible for the insane part. Brian Armstrong was an Airbnb engineer who deeply understood payment infrastructure before proposing a crypto exchange. These founders understand cloud architecture and semiconductors, then pivoted into recruitment. And the idea itself is entirely consensus — "AI for recruiting" is one of the three or four categories that every AI-excited VC is funding right now. A sane person with a sane idea is the quadrant where returns go to die, because the opportunity has already been priced in by every fund with an AI thesis.

The pivot history is telling. The Product Hunt launches — 286 upvotes, 291 upvotes — were for a B2C job seeker product, "Sarah, your AI Job Coach." Now they're pitching a B2B staffing agency platform to YC. That's not a crime, but it reveals a team searching for product-market fit rather than executing from deep structural conviction about how staffing agencies operate. The best version of my "cherry on top" framework requires the founders to have built something great independent of AI — a business with real operational texture where AI non-obviously makes it better. A painting company with a tech layer. A parking infrastructure company with computer vision. Here, remove the AI and there is no business. The AI IS the product. That's a feature waiting to be absorbed.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Paradox getting absorbed into Workday creates a genuine gap for agency-side tooling. Staffing agencies may resist Workday's integrated solution because Workday primarily serves their clients — the enterprises — creating a conflict of interest. An independent, agency-native platform that integrates with 50+ ATS systems could own that wedge. The multi-agent breadth (sourcing plus outreach plus video plus phone screening) is more comprehensive than hireEZ's sourcing-only or Humanly's chatbot-only approach. If Asendia executes fast enough to lock in agency workflows before incumbents consolidate the category, the ATS integration switching costs could compound. That's a real thesis — but it's a timing-and-execution thesis, not a structural one. You're betting you can outrun Bullhorn's product team and Workday's acquisition strategy. I've watched Birchbox play that game — narrative magnitude without structural moat — and it produces the worst venture outcome: too successful to die quickly, too fragile to compound.

There is no new primitive here. Asendia builds an application on top of LLM APIs, candidate databases it doesn't own, and ATS platforms it didn't create. Nothing compounds in the protocol sense — no other companies build on Asendia's layer. Compare this to what got me excited about Venmo: a new payment primitive that became infrastructure for how money moves between people. Or Helium: a new wireless primitive that IoT devices build on. Multi-agent recruitment workflows are an implementation pattern, not a protocol. The 4,000+ databases they search belong to someone else. The 50+ ATS integrations make them dependent on someone else's platform. The "data flywheel" the dossier speculates about is entirely theoretical — no evidence of proprietary models or compounding data advantages exists today. I need to see a business creating a layer that others depend on, and what I see instead is a business that depends on every layer around it.
