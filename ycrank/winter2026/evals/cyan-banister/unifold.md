# Unifold -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Unifold isn't the product -- it's the competitive chess match embedded in the founding story. These founders built wallet-as-a-service infrastructure at Streambird, sold it to MoonPay, and are now building deposit orchestration in the same exact territory. MoonPay -- their former acquirer -- owns their prior IP, has $757 million in funding, and has every reason and resource to extend into on-chain deposit routing. So my first question for Timothy and Quang wouldn't be "tell me about multi-chain routing." It would be: why did you leave? What did you see inside MoonPay that they refused to build? What made you walk away from the safety of an acquirer with billions in valuation to start over in a space where your biggest competitor literally owns the thing you built before? That answer would tell me everything. And I don't have it.

What I do have is strong professional credentials. Cambridge, Columbia, Cornell Tech, Solana Labs, Polymarket. A prior exit. Thirty million users onboarded through their previous infrastructure. These are genuinely skilled crypto infrastructure engineers who've been in the trenches. But professional competence isn't what I'm listening for when I ask founders to tell me their story. I'm listening for the hardship that forged commitment -- the biographical thread that makes it impossible for this person to walk away when things get terrible. And the dossier gives me nothing on that dimension. Maybe it exists and I just haven't heard it yet. But at this stage, what I see is a team that identified a market gap through their work experience and decided to go after it. That's rational entrepreneurship. It's not the kind of obsessive, identity-level commitment that carried Travis through years of regulatory warfare or kept the Crusoe team building when Bitcoin collapsed.

The product positioning is sound but it's exactly the kind of idea that makes me less interested, not more. "Stripe for crypto deposits" is possibly the most consensus pitch in fintech right now. Every infrastructure company in crypto describes itself as "Stripe for X." The deposit fragmentation problem is real -- I understand that users drop off when they have to bridge tokens and manage gas across chains. But this is a problem that every well-funded crypto infrastructure company can see clearly. MoonPay sees it. Transak sees it. Stripe, which acquired Privy in 2025, definitely sees it. When I invested in SpaceX, nobody else thought private space made sense. When I backed Crusoe, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was lunacy. The most promising companies are not popular. Unifold's problem space is popular, legible, and logical -- which means the competitive moat depends entirely on execution speed, and execution speed alone rarely sustains a moat against incumbents with hundred-million-dollar war chests.

I should be honest about the bull case, because it's not trivial. If crypto consumer applications -- prediction markets, DeFi protocols, on-chain games -- grow the way mobile apps did after the iPhone, then whoever becomes the default deposit infrastructure captures an enormous toll-booth position. The timing with prediction markets is genuinely good. The founders have arguably the deepest domain expertise of anyone building in this niche. And the technical complexity of supporting dozens of chains with compliance and gas abstraction does accumulate as a switching cost. If MoonPay is too slow or too focused on fiat conversion to pivot, and if Stripe doesn't extend Privy into this exact use case, Unifold could own the layer. That's a real scenario. But it requires the incumbents to miss the window, and I've seen too many times where the company that built the predecessor technology gets outrun by the company that acquired it.

Where this company does show real strength is in co-founder dynamics. Timothy and Quang built a company together, worked together at multiple companies (Instabase, Polymarket), and went through an acquisition together. That's the kind of battle-tested relationship I look for after the HQ Trivia lesson. Hau Chu brings complementary blockchain engineering depth from Solana Labs and MoonPay. Three-person team with clear technical roles and a proven core relationship. I don't have concerns here.

But I keep returning to what's missing from my framework. This isn't a company that creates economic access for underserved populations -- it smooths deposit flows for people who already hold crypto across multiple chains, which is a relatively privileged user base. It doesn't make an invisible power structure visible the way Affirm exposed hidden credit card fees or Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities. It's infrastructure for infrastructure, and while that can be valuable, it doesn't carry the kind of real-world impact that makes me feel a company has to exist. I'd pass at this stage -- not because the team isn't capable, but because nothing about this opportunity is uncomfortable enough to suggest the market hasn't already priced it in.
