# Stilta -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells me everything I need to know before I examine a single line of code or credential. In the twelve months preceding Stilta's YC acceptance, Solve Intelligence raised $55 million and reached eight-figure ARR with 400+ customers. Patlytics raised $21 million with backing from Google Gradient. Ankar raised $20 million from Atomico. DeepIP raised $15 million. And &AI -- a fellow YC company building the same "agentic AI for patent attorneys" product -- raised $6.5 million from First Round. Five companies, same thesis, same timing window, same technology stack. This is not a market with a secret hiding in it. This is a Girardian nightmare playing out in real time: mimetic rivals converging on the identical insight -- that agentic AI can automate patent search -- and imitating each other into mutual margin destruction. When every sophisticated investor in the ecosystem has independently funded the same thesis within a single year, the thesis is by definition consensus. And a consensus thesis produces competitive markets, not monopolies.

What is the secret here? I cannot find one. Stilta's own framing -- that their approach "has not been technically possible until a few months ago" -- is an admission that the enabling technology is generally available, not proprietary. Every competitor heard the same starting gun. The underlying LLM capabilities, the agentic orchestration frameworks, the retrieval-augmented generation architectures -- none of this is unique to Stilta. The company's self-description as "Cursor for patent practitioners" is itself mimetic: borrowing another company's identity rather than defining a new category. When you describe yourself by analogy to someone else, you are admitting you have not created something genuinely new.

The founding team is four McKinsey and QuantumBlack alumni. I have a particular allergy to this profile. McKinsey trains people to identify opportunities through structured analysis of existing markets -- it is the institutional embodiment of indefinite optimism. Consultants are trained to evaluate options, not to hold a singular vision against consensus pressure. When I backed Zuckerberg, he was a 19-year-old programmer with an unshakeable conviction about real-identity networking. When I co-founded Palantir with Alex Karp, he had a doctorate in philosophy and a specific, idiosyncratic thesis about adapting PayPal's fraud algorithms for intelligence. The Stilta founders have competent engineering backgrounds and Oskar Block bootstrapped two sports betting startups to $1M ARR -- I will acknowledge that entrepreneurial signal -- but there is no evidence of the kind of deep, contrarian conviction that produces category-defining companies. Building "the thing that McKinsey's market analysis says should exist" is the opposite of going from zero to one.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. Patent work is billed at $500-1000 per hour, which means the willingness to pay for productivity gains is high. The auditability and source-traceability emphasis addresses a real requirement in legal work where hallucination is disqualifying -- general-purpose AI tools fail this test. The team's QuantumBlack experience building enterprise AI systems at scale is directly relevant, and McKinsey networks provide warm introductions to exactly the corporate IP departments that buy this kind of software. If Stilta executes faster than its competitors on the specific dimension of trusted, auditable output -- if they nail the compliance and traceability layer that patent attorneys actually need to sign off on AI-assisted work -- they could win a segment of this market. Patent search and analysis, specifically, may reward depth over breadth compared to Solve Intelligence's full-stack approach. And the paying customers in "leading firms" on both continents suggest the product works. But even granting all of this, the best outcome I can model is that Stilta becomes a solid mid-market SaaS company in a fragmented competitive landscape -- a $200M business, perhaps, in a $5B market shared among a dozen players. That is not a power-law outcome. That is not a company that returns a fund.

Entering a market with $500K against competitors holding $55M, $21M, and $20M, pursuing the same thesis at the same time, with no proprietary technology and no structural path to monopoly -- this is precisely the kind of bet I avoid. The technology is competent but commoditized. The market is real but crowded. The founders are capable but not visionary. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. Stilta has not escaped it; it has sprinted directly into it.
