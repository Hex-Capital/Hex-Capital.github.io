# Cofia -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that Bardeen shipped this exact thesis -- AI agents that learn how work happens and automate it proactively -- in May 2025, with $22-25M in funding and distribution partnerships with Dropbox and HubSpot. Cofia is entering YC Winter 2026 with no visible product, no traction, and a nearly identical value proposition. This isn't contrarian timing. This is arriving at a party that's already crowded. Zapier is layering AI onto $310M in revenue. n8n just raised $180M. Microsoft has Power Automate and Copilot observing user behavior across the entire Office graph. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was *illegal* -- the consensus was that it couldn't work. Here, the consensus is that AI-driven workflow automation is the future. Everyone agrees. That means the asymmetric upside has already been priced into the category, and the question becomes pure execution against better-resourced competitors.

The specific knowledge test is where this falls apart for me. Paola led retention at Brilliant.org -- that's product management craft in consumer education. Moses reached Senior Staff Engineer at Duolingo, which is genuinely impressive and indicates deep technical capability. But their combined expertise is in consumer edtech: gamification, monetization, user motivation in learning apps. The problem Cofia is solving -- observing knowledge workers across enterprise SaaS tools, inferring workflow patterns, generating working automations across disparate APIs -- requires knowledge about enterprise integration architectures, the messy reality of how data flows between Salesforce and Slack and Jira, the permission models of dozens of B2B platforms. Neither founder has spent years inside that problem. A strong PM from Brilliant and a strong engineer from Duolingo could identify this opportunity with six months of market research. That's teachable insight, not the kind of non-transferable knowledge that creates durable founder-market fit. When I backed the Notion team, the founders had been thinking about the relationship between tools and cognition for years before they wrote a line of code. I don't see that obsessive depth here.

The leverage architecture is baseline software -- one codebase serving many users, which is fine but unremarkable. Every SaaS company has that. The more interesting question is whether the behavioral observation model creates compounding leverage: does each user's data make the system smarter for everyone? In theory, yes -- a library of discovered automation patterns across thousands of users could create cross-user intelligence that a new entrant can't replicate. But this requires massive adoption to generate meaningful data, and adoption requires deep integration across dozens of SaaS tools, which requires partnerships and API access that incumbents already own. The leverage is theoretical and access-gated. This is an application-layer play built on top of broadly available LLM capabilities, not an infrastructure bet. My portfolio tilts heavily toward picks-and-shovels -- Alchemy, not individual DeFi apps; Stack Overflow, not individual developer tools. Cofia is the app, not the platform.

The strongest bull case: proactive automation is a genuine paradigm shift from "user tells computer what to do" to "computer observes and suggests." If the Duolingo engineering pedigree translates -- and Senior Staff at a public tech company is a real credential -- Moses could build an observation engine that's technically superior to Bardeen's. Paola's retention expertise could make the onboarding and habit loop stickier than competitors who treat this as a pure infrastructure problem. If they achieve density in a specific workflow vertical (say, sales operations or customer success), the behavioral data could create real switching costs. The "why now" is legitimate: LLM inference costs have dropped enough to make continuous observation economically viable for a SaaS product. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that the consumer edtech playbook -- gamification, retention science, user motivation -- is the hidden key to winning in enterprise automation, and that the incumbents' existing integration moats matter less than the quality of the observation-to-automation loop. It's possible. But it requires a belief that I don't hold: that the application layer is where value accrues in AI automation, rather than at the infrastructure or platform layer.

I'm passing. Capable founders in a consensus category without the domain-specific knowledge that would make me believe they see something Bardeen, Zapier, and Microsoft don't. The technology isn't compounding in any distinctive way -- it's standard LLM pattern recognition applied to user workflows, reproducible by any well-funded team. The absence of any visible product or traction isn't disqualifying at pre-seed, but combined with a well-funded direct competitor that shipped nine months ago, it means Cofia is in an execution race without a clear reason to believe they'll win it. I want founders where the knowledge can't be taught, building leverage that didn't exist before, at a time when smart people think they're wrong. This is smart people building in a direction everyone already agrees is right.
