# 10x Science -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is extraordinary -- and I don't use that word often at pre-seed. David Roberts has spent his entire scientific career on top-down mass spectrometry and glycoproteomics, first under Ying Ge at UW-Madison, then under Carolyn Bertozzi at Stanford -- a 2022 Nobel laureate in Chemistry. Thirty-seven publications. A Damon Runyon Fellowship, which is one of the most competitive postdoctoral awards in cancer research. This isn't someone who read about protein characterization for six months and decided to build a SaaS tool. This is someone who has manually curated mass spectrometry data for a decade, understands every edge case in protein chemistry that breaks automated analysis, and knows exactly where the pain is because he lives it daily. Andrew Reiter adds the operational layer -- building mass spec methods at the Broad Institute's proteomics platform gives him knowledge of how protein characterization works at production scale, not just in academic papers. When I invested in Stack Overflow, the insight was that developers building tools for developers creates a knowledge compounding loop that outsiders can't replicate. The same logic applies here: scientists who have spent years doing the exact analysis they're automating understand failure modes that no ML engineer parachuting in from a different domain would discover for years.

The team architecture is what elevates this from "impressive academics" to "fundable company." Vishnu Tejus bridges the gap that kills most deep-science startups. He's a 2x YC founder, was the second employee at Nooks through a $43M Series B from Kleiner Perkins, led the RoboTutor project to an XPRIZE final -- and he researched at Stanford ChEM-H, the institute housing Bertozzi's lab. He has enough domain context to architect the ML pipeline correctly while having shipped production software at scale. I require a technologist on every founding team, and Tejus is a genuine builder with domain fluency. That combination -- Nobel-lab proteomics depth plus battle-tested startup engineering -- is not something a well-funded competitor assembles quickly. The knowledge barrier is measured in years of bench science, not sprints of engineering.

The leverage architecture maps to a pattern I look for systematically: picks-and-shovels for a gold rush. Everyone is funding upstream AI drug discovery -- Recursion, Insilico, Absci. But every AI-designed molecule still needs to pass through the same characterization bottleneck. Software that compresses weeks of PhD-level manual curation into minutes is pure code leverage -- one platform replacing teams of analysts across hundreds of biopharma companies. The "$150,000+ time savings per team per month" claim, even if directionally rather than precisely accurate, signals the kind of value density that supports enterprise pricing. And the structural dynamics are favorable: Genedata was absorbed by Danaher in 2024, Protein Metrics by Dotmatics in 2021. When independent software vendors get swallowed by instrument conglomerates, innovation calcifies. The acquirers optimize for hardware lock-in, not for best-in-class analytical software. That consolidation creates a window for an AI-native independent platform, the same way that every major platform acquisition creates an opportunity for the next generation of startups to serve the customers the acquirer neglects.

The contrarian dimension is real but moderate. This is not Uber-when-ridesharing-was-illegal contrarian. But the non-obvious insight -- that the constraining bottleneck in biopharma is shifting from drug discovery to drug characterization as AI floods the upstream pipeline -- is the kind of structural observation that most investors miss because they're fixated on the flashier upstream problem. Smart biotech investors are still chasing generative biology and molecular design. Few are thinking about the quality control and characterization infrastructure those molecules will require. The timing feels right: you want to build the characterization platform before the pipeline flood arrives, not after.

The honest concerns. Two of three founders appear to hold active academic positions at Stanford. The postdoc-to-CEO transition is not trivial -- academia selects for different behaviors than startup building. Enterprise biopharma sales cycles are long, regulatory validation (FDA 21 CFR Part 11) is resource-intensive, and switching costs in validated workflows heavily favor incumbents. The market, while real, is a software-only slice of a $5B protein characterization market that includes instruments and reagents -- the serviceable portion is meaningfully smaller. And the brand collision with 10x Genomics is a tactical headache in exactly the scientific community they need to reach. These risks are manageable, not structural, but they require the founders to execute a difficult transition from academic researchers to enterprise software operators.

What would make this a defining company: if the AI drug discovery pipeline genuinely floods downstream characterization with 10x the candidates over the next three to five years, and 10x Science becomes the standard analytical platform that every biopharma company runs those candidates through. The data moat from processing diverse protein datasets would compound -- each customer's characterization runs improve the models for every other customer. At that point, you have infrastructure-layer leverage with a knowledge moat that no instrument vendor or horizontal AI platform can replicate, because the training data pipeline requires understanding mass spectrometry edge cases that only this team possesses. The compounding dynamic would look like Alchemy for blockchain or Stack Overflow for developer knowledge -- a platform that gets better the more it's used, built by people who understand the domain at a level that can't be hired or acquired.

I'd write a check. The specific knowledge is among the deepest I've seen at pre-seed. The leverage is clear. The team has a real technologist with startup execution experience. The main risks are execution and go-to-market speed in a slow-moving enterprise vertical, not fundamental thesis misalignment.
