# Travo -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I always want to know is: why is this YOUR problem? How did you get here? And with Travo, I'm looking at four Stanford CS undergrads building real estate data for RV parks and campgrounds, and I cannot find the thread. There's no visible biographical connection -- no one who grew up around campgrounds, no one who worked in commercial real estate, no one who watched a family member get burned by bad property data. Clarence Chen built and sold an education company and researched the intersection of physics and consciousness. Alexander Calafiura worked at Wachtell and the Manhattan DA's office. Michael Dalva wrote code for satellites. Ashwin Sriram built backend systems at Roblox. These are genuinely smart, technically capable people -- but when I ask myself whether these are the people uniquely forged to build the most accurate RV park dataset in the world, I don't feel the signal. The founder-problem bond that I look for -- the kind of biographical inevitability I saw with Crusoe's founders and their obsession with wasted energy -- just isn't here.

Now, I want to be fair about what IS interesting. Alexander Calafiura's story catches my eye -- described as the first YC founder from Tajikistan, getting into YC at 19, writing for Chalkbeat as a high school student, working at one of the most prestigious law firms in the world. There's an outsider journey there that resonates with me. And Chen has a prior exit, which at their age is genuinely meaningful. These aren't founders who lack ambition or capability. But capability detached from a deep personal stake in the specific problem -- that's where I get cautious. I've learned that the founders who persist through the truly brutal stretches are the ones who can't walk away because the problem is woven into who they are.

The bull case deserves real attention. CoStar charges $30K-$100K+ a year and has essentially no incentive to build comprehensive coverage for niche asset classes like RV parks -- that's a textbook innovator's dilemma, and it's a real opening. The transparency angle also maps to something I care about: Travo claims full visibility into data origins and methodologies, which echoes what Carta did for cap tables and what Affirm did for credit -- making opaque systems legible. If these founders could build a genuinely accurate, comprehensive dataset for underserved property categories and then expand outward, the wedge strategy could work. AI-driven data collection is legitimately cheaper than CoStar's army of researchers, and the timing is right with LLM capabilities improving rapidly. If I squint, I can see a version of this that becomes the data infrastructure layer for all the property types the incumbents ignore.

But here's where I keep getting stuck: this doesn't feel weird enough, and it doesn't create the kind of economic access I look for. The customers are PE firms and brokers -- already sophisticated, well-resourced professionals. This isn't opening up opportunity for people who couldn't access it before; it's making existing institutional investors slightly more efficient at deploying capital into RV parks. That's a fine business, but it doesn't give me the jolt I felt with Uber when I understood it was creating income for drivers, or with Affirm when I saw transparent credit replacing hidden fees. The product is useful but not transformative in the way that gets me to write a first check with my own money. And the idea of AI-powered real estate data -- while not exactly consensus in the RV park niche -- is very much a popular category broadly. Every other YC batch has multiple companies applying AI to property data in some form. The niche focus provides some differentiation, but the underlying approach is incremental, not contrarian.

The four-way co-founder dynamic is something I'd probe hard in person. Four Stanford classmates splitting a company is a pattern I've seen go both ways, but with no evidence of how these founders handle real conflict -- not academic disagreements but existential company-threatening crises -- I can't assess resilience. My HQ Trivia experience taught me that even two co-founders with a perfect product can destroy each other; four is geometrically more complex. The shared Stanford context provides common language, but it doesn't prove they've stress-tested the relationship under real pressure.

I'm passing on Travo. The team is technically strong and the market gap is real, but this doesn't fit how I invest. I need a biographical thread connecting the founder to the problem, and I need the idea to be genuinely non-obvious in a way that makes other investors uncomfortable. This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a popular category -- and those are exactly the deals where consensus forms quickly, prices inflate, and the returns don't justify the risk I take at the earliest stages.
