# Unisson -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Unisson is the brand collision: Totango and Catalyst — established customer success platforms — already launched a product called "Unison AI" in the same space. That's not just a naming headache; it's a symptom of a deeper problem. When your brand is nearly identical to a feature an incumbent already shipped, it tells you something about how differentiated your position actually is. The entire customer success category is racing to add AI agents. ChurnZero launched AI agents for CS teams. Sierra hit $100M ARR doing AI agents for customer interactions. When I look at this landscape, I don't see a gap waiting for a startup — I see a feature waiting to be absorbed.

Let me run my primary filter. Varun Mathur spent roughly five years at Ambient.ai leading agent products and VLM research — that's real experience building AI systems in production. Tom Achache led perception at Chef Robotics for three years and has a solid ML research background. These are competent, credible builders. The "sane" half of the equation checks out at a baseline level. But the "insane idea" half? "AI agents for customer-facing teams" is one of the most consensus plays in enterprise software right now. Every VC I know has seen twenty of these pitches. Every CS platform is building this internally. When the idea provokes zero controversy and zero confusion from mainstream investors — when it's the obvious application of current AI capabilities to an existing software category — the non-consensus premium evaporates entirely. You're buying at market price. Also worth noting: no shared employer or university overlap between the co-founders was found, which doesn't inspire confidence about the founding relationship's depth.

Now the AI kill test, which here works in a way I rarely see: the kill runs in both directions simultaneously. First, remove AI from Unisson and you have... nothing. There is no business without the AI component. That's the definitional failure of my "cherry on top" test — the cherry IS the sundae. Second, as AI capabilities improve, the 15-20 minute product-learning claim that sounds impressive today becomes table stakes. Every LLM will be able to ingest documentation and execute multi-step tasks. The moat is pure software, and I've been saying publicly for two years that software moats are dissolving faster than founders realize. The dossier itself acknowledges this plainly: "The underlying technology (LLM-based agents that ingest product documentation and execute tasks) is accessible to well-resourced competitors." When the company's own research dossier can't find defensibility signals, that's not a data gap — that's a data point.

The strongest bull case requires believing that the specific niche of internal technical CS teams — implementation specialists, sales engineers, product administrators — is structurally different enough from end-customer support that incumbents won't chase it. If Unisson's agents accumulate deep, customer-specific product knowledge that creates genuine switching costs, and if the team builds proprietary architecture behind the rapid product-learning capability, you could imagine this becoming the operating system for how implementation teams work. The wedge-to-platform narrative would be: start with technical CS, expand to all internal product expertise, eventually become the "AI SME layer" for every enterprise team. For this to work, Unisson would need to demonstrate that learned product configurations compound into lock-in faster than incumbents can replicate — and that the buyer (CS/SE leaders) is willing to adopt a standalone tool rather than waiting for their existing platform to ship the feature. I don't see evidence for either condition, but I acknowledge the niche is real and underserved today.

This is an application on existing infrastructure — LLM APIs, Slack, email — not a new primitive. It doesn't create a layer that other businesses build on. Nobody is going to build their product on top of Unisson the way applications build on a protocol. That caps the upside ceiling structurally. Compare this to Venmo, which created a new payment primitive that became infrastructure for peer-to-peer money movement, or to Aleo, which established zero-knowledge privacy as a layer other applications integrate with. Unisson is a tool for a specific workflow in a specific team, and its expansion narrative — while theoretically possible — is the same generic "AI agent platform" story that hundreds of startups are telling right now in a market where incumbents have massive distribution advantages.

I respect the founders' technical backgrounds, and building at Ambient.ai and Chef Robotics demonstrates real ML chops. But the structural logic of this investment contradicts nearly every principle in my framework: AI is the product (not the cherry on top), the moat is software (in a world where software moats are dying), the idea is consensus (not insane), the market is hot (not contrarian), and the business model is almost certainly SaaS subscription. I'd rather fund a company doing something operationally complex in the physical world with a thin AI layer than a company doing something entirely in software where AI is the entire value proposition.
