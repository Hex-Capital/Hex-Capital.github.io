# Agentcard -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Karen Serfaty already sold a card-issuance company (Atlas → Remote) and then ran the Cards business unit at Remote — that's the entire interesting fact about Agentcard, and it's also why I'm still passing. The signature here is a domain-fit operator with a real second-rep resume running a thin Stripe-Issuing wrapper directly into a category where Mastercard, Visa, Catena ($48M), and Skyfire ($9.5M) are all already shipping competing products on the same rails. The asset is the founder; the structure is a commodity SaaS cap table; the sleeve is exactly the "AI is just more internet" sleeve I've publicly re-weighted away from. That mismatch is the whole evaluation.

**Solana at $0.05** is what a non-consensus AI-payments round would have to feel like — a category most generalist funds had quit, priced for the people still in the room. Agentcard does not feel like that. Mastercard's Agent Pay for Machines launched in June 2026 with Catena, Skyfire, Basis Theory, and Stripe as initial participants, and Visa shipped an "AI-generated debit card for agents" — Agentcard is conspicuously *not* in the network-incumbent launch lineup, and the category itself is the most consensus YC-S26 pattern in fintech. There is no disclosed price discount, no named tier-1 publicly passing, and the moat-equivalent ("Stripe Issuing + AES-256-GCM") is the standard description any of the thirty agentic-payments seed pitches I've seen would use. Agentcard is closer to my canonical Pass Example — the AI-agent-for-vertical YC pitch — than it is to anything I would underwrite on price alone.

**Teamshares** is the capital-structure rung I care about — an instrument no other VC could underwrite, where the structure itself was the alpha. Agentcard runs the opposite play. The business model is "free tier with $50/card cap, basic with $500/card cap, pay-as-you-go," which is a transaction-fee margin sitting on top of Stripe Issuing fees, which sits on top of Visa interchange. That is a four-decimal-place gross margin business pretending to be venture-shaped, and to defend it against a Catena Labs that just raised a $30M Series A in May 2026 from a16z crypto and Acrew on the way to a national trust bank charter, Agentcard will need three or four more financing tacks at a quality of investor that the dossier gives me no evidence Serfaty has lined up. The pre-Series-A regatta here looks brutal — every tack is into a stiffening wind blown by funded incumbents on the same Visa rails.

On founder conviction, Serfaty's resume is the strongest fact in the dossier — Atlas was a real card-issuance product, the Remote acquisition was real, and the GM-of-Cards-at-Remote role is the rare case of founder-market fit you cannot manufacture. Over 18 months that compounds: Serfaty knows Stripe Issuing's policy team personally, knows what KYA/KYC arguments work with Visa, and probably has the founder network to land merchant integrations beyond DoorDash faster than a non-domain operator would. What I do not have is any public writing from Serfaty or Felipe Abello on *why* virtual cards are the right primitive for agent commerce versus stablecoin rails (Skyfire's bet) or a full bank stack (Catena's bet) — no Twitter for either, no GitHub, no Modest-Proposals-style thread on the category. The conviction is in the resume, not in the artifact, which is exactly the inversion of what my "writing is thinking" filter looks for.

Founder-as-brand is where Agentcard is closest to floor. The score flips a tier only if Serfaty or Abello had a 10K+ engaged developer following pulling agents into Agentcard's MCP server organically — 357 Product Hunt followers and one 5-star review at the "Buy by Agentcard" launch is not that. Distribution is currently a function of Anthropic's MCP runtime list (Claude Code, Claude Desktop, Cursor) — meaning Agentcard's distribution is *Anthropic's* distribution, leased. The Slow Creator Fund thesis does not load here at all; this is not a creator company and was never trying to be.

Skyfire is the single-anchor parallel for category sleeve, and it cuts against Agentcard. Skyfire took the same agentic-payments problem and routed it through stablecoin rails, which lands it in my pre-consensus crypto sleeve next to Lightning Labs and Aleo — a sleeve I'm convicted on because "crypto is fundamentally disruptive" while "AI is just more internet." Agentcard explicitly chose Visa rails for "anywhere Visa is accepted" merchant coverage, which is the right product decision for distribution and the wrong decision for *my* sleeve allocation. If I want exposure to agent payments, I'd rather buy the crypto-rails version at the Skyfire stage than the Visa-rails wrapper at the Agentcard stage, because the crypto version has optionality on becoming a payments network and the Visa version has optionality on becoming a Lithic feature.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Agentcard gets acqui-hired by Stripe, Lithic, or a Mastercard-adjacent issuer for under $25M, with Serfaty rolling into a Cards/Agent-Payments product lead role — the second iteration of the Atlas-to-Remote arc, scaled by network-incumbent encirclement. The mechanism is direct: Mastercard's June 2026 launch already chose its dance partners and Agentcard wasn't named, Catena's $48M war chest funds a regulatory moat Agentcard cannot match, and Stripe Issuing — Agentcard's own infrastructure dependency — will ship a first-party MCP server within 18 months because it would be malpractice not to. The exit is a soft landing, not a zero, because Serfaty's operator quality and the live DoorDash integration are worth a tuck-in price.

The bull case worth taking seriously is the Ro precedent in my Known Exceptions — Ro looked over-capitalized and regulatory-heavy and I underwrote it anyway because regulatory complexity itself was the moat. Someone could argue agentic payments has the same shape: KYA/KYC for autonomous agents will require a regulatory wrapper most issuers can't build, Serfaty is one of maybe ten operators in the world who has shipped a compliant card product end-to-end, and Agentcard's single-use-7-day-expiry primitive is genuinely the right safety architecture for the category. That argument stings — and the dossier dismantles it. Ro had to clear 50-state prescription rails that no other VC could finance; Agentcard's "regulatory" moat is sitting downstream of Stripe and Visa, both of whom *are* the regulator-adjacent layer and both of whom can change agent-initiated transaction policy unilaterally. Serfaty is one operator deep in a category where Visa itself is now a competitor. The verdict flips if Agentcard names a top-three issuer-bank partnership or a non-Stripe processing relationship that survives a Stripe policy change — until then, I'm out.
