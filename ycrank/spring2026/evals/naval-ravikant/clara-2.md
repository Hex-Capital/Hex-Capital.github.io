# Clara -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see is the specific knowledge gap at the center of the founding team. George Favvas built Circle Medical to $100M revenue run rate with profitability. That's not a slide deck claim -- that's a decade of navigating state licensing boards, insurance billing disputes, clinician scheduling, and EHR integration hell. You cannot teach someone that operational knowledge in a classroom. Caitlin Swift practiced as a nurse practitioner inside that same system. They know the regulatory terrain the way a mountaineer knows a route they've climbed before. But Clara isn't being sold as "AI-assisted telehealth operations." It's being sold as an "AI primary care doctor." The word doing all the work in that phrase is "AI" -- and none of the three co-founders appear to be building it. No CTO. No ML researcher. No technical co-founder with a public engineering footprint. The two open job postings are for a Founding Data Engineer and a Founding Product Engineer, which tells me the AI brain of the product is still being recruited, not built by a founder with years of conviction. I require a technologist on the founding team. "This is the technology business, and you want to be investing in technology teams." A healthcare operations team building an AI product is like a media company building a semiconductor -- they understand the customer, not the craft.

The leverage architecture is the part that almost pulls me in. Traditional telehealth is an unleveraged business -- every patient encounter requires a proportional clinician hour. Teladoc proved this at scale: you can put doctors on video, but you can't make one doctor treat fifty patients simultaneously. Clara's inversion -- AI handles the reasoning, clinician handles the review -- genuinely changes the ratio. If a board-certified reviewer can approve thirty AI-drafted care plans in the time they'd spend on three video visits, that's a 10x leverage transformation applied to the most labor-constrained profession in America. The 150,000-facility records integration creates a data surface that deepens with each patient, and the operational wrapper of prescribing, lab ordering, and insurance billing is the kind of infrastructure that takes years to build correctly. Favvas has already built it once. That operational playbook from Circle Medical is a genuine accelerant -- most AI healthcare startups die on the rocks of compliance and billing, not on the quality of their models.

But the contrarian test fails cleanly. AI in healthcare is perhaps the most consensus-hot investment category of 2026. K Health has raised $441M. Hippocratic AI sits at a $3.5B valuation with $404M in. Forward Health has burned through $546M. OpenAI and Google are both circling medical applications. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin, it was dismissed as a toy. There is nothing illegal, dismissed, or misunderstood about "AI doctor" in 2026 -- it's on the cover of every health-tech conference deck. The specific inversion of "AI-first, human-verified" has a contrarian flavor relative to incumbent telehealth models, but the broader category is crowded enough that pricing already reflects optimism. I'm not getting paid for being early here. I'm potentially getting paid for execution, which is a different and less asymmetric game.

The strongest bull case requires me to believe that the operational moat -- not the AI moat -- is what wins this market. If the endgame is that foundation models commoditize medical reasoning entirely, then the companies that win are the ones who can legally prescribe, bill insurance, maintain state-by-state licensure, and handle the messy last mile of actually delivering care. In that world, Favvas's Circle Medical playbook is the sword, and the AI is just the handle. Carbon Health's bankruptcy in February 2026 supports this reading: the capital-intensive, clinic-heavy model failed, which means the asset-light, AI-augmented model inherits demand. If I squint, this looks like my Opendoor exception -- a capital-and-operations-heavy domain where a founder with extraordinary operational knowledge applies technology to create a qualitatively new capability. The team's shared history at Circle Medical reduces co-founder risk. And the regulatory barrier is real -- you can't shortcut state licensing, which means well-funded pure-tech competitors (Hippocratic AI, general-purpose LLMs) face the same operational maze that Favvas has already navigated.

Where I land: strong operational specific knowledge, partially mismatched to the core technical challenge. Real leverage potential in the AI-first care model, but no proprietary technology at the AI layer and no technologist-founder building it. A consensus-hot category where the asymmetric upside has been compressed by competition and capital. Favvas is the kind of founder I'd want to back in a less crowded market, but against $441M and $404M competitors with a team that still needs to hire its technical core, the structural disadvantages are significant. This is a bet on operational execution superiority, not on technology compounding -- and I prefer the latter.
