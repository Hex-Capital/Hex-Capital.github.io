# Nori -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Nori is a $1,288 wheeled bimanual robot in a category where 1X NEO ships at $20K and Unitree G1 at $16K — the dimensional signature is a magically-weird form-factor call (highest rung) resting on a founder whose contact with the problem is Columbia Graphics Lab research, not lived domestic labor (lowest rung). Antonio Sitong Li discovered the household-manipulator problem in a VR-task-demonstration paper, not by scrubbing dishes for rent. That pairing is the whole read: I like the shape of the bet more than I like the biographer.

Crusoe (~2018) is the HIGH rung on "magically weird" — three uninvestable things stapled together, sourced from a founder who was *of* the oil patch. Nori shares the aesthetic-transgression mechanism: while Figure raises at $39B and 1X sells a $20K bipedal, Antonio built a wheeled cart with a 950mm Z-axis at ~12x less. Two things separate Nori from the Crusoe rung, though. First, the humanoid-robotics category is now consensus, not laughed at — YC itself broadcast "Small but capable, Nori L2 is coming next week," and heavy YC social proof inverts as a deselection signal per the Long Journey thesis. Second, Chase Lochmiller could walk on a well pad because he grew up adjacent to those operators; Antonio's non-consensus form-factor choice reads as engineering taste from a Columbia researcher, not social-license conviction from someone who lived the constraint. It is genuinely weird — just not weird from *of*, weird from *visiting*.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling is the LOW rung on "founder surfaced from lived experience" — the ex-FAANG PMs who discovered the problem in a memo. Nori's dossier doesn't fully fall into that trap because Antonio at least sat in the Columbia Graphics and UI Lab shipping VR-taught task goals to robot teams under Eugene Wu, and ran Founders Inc to 1.5M impressions. That's a real research-and-shipping trajectory in the *technique* Nori uses (leader-follower teleoperation recording Z-axis + arm + gripper trajectories). What is missing is the Palmer-Luckey-in-the-garage moment, or the Chase-Lochmiller-adjacent-to-flares moment: the biographical proof that Antonio, before Nori, felt the pain a $1,288 household robot is supposed to erase. Nobody in this dossier tells me he was the kid whose parents worked two jobs and the laundry piled up, or the college roommate who reverse-engineered a dishwasher motor. Research-adjacent counts for something. It does not count for Anduril.

On single unambiguous CEO — the mechanism analysis — Nori is unambiguous by omission: only Antonio Sitong Li is listed as founder on the YC page for a team of 4, which forecloses the HQ Trivia / GameCrush governance risk I screen hardest for. Over 18 months, though, that same solo structure compounds the opposite failure: hardware companies at the sub-$1,500 SKU level need distinct leadership on ML, ops, and manufacturing, and Nori is manufacturing in San Francisco with a summer 2026 ship deadline announced in June 2026. The tiebreaker question is moot; the "who is the COO who owns supply chain" question is not.

On capital-intensive frontier hardware — the counterfactual — Nori sits in the correct lane: physical BOM, dual Z-axis 950mm lift, wheeled base, real supply chain. The score would flip a full tier if Nori shipped 500 pre-order units to homes with independent third-party video of autonomous dishwashing, because the moat in this category compounds with per-household task-data collection, not with model architecture. Right now the moat is a "skill-sharing marketplace" without a cited installed base — a slide, not a flywheel.

On physical-world verifiability — a single-anchor parallel to Flock Safety (~2018) — Flock passed my most literal test: I read the founder's Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons café because the artifact was already deployed in the world. Nori is one step behind that. I can pre-order the L2 at $1,288 or the L2 Grande at $1,322 on norirobotics.com today, but the summer-2026 ship date means the tactile verification I ran on Flock cameras or Ingress-then-Pokémon-Go is still promised, not observable. The launch teaser at 15.7K views day one is attention, not artifact.

Forecast, 36 months: Nori ships between 1,500 and 5,000 units at ~$1,300, reaches roughly $2M–$7M in cumulative hardware revenue, and either gets acqui-hired by a consumer-appliance incumbent (iRobot's parent, an Amazon devices team) or pivots the teleoperation-recording stack into a B2B skill-collection platform sold to the bipedal humanoid players — because Unitree's R1 at $5,900 will keep compressing the consumer-form-factor price wedge before Nori's data flywheel spins up, and a Columbia-researcher founder is more likely to reach the "sell the data pipeline" pivot than a Chase-Lochmiller "walk the well pad" one. The forecast follows directly from the signature: weird enough to matter, not lived enough to compound.

The strongest bear case cuts harder than my Invest lean: Nori is the exact profile my Pass Example warned about, wearing a hardware costume. YC officially promoted it, the June 23 teaser hit 15.7K views, the humanoid category is the loudest room in the batch — that is consensus dressed as contrarian form-factor. Antonio's prior "Founders Inc" was anti-interview-cheating software, not a shipped hardware analog to Oculus-before-Anduril, which means the founder-market fit rests on lab research, not shipped-atoms track record, and Unitree R1 at $5,900 already halves the price wedge before Nori's summer ship. What wins the argument for me is that the wheeled-not-bipedal call at 12x price is a genuinely non-obvious engineering commitment I have not seen elsewhere in the batch, and Antonio's Columbia Graphics Lab work is on the exact teleoperation-to-autonomy loop the product monetizes — that is not memo research, that is thesis research. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict to Strong Invest: an independent, unedited household demo of autonomous dishwashing or laundry-folding on a Nori L2 in a real kitchen within 6 months of ship. If that video exists, the tactile virality overrides the lived-experience gap the way HQ Trivia's addictive product overrode the co-founder governance heuristic. If it doesn't, this is a $3M-ARR curiosity that gets bought for the teleop stack.
