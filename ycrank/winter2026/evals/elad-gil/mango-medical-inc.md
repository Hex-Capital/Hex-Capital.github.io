# Mango Medical -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The number that jumps off the page here is the $10M ARR LOI from an unnamed "top orthopedic company." At pre-seed, that would be extraordinary -- it would mean a major device OEM has validated the technology and is ready to distribute it through their implant ecosystem. But look closer: it's a letter of intent, not a contract. It's from an unnamed company. And it's almost certainly contingent on FDA 510(k) clearance that Mango Medical hasn't even submitted for. Formus Labs, their closest comparable, was founded in 2016 and didn't get 510(k) clearance until 2023 -- seven years. The LOI is a signpost pointing toward a destination that's years away, contingent on a regulatory milestone the team hasn't begun to navigate formally. I've invested in regulated markets -- Color Health taught me the difference between regulatory moats that protect you and regulatory timelines that kill you. This feels more like the latter until proven otherwise.

The market's structural moment is the first thing I evaluate, and here the picture is mixed. The U.S. orthopedic surgical planning software market is $24.63 million. Not $24.63 billion -- million. The broader "AI in orthopedic surgery" market at $307 million growing to $2.7 billion by 2032 is more relevant, but that encompasses far more than preoperative planning. What's the specific capability threshold that's been crossed in the last 12-24 months? The dossier's "why now" is entirely inferred: transformer architectures improving segmentation, FDA frameworks evolving, surgeon shortages. These are real trends but they're gradual curves, not step functions. When I invested around Harvey, there was a specific, trackable capability jump -- GPT-3 to GPT-4 -- that I could map to a timeline. I don't see the equivalent inflection point for CT-to-3D-plan automation. Deep learning for medical imaging has been steadily improving for a decade. Nothing I can find suggests that a threshold was crossed in 2024-2025 that suddenly makes minutes-not-days possible when it wasn't possible before.

The strongest bull case deserves serious consideration. Orthopedic device companies don't build planning software -- they acquire it. Brainlab bought TraumaCad in 2012, Materialise bought OrthoView in 2014, Zimmer Biomet partnered with Formus Labs. This market structure means a company with FDA-cleared, clinically validated AI planning software is a natural acquisition target for any of the big five device makers. Mango Medical's focus on fracture reconstruction is genuinely differentiated -- Formus Labs does hip replacement, Enhatch does patient-specific instrumentation -- and complex fracture planning may be underserved precisely because it's harder to automate than standardized joint procedures. If the team can get through FDA clearance and the LOI converts, they could build a real business in a niche that the larger competitors haven't addressed. The TU Munich academic origin provides IP depth. The "Invisalign for bone fractures" analogy, if it holds, suggests a standardization play that could expand who performs these procedures and how many get done. For this to work, though, everything has to break right in sequence.

And that's where my single-miracle framework intervenes. This company needs FDA clearance (miracle one), LOI conversion into actual revenue (miracle two, likely dependent on miracle one), and clinical validation sufficient for surgeon adoption at scale (miracle three). Compounding low-probability events multiply the failure rate, and I've seen this pattern enough to know that even when each individual step seems achievable, the sequential chain is what kills companies. The team can't just be focused on building great AI -- they need to simultaneously navigate regulatory strategy, clinical study design, and enterprise sales with a major OEM. That's not one miracle, it's an entire chain of them.

The execution velocity concerns me independently. Mango Medical was founded in 2020 as a TU Munich spinoff. Six years later, entering YC W26, they have no recognized revenue, no FDA submission on record, and one public GitHub repository from the CEO. The team has relevant domain backgrounds -- Kilian from dentistry and the TU Munich medtech ecosystem, Padilla Pérez from AI and robotics -- but the output-to-time ratio is low compared to what I look for. Formus Labs, with a similar academic origin and regulatory path, had 3,000+ surgeries planned and $2.9 million in revenue by the time they were at a comparable maturity point. I'm not seeing evidence of the rapid iteration cycles and weekly product velocity that signal a team is moving fast enough to capture a structural moment before competitors lock down distribution.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is constrained by market structure. The natural path is OEM distribution through device companies, which means Mango Medical becomes a feature bundled with someone else's hardware -- valuable, but structurally limited as a platform. Unlike Stripe, which owned the developer relationship and could distribute Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through it, Mango Medical's customer relationship would be mediated by the device OEM. Could they expand from fracture planning to joint replacement to spine to other surgical specialties? Possibly, but each expansion requires new clinical validation and likely new regulatory clearance. The product can grow, but it doesn't naturally become a distribution channel for adjacent products. At best, this is a solid acquisition outcome for a device company paying 10-15x revenue -- a good return for founders, but not the kind of compounding platform that generates venture-scale multiples from my fund.
