# Harbor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Albert Cai spent a month sleeping near a clinical trial site during Biolinq's pivotal study, watching coordinators spend hours per subject per day transcribing handwritten source documents into an electronic data capture system that hasn't fundamentally changed since the 1990s. He didn't read about this problem in a market report. He sat in the room where it was happening, day after day, while simultaneously navigating the FDA De Novo authorization pathway for Biolinq's flagship wearable biosensor. Four years of clinical, regulatory, and product work — prototype to FDA clearance — and the thing that kept gnawing at him wasn't the device. It was the data infrastructure surrounding every clinical trial he touched. When I ask "tell me the story, not the business," Harbor's story starts in that room. Albert's co-founder Nathan Leung brings the engineering forge — employee number one at Jupiter (YC S19), Google, Ramp, smart contracts at Caldera securing millions in assets — and the two share a University of Michigan connection that predates Harbor. Clear CEO/CTO delineation, no 50/50 ambiguity. The biographical connection is genuine and specific, though it's professional forging rather than existential forging — Albert navigated a broken system through his career, not through survival. That distinction matters in how I weight it.

Harbor's invisible system is where my attention locks. Legacy EDC vendors — Medidata ($5.8B acquisition, ~$701.5M estimated revenue), Veeva, Oracle — charge per-subject, per-site, per-CRF fees. Their entire business model is built on manual data entry being slow and expensive. Data management consumes 30% of total clinical trial budgets. That 30% is an invisible tax extracted from every sponsor running a trial, and it ultimately flows downstream into drug development costs that patients bear. Harbor's flat-rate pricing — $2,000/month for commercial studies, free for academics — doesn't just compete on price. It structurally reveals what the incumbents have been extracting. This is the same mechanism I recognized in Affirm attacking credit card opacity: the existing system profits from complexity that ordinary participants can't see through, and the new entrant makes the cost structure legible. When Medidata charges per-subject fees on a 1,600-patient randomized trial, the cost scales with the pain. Harbor configured that same trial database in one week at a flat rate. The pricing model conflict is real — incumbents cannot match Harbor's model without cannibalizing the per-unit revenue that funds their $5.8B valuations.

Medidata's Rave Lite launch in October 2024, specifically targeting simpler study builds for the small-to-mid trial segment, is the bear case I have to stare at directly. Harbor is a two-person pre-seed team going against companies with thousands of employees and decades of regulatory track record. LLM-based document extraction is becoming broadly accessible — any well-resourced team can integrate GPT-4 into a clinical workflow. The bull case an advocate would make: Harbor already has five live clinical studies and six named customers including BioDynamik, NOVA Genomics, and University Hospitals. Customer testimonials cite two-month processes cut to two days. The regulatory validation burden — 21 CFR Part 11, HIPAA, GDPR compliance with immutable audit trails and shipped validation packages — isn't something you bolt on. Harbor built it from day one. Each study processed trains the document extraction models on domain-specific clinical forms, creating a compounding accuracy advantage that a generic LLM integration cannot replicate. And mid-trial switching is operationally infeasible due to audit trail requirements, which gives Harbor Carta-like switching costs once a study goes live. I find the bull case persuasive on defensibility but less persuasive on the contrarian dimension.

Harbor's weakness in my framework is that it doesn't make anyone shift uncomfortably in their chairs. Healthcare AI SaaS is consensus-attractive in 2026. Investors aren't avoiding this space — they're competing for it. Nobody at a dinner party would flinch if you said "we're building a better clinical trial data platform." Compare that to when I backed Flock Safety (surveillance cameras that made civil liberties advocates nervous) or Contraline (male birth control that made VCs squirm) or Anduril (defense AI when Silicon Valley considered it politically toxic). Harbor is a smart bet in a large market with genuine structural advantages, but it's not the kind of bet where 300 investors passed and I'm the first check. The regulatory complexity of clinical trials adds genuine difficulty — this is not trivial to build — but difficulty is not the same as contrarianism. The space itself is well-funded and well-understood.

Harbor's ten-year infrastructure question reveals something worth sitting with. If Harbor's document extraction models get trained on thousands of clinical studies across therapeutic areas, the company stops being an EDC and becomes the intelligence layer for clinical trial data globally. The $1.84B EDC market growing to $5.73B by 2034 is the surface TAM, but the real infrastructure play is that Harbor could own the translation layer between messy clinical reality (handwritten notes, lab printouts, imaging reports) and structured regulatory-grade data. That's an inflection-point infrastructure bet — multimodal LLMs capable of clinical-grade document extraction weren't viable before 2024, and Harbor is building at exactly the moment the technology crosses the regulatory reliability threshold. The free academic tier seeds this data flywheel with studies that might never have used an EDC at all. Cascading impact is real: faster, cheaper trials mean more drugs reach patients sooner, and smaller biotechs like BioDynamik and Kalevala Therapeutics can run trials they couldn't previously afford. Harbor's model creates new clinical trial participants, not just more efficient existing ones.

The condition that would flip me from borderline Invest to strong conviction: evidence that Harbor's document extraction accuracy compounds measurably with each study — specific numbers showing model performance improving across therapeutic areas as the training corpus grows. That would confirm this is infrastructure, not an AI wrapper. The condition that would flip me to Pass: if Medidata Rave Lite ships document extraction with comparable accuracy within six months, Harbor's wedge narrows to pricing alone, and pricing wedges against entrenched incumbents with deep distribution rarely survive. My verdict rests on two dimensions: Albert Cai's biographical forge — he lived inside the broken system for four years and navigated its regulatory architecture personally, which is why Harbor shipped 21 CFR Part 11 compliance from day one — and the invisible system of per-unit EDC pricing that extracts 30% of clinical trial budgets through structural opacity. Those two signals together, at the LLM inflection point, are enough for me to write a check, even though this isn't the kind of "magically weird" bet that defines my highest-conviction portfolio.
