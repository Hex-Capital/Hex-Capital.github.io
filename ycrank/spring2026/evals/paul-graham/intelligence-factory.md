# Intelligence Factory -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Intelligence Factory is, as far as I can tell from the dossier, a five-person team in San Francisco trying to build "general intelligence that works on any robot" against Skild AI's $14B valuation and Physical Intelligence's cumulative $1B raise. That's the kind of pitch that arrives at my inbox already wearing a tuxedo. The dimensional signature here is plain: whatever modest thing Intelligence Factory has going for it on Organic Problem Discovery — Jalaj Shukla's GRASP Lab manipulation research, if that's our man — is overwhelmed by how loudly Intelligence Factory fails the test of being an idea that repels. This is consensus AI robotics in May 2026, and consensus is the opposite of what I'm hunting for.

On the dimension where Intelligence Factory does worst — Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — the ladder runs from Stripe at the top, where the Collisons walked into PCI compliance and bank integrations that every other developer fled from, down through Coinbase in 2012 when serious investors thought crypto was a joke, and at the bottom sits the consultant pass example: a deck that could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece. Intelligence Factory sits squarely on that bottom rung. The State of Robotics 2026 report cited in the dossier already counts "at least eleven commercial deployments using VLA models as the primary policy backbone," NVIDIA shipped Isaac GR00T N1 as an open foundation model, and Skild is reportedly pulling $30M in revenue. When I ask whether knowledgeable people's first reaction to Intelligence Factory's pitch is skepticism, the answer is no — their first reaction is "which of the eight well-funded versions of this are you?"

On Intelligence Factory's strongest dimension — Organic Problem Discovery — the ladder runs from Dropbox at the top (Drew Houston forgot his USB drive), down through Octopart in the middle (physics grad students who couldn't find capacitors), and at the bottom the consultant brainstormers from my pass example. Intelligence Factory sits between the middle and bottom rungs. If the candidate Jalaj Shukla is the right person — UPenn GRASP Lab, "modular robotic arm manipulator for 3D lattice structures, 73% faster and 18% cheaper" — there's a real researcher who lived inside manipulation problems. That's better than a McKinsey alum reading Gartner reports. But the dossier explicitly notes "identity match unconfirmed," and the YC pitch language ("hardware is converging while intelligence is the biggest bottleneck") reads like an analyst's framing, not a frustrated builder's.

Intelligence Factory's Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint is the dimension I'd most want to ask Yash Sinha and Jalaj Shukla about across a dinner table, and the dossier gives me nothing — no cereal-box stories, no shipped artifact, no demo video, no benchmark, no pilot. The Airbnb founders earned my conviction by selling $40 cereal boxes; the Bindwell teenagers earned it by pivoting from selling AI tools to licensing pesticide molecules on my own advice. Intelligence Factory's public surface is "a one-line landing page" with the headline "Generalized Robotics Manipulation" and a single contact email. That's the absent-evidence default territory, and absent evidence is itself mildly negative because resourceful founders generate stories.

Intelligence Factory's Founder Learning Rate I score at the default. Sam Altman at 19 at Loopt is the high rung; the consultant who's been polishing the same deck for a year is the low rung; Intelligence Factory is invisible — Spring 2026 batch, no metrics disclosed. I'm guessing, and I default moderately when I'm guessing. On Earnest Builder Identity, Watsi is my high rung (the only board seat I've ever taken), Replit is my mid (hackers obsessively building for hackers), and the bottom is founders who lead with raises and press. Intelligence Factory hasn't led with raises — there are none to lead with — but the absence of any obsessive public artifact, the way Physical Intelligence ships laundry-folding videos and Skild ships deployments, lands Intelligence Factory below the middle.

My 36-month forecast for Intelligence Factory is acqui-hire to NVIDIA, Meta, or a humanoid OEM (Figure or Agility) for sub-$75M, with team-only retention and the brand discontinued — or quiet shutdown — and no meaningful ARR before that outcome. The mechanism is straightforward: when capital asymmetry is this severe (Skild $1.4B, Physical Intelligence $1B+, Figure $39B post-money) against five people, and the technical recipe is published, the founders' realistic exit is the talent market. Meta already bought Assured Robot Intelligence in May 2026, per the dossier. That's the template.

The strongest case against me is the Bindwell precedent: young technical founders, unsexy schlep domain, I personally invested. Could Intelligence Factory be Bindwell — a GRASP Lab researcher with cross-embodiment tactile data nobody else has bothered to collect? Possibly. But Bindwell tackled pesticide chemistry, a domain venture capital actively fled from. Robotics manipulation in 2026 is the hottest funded category in physical AI, not a schlep. The dossier shows no proprietary dataset, no working demo, no insight that contradicts the consensus playbook — just "hardware-agnostic generalist model" stated as a wedge against three better-funded peers making the same claim. The condition that flips my verdict is concrete: a working manipulation demo from Intelligence Factory showing a non-trivial cross-embodiment task — say, the same policy folding laundry on three different arms — published with reproducible benchmarks. That artifact would tell me Yash Sinha and Jalaj Shukla are building rather than positioning. I haven't seen it.
