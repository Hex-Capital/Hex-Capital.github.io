# Robocurve -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Jay Chooi is a top-7% contributor to UKGovernmentBEIS/inspect_evals and a Rhodes Scholar building the robotics analogue of the UK AISI framework he already ships code into ? that's Robocurve's real asset and it's genuine missionary-temperament pre-pitch evidence. But Robocurve is registered as a Public Benefit Corporation with the mission tagline "Forecasting Physical Automation for Societal Preparedness," and that structure signal is doing more work in my head than the founder credentials. The dimensional signature on Robocurve is high-conviction technical missionary paired with a capital structure that telegraphs "we do not want to be a venture return." Those two facts belong to different companies, and the tension is the entire evaluation.

On founder conviction, the load-bearing analogue in my head is Ben Silbermann at Pinterest ? the missionary who kept building through years of being wrong-in-public before the network resolved. Robocurve's Jay Chooi doesn't have Pinterest-scale artifacts yet, but the top-7% Inspect Evals contribution history is the same *type* of signal: paid or unpaid, the person was working on the exact substrate of the company before the round existed. Where Robocurve diverges from the Silbermann rung is time-on-problem ? Chooi is a 2026 Rhodes Scholar with an AI-safety research arc, not a decade-deep obsession, and Aris Zhu's IEEE RAL 2025 GPC paper is real but recent. The conviction is legible; the tenure isn't yet.

The Pass-Example failure mode I keep testing Robocurve against on capital structure is the canonical YC "AI agent for [vertical], $30M post" shell ? not because Robocurve looks like it (it doesn't, thank god), but because the failure mechanism there is *no reason venture capital should be in this cap table*. Robocurve inverts that in the worst possible way: instead of force-fitting a bootstrap business into a SaaS cap table, it's force-fitting an *AI-safety non-profit* into a for-profit PBC. Hardware operating cost per benchmark run is undisclosed, buyer willingness-to-pay from Physical Intelligence / Skild / 1X is unproven with zero paid pilots, and the PBC + "societal preparedness" language is the tell that the founders themselves aren't sure this is venture-shaped. The right instrument for Robocurve may actually be Open Phil / UK AISI grant capital, not equity.

Round-price non-consensus: Robocurve is *not* the hot 2026 YC deck. Robotics evals infrastructure is an unloved meta-layer, the competitor set (RobotPerf, ManipulationNet, RoboEval) is entirely academic and free, and no tier-1 VC I know is racing to price an evals-as-a-service seed. Over the next 18 months this compounds in Robocurve's favor on Criterion 1 ? nobody bids the round up, entry stays cheap, and if the frontier labs actually need third-party legitimation by 2027-2028 the price optionality is real. But the same thinness that makes it cheap makes the legibility-translation problem to a Series A buyer genuinely hard: I have to story-broker "we score robots" into either a strategic acquisition frame or an MLCommons-style consortium frame, and neither is a clean venture markup.

Founder-as-brand: this is where Robocurve is thinnest. No Twitter presence for either Chooi or Zhu surfaced, no newsletter, no podcast, no owned Discord ? the GitHub @jeqcho 104-repo footprint is craftsman evidence, not distribution. For the score on Criterion 4 to flip a tier, one of Chooi or Zhu would need to be publishing opinionated long-form on physical-AI evals by mid-2027 with measurable inbound from frontier labs. Absent that, the Slow Creator Fund lens does not apply here and I default to my absent-evidence floor.

Category sleeve: Robocurve is not in my convicted crypto or DTC or prescription-rail lanes, but ? and this is the interesting part ? it's also not the "AI is just more internet" pattern I explicitly de-rate on Criterion 5. The single-anchor parallel worth naming is **Sublime Security**: an unglamorous infrastructure position in a category most seed investors ignored because it wasn't the hot AI narrative shape. Robocurve is that same taxonomically ? a picks-and-shovels layer sitting *underneath* the frontier robotics capex wave rather than competing in it. The dossier evidence pinning this: Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, and Figure are all raising at multi-billion valuations per CB Insights, and none of them can credibly grade their own homework.

My 36-month forecast on Robocurve: acquihired by Physical Intelligence, Skild, or 1X for $10-25M as an in-house evals team by mid-2029, OR the PBC structure resolves into a grant-funded non-profit consortium (Open Phil, UK AISI, MLCommons-adjacent) with Chooi and Zhu earning salaries and no equity outcome. I do not see the third path where Robocurve compounds into a $50M-ARR independent benchmarking service, because the frontier labs will not pay a third party to grade them once they can hire the grader.

The bull case that stings: MLPerf became the default reference score for ML training, and any lab that wanted enterprise or government legitimation had to submit. If physical-AI procurement by defense, logistics, or industrial buyers requires third-party capability scores by 2028 ? and given Figure and 1X are already selling humanoids to enterprise pilots, that's not fantasy ? Robocurve is the default operator, and Chooi's Inspect Evals lineage is the credibility moat. The dossier fact that dismantles it: MLCommons is a *non-profit consortium*, not a venture outcome, and Robocurve's PBC registration plus "societal preparedness" mission framing suggests the founders are already anchored to that end-state rather than the venture one. The one verifiable condition that flips me to invest: Robocurve converts from PBC to a standard C-corp AND signs a paid pilot with one of Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, or Figure at a disclosed contract value within 12 months. Until then, the structure telegraphs the outcome.
