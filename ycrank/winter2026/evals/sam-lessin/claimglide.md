# ClaimGlide -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about ClaimGlide is that the CMS-0057-F regulatory mandate -- which the company's "why now" thesis depends on -- is actually an argument *against* this investment, not for it. When the federal government mandates that every payer implement standardized FHIR APIs for prior authorization by January 2026, they're not creating a moat for anyone. They're paving a highway that every developer, every EMR vendor, and every funded competitor can drive on simultaneously. Standardization is the enemy of startup defensibility. The moment athenahealth and eClinicalWorks see a standardized API surface to build against, they can ship native prior auth automation to their entire installed base of private practices -- the exact customer segment ClaimGlide targets -- without a single sales call. This isn't a "why now" for ClaimGlide; it's a "why now" for the incumbents who already have distribution.

Let me run my sane-person-insane-idea filter. Nami Lindquist checks several "sane" boxes: Penn M&T dual degree (CS + Finance from Wharton and Penn Engineering is genuinely elite), a SpaceX engineering internship, and a prior startup -- Sync Labs -- that won a $100K innovation prize and ran seven pilot studies with care facilities. That's real capability. But the "sane" has to be calibrated to *this specific idea*, and here's where it breaks down. There's no documented experience in healthcare revenue cycle management, medical billing, prior authorization workflows, or payer relations. The pivot from senior care AI to prior authorization automation is a domain shift, and prior auth is a domain where the devil lives in the operational details -- payer-specific policies, denial pattern recognition, clinical documentation requirements. When I backed Teamshares, the founders had spent years deep inside the small business acquisition world before proposing something that sounded logistically insane. Here, the founder is smart but new to the domain, and the idea -- automating prior authorizations -- is not insane at all. It's one of the most consensus pain points in healthcare. The AMA has been documenting this for years. Multiple companies with over $200M in combined funding are already attacking it. This is a sane person with a sane idea, which is the configuration that produces the most mediocre venture outcomes.

Now the AI kill test, which is where this really falls apart for me. ClaimGlide is the definitional case of what I've been arguing is uninvestable: the AI *is* the product. Strip out the LLM capability -- the auto-generated payer-approved language, the automated appeal letters, the form auto-filling -- and there is no business. Nothing remains. Compare this to Craftwork, where if you remove the software layer, you still have painting crews, scheduling operations, and physical-world customer relationships. The dossier itself states the quiet part out loud: "The core workflow -- auto-filling forms from EMR data, submitting to payer portals, tracking status, generating appeals -- is technically replicable by any team with EMR integration expertise and LLM capabilities." That's not me being cynical; that's the research itself acknowledging the commoditization risk. And the "potential moat" the dossier infers -- EMR integration depth and accumulated payer language data -- is exactly the kind of software-and-data moat that I believe AI is currently dissolving. Any foundation model can learn payer-specific denial patterns given sufficient training data.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Private medical practices are genuinely underserved -- the big prior auth players (Cohere, CoverMyMeds) focus on payers and large health systems, leaving a fragmented market of small practices that nobody has locked up. If ClaimGlide moves fast, achieves real EMR integration depth, and starts accumulating practice-specific workflow lock-in before incumbents prioritize this segment, there's a real wedge. The market is large ($2.2B heading to $7.4B by 2030), the pain is real, and private practices are desperate for solutions. A solo founder who can sprint to product-market fit before the EMR vendors ship their own features could build something meaningful. But here's why I can't get there: this isn't a new primitive. It's not a protocol layer. It's a workflow application built on top of EMR platforms and payer APIs, with a SaaS or per-transaction business model, competing in a space where the distribution advantage belongs overwhelmingly to the platforms it integrates with. The narrative ceiling is "really good prior auth tool for small practices" -- that's a real business, potentially, but it's not an infinity story, and it's not the kind of structural bet I make.

I'm passing. The company sits at the intersection of three categories I explicitly avoid: AI-first value proposition (AI is the product, not the cherry on top), SaaS business model as the primary innovation (subscription or per-transaction pricing for software access), and an application-layer position on someone else's infrastructure (EMR platforms own the customer relationship). The founder has raw talent that could be applied to a structurally different problem -- one where the business would be good even if AI didn't exist, where the moat is operational or physical, where the product creates a new layer that others build on. This particular configuration, though, is a feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent with distribution.
