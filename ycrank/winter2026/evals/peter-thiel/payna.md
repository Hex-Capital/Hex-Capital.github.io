# Payna -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Brico has $21.6 million in funding, marquee customers including Marqeta and BitGo, Forbes Top 50 recognition, and claims 600% year-over-year revenue growth — all in the exact market Payna now proposes to enter. This is the single most important fact about this company, and it renders most other analysis secondary. When a well-capitalized startup has already validated a thesis, attracted enterprise customers, and established itself as the category leader, a new entrant pursuing the same thesis is not building around a secret. It is engaging in mimetic competition — imitating a successful model because the model's success makes it legible and attractive. René Girard would recognize this immediately: Payna desires what Brico already possesses, and the result will be rivalry that destroys value for both parties while the underlying regulatory data remains public and non-proprietary. I have spent my career avoiding exactly this dynamic.

The absence of a contrarian secret is fatal to my framework. What important truth does Payna see that Brico, Cornerstone Licensing, Regology, and every compliance law firm in America does not? That state-by-state financial licensing can be automated with AI? Brico proved this thesis two years ago. That a "compliance operating system" is better than a "licensing workflow tool"? This is a positioning distinction, not a secret about the world. The underlying regulatory data — state examiner playbooks, NMLS procedures, FinCEN requirements — is publicly available. There is no proprietary data advantage, no network effect in compliance software, and no structural mechanism through which early dominance compounds into monopoly. This is a services business dressed in SaaS clothing, competing on execution quality in a market where execution quality is table stakes.

The pivot history deepens my concern. Three months ago, the company was building "Paena," an on-chain earned-wage-access product connecting DeFi liquidity to paycheck-to-paycheck workers — a fundamentally different product for a fundamentally different customer. The shift to B2B compliance infrastructure during the YC batch is precisely the kind of "test and iterate" behavior that signals indefinite thinking. A founder with a definite vision does not abandon one product category for another in ninety days. The pivot tells me the founders are searching for product-market fit rather than executing a pre-existing conviction about how the world should work. This is lean methodology in practice, regardless of how the founders describe their philosophy.

The bull case, which I take seriously enough to articulate: the GENIUS Act and expanding state-level crypto licensing requirements are genuinely creating new compliance surface area. If the regulatory burden accelerates faster than Brico can scale its human-in-the-loop model, a pure-AI approach could find an opening. Aaron Chen's crypto-native background — Axal experience, ETHGlobal wins, Ritual Fellowship — gives him genuine understanding of what crypto companies need. And compliance software, once embedded in a customer's regulatory workflow, does create meaningful switching costs over time. If Payna could capture a specific segment that Brico is underserving — say, early-stage crypto companies that cannot afford Brico's enterprise pricing — the beachhead-and-expand playbook could work. But this requires two things I do not see evidence of: a specific, narrow market segment they intend to monopolize first, and a technological capability that is an order of magnitude better than what Brico already offers. The website lists nine license categories across fifty states with placeholder "0%" metrics. This is breadth without depth — the opposite of the small-market dominance strategy that created monopoly positions for Facebook, PayPal, and Stripe.

The founder profile raises additional concerns. Aaron Chen is a talented crypto product designer, but compliance software requires deep regulatory domain expertise — understanding how state examiners actually evaluate applications, what triggers enforcement actions, how NMLS procedures vary across jurisdictions. Neither co-founder has documented experience in regulatory compliance or legal operations. He Song's background is entirely opaque, which at pre-seed is not unusual but in a domain-specific compliance product is genuinely concerning. You cannot build accurate state-by-state regulatory tooling through engineering talent alone; you need someone who has lived inside the compliance process. The cleantech parallel applies here, inverted: instead of "men in suits" without engineering, these are engineers without regulatory substance.

I pass. The market has a well-funded category leader, the thesis is consensus, the founders pivoted recently into an unfamiliar domain, and there is no visible mechanism through which Payna escapes the gravitational pull of competition with Brico. Competition in compliance software will look like every other commoditized SaaS category: price pressure, feature parity, and margin compression. The power law demands that I invest only in companies that could return the entire fund. A compliance SaaS tool entering a market where the leader has a two-year head start and twenty million dollars does not clear that threshold under any reasonable scenario.
