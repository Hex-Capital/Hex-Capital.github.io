# ARC Prize Foundation -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Chollet's specific knowledge here is among the deepest I've encountered in any company at any stage. He didn't study AI benchmarking -- he invented the theoretical framework for measuring intelligence itself. "On the Measure of Intelligence" isn't a paper; it's a coordinate system that every frontier lab now navigates by. When OpenAI wanted to validate o3, they reached for Chollet's ruler. That's the kind of knowledge that takes a decade of obsessive intellectual curiosity to produce and cannot be replicated by hiring a smart team for six months. Keras with 63.9K GitHub stars, 18,000+ citations on the Xception paper, TIME 100 in AI -- this isn't a resume, it's a body of work that constitutes the benchmark's moat. Knoop complements with operational specific knowledge from scaling Zapier to $310M revenue. The founder-problem fit is nearly perfect: the person who defined the problem is now building the institution to solve it permanently.

The structural problem is that this institution is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. I invest in equity. Equity represents ownership of future cash flows, and a nonprofit generates none. The benchmark creates enormous leverage -- one evaluation framework serves every AI lab on earth -- but the value accrues to the labs being measured, not to the organization doing the measuring. This is the inverse of my picks-and-shovels thesis. Stack Overflow was developer knowledge infrastructure that captured value through advertising, jobs, and eventually a $1.8B acquisition. Alchemy was blockchain infrastructure that charged API fees. ARC Prize Foundation is AI measurement infrastructure that... accepts donations. Each $1M competition requires new funding. That's not leverage; that's an annual fundraising cycle. The organization creates a new form of leverage for others while operating an unleveraged model itself.

The contrarian angle is weaker than it appears. Everyone agrees existing benchmarks are saturated -- that's not a secret, it's a slide in every AI lab's deck. The specific thesis that "fluid intelligence" matters more than knowledge recall has some intellectual edge, and Chollet's position that scaling alone won't achieve generalization is genuinely contrarian within certain circles of the AI community. But the broad category of "better AI benchmarks" is consensus, not contrarian. LMArena raised $100M at a $600M valuation. Epoch AI has $11M+ from Open Philanthropy. Smart money is flooding into this space. When the crowd agrees with you and the capital is flowing, the asymmetric upside has already been priced -- even in the nonprofit equivalent of mindshare and adoption.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it clearly. If AI benchmarking becomes as structurally important as credit rating agencies -- Moody's, S&P, Fitch -- then the organization that establishes the standard wields extraordinary influence over a multi-trillion-dollar industry. Credit agencies started as information services and became gatekeepers. ARC Prize Foundation could become the gatekeeper of AGI credibility. The community is already compounding: 1,400+ teams annually, adoption by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI, an ARC-AGI-3 launch event at YC with Sam Altman. If Chollet's framework becomes the regulatory standard for measuring AI capabilities under the EU AI Act or US executive orders, the foundation's influence would be enormous. And the nonprofit structure, paradoxically, is what makes it credible -- no commercial AI product means no perceived bias. That structural neutrality IS the moat. If this were a for-profit, I'd evaluate it very differently.

But two problems prevent me from writing a check. First, the Ndea conflict. Chollet and Knoop are simultaneously building a for-profit AI lab that will be evaluated by their own nonprofit benchmark. No public governance policy addresses this separation. That's not a hypothetical concern -- it's a structural integrity risk that could undermine the very neutrality that makes ARC Prize valuable. "If somebody spends ten minutes telling you how independent they are..." -- the absence of a governance framework is more concerning than its presence would be reassuring. Second, the attention split: a 4-person nonprofit where the two co-founders with the deepest knowledge are building a separate company. Greg Kamradt is operationally capable, but the intellectual direction depends on Chollet, who has another company to build.

The technology compounds -- each year of competition data, community contribution, and lab adoption makes the benchmark harder to displace. The 4,700+ GitHub stars, 4,443 Discord members, and the fact that 8+ organizations with $1M+ funding are working specifically on ARC-AGI solutions create real network effects around the standard. But benchmarks are inherently replicable at the technical level. The moat is reputational and community-driven, not scientific. A well-funded competitor with credible researchers could build an alternative. The defense is that coordination problems make it costly to switch standards -- but that defense weakens if the Ndea conflict erodes trust. I respect the intellectual ambition enormously. Chollet is asking the right question about intelligence at the right moment in history. But as an investment of my own capital, I need a return mechanism, and a nonprofit doesn't offer one. This is a donation I'd consider making, not an investment I'd make.
