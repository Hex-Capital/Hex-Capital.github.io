# Tasklet -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$5M ARR with >1,200% growth since January 2026 — that number stops most investors in their tracks, and it should. Andrew Lee built Firebase through YC S11 to Google acquisition, a platform now used by roughly 15% of all developers worldwide. He then built Shortwave, recognized that agent demand was eclipsing email AI, and pivoted Tasklet into existence so fast that it was adding more monthly revenue within weeks than Shortwave had accumulated in four years. USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and the Collison brothers wrote a $20M check at $175M. If I were evaluating Tasklet on execution velocity and founder pedigree alone, I'd be writing a check before finishing this paragraph. But I don't invest in execution velocity. I invest in consequence magnitude. And when I ask the only question that matters — "if Tasklet fully succeeds, does the world visibly change?" — the answer troubles me.

Tasklet's stated ambition is "the cloud agent operating system for knowledge work." That phrase sounds enormous until you decompose it. If Tasklet wins, knowledge workers stop switching between SaaS apps manually. Their AI agent handles cross-tool workflows — updating CRMs, processing data, sending emails — while they focus on higher-order thinking. That is a genuine productivity gain. But it is not a new capability that didn't previously exist. Zapier already generates $310M in revenue doing this with explicit workflow construction. Tasklet replaces drag-and-drop with natural language. That's a UX leap, not a technology discontinuity. The consequence of Tasklet's success is that Zapier's $5B market position gets disrupted and knowledge workers save an hour a day. Compare that to what happens if Commonwealth Fusion succeeds (limitless clean energy), if Impossible Foods succeeds (protein without animal agriculture), or if OpenAI succeeds (artificial general intelligence). Tasklet's ceiling is a very successful SaaS company. It does not change how billions of people live.

Andrew Lee's core technical claim for Tasklet is "cloud-native agent runtime, best-in-class context engineering, a universal connections layer, and multi-model support." Unpack each phrase. The agent runtime runs Ubuntu VMs on Google Cloud — commodity infrastructure. The context engineering is valuable craft but not proprietary science. The connections layer runs through Pipedream's infrastructure, a dependency the dossier explicitly flags. Multi-model support is table stakes. Tasklet's CEO himself frames the innovation as "what if the workflow goes away? And you just let the agent reason about what to do" — that is a product insight, not a technology breakthrough. The dossier's own risk section states the core technology "is reproducible" and that "multiple well-funded competitors (Lindy at ~$50M raised, Relevance AI at $37M) are building similar products." Foundation model providers — Google with Gemini + Workspace, Microsoft with Copilot + M365, OpenAI with Operator — are embedding agent capabilities directly into suites with hundreds of millions of existing users. Tasklet is building on top of commodity models without a proprietary technical wedge, which is precisely the "wrapper, not wedge" pattern I filter against.

Lee and Dimond deserve credit for what they demonstrate as founders. The decade-plus working relationship through Firebase, Google, Shortwave, and now Tasklet shows durability. The pivot from Shortwave to Tasklet — recognizing that agent demand was outstripping their email AI, and redeploying their context engineering work within months — is genuine pattern recognition and learning velocity. That pivot is the kind of evidence I look for: "what surprised them, and what they learned since starting." Tasklet's founders learned that the agent opportunity was larger than email, and they moved. But Lee is a domain expert building another infrastructure platform, which is his lifelong pattern. He is not a naive outsider who "doesn't understand what can't be done" — he is a seasoned infrastructure builder applying proven skills to a new surface area. In my framework, that's competence, not the kind of irreverent first-principles energy that produced Mach Industries' 19-year-old disrupting Lockheed Martin.

The bull case an advocate would make: Tasklet is Firebase for agents. Lee built the platform that became the default for mobile developers; now he's building the platform that becomes the default for agent-based automation. The $5M ARR and explosive growth prove product-market fit. The DoorDash exception applies — a strong execution play with outsized market consequence, funded despite lacking step-function technology. Here's why it doesn't hold: DoorDash's consequence magnitude was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people who couldn't previously get restaurant delivery. Tasklet's consequence is making existing knowledge workers — who already have Zapier, Make, and native integrations — marginally more productive. The addressable population and the depth of impact are categorically different. DoorDash created new demand; Tasklet is redistributing existing enterprise SaaS spend. That distinction is load-bearing in my framework.

Tasklet's CEO volunteered that the service is "super expensive to run," with pricing at $35–$250/month and compute-intensive execution burning through Ubuntu VMs, browser automation, and code sandboxes. This is the opposite of radical affordability — Tasklet is a premium productivity tool, not a path to making knowledge work infrastructure free. My highest-conviction bets converge on collapsing the cost of basic human needs toward zero. Tasklet converges on charging knowledge workers more for a better automation layer. The condition that would flip me: if Tasklet demonstrated a proprietary technical approach — not context engineering on commodity models, but a genuine step-function in agent reliability or capability that Google and OpenAI cannot replicate — and if the consequence expanded from "productivity tool" to "autonomous knowledge work that creates entirely new economic output," I would reconsider. Show me the technology moat that makes Tasklet's agent 10x more reliable than what Gemini + Workspace will offer natively, and we have a different conversation.

Consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach are the two dimensions that keep Tasklet in neutral territory. The team is strong, the traction is real, and this will likely be a successful company. But "likely successful" is not my filter. I either want to lose my money or make 50 times my money. Tasklet is designed to generate solid returns in a competitive market — a fine outcome for most investors, but not the binary payoff profile my framework demands.
