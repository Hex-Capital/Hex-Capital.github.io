# Chert -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Chert is selling AI-driven outbound over iMessage, a channel for which Apple has never published a business API and which is being accessed today through gray-market Mac-relay infrastructure and open-source kits like photon-hq/imessage-kit. That single fact is where my evaluation of Chert begins and largely ends. The "window" Chert is exploiting isn't a structural shift in capability or regulation -- it's an enforcement gap on a consumer messaging platform owned by the most aggressive walled-garden operator in technology. When I check the timestamp on Chert's market opening, I'm not looking at a foundation-model step function the way I was with Harvey in 2022; I'm looking at how long Apple takes to notice.

Chert's entry point fails my market structure test in a specific way that's worth naming. The dossier frames "why now" as email/LinkedIn saturation plus AI-SDR backlash plus cheap iMessage automation kits. The first half is real -- outbound is genuinely broken and the 11x.ai cohort losing 70-80% of customers within months (TechCrunch, 2024) is exactly the kind of category trauma that creates demand for new channels. The second half is not a structural shift; it's an arbitrage. Sendblue (YC) and Project Blue already sell the iMessage primitive, and Chert is a managed-service wrapper on top of that primitive. There is no exogenous catalyst here that I can underwrite -- no Apple policy change opening business iMessage, no carrier-level B2B SMS reform, no regulatory shift. The 10x reply rates Chert claims today exist precisely because the channel is unsaturated -- and the moment Chert and its peers prove the wedge, those reply rates compress and Apple's enforcement attention rises. That's not a nonobvious market window. That's a temporary inefficiency.

Chert is the wrong layer of the stack by my framework. My revealed preference across Stripe, Gusto, Checkr, and Infisical is to back the infrastructure layer that other businesses depend on, not the application sitting on contested infrastructure owned by someone else. Sendblue is the closer analog to my pattern -- API-first, self-serve, the picks-and-shovels for iMessage outbound. Chert is the application layer riding Sendblue-class providers, and the dossier explicitly notes that "any AI-SDR competitor (11x, Artisan, AiSDR) could layer iMessage on top of those APIs." Chert's only durable wedge candidate is the verified-phone-number lead database, and lead-data businesses have historically been low-margin, commoditized, and difficult to defend against ZoomInfo-class incumbents. Nothing in the dossier suggests Chert has built proprietary phone-verification infrastructure that competitors couldn't replicate inside a quarter.

The single-miracle test is where Chert most clearly fails. Chert needs Apple to keep tolerating gray-market iMessage relays at scale, AND needs the AI-SDR retention pathology that gutted 11x to not repeat in its cohort, AND needs deliverability to hold as the channel saturates, AND needs to convert undergrad founder networks into enterprise GTM motion. That's three to four independent miracles compounding. Anduril is the only investment where I've accepted multi-miracle risk, and only because the bipartisan defense-modernization signal was generational. Chert's market structure signal isn't generational -- it's a clever channel pick during an outbound crisis. The override doesn't apply.

Ian Fong is an NYU math/econ undergrad with a JPMC internship pending and an 8x hackathon record; Gary Gao is a Penn CS / Wharton sophomore with Penn Circuits Lab research credentials. Both are technically credible undergraduates and Gao's CURF research on oscillator-based learning networks is genuinely interesting, but neither has shipped a B2B sales-tooling product before, neither has scaled an enterprise GTM motion, and the JPMC internship suggests Fong's full-time commitment to Chert isn't yet locked. My High Growth Handbook lens specifically asks whether founders can navigate the 1-to-100 transition; the Parker-Conrad-rebuilding-Rippling archetype is what I look for. Chert's founders are capable hackers who got into YC -- a real signal -- but they're at the opposite end of the operator-scaler spectrum from where I write conviction checks. Strong markets compensate for execution gaps; Chert doesn't have the strong-market exemption.

The bull case I have to take seriously: iMessage genuinely is the next channel of unsaturated attention, B2B sales teams will pay almost anything for 10x reply rates, and whoever wins the managed-service wedge accumulates a verified-mobile-number lead database that compounds. Chert's logo list (Tour, Symbal, Whop, Autosana) plus testimonials referencing Databricks, Ramp, and Corgi leaders shows the YC-network distribution loop is working, and "75% reply rates and 90% actions" -- if real outside cherry-picked cohorts -- is the kind of asymmetry that sometimes survives saturation if managed-service operators control pacing. The bull case is that Chert plays the same role in iMessage outbound that Sendblue plays in iMessage messaging, but with stickier service revenue. I dismantle this because Chert doesn't own the infrastructure, the 11x precedent shows AI-SDR retention is structurally broken (acquisition-only growth without retention is exactly the High Growth Handbook red flag), and the platform risk is asymmetric -- one Apple policy update zeroes the company. The verifiable condition that would flip me: evidence that Chert has either (a) a direct, contractual relationship with Apple or a major carrier that legitimizes the channel, or (b) twelve-month net revenue retention above 110% on a cohort of at least 20 customers proving the AI-SDR retention curse doesn't apply. Neither is plausible inside the next 18 months.

Pass. The two decisive dimensions are infrastructure positioning and single-miracle viability: Chert is an application on contested infrastructure owned by Apple, requiring simultaneous tolerance from a hostile platform, structural escape from the AI-SDR retention pathology that just gutted 11x, and channel deliverability that survives its own success. Market timing is the third drag -- the iMessage window is an enforcement gap, not a capability threshold, and I don't underwrite enforcement gaps.
