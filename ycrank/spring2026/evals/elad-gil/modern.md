# Modern -- Elad Gil Evaluation

ServiceNow acquired Moveworks for $2.85B and Automation Anywhere bought Aisera for ~$1.5B -- both in 2025 -- and those two acquisitions are the entire structural thesis for Modern's existence. The bull case writes itself: two independent AI-ITSM leaders got absorbed by incumbents with cannibalization conflicts (ServiceNow charges per ticket; deploying Moveworks aggressively to eliminate tickets eats their own revenue model), creating a vacuum for independent AI-native service desk automation. IT budgets allocating 8-12% to AI initiatives in 2026 versus 2-3% in 2023 means the buyer pool is expanding simultaneously. Modern overlays any ITSM system rather than being locked to one ecosystem. Seb Poole has two prior exits -- a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in 2023 -- and previously raised ~20M AUD from investors including Alibaba. That's a real founder profile with demonstrated ability to build, sell, and raise. If ServiceNow drags its feet integrating Moveworks (which is likely given the business model tension), Modern has a 12-18 month window to capture the non-ServiceNow segment of a $15B market growing at 16.5% CAGR.

Here's what breaks the thesis: this market is not nonobvious. Ravenna raised $15M from Khosla Ventures and Madrona in April 2025. Workativ offers a nearly identical AI-agent-for-ITSM product. When two of the sharpest enterprise funds in the business are already writing eight-figure checks to a direct competitor that's been in-market for a year, the window has been spotted. Modern is entering a recognized category with funded competition, not identifying a structural shift that consensus has dismissed. Compare this to when I backed Coinbase in 2012 -- crypto infrastructure was considered a joke, not a funded category with Khosla-backed competitors. Modern's timing is defensible but it's consensus timing, which dramatically compresses the alpha available to early investors.

Modern's positioning fails the infrastructure-layer test that separates my highest-conviction bets from interesting-but-vulnerable applications. Modern deploys on top of ServiceNow, Zendesk, Microsoft Graph, Workday, Salesforce, and 12+ other enterprise systems -- it's an orchestration layer that depends on other companies' APIs, not a platform that other businesses build upon. Stripe became the payments API; Figma became the design platform; Modern becomes... a workflow automation layer sitting on somebody else's stack. The integration surface creates real switching costs -- re-creating custom workflows, approval chains, and audit trails across a dozen systems is painful -- but those switching costs accrue linearly, not exponentially. Modern doesn't generate the kind of compounding network effects where each new customer makes the platform more valuable for existing customers. The "Modern Managed" offering, where Modern operates the customer's service desk directly, compounds this concern: it introduces services revenue that scales with headcount rather than software revenue that scales with usage.

Seb Poole's founder profile shows a pattern I respect: he's shipped products, raised capital, and executed exits. The Frankie Health acquisition by Unmind involved B2B enterprise compliance (mental health platform for organizations), which maps to Modern's enterprise positioning with SOC 2 Type 2 and ISO 27001 certifications. Aleks Tomovski brings DevOps/SRE/platform engineering experience across multiple geographies that aligns with Modern's integration-heavy architecture. What I don't see is hypergrowth operating experience -- neither founder appears to have been inside a company during the 10-to-1,000-employee transition that defines my highest-conviction founder bets. Poole's companies were small-scale relative to the enterprise sales motion Modern needs: Frankie Health raised $1.5M before acquisition; the refurbished-device platform was a pre-scale exit. At pre-seed with a 2-person team targeting enterprise buyers who require extended security reviews and procurement cycles, Modern needs founders who've navigated enterprise sales at scale, not just built and sold early-stage products.

The single-miracle test is where Modern scores best in my framework. The technology miracle -- LLMs capable of natural-language workflow definition and autonomous ticket resolution -- is happening exogenously. Foundation model capability is improving on its own curve, and Modern doesn't need to invent the underlying AI. The remaining miracle is distribution: can Modern win enterprise accounts against Ravenna ($15M, Khosla/Madrona), the soon-to-be-integrated Moveworks/ServiceNow combination, and the inevitable additional entrants this $15B market will attract? That's a single, well-defined execution challenge. The "live in under a day" deployment claim, if true, compresses Modern's sales cycle against legacy ITSM implementations that take months -- a genuine distribution wedge. One miracle needed, standard competitive execution, favorable structure.

The margin architecture remains unproven and the "Modern Managed" signal concerns me. Modern's pricing page shows "Coming soon" -- expected at pre-seed. But the managed-services offering is a deliberate product choice that reveals how the founders think about growth. Operating customers' IT service desks requires headcount that scales linearly, pushing gross margins below the 70%+ threshold that distinguishes venture-scale software businesses. If Modern Managed is a wedge to land enterprise accounts and then upsell to self-serve automation, the strategy is defensible. If it becomes the primary revenue driver, Modern becomes a staffing business with AI tooling -- a fundamentally different margin profile. No revenue data, no churn data, and no confirmed pricing model means I'm scoring on structural signals rather than financial evidence, and those signals are mixed.

Modern is a competent team building in a real market with genuine structural tailwinds -- the Moveworks/Aisera acquisition vacuum, ServiceNow's cannibalization conflict, and expanding AI budgets. But the market is recognized rather than nonobvious, the positioning is application-layer rather than infrastructure, and the managed-services component introduces margin risk. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Modern has built a proprietary workflow data asset -- a library of enterprise-tested automation templates across IT/HR/Finance that creates compounding value with each deployment -- that Ravenna and other competitors lack. That would shift Modern from application to infrastructure. Without it, Modern is a well-positioned but structurally vulnerable entrant in a consensus category.
