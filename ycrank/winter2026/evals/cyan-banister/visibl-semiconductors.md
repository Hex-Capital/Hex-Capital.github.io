# Visibl Semiconductors -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Visibl is what's bearing down on them. ChipAgents has $74 million in the bank, fifty semiconductor companies already using their product, and an advisory board that reads like a hall of fame of EDA leadership -- former CEOs and CTOs of Mentor Graphics, Synopsys, and Cadence. That's the kind of competitive reality that would make most early-stage investors flinch, and honestly, it makes me flinch too. But competition alone isn't what gives me pause. Some of my best investments were in spaces where well-funded players already existed. What matters is whether the founders have the kind of deep, personal, bone-level commitment to a problem that lets them outrun opponents with bigger war chests. And that's where Visibl leaves me wanting.

When I sit down with founders, I don't start with the product or the market. I ask them to tell me their story -- not the pitch, but how they arrived at this exact moment, solving this exact problem. With Visibl, what I see is two talented engineers who noticed coordination bottlenecks at work and decided to build a solution. Jordon Kashanchi has legitimate semiconductor credentials -- Microsoft Azure Maia, Arm, Intel, a master's from UT Austin in integrated circuits. That's real domain depth. Bryce Neil brings software and data systems chops from Deloitte's AI consulting practice. These are competent, smart people. But competence isn't what I invest in. I'm looking for the founder whose biography and their company's mission are so intertwined that walking away would feel like abandoning a piece of themselves. I don't see that thread here. The problem they're solving is real, but it reads as professionally motivated rather than personally forged. There's no hardship narrative, no outsider origin, no signal that these founders arrived at this insight through a path nobody else could have walked.

The "coordination layer" positioning is the most intellectually interesting thing about the company, and I want to give it credit. Synopsys won't build a tool that orchestrates Cadence workflows, and Cadence won't return the favor -- the incumbent business model conflict creates genuine structural space for a startup to occupy. That's the kind of insight that reminds me of Carta, which made cap tables transparent when every law firm had an incentive to keep them opaque. If Visibl becomes the neutral layer that sits across all EDA toolchains, the switching costs compound and the data advantage deepens with every deployment. That's a real moat argument. But the comparison to Carta breaks down quickly: Carta was making an invisible system visible to founders and employees who had no access to equity information that shaped their financial lives. Visibl is making workflow coordination visible to semiconductor engineers who are already among the highest-paid professionals in technology. It's an efficiency tool, not an access tool. It doesn't create economic opportunity for people who lack it, and it doesn't make an opaque power structure transparent to those it disadvantages.

The strongest bull case for Visibl requires believing three things simultaneously: that the coordination layer category is distinct enough from point-tool AI copilots to avoid direct competition with ChipAgents and Silimate; that the increasing complexity of 2nm and 3nm process nodes will make coordination the binding constraint on tapeout timelines, not individual verification or synthesis tasks; and that Jordon's firsthand experience at Microsoft, Arm, and Intel gives him a design-context advantage that no amount of ChipAgents' funding can replicate. If all three are true, a two-person team with the right product could land two or three design teams at major fabs, accumulate proprietary workflow data through on-prem deployments, and become the irreplaceable connective tissue of the semiconductor design flow. The on-prem model, while brutally hard to scale at this team size, is exactly what semiconductor companies require for IP security -- and it creates the kind of deep integration that makes rip-and-replace nearly impossible. I've seen this pattern before: hard deployment creates sticky deployment.

But I keep coming back to the fact that "AI agents for chip design" is a consensus category right now. Multiple companies have raised meaningful capital. VCs understand the thesis. The weirdness has been priced out. When I backed SpaceX, people thought Elon was delusional for trying to build rockets. When I backed Crusoe, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was absurd. Those companies were dismissed. Visibl isn't dismissed -- it's competing in a recognized market with well-funded players and the active attention of $80 billion incumbents. The coordination layer framing is clever, but it's a positioning distinction within a hot category, not the kind of non-obvious bet that keeps me up at night with excitement. I also worry about the domain asymmetry between the co-founders -- Jordon carries the semiconductor expertise, Bryce brings the software platform skills, but neither the dossier nor any public information reveals how they handle disagreement or what stress-tested their partnership. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I always want to understand how co-founders fight before I write a check.

This is a well-constructed enterprise B2B startup in a real market with a defensible positioning angle. A lot of investors would and should look at this seriously. But it doesn't activate the things I specifically look for -- the outsider founder with a survival story, the idea that makes other investors uncomfortable, the company that creates access where none existed. I wish I could hear Bryce and Jordon tell me about the moments that shaped them beyond their engineering careers. Maybe there's a story there I'm not seeing in the public record. But based on what's in front of me, this is a pass.
