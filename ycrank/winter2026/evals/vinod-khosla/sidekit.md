# SideKit -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: SideKit has reduced the probability of failure so effectively that the consequences of success have become inconsequential. If everything goes right -- they capture a meaningful share of indie mobile developers, the SDK becomes a standard part of the production stack, they scale to thousands of paying customers -- the result is a $29-$99/month workflow convenience tool in a market where Firebase is free and PostHog is open-source with $180 million in funding. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No industry operates differently. The mobile analytics market is real, the pain is real, but the ambition here is calibrated to "slightly easier production deployment for small teams," not to transforming how software gets built and maintained. That gap between problem scale and ambition scale is what I evaluate first, and here it tells me everything I need to know.

The competitive landscape is the most striking feature of this dossier, and it's damning. Firebase has 72%+ iOS integration and roughly 99% Android penetration -- it's free, it's embedded in Google's ecosystem, and it already does analytics and remote config. PostHog bundles analytics, feature flags, and session recording with an open-source model and $180 million behind it. Statsig was just acquired by OpenAI for $1.1 billion. LaunchDarkly has $330 million in funding. SideKit is entering this arena with 7 GitHub stars and a 244 kilobyte SDK. The individual components -- analytics, feature flags, version gating -- are described in the dossier itself as replicable "in weeks." When incumbents and well-funded competitors don't dismiss your approach as crazy but could trivially replicate it, that's the opposite of the signal I look for. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing in public telecom was impossible -- that was the signal to invest in Juniper. Nobody is telling SideKit that bundling three existing developer tools is impossible. They're just not paying attention, because it's not threatening enough to notice.

The strongest bull case is the self-healing AI feature and the timing around AI-generated code. There's a real phenomenon happening: Cursor, Copilot, and Claude are creating a wave of new developers who can build apps but have no idea how to operate them in production. If SideKit positioned itself as the autonomous production layer for AI-generated software -- not a convenience bundle but a fundamentally new capability where software monitors itself, diagnoses problems from user feedback, and ships its own fixes -- that could be a company worth building. The technology trajectory of LLMs genuinely supports this: the rate of improvement in code generation and reasoning suggests that autonomous software maintenance will become feasible within the next two to three years. But SideKit isn't making that bet. The self-healing feature is buried on the $99/month tier as one bullet point among several. It's a feature, not a thesis. If the founders walked in and said "we're building the system that makes human software maintenance obsolete," I'd lean forward. Instead, the pitch is "one package to take your mobile app to production." That's a tagline for a convenience product, not a category-defining company.

Ashish Selvaraj has genuine founder-market fit -- he built a consumer iOS app, felt the analytics pain firsthand, and constructed the precursor to SideKit as his own private solution. That's the right origin story. His Databricks experience gives him relevant infrastructure knowledge. But founder-market fit for a small problem is still a small problem. I evaluate whether the founder is attacking something whose consequence of success justifies the effort of any team, and here the ambition doesn't match what these founders could potentially achieve. They're too capable to spend years building a $99/month developer tool in a market where Google gives the basic version away for free. The unverifiable credentials for Ethan Pronev -- the website claims Jane Street and Citadel experience, but no public profile, no LinkedIn, personal website returns 404 -- adds an uncomfortable note for what should be a straightforward due diligence check.

I pass. The problem is real but small. The technology bundles existing capabilities rather than creating new ones. The competitive environment is among the most formidable in all of developer tools. And the consequence of success, even in the best case, is a solid mid-scale SaaS business -- not the kind of outcome that justifies the opportunity cost of these founders' talent or my capital. If they pivoted to making the self-healing AI the entire company -- autonomous software maintenance as the core product, not a feature -- I'd want another conversation. That version of the company has consequence magnitude worth exploring.
