# o11 -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the gap between the founders' knowledge and the founders' market. Ajay Misra shipped Cal AI to $40M ARR and 10M users -- that's real. You don't get there without understanding how AI products survive contact with millions of humans. His Mayo Clinic AI research and the reCAPTCHA model suggest genuine ML depth, not wrapper engineering. But this specific knowledge is about *building and scaling AI consumer products*. It is not about how a Goldman associate structures a waterfall chart at 2am, or how a McKinsey engagement manager audits a financial model, or what a Latham associate needs from a document review workflow. Neither founder has spent a day inside the industries they're targeting. They talked to bankers and consultants -- that's market research, not specific knowledge. When I backed Twitter, Williams had been thinking about public communication for a decade through Blogger. The insight wasn't transferable because it wasn't researched -- it was lived. Here, the technical knowledge is strong, the domain knowledge is absent. Any smart AI team that spends six months interviewing finance professionals arrives at the same product spec.

The leverage architecture is constrained by a structural problem I care a lot about: platform dependency. o11 is a tenant inside Microsoft's and Google's buildings. Microsoft controls the APIs, the add-in store, the distribution, and ships a competing product called Copilot. Positioning yourself as "Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works" is not a long-term strategy -- it's a marketing hook that invites the landlord to evict you. Compare this to Stack Overflow, which *was* the platform. Or Notion, which created its own canvas. o11 adds a layer of intelligence to someone else's product, and the someone else has $3 trillion in market cap and every incentive to recapture that value. The leverage here is standard SaaS -- code serving many users through a subscription model -- but there are no network effects, no protocol dynamics, no data flywheel where each user makes the product harder to replicate. One customer's use of o11 does not improve it for the next customer.

The timing is wrong for my framework. AI productivity tools for knowledge workers is the consensus bet of 2025-2026. Gamma has $100M+ ARR and a $2.1B valuation. Plus AI occupies nearly identical positioning. Templafy has $147M in funding. Microsoft is pouring billions into Copilot. This is not the kind of market where you get paid for being right first -- this is a market where the crowd is already there, bidding up every entrant. The best angel returns come from betting against a gloomy crowd. Here the crowd is euphoric. Every VC deck in San Francisco has a slide about "AI-powered enterprise productivity." When everyone agrees the opportunity is real, the pricing already reflects it, and the competition is fierce enough that execution speed alone won't create durable advantage.

The strongest bull case is execution velocity and the in-app differentiation. If these founders can establish embedded workflows -- templates, chart configurations, model audit patterns -- inside Office for enough finance teams before Microsoft closes the gap, the switching costs could be real. Wells Fargo and Bank of America on the website is striking at pre-seed if those are genuine enterprise deployments rather than individual users who happen to work there. Ajay's proven ability to scale an AI product to millions of users is not common at age 20. And the in-app approach versus standalone tools like Gamma is a genuine strategic insight -- enterprise users won't leave Excel for a new canvas. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe: (1) Microsoft Copilot stays mediocre for finance-specific use cases for 2-3 more years, (2) o11 builds enough embedded workflow lock-in during that window to survive Copilot's improvement, and (3) the founders acquire finance domain knowledge fast enough through customer proximity to build features that a generalist AI team can't. That's three independent assumptions that all need to hold. I don't like bets that require multiple things to go right simultaneously.

The technology doesn't compound in a way that excites me. The core capability -- LLM-powered document generation inside Office apps -- is technically reproducible. The dossier states this directly. There's no novel AI research, no proprietary model, no data moat that deepens with usage. The integrations with PitchBook, Box, and OneDrive create some configuration stickiness, but not the kind of compounding defensibility I saw with Stack Overflow's knowledge graph or Alchemy's blockchain API layer. This is a feature race, and in feature races, the platform owner eventually wins or a better-funded competitor does. I'd want to see something in the technology stack that gets fundamentally harder to replicate over time, and I don't see it here.

These are smart, energetic founders building a real product with real early users. I don't question their intelligence or drive. But intelligence and energy without domain-specific knowledge in a consensus market on someone else's platform -- that's a configuration I pass on. The founders' specific knowledge qualifies them to build *an* AI product. It doesn't uniquely qualify them to build *this* AI product for *this* market.
