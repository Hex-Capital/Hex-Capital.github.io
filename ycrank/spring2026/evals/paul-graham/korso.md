# Korso -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Martin Pan's stint at General Motors is the only thread connecting Korso's founding team to the manufacturing workflows they're automating, and one corporate job is not the same as discovering a problem organically. When I evaluate founders, I'm looking for the specific moment of frustration — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Octopart founders unable to search for electronic parts during their physics research. Korso's framing of their problem is revealing: "the great inefficiency of modern manufacturing is not on the shop floor, it's in the office." That's a thesis statement, not a war story. It reads like something you'd write after studying a market, not something you'd blurt out because you've been drowning in RFQ emails for three years. Pan's GM experience provides adjacency to the domain, but Daichi Hiraoka's background is electrical engineering and ML research, and Alex Liu came from Notre Dame Research and Gatekeeper Systems. None of them appear to have been the person manually chasing delayed purchase orders in WhatsApp, which is the exact pain Korso claims to solve.

Korso is tackling a genuine schlep — ERP integration across SAP, Epicor, Oracle, JobBOSS, and whatever custom monstrosity a mid-market manufacturer has bolted together over twenty years. The company's own YC page acknowledges that "most AI automation breaks down at the ERP integration layer," and their architecture of long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing across multi-week manufacturing processes is technically non-trivial. This activates the part of my framework that got excited about Stripe: the Collisions were willing to tackle PCI compliance and bank integrations that repelled every other developer. But the parallel breaks down on the crucial variable. I knew Patrick Collison since he was a teenager, watched him ship Auctomatic, and invested $15K in Stripe based on years of observing his builder quality. Korso's three founders have no prior startup experience, no public GitHub repositories, no prior shipped products I can find. The schlep is real, but I need evidence that these specific founders have the determination to grind through it, and the dossier gives me almost nothing.

"AI for manufacturing" is emphatically not an idea that repels before it attracts. The global market was valued at $34.18 billion in 2025, growing at 35% CAGR. Eighty percent of CPOs name AI a top priority. Paperless Parts has raised $45-51 million for AI-supported quoting. Leverage AI raised $14.9 million for purchase order automation. Epicor has already launched Prism, a native AI agent for RFQ workflows within its ERP. Turian in Berlin has 17 employees building agentic AI for procurement. When I read that list, I don't see a contrarian bet — I see a consensus category where Korso is the least-funded entrant. The best startup ideas generate skepticism and confusion on first hearing; Korso's pitch generates nodding. That's the tell. The returns in a space where everyone already agrees AI will transform manufacturing operations have been substantially competed away, and a pre-seed company with no visible traction is entering against incumbents who can bundle AI features at marginal cost to their existing install base.

The strongest case for Korso is the ClassDojo pattern: a team tackling an unsexy, schlep-heavy domain that venture capital undervalues, where the integration pain creates real switching costs. Once Korso is embedded in a manufacturer's ERP and has accumulated pricing history, supplier patterns, and workflow-specific context, ripping it out hurts. The "tribal knowledge into data" thesis from korsoai.com describes a genuine compounding data advantage per account. And the team did travel across Asia shortly after YC acceptance to talk directly to clients — that's the "do things that don't scale" instinct I want to see. But ClassDojo overrode my sector preference because the execution quality was exceptional. Korso has zero public traction signals: no customers, no revenue, no user metrics, no press, no community, no job postings. Even by pre-seed standards, the absence of any market validation signal is notable. SOC 2 Type 1 and ISO 27001 certifications in progress suggest the team is building enterprise-safety infrastructure before proving demand, which inverts the sequence I'd want — build something people want first, then add the compliance armor.

Korso's three co-founders know each other through Penn Electric Racing and UC Irvine, which is good — co-founder relationships formed through real collaborative work are more durable than relationships formed for the purpose of starting a company. Hiraoka's ML work reducing radiotherapy dose prediction discrepancies by 97% demonstrates genuine technical capability. But I'm evaluating slope, not position, and I have no data points to calculate a slope. No visible iteration on the product, no growth metrics, no pivot stories, no evidence of the learning velocity that made me bet on Sam Altman at 19 despite zero business experience. The founders may be exceptional — but nothing in this dossier shows me the animal quality, the slightly-too-intense obsession with the problem itself that separates builders from planners.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Korso has a single manufacturer using their system autonomously to process real RFQs, generating real quotes, with the manufacturer preferring Korso's output to their manual process. One obsessive early user in a crappy version one would change everything — it would validate both the product and the founders' ability to close an enterprise customer without a sales team. Without that, Korso is a technically competent team in a consensus AI category with no visible signal of the founder quality or organic problem discovery that my framework requires. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Organic Problem Discovery (the problem reads as market-identified, not lived) and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (everyone agrees this is a good idea, which in my framework means the returns have been competed away).
