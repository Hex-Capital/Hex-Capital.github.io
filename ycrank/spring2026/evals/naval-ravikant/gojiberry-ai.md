# Gojiberry AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Pierre-Eliott Lallemant is listed as co-founder of both Gojiberry AI and Standout, both in YC Spring 2026. Two companies. Same batch. Same founder. I don't need to read further to know this violates my most basic filter. A principal's identity is fused with one company -- that fusion is what lets startups beat incumbents. A founder splitting attention across two ventures in the same accelerator batch is an agent of both and a principal of neither. "I almost won't start a company, or hire a person, or work with somebody if I just don't think they're into what I want them to do." Running two YC companies simultaneously is the structural opposite of that standard.

$1.4M ARR in 9 months, bootstrapped, 1,000+ paying customers, 44% MoM growth. Let me be honest -- those numbers are exceptional for pre-seed. The bull case writes itself: three repeat founders with prior exits (CoCo AI sold in 2025, Edusign sold in 2025), proven ability to find product-market fit through iteration (the original AI sales assistant with 2,000 waitlist signups and zero conversions was killed cleanly), and a distribution engine running 5,000 cold emails plus 800 LinkedIn DMs weekly. Gojiberry AI is growing faster than Artisan was at the same stage despite Artisan's $46.1M in funding. If execution alone determined my investments, I'd write the check today. But execution without structural advantage is a treadmill. Wish had beautiful growth metrics too -- and collapsed 95% from its IPO peak because the business had no specific knowledge moat. Growth in a commoditized space accrues to the customer, not the company.

Gojiberry AI's specific knowledge problem is the central weakness. Pierre-Eliott and Román ran 5,000 cold emails daily at CoCo AI -- that's operational sales knowledge, the kind acquired by tens of thousands of B2B founders worldwide. It's relevant to the product but it's not the product. Compare to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, whose years at Google Brain and OpenAI meant the product literally could not exist without his non-transferable expertise. Gojiberry AI's "30+ LinkedIn intent signals" taxonomy and ICP scoring logic are differentiators on a product page but not in any durable structural sense -- Valley offers "60+ data point enrichment" with claimed 15-45% reply rates, and Artisan, Amplemarket, Persana, and Salesforge all occupy adjacent territory. If society can train you to monitor LinkedIn signals and auto-generate outreach messages, it can train your competitor. No GitHub repos are visible for any founder, and the CTO Dylan Teixeira holds a Master's in Digital Marketing, not machine learning. Gojiberry AI is not a productization of irreplaceable knowledge -- it's competent execution in a space where five well-funded competitors are executing competently too.

The leverage architecture compounds the concern. Gojiberry AI is built on two layers of rented leverage: LinkedIn's platform for distribution and data access, and likely a third-party LLM for message generation. LinkedIn policy changes or API restrictions could disable core functionality overnight -- the dossier notes Reddit complaints about spam behavior already associated with the platform. Code is permissionless leverage; code that depends on a single platform's continued permission is not. Gojiberry AI's $99/month Pro plan with 2 LinkedIn senders scales well per customer but caps upmarket expansion. The five active job postings -- including an SDR at $150K-$180K and a Head of Customer Success -- signal that Gojiberry AI is scaling through labor, not through code. You don't hire SDRs when code is your workforce multiplier. Compare to Notion, which reached millions of users with a team that stayed remarkably small -- the product was the salesperson. Gojiberry AI is hiring salespeople to sell the product.

Gojiberry AI does genuinely democratize access to one thing: intent-based selling for small teams priced out of ZoomInfo and enterprise tooling. At $99/month, Gojiberry AI gives a two-person sales team access to buying signals that previously required a $50K/year tech stack. That's real. But the democratization is thin -- it's a pricing wedge, not a structural unlock. AngelList gave individuals access to an asset class (startup equity) that was legally and structurally restricted. Gojiberry AI gives small teams a cheaper version of what enterprise teams already have. The gatekeeper being removed is a price point, not an institution.

The condition that would flip me: if Pierre-Eliott publicly stepped away from Standout and committed exclusively to Gojiberry AI, and if the team demonstrated proprietary AI capabilities -- a custom model trained on their campaign data that produces measurably higher conversion rates than any competitor using the same base LLMs -- I'd revisit. The traction earns that second look. But specific knowledge plus permissionless leverage plus undivided principal commitment is the formula. Gojiberry AI currently offers operational knowledge, rented leverage, and a divided founder. The traction is real but the structure is fragile. Two of my five criteria -- specific knowledge and principal authenticity -- are the most decisive here, and on both, Gojiberry AI falls short of where I need a company to be before I deploy personal capital.
