# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with Fern in a single sentence: the product IS the AI. Strip away the artificial intelligence layer and there is literally nothing left — no physical operations, no protocol, no data asset, no business. This is the exact inverse of my "cherry on top" framework. When I backed Craftwork, the painting crews exist with or without software. When I invested in Metropolis, the parking hardware is bolted to physical structures. Fern is cherry all the way down, and that's the most dangerous structural position a startup can occupy in 2026.

The founders are exceptionally credible — I want to be clear about that. Amit Yadav trained multimodal LLMs on Meta's LLaMA team and built video assistants for the Ray-Ban smart glasses. Robert Xu built the low-latency inference and orchestration systems for those same glasses over four years, with prior infrastructure work at Citadel Securities. A PhD with 298 citations across CVPR and NeurIPS, paired with a systems engineer who knows how to make real-time multimodal AI actually run at production latency — that's a deeply sane team. They worked together at Meta's Reality Labs, which gives me confidence the co-founder relationship is real, not speed-dating. But "sane person, sane idea" is the wrong combination. Building a smarter AI meeting assistant is possibly the most obvious application play in AI right now. Otter.ai already crossed $100M ARR. Fireflies.ai hit a $1B valuation. Microsoft has Copilot in Teams. Google is embedding Gemini in Meet. When I say I look for ideas that provoke genuine skepticism or confusion from most investors, I mean ideas where the reaction is "that's crazy" — not ideas where every VC on Sand Hill Road nods along and says "yeah, the meeting space is huge." The "social awareness" angle — knowing when to speak versus stay silent — is a feature differentiation within a consensus market, not a non-consensus thesis.

The AI kill test here is almost paradoxical: you can't ask "does AI commoditize this business?" when the business IS the AI. My test is whether the company gets stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper. As foundation models improve — GPT-5, Gemini 2.0, whatever comes next — real-time conversational capabilities with social awareness become table stakes, not differentiation. OpenAI's ChatGPT Voice and Google's Gemini Live are already demonstrating sub-second voice interaction. The gap between what Fern can build and what the foundation model providers ship natively is compressing on a timeline measured in quarters, not years. And the platform dependency is a double kill: Fern needs Zoom, Teams, and Meet to grant bot access, while those exact platforms are building their own native AI meeting features. You're building on someone else's land while they're constructing the same house.

The strongest bull case would require believing that "social awareness" — the ability to understand conversational dynamics and contextual appropriateness — becomes a genuinely proprietary capability that foundation model providers cannot replicate. If Fern accumulates enough real meeting data to train specialized turn-taking and social context models, and if that training data creates a compounding advantage that gets better with each meeting, then the "social awareness layer" starts looking less like a feature and more like a primitive that other applications need. The Meta Ray-Ban experience gives these founders a genuine head start on the engineering — maybe 12-18 months — and if they move fast enough, the data flywheel could create real lock-in before incumbents catch up. I can see a version of the world where "conversational social intelligence" becomes infrastructure, the way Twilio made communications infrastructure. But I don't believe that version is probable. Microsoft has access to every Teams meeting. Google has every Meet call. The data asymmetry runs massively against a two-person startup, and the primitive they'd need to establish doesn't have the protocol-layer characteristics — network effects, ecosystem building, winner-take-most dynamics — that made my infrastructure bets work.

The inferred SaaS subscription model is the final structural concern. I've been saying publicly that the SaaS era is over, that software is a business tool not a business model. An AI meeting assistant charging $20-40 per seat per month is building on the exact business model I believe is being destroyed. There's no physical asset, no regulatory position, no protocol that creates switching costs beyond "our AI is slightly better right now." When I compare this to my portfolio — Solana creating a new Layer 1 primitive, Teamshares buying real businesses with real employees, Craftwork dispatching actual painting crews — Fern exists entirely in the software abstraction layer that AI is rapidly commoditizing. The founders deserve a better structural vehicle for their exceptional talents.
