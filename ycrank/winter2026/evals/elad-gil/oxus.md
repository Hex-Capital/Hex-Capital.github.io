# Oxus -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most structurally interesting thing about Oxus is not the AI capability threshold — that's real but increasingly consensus — it's the channel conflict sitting at the center of their go-to-market. The Big Four firms that charge $200-800/hour for outsourced SOX audit work are simultaneously the dominant distribution channel for audit software, the largest advisory relationships with Oxus's target buyers, and the entities whose revenue would be directly cannibalized by this product. When I built Color Health, we encountered a milder version of this: genetic counselors initially resisted a platform that reduced their gatekeeping role. But in audit, the structural misalignment is sharper. The Big Four won't recommend a tool that eliminates their own billable hours. This means Oxus must sell directly to CAEs and CFOs who are independently motivated to cut outsourcing spend — which is feasible (the $300K-$1M+ annual cost creates strong buyer motivation) but requires navigating 6-18 month enterprise procurement cycles with a 3-person team. That's not a fatal flaw, but it defines the company's real operational challenge in a way I don't think the founders have fully articulated.

The structural moment for AI in audit work is genuine and fits a pattern I've seen produce outsized returns. Internal audit is exactly the kind of market I look for — boring, compliance-driven, built on highly repetitive document review and narrative generation, with professionals billing $200-800/hour for work that LLMs can now meaningfully assist with. The pattern is similar to what I saw early with Harvey in legal: a large professional services market where LLM document comprehension just crossed the capability threshold to make automation feasible. Prior audit tools from AuditBoard and Workiva could manage workflows — scheduling, collaboration, reporting — but couldn't interpret unstructured evidence or draft workpapers. That capability gap closed in 2024-2025. The accounting talent shortage is simultaneously driving up outsourcing costs, creating cost pressure that makes CFOs receptive. These are real structural tailwinds. But here's where the Harvey comparison weakens: "AI for professional services" was genuinely non-obvious in 2022 when legal AI seemed like a science project. By early 2026, after Harvey reached $8B and every vertical AI play has been mapped, "AI for audit" is a logical next category — not a non-obvious insight. The market is boring, which is good. The approach of applying AI to it is becoming obvious, which reduces the asymmetric return potential.

I see a potential multi-miracle problem that would need to be resolved before I could get to conviction. Harvey's single miracle was model fidelity for legal work, and that miracle was being delivered externally by the foundation model labs. Oxus's situation is more complex. The technology miracle is similarly being delivered externally — LLMs can now handle document analysis and structured output generation at audit-relevant quality. But Oxus faces at least two additional challenges that look like independent low-probability events: (1) getting audit committees and CAEs to accept AI-generated workpapers in a domain where SOX Section 302/906 certifications create personal criminal liability for signing officers, and (2) navigating PCAOB/SEC regulatory guidance on AI in audit that hasn't yet been articulated. If regulatory guidance comes back restrictive, the adoption timeline extends by years regardless of product quality. That's two miracles beyond the technology — and compounding low-probability events is exactly the pattern I avoid.

The bull case is real and worth engaging with directly. If I'm wrong about the multi-miracle problem — if CAE adoption moves faster than I expect because outsourcing cost pressure overwhelms liability anxiety, and if PCAOB guidance is permissive or silent — then Oxus is positioned at the exact Harvey structural moment for a different professional services category. The pricing headroom is enormous: a $50-100K SaaS product that reduces $300K-$1M+ in outsourcing spend is a straightforward ROI conversation. The regulatory complexity that slows adoption also creates a genuine moat — once you've encoded SOX compliance standards, PCAOB AS 2201, and control testing methodologies into domain-specific AI, that's not something a horizontal AI tool replicates easily. And the product-to-distribution trajectory is structurally present even if the founders haven't articulated it: SOX audit is a wedge into the CAE's entire workflow, expandable to operational audit, IT audit, risk assessment, and enterprise risk management. That's the AuditBoard feature set rebuilt AI-native. If adoption velocity is faster than I model, this could look like a defining company in audit infrastructure within 3-4 years.

The founders have relevant but not deep domain backgrounds — Federal Reserve summer internship, D.E. Shaw, JPMorgan — which is finance-adjacent rather than audit-specific. I don't see evidence that any founder has actually worked in internal audit or at a Big Four firm, which is where the domain encoding challenge lives. Christine Watts's MIT AI degree and ML systems experience at startups provides the technical foundation, and the team's finance + CS combination is appropriate for the category. But I see no public evidence of shipped product, customer conversations, or iteration velocity — no GitHub repos, no revenue signals, no LOIs. The 2,290 YC Launch upvotes are a social signal, not a market signal. At pre-seed, absence of traction data is expected, but absence of any execution signal beyond the YC application makes it hard to assess whether this team can navigate the enterprise sales complexity their market demands. The sibling co-founder relationship between Janet and Melinda is actually a positive signal for co-founder durability — they've collaborated since childhood on the economics books and nonprofit — but co-founder stability doesn't substitute for execution evidence.

The incumbent expansion risk from AuditBoard ($200M+ ARR, $3B+ acquisition by Hg) is the kind of "market-ending move" I watch for. AuditBoard already serves ~50% of the Fortune 500 and has the customer relationships, data access, and financial resources to build AI audit automation as a bundled feature. If they ship comparable AI workpaper generation within their existing platform, Oxus faces the classic startup dilemma of competing against a feature rather than a product. The counterargument — that audit management platforms have fundamentally different technical DNA from AI-native document intelligence — has some merit but isn't as strong as it was 18 months ago, when building LLM applications required deep ML expertise. With foundation models and frameworks commoditizing rapidly, the technical barrier between "workflow platform that adds AI" and "AI-native platform" is narrowing. Oxus needs to establish customer relationships and build switching costs before AuditBoard's AI features mature — which brings us back to the 3-person team selling to conservative enterprise buyers at 6-18 month sales cycles.

I land at Neutral. The market archetype is right for my framework — boring, compliance-heavy, at a genuine technology inflection. But the channel conflict with outsourcing firms, the multi-miracle problem around adoption and regulatory acceptance, the incumbent expansion risk from AuditBoard, and the absence of execution signals beyond YC acceptance keep me from conviction. This is a category I want to watch rather than a company I'd write a check for today.
