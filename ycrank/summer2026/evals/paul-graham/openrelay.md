# OpenRelay -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Prashant Patel spent his last job as a founding member of Amazon Bedrock managing inference for trillion-parameter models, and Jaden Wang was Lead Engineer at TensorDock before Voltage Park bought it -- so OpenRelay is what you get when two plumbers who ran the inside of the GPU-inference stack quit to build the thing they wanted while they were inside it. That is the highest dimension in the OpenRelay dossier. The lowest is that beyond a live pricing page offering RTX 4090s at $0.29/hr, there is no stunt, no HN demo video, no signed provider count, no cereal box. Domain-native founder-market fit married to a completely quiet pre-funding record: that is the OpenRelay signature.

The **Stripe** (2010) analogue loads the organic-idea axis for me here, because the Collisons had been hacking commerce APIs since **Auctomatic** (2007) and were quite literally building for the version of themselves who couldn't accept a credit card two years earlier. OpenRelay lands adjacent to that rung but not on it: Patel ran Bedrock inference infrastructure and Wang ran TensorDock marketplace ops, so the OpenRelay thesis -- aggregate third-party GPUs behind an OpenAI-compatible endpoint -- is the exact composite of what these two people did at work last year. Where OpenRelay diverges from the **Stripe** (2010) rung is that the Collisons had shipped a $5M outcome before their second YC swing; OpenRelay's founders shipped inside big companies, which is real but not the same evidence of ownership.

The MBA AI-wrapper pass I keep in my head -- glossy deck, $50B TAM slide, no artifact -- is the low rung on animal resourcefulness, and the failure mechanism there is that when the market gets crowded, soft founders reach for a fundraise instead of a stunt. Does that pattern show up in the OpenRelay dossier? Partially. OpenRelay has an artifact -- the pricing page is live, the $5-deposit-yields-$10-credit funnel works, the Docker-in-two-minutes claim is testable -- but there is no pre-funding anecdote, no named provider signed to the "Dedicated provider program," no Product Hunt, no Twitter, no GitHub trail for either Patel or Wang. Animals leave tracks. Two Voltage Park engineers who quit together and put up a marketing site is not the same as Brian Armstrong hand-building an Android wallet after **Coinbase** (2012) got rejected.

The schlep dimension compounds interestingly over 18 months for OpenRelay. Aggregating heterogeneous GPU supply from independent operators -- the "Dedicated provider program for GPU contributors" language on openrelay.inc -- is genuinely unpleasant two-sided marketplace work: you have to call operators, negotiate hourly rates, monitor uptime you don't own, and eat the SLA gap the OpenRelay site quietly admits ("uptime SLA available on dedicated capacity" implies baseline capacity has none). Over 18 months, that schlep either becomes a moat the way **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance grind did, or it eats the team because two people cannot personally onboard enough supply to beat RunPod's owned fleet at $240M ARR. Schlep-as-moat requires founders who like the schlep; the OpenRelay dossier does not tell me whether Patel and Wang like it.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is what would flip the OpenRelay score a tier. Bedrock founding engineer and TensorDock lead engineer are real institutional hacker credentials -- these are not credential-only MBAs. But the persona-level tell I over-index on is the public trail: Patrick Collison won the Young Scientist prize for Lisp before **Stripe** (2010), Aaron Swartz co-authored RSS before **Reddit** (2005), Drew Houston posted the demo before **Dropbox** (2007). OpenRelay's dossier reports "No public repos found" for both founders and "No public account found" for Twitter. If a single GitHub repo surfaced showing Wang's routing layer or Patel's Bedrock-adjacent open-source work, OpenRelay would move from mid-tier hacker signal into upper-tier.

The co-founder configuration compares cleanly to **Twitch** (2007) on a single axis: Justin Kan and Emmett Shear had failed together at **Kiko** (2005) before **Twitch** (2007), which meant they had co-suffered. OpenRelay's Patel and Wang co-worked at Voltage Park, which is co-employment, not co-suffering. It's a pair, not a solo, which clears my hardest bar; the pair shares technical context; the potential IP/non-compete exposure from both founders leaving the same employer -- Voltage Park now owning TensorDock, a direct marketplace competitor per BusinessWire March 2025 -- is the only wrinkle, and it's a real one.

My 36-month forecast for OpenRelay: acquired by Voltage Park or CoreWeave for $15M-$40M in a strategic tuck-in, or dead. Not a $1B outcome. The mechanism producing it: RunPod raised $100M at a $1B valuation in June 2026 with $240M ARR and owned fleets, Together sits at ~$1B ARR, Parasail took $32M in April 2026 for the exact same "supercloud" positioning -- OpenRelay's "90% cheaper than AWS" claim is an arbitrage window that closes when capitalized incumbents match it, and two people without a supply-side liquidity number cannot outrun that clock.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **Airbnb** (2009) known exception: I passed the idea test mentally, the cereal boxes flipped me, and the founders became animals. Someone will argue OpenRelay's organic founder-market fit is stronger than **Airbnb** (2009)'s ever was -- Patel and Wang literally built adjacent systems at Bedrock and TensorDock -- and that the schlep of supply-side recruitment is a **Coinbase** (2012)-style moat that other YC batch applicants are unconsciously routing around. That case stings because founder-market fit here is real. What dismantles it: **Airbnb** (2009) had cereal boxes, **Dropbox** (2007) had a demo video, **Coinbase** (2012) had a hand-built wallet after rejection. OpenRelay has a pricing page and no named provider count. The bull case requires the animal test to have already been passed, and the OpenRelay dossier is silent on it. The single verifiable condition that flips me to invest: OpenRelay publishes a named provider count above 20 with a real utilization percentage, or names one production enterprise inference customer. Absent that, pass.
