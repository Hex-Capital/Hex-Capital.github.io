# Edviro -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hursh Shah shipped a prior smart-glasses startup and put a paper into NeurIPS 2024 at age eighteen, and Tanuj Siripurapu has been programming for nine years and dragged a digital agency to $20K in high school -- so the thing I notice about Edviro before I notice anything else is that these are obviously hackers, but the energy waste in a boiler room is not a problem they have personally suffered. That mismatch -- hacker-grade builders pointed at a problem they observed in their "hometown high schools" rather than one they lived -- is Edviro's analytical signature. It is the inverse of Drew Houston losing his USB stick; it is the silhouette of a smart team picking a market.

Reddit's Aaron Swartz had co-authored the RSS specification before the seed check, which is the kind of pre-startup technical artifact that makes me lean in regardless of what the company does. Edviro is on that ladder, just not at the top of it -- Hursh's NeurIPS 2024 paper and Tanuj's GitHub @TanujKS with full-stack Python/Vue/React/ML range is a real hacker footprint for an eighteen- and nineteen-year-old, and Hursh's prior Skyglass attempt means at least one of them has already taken the swing-and-miss most YC applicants are still scared of. Where Edviro diverges from the Swartz rung is that neither founder is a recognized voice in the building-energy or BMS open-source world; the hacker lineage is real but generic, not domain-specific.

The Pass Example I keep in my head is the two-MBA AI-wrapper team who "spotted a market opportunity" -- founders whose product had no traceable line back to their own lives. Edviro's "hometown high schools" framing on edviroenergy.com sits closer to that failure mechanism than I'd like. The founders did not work in facilities management. They did not run a boiler. They noticed that taxpayer money was being wasted -- which is the empathy register of a concerned student, not the rage register of someone whose USB stick keeps eating their thesis. The organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) is the one criterion Edviro most clearly underperforms on, and I will not score around it.

On schlep: K-12 district procurement is one of the most painful sales motions in American B2B, BMS integration via BACnet/Modbus across heterogeneous boilers and heat pumps is dirty unglamorous work, and the M&V (measurement and verification) report layer is exactly the kind of regulatory-adjacent paperwork mainstream YC applicants route around. Edviro's traction snapshot -- 1 district partner, 7 school sites, 16 meters, $400K+ claimed savings -- means they have already begun to eat that schlep rather than just describe it. Over eighteen months this dimension either compounds into a procurement-cycle moat (district reference accounts beget more districts, slowly) or it eats the company alive when two eighteen-year-olds discover what a school-district legal review actually feels like. I lean toward the former because GridPoint's $584M hardware-led raise and Atrius's dashboard-only posture mean the agentic-software wedge is structurally underdefended.

Counterfactually on animal resourcefulness: Edviro would jump a full tier on this dimension if the dossier named the specific stunt that landed the district partner -- did Hursh cold-walk into the superintendent's office, did they instrument a building for free first, did one of them spend a summer reading utility bills line by line. The dossier is silent on the mechanism, and silence on mechanism is something I have learned to read as absence. What Edviro does have -- Skyglass, NeurIPS 2024, the $20K agency, a real signed district -- is well above the absent-evidence default but well below the cereal-box rung.

Stripe is the parallel I run for the co-founder dimension: two people who had built together for years before the company existed. Edviro is two founders from the same hometown with no documented prior project shipped together. That is not a red flag at pre-seed -- it's an unknown that the YC interview will resolve -- but it is not the Collison-brothers configuration either, and Hursh's CEO/Tanuj's CTO split is conventional rather than load-bearing.

My 36-month forecast for Edviro: stuck between $300K and $900K ARR confined to K-12 and adjacent small-municipal pilots, hitting the wall of district-procurement timelines, and either pivoting toward commercial real estate where buying cycles are shorter, or accepting an acqui-talent offer from Atrius/Acuity or a private-equity-rolled BMS player as the "energy world model" benchmark never gets published. The mechanism is the organic-pain gap: when the K-12 sales cycle starts grinding in year two, founders who personally suffered the problem keep going, and founders who noticed it from outside tend to start asking what else they could build with the same stack.

The strongest case against my forecast is Coinbase. Brian Armstrong had not personally suffered KYC compliance before he started **Coinbase** (2012); he saw the schlep and ran toward it, and the regulatory grind itself became the moat. Edviro's K-12 procurement schlep is structurally similar, and Hursh's hand-shipped Skyglass plus NeurIPS 2024 paper is plausibly the Armstrong-wallet-prototype equivalent -- pre-funding evidence of someone who finishes things. The Known Exception holds if the founder is enough of an animal to override the organic-idea gap. The reason I don't fully credit this counter is that Armstrong had spent years at Airbnb building financial product before Coinbase; Edviro's founders are eighteen and nineteen with no enterprise-energy employment in their backgrounds, and the "energy world model" claim has zero published benchmarks in the dossier. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Edviro publishes a reproducible accuracy benchmark on heterogeneous BMS telemetry, or grows from 1 district to 4+ signed district contracts within nine months on the strength of district-superintendent word-of-mouth alone. Either of those and I am writing a check. As it stands -- meeting, not check.
