# Dispatch -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive topology. Fifteen-plus companies are building reentry vehicles. Varda has $329M in funding and $48M in actual revenue from completed orbital missions. Inversion Space holds a $71M Space Force contract. Space Forge has NATO backing. Dispatch is a two-person team with a ground test and a YC check. When I see a crowded field with well-capitalized incumbents who already have government contracts and operational track records, the asymmetric upside I look for has been priced into the category -- the question is just which horse wins, and at pre-seed, Dispatch is not the horse the market is betting on. This is not a bet against a gloomy crowd. This is a bet in a category where everyone already agrees the future is bright.

The specific knowledge question is where I look first, and the answer is mixed. Both founders worked at Astranis, building and operating MicroGEO communications satellites. That gives them real spacecraft engineering knowledge -- orbital mechanics, avionics systems, hardware integration, the operational realities of putting things in space. Mello led avionics at Astranis and brings autonomy experience from Zoox and hardware depth from Apple. Case studied at Purdue, one of the best aerospace programs in the country. This is meaningfully better than generic technical talent pivoting into space. But reentry is a different discipline from satellite operations. Thermal protection at Mach 20+, plasma dynamics, hypersonic guidance through communications blackout, recovery systems -- these are problems Astranis never solved because their satellites don't come back down. The founders are adjacent to the domain, not native to it. Compare this to someone who spent five years on SpaceX Dragon's thermal protection system or on the Orion heat shield at NASA. That person would possess knowledge about reentry that can't be taught in any classroom or acquired at any satellite company. What Dispatch's founders have is strong spacecraft engineering intuition applied to a neighboring problem -- valuable, but not the kind of irreplaceable specific knowledge that makes me write a check on the spot.

The leverage architecture concerns me most. I look for companies where one unit of input produces nonlinear output -- code that serves millions, protocols that coordinate without headcount, platforms where each user makes the product more valuable for the next. Dispatch is building physical reentry vehicles. Each mission requires manufacturing a vehicle, integrating a payload, launching, recovering, refurbishing. The refurbishability is a cost improvement, not a leverage mechanism -- it reduces the cost per mission but doesn't change the fundamental linearity of the business. Revenue scales with the number of vehicles built and missions flown, which scales with capital deployed and engineering hours. The "Autonomous Space Stations" concept is where leverage could eventually emerge -- a permanent orbital platform where many manufacturers dock, produce, and use Dispatch's return infrastructure. That would be genuine platform lock-in, the kind of infrastructure play I favor. But that vision is multiple funding rounds and many years away, and at pre-seed, I'm evaluating what exists, not what might exist after $200M in additional capital.

The strongest bull case is this: Dispatch is a picks-and-shovels play in a market about to undergo its gold rush moment. Varda just proved that orbital manufacturing works and generates real revenue. SpaceX doesn't want to compete in downmass because it's non-core to their launch economics. If Dispatch's 100x cost reduction in heat shield construction holds at orbital scale, they become the low-cost logistics backbone for an entire industry -- the equivalent of building the railroads while everyone else mines for gold. The market projections ($11.2B by 2030, $62.8B by 2040) would support a massive downmass services business. And being infrastructure rather than vertically integrated manufacturing means they're not competing with their own customers. That's the right structural positioning. If I believed the cost claim were validated and the market were less crowded, this would be a fundamentally different evaluation.

But I keep coming back to two problems. First, you're building logistics infrastructure for a market with perhaps five active customers globally. The railroad needs towns to serve. Dispatch's revenue depends entirely on upstream manufacturers (Varda, Space Forge, a handful of others) succeeding at scale -- that's a dependency chain where you absorb the risk of others' execution without controlling the outcome. Second, the cost claim is extraordinary -- 100x cheaper, built "in an apartment" -- and extraordinary claims about hardware require orbital validation, not ground tests behind a rocket engine in the Mojave. A ground test simulates some thermal conditions but doesn't validate sustained hypersonic heating profiles, plasma dynamics, guidance, or recovery. The gap between ground demonstration and orbital success is where hardware companies go to die, and bridging it against competitors who have hundreds of millions in funding is a capital race this team hasn't yet entered.

The founders seem serious -- building reentry vehicles as a two-person team signals long-term commitment, not quick-flip orientation, and their shared Astranis background suggests a genuine working relationship. No integrity red flags. But seriousness and integrity don't override the structural challenges: linear scaling, crowded market, unvalidated core technology claim, and a dependency on an upstream industry that barely exists. I'd want to see this team after an orbital demonstration, with a signed customer contract, and with the cost advantage validated at scale. Until then, the risk-reward doesn't meet my bar for deploying personal capital.
