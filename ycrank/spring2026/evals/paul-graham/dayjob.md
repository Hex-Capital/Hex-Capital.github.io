# Dayjob -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Dayjob is a strong-pair vertical-SaaS bet with a hacker-genealogy hole — George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks have lived in five houses together over thirteen years and co-built **Gaea** before pivoting into Dayjob in January 2025, but neither has a GitHub trail and the CTO came out of Deloitte ESG analytics. That high/low pairing is what Dayjob actually is: two domain-soaked operators who already ran depot ride-alongs with waste schedulers, attacking a category — skip-hire scheduling — that no software-first YC applicant would willingly walk into.

The dimension where Dayjob sits highest is animal resourcefulness, and the rung above is **Airbnb** (2009) — Chesky's cereal-box financing was the canonical pre-funding stunt that overrode my idea-level skepticism. The mechanism that makes the analogy load-bearing is the same one: revenue generated through an unscalable, unpleasant, founder-personal motion before institutional capital arrived. Dayjob diverges from the Airbnb rung in the specifics — $359K+ ARR within seven months of the August 2025 product launch and a customer-reported 8%+ efficiency lift at named buyers like Allstone and Coastal Recycling is not cereal-box theatrics, it is direct-sale grind into UK waste operators with a prior failed ERP behind them. Dayjob does not have a stunt; Dayjob has a pivot that worked, which is the older and quieter version of the same test.

The dimension where Dayjob sits lowest is hacker genealogy, and the failure mechanism I keep watching for is the **Pass Example** pattern — the polished two-MBA team with no shipped open source, no Hacker News footprint, no Lisp-prize-at-fifteen lineage like Patrick Collison brought to **Stripe** (2010). Dayjob does not fully fit that anti-pattern — Postlethwaite shipped Deliveroo's UK grocery business and Fooks did real AI/data work at Capgemini before Deloitte — but the dossier shows zero GitHub accounts for either founder, no open-source contributions, and a CTO whose most recent title was "Manager, ESG & Supply Chain Analytics." For a company whose entire moat thesis depends on agent reliability across in-day exceptions, the absence of a hacker spike on the team is the dossier fact that nags at me.

Idea-organic-to-founders: Dayjob's founders spent roughly three years building **Gaea**, an ERP for e-waste recyclers, before pivoting because they watched human schedulers route ~100 jobs a day inside the depots they were already integrated into. That is not "spotted a market opportunity" framing — that is the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick pattern translated to industrial logistics, except Postlethwaite and Fooks were not themselves the schedulers, they were the founders standing behind the schedulers. Close to the organic rung but not on it; the pain belonged to the customer, not to the founder, which is the half-step that separates Dayjob from Drew Houston's own forgotten thumb drive.

Schlep zone: Waste management, skip hire, depot ride-alongs, plugging into the back-ends of operators who buy multi-year ERP licenses from AMCS and Routeware — over eighteen months this dimension compounds favorably, because every additional fleet onboarded teaches Dayjob exception patterns that pure route-optimization vendors like OptimoRoute and RouteSmart never see. The schlep is real: founder-led UK direct sales, ERP integration into legacy systems, and now a US expansion against Routeware's 1,000+ hauler installed base. Other Spring 2026 batchmates pitching "AI agents for X" are routing around exactly this kind of work.

Co-founder configuration would only fall a tier if the dossier revealed Postlethwaite and Fooks had met inside an accelerator or paired for the application — instead it shows thirteen years at Oxford, five shared houses, wedding-usher status, and a prior co-founded company. That is closer to the Huffman/Ohanian **Reddit** (2005) dorm-roommate baseline than to anything I'd hedge on.

My 36-month forecast for Dayjob: $4–8M ARR concentrated in UK and early US waste/skip-hire fleets, followed by an acquisition by **AMCS** or **Routeware** in the $40–90M range as the incumbent buys the agent layer rather than rebuild it. The mechanism is the high/low signature — the cofounder pair will out-execute and the vertical schlep will compound, but the missing hacker spike caps the ceiling below the Stripe-shaped outcomes I actually need for this portfolio to work, and the AMCS/Routeware license-conflict math makes acquisition the natural exit rather than a public path.

The bull case that actually stings: Dayjob is **DoorDash** (2013) in the wrong costume. I funded last-mile restaurant delivery when every other YC applicant in that batch was building pure software, and the boring-category-plus-relentless-founders pattern returned a defining outcome. Dayjob's $359K ARR in seven months from a cold August 2025 launch into UK waste depots is the same velocity signature, and the **Gaea** pivot is the kind of prior-failure-compounds-hustle pattern that turned Justin Kan's **Kiko** (2005) into **Twitch** (2007). What dismantles the bull case is the positioning gap — "AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" on the YC page when every named customer is a UK skip-hire operator reads as deck-shaping, not founder mode, and the CTO's Deloitte ESG background is the wrong genealogy for a product whose entire reliability claim depends on agent determinism at the scheduling layer. The verifiable condition that would flip me to a strong-invest position: Dayjob shipping a second vertical — construction-site logistics, industrial gas distribution, anything outside waste — at $200K+ ARR within twelve months, proving the agent layer generalizes off the Gaea-seeded relationships. Until that, this is a soft invest at the rung where DoorDash sat before I knew what it was.
