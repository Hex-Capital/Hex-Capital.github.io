# Instance -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Instance is a deck-shaped company wearing a frontier costume: two MIT classmates who were building "AI data analyst for databases" in January 2026 and arrived at YC Summer 2026 six months later pitching a physics-aware quality layer for synthetic video, with a landing page that says "working on world models" and nothing you can install. Lucy Cai and Claire Mao met in middle school, both went to MIT, and the company has rotated its thesis inside one fundraising cycle — that is the analytical signature here, and it does not match any of my portfolio anchors.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the HIGH rung on lived-experience: he had already shipped Oculus to scale and walked into defense with the social license of someone who'd built the headset on a workbench. The mechanism that makes the analogy load-bearing is that Luckey's biography put him in front of the problem before any market analysis could; defense was the *consequence* of the founder, not the *origin* of the company. Instance diverges precisely there — Lucy Cai's MIT Lincoln Laboratory work on 2D-to-3D point clouds is adjacent to synthetic vision, which is real, but it is *adjacent*, not lived, and three months before YC the same founder's LinkedIn headline read "Building Instance, AI for databases." That is a thesis discovered in a memo about world models, not a problem the founders were *of*.

HQ Trivia (2017) is the LOW rung I keep returning to on co-founder governance — Yusupov and Kroll never resolved who was the tiebreaker and the company imploded in feud by 2020. The mechanism: two founders without a named CEO is not a governance gap, it is a near-term blow-up risk that compounds the first time the product needs a hard call. Instance's dossier names "Cofounder, CTO" for Lucy Cai and "Cofounder" for Claire Mao with no CEO designation, no equity split disclosed, no operating agreement signaled — and the middle-school friendship is exactly the relationship pattern that makes the eventual disagreement personal rather than professional. I'd want to see the tiebreaker named on the cap table before docs.

On magical weirdness, the mechanism over 18 months is whether the category is one sophisticated investors are passing on for aesthetic or capital-intensity reasons (Crusoe burning gas flares) or one academics and incumbents are *already crowding into*. Instance is the latter: T2VPhysBench, WorldModelBench, VideoPhy-2, and LikePhys are four published arxiv benchmarks from 2024-2025 covering physics violations in video, and Encord ($60M Series C, 300+ physical-AI customers including Toyota Woven and Skydio) plus Meta-owned Scale AI both have the workforce to bolt a physics module on without channel conflict. This is not "find them before it becomes consensus" — the consensus is forming in public, and Instance is the commercial wrapper around papers that are free.

For frontier weighting, counterfactual: Instance would flip into my Crusoe lane only if the founders were standing up GPU-instrumented physics simulators or a proprietary capture rig — something a roughneck-equivalent in the robotics-data world would recognize as a tool only they could build. The dossier shows a "physics-aware quality layer" with "human judgment at scale," which is software plus a labeling workforce, the same shape as Encord and Surge AI. Nothing here is capital-intensive in the sense that SpaceX (2008) or Anduril (2017) were capital-intensive; the moat does not compound with regulation, kilowatts, or steel.

Flock Safety (~2018) is the single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability that exposes Instance most clearly: I sourced Flock because I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café — the artifact was *in the room*. Instance's product is tryinstance.app, a minimal landing page with no demo, no metrics, no customer logos, no pricing, registered LinkedIn and Twitter handles whose follower counts cannot even be retrieved. A normal human cannot install Instance, photograph Instance, or hand Instance to a friend within 48 hours, and the buyer set — frontier video-world-model labs at NVIDIA Cosmos, Genie, Veo — is exactly the group most likely to build evaluation in-house.

My 36-month forecast on Instance: a quiet acqui-hire by Encord, SuperAnnotate, or a Meta-adjacent group under $5M for the two MIT engineers, with the physics-benchmark product folded into a larger labeling platform and never standing on its own. The mechanism producing that forecast is the pairing of the lowest dimension (no tactile artifact, two-person team with no CEO) and the highest available dimension (genuine MIT-Lincoln-Lab technical adjacency on the CTO) — talented engineers in a category where the moat sits with the workforce-scale incumbents, which is how engineering teams get absorbed rather than how companies get built.

The bull case that should sting: Lucy Cai's MIT Lincoln Lab 2D-to-3D ML background is *exactly* the polymath-adjacent technical edge that lets a small team build a defensible evaluation harness before the labelers wake up, and DeepMind (~2013) is my own Known Exception proving that elite-pedigreed technical founders can compound across domains even when they fail my lived-experience test. If NVIDIA Cosmos and three robotics-foundation-model labs sign within twelve months, Instance becomes the benchmark vendors quote against, and the academic papers become *citations in Instance's documentation* rather than substitutes. The dossier evidence that wins against this bull case is the January 2026 pivot from text-to-SQL — Hassabis didn't pivot DeepMind from a database tool six months before raising; he had been compounding on AGI since the Gatsby Unit. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a named customer logo from the NVIDIA Cosmos, DeepMind Genie, or Wayve class signed before Demo Day, with disclosed deal size above $100K ARR.
