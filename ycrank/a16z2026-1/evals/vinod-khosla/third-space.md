# Third Space -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Dhruv Chopra spent ten years running Elsewhere, a Brooklyn live music venue drawing 300,000 annual attendees, and now sits on boards of the NY Hospitality Alliance, North Brooklyn Chamber of Commerce, and BABAR -- representing 5,000+ businesses. That embedded distribution is real and rare at pre-seed. But it is also the tell. Third Space is a founder leveraging a Rolodex to build an insurance brokerage, not a founder leveraging a technical insight to rebuild how risk is priced. When the primary competitive advantage is "I know every bar owner in Brooklyn," you are looking at an execution play, not a technology play. I prefer technology risk to market risk. Third Space has it backwards.

The consequence magnitude question kills this for me before I get to anything else. If Third Space fully succeeds -- wins all of nightlife insurance, then expands across brick-and-mortar -- what changes? Bar owners pay lower premiums. That is a fine outcome for those operators, and the $3M in LOI premiums suggests the pain is acute. But nobody's life is fundamentally different. No basic human need -- healthcare, energy, food, education -- becomes radically cheaper. The $115B TAM claim for brick-and-mortar insurance sounds large until you realize that optimizing brokerage commissions within that market is a share fight, not new demand creation. Contrast this with Impossible Foods making protein without animal agriculture, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems generating limitless clean energy. Third Space's best case is a well-run insurance brokerage worth a few billion dollars. I do not get out of bed for 3-5x returns. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money.

The technical approach confirms the concern. Third Space aggregates camera footage, POS data, HR records, and incident reports, then uses AI to analyze them for risk factors. This is existing AI applied to existing data types through an existing business model -- brokerage commissions on commercial insurance. There is no novel science here, no step-function improvement, no proprietary technical breakthrough. Harper raised $47M and already serves 5,000+ businesses doing horizontal AI brokerage. Casey and Panta are in YC doing variants of the same thing. Third Space's differentiation is vertical specialization, not technology. The "data collective" concept has a nice narrative -- each venue's data improves pricing for all -- but this is a network effect story, not a technology discontinuity. Network effects build defensibility, which is valuable. They do not create the 10x improvement my framework demands.

The strongest bull case requires me to see Third Space through a DoorDash lens: an execution-heavy company where the founder's insider distribution and the consequence of transforming access for a large population override the absence of technical novelty. I funded DoorDash because Tony Xu was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people -- the consequence magnitude was planetary-scale. Third Space is transforming insurance purchasing for nightlife venues. The populations are not comparable. Even extending to all brick-and-mortar, you are making a necessary business expense somewhat cheaper -- not collapsing a cost structure by 10x or making something previously inaccessible available to everyone. Chopra's trade association relationships are genuinely defensible distribution, and Rachakonda's trajectory from SpaceX through CTRL-Labs to Ramp shows a builder who can ship across domains. These are competent founders building a sensible business. But competence and sensibility are not what I fund. I fund improbable ambition with catastrophic upside.

One dimension pulls slightly above baseline: Vinai Rachakonda's resume -- SpaceX flight software, CTRL-Labs neural interface algorithms (acquired by Facebook), founding engineer at DoltHub, senior tech lead at Ramp leading international expansion -- shows a pattern of attacking hard problems across unrelated domains. That is genuine learning velocity. But learning velocity in service of building a better insurance brokerage is a misallocation of talent, not a reason for me to invest. The ambition level here would not provoke incredulity from any informed observer. Nobody says "AI insurance brokerage for nightlife venues -- that can't be done." They say "that's a reasonable niche play." When the world agrees your idea is reasonable, the return profile does not match my framework.

Third Space will likely be a successful company. Chopra's distribution through 5,000+ venue relationships is a real asset, the pain point is validated by 50+ LOIs and 300-500% premium increases documented across the industry, and a16z backed them through Speedrun for good reason. This is the kind of bet that generates solid returns for investors who optimize for probability of success. I optimize for consequence of success, and the consequence here -- cheaper insurance for bars -- is not the kind of outcome that justifies the opportunity cost of my attention. Pass.
