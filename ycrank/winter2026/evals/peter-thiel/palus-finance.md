# Palus Finance -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask is always the same: what is the secret? What important truth about the world does this company see that others don't? Palus Finance's implicit claim is that startups are leaving money on the table because they pay for same-day liquidity on cash they won't touch for months. But this is not a secret -- it is a well-understood fact in institutional finance. Every Fortune 500 treasury team already uses duration-matched bond portfolios instead of money market funds. Every fixed-income analyst at every bank knows that accepting T+1 or T+2 settlement buys you extra basis points. The observation that startups don't optimize their treasury the way corporations do is an insight about distribution -- it's a gap in product accessibility -- but it is emphatically not a contrarian truth that most intelligent people reject. When your "secret" is something any CFO would nod along with, you don't have a secret. You have a market segment you think is underserved.

The deeper problem is structural: this is a company built around a spread. The entire value proposition is 100-150 basis points of incremental yield, delivered through agency MBS that are managed by a third party (Regan Capital's MBSF ETF) and custodied by another third party (Alpaca). Strip away the YC branding and what remains is a distribution wrapper around someone else's investment strategy, using someone else's infrastructure, charging 25 basis points for the convenience. This is the definition of a 1-to-n business -- taking an institutional strategy that already exists and making it available to a smaller customer segment. Nothing is being created that didn't exist before. Contrast this with Stripe, which made seven lines of code do what previously required months of payment integration -- that was a genuine 10x improvement in developer experience. Palus offers your money back in two days instead of one, with a slightly higher yield. That is not 10x. That is a modest tradeoff that many startup founders -- who prize control and optionality with their runway -- may rationally decline.

The competitive landscape confirms the absence of monopoly potential. Mercury, Brex, Rho, Arc, and Meow are all fighting over the same pool of startup cash. This is a Girardian swarm -- multiple players imitating each other's treasury products, each trying to differentiate on yield or fees or UX, none achieving structural dominance. Palus enters this crowded field with a differentiation that is, by their own admission, replicable: agency MBS is a well-understood asset class, Plaid is available to everyone, and Alpaca custody is not exclusive. The dossier suggests an "innovator's dilemma" protects Palus from Mercury, but this misapplies the framework. Mercury could offer an optional bond-portfolio tier alongside its existing MMF product without cannibalizing anything -- it would be an upsell, not a substitution. A company with $650 million in annualized revenue and 200,000+ business customers does not lose sleep over a two-person startup offering marginally higher yields through someone else's ETF.

The founders pivoted from a consumer higher-yield savings product to startup treasury after joining YC -- this is the lean startup methodology in action, and it is exactly the pattern I find least compelling. "We entered YC with one idea and pivoted when we saw demand from batchmates" is the language of indefinite optimism. I look for founders who walk into the room with a specific, non-negotiable vision of what the future must look like -- not founders who discovered their market through customer development within an accelerator. Sam Lushtak's experience building investment research platforms for large asset managers provides domain awareness, but domain awareness and founder conviction are different things. There is no evidence of a worldview here -- no thesis about why the financial system is fundamentally broken, no plan for what Palus becomes in ten years beyond a marginally better yield product.

The strongest bull case would require several things to be true simultaneously: that startup founders are far more yield-sensitive than they appear today, that the data Palus accumulates on cash drawdown patterns creates a genuine algorithmic advantage in portfolio construction, and that this advantage compounds into a moat that prevents Mercury from simply copying the strategy. If all three hold, Palus could become the default treasury optimizer for the YC ecosystem and expand outward, eventually managing billions in startup cash with the kind of switching costs that come from deeply integrated financial infrastructure. The YC beachhead is real -- concentrated, high-trust, and founder-to-founder referrals are the most powerful distribution channel in startup finance. But even in this optimistic scenario, the ceiling is visible. Series B+ companies face venture debt covenants that restrict treasury allocation. The addressable market has a structural cap. And the business model -- 25 basis points on AUM -- generates linear, not exponential, returns. At $1 billion in AUM, which would be an extraordinary outcome, annual revenue is $2.5 million. This is a decent small business, not a power-law investment. It cannot return the fund.

This is a company that solves a real but small problem through incremental improvement in a competitive market with no path to monopoly. Everything about it -- the consensus insight, the crowded landscape, the pivot-driven origin, the commodity technology stack, the linear economics -- is precisely the profile I avoid. Competition is what happens when companies lack a secret, and I see no secret here.
