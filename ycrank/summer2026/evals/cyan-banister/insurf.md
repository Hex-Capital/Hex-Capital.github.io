# Insurf -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Insurf is two co-founders -- Chang Lu and Bryan Chung -- pointing at the $35B prior-authorization administrative tax with an AI appeal generator and a stated "coverage-decision graph," entering a lane where Claimable already has $10M raised, Counterforce Health is grant-funded and free, and Fight Health Insurance, PenguinAI, and Aegis Health are all in the same Stateline write-up from November 2025. The signature of Insurf in my book is a regulated-unsexy category (the one thing pulling me in) paired with the loudest consensus-crowd I've seen in a YC dossier this batch (the thing pushing me back out). That pairing is not a Crusoe. That pairing is the pass example with a HIPAA wrapper.

Anduril is the rung I want any healthcare appeals founder to clear: Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus before he ever touched defense, so when he walked into the Pentagon he had artifact in hand and biography on his sleeve. The load-bearing piece of that analogy is not "hardware" -- it is that Luckey was *of* the problem before the company existed. Insurf's dossier gives me Chang Lu's calendly link and Bryan Chung's LinkedIn URL and nothing else -- no prior billing-ops job, no denied-claim story, no specialty-practice founder, no payer-side stint, no GitHub. I am not penalizing pre-seed for thin metrics; I am penalizing Insurf for the absence of the one thing pre-seed founders *can* show me for free, which is a personal moment of contact with the denial-letter pile. When a healthcare-claims founder's biography is blank, my read is they discovered prior-auth in a memo, not at a fax machine.

The pass example -- the consensus YC AI-agent darling -- is the rung I keep flashing back to, and Insurf sits one floor above it only because the regulatory surface is real. The mechanism that killed my interest in the pass example was that six funds were already converging; the mechanism here is that the *category* has already converged before the round. Claimable at $10M with Mark Cuban, Counterforce backed by PennAITech grant capital, Tennr at a $605M valuation in an adjacent lane -- this is not the Crusoe table where two or three credible VCs passed on aesthetic grounds. This is the table where everyone showed up early and the differentiation Insurf is selling, the coverage-decision graph, is a slide claim with no disclosed pilot count, no claim volume, no payer-behavior dataset, and no measured predictive lift. "Find them before it becomes consensus" -- the AI-vs-AI-appeals headline was on PBS NewsHour and ProPublica before Insurf's Demo Day.

Mechanism over 18 months on the moat: Insurf's stated flywheel is that Inveto appeals populate the coverage-decision graph that powers Surely's plan-cost pricing. Two-product sequencing is hard at $0 revenue. To get graph density that out-predicts a Claimable or a payer-side LLM, Insurf needs claim volume that requires distribution that requires a wedge product to already be working -- and the dossier reports the Access Recovery pilot is *recruiting*, not running. Eighteen months of this compounds the wrong direction if Tennr decides to bolt appeals onto its referral parser using the $101M Series C, which is the exact incumbent-eats-adjacency move I'd expect from an IVP-backed company at $605M.

Counterfactual on governance: Insurf has two co-founders and the dossier names no CEO. The calendly link reads chang-insurf/30min, which is a thread, not a tiebreaker. For this dimension to flip a tier in Insurf's favor, I'd need to see one of them carrying the CEO title explicitly with the other on a defined CTO or Head of Product track, ideally with vesting and exit-conflict language already drafted -- the structural inverse of the Yusupov/Kroll situation at HQ Trivia. Until then, this is two YC kids who haven't had the hard conversation, and the hard conversation in a sales-led B2B healthcare company arrives the first time a payer threatens a HIPAA action.

Flock Safety is the single-anchor parallel for verifiability, and it is unkind to Insurf. Flock I found because a founder's Wi-Fi network name floated up on my laptop at a Four Seasons cafe -- the product was in the air, literally. Insurf I cannot touch. There is no Chrome extension, no Product Hunt page, no patient-facing front end I can put my own denial letter into, no clinic I can walk into and watch the workflow. The dossier reports the GTM is a calendly funnel for specialty practices. That is the developer-tools-abstraction failure mode from my pass example, dressed in scrubs.

The 36-month forecast: Insurf is either dead or acqui-hired for under $25M into a larger RCM stack -- Waystar, Availity, or Tennr extending into appeals -- after Inveto fails to outpace Claimable's patient-funded growth and Surely never reaches the data density to price plans against actuaries who already do this for a living. The mechanism is the one I named above: a two-product flywheel needs a first-product wedge that Insurf has not demonstrated, in a category where five funded competitors are racing the same wedge, with founders whose lived contact with the problem the dossier cannot confirm.

The strongest case against my forecast runs through HandUp. HandUp violated my capital-defensibility filter and I wrote the check because the founder's biography mirrored my own homelessness origin -- mission alignment overrode commercial calculus. If Chang Lu or Bryan Chung lived through a parent's cancer-denial appeal, or worked the billing window at a specialty oncology clinic for three years, that is the override that would flip Insurf from pass to invest regardless of the Claimable cap table. The dossier specifically reports founder background as "no public data found" and LinkedIn as blocked. I would flip my verdict on Insurf the day one of the founders shows me a denial letter with their family member's name on it and explains what they did about it before the company existed. Until then, this is a memo founder in a consensus category, and my check stays in the drawer.
