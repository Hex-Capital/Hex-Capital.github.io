# Prism -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Juicebox AI closed an $80M Series B at $850M valuation in March 2026. Moonhub was acquired by Salesforce in June 2025. Dover has raised $23M. Over $208M poured into recruiting tech in the first eleven months of 2025 alone. Prism walks into this market — an AI-native recruiting agency screening inbound applications with LLMs — and the first thing I notice is that nobody would shift in their chair hearing this pitch. AI recruiting is the opposite of a hard space. It's a gold rush. When I backed Anduril, VCs in San Francisco wouldn't touch defense tech because it was politically radioactive. When I wrote my SpaceX check, rockets were blowing up. Prism is entering a category where investors are falling over each other to write checks. That's not contrarian conviction — that's consensus chasing, and consensus chasing with commodity technology (LLM-based resume screening that any foundation model can power) is the exact pattern Lee and I have warned about: "not some B.S. SaaS company or AI wrapper."

Theo Kitsberg studied Philosophy with Psychology at Cambridge and did HCI research at MIT Media Lab. Axel La Pira is a third-year Computer Science and Philosophy student at Oxford who previously served as CEO Founder Associate at Alan. These are genuinely impressive academic credentials — and Eoghan McCabe backing Axel early is a real signal of talent recognition. But when I ask my question — tell me your story, not the business — I don't hear a biographical forge. I hear "I was at a great university and saw a market opportunity." Theo's prior experience with recruiting was "supporting recruiting for critical roles at Nightline Association," which is tangential exposure, not the deep wound of someone who watched the recruiting system fail people they cared about. Axel is still an undergraduate. Neither founder has spent years inside recruiting watching it break. Prism's founding story reads like career optimization — smart people who identified a large market — rather than biographical inevitability. The founders who I've backed at their best, the ones who survived the valley of death at Crusoe or kept building Uber when 300 investors said no, were people whose life stories made them incapable of working on anything else.

Prism rebranded from HireGlide mid-batch — the domain hireglide.ai now 308-redirects to tryprism.com, and the positioning shifted from "AI-native job board" to "AI-native recruiting agency." That mid-flight pivot signals the product scope and market positioning are still being discovered, which is normal at pre-seed, but it compounds my concern about founder-market fit. Recruiting is a relationship-heavy, trust-dependent industry where insider knowledge of workflows, compliance requirements, and buyer psychology drives product-market fit. Prism's founders bring interdisciplinary philosophy-and-CS backgrounds that could produce elegant human-computer interaction — but elegance without domain depth is a feature, not a moat. The dossier explicitly states: "No defensibility signals found in public sources at this stage." And the commoditization risk is real: AI resume screening can be built on commodity foundation models, and software incumbents like Lever, Greenhouse, and Workday face no structural barrier to adding this capability as a feature toggle.

Let me steelman Prism as hard as I can, because that's the discipline. The "agency" framing is strategically interesting — Prism isn't selling software seats to recruiters, it's replacing the entire recruiting agency's human labor model. Traditional agencies charge 20-25% of first-year salary; an AI-native agency that dramatically undercuts that fee while maintaining quality would cannibalize incumbents who can't match the price without destroying their own margins. Moonhub's Salesforce acquisition proves exits exist. The $1.3 trillion global recruiting spend is genuinely enormous. And pre-seed is early — moats emerge later, and YC with Tom Blomfield provides real distribution into the startup ecosystem. If Prism accumulates structured screening data across thousands of job descriptions and builds a proprietary candidate network, a data flywheel could emerge. I've checked my known exceptions: HQ Trivia's cultural timing override doesn't apply here (no cultural moment), RTFKT's narrow inflection window doesn't apply (no timing scarcity), and Superplastic's genre-defying weirdness doesn't apply (nothing weird about AI recruiting). None of my exception patterns rescue this.

Prism targets a market that is already transparent — everyone knows recruiters charge 20-25%, everyone knows ATS keyword filters are crude. There's no hidden tax being revealed, no opaque structure exploiting information asymmetry against people who don't understand it. Compare this to Affirm, which exposed the actual cost of credit that card issuers deliberately obscured, or Ownwell, which automated property tax appeals against a system designed to be too complex for ordinary homeowners to challenge. Prism makes hiring faster and cheaper — that's efficiency, not transparency. The people hiring already understand the system; they just find it slow and expensive. When I was fifteen and navigating systems that had power over me, the danger wasn't slowness — it was opacity. Prism doesn't attack opacity.

The condition that would flip me: if Theo or Axel came to me and told me a story about watching someone they loved get systematically excluded from opportunity by recruiting's hidden filtering — a parent screened out by keyword algorithms despite being the best candidate, a community where talented people never got past the ATS — and they had built Prism's screening model on proprietary data that no competitor could replicate, with evidence that their matching accuracy was measurably superior to Juicebox or Dover, I'd reconsider. The biographical forge and the defensibility signal would need to arrive together. Right now, Prism scores lowest on contrarian conviction (this is a consensus gold rush, not a hard space) and founder biographical forge (credentials without personal stakes in the problem). Those two dimensions together account for 55 of my 100 points, and Prism doesn't register on either.
