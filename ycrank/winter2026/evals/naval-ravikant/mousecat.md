# MouseCat -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founder-specific-knowledge combination here is the most interesting signal. Aldridge isn't just "ex-AWS" -- he's a core maintainer of the Model Context Protocol, the emerging standard for how AI agents interact with tools and data sources. That's protocol-level knowledge. When you co-author the grammar that agents use to speak to the world, you understand the design space of agent applications at a depth that can't be replicated by reading documentation. It's analogous to building the first web application when you helped design HTTP -- you know where the protocol bends and where it breaks. McAllister complements this with production fraud ML from Coinbase -- not theoretical, but actual ATO and ACH risk models running against real adversarial fraud patterns, plus a prior exit. One founder understands how to build agents at the infrastructure layer; the other understands what those agents need to do in the fraud domain. That pairing is genuinely non-obvious and hard to assemble.

But when I run the leverage test, the picture gets more complicated. MouseCat's core promise -- closing the loop from investigation to production rules -- is real leverage for an individual fraud analyst. One person generating production-ready models without waiting for a separate ML team is a step-function improvement. That's individual empowerment, which I care about. Yet it's leverage *within* an existing workflow, not the creation of a new category of capability. Uber didn't make taxi dispatchers more efficient -- it made the dispatcher unnecessary. MouseCat makes fraud analysts faster. The difference matters for the scale of the outcome. The "toolkit" positioning is strategically wise (lower friction than platform replacement), but it also implicitly caps the ambition: you're augmenting the current system rather than reimagining it.

The contrarian timing test is where this falls short for me. AI-powered fraud detection is not a secret. It's a $14.7 billion market growing at 18% CAGR with five well-funded competitors who have collectively raised over $700 million. Sardine is growing ARR 130% year-over-year. Feedzai processes 70 billion events annually. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was *illegal*. When I backed Bitcoin, it was dismissed as nerd money. When I look at MouseCat, I see a market where every smart person already agrees AI will transform fraud detection -- the only question is who executes best. That's an execution race, not a contrarian bet. And execution races against well-capitalized incumbents are exactly the kind of bet where your losses are bounded but so is your upside.

The bull case I take seriously: if MCP becomes the dominant agent protocol (which is plausible -- it has Anthropic's backing and growing adoption), being a core maintainer gives MouseCat a structural advantage in building agent-native applications that incumbents, bolting AI onto legacy architectures, can't easily replicate. The agent-native vs. AI-augmented distinction could be the same gap that separated cloud-native SaaS from on-prem software with a web UI. If that's true, Sardine's $145M and Feedzai's $347M are actually liabilities -- they're invested in architectures that predate the agent paradigm. MouseCat, built agent-native from day one, would have the architectural advantage that matters most. For this to work, the team needs to land a few early customers, build proprietary feedback loops from investigation data, and demonstrate that agent-native investigation is qualitatively different from AI-as-feature. The 2nd-highest rating at YC's W26 showcase suggests the demo is compelling. If they can convert demo energy into enterprise contracts before incumbents ship comparable agent features, there's a path to a meaningful outcome.

The honest assessment: strong founders with genuine specific knowledge, building in a real market, with a credible technical edge. But the market is consensus, the competition is well-funded, and I don't see the leverage architecture that produces the asymmetric outcome I optimize for. This is the kind of company where competent execution yields a solid outcome -- 3-5x -- but the path to 100x requires the incumbents to be structurally incapable of adapting, and I'm not convinced of that. The fraud infrastructure stack is evolving, not being reinvented. I'd want to see evidence that agent-native investigation produces qualitatively different results -- not just faster, but categorically better -- before I'd move from interested to convicted.
