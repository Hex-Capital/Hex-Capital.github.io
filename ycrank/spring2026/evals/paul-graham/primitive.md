# primitive -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that I already wrote a check to AgentMail. They're YC S25, they've got $6M from General Catalyst, 500+ B2B customers, and their user counts were quadrupling in February. And now I'm looking at a solo founder entering the exact same space — email infrastructure for AI agents — nine months later, with no team, no users, and no public product. I've seen late entrants win before; Stripe launched years after PayPal. But Stripe's insight was categorically different from PayPal's approach. The distinction here — webhook-first routing versus mailbox-centric management — is a real architectural choice, but I'm not convinced it's the kind of fundamental divergence that justifies entering a market where the incumbent already has traction and capital. When I backed Stripe, nobody else was treating payments as a developer API. When I look at Primitive, someone else is already treating agent email as a developer API.

The solo founder problem is hard to get past. I've said this many times: starting a startup is too hard for one person. Not because the work is too much — though it is — but because you need someone to talk you out of stupid decisions and to keep going when everything feels broken. Ethan Byrd has the resume of someone who can build things. Google, Meta, AWS, Microsoft — that's exposure to communication infrastructure at enormous scale. And he founded Actual AI, raised $3.2M, got to 32 active pilots. That's real. But Actual AI raises more questions than it answers. What happened to those 32 pilots? Where's the co-founder, John Kennedy? Why does Byrd's LinkedIn still say "Founder & CTO @ Actual AI"? If Primitive grew out of a genuine insight from building Actual AI — that the communication layer was the hardest part of agent infrastructure — that would be a compelling origin story. But I don't have that story. I have an inference.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If you squint, this has the shape of a good bet: a technical founder who spent years building AI agents at his own company, discovered that agent communication was the unsolved infrastructure problem, and decided to build the primitive (lowercase) that was missing. The webhook-first architecture is arguably more developer-native than provisioning mailbox identities — it treats email the way Stripe treated payments, as a pure API surface rather than a product with a UI. And the "why now" is real: agent proliferation is creating genuine demand for communication primitives. If Byrd had a co-founder, a working product with even a handful of obsessive early users, and a clear narrative about what he learned at Actual AI that led him here, I'd be substantially more interested. That combination would signal organic discovery plus builder obsession — the two things I weight most heavily.

But I don't have those signals. What I have is a strong resume, which is the least predictive thing I evaluate. I've seen hundreds of founders from Google and Meta and AWS. The ones who succeed at startups aren't the ones with the most impressive titles — they're the ones with the most compelling stories about hacking around obstacles. I don't have a single such story for Byrd. His GitHub has 15 repos, mostly forks. His Twitter has 305 followers. There's no evidence of the obsessive builder identity — the person who was shipping things at 2am before anyone was paying them to, who built the early version because they couldn't not build it. Maybe that person is there and the dossier just doesn't capture it. But truly resourceful founders tend to leave footprints.

The deeper problem is that "email for agents" doesn't pass the contrarian test. When AgentMail's user counts quadruple in a month, when Gartner is projecting 40% of enterprise apps will feature AI agents, when Google is launching agent interoperability protocols — this is a consensus idea. Everyone agrees it's needed. That means the returns are being competed away in real time. The best startup ideas make smart people say "that will never work." Nobody says that about agent communication infrastructure in 2026. They say "obviously, yes, agents need email." The space is already crowded enough that I'm looking at my second YC company in this category. That's not a sign of untapped opportunity — it's a sign that the opportunity is thoroughly tapped.

I'd pass. Byrd might be exactly the right person to build this, but I'd need to see him do it — ship a product, get those first ten obsessive users, maybe find a co-founder who complements his infrastructure background. The idea is sound but obvious, the competitive position is weak, and the founder signal, while credentialed, is undifferentiated from dozens of smart ex-FAANG engineers I've met who want to start infrastructure companies. Show me the animal quality, and we'll talk.
