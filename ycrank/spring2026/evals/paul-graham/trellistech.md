# Trellis -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Lodovico Benvenuti previously worked at Conduit, a YC W24 company building AI guest messaging for short-term rentals. Jan Sahagun previously worked at Boom, an AI-first PMS competitor that has raised $12.7M. Both founders left direct competitors in the exact same market to start Trellis. This is the most analytically important fact in the dossier, and it cuts in two directions at once. On one hand, it means Trellis's founders have seen the problem from inside the machine — they know where the existing products fail, what operators actually need, and which architectural decisions constrain the incumbents. On the other hand, when both co-founders come from companies doing essentially the same thing, I have to ask: what's the non-obvious insight? What did they learn that their former employers can't learn? The usual power of founder provenance is that someone who worked deep inside one domain brings knowledge into a different domain where nobody has it. But Trellis's founders brought STR knowledge into... STR. That's not schlep blindness. That's schlep familiarity.

Lodovico's operator background is the strongest signal in this dossier. He scaled a property management company to approximately $2M in revenue across 150+ listings. That means he personally experienced the chaos of coordinating cleaners, fielding guest complaints across Airbnb and VRBO, juggling five to ten disconnected tools, and watching his operation buckle under complexity. When Trellis claims that managers get "stuck between 10 and 50 listings," Lodovico lived that ceiling and pushed past it. This is the kind of organic problem discovery I trust — not someone who read a market report about vacation rental software being an $8.6 billion market, but someone who was drowning in the operational mess and started building because they had to. It reminds me, faintly, of how Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive — except Lodovico's frustration was messier and more operationally complex. Jan's path is different: sales roles at Besty AI, Guestway, and Boom, plus founding STR Tech Report as a media platform. That's valuable for distribution — having a media property that reaches your target buyer is a genuine GTM advantage — but it's a career in the STR vendor ecosystem, not a problem-discovery story.

Trellis claims it reached feature parity with competitors in two weeks and positions itself as "AI that builds your software" rather than traditional SaaS with AI bolted on. The company describes a cross-operational data network where resolution patterns and SOPs accumulate across customers. These are interesting architectural claims, but they contain a self-defeating argument that I find troubling. If Trellis can replicate competitor features in two weeks, what prevents Guesty ($409M raised, $900M valuation) or Hostaway ($543M raised, first STR PMS unicorn) from building an equivalent AI agent layer on top of their existing customer bases? Trellis's own logic — "software is being commoditized" — means their product is also commoditizable. The aspirational network effects don't exist yet. The 200+ integrations create some switching cost, but integrations are table stakes in this market; Guesty and Hostaway have them too. The 84.5% AI automation rate Trellis claims from a dashboard screenshot is interesting if verifiable, but a single property management company running "with 1 person" is a proof of concept, not a moat.

The competitive landscape is what concerns me most about Trellis. When I backed Stripe, Patrick and John Collison were tackling payment infrastructure — a domain so terrifying in its regulatory and compliance complexity that virtually no one else was willing to touch it. That disgust reaction from other founders was the moat. With Trellis, I see the opposite: Guesty has raised $409M, Hostaway $543M, Boom $12.7M, Conduit $3.1M — all building AI-powered STR operations tools. Nobody looks at this space and says "that will never work." Everyone looks at it and says "obviously AI should automate short-term rental operations." That's exactly the consensus I warn against in "Black Swan Farming." The best investments are the ones where knowledgeable people's first reaction is skepticism. Trellis's pitch generates immediate nodding, and that's a problem. The returns in a space where four well-funded competitors are already executing the same thesis have already been competed away — or at minimum, Trellis needs a dramatically better team or insight to overcome the capital disadvantage.

Trellis's founding team has no visible GitHub presence, which limits my ability to evaluate technical building capacity. Lodovico built AI agents at Conduit, so he can presumably build, but "presumably" is doing more work than I'd like. The team is six people, relatively large for a pre-seed YC company with one customer. I see no evidence of the obsessive, slightly-too-intense building behavior I look for — no weekend prototypes shared publicly, no technical blog posts, no open-source contributions that would signal the "hacker building for hackers" pattern I instinctively favor. Trellis is building for property managers, not for developers, which is fine — ClassDojo proved that my sector preference isn't load-bearing when execution quality is exceptional and the schlep is real. But ClassDojo was attacking K-12 education, where the procurement complexity actively repelled other startups. STR operations software does not repel anyone. The learning rate signal is moderate: Lodovico's trajectory from operator to Conduit to Trellis shows he's iterating on his understanding of the problem, and the two-week feature parity claim suggests fast execution. But I have no weekly growth metrics, no pivot stories, no evidence of the compounding improvement curve I use as a proxy.

The strongest bull case for Trellis is this: Lodovico is a rare dual-threat founder who has operated 150+ listings AND built AI agents for the same market at a YC company, Jan's STR Tech Report provides organic distribution into the target buyer persona, and their "AI-native from scratch" architecture could be structurally superior to incumbents bolting AI onto legacy SaaS codebases — the way Figma didn't add collaboration to Sketch but rebuilt the category around it. If the 84.5% automation rate is real and the one-person operation story scales, Trellis could be ten times better than Guesty at the operational layer, and that kind of product quality gap can overcome capital disadvantage. I take this bull case seriously. But the condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: I'd need to see evidence that Trellis's AI orchestration produces outcomes that incumbent AI features cannot replicate — a specific workflow, a measurable quality gap, a class of operational decisions that requires their cross-system context architecture. "We're AI-native" is a claim. "We reduced guest complaint resolution time from 4 hours to 12 minutes because our agent simultaneously accesses booking history, cleaning schedules, and maintenance logs" is evidence. Right now, Trellis has the claim but not the evidence.

The dimension that most decisively shapes my verdict on Trellis is Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — and it scores low. This is a consensus-good idea in a space where nearly a billion dollars of venture capital is already deployed. Lodovico's organic problem discovery is genuine and scores well, but it's insufficient on its own to overcome the competitive reality. Trellis needs to be right about something that Guesty, Hostaway, Boom, and Conduit are all wrong about, and I don't yet see what that is.
