# Auto -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Dave Evans built PlayCanvas and sold it to Snap. Sam Hare got a PhD in computer vision, built Seene's mobile 3D reconstruction, and sold that to Snap too. Then they both spent years inside Snap leading camera and AR product engineering — Evans ran Mini-Programs, Games, and SnapKit; Hare ran the Lens Experience team. These are two of the most qualified people on the planet to build a camera product. And they're using that expertise to build... an AI camera app that calls the same multimodal APIs every developer on earth has access to. That's the tension with Auto. The founders are A+. The structural position is terrible.

My first question is always about pricing: is the market wrong? With Auto, the market is emphatically not wrong. AI is the most consensus category in venture capital right now. Google Lens processes 12 billion visual searches a month — up 250% since 2023. Apple shipped Visual Intelligence directly into the OS on iPhone 16. OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are all making the underlying vision models better and cheaper every quarter. Every sophisticated investor on the planet agrees that camera-plus-AI is valuable. The disagreement is about who captures that value. And every time I look at an AI application-layer startup competing against incumbents with distribution measured in billions of users, I know the answer: the value accrues to the platform. Auto is in the a16z Speedrun batch, not some overlooked corner of the market — this is a well-credentialed team in the hottest category, which means the risk-reward asymmetry runs the wrong direction for my framework.

The "Frame" concept — turning each photo into a persistent mini-app rather than returning a one-shot answer — is clever product thinking. But is it a new primitive? Compare it to Venmo, which created a behavior that became a verb. Compare it to Solana, which created a new consensus mechanism at the protocol layer. A Frame is a UX presentation pattern on top of commodity multimodal AI. Google could ship this as a Lens feature in a single product cycle. Apple could expand Visual Intelligence to include persistent task workflows without breaking a sweat. Evans literally built Mini-Programs at Snap — and Snap Mini-Programs, despite having 750 million monthly users as a distribution base, never became a dominant platform. Why would the same concept work as a standalone app with five App Store ratings and zero press coverage? The user-generated Frame ecosystem could theoretically create switching costs, but that requires a scale of adoption that has no supporting evidence in the dossier.

The AI kill test destroys Auto. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The underlying multimodal AI models are available via API to any developer. Building a 'photo → structured action' pipeline is achievable by well-funded competitors." Apple Visual Intelligence is a built-in competitor with zero distribution cost and zero marginal user acquisition expense. Google Lens has 1.5 billion monthly active users. Auto's moat depends on "execution speed, Frame ecosystem network effects, and UX quality" — which is another way of saying there is no moat. This is the exact scenario I describe when I say AI is sustaining technology: it's perfect for Apple, perfect for Google, and a death sentence for the standalone app competing against them. Compare Auto to Metropolis, which I seeded — Metropolis manages physical parking infrastructure that no API call can replicate. That's structural durability. An iOS app calling GPT-4V is the opposite.

The strongest bull case for Auto: Evans and Hare know something about camera UX that Google and Apple's product teams don't, and the Frame abstraction creates a new behavioral loop — snap, interact, return — that turns Auto into a platform rather than an app. If community-created Frames generate genuine network effects, Auto could become the "App Store inside your camera," a meta-platform that incumbents can't replicate because they're optimizing for different objectives (Google for ad-linked search results, Apple for hardware attachment). That would be a real primitive. But there's zero evidence this flywheel is spinning — five ratings, stealth mode, no download data, no Frame creation volume. The founders have the credibility to build it, and the $9.99/month subscription shows revenue model discipline. But credibility in a consensus-hot category with no structural moat is not a bet I make with my own money.

What locked this for me: three of my explicit red flags fire simultaneously. Auto is positioned as an AI company where the AI is the product, not a feature. It's a better version of existing products (Google Lens, Apple Visual Intelligence) rather than a new category. And it's pure software with no network effects, physical moats, or protocol-level lock-in at current scale. Evans and Hare are genuinely excellent — sane, accomplished, calm engineers with exits — but the idea isn't insane. It's obvious. Every product leader at every major tech company is thinking about this exact problem. Sane person, sane idea is just a well-run company in a market that eats well-run companies for breakfast.
