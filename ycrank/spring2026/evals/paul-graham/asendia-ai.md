# Asendia AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Asendia AI is that it's a different company than it was a year ago. In May 2025 they were launching "Sarah, your AI Job Coach" on Product Hunt -- a B2C interview prep tool for job seekers. Now they're positioning as a B2B agentic recruitment platform for staffing agencies. These are fundamentally different products for fundamentally different customers. The 286 Product Hunt upvotes, the 15K minutes of AI interview prep, the 53.5K website visits -- those belong to a product they've walked away from. When I evaluate what they're actually bringing to YC in Spring 2026, the traction cupboard is essentially bare.

The question I always ask first is: how did the founders find this problem? Badis built FAANG AI, an interview prep tool, which suggests he personally knew the pain of preparing for tech company interviews. That's organic -- an electrical engineer from TUM wanting to get into big tech companies. I can believe that. But the pivot to selling to staffing agencies is a different story. Neither founder has ever worked at a staffing agency. Neither has run a recruiting desk. The idea of "AI agents for the recruitment funnel" reads like something you'd arrive at by looking at where enterprise budgets are going, not something you'd stumble into because you lived with the problem. And "AI for recruiting" is about as consensus as it gets right now -- hireEZ has raised $76M, Humanly $24M, Fetcher $40M, and Paradox raised over $250M before Workday acquired them. When every VC in the Valley is funding your category, the upside has already been priced in.

What's also notable is the breadth of what they're claiming to build. Four distinct AI agents -- sourcing, SMS outreach, video interviewing, phone screening -- plus 50+ ATS integrations. With three people. The best startups start deliberately narrow. Facebook was Harvard only. Stripe was developers only. This is the opposite instinct: build a feature checklist that looks comprehensive on a website, rather than doing one thing so well that users can't live without it. When a pre-seed company advertises SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA compliance alongside four AI agents and 50+ integrations, I start wondering whether the product page reflects what actually works versus what the team aspires to build.

The strongest bull case here is the timing created by Paradox's acquisition. Workday bought the largest independent conversational AI recruiting platform in August 2025, which genuinely creates a gap for standalone tools. Staffing agencies that don't use Workday need an alternative. If Asendia were founded by someone who'd spent a decade running recruiting operations at a mid-market staffing firm -- someone who knew exactly which workflows break when your AI vendor gets absorbed into an HCM suite -- this could be a compelling entry point. The CTO has built AI interview technology before, so the team isn't starting from zero technically. And the co-founders have worked together since 2019 on their podcast, which at least means the relationship predates the company. If they're deeply embedded with five or six early agency customers right now, doing things that don't scale and learning the actual operational pain points, this could work. But there's no evidence of that happening.

What I see instead is a pattern I've described before: smart people who decided to start a company and went looking for a good category. Cloud architects and electrical engineers from Google, Microsoft, and Infineon who built something adjacent in B2C, realized the money was in B2B, and aimed at "staffing agencies" because the TAM slide looks good. The schlep in recruiting is real -- compliance, multi-database sourcing, phone screening at scale -- but it's not a schlep that's hiding in plain sight. Everyone can see it. That's why there's hundreds of millions of dollars already chasing it. The founders might prove me wrong through sheer execution, but without domain-specific insight into staffing agency operations, they're competing on features against better-funded teams who also have access to the same foundation model APIs.
