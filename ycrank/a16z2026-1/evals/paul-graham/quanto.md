# Quanto -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Anderson Petergeorge did the thing I always tell founders to do and almost nobody actually does: he performed the service by hand before building the software. He scaled a bookkeeping practice to 40 clients in 6 months as a solo CPA, hit the capacity wall, and then built Quanto to break through it. That's not "I identified a market opportunity." That's "I was drowning in QuickBooks reconciliations at midnight and wrote code to make it stop." Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Anderson built Quanto because he couldn't onboard his 41st client without cloning himself. The origin story has the right shape — frustration first, product second. That matters more than most investors realize.

The founder-market fit here is unusually deep on both sides. Anderson is a CPA with a decade across KPMG, CPP Investments, and UNDP — he doesn't just understand the accounting firm buyer, he *is* the buyer. Kajanth Nithiyananthan ran a 7-figure product studio called Atomic Build that specifically built AI tools for accounting startups and firms before co-founding Quanto. So you have a CPA who coded his way out of a capacity problem paired with a product leader who was already deep in the accounting-AI stack through his studio work. Neither founder discovered this problem through a brainstorming session. They converged on it from opposite directions — domain and technology — which is exactly the founding dynamic that produces products with both technical depth and user empathy. The $800K contracted ARR with six people confirms they're building something firms actually want, not something that sounds good in a pitch.

Quanto occupies a schlep-rich zone that should make it structurally defensible even against much better-funded competitors. Chart of accounts cleanup, bank rule configuration, month-end close — this is the accounting equivalent of Stripe handling PCI compliance and bank integrations. Most AI founders would rather build another chatbot than wire up QuickBooks diagnostics and CRM integrations for mid-market accounting firms. That disgust reaction is a moat. Basis raised $138M to serve the top 25 firms with end-to-end audit agents — they're playing upmarket. Digits raised $95M to replace the general ledger entirely — different product category. Quanto layers onto existing QuickBooks workflows without asking firms to rip anything out, which is exactly the right wedge for mid-market firms that are too small for Basis and too entrenched in QuickBooks for Digits. Botkeeper shutting down in 2026 hands Quanto a pool of displaced customers on a silver platter.

Where Quanto doesn't pattern-match to my typical investments: this isn't hackers building for hackers. Neither founder has public GitHub repos. The CTO's background is product management at SkipTheDishes and Shopify, not deep systems engineering. My portfolio skews heavily toward developer tools — Stripe, Replit, Retool — built by programmers for programmers. Quanto is operators building for operators. But this is the same exception I made with ClassDojo, which tackled K-12 classroom management — an equally unsexy domain with equally painful sales cycles — and reached unicorn status. When the schlep blindness is this extreme and the founders' execution quality is this high, my sector preference should yield. The real question is whether $800K ARR and a $5M pipeline convert into the 5-7% weekly growth that separates startups from small businesses. Anderson's trajectory — 40 clients in six months as a solo CPA, then $800K ARR as a 6-person startup — suggests compounding velocity, but I'd want to see the weekly curve before writing a larger check.

The most thesis-specific risk is platform dependency. Quanto's core diagnostics and onboarding automation are built on QuickBooks integration, which means Intuit holds the oxygen valve. If Intuit decides to build native AI onboarding tools — and they have the AI investments to do it — Quanto's integration layer becomes a liability instead of an asset. The counter-argument is that Intuit sells to SMBs, not to accounting firms, and building AI-powered prospecting tools for firms would cannibalize their professional services partner channel. That structural conflict buys Quanto time. But time is what they need most, because Basis at $1.15B valuation could decide mid-market is worth pursuing tomorrow. The evidence that would flip me from Invest to Pass: if Quanto's $800K contracted ARR is primarily pilot commitments that haven't converted to recurring revenue, or if the pipeline-to-close conversion rate stays below 20%.

Quanto earns an Invest verdict primarily on two dimensions: organic problem discovery — Anderson running his own practice before building the product is the clearest "live in the future, then build what's missing" signal I've seen in this batch — and founder resourcefulness demonstrated through $800K ARR at pre-seed scale with a tiny team. The idea isn't contrarian enough to score high on the Black Swan test, and the competitive landscape against Basis and Digits is genuinely threatening, but the founders' relationship to the problem is authentic in a way that manufactured market-research-driven startups can never replicate. I'd write a check at this stage and watch the weekly numbers.
