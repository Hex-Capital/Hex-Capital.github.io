# RunAnywhere -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about RunAnywhere is that it's easy to understand. "Ollama but for mobile, with a cloud fallback." That clarity is a double-edged sword. The companies I've written my best checks into — Crusoe mining Bitcoin on gas flares, Contraline developing a male contraceptive gel, SpaceX when everyone thought Elon was lighting money on fire — those were hard to explain and harder to assess. RunAnywhere is a multi-runtime SDK with a control plane for on-device AI. It's well-architected infrastructure in the hottest investment category on earth right now. EdgeRunner has $17.5 million. Nexa AI has $16.5 million. Edge Impulse got acquired by Qualcomm. This is not an unpopular idea that the market has dismissed. This is a category with a $25 billion TAM estimate and a 24% growth rate where well-funded competitors are already operating. When every VC in town can see the opportunity clearly, the weirdness has been arbitraged away, and that's where my signal disappears.

I want to talk about Sanchit, because the five YC applications before acceptance — that's the kind of persistence that catches my attention. Growing up in Talwandi Bhai, Punjab, making it to RIT, shipping SDKs at Intuit that ran on millions of devices, getting rejected four times and coming back a fifth — I know what it feels like to hear "no" over and over. I pitched Zivity fifty-plus times before Luke Nosek said yes. So the grit is real, and I respect it deeply. But when I ask myself the question I always ask — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I'm listening for is the biographical thread that makes this founder the only person who could solve this particular problem. Sanchit built SDKs at Intuit. Shubham built distributed systems at AWS. Those are excellent professional credentials. But I don't hear the story of a specific pain that burned so deeply it became a mission. I hear two skilled engineers who identified a real gap in developer tooling and built a clean solution for it. That's competence. It's not the same as biographical inevitability.

The strongest bull case here is structural, and it's genuinely sharp: Google, Meta, and Microsoft each maintain the underlying runtimes (TensorFlow Lite, ExecuTorch, ONNX Runtime) but they monetize through cloud inference. Building a control plane that routes traffic away from the cloud and onto the device would cannibalize their own revenue. That's a real business model conflict, and it's the kind of structural opening that creates room for a startup to grow before the incumbents can respond. If RunAnywhere becomes the default operational layer for on-device AI — the way Twilio became the default for communications APIs — the switching costs compound across every platform integration. The 10.2K GitHub stars, if genuine, suggest real developer interest. The timing is right: quantized small language models just crossed the viability threshold for mobile hardware in the last eighteen months. Someone will own this layer. The question is whether RunAnywhere can convert open-source adoption into paid enterprise relationships before a better-funded competitor does it first, and the dossier gives me no revenue signal at all — not even an LOI or a design partner named.

I also notice what this company doesn't do: it doesn't create economic access for people who lack it. Uber gave drivers income. Postmates gave delivery workers income. Affirm gave consumers transparent credit. RunAnywhere makes it easier for developers to deploy AI models on phones. That's useful — privacy-preserving AI has social value, and I won't dismiss it. But my portfolio pattern is investing in companies that make invisible systems visible or create opportunity for underserved populations. Developer tooling for well-resourced engineering teams doesn't activate that part of my framework.

The co-founder dynamic is actually the brightest signal for me. Six to seven years of trust since RIT, complementary skill sets — one built the mobile SDK layer at Intuit, the other built cloud infrastructure at AWS — and they're a two-person team who clearly know how to work together. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I always probe co-founder alignment, and this pairing looks solid. They found each other's complementary strengths before they started the company, which is the right order of operations.

Ultimately, RunAnywhere is a well-built company by competent engineers in a consensus market. If I were a thesis-agnostic seed investor optimizing for risk-adjusted returns in enterprise infrastructure, this would be interesting. But I'm looking for misfits building things that make other investors uncomfortable. I'm looking for founders whose life story and their company's mission are the same story. RunAnywhere is a good answer to a well-understood problem, and that's precisely why it's not my bet to make.
