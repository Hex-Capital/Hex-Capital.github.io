# Kuli -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

CreatorIQ has raised $80.8 million, generates $70.7 million in revenue, and already uses AI for database curation. GRIN has raised $145 million. These are not sleepy incumbents waiting to be disrupted — they are well-funded platforms with deep enterprise relationships, and the core capability Kuli is building on top of, multimodal video understanding, is rapidly commoditizing through APIs from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic. The dossier itself states the quiet part out loud: Kuli's defensibility "lies more in the domain-specific application layer than in the raw video analysis capability." When Lee Jacobs and I talk about avoiding investments, the phrase we use is "not some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper." Kuli is a beautifully executed application layer — but I need to see what's structurally underneath it that a well-funded incumbent couldn't replicate in a quarter, and I'm struggling to find it.

Michael Hodara holds an MSc in Robotics from EPFL, led AI and data science at Aircall managing $20 million or more in annual marketing spend, and experienced the influencer marketing pain point firsthand as a buyer. Jonathan Hassan brings six years of KPMG M&A advisory and enterprise sales instinct. When I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is: "I was at Aircall, I saw the inefficiency, I built a solution." That's genuine domain expertise, and I respect it. But it's not a biographical forge. Hodara isn't someone whose life broke open around this problem and left him unable to walk away. He's a credentialed engineer who spotted a workflow gap from an enviable professional vantage point. Kuli's founding story reads like market identification, not mission inevitability — and the absence of personal stakes in the problem space is one of the clearest signals in my framework.

Disney, Supercell, and Havas as named users at pre-seed with three people — I have to acknowledge that this is striking enterprise traction. The bull case writes itself: Kuli has cracked enterprise doors that take most startups years to reach, Hodara's buyer-side experience gives him credibility in the room, Microsoft selected Kuli as a top-15 GenAI startup in France, and the proprietary content index — "the world's largest analysed social media content database without storing any video," per the EPFL Investor Day 2024 profile — could compound into a genuine data moat over time. If that index creates measurably better creator recommendations than CreatorIQ's metadata-driven approach, and if switching costs deepen as enterprises build campaign histories inside Kuli, this could become the system of record for content-level influencer intelligence. That's the strongest version of the argument, and those enterprise names deserve weight.

But here is where Kuli falls outside my framework structurally. Influencer marketing is not an opaque, extractive system that exploits information asymmetry against vulnerable people. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was revealing the hidden cost structure of credit card debt — ordinary consumers were being exploited by a system designed to be illegible. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessment opacity was extracting value from homeowners who couldn't navigate the appeals process. Kuli is making marketing teams at Disney and Havas more efficient at reviewing creator content. These are sophisticated enterprise buyers with enormous bargaining power who currently do things manually. There is no hidden tax, no cartel, no information asymmetry being corrected for the powerless. Kuli optimizes a visible, understood process — and optimization of well-functioning enterprise workflows, however valuable commercially, is not where my deepest conviction lives.

The contrarian conviction dimension is where Kuli scores lowest against my criteria. Influencer marketing platforms are a $14.2 billion market growing at 37% CAGR, attracting hundreds of millions in venture funding across CreatorIQ, GRIN, Aspire, and others. "AI for influencer marketing" is the kind of pitch that gets enthusiastic nods at a dinner party — nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair. Compare this to Flock Safety, where I noticed a WiFi network name at a YC event and invested in surveillance technology that made civil liberties advocates nervous, or Contraline, where male birth control provoked visible discomfort from every VC in the room. Kuli operates in a consensus-attractive space with eager investors competing for deals. Microsoft is showcasing them at VivaTech. That's validation, not contrarianism — and validation before product-market fit is established is a signal I weigh differently than most investors.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me: if Kuli can demonstrate that their accumulated content index produces creator recommendations with measurably superior campaign ROI compared to CreatorIQ's metadata approach — not 10% better, but structurally different outcomes that prove the video-content-native architecture creates a compounding advantage incumbents cannot bolt on — and if Hodara reveals a deeper biographical connection to this problem that the public dossier doesn't capture, I would revisit. The enabling technology inflection is real, and the enterprise traction is unusual for pre-seed. But right now, Kuli is a competent team applying commoditizing AI capabilities to a consensus market without an invisible system to disrupt or a founder biography that makes me believe they'll survive the inevitable valley when CreatorIQ ships their own video analysis feature. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Contrarian Conviction (consensus-attractive market, no discomfort signal) and Invisible System (efficiency optimization of an already-visible process, no hidden structure being revealed).
