# Concorda -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Legal AI adoption surging from 19% to 79% in two years is not an opportunity signal — it is a consensus alarm. Every time I see a category where adoption metrics hockey-stick upward and funding floods in simultaneously ($4.3 billion in legaltech in 2025, up 54% year-over-year), I recall the cleantech lesson: entrepreneurs swept up by social trends confuse a rising tide with a secret. Concorda enters a market where Harvey AI commands an $11 billion valuation and $190 million in ARR, where Thomson Reuters bought CoCounsel for $650 million, where EvenUp has raised $385 million, and where Crimson — a YC-backed company with four employees in London — is building what its own listing describes as "the AI associate for litigation lawyers." When I can find a near-identical company in the same accelerator ecosystem with the same positioning, the question answers itself: there is no secret here. "Existing legal AI tools sit on top of workflows instead of executing them" is a product positioning statement, not a contrarian truth that the market actively disputes. Harvey's investors are not shaking their heads at this thesis — they simply have not prioritized it yet, because capturing eight of the top ten US law firms with a horizontal platform was the higher-leverage play first.

The strongest case for Concorda rests on Samuel Oh's $204K in annualized revenue within three months of operation. That number is real. At pre-seed with two people, someone is paying meaningful money for what Concorda delivers — likely through the legal services component of the dual model. This matters because it suggests organic demand, not manufactured traction. Oh's combination of Harvard CS and UChicago Law creates a genuine "lawyer-engineer" profile that is rarer than most AI founders appreciate, and Ke Ma's McKinsey stint plus Cook County prosecution experience adds operational credibility. The founder-market fit is authentic: Oh's quote about "spending years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way" reflects lived pain, not borrowed insight. But founder quality without monopoly structure produces what *Zero to One* calls a merely correct observation — right about the problem, wrong about the structural path to dominance. A determined founder in a competitive market is still a loser, because competition itself is the losing condition.

Concorda's dual services-plus-SaaS model deserves scrutiny through the Palantir lens, because it superficially resembles Palantir's early architecture — embedded engineers working alongside intelligence analysts, building software that encoded operational knowledge. The parallel would suggest that Concorda's legal services work embeds them in active litigation, creating case-level switching costs and generating proprietary training data that competitors cannot access. But the analogy breaks at the structural level. Palantir's embedding worked because the CIA was a single institutional buyer with classified data that no competitor could access and procurement cycles measured in decades. Law firms are the opposite: fragmented across thousands of independent entities, able to switch vendors between cases, and structurally allergic to lock-in. Concorda's firm-specific voice models and case pattern data — mentioned on their website — could theoretically create switching costs over time, but at current scale with a two-person team, no firm has enough accumulated data with Concorda to make switching painful. The malpractice and state bar regulatory exposure from the services model adds liability without adding defensibility.

What would have to be true for Concorda to work despite these structural problems? Three things. First, litigation execution would need to be so fundamentally different from other legal workflows that Harvey's horizontal architecture cannot serve it well — that the gap between "legal AI" and "litigation AI" is as wide as the gap between general-purpose computing and intelligence analysis that gave Palantir its opening. Second, Concorda would need to reach deep operational embedding in enough firms before Harvey or Crimson builds comparable litigation modules — a race against well-funded competitors with superior distribution. Third, the services component would need to generate compounding data advantages that translate into software defensibility rather than remaining a labor-intensive consulting practice. None of these conditions is impossible, but all three must hold simultaneously, and the dossier provides no evidence that the first — the structural gap between litigation and general legal AI — is wide enough to sustain an independent monopoly rather than a feature set within a larger platform.

The power-law calculation further constrains my interest. Legal services have never produced winner-take-all outcomes. The $3.11 billion legal AI TAM growing to $10.82 billion by 2030 is a meaningful market, but it fragments across practice areas, firm sizes, and geographies in ways that resist monopoly concentration. Harvey at $11 billion has not monopolized legal AI — it has captured a large share of a segment while leaving EvenUp, Supio, Thomson Reuters, and now Concorda and Crimson to compete across adjacent verticals. The realistic ceiling for a litigation-specific vertical player is a $500 million to $2 billion outcome — valuable to its founders, structurally irrelevant to a fund constructed around power-law returns. The absence of any physical-world technology moat compounds this: LLM-based document generation, as the dossier itself notes, is "increasingly accessible," and no amount of litigation-domain tuning creates multi-year replication barriers when the underlying models improve quarterly.

Concorda's decisive weaknesses are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Magnitude. The $204K early revenue and Oh's authentic founder-market fit earn respect for execution, but execution in a crowded market without a contrarian insight or structural path to monopoly produces the kind of company that succeeds modestly and returns nothing to a portfolio built for extremes. I pass.
