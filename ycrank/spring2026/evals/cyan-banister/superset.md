# Superset -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: Anthropic has already shipped native multi-agent orchestration for Claude Code. The platform owners are building the feature that IS this company. When I backed Crusoe, nobody at the oil companies was trying to build portable data centers on their flare stacks — the incumbents were structurally incapable of seeing the opportunity. But here, the upstream providers whose CLI agents Superset orchestrates have every incentive and every capability to absorb this functionality. That's not an infrastructure play hiding beneath a weird surface. That's a feature living on borrowed time, and the landlord is already renovating.

When I ask my first question — tell me about these founders, not the business — the dossier gives me three ex-YC CTOs who met through the batch circuit and spotted a workflow gap. Kiet Ho built Onlook to 25,000 GitHub stars, which tells me he can ship open-source developer tools that go viral. That's genuine execution ability and I respect it. But execution ability and biographical inevitability are different animals. Nothing in what I can see tells me these founders were forged by this problem — that managing parallel AI agents is something they'd be working on even if nobody would fund them. They were at Google, Amazon, Facebook, ServiceNow, and they saw an opportunity in the gap between CLI agents and IDE workflows. That's pattern recognition from inside the system, not a life story that made this problem impossible to walk past. The team was assembled for the market, not summoned by a mission. I've learned to feel the difference.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If CLI-based AI agents become the dominant development paradigm — not IDE-embedded agents, but standalone CLI tools — then agent-agnostic orchestration becomes a genuine coordination layer. The structural incentive argument is real: Cursor won't build something that commoditizes its own proprietary agent, so there's a gap that an independent player can fill. Kiet's track record with Onlook proves this team can build something developers love and spread it virally. Eight thousand stars in five months is not nothing. And if multi-agent workflows become as fundamental as Git itself, the orchestration layer could compound. But for this to be a Long Journey bet, I'd need to believe the coordination layer stays structurally independent — that the platform owners don't absorb it, that IDE vendors don't add it as a checkbox feature, and that UX polish creates a durable moat rather than a temporary lead. Anthropic shipping native multi-agent orchestration tells me the window is already closing.

What I don't see at all is a hidden system being cracked open. Developer tooling is one of the most transparent, well-functioning markets in technology. Nobody is being exploited by information asymmetry when they manage tmux sessions. There's no opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it. This is a convenience product that makes already-privileged software engineers slightly more productive — and I say that without judgment, but it's not what lights up my pattern recognition. My strongest investments attack structures that are invisible to the people trapped inside them. Taxi medallion cartels. Credit card fee opacity. Property tax systems. Superset attacks workflow friction in one of the most legible, well-compensated professions on earth.

And this is consensus AI. If I described this company at a dinner party — "a tool that lets developers run multiple AI coding agents in parallel" — nobody would shift in their chair. They'd nod and say "smart." That's the opposite of the signal I look for. My SpaceX check came when rockets were exploding. My Anduril check came when defense tech was politically toxic. My Contraline check came when male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Developer tools for AI agents is the most consensus-attractive category in venture right now. Cursor is valued at $29 billion. This isn't a space where being the first check requires courage — it's a space where everyone is already writing checks. I wish these founders well. They clearly know how to build and ship. But this isn't a Long Journey bet.
