# Robby -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me about Robby: this is Craftwork inverted, and the inversion is fatal. Craftwork is a painting company that uses software to get better at painting. Robby is a software company that helps painting companies sell more painting. One of those businesses has an AI-resistant moat. The other one is a feature waiting to be absorbed by ServiceTitan. The entire reason I love backing home services businesses is that the operational complexity -- the trucks, the technicians, the EPA certifications, the callback visits -- creates a moat that no amount of cheap software can replicate. Robby captures zero of that operational moat. Their customers have moats. Robby doesn't.

Let me run my sane-person-insane-idea filter. Three Harvard MBA graduates building AI-powered revenue intelligence for HVAC contractors is the definition of a sane person with a sane idea. Nobody in the room at Demo Day is going to say "that's crazy." They're going to nod and say "big market, obvious problem, clear monetization." That nodding is exactly what I screen against. The most valuable investments in my portfolio -- Solana at five cents, Helium spending seven years in the wilderness -- provoked confusion and dismissal, not nodding. When your pitch starts with a $650 billion TAM slide and every VC immediately grasps the value proposition, the "insane idea" premium has already been arbitraged to zero. Joseph's Bain PE home services work is genuine domain credibility, but he's applying it to an idea the market has already validated, which means you're competing on execution in a category where the platform owner controls the board.

I need to flag the structural red flags stacking up here. All three founders met at HBS -- that's my speed-dating co-founder pattern. They've pivoted through Importrix (AI customs brokerage), Lifelong Health, and now Robby within roughly a year -- three distinct product directions suggests the current thesis is still finding its footing rather than reflecting deep, patient conviction. And the biggest structural problem: if I remove AI from Robby, the company doesn't exist. That is the exact opposite of my "cherry on top" test. The AI isn't making a great business even better -- the AI IS the business. Which means they're building in the precise category where I think incumbents with distribution will capture all the value. ServiceTitan has a $9 billion market cap, 895 million in revenue guidance, and has already announced Atlas Campaign Recommendations and AI-powered SMS Booking Agent for 2026. When the platform you're building on decides to build your product, your product dies. I've been saying this publicly for two years: software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The 9+ named paying customers at pre-seed is legitimately impressive -- Abe, Barrett, Blue Knight, Lake Norman, and the others represent real adoption, not vapor. The claim of "$40K in new business within the first hour of launch" and "$135K additional revenue per technician annually" suggests genuine product-market fit at the workflow level. And there's a structural argument that ServiceTitan won't build this: their business model depends on being a neutral operational platform, and an aggressive upselling AI layer could create tension with contractors who view their FSM software as a scheduling tool, not a sales coach. The PE consolidation of home services is also real -- larger operators created through roll-ups have the sophistication and budget for revenue optimization tools. If Robby can establish deep workflow lock-in with technicians before ServiceTitan moves, and if the data from each visit genuinely compounds into better recommendations, there's a path to becoming the revenue intelligence layer for the entire ServiceTitan ecosystem. That path exists. I just don't believe the structural moat supports it.

Where I land: Robby is building an application on someone else's protocol. It's an AI-first SaaS tool in a hot market with a consensus value proposition. It triggers three of my explicit avoidance patterns simultaneously -- AI as core value proposition, SaaS subscription as the business model innovation, and a currently hot market where the pitch leads with TAM. The founders are competent but not maniacally wedded to this specific problem -- the pivot velocity suggests they found a good market, not that they're the only people on earth who could build this. The traction is real and that matters, but traction without structural defensibility is the Birchbox pattern: a company too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound into something enormous. I'd rather fund the HVAC company itself -- the one with the trucks and the technicians and the EPA certifications -- and help them build their own AI layer internally. That's where the moat lives.
