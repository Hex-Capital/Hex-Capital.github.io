# Valence -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment in prediction markets is real but it's consensus, not contrarian. Monthly active users went from 4,000 to 600,000+ in under two years. Kalshi won its CFTC lawsuit, Polymarket broke through during the 2024 election, and now Robinhood, DraftKings, and FanDuel are all piling in. Total notional volume hit $44 billion in 2025. Every fintech investor I talk to has a prediction markets thesis. When a market goes from "weird niche" to "$11 billion Kalshi valuation" in 18 months, the non-obvious window has closed. That doesn't mean there's no money to be made -- Coinbase was a consensus crypto bet by Series C and it worked out fine -- but it means the asymmetric returns from identifying the structural inflection before others have already been captured by the primary exchanges. The question for Valence is whether the aggregation layer on top of those exchanges represents a distinct, less-consensus opportunity within the broader consensus market.

The honest answer is: maybe. Valence is doing for prediction markets what smart order routing firms did for equities after Reg NMS fragmented liquidity across multiple exchanges. The analogy is structurally sound -- more venues means more fragmentation, which means more value in aggregation. The problem is that equity market fragmentation was mandated by regulation. There's no equivalent regulatory requirement forcing prediction market fragmentation to persist. If Kalshi or Polymarket achieves dominant liquidity (the way Binance did in crypto), the aggregation value collapses. And the consumer platforms -- Robinhood with 24 million users, DraftKings with its sports betting base -- are building walled gardens, not open venues that feed an aggregation layer. Valence is betting that fragmentation increases. That bet could be right, but it's a market structure bet the team doesn't control.

What genuinely stands out is the founder-market fit, which is the strongest signal in this dossier. Daniel Kasabov-Nouvion built hardware-level trading systems at Optiver and IMC. Neo Wang did latency-sensitive strategy research at DRW and interned at Cubist and Citadel Securities. These aren't fintech generalists who watched the election and decided prediction markets were hot -- they're quantitative trading infrastructure engineers who understand co-location, order book mechanics, and low-latency execution at the hardware level. Wang previously built an event-contract exchange prototype. Kasabov-Nouvion built an order book in Rust. The claimed traction -- $200M+ monthly volume, 1B+ contracts, a quant firm reportedly routing $150M+ monthly through the platform -- is extraordinary for a 3-person pre-seed team if accurate. That execution velocity, combined with domain-specific technical depth, is the profile I look for. But execution velocity in building the product doesn't resolve the business model risk of depending on exchange API access you don't control.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I get stuck. In traditional finance, Bloomberg started as a bond data terminal and became a distribution channel for messaging, analytics, trading, and media across every asset class. Could Valence follow that path? Prediction markets would need to become a much larger asset class for that trajectory to play out. The tiered pricing model (Pro to Premiere to Automated) shows some land-and-expand thinking, and API access for systematic traders creates real switching costs. But the total addressable revenue for an aggregation layer is a fraction of the primary exchange revenue -- Valence captures fees on routed volume, not the full exchange spread. If prediction market firm revenues reach the projected $10 billion by 2030, and Valence captures 3-5% of volume from professional traders, that's a meaningful business but not a platform company. The ceiling feels structurally constrained unless the team expands into adjacent asset classes or builds proprietary data products -- neither of which the dossier suggests they're thinking about yet.

The bull case I'd need to believe: prediction market fragmentation accelerates as more exchanges launch globally, sports events drive consistent non-election volume (89% of Kalshi's 2025 fees came from sports), and Valence becomes the execution infrastructure layer that every serious prediction market trader uses -- the way Interactive Brokers serves active traders across equity markets. In that scenario, the quant trading pedigree becomes a structural advantage because professional traders trust infrastructure built by people who understand their needs. The co-location infrastructure and API access create genuine switching costs, and the data flywheel from routing significant volume generates proprietary signal. That's a plausible $1-2 billion outcome. But it requires prediction market fragmentation to persist without regulatory mandate, exchanges to keep API access open to a firm routing volume across their competitors, and the market itself to sustain growth rates that may have been inflated by a once-in-a-cycle presidential election. That's not quite a multi-miracle plan, but it's more than one thing that needs to go right.
