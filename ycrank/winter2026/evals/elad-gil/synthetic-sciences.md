# Synthetic Sciences -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check with any company is the competitive structure of the market, and what I see here sets off every alarm in my framework. "AI for scientific discovery" is not a non-obvious market -- it's one of the most consensus-hot categories in AI right now. Google announced its own AI Co-Scientist in 2025. Edison Scientific raised $70M in December 2025 with Jeff Dean on the cap table. Elicit already has 400,000+ monthly researchers and $31M in funding. Sakana AI sits at a $2.65 billion valuation. When I mapped AI markets in my "AI Market Clarity" framework, I specifically tracked which categories were reaching "market-ending move" dynamics where well-funded leaders create compounding distribution advantages. This market is converging toward that structure. A 2-person team with $1.5M going against Google's research infrastructure and Edison's $70M war chest isn't "building ahead of the capability curve" -- it's arriving at a party where the seats are being taken by players with fundamentally more resources.

The structural moment for AI-powered research tools is real -- LLM capabilities crossed multi-step reasoning thresholds in 2024-2025, inference costs dropped enough to make sustained agentic sessions economically viable, and GPU availability expanded across providers. I don't dispute the "why now." But when the "why now" is visible to every major AI lab and a dozen funded startups simultaneously, it stops being a structural inflection I can exploit and becomes a competition for execution at scale. My best investments have been in markets that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors -- PagerDuty when operations alerting seemed boring, Coinbase when crypto seemed speculative. This market triggers the opposite reaction: universal excitement, heavy funding, and incumbent attention. That's the wrong signal for my framework.

The multi-miracle problem is severe. To win, Synthetic Sciences needs to: (1) build research agents that outperform what Google, Edison, and Elicit are building, (2) acquire distribution against competitors with 20-200x their funding, (3) generate enough usage to create a meaningful process-data flywheel before competitors do the same, and (4) deliver on a product scope that spans GPU orchestration, biology workflows, RL training infrastructure, and publication tools -- with two people. Each of these is hard independently; sequentially, they compound into the kind of multi-miracle plan I explicitly pass on. The pivot from InkVell (document formatting) to this full-stack AI co-scientist platform during the YC batch amplifies the concern. Scope expansion during a batch often indicates the founders are tracking where the narrative is heading rather than having arrived at deep structural conviction through their own research experience.

The strongest bull case centers on Aayam Bansal's research credentials, which are genuinely remarkable for an 18-year-old: 15+ papers at ICML, NeurIPS, and ACL, research stints at CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL, and a claimed prior exit in AI healthcare. If this founder is building the tool he personally needs as an ML researcher -- the "builder building for builders" pattern I weight heavily -- there's a version where deep domain understanding produces something qualitatively different from what Edison's biology-first approach or Elicit's literature-synthesis approach delivers. The SaaS pricing ($50-$200/month per researcher) creates a more legible business model than FutureHouse's nonprofit structure. And the RL training data flywheel, if it materialized at scale, would be a genuine defensibility mechanism. But "if it materialized at scale" is doing enormous work in that sentence. The flywheel requires exactly the kind of massive usage base that the competitive dynamics make hardest to achieve. I've seen this pattern before: a theoretically sound flywheel that never spins fast enough because the company can't get to the scale where the data advantage compounds.

I also notice the unverifiable benchmark claim -- 92% on "BixBench Verified" when published BixBench results show frontier models at 17% accuracy. The "Verified" variant may be a legitimate subset, but when a key credibility signal can't be independently corroborated, it weakens rather than strengthens my conviction. At pre-seed, I'm looking for transparent signals of execution quality, and benchmark claims that require explanation are a yellow flag.

The product surface area is ambitious in a way that suggests platform thinking -- four modes spanning research, biology, iterative improvement, and writing -- and I appreciate that the founders are conceptualizing this as a distribution channel rather than a point solution. But conceptualizing distribution and executing distribution are different things, and I see no evidence of actual distribution momentum: no community, no social presence, no Product Hunt launch, no public user counts. At this stage that's forgivable, but it means the distribution thesis is entirely forward-looking in a market where competitors already have established user bases.
