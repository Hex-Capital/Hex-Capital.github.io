# Arctic Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Arctic Health is the inverse of the pattern I want: a real schlep being attacked by founders who haven't shown me they're animals and who haven't personally bled from the problem. The 60-90 day credentialing grind across 300+ payer portals is exactly the kind of work "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) said other founders unconsciously route around -- but Sam Slezek covered insurance equities at Morgan Stanley and worked product at Scale AI and Instacart; he hasn't been a clinic administrator stuck on hold with Aetna. The dimensional signature here is high-schlep, low-origin. That's a category where Verifiable, Medallion, and Assured will probably out-execute Arctic Health because their teams arrived first and their ops DNA is deeper.

On the schlep ladder, **Coinbase** (2012) sits at the top -- Brian Armstrong actually wanted the bitcoin compliance grind that scared every other YC applicant -- and at the bottom sits the polished AI-wrapper pass I described in "Schlep Blindness" terms: pure software, no offline grind. Arctic Health sits between **Coinbase** (2012) and **DoorDash** (2013): the dossier names 300+ payer portals, all 50 states + DC, NCQA gating, and "experienced industry experts who handle every edge case." That is genuine schlep. The only thing keeping Arctic Health off the top rung is that Assured already shipped a 2-day NCQA-certified product with $6M from First Round Capital, which means Arctic Health is doing the schlep third, not first -- and the schlep moat belongs to whoever endures it earliest.

On the organic-pain ladder, **Dropbox** (2007) is the top rung -- Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT and built the tool for himself -- and the bottom rung is the MBA team with a TAM slide pitching a market they read about. Arctic Health sits much closer to the bottom: Slezek's Morgan Stanley insurance-equity coverage and Raghav Mehrish's Zenefits engineering work are adjacent to payer data, but adjacent is not organic. None of the three Arctic Health co-founders ever ran a credentialing department or got rejected by an Aetna panel; the YC page explicitly says the system is trained by industry experts, meaning the founders themselves are not the experts. That is the "spotted a market opportunity" pattern from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012), not the Houston-USB-stick pattern.

On the animal-resourcefulness ladder, **Airbnb** (2009)'s cereal boxes are the top rung and the soft-pitching MBA team is the bottom rung, with **Dropbox** (2007)'s Hacker News demo video as the mid rung that flipped my conviction. Arctic Health has no equivalent stunt named in the dossier -- no scrappy revenue improvisation, no pre-funding artifact shipped into the wild, no Slezek-built prototype that overturned a rejection the way Armstrong's Android wallet rescued **Coinbase** (2012). What I see instead is Menlo Ventures already on the cap table, which means the check came through warm SF networks, not through a founder doing something I'd retell at dinner. Animals leave tracks; Arctic Health hasn't left any.

On hacker genealogy, **Stripe** (2010) anchors the top rung with Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize, **Coinbase** (2012)'s wallet prototype is the mid rung, and the no-shipped-code MBA team is the bottom. Arctic Health's Raghav Mehrish was a senior engineer at Airbnb with a GitHub at raghav297, which is real engineering credentialing-by-employer, but the dossier surfaces no open source contributions, no competitive programming history, no Lisp-tribal markers I over-index on. He's a competent engineer, not a hacker the way Aaron Swartz at **Reddit** (2005) was a hacker. On co-founder configuration, **Reddit** (2005)'s dorm-roommate Huffman/Ohanian pairing tops the ladder and a hackathon pair-up sits at the bottom; Arctic Health's three founders share no prior employer or university across Scale AI/Instabase/Airbnb, so they sit closer to the lower rung -- three competent strangers, not a Collison-brothers-grade unit.

My 36-month forecast: Arctic Health gets acquired by Verifiable or Medallion for $20-50M as the credentialing category consolidates, or it plateaus at $3-8M ARR serving the SMB clinic segment Assured is also chasing. The mechanism is that Verifiable shipped CredAgent in February 2026 with Series B capital, Medallion has $130M and a Sequoia/GV-backed AI hub, and Assured holds the NCQA certification Arctic Health does not -- so the schlep moat compounds for whoever was earliest, and Arctic Health was not earliest. This is the **Kiko** (2005) shape, not the **Twitch** (2007) shape: third-to-market in a converging category.

The strongest case against my pass is the **Coinbase** (2012) bull argument: the schlep is the moat, and any founder who genuinely wants to grind payer-portal automation for a decade will eventually win because the incumbents will flinch first. Slezek's Morgan Stanley insurance background is closer to the credentialing operations layer than Armstrong's pre-Coinbase background was to bitcoin compliance. If Arctic Health is in fact the team most willing to do the unglamorous work -- and Menlo wrote the check for a reason I can't see from the outside -- then I'm passing on a Coinbase-pattern company because the dossier didn't name the right stunt. The bear evidence wins anyway: Verifiable's CredAgent shipped three months before Arctic Health's batch began, Assured has NCQA certification Arctic Health does not, and the dossier surfaces no pre-funding artifact equivalent to Armstrong's wallet. The one verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Slezek personally onboarding 10+ named SMB clinics with a sub-2-day median credentialing time, published with logos, within six months -- the Brian Chesky door-to-door "Do Things that Don't Scale" (July 2013) pattern applied to clinic ops. Without that, this is the clever-idea-soft-founders shape where I lose money.
