# Bidflow -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look at is what the founder knows that nobody can teach them. Jesse Choe and Gautham Ramachandran are clearly gifted builders -- USACO Platinum, Jane Street extern at 18, ICML preprints on reinforcement learning, bootstrapped to $120K at 16. That's real technical horsepower. But six months ago they were building QA testing agents for software bugs. Now they're building AI for electrical estimating -- a domain where their direct competitor, Drawer AI, was founded by someone who spent 25 years reading circuit diagrams and bidding on projects. Specific knowledge in electrical contracting isn't "I can train a vision model." It's knowing that a particular symbol on drawing page 47 implies a specific conduit run that changes the labor calculation on three other pages. It's knowing which NECA labor units are systematically wrong for data center work. It's the kind of understanding that lives in the hands of people who've crawled through ceiling plenums. I don't see evidence these founders have that knowledge, and in a domain where estimation errors translate directly to money lost or left on the table, domain ignorance is not a timing issue -- it's a structural one.

The leverage architecture here is vertical SaaS for a specific construction trade. Software serving multiple users is baseline leverage -- every SaaS company has it. But this isn't creating a new form of capability. It's making an existing workflow faster. An estimator who used to take a week per bid now takes a day. That's valuable, but it's linear productivity improvement, not the kind of leverage multiplication I look for. Compare this to what Replit does: it doesn't make existing programmers faster, it turns non-programmers into programmers. Bidflow makes estimators faster. It doesn't create a new class of economic actor. The SAM is bounded -- electrical estimating software is a subset of a $1.5-2.7B market, and the electrical-specific slice is maybe $200-500M. That's a real business, but it's not the kind of market where a 1000x outcome is structurally possible.

The timing is the opposite of contrarian. AI for construction takeoff is consensus right now. Beam AI just raised $48M. Togal.AI has $17-22M and claims 97% accuracy. Drawer AI raised $5M specifically for electrical estimating -- the identical use case -- with founders who have decades of domain context. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I backed Bitcoin in 2014, it was dismissed as nerd money. Here, the thesis that "AI will automate construction estimation" is on every VC's PowerPoint deck. The market has already priced this category as attractive. Multiple well-funded teams are executing. Bidflow is entering a crowded, funded, consensus market with less money, less domain expertise, and less time in market than its direct competitors. The 83.5% accuracy figure is honest transparency, which I respect, but when Togal.AI claims 97% and Drawer AI has been training models for three years, that gap represents a real disadvantage in a domain where accuracy is the product.

The strongest bull case: these are young, fast builders who've already survived one YC batch and demonstrated the pragmatism to pivot when Ghostship wasn't working. Data center construction is genuinely booming, creating acute demand for electrical estimation capacity. If they can iterate faster than the incumbents -- and young, hungry, technically gifted founders sometimes can -- the data flywheel from early customer usage could compound into accuracy improvements that close the gap. Construction is relationship-driven, and if Bass Electric's 5x speed improvement testimonial is real, word-of-mouth in tight contractor networks could create regional distribution advantages. There's a version of this where speed of execution and product craft beat domain experience and capital, the way Notion beat Google Docs despite Google's infinite resources. But that version requires the founders to develop domain-specific insight rapidly, and I see no evidence in the dossier that they've done so. The email domain is still tryghostship.dev. The GitHub bio still references Ghostship. These are signals of a team that pivoted recently and hasn't yet fully committed their identity to this problem.

I'd pass. The founders are smart and technically strong, but they're applying general-purpose AI capability to a domain they just discovered, competing against better-funded teams with deeper roots. This is the kind of company where the technology doesn't compound distinctively -- vision AI on construction drawings is a technique, not a moat -- and the market positioning is consensus rather than contrarian. Smart people building a slightly better tool for a known market at a time when everyone else is building the same thing. The asymmetric upside I need to justify writing a check isn't visible here.
