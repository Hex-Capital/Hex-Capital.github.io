# Andustry -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive terrain. Matchory has $8.86M for supplier discovery. LightSource Labs has $33M and is growing ARR at 10x. Cavela has $8.6M for product sourcing. MESH Works has processed $884M in RFQs. And Xometry is public with $686M in revenue and owns Thomasnet. Andustry is a 2-person team in Munich with pre-seed capital entering a space where well-funded players are already converging on AI-powered procurement. The question isn't whether the market is real -- $810B in industrial machinery, massively underdigitized. The question is whether Andustry has anything that can't be replicated by a competitor with 50x the capital and a 12-month head start. Innovation without a moat is consumer surplus. All the value flows to the buyer and eventually to whoever controls the supplier network.

Now, specific knowledge. Johann Sturken's family background in industrial goods trading is the most interesting signal in this dossier. That's not a resume line -- growing up inside a trading family means you internalize procurement friction at a level no market research can replicate. You know which suppliers actually deliver, how quote negotiations really work, why buyers distrust new platforms. Combine that with mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM, and he genuinely understands both sides of the transaction -- the technical specifications and the commercial dynamics. But I need to be honest about depth. McKinsey A&D consulting from 2022-2026 is adjacent exposure, not specific knowledge forged through years of building. Het Dave brings SaaS-to-AI transformation experience from Aries Global -- competent but generic. If Johann disappeared, could a sharp mechanical engineer with procurement contacts rebuild this? Probably yes, within a year. That's the test. With Perplexity, removing Aravind would have left a shell. Here, the knowledge is relevant but transferable.

The leverage audit reveals a structural tension. The AI agent layer -- RFQ generation, supplier matching, quote comparison -- is pure code leverage in theory. Zero marginal cost per query. But this is a two-sided marketplace, and marketplaces are deceptive. They look like software but behave like networks that require human-intensive supplier onboarding, trust-building, and relationship management. The 500K+ "verified companies" claim is critical: if that's a proprietary dataset with real certification checks and trust scores built through direct supplier engagement, it's a compounding data asset. If it's aggregated from public directories and trade databases -- which the dossier cannot confirm either way -- then any competitor with an API key and a web scraper has the same starting position. Industrial procurement also has a principal-agent problem working against Andustry: buyers making six-figure machinery purchases need trust that a 2-person AI platform has not yet earned. Unlike Perplexity, where Google's ad-revenue dependency creates a structural opening that principals can exploit, there's no equivalent structural paralysis among Andustry's competitors. Xometry could build AI-agent procurement for catalog goods tomorrow. The moat is aspirational, not architectural.

The strongest bull case: EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume from a 2-person team is a legitimate signal. That's real commerce, not signups or waitlist vanity metrics. If they can compound that into proprietary supplier quality data -- actual delivery performance, quote accuracy, certification verification that only comes from processing thousands of transactions -- the marketplace flywheel starts turning. Industrial procurement is the last great analog bazaar. The "why now" is genuine: LLMs crossed the threshold for reliable specification extraction and multi-language supplier communication in 2024-2025, making it possible for two people to automate workflows that previously required a procurement department. And Johann's family context means he knows which workflows matter and which are theater. If this team executes on supplier data depth before the better-funded competitors figure out industrial-specific dynamics, they could own a vertical that Matchory and LightSource Labs are approaching from the wrong altitude. The EUR 1M tells me the product works. The question is whether it compounds.

But my framework keeps returning to the same structural concern: the software layer is commodity. LLM-based RFQ generation, supplier matching, specification parsing -- these are capabilities, not moats. Every procurement AI company will have them within 18 months. The defensibility must come from the network, and networks require density that a 2-person team building through "book a call" founder-led sales cannot achieve fast enough in a market where competitors have raised $8-33M. Neither founder has a public GitHub presence, which for a company whose core product is AI agents is a notable absence -- who built this, and how deep is the technical architecture? The compound interest test is ambiguous: transaction data compounds, but only if you process enough transactions to outpace competitors accumulating the same data type. At EUR 1M in beta volume against MESH Works' $884M in RFQs, the compounding gap is enormous.

This is a competent team with genuine domain proximity attacking a real market at the right time. But I keep asking my question: could this be a billion-dollar outcome with five people? The answer is no -- industrial marketplaces structurally require supplier operations, buyer sales teams, and trust infrastructure that scale with headcount. The AI layer reduces but doesn't eliminate this dependency. And the specific knowledge, while real in Johann's case, isn't deep enough to constitute the company's core engine. I'd want to see evidence that the 500K supplier dataset is proprietary, that the founders built the AI system themselves, and that buyers are returning with repeat purchases driven by data quality no one else has. Without those signals, this is a reasonable bet but not my kind of bet.
