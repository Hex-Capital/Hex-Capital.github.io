# Kinect -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Rep AI has raised $8.2M to $11.2M and signed 500+ merchants. Alhena AI closed a $4.9M seed from Slow Ventures and landed Tatcha and Unilever. Tolstoy has 2,000+ brand partners. OpenAI and Perplexity both launched AI shopping assistants in late 2025. And now Kinect enters this space at pre-seed with two founders, zero verified customers, and a domain name that competes with Microsoft's discontinued motion sensor for every Google query. When I look at this landscape, I don't see a contrarian bet — I see a consensus category where well-capitalized players are already fighting for the same shelf space. The best ideas in my portfolio made sophisticated investors squirm. Kinect's thesis — "AI makes product pages smarter" — would get enthusiastic nods at any dinner party in San Francisco. That's not where I find my conviction.

Kratik Agrawal led Conversational Intelligence at Reevo, which raised $80M. Varun Kandula co-led that same function and previously sourced $200K+ in contracted ARR consulting for Sephora and Ralph Lauren through NextGen Consulting at UC Berkeley. These are capable builders with real domain exposure to both conversational AI and e-commerce brand operations. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — I get professional trajectories, not biographical crucibles. Kratik went from Google Commerce to Anduril to Verkada to Reevo and spotted the e-commerce opportunity. Varun went from MongoDB to Capital One to consulting to Reevo and saw the same thing. That's career progression leading to market identification. I can't find the moment where this problem grabbed them by the throat and refused to let go. The founding story reads like two strong engineers who recognized a gap from inside a well-positioned company — and that's exactly the pattern I've learned to distinguish from the founders who are building because something in their life left them no alternative.

Kinect's core proposition — dynamically adapting product pages and deploying a conversational AI agent across Shopify storefronts — sits in one of the most transparent, well-understood markets in technology. A product detail page is visible to every shopper, every competitor, and every platform. The "problem" is that these pages are static and generic, which is a real UX friction, but it's not a hidden structure extracting value from people who don't understand it. Compare this to what Affirm did to credit card opacity or what Ownwell does to property tax systems — those companies attacked structures where ordinary people were being exploited precisely because they couldn't see how the system worked. Kinect helps merchants convert more efficiently, which is commercially valuable but structurally incremental. There's no invisible tax being illuminated, no gatekeeping cartel being dismantled. The system Kinect operates within is already legible to everyone involved.

The strongest bull case for Kinect: the combination of conversational AI plus adaptive page rendering is genuinely novel among the competitors — Rep AI focuses on proactive chat, Alhena AI focuses on hallucination-free support, Tolstoy uses video — and none of them modify the visual storefront itself. Kinect's intelligence dashboard, which surfaces aggregated shopper questions and comparison patterns, could build a data flywheel that compounds across its merchant base over time. The Shopify integration creates meaningful switching costs once Kinect has live access to catalog, inventory, and policies. And the timing argument has teeth: LLM inference costs dropped dramatically, platform incumbents face channel conflict in building comparable tools, and the category just got validated by the biggest names in AI. If Kinect can move fast enough to lock in merchants before Rep AI or Shopify native features catch up, the integration depth could create a Carta-like switching cost moat. I checked my Known Exceptions — RTFKT proved that narrow timing windows can substitute for infrastructure moats. But RTFKT occupied a genuinely novel category boundary at the dawn of digital fashion. Kinect is entering a category with $16M+ already invested across direct competitors and platform giants converging from every direction. The dossier itself states it plainly: "an experienced team could replicate the feature set in months." That's not a narrow timing window — that's a sprint where Kinect starts behind.

Kinect's defensibility profile concerns me most when I overlay it against my own failure pattern. The companies I've been burned by — HQ Trivia, GameCrush, IRL — all lacked structural moats that would compound with time. Kinect's core components are individually commoditized: LLM-powered chat, behavioral targeting, dynamic page rendering. The data flywheel is theoretical at this stage with no verified conversations or customers. The Shopify platform dependency means Kinect's distribution channel is controlled by a company that already ships ChatGPT-powered shopping through Shop.app. When I run my ten-year question — where does this technology end up in a decade? — I see AI-personalized commerce becoming table stakes, built into every platform natively. I don't see why Kinect becomes the infrastructure layer rather than a feature that Shopify absorbs.

Kinect's real-world impact lands in the incremental-efficiency category rather than the access-expansion category that drives my highest conviction. Better conversion rates help existing DTC brands sell more to existing shoppers. That's commercially useful, and I don't dismiss it, but it doesn't create new economic participants or expand access to something previously gatekept. Serve Robotics expanded delivery to areas without infrastructure. Wonderschool expanded childcare access. Kinect helps a Shopify Plus merchant lift AOV by $12. The one condition that would flip me toward Invest: if Kratik or Varun could show me that their work exposed a genuinely hidden information asymmetry in how e-commerce brands are exploited by their current conversion optimization stack — a predatory pricing structure, an opaque data extraction pattern, something structural that Kinect makes transparent — that would activate my framework in a way the current pitch does not.

The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Contrarian Conviction and Invisible System. Kinect scores lowest where I need the most signal: the space is consensus-attractive with $16M+ invested across direct competitors and category validation from OpenAI and Perplexity, and there is no opaque structure being disrupted. The founders are competent professionals who identified a market gap from inside a well-funded company, but I can't locate the biographical forge that tells me they'd be building this without funding. I wish Kinect well — the founders clearly understand conversational AI and e-commerce — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
