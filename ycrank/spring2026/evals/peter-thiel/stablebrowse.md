# StableBrowse -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

StableBrowse is a thin-secret company stacked on a YC-default founder lineage: the dossier names a real architectural gap in Browserbase and Kernel ? infrastructure-only headless browsers with no persistent semantic layer ? but the team behind the secret reads as four competent engineers (Jay Mehta, Deepit Shah, Sarthak Awasthi, Somansh Shah) with no shipped-to-scale outcome between them. That pairing ? a defensible-on-paper thesis carried by an operator bench that consensus would price exactly where YC priced it ? is the analytical signature.

Stripe (2010) is the reference for what a real contrarian secret looks like in infrastructure: PayPal had "won" payments, and Patrick Collison's hidden truth was that developers, not merchants, were the actual buyers ? a blind spot Braintree and PayPal could not close because their org charts pointed the wrong direction. StableBrowse names a structurally similar shape: Browserbase ($67.5M raised, ~$3M revenue) and Kernel ($22M Series A) sell session infrastructure, and StableBrowse argues the durable layer is a persistent execution graph above the browser, not the browser itself. The divergence from the Stripe rung is that the StableBrowse secret is not hidden ? TinyFish ($47M Series A, ICONIQ) is already at the application layer, Browser Use is open-source at the agent layer, and any of the funded incumbents can ship a graph product in a quarter. A secret that the next three competitor decks will name is not a secret; it is a roadmap item.

The lean-startup failure mode ? what Zero to One named indefinite optimism ? shows up most clearly in companies whose plan is a benchmark rather than a multi-year architectural commitment. StableBrowse's public artifact is exactly this: "70?80% fewer tokens, 3?4x faster execution, 98% success rate" are performance claims, not a 2026?2029 roadmap, and the dossier surfaces no named technical milestone Mehta or Shah have committed not to pivot from. The HIPAA-targeted, mortgage-targeted, insurance-targeted vertical list reads as three indefinite optionalities, not one definite wedge. SpaceX (2008) committed to reusable first stages on a public schedule; StableBrowse has committed to a benchmark a competitor can re-run.

On monopoly trajectory: the StableBrowse moat hypothesis is a per-site knowledge graph that compounds as more agents traverse the same insurance carrier portals and EHRs. The mechanism is real ? cross-customer execution patterns on regulated, high-friction sites do exhibit data-network economics ? but the 18-month compounding curve requires StableBrowse to be the default graph for at least one vertical before Browserbase ships its own persistence layer at $300M valuation scale. The dossier discloses zero paying customers, zero named LOIs, and an engineer-affiliation list (Meta, Google, Stanford, UCSD) that is social proof, not graph density. Without a single named insurance carrier or EHR integration in the dossier, the network effect is a slide, not a moat.

Counterfactually, the Founder Lineage score flips a tier only if one of the four StableBrowse co-founders turns out to have shipped a relevant product at scale ? a Deepit Shah AWS service with named adoption, a Jay Mehta exit on the prior startup the resume site mentions but does not name. The dossier surfaces UIUC CS, an Amazon role, 8+ years of distributed-systems work, and a published RBF numerical-methods paper. That is a credible IC bench. It is not the Levchin-at-Affirm or Moskovitz-at-Asana override that has historically let me write checks past a missing structural moat.

Palantir (2003) is the single anchor for regulated-sector moat, and the StableBrowse positioning gestures at the same customer geometry ? HIPAA-covered healthcare, mortgage processing, insurance quote binding are exactly the regulated, integration-heavy, switching-cost-rich verticals where Palantir's 15-year monopoly compounded. The dossier's own language betrays the gap: "SOC 2 Type I and Type II certifications in progress" means StableBrowse cannot yet sell into the regulated pipeline its marketing targets, and the software-only posture means no atoms-heavy capex barrier behind it. Palantir had a forward-deployed engineer model and classified-customer integration depth at year three; StableBrowse has a website claim and four engineers.

The 36-month forecast: StableBrowse is acqui-hired by Browserbase or Kernel for under $30M in talent-and-graph terms once one of them ships a native persistence layer, or it winds down quietly after a bridge. The mechanism is that the StableBrowse secret is shippable by a better-capitalized incumbent inside one product cycle, the founder bench lacks the lineage override that historically lets a thin-moat company outrun an incumbent, and the regulated-vertical wedge is gated by a SOC 2 timeline StableBrowse has not closed. Power-law math does not work here ? this is not the shape of a fund-returner.

The strongest bear-of-my-bear case ? meaning the bull case for StableBrowse ? is the Quora exception: a company I funded despite a violated monopoly criterion because the data-network-effect compounding on a knowledge graph was real and the founder had a verifiable pedigree. If the StableBrowse per-site execution graph achieves first-mover density on three specific insurance carrier portals before Browserbase ships persistence, the switching cost for any agent vendor binding insurance quotes becomes asymmetric, and the same graph extends to EHRs and lender portals on the same substrate. The bear case loses to dossier evidence: the StableBrowse dossier names zero carriers, zero EHRs, zero lenders, and zero revenue, while Browserbase has 20,000 developer signups and 50M sessions to graph against the moment they decide to. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is one named, paying regulated-vertical customer ? a specific carrier, a specific health system, a specific lender ? with repeat execution traffic on the StableBrowse graph by month nine. Until then, pass.
