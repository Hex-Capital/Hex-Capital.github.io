# Valgo -- Paul Graham Evaluation

These founders wrote the textbook. Literally. "Algorithms for Validation," MIT Press, co-authored by Moss and Katz, with Delecki's thesis providing the sampling methodology underneath it. They teach the Stanford course. Moss spent seven years at MIT Lincoln Lab on the team that built the FAA-certified ACAS Xa collision avoidance system -- the one that's now the worldwide standard for aircraft collision avoidance. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the answer isn't "we noticed a market opportunity in autonomous vehicle insurance." The answer is "we spent a decade building the algorithms that quantify risk in safety-critical autonomous systems, and we realized insurers are drowning without them." That's organic discovery of the highest order. The problem found the founders, not the other way around.

The schlep here is enormous, and it's the kind that's been hiding in plain sight. Everyone in the AV industry knows insurance is the bottleneck. Waymo knows it. Aurora knows it. The insurers know it. But actually solving it requires simultaneous mastery of probabilistic safety validation, autonomous systems simulation, and insurance actuarial science -- three domains that almost never overlap in the same human beings, let alone the same founding team. It's like Stripe: thousands of developers knew payment processing was broken for years before the Collisons showed up, but no one wanted to wade into the mess of banks and regulations. With Valgo, no one has waded into the mess because you need a PhD-level understanding of both autonomous systems validation AND insurance math. The structural reason nobody's built this already isn't that nobody thought of it -- it's that almost nobody on Earth has the training to build it. Three Stanford PhDs from the same lab, plus an insurance veteran who led $5 billion in M&A, might be the only team that can.

On the technical side, Moss is clearly a hacker who builds things. A hundred and four public repositories on GitHub, including BeautifulAlgorithms.jl with 1,400 stars -- that's not someone who manages engineers, that's someone who writes code because they enjoy it. Three technical co-founders who can each build the core product independently is unusual depth for a pre-seed team. The Julia ecosystem presence suggests someone who picks tools for technical merit rather than popularity, which is a good sign in someone building probabilistic risk models.

The bull case is compelling: autonomous vehicles are crossing from R&D into commercial deployment right now, and every single one needs insurance to operate on public roads. Insurers have thirty billion historical claims records for human drivers and approximately zero for autonomous systems. This isn't a nice-to-have -- it's a structural blocker that gets more urgent every quarter as more AV companies try to go commercial. If Valgo becomes the standard risk quantification layer, they'd sit at a chokepoint in the entire autonomous systems industry. Each new customer's simulation data improves the models, creating a data flywheel with real switching costs. The ceiling is very high.

But what concerns me is the dual-sided market problem. Valgo needs autonomy companies to share proprietary simulation data -- data those companies consider competitively sensitive -- and simultaneously needs insurers to trust simulation-derived loss estimates, which is a radical departure from how insurance has worked for centuries. Neither side has established norms for this. That's not one miracle; it's two institutional behavior changes happening in coordination. Koop Technologies has been operating in this space since 2021 with $7 million, a Lloyd's of London partnership, and an established data-sharing framework with autonomy companies. They chose to be the vertically integrated insurance carrier rather than the infrastructure layer, which could mean they've locked up key relationships -- or it could mean they left the infrastructure opportunity open. I don't know which, and that matters.

There's also zero evidence of user pull. No customers, no LOIs, no revenue, no waitlist -- nothing indicating that either autonomy companies or insurers have actually engaged with the product. At pre-seed from fresh PhDs, that's not damning in itself. But for a company that needs to bridge two conservative industries, I'd want some signal -- even a single insurer saying "yes, we'd use simulation-based loss estimates if they were credible" -- before I'd have full conviction. The structural demand is undeniable, but structural demand for a category is not the same as demonstrated demand for a specific product.

What tips this toward investment for me despite the traction gap is the rarity of the team. You could fund a hundred pre-seed companies working on AI applications and never find one where the founders created the field's canonical academic reference. The founder-problem fit isn't just strong -- it's essentially singular. If this problem is going to be solved by anyone, these are the people who will solve it. And the problem itself has the hallmarks of the best startup opportunities: obvious in retrospect, unavoidable in practice, and protected by a schlep so large that it functions as a moat.
