# Coalition Systems -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Coalition Systems wants to be "Slack for Defence." That framing tells me almost everything I need to know about the leverage architecture, and it's the wrong answer. Slack scales because anyone with an email address can create a workspace in thirty seconds -- pure permissionless leverage. Coalition Systems needs FedRAMP authorization, IL-5/IL-6 certification, ITAR compliance, cleared engineers, and SCIFs before a single user touches the product. Every deployment requires a gatekeeper's signature. This is capital and labor leverage wearing a software costume. Mattermost has already walked this path -- $73.6M raised, $33.1M in annual revenue, 20,000+ DoD users -- and it required precisely the kind of proportional headcount scaling that I avoid. The defense procurement cycle doesn't reward small teams shipping fast; it rewards organizations that can navigate multi-year accreditation timelines. A four-person pre-seed company with ~$500K cannot parallelize that process.

The specific knowledge question at Coalition Systems has an unusual structure. Andrew Badger -- former DIA case officer, CIA Farm graduate, Afghanistan deployment, co-author with former Acting DIA Director David Shedd -- possesses genuinely non-replicable knowledge about how intelligence coordination actually works in the field. That's the kind of specific knowledge that takes a decade to accumulate and cannot be hired for. But Badger is Head of Strategic Partnerships, not CEO and not building the product. Vijay Pathak, the 23-year-old CEO, holds an Oxford DPhil in AI/ML focused on computational linguistics for political forecasting. His prior venture was Quantum Politica, an AI political strategy advisory. His CES 2026 talk was titled "Beyond the Algorithm: Gen Z's New Digital Habits." None of this is defense coordination specific knowledge. Pathak identified defense tech as a market opportunity and brought in Badger for domain credibility -- the specific knowledge sits in the wrong seat. If Pathak left, Badger's knowledge survives but has no product builder. If Badger left, the product loses its connection to the actual problem space. That's a fragile knowledge architecture.

The principal-agent diagnostic raises a flag I can't dismiss. A founder whose previous work was AI for political strategy, who speaks at CES about Gen Z digital habits, and who pivots to defense coordination at 23 -- that pattern reads more like someone who identified a fundable sector than someone who couldn't stop thinking about how allied forces coordinate. Compare this to how I evaluate principals: would Pathak be working on allied defense coordination without venture funding? The dossier provides no evidence he was. Meanwhile, CTO Freddie Wollen is described as a mechanical engineer with energy systems expertise -- not a software engineer building collaboration tools. The founding team's collective skill set doesn't naturally produce a real-time coordination platform for classified environments. H.R. McMaster's advisory endorsement lends credibility to the team's access, but advisory relationships are not founder-level principal commitment.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Badger's DIA network combined with McMaster's NSA credentials gives Coalition Systems a go-to-market channel that no other pre-seed defense startup can replicate. The NATO interoperability push post-Ukraine is a genuine structural tailwind -- allied forces are being forced to coordinate across organizational boundaries, and no incumbent owns that cross-national layer. Mattermost serves single organizations; Palantir ($2.87B U.S. government revenue in 2025) does analytics, not coordination; General Dynamics builds hardware-centric single-nation C2 systems. The inter-organizational coordination gap is real. If Coalition Systems landed a single NATO allied coordination contract, the network effects across allied nations would create switching costs that compound. For this bull case to work, Pathak would need to be a genuine principal who chose defense because the problem consumed him, the team would need to hire cleared software engineers who can build at speed, and they'd need to survive two to three years of procurement purgatory on $500K. Each of those is possible. All three together is a narrow path.

Coalition Systems triggers two of my explicit red flags: the business model depends on regulatory barriers as its primary moat, and the company is building a more specialized version of an existing product (Mattermost) in a market where the incumbent already has 20,000 DoD users. Regulatory moats are fragile -- "regulators can't do any cost-benefit tradeoff, they always only restrict." The compliance barriers that protect Coalition Systems today protect Mattermost more, because Mattermost already has the certifications. None of my known exceptions apply here: there's no physics-level leverage like Varda, no overwhelming technological capability like Clearview AI, no marketplace network effect ready to compound like Wish at its best. Coalition Systems is the structural inverse of Notion -- where Notion gave me extreme team-to-value leverage with permissionless distribution, Coalition Systems requires heavy headcount in a permissioned market where every user must be authorized by a government. That contrast is the core of my pass.

The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Permissionless Leverage Architecture and Specific Knowledge Productization. Coalition Systems operates in the most permissioned market on Earth with the CEO's specific knowledge pointing away from the product's core problem. Badger's genuine domain expertise partially compensates, but it's positioned as network access rather than product vision. You don't get asymmetric returns by deploying capital leverage into government procurement cycles -- you get long sales cycles and burn rate pressure on a $500K raise.
