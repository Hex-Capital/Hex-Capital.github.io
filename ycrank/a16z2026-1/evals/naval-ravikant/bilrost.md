# Bilrost -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Bilrost's most interesting feature isn't the $680K ARR claim or the AI agents -- it's the principal-agent trap sitting inside every incumbent. Finastra, FIS, Newgen -- their entire revenue model depends on manual loan processing volume. Seat-based licensing. Billable complexity. Silvia Chen walks into a bank and says "I'll automate 70% of your 90-hour workflow," and the incumbent vendor can't respond because automating that workflow destroys their own economics. This is structurally identical to why Perplexity could attack Google: the incumbent's agents are handcuffed to their P&L. Aravind Srinivas had no ad revenue to protect; Chen has no seat-licensing to cannibalize. When I see a principal with AI leverage facing off against agents trapped by their own business model, the structure alone gets my attention. Five of the top 10 U.S. banks in pilot scoping -- if even two convert, the power-law math starts working.

The specific knowledge question is where my conviction stalls. Silvia Chen "grew up in a CRE family." That's domain exposure, not domain mastery. Her career path runs through Draper Lab deep learning research, Bridgewater with David Ferrucci, Blockchain.com exchange product, crypto asset management at HIAI Capital, then OwnLayer -- an AI evaluation and monitoring platform she co-founded in February 2023 that has nothing to do with commercial real estate. Bilrost appears in 2025. The knowledge stack is assembled from adjacent domains: AI research here, fintech product there, CRE familiarity from childhood. Compare this to how Stack Overflow was built -- Joel Spolsky and Jeff Atwood had spent years writing for developers, accumulating specific knowledge about what developers actually needed that no market researcher could replicate. Chen's 99% accuracy threshold and the insight that "an AI tool that is right 80% of the time is worse than useless" suggest she understands something real about CRE underwriting culture. But I can't distinguish between specific knowledge forged through years of obsession and intelligent pattern-matching by a capable operator who identified an underserved vertical after her previous venture.

The OwnLayer pivot is the principal-agent diagnostic I keep returning to. A founder who spent 2023-2024 building AI DevOps tooling and then pivoted to CRE lending automation in 2025 is -- by my framework -- exhibiting search behavior, not principal behavior. Principals don't pivot across domains; they're already building the thing before they decide to start a company. The Blockchain.com credit product that "generated $100M+ in revenue in under 2 quarters" shows Chen can execute at scale in financial products, which is a valuable signal. But execution capability is not specific knowledge. Peter Hsu's 20 years of platform engineering and prior work at Cogniac (computer vision) adds technical depth without CRE-specific depth. The unnamed founding engineer with "3 successful exits including one Nasdaq-listed company" is intriguing but unverifiable.

The leverage architecture is genuinely strong within the product layer. Three AI agent modules handling document ingestion, underwriting standardization, and portfolio monitoring -- that's code and AI doing work that previously required human loan processors at zero marginal cost per additional document processed. The 6-day integration timeline versus multi-month onboarding compresses the deployment friction dramatically. But enterprise bank distribution is labor leverage wearing a software costume. Compliance reviews, IT security assessments, procurement cycles -- every new customer requires relationship capital and custom integration work. The strategic investor providing "direct access to decision-makers across 73 banks" could compress this, functioning as a distribution lever that most pre-seed companies don't have. If that relationship is real, it's the kind of compound advantage that accelerates everything -- but it's unnamed and unverifiable.

The bull case that would move me: Silvia Chen's CRE family exposure is deeper than the dossier reveals -- she spent formative years inside commercial lending operations, absorbing the tacit knowledge of what makes underwriting workflows break. The $680K ARR in 5 weeks, even if partially composed of signed contracts rather than collected revenue, indicates genuine pull at a velocity that's hard to manufacture. The institutional context persistence -- where each processed deal deepens Bilrost's understanding of that lender's specific models and preferences -- creates a compounding data moat that horizontal competitors like Ocrolus ($80M+ raised, 500+ customers in broader lending) would need years to replicate in CRE specifically. If Chen's family background gave her the pattern recognition to build the right product from day one while Blooma ($15M raised) spent years on deal screening before touching end-to-end processing, that's specific knowledge revealing itself through product decisions rather than resume lines.

The compound interest test partially passes. Institutional context persistence across deals, switching costs from LOS integration, and the 99% accuracy standard calibrated per customer all create advantages that deepen over time. But the compounding is institutional, not network-based -- each bank's data makes Bilrost better for that bank, not for all banks simultaneously. This is linear compounding, not exponential. The access democratization angle is weak: Bilrost sells to the top 10 U.S. banks, not to the underserved lenders who currently can't afford sophisticated underwriting tools. This is an efficiency product for incumbents, not a gatekeeper-removal product for the excluded.

My conviction sits at the boundary between interesting and investable. The incumbent principal-agent trap is real and durable -- Finastra genuinely cannot build this without destroying its own economics. The team-to-value ratio at 5 people is exactly the structure I look for. But the specific knowledge gap -- assembled from adjacent domains rather than forged inside the problem -- and the OwnLayer pivot keep me from writing a check today. The dimension that would flip me is principal authenticity: thirty minutes with Silvia Chen would reveal whether the CRE family background represents childhood-deep specific knowledge or surface-level domain familiarity. The $680K ARR, if verified by a third party and shown to be collected revenue rather than signed intent, would independently push this into invest territory. Without those verifications, Bilrost earns a hold.
