# Sequence Markets -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know about the structural moment -- or rather, the absence of one. Talos raised at a $1.5B valuation. FalconX sits at $8B. CoinRoutes has been shipping smart order routing since 2017. When I backed Coinbase, there was a genuine structural vacuum -- no credible, compliant US exchange existed, and the regulatory complexity of building one deterred most software investors. That was a market where being early created a compounding moat. Sequence Markets is entering a market where the incumbents have hundreds of millions in funding, established exchange integrations, and institutional client bases already generating revenue. Smart order routing is not a novel concept -- it's been the core architecture of equities execution for decades, and multiple teams have already ported it to crypto. The question I keep asking is: what has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this specific execution-layer opportunity structurally different now versus three years ago? The dossier's "why now" is almost entirely inferred -- venue proliferation, institutional adoption, regulatory clarity -- but these have been true and accelerating since 2020. Nothing in the data suggests a capability threshold was just crossed or a structural vacancy just opened.

The bull case would go something like this: Talos and FalconX serve large institutions with enterprise-scale minimums and full-stack prime brokerage. 1inch only routes across decentralized venues. There's a genuine underserved tier of active crypto traders -- prop desks, crypto-native firms, sophisticated retail -- who need multi-venue execution across both CEXs and DEXs but can't access institutional platforms. If Sequence nails developer-friendly API integration with genuinely superior routing quality, it could become the "Stripe of crypto execution" -- the default infrastructure layer that brokers and apps plug into. The non-custodial architecture is smart because it reduces regulatory surface area. And if the data flywheel from execution volume produces measurably better routing over time, that creates a real moat. For this to work, though, Sequence would need to demonstrate that their AI routing produces execution quality materially better than what CoinRoutes has spent seven years refining. I haven't seen that evidence.

Peter Bai's background is the strongest signal in the dossier. TMX Group and University Pension Plan Ontario ($12.8B AUM) are directly relevant -- he's worked on market microstructure and execution quality in institutional settings. That's real founder-market fit, not just adjacent experience. But the co-founder situation concerns me. Muhammad Awan's YC profile says "I like building cool projects" -- and his public footprint reveals no documented technical background, no prior companies, no visible contributions. For a company building sub-millisecond execution infrastructure that competes with teams backed by hundreds of millions, the technical co-founder's credentials matter enormously. I need to understand who's actually building the routing engine.

On distribution trajectory, there's a plausible path: an execution API that brokers integrate becomes a distribution channel for analytics, risk tools, settlement orchestration, and compliance products. That's the multi-product expansion I look for. But it's entirely theoretical -- no evidence the founders have articulated this vision, no partnerships announced, no integrations live. The "$15B+ aggregated liquidity" claim on the website suggests a working product connected to venues, which is a positive execution signal. But the "controlled rollouts, quality over quantity" GTM language is a red flag in my framework. Founders who haven't thought about distribution from day one -- who treat quality product as sufficient for market capture -- are describing a strategy that doesn't scale against well-funded competitors who already own institutional relationships.

The single-miracle test is where this breaks down. What's the one thing that must work? If it's "build better routing technology than Talos and CoinRoutes," that's plausible but requires outperforming teams with multi-year head starts. If it's "win distribution in the underserved mid-market," that requires both competitive routing quality AND effective go-to-market -- which starts looking like two miracles. If it's "AI-powered routing that produces structurally better execution," the claim is unsubstantiated and "AI" in algorithmic trading is decades old, not a new capability threshold. The company's path to success seems to require simultaneously building differentiated technology, securing reliable venue integrations across numerous exchanges, and acquiring traders away from existing platforms. That's compounding obstacles, and I've learned to pass on those regardless of market size.
