# Whitespace -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Whitespace is a horizontal multi-agent wrapper in a steady-state $8.442T US wholesale market (MDM, 2025) with zero disclosed revenue and demo-data logos -- which means its highest-scoring dimension (a real but generic LLM-agent capability catalyst) is dragged down by its lowest (the silence on growth that I publicly call "something's fundamentally broken"). A 2-person London team -- Alex Tung (ex-Chief of Staff/Head of GTM at Ankar AI) and Leon Yao (identity not even confirmed in the dossier) -- is selling an "AI Operating System" into a market where Pepper has already raised $128.1M, Choco $301M, and Proton.ai $24M against the same buyer persona. That is the analytical signature.

Anduril (2022) is the load-bearing analogy for Whitespace on the market-re-opening axis because both pitches lean on the same shape -- a recent capability step-function (autonomy stacks for Anduril; GPT-4-class function calling for Whitespace) being applied to a sleepy, integration-heavy distribution channel. The mechanism that made Anduril work was that Ukraine was a 24-month catalyst *specific to the buyer* (DoD procurement reform), and Trae Stephens and Palmer Luckey were uniquely positioned to harvest it. Whitespace's catalyst -- LLM agents getting reliable in 2024-2025 -- is not specific to wholesale distributors at all; it is the same catalyst available to Proton.ai, Pepper, Choco, and any Epicor/Infor AI module shipping right now. There is no "Ukraine moment" for wholesale distribution in the dossier -- no named regulatory shift, no incumbent collapse, no distributor-side urgency event. Whitespace borrows the catalyst's *existence* but not its *specificity*, which is why it places well below Anduril on this rung.

"AI company with no growth curve disclosed" is the failure pattern I named on Tim Ferriss #863 -- and Whitespace's dossier walks straight into it. The site's traction copy -- "10 hours saved per person per week, 5% order-volume uplift, 1% margin improvement" -- has no customer attribution, the named logos (Mercer & Co., Halton Foods, Brightway Retail) appear in *demo data contexts* per the dossier's WebFetch, and there is no MRR, no ARR, no design partner count, no pilot-to-paid conversion, nothing. Harvey at the same stage was already showing dollar velocity that let me size up at consensus prices; Whitespace is showing a feature list and a "pays for itself in 3 months" assertion. In an AI cycle where real revenue is easy to disclose, the silence is the signal.

Founder-ships-between-meetings compounds or breaks over 18 months as the rhythm decides whether the product gets to its third or fourth ICP without burning the seed. Alex Tung is a GTM operator out of Ankar AI -- a real Atomico/Index-backed company -- which is useful for the first ten distributor conversations but is not a shipping artifact. Leon Yao's likely InstaDeep/NeurIPS profile is research-mode, not iteration-mode, and the dossier explicitly flags that his identity could not be confirmed against Whitespace at all. Neither founder has a named shipped consumer or B2B product in the dossier -- no GitHub, no prior startup, no side-project trail. The "grew up in families running wholesale distribution" line supplies domain context, which I treat as table stakes, not as evidence of execution rhythm. The mechanism that would make Whitespace work -- weekly model improvements per-customer as ERPs get integrated -- requires builder-velocity I cannot verify from the dossier.

For Whitespace to flip a tier on index-like distribution position, the dossier would need to show that the "living model" on top of ERP + inbox becomes the layer every wholesale-distribution AI agent must integrate against -- i.e., the Stripe-on-e-commerce shape. Today the dossier shows the opposite: Pepper is already serving 500+ distributors with ~$30B GMV (BusinessWire, 2026), Choco owns food-distribution order digitization, and Proton.ai has a four-year head start on distributor sales-rep recommendations. Whitespace would have to be the workflow path Pepper and Choco eventually integrate *against*, not a competitor to them. Nothing in the dossier suggests that posture -- no SDK, no integrations marketplace, no developer surface, no published API. Without that, Whitespace must beat three funded vertical players on features, which is the exact "winner-take-all but not by you" anti-pattern from my Lyft pass.

Decagon (2023) is the single closest portfolio parallel for Whitespace on the single-miracle axis, and the comparison is unflattering. Decagon isolated its miracle to *LLM accuracy on customer-support intents* and treated everything else -- CRM integrations, GTM, vertical expansion -- as execution. Whitespace requires at least three: reliable end-to-end LLM agents across order entry / forecasting / RMA / credit / supplier ETA chasing; deep connectors and a "living model" across heterogeneous ERPs (Epicor, SAP, Infor, NetSuite, spreadsheets) built by two people; and a GTM wedge that wins against Pepper, Choco, and Proton.ai in overlapping accounts. Compound those probabilities and the math does not pencil at a consensus YC pre-seed price.

My 36-month forecast for Whitespace: acqui-hired by an ERP incumbent (most likely Epicor or Infor) or a vertical platform (most likely Pepper) for $5-15M, *or* pivoted to a single non-food UK/EU vertical (industrial or building-products distribution) with <$2M ARR. The mechanism is straightforward -- horizontal multi-agent OS pitches in fragmented distribution channels collapse into either a wedge vertical or a tuck-in for a player who already owns the customer relationship, and Whitespace's 2-person team plus undifferentiated catalyst makes the tuck-in the more probable branch.

The strongest bull case against me is that Whitespace's founders are *domain-native* in a way Pepper and Choco's leadership are not -- "families running wholesale distribution businesses" plus Ankar AI GTM is genuinely uncommon, and non-food wholesale (industrial, electrical, building products) is meaningfully under-served by the funded competitors who are food-vertical-locked. If that domain channel converts cold distributor outreach into paid pilots at 3x the rate Pepper sees, the "living model" per customer could create real switching costs before the incumbents notice. I dismantle it on the dossier: there is no named non-food distributor as a paying customer, no pilot count, no shipping artifact from either founder, and the demo-logo framing is the literal pattern I labeled false signal. **Flip condition**: $200K+ MRR with two named non-food distributor logos paying within 6 months, *or* a credible Series A markup led by a brand-name enterprise-software fund within 90 days of Demo Day. Until either lands, Whitespace is a pass -- and a known-exception override (Anduril/Color/Mistral) does not apply because the catalyst is generic, not founder-specific, and no operator-architect pattern is in evidence.
