# Prototyping.io -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is how many companies are already doing this. Xometry does $687M in revenue. Protolabs does $533M. Fictiv just got acquired for $350M. In the YC ecosystem alone, RMFG has shipped 100K+ parts, Jiga has NASA and Siemens as customers, Toolpath raised $10M, and Korso is in a recent batch. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where the incumbents are publicly traded and the other startups have already raised tens of millions, I ask: what do these two people know that all those companies don't? And I don't have a good answer. "AI-native DFM analysis" is a feature, not a secret. Xometry already has an AI quoting engine processing millions of parts. This isn't schlep blindness -- the schlep of manufacturing quality, supplier vetting, and tolerances is something multiple well-capitalized teams are already grinding through.

The origin of the idea is where I get stuck. Revanth has genuine manufacturing credentials -- IIT silver medalist, aerospace research at UT Austin, time at Schlumberger doing mechanical and manufacturing engineering. He's plausibly lived through the pain of uploading CAD files to multiple vendors and waiting days for quotes. But plausibly is doing a lot of work in that sentence. There's no specific moment described, no "I was at Schlumberger and wasted three weeks getting quotes for a part I knew was manufacturable." The problem statement on LinkedIn -- hardware teams are "still stuck dealing with slow, fragmented processes" -- reads like something you'd write after studying the market, not after years of personal frustration boiling over. When Drew Houston forgot his USB drive on the bus, that was a specific moment. When the Collisons tried to integrate payment processing for a side project and got angry, that was a specific moment. I don't see the specific moment here.

The co-founder dynamic concerns me. Prerit's LinkedIn says he "recently joined forces with Revanth." No shared employer, no shared university, no visible prior relationship. I've seen this pattern destroy companies. In one YC batch, nine teams added co-founders they didn't know well between interview and start of batch. All nine fell apart within a year. The co-founder relationship needs to predate the startup idea, not emerge from it. Revanth brings manufacturing domain knowledge. Prerit brings software and AI systems experience -- Microsoft AI, published distributed systems research, a Go-based Kubernetes controller on GitHub. That's a rational pairing on paper. But rational pairings assembled for the purpose of starting a company are brittle under pressure in ways that organic partnerships aren't.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. The incumbents have a genuine business model tension: Xometry makes money on transaction margins from orders, so building a DFM analysis layer that helps engineers fix manufacturability issues *before* ordering could actually reduce Xometry's revenue per customer. That's the kind of structural conflict that lets a startup wedge in. If Prototyping.io builds a tool that mechanical engineers genuinely love -- something that catches design problems before they become expensive manufacturing failures -- and then uses that trust relationship to capture the order, that's a different go-to-market than anyone else is running. Revanth's materials science background (nanowire membranes, aerogel mechanical properties) and Prerit's systems engineering chops are a legitimate combination for building this. If they had deep organic conviction, a battle-tested co-founder relationship, and even twenty engineers using a prototype regularly, I'd be more interested. But those ifs are load-bearing.

The website advertises tolerances of ±0.0001" and part sizes up to 34 feet across six manufacturing processes, with no minimum order quantity. For a two-person pre-launch company, this reads like aspirational marketing rather than demonstrated capability. There's no supplier network validation, no customer testimonials, no quality certifications visible. The gap between what's promised and what can be delivered by two people in early access is wide. I'd rather see a company that does one process extraordinarily well -- the way RMFG started with sheet metal only -- than one that claims everything on day one.

This is a pass for me. The founders are credentialed, the market is real, and the business model tension with incumbents is genuine. But the idea feels assembled from market analysis rather than discovered through experience, the co-founders recently met, the category is consensus rather than contrarian, and there is zero evidence of user pull. When every VC and every accelerator is excited about AI-native manufacturing, the upside has already been partially priced. The companies in this space that will win are the ones with founders who have been obsessing over manufacturing workflows for years before AI made it trendy -- not teams that recognized a hot category and paired up to attack it.
