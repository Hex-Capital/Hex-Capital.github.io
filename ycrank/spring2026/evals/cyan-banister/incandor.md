# Incandor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Incandor is two Stanford CS grads — Matthew Yekell out of Vista Equity and Cboe, Luc Rosenzweig out of NVIDIA, Apple, and Epic — building a "behavioral map" API into the most crowded vertical in fraud, and the dimensional signature that jumps off the page is clear-CEO-structure on top and zero-tactile-artifact on the bottom. That pairing tells me what kind of company Incandor is: a competent, well-credentialed software pair attacking BioCatch's $185M ARR fortress with an abstraction layer I cannot install, photograph, or hand to a friend. I am never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game Incandor seems to be playing — "programmable behavioral intelligence" — is not the game it is actually playing, which is a Stanford-CS pair going door-to-door at JPMorgan procurement against a Permira-owned incumbent that just signed Wells Fargo.

On founder-surfaced-from-lived-experience, my ladder runs Palmer Luckey at **Anduril** (2017) shipping Oculus from his garage before defense at the top, Demis Hassabis at **DeepMind** (~2013) in the middle as the polymath exception that overrode pedigree, and my **consensus YC AI-agent pass** — two ex-FAANG PMs, Stanford CS, $200B TAM slide — at the bottom. Incandor sits one notch above that bottom rung, not because Yekell and Rosenzweig are unserious, but because nothing in the dossier says either of them was a fraud analyst at 2 a.m. chasing a mule ring, watched a parent get socially-engineered into an APP fraud, or shipped a fraud product to scale before. Yekell's Vista Equity and Cboe lines give him buyer-side credibility with banks; they are not the autobiographical contact moment that put Luckey in front of defense or Hanke in front of location data. The dossier reads "discovered the problem in a memo," and I score it that way.

On physical-world verifiability, my ladder runs **Flock Safety** (~2018) — the camera I noticed on a Wi-Fi name at the Four Seasons — at the top, **HQ Trivia** (2017) which I could literally play and feel the viral pull of in the middle, and pure developer-tools abstractions at the bottom. Incandor sits at the bottom rung. The product is an API into the fraud-ops stack of regulated banks; there is no Pokémon Go to play, no scooter to ride, no camera to scan. The dossier confirms it: zero design partners named, zero customer logos, no Product Hunt, no ridable artifact. My gift is making physical connections, and Incandor offers me nothing physical to connect to.

On magically-weird-non-consensus, my ladder runs **Crusoe** (~2018) burning gas flares for Bitcoin at the top, **Mindbloom** ketamine therapy in the middle, and the **consensus YC AI darling** at the bottom. Incandor lives just above the bottom — behavioral biometrics is not weird; the dossier itself names ten incumbents (BioCatch, NuData/Mastercard, BehavioSec/LexisNexis, Sardine, Callsign, ThreatMark, TypingDNA, Feedzai, DataVisor) and Sardine just raised $70M Series C in February 2025 with 130% YoY ARR growth. The "no-label cold-start + programmable API" wedge is a real wedge, but it is a feature differentiator inside a category, not a category nobody else will touch. Crusoe was three uninvestable things stapled together; Incandor is one moderately investable thing stapled to a Stanford CS pedigree.

On single-unambiguous-CEO, my ladder runs **Anduril** (Palmer clear from day one) at the top, **SpaceX** (Musk clear) in the middle, and **HQ Trivia** (Yusupov/Kroll, the feud that calibrates me) at the bottom. Incandor sits closer to the top — Yekell is named CEO, Rosenzweig is CTO, and the Biz/Tiz division of labor between the finance-network founder and the systems founder is unusually clean for a Stanford-pair pre-seed. Equity split and tiebreaker doc are not disclosed, so I cannot push it to the top rung, but this is the dimension where Incandor most plainly clears my screen. On capital-intensive-unsexy-regulated, the ladder runs **SpaceX** (rockets) at the top, **Vigil Labs** (August 2025, regulated finance) in the middle, and the AI SaaS wrapper at the bottom. Incandor is software-only but the buyer is a regulated bank and the data is GDPR-classified biometric — sits between Vigil Labs and the bottom, with no consent flow or DPA disclosed and no hardware moat compounding with time.

My 36-month forecast: Incandor reaches $1–3M ARR with two or three mid-tier fintech logos, fails to crack a top-five US bank, and is acqui-hired by Sardine, Feedzai, or LexisNexis for $25–60M as a tuck-in for the cold-start angle by mid-2029. The mechanism is exactly the high/low signature — clear CEO and clean execution will land a small book of fintech business, but the absence of lived-fraud origin and the absence of any tactile artifact mean Incandor cannot generate the founder-led pull that displaces BioCatch in a Wells Fargo procurement cycle.

The strongest case against my pass: Sardine's 130% YoY ARR proves fintech fraud spend is exploding, Yekell's Cboe and Vista network is a non-trivial bank rolodex for a pre-seed, and the cold-start no-label wedge is a genuinely sharp answer to the cold-start problem that every new neobank actually has. A bull would say I am pattern-matching Incandor to my "consensus YC pass" template when the underlying category dynamics are closer to my **Affirm** exception — a fintech category where a credentialed founder earns the buyer relationship that outsiders cannot. The dossier evidence that wins for me is the competitor table: BioCatch $185M ARR plus Wells Fargo, Sardine $145M raised, NuData inside Mastercard, BehavioSec inside LexisNexis. Incandor is not entering a green field; it is entering a four-way crossfire with no founder-of-the-place social license. The single condition that flips my verdict: Yekell or Rosenzweig surfaces a personal-contact origin story with fraud — a parent defrauded, a prior fraud-ops shift at a bank, a mule-ring chase they ran — or names two bank design partners with active usage. Without one of those, Incandor is the polished networker presentation I have learned to walk past.
