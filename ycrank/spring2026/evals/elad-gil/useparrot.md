# Parrot -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Parrot is a clean single-miracle architecture stapled to a customer-side revenue story, which is the wrong half of the Decagon template — the dossier discloses a pilot shop's revenue going from $150K to $180K MoM, but it does not disclose a single dollar of Parrot's own ARR, and that asymmetry is the whole evaluation. Phillip Baek and Liam McBride are running the right shape of company (voice + email agents writing back to CCC ONE, Audatex, Mitchell 1) in the wrong-shape market position, and the dossier evidence pins Parrot well below the rungs I'd need to write a check.

**Decagon** (2023) is the load-bearing comparison on the dimension where Parrot looks best — single-miracle architecture. Decagon needed one thing to be true (LLM accuracy on customer-support turns) and the rest was GTM and writeback to Zendesk/Salesforce; I funded it. Parrot's miracle is structurally identical — voice-agent reliability on multi-party supplement and parts-sourcing calls — and the dossier correctly isolates it: no new physics, no new regulatory creation, just "did the model get good enough in 2024–2025." That's a real single miracle and it's why Parrot doesn't trip my science-project filter. Where Parrot diverges from Decagon is the regulatory texture the dossier flags — "state-specific insurance regulations and labor-rate disputes" inside supplement negotiation — which doesn't add a second miracle but does add a per-state GTM tax Decagon never paid.

**Lyft** Series C is the failure pattern I have to test Parrot against on revenue velocity, because the mechanism that killed my Lyft thesis was structural #2 economics in a market where the incumbent already owned the buyer relationship — and Parrot's dossier shows exactly that pattern in miniature. The "+20% monthly revenue" and "supplement turnaround 5→2 days" numbers in the dossier are *the pilot shop's* metrics, not Parrot's. Harvey's $3B→$5B→$8B arc was Harvey's own revenue compounding, which is what "just working" means in dollars (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). A single 90-day pilot with no second shop, no MRR figure, no pricing page on useparrot.com, and no retention data is exactly the "false signal vs. just working" ambiguity I called out — and in an AI company, silence on Parrot's own ARR is itself the signal.

Mechanism check on the catalyst dimension: Parrot's dossier names "voice-AI low-latency thresholds in 2024–2025" and "persistent labor shortages" as the why-now, both tagged `[Inferred]` with "no founder or analyst statement of why-now found." Compound that over 18 months — voice-AI latency is a *horizontal* catalyst that opens dozens of vertical agent markets simultaneously, which means Parrot's catalyst is shared with every other YC voice-agent company in S26, including Rote, which the dossier names as directly overlapping. A catalyst that opens the door for everyone is a catalyst that doesn't pick a winner; it's the inverse of defense-post-Ukraine, where the catalyst was specific to defense procurement and Anduril was uniquely positioned for it.

Counterfactual on index position: for Parrot to be an index on a compounding category rather than a feature inside one, the auto collision repair management software market would need to be compounding meaningfully, and the dossier prices it at ~$2B with ~10% CAGR — that's a steady-state market, not Stripe's e-commerce or Coinbase's crypto. The score flips a tier only if Parrot shifts position from "compatible with CCC ONE, Audatex, Mitchell 1" to "the writeback layer every collision-AI vendor routes through," which would require the kind of certified-partnership status the dossier explicitly says is "advertised but not confirmed."

Single-anchor parallel on founder shipping: **Perplexity** (2022) is my template — Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing we'd discussed built. Phillip Baek and Liam McBride previously co-founded Alchemy AI together (life-sciences computer vision) and Parrot appears to be a pivot of that company, per LinkedIn profiles that still list "Alchemy AI." That's a real working relationship and a shipped prior artifact, which beats pedigree-only founders, but neither founder has a public GitHub, a Twitter/X presence, or any documented prior collision-repair or insurance-claims operating experience — and supplement negotiation is the part of the product where domain knowledge buys you the most. Parrot's founders look like Decagon-shaped operators dropped into an Abridge-shaped domain without the Abridge-shaped clinical credentialing.

Forecast — 36 months out, Parrot is acqui-hired by CCC, Mitchell, or Solera in the $20–60M range as a voice-agent feature bolted onto their estimating OS, OR loses the supplement-negotiation slice to Rote (YC) and pivots into pure parts-sourcing call automation at sub-$2M ARR. The mechanism producing this is the dimensional signature: Parrot has the clean single-miracle architecture to *build* the product, but Parrot does not have an independent distribution position, and the dossier names three incumbents that "control API access and are themselves shipping AI features" — that's the substitution path written in the dossier's own words.

The strongest bull case: the pilot shop kept paying long enough to generate 90 days of data, the +20% revenue lift and 5→2 day supplement turnaround are operational metrics shops actually buy on, and Decagon and Abridge (2023) both looked exactly this thin when I wrote checks — single pilot, two founders, infrastructure layer on a recently-catalyzed category. The dossier's "infrastructure on top of hard thing" pattern fits Parrot: voice agents on top of CCC/Audatex/Mitchell, the same shape as Anduril-on-defense-hardware or Color-on-sequencing. That case stings, and the flip condition is concrete: if Parrot discloses five paying shops with >80% 90-day retention and one certified integration partnership with CCC, Mitchell, or Solera within 90 days of YC Demo Day, the catalyst-velocity-distribution math rebalances and I'd reopen the file. Absent that, Parrot is a Decagon-shaped product attacking a Lyft-shaped market structure, and I pass.
