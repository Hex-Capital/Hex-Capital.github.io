# Fed10 -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the consensus problem. "AI agents that replace your consultants" is not a contrarian bet in 2026 -- it's the single most crowded thesis in technology. Every pitch deck I see claims to replace expensive human professionals with AI. FiscalNote already launched PolicyNote. Quorum is adding AI features. USLege is doing nearly the same thing with venture backing. When five companies are racing to apply commodity LLMs to public legislative data, you're not getting paid for being right first -- you're competing in a sprint where the starting gun already fired and the incumbents have a head start on distribution. The asymmetric upside I look for comes from bets where most smart people think you're wrong. Nobody thinks AI won't eventually automate legislative analysis. The only question is who captures the value, and that question favors the company with existing enterprise relationships, not the one that still needs to build them.

The founders have genuine specific knowledge, which is the one thing that keeps me engaged. Armand passed legislation before he could vote and served as the youngest gubernatorial appointee. All three co-founders worked together on actual lobbying before starting the company. This isn't theoretical -- they've sat in the room, navigated the process, and understand the workflow from the inside. That practitioner-to-builder path is exactly the kind of specific knowledge I value. When I backed Stack Overflow, part of the thesis was that developers building tools for developers have non-transferable insight into what actually matters. These founders have analogous insight into what policy professionals actually need versus what tracking tools currently provide. But I have to be honest about the depth: this is early-career knowledge. They're young, and while precocious accomplishment is a signal of capability, it's not the same as a decade of accumulated pattern recognition about how legislation actually affects businesses. A smart generalist with two years in government affairs could articulate similar pain points.

The leverage architecture is where I struggle most. Replacing a $500/hr consultant with software is cost arbitrage, not leverage creation. When I invested in Replit, the thesis was that it enables people who would never have become programmers to build software -- a fundamentally new capability. When I backed AngelList, it created capital leverage that didn't exist before: any accredited investor could access startup deals previously reserved for a closed network. Fed10 makes an existing service cheaper. That's valuable, but it's not creating a new form of leverage. It's not enabling a new type of person to do a new type of work. Organizations that couldn't afford policy consultants might now afford Fed10's software, which is meaningful -- but the output is the same (policy analysis), just at lower cost. The business scales with software economics, which gives it baseline code leverage, but there are no network effects, no protocol dynamics, no platform compounding. Each customer's use doesn't make the product better for the next customer.

The bull case I have to wrestle with: FiscalNote is down 97% from its merger price and undergoing a CEO transition. Quorum is a traditional SaaS company that may struggle to go AI-native. The incumbents are structurally conflicted -- their professional services revenue would be cannibalized by genuine AI automation. A small, AI-native team with domain expertise could move faster than distracted incumbents, build a product that's ten times cheaper, and capture the next generation of buyers who never would have paid $50K/year for legacy platforms. If the founders' policy network gives them distribution into trade associations and mid-market companies -- the underserved segment below Quorum's Fortune 100 customers -- they could build a real business. The market is growing (9.1% CAGR to $3B by 2033), and the volume of legislation is accelerating (1,208 AI-related bills across all states in 2025 alone), which overwhelms human analysts. If Fed10 achieves meaningfully higher accuracy than generic LLM applications through their domain expertise, that accuracy gap could compound into a defensible position.

But that bull case requires Fed10 to win on execution in a race with no structural advantages. The underlying data is public. The LLMs are commodity. The domain expertise helps but doesn't constitute a moat -- FiscalNote has thousands of policy analysts whose knowledge could be encoded into training data. I look for companies where the technology compounds -- where each iteration, each user, each data point makes the system measurably harder to replicate. I don't see that mechanism here. The defensibility is the team, not the technology, and the team's knowledge advantage erodes as competitors hire their own policy experts to fine-tune their own models. This is a competent team in a real market building a product that will work -- but it's a linear execution race in a consensus category, and that's not the shape of bet that produces asymmetric returns.
