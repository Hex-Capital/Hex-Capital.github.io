# Andustry -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

€1M+ in transaction volume on a two-person team is the number that stops me on Andustry's page. Two founders moving physical industrial parts across 100+ countries — that's an output-per-person ratio that would make Cursor's team raise an eyebrow. But here's where I pull the thread: transaction volume is not revenue, and atoms are not bits. Every completed order on Andustry involves physical goods crossing borders — tariffs, compliance, quality verification, logistics. Notion can serve its next million users with zero additional marginal cost. Andustry cannot fulfill its next million orders without the physical world imposing its tax. The AI agents handle quote normalization and supplier matching at near-zero marginal cost, but the moment goods ship, you're back in the domain of labor leverage and regulatory friction. Andustry's leverage architecture is a software layer riding on an irreducibly physical substrate, and that ceiling matters.

Johann Stürken's family background in trading goods and supplier sourcing — noted on Andustry's YC page — is the specific knowledge signal I'm looking for. If he grew up watching his family navigate the exact chaos of cross-border industrial sourcing, that's knowledge absorbed through osmosis over years, not acquirable through a consulting engagement. His RWTH Aachen mechanical engineering degree means he can read the specs he's matching. That combination — lived family experience plus technical fluency in the product domain — is harder to replicate than it appears on a resume. But then I see McKinsey, Airbus, Capgemini Invent. Consulting is the anti-specific-knowledge career. It explicitly trains generalists. The question I'd need thirty minutes with Johann to answer: did he leave consulting because he couldn't stop thinking about how broken his family's sourcing workflow was, or did he identify procurement as a fundable AI application? The dossier can't resolve this, and the difference between those two stories is the difference between a principal and an agent with good domain context. Het Dave brings CS from IIT Jodhpur — solid technical foundation, but no evidence of procurement-specific expertise or prior projects in this space.

Xometry at $687M revenue and ~$2.5B market cap is Andustry's existential question, and the principal-agent analysis cuts in Andustry's favor more than a surface read suggests. Xometry is the manufacturer-of-record — they fulfill orders through their own network, owning the margin and the liability. Pivoting to a pure sourcing marketplace would cannibalize that core model. This is structurally analogous to why Google's ad-revenue-encumbered teams couldn't build what Perplexity built — the incumbent's P&L makes their employees agents of the existing revenue stream. Andustry, with no legacy model to protect, can offer zero upfront fees and pay-on-delivery pricing that Xometry's economics won't support. But the analogy has a limit: Google had 10,000x Perplexity's resources and still couldn't move, because search ads are 80% of revenue. Xometry's manufacturing-marketplace revenue is less structurally locked — they could spin up a sourcing arm under a different brand without existential risk. And Thomasnet, which Xometry acquired for $300M, already has the supplier database.

The co-founder relationship triggers a pattern I watch for. Het Dave attended IIT Jodhpur; Johann Stürken attended RWTH Aachen and TUM. No shared employer, no shared university, no overlapping history found in the dossier. Andustry's founders appear to have connected around the idea rather than through pre-existing trust. This doesn't kill a company — plenty of great teams formed this way — but it increases internal principal-agent risk. When the inevitable hard decisions arrive about pivoting the business model or giving up personal runway, co-founders with deep pre-existing relationships navigate those moments differently than co-founders whose bond is the company itself. At pre-seed with two people, the relationship IS the company.

Andustry's access democratization angle is real and maps to my portfolio pattern. SMEs with 20–1,000 employees currently navigate hundreds of supplier contacts, week-long quoting cycles, and expensive brokers to source mid-size industrial parts. Andustry's AI agents and 500k+ claimed verified supplier network collapse that gatekeeping — giving a 50-person German manufacturer the sourcing power of a Fortune 500 procurement department. AngelList gave every accredited investor access to deals previously restricted to Sand Hill Road insiders. Andustry gives every mid-market manufacturer access to a global supply base previously requiring enterprise procurement infrastructure. The structural parallel is genuine, but the democratization here is operational (automating a manual workflow) rather than architectural (removing a legal or capital barrier). That distinction matters for durability — operational advantages get competed away; structural ones compound.

The strongest bull case for Andustry: Johann's family-embedded domain knowledge, combined with a functioning product processing €1M+ in volume with two people across 100+ countries, shows a team punching far above its weight class. The AI-agent procurement model is genuinely novel — not a wrapper on GPT-4, but an autonomous workflow replacing human brokers. The Xometry channel-conflict moat is real. And the verified supplier network, if genuinely engaged rather than passively listed, becomes a compounding data asset. I take this seriously. But the core AI-matching technology is technically reproducible — Leverage AI, Zip, Fairmarkit, and a dozen YC-batch-mates are building adjacent products. The 500k+ verified companies claim is unverifiable and likely inflated by passive listings. And Andustry's business involves atoms moving across borders at thin margins — every transaction carries marginal cost, regulatory exposure, and operational complexity that scales linearly with volume. This is not the leverage structure that produces power-law returns from a small team. The condition that would flip me: evidence that Andustry's supplier engagement is genuinely active — repeat transaction data showing that the same suppliers fulfill multiple orders through the platform, creating switching costs and a data flywheel that competitors can't replicate by scraping Thomasnet.

Andustry scores modestly on the two dimensions I weight heaviest. Specific knowledge is present but diluted by a consulting-generalist career path — Johann's family background is the load-bearing signal, but the dossier lacks depth on what that background actually entailed. Permissionless leverage is constrained by the physics of moving physical goods across borders. The team-to-value ratio is Andustry's brightest signal today, but the business model's operational demands will likely erode it as volume scales. A competent team in a real market, building something useful — but not the leverage structure I write checks for.
