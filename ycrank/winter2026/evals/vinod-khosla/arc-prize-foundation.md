# ARC Prize Foundation -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately: the most consequential thing François Chollet could do with his insight into measuring intelligence isn't to build a better ruler -- it's to build the intelligence itself. And he knows this, which is why he co-founded Ndea, a for-profit AI lab, alongside this nonprofit benchmark foundation. I'm being asked to evaluate the scorekeeper, not the player. ARC Prize Foundation is a 501(c)(3) with no equity, no revenue model, and no path to financial returns. Even if I wanted to write a check, there's nothing to buy. This isn't an investment opportunity -- it's a donation request dressed in YC branding. And I don't evaluate donations against the same framework I use for bets that either expire worthless or produce transformational outcomes.

Set the nonprofit structure aside for a moment and ask my foundational question: if ARC Prize Foundation succeeds completely, does the world operate differently? The answer is nuanced but ultimately unsatisfying. If they establish the definitive benchmark for artificial general intelligence, AI research gets better directed. Labs stop optimizing for memorization and start optimizing for genuine reasoning. That's valuable -- but it's infrastructure for someone else's transformation. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown wasn't building the thermometer to measure how close we were to replacing meat -- he was building the replacement. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, they weren't measuring proximity to fusion -- they were building the reactor. ARC Prize Foundation measures proximity to AGI. Ndea builds toward it. The consequence of success here is a well-respected nonprofit that influences research agendas -- not a company that reshapes a trillion-dollar system. The problem they're addressing is real, but they've positioned themselves at the wrong point in the value chain to capture or create transformational impact.

The bull case deserves serious examination. You could argue that this falls under "problems too important not to work on" -- that measuring intelligence is foundational to governing the most powerful technology humanity has ever created. If we can't measure AI capability accurately, we can't regulate it, and ungoverned AGI is an existential risk. By this logic, Chollet is doing for AI what the International Bureau of Weights and Measures did for physics -- establishing the units of measurement that make everything else possible. And the timing is genuinely strong: benchmarks are saturating at an accelerating rate (SWE-bench went from 4.4% to 71.7% in a single year), reasoning models like OpenAI's o3 are creating urgent demand for harder evaluations, and regulators need credible independent measurement. If ARC-AGI becomes the standard that the EU AI Act and US executive orders reference, the foundation's influence over a multi-trillion-dollar industry would be enormous. Chollet is perhaps the single most credible person alive to build this -- the Keras creator, TIME 100 AI, with the theoretical framework already published and adopted by every major lab. That's not nothing.

But I keep returning to the structural problem. Chollet and Knoop are building Ndea simultaneously -- the benchmark designer is also the contestant. No public governance policy addresses the separation. This isn't just a conflict-of-interest footnote; it's a fundamental credibility risk for the very neutrality that constitutes ARC Prize's primary moat. And the team question compounds it: four people, two of whom have their primary attention on a separate for-profit venture. The operational leader, Kamradt, is capable but not the intellectual architect -- the foundation's continued relevance depends on Chollet's ongoing involvement, which is now split. This pattern -- a brilliant technical founder whose primary energy is increasingly directed elsewhere -- is one I've seen destroy nonprofit research organizations before.

The technology itself is methodologically interesting but not economically disruptive. Replacing MMLU with ARC-AGI as the benchmark of record doesn't make any incumbent system obsolete -- it changes the scorecard, not the game. LMArena raised $100M at a $600M valuation as a commercial entity doing similar work. Epoch AI gets $11M+ from Open Philanthropy. These competitors have more resources and, in LMArena's case, a business model that actually generates returns. ARC Prize's nonprofit positioning provides neutrality advantages, but neutrality is a moat for influence, not for wealth creation. I invest in companies that make existing approaches economically unviable. A benchmark, however brilliant, doesn't do that.

I respect what Chollet has built. The research community needs durable intelligence benchmarks, and ARC-AGI is the most intellectually rigorous attempt I've seen. But I invest my own money in companies where success means the world operates on fundamentally different terms -- where the consequence of success is worth accepting a 90% chance of total loss. The consequence of ARC Prize Foundation's success is that the AI research community has better instruments. That's important work. It's just not my kind of bet.
