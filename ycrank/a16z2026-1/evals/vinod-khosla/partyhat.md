# PartyHat -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Jarret Cuisinier was VP of Marketing & Operations at Avast when it had 400 million users and got acquired by Norton for $8.1 billion. He saw exactly how consumer cybersecurity works at massive scale — the pricing, the distribution, the churn curves, the fear-based marketing playbook. And now he's doing what domain experts almost always do: he's building a slightly better version of the thing he already knows. That's a red flag in my framework, not a selling point. When I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I've always picked better thinking. PartyHat is the product a seasoned Avast executive would build — bundled VPN, dark web monitoring, scam call blocking, repackaged with TikTok-native branding at $2.99 a month. It's competent. It's also exactly the kind of incremental play I pass on.

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if PartyHat fully succeeds? Younger consumers switch from Norton to a hipper security bundle. Gen Digital loses some market share to a new entrant. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. Consumer security is already a $43.6 billion market with dozens of players — Gen Digital alone does $4.73 billion in revenue with 75 million paying customers. PartyHat winning doesn't create new demand; it redistributes existing spend from incumbents to a startup with better Instagram content. That's one of my explicit red flags. Compare this to Impossible Foods, which created an entirely new category of protein production. PartyHat is competing for a slice of Norton's pie. The consequence of full success is a moderately valuable consumer subscription company — maybe a $500 million to $2 billion acquisition by Gen Digital or a telecom. I don't invest for those outcomes. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money.

The technology picture is worse than the consequence picture. The dossier is unusually clear: "No defensibility signals found in public sources." VPN infrastructure is white-label. Dark web monitoring APIs are sold by SpyCloud and Have I Been Pwned. Scam call databases are commercially licensable. PartyHat has assembled commoditized components into a bundle and wrapped them in irreverent branding. This is the definition of a wrapper, not a wedge. When I invested in Rocket Lab, Peter Beck built novel electric turbopump engines and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a non-linear approach to cost reduction that competitors couldn't trivially replicate. PartyHat's "AI security advisor" is mentioned on the website but there's no evidence of proprietary AI architecture, training data advantage, or technical differentiation that would survive a well-funded competitor spending six months on replication. Any telecom or big-tech company could assemble this feature set tomorrow.

The strongest bull case — and I want to be honest about it — is Dollar Shave Club for cybersecurity. Norton can't pivot to youth branding without alienating its existing base of cautious, older consumers, just as Gillette couldn't match Dollar Shave Club's irreverence. Cuisinier's insider knowledge of Avast's distribution mechanics is genuinely valuable for building the anti-Avast. And AI-generated phishing and deepfake scams are real threats that incumbents are slow to address. But Dollar Shave Club sold to Unilever for $1 billion. That's a fine outcome for most funds, but it's not the return profile I build portfolios around. And Dollar Shave Club at least had a direct-to-consumer supply chain innovation that structurally undercut Gillette's retail economics. PartyHat's $2.99 pricing against Norton's $50-100 per year isn't radical affordability — it's competitive pricing in a market where free VPNs and Windows Defender already exist. Aura offers more features at $12 a month including credit monitoring and up to $5 million in insurance. NordVPN has 15 million subscribers and just launched NordProtect. PartyHat is bringing a branding knife to a distribution gunfight.

Vijay Myneni's background — Stanford CS, visual effects on *The Day After Tomorrow*, indie game development with *Age of Rivals* — shows genuine range across platforms and creative product thinking. But range is not the same as the learning velocity I prize. I see no evidence that either founder has pivoted based on new information since launching in December 2025, no indication of what surprised them in four months of building, no signal of the rapid adaptation that distinguishes founders who reason from first principles versus founders who execute a pre-existing playbook. 1,000 users and 106 App Store ratings in four months is activity, not traction — and it tells me nothing about whether these founders are learning machines or following a script Cuisinier wrote at Avast.

PartyHat fails on the two dimensions I weight most heavily. The consequence of success is market share redistribution in consumer security — not the kind of world-altering outcome that justifies my attention. The technical approach bundles commoditized infrastructure behind branding, offering no non-linear improvement and no defensibility beyond execution speed. Cuisinier's Avast expertise is the right background for this specific company, but it's exactly the domain-expert profile that makes me skeptical — he's building what he already knows how to build, not discovering what couldn't previously exist. Pass.
