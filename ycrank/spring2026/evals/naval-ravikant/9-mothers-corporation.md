# 9 Mothers -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Roman Khomenko is from Kharkiv. That fact changes everything about how I read 9 Mothers' specific knowledge claim. A Staff Data Scientist with Kaggle first-place finishes and eight years of production computer vision work at Rainforest QA — PyTorch, YOLO, real-time ML pipelines — deciding to build autonomous counter-drone systems isn't a pivot driven by market research. Kharkiv has been under persistent drone attack since 2022. Khomenko's MSc is from Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics; he served in the Police of Ukraine. When someone who lived through the problem and possesses the exact technical stack to solve it builds a company around it, that's specific knowledge at its most compressed. But — and this is the tension — the specific knowledge is deep on the detection and tracking side and thin on the weapons systems, defense procurement, and manufacturing side. 9 Mothers is hiring 14 positions including mechatronics engineers, systems engineers, and government relations professionals, which signals they're building the team to fill domain gaps their founders don't cover. The ML/CV knowledge is non-replicable and directly applicable; the defense industry knowledge is being acquired through hiring. That's a split score — half the specific knowledge equation is exceptional, half is absent.

Russell Smith co-founded Rainforest QA in YC S12 and scaled it to $25.7M in revenue over roughly twelve years. That's a proven founder who builds, ships, and persists. He and Khomenko overlapped at Rainforest for eight years — the strongest co-founder signal I look for. When two people have worked together for nearly a decade and then choose to start a company in a completely different domain, the relationship has been stress-tested in ways that a "find a co-founder" match never can be. 9 Mothers passes the principal-agent diagnostic cleanly: Smith left a revenue-generating company he co-founded, Khomenko left a senior technical role, and the third co-founder Bogdan Pyzh (Baylor cybersecurity nationals) rounds out operations. These are principals, not agents. Smith's GitHub shows ESP32 and Rust work — maker-level engagement with embedded systems that suggests the transition to hardware isn't purely aspirational. The principal commitment here is genuine.

The leverage architecture is where 9 Mothers hits the structural ceiling in my framework. EDDA is a physical turret — manufactured hardware sold unit-by-unit through defense procurement channels. Every unit requires materials, assembly, testing, certification. The AI brain scales across units at near-zero marginal cost, which is real code leverage, but it's encased in a hardware delivery mechanism that scales linearly. Defense procurement is the most heavily gatekept market on earth — the opposite of permissionless. 9 Mothers needs ITAR compliance, security clearances, DoD certification, and multi-year contracting cycles just to make a sale. Two of three founders have international backgrounds (Ukraine), which may extend clearance timelines in a domain where clearances are table stakes. Compare this to my Perplexity investment: AI search is pure permissionless leverage, zero marginal cost per query, no gatekeepers between the product and billions of users. 9 Mothers' AI advantage is real but imprisoned inside a hardware-and-procurement delivery model that throttles the leverage.

Anduril at $60B and $2B revenue, Epirus at $550M+ raised, Fortem at $69M with $28.5M revenue and Lockheed Martin backing — 9 Mothers faces competitors with 100x to 1000x their resources. The bull case here is the principal-agent inversion: Anduril is becoming a defense prime, which means its best engineers are increasingly agents of a massive enterprise sales machine rather than principals focused on the cheapest, smallest, most portable turret. Just as Google's search team can't cannibalize its ad model, Anduril's $20B Army framework contract creates institutional inertia that should leave the bottom of the market — portable, sub-$50K point-defense systems — underserved. This is a real dynamic. The counter-UAS market at $6.64B growing 25.1% CAGR with $1.86B in committed DoD spending confirms the budget is there. But Varda Space Industries — my portfolio anchor for hard-tech exceptions — earned its exception because microgravity manufacturing creates leverage through physics that literally cannot be replicated on Earth. 9 Mothers' AI-driven shotgun turret is technically impressive but not physically unique; a well-resourced competitor with computer vision talent could build a comparable system. The Varda exception doesn't apply.

9 Mothers does map to my access democratization pattern more than a surface read suggests. EDDA aims to give every military vehicle and forward unit portable counter-drone protection that previously required million-dollar integrated air defense systems. "Small, low-cost, low-power, fully autonomous-capable" — that's the AngelList move applied to defense: taking a capability gatekept by defense primes and enterprise procurement and distributing it at unit level. If a squad can carry EDDA the way they carry a MANPAD, 9 Mothers has democratized point defense. But this is an incremental expansion of access within an existing military customer base, not the structural gatekeeper removal that characterizes my highest-conviction democratization bets.

The bull case is strong enough to be honest about: a Ukrainian ML expert with Kaggle wins and production CV experience, partnered with a proven YC founder for eight years, building autonomous counter-drone systems during the most urgent drone warfare moment in history, backed by YC with Garry Tan as partner, in a $6.64B market with 25.1% CAGR. If I only evaluated principal authenticity and founder-market fit, 9 Mothers would score in my top quartile. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that the AI detection and tracking layer creates a data flywheel — that field deployment data makes 9 Mothers' system measurably more accurate than anything Anduril or Epirus can build, compounding with each engagement. If the software advantage compounds while competitors' hardware advantages don't, the leverage equation changes fundamentally. Without that evidence, 9 Mothers is a hardware defense company with a strong AI component but no permissionless scaling mechanism — and hardware defense companies require the exact kind of capital leverage, headcount growth, and gatekept distribution that my framework penalizes. The decisive dimensions are Permissionless Leverage Architecture (10/25 — hardware delivery and gatekept procurement throttle the AI advantage) and Team-to-Value Leverage (5/15 — 14 open positions at pre-seed signals a headcount-intensive path). The specific knowledge is genuinely compelling on the ML/CV axis but split; the principal commitment is exceptional. Mixed signals, real founders, wrong leverage architecture for my framework.
