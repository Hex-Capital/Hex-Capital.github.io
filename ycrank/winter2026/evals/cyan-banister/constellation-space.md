# Constellation Space -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Constellation Space is that this is a team other investors will love. SpaceX, Blue Origin, NASA, Google -- these are the credentials that make a deal room nod along. And that's actually what gives me pause. I've learned over the years that the best investments I've made were in founders that other people dismissed, in companies that sounded ridiculous when I tried to explain them at dinner parties. Bitcoin mining on stranded gas flares. A ride-sharing app when ride-sharing was literally illegal. A male contraceptive gel. Constellation Space doesn't sound ridiculous. It sounds impressive. And that's the honest tension I'm sitting with here.

When I evaluate founders, I always want to hear the story before the business -- how did you get here, what forged you, why is this the problem you'd bleed for. The dossier gives me strong professional biographies but almost no personal narrative. Kamran Majid built Starlink ground network optimization tools at SpaceX -- so he literally saw this exact problem from the inside, which is meaningful. That's not just domain expertise; that's the kind of "I couldn't stop thinking about this broken thing I watched every day" energy that produces real companies. But I don't know what drove Kamran to SpaceX in the first place, or what personal adversity shaped any of these founders. The whole team came through Capital One, then scattered across prestigious aerospace and tech companies, then reunited. That's a corporate alumni network -- competent, legit, but it's not a band of misfits who found each other because nobody else understood what they were chasing. Omeed had a startup that failed (Nera, the dating-app-turned-trip-advisor), which tells me something about resilience, but it's a thin thread.

Where this company genuinely activates my pattern recognition is on something I don't usually talk about explicitly: I keep backing infrastructure at major inflection points, even though I think of myself as an intuition-driven investor. SpaceX at the birth of private space, Anduril at the beginning of defense tech modernization, Crusoe Energy when energy-to-compute became real. The mega-constellation era -- from 10,000 satellites today to 70,000+ by 2030 -- is one of those inflection points. And the insight that reactive monitoring breaks when you go from hundreds to tens of thousands of nodes is structurally correct. The physics-informed neural network approach, combining ITU atmospheric propagation models with orbital mechanics and space weather into real-time prediction, creates a genuine engineering barrier. This isn't an AI wrapper on top of someone else's data pipeline. The federated learning architecture -- letting multiple constellation operators improve shared models without exposing proprietary telemetry -- could create a data network effect that compounds over time. That's the kind of moat I look for.

The space and defense operating environment also maps to my comfort zone from IronPort days. Regulated, complex, long sales cycles, strict security compartmentalization -- these are features, not bugs, if you have founders who know how to navigate them. Kamran's SpaceX network and Raaid's Blue Origin embedded flight software background suggest they can speak the language. But I need to be honest about the risk: defense procurement cycles run 12-24+ months, and at pre-seed with four founders burning cash, that timeline is brutal. Quindar needed $27 million before scaling government traction. This team will need real patience and real runway.

Here's the bull case I want to genuinely engage with: if Constellation Space executes, they could become the connective tissue layer for the entire multi-constellation era. As Starlink, Kuiper, OneWeb, and government constellations all scale independently, someone needs to provide the vendor-neutral intelligence layer that no single operator has incentive to build. The federated learning architecture is the right technical answer to the trust problem between competing operators. And Kamran having personally written simulation algorithms for Starlink ground assets means he knows exactly where the existing tools break. If I could sit across from these founders and hear a story about why this problem kept them up at night in a way that went beyond professional frustration -- if there were a personal thread connecting their biographical journey to satellite connectivity for the underserved communities that depend on it -- I'd be more excited. But making invisible network failures visible before they happen, giving operators transparency into systems that are currently opaque, does echo the Carta-Affirm-Flock Safety pattern I keep investing in, even if the end users here are enterprises rather than individuals.

What ultimately keeps me in neutral territory is that this company doesn't trigger the anti-consensus signal I rely on most heavily. It's well-credentialed founders in a category that already has funded competitors. The round will probably be oversubscribed by investors who love the pedigree. And with four co-founders whose primary connection is a shared employer, I haven't seen evidence of how they fight, how they resolve deep disagreements, or whether this team can survive the inevitable crisis that space startups always face. After HQ Trivia, I don't take that question lightly -- especially with a four-way dynamic that's inherently harder to navigate than two. This is a solid company I respect but don't feel the jolt for.
