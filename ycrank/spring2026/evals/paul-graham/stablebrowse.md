# StableBrowse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding is the first number that jumps out of StableBrowse's dossier, and it tells me almost everything I need to know about the opportunity structure here. Browserbase raised $40M at a $300M valuation. Browser Use raised $17M and has 50,000 GitHub stars. Steel.dev raised $17M. Kernel raised $22M. When I see a market where that much capital has already poured in and the category has its own analyst reports projecting $76.8 billion TAMs, I know I'm looking at an idea that sounds good to everyone. And ideas that sound good to everyone are exactly the ones where the returns have already been competed away. The whole premise of my investing approach — the reason I called it "Black Swan Farming" — is that the best bets sit in the overlap between "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." StableBrowse sits squarely in the "seems like a good idea and is a good idea" quadrant, which is the quadrant where well-capitalized incumbents eat you alive.

Somansh Shah and Deepit Shah are the two identifiable founders, and I want to be fair about what I can see. Somansh won first place at JMEC's Startup Expo, built WiseBucks.ai and an AI community called Learn2Grow, and holds a Purdue data science degree. Deepit has a BS/MS in CS from UIUC and interned at Amazon building scalable backend systems. These are credentials that suggest competence — they're the kind of people who do well in structured environments. But when I look for the "animal" quality, for stories of founders hacking their way through walls, I find nothing. No tales of shipping a prototype over a weekend to land a first customer. No evidence of revenue before fundraising. No open-source projects that attracted users organically. What I see instead are certifications — IBM Full Stack Developer, Stanford Supervised ML — which is the résumé-building pattern of someone optimizing for credentials rather than obsessively solving a problem. Worse, Deepit Shah's LinkedIn reportedly indicated he was returning to Google full-time, and two of StableBrowse's four founders — Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta — have such common names that the dossier researchers couldn't even confirm they're the right people. Half the founding team is a ghost. For a company claiming to build something as technically demanding as a new browser engine, this opacity is deeply concerning.

StableBrowse's technical approach — stripping the rendering stack entirely and outputting accessibility trees and semantic markdown instead of pixels — is the strongest card in their hand. The claimed metrics are impressive: 130.6 MB per instance, 4ms cold start, 85% token reduction versus conventional browsers. CDP compatibility for drop-in replacement with Playwright and Puppeteer workflows is smart product thinking. Here's where an advocate would build the bull case: Browserbase, Browser Use, and Steel.dev all wrap or augment standard Chromium, inheriting its rendering overhead. StableBrowse is making the deeper architectural bet. If it works, re-architecting around a rendering-free engine would require competitors to rebuild their core products — a genuine structural moat. The parallel to Stripe is tempting: Patrick Collison tackled the painful schlep of payment infrastructure that repelled other developers. Couldn't StableBrowse be tackling the painful schlep of building a browser engine from scratch? But the parallel breaks down on inspection. Stripe worked because payments were a schlep that *repelled* founders — nobody wanted to deal with PCI compliance and bank integrations, so the Collisons had the field to themselves. StableBrowse operates in a space that *attracts* founders. Five well-funded competitors are already here. The schlep blindness moat doesn't exist when everyone can see the opportunity.

StableBrowse's origin story is absent from the dossier, and that absence is informative. When Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive, that story was inseparable from the product — you couldn't describe Dropbox without it. When the Octopart founders were physics grad students frustrated by searching for electronic parts, that frustration was the DNA of the company. StableBrowse has no equivalent narrative. No moment where the founders were building AI agents, hit the wall of Chromium's rendering overhead, and thought "we need to rebuild this from the ground floor." The dossier marks their GTM, business model, and market timing as "[Inferred]" — the researchers couldn't find the founders telling their own story. In my experience, founders who discovered a problem organically can't stop talking about it. The absence of that narrative, combined with the consensus-hot market, suggests StableBrowse was born from opportunity identification rather than lived frustration. That's the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" as the wrong way to find a startup idea.

The condition that would flip me is straightforward: show me that StableBrowse's rendering-free engine actually works across the messy, JavaScript-heavy, anti-bot-laden modern web at production scale, and show me founders who built it because they personally hit this wall while shipping their own AI agents. A working demo processing real agent workflows across the top 1,000 websites with measurably better reliability than Browserbase — combined with an origin story rooted in genuine technical frustration — would change the calculus entirely. Without that, StableBrowse is four recent graduates entering the most crowded infrastructure market in AI with unproven technology and no visible users. The idea that "browser infrastructure for AI agents" is an underexplored opportunity stopped being true sometime around Browserbase's $300M valuation. StableBrowse's consensus-validated market (scoring lowest on my framework) and the absence of founder resourcefulness evidence (no stories, no public code, no traction) are what lock this into a Pass. I need founders who make me think "these people will find a way regardless," and StableBrowse's dossier gives me no basis for that conviction.
