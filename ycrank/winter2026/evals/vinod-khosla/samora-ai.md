# Samora AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Kartik Sawhney's personal story is one of the most striking I've encountered in a YC cohort. Born blind, first blind student to pursue science education in India, changed national policy, earned a Stanford CS degree, built I-Stem to reach half a million people across 80+ countries. That biography radiates exactly the kind of persistence, outsider audacity, and first-principles stubbornness I look for. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, it was partly because a New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree was the sort of person who didn't know what the industry considered impossible. Kartik has that quality in abundance. The problem is that he's applying it to a market where nobody is telling him it's impossible — because forty other companies are already doing it.

Let me start where I always start: consequence of success. If Samora wins completely, enterprises replace human call center agents with multilingual AI voice agents. Phone operations get cheaper and scale further. That's a legitimate business outcome — the voice AI agents market is projected to hit $47 billion by 2034. But a cheaper, multilingual call center is not a fundamentally different world. Nobody's life changes in the way it changes when you eliminate animal agriculture or achieve fusion energy. UNICEF using Samora to voice-enable youth employment services in underserved languages — that's a hint of something more meaningful. If Samora's real thesis were "voice as the universal interface for the next two billion people who can't access digital services due to language and literacy barriers," the consequence magnitude would be dramatically different. But the commercial positioning — enterprise voice agents, CRM integrations, outbound calls — compresses the ambition into operational efficiency. They've aimed at a real market, but it's the kind of market where success produces a good company, not an obsolete industry.

The technology layer concerns me structurally. The stack is OpenAI's Whisper for transcription, GPT-4/5 for reasoning, OpenAI's TTS for voice output. These are commercially available APIs. Every one of Samora's competitors — Bland AI with $65 million, Synthflow with $30 million, Vapi with $20 million — has access to the same models. The multilingual capability, handling dialects and code-switching, is where the I-Stem team's domain expertise genuinely differentiates today. But as foundation models improve their multilingual performance — and they will, rapidly — that differentiation window narrows. When I invested in Juniper, the technology itself was the disruption: TCP/IP routing that made Cisco's architecture structurally obsolete. Here, the technology belongs to OpenAI. Samora is building a service layer on commodity infrastructure. That can produce revenue — they've apparently reached $1.1 million — but it doesn't produce a moat that compounds with scale.

The timing also fails my contrarian test. When I wrote my check to OpenAI in 2019, AI was considered laughable by most institutional investors. When I invested in cleantech in 2004, most VCs thought I was throwing money away. Those were bets against consensus. Voice AI agents in 2026 is the opposite — it's consensus with capital to prove it. $135 million in combined funding across Bland, Synthflow, and Vapi means the market has recognized this opportunity. Samora isn't skating to where the puck will be; they're arriving at a rink that's already crowded. The strongest bull case would require believing that Samora's multilingual expertise and managed-service approach for non-technical institutional buyers creates a durable wedge that well-capitalized developer-first platforms cannot replicate. That's possible — the UNICEF and Government of India relationships are real distribution into a segment that Bland AI isn't designed to serve. If Samora could own the "voice AI for global institutions and emerging markets" category, processing billions of calls across hundreds of languages in contexts where no call center workforce exists, there's a path to something genuinely transformative. But that path requires Samora to resist the gravitational pull of the enterprise voice automation market that every competitor is targeting, and to lean fully into the harder, less obvious mission that their I-Stem background uniquely qualifies them for.

The team composition itself is a genuine strength — three co-founders with years of shared history building accessibility technology for diverse multilingual populations is deliberate, risk-aware team construction. They've mapped their specific domain expertise to the venture's core technical challenge. At eight people with named enterprise customers and a million calls processed, the execution velocity is credible. But execution velocity in a commoditized market is a treadmill, not a moat. I keep returning to the same dissonance: this is a team whose personal story and domain insight deserve a more audacious problem — or at least a more audacious framing of this one.
