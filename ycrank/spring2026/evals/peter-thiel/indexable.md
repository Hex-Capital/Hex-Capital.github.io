# Indexable -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Six funded startups. Three major cloud platforms launching competing products in the same quarter. An open-source incumbent that claims 88% of the Fortune 100 as sign-ups. If you wanted to construct a laboratory example of the market structure I warn against most explicitly, you would build the AI agent sandbox space in early 2026. Indexable enters a category where the competitive landscape slide would require its own appendix — E2B with $32 million and open-source ubiquity, Daytona with $31 million and $1M ARR within weeks of launch, Modal at $111 million and $50M ARR, Blaxel and Morph and RunLoop and Cloudflare Dynamic Workers and Vercel Sandbox and Fly.io Sprites, all shipping variants of the same thesis within months of each other. This is not a market with a secret waiting to be discovered. This is a market where the thesis is consensus and the contestants are racing to execute on identical insight. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. The question is whether Indexable has found an exit from this arena that the others cannot follow through.

The technical claim deserves honest examination. A custom virtual machine monitor built on KVM that forks an entire running environment — files, processes, memory, databases — in 26 milliseconds is not trivial engineering. Gazelka has built a custom Linux kernel with non-upstream patches, a Rust-based Nix reimplementation, RDMA-capable transport, and content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication. These are irreversible technical commitments that would take a competitor years to reproduce from scratch. His background confirms the capability: building a Minecraft engine in Rust that handles 10,000 concurrent players in a single world is a direct analog to the state management and forking challenges at the heart of this product. The xAI tenure adds credibility. This is a founder who has chosen the hardest possible technical path — not lean iteration, not wrapping someone else's runtime, but building the entire stack from the hypervisor up. I respect the ambition and the architectural commitment.

But a custom VMM is not a secret — it is an engineering choice. The secret test asks: what important truth does this company know that the market actively disputes? "AI agents need stateful environment forking with database-level fidelity" is not disputed by anyone — Daytona, Blaxel, and Morph are all building toward it. "A custom VMM is required to achieve this" is closer to a genuine contrarian position, since competitors have chosen off-the-shelf Firecracker and Docker. Yet the contrarian position must create monopoly structure, not merely produce a faster benchmark. Is the difference between 26ms and 90ms the kind of discontinuity that renders the previous approach obsolete — like reusable rockets versus expendable ones? Or is it an incremental improvement that customers cannot distinguish in production? Daytona already offers fork/snapshot primitives. Blaxel claims sub-25ms resume. The custom VMM creates maintenance burden on a three-person team that competitors avoid by leveraging AWS-maintained Firecracker. The technical moat exists but may cost more to defend than it returns.

The bull case requires believing that stateful forking is not a feature but a platform — that the company controlling the fork primitive for AI agents occupies a position analogous to the operating system layer in traditional computing. If every AI agent needs to branch, test, and merge stateful environments millions of times per day, the infrastructure providing that capability with the lowest latency and highest fidelity becomes indispensable. Cross-tenant storage deduplication could create a data-driven cost advantage that scales with adoption. Deep API integration could produce Palantir-like switching costs. In this scenario, Indexable's custom VMM is not over-engineering but the foundation of a monopoly that off-the-shelf competitors cannot replicate. This would be a genuine zero-to-one moment — not a better sandbox, but the creation of a new computational primitive. For this to work, two things must be true simultaneously: the full-stack forking capability must be meaningfully superior to what Firecracker-based competitors can achieve (not just faster, but categorically different in what it enables), and the company must win distribution against incumbents who already have customers, open-source communities, and five to twenty times more capital.

The distribution problem is where conviction breaks down. E2B has an open-source SDK ecosystem and enterprise adoption. Daytona launched with immediate revenue traction. Indexable's website returns a 403 error. The distribution strategy appears to be "early access DM" via the founder's Twitter account with 86 followers. Superior sales and distribution by itself can create a monopoly, even with no product differentiation — and the converse is not true. Indexable has the converse: potential product differentiation with no visible distribution architecture. In a category where six competitors are simultaneously racing for developer mindshare, the company with the best technology and no distribution strategy loses to the company with adequate technology and a functioning go-to-market engine. This is a pure software infrastructure play with no atoms component, no government customer pathway, and no network-mediated deal flow advantage. The founder does not come from my network, does not have a prior relationship with institutional buyers, and is competing against teams with established developer communities.

Gazelka may be a genuinely brilliant systems engineer building the technically superior product in this category. But the category itself is the problem. When I look at the AI sandbox space, I see the cleantech pattern in miniature — entrepreneurs swept into a trending infrastructure thesis, all building variations on the same theme, competing on benchmarks that customers may not differentiate. The right question is not whether Indexable's VMM is faster than Daytona's Docker isolation. The right question is whether there exists any structural path to monopoly in a market where the primitive is being simultaneously commoditized by open-source projects, well-funded startups, and platform features from Cloudflare and Vercel. I do not see that path. This is a market, not a monopoly.
