# Minicor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with Minicor that jumps out before anything else: the company's core capability -- AI agents that interact with desktop applications through the UI -- is built directly on top of Anthropic's Computer Use API, which went into public beta in October 2024 and is now available to anyone with an API key. Minicor is building an application layer on someone else's primitive. Anthropic owns the foundational capability. That's the exact position I've learned to avoid -- when the protocol-layer player can decide tomorrow to ship a managed desktop automation product, or when any well-funded competitor can access the same underlying model, your "moat" is really just a head start measured in months, not years. This is the DApp-on-someone-else's-chain problem, except the chain here is a frontier AI model that's actively improving and expanding its own use cases.

When I run my sane-person-insane-idea filter on Faizaan and Saheed, I get competent founders pursuing a thoroughly consensus idea. Both have relevant backgrounds -- Faizaan connected systems at Phoenix (YC W21), Saheed lived inside legacy enterprise environments at Ford and Morgan Stanley. That's legitimate founder-market fit for the pain of legacy system integration. They met in 2019, built a nonprofit together, started this in 2023 -- the relationship has depth. But the idea itself? "AI-powered desktop automation for legacy systems with no APIs" is the hottest take in enterprise software right now. UiPath already shipped an AI Healing Agent. Automat raised $19.25M doing a version of this. Microsoft is moving Power Automate in this direction. Every AI infrastructure company I see at YC has some version of "we automate legacy workflows with computer-use agents" in their pitch. The market has priced this idea. There's no non-consensus premium here -- you're buying narrative at market price, which is exactly the kind of deal where my framework says the returns are already competed away before you write the check.

My "cherry on top" test kills this one cleanly: would Minicor be a good business if AI didn't exist? The answer is no -- without computer-use AI, this is just traditional RPA, and UiPath already owns that category at $13B+ market cap. The AI capability IS the product, not the amplifier of an independently strong business. Compare this to Craftwork, where painting crews and scheduling operations exist with or without AI, and the technology layer makes an already-good business better. Minicor is the inverse -- a technology product searching for operational depth. The managed infrastructure layer (VMs, compliance, observability) adds genuine value, and the SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certifications represent real investment. But managed cloud infrastructure is a services business, not a structural moat. Any team with funding and six months can achieve those certifications.

The bull case I take seriously: Minicor could become the Stripe of legacy system access -- the default API that every AI company calls when it needs to read from or write to Epic, CDK Global, or Wellsky. The MCP interface positioning is smart; if coding agents standardize on calling Minicor to interact with legacy systems, that's a genuine network effect. The "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" claim and "thousands of workflows a week" suggest production usage that most pre-seed companies don't have. If they can build proprietary training data from production workflows across dozens of specific legacy applications, that data advantage could compound faster than competitors can replicate. And the customer segment -- AI companies needing legacy connectivity -- is genuinely underserved by UiPath's enterprise sales motion. For this to work, though, two things have to be true simultaneously: the legacy system universe has to stay broken long enough for Minicor to build lock-in, and no well-funded competitor (including Anthropic itself) can replicate the managed infrastructure layer before that lock-in forms. I'm not confident in either condition.

The narrative has a ceiling that bothers me. Legacy desktop applications are, by definition, systems that should eventually get modern APIs. Every year, more healthcare systems adopt FHIR standards, more enterprise software ships REST endpoints. Minicor is betting on the persistence of technical debt -- which is a fine bet for the next five years, but the "infinity story" requires that this bridge to legacy systems somehow expands into something bigger. The best version of the story is "universal translation layer between AI and the physical world's systems of record," but that narrative narrows rather than expands as the world modernizes. Helium's narrative grew from IoT hotspots to decentralized wireless infrastructure over seven years. I don't see the analogous expansion path for "we automate clicks on Windows apps."
