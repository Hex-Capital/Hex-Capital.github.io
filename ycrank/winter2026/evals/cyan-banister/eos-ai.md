# Eos AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Eos AI is what I can't find: the founder's story. I've read this entire dossier and I can tell you Arya Khokhar went to Caltech, did research at Stanford, and published a paper on photoacoustic computed tomography using physics-aware neural operators. Those are real credentials. But credentials are not a story. My opening question is always "tell me how you got here — not the business," and what I hear back from this dossier is a resume, not a life. I don't know what Arya saw in a hospital that broke something open. I don't know if someone they loved fell through the cracks of fragmented medical records. I don't know what adversity shaped them. The ONCOVATE project — cancer patient triage piloted at Stanford Medicine — suggests genuine engagement with clinical pain, but it reads like an academic research project that evolved into a startup, not a founder who couldn't sleep because this problem was personal. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in the weeds of male contraceptive research that everyone else dismissed. That biographical thread — the obsession that preceded the company — is what I need to feel, and I'm not feeling it here.

The second thing that gives me pause is that healthcare data interoperability is not a weird idea. It's not unpopular. It's not difficult to assess. CMS interoperability mandates are literally requiring health systems to adopt standardized data exchange. Grand View Research has sized the market. Commure has raised $820 million and is valued at $6 billion. Redox did $181 million in revenue last year. When I look at my best investments — SpaceX when private space was considered delusional, Crusoe Energy when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded certifiably insane — they shared the quality of making smart people uncomfortable. Nobody is uncomfortable with "healthcare data should be interoperable." The specific ML-readiness angle is a genuine technical twist — the VERA imaging harmonization engine and LUCIA clinical text engine are addressing a real gap between interoperability-as-routing and interoperability-as-AI-preprocessing — but it's a product feature, not a contrarian thesis. The competitors I'd need to wrestle with aren't scrappy startups; they're well-capitalized companies with deep hospital relationships and years of deployment history.

Now, let me be honest about the bull case, because the invisible-systems pattern here is real and it speaks to something I care about deeply. When I think about why I invested in Carta, Affirm, and Flock Safety, the common thread was companies that illuminated what was hidden — cap tables buried in spreadsheets, interest rates obscured by fine print, public safety data locked in silos. Patient medical records scattered across incompatible EHRs, imaging archives, labs, and billing platforms are one of the most consequential invisible systems in American life. A federated data layer that keeps data in place but makes it searchable and coherent as a unified longitudinal patient view — that's making the invisible visible in a way that could genuinely save lives. If VERA and LUCIA work as described, and if those website metrics are real (37% revenue recovery, 18% model performance improvement), this could become deeply embedded infrastructure that health systems can't rip out. The Stanford Medicine pilot relationship through ONCOVATE is a meaningful warm introduction to one of the most prestigious health systems in the country. The bull case is that Arya has precisely the right technical foundation — medical imaging ML at Caltech, clinical AI experience at Stanford — to build the harmonization layer that existing interoperability players aren't technically equipped to deliver. If everything breaks right, this is the data substrate that every clinical AI model needs.

But even in that bull case, I'm still looking at a two-person team selling into 12-to-18-month enterprise healthcare procurement cycles, competing against companies with hundreds of millions in funding and established hospital relationships. My IronPort years taught me that selling security infrastructure to enterprises requires not just great technology but a significant sales and support apparatus. Healthcare is at least as demanding. A team of two cannot simultaneously build a federated data platform spanning imaging and clinical text, navigate HIPAA compliance across every connected system, and manage the relationship-heavy process of landing their first few health system contracts. This isn't a criticism of the founders' capability — it's a structural observation about the mismatch between what enterprise healthcare demands and what two people can deliver, no matter how talented they are. I don't see open job postings, which suggests they're not yet scaling the team to meet this challenge.

I respect what Eos is building. The technical architecture is thoughtful, the market timing with CMS mandates is real, and the problem is genuinely important. But when I run this through my framework — the founder's biographical thread, the anti-consensus conviction, the sense that this is a misfit company other investors would dismiss — I come up empty. This looks like a technically competent company in a well-understood market, and those are investments someone else should make.
