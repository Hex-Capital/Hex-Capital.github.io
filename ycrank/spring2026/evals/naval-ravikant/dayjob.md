# Dayjob -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

$450K ARR in seven months, selling into an industry where operators still schedule trucks with spreadsheets — that's the number that earns Dayjob a serious look. Waste management is one of the most technology-resistant verticals in logistics. When customers adopt that fast, the product is pulling, not being pushed. Marc Williamson at Coastal Recycling reports "5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour" and Stuart Pearce calls Dayjob "the best route-planning software I've used in 10 years" (VWS blog). Organic pull from grizzled transport managers who've seen every tool is the kind of signal I weight heavily. But the question my framework always forces is whether the revenue signals a power-law company or a solid vertical SaaS business. The answer determines everything.

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching their grocery business — that's real fleet scheduling expertise, the kind you absorb through thousands of operational decisions, not from reading case studies. Fred Fooks brings supply chain analytics from Deloitte and an engineering science degree from Oxford. More critically, both founders spent 18 months building Gaea, a waste ERP that generated $150K in revenue before they pivoted to Dayjob's scheduling agent in January 2025 (Pre-Seed Now; YC page). The Gaea chapter is where the waste-specific knowledge lives — skip sizes, delivery stacking constraints, the way planners juggle cancellations against vehicle configurations in real time. Dayjob's specific knowledge is legitimate but acquired through iteration rather than lifelong immersion. Deliveroo logistics plus 18 months in waste gives Dayjob a genuine head start. But a determined competitor with two years of customer exposure could close that gap. Compare this to Perplexity, where Aravind Srinivas spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact retrieval and generation systems the product depends on — that's specific knowledge so deep it functions as the company's engine. Dayjob's founders learned the domain; they didn't grow up inside it.

The leverage architecture is where Dayjob's structure diverges from my highest-conviction pattern. The AI scheduling engine itself is zero-marginal-cost code leverage — once deployed at a customer, optimizing ten routes costs the same as optimizing a thousand. That's the right structure. But Dayjob's distribution is permissioned, not permissionless. Every new customer requires integration with one of three waste ERPs — Weighsoft, PurGo, or Wastelogics (getdayjob.ai). The VWS/PurGo co-marketing partnership (VWS blog, Jan 2026) is smart, but it's a negotiated channel, not a self-serve flywheel. Notion reaches millions of knowledge workers through direct download — each user discovers it independently. Dayjob reaches each customer through a business development conversation with an ERP partner. Code leverage in the product, gated leverage in the go-to-market. That mismatch caps the scaling velocity.

AMCS, the incumbent with $225M raised (Tracxn), presents a structural principal-agent conflict I recognize. AMCS bundles route optimization within its full-stack waste ERP. Unbundling that module to compete with Dayjob head-on would cannibalize their platform lock-in — the same conflict that prevented Google from building Perplexity. Dayjob, as a five-person team with no legacy platform to protect, can build the best scheduling agent without protecting adjacent revenue streams. But the parallel breaks down on market scale: Perplexity addresses every person who searches the internet. Dayjob addresses UK skip hire operators. The principal-agent advantage is real but bounded by the size of the arena. The dossier notes that general-purpose vendors like Routific ($1.8M raised) and FarEye ($152M raised) could add waste-specific features — the core AI scheduling technology "is not inherently defensible" per the dossier's own assessment. Dayjob's moat is integration depth and domain tuning accumulated over deployments, not a technological advantage that compounds independent of customer count.

The co-founder relationship — long-time friends from Oxford (Pre-Seed Now), shared experience building and pivoting Gaea — passes the principal filter. The pivot from Gaea (waste ERP, $150K revenue) to Dayjob (scheduling agent) is particularly telling: they stayed in waste operations and narrowed to the highest-leverage pain point rather than abandoning the domain. That's principal behavior. Five people at $450K ARR means $90K per person — capital-efficient by any standard, but not the Midjourney structure ($3M+ revenue per employee) that validates my shrinking-company thesis at the extreme. Dayjob democratizes access to AI route optimization for waste operators whose only prior options were spreadsheets or buying AMCS's full-stack ERP. That's genuine, but narrow — a few hundred UK skip hire companies gaining a better planning tool, not millions of people accessing a previously gatekept resource.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: skip hire is the wedge, not the ceiling. Dayjob's scheduling engine expands to general waste collection, then recycling, then bulk haulage, then last-mile logistics broadly. The $450K ARR at seven months proves product-market fit. The ERP partnership playbook replicates across verticals. The AMCS principal-agent opening creates a structural window. The founders' Deliveroo-to-Gaea-to-Dayjob arc shows they iterate toward higher-leverage opportunities within domains they understand. And at pre-seed, you're buying the option on that expansion, not pricing in the current TAM. I take this seriously — the traction is real, the founders iterate well, and the AMCS conflict is favorable. But my framework demands I ask: can Dayjob compound without linear effort? Each new vertical requires new domain tuning, new ERP integrations, new partner negotiations. Expansion is additive, not multiplicative. There are no network effects — one waste operator using Dayjob doesn't make it more valuable for another. The data flywheel is per-customer, not cross-network. None of my Known Exceptions apply here — Dayjob isn't creating leverage through physics like Varda, nor generating network effects that override missing specific knowledge like Wish attempted. The condition that would flip me: evidence that Dayjob's scheduling engine generalizes across logistics verticals through a self-serve or API-first model, making expansion multiplicative rather than requiring per-vertical ERP negotiations.

Dayjob is a well-executed vertical SaaS company with genuine traction in a hard market, built by founders who iterate intelligently and have real domain exposure. I respect the revenue, the customer love, and the AMCS structural opening. But permissionless leverage is my first filter for power-law outcomes, and Dayjob's ERP-dependent distribution makes every expansion a negotiation rather than a deployment. The specific knowledge is real but acquirable. The compound interest test — does the advantage grow with each passing month independent of effort? — returns a weak signal. This is someone else's good investment, not mine.
