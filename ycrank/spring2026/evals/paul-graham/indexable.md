# Indexable -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Indexable is the sheer audacity of the technical bet. A three-person team building a custom virtual machine monitor on KVM, a custom Linux 6.19 kernel with patches that aren't even upstream, a Rust-based Nix reimplementation, and an RDMA-capable transport layer — against competitors who've collectively raised over $200M. Most people would look at this and think it's suicidal. But I wrote about exactly this pattern in "Schlep Blindness": "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." The reason Stripe sat unsolved for a decade wasn't that payments were a mystery — it was that building the infrastructure was so intimidating that founders' minds skipped right over it. E2B and Daytona chose the easier path — off-the-shelf Firecracker and Docker — because building a custom VMM from scratch is genuinely terrifying. If full-environment forking including databases in 26 milliseconds turns out to matter, Indexable is the only team building the architecture that can deliver it. The schlep is the moat.

Andrew Gazelka is the kind of founder I'd want to bet on technically. His Hyperion project — a Minecraft game engine in Rust targeting 10,000+ concurrent players in a single world, with a thousand GitHub stars — is almost absurdly relevant to the problem he's now solving. Managing massive concurrent state, forking running processes and memory, deduplicating storage across thousands of simultaneous sessions — he was doing this for fun before it became a commercial problem. His GitHub bio says "do less, deeply," which is exactly the kind of obsessiveness I look for. Someone who does what they do so well they cross from professional into obsessive. The xAI tenure adds credibility. The prior exit with BountyBot shows he can ship something people buy, not just build impressive technology. He's lived in the future of high-performance concurrent systems and noticed a problem there — which is exactly how the best startup ideas emerge.

But there's a real tension here that I can't ignore. I've said many times that "starting a startup is too hard for one person." Gazelka is the only named founder. The team size is listed as three, but no one else is publicly identified, and the founding engineer job posting suggests the core team is still assembling. If you can't convince someone to co-found this with you, what does that signal? Maybe it signals that the technical bar is so high that he hasn't found someone at his level yet — plausible given the kernel-level expertise required. But it could also signal that others have looked at this and decided the competitive dynamics are too brutal. Either way, the key-person risk is severe. The entire company's defensibility rests on custom systems engineering that lives in one person's head.

The competitive density is genuinely concerning, though I'd weight it differently than most investors would. I've said that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." The question isn't whether E2B has 88% of Fortune 100 "signed up" — sign-ups are not revenue, and E2B's $1.5M in revenue at $32M+ raised suggests their conversion isn't exactly crushing it either. The real question is whether stateful AI agents — ones that need to fork databases and running processes, not just execute code in ephemeral containers — are the future. If they are, then the off-the-shelf approaches fundamentally cannot serve them, and Indexable's architecture becomes necessary infrastructure rather than a nice-to-have optimization. If ephemeral containers turn out to be good enough for 80% of agent use cases, then 26ms forking with database state is a technical marvel that nobody needs. The bull case requires that the agentic AI market evolves toward increasingly stateful operations — which the trajectory of Claude Code, Codex, and Devin suggests but hasn't proven.

What concerns me most is the complete absence of any evidence that someone wants this. The website returns a 403. No users, no revenue, no press, no Product Hunt, no inclusion in any comparison article. The "early access DM" approach on Twitter reminds me a little of the Collison installation — "Right then, give me your laptop" — but the Collisons were installing Stripe into companies that were already trying to process payments. Gazelka's DMs are going out to a market that may not yet know it needs full-environment forking. There's a version of this story where he's so far ahead of the market that nobody understands the product yet, and a version where he built something technically extraordinary that solves a problem that's less acute than he believes. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of traction, but I do notice when a founder has clearly spent months on deep systems engineering without any signal that the product has touched a customer's hands. The best founders I've backed were obsessive about users from day one, not just about the technology.

If this works, it could be very big. The agentic AI market is growing at 43-46% CAGR and every major cloud provider validated the sandbox category in the last six months. A custom VMM that can fork entire running environments — databases, processes, memory — in 26 milliseconds would be genuinely differentiated infrastructure that compounds in value as the content-addressable storage engine gets more efficient with more tenants. The maintenance burden that looks like a liability today becomes an impossible-to-replicate moat in two years. Gazelka is the rare founder with the exact technical background this problem demands, arriving at exactly the right moment. The pattern matches something I've seen before: a technically brilliant founder attacking a painful schlep that incumbents won't touch, building from first principles while competitors assemble commodity components. That's how category-defining infrastructure companies start. What keeps me from higher conviction is the single-founder question and the total absence of any market signal — not even an anecdote about one team using the product. I'd want to meet Gazelka and understand whether he's been talking to customers as obsessively as he's been writing kernel patches.
