# Lucent -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The pivot is the first thing I see, and it tells a structural story. Lucent raised $1.3M in October 2025 for "behavioural datasets for browser agents" -- a data infrastructure play for training AI agents. By Winter 2026, the product is "AI that watches session replays to detect bugs." These aren't adjacent pivots; they're fundamentally different businesses targeting different buyers with different value propositions. The browser agent dataset concept was at least positioned at an interesting structural moment -- the explosion of browser-based AI agents creating genuine training data scarcity. The current product is an AI feature layer on top of existing developer tools. When a pre-seed company pivots away from a more structurally interesting market toward a more crowded one, that's a signal worth interrogating.

The market question is where this falls apart for me. Session replay is not non-obvious -- it's a well-mapped, well-funded category. FullStory at $93M revenue, Sentry at $100M revenue and $3B valuation, PostHog at $1.4B valuation with an open-source community. The total market is $267M-$502M depending on who's counting, growing at ~14% CAGR. That's steady, not inflecting. The "why now" offered is that multimodal AI models got good enough to analyze session recordings. But "multimodal AI improved" is a generic enabling event that applies to thousands of possible applications -- it's not a structural moment specific to session replay analysis. When I invested in Harvey, the "why now" was that GPT scaling laws predicted legal-grade fidelity within 12-18 months, applied to a $900 billion market of repetitive high-value tasks. Here, the capability improvement is real but the target market is small, well-served, and the AI analysis layer is an incremental improvement to existing workflows rather than a new category.

The feature-versus-company problem is severe. Every incumbent in this space already possesses the raw material -- the session recordings -- and the customer relationships. FullStory, Sentry, and PostHog don't need to acquire Lucent's data or distribution; they need one product sprint to ship "AI-powered session analysis." PostHog's open-source community could build this organically. Sentry already pairs error monitoring with session replay and has the engineering depth to add AI triage. This is the structural inverse of what I look for: instead of the startup owning the distribution channel and putting products through it, the incumbents own the distribution channel and the startup is building a feature that flows through the incumbents' pipes. A smart toaster dynamic where the AI layer doesn't fundamentally change the competitive structure of the market.

The strongest bull case would require believing that AI session analysis creates a new category rather than extending an existing one -- that the volume of unwatched sessions represents latent demand for a standalone product, not a feature gap that incumbents will close. If Lucent could become the intelligence layer across all session replay tools -- agnostic to whether you use FullStory, PostHog, or Sentry -- there's a narrow path to platform status. The comparison would be how Datadog became the abstraction layer across cloud infrastructure. But Datadog succeeded because no single cloud provider could offer cross-cloud observability; session replay vendors can each add AI analysis to their own data without leaving value on the table. The cross-platform aggregation story is weaker here because customers rarely use multiple session replay tools simultaneously.

On the founder side, Alisa Wu shows real execution velocity -- two acquisitions by age 22, even if small, and a 36-hour fundraise. That's builder energy. But the technical demands of this specific product concern me. Reliably identifying bugs from visual and DOM replay data at scale is a non-trivial ML problem, and the dossier shows no deep AI/ML expertise on the team. Describing ChatGPT as your "Chief Technology Officer" is charming in a press quote but worrying when your core product IS the AI analysis. The rrweb fork shows engagement with session replay mechanics, which is encouraging, but the miracle here isn't session recording -- it's building AI detection models that are accurate enough to be trusted over manual review. That's the single miracle, and I don't see evidence the team is uniquely equipped to deliver it.

I'm passing. The market isn't at a structural inflection -- it's a mature developer tools category with well-capitalized incumbents who already own the data and distribution. The AI analysis layer is an application of broadly available capabilities, not a proprietary breakthrough. The pivot from a more structurally interesting thesis (browser agent training data) to a more crowded one (AI feature on session replay) moves in the wrong direction. And the product-to-distribution trajectory is inverted: the startup needs the incumbents' platforms more than the incumbents need the startup's feature.
