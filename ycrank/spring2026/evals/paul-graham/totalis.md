# Totalis -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Polymarket launched perpetual futures on April 22, 2026. Kalshi announced perps the same week. Fanatics shipped "Combos" parlays. DraftKings acquired Railbird, a CFTC-licensed derivatives exchange, in December 2025. Totalis is building a derivatives layer for prediction markets at the exact moment when every major incumbent is building derivatives themselves, with billions of dollars and millions of existing users behind them. The structural question for Totalis isn't whether prediction market derivatives are a real category — that's been validated — it's whether a two-person pre-launch startup can build a durable position on top of platforms that are actively vertically integrating into exactly this space. When I backed the Collisons at Stripe, the critical insight was that banks *structurally could not* build developer-friendly payment APIs. Their organizational DNA prevented it. Totalis doesn't have that structural advantage. Polymarket can ship multi-leg products. Kalshi already is. The schlep here is real but not exclusive.

Eric Liu and Pravesh Mansharamani are genuine builders — I don't question that. Pravesh's 11 hackathon wins and roughly $55,000 in prize money, five DeFi engineering internships spanning Nethermind to EigenLabs, and 51 GitHub repos tell a consistent story of someone who ships. Eric won $8,500 at ETHDenver 2025 and collaborated with Pravesh on goddid.money at ETHGlobal Buenos Aires. But hackathon intensity and startup determination are different muscles. Hackathons reward the 48-hour sprint — build fast, demo well, move on. Startups reward the founders who do the unglamorous work for months: personally onboarding traders, negotiating API access with Polymarket's team, navigating CFTC jurisdictional ambiguity for cross-venue derivatives. I don't see evidence of Totalis's founders doing that kind of work yet. The dossier shows zero users, zero revenue, two GitHub repos with one star between them, and "early access opening for select users." That's not damning at pre-seed — but the absence of any creative workaround story, any evidence of doing things that don't scale, leaves me evaluating potential rather than demonstrated resourcefulness.

Totalis's strongest signal is organic problem discovery. Pravesh built EigenBets — a prediction market platform with AI-agent oracle verification — at ETHDenver before Totalis existed. Eric and Pravesh together built goddid.money, converting Polymarket trading history into on-chain credit scores. These weren't market-research exercises. These were two math students at Waterloo who were living inside the prediction market ecosystem, saw what was missing, and kept building toward it. The progression from hackathon prediction market tools to a prediction market derivatives company is the "live in the future, then build what's missing" pattern I look for. They didn't sit down and brainstorm startup ideas. They were Polymarket power users who recognized that single-outcome bets were capital-inefficient, that liquidity was fragmented across venues, and that traders wanted multi-variable positions. That's authentic.

The bull case for Totalis goes like this: these founders match the Bindwell pattern — young, technically brilliant builders tackling a problem from inside the ecosystem, with Waterloo math backgrounds providing the quantitative chops to build financial primitives. The $44 billion prediction market trading volume in 2025, growing at 80% CAGR per Bernstein, creates enough underlying liquidity to support a derivatives layer. Cross-venue, cross-category parlays are structurally different from what Polymarket's single-market perps or Fanatics' sports-only Combos offer — Totalis is combining geopolitics with crypto with sports in a single position, which requires integration complexity that primary venues may not prioritize. YC's first all-stablecoin investment ($500K USDC on Solana) validates the crypto-native approach. And being small and fast in a rapidly evolving market has advantages over being Polymarket's derivatives team, where priorities compete with the core business. I've seen this argument before. It's not wrong on any single point. But it underestimates how quickly well-funded platforms absorb adjacent features. Totalis's cross-venue parlay engine is a feature, not a company — unless they can build liquidity network effects or secure integration agreements that create real switching costs. I see no evidence of either.

Totalis operates in a consensus-hot market, which is the opposite of what I look for. Nobody hears "prediction market derivatives" and says "that will never work." They say "Polymarket is raising $400 million at a $15 billion valuation to do exactly that." The idea doesn't repel; it attracts well-funded competition from every direction — Polymarket, Kalshi, DraftKings, Fanatics, the traditional finance firms entering the space. Compare this to Airbnb, where every investor said "no one will sleep in a stranger's house," or Stripe, where developers knew payment integration was terrible but VCs thought it was solved. Totalis's idea is immediately legible as good, which means the returns have already been competed away. The regulatory ambiguity around derivatives-on-prediction-markets could theoretically create a schlep that repels competitors, but Totalis has no public regulatory strategy and no evidence of pursuing the compliance work that would turn regulation into a moat rather than a liability.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Totalis could show me signed integration agreements with Polymarket or Kalshi granting privileged API access for cross-venue settlement, or a few hundred active traders generating real volume on their platform, I'd reassess the platform dependency risk entirely. Liquidity network effects, once established, compound — but Totalis needs to demonstrate they can bootstrap that initial liquidity against incumbents who already have it. The decisive dimensions here are Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (scored low: the market is consensus-hot with $15-22 billion-valuation incumbents actively entering derivatives) and Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint (scored at default: hackathon wins demonstrate technical ability but not the sustained, creative determination I need to see from founders who are about to fight Polymarket and Kalshi for the same traders). Totalis's organic discovery is real and their builder identity is genuine — but discovery and identity aren't enough when the competitive landscape requires the kind of resourcefulness I can't yet see evidence of.
