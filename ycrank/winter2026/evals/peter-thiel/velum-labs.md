# Velum Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact about Velum Labs is not what they are building today, but the trail of what they were building yesterday. The company has cycled through at least three distinct framings before shipping a product to a single paying customer: a homomorphic encryption firewall for content-level access control, an "operating system for data quality," and now "the ontology engine for enterprise AI." The GitHub organization is still littered with forks of Microsoft's SEAL homomorphic encryption library from the prior incarnation, while the flagship Firewall repository sits at one star. This is not the profile of definite optimism. This is the profile of founders searching for a thesis, testing labels the way a lean startup tests landing pages. When I hear founders describe three different visions for the same company in rapid succession, I hear the absence of a secret. A founder who has discovered a genuine truth about the world does not pivot away from it -- they hold it against consensus pressure, the way Musk held reusable rockets after three consecutive failures. Velum's founders appear to be iterating toward a thesis rather than executing one they already possess.

The question I always ask first is: what is the secret? What important truth does this company see that the consensus actively rejects? The closest candidate here would be something like: "Data quality is fundamentally an ontology problem, not a monitoring problem, and LLMs have made automatic ontology derivation feasible for the first time." This is a reasonable technical observation, but it is not a secret. It is the natural inference that any competent data engineer would draw from the last three years of LLM capability improvements. No one actively disagrees with the proposition that automation should replace manual rule-writing. The five well-funded competitors in this space -- Monte Carlo at $1.6 billion valuation, Atlan at $750 million, Great Expectations, Bigeye, Soda -- are all converging on the same broad thesis with varying architectural approaches. This is a mimetic landscape: companies imitating each other's positioning while burning capital to differentiate through features rather than fundamental insight. When I see a market with five funded players and a pre-seed entrant offering a slightly different angle on the same consensus problem, I see a Red Ocean. The ontology-first framing is an architectural variation, not a worldview that creates monopoly.

The competitive dynamics here are structurally unfavorable in a way that goes beyond the current startup landscape. Snowflake's Horizon governance suite and Databricks' Unity Catalog are building data quality and governance features natively into the platforms where enterprise data already lives. If ontology-based contract derivation proves valuable -- and Velum is betting their company that it does -- the platform incumbents can integrate similar capabilities as native features with zero-friction distribution to their existing customer base. This is the feature-not-a-company problem: Velum is building functionality that is more naturally a module inside a data platform than a standalone product. Palantir survived this dynamic because intelligence analysis was so specialized and so high-stakes that no general-purpose platform could replicate the domain expertise. Data quality monitoring does not have that character -- it is an operational concern that platform vendors are economically motivated and technically equipped to absorb.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the ontology-first approach creates genuinely irreplaceable switching costs once adopted, that LLM-powered ontology derivation is a timing secret the incumbents will be too slow to exploit, and that these two young founders can navigate enterprise sales cycles at hospitals, banks, and government agencies with a two-person team and $500,000. The switching-cost argument has some merit -- once an organization's data contracts and semantic lineage are expressed through Velum's ontology layer, re-deriving those models elsewhere would be painful. This is the Palantir integration playbook in miniature. And the CTO's technical profile is genuinely interesting: topological neural networks, SO(n)-equivariant models, a pinned "ontology" repo on GitHub, and a CalHacks overall win demonstrate the kind of raw intellectual horsepower I value. If this team had arrived with one unwavering thesis, deep domain conviction, and a specific beachhead they intended to monopolize before expanding, the evaluation would be different. But the pivot pattern, the absence of any customers or even named design partners, and the simultaneous targeting of three of the most difficult enterprise verticals (healthcare, financial services, government) suggest a team that has not yet found its secret -- and is spreading thin rather than concentrating force.

The go-to-market compounds the structural problems. Targeting hospitals, banks, and government teams simultaneously with a two-person team is the opposite of small-market dominance. Facebook started with Harvard. PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Those were deliberate choices to monopolize a concentrated niche before expanding concentrically. Velum is targeting three verticals with the longest procurement cycles and highest compliance burdens in enterprise technology, with no evidence of a wedge that makes the product 10x better for any one of those segments specifically. The SOC 2 Type II certification is a necessary condition for enterprise sales, not a differentiator -- every serious data infrastructure vendor achieves it. Without a specific niche they can completely own, they are competing broadly in a market where better-funded competitors have years of head start and platform incumbents have natural distribution.

The founders have real technical talent, and the ontology-based approach to data quality is defensible as an engineering thesis. But an engineering thesis is not a secret, and technical competence is not conviction. I need founders who can articulate why the world is wrong about something important, and who have organized their entire company around that contrarian truth. Velum's founders appear to be searching for that truth. They may find it. But I do not invest in search processes -- I invest in secrets that have already been found and are being exploited with singular determination.
