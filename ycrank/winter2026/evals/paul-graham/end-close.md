# End Close -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Sean Bolton spent six years building reconciliation infrastructure at Modern Treasury. Not advising on it. Not selling it. Building it — processing a trillion dollars in annual payments across 40 banks with 99.995% automation. When someone who has done that leaves to start a company addressing the same problem, the first question isn't "is this a good idea?" The first question is "what did he see from inside that nobody outside can see?" That's the most interesting thing about End Close. Bolton isn't proposing to solve payment reconciliation because he read a market report. He's proposing to solve it because he spent half a decade watching the limitations of doing it as a feature inside a broader platform. He knows exactly which edge cases break at scale, which bank integrations are nightmares, and where 99.995% falls short. That kind of knowledge doesn't come from research. It comes from scar tissue.

The schlep here is genuine and massive. Payment reconciliation across diverse stacks — matching transactions against internal ledgers, bank records, and third-party data from different processors, each with their own formats and quirks — is exactly the kind of tedious, unglamorous complexity that causes smart founders to unconsciously look away. Nobody at a hackathon says "I want to build a reconciliation engine." The exception handling alone, which accounts for 99% of the manual work, requires gathering context across data warehouses, invoicing platforms, and payment processors. This is infrastructure plumbing at its most painful. And that ugliness is precisely what creates structural protection. The companies that win in schlep-heavy domains win because competitors keep flinching.

The technical founding team is strong. Bolton codes — his open-source LedgerSync project for syncing with QuickBooks, Xero, and NetSuite demonstrates he builds, not just manages. David Newell taught himself to code as a teenager, co-founded Unflow (YC S19) as CTO, then went to PostHog and led their error tracking team, which they describe as their fastest-growing product. He's a second-time YC founder who has already been through the cycle of starting, learning, and starting again. That pattern — founding a company, it not becoming huge, going to build at a strong company, then coming back to found again with sharper instincts — is one I've seen produce strong outcomes before. The co-founders are both from Galway, both studied the same engineering program, and both are embedded in the YC ecosystem through different companies. This is a real relationship, not a co-founder matching service.

The competitive landscape deserves honest scrutiny. Modern Treasury, Bolton's former employer, already does reconciliation. Ledge raised $9M from NEA. Osfin has $9.91M at Series A. End Close is entering a space where funded competitors already exist. But here's what I notice: BlackLine and Trintech are selling to CFOs doing month-end close. Ledge targets finance teams with treasury management. End Close is building developer APIs for continuous, real-time reconciliation embedded into product experiences. That's a different buyer (engineering), a different architecture (continuous vs. batch), and a different use case (product-facing vs. back-office). Bolton's insight seems to be that reconciliation is becoming a product feature, not just an accounting function — and building for that requires someone who understands both the payment infrastructure and the developer tooling layer. Whether Modern Treasury could build this internally is a real risk, but companies rarely cannibalize their own product architecture to serve a different buyer persona. The channel conflict is real.

The gap that concerns me most is the total absence of evidence for user pull. No revenue, no LOIs, no named customers, no waitlist, no organic inbound — nothing. At pre-seed this isn't disqualifying, especially when the founder's network should make early customer acquisition straightforward. Bolton worked with 40+ bank integrations and payments clients at Modern Treasury. If he can't convert that into early design partners quickly, something is wrong. But right now, the demand signal is entirely theoretical — I'm betting on domain knowledge implying demand rather than observing it. That's a weaker position than I'd like.

The bull case is straightforward: Bolton may be one of a handful of people in the world who has both the technical depth and operational experience to build a production-grade reconciliation engine for high-volume payments companies. The problem is real, the schlep is enormous, and the shift from batch to continuous reconciliation creates genuine architectural differentiation against incumbents. If End Close can land two or three design partners in the next few months — which Bolton's network should enable — this becomes a significantly stronger bet. I'd want to see that evidence before writing a larger check, but the founder-problem fit alone is strong enough to justify a small one.
