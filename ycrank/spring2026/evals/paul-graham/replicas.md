# Replicas -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Replicas is that the idea is obvious. And that's a problem. The best startup ideas look initially like bad ideas -- "Facebook sounded to me... a site for college students to waste time? It seemed the perfect bad idea." When I hear "cloud VMs that run coding agents so developers don't have to run them locally," nobody's reaction is "that's crazy." Everyone's reaction is "oh, of course someone should build that." Which means everyone *will* build that. And several already have, with hundreds of millions of dollars behind them. Devin at $10.2B valuation, Factory at $300M, Augment at $977M -- these aren't hypothetical competitors, they're heavily capitalized companies that already occupy overlapping territory. Worse, the upstream providers themselves -- Anthropic, OpenAI -- have every reason to add cloud execution natively. OpenAI's Codex already runs in a cloud sandbox. When your value proposition can be absorbed as a feature by your own suppliers, you're not building on bedrock.

"Starting a startup is too hard for one person." I've said this many times and I mean it. If you can't convince a single friend, co-worker, or classmate to join you -- that's a signal. Not always fatal, but concerning. Connor Loi is a University of Waterloo CS student, solo, with professional experience limited to 8 months on Helicone's core team. That Helicone experience is genuinely relevant -- building an AI Gateway that routes LLM requests is adjacent to orchestrating coding agents, so this isn't someone who stumbled into the space. He has real domain knowledge. But domain knowledge and one person's bandwidth are two different things. He's simultaneously doing product, engineering, sales, and support against teams of 50-200 people with nine-figure war chests. The projects on his GitHub (a language learning app, a ray tracer, a space RTS, a PR stacking tool) show breadth and technical chops, but they don't show the single-minded obsession I look for. "Someone who does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive" -- I don't see that signal yet.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." Maybe Replicas starts as the plumbing layer and becomes the operating system for all agent-driven software development. The agent-agnostic positioning has a structural logic: Anthropic won't showcase Codex, OpenAI won't promote Claude Code, so there's a Switzerland opportunity for a neutral orchestration platform. That's the same structural opening Stripe exploited between banks and merchants -- nobody in the old guard had an incentive to make the payment layer work well for developers on the other side. If the coding agent landscape genuinely fragments into 4-5 viable agents (Claude Code, Codex, Cursor, OpenCode, whatever comes next), teams will want a single dispatch layer rather than managing each agent's infrastructure separately. Connor's 30% PR completion rate, if real, suggests the product works. And three named customers within weeks of V1 launch isn't nothing. Helicone being a former employer is a yellow flag for network dependency, but at least it means someone who understands the product is actually using it in production.

But here's why I don't buy the Stripe analogy: Stripe solved a painful schlep -- "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps" -- because processing payments involved regulatory complexity, bank integrations, and fraud detection that sat unsolved for a decade because it was genuinely hard and unpleasant. Spinning up a VM, cloning a repo, and running `claude` is not that kind of schlep. It's infrastructure convenience, not infrastructure revolution. The technical moat is shallow. Any competent team could build the core in weeks. The integrations with Slack, Linear, and GitHub add some stickiness, but they're standard API work, not the kind of deep systems problem that compounds into defensibility over time.

What concerns me most is the absence of any evidence that this startup has to exist as an independent company rather than a feature. "If you make something users want, you'll probably be fine, whatever else you do or don't do." But the question with Replicas isn't whether developers want this -- of course they do. The question is whether they need *Replicas specifically* to get it, or whether this functionality arrives as a checkbox in Claude Code's settings panel or a toggle in Codex's dashboard within the next two quarters. The 30%+ PR completion rate is a product metric, not a moat metric. I'd want to see evidence of something that compounds -- proprietary data on which agent performs best for which types of tasks, environment configuration intelligence that gets better with scale, or some emergent behavior from the multi-agent dispatch pattern that single-agent platforms can't replicate. None of that is visible yet.

Connor Loi might be the rare solo student founder who pulls this off through sheer determination and resourcefulness. But "good people can fix bad ideas" works best when the person is demonstrably exceptional -- when you meet them and think, as I did with the Airbnb founders, that "it was impossible not to like them," that "no one ever worked harder." I haven't met Connor, but the dossier doesn't surface the kind of signal that would override the structural concerns: no prior exit, no evidence of cockroach-like survival instincts, no sign of doing things that don't scale in ways that reveal unusual insight about the market. This feels like a capable technical builder who noticed a real gap and moved quickly to fill it -- which is commendable -- but who is now in a footrace against opponents with 1,000x more resources, in a market where the gap itself may close from above before it can be defended from below.
