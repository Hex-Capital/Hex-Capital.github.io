# Mantis -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier is not about Mantis -- it is about Louiza Labs. Georgia Witchel co-founded Louiza Labs, which does physics-based simulation of human biology for medical device testing, went through YC S25, raised over $5 million, and now -- one batch later -- she appears at YC W26 as CEO of a different company in a closely related domain. The relationship between these two entities is undocumented publicly. This pattern demands scrutiny. When a founder leaves a company she co-founded after a single YC batch to start something adjacent, the best interpretation is that she identified a higher-leverage opportunity and had the independence to pursue it. The worst interpretation involves co-founder disagreements, IP entanglement, and a founder who iterates across companies rather than within one. I have backed founders who left previous ventures -- but they left because they had a definite vision so compelling it justified the disruption. The evidence here does not reveal which interpretation applies, and that ambiguity is itself a negative signal.

The more fundamental problem is the absence of a secret. I ask every founder one question: what important truth do very few people agree with you on? The thesis that clinical trials suffer from data quality problems is not a secret -- it is a consensus complaint repeated in every pharma boardroom and every FDA advisory committee. The $15 million loss figure per trial from data inaccuracies is a well-documented industry statistic, not a hidden insight. When you describe your company as "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," you are engaging in the most revealing form of self-definition: mimetic analogy. You are telling me that someone else already built the paradigm, and you are transposing it into an adjacent vertical. That is a "1 to n" move by definition. Databricks created a new category; calling yourself the Databricks for anything is an admission that you did not. The specific technical approach -- combining physics simulation with LLMs -- is more interesting than the framing suggests, but even here, Unlearn.AI has already raised $130 million pursuing AI-based digital twins for clinical trials, Simulants leverages Medidata's repository of 30,000+ trials and 9 million patients, and Syntegra generates synthetic patient data with NIH partnerships. This is not a blue ocean where one company holds a secret. This is a red ocean forming in real time, with multiple well-capitalized players converging on the same insight: simulation and AI will transform clinical data.

The product positioning scatter reinforces my concern about indefinite thinking. Across various sources, this company has been described as a "digital twin company," a "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," a "domain-aware data platform," and a "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform." Four framings for one company is not versatility -- it is the absence of a definite plan. When Zuckerberg described Facebook, there was one sentence: a social network for college students using real identities. When Musk described SpaceX, there was one sentence: reusable rockets to reduce launch costs by an order of magnitude. A founder who requires four different descriptions has not yet discovered what she is building. This is precisely the indefinite optimism I avoid -- the belief that the future will be good, but without a specific plan for what "good" means. The lean methodology dressed in physics-simulation vocabulary.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? Georgia Witchel would need to have discovered, through her work at Louiza Labs, that physics-based biological simulation creates a fundamentally different kind of data asset -- one that compounds in value as it absorbs more clinical context, eventually becoming the canonical digital representation of human biology for drug development. If that data flywheel is real, and if the physics layer creates structural accuracy advantages that purely statistical approaches cannot match, then the first platform to achieve this becomes the standard of record for the entire pharmaceutical industry. The regulatory environment is moving in this direction -- EMA has already qualified Unlearn's digital twin methods. And the founder's technical depth is genuine: Harvey Mudd computer science, Johns Hopkins computational math, UW biomedical engineering, and hands-on experience building physics simulators at Louiza Labs. Her athletic record -- eight world records in ice climbing -- demonstrates the kind of extreme personal conviction that occasionally translates to startup tenacity. If the Louiza Labs departure was a deliberate pivot toward a larger opportunity, and if the physics-simulation approach truly produces an order-of-magnitude improvement in clinical data fidelity, the ceiling is meaningful. But these are layered conditionals, not demonstrated facts.

The durability question is unfavorable at this stage. Medidata has 30,000+ cross-sponsor trials and 9 million patients in its repository. That data advantage took decades to accumulate and represents the kind of structural moat that a three-person startup cannot replicate through superior technology alone. In clinical data infrastructure, the last mover will be the company that owns the most comprehensive, highest-fidelity representation of biological systems -- and the incumbents start with an enormous head start on the data dimension, even if their analytical tools are inferior. A pre-seed company with no confirmed customers, no public revenue, and no regulatory certifications faces an enterprise sales cycle measured in years against incumbents whose procurement relationships are already embedded. The physics-simulation approach would need to be so dramatically superior that it overcomes institutional inertia, and I see no evidence of that demonstrated superiority.

I pass. The company operates in an increasingly crowded space without a visible contrarian secret, the founder's vision has not crystallized into the definite plan I require, and the competitive dynamics -- multiple well-funded players, massive incumbent data advantages, long enterprise sales cycles -- point toward exactly the kind of mimetic competition that destroys value for everyone.
