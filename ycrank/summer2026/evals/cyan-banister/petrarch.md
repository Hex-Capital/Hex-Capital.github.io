# Petrarch -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Petrarch is a bankruptcy-trustee data broker with three co-founders, no named CEO, and a pre-launch "Coming 2026" splash page -- an unsexy, mildly regulated corner (its highest-scoring dimension in my framework) wrapped around a product you cannot touch, install, or photograph (its lowest). That pairing tells me what Petrarch actually is: a legal-diligence business dressed up as an AI marketplace, pitched by two Harvard undergraduates and a college sophomore whose ISEF award was in chemotherapy modeling, not orphaned-dataset acquisition. I am never playing the game I seem to be playing, and neither is Petrarch -- but I am not sure Samuel Hahn, Ian Lee, and Sudhish Swain know which game they are actually in yet.

Vigil Labs is the closest thing in my book to what Petrarch wants to be -- regulated finance software where the moat compounds because normal investors flinch at the rulebook. The mechanism that makes that analogy load-bearing is the same one Petrarch is implicitly claiming: clean provenance and license diligence on datasets from wound-down startups is a workflow Scale AI and Snorkel structurally cannot enter without channel conflict on their $1.5B ARR labeling business. The problem is where Petrarch diverges: Vigil's founders lived the regulated-finance domain before shipping, whereas Petrarch's dossier gives me Ian Lee at CBRE and DPR Construction building Google and Apple corporate campuses -- adjacent to complex asset acquisition, yes, but nowhere near GDPR anonymization, bankruptcy trustee negotiation, or AI-lab procurement. The unsexy-regulated lane is real, and Petrarch is standing in it; I just do not see the founder who has walked it before.

HQ Trivia is what I reach for on Dimension 5, because its failure mechanism was tactile virality masking a co-founder governance hole -- and Petrarch inverts that pattern in the worst possible way. There is no tactile virality here to override anything: I cannot download Petrarch, cannot browse a catalog, cannot see a certified dataset move between a wind-down and an AI lab. The dossier tells me the site says "Coming 2026," the only shipped artifact is a $25/hr Business Ops Intern job post, and the LinkedIn company page follower count is not even retrievable. Petrarch does not fail like HQ Trivia failed -- it fails earlier, because there is nothing yet for a governance failure to destroy.

Founder-market fit for Petrarch reads to me like a mechanism that will erode over 18 months, not one that compounds. Sudhish Swain's ISEF 2023 grand award for computational modeling of low-dose chemotherapy on cancer metabolism is genuinely impressive polymath signal -- but the DeepMind exception requires cross-domain compounding *toward the problem*, and cancer-metabolism modeling does not compound toward brokering chain-of-title on distressed-startup data. Samuel Hahn is studying Economics and History at Harvard; Ian Lee's most relevant chapter is Harvard Innovation Labs. None of the three has a public GitHub, none has an X account, and none has ever been inside a bankruptcy proceeding or an AI lab's data-procurement conversation as far as the dossier reveals. In my framework this is analytical origin, not lived origin.

Counterfactually, what would flip Dimension 3 for Petrarch from default to strong is one sentence the dossier does not contain: a named CEO with tiebreaker authority and a documented founder-vesting protocol. Sudhish Swain is explicitly labeled "CTO and Co-Founder," which implies Samuel Hahn or Ian Lee is CEO, but neither is named as such -- and in a three-way undergraduate/sophomore team where the two Harvard economists are undifferentiated, that is precisely the HQ Trivia and GameCrush setup I have been burned by before. If Petrarch's next update names Ian Lee CEO with operating authority and cites a vesting schedule, I move this dimension up meaningfully. Right now the silence is the signal.

Scale AI at $1.5B ARR growing 97% year-over-year is the single-anchor parallel I want to stress for Dimension 2. Petrarch is not weird in the Crusoe sense -- it is weird in a slightly commercial sense: acquiring proprietary datasets from distressed startups is a real gap Scale and Snorkel structurally cannot fill without cannibalizing themselves, and Datarade's self-serve model does not do exclusive acquisition. That is genuine non-consensus positioning. But Gretel raised $50M for synthetic data in 2021 precisely because AI labs prefer manufactured data of known provenance to acquired data of uncertain chain-of-title, and Petrarch's dossier lists zero mitigation for GDPR/CCPA or IP transfer risk from bankruptcy trustees. Magically weird works when the founder is *of* the place; Petrarch's founders are not of bankruptcy court.

My 36-month forecast for Petrarch: it pivots off distressed-asset acquisition within 18 months toward a conventional labeled-data enablement or synthetic-data brokerage play, and settles under $500K ARR before quiet wind-down -- no strategic acquirer, because Scale, Snorkel, and Datarade will not pay for a catalog they can source directly once the legal template exists. The mechanism producing this is founder-market mismatch compounding against a supply-side that is episodic and adversely selected: bankruptcy trustees release the datasets of the worst-performing companies, and Petrarch has no procurement-side founder to convince an AI lab to buy those datasets over Gretel's synthetic alternative.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-pass -- that is, the bull case -- is that Ian Lee's CBRE, Lincoln Property, and DPR Construction background is genuinely underrated deal-sourcing muscle. Negotiating with a bankruptcy trustee is closer to negotiating a distressed real-estate asset than most VCs realize, and Petrarch may have accidentally stumbled onto a founder whose lived expertise is *procurement of orphaned assets under legal complexity*, which is exactly the sourcing edge the business requires. I take that seriously, and I checked it against my HandUp exception -- I backed HandUp because the founder mirrored my own homelessness origin, but no Petrarch founder mirrors a distressed-data origin. I checked the DeepMind polymath exception -- Sudhish is early-career impressive but not Hassabis. What would flip me: Petrarch signing three named exclusive dataset acquisitions from wound-down AI startups at more than $1M per dataset within twelve months, with Ian Lee designated CEO. Absent that, I pass and keep an eye on Ian.
