# Apollo Atomics, Inc. -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Apollo Atomics is the inverse-Anduril: maximally correct on the sector axis I have been writing checks against for twenty years, weakest on the axis I screen first. Assil Halimi's 2021–2025 MIT PhD under Koroush Shirvan is on the exact compact iPWR steam generator Apollo Atomics intends to commercialize — the founder-to-product fit is tighter than almost anything in the batch — but the chosen architecture is a pressurized water reactor leaning on "15,000+ reactor-years of PWR operating experience" as its licensing basis. That is not a secret. That is the most consensus nuclear technology on Earth, dressed in factory-built clothing, sharing a customer (the data center) with at least five funded competitors.

**Anduril (2017)** is the load-bearing analogy on the regulated-moat axis, and it is where Apollo Atomics earns its only thesis-shaped paragraph. The mechanism that made Anduril a check was a named incumbent failure mode — Lockheed/Raytheon cost-plus contracting structurally cannot ship software-defined defense — paired with regulated-customer integration depth as the moat. Apollo Atomics inherits the regulated-customer half of that structure cleanly: an NRC construction permit targeted for 2028, a fuel-qualification regime that takes a decade, and capex barriers that vaporize pure-software entrants. Where Apollo Atomics diverges from the Anduril rung is the incumbent story. Westinghouse already markets the AP300 small modular PWR. GE-Hitachi has the BWRX-300. Apollo Atomics' implicit claim is that a 40x-smaller steam generator from Shirvan's lab gives them a power-density edge the primes will not match — but the dossier does not name a structural reason Westinghouse cannot license the same compact steam-generator topology once Apollo Atomics proves the two-phase flow. That is a feature gap, not a Lockheed-cost-structure gap.

The contrarian-secret rung Apollo Atomics most resembles is the **"AI for X"** pass-example, and the failure mechanism there is the one I want to trace honestly. That mechanism is: the founder names a market without naming why the incumbent and the obvious competitor cannot ship the proposed product. Apollo Atomics names a market — "low-carbon baseload power for utilities, industrial heat users, and data center operators" — and a target price of 3¢/kWh, but the proposed differentiator is a smaller steam generator, which is a tighter engineering loop on a public-domain architecture, not a secret the market actively rejects. The five-competitor density (NuScale, Oklo, X-energy, Aalo Atomics, Kairos Power) is the tell: when consensus has converged on "small modular reactors for data centers" hard enough that Aalo Atomics raised $100M in August 2025 on the same pitch, the secret has been priced in.

Halimi's pedigree compounds in an unusual direction over the next eighteen months, and that is the mechanism worth isolating on the lineage dimension. PhD-to-product fit this tight — Shirvan's lab, compact iPWR steam generator, high-burnup fuel, the exact thesis topic — converts into NRC pre-application engagement faster than competitors whose CEOs are operators rather than nuclear engineers. The compounding is regulatory, not commercial: every NRC interaction Halimi runs personally is one Aalo Atomics' Matt Loszak cannot. The counter-mechanism is that nuclear commercialization at $30M+ Series A capital intensity demands a COO who has closed nine-figure infrastructure contracts, and Drew Walker's Blue Innovations Group electric-boat exit plus White House staff time is not that. Halimi-shaped without Levchin-shaped beside him caps Apollo Atomics at technical credibility, not commercial velocity.

The monopoly-trajectory dimension flips a tier only if Apollo Atomics signs a firm offtake with a named hyperscaler before the 2028 permit application — that is the counterfactual. The dossier shows zero LOIs, zero named utility customers, zero data center counterparties; what it shows is a "target cost of 3¢/kWh" published on the website. Three cents is roughly Vogtle-3-delivered electricity if Vogtle had been built on schedule, which it was not, which is why no one believes the published number until a regulated customer signs against it. Without a Microsoft-Amazon-Google name attached, Apollo Atomics is one of five Series-A racers chasing the same procurement officer, and a five-way race in a commoditizing kilowatt-hour market is the Lyft-Uber duopoly without the network effect override that made me write the Lyft check.

**SpaceX (2008)** is the single-anchor parallel on the definite-plan axis, and Apollo Atomics earns the comparison narrowly. The A-10, A-50, A-300 roadmap with a 2028 pilot and a 2028 NRC construction permit application is a definite plan in the Zero to One sense — named milestones, multi-year horizon, an architectural commitment Halimi cannot pivot from without abandoning the PhD. What separates Apollo Atomics from the SpaceX rung is that Musk's reusability roadmap had no competitor running the same play; Apollo Atomics' 2028 milestone arrives twelve to eighteen months after Aalo Atomics' planned July 2026 cold criticality at INL. Definite optimism is a virtue when you are alone in the category and a treadmill when you are fourth.

My 36-month forecast: Apollo Atomics is alive in 2029, pre-revenue, has raised $30–60M Series A from generalist climate-and-defense crossover funds rather than Founders Fund or a strategic, has filed NRC pre-application engagement letters but not the construction permit, trails Aalo Atomics' INL demonstration by 12+ months, and is in early acquisition conversations with Westinghouse or BWXT rather than category-defining on its own. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature itself — atoms-heavy regulated moat keeps Apollo Atomics alive past the cash crunch that kills software-only YC companies, while the weak contrarian secret keeps it from breaking out of the SMR peloton.

The strongest bull case against my pass leans on the Halimi-Shirvan-to-product fit as an Asana-class override: pedigree carries a thesis that would otherwise fail the secret test, the way Moskovitz's Facebook ship overrode Asana's missing structural moat. The case stings because PWR with commercial fuel really is the fastest NRC path — the agency has approved this physics for fifty years — and Oklo's sodium-cooled and X-energy's HTGR designs will burn 24+ months on fuel qualification Apollo Atomics skips entirely. If hyperscaler power demand pulls forward and the NRC accelerates Part 53 framework, Apollo Atomics ships first. The dossier evidence that wins is the competitor count: Westinghouse already markets the AP300 with the same fuel and the same licensing path, and the moat I would be funding is "Halimi's lab built a smaller heat exchanger than Westinghouse," which is a feature delta, not a monopoly. The flip condition is specific — a signed firm offtake or PPA with a named hyperscaler (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, or Meta) at $500M+ commitment announced before end of 2026, or Founders Fund leading the Series A. Either rewrites the secret. Absent that, Apollo Atomics is a thesis-shaped sector pick wrapped around a consensus architecture in a crowded race. Pass.
