# General Legal -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about General Legal is that these three founders are, as far as I can tell, one of the few teams on Earth who could actually build this company. They are all three former practicing attorneys at the exact firms that charge growth-stage startups $1,500/hour for contract review -- Fenwick and Cooley -- who then spent a decade building AI legal tools together at Casetext, which they scaled to a $650M acquisition by Thomson Reuters. Ryan Walker was CTO. Javed Qadrud-Din led ML. J.P. Mohler deployed AI in federal courts. They didn't brainstorm this idea over coffee. They spent ten years watching from the inside as law firms charged obscene hourly rates for work that their own AI tools could accelerate tenfold. The idea didn't emerge from market research. It emerged from a decade of lived contradiction.

The schlep here is large enough to be interesting. Everyone knows law firms are expensive and slow. But building an actual law firm -- not a tool for lawyers, an actual law firm -- means carrying malpractice insurance, employing barred attorneys in every relevant jurisdiction, managing professional liability on every contract you touch, and navigating bar association rules that vary by state. Robin AI tried to do contract review as pure software and just collapsed financially, acquired in distress by Scissero. Harvey and Spellbook sell tools to lawyers precisely because they don't want to become lawyers. The regulatory overhead of being a law firm is the kind of ugly, tedious complexity that causes smart founders to unconsciously look away. That's why the opportunity persists. And the incumbents face the classic cannibalization trap: Cooley and Fenwick would have to destroy their hourly billing model to compete on flat-fee AI-augmented contracts. The founders know this intimately because they used to bill those hours.

One concern: YC's 2025 Request for Startups literally said "start your own law firm, staff it with AI agents, and compete with existing law firms." When an accelerator publishes a prompt and then funds the company that answered it, you wonder whether the idea was discovered or assigned. But the timeline resolves this cleanly. Casetext was YC S13. These founders have been building AI for lawyers since 2013-2014. They were doing this work seven years before YC wrote that RFS. If anything, YC wrote the RFS because they saw what teams like this one were already capable of. The RFS validated the thesis; it didn't generate it.

The pattern that excites me is the Casetext alumni returning through YC with a new company. I've seen this before. When founders go through the startup lifecycle once -- building, scaling, getting acquired -- and then come back to do it again, they bring a kind of calibration that first-time founders lack. They know how hard it gets. The fact that all three left comfortable post-acquisition roles at Thomson Reuters to start over tells me the motivation is genuine. They're not doing this because they need money. They're doing it because they saw the next move clearly and couldn't not make it.

The early traction is thin but real: 20+ growth-stage companies moved their commercial contracting to the firm during private preview. At $500 per contract that's modest revenue, and the free first contract offer makes it harder to distinguish genuine pull from free-trial sampling. I'd want to see repeat usage data -- how many of those 20 companies came back for a second and third contract at full price? That's the signal that separates "people will try this" from "people want this." Still, the fact that companies are trusting an unknown firm with their actual commercial contracts -- not a demo, not a sandbox, but real deals they need to close -- is a meaningful early signal.

The main risk I see is whether a law firm can scale like a technology company. Every contract needs a licensed attorney to review and sign off. At $500 per contract with a one-hour turnaround, the unit economics depend heavily on how much of the work the AI actually handles. If the AI does 90% and the attorney spends six minutes reviewing, the margins could be excellent. If complex contracts routinely need 45 minutes of attorney time, $500 is a money-losing price point. The founders have the AI expertise to push the automation ratio higher over time, but this will always be a human-in-the-loop business. It won't have pure software margins. Whether that matters depends on whether you think the moat is in the AI workflows or in the client relationships -- I suspect it's in both, compounding over time as they accumulate knowledge of each client's patterns and risk tolerances.

I would invest. The organic problem discovery is strong, the schlep is real and structural, and this founding team has already built and exited an AI legal company together. The attorney-dependent model is a legitimate concern for venture-scale returns, but at the angel stage I'm betting on the founders, not the spreadsheet. These three people will figure out the scaling problem because they've already solved harder versions of it at Casetext.
