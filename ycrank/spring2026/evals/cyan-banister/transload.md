# transload -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Julius Scheel is a McKinsey Fellow out of the Zurich office, Jago Wahl-Schwentker shipped code at Veli, and Nils Börner is not even disambiguable on LinkedIn — that is what kind of company transload is. The dimensional signature is loud: a real, unsexy industrial wedge sitting on top of the July 19, 2025 NMFTA density-based reclassification — that is the strongest dimension here — but a founding team whose biographies put them in a Munich incubator and a Zurich consulting office, not on an LTL terminal floor at 5am. transload is the inverse of what I usually buy. The problem is correctly chosen and the founders found it in a deck.

On Founder Lived Experience — my heaviest dimension — the HIGH rung is Palmer Luckey at **Anduril** (2017), who had already shipped Oculus out of his parents' garage before he ever pitched a defense primitive; the MID rung is John Hanke at **Niantic** (~2015), who had a decade of Keyhole/Google Earth under his belt before Ingress; the LOW rung is the consensus YC AI-agent pass — two ex-FAANG PMs who discovered the back-office problem in a memo. transload sits a half-step above that LOW rung and well below the MID. The dossier is explicit: "No co-founder has a disclosed prior LTL carrier, 3PL operations, or NMFC classification role in public sources." Julius's McKinsey-Zurich line is the exact "Stanford or Harvard, Goldman Sachs or McKinsey" pattern from the Hustle Fund profile. Jago at Veli plus a TUM Venture Labs / robo.innovate path gives me applied CV talent but not the lived freight knowledge — nobody on this team can talk to a roughneck at a Reddaway dock without flinching, and the chargeback-dispute workflow lives in that conversation, not in the model.

On Single Unambiguous CEO — my lowest-placement dimension after lived experience — the HIGH rung is Musk at **SpaceX** (2008), where the tiebreaker authority was never in doubt; the MID rung is Hanke at **Niantic** (~2015), single CEO with clear operating control; the LOW rung is the Yusupov/Kroll partnership at **HQ Trivia** (2017) that I have publicly post-mortemed as a governance failure I should have weighted harder. transload has three co-founders and the dossier names zero CEO. No equity split disclosed, no operating agreement disclosed, no tiebreaker disclosed. That is the **HQ Trivia** (2017) and **GameCrush** (~2011) silhouette before either of them blew up — "who's the tiebreaker?" (Tim Ferriss #780, November 2024) — and I scored the default downward because silence on governance at three-co-founder stage reads as "they have not had the conversation yet."

On Magically Weird and Underpriced, the HIGH rung is **Crusoe** (~2018) burning North Dakota gas flares — a thesis other VCs passed on for aesthetic reasons; the MID rung is **Mindbloom** ketamine therapy; the LOW rung is the consensus YC AI darling. transload sits at MID. The CCTV-reuse wedge is genuinely contrarian against the FreightSnap ($1M–$4.6M revenue, hardware kiosks) and Cargo Spectre (~1,000 deployed 3D scanners) hardware incumbents, and the NMFTA density-based shift on July 19, 2025 is a regulatory unlock most software investors are not pricing in. But no credible VC has publicly passed on this for aesthetic reasons — that is the **Crusoe** tell I cannot find here.

On Capital-Intensive / Unsexy-Regulated, the HIGH rung is **Anduril** (2017) defense hardware; the MID rung is **Vigil Labs** (August 2025) regulated finance; the LOW rung is the AI-app SaaS wrapper. transload is mid-leaning-low — software-only on top of someone else's CCTV, but the buyer is an LTL terminal operator and the regulatory tailwind is the NMFC density rule. Not frontier hardware. Adjacently unsexy. On Physical-World Verifiability, the HIGH rung is **Flock Safety** (~2018) — I read the Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café and the camera was on a pole I could literally drive past; the MID rung is **Postmates** (2011), the delivery happened in front of me; the LOW rung is the developer-tools B2B API I cannot touch. transload sits between MID and LOW: in principle I could walk a 3PL warehouse and watch a pallet get dimensioned by an overhead camera, but the website (transload.io) is a single-line tagline page, there is no Product Hunt, no app, no demo video, no LinkedIn company page activity, no named customer. The artifact exists in concept; I cannot photograph it in the wild yet.

My 36-month forecast: transload does not become a category-defining outcome. It hits a $1–3M ARR ceiling against a competent CCTV-native entrant who actually came out of an LTL terminal (or out of FreightSnap itself), and by Q2 2029 it is either dead or acqui-hired into a WMS/TMS vendor like Project44 or a freight-billing platform — most likely an integration tuck-in rather than a venture-scale outcome. The mechanism producing this is the lived-experience gap meeting the accuracy ceiling: the chargeback-dispute workflow on legacy-CCTV inference is where freight buyers actually evaluate the product, and that workflow is designed by someone who has worked a dock, not someone who has Fellow'd at McKinsey.

The strongest case against me is the NMFTA July 19, 2025 density-based reclassification — every LTL shipment now requires accurate dimensions to bill correctly, the "$50k per site per month in rebillings" claim is a real regulatory forcing function, and CCTV-reuse is the only architecture that avoids consuming dock-floor real estate the way a FreightSnap kiosk does. If transload's accuracy on legacy cameras is genuinely billing-grade, adoption is involuntary, not discretionary, and the wedge against $12,300–$15,300 vMeasure hardware becomes structural. I have a Known Exception for outsider-trajectory founders who lacked the pedigree (**Thumbtack**, **HandUp** ~2014) — but the exception runs in the *opposite* direction from transload, which has the pedigree (McKinsey) and lacks the trajectory. The bull case stings because the regulatory unlock is real, but the founders' accuracy benchmark will be set in a TUM lab, not on a Yellow Freight terminal, and that is where this breaks. One condition flips me: a named top-25 LTL carrier signs as a paying customer in the next two quarters and one founder turns out to have a verified prior LTL operations role I missed — then I am rebutted, not refused.
