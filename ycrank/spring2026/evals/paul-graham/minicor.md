# Minicor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Minicor pivoted from a low-code integration platform called Laminar into Windows desktop automation after hitting a wall connecting to an on-premise medical record system with no API. That origin story is the most interesting thing in this dossier. They didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's a big market in RPA." They were building something else entirely, ran headfirst into a problem that blocked them, and recognized the blockage was the real opportunity. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Faizaan Chishtie and Saheed Akinbile built Minicor because a healthcare customer's Windows-based EHR had no writable API and no intention of creating one. The structure of the discovery — frustration during adjacent work, not market analysis — is exactly the pattern that produces founders who understand the problem at the level of nerve endings rather than spreadsheets.

Faizaan and Saheed have known each other since University of Ottawa, where they co-founded Software For Love, a nonprofit mobilizing student volunteers to build software. Faizaan previously went through YC with Phoenix (W21) and built Homeboard Technologies to mid-five-figure ARR. Saheed spent three years at Ford on Android Auto and did time at Morgan Stanley. Neither of them is Patrick Collison at seventeen — I don't see the extraordinary raw brilliance signal that makes me ignore everything else. But I see something I value almost as much: a pair of engineers who've shipped together before, who have a relationship that predates any financial incentive, and who've navigated the disorienting leap from "our integration platform hit a wall" to "the wall is the product." Faizaan's GitHub tells me he builds things because he can't help it — 49 repos, a Rust in-memory vector database with 38 stars. That's not a resume project; that's compulsion. Minicor's achievement of SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance at pre-seed with five people is the kind of unglamorous operational grind that most early-stage teams would defer for two years. They didn't defer it because their healthcare customers wouldn't let them.

Minicor is targeting what might be the purest schlep blindness opportunity I've seen in this batch. Automating clicks inside Epic and Cerner on Citrix environments — try saying that at a dinner party and watch people's eyes glaze over. Every browser-based competitor has implicitly conceded this territory: Skyvern ($2.7M seed) does browser automation, Induced AI ($2.3M, Sam Altman-backed) does browser automation, Browserbase ($40M Series B) does browser infrastructure. They're all allergic to the Windows desktop. The reason is obvious once you name it: dealing with heterogeneous Windows UIs, Citrix session management, healthcare vendor hostility, and HIPAA requirements is genuinely miserable work. That disgust reaction is Minicor's moat. When Stripe launched, the reason no other developer had built a simple payments API wasn't that nobody wanted one — it was that nobody wanted to deal with PCI compliance and bank integrations. Minicor occupies the same structural position: the schlep repels competitors and the compliance certifications compound the barrier over time.

One customer processing 25,000 patients per day and six logos on the website — for a recently-pivoted pre-seed company, Minicor has real production usage, not just pilot agreements. The "sell to AI companies who need to connect to legacy systems" GTM is picks-and-shovels positioning that creates a natural referral loop: every AI startup selling into healthcare hits the same EHR wall Minicor hit. But here's where I start worrying about the growth compass. Minicor's dossier shows no revenue figures, no growth rate, no evidence of the 5-7% weekly compounding that tells me a company has found the grain of demand it can follow upward. The supported systems list is heavily healthcare — six of eight named systems are EHRs or practice management tools. Concentration in one vertical isn't fatal, but it means Minicor hasn't yet proven the problem generalizes beyond healthcare legacy systems, and healthcare has a nasty habit of producing long sales cycles that flatten growth curves.

The commoditization risk is the structural question Minicor has to answer and the dossier doesn't convince me they've answered it yet. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are all shipping computer-use capabilities. Minicor claims 93-96% click accuracy versus the foundation models' 80-85%. But accuracy gaps in AI close fast — that delta might be worth two years of differentiation or six months. If it's six months, Minicor's value has to live entirely in the managed infrastructure, orchestration, observability, and compliance layers. Those are real but they're the kind of value that scales linearly — you add customers, you add operational overhead — rather than superlinearly. The self-healing reflection agent is clever engineering, but I need to see evidence that Minicor's compounding advantage is in the workflow-specific knowledge and customer integration depth, not just the accuracy number on the website. The Stripe parallel holds only if Minicor's infrastructure layer becomes as embedded and switching-cost-heavy as Stripe's payment rails. One customer at 25,000 patients per day suggests that embedding is starting, but I'd want to see three or four customers at that depth before I'm confident.

The bull case for Minicor is genuinely strong: organic problem discovery, deep schlep that competitors avoid, production-scale usage, SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA at pre-seed, co-founders who've built together since college. The bear case is that foundation model computer-use commoditizes the core agent layer within 18 months, legacy vendors like Epic eventually open APIs under regulatory pressure, and Minicor becomes a thin compliance wrapper rather than a platform company. I'm landing on invest because the schlep blindness signal is one of the most reliable predictors in my framework — the problems people can't even bear to look at are the ones where small teams build durable advantages. Minicor's founders discovered this problem the right way, they're building in a domain that repels their competitors, and they've already shipped into production at meaningful scale. The commoditization risk is real but manageable if they deepen customer integration faster than the models improve. The condition that would flip me to pass: if in six months, foundation model computer-use accuracy reaches 93%+ out of the box and Minicor hasn't expanded beyond healthcare or shown compounding revenue growth.

The two dimensions that lock Minicor into the invest tier are organic problem discovery — the Laminar-to-Minicor pivot originated from a real customer encounter, not a market sizing exercise — and the schlep blindness quality of the idea itself, where every browser-based competitor's avoidance of Windows desktop automation validates the competitive moat Minicor occupies by default.
