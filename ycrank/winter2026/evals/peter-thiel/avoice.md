# Avoice -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

"Harvey for Architecture." The tagline tells you everything you need to know, and what it tells you is fatal. When a company defines itself as the application of someone else's insight to a different vertical, it has already confessed that it possesses no secret of its own. Harvey's secret -- that large language models could handle the adversarial complexity of legal reasoning at a quality threshold attorneys would trust -- was a genuinely non-obvious thesis in 2022. Copying that thesis and applying it to architecture is the definition of going from one to *n*. It is imitation, not creation. The Girardian dynamics are visible from orbit: across every professional services vertical, dozens of startups are racing to become "Harvey for X" -- for accounting, for medicine, for consulting, for compliance. When everyone can see the same playbook, no one has a monopoly.

The deeper problem is that the thesis itself -- "architecture firms waste too much time on documentation, and AI can automate it" -- is not a truth that anyone disagrees with. It is consensus. Autodesk knows it. Swapp AI knows it ($21.3 million raised, already integrated into Revit). Cove.tool knows it ($36.6 million raised). Togal.AI knows it ($22.65 million raised). When I count over $100 million in aggregate funding across four competitors pursuing adjacent versions of the same thesis, I am looking at a market with no secret -- only a crowd. The architecture AI space is becoming a Red Ocean before any player has achieved meaningful scale. This is precisely the dynamic I avoid: multiple funded players imitating each other into mutual margin destruction.

The market structure compounds the problem. Architecture is one of the most fragmented industries on earth -- 110,000+ firms, predominantly small, conservative in technology adoption, and operating on thin margins. This is the structural opposite of a monopoly-enabling market. Palantir succeeded because it started with a single customer -- the CIA -- that had virtually unlimited budget, existential urgency, and no alternative. Architecture firms are the anti-Palantir customer: thousands of small buyers with tight budgets, long evaluation cycles, and a strong cultural preference for the tools they already know. Selling enterprise software to fragmented small businesses is one of the most capital-intensive, lowest-leverage go-to-market motions in technology. The "$300M+ in active projects managed on platform" metric is carefully chosen to obscure what matters -- it tells me nothing about revenue, number of firms, retention, or willingness to pay. Five firms with large projects could generate that number.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Chawin Asavasaetakul is not a first-time founder -- he built Yindii to 700,000 users across three countries and reached profitability in Hong Kong, scaled an edtech company to $1M+ revenue and sold it, and spent two years building architecture sales teams before founding Avoice. That is a meaningful track record of execution across multiple domains. The firm-specific learning loop -- the Studio Library that accumulates a firm's standards, past projects, and material preferences -- could in theory create switching costs that deepen with usage, similar to how Palantir's government deployments become harder to extract over time. If Avoice could achieve deep penetration in a concentrated subsegment of architecture firms and build a data flywheel that makes the product dramatically better for long-tenured customers, there would be a path to defensibility. The claim of 13 hours saved per user per week, if real, suggests meaningful product-market fit among early adopters. But switching costs from accumulated data are not network effects -- they do not create the kind of compounding, winner-take-all dynamics that produce power-law returns. Each firm's data is siloed. There is no inter-firm value creation. The moat is shallow.

The technology does not clear my threshold for discontinuity. Avoice is an application layer built on third-party foundation models -- the dossier itself flags LLM infrastructure dependency as a risk. Multimodal comprehension of architectural drawings is a useful capability, but it is available to any team that combines GPT-4V or Claude's vision with domain-specific prompting and fine-tuning. The Research Agent that emails suppliers is clever workflow automation, not proprietary technology. Nothing here is 10x better in a dimension that structural competitors cannot replicate within 18 months. Swapp, with its deep Revit integration and $21 million in funding, arguably has a stronger technical moat by operating inside the dominant design tool rather than alongside it. Autodesk itself, which controls the architectural software stack, could build or acquire these capabilities with minimal friction -- and Autodesk's distribution advantage across its installed base of millions of seats would be insurmountable.

I respect the founders' execution history and their deliberate preparation -- two years in architecture sales before building is not the behavior of lean-startup iterators, and that counts for something. But execution ability deployed against a consensus thesis in a competitive market produces, at best, a modestly profitable business. It does not produce a monopoly, and it will not return a fund. The absence of a genuine secret, combined with active competition from multiple well-funded players and a market structure hostile to concentration, makes this a clear pass.
