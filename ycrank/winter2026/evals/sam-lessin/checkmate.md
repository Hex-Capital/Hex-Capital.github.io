# Checkmate -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Checkmate in one sentence: AlphaSense is a $4 billion company with $500 million in ARR and 6,500 enterprise customers, and they already shipped an AI Agent Interviewer in 2025 built on top of their $930 million Tegus acquisition -- 200,000+ expert transcripts of proprietary training data. That's not a theoretical competitive risk. That's an incumbent with overwhelming distribution doing exactly what you do, as a feature, right now. When I see a startup whose entire value proposition is already a line item on a dominant incumbent's product roadmap, my first instinct is to ask: what structural advantage does this startup have that the incumbent cannot replicate? And the honest answer here is: almost nothing. "Network-agnostic" is a positioning choice, not a moat. It means Checkmate plugs into other people's calendar invites from GLG, AlphaSights, and Guidepoint -- networks that have every incentive to restrict AI agents from joining their calls the moment they decide autonomous interviews threaten their brokerage economics.

The founder credentials are genuinely strong -- Garde's eight years as a Google PM plus building Monzo US from scratch, and Sha's NLP background at Google, computer vision research with Pieter Abbeel, and a prior CTO exit (Onyx, acquired by CureFit at 300K+ users). These are sane people. The question is whether they're pursuing an insane idea, and the answer is clearly no. "Use AI voice agents to automate expert network calls for hedge funds" is the most obvious application of current LLM voice technology to a known, expensive workflow. Every investor in finance is already expecting this transition. AlphaSense built it. InsightAgent built it. This is a consensus idea in a hot category with well-funded competitors -- the exact configuration my framework penalizes most heavily, because the returns have already been priced into the market.

The structural moat problem is severe. Strip away the AI and ask what business remains -- there's nothing. No physical operations, no protocol layer, no proprietary data network, no hardware installed anywhere. The AI IS the product. This fails my "cherry on top" test so completely it almost serves as the pedagogical example. Craftwork is a painting company that happens to use software for scheduling and dispatch -- take away the AI and you still have crews, trucks, and customer relationships. Checkmate without AI is a landing page. The compliance infrastructure (SOC II, MNPI guardrails) creates some switching costs once embedded in a fund's workflow, and I don't dismiss that -- hedge fund compliance teams move slowly and approve vendors reluctantly. But compliance certification is table stakes, not a moat. Every serious competitor in this space will have SOC II within a year. The transcript corpus could theoretically compound into something valuable, but AlphaSense starts with a 200,000-transcript head start, and any network effects from interview data are dwarfed by the incumbents' existing libraries.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: institutional investors use four or five expert networks simultaneously and want a single AI layer across all of them. AlphaSense's AI Interviewer only works within AlphaSense's walled garden, so the network-agnostic wedge gives Checkmate a real opening with multi-network power users. If Checkmate embeds deeply enough into fund workflows -- becoming the compliance-approved, always-on research layer -- the switching costs compound, and the proprietary transcript corpus becomes a genuine asset. The expert network market is growing at 15% CAGR toward $17 billion by 2035, and the automation layer could capture meaningful share of per-call economics. With the right enterprise sales motion, Checkmate could own the interstitial position between funds and their expert networks. I'd need to believe that expert networks won't block AI agents from their calls, that compliance switching costs are deep enough to hold against AlphaSense's distribution advantage, and that the founders can close enterprise deals fast enough to build a defensible corpus before the market consolidates. That's a lot of things that need to go right simultaneously -- and even in the best case, this remains an application-layer software business whose moat gets thinner as voice agent technology gets cheaper.

This is exactly the kind of company I pass on -- not because the founders aren't talented or the market isn't real, but because the structural logic is broken. A software tool that an LLM integration or incumbent feature launch could replicate within twelve months, in a market where the dominant incumbent has already replicated it. The founders would be better served applying their considerable skills to a problem where AI is the cherry on top of a fundamentally defensible business, rather than a problem where AI is the entire sundae.
