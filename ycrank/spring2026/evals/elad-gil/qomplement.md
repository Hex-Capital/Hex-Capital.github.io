# qomplement -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about qomplement is the commoditization problem. Document filling — taking extracted data and populating PDF forms and spreadsheet templates — is a task that frontier LLMs handle increasingly well out of the box. When I evaluate whether a company is "software-driven" versus "software-aware," the test is whether software is fundamental to competition or just a tool bolted onto the workflow. With qomplement, the core capability — mapping data fields from one document format into another — sits squarely in the zone where Claude, GPT-4, and Gemini are improving quarter over quarter without any startup needing to exist. An enterprise team with a systems integrator and API access to a frontier model can build a working version of this in a sprint. That's not a product category. That's a feature.

The "why now" story here is LLM document understanding crossing a fidelity threshold — and that's real, the capability improvement from 2023 to 2025 in multimodal document parsing has been substantial. But the structural problem is that this same "why now" applies equally to Rossum ($111M raised, $44.9M in 2024 revenue), Reducto ($108M, a16z-backed), and Nanonets ($42M, Accel-backed). These companies already own the extraction relationship with enterprise customers. Adding a filling step downstream of their existing extraction pipeline is a natural product extension, not a new market. When I think about the markets I've invested in — PagerDuty in operations alerting, Gusto in payroll — the common thread was that no adjacent incumbent could trivially absorb the category. Operations alerting wasn't a feature of your monitoring tool; payroll wasn't a feature of your accounting software. Document filling *is* a feature of your document extraction platform. The "extraction vs. filling" distinction qomplement draws may be real in the workflow, but it doesn't map to a standalone product category with structural barriers.

The strongest bull case would go like this: enterprise document workflows are deeply fragmented, every company has unique internal forms, and the template-mapping layer — understanding that *this* field on *this* company's purchase order maps to *that* cell in *their* specific spreadsheet template — creates accumulated institutional knowledge and switching costs that compound over time. If qomplement could become the universal "last mile" connector between extracted data and internal systems, the template library itself becomes a distribution moat. The Contalink connection in the CEO's background is interesting here — Contalink operates in cloud accounting automation in Mexico, which is directly adjacent to invoice and PO workflows. That domain familiarity isn't nothing. And the "turning companies into multi-agent systems" vision on Codemix gestures toward a broader platform ambition beyond single-step document filling. If the team executes fast enough to build deep template libraries across verticals before incumbents add filling, there's a version of this that works.

But I don't find that bull case convincing enough to invest. Three things hold me back. First, the market isn't non-obvious — it's a consensus-hot, well-funded space where the differentiation (filling vs. extraction) is too thin to sustain a standalone company. Nobody is dismissing this market; it has $260M+ in recent funding across four competitors. Second, the founding team's public track record is limited. The CEO graduated from Tec de Monterrey in 2024 and co-founded a bootstrapped manufacturing marketplace. The CTO has no findable public profile — no LinkedIn confirmed, no GitHub, no prior work history. When I can't assess the technical depth of the team building a product where the technical moat is the entire value proposition, that's a real gap. Third, and most critically, I don't see a product-to-distribution trajectory. Document filling doesn't naturally expand into adjacent product categories the way Stripe's payment API became a channel for Atlas and Treasury. A form-filling agent is a form-filling agent. The multi-agent vision is stated but not grounded in a specific distribution wedge.

The technology cycle position is also working against qomplement rather than for it. Harvey succeeded because they built *ahead* of the capability curve — legal AI was impossible before GPT-4, so building the workflow layer early let them capture the market when the models caught up. Document filling is already possible with current models. qomplement isn't building ahead of a curve; they're building at the moment when the capability is becoming table stakes. That's the wrong side of the timing equation.
