# Maywood -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Maywood in one sentence: this is a sane team with a sane idea, and that's the worst combination in my framework. Drake Goodman left Blackstone to build AI for M&A document generation. Every associate at every bank in America has had the same idea in the shower. Every VC in the market thinks "AI for investment banking workflows" is brilliant. When the idea provokes zero confusion -- when every investor nods immediately -- the opportunity has already been priced into the market. The Blackstone pedigree, the MIT EECS credentials, the BCG automation experience -- all deeply credible. The brothers-as-cofounders dynamic with a long-time friend as CTO is actually the kind of relational depth I love seeing in founding teams. But founder quality without idea non-consensus means you're buying a lottery ticket at market price. Compare this to the Coinbase bet: Brian Armstrong proposing regulated crypto exchange infrastructure when Bitcoin was a punchline. THAT was a sane person with an insane idea. "AI automates tedious banking workflows" is a sane person with an idea that made the cover of every management consulting deck in 2024.

The structural moat question is where this really falls apart for me. Strip away the language and ask my core question: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? The answer is unambiguously weaker. Maywood's entire value proposition IS the AI-generated output -- CIMs, pitch decks, models, memos. The dossier itself acknowledges that "large language model providers could enable competitors to build similar generation capabilities." When the next generation of models makes it trivial for Datasite to bolt on a generative layer to their existing VDR platform -- and Datasite has $2 billion in funding, 1,400 employees, and is already acquiring AI companies like BlueFlame and Grata -- Maywood's position becomes structurally indefensible. The SOC 2 Type II certification and single-tenant deployment are real barriers, but they're barriers any well-capitalized competitor clears in 12-18 months. These are compliance checkboxes, not moats. The "firm-specific style learning" argument is the strongest defensibility claim, but I've heard this exact pitch before -- it's the same data-flywheel story Birchbox told about customer preference data. It sounds like compounding lock-in until a competitor ingests the same corpus of historical documents and matches your output quality with a better underlying model.

This fails my "cherry on top" test definitively. Would this business exist if AI didn't exist? No. Remove the LLM layer and you have nothing -- no physical operations, no installed infrastructure, no protocol, no primitive. The AI IS the product, which is exactly the category I've been arguing is a terrible startup investment since 2024. Apple, Google, Microsoft -- they all have enterprise distribution into banks. When Microsoft integrates deeper financial document generation into Copilot with enterprise-grade compliance, what's Maywood's response? Domain specialization is a temporary advantage, not a structural one.

Now, the bull case, because I want to be honest about what would have to be true for me to be wrong. If Maywood embeds deeply enough into bank workflows -- becoming the single source of truth for deal execution, with integrations touching every system, every data vendor, every internal database -- the switching costs become about integration depth, not AI capability. Think Bloomberg Terminal: the AI isn't what makes Bloomberg sticky, it's the infrastructure tentacles. If Maywood achieves that kind of integration density at five or ten banks before Datasite or Microsoft moves, they become an acquisition target worth $500M+ to an incumbent who'd rather buy than build. The team composition supports this thesis -- Drake knows exactly what bankers need, Kent and Esteban can build it, and they're already "working with several banks" with a customer claiming doubled capacity. That's a real demand signal. The question is whether they can convert that into deep enough infrastructure integration to create genuine lock-in before the window closes. In a world where they execute perfectly and the incumbents move slowly, there's a path to a meaningful outcome. But it's a narrow path, and I don't bet on narrow paths when the structural dynamics favor the incumbents with distribution.

On the primitive test: Maywood is building an application on someone else's AI infrastructure for someone else's existing workflow. It's not creating a new financial primitive that other companies build on. It's not establishing a protocol or standard. It's a tool. Tools get commoditized. This is the opposite of what I look for -- compare it to Venmo creating peer-to-peer payment rails, or Aleo establishing zero-knowledge privacy as infrastructure. Those investments create compounding network effects. Maywood creates a better way to make PowerPoints, and I say that with full respect for how painful those PowerPoints are to make manually. The narrative can expand -- M&A execution to all of investment banking to all of financial services -- but that expansion path is shared with every horizontal AI tool that decides to go vertical into finance. The ceiling isn't infinite; it's defined by competing with every AI company targeting the same adjacencies.
