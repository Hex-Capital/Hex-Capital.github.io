# AICE -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

AICE is a technically-credible aerospace founder building the most permissioned business architecture I underwrite: submarine drones sold to naval ministries, manufactured in France, gated by ITAR and defense procurement cycles. Greg Chomette's MIT AeroAstro MSc and NASA asteroid-risk autonomy work give AICE a real specific-knowledge floor, and that high-floor founder pairing against a zero-floor leverage architecture is the entire analytical signature here. The dimensions are inverted from what my ledger funds.

**Twitter** in 2007 is the high rung on leverage architecture -- media as protocol, anyone posts, marginal cost of replication is zero, no gatekeeper between Jack Dorsey and the user. AICE is the structural opposite on every clause of that sentence: hardware rated to 600m depth, manufactured in Paris, distributed via direct sales to "defense ministries" per the dossier's inferred GTM, gated by qualification cycles that take years and security clearances that take longer. The leverage here is capital and regulatory permission -- precisely what nav.al/product-media calls out as requiring gatekeeper approval. The Anchorage Digital exception is the only door AICE could walk through, but Anchorage rode a pre-consensus crypto-category bet I had been compounding into since **MetaStable** (2014); I have no equivalent multi-year accumulation thesis on naval AUVs that would let me flip "permissioned" into "permission-as-moat" for AICE.

Single-sided defense hardware is the low rung on network compounding -- the Nth navy buying an AICE swarm does not make the (N-1)th navy's swarm more valuable, and there is no two-sided supply-demand loop the way **Uber** (2010) or **OpenSea** (2018) compounded. The failure mechanism at the low rung is that asymmetric-upside math collapses: 1x downside, but the upside ceiling is bounded by procurement budgets, not by N-squared user value. AICE's dossier confirms the pattern -- "defense procurement contracts with navies and critical-infrastructure operators" is the inferred business model, which is exactly the linear-revenue shape that does not produce the 10,000x geometry the Knowledge Project #18 framework requires.

Specific knowledge is where AICE actually scores above its category. Chomette's MIT AeroAstro thesis and NASA autonomy/risk work map to AUV stack components -- sensor fusion, autonomous decision-making under uncertainty -- and Bouteiller's airplay-esp32 repo at 282 stars signals real embedded-systems shipping. But the comparison that loads is **Perplexity** (2022): Aravind Srinivas brought a Berkeley ML PhD specifically in retrieval to a retrieval product. AICE brings aerospace autonomy to subsea, and the dossier is explicit that "neither founder has a documented prior naval-defense or marine-systems role." Specific knowledge in the wrong adjacency is the exact failure mode the Almanack flags -- replicable by any capable aerospace generalist a defense incumbent could hire.

Mechanism on market pivot-room over 18 months: the AUV TAM cited is $3.13B growing to $4.64B by 2030, of which defense/homeland-security is 45.06%. That floors AICE's reachable market at roughly $1.4B in 2025 dollars, with the realistic obtainable slice gated by which navies will buy from a 2-person, 2-week-post-pivot French-manufactured startup over Anduril's Dive-LD. The pivot vectors out of "subsea defense swarm" are narrow -- commercial subsea inspection (oil & gas, telecom cable monitoring) is the obvious adjacent, but that path requires a different sales motion AICE has not signaled. This is the inverse of the "niche markets don't work" axiom from Venture Hacks: AICE is in a niche of a niche with one realistic pivot lane.

Counterfactual on founder trio: for AICE to clear the 10-year-partner gate above default, the dossier would need to show either a prior shipped product the two founders took to a customer together, or public writing/speaking from Chomette of the kind that signals depth rather than promotion. Neither exists in the dossier -- no Twitter/X presence, no GitHub for Chomette, no advisors, no investors named beyond YC. The integrity reads clean by absence of red flags, not by presence of signal, which is exactly the default-7 case my framework anticipates.

AICE's 36-month forecast: AICE either dies inside 24 months from defense-procurement cycle starvation, or pivots to commercial subsea inspection (telecom cable monitoring, offshore wind foundation survey) at sub-$2M ARR by mid-2028; AICE does not get acquired by Anduril, because Anduril already bought Dive Technologies in February 2022 for exactly this capability and does not need a 2-person swarm team in San Francisco/Paris to reach the small-form-factor end of the AUV stack. The mechanism is simple -- a 2-person team that pivoted two weeks ago cannot close ITAR-gated contracts faster than Anduril, Helsing, and Saronic can ship from their existing $60B / $18B / $9.25B-valuation positions.

The strongest objection is the sovereign-defense bull case: France's May 2024 underwater-acoustics AI program (Defense News) plus Nicolas Dessaigne's France ties at YC plus EU sovereign-tech procurement pressure could open DGA contracts that AICE's Paris manufacturing footprint is uniquely positioned to capture, and Helsing's October 2025 acquisition of Blue Ocean for Australian AUV capability proves the acqui-hire window for swarm IP is open at $20-50M outcomes. That case stings -- it would map AICE onto the Anchorage "permission-as-moat" exception template. The dossier dismantles it on execution: zero named DGA contacts, zero advisors with naval pedigree, zero LOIs, and the swarm-architecture claim is itself "[Inferred]" rather than demonstrated. One verifiable condition would flip the verdict -- a signed DGA or French Navy contract of any size announced within 12 months. Until then AICE fails my leverage check before the founder check loads, and the asymmetric-payoff math doesn't redeem it.
