# Taiga -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Candid Health has raised $99.5 million. AKASA has raised $205 million. LunaBill, from YC's own Fall 2025 batch, already reports $764K in contracted ARR. Taiga enters this landscape with two recent university graduates, no disclosed traction, and zero public revenue signals. When I look at AI-powered medical billing in 2026, I see a space where "AI-enabled healthcare startups captured 62% of all digital health venture funding in H1 2025" — this is not a frontier that makes VCs shift in their chairs. It's a consensus thesis with consensus capital behind it. Nobody at a dinner party would flinch if you said "I'm using AI to fix medical billing." They'd nod and say "oh, that makes sense." That nod is a warning sign in my framework. Taiga is operating in exactly the kind of well-funded, eagerly-competed-for space that I've learned to approach with deep skepticism — not because the problem isn't real, but because the path to winning requires something structurally different from what everyone else is building, and I don't yet see what that is.

Nanda Guntupalli and Adam Wax grew up in physician households — both founders' parents own independent practices and both watched medical billing consume their families' time and revenue. They've been friends since third grade. I recognize the emotional truth in that origin. Watching your parents struggle with a broken system at the dinner table is real. But when I ask "tell me your story," I'm looking for something deeper than proximity. I'm looking for whether the problem found the founder in a way that left scars, that created a biographical inevitability. Nanda studied CS at Penn. Adam studied CS and Math at Lehigh, interned at construction firms, and was accepted to Goldman Sachs programs. These are talented young people who observed a problem through their parents' experience and decided to solve it — that's admirable, but it reads more like "I saw a market opportunity through a personal lens" than "my life made me incapable of ignoring this." Compare this to the Crusoe founders who physically stood in North Dakota watching billions of dollars of energy burn into the sky and couldn't walk away. Taiga's founders watched from a comfortable distance. Neither has prior healthcare industry work experience, no previous startup, no exit. The childhood friendship is a genuine durability signal — I've seen co-founder relationships forged in adolescence survive stress that MBA-paired teams can't — but durability of relationship isn't the same as being forged for this specific fight.

Medical billing is, without question, one of the most opaque and extractive systems in American life. The $265 billion lost annually to billing errors, denied claims, and administrative overhead — that's not inefficiency, that's a system designed to be navigable only by specialists, extracting value through deliberate complexity. Insurance companies benefit when claims get denied. Billing codes are intentionally arcane. I lived inside systems like this as a teenager — systems that punish you for not knowing the rules that nobody taught you. So when Taiga says they're attacking medical billing, I feel the pull. But here's where I pause: Taiga is optimizing within the extractive system, not dismantling it. They're helping doctors navigate the opacity more efficiently — submitting cleaner claims, predicting denials, generating appeal letters grounded in payer policy. The system itself stays opaque. Patients still don't understand their bills. Payers still benefit from complexity. Compare this to Affirm, which made lending terms transparent to consumers and structurally shifted power from credit card companies to borrowers. Taiga makes the hidden system more navigable for one privileged party (physicians), but it doesn't make the system visible to the people most harmed by it (patients). That's a meaningful distinction in my framework — it's the difference between infrastructure that reveals and infrastructure that optimizes.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Taiga's full-stack agency model — upload your visit data and we handle everything downstream — might be exactly right for small independent practices that can't hire billing staff and can't use SaaS tools like Candid Health because they don't have anyone to operate the software. The structural argument is real: Waystar and R1 RCM's cost structures make small practices unprofitable, creating a genuine disruptive innovation gap. Two hundred thousand physician-owned practices in the U.S. are slowly being absorbed by private equity groups and hospital systems partly because billing complexity makes independence unsustainable. Taiga could be the thing that preserves independent medicine. And the EHR integrations — seven named platforms including Athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, and Epic — create real switching costs once a practice is live. If Taiga accumulates enough denial-prediction data across payers and specialties, the data flywheel could become a genuine moat. I've seen this movie before with Carta, where cap table software became infrastructure that nobody could leave. But Carta had network effects — investors, lawyers, and companies all needed to be on the same platform. Taiga's lock-in is operational (switching costs from re-integration), not networked. And the core AI capabilities — coding from clinical notes, denial prediction, appeal generation — are built on foundation models that any well-funded competitor can access. The dossier explicitly notes: "LLM-based medical coding and claims scrubbing are technically reproducible."

The condition that would flip me to invest: if Nanda and Adam showed me that Taiga had already processed enough claims to demonstrate a measurable, payer-specific denial-prediction advantage that no competitor could replicate without equivalent volume — real data showing their models outperform Candid Health or AKASA on denial rates for specific payer-specialty combinations. That would be evidence of an emerging infrastructure moat, not just an AI wrapper on billing workflows. Without that, Taiga is two talented CS graduates building a technically reproducible product in a consensus market where competitors have hundred-million-dollar head starts. The biographical connection through their parents is genuine but not forging. The invisible system is real but Taiga optimizes within it rather than dismantling it. The space is crowded, not contrarian. I wish Nanda and Adam well — the problem they're solving matters, and their family connection to it is authentic — but this isn't a Long Journey bet. It lacks the biographical inevitability, the contrarian discomfort, and the structural moat that my highest-conviction investments share.
