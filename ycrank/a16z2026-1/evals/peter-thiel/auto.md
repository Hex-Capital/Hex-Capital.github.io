# Auto -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Dave Evans built Snap's Mini-Programs platform. Sam Hare built Snap's AR Lens experience. Both sold separate camera startups to Snap — PlayCanvas in 2017, Seene in 2016 — and now they have reunited to build Auto, which is structurally a Mini-Programs platform for camera output, minus Snap's 800 million users. The founder credentials are the strongest signal in this dossier and the only reason Auto merits serious analysis. A PhD in computer vision and a decade of shipping camera products at scale is not fungible expertise. But my framework evaluates the idea's monopoly structure first and the founder second, because a brilliant team pursuing a competitive market is still a team that failed to escape competition.

Where is Auto's secret? The implied thesis is that photos should become persistent, task-specific mini-applications — "Frames" — rather than static images or one-shot answers. Ask yourself: would any informed observer disagree with this? Google Lens processes 12 billion visual searches per month and grows 250% year-over-year. Apple shipped Visual Intelligence as a built-in OS feature on iPhone 16. OpenAI integrated vision into ChatGPT for 200 million weekly users. The entire technology industry agrees that camera output should be actionable and intelligent. Auto's Frame abstraction is a specific UX design choice — a good one, perhaps — but a UX pattern is not a secret. It is not an important truth that few people agree with. It is an interface decision that any of three trillion-dollar incumbents could ship in a quarterly product update. The dossier itself concedes the point: "The underlying multimodal AI models (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) are available via API to any developer." When your own competitive analysis acknowledges that your technology stack is commodity, you have diagnosed the absence of a secret.

The competitive landscape activates every red flag in my framework simultaneously. Google Lens has 1.5 billion monthly active users and zero distribution cost through Chrome and Android integration. Apple Visual Intelligence is embedded in the operating system with zero acquisition cost — and Auto is iOS-only, meaning every Auto user already has Apple's competing product installed by default. ChatGPT Vision has OpenAI's $157 billion war chest behind it. This is not a market with "few or no competitors" — it is a market where the three most powerful technology platforms on earth are active participants optimizing their products continuously. Auto's $9.99/month subscription competes against Google Lens at free and Apple Visual Intelligence at free. The existence of a "competitive landscape" comparing five players is itself the clearest red flag: it means the market is competitive by definition.

The strongest bull case rests on the user-generated Frame ecosystem creating network effects that neither Google nor Apple has incentive to build — Google because it optimizes for ad-linked search results, Apple because it optimizes for hardware attachment. If Auto's Frame library reached critical mass, switching costs would compound as users accumulated custom Frames and community-created workflows. This is a structurally sound argument about incentive misalignment among incumbents. For it to work, three things must be true simultaneously: the Frame abstraction must be sufficiently compelling that users choose a $9.99/month third-party app over free built-in alternatives; the user-generated ecosystem must achieve density before any incumbent replicates the pattern; and Apple must not restrict or deprecate the APIs Auto depends on. Five App Store ratings and zero press coverage three weeks after launch provide no evidence that the first condition is met. The Wish parallel haunts this structure — a consumer app entering a market dominated by platform incumbents with massive distribution advantages, where the value proposition (convenience, lower friction) could not overcome the structural advantages of the existing players. Wish IPO'd at $14 billion and lost over 90% because no-moat consumer products in incumbent-dominated markets do not compound.

Auto's power-law ceiling is constrained by the same structural problem. Even if Auto executes flawlessly, the realistic upside is a $500 million to $1 billion acquisition — a repeat of the founders' previous Snap exits at larger scale. That is a good outcome for the founders and a terrible outcome for a fund structured around power-law returns. Consumer camera apps do not produce winner-take-all dynamics when the camera is controlled by platform owners (Apple, Google) who can replicate any third-party innovation as a system feature. The distribution problem compounds the ceiling: iOS App Store only, generic name creating discoverability headwinds, no viral mechanism yet demonstrated. Evans and Hare know distribution from their Snap tenure, but Snap's distribution came from its own social network — Auto has no equivalent organic channel. The user-generated Frame sharing mechanic could theoretically produce viral loops, but this is speculation built on five ratings, not evidence.

I respect these founders — dual exits, deep technical credibility, genuine domain mastery. Paul Graham would invest on founder quality alone. But my framework is unambiguous: the monopoly structure does not exist. Auto is a pure software layer built on commodity APIs, entering the most competitive consumer AI market imaginable, with no physical-world technology barrier, no government customer pathway, no proprietary dataset, and no contrarian insight that the market disputes. The absence of a secret and the presence of trillion-dollar competitors in the identical category are the decisive factors. The Monopoly Secret score and the Physical-World Technology Moat score — the two dimensions where Auto scores lowest — together define why this falls outside my investment criteria regardless of team quality.
