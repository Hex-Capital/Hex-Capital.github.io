# InLoop Robotics -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: warehouse robotics is one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture right now. Nimble just closed at a billion-dollar valuation. Dexterity is at $1.65 billion. Amazon acqui-hired the entire Covariant team. Every sophisticated investor on Sand Hill Road is tripping over themselves to fund robots that pick things up and put them down in warehouses. When I look at InLoop, the first question isn't "is this a good team building a good thing?" -- it's "where's the mispricing?" And I don't see one. The market has priced warehouse robotics at a massive premium. You're buying into a category where the upside is already in the price, and you're doing it with a team that has 500x less capital than their direct competitors. That's not contrarian investing. That's walking into a knife fight with a plastic spoon.

The human-in-the-loop concept is intellectually honest -- I'll give them that. Most robotics companies pretend they've solved autonomy and delay deployment forever while they burn cash in the lab. InLoop says "our robots are imperfect, they'll ask for help, and every time a human intervenes we get better training data." That's a sane deployment philosophy. Problem is, it's *too* sane. It's a sensible engineering decision, not a category-creating insight. Tesla did shadow mode. Waymo uses teleops for edge cases. The concept of shipping imperfect autonomy and improving from production data isn't new -- it's standard ML practice applied to robotics. This doesn't pass my primitive test. They're not creating a new behavior or a new financial instrument or a new market structure. They're building a warehouse robot with a different deployment strategy than other warehouse robots. That's execution differentiation, not primitive creation, and execution differentiation in a capital-intensive hardware market where your competitors have raised a combined half billion dollars is a losing position.

The founders are young engineers from TUM Munich with real robotics chops -- Sharfeddine won the 2025 BEHAVIOR Challenge, Rizali co-founded what's apparently Germany's largest student robotics initiative. Those are legitimate credentials for smart, capable engineers. But I haven't watched these people across multiple ventures. I don't know how they respond when the third deployment breaks in a way the AI can't handle and the customer is screaming. And critically, the idea they're pitching isn't insane -- it's prudent. "Ship robots that ask for help" is the kind of thing a reasonable engineer says and everyone nods. The sane/insane ratio is inverted from what I want: sane people pitching a sane idea. That's fine if you're an analyst at Bain. It doesn't make my heart rate change.

The bull case deserves a real paragraph because it's not nothing. If the data flywheel works -- if every teleoperator intervention genuinely feeds back into the model at a rate that outpaces lab-trained competitors -- InLoop could achieve deployment velocity that the big players can't match. Small and mid-sized warehouses are genuinely underserved by the current crop of automation vendors who all want to sell $2M+ systems to Amazon and FedEx. The zero-CapEx RaaS model is the right economic structure for that segment. And the Metropolis comparison is real -- I seeded Metropolis when it was just an AI parking play, and it's now worth $5 billion. Physical-world robotics deployed at scale can create enormous value. The difference is that Metropolis controls parking infrastructure. InLoop doesn't control warehouses -- they're a vendor inside someone else's facility, and their AI stack appears to be built on foundations (like Physical Intelligence's openpi, which Sharfeddine has literally forked on GitHub) that any funded competitor can access. The moat isn't structural. It's a head start, and head starts in a category with this much capital flowing in are measured in months, not years.

Bottom line: this is a competent team building a reasonable product in an absurdly crowded, consensus-priced category. Every time I see a robotics pitch against competitors with 200x your funding, I think about how many turns of the crank you need to survive. Venmo needed many turns -- but Venmo wasn't competing against companies with a billion-dollar war chest building the same thing. The risk here isn't that InLoop is bad. The risk is that InLoop is good enough to attract attention but undercapitalized enough to get crushed or acqui-hired before the data flywheel matters. Pass.
