# Snapp Stats -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Shawn Tsao built Caviar and sold it — first to Square for over $100 million, then Square sold it to DoorDash for $410 million. That's a real exit from a real company, and it's the strongest signal in Snapp Stats's entire dossier. I've made my best investments by following founders across companies: I backed Patrick Collison at Auctomatic when he was a teenager, then put $15K into Stripe for 2% because I already knew what he was. The question with Tsao isn't whether he can build and ship — Caviar proved that. The question is whether the thing he's building now is the right thing, and whether the reason he's building it is the right reason.

Here's what bothers me. Tsao's whole career is food: Munch On Me (YC S11, daily deals for food), Caviar (food delivery), Umai Hospitality Group (The Halal Guys, Rooster & Rice), investing in an Oakland soccer club. Andrew Tamura was building Rosie AI, an agent for Excel modeling — his LinkedIn still lists it as his current role, which is either a minor oversight or a more concerning signal about commitment. Alex Marshall built AI models at Genentech and for the Pentagon. Min Park's background is largely opaque. None of these four people appear to have been a coach scrubbing game film at 2am, or an athlete frustrated by the gap between what they watched and what they understood. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas — it's to notice problems you already have. When a food delivery founder, an Excel AI founder, a biotech data scientist, and a finance person converge on "AI for sports video," I don't hear organic discovery. I hear a team that assembled around a market opportunity identified through trend analysis. The origin story is missing, and with truly organic discovery, the origin story is the part founders can't stop telling you.

"AI for sports analytics" is precisely the kind of idea that sounds good to everyone. Nobody reacts to it with confusion or skepticism — they nod and say "that makes sense." WSC Sports has raised $136 million doing AI highlights for broadcasters. Genius Sports is public at $670 million in revenue. Sportradar does over a billion. The English Premier League partnered with Microsoft for AI features in 2025. When every major sports organization is already investing in AI, and multiple well-funded companies are expanding into adjacent segments, the returns have been competed away from the category level. Snapp Stats would need a genuinely non-obvious insight to find alpha here, and the dossier's description of a "Large Visual Memory Model" with "persistent, searchable memory" reads more like a technical architecture choice than a contrarian bet. Calling your product a "Superhuman Sports AI Teammate" is the kind of marketing language that makes me reach for the next application.

The strongest bull case for Snapp Stats is that Tsao's operational track record is genuinely exceptional. Building Caviar required exactly the do-things-that-don't-scale mentality I prize — coordinating restaurants, delivery logistics, and demanding consumers in a market that looked impossibly competitive before DoorDash, Uber Eats, and Postmates validated it. If Tsao brings that same operational intensity to sports video analysis, and if Marshall's AI expertise from Genentech and the Pentagon translates to sports-specific model quality, the team has enough talent to execute. The "10x pipeline growth" during a16z Speedrun and closing at least one customer during the program shows some early momentum. The dual B2C/B2B positioning — a personalized sports feed for fans alongside a coaching analytics tool for teams — is concerning with six people, but Tsao has navigated multi-sided marketplaces before. If they pick one segment and attack it with the focus that made Caviar work, there's a path. But that path requires this team to develop deep domain expertise they don't currently demonstrate, in a space where well-capitalized incumbents are actively building the same capabilities.

I keep comparing Snapp Stats to the Collison trajectory and finding the analogy breaks. Collison went from Auctomatic (web auctions) to Stripe (payment APIs) — both were developers building infrastructure for developers. The domain knowledge compounded. Tsao is going from food delivery to sports AI, and there's no compounding. The schlep in sports video analysis — the domain-specific training data, the integration with coaching workflows, the sales cycles with college athletic departments — is real, but it's not the kind of schlep that repels competitors because it's unglamorous. It's the kind that attracts them because AI + sports is exciting. The competitive moat Snapp Stats describes — persistent memory across analyzed footage — is a feature, not a structural barrier. General-purpose multimodal models from OpenAI and Google are improving at video understanding quarterly.

Tsao's Caviar exit earns Snapp Stats a hearing that most pre-seed companies don't get. But a hearing isn't a check. I don't see the organic connection between these founders and this problem, the idea occupies consensus territory rather than the "seems bad, is good" sweet spot, and the competitive landscape is dense with well-funded players moving in the same direction. The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery — where the absence of any founder with a sports background is a specific, material gap — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where "AI for sports" fails the Black Swan test by being exactly the kind of idea that generates immediate enthusiasm from everyone who hears it.
