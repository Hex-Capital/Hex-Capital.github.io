# Kaaro -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

"Second-generation railroader" — that phrase caught me before anything else in Kaaro's story. Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran are both described this way, and if it means what I think it means — that they grew up in families where railroads put food on the table, where the dinner table conversation was about car tracings and interchange rules and crew schedules — then there's something real underneath this company. But I need to be honest about what I can and can't see. When I ask "tell me the story," what I get back is: two teenagers from Alberta who were the first two engineers at Toma, scaled an AI voice agent product from 20 to hundreds of car dealerships, dropped out of Waterloo and UBC respectively, and now they're bringing the same playbook to railroads because they have family connections to the industry. That's a smart strategic read. It's resourceful. But it's not the same thing as a founder who was broken open by the problem and rebuilt themselves around it. At 19 and 20, Sai and Gautham haven't had enough life to be forged in the way I look for — they've had enough life to be impressively precocious, which is a different thing entirely. The Toma experience matters: building AI agents that automated phone workflows in auto dealerships and scaling to hundreds of customers is directly transferable execution proof. That's more than most pre-seed founders can show. But execution proof is not biographical inevitability.

The comparison that keeps surfacing for me is Flock Safety — another company selling into an insular, conservative industry where outsiders get nowhere and trust gates every deal. When I found Flock Safety through a WiFi network name at a YC event, the founders had already figured out that you can't sell surveillance technology to law enforcement by being a Silicon Valley outsider. You need to be someone they'll take a meeting with. Kaaro's answer to this problem — CPKC engagement, CN conversations, Gulf & Ohio Railways building, memberships in CARS and Canadian Railway Club and NAMRC — all flow from the same structural insight. If your family is inside the railroad world, you don't need to cold-call a Class I procurement department. That's a genuine advantage that no horizontal AI agent startup can replicate by throwing money at business development. But Flock Safety had something else: the surveillance space made civil liberties advocates genuinely nervous, VCs genuinely uncomfortable. Kaaro's space doesn't provoke that reaction. "AI workflow automation for railroads" is niche, but it's not contrarian — nobody shifts in their chair when they hear it. They just wonder if the TAM is big enough. The difficulty here is in execution (18-month Class I sales cycles, legacy green-screen integration with Wabtec RailConnect) rather than in the thesis itself being uncomfortable or socially stigmatized. That distinction matters in my framework.

Railroads genuinely do run on tribal knowledge, phone calls, and opacity — "Teams chase. Systems lag. Revenue leaks." resonates as a description of how freight rail operations actually work. There IS a hidden system here: billing errors from manual data entry, idle cars sitting for 36+ hours because nobody flagged them, incident reports filed by phone and email with no structured capture. Revenue leaks through the cracks of fragmented, manual workflows that Wabtec RailConnect and Railinc's interoperability layer don't address. But the hidden system Kaaro exposes is internal enterprise inefficiency — the railroad companies themselves are losing money through their own operational opacity. That's different from the pattern that activates my deepest conviction: Uber attacked a cartel that gouged *consumers*. Affirm made lending terms transparent to *borrowers*. Ownwell fights property tax opacity that extracts from *homeowners*. Kaaro fights operational opacity that costs CPKC and CN revenue. Those are legitimate problems — billion-dollar problems — but they don't carry the same structural charge of making an extractive system accountable to the people it exploits. The power asymmetry runs inside the enterprise, not against vulnerable humans outside it.

The bull case I have to genuinely sit with: these founders have already executed the exact playbook. Toma went from founding to hundreds of customers to a $17M Series A led by a16z — and Sai and Gautham were the engine room for that scaling. They know how to build AI agents that replace phone-based workflows in traditional industries. They know how to integrate with legacy dealer management systems (CDK Global, Reynolds & Reynolds) which is structurally similar to integrating with legacy railroad TMS. They have a16z network from Toma facilitating their Speedrun admission. And the timing is genuinely interesting: CPKC completed the CP-Kansas City Southern merger in April 2023, creating new operational complexity across a three-country network that's crying out for unified workflow tools. Nobody else is building AI agents for the communication layer that sits around the structured railroad data. Wabtec ($25B market cap) has business model cannibalization risk — their revenue depends on the TMS licensing that Kaaro's agents would reduce dependence on. If Kaaro can convert the CPKC engagement to a contract and use that as proof-of-value for the remaining six Class I railroads, the concentrated market structure that looks like a risk actually becomes a feature: you only need a handful of customers to own the space. I take this seriously. But "we executed a playbook in auto dealerships and we're applying it to railroads" is strategic competence, not the kind of founder-problem fusion that makes me write first checks into companies that are literally blowing up on launch pads.

What would flip me: if Sai or Gautham could tell me a story about watching their parent — a railroader — come home frustrated night after night because the system was broken, because cars were lost, because revenue was leaking, and they couldn't unsee it. If the CPKC engagement converted to a signed contract with real revenue. If Wabtec's modernization cycle stalled and the AI overlay window widened. Right now Kaaro sits in a space where the founders are proven operators with genuine industry access building into a real gap — but the biographical forge isn't deep enough, the space isn't uncomfortable enough, and the invisible system isn't exploitative enough to trigger my highest conviction. Smart founders, real market, legitimate connections, missing the fire.
