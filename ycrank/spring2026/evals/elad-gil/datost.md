# Datost -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Datost is the structural conflict at the center of its distribution strategy. The company is building an AI data analyst inside Slack. Slack is owned by Salesforce. Salesforce also owns Tableau. Salesforce has already shipped Einstein AI features into Tableau and has every economic incentive to build exactly this product -- a conversational data agent inside Slack that connects to enterprise data sources. This isn't a hypothetical competitive threat on a roadmap somewhere; it's the natural convergence point of two major Salesforce acquisitions totaling $43B+. When I see a startup whose entire distribution surface is a platform owned by the company most motivated to build the competing product, that's not just platform risk -- it's building in a kill zone. I've seen this pattern before and it rarely ends well for the startup.

On market structure timing, I'll be direct: "AI data analyst" is one of the most consensus application categories in venture right now. Julius AI already has 2M+ users and $10M from Bessemer. Defog.ai is open-sourcing text-to-SQL. Microsoft is shipping Copilot into Power BI, Google has Looker AI, and Salesforce has Einstein in Tableau. The structural shift -- LLMs crossing the reliability threshold for SQL generation -- is real, but it's the opposite of nonobvious. Every major platform company and hundreds of startups are racing into this category simultaneously. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed. When I backed Perplexity, AI search was considered a failed concept. Datost is entering a market where the structural shift is so widely recognized that incumbents with existing enterprise distribution are already shipping products. That's not the kind of timing window where a two-person startup has a structural advantage.

The infrastructure-layer test is where this falls further. Datost is an application sitting on top of three infrastructure layers it doesn't control: Slack's platform APIs, foundation model APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.), and customer database connections. Other businesses don't build on Datost -- it's an end-user tool, not a platform. The "own computer" execution environment is interesting as a feature but doesn't constitute an infrastructure layer. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments API that thousands of companies integrated into their core transaction flow, or even Retool, which became the internal tools platform. Datost is a Slack bot. The data context it accumulates over time creates some switching friction, but that's incremental stickiness, not compounding infrastructure lock-in. I consistently prefer to invest in the layer that other products depend on, and this isn't that.

The single-miracle test is unfavorable. I count at least two independent breakthroughs required. First, the technology needs to reliably generate correct analysis across diverse database schemas, data lakes, and codebases -- this is harder than generating SQL for a single well-documented database, and reliability at production-grade accuracy is still a frontier problem. Second, and this is the bigger one: a two-person pre-seed team with no enterprise security credentials needs to convince companies to connect their production databases and data lakes to a Slack bot. Enterprise procurement for tools with database access involves SOC 2 compliance, security reviews, data governance approvals, and lengthy sales cycles. That's a distribution and trust miracle on top of a technology reliability miracle. Two miracles, compounding odds.

The strongest bull case would be this: Salesforce is too bureaucratic to build a good version of this, incumbents will cannibalize their own dashboard seat revenue if they ship a free chat-based analyst, and Datost's focused execution and Slack-native distribution could win bottom-up adoption before the market consolidates. There's something to the business model cannibalization argument -- Tableau charges per seat for dashboard access, and a Slack bot that eliminates the need for dashboards threatens that revenue stream. If Datost could become the default way non-technical teams query data, the habit formation and data context accumulation could create meaningful retention. But this requires the founders to navigate enterprise sales cycles they have no experience with (both are 21, with software engineering backgrounds at Traba, no enterprise analytics domain expertise, no prior scaling experience), while simultaneously outrunning every well-resourced team building the same capability. The market is large enough -- BI is $34B and growing -- but the question isn't market size, it's whether this team in this competitive structure can capture enough of it before the window closes.

I'm passing. The market timing is consensus rather than nonobvious, the product sits at the application layer rather than the infrastructure layer, the distribution strategy depends on a platform whose owner is the most natural competitor, and the miracle count is too high for the founder profile. Good engineers building a real product in a real market, but not my kind of bet.
