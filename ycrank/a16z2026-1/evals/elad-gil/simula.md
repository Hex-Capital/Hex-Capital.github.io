# Simula -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking fact about Simula isn't what the company is building -- it's what the competitive landscape tells you about market timing. Koah Labs has raised $26M+ including a $20.5M Series A in February 2026. ZeroClick launched with $55M from Ryan Hudson, who sold Honey to PayPal for $4B. OpenAI's own ad pilot hit $100M+ ARR in under two months. Google has announced plans to bring ads to Gemini this year. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, I was reading a structural shift that the market had dismissed -- crypto infrastructure was nonobvious. AI ad monetization in 2026 is the precise opposite: every sophisticated investor and platform operator has already identified this as a high-value category. Simula is entering a market that's real but entirely consensus, and that's the most important thing to understand about the setup.

The structural catalyst here -- consumer AI apps maturing past subscription-only monetization -- is genuine. The eMarketer projection of AI-driven search ad spend growing from $1.1B in 2025 to $26B by 2029 reflects a real shift. But I keep asking myself: what does Simula see that Koah, ZeroClick, OpenAI's ad team, and Google's Gemini monetization group do not? The answer appears to be format innovation -- brand ambassadors, sponsored mini-games, rewarded video after AI responses. These are creative ad unit designs, not infrastructure. Compare this to Stripe: Stripe didn't differentiate on the checkout button's color, it built the payment rails that an ecosystem depended on. Simula's 30-minute SDK integration and interactive formats sit at the application layer of advertising, not the infrastructure layer. Ad networks that compete on format innovation face a well-documented commoditization pattern -- whatever format works gets copied by the network with more publisher distribution. Koah's 35M+ ad impressions and 7.5% CTR already suggest they're building distribution velocity that Simula can't match with pre-seed capital.

Simula fails my single-miracle test decisively. The company needs at least three independent things to go right simultaneously: win publisher distribution against competitors with 10-50x more funding, prove that interactive ad formats sustain higher engagement than simpler contextual units (which publishers historically prefer for lower integration friction), and avoid disintermediation by OpenAI and Google extending their first-party ad products to third-party developers. That's compounding odds in a category where the incumbents building their own solutions aren't sleepy legacy players -- they're the most capable technology companies on earth. When OpenAI can reach $100M+ ARR in ad revenue in under two months on their own platform, the assumption that they won't eventually offer an ad SDK to their ecosystem partners is a bet I wouldn't take.

Yizhen Zhen graduated from Dartmouth in 2024 -- roughly two years ago. The Google SWE and Bain Capital PE credentials are solid for someone that early in their career, and the lifelong game development background is relevant to Simula's gaming-focused GTM. Logan Nicholson's Talsey AI founding experience and gaming industry connections (Mobidictum Istanbul attendance) provide commercial coverage. But I've seen this pattern before: young, talented founders entering a capital-intensive land-grab market where execution speed is necessary but insufficient without capital parity. The absence of any shared employer or university overlap between the co-founders also gives me pause -- in the scaling challenges ahead, founder relationships forged through prior shared intensity tend to hold up better than ones assembled for a venture.

The strongest bull case for Simula requires believing three things: that interactive formats (games, brand ambassadors) will command enough of a premium over display ads to justify publisher switching costs, that third-party AI app publishers will prefer independent ad networks over platform-native solutions, and that Simula can build enough distribution before Koah and ZeroClick lock up the major publishers. The $15+ annual ad revenue per DAU claim, if real, would represent meaningful publisher economics -- at 5M+ DAUs, that's $75M+ in annual publisher revenue, making Simula's network extremely valuable. But the qualifier "at full rollout" and the absence of any independent verification of the 5M+ DAU figure make this aspirational math rather than traction evidence. I've written checks on less traction at pre-seed, but only when the market was nonobvious enough that I didn't need to worry about capitalization disadvantage against established competitors.

My framework points clearly to a pass. The decisive dimensions are Nonobvious Market Timing -- this market is consensus, crowded, and attracting the most sophisticated operators in tech -- and Single-Miracle Viability, where Simula needs simultaneous wins in distribution, format validation, and platform avoidance. I've seen the Wish pattern before: riding growth velocity in a hot category without structural moats, and it ended with a 99% drawdown from peak. Simula's category is real, but being right about the market isn't enough when everyone else is right about it too, and when the largest platforms are building their own solutions. I'd rather invest in whatever infrastructure layer all these AI ad networks depend on than pick which ad network wins the distribution race.
