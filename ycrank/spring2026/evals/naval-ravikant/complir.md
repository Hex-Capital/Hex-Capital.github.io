# Complir -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

107,489 products monitored, seven named Scandinavian retailers, 45% month-over-month growth, and 13 employees — those are the numbers on Complir. Strong enterprise traction at pre-seed is unusual and worth respecting. But the number that catches my eye is 13. Thirteen people burning through a €1.7M pre-seed to serve seven enterprise customers. Cursor hit $500M ARR with fewer than 50. Midjourney hit $500M revenue with 163. Complir has 13 people with no disclosed revenue and a handful of Nordic retailers. That ratio tells me something about the leverage architecture before I look at anything else — this is a labor-intensive enterprise business wearing software clothes.

Gustav Bang has 10 years in startups and ran Legora's Denmark operations. Tine Kühnel has a decade of B2B platform design and legaltech adjacency. Marc Kaa Brejner built systems for Danish government authorities. Solid operators, all three. But here is my problem with Complir's founding team: none of them spent years inside a compliance function at a retailer, a regulatory body, or a product safety consultancy. They identified regulatory compliance as a market opportunity — likely sharpened by GPSR enforcement in December 2024 — and moved quickly. Moving quickly is admirable. But specific knowledge cannot be taught, and it cannot be acquired in 18 months of customer work. If these founders disappeared tomorrow, any competent team with access to the same regulatory databases and LLM APIs could rebuild Complir's core product. The fact that Reglyr — backed by F4 Fund, launched April 2026 from San Francisco — is already doing exactly that confirms the replicability. When your competitive advantage can be replicated by a new entrant in under two years, you do not have specific knowledge. You have a head start.

The bull case deserves its full articulation. Complir's real advantage is not the founders' knowledge but the structural dynamics: the principal-agent problem at incumbents is genuine. Compliance & Risks, with 300+ enterprise clients and PE backing from Luminate Capital, generates revenue from consulting-augmented workflows. Automating interpretation would cannibalize their highest-margin service line — they are agents of their own P&L, unable to move where Complir, as a principal, can. The GPSR enforcement creates a regulatory forcing function that compresses sales cycles. The ERP/PIM/PLM integrations and shared supplier workspace create real switching costs once Complir is embedded in a retailer's product launch process. Angel investors — former Coop Group CEO Kraen Østergaard Nielsen, GS1 board member Per Thau — validate that industry insiders see Complir as credible infrastructure, not a toy. And 107K products monitored creates a data flywheel: each product-market regulatory mapping enriches the system for the next customer. An advocate would call Complir the Vanta of physical products and point out that Vanta is worth $2.45 billion.

The structural comparison to Vanta is precisely where Complir's leverage thesis breaks down. Vanta automates digital processes checking digital systems — SOC 2 evidence collection is fundamentally software checking software, pure zero-marginal-cost leverage. Complir must map physical product attributes to regulatory requirements across 40+ markets and 20+ product categories, where each market-category combination introduces non-linear complexity maintained by humans who understand local law. The AI layer accelerates document generation and data extraction — genuine code leverage — but the regulatory knowledge engine underneath requires continuous human curation as regulations change across 40 jurisdictions. Every new market Complir enters requires regulatory expertise that cannot be fully automated. That is labor leverage dressed up as AI leverage. The permissionless leverage exists in the software layer, but it sits on top of a permissioned foundation of regulatory interpretation that scales linearly with coverage breadth.

Complir's access democratization angle is real but unrealized. The EU Commission identifies regulatory compliance as a top-three barrier preventing European companies from exporting — Complir could give small brands access to cross-border compliance previously locked behind expensive consultancies. But every named customer — Flying Tiger Copenhagen, Matas, COOP, Konges Sløjd — is an established Scandinavian retailer, not the underserved SMB locked out of new markets. Complir is selling enterprise software to enterprises. The democratization potential exists in theory; in practice, the high-touch enterprise sales model and ERP integration requirements create exactly the gatekeeping that would need to be removed for true access expansion. My most enduring investments — AngelList, CoinList, Polymarket — gave millions of people access to something previously restricted to thousands. Complir gives a handful of Nordic retailers a better compliance workflow.

The compound interest test is where Complir shows its best structural signal. Each product-market regulatory mapping enriches the knowledge engine. Each customer's product catalog adds training data for the AI extraction models. Each new compliance document validates the regulatory interpretation layer. These effects compound — if Complir reaches sufficient density across product categories and markets, the knowledge engine becomes genuinely difficult to replicate from scratch. But compound interest requires time, and Reglyr's April 2026 launch means Complir's compounding window is already being contested. The question is whether Complir's 18-month head start and seven enterprise relationships represent enough accumulated advantage to outrun a well-funded SF competitor targeting the same buyer. My experience says head starts in enterprise software erode quickly when the underlying technology is commoditized — and LLMs for document generation are commoditized.

One verifiable condition would flip me from pass to invest: if Complir demonstrates that adding a new market-category regulatory mapping requires less than one person-week of human work per mapping and that 80%+ of the regulatory interpretation is fully automated, that would prove the leverage architecture is permissionless at its core. That would transform the 40+ markets claim from a maintenance burden into a compounding advantage. Until that is demonstrated, Complir is a competent enterprise software company solving a real problem with real customers — but through my framework, it lacks the specific knowledge density, the permissionless leverage architecture, and the team-to-value ratio that produce outlier returns.
