# CellType -- Paul Graham Evaluation

David van Dijk has been doing computational biology for over a decade. He built his lab at Yale in 2019. Cell2Sentence grew out of years of research at the intersection of machine learning and single-cell biology -- published at ICML, built in collaboration with Google DeepMind, endorsed by Sundar Pichai. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the answer is clear: van Dijk didn't find this problem, he lived inside it for ten years. He turned down Google to commercialize his own research. That passes my organic test convincingly. This is not a brainstormed idea. This is someone who spent a career building a tool and realized it could be a company.

But here's what troubles me. The methodology is published, the code is open-source under Apache 2.0, and the collaborator who helped build the 27B-parameter model is Google DeepMind -- which happens to own Isomorphic Labs, sitting on a $600 million war chest. Van Dijk essentially handed his recipe to the best-funded kitchen in the world. In software, open-sourcing your core can be a brilliant distribution strategy -- Redis, MySQL, the whole playbook. In drug discovery with a two-person team against Google? That's a different equation. The moat here has to be built through pharma partnerships and proprietary validation data, not through the technology itself. And building that moat requires commercial execution skills that neither founder has demonstrated.

The schlep factor is real but oddly configured. Drug discovery involves genuine regulatory and commercial schlep -- dealing with pharma bureaucracies, validation timelines, FDA pathways. Most ML researchers would rather publish papers. Van Dijk chose the harder path. But "AI drug discovery" is not a problem hiding in plain sight. It's a problem that Recursion ($461M raised), Insilico ($408M), Isomorphic Labs ($600M), and dozens of others are actively attacking. I've said the most impressive companies I've seen recently are not working on AI. This is squarely in the category where every VC is already excited, which means the upside has been partially priced. Van Dijk's organic path to this problem is genuine, but the destination is crowded.

The strongest case for CellType is this: van Dijk isn't just another team applying AI to drug discovery -- he invented a genuinely novel paradigm. Treating gene expression as language, converting cellular states into sentences that LLMs can process natively, is a fundamentally different approach from structure-based prediction (Atomwise, Isomorphic) or phenomic screening (Recursion). If Cell2Sentence turns out to be the right abstraction for modeling human biology, van Dijk has a meaningful head start as its originator. The Senhwa MOU and claimed Top 10 pharma engagement at pre-seed with "100% inbound deal flow" suggest real pull -- pharma companies are seeking CellType out, not the other way around. That's a signal you can't manufacture. The screening of 4,000+ compounds and the validated result of turning cold tumors hot represents actual scientific output, not a pitch deck promise. If I squint, this has shades of what made Stripe work: the originator of the insight who is willing to do the commercial schlep nobody else wants to touch.

Where it falls short of that analogy is in the team's readiness for the commercial transition. The Collison brothers were already building products for users when they started Stripe. Van Dijk is transitioning from academia -- a different operating mode entirely. The professor-student cofounding dynamic between van Dijk and Vrkic is functional but asymmetric, and neither has pharma business development experience. The team of two will need to grow quickly in dimensions (regulatory, BD, clinical) that are far from their core competence. This isn't a software product where two great hackers can ship to thousands of users from a laptop. Drug discovery requires navigating institutional relationships, and the founders will soon need to become managers rather than makers -- one of the patterns I watch for as a warning sign.

The technical credentials are exceptional. Van Dijk has 11,589 citations and built the core technology himself. Vrkic wrote software for CERN's Large Hadron Collider and has experience training foundation models at scale. These are genuine hacker-builders, not people planning to hire engineers after raising money. The prototype isn't hypothetical -- Cell2Sentence exists, the 27B model is trained, and wet-lab validation has been performed. On the technical axis, this is as strong as pre-seed gets.

I'd invest a small check. The organic origin is real, the technical founders are exceptional, and there's genuine inbound pull from pharma. But the open-source IP exposure, the consensus category, and the academic-to-commercial transition gap keep me from writing a larger one. The company needs to prove it can convert academic credibility into proprietary commercial advantage before the well-funded competition catches up.
