# Loops AI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Hakan Baş co-founded Peak Games and sold it to Zynga for $1.8 billion. That exit is the single most compelling fact in this dossier — and it is precisely what makes Loops AI a tragedy of misallocation. A founder capable of building a billion-dollar outcome has chosen to deploy that capability into AI chatbots for e-commerce, a market where Gorgias has raised $114 million, Rep AI has raised $11.2 million, Tidio has raised $26.8 million, Phia just closed a $35 million Series A, and — most damningly — OpenAI launched native agentic checkout in ChatGPT while Google launched agentic commerce directly in Search and Gemini. When your entire value proposition is being absorbed as a feature by the platforms you depend on, you do not have a business. You have a timing arbitrage that is about to close.

The canonical question — what important truth do very few people agree with you on? — yields nothing here. "AI can improve e-commerce product discovery and customer support" is not a secret. It is the literal thesis of every company in the competitive landscape table, every McKinsey deck on retail transformation, and now the product roadmap of OpenAI and Google themselves. Gartner forecasts 75% of online shopping interactions will involve AI-driven conversation by 2026. When Gartner agrees with your thesis, there is no contrarian insight left to exploit. Loops AI's stated differentiation — pre-purchase sales conversion versus Gorgias's post-purchase support, enterprise-grade versus Tidio's SMB focus — describes market positioning within a competitive category, not a monopoly-creating secret. Positioning is what you do when you lack structural dominance. Every player in a competitive market positions differently; none of them escapes competition by doing so.

The Wish comparison is structurally precise. Wish operated in e-commerce without a moat, competing on execution and geographic arbitrage (cheap Chinese goods to Western consumers) rather than on a proprietary insight or monopoly structure. Loops AI follows the same pattern: strong early execution in Turkey and MENA — 50+ brands including Tudors, P&G Fairy, Radisson Collection, and Mövenpick, all acquired with zero ad spend — constitutes geographic arbitrage, not monopoly. The Turkish and MENA e-commerce AI market may be underserved relative to the U.S., but dominating an underserved geography does not create the kind of structural moat that survives contact with well-funded competitors. When Loops AI enters the U.S. market, it faces Gorgias at $80 million-plus ARR with a $530 million valuation and the full weight of Shopify's ecosystem behind it. Geographic head starts are not last-mover advantages.

The distribution signals deserve acknowledgment because they are genuinely strong for a pre-seed company. Fifty-plus brands, $500K ARR with claimed 2x growth over 45 days, zero churn, and zero ad spend — if verified — represent unusual organic pull. This is the one dimension where Loops AI performs above its category. Ari Nazir and the team have clearly leveraged Baş's Turkish business networks to drive rapid adoption. The named customer list spanning hospitality (Radisson, Mövenpick), consumer goods (P&G Fairy), and fashion (Tudors) shows cross-vertical applicability. But superior distribution in a commodity market creates a nice business, not a monopoly. "Superior sales and distribution by itself can create a monopoly, even with no product differentiation" — but only when the distribution mechanism compounds into a structural barrier. Loops AI's distribution advantage is relational and geographic, not structural. It does not get stronger with each additional customer the way PayPal's eBay embedding or Facebook's campus-by-campus density did.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the multi-modal product stack (semantic search, virtual try-on, image search, sales agents combined) creates sufficient bundling complexity that no single competitor replicates it; that the Turkish/MENA beachhead generates enough proprietary interaction data across 50+ brands to create a defensible intelligence layer; and that OpenAI and Google's agentic commerce features commoditize Loops AI's competitors more than they commoditize Loops AI itself. The first is plausible but not monopoly-creating — product breadth is a competitive advantage, not a structural moat. The second requires orders-of-magnitude more data than 50 brands generate. The third is wishful thinking: when the platform eats the application layer, the application with $500K ARR is consumed before the one with $80 million ARR. Baş's proven ability to build and exit companies makes Loops AI a reasonable acquisition candidate for Gorgias or Shopify at some modest multiple — but companies built to be acquired never achieve the power-law returns that make an investment structurally relevant.

The power-law math eliminates this from consideration. Gorgias, the category leader with twelve thousand merchants and $80 million-plus in ARR, is valued at $530 million. Even if Loops AI executed flawlessly and captured the entire AI commerce agent category — displacing every competitor in the landscape — the realistic ceiling is a single-digit-billion outcome in a fragmented, multi-player market where the largest players are platform companies giving the capability away for free. This is not a category with winner-take-all dynamics. It is a category with many funded players fighting for share — the exact market structure I avoid. The absence of any physical-world technology, regulatory moat, or government customer further confirms that this is a pure software layer vulnerable to replication by any well-funded team with access to the same foundation models.
