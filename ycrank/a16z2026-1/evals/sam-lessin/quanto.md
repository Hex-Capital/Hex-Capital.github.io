# Quanto -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Quanto has $800K in contracted ARR on roughly $500K of funding. That's a genuinely impressive ratio — almost nobody at pre-seed is generating more revenue than they've raised. Anderson Petergeorge didn't read about the CPA shortage in a McKinsey report and decide to build an AI startup; he's a CPA who ran a bookkeeping practice, scaled it to 40 clients in six months, hit the capacity wall himself, and then productized the solution. That's the kind of founder-market fit that deserves respect. But founder-market fit and investment-worthy mispricing are two completely different things, and for Quanto, the gap between those two is the whole story.

Let me run the mispricing test. AI for accounting is one of the hottest consensus categories in B2B SaaS right now. Basis just closed a $100M Series B at a $1.15B valuation. Digits has $95M raised and $29.9M in 2024 revenue. Docyt has $27.2M. Finally has $305M in debt and equity. When I look at this landscape, I see a category where every sophisticated investor is already bidding. The risk isn't in the price — the hype is. Quanto's mid-market positioning between Basis (top 25 firms) and Finally (direct to SMBs) is a reasonable segmentation play, and Botkeeper shutting down in 2026 opens a customer vacuum, but "we found a segment the big players haven't saturated yet" is a timing advantage, not a mispricing. Timing advantages evaporate when Basis decides to move downmarket with $138M in the bank.

The AI kill test is where Quanto falls apart for me. The entire product — AI outreach, QuickBooks diagnostics scoring, automated reconciliation, bank rule configuration — is built on top of Intuit's platform. Quanto's domain is quantobooks.com. The "books" is QuickBooks. You know who's really good at building AI features for QuickBooks? Intuit. They have the data, the distribution, the API control, and they're actively investing in AI. Every time I see a startup whose core value proposition layers onto an incumbent's platform, I think about what happens when the incumbent wakes up. Intuit adding an "AI onboarding assistant" to QuickBooks Pro is not a five-year hypothetical — it's a product roadmap item. Quanto has no physical assets, no protocol-level lock-in, and no moat that survives Intuit deciding to compete. The switching costs from CRM integrations and bank rule configurations are real but shallow — they'd slow a migration by weeks, not years.

The primitive test fails cleanly. Quanto isn't creating a new behavior or financial instrument. It's automating three existing workflows — prospecting, onboarding, bookkeeping cleanup — and bundling them together for accounting firms. That's execution, not category creation. Nobody will describe a new category by saying "the Quanto of X." Compare this to Venmo, which literally created the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments, or Patreon, which created direct creator monetization as a category primitive. Quanto is "better, faster accounting firm operations." Useful, sure. But the upside is capped by the existing market's willingness to adopt, not by a new market being born.

Anderson and Kajanth are competent operators — Harvard/Oxford MBA CPA paired with an ex-Head of Product at SkipTheDishes who ran a 60-person org and later built a 7-figure product studio focused on AI for accounting. That's a solid team. But the sane-insane ratio is inverted: these are sane people pitching a sane idea. Nothing about "AI agents for accounting firm workflows" makes you think "that's fucking crazy." It makes you think "that's sensible and probably works" — which is exactly what every other VC also thinks, which is why the category has attracted hundreds of millions in capital. The co-founder relationship also triggers a concern: no shared prior employer or university overlap between Anderson and Kajanth, with the connection likely through Toronto's startup ecosystem or Kajanth's accounting-focused studio work. Synthetic co-founder relationships don't kill companies, but they don't give me the signal I get from watching founders across multiple ventures together.

The bull case deserves honest articulation. The CPA retirement crisis is structural and worsening — 75% reaching retirement age is not a cyclical problem. Quanto's $800K ARR proves firms are paying for this. The $5M pipeline suggests real demand, and the capital efficiency (6-person team, revenue exceeding funding) is legitimately strong. If Quanto can penetrate mid-market firms fast enough to build a data moat on book health patterns and pricing benchmarks before Basis moves downmarket, and if Intuit's incentive is to partner rather than compete (because Quanto makes QuickBooks stickier for firms), then this could be a $50-100M ARR vertical SaaS business. That's a real outcome. It's just not my kind of bet — it requires outrunning incumbents with distribution advantages in a consensus-hot category where the underlying technology is commoditizing fast.

Quanto is a well-run company in exactly the kind of category I avoid. Consensus mispricing is absent — this is a hot space with massive capital already deployed. Structural durability fails the AI kill test — QuickBooks platform dependency and pure-software architecture leave no moat against Intuit or well-funded competitors. The one bright spot is capital discipline: $800K ARR on $500K raised is genuinely excellent, and the Gusto comparison is instructive — both target financial infrastructure for small businesses, but Gusto built its own platform while Quanto depends on someone else's. That architectural choice is the difference between building a railroad and building a ticket booth on someone else's tracks.
