# Cedar -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Greg Gunn and Beier Cai have been circling the same problem for over a decade. Gunn was the first business executive at Hootsuite, helping scale it from 5 to 1,500 employees and $0 to $200M+ ARR. Cai wrote the first line of Hootsuite's code and rose to Senior Director of Engineering. They watched referral hiring break in real time as their company grew — Stripe reportedly spent $10M per year and 100 people just to keep referral pipelines running at scale. Then they co-founded Commit.dev in 2019, a professional network for startup engineers, which gave them five more years inside the talent-matching problem. Cedar is their third company together. When I ask "tell me their story," the answer is clear: these two have been living inside the problem of who-knows-whom-and-trusts-them since before most founders in this space knew the problem existed. That decade-long thread across three companies is genuine. But I want to be honest about what it is and what it isn't — this is professional evolution toward a problem that frustrated them operationally. It's not the kind of biographical inevitability I feel in my bones, where the founder's life story left them no choice. Gunn and Cai are experienced operators who spotted the gap through their careers, not people who were broken and rebuilt by an opaque system that exploited them.

The invisible system beneath Cedar is more compelling than the founders' biographical fit suggests. LinkedIn owns the dominant professional graph, but it monetizes connections — not trust. The actual referral network, who you'd personally vouch for with your reputation on the line, is locked inside people's heads, invisible even to the people who hold it. LinkedIn charges recruiters $10,000+ per seat for access to a graph that doesn't even contain the most valuable signal. Cedar's 15-minute AI-agent calls extract that private trust data and build a relationship graph that LinkedIn structurally cannot build — because doing so would cannibalize LinkedIn Recruiter's revenue model and antagonize enterprise customers by surfacing their employees' private networks. This is an innovator's dilemma play, and it reminds me of what Affirm did to credit card opacity: the incumbent profits from keeping the real information hidden, so it falls to an outsider to make the invisible visible. The parallel isn't exact — LinkedIn's opacity hurts employers and candidates, not vulnerable populations — but the structural dynamic is real.

Cedar's defensibility thesis hinges on a compounding data asset: every employee conversation adds private relationship data to a proprietary graph that competitors without the same access cannot replicate. The LLM inflection point is genuine — conducting structured relationship-mapping interviews through AI agents wasn't technically feasible before 2023-2024, so Cedar is building on an infrastructure shift that creates real timing advantage. But the core technology, conversational extraction via LLMs, is not proprietary. Perfect ($23M seed), Teamable ($10M total), ERIN, and any well-funded incumbent could replicate the call format. The moat is the accumulated data, not the method, and that data moat requires significant customer density before it compounds meaningfully. With four employees and an unverified customer base, Cedar hasn't proven the graph's network effects yet. The $2.5M–$3M contracted revenue claim — with an unexplained discrepancy between those two figures — is extraordinary for a pre-seed team of four, but both numbers are self-reported through an accelerator listing with no independent validation, no customer logos, and no case studies. If real, that revenue signals genuine enterprise demand. If overstated, it triggers my IRL pattern-match where storytelling outpaces building.

The strongest bull case: Cedar is an infrastructure play hiding behind a recruiting tool. If the trust graph achieves density across enough enterprise customers, it becomes the definitive map of professional trust relationships — something fundamentally more valuable than LinkedIn's public connection graph. The advisory board (Laszlo Bock from Google People Ops, Tiffany Fenster from Stripe recruiting, Brendan Browne from LinkedIn/Replit talent acquisition) provides direct access to exactly the enterprise buyers who would pay for this. The Commit.dev-to-Cedar thread shows founders who have been iterating toward the right product for five years. And the Moonhub acquisition by Salesforce in June 2025 validates enterprise appetite for AI-native recruiting at acquisition scale. For this bull case to materialize, Cedar needs the graph to compound fast enough to create a data moat before Perfect or a LinkedIn defensive move closes the window.

What keeps me from conviction: AI recruiting is consensus. There is nothing uncomfortable about this deal in a room of sophisticated VCs — no stigma, no regulatory frontier, no political risk, no "this sounds insane" reaction. Perfect just raised $23M. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce. The space is crowded with eager capital. Cedar's approach is differentiated, but differentiated is not contrarian — a better referral tool in a well-funded market is incremental, not paradigm-breaking. The data sensitivity risk is real — enterprise legal teams may resist having their employees' private networks extracted and mapped by a third-party AI agent, especially under GDPR — but that's a go-to-market friction, not the kind of regulatory frontier that signals a hard space. Cedar is a competent team with genuine domain depth executing in a market where many competent teams are executing. The co-founder dynamic is strong (decade-long partnership with clear CEO/CTO delineation — the opposite of my HQ Trivia lesson), and the Commit.dev transition deserves scrutiny rather than alarm. But the absence of contrarian conviction and the lack of a biographical forge beneath the professional expertise cap my enthusiasm. Cedar could be a strong company. It's not a Long Journey bet.
