# Ditto Biosciences -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the specific knowledge depth, and it's unusually strong. Adair Borges spent her PhD at UCSF studying how bacteriophages interact with CRISPR -- host-pathogen immune evasion at the molecular level -- then did a Miller Fellowship at Berkeley studying how organisms evolve alternative genetic codes. Dennis Sun did a PhD in molecular and cellular biology with a computational genomics emphasis, studying cis-regulatory evolution in arthropods. Emily Weiss brings microbiology, genomics, and high-throughput platform experience from Illumina and DuPont. Three scientists who then spent three years together at Arcadia Science -- a startup built around studying non-model organisms. That's the apprenticeship. The insight that parasites have spent millions of years evolving proteins to suppress the human immune system, and that those proteins can be extracted and engineered into drugs -- that insight doesn't come from reading a textbook. It comes from years of working at the intersection of evolutionary biology, parasitology, and computational genomics. You can't train someone into this. You can't hire a consulting firm to produce it. These founders have accumulated knowledge that would take a competitor three to five years to replicate, even with unlimited funding.

The contrarian angle is real, and it's where the asymmetry lives. Coronado Biosciences tried helminth therapy for autoimmune disease -- feed patients live pig whipworms -- and failed Phase 2 trials for Crohn's. That failure created a consensus: parasite-based approaches don't work. Smart money moved on. But Ditto's insight is precise and non-obvious: the biology was right, the delivery was wrong. Parasites do produce proteins that modulate human immunity with extraordinary precision -- 1-2 nanomolar binding affinities demonstrated in early experiments. The error was using live organisms with all their variability and immune-triggering complexity instead of isolating the specific molecular agents. This is the kind of distinction that requires genuine domain knowledge to make. It's the difference between dismissing nuclear energy because a specific reactor design failed and understanding that the physics still works. Most biotech investors will pattern-match to Coronado's failure and pass. That's exactly the setup where you get paid for being right first.

The platform dimension is what separates this from a single-drug bet. Ditto has screened over a million proteins across primate-infecting viruses, ticks, and parasitic worms, and over 98% of the parasite protein space remains uncharacterized. They're not just developing one drug candidate -- they're building a computational discovery engine that converts evolutionary biology into a therapeutic pipeline. The proprietary protein database plus the tissue biobank mapping immune memory creates a compounding data asset. Each protein analyzed, each immunogenicity profile mapped, each binding experiment validated makes the platform harder to replicate. This rhymes with what I saw in Stack Overflow -- a knowledge graph that becomes more valuable with each contribution and creates a moat through accumulated data rather than through engineering alone. Composition-of-matter patents on novel parasite-derived proteins would be the strongest form of pharmaceutical IP. If the platform works, it generates multiple shots on goal across rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, psoriasis, lupus, MS, and other autoimmune conditions.

Where I hesitate is leverage architecture. Drug development is the canonical unleveraged business. The discovery phase has computational leverage -- one algorithm screening a million proteins versus a medicinal chemistry team testing hundreds. But clinical development requires individual trials, regulatory filings, and manufacturing for each candidate. It scales linearly with headcount and capital. This is structurally different from software, where one engineer's work serves millions of users. The company will need to raise hundreds of millions in follow-on capital to advance any candidate through IND filing and clinical trials, diluting early investors significantly. The immunogenicity challenge with foreign-origin proteins adds fundamental technical risk -- the human immune system may attack the drug before it reaches its target. These are physics-level constraints, not execution challenges.

The bull case, though, is that if parasite-derived proteins represent a genuinely new therapeutic modality, the first company to validate it owns the entire category. Nature already did billions of years of R&D. The founding team has precisely the right specific knowledge to extract value from that R&D. Computation has reached the point where screening millions of proteins against validated targets is feasible. And the competitive landscape has cleared -- no one else is systematically mining this space because the previous approach failed and scared everyone off. This is the Extropic pattern from my portfolio: a deep-science bet where the fundamental insight is correct about nature, the technology is newly sufficient to exploit it, and the consensus has been driven away by a misattributed failure. The bet size at pre-seed is small enough to be optionality rather than thesis concentration.

The three founders' prior collaboration -- three years at Arcadia, co-authored publications, shared intellectual development -- is one of the strongest team coherence signals I've seen at pre-seed. They didn't meet at a co-founder matching event. They evolved together in the adjacent scientific space and then made a deliberate decision to start a company around a specific insight. That pattern of long-term collaboration before founding correlates with durability under pressure. Drug development is a decade-plus game; you need founders who chose each other for reasons deeper than opportunism.
