# Mantis -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumped out at me about Mantis isn't the product or the market — it's the Louiza Labs question. Georgia Witchel co-founded and served as CTO of Louiza Labs, which went through YC S25 and raised over $5 million building physics-based simulation of human biology for medical device testing. Now, one batch later, she's back in YC W26 as a solo founder building what sounds like a closely adjacent platform — physics simulation combined with LLMs for clinical and biomedical data. I've been burned enough times by unresolved founder dynamics to know this is exactly the kind of thing I need to understand before writing a check. After HQ Trivia, I always ask: what happened between the people? Was this a clean separation where she identified a bigger vision the original team didn't share? Is there IP overlap? Does Louiza Labs consider Mantis a competitor? None of this is documented publicly, and that ambiguity sits in a space where my pattern recognition fires warning signals. Solo founding can be a strength — no 50/50 split to implode — but when the solo founding emerges from a recent co-founding relationship in the same domain, I want to hear that story told directly and openly before I can evaluate anything else.

Georgia's biographical grit is real and I want to honor that. Eight world records in ice climbing across four World Cup circuits, competing at Lillehammer in 2016 — that's not a hobby, that's someone who has pushed her body and mind to extremes most people can't comprehend. I understand what it takes to be world-class at something physically brutal, and that kind of endurance does translate to the long slog of building a company. Her technical foundation is also genuinely impressive: Harvey Mudd computer science, computational math at Johns Hopkins, biomedical engineering at UW. That's not a surface-level credential stack. But when I ask myself the question I always ask — "tell me how you got here, to this specific problem" — the thread connecting Georgia's personal story to clinical trial data quality specifically feels more lateral than direct. She has deep expertise in physics-based biomedical simulation from Louiza Labs. The pivot to clinical trial data infrastructure makes technical sense. But I'm listening for the biographical urgency that tells me this founder will persist through the seven years of enterprise pharma sales cycles and FDA friction that this company will inevitably face, and I don't hear that specific note yet.

The positioning itself concerns me. "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data" is a framing I've heard dozens of times with different domain modifiers attached. It's legible, it's investable-sounding, and it's exactly the kind of consensus pattern I tend to avoid. The most promising companies I've backed looked ridiculous to other investors — not like well-structured analogies to successful companies. Unlearn.AI has already raised $130 million and has EMA-qualified methods. Simulants sits on top of Medidata's repository of 30,000 clinical trials and 9 million patients. This is not an unpopular space where a scrappy outsider can establish herself before consensus forms. It's a space with well-resourced incumbents who have the exact data moats that Mantis would need to build from scratch. The physics-simulation-plus-LLM technical combination is somewhat differentiated, but "somewhat differentiated" doesn't give me the feeling I'm looking for.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Georgia is one of very few people in the world with genuine depth at the intersection of physics simulation, machine learning, and biomedical engineering. If the "physics-enhanced" approach produces meaningfully better data quality than purely statistical methods — and if pharma companies can feel that difference in their trial timelines — then the technical moat could be real. Clinical trial data is genuinely fragmented and opaque, and there's a version of this company that makes an invisible system visible in exactly the way I've been drawn to with Carta and Affirm. If she can land two or three pharma design partners and demonstrate that her canonical datasets reduce the 80% delay rate, the enterprise switching costs could compound quickly. The $15 million per-trial cost of data quality issues means even a modest improvement justifies significant spend. And a sole founder with world-record athletic discipline, deep technical chops, and prior YC experience is not someone to dismiss lightly.

But I keep coming back to what I don't feel. There's no jolt here for me — no sense that this company is going to remake how the world works in a way that nobody else sees coming. It's a smart technical play in a recognized market by a capable founder, and in my experience, that profile produces solid companies but not the kind of outsized, world-reshaping outcomes I'm oriented toward. The competitive landscape is too populated, the positioning too legible, and the biographical connection to the specific mission too indirect for me to develop the conviction I'd need to write a personal check.
