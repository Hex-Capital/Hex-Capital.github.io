# Imperfect -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Matin Tamizi built garth — an open-source Garmin Connect Python client with 810 GitHub stars — and then Garmin broke the authentication flow that made it work, forcing him to deprecate his own library in March 2026. That single fact tells me almost everything I need to know about Imperfect's structural position. The founder's deepest technical contribution to this domain was killed by a platform decision he had no control over, and now he's building a company whose entire product depends on those same third-party wearable APIs continuing to cooperate. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin wasn't building on top of credit card companies' goodwill — he was building around them. When I backed Carta, the cap table data lived inside Carta, not inside someone else's system. Imperfect sits on the wrong side of that line. Garmin, Apple, and Google all have first-party biometric data and could ship native AI coaching without asking anyone's permission. Imperfect would need to ask all of them for permission, continuously.

Matin's professional biography is genuinely strong — Balanced processed $500M+ in card volume before transitioning customers to Stripe, he spent nearly a decade at Stripe itself across six functions, and Cuenca secured CNBV regulatory approval in Mexico and achieved what Kaszek lists as an "exit." That's a founder who has survived hard things and shipped in regulated, complex environments. But when I ask my first question — tell me his story, not the business — I hear a fintech infrastructure builder who created an open-source fitness tool on the side and decided to build a company around it. The garth library is evidence of genuine personal engagement with endurance sports and wearable data; you don't maintain an 810-star Garmin library as a market exercise. But the biographical thread connecting Matin to the *coaching* problem — why athletes are failed by static training plans, why this matters to him at the level of identity — is absent from everything I can find. Imperfect reads like a talented builder's passion project graduating to a company, not like a problem that found its founder and wouldn't let go.

TrainerRoad's 250M+ training activities and ~$15.1M in revenue on just $350K raised is the competitive fact that should keep Imperfect's team up at night. Athletica, HumanGO at $40/month, Spoked, and Cycling Coach AI already exist in the space. Imperfect is entering the AI fitness coaching market in 2026 — this is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs. It's the space that makes them nod enthusiastically. "AI Assistant" and "Health & Wellness" are the tags; these are consensus categories that every accelerator batch includes. When I wrote my check for Anduril, defense AI was politically toxic. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Imperfect's pitch — AI that connects your wearables to coach you for race day — would get approving nods at any dinner party in San Francisco. That's a problem for my framework. The absence of contrarianism means the company needs to win on pure execution against incumbents with data advantages, first-party platform access, and years of head start. The dossier explicitly flags this: "LLM-based coaching interfaces are increasingly commoditized. Any team with wearable API access and an LLM can build a conversational training assistant."

The bull case an advocate would make: Matin is a rare breed of repeat founder — second-time YC, Garry Tan as partner, two prior companies with institutional backing from a16z and Stripe, proven ability to navigate regulatory complexity (Cuenca's CNBV approval). The multi-sport gap is real; TrainerRoad is cycling-only, TrainingPeaks depends on human coaches whose business model conflicts with autonomous AI, and no competitor covers cycling, trail running, swimming, CrossFit, and climbing in one adaptive system. The convergence of ubiquitous wearables and capable LLMs creates a genuine "why now" — this product was not technically feasible before 2023. And the data flywheel could compound: every athlete training with Imperfect makes the model smarter, creating switching costs that deepen over time. This is a credible case. But it describes a well-executed startup in a crowded market, not a structural disruption of a hidden system. My biggest wins — Uber against the medallion cartel, Affirm against credit card opacity, Flock Safety in surveillance that VCs wouldn't touch — all had a structural thesis beneath the product. Imperfect's structural thesis is "coaching should be cheaper and more responsive," which is true but incremental. There's no invisible tax being eliminated, no opaque system being revealed, no information asymmetry being unwound. Expensive coaching is not the same as extractive coaching.

Imperfect's real-world impact potential is genuine but bounded. Making coaching accessible to athletes who can't afford $150–400/month democratizes training guidance, and the multi-sport breadth could serve communities beyond elite cyclists. But the addressable population — endurance athletes who own wearables and train for races — skews affluent, educated, and self-selected. Imperfect isn't expanding who can participate in fitness the way Postmates expanded who could get delivery or Wonderschool expanded who could find childcare. It's making an existing activity marginally more accessible within a population that already has significant resources. The cascading second-order effects are limited compared to companies that create new economic participants rather than better-served existing ones.

The one condition that would flip me: if Matin showed me that Imperfect owns its own physiological model — not an LLM wrapper but a proprietary adaptive engine trained on data that no competitor can replicate, with embedded switching costs that deepen with each training cycle — and if the founding story included a biographical crucible I can't see in the public record, a specific experience with coaching failure or athletic injury or systematic exclusion that made this problem personal and inevitable, I would revisit. The professional forge is there. The personal forge, the structural disruption, and the contrarian positioning are not. Imperfect is a competent team in a consensus market building on borrowed infrastructure, and the dimension that locks this verdict is Contrarian Conviction at 7/25 — when the space makes everyone comfortable, the returns accrue to the incumbent with the data moat, not the newcomer with the better interface.
