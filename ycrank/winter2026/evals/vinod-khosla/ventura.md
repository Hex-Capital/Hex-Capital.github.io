# Ventura -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Ventura: Swen Koller already built this company. He co-founded bravo, a B2B SaaS for food wholesalers that digitized the same phone-email-fax ordering workflow, grew it to CHF 20M in transaction volume, and sold it. Now he's doing it again in industrial distribution, this time with LLMs instead of traditional software. That's impressive execution pedigree -- and it's exactly the pattern that makes me nervous. A founder who knows what works in wholesale distribution digitization because he's done it before is not an outsider attempting what experts consider impossible. He's an insider running a proven playbook with better tools. The probability of failure is genuinely lower than average. But I invest for the consequence of success, and when you reduce failure probability by staying within what you know works, the consequence of success shrinks accordingly.

If Ventura succeeds completely -- automates every quote, every order, every back-office task for industrial distributors globally -- what changes? Sales reps save two to three hours of data entry per day. Distributors capture 75 to 100 basis points of EBIT improvement. That's real value in an enormous market. But the industrial distribution system itself is unchanged. Distributors still sit between manufacturers and buyers. SAP and Oracle still run the back office. The supply chain structure remains intact. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. Compare this to what gets me to write a check: Impossible Foods succeeding means we stop using half the planet's arable land for livestock. Commonwealth Fusion succeeding means fossil fuels become obsolete. Ventura succeeding means orders get processed faster. That's a business. It is not a transformation. I keep telling founders: you're too talented to spend your career making a workflow 30% more efficient. The tragedy here is that Collins and Koller clearly have the capability to attempt something much more ambitious -- Collins built a 2.4K-star open source LLM framework and was founding engineer at a company that scaled to $7M ARR in 14 months. Koller has a Harvard MBA/MS Engineering and a successful exit. These are serious people working on an insufficiently serious problem.

The technology itself confirms my concern. The dossier states it plainly: "The core technology -- LLM-based document parsing, product matching, and ERP integration -- uses generally available AI capabilities." There is no proprietary breakthrough here. No one will look at Ventura's technology and say "that's impossible." Four funded competitors are building adjacent solutions -- Distro in the same YC network, Endeavor AI with $7M from Craft Ventures, Go Autonomous with enterprise customers like Grundfos and Saint-Gobain, Parspec with $31.5M and 288 customers. When incumbents and competitors can all see the same opportunity and access the same underlying technology, you're in a race for execution, not a bet on invention. I don't invest in execution races. I invest in bets where if the technology works, the incumbent system becomes economically unviable.

The strongest bull case would require Ventura to transcend its current positioning. If processing every quote and order in industrial distribution gives you the data layer that eventually disintermediates the distributors themselves -- becoming the intelligence that connects manufacturers directly to buyers, making the $8 trillion intermediary structure obsolete -- that would be transformational. The data moat from customer-specific pricing history, product matching patterns, and catalog knowledge could theoretically compound into something structural. And the timing is genuinely favorable: LLMs crossed the accuracy threshold for parsing unstructured commercial documents only recently, ERP APIs have opened up, and the distribution industry faces acute labor shortages. Koller's intimate knowledge of wholesale customer acquisition from bravo means the go-to-market motion is de-risked in a way most pre-seed companies can't claim. If they executed flawlessly and expanded from quote automation into becoming the operating system for distribution -- pricing intelligence, demand forecasting, inventory optimization, eventually autonomous procurement -- the outcome could be a multi-billion dollar vertical platform. But nothing in the current positioning suggests that ambition. They're building "AI employees" that help distributors do what they already do, faster. They're entrenching the existing structure rather than disrupting it.

The timing trajectory is real but consensus. Every VC in 2026 is funding "AI agents for [industry] workflows." This is not 2019 when I wrote the check to OpenAI and people thought AI was laughable. This is not 2004 when we pioneered cleantech and everyone said we were crazy. AI for B2B order processing is the most obvious application of current LLM capabilities -- it's where every analyst report points, where every accelerator batch has three companies, where the technology curve has already been identified and the talent is already flowing. Being right about timing doesn't matter when everyone else is also right about timing. The competitive moat comes down to who builds the deepest ERP integrations and accumulates the most distributor-specific data fastest. That's a grinding execution game, and a two-person team maintaining nine ERP integrations is going to be stretched thin against competitors with $7M to $31M in funding.
