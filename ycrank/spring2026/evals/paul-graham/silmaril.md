# Silmaril -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Silmaril is what happens when two animals with unverified chemistry walk into a crowded room and start breaking things — Aum Upadhyay and Eduardo Velasco hacked OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Microsoft 15 times in two weeks as whitehats, which is the dossier line I keep re-reading, and then they shipped a Microsoft Copilot patch credited with protecting "millions of users." The animal test from "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) doesn't usually get answered this loudly at the pre-seed stage. What I cannot tell from the dossier is whether these two people have ever lived in the same apartment.

Brian Armstrong's rejected-then-rebuilt Android wallet for **Coinbase** (2012) is the right comparison for Silmaril on animal resourcefulness — both stories share the same mechanism, which is that the founders went and *did the thing* before anyone gave them permission or a check. Armstrong shipped a working bitcoin wallet to overturn a written YC rejection; Silmaril's pair attacked four frontier labs 15 times in a fortnight and then mailed the patches in. The divergence from Armstrong's HIGH rung is that Armstrong's stunt was solo, deliberate, and aimed at one specific institutional door, whereas Silmaril's stunt is a portfolio of 15 exploits which reads more like sport than like a single load-bearing act of will — closer to a Capture-the-Flag run than to the cereal boxes. Still: $68M and $20M of damages prevented in two named case studies, plus a shipped Copilot patch, is more animal evidence than I usually see from a P26 dossier.

The pattern that kills companies on the co-founder axis is **Kiko** (2005) — two people who worked together, shipped, failed, and at least had the prior reps to come back as **Twitch** (2007); Silmaril has the inverse problem, which is that the dossier shows zero shared history between Aum (Columbia, ex-AWS) and Eduardo (UT Arlington, ex-Amazon) prior to "became whitehat hackers" together after Velasco left Amazon. Different schools, different employers in different functions, and a partnership formed in what looks like the months adjacent to applying to YC. That is the configuration that scares me most in this dossier, because the in-person interview is where this gets resolved and I am reading text. The 15-exploits-in-two-weeks run is *some* evidence the pair can grind together, but two weeks is not two years.

On organic-idea fit, the mechanism question is whether Silmaril's founders were *customers* of prompt-injection defense before they were vendors of it — and the honest answer in the dossier is no, they were attackers, not defenders running an AI app that kept getting compromised. That is adjacent to organic but not the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick pattern. Over 18 months this compounds badly or well depending on which way you read it: attackers-turned-defenders often build the best defense because they know what the attack actually feels like (HiddenLayer's founders came from Cylance), but they also tend to over-index on exotic attacks the median customer doesn't care about while under-investing in the boring integration work that wins enterprise deals.

The schlep test on Silmaril is interesting because the schlep is real but it is *attended* — Lakera ($30M, Check Point), HiddenLayer ($56M), Promptfoo ($23.6M, reportedly OpenAI), and Protect AI Guardian (Palo Alto) are all running at this same compliance-and-latency grind. For Silmaril's schlep score to flip a tier from neutral-to-strong, the autonomous threat-hunting + sub-one-hour retraining loop would need to be a genuinely unpleasant operational commitment that the four better-funded competitors are *unconsciously routing around*, not just one feature on a roadmap. SOC + ISO 27001 + CSA at this stage is real schlep tax paid; the 20ms P90 vs. Lakera's sub-50ms is a real latency claim. But this is a schlep zone with a queue, not **Coinbase** (2012)'s empty regulatory hallway.

On hacker genealogy, the single anchor is **Reddit** (2005) — Aaron Swartz had co-authored the RSS spec before the company existed, which is the kind of public artifact that told me the team had taste before I ever met them. Silmaril's dossier explicitly says "GitHub: No public repos found" for both founders, which is the absence-leaves-tracks problem from my own playbook. The whitehat exploits against four frontier labs are *evidence* of hacker culture and they partially compensate; Eduardo chaining a prompt injection to root inside ChatGPT is a real hacker move. But the trail is private, not public, and I over-index on hacker tribal markers in the open.

My 36-month forecast for Silmaril is acquisition by a security incumbent — Palo Alto Networks, Check Point, Cloudflare, or CrowdStrike — at roughly $40–150M as a feature tuck-in to a broader AI-security suite, on the same consolidation curve that already swallowed Lakera and Protect AI Guardian in 2025. The mechanism is straightforward: the category is being absorbed faster than independent runtime-defense companies can compound moat, Silmaril launched on April 14, 2026 into a market where four better-capitalized players already have enterprise distribution, and the founders' attack-research brand is exactly the asset a PANW or Check Point pays a premium to bolt onto an existing AI firewall SKU. That is the **Kiko** (2005) shape of outcome, not the **Twitch** (2007) shape.

The opposing case that stings: the self-retraining-within-one-hour loop is not a feature, it is infrastructure, and if Silmaril's ModernBERT classifier genuinely holds 20ms P90 at 95.60% accuracy across 131 attack techniques while the competitor static-rule classifiers decay every time a new jailbreak hits Twitter, then Silmaril becomes the default runtime layer the way **Stripe** (2010) became default payments — because defenders need a vendor whose model is *moving* at the speed attackers are moving. That mechanism is real and I am giving it weight. What dismantles it in the dossier is that the 20ms / 95.60% / "2x more threats / 10x lower latency" numbers are all self-published with no third-party benchmark cited, and the customer logos (Microsoft, OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Perplexity, Dropbox) are described in the LinkedIn narrative as targets of whitehat findings rather than as paying customers. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict from lean-invest to high-conviction invest: a TrueFoundry-style independent benchmark within 6 months confirming Silmaril's latency and accuracy claims against Lakera and a paid enterprise contract — not a logo, a contract — with one of the six named labs.
