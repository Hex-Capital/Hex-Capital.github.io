# Mercury -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Mercury sits on top of agents it doesn't control. The homepage shows Claude Code, Devin, and gstack operating under Mercury's orchestration layer -- which means Mercury's entire value proposition depends on Anthropic, Cognition, and other agent providers not building native multi-agent coordination themselves. OpenAI already ships an Agents SDK with multi-agent orchestration. This is my "building an application on a contested infrastructure layer" red flag in its clearest form. When I backed Stripe, Stripe didn't orchestrate between PayPal and Braintree -- it owned the payments infrastructure outright, which is why switching costs compounded over time. Mercury's orchestration layer, by contrast, is a coordination skin over third-party agents whose APIs, capabilities, and business models can change without Mercury's consent. If Anthropic decides Claude Code should natively coordinate with other Claude instances -- and they have every commercial incentive to do so -- Mercury's orchestration value collapses overnight.

The market structure timing test fails in a way I find instructive. The underlying structural shift is real: the emergence of multiple capable AI agents in 2024-2025 created a heterogeneous ecosystem requiring coordination, a problem that genuinely didn't exist when ChatGPT was the only game in town. But this shift is consensus-obvious, not nonobvious. CrewAI has raised $24.5M and reached $3.2M in revenue. Relevance AI closed a $24M Series B. Lindy has approximately $50M in funding and $5.1M in revenue. Every AI-focused investor I talk to has "agent orchestration" on their market map. When a pre-seed company enters a market where competitors have $24-50M head starts and established user bases, and where adding multi-agent features is an incremental extension for those competitors rather than a ground-up rebuild, I need to see a dramatically differentiated structural advantage. Mercury's differentiation -- no-code interface for non-technical users -- is a UX choice, not a structural moat. Lindy or Relevance AI can ship a multi-agent orchestration feature in a quarter; Mercury has to build the full product and acquire users from scratch.

The single-miracle test compounds the concern. Mercury needs non-technical business users to adopt multi-agent AI workflows as a primary work pattern -- that's not capturing existing behavior in a new medium, it's creating entirely new behavior. Most business users today don't manage teams of AI agents. Mercury's manifesto describes replacing "20+ browser tabs and 9+ disconnected tools" with a coordinated agent team, but the people struggling with 20 tabs are mostly struggling with the tabs, not yearning for an AI agent coordinator. Then Mercury needs agent providers not to build competing orchestration, AND needs to outpace well-funded competitors converging on the same feature set. Three independent breakthroughs. I've written before that compounding small odds means you're very, very likely to fail, and Mercury's path requires at least two miracles that are outside the founders' direct control.

The strongest bull case rests on the Palantir FDE background and a Kubernetes analogy. Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma spent years as Forward Deployed Engineers at Palantir -- a role that is specifically about bridging complex technical systems and non-technical end users, which maps directly to Mercury's no-code positioning. FDEs deploy in small autonomous teams, make fast decisions, and ship in production environments with demanding customers. That operational muscle is real and relevant. The Kubernetes analogy -- that Mercury becomes the orchestration standard for a fragmenting agent ecosystem the way Kubernetes standardized container orchestration -- has structural appeal. If the agent ecosystem fragments into dozens of specialized providers with no single winner, a neutral orchestration layer captures genuine value. But Kubernetes emerged after container primitives (Docker, rkt) had stabilized and after enterprise adoption of containers was already underway. Agent primitives in April 2026 are wildly unstable -- capabilities, APIs, pricing models, and even the fundamental abstraction of what constitutes an "agent" are all in flux. Building the orchestration standard before the underlying layer has settled is like building Kubernetes in 2010 before Docker existed. The timing might be right in 18 months, but right now Mercury is premature for a durable infrastructure position.

The third co-founder opacity is a secondary concern but worth flagging: the manifesto names "Jayson" as a co-founder with no last name, role, background, or social presence available. On a five-person team where co-founders constitute the core leadership, a fully opaque co-founder is a diligence gap I'd want closed before writing a check. The name collision with Mercury Financial (mercury.com, $3.5B+ fintech serving 200,000+ startups) creates a persistent SEO and brand handicap in exactly the startup ecosystem Mercury needs to penetrate.

My verdict is Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. Multi-agent orchestration is a consensus market with $24-50M-funded competitors already generating revenue -- there's no nonobvious window here. Mercury's orchestration layer sits atop third-party agents whose providers have both the incentive and capability to build native coordination, undermining the infrastructure positioning that would otherwise create durable switching costs. The Palantir FDE background is the best signal in the dossier, but strong operators in an obvious, crowded market with platform risk from above don't clear my bar.
