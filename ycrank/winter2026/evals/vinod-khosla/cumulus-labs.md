# Cumulus Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know before I evaluate anything else. Modal has raised $111M at a $1.1B valuation and is reportedly in talks at $2.5B. Baseten has $585M in funding at a $5B valuation with 100x inference volume growth. Together AI has $533M raised and $300M in annualized revenue. RunPod has $120M+ ARR with 500,000 developers. Into this walks a two-person pre-seed team claiming 4x faster cold starts. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing in telecom was impossible -- the experts dismissed it. When I backed Impossible Foods, the food industry dismissed plant-based meat. Here, the incumbents aren't dismissing serverless GPU inference -- they've collectively deployed over $1.3 billion validating the exact same thesis. When established players endorse your approach with that kind of capital, it usually means the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening. Nobody is laughing at Cumulus. That's a problem.

The consequence-of-success question kills this for me. If Cumulus succeeds spectacularly -- captures a meaningful share of serverless GPU inference -- the result is a successful cloud infrastructure company. AI teams pay 50-70% less for GPU compute. That is a real business. It is not a real transformation. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No industry gets structurally replaced. The world has slightly cheaper inference. Compare that to what I look for: Impossible Foods making animal agriculture obsolete, Commonwealth Fusion eliminating fossil fuel dependency, OpenAI creating artificial general intelligence. "Performant serverless GPU inference" is an optimization of an existing system, not its replacement. The TAM numbers are large ($50B by 2032 for GPU-as-a-Service), but capturing a slice of a large market through incremental performance advantages is precisely the kind of bet where you've narrowed your ambition to ensure survival while making the upside inconsequential. I don't invest in companies whose best outcome is being a slightly better cloud vendor.

The founding team compounds my concern rather than alleviating it. Suryaa was Lead Engineer at TensorDock, literally a GPU marketplace. He's an industry insider building a better version of what he was already building. That is the pattern I explicitly avoid -- domain veterans who "know what's not possible" and therefore constrain their ambition to what the industry considers feasible. A 4x cold start improvement is exactly the kind of innovation an insider would pursue: measurable, benchmarkable, safe. Veer's background in Space Force SBIR contracts and NASA programs adds relevant ML workload experience, and the childhood friendship since third grade suggests genuine trust. But neither founder is approaching this problem from the outside. Neither is attempting something the consensus considers crazy. They're executing a well-understood playbook in a well-validated market.

The strongest bull case goes like this: GPU inference is to the 2020s what cloud computing was to the 2000s -- a generational infrastructure shift where multiple large companies will be built. The market is growing at 35.8% CAGR. The open-source LLM explosion (Llama, Mixtral, DeepSeek) is creating massive demand for self-hosted inference as an alternative to proprietary APIs. Cumulus's Ion inference engine could be a genuine differentiator if it sustains performance advantages, and the complementary founder backgrounds (supply-side infrastructure from TensorDock, demand-side workloads from aerospace) give them authentic two-sided market insight. If the inference engine maintains its edge and the team executes on developer adoption, this could be a Square-like outcome -- not replacing the system, but making it accessible to a vastly larger customer base. I'll acknowledge that potential. But Square invented a new form factor (phone as POS terminal) when zero competitors existed. Cumulus is claiming a performance delta (4x cold starts) in a market where five companies with $100M-$585M in funding are already competing. Performance deltas get replicated. Category creation doesn't.

The rate-of-change curve underlying this market is real and accelerating -- inference is now the dominant GPU spend category, and open-source models are proliferating. But Cumulus isn't ahead of the curve. They arrived after Modal, Baseten, RunPod, and Together AI already staked their claims. The messaging pivot noted in the dossier -- from "aggregating idle GPU capacity from distributed sources" to "serverless GPU cloud with proprietary inference engine" -- could signal healthy iteration, but it also suggests the founders are still searching for their structural wedge rather than having found it. I look for companies skating to where the puck will be. The puck is already here, and a dozen well-capitalized teams are already playing.
