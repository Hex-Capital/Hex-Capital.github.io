# Cardboard -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with what struck me most about Cardboard: two genuinely talented engineers have built something technically impressive -- a custom hardware-accelerated renderer on WebGPU/WebCodecs coupled with an agentic AI editing layer, both from scratch, in a few months -- and they've aimed it at making testimonial reels and ad variants for marketing teams. That's the disconnect. The technology is harder than the problem it serves. When I see founders capable of building two simultaneously challenging systems (a browser-native NLE and reliable agentic video reasoning), my immediate question is: why are you spending your careers making it 3x faster for a growth team at PostHog to cut a product launch video? If this succeeds spectacularly, the consequence is that B2B marketing departments produce more short-form content at lower cost. That is not a consequence worth the engineering talent deployed here.

The market data confirms the constraint. The AI video generator market is projected at $3.4 billion by 2033. The broader video editing market sits at $3.5 billion growing at under 6%. Even if Cardboard captures a dominant share of the AI-assisted editing segment, you're looking at a solid SaaS business, not an industry transformation. Nobody's life changes. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. Compare this to when we backed Square -- mobile payments didn't optimize existing POS infrastructure, they replaced it structurally, collapsing an entire industry's cost structure. Cardboard's "footage + goal → first cut" paradigm is clever, but it accelerates a workflow rather than eliminating one. Adobe's creative professionals still exist in this world; they just get a faster rough cut to refine.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If the agentic editing layer becomes reliable enough to handle not just marketing clips but full-length professional video production, Cardboard could be building the interface that makes professional editors unnecessary for 80% of video content -- the way we predicted machines would handle 80% of what doctors do. That would genuinely threaten Adobe's $20 billion creative cloud empire. The browser-native architecture would become the collaborative standard, analogous to Google Docs displacing Microsoft Word. And the timing convergence is real: multimodal LLMs crossing the threshold for video understanding and WebGPU/WebCodecs maturing for professional rendering happened in the same 12-18 month window. That's the kind of technological convergence that creates category-defining companies. But the founders aren't positioning for this transformational case -- they're positioning for "growth and marketing teams" making "3 ad variants" and "30s hooks." They've already narrowed the aperture.

The competitive landscape reinforces my concern. Descript has $101 million in funding and roughly $55 million in ARR. OpusClip has $50 million from SoftBank and 10 million users. Kapwing has a $10 million revenue base. Multiple Hacker News commenters -- the company's own launch audience -- flagged "lots of players in the market doing this," including other companies in the same YC batch. When the experts and the crowd both validate your approach, that's usually a sign the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening. My best investments were in companies that incumbents mocked or declared impossible. Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router; the food industry laughed at plant-based heme. Nobody is laughing at "AI video editing" -- they're all building it simultaneously.

The founders have complementary skills that map precisely to the two technical challenges: Ishan's five years building performance-critical browser applications at HackerRank maps directly to the NLE, and Saksham's AI agent work at Iterate AI maps to the agentic layer. The 13 releases shipped since November 2025 signal genuine execution velocity. But execution speed on a problem with insufficient consequence magnitude is like running fast in the wrong direction. The 1,000 early users and named customers (PostHog, Hyperspell) show market demand exists -- people want faster video editing. I don't dispute that. I dispute whether solving that demand produces an outcome large enough to justify the opportunity cost of two engineers this capable building something this bounded.

I'd tell Saksham and Ishan what I tell many YC founders: you're too smart to spend your careers on a problem this contained. The technology you've built -- agentic reasoning over multimodal content, real-time browser rendering -- has applications that could transform how humans interact with all visual media, not just marketing clips. But as currently scoped, this is a workflow optimization company competing against well-funded incumbents in a market where success means a $100-200 million ARR SaaS business. That outcome doesn't justify my involvement.
