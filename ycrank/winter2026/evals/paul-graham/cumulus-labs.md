# Cumulus Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cumulus Labs is that they're entering a market where the problem is the opposite of invisible. Serverless GPU inference has attracted over a billion dollars in aggregate funding. Modal raised $87M at a $1.1B valuation. Baseten raised $300M at $5B. Together AI has $533M and $300M in annualized revenue. RunPod already has $120M+ ARR and 500,000 developers. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight because the schlep scared everyone off. This is a problem standing in a spotlight with a line of well-funded companies racing to solve it. When I wrote that the most impressive YC companies "are not working on AI," this is the kind of category I was thinking about. Consensus hot categories are where the returns have already been priced in.

And yet the organic origin is real. Suryaa was Lead Engineer at TensorDock, where he literally built a distributed GPU marketplace and saw firsthand how inefficient GPU provisioning was. Veer ran mission-critical ML workloads for Space Force and NASA -- he felt the pain from the customer side. They didn't sit down with a whiteboard and brainstorm "what's hot right now." They stumbled into this from opposite sides of the same market, which is exactly how the best ideas get discovered. The fact that they met in third grade and have been building together since childhood is the strongest co-founder signal I see at pre-seed -- much stronger than "we met at a hackathon last year." This isn't manufactured. The problem is genuine.

The trouble is that organic discovery of a problem everyone else has also discovered doesn't give you the structural advantage it gives you in a schlep-blind market. When Stripe tackled payments, thousands of programmers knew it was broken but nobody wanted to deal with banks and regulators -- the schlep created a moat before the first line of code was written. When Cumulus tackles GPU inference, they're competing head-on with engineering teams that have hundreds of people and hundreds of millions in capital. Ion, their proprietary inference engine, is the key differentiator. They claim 12.5-second cold starts versus 60 seconds for Modal on an equivalent benchmark. If that holds, it's meaningful. But cold start optimization is not a permanent moat -- it's a temporary lead in a race where well-funded competitors are sprinting the same direction. Open-source engines like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM are improving fast with contributions from Berkeley and NVIDIA. Performance edges in inference are perishable.

The bull case is that focused technical teams can carve out significant niches even in heavily funded markets. Cloudflare carved out edge computing despite AWS. Vercel carved out frontend deployment despite everyone. If Ion genuinely delivers sustained performance advantages that compound with scale -- if the predictive packing and workload migration get better with more customer data -- then two smart founders who deeply understand both sides of the GPU market could build something that compounds in a way that bigger, more diffuse competitors cannot. Suryaa's TensorDock experience means he knows the operational details of running GPU infrastructure that a team of new hires at a better-funded competitor might take a year to learn. And the pay-per-second, scale-to-zero model has real economic logic -- hyperscalers profit from idle capacity, which creates a structural disincentive to optimize it away. That tension is real and could be exploitable.

But I see no evidence that anyone is actually using this product. No revenue, no user counts, no testimonials, no community, no organic adoption signals. The access is gated behind "Request Access" and "Book a Demo." In a market this hot with this many alternatives already available, the complete absence of traction is not just neutral -- it's informative. If Ion were genuinely 4x faster, developers would be talking about it. I also notice the messaging has shifted between the January launch post (aggregating idle GPU capacity from distributed sources) and the current website (serverless cloud with proprietary inference engine). At pre-seed that's normal exploration, but it suggests the positioning is still solidifying. Their GitHub presence is thin -- three repos, the most notable a fork with zero stars. For a team whose core differentiation is technical, I'd want to see more evidence of the hacker-craftsman mentality in the public work.

These are capable, domain-experienced founders with a genuine co-founder bond working on a real problem they discovered organically. In a less crowded market, I'd be more excited. But the competitive landscape means this company needs not one but a sustained sequence of technical wins to survive -- and at pre-seed with two people against teams of hundreds, the odds are long. I'd watch them, but I wouldn't write a check today.
