# Runtime -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Runtime: this is the most consensus pitch in my inbox right now. "AI governance for coding agents" — every investor at every dinner party in San Francisco is nodding along to this thesis in 2026. Nobody shifts in their chair. Nobody says "that's crazy." E2B has $32M. Daytona has $30.7M. Galileo has $68M. The space is crowded with well-capitalized players, and the technical barriers between "sandbox infrastructure" and "sandbox infrastructure plus governance dashboard" are thin enough that any of these companies could ship Runtime's core feature set in a quarter. When I think about where my biggest wins came from — SpaceX when rockets were blowing up, Anduril when defense tech was political poison, Flock Safety when surveillance cameras made civil liberties people nervous — I look for the ideas that make a room of VCs uncomfortable. Runtime makes them comfortable. That's not inherently disqualifying, but it means the bar for everything else goes up significantly.

When I ask about Gus Trigos's story — not the business, his story — I see a resourceful person. Self-taught programmer in high school, BlackRock quant analyst, built Mentum through multiple pivots and got it to an exit through Nuvocargo. That's a real forge — navigating a pivot from fintech API to procurement automation to acquisition shows the kind of adaptive survival instinct I recognize. He's a second-time YC founder, and he's shipped a working product fast. I respect all of that. But the question I keep returning to is: what happened to Gus that made coding agent governance find *him*? His background is quantitative finance and supply chain, not developer infrastructure or security. The leap to this problem reads like a smart operator spotting a timely market gap after his exit — career optimization, not biographical inevitability. When the Crusoe founders saw gas flaring in North Dakota, they couldn't unsee it. When I look for that kind of lived-in-the-problem signal with Gus and coding agent governance, it's not there.

The structural argument for Runtime is real and I want to engage with it honestly. The incentive misalignment is genuinely interesting: Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google profit from maximizing agent usage, not from imposing guardrails that reduce API calls. A neutral third-party governance layer has a structural opening that could persist. If Runtime became the default control plane for coding agents the way Datadog became default for infrastructure observability, the $99/seat/month pricing in a market growing at 45% CAGR could compound into something massive. The "why now" is legitimate — autonomous coding agents crossed a practical threshold in 2025, and governance hasn't caught up. If you believe the platform vendors will be slow to build native governance because of this incentive conflict, and if you believe the sandbox incumbents will stay focused on infrastructure primitives rather than moving up-stack to governance, then Runtime is sitting in exactly the right gap at exactly the right moment. That's the bull case, and it's credible.

But I keep running it through my filters and the picture doesn't sharpen. Is there a hidden, extractive system being made transparent here? Runtime adds visibility to coding agent sessions — that's operational governance, not structural disruption of an information asymmetry that victimizes people. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity, ordinary consumers were being exploited by terms they couldn't understand. When Ownwell attacked property tax systems, homeowners were overpaying because the appeals process was deliberately opaque. Runtime's opacity problem is real but it's a B2B operational gap, not a hidden tax being levied on people who don't know better. That's a meaningful distinction in how I evaluate conviction. And when I run the ten-year imagination exercise — where does this go? — I struggle to see the infrastructure compound. Datadog's moat was built on deep instrumentation, proprietary data pipelines, and correlation engines that took years to develop. Runtime's governance features — sandboxed VMs, network allowlists, access controls, deployment gates — are configuration layers, not deep infrastructure. The 64 GitHub stars and 7,000 PyPI downloads suggest the market hasn't yet validated the product as essential. The platform vendors will inevitably build org-level controls into their own agents. The window exists, but it may close before Runtime can build the switching costs that would make it durable.

Gus is clearly capable of building and shipping. He's proven he can survive a company's near-death and come out the other side with an exit. If he were bringing this operational discipline to a problem that made investors uncomfortable, in a space with a hidden structure to disrupt and infrastructure that compounds with each customer, I'd be leaning forward. But a competent operator in a consensus market with thin moats and well-funded competitors isn't a Long Journey bet. I wish him well — the timing sense is good and the execution looks solid — but this isn't the kind of thing where I write a check from my own money.
