# Regbase -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

"AI for compliance officers" is the exact pitch structure I see fifty times a year and pass on forty-nine. Regbase is building a regulatory intelligence platform that uses agentic AI to track laws across jurisdictions and languages -- replacing what the founders describe as a "100 hour project" done manually at firms like Paul, Weiss. I start every evaluation with the same question: if Regbase fully succeeds, does the world visibly change? The answer is no. Compliance officers at Fortune 500 companies save time. Legal research teams at big law firms spend fewer hours crawling obscure government websites. That is a useful product. It is not a consequential one. The $2.8 billion TAM projected to reach $7.6 billion by 2034 at 11.6% CAGR -- that is a spreadsheet pitch, and spreadsheets are fiction. A moderately growing enterprise software market where Regbase competes with Thomson Reuters (750+ regulatory bodies, 130+ countries), CUBE Global ($22 million raised, 1,000+ clients), and a half-dozen other funded regtech players is the definition of fighting for share of existing budgets rather than creating new demand.

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy both come from the ESG research practice at Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison -- a top-tier law firm. Aresta-Katz holds an MBA from Columbia and a business degree from NYU Stern. Godoy was a senior ESG coordinator there before moving to Morrow Sodali. These are domain experts with impressive credentials in the exact field they are building for. I have said repeatedly that when I choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I pick better thinking every time. Most large innovations do not come from people who are experts in the field involved. Regbase's founding story -- born from a research project at a big law firm that could not find a solution -- is a classic domain-expert origin. Nothing in the dossier shows me rapid pivoting, first-principles reasoning that surprised the founders themselves, or trajectory of capability growth during YC. Ethan Shea's background at Microsoft, Citadel, and Vercel as a staff engineer on agentic AI is technically strong and directly relevant, but technical competence is not the same as the outsider irreverence I look for. Compare this to the Mach Industries founder -- a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin precisely because he did not know what could not be done. Regbase's founders know exactly what the limitations are in regulatory research because they lived inside those limitations. That knowledge is valuable for building a product. It is a warning sign for building something that breaks the category.

Regbase's technical approach -- "agentic AI research tools with human verification to identify and interpret regulatory changes worldwide" -- is applying existing LLM capabilities to a document parsing and monitoring problem. The dossier itself flags the commoditization risk: "General-purpose LLMs can increasingly parse government websites and translate content." The operational complexity of crawling thousands of fragmented subnational sources, handling content behind QR codes, and managing multilingual regulatory filings is real, but it is not a technology discontinuity. There is no proprietary science, no novel architecture, no step-function improvement in a core capability. Regbase is building a data pipeline with an AI layer -- the same thing every enterprise AI company in 2026 is doing. The Rutherford engine at Rocket Lab used electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers -- that was a non-linear approach to rocket propulsion that fundamentally changed cost structures. Regbase's approach would make regulatory monitoring maybe 3-5x faster. That is incremental. It is fine for an incremental problem, but regulatory intelligence is not a monumental threat requiring discontinuous innovation -- it is a workflow that large firms already budget for.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: regulatory complexity is exploding globally -- EU CSRD, SEC climate rules, state-level AI legislation -- and the volume of new regulations is growing faster than any legal team can monitor manually. Regbase's subnational and multilingual focus targets a genuine gap that Thomson Reuters and CUBE underserve. The Paul, Weiss network gives Regbase distribution into exactly the firms that spend the most on compliance research. The $400 million in expiring school safety grants identified for a protective equipment client and the 80 greenhouse gas disclosure requirements mapped across 45 countries show real customer value delivery. The "trusted by Fortune 500 companies, global consultancies, and leading law firms" claim, if verifiable, would suggest product-market fit ahead of most pre-seed companies. One could invoke my Oscar Health exception -- where I accepted regulatory risk because the affordability thesis was strong enough. But Oscar Health was trying to make healthcare affordable for millions of American consumers. Regbase is making compliance monitoring easier for corporate legal departments. The consequence magnitude is categorically different. Oscar changed who could access healthcare; Regbase changes how efficiently law firms bill for regulatory research.

Regbase's ambition level is well-understood and achievable -- nobody would look at a regulatory intelligence platform and say "that cannot be done." That is precisely what makes it uninteresting to me. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Regbase is designed to generate a 3-5x return through steady enterprise SaaS growth in a defined market segment. That is a perfectly fine outcome for most investors. It is a waste of my time and attention. The path to radical affordability is absent -- Regbase will not make compliance free or 10x cheaper. It will make it somewhat more efficient at enterprise pricing that captures, rather than destroys, the value of the cost savings. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Regbase demonstrated that its platform could autonomously handle 90%+ of a compliance officer's job -- not assist them, but replace the function entirely -- reducing the cost of regulatory compliance from millions of dollars per year to thousands. That would be a consequence worth betting on. As currently described, Regbase is an AI copilot for a professional vertical, and I have been explicit about what I think of those.

The two dimensions most decisive for this Pass are consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. Regbase, if fully successful, produces a profitable SaaS company in a $2.8 billion market -- not a world that looks different. The technology is an application of existing LLM capabilities to a known workflow, not a proprietary breakthrough that creates a step-function change. Both dimensions score in the bottom quartile of my framework, and no Known Exception applies because no criterion -- not founder learning rate, not affordability endgame, not ambition level -- scores high enough to override the fundamental mismatch with my investment thesis.
