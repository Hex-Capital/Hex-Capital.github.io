# Terminal Use -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Terminal Use isn't the founders or the product -- it's the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about consensus. AI agent infrastructure is the hottest category in venture capital right now. E2B has raised $32 million. Daytona closed a $24 million Series A. Modal Labs is at $111 million and a billion-dollar-plus valuation. OpenAI itself is bundling hosted runtimes through Frontier. When I see a space where every investor in town already agrees the opportunity is enormous, that's the opposite of the signal I've trained myself to follow. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. "Vercel for background agents" is an instantly legible pitch -- any partner at any firm nods along without friction. That legibility is a warning to me, not a comfort. The weirdness has already been arbitraged out of this category before Terminal Use has even launched.

If I could sit down with Vivek, Stavros, and Filip, my first question would be the one I always ask: tell me your story. Not the business. How did you get here? And what I'd hear is three engineers from Palantir who saw the agent deployment problem up close and decided to build the infrastructure layer. Vivek led a large agent use case across US hospitals -- that's genuine domain credibility. They all overlapped at Palantir, and Vivek and Filip go back to Edinburgh together. I respect the professional depth. But what I'm listening for is something different -- the biographical thread that makes this problem personal, the adversity that forged an obsession. I don't hear a founder who was told no fifty times and kept building anyway. I hear competent engineers making a rational career move into a hot market. That's not a criticism of them as people -- it's a statement about the type of signal I need to write a check from my own money.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. Vercel carved a multi-billion-dollar business despite AWS existing, by being opinionated and developer-first. The filesystem-specific primitives here -- forking, manifest-based sync, task-pinned versioning -- show genuine product thinking about what makes agent deployment distinct from generic compute. The Palantir background means this team has actually shipped agents in production at enterprise scale, which most founders in this space cannot claim. And the timing window is narrow: Claude Agent SDK and Codex SDK just created standardized frameworks that need deployment infrastructure, and the cloud providers are structurally incentivized to sell raw compute rather than opinionated orchestration layers. If Terminal Use can establish developer mindshare before E2B, Daytona, or the model providers themselves close the gap, the category position could be worth something. The Woz Slack integration is clever distribution that could create real workflow-level switching costs inside organizations.

But I keep coming back to what's absent. There's no economic access story here -- no one who was previously excluded gaining something they couldn't have before. This serves well-resourced engineering teams at companies already deploying AI agents. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or create opportunity for underserved populations. There's no regulatory moat, no stigmatized category that deters other investors. And the traction signals are concerning even by pre-seed standards: four GitHub repos with zero stars, no community, no press, no public evidence of users. The co-founder dynamics are actually the strongest signal in this evaluation -- three founders with deep shared history at university and Palantir, an odd-numbered team that avoids the 50/50 trap I learned about the hard way with HQ Trivia. That gives me confidence they can survive the long journey together. But co-founder alignment alone isn't enough when the category is this crowded and the biographical conviction isn't there.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a space that's already attracted hundreds of millions in funding from competitors. That's exactly the kind of bet I don't make. I need the founder whose story keeps me up at night, the company that looks ridiculous to everyone else, the problem that creates real opportunity for people who didn't have it before. Terminal Use is none of those things. It's a well-constructed enterprise infrastructure play, and I'm sure there's an investor whose framework rewards exactly that. It's just not mine.
