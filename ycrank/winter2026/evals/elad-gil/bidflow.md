# Bidflow -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check when I see "AI for construction" is whether the structural moment is actually non-obvious. In 2024-2025, multimodal vision models crossed the fidelity threshold to extract structured data from engineering drawings -- that's real. But $70M+ has already flowed into AI construction estimating across Beam AI ($48M), Togal.AI ($17-22M), and Drawer AI ($5M). This is not a market that other investors are dismissing as too boring or too niche. It's consensus. When I backed PagerDuty, nobody else wanted to fund operations alerting -- that's what "too boring" actually means. Bidflow is entering a space where the structural inflection has been widely identified, well-funded competitors have multi-year head starts, and their closest direct competitor (Drawer AI, founded 2021) is building the identical product with founders who have 25 years of electrical contracting experience. The market is real, but the asymmetric information advantage that creates outsized returns isn't here.

The founder-market fit gap is the core concern. Jesse and Gautham are technically exceptional -- USACO Platinum, Jane Street extern at 18, ICML preprints on reinforcement learning. These are builders who can ship fast, and I respect that. But they pivoted from Ghostship (QA testing agents) to electrical estimating within roughly six months, and neither has documented experience in construction. In a relationship-driven industry where contractors need to trust that your tool won't cause them to underbid a $10M data center project, domain credibility matters. When I invested in Harvey, the team was building ahead of the capability curve in legal AI -- but they had lawyers on the founding team who understood how legal work actually flows. Bidflow's 83.5% accuracy against Togal.AI's claimed 97% suggests the domain-specific training data gap is real, and closing that gap requires the kind of industry relationships and estimation datasets that domain outsiders struggle to access.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this bet structurally caps out for me. Bidflow positions as "an AI copilot for electrical estimating" -- a single-product tool sold to electrical contractors region by region. The website lists geographic focus areas: Tri-State, Mid-Atlantic, New England, Great Lakes. That's a boots-on-the-ground sales motion into a fragmented buyer base with no viral distribution mechanism, no developer ecosystem, no natural path to becoming a platform. Compare this to how Stripe started with a payments API that became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. Could Bidflow expand from electrical to mechanical, plumbing, and general construction estimating? Theoretically, but there's no evidence the founders are thinking about that expansion, and each trade has its own domain-specific knowledge requirements that would essentially require rebuilding the domain expertise from scratch. The company is a feature, not a distribution channel.

The bull case I'd construct: these two are exceptionally fast builders who have already shipped a working product with named customers (Bass Electric reports 5x faster estimates). The data center construction boom creates acute demand specifically for electrical estimating capacity. If they can rapidly improve model accuracy through customer data accumulation and outpace Drawer AI on product velocity -- the technical generalist outruns the domain expert through faster iteration -- they could own this niche before incumbents react. Construction is one of the last trillion-dollar industries to be software-driven rather than software-aware, and sometimes the outsiders who reframe a workflow from first principles beat the insiders who replicate existing processes. The "why now" of converging AI capability thresholds plus data center demand plus skilled labor shortage is legitimate. If Bidflow were the only team going after electrical estimating with AI, I'd be more interested -- the market archetype of "boring infrastructure for tradespeople" fits my pattern. But they're not the only team. They're arguably the least credentialed team in a competitive field.

The single-miracle test clarifies my concern. Bidflow needs to simultaneously (1) build domain expertise in electrical contracting from zero, (2) close a significant accuracy gap from 83.5% to production-grade 95%+, (3) win trust from conservative contractors who've never heard of them, and (4) outrun a better-funded, domain-expert competitor in Drawer AI. That's not one miracle -- that's four compounding low-probability events. When the miracle is being delivered externally (as with Harvey, where foundation model labs delivered the fidelity improvement), the startup can focus on workflow and distribution. Here, the miracles are all internal to Bidflow, and the team doesn't have the domain foundation to shortcut any of them.
