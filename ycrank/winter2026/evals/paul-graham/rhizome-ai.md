# Rhizome AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The schlep here is enormous, and that's the first thing I noticed. Regulatory document processing across six global health authority markets, 2.5 terabytes of scanned PDFs with inconsistent metadata, 30 different datasets that all need to be normalized into something a machine can reason over. This is exactly the kind of problem that causes what I've called schlep blindness. Every regulatory affairs professional at every pharma company knows this work is painful. They spend weeks manually searching through document repositories, reading FDA guidance letters, cross-referencing EMA decisions. And thousands of smart ML engineers know you could theoretically build something better. But when they contemplate the actual work -- ingesting government PDFs, dealing with formatting inconsistencies across six countries, building citation verification systems for a domain where hallucinations could have legal consequences -- their unconscious minds say "let's work on something else." The incumbents like Clarivate and IQVIA have an even deeper problem: they sell access to the documents themselves. Building a system that synthesizes answers and reduces time spent reading documents would cannibalize their core business model. That structural conflict is exactly the kind of opening that creates room for startups.

The origin of this idea passes my organic test, though not at the highest level. Chetan Mishra wasn't a regulatory affairs professional who suffered through FDA submissions personally -- which would be the strongest possible signal. Instead, he discovered the problem through professional adjacency. At EvolutionaryScale, he was the founding product engineer building tools for scientists designing new proteins and drugs. He was inside the life sciences AI ecosystem and saw the gap: companies were using AI to discover new drugs, but the regulatory pipeline those drugs needed to pass through was still stuck in the manual document-reading era. That's a genuine observation from lived experience, not a brainstorming session. He didn't read a market research report about regulatory affairs TAM. He watched the problem from ten feet away.

What's particularly interesting about Mishra is that his entire career seems to have been unconsciously building toward this specific company. IBM Research gave him ML fundamentals. Instabase -- where he was employee number sixteen -- gave him document intelligence in regulated industries; he was processing bank documents, dealing with the same kind of messy, scanned, inconsistent data that regulatory documents present. He closed $7M in enterprise deals there as a solo technical lead, which tells me he can sell to conservative organizations that have long procurement cycles. Then EvolutionaryScale put him at the intersection of AI and life sciences, where he co-authored a paper published in Science. Each of those experiences built a specific muscle this company needs. That's the kind of founder-market fit that's hard to fake.

The user pull signals are strong for this stage. An RA Manager at a drug delivery device company said "I would cry if I lost access to Rhizome." That's the sentence I listen for. Not "it's useful" or "it saves time" -- but genuine emotional dependency. A VP of Regulatory Affairs said work that previously took weeks was done immediately. A director at a top-five pharma company said a month-long task took two days. Twenty-plus customers. Six-figure revenue in 2025, built by one person. These aren't hypothetical users or waitlist signups. These are regulatory professionals in an industry that does not adopt new tools lightly, who are paying real money and would be genuinely upset if the product disappeared.

The solo founder situation is my biggest concern, and I don't want to understate it. I've been consistent about this: cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. I made Drew Houston find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox into YC. Enterprise SaaS in pharma is particularly demanding -- long sales cycles, compliance reviews, dedicated support expectations. One person doing ML engineering, data pipeline maintenance, enterprise sales, and customer support simultaneously is a capacity constraint that becomes more acute as the company grows. But I have to weigh this against what Mishra has already accomplished alone. He got to six-figure revenue and twenty-plus customers with no co-founder. He scaled the EvolutionaryScale platform to tens of thousands of users. He closed $7M at Instabase as a solo technical lead. The pattern here isn't a founder who needs a co-founder to be effective -- it's someone who has repeatedly demonstrated the ability to execute across functions. The risk is real, but the evidence that he can manage it is also real. If I were advising him -- and I would, if I invested -- I'd push hard on finding a co-founder, particularly someone who can own the enterprise sales motion while he focuses on the technical architecture.

The bull case is straightforward: this is Stripe for regulatory intelligence. An obvious problem hiding in plain sight for decades, protected by a schlep that keeps competitors away, built by a technical founder who constructed the data moat himself. The 2.5 TB normalized corpus is the kind of asset that compounds over time -- every new regulatory document ingested makes the system more valuable, and the normalization pipeline represents months of unglamorous engineering work that a competitor would need to replicate. The zero-hallucination claim throughout 2025 is striking in a domain where accuracy is non-negotiable. If Mishra can find a strong co-founder and the growth trajectory holds, this could become the default regulatory intelligence platform for the entire pharmaceutical industry. The pricing architecture -- from $400/month individual seats up to custom enterprise -- is well-designed for land-and-expand.

I'd invest. The schlep is real, the discovery was organic enough, the founder is technical and has already built the product, and the early users are showing the kind of dependency that indicates genuine demand. The solo founder risk is meaningful but not disqualifying given his track record. This is the kind of ugly, document-heavy, regulation-adjacent problem that most investors unconsciously filter out -- which is exactly why there's room for a startup to win.
