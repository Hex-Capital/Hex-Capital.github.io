# Oasiz -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Oasiz is sitting in maybe the hottest intersection in venture right now — AI plus gaming plus UGC — and that's exactly the problem. a16z backed Rosebud AI's $15M Series A with Khosla. CodeWisp and Playabl.ai are both YC-backed. a16z's own Speedrun program funded Oasiz's $2.5M seed. When the same fund is writing checks into multiple companies in the same category, that's not a signal of opportunity — it's a signal that the upside is already priced in. Every sophisticated investor on Sand Hill Road hears "AI-generated games in a TikTok-style feed" and reaches for their checkbook. There is no consensus mispricing here. Zero. The market is not wrong about this category — the market is euphoric about this category, and euphoria is the opposite of mispricing.

The primitive question is where I spent the most time, because there's a version of Oasiz's pitch that almost works. "Interactive software as social content" — a feed where you don't watch, you play — that's at least a novel framing. Video feeds distribute passive content. Game platforms distribute interactive content. Nobody has fused these into a single consumer behavior at scale. That's the bull case. But I keep coming back to Clubhouse. I backed Clubhouse because "synchronous audio at scale" felt like a genuinely new social primitive. It was! And then Twitter shipped Spaces in three months, Spotify added live audio, and Clubhouse's usage cratered. The primitive was real, but the company couldn't own it because it was pure software with no structural lock-in. Oasiz has 21 App Store ratings and 230K plays. Roblox has 380 million MAU and is actively building AI creation tools. TikTok's Chinese counterpart Douyin already has mini-games baked into its feed. The behavioral insight might be directionally right, but Oasiz is trying to own a primitive that two incumbents with billion-user bases can absorb as a feature.

Abel Dagne's prior track record — 500K+ downloads, #1 on App Store social charts — is genuinely impressive for a pre-seed founder and represents the strongest signal in this dossier. Consumer distribution is the hardest skill in tech, and he's demonstrated it. Jonathan Dinh brings Berkeley AI research and Amazon search optimization, plus top-0.01% mobile gaming ranking, which is relevant domain conviction. These are sharp, credentialed, capable founders. But that's the thing — they're sane people pitching a sane idea. AI-generated casual games in a social feed is what every product person in gaming is talking about right now. There's nothing about this that makes you think "that's fucking crazy." It's sensible. It's well-reasoned. It's exactly the kind of bet that 15 other VCs are also making. Sane person, sane idea doesn't excite me.

The AI kill test is where Oasiz really struggles, and the dossier provides the damning evidence itself: "The AI game generation layer relies on generally available LLMs and generative models." That sentence is a death warrant. When your creation engine runs on the same foundation models everyone else has access to, your moat cannot be the creation tool — it has to be the distribution layer. But Oasiz's distribution is a social network that needs critical mass to function, and they're competing for attention against platforms with hundreds of millions of daily active users. The 5,000+ playable experiences built in closed beta represent real content velocity, and the Jemz virtual currency introduced in v1.3 shows monetization experimentation — but 7,000 user hours total is roughly what Roblox does every few seconds. The social network moat is the right strategic instinct, but it's aspirational rather than structural at current scale.

The strongest bull case for Oasiz goes like this: Roblox's business model depends on $1B+ in annual developer payouts to creators who invest weeks building complex Lua-scripted experiences. If Roblox adds AI that generates casual games in seconds, it cannibalizes the economic incentive structure that keeps its developer ecosystem alive. TikTok's core competency is video recommendation, and building a real game engine is a fundamentally different technical challenge. So maybe there's a structural opening between these two giants that neither can organically fill without damaging their existing business. Abel Dagne is exactly the kind of founder who could exploit that opening — someone who's already proven he can get a consumer app to #1. For this to work, Oasiz would need to reach 10M+ monthly active users before either incumbent responds, which requires a specific kind of viral velocity that the 6.5M TikTok views suggest is possible but the 21 App Store ratings suggest hasn't converted yet. I don't see enough evidence that the window stays open long enough.

I'm passing on Oasiz because of two dimensions that are decisive for me. First, consensus mispricing: this is a category where sophisticated capital is pouring in from every direction — a16z, Khosla, YC — and the pricing already reflects maximum optimism. I make money when the market is wrong, not when the market is excited. Second, structural durability: generally available LLMs as the creation engine, pure software with no physical moats, and a social network at 21 ratings competing against 380M-MAU Roblox and billion-user TikTok. The Clubhouse lesson cost me real money, and this looks like the same structural pattern in a gaming wrapper.
