# arc-prize-foundation -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the structural problem, and it's fundamental: this is a nonprofit. I invest my own money in startups -- entities designed to grow fast and generate returns. A nonprofit benchmark competition, no matter how important, is not a startup. You can't own equity in a foundation. There's no path to financial return. This alone puts ARC Prize Foundation outside the frame of angel investing. I've funded nonprofits through YC before, but funding and investing are different verbs.

That said, let me look at what's actually here, because the founders deserve serious analysis. François Chollet's connection to this problem is about as organic as it gets. He didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's a good AI startup idea." He spent years at Google building Keras -- which became the standard deep learning API, 63.9K GitHub stars -- and through that work developed a deep conviction that existing AI benchmarks measure the wrong things. The ARC benchmark emerged from his own research. This is someone who has been living inside the problem of intelligence measurement for a decade. That passes my organic test cleanly. And the schlep is real: building a benchmark the entire AI community takes seriously requires enormous credibility, careful design, and the willingness to referee a field full of people who think they're smarter than you. Most researchers would rather publish papers than maintain infrastructure.

The founder quality here is genuinely exceptional. Chollet is in the top fraction of a percent of technical builders in AI -- 71,000+ citations, TIME 100 AI, creator of a framework used by millions. Mike Knoop built Zapier from a YC S12 company into a $5 billion business that's been profitable since 2014. Greg Kamradt created the Needle In A Haystack benchmark that became a standard LLM evaluation tool. If these three told me they were starting a for-profit company attacking a real market, I'd pay close attention. The technical depth is there. The execution track record is there.

But here's where it falls apart from an investment standpoint. Knoop and Chollet simultaneously run Ndea, a for-profit AGI research lab that raised $43 million from Coatue. Ndea is also listed as an AI lab donor to ARC Prize. So the people who create the benchmark for measuring AGI progress also run a company that commercially benefits from insights derived from that benchmark. This is the kind of governance entanglement that makes me uncomfortable. It's not necessarily corrupt, but the incentive structure is messy. If I were investing, I'd want to invest in Ndea, where the commercial upside lives -- not the Foundation, which is the research arm that feeds Ndea's work. The Foundation is a cost center, not a business.

The strongest bull case: Chollet and Knoop could be building the "picks and shovels" for the AGI race. If ARC-AGI becomes the definitive measure of machine intelligence -- the benchmark that every lab, government, and corporation references -- then the people who built it have extraordinary leverage. Imagine owning the standard. That's genuinely valuable, and it's the kind of infrastructure play I've historically been drawn to. The community engagement is real: 34.3K followers on the ARC Prize account, $1M+ in annual prize pools, donations from xAI, Google, and Prime Intellect. The AI community is watching. If there were a way to invest in the commercial entity that sits on top of this benchmark infrastructure, this would be a different conversation.

But there isn't. The entity I'm evaluating is a nonprofit that depends on donations, has no revenue model, and generates no financial returns for backers. "Make something people want" is my test, and what people want here is a trustworthy AGI benchmark -- which is important, but it's not a product in the commercial sense. Nobody's economic livelihood depends on ARC Prize existing. The growth trajectory question is moot: nonprofits don't have growth rates in the way startups do. Default alive doesn't apply when your existence depends on the ongoing generosity of donors. This is closer to a research institute than a startup.
