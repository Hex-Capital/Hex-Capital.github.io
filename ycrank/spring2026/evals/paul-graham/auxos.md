# Auxos -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three Team USA fencers walk in with a synthetic-audience tool: Auxos is a technically competent trio attacking a software category with no unpleasant work in it, which is the wrong combination — the dimension where Auxos rates highest (hacker genealogy via Kerry Lu's Amazon Ads simulation infra modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic) and the dimension where it rates lowest (schlep zone — there isn't one) form the signature of a company that builds well and competes against six funded peers including a $1B-valued Aaru.

Schlep first, since that's where Auxos breaks. The high rung here is **Coinbase** (2012) — Armstrong took on bitcoin compliance because nobody else would touch it, and the regulatory grind became the moat. The mid rung is **Stripe** (2010) — the Collisons did the boring API-plumbing nobody wanted to do, and developer adoption compounded from it. The low rung is the MBA AI-wrapper I'd pass on without reading further. Auxos sits at the low rung: LLM-driven persona simulation is reproducible by anyone with a frontier-model API key, PyMC Labs has published practical guides, and the dossier names six direct competitors already shipping (Aaru, Evidenza, Synthetic Users, Viewpoints.ai, Brox.ai, Synthelos). There is no compliance grind, no physical-world operation, no manual integration that scares smart founders off. Whatever Ashton Daniel learned at BCG about F500 buying behavior, the work itself is not unpleasant — which means Auxos has no schlep blindness to exploit.

Hacker genealogy is where Auxos earns its keep. The high rung is **Stripe** (2010) — Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize at 17 preceded the company. The mid rung is **Dropbox** (2007) — Houston's MIT hacker demo on Hacker News. The low rung again is the MBA wrapper team. Auxos sits closer to mid than I expected: Kerry Lu shipped "the fastest seismic wave simulator in the world" at Vorticity (YC S19) and built ad simulation infrastructure at Amazon Ads modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic; Jerry Wu built Meta ranking infrastructure cited at $100M+ incremental revenue. The penalty is that none of the three founders has a public GitHub, no open-source artifact, no Hacker News presence. They built important systems inside large companies — that's hacker labor, but it's invisible to the tribal markers I over-index on.

Animal resourcefulness pre-funding, anchored by Airbnb's cereal boxes at the high rung and Armstrong's hand-built Android wallet rescuing Coinbase's rejected application at the mid rung. The low rung is any team with credentials and no shipped artifact. Auxos: no demo video on Hacker News, no pre-funding revenue stunt, no manual-research-operation they ran for a paying customer before the YC check. What the dossier offers instead is athletic résumé — Ashton Daniel was the 2022 NCAA individual fencing champion, all three are Team USA fencers. Fencing at that level is animal in a different arena, and I take it as partial signal, but it isn't the same as having shipped revenue from cereal boxes. Auxos lands between low and mid, closer to the default-when-silent.

Organic-idea fit, anchored by **Dropbox** (2007) (Houston's USB stick) at high and **Stripe** (2010) (Collisons hacking commerce since Auctomatic) at mid. Daniel encountered the pain at BCG running surveys for Fortune 500 clients who "spend a fortune for insights" — that's real, but it's consultant-observed pain, not founder-lived pain. Daniel is not now the daily user of synthetic audiences in the way Houston was the daily user of file sync. Auxos sits below mid. Cofounder configuration is the cleanest signal: a trio with a prior-relationship through the U.S. competitive fencing circuit, Stripe-brothers pattern in spirit if not in DNA — pinned mid-to-high on that ladder.

Forecast, 36 months: Auxos lands at $1-3M ARR, fails to differentiate from Evidenza's 100-client bootstrapped enterprise wedge or Aaru's election-grade $1B war chest, and is acquihired by Qualtrics, SurveyMonkey, or Sprig for the engineering team. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: strong builders with no schlep moat in a category where six funded competitors are already shipping cannot win on execution alone, because execution is the commodity. NN/g's warning that synthetic users are valid only for "hypothesis generation and early-stage testing, not final design decisions" caps the contract size from above, while frontier-model API access caps the moat from below.

The strongest opposing case: Lu's Amazon Ads simulation work is genuinely unusual — modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic is not a résumé line, it's a technical specialization that almost no other YC team in P26 will have, and per-customer audience calibration on first-party data could plausibly compound into real switching costs. **Loopt** (2005) is the Known Exception that applies here: I funded Sam Altman before mobile location was usable infrastructure because his force-of-personality animal test result overrode my product-timing skepticism. The Auxos parallel would be: three Team USA fencers, NCAA champion CEO, top-tier eng résumés, and the category timing (HBR validation November 2025, Aaru's December 2025 Series A) is exactly right. The bear-case rebuttal that wins: Altman in 2005 was solo on a contrarian thesis no one else in the batch was running; Auxos is one of seven public players in a category HBR has already canonized — the timing argument cuts against them, not for them. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: a named enterprise logo (Salesforce-tier or a YC alumnus paying >$50K ARR) signed before Demo Day, accompanied by Lu open-sourcing the per-customer calibration layer on Hacker News. Absent that, pass.
