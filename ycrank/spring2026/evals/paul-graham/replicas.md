# Replicas -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Replicas is that the problem it solves is visible to everyone. That's usually bad. When I wrote about schlep blindness, the key insight was that the best startup ideas hide in plain sight because the work required to solve them is so ugly that your unconscious literally prevents you from seeing them. Stripe worked because every programmer knew payments were broken but nobody wanted to deal with banks, fraud, and PCI compliance. Replicas is the opposite case: "running coding agents in the cloud" is a problem that every developer tools company, every AI lab, and every competent infrastructure engineer can see clearly. And they're all building it. OpenAI's Codex already runs in a cloud sandbox. Devin has $900 million. Factory and Augment Code have hundreds of millions more. The problem isn't hidden. It's a land rush.

The origin story matters to me, and here it's ambiguous. Connor worked at Helicone for eight months building LLM observability infrastructure -- the AI Gateway, request routing, monitoring at scale. That's genuinely adjacent to orchestrating coding agents. It's not the worst kind of manufactured idea, where someone reads TechCrunch and brainstorms for two weeks. But it's also not the Drew Houston pattern, where a specific personal frustration became impossible to ignore. "I worked in AI infrastructure and saw that coding agents need cloud execution" is an observation about a market, not a compulsion born from lived pain. The problem doesn't have the characteristics that deter competitors: no regulatory complexity, no trust issues between strangers, no banking relationships to navigate. Provisioning VMs and wiring up Slack integrations is clean engineering work. Clean engineering work attracts competition. Ugly schlep repels it.

The strongest thing here is that Connor is clearly a builder. A Monte Carlo ray tracer, a space RTS game with gRPC/QUIC networking, a PR stacking tool, meaningful contributions to a YC company's core infrastructure at scale -- this is the profile of someone who makes things because they can't help it. He shipped a working V1 and got three companies using it. At Waterloo CS, surrounded by that culture. If I were evaluating him purely on the "technical hacker who builds" dimension, he'd score well. The CalHacks win, the breadth of projects, the Helicone tenure -- these are real credentials for a young founder.

But the solo founder problem is significant, and not just as a rule I apply mechanically. I've watched enough YC batches to know that the hardest period for a startup is months six through eighteen, when the initial excitement fades and the grind of finding product-market fit begins. A solo student founder competing against teams with hundreds of millions in funding -- that's not a schlep problem, that's a structural disadvantage. The three named customers include Helicone, his former employer, which is exactly the kind of network-dependent early traction that doesn't generalize. The 30% PR completion rate is an interesting product metric, but without knowing how many tasks are being dispatched or by how many teams, it's hard to assess whether this represents real pull or a small number of friendly users.

Here's the bull case I genuinely considered: if the AI coding agent market fragments across Claude Code, Codex, OpenCode, and others, there's a real structural argument for a neutral orchestration layer. Anthropic won't promote Codex; OpenAI won't promote Claude Code. Someone has to be Switzerland. The Slack and Linear integrations are a smart wedge -- dispatching agent tasks from where developers already work rather than requiring them to open a new terminal. If Connor can build deep enough workflow integrations fast enough, the switching costs could compound. And the "why now" timing is legitimate: CLI-based headless agents only became viable in 2025, so the orchestration layer couldn't have existed before. In a world where Connor finds a co-founder, the agent market fragments as expected, and the major providers don't build multi-agent support themselves, this could work. But that's three conditional clauses in one sentence, and each additional miracle multiplies the risk rather than adding to it.

The core issue is that this company faces the wrong kind of competition for the wrong kind of problem. When a problem is technically clean and the market is obvious, the winners are usually the companies with the most resources, not the scrappiest founder. Stripe won despite massive incumbents because the schlep created a decade-long head start -- nobody else wanted to sit in meetings with banks. Here, any well-funded team can spin up VMs and build Slack integrations in weeks. Connor is a talented builder, and I'd be interested to see what he does next, especially if he finds a co-founder and a problem with more friction. But for this specific company in this specific competitive landscape, the thesis fit isn't there.
