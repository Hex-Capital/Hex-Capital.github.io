# Avoice -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Avoice is that it's the opposite of weird. "Harvey for Architecture" is literally the pitch format every vertical AI company uses right now — Harvey for legal, Harvey for accounting, Harvey for healthcare, Harvey for architecture. In 2026, this is the most consensus bet in Silicon Valley. Every investor I know is hunting for the next vertical AI workspace to fund. And that's a problem for how I invest, because the most promising companies tend to be unpopular and difficult to assess, and this one is neither. The positioning is immediately legible, the market is obviously large, the pattern has been validated by Harvey's $2B+ valuation. When the thesis is this easy to explain at a dinner party, the weirdness has usually been arbitraged away before I even get to write a check.

Now, before I dismiss this entirely — let me talk about what draws me in, because there is something here. Chawin Asavasaetakul is a real entrepreneur. He built Yindii, a surplus food marketplace, to 700K+ users across Thailand, Hong Kong, and Singapore and reached profitability in Hong Kong. He built an edtech company to $1M+ revenue and sold it. He's operated across multiple countries, multiple industries, and multiple business models. That's genuine founder resilience — the kind of serial builder who's been through enough cycles to know what pain feels like. But when I ask myself the question I always ask — why THIS problem, why THIS person — the thread gets thin. Two years building architecture sales teams gives you customer empathy, absolutely. It gives you domain knowledge. But it doesn't give you the biographical imperative I look for, the kind of connection where the founder and the problem are inseparable. Chawin chose architecture strategically, not because architecture chose him. That's the difference between a founder who's chasing a well-sized market and one who can't sleep until this specific problem is solved.

What I genuinely appreciate is the sibling co-founder dynamic. After what happened with HQ Trivia — a company that had perfect timing, perfect product, and failed entirely because two co-founders couldn't resolve their conflicts — I started paying close attention to how founders handle disagreement. I've come to believe that family-run businesses often have an advantage here, because the relationship predates the company and will outlast it. Chawin and Chawit have navigated decades of shared history before writing a single line of code together. That's a form of stress-testing most co-founder pairs never get. The concern is that Chawit's background is almost entirely opaque — no LinkedIn, limited public track record, a single iOS app. For a company whose entire value proposition rests on deep AI and architectural domain expertise, I need to understand the technical co-founder's depth, and right now I can't.

The economic access angle is the strongest thesis-fit signal I see. Small architecture firms — and there are over 110,000 of them worldwide — genuinely cannot afford the documentation rigor, compliance checking, and QA infrastructure that large studios maintain. Avoice's promise to "level the playing field" resonates with how I think about the best companies: they take capabilities that were locked behind scale or capital and distribute them broadly. That's what Uber did for drivers, what Affirm did for credit access, what Carta did for cap table transparency. If Avoice actually delivers on making the invisible documentation layer of architecture accessible to a two-person firm in Des Moines, that's real impact. The $300M in active projects managed on the platform suggests early adoption is happening, though I'd want to understand how many firms that represents and whether they're paying.

If I'm being honest about the bull case: architecture is a harder space than it looks. Building codes vary by jurisdiction, specification standards like MasterFormat are deeply specialized, and the cross-referencing between drawings, schedules, and specs requires genuinely sophisticated document comprehension. A general-purpose AI assistant can't do this well. If Avoice builds the firm-specific learning loop — where each firm's past projects, standards, and material preferences compound into a proprietary knowledge base — that creates real switching costs and a data moat that compounds over time. The competitors have raised more money but are focused on different slices: Swapp on Revit documentation, Monograph on financial management, Cove.tool on being a replacement architecture firm. Avoice's positioning as a broad operational workspace that serves architects rather than replacing them is defensible if they execute. And Chawin's track record of scaling across markets suggests he can execute.

But execution in a well-understood pattern isn't what gets me to write a check from my own money. I invest in the companies that make other investors uncomfortable, the ones where I have to explain to my partners why something that sounds ridiculous is actually grounded in reality. Avoice doesn't make anyone uncomfortable. It makes perfect sense. And in my experience, the investments that make perfect sense at the seed stage tend to attract lots of capital, compress margins on returns, and produce modest outcomes. The truly generational companies — the SpaceXs, the Crusoe Energys — start in a place where conviction is required precisely because consensus hasn't formed yet. I respect what Chawin has built and I think this company will likely find product-market fit. But it's not my kind of bet.
