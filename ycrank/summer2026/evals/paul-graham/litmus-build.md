# Litmus -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Litmus is a hustle-shaped company without a hacker spine — Shaivi Rau and Elena Zhao shipped $60K ARR and three ATS integrations (Ashby, Greenhouse, Lever) while still finishing Columbia degrees, but neither founder has a GitHub trail, an open-source artifact, or a shipping-hacker biography anywhere in the dossier. That pairing — operator animals without Lisp genealogy — is a specific archetype in my portfolio, and it usually produces respectable outcomes rather than defining ones.

On the animal test, the closest rung above Litmus is **Airbnb** (2009), where Chesky and Gebbia bootstrapped with $40 cereal boxes because no investor would give them a check. Litmus doesn't have a cereal-box story, but Rau and Zhao shipped revenue and three named ATS integrations while carrying senior-year courseloads at Columbia SEAS and CC — and they landed Neo Scholars as a paying customer. The mechanism that makes the Airbnb analogy load-bearing is: revealed hustle before the institutional check. Where Litmus diverges from the **Airbnb** (2009) rung is that the $60K ARR came through a conventional B2B SaaS motion — ATS integrations plus outbound — not through an act of improvisation I couldn't have thought of. Animals of the operator variety, not the stunt variety. Good, not black-swan.

On hacker genealogy, the low rung is my own pass example — the polished AI-wrapper team with credentials but no shipped code — and I have to check whether Litmus fits it. The dossier says "No public repos found" for both Rau and Zhao. Zhao has Meta on her resume and Two Sigma incoming; Rau built LangChain pipelines at August Law. These are credentials, not artifacts. Compare to Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010), or Aaron Swartz co-authoring RSS before **Reddit** (2005) — Litmus has no equivalent tribal marker. This is the unstated-pattern violation that matters most to me: I disproportionately fund Lisp-literate and competitive-programming founders, and Litmus's dossier gives me zero purchase on that filter.

Litmus's organic-idea claim runs through Rau's brother, a Series A founder who couldn't hire well. That is secondhand pain. Drew Houston forgot **his own** USB stick; the Collisons hacked commerce **for themselves** at Auctomatic before **Stripe** (2010). Rau and Zhao are on the candidate side of the interview table — Zhao is literally about to intern at Two Sigma — while selling to hiring managers whose job they have never done. The organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) reads this as proxy pain, and proxy pain scores meaningfully lower than the USB-stick pattern.

The schlep dimension is the counterfactual worth thinking about. For Litmus to score like **Coinbase** (2012) here, the schlep would have to be sandboxed grading accuracy across arbitrary customer stacks — a genuinely miserable problem that HackerRank and CodeSignal are unconsciously routing around because it would cannibalize their shared-library revenue. The dossier hints at this (3,000+ assessments graded, per-customer tuning) but doesn't name a specific technical grind Litmus has personally suffered. If Rau were quoted saying "we spent three months hand-labeling grading rubrics on 500 submissions before the model was usable," that would flip this dimension a full tier. Instead the schlep reads as ordinary integration work.

Cofounder configuration is where Litmus lands closest to the portfolio. Rau and Zhao are a two-person Columbia pair — one CC, one SEAS, product/CEO plus technical — applying together, interviewing together, shipping together. The parallel is **Reddit** (2005)'s Huffman/Ohanian dorm-roommate pair, minus the multi-year hacker collaboration history the dossier doesn't establish. A clean 2-founder complementary configuration is exactly what "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) prescribed.

My 36-month forecast: Litmus reaches $2-4M ARR selling repo-generated assessments to YC-adjacent Series A engineering teams, then gets acquired by Ashby or Greenhouse in the $30-80M range as a hiring-assessments module. The signature — operator hustle without hacker lineage — produces exactly this shape: too resourceful to die, not technically contrarian enough to become the next **Stripe** (2010). This is the modal Wufoo outcome, not the modal Airbnb outcome, and "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) tells me the fund math prefers I pass on modal outcomes and keep the slot open.

The strongest objection is that I am underweighting an Airbnb-shaped exception. Rau and Zhao shipped revenue and integrations as undergraduates in a batch where most teams are still building. My **Airbnb** (2009) rule was: when founders are animals and the idea looks softly wrong, lean in. The idea here — "run an async work trial on every engineer you interview" — is not obviously dumb the way strangers-in-your-home was obviously dumb, which paradoxically is the bearish tell. This is a **reasonable** idea, not a Black Swan idea, and reasonable ideas with strong founders are where the middle of the return distribution lives. What would flip me: if Litmus posts $500K ARR by December 2026 with three named Series A customers beyond Neo Scholars, or if either founder ships a public grading-benchmark artifact that shows the LLM-wrapper concern was wrong. Absent that, pass — but a warm pass, and I'd take the meeting again in 12 months.
