# TakeCareOS -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ragav Sachdeva is an Oxford Visual Geometry Group PhD candidate shipping a "rostering, care notes, billing, and compliance" platform for home care agencies, and the dimensional signature of TakeCareOS is the cleanest part of him — solo founder, unambiguous CEO — sitting on top of the weakest part of the company, a B2B back-office abstraction I cannot install, ride, or photograph in a Four Seasons café. That pairing is what I have to evaluate: clean governance wrapped around an artifact I can only access through a 60-minute demo gate.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling from my own pass example is the rung TakeCareOS sits closest to on lived-experience: two ex-FAANG PMs who "discovered the problem in a memo, not by running the back-office their product claims to automate." The mechanism that disqualified them disqualifies TakeCareOS the same way — Ragav Sachdeva's biography is Oxford VGG under Andrew Zisserman, an NVIDIA PhD internship, a Shaped ML stint, Google and Microsoft pre-grad rotations, and zero documented hours inside a home care agency coordinator's chair. Anduril worked because Palmer Luckey had already shipped Oculus before the defense pivot; TakeCareOS has a founder who has shipped computer vision papers, not rosters, not NDIS audits, not an EVV submission to a state Medicaid office. The dossier even surfaces the tell — "engineering talent sourced from Google, Microsoft, and Nvidia" is the line on the site, which is exactly the four-logo team page my Red Flag list calls out by name.

HQ Trivia's failure mechanism was Yusupov and Kroll with no tiebreaker, and I scan TakeCareOS for that pattern and find the inverse: team size 1, founder is CEO, there is no Day-1 governance fracture because there is no second person to fracture against. That is the rung TakeCareOS sits highest on — the structural inverse of my GameCrush and HQ Trivia post-mortems. The asterisk is that solo in a regulated category means the execution risk concentrates instead of disperses, and HIPAA-adjacent care documentation with one engineer is its own variety of fragility, but on the criterion as I have defined it for years, Ragav Sachdeva clears it.

Mindbloom and Vigil Labs are the unsexy-regulated lane I fund, and TakeCareOS is adjacent to that lane without being in it — NDIS rostering, EVV-style compliance, and Xero/QuickBooks/MYOB billing integrations are all regulated plumbing, but TakeCareOS is a software wrapper on top of the regulation, not capital-intensive frontier hardware. Eighteen months of compounding here looks like one of two things: either TakeCareOS grinds into an EVV certification and a state Medicaid integration and earns a moat that AlayaCare and HHAeXchange have already paid for, or it stays a thin agent layer that those incumbents replicate as a feature ship in their next release. The MYOB integration tells me Australia/NZ NDIS is the actual ICP, which is a smaller and more navigable regulatory surface for a solo founder than US Medicaid — that is the only piece of the dossier that makes the regulated-swing dimension non-trivial for TakeCareOS.

The non-consensus filter is where I want TakeCareOS to surprise me and it does not. "AI-first operating system for home care" is the modal P26 pitch, and the only competitive differentiation in the dossier is "conversational AI as the primary entry point rather than a feature add-on" — a positioning claim that AlayaCare and HHAeXchange can erase with a quarter of engineering. For the score to flip from Pass to Neutral on this axis, TakeCareOS would need a sophisticated investor to have publicly passed on aesthetic or capital-intensity grounds, or the founder would need to articulate a contrarian reason the incumbents structurally cannot follow — the dossier offers neither, only "investors from Stripe, Airbnb, DoorDash, and Dropbox" name-checked on the site, which is consensus social proof, not the inverse.

Flock Safety is the single anchor on physical-world verifiability — sourced from a Wi-Fi network name in a hotel café, a tactile artifact in the founder's environment. TakeCareOS gives me a demo-gated B2B SaaS, no Product Hunt launch, no public app, six pilot agencies and 200+ employees onboarded that I am told about but cannot observe. I cannot watch a coordinator use it the way I watched Travis Kalanick at The Lobby or played Ingress in 2012. The "investors from Stripe, Airbnb, DoorDash, and Dropbox" line on takecareos.com is the wrong direction of evidence — it is logo-borrowed credibility for an artifact I still cannot touch.

My 36-month forecast for TakeCareOS is that the company plateaus under $1M ARR on Australian NDIS pilots, Ragav Sachdeva either accepts an acqui-hire from AlayaCare or ShiftCare for the agent layer, or quietly pivots to a horizontal AI-coordination infrastructure play that is closer to his actual VGG/NVIDIA background — the mechanism is that a solo CV-PhD founder cannot simultaneously build EVV compliance, win NDIS audits, scale past six pilots, and outpace incumbents who already hold the certifications, and the dossier shows him optimizing for the wrong half of that equation.

The strongest opposing case is the DeepMind Known Exception — Hassabis, Suleyman, and Legg were pedigreed PhDs and I funded them anyway because the polymath cross-domain compounding overrode the lived-experience criterion. Could Ragav Sachdeva be that profile? The dossier does not support it. Hassabis was a chess prodigy, neuroscientist, and game designer compounding across three distinct domains; Ragav Sachdeva is deep in one domain — computer vision and ML engineering — with internships that are all variations of the same skill. The polymath exception requires compounding across disciplines, not depth within one, and TakeCareOS does not present the multi-disciplinary biography that triggered the DeepMind override. The HandUp exception also does not fit — that override was personal mission alignment with my own homelessness origin, and there is no signal in the dossier that Ragav Sachdeva has any autobiographical connection to home care, disability care, or aged care. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Ragav Sachdeva adds a co-founder or operating partner who has personally run a home care or NDIS agency, and the pilot count goes from six to thirty with disclosed revenue inside the next nine months. Until then, TakeCareOS is a thoughtful founder building the wrong company for him in the right tier of regulation.
