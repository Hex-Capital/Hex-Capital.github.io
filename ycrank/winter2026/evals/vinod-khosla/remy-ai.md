# Remy AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Remy AI is what's missing from the picture: nobody is laughing at them. Two billion dollars of venture capital has already poured into warehouse robotics — Nimble at $221M, Covariant at $222M, Dexterity at $1.65 billion valuation, Physical Intelligence at over a billion. When Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router, that was my signal to back Juniper. When five well-funded competitors are already deploying AI-powered warehouse robots in production, that is a very different signal. Remy is entering a market where the consensus has already arrived and written large checks. The question then becomes: does their specific angle — brownfield, low-cost, targeting the unserved 80% — constitute a genuinely different market, or is it the same market approached from the vulnerable end?

The consequence-of-success question here is real but bounded. If Remy succeeds fully, they automate dexterous tasks across the 80% of warehouses currently running with zero automation. Labor at 50-70% of warehousing costs, wages climbing 7-9% annually, vacancy rates above 8% — the economic pain is genuine. But automating warehouse operations does not change the physical structure of an industry the way Impossible Foods would eliminate animal agriculture or Commonwealth Fusion would replace fossil fuel generation. The world still has warehouses, still has 3PLs, still ships goods the same way — it just does it with robots instead of people. That is a large business opportunity but not a civilization-scale transformation. I compare it to Square, where we democratized payment processing for millions of merchants who couldn't access traditional POS systems. Square's consequence was enormous because it unlocked an entirely new economic layer. Whether Remy can achieve that same structural unlock for the long tail of warehousing is plausible but unproven.

The founding team concerns me through my specific lens. Oscar Brisset spent his career at BCG advising Fortune 500 logistics companies on warehouse automation. He is, by definition, an industry insider — someone who has absorbed the established industry's framing of what automation should look like, what deployment timelines are acceptable, what customers will pay. I have said repeatedly that experience creates biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing. This does not mean BCG alumni cannot build transformative companies, but the outsider advantage I look for — the irreverence and foolish confidence of a Peter Beck building rockets from New Zealand — is absent. Ben Kaye's credentials are strong. A CVPR 2025 publication on 3D reconstruction from Oxford's Visual Geometry Group is directly relevant to robotic manipulation. But he is operating in the center of the academic robotics consensus, not against it. Neither founder is attempting something the experts consider impossible. They are attempting something the experts consider inevitable, which is a fundamentally different bet.

The strongest bull case deserves serious engagement. The brownfield angle — robots that slot into existing workstations without requiring warehouse redesign, deployed in days by taking photos of the customer's environment — could represent a genuine 10x deployment speed advantage over incumbents who require months of integration. If the claim of 50% lower cost holds, combined with RaaS pricing, this could unlock demand from 70,000+ U.S. 3PLs that cannot justify million-dollar capital expenditure on traditional automation. This is the Christensen disruption pattern: incumbents like Daifuku and Dematic serve the top 20% with expensive, high-margin systems and structurally cannot address the long tail without cannibalizing their own business model. A two-person team with a lean hardware approach and data-efficient ML could move faster than incumbents and cheaper than well-funded startups that have raised hundreds of millions and built corresponding cost structures. The timing argument is also legitimate — VLA models crossed practical thresholds in late 2024, making it feasible for a small team to achieve capable manipulation without the massive data infrastructure that Covariant spent years building. If I believed the technical differentiation was durable, this would be compelling.

But I do not believe it is durable, and that is the crux. The VLA model space is commoditizing at exactly the pace that makes proprietary training pipelines fragile. Physical Intelligence published π0 in October 2024, Alibaba released RynnBrain as open-source, Covariant's RFM-1 is available. Remy claims their pipeline "learns new tasks with less data than standard VLA fine-tunes," but this claim is unverified by any independent benchmark, and data efficiency is precisely the dimension along which the entire field is rapidly improving. If data-efficient fine-tuning becomes widely available — which the trajectory strongly suggests it will — Remy's core technical moat dissolves, and they are left competing on hardware and deployment speed against companies with 100x their capital. The hardware side may offer more durable differentiation, but a two-person pre-seed team claiming both novel ML and production-grade warehouse hardware stretches credibility. Warehouses involve dust, temperature extremes, continuous operation, and collision risk — environments that punish hardware immaturity mercilessly.

This is ultimately a company that has reduced its probability of failure by choosing a validated market with proven demand and established technology approaches. The consequence of that risk reduction is that, even if they succeed, they are building a good business in a competitive market — not a category-defining company that makes incumbents obsolete. I would tell these founders: you are clearly smart enough to work on a harder problem. The warehouse robotics opportunity is real, but the six companies ahead of you with two billion dollars in combined funding have already placed the obvious bet. Find the bet that nobody else is willing to make.
