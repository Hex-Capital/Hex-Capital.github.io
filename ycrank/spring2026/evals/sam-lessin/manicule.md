# Manicule -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Naman Bansal has been writing paid technical docs for dev-tool companies since he was 13 — PromptLayer, Brightdata, Courier.com — and Shreyans Jain was Supermemory's founding engineer before age 18, which makes Manicule the rare YC P26 dossier where the founders have lived inside the problem longer than most of their batchmates have been programming. That's the signature: a missionary-conviction pair attacking a niche they obsessed over pre-pitch, without any owned audience on the cap table. Manicule is shaped like a young **Drop.io**, not like a creator-fund bet — it's an unsexy execution-machine selling to a peer-network of buyers, and the asset on the table is the operator pair, not a community.

On founder-conviction, the HIGH rung in my book is **Ben Silbermann** at Pinterest — a missionary working a category most people dismissed for years before the markup. Manicule's load-bearing analogy to that rung is the pre-funding artifact trail: Naman and Shreyans recorded their first YC founder intro from their bedrooms in Agra at age 15, four years before P26, and Naman had already shipped paid work for three named dev-tool companies before he could legally vote in the US. Where Manicule diverges from Silbermann is the absence of opinionated long-form public writing on why devrel-as-a-service is a non-obvious bet — I get the resume, I don't yet get the manifesto. But "what if they're right?" applied to two 18-year-olds who have been doing this work since 13 is exactly the kind of inversion I underwrite.

The canonical failure mode on Dim 4 — founder-as-brand — is the lifestyle-vlogger pattern the Slow Creator Fund explicitly screens out: visible reach, no business system, no distribution that compounds into product moat. Manicule does not fail that way; it fails a quieter way. The dossier lists Twitter handles @namanbansal0611 and @CodeWShreyans with follower counts "not retrievable," no newsletter, no podcast, no Discord, no Substack. Distribution here is operator-network sourcing — Supermemory begat Greptile begat Reducto begat Mastra — which is the Facebook-IPG / Kortina-graph channel I respect, but it is not a moat I can underwrite as a creator check. Manicule is not on the Slow Creator Fund's books; it's on the regular books, and Dim 4 scores accordingly.

On round dynamics (Dim 1), the mechanism over 18 months is what matters: $500K in at YC P26, no tier-1 name visibly bidding, and the category — "AI native developer relations" as a human-plus-agent studio — reads to most software-trained VCs as a services agency, which they reflexively deselect. That is precisely the asset-class mispricing I look for. Compare against my Pass Example: $30M post-money seed, four tier-1s in, AI agent for enterprise X. Manicule is the inverse of that dossier on every axis except "uses LLMs." The MRR variance in the dossier ($58K MRR per YC, $15K and profitable per ac0.ai, $150K+ ARR per Proxima Mumbai) is a diligence ask, not a deselection — services revenue is just harder to snapshot than SaaS MRR.

Counterfactual on Dim 2 — capital structure: to flip Manicule from mid-tier to top-tier here, I'd need either a non-standard instrument (revenue share against the studio's per-customer retainer stream, employee-equity, holdco wrapping multiple agency lines) or visible bootstrap-without-this-round optionality. Manicule has the second — $58K MRR with 100% MoM and ac0.ai's "profitable" tag means $500K is acceleration capital, not survival capital, which is the Slow letter's "capital efficiency as guiding light." What it does not have is structural creativity in the financing. A standard SAFE on a services biz prints fine for me at this price, but it forecloses the **Teamshares**-shaped upside where the instrument itself does the work.

The single-anchor parallel for Dim 5 is **Fin** — my own 2015 co-founder arc with Kortina, where the entire bet was "manual process first, agent layer second, find the productizable wedge by running the work." Manicule's "human-plus-agent studio" framing — agents for code verification and doc auditing, humans for the thinking — is the Fin template aimed at a tighter wedge (devrel for AI-dev-tool buyers) than we ever had. The 30% Supermemory answer-success-rate jump in 23 days and ~37% support-ticket reduction across customers is the kind of operational telemetry that proves the manual process is generating data the productized version could compound on. This is in my sleeve.

36-month forecast: Manicule reaches $4–8M ARR as a profitable boutique serving the AI-dev-tool cohort, ships a productized agent layer for code-verified docs, and either gets acqui-hired by **Mintlify** for the services wedge Mintlify can't run from a SaaS cap table or raises a $10–15M Series A on the productized layer. Not dead, not a $100M outcome, very plausibly a 3–6x on a $500K check — which is exactly the regatta-tack shape I underwrite at pre-seed.

The bear case stings: Manicule's six named customers — Supermemory, Greptile, Reducto, Mastra, Agentmail, Rootly — are all early-stage AI-dev-tool companies in the same funding cohort, which means one cycle contraction takes out the customer base in correlated fashion. Mintlify at $67M raised and a $500M April-2026 valuation can bolt on a services arm against Manicule's $58K MRR in a quarter if they decide the channel conflict is worth eating. And the "agents handling code verification" framing might be marketing veneer on what is structurally a Draft.dev competitor with younger writers — in which case there is no software pivot path, just a margin-capped agency. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway is the founder pair: two operators who have been doing the work for five years pre-funding and chose to start with the manual process is the Wizard-of-Oz signal that historically outruns category-level commoditization. The one verifiable condition that flips me to pass: if Naman or Shreyans publicly indicate they intend to stay services-only and ship no agent product by month 18, the regatta loses its tacks and I'm out at the next round.
