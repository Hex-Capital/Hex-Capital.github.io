# Oddpool -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Oddpool is a competent technical pivot into a structural gap with no founder forging beneath it — Avi Arora and Ritesh Malpani are Georgia Tech alumni from Microsoft Prompt Shields and Bloomberg trading infrastructure who appear to have pivoted from Benchspan inside the same YC P26 batch into a Kalshi/Polymarket data broker, and the dossier carries zero personal story about why prediction markets found them. The signature here is high on infrastructural opportunism, lowest on biographical inevitability — Oddpool looks like the kind of bet where the gap (Polymarket acquired Dome in early 2026) recruited the founders, not the other way around.

On Founder's Biographical Forge — my opening question, the one that decides everything — the HIGH rung is Substrate's James Proud, who I described as "forged in fire and came out unbroken" and tattooed on my body; the MID rung is Affirm's Max Levchin, credentialed but with payment-systems obsession in his bones; the LOW rung is my Pass Example, the Google PM who pitched me an AI wrapper and answered "tell me your story" with "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Oddpool sits closer to that LOW rung than the MID one. Avi's Microsoft Prompt Shields work and Ritesh's 100K TPS Bloomberg systems are real engineering credentials, and Avi's 352-star Awesome-Prediction-Market-Tools repo is genuine domain immersion — but the dossier contains no incident, no loss, no encounter that explains why these two specific people had to build Oddpool. The Benchspan?Oddpool pivot inside one batch tells me they're hunting for product-market fit, not solving a wound. Career optimization, not biographical forge.

On Defensibility-at-Inflection — Oddpool's highest dimension — the HIGH rung is Flock Safety's compounding camera network, the MID rung is Carta's cap-table switching costs, and the LOW rung is IRL with its fabricated metrics and no moat at all. Oddpool sits between MID and LOW, leaning toward MID for one specific reason: an orderbook history archive starting March 2026 with 1?5 minute granularity is forward-only — a competitor entering in 2027 cannot rebuild 2026. The Polymarket-acquired-Dome event genuinely cleared the YC-backed neutral aggregator out of the room. But Prediction Hunt covers five venues to Oddpool's two, FinFeedAPI is shipping, pmxt is open-source, and the underlying APIs are public — the technical replication barrier is low. The moat is real but narrow, and venues have already shown (Dome) they prefer to absorb aggregators rather than tolerate them.

On Contrarian Conviction in Hard Spaces, the HIGH rung is Anduril (politically toxic defense tech), the MID rung is RTFKT (culturally weird, not stigmatized), and the LOW rung is again my AI-wrapper Pass Example. Oddpool sits between MID and LOW. There's regulatory edge underneath — Kalshi's CFTC clearance, Polymarket's US legal posture — but Oddpool itself is a data broker layered above that risk, not operating inside it. "Bloomberg for prediction markets" is a phrase that lands comfortably at any VC dinner; nobody shifts in their chair. The 300-investor-pass test fails here.

On Invisible System Made Visible, the HIGH rung is Affirm exposing credit card opacity, MID is Carta exposing cap tables, LOW is HQ Trivia where there's no system at all. Oddpool's "fee-adjusted arbitrage detection" and whale-trade alerts do expose pricing asymmetries between Kalshi and Polymarket — a real informational structure — but the beneficiaries are quant funds and prop shops who already have Bloomberg terminals, not the homeless 15-year-old I was. On Cascading Real-World Impact, against the HIGH rung Uber (transportation access for everyone) and MID Depop (democratized resale), Oddpool sits at the LOW end: 40% of Kalshi flow is already institutional, and Oddpool's $100/month Premium and Enterprise S3-dump tier are aimed precisely at that institutional layer. No new economic participants are created.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool gets acqui-hired by Kalshi, a CoinAPI-style aggregator, or a mid-tier prop shop for $8?25M, most likely Kalshi consolidating the data layer the way Polymarket consolidated Dome. The mechanism is the same one Oddpool's own thesis depends on — venues prefer to absorb neutral aggregators rather than tolerate them — and the historical archive plus the cleared competitive field make Oddpool the obvious next target. That's a fine outcome for the founders. It is not a Long Journey check.

The strongest case against my pass is the RTFKT precedent: I broke my "infrastructure-grade defensibility" rule for a digital-sneaker company because the acquirer landscape was identifiable and the timing window was narrow, and Nike paid. Oddpool has both — Polymarket already paid for Dome, Kalshi is the obvious next buyer, the window between Dome's exit and venue-side consolidation is genuinely narrow. The bull would say: this is RTFKT for prediction-market infrastructure, take the acqui-hire bet. I don't take it because RTFKT had creative weirdness at a category boundary that Justin Timberlake wanted to back; Oddpool is a normalized REST API. The acquirer outcome is real but the price isn't a power-law return, and an acqui-hire isn't the swing I take with my own money. One condition flips this: if Avi or Ritesh discloses they personally lost meaningful capital trying to arb between Kalshi and Polymarket and built Oddpool from that wound — or if a named desk like Jane Street or Susquehanna signs the Enterprise tier — I revisit.
