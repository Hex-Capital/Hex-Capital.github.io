# Maquoketa Research -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Anduril has raised $6.9 billion and is building a $1 billion factory to produce tens of thousands of autonomous systems annually starting July 2026. AeroVironment holds a $990 million Army Switchblade contract and is scaling to 1,200 units per month. Maquoketa Research is a five-person pre-seed startup in Elk Grove Village, Illinois, entering this arena with forked open-source repositories and a Berkeley RF engineer. The leverage arithmetic here is brutal and immediate. In software, a five-person team can outship a thousand-person incumbent because code is permissionless leverage — zero marginal cost, infinite replication. In defense hardware, a five-person team cannot outmanufacture a company with a five-million-square-foot factory. Every drone Maquoketa Research ships requires materials, assembly, testing, and ITAR-compliant production. That is labor leverage and capital leverage — the two forms I explicitly deprioritize because they require permission and scale linearly with headcount.

Alejandro Hernandez's phased array and RF design expertise from Berkeley Wireless Research Center is the strongest specific knowledge signal in Maquoketa Research's founding team. Phased array systems for contested electromagnetic environments are genuinely specialized — you cannot hire your way to that knowledge quickly. But one component does not constitute a company-level specific knowledge moat. Evan Yeager's background spans SpiderRock (trading infrastructure) and Stylis.AI — technically capable, but nothing in his public history suggests years of obsessive engagement with autonomous weapons or drone design. The GitHub organization confirms the picture: nine public repositories, all forks of PX4-Autopilot, QGroundControl, HackRF, and other existing open-source projects. No original repositories. Maquoketa Research is assembling known components, not productizing non-replicable knowledge. If Yeager disappeared tomorrow, the company's core capability would survive with any competent systems integrator — the exact failure mode of my specific knowledge test.

The bull case is structural and worth articulating honestly. The principal-agent problem at Lockheed, Raytheon, and Northrop is real: their revenue models depend on $100K+ precision munitions, creating an economic disincentive to aggressively compete in the sub-$10K expendable drone segment. This is structurally identical to Google's ad-revenue-driven inability to cannibalize search with AI — the same dynamic that made Perplexity compelling. Ukraine has validated the doctrinal shift: cheap autonomous drones neutralizing armored vehicles costing orders of magnitude more. The $5.36 billion loitering munition market growing at 19.9% CAGR to $13.26 billion by 2030 is not speculative — it is backed by $70 million in U.S. Army fiscal 2026 procurement requests and €268 million in German government contracts to Helsing. Maquoketa Research's "built entirely in the USA" positioning addresses a genuine NDAA compliance gap. An advocate would argue this is my Varda Space Industries pattern: deep-tech hardware creating leverage through physics that software companies cannot replicate.

Maquoketa Research is not Varda. The Varda exception applied because microgravity manufacturing creates capabilities physically impossible on Earth — no competitor can replicate the leverage without going to space. FPV attack drones are widely commoditized in Ukraine, with dozens of manufacturers producing similar platforms. The autonomy software stack is the claimed differentiator, but Anduril and Shield AI (projecting $540 million in 2026 revenue) are investing billions in autonomy. Maquoketa Research's hardware advantage is manufacturing location and cost structure, not physics impossibility. And unlike Perplexity, which could exploit the incumbent principal-agent problem with pure software leverage — zero marginal cost per query, scaling to billions of users — Maquoketa Research needs the exact same factories, supply chains, security clearances, and headcount that incumbents require. The principal-agent insight identifies the opening correctly but the leverage architecture cannot exploit it. The insight is software-shaped; the execution is hardware-shaped.

The principal authenticity signal at Maquoketa Research is genuinely ambiguous. Choosing defense hardware as a pre-seed startup is not the path of least resistance — it signals conviction over convenience. Yeager and Hernandez sharing UC Berkeley EECS reduces the co-founder-met-at-a-startup-weekend risk. But no evidence exists that any founder was building drones or autonomous systems before starting this company. No prior projects, no public writing, no open-source drone contributions under their personal accounts. The revealed behavior suggests smart engineers who identified a massive market opportunity, not principals whose identity is fused with the problem. Defense procurement cycles of 18-36+ months before meaningful revenue will test whether this is principal commitment or venture-funded exploration.

The access democratization pattern — my strongest unstated portfolio filter — is absent from Maquoketa Research. AngelList, CoinList, Polymarket, Udemy, and Gumroad all gave millions of people access to something previously restricted to thousands. Maquoketa Research sells to the most centralized buyer on earth: the U.S. Department of Defense. This is not inherently disqualifying, but it means the company activates none of the structural dynamics that have produced my highest-conviction bets. One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: evidence that the autonomy software stack is genuinely proprietary and licensable across multiple hardware platforms — transforming Maquoketa Research from a drone manufacturer into an autonomy-as-a-service layer with software-like leverage. A working prototype demonstrating autonomous target acquisition in GPS-denied, jammed RF environments at performance levels exceeding Anduril's Altius would constitute specific knowledge I cannot dismiss. Without that evidence, this is a competent technical team entering a capital-intensive market dominated by companies with three orders of magnitude more funding, using leverage forms my framework explicitly deprioritizes.
