# Unisson -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing that jumped out at me is the name collision. Totango and Catalyst — established customer success platforms — merged and launched a product called "Unison AI" in the same space where Unisson operates. When an incumbent accidentally takes your name in your own market, that's not just a branding problem. It's a signal about how fast the space is converging around you. Every CS platform is adding AI agents right now. ChurnZero launched AI agents for CS teams. Sierra hit $100M ARR doing customer AI. The question isn't whether AI will transform customer success — it's whether a two-person pre-seed startup can carve out defensible territory when companies with hundreds of millions in funding and existing customer bases are building adjacent products at speed. This is the crowded-hot-category pattern I worry about. When every VC is excited about AI agents, the upside has already been partially priced in.

How did these founders find this problem? Varun spent five years at Ambient.ai building agent products and leading VLM research. Tom spent three years at Chef Robotics leading perception. Both are deeply technical AI/ML people. But neither, as far as I can tell from public information, has ever been a technical customer success manager, an implementation specialist, or a sales engineer. There's no story here about a founder who spent years watching customer deployments stall because the one person who understood the product configuration was on vacation. The idea appears to flow from their technical capability — "we know how to build AI agents, let's find a high-value application" — rather than from a specific moment of personal frustration with broken customer onboarding. That's the direction of reasoning that produced most of the forgettable startups I've seen: technology looking for a problem rather than a problem demanding a technology.

The differentiation angle — targeting internal specialist teams instead of end-customer support — is genuinely interesting as positioning. It's the kind of insight that suggests careful market analysis. But careful market analysis and organic problem discovery are different things, and they predict different outcomes. The founder who stumbled into a problem because they lived inside it will have hundreds of micro-insights about what the actual workflow looks like, what the real blockers are, what the users will accept and reject. The founder who identified a gap in the competitive landscape will have a clean thesis but will be learning these micro-insights on the job. That's not fatal, but it means more iterations, more pivots, and a weaker starting position against incumbents who already have the customer relationships.

The bull case is worth articulating. If Varun, while building agent products at Ambient.ai, watched their own customer success and implementation teams struggle to transfer product knowledge — if he saw firsthand that every new deployment required the same senior engineer to hand-hold the customer through configuration — then the origin story is actually organic, just not publicly documented. The technical team is strong: two ML engineers who've both shipped production AI systems at real companies. The 15-20 minute product-learning claim, if it reflects genuine architectural innovation rather than marketing, could represent a meaningful technical wedge. And the specific insight that existing CS platforms solve for support ops workflows while nobody solves for the implementation specialist's need to actually understand and operate the product — that's a real gap. If the team can demonstrate early user dependency before incumbents close it, this could work. The problem is I don't see evidence of any of that yet.

On the technical founder dimension, this team is credibly strong. Varun combined product leadership with engineering at an AI company for five years. Tom has production ML and perception engineering experience plus public repos showing genuine technical range — quantum computing, computer vision, medical imaging. These are builders, not managers. They can presumably ship a working product without hiring anyone else, which is exactly the kind of founding team I want to see at pre-seed. This is the one dimension where Unisson clearly passes the test.

What's missing is any signal of user pull. No revenue. No waitlist numbers. No organic sharing. No anecdote about a customer success leader who tried the product and immediately asked to pay. At pre-seed that's common, but combined with the absence of an organic founder-problem backstory, it leaves me without a strong reason to believe this specific approach will generate the kind of desperate early adoption that separates startups that work from startups that don't. The product claims to learn any product in 15-20 minutes — that's a bold, testable claim, and I'd want to see a customer say it's true before I'd get excited.
