# Approxima -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

This is the company I describe when people ask me what I mean by "AI is a bad startup investment." Two University of Waterloo CS grads, mid-batch pivot from a mobile SDK nobody used, building an autonomous bug-fixing agent in a market where Cognition went from $1M to $73M ARR in nine months, Anthropic ships Claude Code directly, OpenAI ships Codex directly, and GitHub Copilot is bolting on agentic capabilities as a feature update. The website returned a connection error when the dossier was researched. The GitHub org still contains only code from the prior product. If I drew a diagram of the exact company my framework tells me to avoid, it would look like this.

Let me run the sane-person-insane-idea filter. Selvaraj interned at Databricks and Microsoft — solid, not extraordinary. Pronev has competitive programming chops. These are capable young engineers from a strong CS program. The "sane" box is partially checked, though neither founder has shipped anything at the scale of infrastructure they're proposing to automate, and neither has the kind of deep domain credibility in software reliability engineering that would make me say "this specific person should build this specific thing." But the real problem isn't the founders — it's that the idea is the single most consensus idea in technology right now. "AI that finds and fixes bugs" is what every foundation model company, every well-funded coding startup, and half the YC batch is building. The whole point of my framework is that the idea needs to be insane — something that makes smart investors uncomfortable. Nobody is uncomfortable with this idea. They're oversubscribed to it. This is a sane person with the most vanilla idea in the market, which produces zero signal in my system.

The structural moat question is where this gets genuinely ugly. I have been saying for two years that software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore. Approxima is building a software wrapper around foundation models whose providers are shipping the identical capability natively. Claude Code — the tool I'm metaphorically speaking through right now — does autonomous bug detection and fixing. When the model provider IS your competitor and they have the distribution, you are building a feature, not a business. The dossier found zero defensibility signals. No proprietary data. No physical-world operations. No regulatory position. No protocol lock-in. The inferred moat — "proprietary training data from customer codebases" — is the same hand-wave every AI wrapper startup makes, and it almost never materializes into real lock-in because customers don't want their code locked into a vendor and the underlying models keep getting better at zero-shot performance anyway.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously: Sweep AI vacated the autonomous bug-fix niche and pivoted to IDE tooling. Maybe there's something structurally hard about the "self-healing" positioning — proactive detection rather than reactive generation — that the big players won't prioritize because their business model is developer augmentation, not developer replacement. If Approxima could become deeply embedded in CI/CD pipelines and build a genuine data flywheel around specific codebases, they could create switching costs that look more like infrastructure than application. The "self-healing software" framing, if taken to its logical extreme, could become a new category of always-on reliability infrastructure. For this bull case to work, you'd need to believe that (a) the founders can build something meaningfully differentiated from what Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft are shipping for free or near-free, (b) they can do it faster and better than Cognition with its $400M war chest, and (c) the switching costs from CI/CD integration are high enough to survive the next three model generations. I don't believe any of those three things, but I want to name them explicitly.

The mid-batch pivot from SideKit is an additional concern. The prior product — an iOS/React Native SDK for taking mobile apps to production — had 7 GitHub stars and no visible traction. Pivoting is fine; some great companies pivot. But pivoting from a mobile SDK into the hottest, most competitive category in AI, without any visible prior expertise in the new domain, looks more like chasing the market than pursuing a deeply held conviction. My best investments have been founders who were building toward an idea for years before anyone else cared — Anatoly at Solana had been thinking about proof-of-history since his Qualcomm days. The pattern here is the opposite: two talented engineers looking for a wave to ride.

There is no primitive being created here. There is no protocol position. There is no physical-world moat. There is no revenue cushion. There is a software wrapper on foundation models in the most competitive category in technology, built by capable but inexperienced founders who pivoted into it weeks ago. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer.
