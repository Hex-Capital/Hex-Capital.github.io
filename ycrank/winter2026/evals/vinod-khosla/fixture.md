# Fixture -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me describe the company I pass on more than any other: strong founders, proven market, working technology, and if it succeeds, nobody's life changes. Fixture is that company. Two talented engineers who built and sold a search-as-a-service business to a public company, who then spent years in senior roles at Elastic, looked at the world and decided the problem worth their next decade was... a better CRM for startups. I have enormous respect for Matt Riley and Quin Hoxie's execution track record. And that is precisely what makes this frustrating. They have the caliber to attempt something that would reshape an industry. Instead, they are aiming at a target where even total victory — becoming the dominant startup CRM — produces a mid-scale SaaS business competing with HubSpot's free tier, Attio's $116 million war chest, and Clay's $3.1 billion valuation. The consequence of success here is a comfortable business, not an industry transformation.

The "AI-native CRM" framing deserves direct scrutiny because it is the strongest version of the bull case. If AI agents truly become the primary interface for sales workflows — not humans clicking through dashboards but autonomous agents managing deal pipelines — then a CRM built as an agent-native data layer from day one could be structurally different from Salesforce or HubSpot the way Square was structurally different from a credit card terminal. That is a real thesis. But here is the problem: it is not a contrarian thesis. Attio explicitly brands itself as an "AI-native CRM." Clay has $204 million building AI-powered GTM tools. Every CRM startup launched after 2023 claims to be AI-native. When the experts and incumbents are all validating your approach, that is the signal I worry about most — you are building something incremental enough to be non-threatening. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. Nobody is telling Fixture they are crazy. That is not a good sign.

The founders embody a pattern I have seen enough to name: industry insiders returning to solve a pain point they experienced firsthand. Matt Riley was a YC Visiting Partner, watched startups struggle with CRM tools, and decided to build a better one. That is a legitimate product insight. It is also exactly the kind of "experience-derived" opportunity I find least compelling. The most impactful companies I have backed were not born from observing workflow friction — they were born from someone staring at a massive systemic problem and asking whether the entire system could be replaced. Pat Brown did not build a better slaughterhouse; he asked whether you needed the cow at all. The rate of learning I look for in founders is the willingness to discard assumptions and attack from first principles. Nothing in this dossier suggests Fixture is rethinking the CRM concept from the ground up — it is rethinking the CRM interface by layering agents on top.

I will give the bull case its due. If the agentic software paradigm truly makes UI-first CRMs obsolete — if in three years, no human ever opens a CRM dashboard because their AI assistant manages all customer context — then whoever owns the canonical data layer wins. Fixture's "zero data lock-in" philosophy, while counterproductive for moat-building, could accelerate early adoption by lowering switching friction from HubSpot. The founders' shared decade of experience building search and data infrastructure at Elastic is directly applicable to building a context-aggregation layer. And the co-founder pair is genuinely strong: 10+ years together, a successful exit together, complementary CEO/CTO skills. If I squint, I can see a path where agent-native architecture becomes the defining differentiator and Fixture's early bet on that architecture gives them a two-year head start over Salesforce's inevitable retrofit. But that path requires the technology thesis AND the interface paradigm shift AND winning against Attio's $116 million AND displacing HubSpot's free-tier entrenchment — simultaneously. That is four low-probability requirements that must all resolve favorably, and I have learned that plans requiring everything to work at once rarely produce anything at all.

The team is the dimension where Fixture scores highest, and it is the dimension that matters least for my investment decision here. Riley and Hoxie are a battle-tested pair — co-founded together, scaled together, got acquired together, operated inside a public company together. That is the kind of co-founder relationship that survives hard pivots and long winters. A team of five with this depth of shared history and technical capability is well-assembled for the specific risks of building a data-intensive product. But gene pool engineering does not compensate for the fundamental calculus: the consequence of success does not justify the opportunity cost of what these founders could otherwise build. I tell people like this the same thing every time — you are too talented to spend your best years making Salesforce marginally less annoying for twenty-person startups.
