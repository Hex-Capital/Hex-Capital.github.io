# Payna -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Payna isn't the team or the product -- it's that Brico already exists. Brico has $21.6M in funding, Marqeta and BitGo as customers, 600% year-over-year revenue growth, and Forbes Top 50 FinTech recognition. Payna is entering the exact same market -- state-by-state financial licensing automation -- with zero disclosed customers, a product that appears to be roughly two months old after a pivot from a DeFi earned-wage-access hackathon project, and founders who have no documented experience in regulatory compliance or legal operations. When I ask my first question -- sane person, insane idea? -- the answer here is neither. Aaron Chen has crypto product design chops from his time at Axal and ETHGlobal wins, which makes him partially credible in the crypto world, but compliance licensing is a regulatory domain business, not a design problem. And the idea itself isn't insane -- it's already been validated and funded to the tune of tens of millions. This is a consensus play arriving late. The worst possible combination for my framework.

Let me run my AI kill test on the product itself, because this is where it gets really damning. Payna is a pure SaaS compliance tool. The dossier explicitly notes that "the core technology -- AI document generation and regulatory tracking -- relies on publicly available regulatory data, making the underlying data non-proprietary." This is the precise category I've been writing about for two years. The moat here is software, and software moats are dissolving faster than founders realize. An LLM can read state examiner playbooks. An LLM can generate BSA/AML policy documents. An LLM can track renewal deadlines. Every major compliance platform, every law firm with a tech budget, and certainly Brico itself can replicate whatever AI-driven document generation Payna builds. There is no physical infrastructure, no installed hardware, no operational complexity that software can't replace. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is actual painting crews showing up to houses -- you can't commoditize that with a better model. Here, the entire business IS the software layer, and that's exactly what gets eaten.

The primitive-versus-application test fails cleanly. Payna is building an application on top of existing regulatory infrastructure -- NMLS, NIPR, Sircon. They're not creating a new protocol or standard that other companies build on. "Compliance operating system" is marketing language for a workflow automation tool. The cryptographic timestamping of compliance records is mildly interesting -- in theory, if that became the standard audit trail format that regulators and companies universally adopted, you'd have a protocol argument. But that's speculative, and the dossier shows no evidence the team is thinking about it at that level. They're building a dashboard, not a primitive.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the GENIUS Act and expanding state-level crypto licensing requirements will create a compliance burden so massive that the market can support multiple well-funded players; that Payna's "continuous compliance scoring" is architecturally different enough from Brico's licensing workflow to carve out a distinct category; and that the team can somehow accumulate jurisdiction-specific regulatory knowledge faster than a competitor with an 18-month head start and real enterprise customers. I take the regulatory tailwind seriously -- compliance costs rising 25% and new federal stablecoin frameworks do create genuine demand. But demand alone doesn't make a good investment when someone else is already capturing it with dramatically more resources. The bull case also requires ignoring the pivot signal: the team was building consumer DeFi two months ago. Conviction about the compliance space that's two months deep is not the kind of structural patience I look for. When I backed Helium, the team had been grinding on decentralized wireless for years before anyone cared. That's a different animal than pivoting mid-batch.

The co-founder opacity is a practical concern I can't dismiss. He Song -- one of two co-founders -- has essentially no public digital footprint. No education, no work history, no visible domain expertise. In a compliance software business where credibility with financial institutions is table stakes, having a co-founder whose background you can't verify is a real problem. Shaamak Goyal's business economics background from UChicago adds some analytical capability, but consulting internships and student VC clubs don't substitute for deep regulatory domain knowledge.

I'm passing. This is a SaaS tool built on public data competing against a well-funded incumbent in a space where the founders lack domain expertise. The AI angle is obvious -- which means it's not defensible. The idea isn't non-consensus. And the product is two months old after a pivot. There's no infinity story here, no new primitive, no structural moat that survives the next platform shift. If I wanted exposure to the RegTech expansion, Brico is the horse that's already running.
