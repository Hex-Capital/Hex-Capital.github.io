# Carrot Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Carrot Labs is the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about the idea's origin. Braintrust just raised $80M. Arize has $131M. LangChain/LangSmith sits at a $1.25B valuation. When a pre-seed company enters a space where four or five competitors have collectively raised over half a billion dollars and are explicitly expanding into the same territory, I need to see an extraordinary reason why this team will win despite a 100x capital disadvantage. The usual reason is that the founders see something the incumbents don't — some angle born from deep personal experience with the problem. I don't see that here.

How did the founders find this problem? Yuta Baba was a senior data scientist at Snowflake working on financial forecasting. Before that, he founded CareVo, a senior care startup in Japan. Christopher Acker's professional background is barely documented publicly. Neither founder appears to have been building production AI agents and running into the optimization wall firsthand. The path from Snowflake forecasting algorithms and Japanese elder care to "continuous learning platform for AI agents" looks like a category selection — someone surveyed the landscape, identified that AI agent tooling was growing fast, and decided to build there. That's exactly the failure mode I've written about: smart people sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than stumbling into problems they can't ignore. The continuous fine-tuning angle shows technical sophistication, but sophistication isn't the same as organic discovery. A clever approach to a problem you chose from a menu is still a chosen problem.

The strongest argument for Carrot Labs is that the "continuous optimization" positioning is genuinely more technical than what most competitors offer today. Monitoring agents is one thing; automatically improving them through fine-tuning loops is harder, and if you build it well, the accumulated optimization data creates real switching costs. Baba's ML background at Snowflake is relevant to building that system. And the timing is legitimate — production AI agents are proliferating faster than teams can maintain them, and the fine-tuning APIs that make this possible barely existed eighteen months ago. If I squint, I can see a version of this where Carrot Labs becomes the specialized optimization layer that the big observability platforms are too horizontal to build well. Stripe entered payments when PayPal and Authorize.net already existed, after all. But Stripe's founders were hackers who personally hated integrating payment APIs. The organic connection was unmistakable. Here, I'm looking for that connection and finding market analysis instead.

The technical founder question compounds my concern. No public GitHub repos for either founder. Baba is a data scientist, which involves real coding, but the "hacker who builds the product themselves" archetype is different from data science experience at a large company. Acker's technical depth is a complete blank. For a company selling AI optimization tooling to engineering teams, the founders' credible technical identity matters enormously — your customers are engineers, and engineers can smell whether you're one of them. The performance claims on the website (43% latency reduction, 18% quality improvement) suggest some customers exist, but these are unverified marketing numbers with no customer names, no case studies, and no independent confirmation. I can't distinguish between "we have real customers seeing real results" and "we ran benchmarks on a demo."

I keep coming back to the consensus problem. I said recently that the most impressive companies I've seen in the current batch are not working on AI. That observation reflects something structural: when every investor is excited about a category, the returns have already been priced in. AI agent tooling is the definition of a hot category right now. Braintrust, Arize, LangSmith, Maxim, DeepEval — there are more well-funded competitors than I can count on one hand, and each one is expanding toward exactly the territory Carrot Labs claims. The continuous fine-tuning piece is a real differentiator today, but it's the kind of feature that Braintrust can build with their $80M war chest in a quarter. For a two-person pre-seed team, winning here requires either a dramatically deeper understanding of the problem than anyone else has, or execution intensity so extreme it compensates for the resource gap. I see no evidence of either.
