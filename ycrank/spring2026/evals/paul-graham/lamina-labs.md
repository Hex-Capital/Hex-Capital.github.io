# Lamina Labs -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

"Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." That's the first thing I notice here. Billions of dollars are flowing into AI-generated Hollywood video — Synthesia at $4B, Runway, HeyGen — and nearly everyone building in "AI video" is chasing that same cinematic dream. Meanwhile, Manim sits there with 35,800 GitHub stars, proving that hundreds of thousands of people want to make mathematical animations, and the best tool available requires them to write Python scripts. That gap — massive proven demand, terrible user experience — has been sitting in plain sight for years. Nobody builds for it because it's not glamorous. It's a schlep. You have to care about getting Fourier transforms to render correctly frame-by-frame, not about making pretty faces talk. Lamina Labs saw the schlep and walked toward it. That's worth noticing.

The founder combination is unusually well-mapped to this specific problem. Sudip Rokaya — MIT CS and Math, systems-level C++ programmer (ghostclaw), LLM research. Rohan Bhattarai — Caltech Physics, published astrophysics researcher, International Computer Olympiad bronze medalist at roughly age 14, physics-math olympiad finalist. If you were designing founders in a lab to build "mathematically precise animation from natural language," you'd end up with something close to this pair. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." These aren't business school students who read a market report about EdTech. They've lived inside mathematical visualization as students and researchers. The physics and math expertise isn't decorative — it's load-bearing for the core product claim of deterministic, hallucination-free STEM animations.

But here's where I get cautious. I don't yet see the thing I care about most: determination under pressure. Credentials tell me they're smart. Olympiad medals tell me they can solve hard problems under time constraints. But "the most important predictor of success is determination," and I have no evidence of the cockroach quality. The Airbnb guys sold cereal boxes to fund their company. The Collisons grabbed laptops out of people's hands to install Stripe. What have Sudip and Rohan done that's comparably scrappy? Leaving MIT is a signal — it costs something real — but it's also a signal that lots of smart undergrads send before returning to school a year later. They're at waitlist stage with no users, no revenue, and no stories of doing things that don't scale. The LinkedIn post with 1,375 reactions is social media performance, not product-market fit. I can't distinguish "earnest and obsessive" from "impressive résumé, early-stage enthusiasm" without more signal.

The commoditization question is the elephant. Today, you can prompt Claude or GPT-4 to write Manim code. It's imperfect, but it works for simple cases and it's improving fast. If Lamina's product is architecturally a thin layer — LLM converts prompt to Manim script, Manim renders — then this is a feature, not a company. The 8-second generation claim suggests something faster and more purpose-built than piping through Manim, but I have zero evidence of what's actually under the hood. "No hallucinations. No approximations. Every frame is mathematically precise and deterministic" — these are website claims at waitlist stage. I've learned the hard way: "Now I don't laugh at ideas anymore, because I realized how terrible I was at knowing if they were good or not." So I won't dismiss the claims. But I can't invest on claims either.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it clearly: if these two have built (or are building) a genuine custom rendering engine — not a Manim wrapper, but a purpose-built pipeline that converts natural language to structured scene graphs to mathematically verified animations in seconds — then the technical moat is substantial. That's the kind of infrastructure schlep that protects you precisely because it's boring and hard. Manim took years to build. A next-generation system that's both faster and prompt-driven would have the same "narrow and deep" quality I look for: intense need among STEM educators and course creators, the kind of users who will tell every colleague about a tool that saves them days per video. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." STEM animation tools sounds niche until you realize every online course, every Khan Academy-style explainer, every university lecture could use this. If the product genuinely works and they attack distribution aggressively — doing things that don't scale, going out and getting users one by one — this could compound fast through word of mouth in education communities.

Where I land: the founder-problem fit and schlep-blindness angle are genuinely compelling. The raw intellectual talent is exceptional. But I'm picking founders at this stage, and I don't have the signal I need on the qualities that actually predict startup success — determination, resourcefulness, the willingness to do whatever it takes. Smart undergrads with no traction, no shared work history, and unverified technical claims are a bet on potential, and I need to see something beyond potential. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." I believe the people might be good. I just can't tell yet whether they're the kind of good that survives contact with reality.
