# Cajal -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see is a $295 million elephant in the room. Harmonic AI raised that war chest at a $1.45 billion valuation in November 2025 to pursue "mathematical superintelligence" with formal verification in Lean 4 — the same core technology stack Cajal is building on. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, I was backing an outsider against a slow-moving incumbent whose CTO publicly declared they'd never build a TCP/IP router. That's the kind of dismissal that creates opportunity. But Harmonic isn't Cisco. Harmonic is a well-funded startup moving fast in the same direction, with 100x more capital. This isn't an outsider-versus-incumbent story — it's a resource asymmetry between two ventures sharing the same technological thesis. Cajal differentiates by targeting applied science domains (quantum computing, finance) versus Harmonic's competition-math focus, but that boundary is thin and permeable. If Harmonic decides to walk into applied verticals, what stops them?

Now let me ask the question I always ask first: if Cajal succeeds completely, does the world change? Autonomous formal verification for scientific discovery is a genuinely interesting idea. Mathematical proof is the bedrock of certainty in engineering, physics, cryptography — every domain where "probably correct" isn't good enough. If you can build a system that autonomously discovers and machine-verifies proofs in applied mathematics, you've created something that doesn't exist today. That's not a 20% improvement to an existing workflow. But here's what tempers my enthusiasm: the initial customers are a narrow band of AI labs, quantum computing companies, and quant funds. The broader vision — accelerating all of science through automated proof — is magnificent, but the dossier positions this as B2B tooling with datasets, benchmarks, and RL environments as product lines. That sounds like selling picks and shovels to a small gold rush, not transforming how civilization generates knowledge. The consequence of success is real but bounded by how they've scoped the ambition. A company that could automate mathematical discovery for all of science shouldn't be leading with training data sales.

The founders are early-career researchers — Luke Johnston is an MPhil student at Cambridge's Computational and Biological Learning Lab working on meta-reinforcement learning, and Pedro Nobre has a CS background from Spanish universities with an EF Bridge connection. Here's what concerns me: neither has documented experience in formal verification, dependent type theory, or theorem proving. When I backed Pat Brown at Impossible Foods, he was an outsider to the food industry — but he was a Stanford biochemistry professor with deep expertise in the core science of molecular biology. The outsider advantage works when you bring a different discipline's toolkit to an industry's problems. These founders bring ML and CS, which is relevant, but formal verification in Lean requires a specific kind of mathematical sophistication that I can't confirm from their public profiles. The Cambridge affiliation might provide access to that expertise, but "might" isn't a team construction strategy. I'd want to see evidence of deliberate gene pool engineering — have they identified the formal verification risk as their number one challenge and recruited or partnered to address it?

The timing argument is the strongest part of this thesis. Lean 4 has matured, LLM mathematical reasoning has crossed a capability threshold, and the convergence of these two trends genuinely creates a window that didn't exist two years ago. I think humans dramatically underestimate where mathematical AI will be in three years — the rate of improvement in model reasoning is exponential, and formal verification is the natural endpoint for AI systems that need guaranteed correctness. Cajal is positioned at this intersection. But the problem is that this timing insight is no longer contrarian — Harmonic's $295 million raise, Google DeepMind's mathematical reasoning work, and multiple academic groups (LeanDojo, etc.) all signal that the consensus has formed. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, I was the only VC writing that check because everyone else thought AI was laughable. That solitude was the signal. Cajal is entering a space where smart money has already arrived in force.

The bull case that would make me wrong: Cajal's applied-science focus could be precisely the right wedge. If they can demonstrate that Tau produces novel, verified results with real economic value for a quantum computing company or a trading firm — results that pure-math-focused Harmonic can't deliver — they'd have proven product-market fit in a niche that justifies the venture before expanding. The compounding data moat from verified mathematical corpora is real — each new proof makes the system better at finding the next one. And two hungry founders with Cambridge connections, moving at startup speed against a $1.45 billion company that might be building for a broader, slower vision, could win the applied vertical by sheer focus. If formal verification becomes the standard for certifying AI systems (which regulators may demand), the market isn't $100 billion — it's whatever the AI industry itself is worth. That scenario would make my concerns about market narrowness look foolish.

But I keep coming back to the calibration of ambition versus the scale of the problem. Automating mathematical proof for science is a problem worth dedicating a life to. The question is whether these particular founders, at this particular moment, with this particular competitive landscape, are the right bet. The absence of core domain expertise in formal verification, the resource asymmetry with Harmonic, and the scoping of the product as B2B tooling rather than as a category-defining system for scientific discovery all pull me toward watching rather than writing a check. This is a legitimately interesting space, and I'd want to meet these founders and assess their learning rate directly — because the dossier can't tell me whether Luke and Pedro are evolving their thinking at the pace this challenge demands.
