# Snapp Stats -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Shawn Tsao co-founded Caviar, grew it through a Square acquisition, and watched it sell to DoorDash for $410M. That's a real exit -- not a soft landing, not an acqui-hire. Tsao knows how to build a consumer product, get it acquired, and navigate post-acquisition integration. But I've seen great operators pivot into domains where their pattern matching doesn't transfer. Food delivery logistics and sports video AI are fundamentally different businesses. Caviar succeeded because the marketplace dynamics were well-understood and Square provided distribution. Snapp Stats requires building a domain-specific Large Visual Memory Model, selling into college athletic programs, and potentially building a consumer sports feed -- none of which maps to Tsao's operational playbook. Alex Marshall's Pentagon AI work (allocating $160B for the Air Force) and Genentech data science tenure add genuine AI credentials, but the sports-specific computer vision expertise isn't evident anywhere on this team. The CTO, Andrew Tamura, still lists Rosie AI on LinkedIn as of April 2026, which raises a commitment question I'd want answered before writing a check.

The market structure timing here is where Snapp Stats loses me. When I evaluate a company, the first question is always: what structural shift is creating a window right now that wasn't open two years ago, and has consensus missed it? Multimodal AI crossing a performance threshold for video understanding is real -- I've been tracking this capability curve closely. But this is the opposite of nonobvious. WSC Sports has raised $136M and added generative AI features in 2024. Genius Sports launched GeniusIQ and generates $670M in annual revenue. Sportradar does over $1B. Microsoft partnered with the English Premier League on an AI Companion. Every major sports data company is racing to add AI video capabilities. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by consensus investors. When I backed Perplexity, AI search was considered a dead category. Sports AI video analysis is neither dismissed nor dead -- it's a crowded, well-funded market where incumbents are actively building the exact capabilities Snapp Stats describes.

The infrastructure-layer test fails cleanly. Snapp Stats is building an end-user application -- a consumer sports feed on one side, a coaching tool on the other -- using third-party infrastructure (LangChain, Crew.AI, TensorFlow on AWS/GCP/Azure). There's no API, no developer platform, no indication that other businesses would build on top of Snapp Stats. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments API that an entire generation of companies integrated into their core workflows. Or Braintrust, which became evaluation infrastructure for AI applications. Snapp Stats sits at the application layer, dependent on foundation model providers and agentic frameworks that could commoditize the exact capabilities they're building. The "sports LVMM" could theoretically become a platform, but the current positioning -- split between a consumer feed and a B2B coaching product -- reads as an application searching for a wedge, not infrastructure establishing a standard.

The dual B2C/B2B positioning is the most operationally concerning signal. The a16z Speedrun description emphasizes a consumer "personalized sports feed," while Built In SF materials describe a "Superhuman Sports AI Teammate" for coaches and athletes. These are different products, different buyers, different distribution channels, different retention mechanics. At pre-seed with six people, pursuing both simultaneously is exactly the kind of focus dilution that kills startups before they find pull. I've watched hundreds of companies try to serve two segments at once at this stage. The ones that work pick a beachhead, dominate it, then expand. Snapp Stats hasn't made that choice, and the 10x pipeline growth mentioned during a16z Speedrun doesn't tell me which segment is pulling.

The strongest bull case: Tsao's operator network from Caviar and his restaurant ventures (Umai Hospitality, investor in The Town FC soccer club) could unlock sports-adjacent distribution. If Snapp Stats narrows focus to the B2B coaching segment -- specifically college athletic programs newly motivated by NIL economics to invest in athlete performance analytics -- there's a plausible wedge. College athletic departments have budgets, and the coach/athlete segment is genuinely less contested than the broadcaster/sportsbook segment where WSC Sports and Genius Sports dominate. But this would require abandoning the consumer positioning, building deep integrations into coaching workflows to create switching costs, and competing against SportsVisio and CoachNow while fending off inevitable incumbent expansion. That's at least two independent breakthroughs -- a technology miracle (sports LVMM significantly outperforming GPT-4o on sports video) and a distribution miracle (cracking college athletic program procurement). My single-miracle test says compounding those odds produces a low-probability outcome.

Snapp Stats has strong founder credentials in a market where those credentials don't transfer, obvious timing in a space the founder framework penalizes, application-layer positioning where I prefer infrastructure, and dual-segment ambiguity where I look for focused conviction. The $410M Caviar exit earns respect but doesn't earn a check when the market structure analysis points the wrong direction. Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning -- the two dimensions that carry the most weight in my framework -- both score below default, and that's decisive.
