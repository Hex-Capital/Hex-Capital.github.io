# Lance -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Lance: this is a popular idea. AI voice agents for hospitality is exactly the kind of category that every investor I know is talking about right now. Canary Technologies has raised $180 million and serves 20,000 hotels. Duve just closed $60 million. HiJiffy, Asksuite, Akia — the space is crowded with well-capitalized players who already have deep hotel relationships. When I look at a deal and see that kind of competitive landscape, my instinct is to pull back, not lean in. The most promising companies I've backed were ones nobody else wanted to fund. SpaceX was "insane." Uber was illegal. Crusoe was mining Bitcoin on gas flares. Lance is building AI agents for hotels at a moment when building AI agents for hotels is the consensus play. That's a problem for how I invest.

The founders are clearly talented — I want to be honest about that. Caleb Chan was a founding engineer at a company that achieved a 9-figure exit, and he built voice and AI products at Google and Salesforce. Gatik Trivedi has speech and language research from Stanford, agent infrastructure experience, and already exited a healthcare startup he founded. These are impressive credentials. But when I sit down with founders, my first question is always about their story — not the business, not the resume, but the thread that connects who they are to why this problem matters to them personally. Gavin Brennen "grew up around Marriott operations," and that's the closest thing to a biographical thread I can find here. It's real, but it's thin. I don't know what that phrase means in practice — did his parents manage a property? Did he watch the front desk struggle? That biographical specificity is what I listen for, and I can't hear it yet. These founders have relevant experience, but I'm not sensing the kind of hardship-forged commitment that tells me someone will survive the inevitable crisis points ahead, especially when they're up against competitors with fifty times their capital.

The Computer Use Agent approach — vision-based agents that navigate hotel PMS software visually rather than through APIs — is the most genuinely interesting technical element here. It's a clever solution to a real problem: legacy hotel software wasn't built with API integrations in mind, and forcing those integrations has been a bottleneck for every competitor. If Lance can reliably navigate diverse PMS interfaces by sight, that reduces integration friction dramatically. But this is also fragile in ways that worry me. A PMS vendor pushes a UI update, and suddenly your automation breaks. You're building on ground you don't control. And as PMS vendors inevitably build their own AI features — Oracle Hospitality and Amadeus have every incentive to do so — the Computer Use Agent approach becomes a bridge technology, not a moat.

Let me engage with the bull case seriously, because 50+ hotels across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at the pre-seed stage is genuinely strong traction. That's not a prototype in a lab — that's real deployment in real hotel operations with real guests calling. The combination of voice-first (versus text-first chatbots), operational task execution (dispatching staff, not just answering questions), and legacy system navigation is a differentiated stack. If the team can build enough operational data and workflow embedding before the well-funded incumbents move on voice, the switching costs become real. Hotels that retrain their staff around Lance Pad and restructure call routing around the AI agent won't rip it out easily. And the 30% reduction in front desk call volume is the kind of metric that hotel operators write checks for. If I believed this team had the resilience and biographical obsession to outrun Canary's $180 million, I could see a path to a meaningful outcome. But I've also seen too many competent teams get crushed by well-funded incumbents in categories where the technology layer is commoditizing — and with OpenAI, ElevenLabs, and Vapi all driving voice AI toward table-stakes, that's exactly the risk here.

What's missing for me is the dimension I care about most beyond founder story: does this company create economic access or make an invisible system visible? Uber created income for drivers. Postmates created income for delivery workers. Affirm made credit transparent. Lance makes hotel operations more efficient — which benefits hotel operators and shareholders, but doesn't obviously create economic opportunity for underserved populations. If anything, the staffing reduction value proposition means fewer jobs for front desk workers. The multilingual support is a nice touch for non-English-speaking guests, but that's a feature, not a thesis. I need to see that a company is going to change the economic equation for people who currently lack access, and I don't see that here.

This is a competent team building a real product in a real market with real traction. For many investors, that's enough. But I'm not a consensus-based investor — I'm looking for the weird, the rejected, the biographical, the economically transformative. Lance is none of those things. It's a well-executed application of currently popular technology to a category where multiple well-funded competitors already exist. I'd pass, but I'd wish them well — and I'd pay attention if they survive the next eighteen months against Canary, because that survival itself would tell me something new about this team.
