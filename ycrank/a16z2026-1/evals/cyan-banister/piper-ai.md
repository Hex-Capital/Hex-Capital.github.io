# Piper-ai -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Piper-ai hit $100K ARR in twelve weeks selling AI bid document analysis to multi-billion-dollar general contractors. That number stopped me — at pre-seed, revenue that fast from enterprise construction buyers is not normal. Enterprise GC procurement is glacial, relationship-gated, and allergic to new vendors. So either these founders cracked something real in the product, or they brought deep construction relationships through the door. When I look at the team — Ido Gedanken (Microsoft PM, university at 14, robotics world finalist), Erez Tepper (production AI since GPT-3, defense and government systems), Roi Menzin (special ops commander, bootstrapped a cybersecurity startup to ~$1M ARR) — I see people who are undeniably impressive and ferociously capable. What I don't see is a single documented connection to construction. No one watched their family lose a project to a buried scope contradiction. No one spent years inside a GC's estimating department watching millions evaporate from unread clauses. When I ask my first question — tell me the story, not the business — I get elite military units, early academic achievement, and AI engineering across defense and government. That's a story about capability, not about inevitability. These founders chose construction the way a talented engineer chooses a market, not the way a problem chooses a person.

The space Piper-ai occupies has a quality I respond to: construction bid documents are genuinely opaque. Thousands of pages of fragmented specifications, drawings, and contracts hiding scope gaps and contradictions that silently extract millions from whoever misses them. That IS a hidden system — whoever catches the buried risk wins, and the information asymmetry between what's in the documents and what a human estimator can absorb under deadline pressure creates a structural tax on every bid. I compared this to Carta, which made cap table opacity visible, or Ownwell, which automated property tax appeals. The difference is that Piper-ai's invisible system operates between sophisticated commercial parties — multi-billion-dollar contractors — not between a powerful institution and a vulnerable individual. When Affirm exposed credit card opacity, it shifted power to consumers who didn't understand compound interest. Piper-ai shifts power to estimating teams who already understand construction but can't physically read fast enough. That's a real problem being solved, but it's professional efficiency, not structural liberation. My deepest conviction fires when technology gives power to people who had none.

I ran Piper-ai against the Flock Safety parallel — another case where outsiders entered a domain they didn't come from. But Garrett Langley at Flock had a personal encounter with neighborhood crime before building surveillance cameras. The WiFi network name I noticed at that YC event was already a signal that the product had escaped the building and was living in someone's real life. With Piper-ai, the product is clearly living in contractors' workflows — $100K ARR proves that — but the founders' path to construction feels discovered rather than forged. Erez's experience building production AI for defense and government document-heavy domains is the strongest bridge: complex, heterogeneous document analysis under high stakes is transferable. That's a capability forge, even if it's not a domain forge. And Roi's track record bootstrapping to ~$1M ARR in cybersecurity suggests he knows how to sell into risk-averse enterprises. The CRO function is often the weakest link in technical founder teams; having someone who's already done a cold-start enterprise sales motion is a genuine asset.

The defensibility question concerns me more than the founder question. Provision AI is YC-backed with $8.7M in funding and already serves Ferrovial, EllisDon, PCL, and Acciona — multi-billion-dollar GCs, the exact customer profile Piper-ai is targeting. Provision claims $100B in projects reviewed. Document Crunch has $30.5M and could expand upstream from post-award compliance into pre-bid analysis. The core technology — LLM-based document reasoning — is improving generically every quarter. Piper-ai's potential moat is in construction-specific training data and workflow integration, but at twelve weeks in, neither has compounded enough to be structural. This feels more like a footrace than an infrastructure play, and footraces in AI tend to be won by whoever has more capital and distribution, not whoever started first.

The strongest bull case: maybe I'm wrong about the forge, and what matters is that Erez built production AI systems across defense and government before the generative AI wave — the exact kind of complex document analysis that transfers to construction. Maybe the $100K ARR in twelve weeks IS the evidence of founder-market fit that my biographical filter is too narrow to capture. Maybe construction tech is more contrarian than I'm giving credit for — it's not stigmatized like defense AI, but most VCs avoid it because the sales cycles are brutal and the industry is technophobic, which creates a mild version of the 300-investor-pass dynamic. And the pipeline of $1M+ with multi-billion-dollar contractors suggests that if even a fraction converts, this company has a real business. If Erez's document analysis capabilities prove genuinely superior to Provision AI's, and Roi can close these enterprise deals before the competition consolidates, Piper-ai could build the data moat fast enough to matter.

What locks my score: the biographical forge is the dimension that most separates Piper-ai from my highest-conviction pattern. I can see these founders succeeding — they're clearly talented and executing — but I can't see why they MUST be the ones to solve construction bid analysis. Coupled with a well-funded direct competitor already serving the same customer archetype and a core technology that's replicable, this is a competent team in a real market that doesn't activate my framework's deepest convictions. I wish them speed — they'll need it.
