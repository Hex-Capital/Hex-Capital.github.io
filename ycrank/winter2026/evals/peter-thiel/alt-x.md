# Alt-X -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking fact about Alt-X is not what the company is building but who else is building it. Endex -- founded by Tarun Amasa, one of my own Thiel Fellows -- has raised $14 million from the OpenAI Startup Fund with backing from executives at Palantir, Two Sigma, and Coatue. Shortcut, built by an MIT faculty member, has raised $78 million from Sequoia at a $300M+ valuation. Apers is targeting real estate underwriting directly. Datarails has $175 million in funding and is approaching $100 million in revenue validating the broader thesis of AI-native Excel workflows. When I look at this landscape, I see the early formation of exactly the competitive structure I spend my career avoiding. Five or more funded companies, all converging on the same basic insight -- that financial analysts spend too much time in spreadsheets and AI can automate the work. This is not a secret. This is a consensus masquerading as an opportunity.

What is Alt-X's contrarian thesis? That alternative-asset underwriting is a specific enough vertical that a dedicated tool will beat horizontal Excel AI agents. But this is a market segmentation bet, not a truth about the world that most intelligent people would reject. No one disagrees that PE and RE analysts waste time copying data from CIMs into Excel. The entire competitive landscape exists precisely because everyone agrees. When every smart person in the room shares the same observation, you are not looking at a secret -- you are looking at a crowded market where mimetic forces will drive margins toward zero. The vertical specialization argument is a positioning strategy, not a monopoly-creating insight. Endex or Shortcut could add alternative-asset templates in a quarter; nothing about Alt-X's domain focus creates structural barriers that a well-funded horizontal player cannot cross.

The $30/month price point tells a revealing story. If Alt-X genuinely saves an analyst 30-50 hours on a deal where billions of dollars are being deployed, the value created per transaction is enormous -- tens of thousands of dollars in labor costs alone. Pricing at $30/month for that value creation signals one of two things: either the founders do not believe their own value proposition, or they are pricing for competitive acquisition in a crowded market. Neither interpretation is consistent with monopoly. Stripe could charge premium developer pricing because its API was an order of magnitude simpler than anything else available -- the pricing reflected genuine 10x superiority. Alt-X's pricing reflects a commodity market where the cheapest tool wins, which is precisely the dynamic that destroys value for everyone.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Ryan Samadi has lived inside the pain he is solving -- Citadel, Bank of America fixed income, PE at IIFG, Stanford CS with an AI track. This is the type of founder-market fit that would matter if the competitive structure were different. If Alt-X could build deeply specialized extraction models for alternative-asset KPIs -- cap rates, NOI waterfalls, IRR sensitivities -- that are genuinely an order of magnitude better than what horizontal tools deliver, the switching costs from workflow integration could compound. Each deal processed would improve template quality and extraction accuracy, creating a data flywheel that horizontal competitors cannot replicate because they spread their training data across all Excel use cases rather than concentrating on alternative assets. The scenario where this works is one where vertical depth creates a quality gap so large that mid-market PE and RE firms become locked in -- similar to how Palantir's deep integration with intelligence workflows created switching costs that compounded over years. But Palantir's secret was not "intelligence analysts need better tools" -- it was that PayPal's specific anti-fraud technology could be repurposed for counterterrorism, an insight that came from building something else entirely. Alt-X's insight is the obvious one: analysts need better tools. That is not a secret. That is a job description.

The technology here is fundamentally 1-to-n. Document extraction, KPI mapping, and Excel model generation all use foundation models that are broadly available. The core AI capabilities are not proprietary -- they are API calls to the same models that every competitor uses. What differentiates Alt-X is domain-specific training data and template quality, which are valuable but replicable given sufficient deal volume. Microsoft's own Copilot integration into Excel represents an ambient existential threat: when the platform owner builds native AI capabilities, add-in developers face structural obsolescence. This is not the kind of technology that makes the old way structurally impossible. It makes the old way somewhat faster -- a useful product, perhaps a viable small business, but not the foundation for a monopoly.

I respect Samadi's background and the genuine problem Alt-X addresses. But I invest in companies built on secrets, and I see no secret here. The market is forming a Red Ocean in real-time, the technology is commoditized at the foundation-model layer, and the pricing signals competitive positioning rather than monopoly power. This is the kind of company where even success -- modest adoption among mid-market family offices -- produces returns that are merely linear, not exponential. The power law demands that every investment have the potential to return the fund. A $30/month SaaS tool competing against five funded alternatives in a market that Microsoft itself is entering does not clear that threshold.
