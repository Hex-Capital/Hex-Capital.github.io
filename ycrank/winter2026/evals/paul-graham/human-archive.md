# Human Archive -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Human Archive is the split personality of its schlep. Four college dropouts physically relocated to India to deploy custom sensor hardware in homes and factories, hire videographers, and build annotation pipelines from scratch. That's a real schlep -- the kind of thing that makes comfortable people uncomfortable. You don't move to Ahmedabad to collect robotics data unless you're willing to do genuinely hard work. But here's the problem: this is an operational schlep, not a structural one. When Stripe waded into banking relationships and fraud prevention, they were navigating regulatory complexity that scales with the difficulty of the domain itself. The harder Stripe pushed, the wider the moat became, because nobody else wanted to deal with banks. Deploying cameras and hiring videographers in India is hard, but it's logistically hard, not structurally hard. Scale AI entered this exact space in September 2025 with contractor-based collection, and Scale has $2B in revenue and existing relationships with the very labs Human Archive wants to sell to. An operational schlep protects you until someone with more money decides to operationalize too.

The origin of this idea worries me more than the competition does. How did these founders find this problem? Samay Maini's research is in document image denoising and augmentation -- real ML work, but not robotics. Rushil Agarwal was doing product at Coinbase. Shloke Patel's Stanford mechanical engineering background is adjacent but his publication is at CHI, which is human-computer interaction, not robotic manipulation. Raj Patel's background includes farming. None of them appear to have been building robots and smashing into the wall of insufficient training data. The more natural reading is that they watched Physical Intelligence raise $1.1B and Generalist AI get backed by Nvidia, reasoned backward to "these companies need data," and built a plan around that market observation. That's exactly the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" as the failure mode -- sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than stumbling into one through lived experience. Market-backwards reasoning can work, but it produces companies that are one well-funded competitor away from irrelevance.

The absence of any customer signal is conspicuous even at pre-seed. This is a B2B data business selling to maybe a dozen identifiable frontier labs. At this stage, I'd expect at least one named pilot, one LOI, one lab saying "we're using their data and it's different from what we can collect ourselves." There's nothing. And the customer base has a structural problem: Generalist AI already collects 10,000 hours of manipulation data per week internally. Physical Intelligence, Figure AI, Tesla -- these companies view proprietary data as a competitive advantage, not something they outsource. The addressable market isn't "all robotics companies." It's "robotics companies that don't want to collect their own data." That's a smaller and less stable set than it first appears.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. The timing is genuinely excellent -- massive capital is flooding into robotics foundation models right now, and the gap between what these models need and what's available is enormous. If Human Archive's custom hardware produces qualitatively different data -- aligned multimodal streams (egocentric video, force-feedback, teleoperation) that you genuinely can't get from a contractor with a GoPro -- then they have something Scale AI can't easily replicate. Shloke's mechanical engineering background and Samay's ML/CV expertise could combine to produce novel capture hardware that constitutes a real technical moat. And the willingness of the entire team to drop out and relocate to Asia is the kind of commitment signal I take seriously. If I could see the hardware, understand what makes it special, and hear from one customer who says "this data is categorically different," my assessment would shift significantly. The robotics data problem IS hiding in plain sight. But hiding in plain sight requires that the people who find it found it because it was blocking something they were already doing -- not because they went looking for underserved markets.

The team is young and technically mixed. Samay's open-source contributions to Augraphy and his published research show real ML craft, but this isn't the same as deep robotics expertise. Four co-founders for what is fundamentally a data collection operation feels heavy, and only one has demonstrable technical depth in the relevant domain. The Berkeley connections suggest the co-founder relationships are genuine, which matters, but the team composition looks more like "smart students who want to start a company together" than "people who can't stop working on this specific problem." I've seen the former succeed, but it requires the idea to be strong enough to carry them through the learning curve, and here the idea has significant competitive and market-structure headwinds.
