# August -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Three people. $800K ARR. Three months. Deployed at a $170B+ bank. These numbers are doing real work, and they're the reason August crossed my desk rather than ending up in the pile of "AI for banking" pitches I never open. The team-to-value ratio here — roughly $267K ARR per person per quarter — belongs in the same conversation as Cursor and Bolt.new, companies I cite when explaining why the efficient size of a firm is collapsing toward zero. Banks don't adopt software in three months. They run twelve-month procurement cycles, six-month pilots, and eighteen months of compliance review. For August to compress that timeline, the product had to be solving a problem so acute that the normal institutional immune response was overridden. That compression IS the signal.

Where August creates tension with my framework is specific knowledge. Bar Ittah built fraud prevention infrastructure at Xperiti before it was acquired by Ipsos, and scaled AI at OnFire AI. Tom Tankilevitch architected ML systems in Unit 8200, built backend at Iguazio before McKinsey acquired it, then led a twenty-person engineering group at Port during its growth to an $800M valuation. Both founders have deep AI systems engineering expertise and two exits between them. What they don't have, at least visibly, is years of obsessive engagement with commercial banking operations — the kind of domain immersion that makes you dream in credit memos and wake up thinking about Basel III risk-weight calculations. Their specific knowledge is in building AI systems for high-stakes, data-dense environments. Unit 8200 trains exactly this: processing unstructured intelligence data and making consequential decisions under uncertainty. That's structurally analogous to banking underwriting. But it's adjacent knowledge, not identical knowledge. If Bar and Tom disappeared tomorrow, you could hire another strong 8200-trained ML team and they'd have a plausible shot at rebuilding August's core capabilities. That's the test of specific knowledge, and August doesn't fully pass it.

The leverage audit is more encouraging. August replaces human labor in underwriting, servicing, and risk with AI-automated workflows — a direct substitution of code leverage for the worst form of leverage (managing people). nCino, their public-market competitor at $500M+ revenue, is built on Salesforce, which constrains its AI architecture to someone else's platform limitations. Temenos and FIS earn professional services revenue from the manual complexity August eliminates. This is the incumbent principal-agent problem playing out in banking infrastructure: the agents who profit from operational complexity cannot build the tool that destroys it. August, as a principal with no legacy services revenue, can move where the agents structurally cannot. The "proprietary context layer" — where usage compounds bank-specific knowledge — is the right architecture for a data flywheel. Every credit workflow processed, every risk decision automated, makes August harder to replace. Compound interest applied to institutional knowledge. Every bank deployment becomes a moat that didn't exist before deployment.

The principal authenticity question is the gap I can't close from this dossier. Both founders left well-compensated positions to build August — that's principal behavior, not agent behavior. But I see no evidence that Bar or Tom were already working on banking automation before deciding to start a company. No side projects in fintech, no blog posts about banking inefficiency, no personal history with the problem. The strongest principal signal would be a founder who was automating banking workflows for fun before anyone would pay them for it. August's founders look more like exceptional operators who identified a massive, structurally underserved market and attacked it with their AI engineering arsenal. That can work — Travis Kalanick wasn't a lifelong transportation obsessive — but it's a weaker signal than Srinivas at Perplexity, who literally couldn't stop building AI search.

The access democratization angle is August's weakest dimension under my framework. August makes existing banks more efficient. It doesn't give new populations access to credit or capital that was previously gatekept. Compare this to AngelList, which opened startup investing to anyone, or CoinList, which removed securities lawyers from token launches. August is an enterprise efficiency play, not a democratization platform. One could construct a second-order argument — cheaper underwriting enables banks to profitably serve smaller commercial borrowers — but that's inference, not product thesis.

The risk that would flip me: single-customer concentration. If August's $800K ARR comes substantially from one $170B+ bank, this is a pilot contract masquerading as product-market fit. Enterprise banking pilots can be lucrative and short-lived. I'd want to see two things before increasing conviction: a second named bank deployment, and evidence that the proprietary context layer is producing measurable performance improvement over time. The bull case is that this traction, achieved by three people in three months in the most procurement-hostile industry on earth, reveals execution capability and product quality that the dossier's resume facts alone cannot capture. If August signs two more banks in the next two quarters, the specific knowledge question becomes academic — the product itself becomes the specific knowledge.

The two dimensions that determine my score: Team-to-Value Leverage, where August is exceptional ($800K ARR, three people, three months), and Specific Knowledge Productization, where the founders' AI engineering depth is strong but banking domain immersion is unproven. The traction pulls August across the Invest threshold; the specific knowledge gap prevents it from reaching high conviction. I'd write a small check and watch the next two quarters.
