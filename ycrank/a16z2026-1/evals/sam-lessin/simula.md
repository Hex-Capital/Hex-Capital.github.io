# Simula -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Koah Labs has $26M. ZeroClick has $55M and a founder who sold Honey to PayPal for $4 billion. OpenAI launched its own ad product in February and hit $100M+ ARR in under two months. Google announced ads coming to Gemini this year. And Simula is showing up to this party with a pre-seed check, five people, and an SDK. This is not a contrarian bet — this is the single most consensus category in venture right now. Every sophisticated investor I talk to is looking at "how do AI apps monetize through advertising." The risk isn't in the price. The premium is in the price. When I backed Solana at four cents, every crypto person had written off alt-L1s. When I look at AI-native advertising in April 2026, every VC in San Francisco is circling it with a checkbook. That's the opposite of mispricing — that's a crowded auction where the upside has already been bid away.

Simula's pitch — interactive ad formats like "AI Brand Ambassadors" and "Sponsored Games" instead of static display — is not a new primitive. It's a format innovation within the existing advertising paradigm. AdMob did this for mobile. Unity Ads did this for gaming. Rewarded video and playable ads are a decade old. Wrapping them in AI-native packaging and placing them after chatbot responses is adaptation, not creation. Nobody is going to say "Venmo me" about Simula. Contrast this with what I look for: Venmo created peer-to-peer payments as a behavioral category. Patreon created direct creator monetization before "creator economy" existed. Simula is building a better ad unit for a new surface. That's execution, not category creation.

The structural durability problem here is severe and specific. Simula is a pure software SDK — no physical assets, no protocol-level lock-in, no proprietary data flywheel that's active today. The dossier explicitly notes "no patents, open-source repos, or proprietary model references found." This is the exact pattern I've been warning about on CNBC: software companies discovering their moats aren't moats. But it's worse than typical SaaS erosion because the platforms themselves are the threat. OpenAI controls the chat surface. Google controls Gemini. When the platform decides to monetize — and OpenAI already has — why would a publisher choose a third-party SDK over the platform's native ad infrastructure? This is my scooter company thesis applied to ad tech: I don't think the AI platforms are going to let independent ad networks capture meaningful margin. The platform always wins on its own surface.

Yizhen Zhen has a strong resume — Dartmouth '24, HBS, Google engineering, Bain Capital PE — but that's a smart person with a sensible idea, not a sane person with an insane idea. Building an ad SDK for AI apps is exactly what a smart HBS student with a Google background would build. It's rational and consensus-validated. The co-founder relationship flag matters too: Yizhen (Dartmouth/HBS) and Logan Nicholson (BYU/Talsey AI) have no shared history that I can identify. My best returns — Venmo with Kortina and Magdon-Ismail, Solana with Raj — come from founders with deep pre-existing relationships forged across multiple ventures. Matched co-founders correlate with synthetic commitment, and in a category this competitive, you need missionary-level cohesion to survive.

The bull case would be that AI app distribution fragments like mobile did, creating thousands of long-tail publishers who need independent monetization infrastructure — and that Simula's interactive formats deliver genuinely higher eCPMs ($15+ per DAU annually, per their claim) that lock publishers in. If the AI app ecosystem looks more like the Android app ecosystem than the ChatGPT-dominated landscape, there's a real ad network to build. And the Aryel acquisition by OneTag in March 2026 suggests consolidation is coming, which could favor a well-positioned independent player. But I don't see evidence that Simula is that player. The "5M+ DAUs" claim is qualified as "integrating into" apps, not "actively serving ads in" apps — a distinction that matters enormously — and no independent source validates any of these numbers. ZeroClick's Ryan Hudson has the credibility and capital to win the platform deals that matter. Koah already has 2M MAUs and 35M+ ad impressions. Simula is bringing format innovation to a distribution war, and distribution wars are won with capital and relationships, not creative ad units.

Simula fails my framework on the two dimensions that matter most to me: consensus mispricing and primitive creation. The AI advertising category is priced to perfection by the market, with $80M+ already deployed to direct competitors and the platforms themselves entering. The product is a format adaptation, not a behavioral primitive. The founders are talented but first-time, building in the most competitive corner of the AI ecosystem, against opponents with 50-100x more capital and, in ZeroClick's case, a founder with a $4B exit. Pass.
