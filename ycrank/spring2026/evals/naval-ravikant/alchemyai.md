# Alchemy -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what's missing. Neither founder has spent time inside a microscopy lab building analysis pipelines. Baek studied biomedical engineering and did EP mapping at Boston Scientific -- that's cardiac electrophysiology, not bioimage analysis. McBride is a strong engineer out of UIUC with a patent at 19, which signals raw technical talent. But the specific knowledge test asks: what does this team know about how researchers actually interact with microscopy data that couldn't be replicated by any smart biomedical engineer paired with any strong ML engineer in six months? The dossier answers this directly -- "neither founder has documented prior employment in bioimage analysis, computational biology software, or research microscopy." Adjacent knowledge is not specific knowledge. The person who has spent three years cursing at CellProfiler scripts at 2am, who understands why certain segmentation approaches fail on confocal versus widefield images, who knows which labs will pay and which will never leave free tools -- that person has non-teachable insight. These founders have credentials in the neighborhood, not in the house.

The leverage question is where I'd normally get interested in a software platform, but this one concerns me. It's an application-layer tool, not infrastructure. Compare it to my investment in the other Alchemy -- the blockchain developer platform -- which sits underneath every Web3 application. That's a picks-and-shovels play where one API layer serves thousands of applications. This Alchemy is a vertical SaaS tool that sits on top of open-source models like Cellpose and StarDist and wraps them in an agentic interface. The underlying models are free. The pipeline assembly pattern -- "describe what you want in plain language, AI builds it" -- is the default architecture for every vertical AI company in 2026. There's software leverage in the sense that the platform serves many labs from one codebase, but there's no protocol-level or marketplace-level leverage. No network effect where each new lab makes the product fundamentally better for every other lab. Each research group has idiosyncratic imaging modalities, specimen types, and analysis requirements. The question is whether this scales like Notion -- where every user-created template enriches the ecosystem -- or like a consultancy with an AI front end.

The timing is the opposite of contrarian. "AI agents for professional workflows" is the most consensus thesis in venture capital right now. The specific vertical -- bioimage analysis -- is less crowded than AI for code or AI for sales, which is mildly favorable. But the structural approach is indistinguishable from hundreds of other YC companies applying LLMs to domain-specific workflows. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I invested in Bitcoin, it was considered worthless. Here, the macro thesis -- that foundation models will transform scientific workflows -- is something every investor already believes. The money is made by being non-consensus and right. Being consensus and right still works, but the pricing reflects the optimism, and the competition reflects the opportunity.

The strongest bull case: the market gap is real. Open-source tools require scripting expertise that most bench scientists lack. Enterprise platforms like Visiopharm target pathology departments with six-figure contracts, leaving individual research labs underserved. If Alchemy nails the researcher UX and builds a validated node library that becomes the standard, switching costs from embedded pipelines in publications and SOPs could be meaningful. McBride's early patent and engineering depth suggest genuine building capability. And if they accumulate enough image-model performance data across diverse imaging modalities, that dataset becomes a compounding asset. The path exists -- it's the "Stack Overflow for bioimage analysis" thesis, where curated domain knowledge compounds into a moat. But that path requires deep understanding of researcher workflows that I don't see evidence these founders possess yet. And it requires winning against free alternatives in a market -- academic research -- that is notoriously resistant to paying for software when grant budgets are tight.

The name collision with Alchemy (the blockchain platform, $345M+ raised) is a tactical nuisance, not a strategic concern -- but it does signal something about the founding team's market awareness. When you're building a company, you're also building a brand. Choosing a name already occupied by a well-funded, well-known company in tech suggests either insufficient research or insufficient concern about discoverability. Minor, but it's the kind of detail that makes me wonder about execution rigor.

I'd pass. The founders are smart and the market is real, but the specific knowledge gap is significant. Adjacent biomedical training plus strong ML engineering is a team that could have been assembled by anyone scanning the opportunity landscape -- it's not the kind of founder-problem fit where the company couldn't exist without these specific people. The product architecture is application-layer, not infrastructure. The timing is consensus, not contrarian. And the technology, built atop freely available open-source models, doesn't compound in a way that creates durable defensibility. This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a real market -- and that description applies to a thousand companies. I need to see something that can't be taught.
