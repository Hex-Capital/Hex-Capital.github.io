# Ventura -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Ventura: this is one of the most consensus-shaped deals I've seen in a while. AI agents for enterprise workflow automation is the hottest category in tech right now. Every fund deck I see references it. Every YC batch has multiple companies doing it. There's a direct competitor -- Distro -- in a recent YC batch targeting the exact same customer. There are well-funded companies like Parspec with $31.5M raised and 288 customers, Go Autonomous with $13.4M and enterprise logos like Grundfos and Saint-Gobain. When I see a space that crowded and that obviously appealing, I know the weirdness has already been priced out. The most promising companies are not popular. Ventura is building something that every investor in Silicon Valley would nod along to -- "yes, industrial distribution needs AI automation, big TAM, makes sense." That nodding is exactly the signal that makes me nervous.

When I try to understand the founders' stories -- the personal thread that connects them to this specific problem -- I find credentials where I'm looking for conviction. Jack Collins has an exceptional technical resume: Imperial College ML degree, nine years of production systems, an open-source LLM framework with 2,400 GitHub stars, and he was the founding engineer at Develop Health which hit $7M ARR in fourteen months with five people. That's genuinely impressive execution. Swen Koller went to Harvard for his MS and MBA, consulted at BCG, and co-founded bravo, a B2B SaaS for food wholesalers in Switzerland that was acquired by Sensational AG. He's lived in eight countries across four continents. These are accomplished people. But when I listen for the biographical thread -- the hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this particular problem -- I hear market analysis, not personal urgency. Koller saw the wholesale digitization opportunity professionally through bravo. Collins saw the AI engineering opportunity through his ML career. That's how consultants and engineers find problems. It's not how the founders I back find them. I backed Travis Kalanick because years of conversations with my husband about the taxi medallion racket had taught me that someone with his gravitas and fury at the system could actually break it open. I need to feel that the founder would pursue this problem even if no one gave them money.

The bull case is real, and I want to give it its full weight. Swen Koller is a repeat founder who literally built and exited a company solving the same fundamental problem -- digitizing ordering workflows for wholesalers -- just in a different vertical. He's done the customer discovery, he's made the sales calls, he knows the buyer psychology of conservative wholesale distributors. And now he has a technical co-founder whose AI engineering credentials are among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed: Collins didn't just study machine learning, he built an open-source framework that thousands of developers use and was the primary engineer behind a company that scaled to $7M ARR absurdly fast. The timing argument is sound -- LLMs finally make unstructured document parsing reliable enough for commercial use, and ERP vendors have opened APIs that were locked down five years ago. If I were evaluating this purely on founder competence, market timing, and domain expertise, I'd be much more interested. For an investor who scores on execution probability in known markets, this is probably a strong bet.

But that's not how I invest. I'm looking for the founder who's been told no fifty times and kept going because they couldn't stop themselves. I'm looking for the idea that makes other investors uncomfortable, the company that's hard to assess because it doesn't fit into any existing category. Ventura fits neatly into the "AI agents for enterprise" category -- it's not hard to assess at all. The industrial distribution angle adds vertical specificity, and the nine ERP integrations represent real engineering effort, but the core approach is well-understood and replicable. The dossier itself notes that "a well-funded competitor with distribution expertise could replicate the approach within 12-18 months." When the moat relies primarily on accumulating customer data and maintaining integrations rather than on something structurally defensible -- a regulatory barrier, a network effect, a technological insight others can't see -- I worry about what happens when a company with $30M shows up at the same customer's door.

I also notice that Ventura doesn't create economic access in the way that matters to me. It makes existing sales reps more efficient -- that's valuable to the distributor's bottom line, but it's not creating income for someone who didn't have it, or making an opaque system transparent to people who were excluded from it. When I backed Uber, the economic access story was about drivers. When I backed Affirm, it was about showing people the true cost of credit. Ventura saves mid-market distributors money on back-office labor. That's a fine business outcome, but it doesn't give me the feeling that this company is going to change who gets to participate in the economy.

On the co-founder dynamic, I don't have enough information to judge. Both founders share a connection to Imperial College London, and their skills are genuinely complementary -- deep AI engineering plus B2B wholesale sales experience. But I haven't heard how they fight, how they've survived a crisis together, or whether their commitment to each other has been tested under real pressure. For a two-person team about to take on well-funded competitors in a consensus market, that resilience will be tested soon.
