# Corvera -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Christopher Kong spent six years scaling a tempeh brand to 5,000 retail locations across six countries. That's not the kind of knowledge you acquire from a consulting engagement or a market research report — it's knowledge earned through thousands of purchase orders, fulfillment mistakes, and stockout crises. When someone tells me they lived inside the problem, I check whether the scars match the thesis. Kong's scars match. He knows exactly which spreadsheet breaks at 500 SKUs, which 3PL partner drops the ball during a retailer promotion, and how demand signals distort when you expand from one country to six. Breeuwer's Google AI background — building multi-agent workflows that improved compliance review efficiency by 90% — maps to the technical architecture. This is a team where the CEO knows which workflows to automate and the CTO knows how to build the agents. The specific knowledge is real, but it's operational knowledge about running a CPG brand, not architectural knowledge about building a compounding software platform. Those are adjacent but distinct skills.

The leverage question is where my conviction drops. Corvera replaces an ops team with AI agents — one person with Corvera does what previously required three hires processing orders and managing inventory. That's labor substitution. It's real value, but it's not new leverage. Compare it to what Replit did for coding: Replit didn't automate an existing developer's workflow — it gave entirely new populations the ability to write software who never could before. Corvera takes existing CPG operations and makes them faster. The difference between "faster" and "new capability" is the difference between a good business and a leverage-creating one. Standard SaaS economics — one codebase serving many customers — gives Corvera baseline software leverage, but nothing structurally distinct from any other vertical SaaS play.

The timing concern is significant. "AI agents for X" is the most consensus pitch in technology right now. CPG AI adoption jumped from 42% to 71% in a single year. Every vertical SaaS company is adding an "agentic" layer. Crisp just announced an "AI Agent Studio" backed by $127M+ in funding. Unioncrate has been in CPG supply chain AI since 2016 with Founders Fund behind them. Settle has $506M raised and sits adjacent. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal — the consensus was wrong, and that's where the asymmetric upside lived. When I invested in Bitcoin, it was nerd money that serious people dismissed. Here, serious people agree: CPG operations need AI automation. That means the pricing already reflects the optimism, and the competitive dynamics are a race rather than a moat.

The bull case I want to genuinely engage with: Kong's lived experience creates a product that actually works because it's built by someone who has processed thousands of orders himself, not by engineers who interviewed CPG operators for three months. The 15 design partners and 50+ pipeline brands suggest real pull — CPG founders recognize that Kong understands their pain because he's been there. If Corvera embeds deeply into the order-to-cash workflow of hundreds of brands, switching costs become real — you don't rip out the system that processes your orders and manages your inventory lightly. And the mid-market CPG segment ($5M-$500M revenue) is genuinely underserved by enterprise ERP systems that cost more to implement than these brands spend on operations annually. If Corvera wins this wedge, the data accumulated across diverse CPG supply chains could improve forecasting accuracy in ways that a new entrant couldn't replicate without years of operational exposure. For this to work, execution has to be exceptional and the product has to earn trust fast — one wrong order quantity and a brand's retailer relationship is damaged.

But the core technology — LLM-based document parsing, entity matching, inventory tracking, demand forecasting — is built on generally available capabilities. Every model improvement from OpenAI or Anthropic benefits Corvera and every competitor equally. The workflows being automated — order processing, inventory management, purchase orders — are well-understood problems with decades of existing software solutions. What Corvera is really selling is the integration of AI across these workflows in a CPG-specific context. That's valuable but it's a feature, not a platform. And features get absorbed by platforms with distribution advantages. The rebrand from "Sarah AI" suggests some identity searching, though that's a minor signal. More concerning is the geographic stretch — UK-based customer logos, SF headquarters, a four-person team serving two markets. At this stage, focus is survival.

This is a competent team building a real product for a real pain point. Kong's specific knowledge is genuine, and I'd be excited to meet the founders. But my framework requires more than founder-problem fit — it requires leverage creation, contrarian timing, and technology that compounds. Corvera automates known operations with commodity AI in a hot market. It's closer to "Salesforce for CPG ops" than to a new category of capability. Good business, possibly. My kind of bet, no.
