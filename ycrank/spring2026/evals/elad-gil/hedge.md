# Hedge -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Hedge is a wholesale insurance brokerage with AI bolted onto the intake layer, and that single sentence captures both why it's executable and why it doesn't fit my book. The dimensional signature: Hedge scores highest on single-miracle viability -- Luke Button has done this once already at Fernstone (YC F25), the LLM-document-ingestion miracle is happening exogenously, and the $90.3B US surplus-lines pool grew 7.8% YoY through year-end 2025 -- and lowest on infrastructure-layer positioning, because Hedge Specialty dba Taven Insurance Services LLC, CA license #6018729, is a licensed placement shop earning 10-15% commissions, not a layer that other businesses build on top of.

On infrastructure-layer positioning, my ladder runs from Stripe (the payments API every internet business compounds switching costs into) at the top, through Gusto in the middle (embedded workflow but more application than platform), down to Wish at the bottom (pure marketplace, no API, no B2B leverage, collapsed 99% post-IPO). Hedge sits closer to the Wish rung than the Gusto rung -- not because it will fail like Wish, but because the structural shape is the same: Hedge takes a cut of someone else's transaction (the carrier's premium) without becoming the substrate that other businesses depend on. The 140+ retail agencies in 36 states are customers of a brokerage service, not developers integrating an API. Federato, Sixfold, and Cytora -- the three named AI-underwriting competitors in the dossier -- are actually closer to the infrastructure layer than Hedge is, because they sell software into carriers and MGAs. Hedge has explicitly chosen the brokerage seat over the platform seat. That's a real business; it's not the layer I write checks into.

On single-miracle viability, my high rung is Harvey -- one miracle, foundation-model capability, happening exogenously, with proven legal-tech buyers waiting on the other side. My low rung is the multi-miracle profile I usually pass on: tech + regulation + distribution simultaneously. My exception rung is Anduril, where I overrode the framework because the market structure signal was generational. Hedge sits comfortably between Harvey and the multi-miracle floor: the document-ingestion miracle is off-the-shelf, the 30+ carrier appointments are claimed already, CA licensure is in place, and retail-agent distribution is the standard wholesale GTM that CRC and Amwins have been running for decades. Hedge needs execution across several fronts -- state-by-state licensure expansion, carrier-appointment retention, retail-agent acquisition -- but none of these are miracles. They're a checklist. That's good for survival probability and bad for venture math, because executable checklists tend not to compound into 100x outcomes.

Nonobvious Market Timing places Hedge between Harvey (mid-rung -- real capability threshold crossed) and the consumer-social pass (low rung -- no exogenous shift). The ingestion miracle is real -- Sixfold founded 2023, the AI exclusion wave forcing emerging risks into E&S -- but Hedge's window is not nonobvious anymore. Sixfold, Cytora, and Federato are already in market; Newfront has scaled the modern-brokerage thesis at retail; the question of "AI-native specialty insurance" is closer to consensus than to Coinbase-in-2012. Operator-Scaler Founder Profile places Hedge between Parker Conrad/Rippling (high -- rebuilding after Zenefits with deep operator scars) and a generic first-time team (low). Luke Button is second-time in the exact vertical and Luke Rosa shipped AI agents over $20MM in revenue at Traba, which is the right resume; but the Fernstone (YC F25) overlap is unresolved in public sources, and that ambiguity is the kind of thing Parker Conrad would have closed before raising. Margin Architecture sits between Gusto (mid -- sticky payroll, negative churn) and Wish (low -- commission compression in a soft cycle). Commission revenue at 10-15% of premium with AM Best revising the E&S outlook from positive to stable in November 2025 and rate reductions expected through Q1-Q2 2026 means Hedge is rowing into a softening market. That's not Abridge billing per encounter; it's a brokerage taking its cut of a number that's compressing.

My 36-month forecast: Hedge reaches $4-12M in commission revenue and gets acquired by a mid-tier wholesaler -- Risk Strategies, Worldwide Facilities, or a CRC bolt-on -- at a 2-3x revenue multiple, because wholesale brokerage is a category where consolidators buy distribution and AI tooling and the math doesn't support holding for venture-scale exit. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature itself: high single-miracle viability + low infrastructure positioning equals "good operating business that exits to a strategic," not "compounding category-defining platform."

The bull case stings: Luke Button is the Parker Conrad analog -- second-time founder in the same vertical, which my own Unstated Patterns flag as a high-conviction profile, and Fernstone may be exactly the failure that taught him to attack the wholesale layer rather than the retail layer. Surplus lines has grown double-digit for seven years (Carrier Management, September 2025), the AI exclusion wave is forcing new risks into E&S that incumbent wholesalers' legacy ACORD pipelines can't absorb, and a 3x claimed hit ratio with 30-minute median first response is the kind of operational gap CRC and Amwins genuinely cannot close without cannibalizing their FTE-heavy underwriting teams -- exactly the "boring but critical" pattern I've ridden with Gusto and Checkr. The counter that wins: Hedge is structurally a commission shop on someone else's premium pool, and even the largest wholesalers (CRC, RT Specialty, Amwins) are not venture-scale outcomes for early equity holders -- they're PE-rollup outcomes. Adding AI to the intake doesn't change the unit economics of taking 10-15% of a softening premium base. The condition that flips my verdict: if Hedge transitions from pure brokerage to a delegated-authority MGA with proprietary loss-ratio data feeding a pricing model that carriers cede underwriting authority to -- that is the move from brokerage seat to infrastructure seat, and it's the only path I see to a venture-scale outcome here.
