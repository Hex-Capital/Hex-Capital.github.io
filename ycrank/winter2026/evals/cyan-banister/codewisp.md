# CodeWisp -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out about CodeWisp is that it's building in the exact kind of space I tend to avoid -- a hot, consensus category with well-funded competitors already generating revenue. AI game creation is trending. There's a "Vibe Coding Game Jam" with a thousand submissions. Rosebud AI has $9.2 million raised and $2.7 million in revenue. Replit has over $222 million in funding and already ships a game builder. When I look at a deal and every VC in the ecosystem is nodding along because the category is obviously exciting, the weird signal I depend on is already gone. The most promising companies are not popular -- and AI game generators are very popular right now.

Elvin Fu clearly knows this domain. Making games since age 10, building two engines from scratch, teaching 20 million students game development, landing in the top 20 most followed users on Scratch out of 135 million -- that's genuine, deep expertise accumulated over more than a decade. His YouTube channel with 84,000 subscribers and 21.7 million views represents a distribution asset that's hard to replicate. I respect the craftsmanship and the persistence it takes to build that kind of audience. But when I ask my foundational question -- "tell me your story, not the business" -- what I'm listening for is the specific hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this problem. CS at University of Washington, work at AWS, a successful YouTube education business -- this is the biography of a competent, talented builder, not an outsider who's been told "no" fifty times and kept going. The biographical thread connecting founder to problem here is passion and expertise, not survival. That matters to me because when the inevitable crisis comes -- and in a category this competitive, it will come fast -- passion alone doesn't always sustain the way that a deeper, more personal stake does.

The bull case deserves real engagement. If Elvin can convert even a fraction of his 20 million student reach into platform users, CodeWisp could establish itself as the default on-ramp from Scratch-style block coding to real game development, powered by AI. That pipeline -- from educational toy to creative tool to publishing platform -- is genuinely clever, and nobody else in the competitive landscape has the founder audience to seed it organically. The 1,513 published games represent early community formation, and community network effects in creator platforms can compound powerfully once they cross a threshold. If CodeWisp becomes for AI-native game creation what Roblox became for user-generated gaming -- a place where kids grow up making and sharing games -- the outcome could be massive. That's the dream. But Roblox had years of head start with no real competitors; CodeWisp is entering a space where Rosebud already has revenue and Replit has hundreds of millions in capital. The underlying LLM technology is commodity, and the game-specific scaffolding on top of it is replicable by any well-resourced team.

On economic access, there's a version of this story where democratizing game creation empowers kids without access to expensive tools or formal CS education -- and that resonates with my own history of learning to code at public libraries. But the impact pathway is indirect. This isn't creating income for an underserved population the way Uber did for drivers or Affirm did for people shut out of transparent credit. It's making a creative tool more accessible, which is good, but it doesn't hit the specific nerve of economic empowerment that draws me into a deal. There's also no regulatory complexity here, no stigmatized category that creates a natural moat -- this is wide-open consumer software where the biggest players in tech could ship a competing feature in a quarter.

The team structure gives me pause. The YC page lists only Elvin as founder with a team of three, but the other two members are invisible. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder dynamics can kill even the best-timed products, I want to understand who's in the room and how they work together under pressure. A solo founder avoids the co-founder conflict problem, but building in this competitive a space with unidentified teammates and no visible revenue model feels precarious. I'd need to sit down with Elvin, hear his full story -- the parts that aren't on his LinkedIn -- and understand what drives him at a level deeper than "I love making games." But based on what's in front of me, this is a talented builder in a crowded, consensus category, and that's not where my best investments come from.
