# Lumius -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Junjie Yao has 11,000 citations and an OPTICA Fellowship, and he's spinning a device out of Duke that makes real-time 3D ultrasound portable and cheap. That's the headline. And the first question I ask isn't "is this good?" — of course affordable 3D volumetric imaging is good. The question is: has the market already priced this in? It hasn't. Butterfly Network raised $353 million and Exo raised $428 million, and both of them are building 2D probes with AI stacked on top. The entire portable ultrasound category has organized itself around the thesis that "2D plus machine learning equals good enough." Lumius is betting that thesis is wrong — that you actually need volumetric data, not algorithmic guesses from flat slices, and that 50% first-attempt failure rates on vascular access prove the point. Every dollar that's gone into 2D POCUS is implicitly a bet against Lumius. That's not extreme mispricing — it's not Solana at four cents — but it's a real gap between where capital has concentrated and where the physics actually points.

Lumius maps cleanly to the Metropolis pattern in my portfolio — the physical-world moat thesis. Metropolis was AI-powered parking infrastructure, seeded early, now valued around $5 billion, because the physical parking assets can't be replicated by software alone. Lumius's 3D diffractive acoustic tomography requires a specific hardware configuration — a single-slit acoustic diffraction grating on a linear-array transducer — that was invented in Yao's lab at Duke by Luca Menozzi, the CTO. You cannot replicate this by fine-tuning an LLM. You cannot add it as a feature to Butterfly's chip-on-a-chip 2D probe. GE and Philips sell cart-based 3D systems at $50K-$200K+, and building a portable affordable version would cannibalize their own margins — textbook innovator's dilemma. Lumius passes the AI kill test more cleanly than almost anything I see from a YC batch. The moat is in the physics, not the software.

Portable real-time 3D volumetric imaging at point-of-care is a genuine new primitive — not "better 2D," but a fundamentally different capability. When 50% of central line placements fail on the first attempt because clinicians are mentally reconstructing 3D anatomy from 2D slices, Lumius isn't offering an incremental improvement. It's offering a new way to see. That's closer to how I think about Venmo creating peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavioral category — not a better bank transfer, but a new verb. I won't overstate it: 3D ultrasound as a concept exists in radiology suites. But portable, affordable, real-time 3D at the bedside for a nurse doing a central line? That's a new primitive. It creates clinical workflows that literally cannot happen today. The POCUS market is projected to grow from $1.5 billion to $5.56 billion by 2034, and Lumius is the only company I can find attacking it with native volumetric imaging rather than AI overlays on flat data.

Tri Vu (CEO), Luca Menozzi (CTO), and Chenhang Li (COO/CFO) are all Duke doctoral researchers — deeply rational, deeply credentialed, steeped in the exact physics required. On the "sane" side of my sane-person-insane-idea ratio, they score about as high as possible: an NSF Fellow, a research assistant professor, and they're advised by a lab PI with 11,000 citations and an NSF CAREER award. But here's where Lumius loses points for me: the idea isn't insane enough. Real-time 3D portable ultrasound is obviously desirable. Every clinician who's fumbled a central line placement knows they want volumetric imaging. The "insanity" is in the execution challenge — building a medical device startup against Butterfly and Exo with $780 million combined — not in the concept. I get most excited when reasonable people pitch me something that makes smart people say "that's impossible and also why would you want that?" Lumius makes smart people say "that would be amazing, but can you actually build it?" Different energy. Still interesting, but a different bet.

The bear case is real and specific. FDA clearance for a novel imaging modality — potentially de novo classification — means 12-24+ months before any commercial revenue. Lumius's website explicitly states the product hasn't been evaluated by the FDA. During that window, Clarius is already developing 3D volume registration using IMU data, and Exo just attracted potential Samsung investment of $100 million. Four academic founders with zero documented startup experience or exits have to navigate supply chain, manufacturing scale, regulatory affairs, and hospital procurement cycles that eat first-time founders alive. Medical devices are inherently capital-intensive — Lumius may need exactly the multi-round factory-farm fundraising model I've been saying is dead. And the $525 million 3D vascular ultrasound TAM, while real, isn't the kind of market where you get 100x outcomes without expanding the category dramatically.

Here's why I still lean invest despite all of that. The team didn't find the technology — they built it. Luca Menozzi developed 3D-DAT. Junjie Yao's lab at Duke originated the underlying science. That founder-domain lock-in is as tight as it gets: three of four co-founders have multi-year shared history in the same photoacoustic imaging program, and the fourth adds complementary hardware expertise from Duke's Ni Lab. The competitive moat isn't a business model insight or a data advantage that erodes — it's novel acoustic physics with likely patentable IP. FDA clearance, once obtained, becomes a barrier to entry rather than just a cost. And the Metropolis parallel holds: physical-world technology that software amplifies but cannot replace, seeded at pre-seed prices before the market recognizes what it is. If Lumius gets FDA clearance and demonstrates clinical equivalence or superiority in vascular access, this becomes a very different conversation. That clearance milestone is the concrete near-term evidence I'd watch — if it stalls past 2028 or if Clarius ships adequate 3D first, I'd flip to pass.

The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Structural Durability — Lumius's hardware physics moat is among the strongest I've seen in a YC batch, immune to the AI commoditization eating every SaaS company alive — and Primitive Creation, because portable real-time 3D at point-of-care enables clinical behaviors that don't currently exist. The mispricing is moderate, the founder sanity-insanity ratio favors sane over insane, and the capital discipline question is unanswerable at pre-seed for a medical device. But the physical moat is the thing. Software moats are melting. This one isn't made of software.
