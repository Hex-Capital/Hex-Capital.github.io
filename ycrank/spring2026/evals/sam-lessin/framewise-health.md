# Framewise Health -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$320 million. That's the entire CMS HRRP penalty pool across 2,273 hospitals in FY2023. Framewise Health has built its entire value proposition around helping hospitals avoid their share of that pool — "the average hospital pays $1M/year in Medicare readmission penalties," per David Cui's LinkedIn. Here's my problem: when your total addressable pain is defined by a government penalty program, you're not investing in a company, you're investing in the permanence of a regulatory structure. I explicitly avoid companies whose unit economics depend on favorable regulation that could change. CMS has already shown willingness to revise HRRP penalty calculations — a 2024 study identified $284M-$297M in potentially redistributed penalties related to Medicare Advantage penetration. Framewise Health's economic engine is a policy decision, not a market force. That's not where I put my money.

Synthesia has $146M ARR and a $4B valuation generating AI video for enterprises. VidifyAI already advertises HIPAA-compliant healthcare video with BAA and SOC 2 Type II. Epic controls the EHR ecosystem and could add personalized video education to MyChart with a product sprint. Framewise Health is doing what I see in half the YC batch — taking commoditized AI components (LLM text generation, avatar video synthesis, FHIR data extraction) and stitching them together for a vertical. The dossier's own defensibility section concedes: "The core technology uses components that are increasingly commoditized" and "defensibility rests more on clinical workflow integration and hospital sales relationships than on the underlying AI technology." When your own competitive analysis says the moat is relationships, not technology, you're telling me an incumbent with existing relationships can replicate you. Framewise Health is Adobe for AI — it's perfect for the companies that already own the hospital IT stack.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Tane Kim's clinical background — Lundquist Institute at Harbor-UCLA, Sarepta Therapeutics translational biology, medical school training — gives Framewise Health genuine domain credibility that a Synthesia product manager doesn't have. The EHR/FHIR integration creates real switching costs once deployed. Value-based pricing tied to HRRP penalty savings is a smart GTM wedge that lets Framewise Health sell ROI, not features. And the "why now" convergence — AI video quality crossing usability thresholds in 2024-2025, FHIR standardization via CMS mandate, persistent ~75% hospital penalty rates — is legitimate timing. If Framewise Health can land five major health systems before Synthesia or Epic notice, the switching costs could compound. But I checked my known exceptions: Slack got consensus pricing because it created a new behavioral primitive (real-time workplace messaging replacing email). Framewise Health is not creating a new behavior — it's a better delivery mechanism for discharge education that already exists. Mytonomy was doing video-based patient education before Elsevier acquired them in March 2026. GetWellNetwork had 10M+ annual patient interactions. Framewise Health is entering a category with well-funded incumbents, not creating one. The condition that would flip me: if Framewise Health showed me a signed multi-hospital system contract with measurable readmission reduction data from a controlled pilot, proving the videos actually change patient outcomes at scale — not just that hospitals want to buy them, but that they work — I'd reconsider. Outcome data would be a genuine moat that AI wrappers can't replicate.

David Cui and Tane Kim met at Brown, which is fine, but I don't have the signal I need on the sanity-insanity ratio. Framewise Health is a reasonable idea pitched by what appear to be reasonable people. "Let's use AI to make personalized discharge videos so hospitals avoid Medicare penalties" — there's nothing about that sentence that makes me uncomfortable, nothing that makes other investors recoil, nothing that sounds fucking crazy. Compare this to when I invested $1.7M in Marina Mogilko for 5% of her earnings for 30 years — people thought I was a lunatic. That's the asymmetry I'm looking for. Framewise Health's problem is that it's legible. Every health tech VC understands the HRRP pitch on the first slide. When every investor at demo day nods along, the upside is already in the price.

Framewise Health is tagged "SaaS, Health Tech, B2B" — three words that describe the most consensus-priced category in venture capital right now. No revenue, no named customers, no pilot data, competing against Synthesia ($536.6M raised), Health Recovery Solutions ($84.7M raised), and a recently-acquired Mytonomy ($46.48M raised). The 2-person team is lean — I give them credit for that — but enterprise hospital sales cycles involving procurement, compliance, IT security review, and clinical committee approval will burn through pre-seed capital before they close their first five deals. Framewise Health needs to raise multiple rounds to survive the sales cycle, which is the opposite of the capital discipline I look for. The two dimensions that locked this verdict: Consensus Mispricing is near-zero because health tech AI is the hottest category in venture and this specific problem (HRRP penalties) is well-understood by every hospital CFO and every health tech investor; and Structural Durability fails the AI kill test because the components are commoditized and the regulatory dependency adds a second layer of fragility. Pass.
