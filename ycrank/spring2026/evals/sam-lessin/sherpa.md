# Sherpa -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

This is the company I describe when someone asks me what I don't invest in. If I were building a teaching case for "AI application that incumbents will absorb as a feature," Sherpa would be the slide deck. The core product is: use an LLM to generate marketing copy variants, inject them via JavaScript, measure which wins. That's not a company. That's a product update Optimizely ships in Q3. Optimizely has $400M in ARR and thousands of enterprise customers who are already asking for exactly this. Mutiny has $72.6M raised, $20.7M in revenue, and already does AI-powered website personalization — adding automated A/B test generation is a Tuesday afternoon feature sprint for their engineering team. The dossier itself says the quiet part out loud: "LLM-powered variant generation is technically reproducible. Any team with access to foundation models and a JavaScript injection layer could build a comparable product." When your own competitive analysis writes your obituary, listen to it.

There is zero consensus mispricing here. AI agents automating marketing workflows is the single hottest category in venture right now. Half the startups at every demo day are some variation of "we use AI to do [marketing task] automatically." VCs are tripping over each other to fund these things. The price already reflects the upside and then some. When I seeded Solana, every smart person told me Ethereum had won and I was wasting my money — that's mispricing. When I looked at Eaze, every mainstream VC wouldn't touch cannabis — that's mispricing. "AI-powered CRO tool" makes every VC in the room nod approvingly. That's the opposite of mispricing. That's a crowded auction where you're guaranteed to overpay.

The primitive test is equally brutal. A/B testing has existed for decades. CRO software is a mature, well-defined category with clear incumbents and established buyer behavior. Sherpa isn't creating a new verb or a new behavior — it's automating an existing one. Nobody's going to say "Sherpa me that landing page." Compare this to Venmo, which created the behavior of sending money to a friend by tapping their name on your phone — that behavior literally didn't exist before. Or Patreon, which created direct creator monetization as a behavioral pattern. Sherpa is "faster A/B testing." That's an execution improvement in a known category, not a primitive. The ceiling is capped by the existing market's willingness to switch tools, which historically in CRO is slow and painful.

Let me steelman the bull case because I owe it intellectual honesty. The argument would be: Optimizely and VWO have a business-model conflict. They monetize through enterprise contracts, professional services, and the complexity of manual test setup. Fully automating the loop threatens their revenue model the same way digital cameras threatened Kodak's film processing business. If Sherpa captures the long tail of companies that can't afford Optimizely's enterprise pricing or Mutiny's sales-led motion, maybe they build a PLG wedge that scales before incumbents cannibalize themselves. The data flywheel — learning from cross-customer conversion patterns — could theoretically compound into a real moat. And the founders worked at Lindy, an AI agent company, so they've seen the agent paradigm from the inside. That's the bull case. But it falls apart on two facts: Mutiny already exists in exactly this wedge with $20.7M in revenue, and "1,047,213 website visits" is a rounding error at the scale needed for a meaningful data moat. Optimizely processes billions.

The founders are young — Kinnan graduated in 2024, Sonam is fresh from RPI and Microsoft. Sonam's Microsoft Copilot work is genuinely impressive for his career stage; shipping image generation to GA for a product that ran a Super Bowl ad is real. But the idea isn't insane. It's sensible. My framework specifically looks for sane people pitching insane ideas — the dissonance between the founder's rationality and the idea's apparent impossibility is the signal. "We'll automate A/B testing with AI" is the kind of idea that every product manager in a CRO company has already thought of. There's no cognitive dissonance, no moment where I think "that's fucking crazy but... what if they're right?" And their only named customer is Lindy — their former employer. That's not market validation, that's a going-away present. When I back someone, I want to see that strangers with no social obligation are paying them money. I don't see that here.

Pass.
