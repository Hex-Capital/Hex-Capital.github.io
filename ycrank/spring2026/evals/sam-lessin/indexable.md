# Indexable -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka is a competent ex-xAI Rust systems hacker building a KVM-fork sandbox in the exact category I have publicly down-weighted, which means Indexable's analytical signature is "real technical conviction" paired with "wrong sleeve at wrong price" — and the sleeve question dominates the conviction question every time I've tested it on the portfolio. The dossier names Modal at $466M total raised (Series C May 2026 at $4.65B), E2B at $32M and already inside half the Fortune 500, and Daytona at $24M as of February 2026 — Indexable is entering a regatta where three boats already crossed the starting line with multi-hundred-million dollar sails.

The Solana seed at $0.05/coin is the HIGH rung on my non-consensus price ladder because the entire generalist VC class had quit crypto post-ICO crash; I got to be the only serious bid in a sleeve everyone had written off. The mechanism that made Solana work — pre-consensus entry into a sleeve I had independently underwritten as fundamentally disruptive — is exactly the mechanism Indexable inverts. Indexable lands in Spring 2026 YC with Pete Koomen as partner, in the AI-agent-infrastructure category that Modal, E2B, Daytona, CodeSandbox SDK, Runloop, Northflank, and Vercel Sandbox have all converged on inside eighteen months. That is not a sleeve where I'm the only bid; that is a sleeve where my $0.05/coin equivalent already traded at Modal's $4.65B mark. The dossier doesn't even bother giving me a round price for Indexable, which is itself a tell — YC Spring 2026 AI-infra rounds are not pricing themselves at discounts to attract me, they're pricing themselves to absorb the demand stack from funds that don't share my "AI is just more internet" view.

The canonical Pass Example I wrote up — "We're building an AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $25K MRR, three months out of YC, $30M post-money seed, four tier-1 VCs already in" — fails on Criterion 2 because the cap table is a standard SaaS shape that will need five more rounds to reach default-alive against funded incumbents. Indexable has the same shape with worse arithmetic: Modal raised $355M in May 2026 specifically to expand agent infrastructure, and Indexable is hiring a Founding Engineer at $150–175K with 0.75–1.50% equity off what is presumably the YC standard $500K. The regatta tack count to a Series A here is not two or three — it is "raise enough to survive Modal cutting prices while you build feature parity." That is not a venture moment in the Slow Letter sense; that is a capital-intensity death march where the venture capital itself is the moat for the better-funded competitor, not for Indexable.

Andrew Gazelka has the missionary-systems-builder temperament — Hyperion at 1k GitHub stars is a real Minecraft engine, #1 on r/rust for 24 hours is a real signal of obsession with low-level performance, and ex-xAI plus the rumored BountyBot acquisition (unverified in the dossier) suggests he's been compounding systems craft for years. If the dimension were "can this founder ship a hypervisor primitive," I'd score Indexable in the top quartile. Over 18 months, that conviction compounds into shipped code and a sharper SDK — Andrew is not the failure risk here. But conviction compounds inside the price/sleeve frame, not around it; a missionary founder in a commoditizing category at consensus price is exactly the Robinhood-shaped exception I cannot invoke at entry, because Robinhood worked as an override only because I was already on the cap table before the consensus arrived. Indexable would be entry into consensus, not absorption of it.

For Indexable to clear Criterion 4 — founder-as-brand — Andrew would need a measurable owned channel before the round, and the dossier gives me 202 GitHub followers and an X account whose follower count couldn't be retrieved. That is not the John Fish / Bookshelved profile, where the creator's audience IS the cap table; it is a working engineer with normal developer-Twitter reach. To flip this dimension a tier, Andrew would need to be publishing opinionated essays on hypervisor design that 10K+ infra engineers read — the equivalent of what made Sublime Security's founders legible to me pre-round — and nothing in the dossier suggests that artifact exists. The absence isn't disqualifying on its own; it just removes the one mechanism that could make Indexable's distribution venture-shaped without Series-A-sized sales spend.

Sublime Security is the single anchor I'd use on Criterion 5, because Sublime was pre-consensus enterprise security at a moment when most security capital was crowding into late-stage SOC SaaS — same B2B infra archetype, opposite sleeve dynamic. Indexable is the inverse: the AI-agent sandbox category has, per the dossier's own competitor list, eight named platforms with disclosed funding, and the "why now" reasoning ("coding-agent runtimes reached production usage in 2024–2025") is precisely the consensus story Modal and Daytona used to raise their last rounds. Indexable does not live in a sleeve I'm convicted on; it lives in the sleeve I built the "AI is just more internet" line to deselect against.

My 36-month forecast: Indexable gets acqui-hired by Modal, Vercel, or E2B for sub-$30M on the strength of Andrew's KVM-fork primitive and team, with the round-one SAFE investors taking a 1–2x markup at best, before reaching $1M ARR as an independent company. The mechanism is straightforward — Modal's $466M war chest lets them either build the in-memory fork primitive in-house in 9–12 months or buy the team that built it cheaper than fighting them; Andrew's Rust/hypervisor profile makes him an extremely attractive principal-engineer hire for any of the three incumbents, which converts the technical moat into an acqui-hire premium rather than a standalone outcome.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the Aleo / Lightning Labs analogy — pre-consensus infra at seed pricing where the primitive turned out to be genuinely non-reproducible by incumbents, and where my "everyone passed, what if they're right" inversion paid off. The argument runs: in-memory VM forking with cross-tenant content-addressable dedup is harder than Modal or E2B's roadmaps suggest, Andrew's Hyperion + xAI background is exactly the rare profile that can ship it, and the agent-runtime market is large enough that even a #4 player at a real primitive captures $50M+ ARR. It stings because Andrew genuinely might be one of the twenty people on earth who can ship this. But the dossier gives me no customer logos, no revenue signal, no Discord, no benchmark, and a Crunchbase entry with no disclosed funding — and my Aleo override required a sleeve I had independently underwritten as disruptive, not just a hard technical problem in a consensus sleeve. The verifiable condition that would flip me: Indexable lands two named coding-agent runtimes (Cursor, Cognition, Replit, or equivalent) as paying customers at >$200K ARR each within twelve months, on a SAFE priced at or below YC-batch median. Absent that, this is a pass on price and sleeve, not on Andrew.
