# Laurence -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice immediately: this company is legible. A Jump Trading quant and a Google/Meta ML engineer walk into Amazon advertising and say, "this bidding problem is just stochastic optimization with noisy data — we've been solving this at nanosecond scale for years." Every VC hearing that pitch nods along. That's a clean narrative, a clear technical edge, and a big market. And that legibility is precisely what gives me pause. The most promising companies I've backed have been the ones that made people squint — SpaceX sounded delusional, Crusoe Energy sounded absurd, Contraline made investors physically uncomfortable. Laurence sounds... sensible. When something sounds this sensible to this many people, the price is usually wrong and the opportunity has already been partially arbitraged.

I want to know how Matthew and Leo got here — not the business logic, but the human story. Matthew built ML at Google and Meta, spent three months building tools for Amazon Ads agencies, and saw the gap. Leo ran billions in trading volume at Jump and played high-stakes poker. These are genuinely impressive credentials, and the intellectual transfer from quantitative trading to ad bidding is real. But I'm not hearing a story about hardship or obsession that binds these founders to THIS specific problem. Three months of domain exploration before founding tells me this was identified as a market opportunity, not felt as a personal wound. When I backed Uber, Travis had been stewing about the medallion racket for years — it was personal, almost visceral. When I ask "why you, why this?" I want to hear something that makes the hair on my arms stand up. Here I'm hearing a smart career decision by talented people, and that's a different signal.

The traction deserves respect — $10 million in managed ad spend growing from $2.5 million, a 40% decrease in ACoS, named clients with real results. For pre-seed, that's meaningful execution. Aaron Apple as an advisor is a genuine asset — someone who built and exited a top-75 Amazon seller and ran an eight-figure PPC consultancy gives them distribution credibility most seed-stage companies dream about. If I were evaluating purely on execution quality and team caliber, this would score higher. But execution quality in a crowded, consensus-attractive market is a different bet than what I typically write checks for. Pacvue is doing $50 million in revenue. Quartile manages $2 billion in ad spend. Teikametrics has raised $65 million. These are not sleepy incumbents — they're well-resourced companies in a market that's obviously attractive, which means Laurence is running into headwinds from day one rather than operating in a space others have dismissed.

The strongest bull case is that reinforcement learning genuinely outperforms rule-based optimization by a wide enough margin to create switching momentum, and that the data flywheel from managed ad spend compounds that advantage over time. If Laurence can demonstrate consistent 40% ACoS improvements at scale, brands will switch regardless of incumbent relationships, and the hourly recalculation cadence versus competitors' weekly cycles could be a real structural edge. The quant trading pedigree is not just a narrative device — it represents genuine pattern recognition about decision-making under uncertainty that most e-commerce software engineers lack. If this team executes, they could become the "Citadel of ad bidding" and expand beyond Amazon into Walmart, Instacart, and eventually all programmatic media. That's a big outcome. But it requires winning a competitive market through superior performance, not through being first to an insight nobody else sees — and winning through superior performance in a crowded market is a very different risk profile than what I'm drawn to.

What I don't see here is what I care most about: a company that creates economic access for people who don't have it, or that makes an invisible system visible to those harmed by its opacity. Laurence makes ad spending more efficient for CPG brands already selling on Amazon. That's value creation, but it's optimization for existing market participants, not liberation for underserved ones. When I think about Uber creating income for drivers, Affirm giving transparent credit to people shut out by hidden fees, or Flock Safety bringing public safety data to communities — those investments changed who could participate in an economy. Laurence changes how much profit a brand extracts from its advertising budget. Those are different categories of impact for me.

The co-founder dynamic is hard to assess — I don't see how Matthew and Leo met, how long they've worked together, or what happens when they fundamentally disagree. The rapid team expansion to six people is either a sign of strong execution or of building ahead of product-market certainty. Without more information about the relationship's foundation and stress-testing, I can't fully evaluate this dimension, and after HQ Trivia I'm not comfortable filling that gap with optimism.
