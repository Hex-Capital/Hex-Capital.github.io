# Modern Industrials -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Modern Industrials has a direct competitor -- Ventura, YC W26 -- building the identical product for the identical vertical, and Ventura's CEO has a prior exit in wholesale distribution SaaS. When two companies in adjacent accelerator cohorts converge on the same use case (AI quoting for building materials distributors), the market timing isn't nonobvious -- it's consensus. The structural shift here, LLMs crossing a capability threshold for parsing unstructured trade documents like faxes and emailed PDFs, is real. But "apply AI to document processing in an under-digitized vertical" is the single most common pitch in 2025-2026. I've seen dozens of these across construction, logistics, insurance, and legal. The specific choice of LBM distribution shows some taste -- most prestige-seeking founders wouldn't touch lumberyards -- and that aligns with a pattern I've profited from. But the nonobviousness score requires that consensus underestimate the window, and when your closest competitor launched in the same quarter with the same thesis, consensus has already priced this in.

The layer positioning concerns me more than the timing. Modern Industrials explicitly describes itself as an "AI execution layer that sits on top of existing systems" -- meaning DMSi Agility, ECI Spruce, and Epicor BisTrack remain the infrastructure, and Modern Industrials is the application. Compare this to Gusto, which I backed specifically because it replaced the payroll system of record. Gusto didn't sit on top of ADP; it became the infrastructure layer, which is why its switching costs compound year over year. Modern Industrials' switching costs depend on ERP integration depth and distributor-specific pricing rules, but the ERP vendor controls the API access. If DMSi decides to build native AI quoting or partners exclusively with Ventura, Modern Industrials faces platform risk with no recourse. The dossier's [Inferred] data moat -- cross-customer learning on pricing patterns -- is theoretically sound but entirely unproven, and it's the same data moat every vertical AI company claims at pre-seed.

The single-miracle math is moderately favorable. The technology miracle is happening exogenously: foundation models are getting better at document understanding on their own curve, and Modern Industrials doesn't need to build a frontier model. The remaining question is distribution -- can this team win accounts in a relationship-driven industry where buyers are traditional distributors, not software-native companies? That's one miracle, but it's a hard one. Ankit Bhargava's xAI and Uber ML credentials and Vatsal Bhargava's Google Ads bidding work demonstrate genuine ML engineering depth, and Austin Mao's McKinsey F500 engagements suggest he can navigate enterprise sales cycles. But none of the three founders have any documented experience in building materials, lumber, or distribution. In an industry where multi-generational family businesses make purchasing decisions based on trade show relationships and local reputation, the absence of domain credibility adds friction that compounds the distribution challenge into something closer to 1.5 miracles.

The bull case that would change my mind: Modern Industrials' claim to have "gone live with national players" (Vatsal Bhargava's LinkedIn, April 2, 2026) is, if true, a meaningful signal -- it suggests the distribution miracle is already underway. National LBM distributors processing hundreds of daily quotes would generate real revenue and real data quickly. If Modern Industrials can name two or three national accounts with measurable ROI within six months, the domain expertise gap becomes irrelevant because customer intimacy substitutes for founder background. The "boring but critical" pattern here is genuinely attractive -- I've watched Samsara build a $20B+ business in industrial IoT operations precisely because the category repels competition. If Modern Industrials can win the race against Ventura for the first five to ten national distributor accounts, the switching costs from trained pricing models and workflow integration could make this a durable business. But "could" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

The team's technical credentials are strong but unexceptional for this evaluation. Ankit Bhargava's xAI stint building agentic AI systems is the most relevant credential -- it maps directly to the product architecture. Vatsal Bhargava's $10K MRR side project (NBA injury intelligence platform) shows he can ship and monetize, which matters more than his Google pedigree. Austin Mao's Roon experience (Head of Growth at a Sequoia Series A) gives him some startup muscle memory, though Roon is not a hypergrowth story. The three-person founding dynamic -- two brothers plus a childhood friend of 20+ years -- provides the trust foundation that matters in early-stage execution, but I'd want to understand the decision-making hierarchy. Austin holds the CEO title, which is the right structure. No revenue data, no pricing model disclosed, no margin evidence -- at pre-seed this is expected, not penalized, but it means I'm scoring margin architecture on inference alone.

The two dimensions that lock my verdict are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. The existence of Ventura in the same cohort targeting the same use case, combined with Flitch's $3.16M raise for the same customer base, proves the market window is recognized -- it's not nonobvious. And the explicit application-layer positioning on top of legacy ERPs puts Modern Industrials at the wrong layer of the stack for my framework. A technically capable team in a boring, critical industry checks some of my pattern-recognition boxes, but without infrastructure-layer compounding and without a timing edge that consensus hasn't found, this is a pass.
