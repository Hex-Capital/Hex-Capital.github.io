# Datost -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me before I even finished reading this dossier: Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack. Salesforce also owns Tableau. So Datost is building an AI data analyst *inside Slack* — a platform owned by the company that also owns the single largest enterprise analytics product on the planet. This isn't building on someone else's platform. This is building on the platform of the exact company with the most obvious incentive and capability to ship exactly this product themselves. You could not design a more structurally precarious competitive position if you tried. I don't need to run the AI kill test here — the kill is already in progress. Salesforce has been shipping Einstein AI across every product they own for years. The only question is when, not whether, they unify Slack + Tableau + AI into the thing Datost is trying to be.

And zoom out from Salesforce specifically. Every single major BI vendor — Microsoft with Copilot in Power BI, Google with Looker AI, Tableau Pulse — is actively shipping AI analytics features within platforms that already have enterprise distribution, already have the data connections, already have the security certifications, already have the procurement relationships. This is the most consensus-hot category in enterprise software right now. Every demo day I attend, half the batch is some version of "we put an LLM on top of [enterprise data source]." When every other investor in the room is nodding along, the upside is already in the price. There is no mispricing here. The market is *correctly* excited about AI analytics, and that excitement is flowing to incumbents with distribution, not to two-person startups.

The primitive test is equally clear: this is not creating a new behavior. People already ask data questions. They ask them to analysts, they ask them in BI dashboards, they ask them in spreadsheets. Datost changes the *interface* — you ask in Slack instead of opening Tableau — but the behavior itself is decades old. Venmo created a verb. Patreon created the behavior of paying creators directly. "Ask a question about data in a chat window" is not a new primitive. It's a UX improvement on an existing workflow, and UX improvements get commoditized. The "own computer" execution environment is a nice architectural choice, but it's a feature differentiator, not a category creator.

Let me steelman this for a second, because the bull case deserves a real hearing. The argument would be: big companies are slow, Salesforce specifically is bureaucratic as hell, and there's a real window where a fast-moving startup nails the multi-source integration — databases, docs, Slack history, codebase — and builds such deep organizational context that switching becomes painful. The accumulated understanding of your company's specific data schema, terminology, tribal knowledge — that's a real switching cost. And Slack-native distribution is genuinely clever for bottom-up adoption. One person asks a question in a channel, everyone sees the answer, adoption spreads virally. If Datost can get into 500 companies before Salesforce ships their integrated version, maybe the switching costs hold. I take this seriously. But "maybe the incumbent is slow enough" is not a thesis I want to bet my own money on. It's a timing arbitrage, and timing arbitrages in software against well-capitalized incumbents have a brutal track record. You're not building a moat — you're renting a window.

On the founders: both are 21, both worked at Traba, both are software engineers. They can clearly build product. But the idea itself isn't insane — it's actually very sensible, which is the problem from my framework. I want a sane person doing something that makes smart people say "that's impossible." An AI data analyst in Slack makes every smart person say "yeah, obviously someone should build that." When the idea is consensus-obvious and the founders are early-career without deep domain expertise in enterprise data infrastructure or analytics sales cycles, I don't see the sanity-insanity ratio that gets me excited. The enterprise data trust barrier — getting security teams to let an AI agent touch production databases — is a real go-to-market challenge that typically requires experienced enterprise sellers and SOC 2 compliance, not something two 21-year-old engineers from a staffing startup are positioned to navigate quickly.

This is a pure software play with no physical moats, no protocol-level infrastructure, no network effects, sitting on top of commoditized LLM capabilities, inside a platform owned by its most dangerous competitor. It's the canonical example of what I've been saying publicly for two years: AI is sustaining technology that benefits incumbents. It's great tech. It's not disruptive. The value here accrues to Salesforce, Microsoft, and Google — not to Datost. Pass.
