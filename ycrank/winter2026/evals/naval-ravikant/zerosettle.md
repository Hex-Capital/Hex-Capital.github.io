# ZeroSettle -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The leverage question is where this deal breaks down for me. ZeroSettle doesn't create a new form of leverage. It recaptures margin. A developer using ZeroSettle has the same product, reaches the same users, delivers the same value -- they just keep 25 more cents on the dollar. That's a meaningful financial optimization, but it's not the same as giving someone a new capability they didn't have before. When I backed Notion, one person could suddenly do the organizational work of a five-person team. When I backed Replit, someone who couldn't code could suddenly build software. ZeroSettle gives developers back money Apple was taking. That's a billing optimization, not a new form of leverage. The distinction matters because leverage-creating companies generate compounding returns as usage grows. Cost-saving tools generate linear value proportional to the spend they optimize.

The founders have genuine insider knowledge of Apple's platform -- both were systems software engineers there, Ryan Elliott holds an Apple patent, and they understand StoreKit, App Review, and iOS architecture from the inside. That's real and relevant for execution. They'll understand Apple's compliance maneuvers faster than outsiders when Apple subtly adjusts its policies to frustrate alternative billing. But there's a difference between engineering knowledge about how a system works and deep insight about an unseen opportunity. The opportunity here is visible to everyone. Epic v. Apple was front-page news. Every mobile developer knows they want to avoid the 30% tax. What the founders know is how to build the bypass cleanly -- that's execution knowledge, not the kind of non-transferable domain obsession I look for. Compare this to the founders of Stack Overflow, who understood something fundamental about how developers learn that couldn't be extracted from any platform engineering job.

The timing argument deserves honest engagement. The regulatory window is real -- the U.S. court ruling, the EU DMA, Japan's new law -- multiple jurisdictions converging on open billing simultaneously. And there's a genuine structural insight in the competitive landscape: Apple can't offer this solution without cannibalizing $27 billion in annual commissions, and RevenueCat can't offer it without jeopardizing its Apple relationship. That's a real moat -- not a technology moat, but a strategic conflict moat. If I'm being fair, the bull case looks like this: if the Ninth Circuit ultimately rules Apple cannot charge any fee on linked-out purchases, the savings for subscription apps become enormous, adoption becomes almost inevitable, and ZeroSettle -- as the first turnkey migration-and-retention solution -- captures the default position. In that world, this becomes the RevenueCat of post-monopoly mobile billing. That's a large outcome.

But I can't ignore that this is a consensus bet dressed in execution complexity. Neon Pay already raised $14 million from Thrive Capital and a16z for essentially the same regulatory thesis in gaming. Stripe published its own iOS alternative billing guide. Paddle explicitly cited Apple's ecosystem opening as a growth driver. When four well-funded players are already positioning for the same regulatory tailwind, the asymmetric upside I need -- being right when others are wrong -- has been priced away. I'm the investor who backed Uber when ride-sharing was illegal and Bitcoin when it was dismissed as nerd money. This is the opposite pattern: everyone agrees the opportunity exists, and the only question is execution speed. That's a race, not a contrarian bet.

The compounding and defensibility picture is mixed. The Merchant of Record function creates real switching costs -- once a developer's subscribers are on ZeroSettle-managed billing, reversing the migration is genuinely painful. That's structural stickiness. But the SDK technology itself is acknowledged as replicable, and the entire payment layer runs on Stripe. If Stripe decides to add migration campaigns and retention flows to its existing iOS billing infrastructure, ZeroSettle becomes a thin wrapper on someone else's platform. Building a business that depends on navigating between two platforms that could crush you -- Apple on one side, Stripe on the other -- is a precarious architectural position. I prefer to invest in companies that become platforms themselves, not companies that fill the gap between someone else's platforms.

Two smart ex-Apple engineers in a real market with a working product and clear regulatory tailwinds. I don't see red flags on integrity or capability. But the absence of any traction signal -- no customers named, no revenue disclosed, no community evident -- at a moment when developer demand for this solution should be intense tells me something. Either the product is earlier than it appears, or adoption friction (fear of Apple retaliation, inertia, wait-and-see on the fee question) is higher than the regulatory narrative suggests. The business is structurally a regulatory arbitrage play, and the regulatory question that matters most -- whether Apple can charge fees on linked-out purchases -- remains unresolved on remand. That makes this a legal bet, not a technology bet. I don't have edge in predicting court outcomes.
