# Foaster -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most telling signal about Foaster is its own self-description: "AI-native consulting firm." That phrase tells me almost everything I need to know about where this company sits in my framework. When I backed Harvey, I was backing a platform that law firms would integrate into their workflows -- infrastructure that compounds. When I backed Stripe, I was backing the payments API layer that thousands of businesses would build on. Foaster is building a consulting firm. It uses AI as its production tool, but it's still a firm that sells engagements, delivers reports, and invoices per project. That's a services business at the application layer, not a platform at the infrastructure layer. The operational map they generate per client has genuine infrastructure potential -- if that map became a persistent, always-on organizational intelligence layer that other tools plugged into, I'd be having a different conversation. But that's a future pivot possibility, not what they're building today.

The market timing question here is subtle because the structural shift is real but obvious. LLMs crossing the threshold for conducting nuanced, open-ended organizational interviews is a genuine capability unlock -- two years ago, you couldn't get a model to navigate the ambiguity and political sensitivity of asking employees how their department actually works versus how the org chart says it works. That's a real threshold crossing. But "AI will disrupt management consulting" is one of the most consensus theses in venture capital right now. The competitive landscape confirms this: Distyl AI has raised $202M, Aily Labs $101M, PromptQL $136M+, Skan.ai $57.6M. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was considered fringe. When I wrote the Perplexity seed check, AI search was considered a failed category. The question I always ask is whether consensus underestimates the window -- and here, if anything, consensus may overestimate it, because the enterprise trust barriers for AI interviewing employees about sensitive organizational dynamics are steeper than most investors appreciate. Data sensitivity, employee consent, legal review, AI governance policies -- these aren't technical problems, they're procurement and compliance barriers that slow adoption regardless of how good the product is.

The single-miracle test is where this gets concerning. The technology miracle -- LLMs capable of nuanced interviews -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable. But Foaster needs at least two additional things to go right independently: enterprise buyers must trust AI agents to interview their employees about sensitive operational topics (a trust and compliance hurdle, not a technology one), and two 23-year-old founders still finishing their degrees at ENSAE Paris must build enterprise sales credibility against competitors who have hundreds of millions in funding and established go-to-market machines. Those are independent challenges, and the compounding starts to hurt. Combes's Repondia experience -- 60K+ AI-conducted calls -- is the strongest technical signal, demonstrating he's built conversational AI at scale before. But Repondia has no independent web presence or verifiable traction, which makes it hard to assess whether that scale was real production deployment or a research prototype.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If Foaster's operational maps become the persistent asset -- not the consulting engagement, but the map itself -- this could evolve into something that looks more like Gusto than McKinsey: boring, essential organizational infrastructure with deep switching costs. A company that has a living, AI-maintained map of how it actually operates would find enormous value in that asset for restructuring, M&A due diligence, compliance audits, and ongoing process optimization. The innovator's dilemma argument is also real: MBB firms structurally cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper alternative without cannibalizing their entire leverage model. If Foaster captures the mid-market segment that MBB won't serve at their price points, there's a real wedge. But this requires executing the pivot from "consulting firm" to "organizational intelligence platform," and the current positioning -- per-engagement pricing, "book a call" GTM, human consultant in the loop -- doesn't suggest that pivot is the plan.

The margin architecture confirms my concern. Consulting firms, even AI-augmented ones, operate on services margins. Competing on being "3-5x more cost-effective" means competing on price, which compresses margins further. The per-engagement model doesn't generate the negative net revenue churn or expansion dynamics I look for. Compare this to Harvey, which bills by work product and embeds in legal workflows so deeply that switching costs compound over time. Foaster's current model produces a deliverable and moves on. The operational map creates some retention potential, but it's not being monetized as a subscription platform today. I've seen technically sharp founders build real businesses in the AI-services space, but the ones that generate venture-scale returns are the ones that transition from services to software early -- and the dossier doesn't show me that trajectory.
