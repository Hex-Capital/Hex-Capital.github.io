# Praxis AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Praxis AI is my Pass Example with the serial numbers filed off and a hard-hat glued on top. Three Penn kids -- Rohan Seelamsetty out of Computation and Cognition and Oxford's Apollo Fellowship, Dev Karpe out of Wharton Huntsman, Tommy Li out of Penn CS/Finance by way of Morgan Stanley PE and **a16z industrial tech** -- pitching "turns every company into a data vendor" into the exact lane Scale AI is defending with Meta's $14.3B stake behind it. The signature is a founder team with the highest-pedigree profile I see all week, sitting on top of the dimension I care about least, and the dimension I care about most -- did any of these three ever *live* inside one of those 150+ environment types before they wrote the deck -- is silent.

**Anduril**-Luckey, 2017, is the HIGH rung on founder origin: Palmer shipped Oculus from a garage before he ever touched defense, and the biography made the license to walk on a DoD range legible before the pitch started. Praxis AI's analog would be a founder who worked the floor of a Foxconn or a Reliance conglomerate, got yelled at by a shift supervisor, watched an unlabeled 3D scan get thrown away, and decided the world needed egocentric capture from *inside*. What I get instead in the Praxis AI dossier is Tommy Li publishing IEEE papers at **NASA JPL and PerceptIn Robotics** -- adjacency, not embedding -- and Karpe rotating through an F500 CEO's office. Adjacency is what Peter interrogates when he's looking for a market secret. I'm looking for a founder who was *of* the place, and Praxis AI reads *visiting*.

The **consensus YC AI-agent darling** is the low rung on non-consensus, and its failure mechanism is that the round is priced as if the outcome is already certain because six tier-1 funds are chasing. Praxis AI's dossier names **Scale AI at $29B**, **Micro1 at a $2.5B offer with $100M ARR**, **Encord at $110M raised including a Feb 2026 Wellington-led Series C**, plus Cortex AI and Luel operating the same egocentric-data lane. That's not a lane I have to convince anyone about -- MIT Tech Review already wrote the humanoid-data thinkpiece in April 2026. The differentiation Praxis AI claims -- embedding in conglomerates instead of shipping Ray-Bans to gig workers -- is a GTM wrinkle inside a category the buyers have already agreed exists. When I ask "would two credible VCs pass on aesthetic grounds," the answer is no; the aesthetic here is the *default*.

On single-CEO clarity, Praxis AI names Seelamsetty as CEO on LinkedIn -- "CEO @ Praxis (YC S26) | Penn & Team India" -- which is more than the **HQ Trivia** Yusupov/Kroll setup ever gave me on day one, but the compounding question over 18 months is whether an Oxford-trained debater with two Wharton/Penn co-founders and no disclosed operating agreement can hold tiebreaker authority when the first conglomerate contract renegotiates exclusivity. The dossier is silent on equity split, and silence on governance is the near-term blow-up risk I've written about since **GameCrush**. I'm giving Praxis AI credit for a named CEO, not credit for a documented structure.

On capital-intensive/unsexy-regulated, the counterfactual I run is: what would flip Praxis AI a tier here? It would take a signed exclusivity clause with a named publicly listed conglomerate -- Reliance, Foxconn, JBS, someone I can point at -- carrying labor and data-rights carve-outs that a Scale AI can't replicate because their gig-recruit model conflicts with in-facility employment law. Praxis AI's dossier gestures at "publicly listed and unicorn-scale conglomerates" and "60k workers across 5 continents and 150+ environment types" but names zero of them. Without the named counterparty, I'm underwriting a data-services pitch, not the regulated-industrial moat that made me tattoo **Anduril** on my body.

Physical-world verifiability against a **Niantic** parallel: Ingress I could play in 2012, four years before the Pokémon Go check, and the tactile pull of the product *was* the diligence. Praxis AI's own website -- praxisrobotics.io -- as of July 2026 exposes a tagline, "Great machines will be forged by human intuition," and nothing else. I cannot install Praxis AI, ride Praxis AI, photograph Praxis AI, or read a Praxis AI worker's shift log at a Four Seasons café. The captured artifact -- egocentric video from a factory floor -- is real, but it lives inside a B2B pipeline I have no line of sight into. That is the developer-tools abstraction anchor, not the Ingress anchor.

Thirty-six-month forecast for Praxis AI: acqui-hired by **Micro1** or **Scale AI** at a modest premium to seed valuation between now and mid-2029, most likely as a sourcing team plugged onto an existing gig-capture backbone; or dead after the first conglomerate contract renegotiation strips resale rights. Below $8M ARR at the point of the exit. The mechanism is that Praxis AI is a wedge -- conglomerate embed -- inside a category where the winners already have $100M+ of ARR and can pay to license the wedge rather than build it. Praxis AI does not have the founder biography that lets it hold a conglomerate CEO's hand through a labor-law fight, and it does not have the balance sheet to outlast Scale AI in a bidding war for exclusivity.

The strongest bull case is the polymath-override that got me to yes on **DeepMind** despite the elite-PhD pattern: Tommy Li's a16z industrial-tech seat plus NASA JPL/PerceptIn IEEE research plus Karpe's F500 supply-chain-ops seat plus Seelamsetty's Apollo/Oxford AI-risk lens is a genuine cross-domain stack, and the 60k-worker distribution figure -- if it holds up under contract review -- is a real Day-1 asset most pre-seed data plays would kill for. Hassabis was a chess prodigy stacked on a neuroscience PhD; Li stacked on JPL and a16z is the same shape of compounding. I hear the argument. The reason I don't fold to it is that the DeepMind override still required Hassabis to have *built the thing before* -- Elixir, Theme Park, decades of game design -- and the Praxis AI founders' prior *building* is a website with a tagline. Founder-market fit via prior job title is exactly what the Hustle Fund profile calls the "replaceable operator" pattern; polymath compounding requires a shipped artifact I can point at. **The one condition that would flip me: Seelamsetty, Karpe, or Li puts on record a specific autobiographical moment -- a shift on a factory floor, a family business inside one of these 150+ environments -- that put them in front of this problem before Y Combinator did.** Absent that, this is a pass.
