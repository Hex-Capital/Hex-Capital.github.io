# Inth -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch — the CEO of Vercel, the platform that defines modern web development — publicly called Inth's c15t "@shadcn for Consent Management" to an audience of ~79K. That's not a testimonial you can buy. Rauch endorsed Inth because Burns built something that fits natively into the architecture Rauch's own platform promotes — server-rendered, composable, zero external dependencies. Vercel is also a listed customer. When the CEO of your target ecosystem's dominant platform both uses your product and tells the world to use it, you've achieved something most pre-seed companies spend years chasing. But I need to understand whether this is organic pull from genuine technical superiority or a well-networked founder getting a signal boost. The 1.8M npm downloads with 388.3K monthly and +26.9% growth from a 3-person team suggest the former — code doesn't lie about adoption velocity.

Christopher Burns has the right specific knowledge but not the deepest version of it. Ten years of TypeScript, CTO of Everfund processing donations across 33+ countries with multi-jurisdiction regulatory handling, contributor to RedwoodJS's authentication layer — Burns didn't research consent management as a market opportunity, he lived inside the intersection of web frameworks and cross-border compliance at Everfund. That's genuine specific knowledge. But consent management isn't AI search or microgravity manufacturing — the domain expertise is real but reproducible by any senior TypeScript developer who spends two years studying GDPR/CCPA/IAB TCF 2.3 compliance. Inth's specific knowledge advantage is measured in months to a few years, not decades. The 411 releases on GitHub tell me Burns ships with the cadence of someone who can't stop building, which partially compensates — velocity IS a form of specific knowledge accumulation.

The leverage architecture is where Inth shines brightest through my framework. 0.6KB total size, 30ms FCP, zero network overhead — this is pure code leverage with zero marginal cost per additional website. Every new developer who runs `npm install @c15t/react` gets the product without Inth hiring anyone. The open-source distribution via npm is permissionless leverage in its most literal form — no sales team, no gatekeepers, no approval process. Inth's architecture reminds me of the Notion parallel from my portfolio: a small team building composable tools for builders that scale through the product itself, not through headcount. Notion reached millions of users with a tiny team because the product replicated at zero marginal cost. Inth has the same structural advantage — 3 people, 1.8M downloads, customers including Vercel, Expo, and Zed. The contrast with OneTrust's 14,000+ customers requiring enterprise sales teams and $10K+ ACV minimums is the principal-agent problem made visible. OneTrust's sales organization is structurally incapable of selling a free, self-hosted SDK to developers — their agents sell to compliance officers through enterprise contracts, and rebuilding as a headless SDK would cannibalize the external-script delivery model their entire revenue depends on. Burns, as a principal with no legacy revenue to protect, occupies the exact structural position that creates outlier returns when incumbents cannot respond.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Inth democratizes compliance infrastructure the way AngelList democratized startup investing — OneTrust's $10K+ minimum ACV locks out every small-to-medium developer team, and Inth gives them the same capability for free through open-source, with a paid hosted tier for those who want managed infrastructure. The regulatory tailwinds are structural, not speculative — EU Digital Markets Act enforcement began March 2024, India's DPDP Act and Canada's CPPA are expanding jurisdictional requirements, and Core Web Vitals as a Google ranking factor makes CMP performance directly impact search rankings and therefore revenue. Inth's 89ms total consent runtime versus 445-709ms for legacy CMPs isn't a nice-to-have — it's a measurable SEO advantage. The access democratization pattern here maps cleanly onto my portfolio: restricted resource (compliance infrastructure), structural gatekeeper (enterprise pricing), permissionless alternative (open-source SDK). This is a legitimate bull case.

The bear case deserves equal weight. Inth has zero visible revenue — no pricing page, no public monetization signal, 1.8M npm downloads converting to $0 disclosed. The consent management TAM of $732M-$1.82B sounds meaningful, but Inth's addressable segment — developer teams on React/Next.js who prioritize performance over compliance-officer-friendly dashboards — is a fraction of that. CookieBench, the benchmark proving Inth's performance superiority, was created by Inth's own team — a self-referential credibility problem that enterprise buyers will question. The regulatory complexity risk is real and linear: supporting GDPR, CCPA, UK GDPR, ePrivacy, IAB TCF 2.3, GPC, Quebec Law 25, DPDP, PIPL, and CPPA requires continuous legal monitoring that scales with jurisdictions, not with code. A 3-person team maintaining compliance coverage across ten regulatory frameworks while simultaneously building product and figuring out monetization is stretched thin. And only Christopher Burns is named as founder — the other two team members are invisible in public sources, which limits my ability to assess whether Inth has the regulatory domain expertise in-house or is solely a developer tools play hoping compliance correctness follows from good code.

The condition that would flip me from invest to pass: if Inth reaches 5M npm downloads without converting to measurable revenue — say, $50K+ MRR — that signals an open-source adoption curve that never monetizes, which is the graveyard of developer tools companies that optimized for stars over dollars. Conversely, what keeps me invested despite the monetization uncertainty is the structural position: Burns occupies the exact leverage-plus-principal-versus-agents configuration that I've seen produce outlier returns at Perplexity, Notion, and in my own experience building AngelList. The permissionless leverage architecture and the access democratization pattern are the two dimensions that most distinguish Inth from a generic pre-seed company — a 3-person team with 1.8M npm downloads and Vercel as both customer and evangelist has already demonstrated the leverage thesis. The specific knowledge is solid but not exceptional, and the monetization gap is real but solvable. I'd write a small check at pre-seed and watch the revenue conversion closely.
