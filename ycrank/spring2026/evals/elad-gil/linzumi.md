# Linzumi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Sean Grove's resume -- OneGraph (YC W19, acquired by Netlify), Principal Architect at Netlify, OpenAI deliberative-alignment work, AI Engineer World's Fair 2025 keynote -- is the most legible thing in the Linzumi dossier, and the least legible thing is whether anyone is buying. That high/low pairing -- credentialed shipper, zero growth curve -- is exactly the profile I'm supposed to skip in AI per Tim Ferriss #863 ("if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken"). Linzumi is a pre-launch waitlist with 79 LinkedIn followers selling $100/mo flat-rate unlimited into a category where Cognition is at $492M ARR growing 50% MoM. The shipping pedigree is real. The "just working" signal is absent.

**Perplexity** (2022) is the load-bearing analogy on the founder-shipping axis -- Aravind would "show up a week later with the thing we discussed built," and that rhythm, not pedigree, was the wedge. Sean Grove at Linzumi has the prior-shipping half of the Perplexity template: OneGraph all the way through a Netlify acquisition is real founder-built distribution in developer tooling, and his OpenAI model-spec / deliberative-alignment work maps almost embarrassingly cleanly onto Linzumi's pitch of "compiling team decisions into a source-of-truth spec." Where Linzumi diverges from the HIGH rung is that I cannot see the iteration artifact -- the dossier shows me a waitlist page, a Twitter account I can't fetch, and a GitHub org with no public members. Aravind's signal was *between meetings*, not *on a resume*. Linzumi gives me the resume half and asks me to take the iteration half on faith, and two of three co-founders aren't even publicly named.

**Lyft** Series C is the failure pattern on revenue velocity -- not absent revenue, but absent disclosure of a curve that distinguishes the bet from the obvious bet, and I passed and was right to pass on the framework even though the outcome was a regret. Does that pattern appear in Linzumi? The dossier reports "coming soon / waitlist" on LinkedIn, no Product Hunt launch, no press, no ARR, no customer count, $100/month flat pricing without a single named pilot. In the AI cycle, silence on growth is itself a negative signal -- real growth is easy to disclose, and Cognition at $492M ARR and Factory "doubling MoM" prove the disclosure norm in this exact category. Linzumi's silence isn't pre-seed stage default; it's silence in a sub-market where peers are screaming numbers.

On market catalyst, the mechanism over 18 months is straightforward and partially favorable to Linzumi: Cognition's 50% MoM for six months and Factory's enterprise traction at Nvidia/Adobe/Adyen prove fleets-of-agents is real, and a coordination-gap thesis is a credible second-derivative bet on that catalyst. But Linzumi is not opening a dead market the way **Anduril** opened defense post-Ukraine -- Linzumi is the *third wave* riding a catalyst Cognition already monetized, which is the consensus-pays setup only if the revenue curve confirms. It doesn't, yet.

On index position, the counterfactual question is: what would have to be true for Linzumi to score a tier higher? It would have to be the case that multi-player chat is a structurally different surface that Cognition and Factory cannot ship as a feature. The dossier itself flags that "Cognition's Devin already ships a Slack integration referenced in its product docs." That single sentence collapses the index thesis -- Linzumi isn't sitting in a workflow path every entrant must touch like **Stripe** sat on payments; Linzumi is sitting on a surface the category leader already shipped. The spec-compiler layer might create switching costs eventually, but it's currently a claim, not a moat.

On single-miracle, **Decagon** is the right single-anchor parallel -- workflow infrastructure on a recently-catalyzed category, one assumed capability (LLM accuracy on support tickets), rest is GTM. Linzumi is structurally more demanding: it needs (1) LLMs reliable enough to extract durable decisions from messy chat into executable specs, (2) engineering teams to adopt a new chat-native primary surface over Devin-in-Slack or Cursor-in-IDE, (3) Cognition not to ship the same surface in the next 12 months. Compound those probabilities and you have the multi-miracle pattern NFX flagged. Decagon needed one miracle. Linzumi needs at least two and a half.

The 36-month forecast: Linzumi gets acqui-hired by Cognition, Anthropic, or OpenAI for sub-$100M based primarily on Sean Grove's deliberative-alignment IP, or pivots to a spec-management tool for engineering managers -- not an independent venture-scale outcome. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a credentialed alignment researcher building a coordination layer the category leader can clone, on a flat-pricing model that won't survive compute scaling, without a revenue curve to force a markup. That ends in talent absorption, not Series C.

The strongest bull case is that "deliberative alignment for companies" is a genuinely different product wedge that Cognition's per-user cloud-sandbox architecture is structurally awkward to replicate, and Sean Grove is the only person in the world whose OpenAI work makes that pitch credible -- the **Color Genomics** template of workflow infrastructure on top of a hard layer, but pointed at coding agents instead of sequencing. It stings because it's the right template, applied by the right founder, on the right catalyst. What dismantles it is the dossier's own admission that Devin ships a Slack integration today and Linzumi ships nothing -- the moat is a claim about future spec compilation, the competitor is a $492M ARR machine, and "$100/mo unlimited" is not pricing power, it's compute exposure. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: 20%+ MoM paid revenue growth within 90 days of GA launch with three named enterprise logos. Until then, Linzumi is a Pass on the velocity criterion alone.
