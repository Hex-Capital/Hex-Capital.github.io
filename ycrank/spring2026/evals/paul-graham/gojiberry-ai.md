# Gojiberry AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Pierre-Eliott Lallemant is co-founding two YC companies in the same batch. Gojiberry AI and Standout, simultaneously, Spring 2026. I've seen founders spread across multiple projects before, and it almost never works. The reason is simple: startups require the kind of focus where you dream about the problem. You can't dream about two problems. When I backed Patrick Collison at Stripe, he had dropped everything else — school, his previous company, his other interests — because payments infrastructure consumed him. Gojiberry AI's CEO has not made that choice, and at the pre-seed stage, where the founders ARE the company, a divided CEO is a divided company.

Gojiberry AI's traction, though, is hard to dismiss. $112K MRR in 9 months, 1,000+ paying customers at $99/month, 44% month-over-month growth, all bootstrapped. That is "make something people want" reduced to its most measurable form. The pivot story has the shape I look for: their original AI sales assistant generated a 2,000-person waitlist that produced zero conversions, so they scrapped it and rebuilt around intent-based LinkedIn outreach. Zero to $1.4M ARR in 9 months post-pivot. The running back quality — persistent on the goal, flexible on the path — is visible in that trajectory. But I've learned to distinguish between founders who are resourceful and founders who are excellent marketers operating in a frothy market. Pierre-Eliott bootstrapped €500K revenue at 22 through eBay dropshipping. Román came from affiliate marketing. The distribution engine they built — 5,000 cold emails weekly, 800 LinkedIn DMs, 5 YouTube videos, Reddit posts, newsletter sponsorships — reads like a playbook from growth marketing, not from obsessive product building. Gojiberry AI's founders are clearly capable operators, but the instinct I'm pattern-matching against is builder obsession, and what I see here is distribution obsession.

Gojiberry AI's problem discovery has a legitimate claim to organic origin. Pierre-Eliott and Román ran CoCo AI's outbound sales — 5,000 cold emails daily — before building Gojiberry AI. They lived inside the pain of cold outreach to low-intent prospects. That's meaningfully different from a consultant who "identifies an opportunity." But it's also not quite Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. They didn't stumble onto a gap while living in the future; they identified an adjacent business opportunity while running their previous one. The distance between "I experienced this pain every day" and "I noticed this market while running a related company" matters, because the first produces the kind of understanding that generates non-obvious product decisions, while the second produces competent execution in a known category.

Artisan has raised $46.1M. Amplemarket, $12M. Persana, $2.8M. Valley is building the exact same LinkedIn-first intent-based outreach product. Salesforge has 254 G2 reviews to Gojiberry AI's 11. This is not a space where the first reaction is "that will never work." The first reaction is "of course — everyone's doing that." AI-powered sales automation could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece from 2025 or 2026 or any quarter in between. When I invested in Stripe, the entire VC industry thought payments infrastructure was boring, regulated, and already solved by PayPal. That disgust reaction was Stripe's moat. Gojiberry AI has no such moat. The competitive landscape is a crowd of well-funded companies converging on the same insight — that AI can replace SDRs — and the only differentiation Gojiberry AI offers is LinkedIn-native focus and signal-first sequencing, neither of which is technically protected or hard to replicate. The LinkedIn platform dependency compounds this: if LinkedIn changes its API policies or builds native intent features, Gojiberry AI's core value proposition evaporates overnight.

Dylan Teixeira, the CTO, holds a Master's in Digital Marketing & Innovation from IÉSEG. None of the three founders have public GitHub repos. Gojiberry AI is an AI product built by a team whose visible technical credentials are in marketing. I don't want to overweight credentials — some of the best hackers I've funded had unconventional backgrounds — but the complete absence of public code, combined with a CTO whose degree is in marketing, triggers my "can this team build the product themselves?" filter. Edusign scaling to 600K users suggests Dylan can ship, but the question of whether Gojiberry AI's technical architecture can outpace Artisan's $46M in engineering investment is unanswered. The learning rate evidence is strong on the business side — the pivot speed, the growth trajectory, the Product Hunt progression from March 2025 to #1 Product of the Day in March 2026 — but I don't see evidence of product iteration depth, only distribution iteration depth.

The strongest bull case for Gojiberry AI is straightforward: ignore everything except the numbers. $1.4M ARR bootstrapped in 9 months with three repeat founders who have all had exits. At YC, we funded plenty of companies with worse numbers and more concerning founding teams. The 44% MoM growth rate dwarfs the 5-7% weekly rate I use as a compass. If you just followed the growth curve, you'd invest without hesitation. And maybe a pure traction investor should. But my framework exists because traction in hot markets is the most perishable kind of signal. Gojiberry AI is growing fast in a space where multiple companies are growing fast, because the underlying tailwind — LLMs enabling automated outreach — lifts all boats. The question isn't whether Gojiberry AI can grow; it's whether Gojiberry AI can win, and winning in a consensus-obvious market against $46M-funded competitors requires either a structural advantage I don't see or a quality of founder obsession the dossier doesn't demonstrate. The condition that would flip me: if Pierre-Eliott dropped Standout entirely and Gojiberry AI demonstrated a technical moat — proprietary signal data, a novel ML architecture, something that Artisan's $46M can't simply replicate — I'd move from Neutral to Invest.

Gojiberry AI scores highest on founder resourcefulness (the pivot from zero conversions to $1.4M ARR is genuinely impressive) and learning rate (the growth curve speaks). It scores lowest on the contrarian idea test — this is a consensus-obvious space with well-funded competitors — and earnest builder identity, where the serial-entrepreneur-with-dropshipping-origins profile and dual-venture split point toward opportunity optimization rather than problem obsession. The gap between the traction and the thesis fit is the whole story. Gojiberry AI has built something people want, but in a market where the returns have already been competed for.
