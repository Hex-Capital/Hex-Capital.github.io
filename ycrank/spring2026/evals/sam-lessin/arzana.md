# Arzana -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumped out: a 21-year-old from Okoboji, Iowa who took one coding class in his life is selling AI to manufacturers and has Schneider National and Zeiss on his customer page. That's a genuinely interesting founder profile. Most YC AI companies are started by Stanford ML PhDs who've never set foot on a factory floor. Alexander grew up around these businesses, has a field engineer from his hometown doing implementations, and keeps a physical presence in Iowa alongside the San Francisco office. The NYT wrote about him precisely because he doesn't fit the mold. If I squint at this the right way, these are kids who understand the actual humans running manufacturing operations in the Midwest — not because they studied it, but because they lived it. That's the kind of founder-market relationship I pay attention to.

But here's my problem, and it's a structural one: this is an AI application layer company in one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture right now. "AI for vertical workflows" is what every other seed pitch in 2025-2026 looks like. Endeavor AI raised $7M from Craft Ventures attacking the same market. Canals AI has backing. Multiple funded competitors going after the same manufacturing front-office. Where's the mispricing? There isn't one. When Craft Ventures is already writing $7M checks into your direct competitor, the asymmetry is gone. The market has priced this category as attractive, which means the upside is already in the price. Compare that to when I backed Solana at four cents — every smart person thought the thesis was dead. Nobody thinks manufacturing AI is dead. Everyone thinks it's great. That's the problem.

The primitive test is even more damning. Quoting, order entry, purchasing, accounts payable, CRM — these are workflows that have existed for decades. Arzana does them faster with LLMs. That's automation, not creation. Venmo created a behavior that didn't exist — "Venmo me" became a verb. Nobody's going to say "Arzana me this RFQ." The "Office Execution System" branding is clever marketing, but underneath it's a workflow automation tool that competes on speed and accuracy within an existing operational paradigm. Better is not new.

The AI kill test is where this really falls apart for me. Epicor, SAP, Oracle — they own the ERP systems of record that Arzana is building integrations *to*. Arzana is building its house on someone else's land. When Epicor ships an AI-powered front-office module — and they will, because this is obviously a "fuck yeah" for them — Arzana's integration advantage becomes Epicor's native advantage. The dossier even acknowledges it: "the core technology — LLM-based document parsing and workflow automation — is buildable by any team with AI engineering capability." The CEO took one coding class. The entire technical capability sits on two engineers with no public track record. That's not a moat, that's a hope. The 11+ ERP integrations sound impressive until you realize each one is a maintenance obligation that a four-person team has to keep current as ERP vendors push updates. That's a treadmill, not a flywheel.

Now, the strongest bull case: what if the ERP vendors are structurally incapable of building fast, lightweight AI layers because their business models depend on complex, high-touch implementations that generate professional services revenue? What if Arzana is Gusto to ADP — the nimble, modern layer that wins SMB manufacturers precisely because SAP will never make deployment take less than six months? There's something to that. The "custom integrations in under one week, implementation in 1-3 months" claim is a real wedge against enterprise software incumbents that measure deployment in quarters. And $2,500-$7,000/month is priced right for the mid-market plants that SAP will never prioritize. If Arzana can lock in 500 of these plants before the incumbents wake up, the switching costs from embedded workflow data could be real. Kools made ten grand last year — that's missionary commitment, not mercenary positioning. The traction at pre-seed is legitimately good: named Fortune 500 logos, a customer testimonial citing 5-day to 1-day quoting improvement. These are real signals.

But "real signals" and "my kind of bet" are different things. The category is consensus. The product is incremental. The moat is thin. The founders are young, earnest, and clearly hustling — but "sane person, sane idea" doesn't get me excited. I want the person where I'm nodding along thinking "you're incredibly reasonable" while simultaneously thinking "what you're building is completely insane." AI for manufacturing quotes is the opposite of insane — it's the most obvious application of LLMs you could pitch me. This might be a perfectly fine business that generates $5-10M ARR before getting acquired by Epicor or compressed by commoditization. But I'm not investing my own money into consensus-priced bets with thin structural moats in the category I've been loudest about avoiding. Pass.
