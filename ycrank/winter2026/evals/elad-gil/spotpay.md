# SpotPay -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive convergence here is the first thing I notice, and it tells the whole story about where this market sits structurally. KAST raised $10M, Dakota raised $12.5M, Slash has $60M, Chipper Cash has $305M — and SpotPay has $450K. When I see four or five well-funded companies pursuing the same thesis in the same 18-month window, I'm not looking at a non-obvious market. I'm looking at a consensus opportunity that's already attracting the kind of capital pileup I wrote about in my "End of Cycle" piece. Cross-border payments is a $212 billion revenue market, stablecoins are the obvious enabling technology, and every fintech-focused fund in the valley can articulate this thesis. That's the opposite of the pattern where I've made my best investments — PagerDuty when operations alerting seemed boring, Coinbase when crypto seemed speculative, Harvey when legal AI seemed impossible. The markets that generate asymmetric returns are the ones most investors dismiss. Nobody is dismissing stablecoin-powered cross-border payments right now.

The stablecoin infrastructure moment is real — supply grew from $5 billion to $305 billion, payment volume doubled in 2025, Visa expanded stablecoin settlement, Stripe acquired Bridge. I don't dispute the structural shift. But a genuine technology inflection and a good investment are different things. When the inflection is visible to everyone, the question becomes pure execution against better-capitalized competitors, and that's a game where $450K in funding against teams with 20-100x more capital is a structural disadvantage, not a character flaw. Consumer fintech is especially unforgiving here because customer acquisition cost is the dominant competitive variable — you can't win corridors on product quality alone when the neobank experience converges across players.

The bull case worth engaging with honestly: the Caribbean and Latin American corridor focus could be a wedge that's more defensible than it appears. Zsika grew up between the Caribbean and Latin America, understands those specific markets personally, and most of the well-funded competitors are targeting either Africa (Chipper Cash), businesses (Slash, Dakota), or global consumers broadly (KAST). If SpotPay becomes the trusted product in 2-3 specific corridors before anyone else builds cultural fluency there, corridor-specific network effects could kick in — recipients pull senders, senders pull recipients. That's a plausible path. And the CTO credential is genuinely strong: Thomas as engineer #4 at Brex, rising to Principal SWE managing three teams, is exactly the kind of fintech infrastructure builder you'd want constructing reliable payment systems. Five app updates shipped in three months since launch demonstrates real execution cadence.

But I keep coming back to the multiple-miracle problem. To win, SpotPay needs to simultaneously achieve consumer acquisition density in specific corridors, navigate bilateral regulatory regimes across multiple countries, maintain Visa card issuance and stablecoin infrastructure partnerships, and build enough network effects to create switching costs — all with a two-person team and minimal funding. Each of these is a substantial challenge on its own. Together they compound into exactly the kind of multi-miracle plan I explicitly avoid. Compare this to Harvey's structure: Harvey's single miracle — model fidelity for legal work — was being delivered by the foundation model labs, freeing the team to focus entirely on workflow and distribution. SpotPay doesn't have that luxury. No one is solving their corridor-specific regulatory and distribution challenges for them.

The product-to-distribution trajectory follows the standard neobank playbook: start with transfers, expand to spending (the Calypso Card), add yield on balances, eventually layer on lending, insurance, and other financial products. Nubank proved this playbook works in Latin America at scale. But the path from "neobank with an app" to "distribution channel for multiple financial products" requires a user base large enough to create distribution leverage — and with 11 iOS ratings three months after launch, that base doesn't exist yet. The distribution thinking isn't differentiated; it's the same expansion logic every neobank articulates. I look for companies where the initial product creates a distribution relationship that's structurally hard to replicate. Here, the distribution challenge IS the business, which means the moat has to come from corridor-specific execution and regulatory licenses rather than from product architecture.

I respect the founder-market fit and the shipping velocity, but this isn't my kind of bet. The market is consensus, the competitive landscape is crowded with far better-capitalized players, the business requires multiple concurrent miracles, and the consumer-facing model in emerging markets demands exactly the kind of capital-intensive distribution play that doesn't match what a $450K pre-seed can attack. The stablecoin infrastructure shift is real, but the right way to invest in that shift is probably through infrastructure companies that serve all the consumer-facing players — which is exactly what Stripe did by acquiring Bridge — not through one of many consumer neobanks competing for corridor share.
