# Cova -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Cova is the right lane, wrong biography. The lane is Michigan Medicaid Home Help at $12.25/hour state-approved reimbursement, an "ugly, regulated, capital-intensive, or aesthetically transgressive" category I would happily back -- adjacent to Mindbloom ketamine, adjacent to Vigil Labs. The biography is Ansh Tandon, ex-Apple AI and ex-Tesla vehicle software out of Purdue CS, paired with a Jordan Ibe whose LinkedIn the dossier cannot even conclusively confirm. The signature pairing is highest on regulated-unsexy-swing and lowest on founder-lived-experience, which makes Cova a clean engineering team entering a Medicaid back-office arbitrage as a market thesis rather than as people who ever sat in a Wayne County intake office.

Anduril-Luckey 2017 is the founder template I keep returning to -- Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage before he ever talked to a defense procurement officer, and the trajectory was load-bearing because the founder *was of* the problem. Cova's Tandon trajectory is the opposite: vehicle software platforms at Tesla and AI work at Apple are real engineering credentials, but neither founder is described in the dossier as having cared for an aging parent on Michigan Home Help, billed a Medicaid claim, or sat across the kitchen table from a caregiver earning $12.25/hour. Cova's founder origin reads as "we saw the margin in home care" rather than "we lived the call from the discharge planner at 11pm" -- which is exactly the analytical entry the pass example penalizes, and the dossier offers no countervailing autobiographical moment to pin Cova above the floor on this axis.

HQ Trivia 2017 is the low rung I keep in front of me whenever a dossier names two founders and no CEO -- the Yusupov/Kroll partnership did not collapse because the product was bad, it collapsed because nobody could name the tiebreaker when the disagreement came. Cova lists Ansh Tandon and Jordan Ibe as co-founders with no CEO designation, no documented operating agreement, and no equity split visible to me. In a regulated-billing business where one of the two founders has to own the relationship with Michigan MDHHS auditors and the other has to own the AI-agent compliance posture, the absence of an identified principal is not a stage-default omission -- it is the exact pre-condition for the GameCrush activist-investor collapse. Cova has not had that conversation yet, or has had it and chose not to disclose it; either way I apply the absent-evidence floor.

Crusoe 2018 is the rung Cova reaches for on the magically-weird axis, because operating the licensed Michigan agency rather than selling SaaS to incumbent agencies is genuinely contrarian -- the verticalized-AI-agency wedge cannibalizes the franchisee economics that Honor's $1.25B Series E was built on. Mechanism analysis on 18 months: if Cova's AI agents actually compress intake, eligibility, and claims work enough to fund "highest base rate in the state," the cost-structure wedge compounds with every caregiver hired away from Visiting Angels and Comfort Keepers, and the regulatory license becomes a real asset because incumbents cannot shed back-office headcount without union and franchise blowback. The drag is that the YC directory already lists Sage Care, MochaCare W26, Kaigo F25, and Helpcare F24 in the adjacent slice -- Cova is not finding the deal before consensus, Cova is one of five agents in this space YC has stamped, and "find them before it becomes consensus" was the actual instruction.

Mindbloom is the single-anchor parallel I want to draw on the capital-intensive-regulated axis -- ketamine therapy was unsexy, state-by-state regulated, reimbursement-fragile, and exactly the category most of my peers refused to underwrite, and the moat compounded in the domain other investors would not enter. Cova lives in that same lane: Michigan Home Help is a Medicaid program with a state-approved $12.25/hour rate, a 60.52%-of-market payer dependency per Mordor Intelligence, and single-state license concentration that slows every software competitor who is not willing to file licensure paperwork in 49 more states. This is the dimension where Cova earns its real score in my book -- the dossier specifically names Medicaid Home Help, LTCI, and private-pay billing as the operating model, and I do not flinch at reimbursement-rate risk the way the spreadsheet investors will.

Counterfactual for the physical-verifiability dimension: for Cova to flip a tier here, I would need to be able to call (734) 707-7330 today and have a caregiver dispatched to a real Wayne County address within 48 hours, the way I rode Uber in 2010 and ordered Postmates in 2011. The dossier confirms the phone line is live and the email questions@cova.care is operational, which is more than the average pre-seed B2B API offers, but Cova's product is invisible to me unless I have a Michigan Medicaid-eligible family member -- the Niantic-Ingress test of "I can play it obsessively from 2012" does not transfer to a service I cannot consume in California. Cova clears the floor because the artifact is a caregiver visit, not a developer abstraction, but it does not approach the rung where Flock Safety's cameras were photographable from a Four Seasons parking lot.

Forecast 36 months out: Cova either pivots to selling its AI-agent stack to other licensed Michigan agencies (becoming Sage Care or MochaCare with a more credible operating-history wrapper) at sub-$15M ARR, or is acquired by a regional Medicaid managed-care plan for under $30M as a captive intake-and-billing arm. The mechanism is the founder-market gap: Tandon's Apple/Tesla pedigree builds the AI stack faster than the regulated-operations muscle accretes, and the first Michigan MDHHS audit finding will reveal whether AI-agent eligibility determination meets the compliance bar that Honor and Papa staff with licensed humans. Single-state licensing concentration means Cova cannot outrun the audit by geographic diversification, so the exit becomes either software-vendor pivot or trade sale to the payer that controls the reimbursement.

The strongest objection is HandUp -- I wrote a check into a dental-financing-for-homeless company in 2014 whose ceiling was modest because the founder's biography mirrored my own homelessness origin, and Hustle Fund cites this as the override that beats commercial-defensibility every time. Cova's dossier offers nothing analogous: Tandon is Purdue/Apple/Tesla, Ibe is plausibly Cornell CS, and neither founder is described as having lived the home care problem from inside the family. The HandUp exception requires biographical alignment, not category alignment, and Cova has neither founder origin on the page that activates it. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: a founder interview or a backstory page confirming that either Tandon or Ibe spent time as the family caregiver for a Medicaid Home Help-eligible relative, with a specific physical moment they felt the $12.25/hour wage as inadequate -- the Anduril-Luckey-garage equivalent for Detroit home care. Without that, Cova is the consensus YC AI-agent darling pattern dressed in the right regulatory clothes.
