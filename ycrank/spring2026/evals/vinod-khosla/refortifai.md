# Refortifai -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Microsoft released its Agent Governance Toolkit as free, MIT-licensed open-source software on April 2, 2026 — covering all ten OWASP agentic AI risks with sub-0.1ms latency across five programming languages. That single fact tells me almost everything I need to know about Refortifai's position. When the largest software company on earth gives away for free the exact capability you're trying to sell — intercept tool calls, apply policy, allow or deny — you are not sitting on a technology discontinuity. You are competing for implementation convenience in a commodity layer. Refortifai's YC page describes a "security agent that monitors your agent," and that's precisely what Microsoft just open-sourced with LangChain and CrewAI integrations already built. The pattern is architecturally straightforward: hook into the agent's tool-call pathway, evaluate against a policy engine, block violations. That's plumbing, not a breakthrough.

Refortifai's consequence magnitude if fully successful is "enterprises deploy AI agents with better governance." I need to be blunt: that is a useful outcome, but nobody's life fundamentally changes. Compare this to what I look for — OpenAI potentially reshaping all of human cognition, Commonwealth Fusion Systems delivering limitless clean energy, Impossible Foods feeding billions without animal agriculture. Refortifai at full success means CISOs sleep slightly better. The company is redistributing existing enterprise security spend into a new category, not creating demand that didn't exist. Noma Security has already raised $132M and reported 1,300% ARR growth in this exact space. Capsule Security raised $7M in the same month as Refortifai's YC batch. PromptArmor, a YC W24 company, already has Accel and Lightspeed behind it. Refortifai is entering a share fight, not building a new market.

Refortifai's website — which returned a 403 error during research — describes an entirely different product: "anti-cheat for AI models" that obfuscates model weights before distribution with "negligible overhead" and "full inference speed on any hardware." That's a fundamentally different technical challenge, a different buyer persona, and a different competitive set than agent runtime security. The dossier flags this as product direction ambiguity, and I'd frame it more harshly: if Refortifai's own public-facing artifacts can't agree on what the company does, the founders haven't yet found the problem worth their full commitment. A pivot can signal learning — I respect founders who change direction based on evidence. But the evidence of learning needs to be visible. I see no public demos, no press coverage, no traction metrics, no community adoption, no signal that Refortifai tested one hypothesis, learned from it, and moved decisively to the next.

Atman Kar's background as an RTL Digital Design Engineer at Texas Instruments building automotive RADAR hardware accelerators is genuinely strong systems engineering — exactly the kind of expertise that would matter for the model-weight obfuscation product, where hardware-level understanding of memory management and inference pipelines is the wedge. But Refortifai appears to have pivoted away from that product toward agent runtime security, where the relevant expertise is application security, policy engine design, and threat modeling. Rithik Jain completed 21 CS courses after starting in economics — that's adaptability, but not the kind of security domain depth this market requires. No founder has a confirmed cybersecurity background. When I funded Rocket Lab, Peter Beck didn't have an aerospace degree, but he'd built rocket engines in his garage — the outsider had done the work. Refortifai's team are outsiders to security without visible evidence that they've compensated through obsessive learning in the domain.

The bull case deserves construction: AI agents will become the primary computing interface for enterprises, and whoever controls the security checkpoint controls the most valuable chokepoint in the stack. CrowdStrike built a $70B company by securing endpoints when that was the emerging attack surface. If Refortifai becomes the CrowdStrike of agentic AI, the consequence magnitude jumps dramatically — every autonomous agent running through their layer. The model-weight obfuscation IP, if it actually delivers negligible-overhead protection, could be a genuine technical moat with no open-source substitute. And the team's hardware engineering depth is non-obvious for a security company but could enable runtime instrumentation that software-only competitors can't match. I've checked my Known Exceptions: DoorDash overcame weak technical novelty through enormous consequence magnitude and a high-velocity learning founder. Could Refortifai be similar? No — because DoorDash's consequence magnitude was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people, and Tony Xu demonstrated unmistakable execution velocity. Refortifai's consequence caps at enterprise security tooling, and neither the learning velocity nor the execution evidence is visible. The condition that flips me: if Refortifai demonstrates a proprietary runtime instrumentation technique — perhaps leveraging Atman Kar's hardware expertise — that achieves 10x better detection accuracy than Microsoft's toolkit with measurable, published benchmarks, and if enterprises are already paying for it, I would reconsider. Technology conviction, not a TAM slide.

Refortifai scores low on every dimension I weight most heavily. The consequence of success is moderate infrastructure improvement, not world transformation — and that's the filter that matters most to me. The technical approach is being commoditized in real-time by Microsoft's open-source release and $132M-funded Noma Security. The founders show no visible security expertise or demonstrated learning velocity in the domain. The ambition level is "build a security product for a recognized category" — competent but not audacious. And there is zero affordability thesis; Refortifai adds cost rather than collapsing it. This is the kind of company I described when I said I regularly see YC companies building AI tools for specific enterprise functions, and my response is: if you succeed, the world looks the same, just with slightly better plumbing.
