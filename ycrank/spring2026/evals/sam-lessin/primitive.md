# primitive -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Primitive is the competitive landscape it's entering. AgentMail -- a YC S25 company that does essentially the same thing -- raised $6M from General Catalyst with Paul Graham as an angel, already has 500+ B2B customers, and saw user counts quadruple in February 2026. Meanwhile, Google launched the Agent2Agent protocol under the Linux Foundation with 100+ technology partners. Primitive is a solo founder arriving 6-9 months late to a market that already has a funded winner and an open-source standard backed by the largest email provider on earth. This isn't a contrarian entry into an overlooked space -- it's a late entry into a validated and increasingly crowded one. That distinction matters enormously in my framework.

Ethan Byrd checks the "sane" box cleanly. Engineering at Google, Meta, AWS, and Microsoft. Founded Actual AI, raised $3.2M, had 32 active pilots. He's built things at scale and he's built in the agent space specifically. I take that seriously. But the idea -- email infrastructure for AI agents -- is about as insane as saying "agents need APIs." Gartner projects 40% of enterprise apps will have AI agents by 2026. Every developer building autonomous systems is thinking about communication channels. When AgentMail can raise $6M with Paul Graham writing a check, you're not operating in non-consensus territory. You're operating in a hot market where the signal-to-noise ratio is terrible. In my sane-person-insane-idea framework, a sane person with a sane idea is almost worse than the reverse -- you're buying a consensus opportunity where the founders' operational excellence gets arbitraged away by the fifteen other teams with the same insight.

My "cherry on top" test destroys this one completely. If AI agents don't proliferate massively, Primitive has zero business. Nobody needs another email webhook service for humans -- SendGrid, Mailgun, and Postmark already exist. The entire value proposition is load-bearing on AI being the core product dynamic. This is exactly the category I've been arguing against on CNBC and in the WTF VC deck: AI-first companies where AI IS the product. The company that would have been worthless pre-AI doesn't get stronger as AI commoditizes -- it gets more exposed, because every infrastructure provider with distribution can add the same webhook routing as a feature. AWS SES adding an agent-focused webhook endpoint isn't a product launch, it's a Tuesday afternoon.

The name is ironic because the company is not actually building a primitive. Email is the primitive -- SMTP has been a protocol since 1982. What Primitive is building is middleware on top of an existing protocol, and Google's A2A is the actual new protocol for agent interoperability. Calling yourself "Primitive" while operating at the application layer on someone else's protocol is aspirational branding, not architectural reality. Compare this to Helium, which built actual wireless infrastructure -- physical hotspots creating a new decentralized network -- or Aleo, which built genuinely new cryptographic primitives for zero-knowledge proofs. Those are protocol-layer bets where the company IS the infrastructure. Primitive routes emails to webhooks. That's a feature, and features get absorbed by platforms.

Here's the bull case, and I want to be honest about it: if agent-to-agent communication becomes as fundamental as human-to-human email, and Primitive somehow establishes itself as the default routing layer before A2A or an incumbent email provider absorbs the market, the narrative ceiling is genuinely enormous. Every agent sending every message through your infrastructure is an infinity story. And Byrd's unique experience -- having built at all four major cloud platforms AND having built an AI agent company -- gives him a specific vantage point on what agent communication infrastructure needs to look like that most founders wouldn't have. If Actual AI's 32 pilots taught him exactly where existing email tooling breaks for agents, that's the kind of operator insight I look for. The webhook-first architecture versus AgentMail's mailbox-centric approach could matter if developers strongly prefer event-driven patterns over inbox metaphors. But "could matter" is doing enormous work in that sentence, and I'd need to see developers actually choosing Primitive over a funded, established competitor with hundreds of customers to believe it.

The prior-venture ambiguity is the final piece that gives me pause. Byrd's LinkedIn still lists Actual AI. That company had $3.2M in funding and 32 pilots. What happened? Did those pilots convert? Did the company shut down? Is Primitive a pivot? The absence of any public documentation on this transition is concerning -- not because founders can't pivot, but because the pattern of "funded AI agent startup leads to new AI agent infrastructure startup" raises the question of whether the first attempt revealed that the agent space is harder than it looks, and whether infrastructure is the right response to that lesson or just a lateral move.
