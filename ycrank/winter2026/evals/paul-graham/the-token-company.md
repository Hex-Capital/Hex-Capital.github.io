# The Token Company -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The idea for The Token Company was conceived at a hackathon in Sweden. That's the first thing I noticed, and it concerns me more than anything else in the dossier. When I wrote about how to get startup ideas, the core point was that the best ones aren't generated -- they're discovered. Drew Houston didn't brainstorm file synchronization. He forgot his USB drive. The Collison brothers didn't attend a fintech hackathon. They'd been personally frustrated by payment APIs for years. Hackathons produce clever demonstrations of technical skill. They rarely produce the kind of deep, obsessive understanding of a problem that sustains a company through years of unglamorous work. An 18-year-old building a compression model at a hackathon is impressive engineering. But it's not the same as someone who has been running production LLM workloads at scale, watching their inference bill climb month after month, and finally snapping and deciding to fix it.

The competitive landscape confirms this isn't a schlep blindness opportunity. It's closer to the opposite. Microsoft Research published LLMLingua -- open source, 4,400 GitHub stars, integrated into LangChain and LlamaIndex. Apple has published research on prompt compression. There's a NAACL 2025 survey paper cataloging the breadth of existing approaches. A direct YC batchmate, Compresr, offers the same thing with a team of EPFL ML PhDs. When that many smart groups are already working on a problem, you're not looking at an idea that's been hiding in plain sight because the schlep scared everyone off. You're looking at a consensus category. The structural opening the company identifies -- LLM providers won't compress because they monetize tokens -- is weaker than it appears. Providers are already cutting prices aggressively, about 10x per year. And if compression genuinely improved output quality, providers would internalize it immediately, because quality is their actual competitive moat. The misalignment argument only holds for pure cost savings, which is precisely the value proposition that's evaporating.

The most interesting claim in the dossier is that compression actually *improves* LLM output quality -- +2.7 percentage points on financial QA, +4.0 on reading comprehension, and a +5% purchase amount lift for Pax Historia in a 268K-vote blind arena. If this is consistently true across workloads, it transforms the company from "save money on tokens" to "make your LLM applications work better," which is a fundamentally different and more durable value proposition. But extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and right now the benchmarks come from the company's own website. The Pax Historia case study is real -- 193 billion tokens per month, measurable commercial impact -- and that's more than most pre-seed companies can show. But one customer, likely connected through the YC network, running one specific workload type (game AI), doesn't yet prove the general claim.

Otso Veisterä is clearly a talented hacker. Programming since age seven, national competitions, Rust and CUDA and PyTorch, and he built the bear-1 and bear-1.1 models himself. His chrome2moz tool on GitHub -- a Chrome-to-Firefox extension converter in Rust -- shows the right instinct: build useful tools that scratch a personal itch. At 18, his technical ceiling is high. If I were evaluating him purely as a founder to back on whatever he eventually works on, I'd be interested. The concern is his co-founder situation. Rasmus Uusipaikka's role isn't publicly defined as co-founder, he has no GitHub presence, and his background is business analytics and a VC internship. I've seen this pattern before -- a strong technical founder and an early team member whose commitment and complementarity haven't been tested. The relationship's origin isn't documented. I once reported that nine companies in a single batch added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine fell apart within a year. I'm not saying that's what's happening here, but the absence of a clear co-founder story is a gap I'd want to understand.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. If you believe the quality-improvement claim generalizes, then The Token Company isn't selling cost reduction -- it's selling better AI outputs, and the market for that grows with every new LLM application. Otso has genuine ML engineering depth, which means the proprietary models could compound in quality as more diverse prompt data flows through the system. The consumption-based pricing at $0.05 per million tokens is clean and scalable. The Finnish operator angels -- from Wolt, Supercell, Hugging Face -- suggest that people who've built real companies find something credible here. And 18-year-old hackers who get into YC sometimes turn into formidable founders. But for me to invest my own money, I need the problem origin to feel inevitable rather than incidental, and I need the competitive landscape to suggest structural advantage rather than a footrace with well-credentialed rivals on the same track. Right now, this is a smart engineer building in a crowded, consensus category where the underlying economics are shifting against the core value proposition. I'd want to see the quality-improvement thesis validated independently and broadly before this becomes my kind of bet.
