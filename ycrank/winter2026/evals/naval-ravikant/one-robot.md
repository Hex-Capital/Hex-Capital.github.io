# One Robot -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is unusually deep, and it's the kind that emerges from years of direct struggle rather than career planning. Hemanth Sarabu and Elton Shon didn't study the VLA evaluation bottleneck from the outside — they hit it themselves at Industrial Next, training robots for precision manufacturing assembly. "Evaluating a new policy started taking longer than training it" is not a market research finding. It's a lived frustration. Hemanth carries dual masters from Georgia Tech (4.0 in both), a NASA JPL Early Career Achievement Medal, published robotics ML research, time at Google, and — crucially — bootstrapped a previous company to profitability before age 23. Elton spent five years inside Tesla's most intense hardware-software integration period, building Dojo infrastructure and surviving the Model 3 production ramp, then ran software at Industrial Next. They've been training robots together since 2020. This is the kind of founder-problem entanglement where the insight couldn't have been acquired through any shortcut — not reading papers, not doing a sprint in the domain, not hiring consultants. They know where physics-based simulators break down on deformable objects and contact-rich tasks because they've watched them break down, repeatedly, on their own robots.

What catches my attention beyond the founders is the infrastructure positioning. One Robot is not building a robot. They're building the evaluation and training layer that every team developing VLA policies needs. This is picks-and-shovels for the robotics AI gold rush — the same structural position as Alchemy for blockchain developers or Stack Overflow for the broader engineering ecosystem. When a new category of technology emerges, the teams building the tooling layer tend to capture durable value because they serve the entire ecosystem rather than competing within it. The question is whether this is truly a platform or a specialized consulting engagement with a software wrapper. Task-specific world models trained on individual customer data could mean each new engagement is bespoke — tight integration with unique robot configurations, camera angles, manipulation workflows. That's high switching costs but potentially low leverage. The bull case is that building world models across many customers produces transferable know-how and reusable model architectures that compound. The bear case is that each customer is essentially a custom project.

The contrarian angle is real but narrower than ideal. "Learned world models instead of hand-crafted physics engines" is a genuine paradigm shift — most robotics simulation practitioners still think in terms of better MuJoCo environments and URDF files. One Robot is saying: skip the physics engine entirely, train a generative model on real robot video data, and produce simulation rollouts that capture visual and interaction realism simultaneously. That's a different bet on how simulation should work. But NVIDIA validated the world foundation model concept with Cosmos in January 2025 and has 3M+ downloads of open-source models. The category is no longer non-consensus at the conceptual level. What remains contrarian is the task-specific application to contact-rich industrial manipulation — textiles, deformables, sub-millimeter assembly — where general-purpose world models fail. That's a narrower wedge of non-consensus, and it depends on whether the broader market comes to these founders or whether they remain niche.

The risk that keeps me honest: NVIDIA has compute, distribution, open-source models, and $1.23B-funded World Labs is building spatial AI from the other direction. A two-person team outrunning these organizations requires the structural argument to hold — that NVIDIA's incentive is to sell GPUs horizontally, not to build task-specific manipulation simulators for individual customer workflows. I believe that argument, but it's not guaranteed. The initial addressable market is also genuinely narrow: how many teams worldwide are actively training VLAs for industrial manipulation today? Dozens, maybe. Growth depends on the VLA ecosystem itself maturing, which adds a layer of dependency risk. The sim-to-real transfer gap for contact-rich tasks is a physics problem that no amount of engineering enthusiasm can wave away — and no public validation data has been shared.

The bull case that would make this exceptional: VLA models are the next foundation model category after LLMs, every robotics company will need evaluation infrastructure, and One Robot becomes the standard simulation layer the way Isaac Sim became the standard physics engine — but with learned models that are qualitatively better for the tasks that matter most. In that world, the data flywheel kicks in, each customer's robot data improves the foundation world models, and the task-specific expertise compounds into something NVIDIA can't replicate by staying horizontal. The founders' combined Tesla/JPL/Google/Industrial Next lineage gives them credibility to sell into enterprise robotics teams that most YC pre-seed founders couldn't access. If the VLA market grows at even half the rate the funding signals suggest, One Robot is positioned at the infrastructure layer where value concentrates. That's the bet I'd be making — not on this company's current revenue, but on the probability that they've identified the right constraint at the right moment with the right specific knowledge.

I'd write a check here. The founders pass my specific knowledge filter at a level I rarely see — they didn't just study this problem, they lived inside it and broke through the other side with a technical insight about how to solve it. The infrastructure positioning maps to my revealed portfolio preference. The contrarian timing is decent though not spectacular. My reservation is the leverage architecture — this could be a high-value but ultimately linear business if each customer requires bespoke world model construction. But at pre-seed, I'm betting on founders and positioning, not on proven unit economics. The specific knowledge is non-replicable, and the market timing window is open.
