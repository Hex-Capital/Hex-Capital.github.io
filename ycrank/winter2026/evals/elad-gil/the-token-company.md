# The Token Company -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The central question with The Token Company is whether you're building cost-optimization middleware for a commodity whose price is falling 10x per year. That's the structural reality: LLM inference costs are declining at a rate that historically erodes the value proposition of any efficiency layer sitting on top. If I'm evaluating the market's structural moment -- what changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this possible now -- I see a market that's moving *against* this company, not toward it. Enterprise LLM spend is scaling, yes, and 53% of AI teams report costs exceeding forecasts. But the providers themselves are attacking cost aggressively through model efficiency, speculative decoding, quantization, and architecture improvements. The absolute dollar savings from compressing input tokens shrink with every pricing reduction OpenAI and Anthropic announce. When I invested in companies like PagerDuty or Gusto, the underlying market was expanding and the pain was intensifying. Here, the pain is real today but structurally diminishing. That's the opposite of an inflection point.

The company's counter-argument is worth examining honestly: compression doesn't just save money, it *improves* output quality. The benchmarks claim +2.7pp on financial QA, +4.0pp on reading comprehension, and a 268K-vote blind arena showing user preference for compressed prompts. If this holds up across diverse workloads and is independently validated, it represents a genuinely non-obvious insight -- that LLMs perform better on cleaner inputs. That would reposition the company from cost optimization (shrinking market) to quality infrastructure (potentially expanding market). The +5% purchase amount lift for Pax Historia is the most compelling data point because it ties compression to a business outcome, not just a benchmark score. If I could see this pattern replicated across 10 different enterprise workloads with independent measurement, my conviction would change materially. But right now these are self-reported benchmarks from one named customer, and the quality improvement claim sits at the center of a well-studied academic space -- Microsoft's LLMLingua, Apple's hierarchical compression research, and a full NAACL 2025 survey paper all document approaches to this problem. The concept is not proprietary.

The product-to-distribution trajectory concerns me as much as the market timing. Compression middleware is a utility function, not a platform. When I look at my best investments -- Stripe becoming a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury; Figma's design tool expanding into FigJam and developer handoff -- the initial product created a customer relationship that made distributing additional products natural. What does a compression API distribute beyond compression? There's no obvious second product, no platform dynamic, no reason the customer relationship deepens over time. The API integration is lightweight by design, which means switching costs are near zero. Easy to adopt, easy to replace. A YC batchmate, Compresr, offers a directly competing API with a 4-person team of EPFL PhD-level ML researchers claiming 100x compression versus The Token Company's roughly 3x. Microsoft's LLMLingua is free and integrated into LangChain and LlamaIndex. This is a feature that any LLM orchestration framework can absorb.

On the founder side, Otso Veister? has shipped real product at 18 -- trained two model versions, built SDKs in Python and Node.js, secured a named customer processing 193B tokens monthly, designed a creative blind-arena validation study, and gotten into YC. That's genuine execution velocity, and I weight demonstrated building ability heavily. But the team composition raises questions about commercial execution. The second team member's role isn't even defined as co-founder, his background is analytics and VC internship, and neither has enterprise sales experience. Selling infrastructure middleware to developers is distribution-intensive work -- the founders who succeed at it are aggressive about go-to-market from day one, not just product from day one.

The strongest bull case: what if prompt compression is the "boring but critical" infrastructure layer I typically love? Every company running LLM applications could use it, it's unglamorous, and most investors dismiss it. That pattern -- the thing everyone overlooks because it seems like a feature rather than a company -- has worked for me before. But the difference between PagerDuty and a prompt compression API is structural defensibility. PagerDuty became deeply embedded in enterprise operations workflows with high switching costs and an expanding product surface. Compression middleware adds a stateless API call that can be swapped for an open-source alternative or absorbed by the platform layer above or below it. The "boring but critical" archetype requires that the boring thing also be sticky and expansible. I don't see evidence of either here.

I'm passing. The market is structurally challenged by declining inference costs, the competitive landscape includes open-source alternatives and a directly competing YC batchmate with stronger research credentials, the product-to-distribution trajectory is flat, and the quality-improvement thesis -- while intriguing -- is unvalidated outside self-reported benchmarks. The founder's execution velocity is the strongest signal, and if Otso pivots to a problem with better structural dynamics, I'd want to see it.
