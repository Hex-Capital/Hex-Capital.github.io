# Rubric AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Rubric AI is that it's entering one of the most crowded, consensus-funded spaces in tech right now. Scale AI is valued at $29 billion. Surge AI is bootstrapped to $1.2 billion in revenue and seeking a $25 billion valuation. Mercor is at $10 billion. Labelbox -- which has already launched its own "rubric evaluation" tooling -- is at a billion. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight. This is a problem that multiple billion-dollar companies are visibly, aggressively, and expensively attacking. When I wrote about schlep blindness, the key insight was that the best opportunities are ones people unconsciously filter out because the work is ugly. Nobody is filtering out "evals for frontier labs." Every AI infrastructure company on Earth is trying to win this contract.

The second thing I look for -- and the thing I care about most -- is how the founders found this problem. Did it grow organically from their own experience, or was it manufactured from reading about market trends? Pragya Saboo's background is in product management at Asana, Oscar Health, and TodayTix, plus investing at a climate fund. None of that connects to RLHF, model training, or AI evaluation in any way I can trace. There's no story here about personally struggling with evaluation quality at a frontier lab, no experience building reward models, no years spent watching the annotation bottleneck from the inside. The dossier shows a PM who shipped features at mid-stage companies and then started a company in the hottest category of 2025-2026. That pattern -- smart person notices a hot market and decides to build something in it -- is precisely the failure mode I wrote about in "How to Get Startup Ideas." The best founders work on problems because they can't stop thinking about them, not because the TAM chart looks compelling.

There's a ghost in this company: the unnamed second team member. The YC page lists team size as 2 but only identifies Pragya. It's entirely possible this person is a deep ML researcher who provides the technical credibility the "research and product lab" positioning demands. But I can't evaluate what I can't see, and the absence itself is a signal. When you're building a company whose entire value proposition is technical methodology for training frontier models, your technical co-founder should be your strongest public signal, not an unnamed phantom. The visible founder has no public GitHub repos and an industrial engineering degree. For a company that calls itself a "research lab," that's a significant gap between positioning and evidence.

Let me lay out the strongest bull case, because I want to be fair. The Meta acquisition of 49% of Scale AI genuinely disrupted the market. Google, OpenAI, and xAI reportedly pulled work from Scale AI over data-sharing concerns, creating real demand for independent alternatives. A small, trusted, non-conflicted evaluation vendor could be exactly what frontier labs want right now. If Rubric AI's unnamed co-founder is a former research scientist at one of these labs who watched the Scale AI acquisition happen and thought "I know exactly what these teams need and can't get anymore" -- that would be an organic discovery story worth paying attention to. And the "research lab" model, if executed well, could be genuinely higher-margin and stickier than the workforce-marketplace approach. But those are hypotheticals. The visible evidence points the other direction.

The customer concentration issue compounds every other concern. Fewer than twenty frontier labs exist globally as potential buyers. That means every customer relationship is make-or-break, and those customers are among the most technically sophisticated organizations on Earth. They will evaluate Rubric AI's technical depth with extreme rigor. A PM-led team will need to demonstrate research credibility that I don't see evidence of here. And because the market is so concentrated, you can't outrun a competitive disadvantage by finding more customers -- you have to go deeper with the same tiny set of buyers, which means your methodology and your people need to be world-class from day one.

I'm passing on this one. The idea exists because AI post-training is a visible trend, not because the founders stumbled into a painful problem nobody else wanted to touch. The competitive landscape is brutal and well-capitalized. The visible founder's background doesn't connect to the domain. And the "research lab" positioning requires technical credibility that isn't publicly demonstrated. Sometimes the right problem at the wrong time with the wrong team is just the wrong bet.
