# Foaster -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me state what I see here plainly: Foaster is making management consulting cheaper and faster. Not obsolete -- cheaper. They still have a human consultant scoping the engagement and delivering the final recommendations. The AI conducts the interviews, synthesizes the data, generates the analysis. The output is the same PowerPoint deck McKinsey would produce, delivered in days instead of months at a third of the price. That is an efficiency improvement to an existing workflow. It is not a new way for the world to operate. I invest in companies where success means the old system can't compete. Here, the old system just loses some margin.

The management consulting market exceeds $300 billion, and the innovator's dilemma for McKinsey, BCG, and Bain is genuine -- their entire business model is partners billing out junior analysts at 3-5x cost, and they structurally cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper alternative without destroying their own leverage economics. I find that dynamic real and interesting. But when I look at where the technology is heading, I see a problem: AI-conducted interviews and synthesis use generally available foundation models. Distyl AI has $202 million. Aily Labs has $101 million. PromptQL has $136 million. These are not incumbents defending a legacy -- these are well-funded startups converging on the same territory. When the established venture ecosystem validates and funds your category to the tune of half a billion dollars before you've launched, you are not an outsider pursuing something the consensus rejects. You are the consensus. My best investments -- Juniper, Impossible Foods, OpenAI -- were in spaces where the experts told me I was crazy. Nobody is telling anyone that AI will transform consulting is crazy. Every consulting firm, every AI startup, every business school case study is saying the same thing. That is not where I find asymmetric returns.

The founders are genuinely talented. Raphael Dabadie and Alexandre Combes are 23-year-old statistics students from ENSAE Paris -- outsiders to the consulting industry, which I like. Combes claims 60,000 AI-conducted calls at his prior venture Repondia, which, if verifiable, demonstrates real execution capability in the exact skill Foaster needs. Their Werewolf benchmark on LLM social intelligence attracted attention from OpenAI's president and xAI's co-founder -- 12 million views across their research papers at age 23 is a signal of intellectual velocity. Dabadie built a 400,000-person community starting at age 11, which tells me something about his ability to attract attention and build relationships. These are fast learners. But fast learners pursuing a consensus idea is not my pattern. I look for fast learners pursuing ideas that everyone else says are impossible. "AI makes consulting cheaper" is not that idea.

The strongest bull case is this: if Foaster's operational maps become a persistent data asset -- a living, continuously updated picture of how an organization actually functions -- they have created something genuinely new. Organizations today have no real-time understanding of their own operations. The consulting industry sells expensive periodic snapshots. If Foaster turns that into a continuous intelligence layer, the consequence of success becomes transformational: every company in the world understands itself in real time for the first time. That would be worth backing. But the company as described is not building that. Their framing is consulting engagements -- scoping, interviews, recommendations, delivery. The operational map is positioned as a follow-on asset, not the core product. The vision I'd need to see is "we are building the organizational operating system that makes consulting unnecessary." Instead, what I see is "we are a consulting firm that uses AI to do consulting better." There is a vast difference between those two ambitions.

I have a visceral reaction when I see a startup that has reduced its probability of failure to the point where the consequence of success is manageable. A consulting firm with AI tools is very buildable. Two smart founders from YC can absolutely get clients, deliver engagements, and grow revenue. They will probably build a good business. But I am not in the business of funding good businesses. I fund companies that, if they succeed, change how the world works. Making consulting 3-5x cheaper changes McKinsey's P&L, not civilization. These founders are too smart and too fast to spend their careers on a problem this incremental. If they came back to me and said "we're building the system that makes every organization self-aware in real time, and the consulting engagement is just how we get the first data" -- that would be a different conversation.
