# Ontora -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural opening here is genuine. Management consulting is a trillion-dollar industry built on billable human hours, and the principal-agent problem inside McKinsey is the same one I recognized at Google when I looked at Perplexity: the incumbents have the talent to build this, but their revenue model makes them agents of their own P&L. Every autonomous AI interview Ontora conducts is a consulting hour that McKinsey can never bill again. Celonis, meanwhile, is optimized for structured ERP event logs -- pivoting to unstructured conversational intelligence is a different technical and sales motion entirely. The incumbents are structurally trapped. That's the kind of opening where startups win.

But a structural opening is necessary, not sufficient. I need to see specific knowledge filling that opening, and here the signal thins. Maximilian Arnold spent two years at Porsche building CoAnalyst, an agentic RAG system for SAP procurement data. That's technically analogous to what Ontora does -- same architecture, same enterprise context. It's more than a resume line. But it's not the kind of knowledge density I look for. Two years as an AI product engineer inside one automotive company doesn't give you the deep, non-replicable understanding of how organizations actually diagnose themselves -- the political dynamics of stakeholder interviews, the ways process maps lie, the failure modes consultants have learned over decades. Arnold identified this market from the outside and applied a technical toolkit to it. That's competence, not obsession. If he left tomorrow, another strong engineer with LLM experience could rebuild the technical core. The company is not yet an extension of irreplaceable founder knowledge.

The leverage audit raises a harder concern. Yes, AI-conducted interviews scale at zero marginal cost per conversation -- that's permissionless leverage in form. But the leverage is rented. The underlying capability -- LLMs conducting open-ended interviews, RAG over enterprise documents -- sits on foundation models anyone can access. The dossier's own inference captures this precisely: "barriers to replication are moderate." This is the architecture I see in what I call the AI wrapper pattern. The moment Anthropic or OpenAI ships better enterprise agent frameworks, or when a team with deeper consulting domain knowledge enters, Ontora's orchestration layer compresses. Innovation without a moat flows to the customer. The question is whether accumulated organizational data across deployments could create a proprietary benchmark dataset -- a genuine data flywheel. That's plausible but unproven, and at waitlist stage with one testimonial, there's no evidence the flywheel has begun spinning.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Arnold's CoAnalyst experience is the seed of something deeper, and the Vertiv reference -- a Strategy Manager at a public company saying Ontora delivered in "a single afternoon" what took months -- represents genuine product-market pull, not a courtesy quote. If Ontora can land five more Vertiv-scale deployments through YC's network and accumulate cross-company process data, the benchmarking dataset becomes the moat. The consulting industry's structural inability to self-disrupt means the window stays open longer than in typical software markets. And three people shipping enterprise-grade diagnostics is exactly the team-to-value ratio I look for. If all of that compounds, this is a real business. But that's a lot of "if" stacked on thin specific knowledge and rented leverage.

Two co-founder signals give me pause. No visible shared history between the founders -- no common employer, no common university. And David Korn is essentially invisible: no confirmed LinkedIn, no Twitter, no GitHub. I don't invest in teams where I can't evaluate a third of the founding group. The principal authenticity signal is weak across the board -- Arnold transitioned from a Porsche engineering role to this startup, which reads more like opportunity identification than someone who couldn't stop working on this problem. Leon's HSG and START Global background adds business context but not the kind of consulting-industry immersion that would make this team uniquely qualified to replace McKinsey.

The democratization angle is the strongest dimension here. Organizational diagnostics -- understanding how your company actually works versus how the org chart says it works -- has been restricted to companies that can write seven-figure consulting checks. Giving every mid-market company access to that diagnostic capability is genuine access expansion. That pattern has produced my best investments. But democratization alone doesn't overcome thin specific knowledge and commoditization risk. I'd want to see this team accumulate domain depth rapidly through deployments, build proprietary data assets, and demonstrate that no one else is executing on this exact wedge. Right now, I see a competent team in a real structural opening, wielding borrowed tools.
