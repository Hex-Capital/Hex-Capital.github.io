# Inth -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch, the CEO of Vercel, called Inth's c15t product "@shadcn for Consent Management" in a tweet that reached 79,000 people. That's a powerful endorsement from the center of the modern web development ecosystem, and it tells me something specific: Inth has already escaped the building. Developers at Vercel, Expo, Zed, and Infisical are using it. The npm numbers — 1.8 million downloads, 388,000 in April 2026 alone, growing at 26.9% — confirm real adoption, not vanity metrics. Christopher Burns has built something developers genuinely want. But when I sit with this, I keep circling back to the question I ask every founder before anything else: tell me your story, not the business. And what the dossier gives me for Burns is a second-time founder, ex-CTO of Everfund, ten years of TypeScript, podcast host, open-source contributor. That's a capable technologist who spotted a real gap in existing consent tools. It's not a person whose life made them incapable of ignoring this problem. The founding narrative reads as "I was a developer who saw that CMPs were terrible" — and that's true, they are terrible — but it's career insight, not biographical forge.

Christopher Burns built Everfund processing donations across 33+ countries, which gave him direct exposure to multi-jurisdiction regulatory complexity. That's relevant experience, and I don't dismiss it. But Inth is competing in consent management, a space that every sophisticated investor immediately understands. Privacy regulation is expanding — the EU Digital Markets Act, India's DPDP Act, Canada's CPPA — and every developer hates OneTrust's 514ms script injection. Nobody shifts in their chair when you pitch "better consent tooling for developers." The Vercel endorsement is a signal of quality, but it's also a signal of consensus: this is the kind of bet that makes a room of VCs nod approvingly, not lean back uncomfortably. When I wrote my check for Flock Safety after spotting a WiFi network name at a YC event, the surveillance angle made half the people I told flinch. When Anduril was raising, defense tech was politically radioactive in San Francisco. Inth provokes no discomfort. It's a good product in a well-understood regulatory tailwind, and that's precisely the profile where I add the least differentiated value as an investor.

OneTrust has raised $1.13 billion and generates $500 million in annual revenue selling to compliance officers through enterprise sales motions. Inth is attacking from a completely different angle — bottom-up through developers, open-source distribution, zero-script architecture that benchmarks at 89ms versus OneTrust's 514ms. The structural insight is sound: incumbents built dashboard-driven tools for legal buyers and cannot easily pivot to developer-native SDKs without cannibalizing their core delivery model. This pattern reminds me of what Carta did to cap table management — taking something opaque, expensive, and locked behind gatekeepers, and making it accessible to the people who actually needed it. The CMP industry does extract value through information asymmetry: OneTrust charges $10,000+ minimum ACV for what amounts to a script injection, and developers have no visibility into how that script degrades their site's Core Web Vitals. There is a hidden tax here. But the victims of this particular invisible system are companies and developers, not vulnerable individuals navigating structures they don't understand. The opacity is commercial and technical, not the kind of structural exploitation of powerless people that drives my deepest conviction — the taxi medallion cartel, credit card terms that bury the real cost, property tax assessments that overcharge people who can't fight back.

Inth's defensibility picture is the strongest dimension. The 1,678 GitHub stars, 132 forks, and 411 releases show an active open-source community with contributors from Expo, Adobe, Vercel, and Google. Framework-native SDKs for React, Next.js, and Svelte create real switching costs once embedded in application code — you don't casually rip out your consent layer. The regulatory inflection point is genuine and compounding: every new jurisdiction (Quebec Law 25, India's DPDP Act) adds complexity that favors a well-maintained, jurisdiction-aware engine over ad hoc solutions. Burns created CookieBench as a benchmarking tool, which is a smart category-definition play, though its credibility is limited by being self-authored. The risk I weigh against this: the core consent UI layer is technically reproducible, the product is open-source under Apache 2.0, and a well-resourced player like Vercel itself could build a competing solution. Inth has no pricing page, no public revenue, and the conversion path from free open-source users to paying hosted customers is entirely unproven. The Carta parallel holds on the disruption vector but breaks on the moat — your cap table is existentially sticky in a way that a consent SDK is not.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Inth is becoming the default consent infrastructure for the modern web stack at exactly the moment when global privacy regulation is fragmenting across dozens of jurisdictions. The 1.8 million npm downloads and named customers like Vercel and Expo represent genuine infrastructure adoption, not hype. Burns has the technical credibility (decade of TypeScript, prior multi-jurisdiction compliance experience at Everfund) and ecosystem relationships (Rauch endorsement, Vercel community sessions) to capture this category before incumbents can pivot their architectures. If Inth becomes what Stripe became for payments — the developer-first layer that every modern application uses by default — the $732 million CMP market is just the starting point. I take this seriously. But I keep running the ten-year question: where does consent management go in a decade? It becomes table stakes, embedded invisibly in every framework. That's a good business outcome but it's not a transformation of what's possible. I don't see the infrastructure beneath the surface that secretly becomes something larger — Crusoe started as Bitcoin mining and became AI infrastructure because the underlying capability (portable compute at stranded energy) had a much bigger second act. Inth's consent engine in ten years is still a consent engine. The condition that would flip me: if Burns showed me a biographical reason this problem is personal to him — maybe a privacy violation that changed his life trajectory, or a regulatory failure he witnessed destroying something he cared about — AND evidence that the consent layer is a wedge into a broader privacy infrastructure platform (the DSAR SDK in alpha hints at this), I'd revisit.

Inth is a well-built developer tool with real traction and a sound competitive positioning against slow-moving incumbents. Christopher Burns is a competent second-time founder with relevant domain experience. The regulatory tailwind is real and expanding. But through my lens, this is a consensus bet in a consensus-attractive space, built by a founder whose connection to the problem is professional rather than biographical, attacking a system that is commercially opaque rather than structurally exploitative. The two dimensions that lock my verdict are the biographical forge — where the absence of a personal narrative beyond technical competence puts Burns in the "saw a market opportunity" category rather than the "this problem found me" category — and contrarian conviction, where the warm reception from the developer ecosystem (Rauch endorsement, Vercel adoption) is exactly the signal that tells me this isn't a Long Journey bet. Good company. Not my kind of weird.
