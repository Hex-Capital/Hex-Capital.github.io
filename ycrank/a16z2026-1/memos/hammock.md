## The Idea

**Problem:** ~40 million Americans hold HSAs with $159 billion in total assets (Truemed/Devenir, mid-2025), yet most account holders underutilize their funds because wellness expenses—gym memberships, massage, supplements, recovery devices—require a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN) from a licensed provider to qualify as HSA/FSA-eligible. Obtaining an LMN typically costs $50–$150 per letter and takes days to weeks through a traditional doctor (Hammock blog, Apr 2026). FSA holders collectively forfeit ~$4.5 billion annually due to complexity and missed deadlines (Burst/Pear VC press release, Feb 2026). Consumers currently navigate this with standalone LMN services, manual reimbursement claims, or simply forgo the tax benefit.

**Approach:** Hammock provides an HSA debit card bundled with an AI agent and an integrated doctor network. The agent scans users' existing purchase history to identify missed HSA/FSA-eligible expenses, then automatically generates LMNs through licensed providers for qualifying wellness purchases—gym, massage, supplements, sleep tech, and recovery (usehammock.co). The card enables direct payment at 200+ wellness brands including Equinox, Peloton, Eight Sleep, Therabody, ClassPass, Whoop, Oura, and AG1 (usehammock.co). The platform also supports interest/investment on HSA balances, expense consolidation (medical, Rx, vision, wellness), and Rx discounts (usehammock.co).

**Differentiation:** Competitors are merchant-side: Truemed ($34M Series A, a16z-led, Dec 2025) enables HSA/FSA checkout at participating merchants; Flex ($18M raised, YC-backed) provides "Stripe for HSA/FSA" payment infrastructure for merchants; Sika Health ($6.2M seed, Forerunner) operates an HSA/FSA marketplace; Burst ($3M seed, Pear VC, Feb 2026) offers post-purchase reimbursement for retailers. All require merchant integration. Hammock is consumer-side: it issues its own HSA card with unlimited LMNs, enabling tax-free wellness spending at any merchant, not just integrated partners. The retroactive purchase-scanning feature addresses already-spent money that competitors do not recover.

**Business Model:** Membership model with unlimited LMNs included; specific pricing tiers not publicly disclosed (usehammock.co, Apr 2026). The blog contrasts Hammock's bundled model against standalone LMN services charging $50–$150 per letter, positioning the membership as "often the most cost-effective option if you have multiple wellness expenses" (Hammock blog, Apr 2026). [Inferred]: Revenue likely comes from annual/monthly membership fees and potentially interchange revenue from the HSA debit card. The HSA custodial relationship may also generate yield on deposits and investment fees.

Projected to reach $168 billion by end of 2026 and $208 billion / 47 million accounts by end of 2027 (Devenir via Truemed). ## Defensibility

- **Data flywheel:** As users transact through the Hammock card, the platform accumulates purchase and eligibility data that could improve the AI agent's scanning accuracy. This does not yet exist at scale given the waitlist phase.
- **Doctor network and LMN infrastructure:** Building a compliant integrated provider network for LMN generation is operationally complex—requires licensed provider relationships, HIPAA compliance, and state-by-state telehealth regulations (Hammock blog, Apr 2026).
- **Custodial HSA relationship:** If Hammock acts as HSA custodian, switching costs increase significantly as users would need to transfer balances and re-establish investment positions.

[Inferred]: Defensibility is nascent. The primary moat opportunity is the combination of custodial account + LMN network + spending intelligence, which no single competitor currently bundles on the consumer side.

**Market structure:** Incumbent HSA custodians (Fidelity, HealthEquity, Optum Bank) focus on employer-sponsored distribution and investment yield, not consumer wellness optimization. [Inferred]: Adding an LMN network and AI purchase-scanning agent would be orthogonal to their core business of custody and investment management, creating a channel conflict with their employer clients who may not want to encourage expanded wellness spending.

**Commoditization risk:** The LMN-generation service alone is replicable—multiple telehealth platforms could add it. The AI purchase-scanning capability uses transaction categorization that is technically accessible to any fintech with card data. Truemed, with $34M in funding and a16z backing, could build a consumer-facing HSA card. The bundled consumer experience (card + agent + LMN + investment) is harder to replicate but not technically defensible.

## Key Risks

**Regulatory and compliance complexity:** Generating LMNs at scale through a telehealth provider network requires state-by-state telehealth licensure compliance, HIPAA adherence, and potential scrutiny from the IRS if LMNs are perceived as rubber-stamped. If the IRS tightens LMN requirements or audits wellness-related HSA deductions, the core value proposition erodes. No public mitigation strategy found.

**Truemed competitive escalation:** Truemed raised $34M from a16z (Dec 2025) in the same HSA/FSA wellness space (Athletech News). Truemed could launch a consumer-facing HSA card or LMN service, leveraging its existing merchant network of thousands of brands and a16z relationship. Both Hammock and Truemed are a16z-backed, creating potential portfolio tension.

**HSA custodial barrier:** If Hammock issues its own HSA debit card, it must either become a registered HSA custodian (requiring bank charter or trust company status) or partner with an existing custodian. This adds regulatory overhead, capital requirements, and dependency on banking partners. No public disclosure of custodial arrangement found.

**Name collision:** "Hammock" is shared with Hammock (usehammock.com), a UK-based landlord accounting software company with Trustpilot reviews and active press coverage (Trustpilot; TechCrunch, Aug 2020). This creates SEO competition and potential brand confusion.

**Founder departure risk (Berry Street):** Jesse Rose's departure from Berry Street—a company he co-founded that recently raised $50M (Fierce Healthcare, Mar 2025)—is not publicly explained. The timing and circumstances may warrant diligence.
