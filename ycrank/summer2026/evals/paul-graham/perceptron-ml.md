# Perceptron ML -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Michael and Peyton Marcotte are brothers, which is the first fact about Perceptron ML I actually like -- the Collison-brothers structural setup -- and the second fact I dislike is that neither brother has ever personally needed to find a mass-tort plaintiff before a competing law firm does. That pairing is the analytical signature of Perceptron ML: a Stripe-shaped team configuration wrapped around an idea neither founder has lived inside, and a website that returned HTTP 403 when the dossier writer tried to fetch it. The Marcottes look structurally promising and biographically distant from the problem at the same time.

**Airbnb (2009)** is the rung I keep reaching for on the resourcefulness ladder, because Chesky and Gebbia shipped revenue via the cereal-box stunt before any investor would write a check -- they left tracks. Perceptron ML's dossier names a "book a demo" page, a waitlist, a 478-follower LinkedIn, and no Product Hunt, no press, no disclosed pilot, no GitHub for either brother. The mechanism that made Airbnb worth $20K for 6% was that the founders generated the proof themselves before they walked in; the mechanism in front of me at Perceptron ML is a gated demo URL and a YC page. Animals leave tracks, and what the dossier records around Michael and Peyton Marcotte is a clean snowfield.

The pass example I wrote about the polished two-MBA AI-wrapper team is the failure mechanism I want to test Perceptron ML against on organic-idea fit -- that team failed because none of them had personally suffered the problem, and the deck did the talking instead of the artifact. Michael Marcotte's NVIDIA background is in hardware debugging and formal-verification agents; Peyton Marcotte's prior project, PMARC, built exercise equipment for astronauts with 1517 Fund support. Neither brother has ever had to identify a class-action signal at a plaintiff firm at 11pm and lose the matter to a rival who saw the filing first. The Marcottes spotted a vertical they could agent-ify, not a problem they have been bleeding from -- and the dossier offers no founder anecdote that contradicts this reading.

**Coinbase (2012)**'s compliance grind is the schlep parallel for Perceptron ML, and here Perceptron ML is closer than I expected. Selling to plaintiff/contingency law firms is unpleasant work -- relationship-driven, slow procurement, partner-level approvals, and the "human approval" gate Perceptron ML explicitly bakes into the loop suggests the brothers already know the buyer won't let an agent send outbound un-reviewed. That's a real schlep. But naming four verticals -- law, construction, investors, trading desks -- with a two-person team is the opposite of the Armstrong move; Armstrong narrowed into the one schlep nobody else wanted, while Perceptron ML's positioning fans out before there is one disclosed customer.

Hacker genealogy compounds over 18 months through the artifacts already shipped, and the Perceptron ML dossier is thinner than the Marcotte credentials should produce. Michael Marcotte's NVIDIA AI Research role on "production AI agents for hardware debugging and formal verification" is genuinely substantive technical work -- this is not a credential-only CS degree -- and Peyton Marcotte building astronaut equipment with NASA and Brown support is a hardware-hacker signal, not a resume bullet. What's missing is any public footprint: no GitHub repos, no Twitter presence for either brother, no open-source artifact, no Hacker News thread. The genealogy is there in employment history; the leave-tracks evidence I over-index on is not.

The counterfactual that would flip the co-founder score from a high mid to a top rung is two more years of documented Marcotte-on-Marcotte building -- the Collisons' 2005 Young Scientist work preceded Stripe by five years, and Patrick and John had been hacking commerce together since Auctomatic. The Marcotte dossier confirms they are brothers attending S26 together and confirms each has independent prior projects, but does not document a shared build history. Brothers is the right shape; co-built is the missing word.

My 36-month forecast for Perceptron ML: the company hits under $500K ARR on the legal vertical against EvenUp ($385M raised), Eve ($164M raised), and Darrow ($60M raised) extending into the detect-and-act seam, then pivots horizontally into general agentic outbound for sales teams, or gets absorbed sub-$25M by FirmPilot as a signal-trigger feature bolted onto its marketing engine. The mechanism is that the human-approval throughput ceiling Perceptron ML built in caps the "hours, not weeks" speed claim that is the entire pitch, while Eve and EvenUp's capital lets them ship the same loop with existing law-firm relationships the Marcottes do not have.

The strongest case against my forecast is the **Airbnb (2009)** known exception -- I funded a team whose idea I had silently rejected because the founders passed the animal test, and the Marcotte-brothers configuration plus Michael's production AI-agent experience plus Peyton's NASA-backed hardware shipping is a real founder profile, not a credential profile. The bull mechanism is that the brothers replicate the Collison trajectory in a different vertical and the legal entry point is a wedge, not a destination. What dismantles this is the single dossier fact that Airbnb at the equivalent stage had 1,000 cereal boxes sold at $40 each and Perceptron ML has a demo waitlist -- the override signal in the Airbnb exception was a pre-funding stunt I would not have thought of, and Perceptron ML has shipped nothing comparable. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: one named plaintiff-firm customer using Perceptron ML in production with disclosed signal-to-campaign cycle time under 24 hours within the next six months. Until then, pass.
