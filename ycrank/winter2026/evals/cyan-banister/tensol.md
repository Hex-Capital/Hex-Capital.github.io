# Tensol -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that jumped out to me first isn't the team or the technology — it's that this company pivoted mid-batch from Solstis, an AI agent for ERP systems in manufacturing, to Tensol, a managed hosting platform for the most-starred open-source project on GitHub. That's not a pivot born from deep insight. That's a pivot toward what's popular. And I've learned, sometimes painfully, that the most promising companies are not popular. When I see 265,000 GitHub stars, a dozen managed hosting competitors already launching, and an open-source MIT license that means any cloud provider can replicate your product in a quarter — that's consensus, not conviction. The whole AI agent space right now is the definition of a hot market where everyone is chasing the same ball. This is the opposite of how I find my best companies.

When I think about the founders, I want to understand the biographical thread — why these two people, why this problem. Oliviero taught himself to code starting at 17, attended École 42 and a hospitality business school, dropped out of NUS, and previously worked at Stacksync building workflow automation. There's a scrappiness there that I notice. Pratik has a CMU AI engineering degree and worked at Rivian and Magna. They're clearly capable engineers. But here's what I can't find: the story that connects either founder to managed AI employee deployment specifically. With Solstis, at least Oliviero's hospitality background gave him a biographical anchor to ERP systems in that industry. They walked away from the version of the company where the founder's story matched the problem. Now they're building infrastructure for an open-source framework they didn't create, whose creator just left for OpenAI. I keep asking myself — would I drop everything to work for these founders on this specific problem? — and the honest answer is I don't feel that pull. The biographical connection between founder and mission, the thing I always look for first, isn't there.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it: there's a pattern in my portfolio where I back infrastructure at inflection points — SpaceX for private space, Crusoe for energy-to-compute, Together.ai for AI infrastructure. You could argue Tensol is "Heroku for AI agents" at exactly the moment the market is exploding. If OpenClaw truly becomes the standard framework for autonomous agents the way React became the standard for web UIs, then the managed deployment layer captures enormous value. The enterprise features — SOC 2 compliance, credential injection at the network level, sandboxed VMs — are genuinely useful and take time to build well. And the founders showed adaptability by pivoting quickly when their original thesis wasn't working. If you believe AI agents are about to become as ubiquitous as SaaS apps, and that most companies won't want to self-host, then being early with enterprise-grade hosting is a big opportunity. For this to work, Tensol would need to build such deep integrations, organizational memory, and ecosystem lock-in through ClawHub that switching costs become prohibitive before the larger players arrive. It's possible, but it requires winning a footrace against well-capitalized incumbents with no structural head start.

What I don't see is a moat. OpenClaw is MIT-licensed — anyone can build a managed layer. Kimi Claw launched the same month. TrustClaw is targeting enterprise. Perplexity has its own agent product. AWS, GCP, and Azure could offer managed OpenClaw hosting as a checkbox feature. The 13,000 ClawHub skills and persistent organizational memory create some switching costs over time, but those are earned advantages, not structural ones, and they take years to compound. Meanwhile, the NBER study finding that 90% of firms report zero measurable productivity impact from AI agents gives me real pause about the entire category. I've been comfortable investing in spaces that look uncertain — Crusoe mining Bitcoin on gas flares, Contraline building male contraception — but those were uncertain because they were weird and unpopular, not because the underlying value proposition hadn't been proven. Here the uncertainty is whether the core product category actually works, which is a different and more dangerous kind of risk.

The co-founder dynamic is hard to read. No shared history before what appears to be a YC-era connection. They've already pivoted once together, which tells me they can make decisions under pressure, but it doesn't tell me how they fight or how they resolve fundamental disagreements. After HQ Trivia taught me that even perfect product-market timing can't survive founder conflict, I probe this hard, and there just isn't enough signal here. The fact that one founder comes from hospitality and the other from automotive engineering means they have complementary skills but possibly very different worldviews about what this company should become — especially given they've already changed direction once.

I respect the hustle and the technical competence, but this doesn't fit how I invest. There's no biographical grit story forging the founders to this problem, no weird non-consensus angle that other investors are dismissing, no clear path to economic access for underserved populations, and no structural moat in a space where barriers to entry are essentially zero. This is a capable team chasing the hottest market in tech with a product anyone can replicate. I pass.
