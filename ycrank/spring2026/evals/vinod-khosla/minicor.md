# Minicor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the question I keep coming back to: if Minicor succeeds completely -- dominates self-healing desktop automation, displaces UiPath, captures the entire legacy RPA market -- what has changed about the world? Healthcare organizations process patient records a bit faster. Accounting firms click fewer buttons in QuickBooks. Citrix sessions get automated without someone rebuilding a script every time a dialog box moves three pixels. That is a real business. It might even be a big business. But nobody's life is fundamentally different. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The consequence of this company's maximum success is operational savings in back-office workflows. I have built a career around the distinction between companies that make existing systems 30% cheaper and companies that make existing systems irrelevant. Minicor is firmly in the former category.

The bull case deserves real engagement because the traction signals are unusually strong for pre-seed. Processing 25,000 patients per day in production, SOC 2 Type II certification, HIPAA compliance -- for a four-person team founded in 2024, that is exceptional execution. Most YC companies at Demo Day have a landing page and a waitlist. These founders have enterprise customers running production workloads. If you believe legacy desktop software will persist in regulated industries for two more decades -- and in healthcare, that is a reasonable bet -- then the market is not shrinking, it is growing as labor costs rise and the administrative burden accelerates. The pivot from Laminar to Minicor suggests founders who are tracking signal, adapting, and moving fast. If computer use models improve at the rate foundation model capabilities have been improving, self-healing agents could eventually achieve near-perfect reliability, making scripted RPA structurally obsolete. In that scenario, Minicor becomes the infrastructure layer between AI capabilities and enterprise legacy systems -- a genuinely valuable position. That is a plausible path to a multi-billion-dollar outcome.

But I still cannot get past the structural ceiling. The technology Minicor wraps -- vision-based computer use -- is not theirs. It is Anthropic's, OpenAI's, Google's. They are building an infrastructure and compliance layer on top of foundation model capabilities they do not control. When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu was building the core routing technology. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown was engineering the heme molecule. The deep technology was the company's own. Minicor's self-healing mechanism is a sophisticated application of someone else's models with an operational moat of compliance certifications and deployment infrastructure. That is defensible against other startups today, but if Anthropic decides to ship a HIPAA-compliant computer use product for healthcare -- and their computer use capabilities are already the foundation Minicor likely builds on -- the moat evaporates. The company is one platform decision away from competing against its own supplier.

The founding team exemplifies a pattern I am skeptical of: enterprise insiders executing a logical playbook. Microsoft, Ford, Morgan Stanley, Kinaxis -- these are establishment credentials. The founders are not outsiders bringing irreverence to a stale industry; they are experienced enterprise engineers who saw the computer use capability emerge and built the obvious infrastructure layer around it. That is smart engineering, not contrarian thinking. Nobody laughed at them for this idea. No incumbent CTO told them it was impossible. The consensus is that AI-powered automation will replace scripted RPA -- every major RPA vendor, every cloud provider, every AI lab is moving in this direction. When the experts endorse your approach rather than dismissing it, I worry you are building something incremental enough to be non-threatening.

The timing is genuinely favorable -- computer use models crossed a reliability threshold in late 2024, creating a narrow window to build the production infrastructure before incumbents or model providers capture it. But favorable timing that is visible to everyone is not a timing advantage. Browserbase raised $40 million. Browser Use raised $17 million. Skyvern is in market. UiPath is actively integrating AI. The window is real but crowded, and Minicor's differentiation -- desktop and Citrix rather than browser -- targets a deployment surface that is, by definition, the technology the world is migrating away from. They are building increasingly sophisticated tooling for a class of software that enterprises are, slowly but inevitably, replacing. In regulated industries, "slowly" might mean two decades, which is enough to build a substantial business. But it is not enough to build a category-defining company that transforms how an industry operates.

This is a competent team, in a real market, with genuine traction, executing well. And that is exactly the profile I pass on. They have reduced uncertainty to the point where the upside is bounded. A successful Minicor is a solid enterprise software company worth hundreds of millions, maybe a few billion. It is not a company that makes me say the world operates differently because they exist.
