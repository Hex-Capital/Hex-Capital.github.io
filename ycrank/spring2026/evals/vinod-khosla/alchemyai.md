# Alchemy -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Alchemy's dossier itself names the core problem: "The core technical challenge — assembling domain-specific CV pipelines from natural-language prompts — relies on prompt engineering and model orchestration rather than proprietary model architectures." That sentence, buried in the commoditization risk section, is the entire evaluation. Alchemy is orchestrating CellProfiler, Cellpose, and other open-source segmentation tools through an LLM interface. There is no novel science here. No proprietary model. No step-function technical insight. This is a UX layer on top of free, well-maintained tools backed by the Broad Institute and Chan Zuckerberg Initiative. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown had synthesized heme protein — a genuine biochemistry breakthrough that no competitor could replicate by calling an API. Alchemy's 98% time reduction claim is a workflow improvement, not a technology discontinuity. Leica could ship an LLM integration into Aivia in a quarter. Any competent ML team with access to GPT-4 and Cellpose can build a natural-language pipeline assembler over a weekend hackathon.

Alchemy's consequence magnitude is where I lose interest entirely. If Alchemy fully succeeds — becomes the dominant microscopy analysis platform for every research lab on Earth — what changes? Tens of thousands of researchers save a few hours per week on image analysis. That's useful. It is not consequential. The chain from "faster microscopy pipelines" to "better human health outcomes" has too many links, each with its own bottleneck. Drug discovery doesn't fail because a PhD student spends three hours on H. pylori image analysis instead of ten minutes. It fails because of target validation, clinical trial design, regulatory pathways, manufacturing scale-up. Alchemy is optimizing one node in a complex system, and not the rate-limiting node. The live cell imaging TAM is $3.13B growing to $4.75B by 2030 — and Alchemy's serviceable segment is a fraction of that. I regularly pass on companies where the consequence of full success is "a moderately profitable SaaS company in a real but bounded market." Alchemy fits that pattern precisely.

Phillip Baek brings a BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin and a stint at Boston Scientific — relevant background, but no published research in microscopy or computational biology. Liam McBride holds an MS in CS from UIUC and built a shared-living app that raised $20K in non-dilutive funding. Neither founder has Google Scholar results, PubMed publications, or confirmed GitHub repositories. I don't require domain expertise — I funded a 19-year-old dropout at Mach Industries to compete with Lockheed Martin — but I require evidence of extraordinary learning velocity or first-principles thinking that overrides the credentials gap. Alchemy's founders show competence (YC acceptance, a working product, a PhD student endorsement) but nothing in the dossier signals the kind of intellectual escape velocity I look for. The BoBo app collaboration demonstrates they can ship product together, which matters, but it's baseline.

The strongest bull case for Alchemy: PathAI raised $490M and was acquired by Quest Diagnostics, proving AI-powered image analysis in life sciences can become very large. Alchemy targets the underserved research lab segment that PathAI, Proscia, and Aivia have ignored in favor of clinical diagnostics and large pharma. The natural-language interface is genuinely novel for this user base — CellProfiler requires Python scripting, ImageJ requires manual parameterization. If Alchemy accumulates a proprietary model library through researcher contributions, network effects could create defensibility over time. The UC Santa Cruz PhD endorsement — three hours reduced to under ten minutes — is real user love. And the timing is right: LLM orchestration of domain-specific tools was not feasible before 2024. I've checked my Known Exceptions: DoorDash had no technical novelty but I funded it because the consequence magnitude was planetary-scale (food access for hundreds of millions). Does that exception apply? No. Alchemy serves thousands of researchers, not hundreds of millions of consumers. The consequence magnitude gap is too large.

Alchemy would need to show me one thing to flip my evaluation: evidence that their platform is becoming the system of record for an entire research institution's imaging data, with proprietary models that produce materially better biological insights than any open-source alternative — not just faster analysis, but analysis that discovers things human researchers miss. That would shift the consequence from "productivity tool" to "scientific discovery engine," which is a different company entirely. Without that signal, Alchemy is a well-executed vertical AI copilot — exactly the category I pass on because the upside ceiling doesn't justify my attention. I want to either lose my money or make fifty times my money. A SaaS tool saving researchers time in a sub-billion-dollar serviceable market is designed to return 3-5x, which in my framework is indistinguishable from zero.
