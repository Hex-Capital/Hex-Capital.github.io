# Modern -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Seb Poole has two prior exits — a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in 2023 — and raised roughly 20M AUD for an e-commerce venture that included Alibaba among its investors. That's a real track record. I follow founders across companies; it's one of the strongest signals I know. I backed Patrick Collison at Auctomatic years before Stripe existed, because I'd seen what he could do. Seb has shipped, sold, and come back to build again, and he's paired himself with Aleks Tomovski, whom he calls "the most exceptional engineer, leader and executor I have met." A repeat founder with two exits and a strong technical co-founder is a combination I take seriously. But what I can't find anywhere in Modern's dossier is the story — the specific moment of frustration, the creative hack, the weekend prototype built from obsession rather than opportunity identification. Prior exits tell me Seb can execute. They don't tell me he has the animal quality for this particular problem.

Modern is building AI agents for IT, HR, and Finance service desks — resolving tickets autonomously rather than routing them. The market is real: ITSM spend sits at roughly $15 billion in 2026 and is growing at 16% annually. And the timing argument is legitimate. Moveworks was acquired by ServiceNow for $2.85 billion, Aisera by Automation Anywhere for roughly $1.5 billion, both in 2025. The two largest independent AI-ITSM players have been absorbed into incumbent platforms whose roadmaps now serve acquirer strategy rather than customer need. That creates a genuine vacuum. An advocate for Modern would argue this is exactly the moment for a fast, independent entrant — the incumbents are distracted by integration, mid-market buyers have nowhere to go, and Modern's "live in under a day" deployment claim is the kind of compressed value proposition that wins in the gap. Add SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, and GDPR certifications at a two-person team, and you have enterprise readiness that most pre-seed companies can't match.

Here's my problem: everyone sees this opportunity. Ravenna raised $15 million from Khosla Ventures and Madrona in April 2025. Workativ offers a nearly identical AI-agent feature set with Slack and Teams integration. The ITSM category has produced multi-billion-dollar acquisitions precisely because the market is obvious and large. Nobody hears "AI agents that resolve IT tickets" and says "that will never work." They say "of course" — and then they ask which of the fifteen companies doing it will win. My entire Black Swan framework is built on the premise that the best investments initially look wrong. Modern's idea looks right to everybody, which in my experience means the return premium has already been competed away. Stripe worked not because payments were a big market — though they were — but because the specific schlep of PCI compliance and bank integrations repelled every other developer-founder. IT ticket automation repels nobody. It's one of the most popular pitches in enterprise AI. Modern is swimming in the same direction as the current, which feels safe but means it has to out-execute rather than out-think.

Neither Seb nor Aleks appears to have been an IT operations worker, HR administrator, or finance ops person. Seb's background is e-commerce and mental health tech. Aleks has DevOps and SRE experience across multiple geographies, which gives him adjacency to ITSM tooling — he's almost certainly used ServiceNow or Zendesk as a ticket submitter — but adjacency is not the same as living inside the problem. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he was solving his own daily frustration with forgotten USB drives. When the Octopart founders built a parts search engine, they were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic components. Modern's origin story, to the extent one exists in public, reads more like market opportunity identification than organic discovery. The customer outcome metrics on Modern's website — 62% ticket reduction, 74% faster response — appear without confirmed user counts or revenue, and the pricing page displays "Coming soon" as of April 2026. I can't tell whether these numbers come from real deployments or projections.

The "Modern Managed" offering worries me structurally. When Modern operates the customer's entire IT service desk, that's a services business. It scales linearly with customer count and requires headcount growth. If doubling customers merely doubles the operations team, the company is a consultancy, not a startup. I wrote that a startup is a company designed to grow fast, and the growth must compound — how well you do has to depend on how well you've done. A managed-services tier alongside a SaaS platform creates tension between the margin profile investors want and the revenue that's easiest to close. At two people, Modern can't run managed services for more than a handful of accounts anyway, so this offering is either aspirational or it's consuming bandwidth that should go toward building the autonomous product.

One verifiable condition would flip me toward Invest: if Modern can show me three to five paying customers whose ticket resolution rates match the website claims, with organic retention — meaning those customers stayed and expanded without hand-holding — that would demonstrate that the product creates genuine value despite the crowded field. Execution in a consensus market can work when the execution is meaningfully better, and Seb's repeat-founder pattern suggests he might be capable of it. But right now, Modern sits in a gap between a strong founder profile and an idea that fails my most basic filter. Seb Poole's track record keeps Modern out of Pass territory — I respect what two exits and a third at-bat represent. But the maximally consensus market and the absence of organic problem discovery are the dimensions that define this evaluation, and on both, Modern falls short of what I need to write a check.
