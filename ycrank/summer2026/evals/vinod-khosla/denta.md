# Denta -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Jack Beecher is a solo Dartmouth dropout selling AI back-office automation into his father's 10-practice Iowa DSO, and that single sentence tells me what kind of company Denta is in my framework: a domain-adjacent workflow optimizer whose ceiling is bounded by the $2.15B global dental PMS TAM and whose floor is set by the fact that **Adit** already ships an AI Front Desk module natively. The highest dimension on the Denta ladder is a thin sliver of founder-defies-consensus -- a college dropout who saw his father's back office and decided to rebuild it -- and the lowest is consequence magnitude, because nothing about a world where Denta wins is civilizationally distinct from a world where CareStack ships the same module 18 months later. That high-low pairing is the analytical signature, and it is the exact signature of my Pass Example: an LLM wrapper on a single back-office workflow inside one persona.

**Impossible Foods** (2011) sits at the high rung of my consequence ladder because the success-state collapses animal-protein production -- a named incumbent regime measured in hundreds of billions. Denta's success-state, by contrast, is a dental practice that posts insurance payments at 9am instead of at 11pm; the mechanism is the same shape (eliminate a labor line), but the consequence is bounded by a $0.88B North American SAM and 60 piloting practices. The dossier offers no labor-cost displacement frame at scale, no "$15 trillion of GDP" anchor, no incumbent regime that disappears -- it offers a friendlier front desk at Kimball & Beecher Family Dentistry. That is the divergence point: Denta is a feature inside a vertical SaaS category, not a category collapse, and I do not write checks against features.

**Range Fuels** (~2007) failed because the capital plan understated the burn required for the science to work, but the more instructive failure mode for Denta sits at the low rung of my technical-risk ladder -- the GPT-4 sales-call summarizer I pass on in 30 seconds. That pattern -- LLM agent on top of fragmented vendor APIs, no proprietary stack, no science risk -- appears almost cleanly in Denta's dossier: the moat is described as "Pre-existing access to a 10-practice DSO" and the future moat is an [Inferred] structured data layer that is "unproven because product launched ~two weeks before YC profile capture." There is no falsifiable miracle here. HIPAA-compliant integrations with Dentrix and Eaglesoft are engineering, not physics. Denta could be built by any Tier-1 generalist's SaaS portfolio company, and that is precisely why my edge is absent.

On Denta's founder-defies-consensus dimension, the mechanism analysis matters more than the rung -- Jack Beecher dropped out of Dartmouth CS & Math, which is a real defiance signal, but he dropped out *into* his father's dental operation, which is the inverse of the **Physical Intelligence** (2024) Levine/Finn pattern where the founder is a Stanford or Berkeley PI who has publicly contradicted their field. Eighteen months of compounding here either looks like a dropout who builds a $5M ARR vertical SaaS and stalls, or a dropout who discovers the back-office data layer was the wrong wedge and pivots -- but the dossier shows no public thesis Jack Beecher has staked that experts would dismiss.

On learning rate, the counterfactual: for Denta to flip from a 5/15 to a 10/15 on this dimension, Jack Beecher would need a documented prior pivot -- a sold company, a public "we were wrong about X" moment, a Kanye-pivot story of the kind that justified my $100M Series C into **Slash** (2024). The dossier shows a linear plan: dropped out, joined family business, built software for family business, launched two weeks before YC capture. No pivot evidence does not mean rigidity -- the company is two weeks old -- but it means I cannot underwrite the override that **Slash** got, and the YC pedigree alone is not enough.

On required-capital profile, the single-anchor parallel is **DoorDash** (2013): Denta and DoorDash both run capital-light SaaS economics where my $50M check size is structurally over-resourced for the round. DoorDash earned the exception on ambition and consequence -- consumer-commerce flow at national scale. Denta's $3,000 MRR on 60 piloting practices, with no pricing page on trydenta.com, implies a per-practice subscription that reaches $10M ARR through ~3,000 practices and stops -- a deployment curve no version of my check size fits.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Denta either gets acqui-hired by CareStack or Adit for under $20M as an AI back-office module, or trickles to $1-3M ARR serving sub-150 practices concentrated in Iowa and adjacent DSO referrals, before consolidating into an incumbent's roadmap by month 30. The mechanism is straightforward -- Adit already ships AI Front Desk, CareStack has $145M+ in capital, and a solo founder running a multi-system HIPAA integration layer against two well-capitalized PMS incumbents is the unit-economics fight Denta loses by attrition, not by a single bad quarter.

The strongest bull case against my pass is that Denta's father-DSO access is a genuinely unusual distribution wedge -- 10 paying Iowa practices as design partners is a denominator most pre-seeds would kill for, and if Jack Beecher converts that into a proprietary structured data layer across Dentrix, Eaglesoft, and Open Dental, the switching costs compound and Denta becomes the vertical Glean of dental back-office. The bear case wins because **Glean** got my override on capital scale and Fortune-500 information-asymmetry consequence -- Arvind Jain's domain-expert profile was acceptable because the consequence was civilizational. Denta has neither the capital scale nor the consequence magnitude to justify the same override, and the McKinsey 1986 forecast of <1M cellphones by 2000 cuts the other way here: experts extrapolate the past, but so do dropouts who only know their father's practice. The condition that flips my verdict is a signed multi-DSO contract -- say, a 50+ location DSO outside Iowa with no Beecher-family connection, paying real per-location pricing -- because that would falsify the design-partner-concentration risk in a way nothing in the current dossier does.
