# RunAnywhere -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most revealing fact in this entire dossier is the Product Hunt tagline: "Ollama but for mobile, with a cloud fallback." That tells me exactly what this company is. Ollama is a useful piece of software. It is not a civilization-scale bet. RunAnywhere has taken a real problem -- the fragmentation of on-device AI runtimes -- and calibrated their ambition to the scale of a developer convenience tool, not an industry transformation. If RunAnywhere succeeds completely, what changes? Developers have a nicer SDK. Deploying models to phones takes three minutes instead of three days. Fleet management gets a dashboard. That is a business. It might even be a good business. But nobody's life changes, no trillion-dollar industry gets disrupted, and the world operates exactly the same way it did before. The consequence of success is a managed infrastructure layer -- not the kind of outcome that justifies the opportunity cost of two talented engineers' careers.

The structural problem is deeper than ambition calibration. RunAnywhere builds an abstraction layer over runtimes owned by Google (TensorFlow Lite, MediaPipe), Meta (ExecuTorch), Microsoft (ONNX Runtime), and Apple (CoreML). The dossier's inferred insight -- that these companies won't build a competing control plane because it would cannibalize their cloud inference revenue -- is clever but fragile. Google doesn't need to monetize on-device fleet management directly; they need developers locked into their ecosystem. The moment on-device AI becomes strategic enough to matter at scale, any of these companies ships a first-party fleet management solution as a free feature of their platform, and the abstraction layer above their runtime becomes redundant. This is not Juniper challenging Cisco with a fundamentally different architecture. This is a middleware company whose foundation is controlled by potential competitors. When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu was building technology that would make Cisco's approach *structurally obsolete*. RunAnywhere makes Cisco's equivalent -- in this case, Google's and Apple's runtimes -- slightly easier to use together. That is the opposite of disruption.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. On-device AI is at a genuine inflection point -- quantized LLMs running on phones is a 2024 phenomenon, NPUs are proliferating, and cloud inference costs are pushing enterprises toward edge deployment. The timing is the strongest dimension of this company. If I apply my rate-of-change framework, the trajectory of on-device AI capability is exponential and the category is pre-consensus. Docker became a $2 billion company by winning the containerization abstraction layer at the right moment. If RunAnywhere wins the equivalent position for on-device AI, the 10.2K GitHub stars could be the leading indicator of a developer ecosystem that compounds. And the control plane -- OTA model updates, policy-based routing, fleet analytics -- addresses a genuine operational gap that no incumbent currently fills. I take that seriously. But Docker, even at its best, is a useful infrastructure company, not an industry-transforming one. The category ceiling for "best abstraction layer for deploying existing technology" is a successful mid-cap outcome, not the kind of return that justifies the portfolio math I run.

The founders show persistence -- five YC applications before acceptance is a signal I don't dismiss. But their profiles are precisely the pattern I worry about: an Intuit SDK engineer and an AWS distributed systems engineer building exactly what their former employers trained them to build. They are insiders executing the insider playbook. Sanchit shipped SDKs used on millions of devices at Intuit; now he is building... an SDK. Shubham built distributed systems at AWS; now he is building... a distributed control plane. There is no outsider perspective here, no first-principles rethinking of how AI should run on devices. They are making the existing approach more convenient, not inventing a new one. Compare this to Peter Beck at Rocket Lab -- a self-taught engineer from New Zealand who didn't know what the aerospace industry considered impossible, which freed him to build a commercially viable launch vehicle. The RunAnywhere founders know exactly what is considered possible and are building within those boundaries.

The GitHub star discrepancy noted in the dossier -- sources citing both ~3.9K and 10.2K stars -- warrants attention. If the primary traction signal has integrity questions, the entire open-source flywheel narrative weakens. Even at 10.2K stars, this is developer interest in a free tool, not evidence of willingness to pay for a control plane. The open-core monetization challenge is well-documented: most open-source users never convert. Without any revenue signal, any named enterprise customer, or even disclosed pricing, the path from "thousands of developers exploring our SDKs" to a revenue-generating business remains entirely theoretical.

I pass. The timing in on-device AI is real, and these founders could build a functioning company in this space. But the consequence of success -- even in the best case -- is a well-run infrastructure middleware business, not the kind of outcome that makes the attempt worthwhile at the scale I invest for. They have reduced their risk of failure by choosing a well-defined, solvable problem, and in doing so, ensured that success, if it comes, will be modest by the standards I apply.
