# Ontora -- Elad Gil Evaluation

"Disrupting management consulting with AI" is one of the most popular pitches in the current cycle. I've seen a dozen variations in the last six months -- AI that replaces McKinsey, AI that automates Bain-style diagnostics, AI that conducts stakeholder interviews. That's my primary concern with Ontora: this isn't a non-obvious market. It's consensus. When I look at the competitive landscape -- Celonis pivoting toward AI-powered process intelligence with $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in revenue, Worktrace AI with $9.3 million from an ex-OpenAI PM, plus the dozen unfunded teams I've met working on variants of this -- the market structure looks more like "seemingly crowded" than "too boring" or "too niche." My best investments have been in markets that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors. Nobody dismisses "AI for consulting" in 2026. Everybody's excited about it. That's exactly backward from where I want to be.

The structural moment question is worth examining honestly. Yes, LLM capability crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 for conducting open-ended, contextually aware conversations at scale. That's real. But this capability threshold unlocks the opportunity for every team, not just Ontora. Compare this to Harvey's situation: legal AI required a specific fidelity threshold, and the team that built workflow and enterprise trust ahead of that threshold captured the market when it arrived. Ontora doesn't have that same structural positioning because the capability they need already broadly exists -- GPT-4 class models have been available for two years. If you're building an AI interview product in March 2026 and you're still in waitlist/demo phase, you're not building ahead of the capability curve. You're building alongside it, with everyone else.

The bull case deserves a full hearing, because the underlying market dynamics are genuinely interesting. Consulting firms face a textbook innovator's dilemma: McKinsey can't sell an AI diagnostic for $50K that cannibalizes a $5M engagement. That structural incentive misalignment is the kind of thing I look for -- it's analogous to how traditional payment processors couldn't offer Stripe's developer-first model without undermining their existing sales channel. If Ontora established itself as the organizational intelligence platform, the product-to-distribution trajectory writes itself: diagnostics becomes the wedge, then you distribute ongoing monitoring, benchmarking, process optimization, and change management through that enterprise relationship. Arnold's CoAnalyst experience at Porsche -- an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data -- is the right technical background for building this. And the Vertiv testimonial, while thin, comes from a strategy manager at a public company, not a friend doing a favor. If everything breaks right, this could be the Gusto pattern: "organizational diagnostics" sounds boring but leads to a deeply embedded enterprise platform. What would need to be true is that the interview-quality bar is high enough to deter casual entrants, that the Unified Context Layer creates genuine integration depth as a moat, and that the team can close enterprise deals at speed. Those are real conditions, not impossible ones.

But here's where the plan breaks down for me: this business requires multiple miracles, not one. Miracle one: AI interview quality reaches the threshold where enterprises trust it for organizational diagnostics (credible, but unproven -- no accuracy benchmarks disclosed). Miracle two: enterprises allow a three-person startup to ingest their CRM, email, documents, and meeting transcripts (this is a security and procurement obstacle that takes months to clear even for established vendors). Miracle three: the team builds enterprise sales motion without a single GTM hire and zero job postings. Each of these is achievable individually, but the compounding odds concern me. When I funded Airbnb, the team needed one miracle -- marketplace liquidity -- and every ounce of energy went into solving it. I don't see that operational clarity here. What's Ontora's single hardest thing? The dossier doesn't tell me the team has identified it.

The commoditization risk is the factor I keep returning to. The underlying technology stack -- LLM-based interviewing, RAG over enterprise documents, integration APIs for CRM and email -- uses broadly available foundation models and well-understood patterns. The dossier's own assessment acknowledges this: "barriers to replication are moderate." When I invested in PagerDuty, the product seemed simple but the operations data it accumulated over time became irreplaceable. I could see a path where Ontora's cross-company process benchmarks create that kind of data moat -- but that's a second-order effect requiring significant scale that this team hasn't demonstrated a path to reaching. A third co-founder with no confirmed public background adds uncertainty to the team composition. This is a real market with a real technology enabler, but I don't see the structural advantage that prevents a well-funded competitor -- or Celonis itself -- from building the same thing with existing enterprise distribution. I'm passing.
