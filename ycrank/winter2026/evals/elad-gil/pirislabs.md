# Piris Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Piris Labs is the collision between world-class founder credentials and a consolidating competitive landscape. Ali Khalatpour built the first room-temperature terahertz semiconductor laser and led NASA's GUSTO optical engine -- that's not a line on a resume, that's a genuine 1-of-100 photonics pedigree. Paired with Moghadam's 0-to-1 AI infrastructure experience at Meta and X, the technical team is exactly what you'd want for a photonic inference startup. But the market they're entering has already attracted Lightmatter ($850M raised), Ayar Labs ($500M Series E with NVIDIA backing), and has seen two massive acquisitions in the last few months -- Celestial AI by Marvell for $3.25B, Groq by NVIDIA for ~$20B. The photonic interconnect market in early 2026 isn't PagerDuty in 2010. Everyone knows it matters. NVIDIA has invested $4B in photonic manufacturing firms. DARPA is funding photonic-Cerebras integration. This is a consensus-hot infrastructure bet, not a non-obvious market -- and my best returns have consistently come from markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors, not excitement.

The structural moment is real and I don't dispute it. Trillion-parameter models have made the memory wall commercially acute. Copper interconnects are hitting physical limits. Data center AI capex is projected at $400-450B globally in 2026. These are genuine inflection signals. But a real structural moment and a good investment are different things. When I invested in Coinbase, the structural moment (blockchain infrastructure emergence) was there AND the market appeared "too unfamiliar" to most software investors -- the combination of real inflection plus investor dismissal is what creates asymmetric returns. Photonic AI interconnects in 2026 have the inflection but not the dismissal. The TAM slides write themselves, which is usually a warning sign in my framework.

The full-stack inference service positioning is the most interesting strategic angle here. Lightmatter and Ayar Labs sell photonic components to OEMs. Piris Labs claims to sell inference outcomes -- lower cost per token, not just faster interconnects. If executed, this is a fundamentally different value capture model, similar to the difference between selling chips and selling cloud compute. It also creates a potential distribution trajectory: once customers integrate your inference API, you can expand into fine-tuning, model hosting, and adjacent compute services. The Stripe analogy applies structurally -- start with one integration, become the distribution channel for additional services. But this vision requires building BOTH the photonic hardware AND the software stack AND the service layer AND the customer relationships. Which brings me to the core problem.

This company fails my single-miracle test definitively. The plan requires at minimum: (1) develop production-grade photonic interconnects that deliver the claimed 5x latency and 10x power improvements, (2) build a co-optimized inference software stack, (3) raise the $100M+ needed to move from lab to production manufacturing, (4) validate performance claims at customer scale, and (5) win enterprise deals against incumbents with established reliability track records. That's five sequential challenges, each with meaningful failure probability. When Harvey needed model fidelity to reach legal-grade accuracy, that miracle was being delivered by foundation model labs -- Harvey could focus on workflow and distribution while someone else solved the hard technical problem. Piris Labs must deliver its own miracle with its own capital, and it's not just one miracle. Compare that to the $500K they have on hand today versus the $500M-$850M their competitors have raised.

The bull case deserves a genuine hearing. If Khalatpour's photonics breakthrough is real and demonstrable at even modest scale, Piris Labs becomes an acquisition target in a market where acquirers are paying $3B-$20B. The recent acquisitions of Celestial AI and Groq show that large semiconductor companies are buying photonic and inference startups at extraordinary multiples. You could argue that the goal isn't to outcompete Lightmatter at scale but to demonstrate enough differentiated technology to attract a strategic acquirer. Khalatpour's publication record (572 citations) and the NASA/MIT/Harvard/Stanford institutional lineage provide scientific credibility that most hardware startups lack. And the full-stack service model means any successful demonstration is immediately commercially legible -- you're showing cost-per-token improvement, not abstract hardware benchmarks. If I'm wrong about this being a multi-miracle plan -- if Khalatpour has already solved the core photonics challenge in the lab and the remaining work is engineering, not science -- then the risk profile changes dramatically. But I have no evidence of that. The performance claims are unvalidated publicly, and the website showed only a name and tagline at time of research.

I respect the founders' ambition and technical depth. But my framework is specific: I need a non-obvious market at an inflection point, a single miracle the team is organized around, and a path from product to distribution. Piris Labs has a real inflection point in a consensus market, multiple miracles required with minimal capital to pursue them, and a theoretical distribution trajectory with no demonstrated first step. The founder-market fit on the photonics side is exceptional, but the structural economics of competing in photonic hardware against billion-dollar-funded incumbents with $500K in seed capital represents a challenge that founder quality alone cannot resolve.
