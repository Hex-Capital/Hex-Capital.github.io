# Menza -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Qasim Munye's prior exit is the most interesting signal here, and it's interesting for the wrong reasons. He built ShortlyAI, an AI writing assistant, which got acquired by Conversion.ai — now Jasper. And what happened to Jasper? It's fighting for survival in a world where ChatGPT and Claude do AI writing for free. Qasim lived through the exact cycle I keep warning about: build an AI tool, get early traction, watch the underlying capability get commoditized out from under you. Now he's doing it again with analytics instead of writing. A medical doctor who taught himself ML and shipped a product that got acquired — that's genuinely impressive execution history, and Mariam's Goldman Sachs background in portfolio optimization and reporting is directly relevant. These are credible operators. But the idea — "AI-powered analytics for DTC brands" — is not remotely insane. It's one of the most consensus AI application plays in the entire YC ecosystem. Every demo day has three of these. Julius AI already raised $11M from Bessemer. ThoughtSpot does $318 million in revenue. This isn't a sane person with an insane idea; it's a sane person with a sane idea, which is the configuration that produces market-rate returns at best.

Here's what I keep screaming on CNBC and everywhere else: software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore. Menza's claimed defensibility is 650+ data source integrations and vertical-specific insight models for e-commerce. But data connectors are engineering hours, not structural lock-in. Fivetran, Airbyte, and a dozen open-source projects already provide connector infrastructure. The AI layer — natural language querying, anomaly detection, automated reporting — is precisely the category that gets cheaper and more accessible with every new model release. If I apply my "cherry on top" test, the answer is fatal: remove the AI and there is no business. The AI IS the product. That's not cherry-on-top positioning, that's building your house on someone else's foundation and hoping they don't notice. Shopify is already building Sidekick. Meta expands its analytics dashboard every quarter. Google has been doing this since Analytics launched. The platform owners will absorb this functionality because they have the data advantage and the distribution.

The bull case I'd want to genuinely engage with: maybe the cross-platform synthesis — connecting Shopify plus Meta Ads plus Klaviyo plus Xero and generating insights across them — is a genuine wedge that no single platform will replicate, because each platform only sees its own data. Maybe the 12 named customers (Evolve Beauty saving 60 hours monthly, The Keto Shop finding a 10% revenue leak from stockouts) represent real love for a tool that solves a painful coordination problem these brands can't solve elsewhere. And the business model conflict argument has some merit — ThoughtSpot's $4.2B valuation comes from enterprise deals, and they structurally can't serve a DTC brand paying five hundred bucks a month. If Menza could lock in thousands of SMB e-commerce brands before the market consolidates, there's a version of this that works. But I don't think that version survives three years. The integration layer commoditizes, the AI layer commoditizes, and eventually Shopify or one of the marketing platforms launches a "connect your other tools" feature that does eighty percent of what Menza does for free. The precedent isn't ambiguous — it's exactly what happened to the company Qasim already built and sold.

Menza creates no new primitive. It's an application sitting on top of other platforms' APIs, entirely subject to their terms and rate limits. Nobody builds on Menza; Menza builds on everybody else. There are no network effects — each customer's data is siloed, and one brand's analytics don't make another brand's analytics better. The narrative can theoretically expand from "DTC analytics" to "every SMB's AI data team," but that expansion path leads directly into ThoughtSpot's, Microsoft Power BI's, and Salesforce Tableau's crosshairs. The narrative gets harder as it gets bigger, which is the opposite of what I look for in infinity stories. The twelve customers are real and meaningful at pre-seed — that's not nothing — but they're small UK DTC brands, and there's no evidence of revenue metrics that would suggest a meaningful floor. This is a company that checks none of my boxes: consensus idea, software-only moat that AI will erode, application-layer positioning, no physical-world operations that software amplifies but cannot replace. I've seen this movie before — I produced the first version of it when I backed Birchbox. Narrative without structural moat is the worst venture outcome. Pass.
