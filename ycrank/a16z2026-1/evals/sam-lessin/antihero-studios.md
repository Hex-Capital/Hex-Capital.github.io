# Antihero Studios -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Frank Yan drove Brawl Stars from its all-time revenue low to 8.8x growth. Brice Laville Saint-Martin art-directed Clash Royale. Andre Parodi ran technical infrastructure at King for a decade. Collectively, leads on eight billion-dollar-per-year games. That's a genuinely elite roster, and every gaming-focused VC on the planet can see it -- which is exactly the problem. a16z Speedrun co-led a $4.5M seed. Sisu Game Ventures came back for seconds. Laton Ventures is in. This is a deal where every smart gaming investor is already at the table bidding. When I look at Antihero Studios, the first thing I see is that the risk is absolutely not in the price. The team pedigree means this is priced as a high-conviction bet, and the upside I'm buying is execution in a hits-driven category against Tencent -- which owns both Arena Breakout and Supercell. The consensus is that this team can ship a great game. I don't disagree. But I'm not in the business of paying consensus prices for consensus outcomes.

The primitive test kills this for me. MISFITZ is "ARC Raiders meets Brawl Stars on your phone" -- Antihero Studios' own words. That's a genre mashup, not a new behavior. Extraction shooters exist. Top-down mobile multiplayer exists. Combining them is a product design bet, not a category creation event. Nobody's going to say "MISFITZ me" the way they say "Venmo me." Compare this to Patreon, which created the behavior of paying creators directly -- a financial primitive that spawned an entire ecosystem. Antihero Studios is building a game in an established format for an established platform. The "A24 of games" brand aspiration is genuinely interesting, but A24 built its brand over a decade across dozens of films. Antihero Studios is a single-title studio. If MISFITZ underperforms, there is no brand flywheel -- there's a fundraise.

The sanity-insanity ratio is off. These are deeply sane people -- Frank Yan gave a GDC talk on removing loot boxes from Brawl Stars, which is about as rational and process-oriented as game design gets. But what they're doing isn't insane. Former Supercell leads making a mobile multiplayer game is the most logical thing in the world. It's sane people doing a sane thing. I get excited when a very reasonable person tells me something that sounds fucking crazy -- like investing $1.7M in a YouTuber for 5% of her earnings for 30 years, or seeding a new Layer 1 blockchain when everyone says Ethereum already won. "We're going to make a mobile extraction shooter" from the team that made Brawl Stars doesn't make me lean forward. It makes me say "of course you are."

The bull case deserves attention. 70,000 pre-alpha players averaging 50 minutes of daily play across three sessions -- with eliminated players 6% more likely to restart than successful extractors -- is a legitimate engagement signal. The post-IDFA distribution thesis is real: Apple broke performance marketing, and Antihero Studios' 750-creator organic network is a genuinely differentiated acquisition strategy in a world where most mobile studios are still trying to buy users on Meta and Google. If Antihero Studios is right that the extraction genre translates to casual mobile the way battle royale translated from ARMA to PUBG Mobile, the TAM is enormous -- 40% of mobile gamers play multiplayer in a $121-165B market. Frank Yan specifically has the scar tissue from live-service monetization that makes the cosmetic-only F2P model credible. For a gaming-focused fund, this is probably a great bet. It's just not my kind of bet.

Structural durability is where Antihero Studios sits in an uncomfortable middle. Games can't be commoditized by AI the way SaaS workflows can -- you can't just bolt GPT-4 onto Salesforce and get a hit game. That's real. But games also have no protocol-level lock-in, no physical-world operations, and no financial rails. Multiplayer network effects exist but are notoriously fragile -- players migrate to the next hit. Tencent owns Supercell. If casual mobile extraction proves viable, Tencent can build a competing title with Supercell's existing player base and distribution. The 750-creator program is an early moat signal, but KairosTime is a Supercell community creator -- that loyalty could flip if Supercell enters the category. On capital discipline, $4.5M across 13 people in Barcelona gives maybe 18 months of runway, and cosmetic-only F2P monetization requires millions of players before generating meaningful revenue. Antihero Studios will need to raise again. The organic growth strategy is more capital-efficient than UA-heavy approaches, but this is still a game studio that burns cash before it earns any.

The two dimensions that lock my tier are Consensus Mispricing and Primitive Creation. Antihero Studios is a well-funded team building a game -- not a mispriced contrarian bet in a category others have dismissed, and not a new behavioral primitive. The team is exceptional by any standard, the engagement data from 70K pre-alpha players is strong, and the post-IDFA organic distribution thesis is smart. For a gaming-focused investor, this could be a portfolio anchor. For me, there's no asymmetry. The price reflects the quality, and the quality is execution in a hits-driven market against Tencent. Pass.
