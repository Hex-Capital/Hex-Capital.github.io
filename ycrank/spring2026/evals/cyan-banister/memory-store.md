# Memory Store -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Memory Store is the shape of company I see most often in 2026 and write checks on least: Diwank Singh Tomer and Ishita Jindal are real builders — they shipped Julep to 6.6k GitHub stars and $400k ARR before this — operating in the most consensus corner of agent infrastructure, where Mem0 already raised $24M, Letta has 13k stars, and Zep, Supermemory, Cognee, LangMem, and Hindsight are all elbowing for the same surface area. The signature is competent-builders-in-consensus-territory. The forge is real. The space is crowded. That's a hard combination for the kind of power-law swing I write checks for from my own money.

On the founder forge ladder, the high rung is Substrate's James Proud — "forged in fire and came out unbroken," a person whose biography made the company inevitable. The low rung is the founder I keep passing on: "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Memory Store sits closer to my Crusoe placement — Diwank and Ishita are infrastructure people who literally had a `src/memory-store` directory inside the Julep monorepo before it became a product, which is the cleanest possible signal that the problem found them instead of the other way around. But when I ask "tell me their story, not the business," the dossier gives me Columbia physics, Babson MBA, Head of Engineering at Jar — sophisticated builder credentials, not the survival biography that lets me feel the inevitability. They've been forged by three years inside agent infrastructure, which is real, but it's career-forging, not life-forging. Above the Pass Example, well below Substrate, roughly at Crusoe's pre-conviction level.

On the contrarian conviction ladder, my high rung is Anduril — defense tech that was politically toxic in Silicon Valley when Palmer started, the kind of bet that made sophisticated investors squirm. My low rung is the Pass Example: consensus AI in a consensus market. Memory Store sits at the low rung. AI memory is the most-funded, most-discussed problem in agent infrastructure right now — the dossier names seven direct competitors and a $24M-funded category leader in Mem0. There is no room in this category that requires courage to enter. The "voted #1 most promising startup at YC P26 kickoff" line tells me the opposite of what I want to see — when the consensus inside a YC batch loves a company, that is not the 300-investor-pass signal that got me into Uber. This is a comfortable bet, and comfort is not my edge.

On the invisible system ladder, Affirm sits at the high rung — it ripped open credit card opacity, an extractive system that profited from people who couldn't see the math. Carta sits in the middle — cap table mystery made transparent. HQ Trivia sits at the low rung — no hidden system, just cultural attention. Memory Store sits between low and mid, closer to low. The dossier describes the problem as "users copy-pasting old context into every new chat" — that is workflow friction, not an extractive structure. There is no taxi medallion cartel here, no opaque tax being levied on people who don't understand the rules. Anthropic is not exploiting you by not remembering your context; they will simply ship the feature themselves, which they already started doing in 2025. Memory Store fails my deepest test.

On infrastructure defensibility, Flexport's logistics network effects sit at the high rung, Niantic's spatial data platform in the middle, and IRL — fabricated metrics, no moat — at the low rung. Memory Store sits between low and mid. The dossier itself concedes the point: "the underlying technical pattern (RAG over synced sources + MCP server) is replicable" and "the MCP protocol is open and the data sources expose APIs to any vendor." Corpus accumulation is a real switching cost but a small one at pre-seed scale, and the nine integrations are exactly the kind of breadth a well-funded competitor or a platform owner can replicate in a quarter. On cascading impact, Uber expanded transportation beyond who could hail a taxi, Wonderschool expanded childcare access, the Pass Example serves only enterprises and the affluent. Memory Store serves AI-using knowledge workers in San Francisco — already the most privileged users of the most leveraged tools in human history. The product makes Cursor users 10% more efficient. That's not the access-expansion mechanism I look for.

My 36-month forecast: Memory Store is acquired by Anthropic, Notion, or Slack for $30-80M as a feature-and-team tuck-in, OR settles at $1-3M ARR while Mem0's funded distribution grabs the developer mindshare and the founders explore a second pivot from the original Julep base. The mechanism is straightforward — in a consensus category with a funded leader and platform owners shipping native versions of the wedge, competent builders without a structural moat get acquired or stranded. The pairing of high-forge and low-contrarianism almost always lands here.

The strongest case against my pass: Memory Store has been quietly building this since 2022, the team-shared MCP positioning is genuinely different from Mem0's developer-SDK wedge, and the YC P26 timing aligns with the real MCP inflection from November 2024. If Memory Store becomes the default team memory layer for AI-builder organizations and the corpus genuinely accumulates into team-graph switching costs, this could be Carta for AI memory — and Carta was a "boring" infrastructure bet most people underestimated. RTFKT is my Known Exception precedent for cultural-timing bets without infrastructure moats. But RTFKT had a narrow window and an obvious acquirer landscape; Memory Store has a wide-open window with seven competitors and platform owners actively eating the surface. The bull case requires me to believe corpus switching costs solidify before Anthropic ships native cross-tool memory — and Anthropic is already shipping memory features. The flip condition is concrete: if Memory Store closes a paying customer with 100+ seats in the first six months and shows cross-tool team-graph data that Mem0 structurally cannot replicate, I would re-evaluate. Without that, I wish Diwank and Ishita well — they're real, and I'd take a meeting on their next thing — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
