# Rubric AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Rubric AI is the market it has chosen to serve: fewer than twenty frontier AI labs globally. That's not a market -- that's a customer list you can write on an index card. When someone tells me they're building a company whose total addressable buyers fit in a conference room, I ask: if everything goes right, what have you built? The answer here is a specialized vendor to the most technically sophisticated and demanding buyers on Earth, competing against Scale AI at $29 billion, Surge AI generating $1.2 billion in revenue bootstrapped, Mercor at $10 billion, and Labelbox which has already shipped "rubric evaluation" tooling with their name on it. The consequence of full success is becoming a valued supplier in someone else's supply chain. That's not the kind of outcome I get excited about.

I understand the bull case, and I want to engage with it honestly. Meta's acquisition of 49% of Scale AI genuinely fractured the market -- OpenAI, Google, and xAI all have reasons to diversify their evaluation vendors now. The shift from static benchmarks to dynamic rubric-based evaluation is real and accelerating. Academic research shows rubric-based reward signals can produce 13% gains in domains where frontier models still fail 22-30% of the time. A nimble two-person team with deep evaluation methodology could wedge into this fragmentation and become indispensable to two or three labs, then expand. If the post-training evaluation layer becomes the critical bottleneck in the race to AGI -- the thing that determines which lab wins -- then the company closest to that methodology could command extraordinary pricing power despite the narrow customer base. For this to be a great investment, Rubric AI would need to develop proprietary evaluation methodologies so deeply embedded in a frontier lab's training pipeline that switching costs become prohibitive, and the unnamed second team member would need to be a world-class ML researcher who gives the company genuine technical credibility. That's a plausible path. But even in the best case, I see a high-margin services company, not a transformation of how the world works.

My deeper concern is what this company reveals about its own ambition. Calling yourself a "research and product lab" while building evaluation tooling for existing model training workflows is dressing up a services business in research clothing. The incumbents -- Scale AI, Surge AI -- are the ones actually enabling the transformation; Rubric AI is optimizing a step in their customers' pipeline. When I backed OpenAI in 2019, I was betting that AI itself would reshape every industry. That's a consequence of success worth the risk. Rubric AI's success means frontier models improve slightly faster through better human feedback loops. Important work, but derivative -- the transformational value accrues to the labs, not to the evaluation vendor.

The founder profile compounds my concern. Pragya Saboo is a competent product manager -- shipped Time Tracking at Asana, worked at Oscar Health, has investing experience at Climate Capital, and has prior founding experience. That's a solid profile for many types of companies. But for a "post-training research lab" selling into Anthropic and OpenAI, I need to see someone who makes those labs' research teams lean forward. The publicly visible background has no ML research, no RLHF experience, no AI training infrastructure work. The second team member is entirely invisible -- no name, no background, no public trace. I'm being asked to evaluate a "research lab" where I can see one product manager and one ghost. That's not contrarian courage; that's an information gap I can't bridge from a dossier. The outsiders I back -- Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, Pat Brown at Impossible Foods -- brought outsider perspectives to physical-world problems where fresh thinking was the advantage. In AI evaluation infrastructure, the buyers are the world's foremost ML researchers. You need credibility they recognize, not just good product instincts.

The timing angle is the strongest element here, but even that cuts both ways. Yes, the post-training paradigm is ascendant and the Scale AI fragmentation opened a window. But "evals are the new frontier" is already the consensus view -- Mercor's blog literally says "Welcome to the era of evals." When the incumbents are blogging about how important your market is, you're not skating to where the puck will be. You're arriving at the rink after warmups started. The exponential technology curves I look for -- AI models in 2019, plant-based protein in 2010, TCP/IP routing in 1996 -- were trends the mainstream actively dismissed. Nobody is dismissing the importance of AI evaluation in 2026.

This is a company that has reduced its risk profile -- known market, known buyers, proven demand -- to the point where I can see it becoming a solid $20-50 million revenue business serving a handful of frontier labs. That's exactly the outcome I'm not looking for. The probability of moderate success might be reasonable, but the consequence of that success is a nice vendor relationship, not the kind of disruption that justifies the effort of building something from nothing. Some companies are too safe to fund.
