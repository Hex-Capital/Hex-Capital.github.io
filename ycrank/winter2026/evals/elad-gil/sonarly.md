# Sonarly -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a competitive dynamics problem that undermines the structural thesis. Sentry shipped Autofix in March 2024 with 95% root cause accuracy and 54% fix rate. Datadog launched Bits AI Dev Agent in 2025, tested across 2,000+ customer environments. These aren't incumbents asleep at the wheel -- they're the technology companies that own the data, and they're actively building the exact capability Sonarly is positioning around. When I invested in Harvey, the incumbents (law firms, legal publishers) couldn't build the technology. Here, the incumbents *are* the technology. That's a fundamentally different competitive setup.

The cross-platform aggregation angle is the most analytically interesting element. I'm pattern-matching this to PagerDuty, which I know well: PagerDuty succeeded by sitting at the intersection of multiple monitoring tools and owning the "who gets paged" orchestration layer -- something no individual monitoring vendor had incentive to build well, because it required being tool-agnostic. Sonarly is attempting the same structural play for AI-powered remediation. The logic is sound: engineering teams run 3-5 monitoring tools, and no single vendor will integrate deeply with competitors' telemetry data. But there's a critical difference. PagerDuty's value -- routing alerts to the right human -- required cross-tool awareness by definition. Sonarly's value -- fixing code -- can be delivered by each monitoring tool independently from its own error data. Cross-tool correlation adds accuracy at the margin, but the question is whether it's a 2x improvement or a 10x improvement. At 30% mergeable PRs (per the HN commenter), they haven't proven it's either.

The market itself is consensus hot, not non-obvious. "AI-powered production remediation" is exactly what every monitoring vendor, every AI coding tool vendor, and dozens of funded startups are building right now. There's no structural inflection I can identify that uniquely advantages a 2-person startup over Sentry's existing platform with millions of users. The "why now" -- LLMs can generate code, MCP enables tool integration -- applies equally to every participant. When the enabling technology is generally available and the incumbents are shipping, you need either a distribution wedge they can't replicate or a data advantage they can't match. Sonarly's "living map" of production architecture stored as a .md file is conceptually interesting as a data moat, but it's unproven and architecturally simple enough that any competitor could replicate the approach.

The strongest bull case: if LLM fix quality improves from 30% mergeable to 80%+ mergeable in the next 12-18 months -- which the scaling curves suggest is plausible -- then the orchestration layer that aggregates across monitoring tools becomes extremely valuable, and Sonarly's early positioning could matter. Enterprise engineering teams won't consolidate onto a single monitoring vendor (the multi-vendor pattern is deeply entrenched), so a tool-agnostic remediation layer fills a structural gap. If the founders move fast enough to build distribution and accumulate per-customer architectural context data before the incumbents figure out cross-platform, there's a path to becoming the "PagerDuty of automated remediation." The production system map could evolve into a platform for deployment safety, architecture documentation, and performance optimization -- a genuine multi-product trajectory. For this bull case to work, three things need to be true simultaneously: fix quality needs to reach trust threshold, cross-tool correlation needs to materially outperform single-tool remediation, and the team needs to scale integration maintenance faster than a 2-person team can typically manage.

That "three things need to be true" structure is exactly what triggers my multi-miracle concern. The founders need to: maintain reliable integrations across multiple monitoring platforms whose APIs change regularly, achieve fix quality high enough for production trust, and build distribution against incumbents who have millions of existing users. Each of these is a real obstacle, and they compound. The team's prior experience is co-founding a B2C edtech app in France -- relevant for demonstrating they can ship, but not for the infrastructure engineering credibility that enterprise devtools buyers require. I respect that they've shipped a working product and gotten into YC, and the case study of reducing 180 alerts to 5 actionable issues is a real signal. But the gap between "useful triage tool" and "trusted to merge PRs into production" is enormous, and that's ultimately where the value has to live.

I'm passing. The market is real but consensus, the competitive position requires the incumbents to fail at something they're already doing well, and the technology moment that enables Sonarly enables everyone else equally. This isn't a structural vacancy like Anduril found in defense AI -- it's a crowded field where the largest players have distribution, data, and engineering advantages. A different version of this company -- with deep infrastructure engineering founders, a unique data advantage, or a distribution wedge I can't see -- might change my calculus.
