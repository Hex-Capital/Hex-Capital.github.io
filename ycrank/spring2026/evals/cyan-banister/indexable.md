# Indexable -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what hits me first about Indexable: this is one of the most crowded investment spaces I've seen in years. Six funded startups, three major cloud providers, and an open-source project all building AI agent sandboxes — right now, today. E2B has 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. Daytona crossed a million dollars in ARR within two months. Modal is running at fifty million. When I wrote my check for Uber, over 300 investors on AngelList had already said no. That's contrarian. When every institutional investor in Silicon Valley is eagerly competing to fund your category, that's consensus. And consensus deals are not where I build conviction. The best ideas make people uncomfortable at first — this one makes VCs reach for their checkbooks.

When I sit down with a founder, the first thing I want to hear is their story. Not the technology, not the market size — I want to understand what happened in their life that made this problem impossible for them to walk away from. Andrew Gazelka is clearly a gifted systems engineer. The Hyperion project — building a Minecraft engine in Rust that handles ten thousand concurrent players — that's not trivial. His time at xAI and the BountyBot exit tell me he can ship. But when I ask "what happened to you that made this idea find YOU?" — I hear a career trajectory, not a crucible. He was at xAI, he saw AI agents needed sandboxes, he started building one. That's a market observation. The founders I've backed who built lasting infrastructure companies — the ones who survived their companies nearly dying — they had a different relationship with the problem. It wasn't optional for them. I don't see that inevitability here, and its absence weighs on me.

The strongest case for Indexable lives in the technical stack, and I want to give it its full due. A custom virtual machine monitor on KVM that forks an entire running environment — files, processes, memory, databases — in twenty-six milliseconds is genuinely differentiated engineering. That's not an API wrapper. That's not a thin layer on top of someone else's infrastructure. The content-addressable storage engine, the RDMA transport layer, the custom Linux kernel — this is the kind of deep systems work that reminds me of what drew me to Substrate, where the founder was "forged in fire" and building at the semiconductor level while everyone else was optimizing at the application layer. If the right architecture for the agent era is a new hypervisor rather than containers or V8 isolates, then Gazelka may be one of very few people on the planet capable of building it. The infrastructure-at-inflection pattern is genuinely here — we're at the dawn of autonomous agents, and stateful sandbox infrastructure could become as critical as cloud compute was to web applications. I see that thread clearly.

But here's where my framework breaks against this company: there is no invisible system being made visible. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was exposing the hidden structure of credit card fees that extracted value from people who couldn't see what they were paying. When I backed Ownwell, the property tax system was an opaque machine that punished people who didn't know how to fight it. Indexable builds new infrastructure for developers building AI agents. That's valuable — but it doesn't attack any hidden, extractive structure. There's no information asymmetry being disrupted, no opaque system being made transparent, no "invisible tax" being eliminated from ordinary people's lives. The product serves an already-transparent market of sophisticated developers who understand exactly what they're buying. That absence matters deeply in how I evaluate companies, because the structural revelation is what creates the kind of loyalty and mission-driven building that carries a company through its darkest moments.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The agent era would need to demand a fundamentally new virtualization architecture — not containers, not microVMs, but a custom hypervisor built for stateful forking from the ground up. And Gazelka would need to be so far ahead technically that the eighteen months it takes for Daytona or E2B to attempt this approach gives Indexable enough time to lock in customers through switching costs. If that's the world, the custom VMM becomes the kind of compounding infrastructure moat I look for — each customer's integration deepens, each tenant's data improves deduplication ratios, and higher-level competitors can never match the latency without rebuilding from scratch. There's a version of this where Indexable is to AI agent infrastructure what SpaceX was to launch — the team that went deep on first principles while everyone else iterated on existing paradigms. I can see that outline. But I can also see Daytona shipping fork-and-snapshot primitives on Docker, and E2B extending Firecracker to add database-level state management, and the twenty-six millisecond advantage becoming a feature rather than a moat. The maintenance burden of a custom kernel and VMM on a three-person team, against competitors with ten to twenty times the capital and established customer bases, is the kind of structural risk that keeps me up at night even when the technical vision is right.

This is a talented engineer building real infrastructure at a real inflection point. But the biographical forge is absent, the space is as consensus as anything in tech right now, and there's no hidden system being disrupted — three of my five core criteria read neutral or negative. I respect the technical depth, and if Andrew shows me something in his story I'm not seeing in this dossier, I'd take another meeting. But today, this isn't a Long Journey bet.
