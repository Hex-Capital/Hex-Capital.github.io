# Compresr -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Compresr: this is four brilliant EPFL researchers building a cost optimization layer for the hottest, most consensus investment category on the planet. AI infrastructure is what literally every venture capitalist in San Francisco wants to fund right now. Every pitch competition, every demo day, every fund thesis deck -- AI picks and shovels. That's not where I find my best investments. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Compresr is neither. Any investor can look at this and immediately understand the pitch: "LLM tokens are expensive, we compress them, you save money." That's a clean story, and clean stories at the earliest stage make me nervous because it means the price will be wrong and the differentiation has already been arbitraged away by attention.

When I think about the founders, I see strong technical credentials -- a peer-reviewed paper benchmarking 25 models, genuine ML research depth, a CEO who did time at Meta and Philips and previously started and shut down a company called Iterate. I respect the intellectual horsepower. But when I imagine sitting across from Ivan Zakazov and asking him to tell me his story -- not the business, his story -- I'm hearing about a PhD program at EPFL, internships at large companies, and an academic research interest in compression. That's a perfectly respectable path, but it's not the kind of biographical thread that makes me lean forward. I'm listening for the moment where the founder's life and the company's mission become the same thing -- where solving this problem isn't a career choice but something closer to a calling. I don't feel that here. These are smart people who identified a technical gap in the market. That's different from someone who's been grinding on this problem because they have no choice.

The competitive dynamics trouble me more than most things in this dossier. Microsoft Research already has LLMLingua -- free, open-source, integrated into LlamaIndex and Azure Prompt Flow. That's a formidable baseline that any developer can grab today. Meanwhile, LLM inference costs are dropping roughly 10x per year, and DeepSeek entered at 90% below incumbent pricing. Compresr's website claims "up to 200x compression" but their own published benchmark shows 10x on actual SEC filings. That gap between the marketing claim and the demonstrated result is the kind of thing that triggers my skepticism about polish versus substance. The accuracy improvement -- 72.3% to 74.5% on FinanceBench -- is a more durable value proposition than cost savings, but it's a subtle sell when the headline pitch is about saving money on tokens that are getting cheaper every quarter.

I want to be fair about the bull case, because it exists. The structural observation that LLM providers have a financial disincentive to offer compression -- because it cannibalizes their per-token revenue -- is genuinely clever. If that dynamic holds, it creates a real niche for a third-party compression layer. And the team's approach of combining an open-source gateway (the Context Gateway with 117 GitHub stars) with a commercial API is the right distribution playbook for developer infrastructure. If agentic workloads explode the way the market expects, and context windows become the primary cost bottleneck, Compresr could be well-positioned. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that compression becomes a permanent infrastructure layer rather than a temporary bridge to cheaper tokens -- that it's more like CDN caching (which persisted even as bandwidth got cheaper) than like data compression utilities that faded as storage costs dropped. That's a real analogy and a real possibility. But it requires a level of conviction I can't muster from what I see here.

What I notice that others might not: this company doesn't make any invisible system visible, doesn't create economic access for underserved populations, and doesn't operate in a space that other investors are afraid to touch. It helps well-resourced engineering teams building AI applications shave costs. That's a legitimate business, but it's not the kind of bet I write checks for. The co-founder dynamic is actually the strongest signal I see -- four people from the same institution, same program, who collaborated on serious research together. That shared foundation matters, and it reminds me of why I've come to appreciate family-run businesses where conflict resolution patterns are already established. But strong co-founder alignment in a consensus category solving someone else's cost problem doesn't add up to my kind of investment.
