# Arden -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Arden is a clean single-miracle agentic-workflow bet that walks into a sub-market where I am already on the cap table of the better-capitalized direct competitor -- Petual's $20M a16z round in April 2026 lists "First Round, Cowboy, Elad Gil" as participants per *International Accounting Bulletin*, and that fact alone collapses the decision before I get to the dimensional read. The dimensional signature confirms the verdict: Arden's highest axis is single-miracle architecture (the company isolates one validated capability -- multi-step computer-use agents over 20+ enterprise systems -- and treats the rest as GTM), and its lowest is revenue velocity ("Revenue/customers: No public data found" per the Key Facts table). That high/low pairing names a category I've seen many times in P26 -- a thesis-clean agentic wrapper with no curve yet -- and in a sub-market where I've already placed the bet I want to place.

**Decagon** (2023) is the analogue I keep returning to on the single-miracle axis -- one miracle (LLM accuracy at a structured enterprise task), one workflow surface, the rest is enterprise GTM execution against a wide-open category per my *AI Market Clarity* taxonomy. Arden has the same architectural posture: the founders aren't asking me to bet on new physics or new regulation, they're asking me to bet that a computer-use agent can pull audit evidence out of Workday, Okta, NetSuite, SAP, ServiceNow, Azure AD, AWS, Snowflake, and GitHub reliably enough that the workpaper is reviewer-ready. Where Arden diverges from the Decagon template is the second half-miracle the dossier under-discloses -- "audit-ready workpapers that external auditors can accept" (ardentech.ai) is a PCAOB-acceptance question, and the dossier confirms no Big Four partnership, no PCAOB position, and SOC 2 Type I only "in progress." That's not a science-project second miracle, but it isn't pure GTM either, which is why Arden lands closer to Color Genomics-style workflow-on-hard-thing than to Decagon-style pure-GTM.

The Harvey (2023) pattern is "$3B ? $5B ? $8B in months" because the curve was just working, and that is precisely the signal Arden's dossier does not carry -- "No public data found" appears under Revenue Signal, customer logos, press coverage, and distribution signals. In the AI cycle silence on growth is itself a negative signal, because real growth in this sub-market is easy to disclose -- Fieldguide announced its Protiviti alliance in March 2026, Petual surfaced a16z + naming partners in April 2026, and Arden's loudest public artifact is Aryaman Khanna's LinkedIn launch post (activity 7450643893903208448). I don't penalize pre-seeds for lacking a curve as a default, but in a sub-market that *has* curves visible from $20M and $75M rounds within the last 90 days, the absence of a competing artifact from Arden is informative.

The market re-opening test is where Arden's dossier is weakest on substance, not on framing. SOX has been steady-state regulation since 2002, and KPMG's "45% YoY cost increase" line is a TAM-math argument, not a catalyst -- the catalyst the company *could* claim is the 18-month computer-use agent step-function, but the dossier explicitly labels the why-now as "[Inferred]" because the company has not published one. Compounded over 18 months, the lack of a named catalyst means Arden has to win on product execution against Optro's $300M ARR installed base, Workiva's $884.6M FY2025 distribution into the same CAEs, and Petual's a16z-funded direct overlap -- without the wind a real re-opening provides. This is Lyft Series C territory in miniature: a real market, a credible team, but the structure rewards the leader rather than re-pricing the field.

Counterfactually on index position: for Arden to flip from a feature-fight to an index, the dossier would have to show that internal SOX teams are *adopting* agent-executed testing as the default workflow path that downstream auditors require -- the way every e-commerce entrant has to touch Stripe. The regulatory floor is real (every US public company must perform SOX testing per the company description), but the workflow path is currently owned by Optro/SOXHUB and Workiva at seat-based pricing, and Arden's positioning explicitly cannibalizes seats rather than sitting downstream of them. Without a Big Four channel partnership or a PCAOB-acceptance signal, Arden has to displace incumbents on features in the buyer's existing procurement cycle, which is the inverse of an index position.

David Lomelin is the single Perplexity-shaped signal in the founder section -- the dossier names Blueprint ("2,500+ businesses") and Nivaro ("1,000+ students") as products he shipped independently with user-feedback loops, which is exactly the artifact I look for and exactly what Aravind Srinivas had when he pinged me in 2022. Aryaman Khanna is the inverse profile: UC Berkeley + Databricks + a NeurIPS 2025 co-authorship on federated learning for LiDAR -- that's a credential stack, not a shipped-artifact stack, and the federated-learning paper has zero adjacency to internal audit workflow. Neither founder shows a CPA, audit, or SOX line, and the dossier names no audit-firm advisor; for a workpaper product that needs to be PCAOB-defensible, that is the gap I'd want closed before a check.

My 36-month forecast: Arden is either acqui-hired by Workiva or Optro for under $75M, or it pivots out of public-company SOX into mid-market continuous-compliance evidence collection (SOC 2 / ISO 27001) where Petual's a16z capital and the incumbents' Fortune 500 distribution don't preempt -- I weight the pivot higher than the acqui-hire because Lomelin's Blueprint shipping rhythm suggests the team will adapt rather than wind down. The mechanism producing this forecast is the high/low signature: a clean single-miracle architecture survives, but without a curve and against a $20M-funded direct competitor I'm already inside, the path to category leadership in public-company SOX closes within 12-18 months of demo day.

The strongest bull case against my pass is that Arden's "end-to-end test execution" framing is genuinely different from Petual's broader "agentic SOX" positioning, and that the 20+ integrations are a real switching-cost asset that compounds once embedded in an annual SOX cycle -- if Arden lands three mid-cap public-co customers in 12 months, the Harvey-style curve triggers and category-leader concerns reset. The dossier evidence that wins for me is the Petual cap table including my own name -- I cannot rationally angel a head-on competitor to a position I already hold, and the audit buyer is a CAE who evaluates on Big Four acceptance, not on integration count, which Arden's "SOC 2 Type I in progress" status does not yet credential. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a publicly disclosed Big Four or top-10 mid-tier audit-firm partnership accepting Arden's workpapers, paired with at least one named Fortune 1000 customer logo, within 6 months of demo day -- absent that, this is a Pass even before the Petual conflict closes the door.
