# Perceptron ML -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Michael Marcotte built production AI agents for hardware debugging at NVIDIA and his brother Peyton built astronaut exercise equipment at Brown with 1517 backing, and now the two of them are pointing that talent at plaintiff law-firm outbound marketing — a category where FirmPilot ($27.25M), Darrow ($60M), EvenUp ($385M at $2B), and Eve ($164M at $1B) have already raised roughly $636M between them. Perceptron ML is the Pass Example I keep in my back pocket made flesh: two credentialed engineers who discovered the problem in a market memo rather than in their own lives, swinging at a vertical the well-capitalized incumbents are already squeezing from both ends. I am never playing the game I seem to be playing — but the game Perceptron ML seems to be playing and the game it actually is playing look like the same game, and that itself is the tell.

Anduril is the HIGH rung on lived-experience for me — Palmer Luckey had shipped Oculus to scale before he ever touched a Lattice console, and the defense problem was autobiographical to a kid who built VR in his parents' garage and got chewed up by Facebook. The load-bearing mechanism is that the founder's hands had already been on a comparable artifact at scale, in an adjacent domain, before the pitch existed. Perceptron ML diverges at the domain hop: Michael Marcotte's NVIDIA work on formal-verification and hardware-debugging agents is genuinely an adjacent agent-orchestration credential, and I will not pretend otherwise — that is the part of Perceptron ML that earns more than the absent-evidence default. But the domain Perceptron ML is selling into is plaintiff and contingency law, and neither Marcotte brother has a disclosed minute of life inside a mass-tort intake desk, a contingency-fee P&L, or a roughneck conversation with a class-action partner. The Anduril analogy loads on the wrong axis — Perceptron ML has the agent-builder pedigree but not the social license to walk on the well pad.

HQ Trivia is the LOW rung on CEO authority — Rus Yusupov and Colin Kroll split tiebreaker authority, the partnership feuded, and the implosion in 2020-21 traces straight to that unresolved seat. The failure mechanism is not co-founding per se; it is the absence of a named, documented, single operator when the company hits its first real disagreement. Perceptron ML is two brothers, no disclosed CEO, no disclosed equity split, no disclosed operating agreement — and brothers are not insulation from this pattern; they are often an accelerant for it because the conflict is already pre-loaded with thirty years of family ledger. Michael's LinkedIn reads "attending YC S26 with my brother," which is a sibling note, not a governance structure. I have written this lesson into my body after GameCrush and after HQ Trivia, and Perceptron ML has not done the conversation yet.

The "magically weird" filter is the one that fails Perceptron ML on category compounding. Plaintiff-side legal AI is now the most-crowded sub-vertical inside the most-funded AI category — EvenUp at $2B, Eve at $1B, FirmPilot's $22M Series A-1 announced February 2026 — and the rule of thumb is that when four credible incumbents already occupy adjacent halves of your stated loop, the round you are buying into is priced as if the outcome is decided. Perceptron ML's positioning is "we couple detection plus campaign launch into one loop," which is a seam, not a moat, and seams close in eighteen months when Darrow ships a campaign action or FirmPilot ships an event trigger. The thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus, and Perceptron ML is being introduced into a category that consensus reached two funding cycles ago.

If the score on capital-intensity / unsexy-regulated were going to flip a tier, Perceptron ML would need to be operating somewhere the moat compounds with regulatory time — the Vigil Labs lane, where the founder lives inside a regulated-finance domain that compounds because nobody else will enter it. Perceptron ML is software-only marketing automation pointed at a regulated buyer; that is not the same thing as a regulated product. The legal vertical is the buyer's problem, not Perceptron ML's defensibility. To flip this dimension, Perceptron ML would need to be building inside bar-association compliance, ABS structures like Justpoint, or jurisdictional licensing — and the dossier shows no such posture.

Flock Safety is my single anchor on physical verifiability — I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café and a normal human could photograph the camera on a telephone pole within forty-eight hours. Perceptron ML is the inverse: the website returned HTTP 403, the product is gated behind a demo/waitlist, there is no app, no extension, no public artifact, and the YC description is the only window. I cannot install Perceptron ML, I cannot watch it run on a friend's screen, and the Marcotte brothers have not posted a GitHub repo I can clone. That is the developer-tools-abstraction failure mode taken to its purest form: founder testimony is the only available evaluation surface.

Forecast at 36 months: Perceptron ML is acqui-hired into FirmPilot or Eve for engineering talent at sub-$30M, with the legal-vertical thesis quietly dissolved, OR Perceptron ML pivots into one of the four verticals the YC description floats (construction RFPs, listings, trading-desk filings) where the incumbents are absent and the agent-orchestration credential transfers cleanly. The dimensional signature drives the forecast — strong agent-builder pedigree, absent domain biography — which is exactly the profile that historically converts to talent-acquisition or vertical-pivot, not category leadership.

The strongest case against my pass is the polymath override I gave DeepMind — Hassabis arrived with elite credentials, no outsider trajectory, and I wrote the check anyway because cross-domain compounding was the real moat. Michael Marcotte stacking Stanford math, Stanford CS, and NVIDIA agent infrastructure is exactly the polymath shape, and the U.S. law-firm AI adoption curve jumping from 19% in 2023 to 79% in 2024 is the kind of timing window where a well-built agent loop can land before incumbents notice the seam. I respect that case. It does not survive Perceptron ML's specific dossier, though: DeepMind's polymath was Hassabis cross-compounding chess, neuroscience, and game design into a research bet with a decade-long horizon, not two brothers building marketing automation for plaintiff firms over an eighteen-month commoditization window against $636M of adjacent capital. Pass with a rebuttal, not a hard no — the verdict flips if Perceptron ML produces a signed pilot with a named plaintiff firm (Morgan & Morgan, Sokolove, or equivalent) inside six months at contracted dollars, because that would prove the loop runs at the promised "hours" SLA in the real world and that the brothers have actually walked onto the well pad.
