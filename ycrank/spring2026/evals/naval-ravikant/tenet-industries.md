# Tenet Industries -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Emil Falk's WindowGlass Recycling machines deployed at four Ragn-Sells facilities by autumn 2024, and won Venture Cup Startup of the Year 2023. That's the single most interesting data point in Tenet Industries' dossier — a co-founder who has actually taken a hardware product from concept through automated manufacturing and into commercial deployment. Tenet Industries' thesis is "design-for-automation: engineering the drone around the factory rather than the reverse." If the core insight is that the factory is the product and the drone is merely its output, then Falk's specific knowledge is genuinely relevant. This is the kind of non-obvious founder-market fit that gets me curious. But specific knowledge in automated glass recycling and specific knowledge in automated strike drone manufacturing are separated by a chasm of domain expertise — materials science, guidance systems, electronic warfare hardening, explosive payload integration, military qualification testing. Hugo Frisk brings self-taught electronics design, which is a starting point, not a destination. No one on Tenet Industries' team has visible defense industry experience, military service, or aerospace engineering credentials. The manufacturing-automation meta-skill is real, but the drone-warfare domain knowledge is absent from the public record.

Tenet Industries' leverage architecture is where my framework gets uncomfortable. "Code and media are permissionless leverage" — but drones are physical objects with material costs, assembly costs, and delivery logistics. Every unit Tenet Industries produces requires aluminum, motors, batteries, explosive charges, and factory floor time. This is capital-plus-labor leverage, the lowest two rungs of my hierarchy. The design-for-automation thesis attempts to inject code leverage into the manufacturing process — automating production to reduce per-unit human labor. That's valuable, but it doesn't create zero-marginal-cost scaling. Compare this to Notion, where the same team serves one user or ten million users with negligible cost difference. Tenet Industries' revenue scales linearly with units shipped. Even the most automated factory in the world still has a marginal cost per drone that makes this structurally different from software.

Helsing has raised €1.37B and is delivering hundreds of HX-2 drones monthly to Ukraine. Stark Defence raised ~$100M from Peter Thiel, Sequoia, and NATO Innovation Fund, and was founded by Florian Seibel who already built Quantum Systems into a unicorn. Tenet Industries has four people, YC funding, and zero public traction. The bull case would invoke my Varda Space Industries exception — hardware bets where physics creates leverage that incumbents cannot replicate. But Varda manufactures pharmaceutical compounds in microgravity that are physically impossible to produce on Earth. Tenet Industries manufactures quadcopter drones, which Ukrainian workshops produce by the thousands monthly. The manufacturing-automation angle is a process efficiency claim, not a physics impossibility claim. The Varda exception requires leverage so structurally unique that it overrides my team-density preference — cheaper drone manufacturing does not clear that bar.

The principal-agent dynamics in the defense prime market are genuinely interesting for Tenet Industries. Rheinmetall's revenue depends on high-margin platforms; mass-produced expendable drones at sub-thousand-dollar price points cannibalize their core business. This mirrors the Google-Perplexity dynamic — incumbents as agents of their own P&L, unable to cannibalize. But when I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had spent years at Google Brain building the exact systems he was now competing against. He carried the incumbent's specific knowledge into a principal's incentive structure. Tenet Industries' founders don't carry defense-prime insider knowledge — they're attacking the incumbents' business model without possessing the incumbents' domain expertise. The principal-agent advantage exists in theory but is under-armed in practice.

"No shared prior employer or educational institution identified across the three founders" — this sits close to one of my red flags. Co-founders without pre-existing trust and shared history create internal principal-agent problems. I won't weight it as heavily as a startup-weekend match, because YC itself serves as a pressure-test, but combined with no evidence that any Tenet Industries founder was building drones before deciding to start a company, the principal authenticity signal is muted. Falk's prior company was in glass recycling. Frisk's LinkedIn says electronics designer. Neither was obsessively building strike drones because they couldn't stop. They identified an opportunity — Sweden's $440M unmanned combat systems allocation, NATO expansion, the Ukraine demand signal — and applied their manufacturing skills to it. Opportunity identification is not the same as specific knowledge productization.

The strongest bull case: Tenet Industries is positioned inside a NATO member state (Sweden joined March 2024) at the exact moment European defense spending is surging to 3.5% GDP targets. Sweden allocated SEK 4B (~$440M) for unmanned combat systems with 2026-2028 delivery timelines. Germany awarded ~€900M in drone brigade contracts. The timing is extraordinary. A manufacturing-first team with Falk's proven track record could capture a niche where the large players — Helsing with its AI focus, Stark with its premium systems — are overengineered and overpriced for the attrition-warfare use case. If Tenet Industries demonstrates a genuine 10x cost advantage at production scale, government buyers would have economic incentive to dual-source from them alongside the incumbents. The condition that would flip me: if Tenet Industries showed me a working prototype with a verified per-unit cost under $500 at batch production, plus a signed LOI or evaluation contract with the Swedish Armed Forces, that would change the calculus. Manufacturing-specific knowledge plus a government buyer in hand plus cost proof would be sufficient signal.

But I cannot get there today. Tenet Industries is a capital-intensive defense hardware company with no public traction, no battlefield validation, no visible drone-domain expertise, competing against billion-dollar-funded competitors with unicorn-founder pedigrees and active government contracts. The manufacturing-automation insight from Falk is real but insufficient to overcome the structural mismatch with my framework. I need permissionless leverage, and drones have marginal cost. I need specific knowledge density, and the team has manufacturing knowledge but not drone-warfare knowledge. I need a company that could be a billion-dollar business with five people — Tenet Industries will need hundreds of employees, factory floor space, and defense procurement staff long before it reaches meaningful revenue. The two dimensions most decisive here are Specific Knowledge Productization — where the team has adjacent but not direct domain expertise — and Permissionless Leverage Architecture — where the physics of hardware manufacturing makes zero-marginal-cost scaling impossible. Pass.
