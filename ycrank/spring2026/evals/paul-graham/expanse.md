# Expanse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Ismaeel Bashir's MSc dissertation at EPCC is the product, and the company's lowest dimension is that GPU efficiency in 2026 is the most consensus-hot infrastructure topic on the planet — that pairing tells you what Expanse is. It is a founder-market-fit company built by people who genuinely live in the future, attacking a problem that every other infrastructure investor is already chasing through the front door of NVIDIA Run:ai's $700M wake. The authenticity is real. The contrarian premium is mostly gone.

On organic problem discovery, where Expanse is strongest, the high rung is Dropbox — Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive and built a company out of the frustration. The mid rung is Octopart, where physics grad students were tired of searching for electronic parts. The low rung is the consultant team in my pass example, who "identified an opportunity" through 50 customer interviews. Expanse sits closer to Dropbox than to Octopart: Bashir's master's thesis was literally titled "Multi-modal HPC Resource and Failure Prediction," and Melake and Mendi were running HPC and GPU training workloads at G-Research before they wrote a line of Expanse code. They didn't read a McKinsey deck about GPU waste. They were the people staring at a 32-GPU cluster running at 37% utilization and thinking the obvious thought.

On the idea repelling before it attracts — Expanse's weakest dimension — the high rung is Stripe, which tackled PCI compliance and bank integrations when every other developer-tools company avoided regulated infrastructure. The mid rung is ClassDojo, selling into K-12 procurement bureaucracy. The low rung is again the workflow-automation consultants whose pitch could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece. Expanse sits between mid and low: the Slurm/national-supercomputing slice is a real schlep that hyperscaler tooling ignores, but "30% of cloud spend is lost to over-allocation" is the most pitched sentence in infrastructure right now, and the dossier names four direct or adjacent competitors (Run:ai, WoolyAI, hosted.ai, SchedMD plus open-source predictors on arXiv). When NVIDIA is open-sourcing Run:ai and academic papers already publish Slurm prediction frameworks, the "seems like a bad idea" filter has not screened anyone out.

Founder resourcefulness under constraint is where the dossier is silent — and like the consultants in my pass example versus the Airbnb cereal-box story, silence is mildly negative. Expanse has no scrappy artifact: no shipped product before YC, no story of selling a pilot before raising, no $40-cereal-box equivalent. Bashir is a 2026 master's graduate with a 86% first-class bachelor's; the team looks formidable on paper but resembles the polished pre-seed profile rather than the Brian-Chesky-eats-cereal one. I'd grade this at the absent-evidence default minus a hair.

On founder learning rate, Patrick Collison shipped Auctomatic before Stripe and that was the signal; Sam Altman at Loopt couldn't have predicted he'd run YC. Expanse's slope is observable only through Bashir's thesis publication trajectory and Mendi's HackTheBurgh Marshall Wace prize — real but thin. On earnest builder identity, Watsi is the high rung, the consultants are the low rung; Expanse looks earnest in the Bashir-thesis-is-the-product way, but the brand fragmentation across expanse.sh, expanse.org.uk, and getexpanse.io is the kind of detail that suggests the company hasn't yet had its serious naming conversation. Not disqualifying. Not Watsi.

My 36-month forecast: Expanse reaches $1–3M ARR with four to six enterprise HPC and quant-fund logos, then gets acquired by SchedMD or NVIDIA in the $30–80M range as Run:ai's open-source release pulls the predictive-scheduling layer toward Kubernetes and Slurm-native productization becomes a feature, not a company. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: genuine founder-market fit drives real early traction in the underserved Slurm/HPC niche, but the idea's consensus-hot framing means the exit-defining moat never materializes against an incumbent that owns the GPU itself.

The bull case stings, and it's the Bindwell case. Two teenagers with novel research IP attacking an unsexy domain — pesticide chemistry — is exactly the override pattern I just personally funded. Bashir's "beat every published baseline" claim, if it survives peer scrutiny, plus G-Research user-side credibility from Melake and Mendi, plus national-supercomputing procurement as a genuine schlep moat, is a credible Bindwell-shaped argument. The reason it doesn't quite carry: Bindwell's molecules are things the world has not yet made; Expanse's predictor is a productized version of published academic work. Schlep blindness needs the schlep to repel competitors, and Run:ai's $700M acquisition and open-source plan demonstrate that this schlep does not repel — it attracts the largest possible incumbent. The condition that flips my verdict is a signed paid pilot with a national supercomputing centre or a top-five quant fund within six months of YC Demo Day, demonstrating that the Slurm-native procurement schlep is in fact a moat rather than a delay.
