# Oxus -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first question I ask is what the founders know about internal audit that cannot be taught in a classroom. Janet Liu interned at the Federal Reserve on CBDC research and did investment strategy at D.E. Shaw. Melinda Liu worked in finance at JPMorgan and built AI products for fintech. Christine Watts consulted at BCG and NERA and built ML systems at startups. These are impressive credentials from impressive institutions. But none of them are audit credentials. SOX compliance is a peculiar discipline -- it lives at the intersection of accounting standards, control testing methodology, regulatory liability, and organizational politics. The person who should build this company has spent years inside an internal audit function, has personally drafted hundreds of workpapers, has argued with external auditors about control deficiencies, and has felt the specific pain of PCAOB inspection findings in their bones. What I see instead is three MIT graduates with finance-adjacent backgrounds who identified a large, painful, automatable workflow. That's pattern recognition, not specific knowledge. Anyone who spent six months studying internal audit departments could reach the same conclusions about what to build.

The leverage question is where this company gets interesting -- and also where it falls short of what I look for. Replacing $200-800/hour outsourced audit labor with software is genuine economic leverage. The pricing headroom is enormous. But the leverage architecture itself is standard vertical SaaS: build a tool, sell it to enterprises one at a time through direct sales to Chief Audit Executives. There are no network effects. No protocol. No platform that other tools build on. Each customer deployment is independent -- an auditor using Oxus at Company A creates no value for Company B. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question answered makes the platform more valuable for every future developer. Or AngelList, where every new investor on the platform increases deal flow for every founder. Oxus is a productivity multiplier for individual audit teams, not a leverage-creating platform. That is a meaningful business, but it is a linear one in the dimension that matters most to me.

The timing argument is the strongest part of the bull case, and I want to engage with it honestly. LLMs crossed a capability threshold for document understanding and structured output that makes automated evidence review technically feasible for the first time. The accounting talent shortage is real and worsening. SOX compliance costs are rising. If you believe AI can generate trustworthy audit workpapers -- and the auditor-in-the-loop design is the right trust architecture -- then whoever builds this first and earns the trust of a few dozen Chief Audit Executives could own a category. AuditBoard's $3B acquisition proves the market rewards audit software at scale. The question is whether Oxus is positioned to be that winner. The structural channel conflict concerns me: the Big Four firms who currently bill $200-800/hour for this work are also the trusted advisors who influence purchasing decisions at exactly the companies Oxus needs to sell to. You are building a product whose value proposition is to eliminate the revenue of your most natural distribution partners. That is a solvable problem, but solving it requires deep relationships inside audit departments -- the kind of relationships that come from having been an auditor, not from having interned at the Fed.

The contrarian positioning test is where this company scores lowest for me. "AI will automate professional services workflows" is not a contrarian thesis in 2026 -- it is the consensus view of every venture capital firm, every consulting company's strategy deck, and every enterprise software incumbent's roadmap. AuditBoard is already marketing "AI-driven insights." Workiva has launched "Workiva AI." The Big Four are all building internal AI tools for audit. Oxus is not betting against a gloomy crowd; it is racing alongside an optimistic one. The specific SOX niche narrows the competition somewhat, but the thesis itself -- that AI can automate document-heavy compliance workflows -- is one of the most widely held beliefs in technology right now. When everyone agrees the future looks a certain way, the pricing already reflects that optimism.

I give the founders the benefit of the doubt on integrity and long-term orientation. Two sisters who co-authored children's economics books together, co-founded a nonprofit, and then started a company together have a relationship that predates the startup by decades. That is the kind of foundation that survives hard times. No red flags on character. The children's books are actually an interesting signal -- not for audit expertise, but for the ability to distill complex financial concepts into clear language, which is relevant to generating readable audit documentation. But liking the founders and wanting to work with them for a decade is necessary, not sufficient. The gap remains: specific knowledge of the audit domain that would take a competitor years to replicate. I do not see evidence of that gap.

The defensibility question comes down to whether domain-specific AI for SOX compliance compounds over time or gets commoditized. Today, encoding PCAOB standards and control testing methodologies into AI workflows is genuinely hard -- it requires both technical and audit expertise. But LLM capabilities are commoditizing rapidly, and AuditBoard with $200M+ ARR and $3B in backing can hire audit domain experts and ML engineers to build comparable features as a module within their existing platform. Oxus's best defense would be accumulated customer data and workflow templates that make each deployment stickier, but at pre-seed with no disclosed customers, that compounding has not begun. The flexible deployment option (customer-hosted) is smart for audit data sensitivity but also limits the data network effects that could create a moat.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a real market with good timing. But through my lens, it is missing the ingredients that produce asymmetric returns: non-teachable founder knowledge, a new form of leverage that did not exist before, and a contrarian position that smart people currently dismiss. It is vertical AI SaaS applied to a specific workflow by smart generalists. I pass.
