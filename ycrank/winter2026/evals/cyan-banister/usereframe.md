# Reframe -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Reframe: "AI agents for procurement" is one of the most popular categories in venture right now. Procure AI just raised $13 million. Pavus AI raised $5.3 million. Seventy-three percent of procurement leaders say they've adopted generative AI. When I see a crowded category where well-funded competitors are racing toward the same vision, my instinct isn't excitement -- it's caution. The most promising companies I've backed were ones nobody else wanted to fund. SpaceX when private rockets were a punchline, Crusoe when burning gas flares for computation sounded deranged. Reframe is building something that sounds completely reasonable to every other investor in Silicon Valley, and that's precisely what makes me hesitate. The vertical focus on hardware build matrices is a genuine differentiation, but it's a niche carved within a consensus wave, not a contrarian bet against the grain.

When I try to understand the founders' stories -- not the business, the stories -- I find two impressive résumés but no biographical thread that answers the question I always ask: why does this problem haunt you? Bryan Zin went to Cornell, designed iPhones at Apple, worked at NASA and BAE Systems. Eric Wiener did ML engineering at DoorDash Labs and Zoox. These are serious people with serious credentials. Bryan clearly saw hardware supply chain chaos from inside Apple's machine, and that proximity to the problem is real. But experiencing a workflow inefficiency at a Fortune 10 company is fundamentally different from the kind of founder-problem bond I've learned to trust. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research that nobody cared about, driven by conviction about a problem most people thought was unsolvable. I need to feel that the founder would be doing this work even if no one was watching, even if no one was funding it. Here, what I see is two talented engineers identifying a logical opportunity coming out of strong companies. That's how most good startups begin -- but it's not the signal that makes me write checks from my own money.

I also want to be honest about what this company does and doesn't do for the world. My portfolio keeps pulling me toward companies that open doors for people who didn't have access before -- Uber creating income for drivers, Affirm giving transparent credit to people shut out of the system, Flock Safety making communities safer. Reframe makes hardware procurement faster for consumer electronics companies managing overseas production. That's genuinely useful, but the beneficiaries are already well-resourced engineering teams at companies that can afford international manufacturing. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to people who need it. It doesn't create economic opportunity where none existed. For some investors, that's irrelevant -- they just want a returning dollar. But impact is how I calibrate conviction, and I can't manufacture it here.

The strongest bull case is this: Bryan Zin is one of maybe a few hundred people on earth who understand iPhone-scale build matrix coordination from the inside, and that domain specificity is exactly what horizontal AI procurement tools will never replicate. If Reframe accumulates proprietary data on supplier pricing, lead times, and reliability across dozens of hardware customers, that dataset becomes a genuine moat -- a network effect where every build processed makes the next negotiation smarter. The large incumbents like SAP and Oracle won't dedicate engineering resources to this niche. The timing is real, the LLM capability threshold has been crossed, and the vertical wedge strategy is sound. If Bryan's Apple network opens doors to the first ten customers and the product delivers measurable cost savings, this could compound quickly within the hardware manufacturing world. I can see it. But seeing the rational case isn't the same as feeling the jolt.

Two final observations. The co-founder relationship shows a shared employer in Apple but no evidence of how they navigate disagreement, and after HQ Trivia I always probe that. And the commoditization risk is genuine -- the underlying LLM capabilities powering these agents are broadly available, so differentiation depends entirely on domain-specific workflow design and accumulated data, neither of which exists yet. This is execution risk dressed up as technology risk, and I've found that execution-only bets in hot categories tend to compress toward commodity returns. Reframe is a well-conceived company by competent founders in a market that's ready. For many investors, that's enough. For me, it's missing the weirdness, the biographical urgency, and the world-changing ambition that make me reach for my checkbook.
