# 9 Mothers -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the first thing I'd scrutinize. Allen Control Systems raised $30M from Craft Ventures in March 2025, won the Army's xTechOverwatch competition in February 2026, secured a SOCOM maritime contract, and tripled its Austin operations. ACS is building essentially the same thing -- an autonomous kinetic turret for counter-drone defense -- and is meaningfully ahead in the one dimension that matters most in defense: government procurement relationships. When I mapped AI market segments in my "Market Clarity" framework, I specifically warned about entering categories where a credible player has already made what I'd call a market-establishing move. ACS hasn't made a market-ending move -- the C-UAS kinetic turret space is early enough that multiple vendors will likely win contracts -- but 9 Mothers is entering a foot race where the leading competitor has a 12-18 month head start in procurement, broader weapon integration (Groups 1-3 versus EDDA's Group 1 focus), and significantly more capital. That's not disqualifying, but it means the burden of proof on differentiation is higher.

The structural moment itself is as clear as anything I've evaluated recently. Ukraine fundamentally rewired how every NATO military thinks about drone threats. Cheap FPV suicide drones -- $500 platforms destroying $5M vehicles -- created an unfavorable cost exchange ratio that existing missile-based and electronic warfare countermeasures can't solve economically. The U.S. Army requested $500M for counter-UAS in FY2025. And the emergence of autonomous and fiber-optic-guided drones that don't rely on RF links rendered jamming-based approaches structurally obsolete for a growing threat category, creating specific demand for kinetic effectors. This is a genuine technology-and-battlefield inflection point. But here's my concern: this inflection is consensus. Every defense-focused investor sees it. Defense-tech venture funding nearly doubled to $49.1B in 2025. Counter-UAS is not a non-obvious market -- it's the most obvious market in defense technology right now. My best returns come from markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors. Nobody is dismissing C-UAS.

Where 9 Mothers might have a non-obvious angle is the specific product segment: small, low-power, low-cost, soldier-portable. ACS's Bullfrog mounts M240s and M134 miniguns -- that's a vehicle weapon system, not something a dismounted infantry squad carries. If EDDA is genuinely portable enough for individual soldiers and forward positions, that's a different customer and a different procurement pathway than what ACS is pursuing. It's the difference between selling enterprise servers and selling developer tools -- both serve the same underlying need but through radically different distribution models. If I think about this through a Samsara lens -- IoT hardware that seems too small and too cheap to matter but becomes infrastructure because every unit in the field needs one -- there's a plausible path to scale through ubiquity rather than unit price. But I haven't seen evidence that 9 Mothers has articulated this segment distinction clearly, and no public data confirms EDDA's actual form factor, weight, or price point compared to Bullfrog.

The founding team has real execution history but a significant domain gap. Smith co-founded Rainforest QA through YC S12, raised $41.2M, and scaled to ~$25.7M revenue with ~141 employees -- that's operational credibility in the venture context. Khomenko spent 8 years as a senior/staff AI engineer at Rainforest working on computer vision and production ML pipelines, which is directly relevant to the core technical challenge of real-time drone detection and tracking. Their 8-year working relationship is genuinely valuable -- co-founder conflict is one of the three things that kills pre-PMF companies, and this risk is substantially mitigated. Pyzh's cybersecurity background adds adversarial resilience thinking. But none of them have documented defense procurement, weapons engineering, or military systems integration experience. When I backed Anduril, the structural market vacuum was so large that execution against an empty competitive set could compensate for learning curves. Here, the competitive set isn't empty -- ACS already has the procurement relationships and military partnerships that 9 Mothers will need to build from scratch.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: (1) the "light/portable" kinetic C-UAS segment is distinct enough from ACS's "heavy/vehicle-mounted" approach that they don't directly compete for the same contracts, (2) the AI/CV core -- where Khomenko has genuine depth -- creates a performance advantage that matters more than ACS's weapon integration breadth, and (3) DoD will procure from multiple vendors in this category as it typically does for critical defensive systems. If all three are true, the market is large enough ($6B growing at 25% CAGR) and urgent enough that a fast-executing team with strong AI capabilities could build a substantial defense company. The Garry Tan YC partnership and Theory Forge (defense-focused) backing suggest smart investors see this path. But I count at least two miracles here: the AI tracking system needs to reliably engage small fast-moving targets kinetically, AND the team needs to navigate defense procurement without prior domain experience. That's compounding low-probability events from a team making a significant domain transition.

I'd watch this company closely but wouldn't write a check today. The structural moment is real, the team has execution history and relevant AI skills, and the portable segment positioning could be genuinely differentiated. But the market is consensus rather than non-obvious, the competitive position against ACS is unfavorable on procurement timing, and the domain transition from QA software to autonomous weapons systems requires more evidence of bridging -- whether through advisors, hires, or early DoD engagement -- than I can see in the current dossier.
