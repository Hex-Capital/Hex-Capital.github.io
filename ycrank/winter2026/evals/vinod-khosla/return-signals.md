# Return Signals -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first: Ilya Valmianski built one of the largest patient-facing ML applications deployed across Kaiser Permanente's Southern California network -- a system where conversational AI errors have life-or-death consequences. He has a PhD in physics, published research in medical NLP, and founded a company that reached $40M ARR. And he's using all of that firepower to... text people about whether their jeans fit. This is a founder who has dramatically over-indexed on reducing the probability of failure. He picked a domain where the conversational AI bar is trivially low compared to medical triage, where the customers are mid-market apparel brands, and where the upside if everything works perfectly is a "5-10% margin uplift" for Shopify merchants. He's applied world-class capability to a problem calibrated two orders of magnitude below his talent. That mismatch tells me everything I need to know about the ambition level.

The consequence of success here is a good SaaS business. Let me be precise about that. If Return Signals wins -- dominates post-purchase conversational SMS for apparel, footwear, and accessories -- they've built a tool that saves retailers some percentage of their return costs. The company itself frames its value through an ROI calculator showing exchange value, keep-item value, and retention value. That's spreadsheet logic, not a vision for transforming an industry. Nobody's life changes. No incumbent system becomes obsolete. The apparel return problem is real -- it erodes margins -- but solving it is an optimization, not a disruption. They've explicitly narrowed to "e-commerce apparel, footwear, and accessories," which is the exact kind of manageable niche I react to most negatively. When I hear a company describe itself as targeting a specific sub-vertical of a sub-category of SMS marketing, they've already told me the ceiling.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. E-commerce returns globally exceed $800 billion annually. If the conversational AI layer works at scale, Return Signals could expand beyond apparel to all e-commerce categories, then beyond returns to become the post-purchase relationship platform -- the system that owns every interaction between a brand and customer after checkout. The 70% return-to-exchange conversion rate, if validated at scale, represents genuine economic value. The timing is real: LLMs crossed the conversational quality threshold in the last two years, Emotive got absorbed by Privy in July 2025 leaving a gap, and SMS adoption is accelerating at 40% year-over-year. Ilya's healthcare conversational AI expertise is legitimately differentiated -- he's built these systems at far harder difficulty levels. If you squint, this looks like Square in its earliest days: a simple entry point (mobile card reader / post-purchase SMS) that becomes a platform. But Square's entry point connected to the financial system -- every transaction created network effects and data that compounded into a lending business, a payroll business, a banking business. Return Signals' entry point connects to... SMS messaging about shoe sizes. The compounding mechanism isn't there.

The technology disruption potential is the weakest dimension. The dossier itself acknowledges it: "the core technology (LLM-driven SMS conversations) is accessible to any team with API access to foundation models." Attentive at $500M ARR, Klaviyo at $1.2B ARR, Loop Returns at $176M in funding -- any of these could ship a post-delivery AI conversation feature in a quarter. There's no architectural shift here analogous to Juniper's TCP/IP routing versus Cisco's legacy systems. It's the same SMS pipes, the same Shopify integrations, the same customer data. The "business model conflict" argument -- that Attentive's revenue scales with marketing volume while Return Signals reduces support tickets -- is real but thin. Business model conflicts protect you for 18 months, maybe 24. Then the incumbent builds a separate product line. When your moat is "we focused on this use case first," you don't have a moat.

What I'd tell these founders: you are too capable to spend your careers on this problem. Ilya built medical conversational AI at Kaiser Permanente scale. The rate of improvement in LLMs applied to healthcare -- diagnostic conversations, treatment adherence, chronic disease management -- represents a technology curve that could replace hundreds of billions of dollars in healthcare labor. That's a consequence of success worth the attempt. Instead, he's chosen the path where failure is unlikely and success is modest. I pass.
