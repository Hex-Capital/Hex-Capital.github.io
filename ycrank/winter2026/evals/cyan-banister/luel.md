# Luel -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Luel: this is a company operating in one of the hottest, most consensus categories in tech right now. Every investor I know understands that AI labs need training data. Every VC has seen the Scale AI valuations, read about the copyright lawsuits, and heard the pitch about rights-cleared data. When I look at a company and the first reaction from most investors would be "oh yeah, that makes sense" — that's not my signal. My signal is when people say "that's crazy" or "that'll never work." Nobody is saying that about AI training data marketplaces in 2026. The opportunity is real, but the contrarian edge has been completely arbitraged away. Defined.ai just reported 1,200% growth in marketplace partner datasets. Surge AI is doing over a billion in revenue. This is a crowded, well-understood space with well-capitalized players.

What I can't find — and this matters more to me than any market analysis — is the story. Tell me how William Namgyal ended up here. Not the business case, not the TAM slide, but the personal thread. What I see is two smart Berkeley students who spotted a market opportunity, dropped out, and executed well. That's admirable, and William's 200K+ hours of data collection experience is genuinely relevant domain expertise. But when I compare this to the founders I've backed who kept me up at night with excitement — the Crusoe team who independently stumbled onto an insight about waste gas that everyone else thought was insane, or the Contraline founder who spent years in academic research on male contraception because he was personally driven by the problem — I'm not finding that biographical thread here. William has professional proximity to the problem, not the kind of lived-experience commitment that tells me he'll still be grinding on this when three well-funded competitors launch identical products next quarter.

Now, let me be honest about what I do find compelling, because there's a real thread here that speaks to something I care deeply about. The contributor side of this marketplace — paying people $4.50 per video clip, $0.80 per audio recording, payouts via Venmo within days — that's creating income for real people from their everyday actions. Three million global contributors monetizing things they already do. That pattern reminds me of what I loved about Uber and Postmates: ordinary people gaining economic access through a platform. Patient-doctor conversations from South Asia, gemstone footage, Japanese medical interactions — these are contributions from people around the world who wouldn't otherwise participate in the AI economy. If I squint, I can see a version of this company that becomes the primary mechanism through which billions of people get paid for their contribution to AI training, making visible and compensated what is currently invisible and uncompensated. The rights-clearance documentation also fits my pattern of backing companies that make opaque systems transparent — like Carta did for cap tables or Affirm did for interest rates.

The bull case is genuinely interesting: Meta's acquisition of a controlling stake in Scale AI creates a structural opening for independent data providers. If you're xAI or Google DeepMind, you probably don't want your training data pipeline controlled by a Meta subsidiary. Luel could be the Switzerland of AI training data — the independent, neutral marketplace that competing labs trust precisely because it isn't aligned with any one player. The regulatory tailwinds from the EU AI Act and copyright litigation are real, and navigating consent management across jurisdictions with millions of contributors is operationally complex enough to create a moat. If both of those dynamics play out, and if the 3M+ contributor claim holds up under scrutiny, this could be a genuinely valuable infrastructure play.

But I keep coming back to the same concern: this is a well-executed company in a consensus space, built by founders whose biographical connection to the problem is professional rather than personal. The technology — a contributor marketplace with automated QA — is replicable by any of the incumbents who have orders of magnitude more capital. When Scale AI has raised $15.9 billion and Surge is doing a billion in revenue, the moat question becomes existential, and I'm not convinced that rights-clearance documentation alone creates enough switching cost to survive a determined competitive assault. I'd need to sit across from William and Inigo and hear something in their story that tells me they'll persist through that level of competitive pressure — not because the market is attractive, but because they can't imagine doing anything else.
