# ProjectX -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Two of ProjectX's three original co-founders are gone. Crunchbase lists Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das as co-founders from 2022; the YC page now shows Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder alongside Rounak Adhikary. At pre-seed, co-founder departures are either the best thing that happened — the team upgraded — or the worst. I don't know which this is, but I do know that four years after founding and two years after announcing a public beta, ProjectX's website returned a minimal loading page when researchers tried to access it. No user counts. No customer logos. No revenue signal. When I funded Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had engines firing. When I backed OpenAI, they had published research shaking the field. ProjectX has $204K from a Google Cloud grant, $72K in government stipends, and startup competition trophies. Competition wins tell me a founder can pitch. They do not tell me a founder can build.

ProjectX claims to be building "the computer that does everything at once" — a cloud OS where every application runs in its own isolated virtual computer with dedicated GPU, supporting Windows and Linux simultaneously in a single browser session. The consequence question: if ProjectX fully succeeds, what changes? Developers and power users get a better cloud desktop. The DaaS market is $4.5–10 billion depending on the analyst, growing at 12–19% CAGR. That is a real market. It is not a transformative one. Nobody's life fundamentally changes because their cloud computing session handles Windows and Linux concurrently. Compare this to fusion energy changing the cost basis of civilization, or AI replacing $200K specialists at near-zero marginal cost. ProjectX's ceiling, even in the generous case, is a well-run infrastructure company competing for share of existing IT budgets. That is precisely the kind of outcome I do not invest for — I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money, and the consequence magnitude here does not support the latter.

Bishal Karmakar's GitHub shows expertise in Go, Rust, C++, Kubernetes, and Docker — exactly the systems-level toolchain you need for GPU orchestration and container management. The per-app isolation architecture with sub-second cold starts is non-trivial engineering. I will grant ProjectX that. But the dossier itself states the quiet part: "The primary differentiation hinges on execution quality of GPU orchestration and cold-start performance, which are reproducible given sufficient engineering investment." This is not a physics breakthrough. This is not a novel engine design or a synthesized protein. ProjectX is combining existing container orchestration, GPU virtualization, and browser streaming into a better configuration. When I look for non-linear technical approaches, I look for the Rutherford engine — electric turbopumps that nobody thought would work. ProjectX's architecture is clever systems engineering, and clever systems engineering is exactly what Microsoft or Amazon can replicate with a team of fifty and six months. Neverinstall raised $1.21M building an adjacent browser-based cloud PC. Vagon raised $542K doing GPU streaming for creative apps. The technical barriers between these products and ProjectX's architecture are measured in engineering hours, not in breakthrough insights.

Rounak Adhikary started ProjectX at age 20 in Kalyani, West Bengal, bootstrapped initial development by running a web development agency called Woldix, and won Tiger Launch Global Finals at Princeton against India's top student startups. That biography activates something in me — I came to America with no connections, and I have a revealed preference for outsider founders from India building ambitious things. But my criterion is not grit. My criterion is learning rate. What has ProjectX learned in four years? The dossier shows no evidence of pivots, no evidence of what surprised the team, no articulation of what changed between 2022 and 2026. The beta launched in June 2024, and since then — silence. No public metrics, no product iteration narrative, no user community. When I evaluate YC companies, I look at the three months they have been in the batch and their rate of change. ProjectX gives me nothing to measure that rate against. Winning the WTC Innovation Award at IIT Bombay among 17,000 startups demonstrates the ability to compete in a structured environment. It does not demonstrate the rapid, self-directed adaptation I need to see.

The strongest bull case for ProjectX: GPU costs are collapsing as hyperscalers expand AI infrastructure, making per-app GPU allocation economically viable for the first time. AI agents need environments to operate alongside humans — ProjectX's multi-session, multi-OS architecture could become the default substrate for human-agent co-working. The team is young, hungry, six people deep, YC-backed, and attacking an incumbent-dominated market (Citrix, Microsoft AVD, Amazon WorkSpaces) that has not innovated architecturally in over a decade. If ProjectX nails the AI agent workflow angle, the TAM could expand far beyond the current DaaS market into a new computing paradigm worth tens of billions. I have funded young outsiders attacking calcified incumbents before — Mach Industries is a 19-year-old competing with Lockheed Martin. But the difference is Mach Industries is building weapons systems, where the consequence of success is reshaping national defense. ProjectX is building better cloud desktops. The exception does not apply. DoorDash lacked technical novelty but had planetary-scale consequence magnitude in transforming food access for hundreds of millions. ProjectX's consequence magnitude does not reach that threshold — even a perfect cloud OS serves thousands of power users and enterprise IT departments, not billions of people accessing a basic human need.

ProjectX would flip me from Pass to Invest on one verifiable condition: demonstrate that the per-app cloud OS is not a better desktop but the operating layer for autonomous AI agent fleets — show me 10+ AI agents running concurrently on ProjectX performing tasks that are impossible on conventional infrastructure, with a named enterprise customer paying for it. That would change the consequence magnitude from "better DaaS" to "the substrate of the agent economy," which is a genuinely different and larger bet. Until then, ProjectX is a competent team building an incremental improvement to an existing market. Consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach are the two dimensions that lock this verdict. A $5–10B DaaS market with reproducible systems engineering does not generate the asymmetric payoff I require. I pass.
