# AICE Power -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Rémi Bouteiller won the French Eurobot Championship in 2020, designed multi-chip PCBs at Amira Health, managed manufacturing runs in Shenzhen, and built an open-source ESP32 project that pulled 223 GitHub stars. That's a real hardware builder — someone who crosses from professional into obsessive. When I look at AICE Power's founding team, Bouteiller is the reason I don't immediately move on. He's shipped physical products end-to-end, from PCB layout through firmware through factory floor. That skill is rare and painful to acquire. Grégoire Chomette brings a different flavor — MIT aeronautics, NASA asteroid modeling, EPFL biogas supply chain research, seven peer-reviewed publications. The computational chops are real. But what I don't see from either founder is the story. The anecdote where they were broke and figured out something clever. The weekend where they personally wired sensors into twenty electrical panels because no one else would. AICE Power has 10+ sites live with logos like E.Leclerc and Accor, which at two people means they're working hard. But working hard and being relentlessly resourceful are different things, and the dossier gives me credentials where I want war stories.

Chomette's 2018 publication on biogas supply chain optimization at EPFL puts him in the energy domain, which is more than most founders can claim — he wasn't a consultant who "identified a market opportunity." But AICE Power's origin story, as presented, is a market thesis: smaller commercial buildings lack BMS coverage, and the existing solutions from Honeywell and Siemens cost too much. That's true. It's also the kind of observation you'd get from reading an industry report. Compare this to Drew Houston building Dropbox because he literally kept forgetting his USB drive on the bus. The frustration was physical, personal, and specific. I can't tell whether Chomette walked through an E.Leclerc, stared at an electrical panel drawing 40% more power than it should have been, and thought "this is insane" — or whether he read about the BEMS market growing at 13.2% CAGR and decided to enter it. The EPFL connection is suggestive (EPFL is listed as a client), so there may be an organic thread. But it's not visible in the dossier, and founders with genuine problem discovery usually can't stop talking about it.

AICE Power's biggest structural problem from my framework is that the idea sounds good to everyone. "Cheap energy monitoring that saves buildings 15-20% on electricity" — who disagrees with that? Nobody hears this and says "that will never work." Verdigris Technologies has been executing on essentially the same technical approach since 2011 with $41.8M in funding. Facilio raised $45.9M. Infogrid raised $148M. The BEMS market has attracted hundreds of millions in venture capital. When I see that level of consensus validation, my instinct is that the returns have already been competed away. The best startup ideas live in the overlap between "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea" — AICE Power lives squarely in the "obviously good idea" circle. Their differentiation is targeting smaller venues at 5% of traditional BMS cost, which is a real insight about an underserved segment, but it's a positioning choice within a known category, not a contrarian bet.

Now, an advocate would point out that AICE Power resembles early Stripe in one specific way: taking something that required expensive specialists and weeks of setup (payment integration then, building energy monitoring now) and turning it into a 30-minute self-serve deployment at a fraction of the cost. That's a legitimate parallel, and the advocate would add that Bouteiller's Shenzhen manufacturing experience makes the hardware scaling challenge less terrifying than it looks for a two-person team. The EU's Energy Performance of Buildings Directive creates regulatory pull. E.Leclerc and Accor are real enterprise logos at pre-seed. The advocate's case is that AICE Power is attacking a massive, underserved segment with genuine founder-market fit and a hardware moat that software-only competitors can't replicate. I take this seriously. But the Stripe parallel breaks down on the moat: the Collison brothers built a developer experience that was genuinely 10x better and accumulated network effects through platform integrations. AICE Power's clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware, and their AI disaggregation approach uses techniques available in open-source implementations. Verdigris has thirteen years of training data. The switching costs AICE Power creates are real but modest — any competitor can clip sensors onto the same panels.

AICE Power's learning rate is invisible to me from this dossier — no weekly growth metrics, no iteration narrative, no evidence of pivots or product evolution since founding. The company was registered in 2025 in Lausanne, they're in YC Spring 2026, and they have 10+ sites tracking 5+ MW. I can see the snapshot but not the derivative. For earnest builder identity, Bouteiller's open-source projects and competition wins provide some positive signal — this is someone who builds things because he can't not build things. Chomette's publication record suggests intellectual seriousness rather than status-seeking. Neither founder appears to be optimizing for press coverage or fundraising optics. That's worth something, even if it's hard to weight heavily from a dossier alone.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: show me that AICE Power's 10+ deployed sites are generating recurring revenue with net retention above 100%, and that the founders personally installed every sensor in the first fifty locations while simultaneously iterating the disaggregation algorithms based on what they learned in each building. That combination — revenue proof plus unscalable founder hustle plus technical learning loop — would demonstrate both the resourcefulness and the organic depth I'm not seeing. Without it, AICE Power is a competent team in a consensus market where the idea's obvious virtue is, from my perspective, its most concerning feature. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation are Organic Problem Discovery — where the EPFL energy connection exists but the personal frustration narrative doesn't — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where $235M+ in competitor funding confirms that this idea attracts before it repels.
