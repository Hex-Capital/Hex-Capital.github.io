# Checkmate -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking feature of Checkmate is what it reveals about the competitive landscape forming around it. AlphaSense -- a company valued above $4 billion with $500 million in annual recurring revenue -- launched its own AI Agent Interviewer in 2025, built on 200,000 expert transcripts acquired through its $930 million Tegus deal. InsightAgent is building the same thing with both autonomous and co-pilot modes. And Checkmate enters this field as a pre-seed startup with anonymous pilot testimonials and a Typeform access request page. This is not a founder who has discovered a secret. This is a founder who has noticed an obvious capability -- LLM voice agents just got good enough to conduct structured interviews -- at the same moment that multiple incumbents and competitors noticed the same thing. When everyone sees the opportunity simultaneously, there is no secret. And without a secret, the path to monopoly closes before you even start walking it.

The implicit thesis here is that being "network-agnostic" -- working across GLG, AlphaSights, Guidepoint, and any other expert network -- creates structural value that AlphaSense's proprietary-library approach cannot replicate. This is a positioning choice, not a contrarian truth about the world. It is the kind of strategic differentiation that an MBA would identify in a competitor matrix. Nobody smart actively disagrees with the claim that platform-agnostic tools can be valuable. The real question is whether this agnosticism creates a monopoly, and the answer is clearly no. Checkmate depends on the continued willingness of upstream expert networks to allow AI agents onto their calls. If GLG or AlphaSights decides to restrict automated participants -- or launches its own AI interview feature, which is the obvious move -- Checkmate's entire workflow breaks. A company whose existence requires the ongoing permission of platforms it does not control is the structural opposite of a monopoly. It is a feature waiting to be subsumed.

I will grant the bull case its due hearing, because the founders are not unserious people. James Sha has published robotics research with Pieter Abbeel, built and sold a company to CureFit, and worked on NLP at Google. Neil Garde spent eight years as a Google PM and built Monzo US from scratch. The technical capability to build this product is clearly present. The bull case would be something like: Checkmate becomes the "Stripe of expert research" -- a network-agnostic infrastructure layer that handles compliance, consent, transcription, and synthesis across all expert networks, creating switching costs through SOC II integration and accumulating proprietary interview data that makes the AI progressively better. If institutional investors standardize on Checkmate the way developers standardized on Stripe's API, the compliance infrastructure becomes a genuine moat. For this to work, Checkmate would need to reach critical adoption before AlphaSense's distribution advantage overwhelms them, and before expert networks restrict AI participation. It is a narrow window, and the company would need to demonstrate the kind of 10x quality superiority in interview output that makes compliance teams at top-20 hedge funds bet their regulatory posture on a pre-seed startup. I do not see evidence that this quality threshold has been reached.

The technology itself is the weakest element. LLM-powered voice agents conducting structured multi-turn conversations represent the application of generally available infrastructure to a specific vertical. The memo itself acknowledges this: "The core technology is increasingly accessible." This is not a 0-to-1 creation -- it is a 1-to-n deployment of capabilities that OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google have made broadly available. When I backed Palantir, the data-fusion technology being adapted from PayPal's fraud systems was genuinely proprietary -- no one else had built anything comparable for intelligence analysis. Checkmate is building on foundation models that its competitors use as well. The differentiation is in the compliance wrapper and the workflow integration, not in the underlying technology. Compliance wrappers are valuable but they do not create the kind of compounding technological advantage that sustains a monopoly over decades.

The company operates under three names -- Checkmate Research, Expert Insights, and the corporate entity Labyrinth Technologies Inc. This detail might seem trivial, but it signals something I pay close attention to: indefinite positioning. A founder with definite conviction about what they are building does not hedge across three brand identities. Zuckerberg called it Facebook and never wavered. The Collison brothers called it Stripe from the start. Multiple names suggest a company still searching for its identity, which in my framework correlates with the iterate-and-discover mentality rather than the build-toward-a-specific-future mentality. Combined with the a16z Speedrun participation -- which is explicitly designed around rapid iteration and testing -- the overall signal is lean-startup epistemology, not definite optimism.

I pass. The expert network market is real and growing, the founders are technically capable, and the compliance angle has modest value. But this is a company entering an increasingly crowded field without a genuine secret, built on commodity technology, structurally dependent on platforms it does not control, and facing a $4 billion incumbent that has already shipped the same capability. Girard would recognize the dynamic immediately: multiple players imitating each other into the same market, competing for the same customers with the same tools, driving toward the mutual margin destruction that defines mimetic rivalry. I invest in companies that escape this dynamic entirely by creating new categories. Checkmate is entering an existing one.
