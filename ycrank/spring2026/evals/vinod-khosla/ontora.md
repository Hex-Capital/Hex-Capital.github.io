# Ontora -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Max Arnold built agentic SAP process mining at Porsche and deployed it across four Volkswagen subsidiaries — before graduating. That's the single most interesting data point in Ontora's dossier. A 24-year-old student walked into one of Europe's largest industrial conglomerates, built the thing, and shipped it to production at scale. That trajectory — not the resume, but the rate of change visible in it — is what I look for in founders. Arnold went from leading seven engineers at a renewable energy SaaS startup to winning hackathons at ETH and Intel to building enterprise-grade process mining inside Porsche's procurement organization. Leon Iwanowitsch brings the consulting-side perspective, having built AI automations at consultancies and studying at St. Gallen. David Korn adds data pipeline engineering from finance. The three of them quit their jobs after visiting Stockholm's fastest-growing startups — that kind of impulsive conviction matters more to me than any business plan. Ontora's founding team demonstrates real learning velocity, and Arnold's Porsche deployment is not theoretical domain expertise but proven execution inside the exact buyer persona Ontora targets.

Ontora's structural bet against McKinsey, BCG, and Bain is where the strategic logic gets interesting. A $500K, three-month consulting engagement that samples 10% of employees, compressed to $50K and a single afternoon with 100% coverage — that's a 10x cost reduction and a 10x improvement in coverage simultaneously. The Vertiv testimonial confirms this isn't hypothetical: their Strategy Manager states that "what used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon." More importantly, the incumbents cannot respond. McKinsey cannot automate a $500K engagement into a $50K product without eviscerating the billable-hour model that generates their revenue. McKinsey's 2025 layoffs attributed to AI disruption suggest they feel the pressure. Ontora sits in a genuine innovator's dilemma — the kind of structural barrier that matters more than any patent. But I need to be honest about what this structural advantage produces: cheaper consulting diagnostics for Fortune 500 companies. Not a new market. Not new demand. Redistribution of existing spend.

Here's where Ontora loses me. I keep asking the question: if Ontora fully succeeds — if every Fortune 500 company maps its operations in hours for $50K — what changes for the world? Companies run more efficiently. Some consultants lose work. C-suite leaders make better-informed decisions about automation. That's a profitable outcome — maybe a billion-dollar SaaS business — but it doesn't change how millions of ordinary people live. Ontora's YC page hints at a bigger vision: the "operational context layer" that makes 95% of failing enterprise GenAI pilots actually work. If that framing is real — if Ontora becomes infrastructure for all enterprise AI deployment — the consequence magnitude rises substantially. But the evidence points to consulting replacement, not AI infrastructure. The Vertiv testimonial is about replacing manual interviews, not about enabling AI deployments. Ontora needs to show me the context layer working as infrastructure, not just as a faster way to produce process maps.

Ontora's core technology — LLM-driven structured interviews plus synthesis — is the part that concerns me most. The dossier states it plainly: "The core capability could be replicated by any team with access to frontier LLMs." Celonis has $2.4B in funding and $771M in revenue. Skan AI has $57.6M. Either could bolt on conversational process discovery in a quarter. Ontora has no patents, no proprietary models, no open-source presence, no technical moat beyond first-mover speed in a narrow application of commodity LLMs. This is what I mean when I talk about the difference between a technology wedge and an execution play. Rocket Lab built novel electric turbopump engines and 3D-printed combustion chambers — that was a non-linear technical approach. Ontora is applying GPT-4 to structured interviews. It works, it's clever, it produces real value — but any well-funded competitor can build the same thing. The moat, if it exists, is in accumulated process data across deployments creating benchmarking advantages, but the dossier finds no evidence of this capability today.

The strongest bull case for Ontora goes like this: enterprise AI is a $90B market growing at 26% CAGR, and 95% of AI pilots fail because agents lack operational context. Ontora is building the missing layer — the operational knowledge base that makes every other AI tool in the enterprise actually work. If that's true, Ontora isn't a consulting replacement; it's API infrastructure, a context layer that every enterprise AI deployment needs, sold once and embedded forever via API/MCP server integrations. The consulting cannibalization means McKinsey can't build it. The switching costs mean once Ontora's context layer feeds into a company's AI stack, ripping it out means rebuilding the entire operational knowledge base. And the founding team has the exact right composition: Arnold built the enterprise process mining product, Iwanowitsch knows the consulting buyer, Korn handles data infrastructure. The DoorDash exception in my framework — override the technical novelty requirement when consequence magnitude is large enough and the founder is a high-velocity learning machine — could apply here. The case is real. But DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of people. Ontora makes Fortune 500 operations teams more efficient. The consequence magnitudes are not in the same universe. And unlike DoorDash's marketplace network effects, Ontora's competitive position against Celonis adding the same feature is fragile.

The ambition level is what ultimately separates Ontora from my portfolio thesis. Nobody says "that can't be done" about AI-powered employee interviews. Industry experts would call this a sensible application of current LLM capabilities — and consensus validation is exactly the signal that makes me less interested. Ontora is solving a known problem with a well-understood approach, competently and with genuine domain insight. That makes it a good company. It doesn't make it my kind of bet. The condition that would flip me: if Ontora demonstrated that its context layer measurably improved AI deployment success rates at multiple enterprises — turning that "95% of GenAI pilots fail" statistic into hard evidence that Ontora-equipped companies succeed — then the consequence magnitude jumps from "cheaper consulting" to "enterprise AI actually works," and I'd want a second look. Arnold's learning trajectory and the consulting cannibalization dynamic are genuinely compelling. But consequence magnitude and technical non-linearity are the two dimensions that lock my verdict, and Ontora scores below my threshold on both.
