# Asimov -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about "internet-scale marketplace for robot training data" as a pitch: every investor who's been paying attention for the last eighteen months already believes robotics has a data bottleneck. Physical Intelligence raised $400M+, Skild AI went from zero to ~$30M revenue overnight, and the entire VC ecosystem has been repeating "data is the new oil for physical AI" at dinner parties since mid-2024. When I hear an idea and think "yes, obviously" -- that's a problem. micro1 already has $100M+ in ARR and is shipping Ray-Ban glasses for the exact same crowdsourced capture model. General Intuition raised a $134M seed. This isn't a sane person with an insane idea. This is an early-career team entering a market where the starting gun already fired and several runners have a quarter-mile head start.

The founders are technically capable -- Anshul Verma's time at Scale AI means he's seen the dominant data labeling playbook from inside, and BAIR research gives real ML depth. Lyem Ningthou has robotics background and prior YC experience at Blume. For undergrads still completing their Berkeley degrees, that's an impressive resume. But "impressive for their age" isn't the same as "uniquely credible for this specific problem." When I backed Solana at $0.05, Anatoly had spent years building low-latency systems at Qualcomm -- the "sane" part was so deep that it made the "insane" part (yet another Layer 1 during crypto winter) investable. Here, the founders' domain experience is measured in internships and research stints, not years of building the infrastructure they're now trying to disrupt. And the idea itself doesn't need a "sane person" to derisk it -- the idea is already consensus.

The structural moat problem is what really kills this for me. Strip away the narrative and ask: what is Asimov's business? It collects video of humans doing tasks, processes it into annotated datasets, and sells those datasets to robotics companies. The collection happens through a contributor network wearing headband cameras. That's operationally valuable but not structurally defensible -- the dossier itself acknowledges that "the core concept is replicable" and that micro1 is already doing it. The annotation pipeline is real engineering work, but it's the kind of technical infrastructure that a well-funded competitor builds in months, not years. And the 5,000 contributors across three continents, while impressive for a pre-seed company, is a distribution asset that micro1 could replicate by writing checks from their $35M war chest. The Birchbox lesson I carry with me is exactly this: operational execution without structural defensibility produces companies too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound. Asimov's moat is operational complexity -- managing hardware logistics and contributor quality across geographies -- and operational complexity alone is not a moat, it's a cost center.

What concerns me most is this fails my most basic filter: would this business exist without AI? No. Remove AI and there are no robotics labs buying training data, no demand for human motion capture at scale, no business at all. This is AI as the entire value proposition, not AI as cherry on top. And the downstream risk compounds: if robotics training shifts toward simulation (NVIDIA Cosmos), self-play, or data generated by deployed robots themselves, the external training data market could contract as fast as it expanded. You're not just building on AI -- you're building on a specific paradigm of how robotics AI gets trained, and paradigms shift. The customer base reinforces this fragility: a handful of frontier robotics labs constitutes the entire addressable market today, creating concentration risk that would make any investor nervous.

The bull case I have to acknowledge: if we're at the beginning of a robotics data arms race analogous to the LLM training data explosion of 2020-2023, then the company that builds the largest and most diverse corpus of real-world human motion data could become genuinely foundational infrastructure. The marketplace flywheel -- more contributors produce more diverse data, attracting more robotics customers, funding more contributor acquisition -- could theoretically create compounding network effects. And the Verma-Scale AI connection means the team has seen how a data marketplace scales from the inside. If Asimov captures even a fraction of the data standard for training humanoid robots, the outcome could be enormous. But this bull case requires Asimov to outrun competitors with 100x their capital, in a market where the idea is already consensus and the barriers are operational rather than technical. I don't bet on foot races against faster runners in lanes everyone can see.
