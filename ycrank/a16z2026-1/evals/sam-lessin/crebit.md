# Crebit -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Stablecoins for cross-border payments in 2026 is the single most consensus category in fintech. The GENIUS Act passed in July 2025. Stripe launched stablecoin payouts across 100+ countries. Mastercard unveiled end-to-end stablecoin transaction capabilities. Brex announced stablecoin payments. Latitude raised $8M from NEA with ex-Stripe, ex-Coinbase, ex-Uber founders building the same stablecoin-to-fiat bridge in the reverse direction. Every sophisticated fintech investor on the planet is looking at this category and saying "fuck yeah." And when every sophisticated investor says fuck yeah, the upside is already in the price. Crebit is building in the exact category where I have the least edge as an investor — because the market has already correctly identified that stablecoins will eat correspondent banking. The question was never "will stablecoins improve cross-border payments?" The question is "who captures the margin?" And when Stripe is your competitor for that margin, the answer is almost certainly not a two-person pre-seed startup.

Jensen Coonradt and Simmi Sen are clearly talented — MLH Top 50 Hacker out of 600,000 participants, MIT EECS, a prior project (Lendora) acquired by BankSocial, Stanford CS+Design, a streetwear brand that got seed funding from Taco Bell and was worn by Offset. Both are 19-20 years old and have reportedly signed 45+ U.S. university partners and processed $1M+ in FX volume within three months of launch. That execution velocity is real. But this is a case of capable founders doing the most obvious thing — and my framework specifically penalizes that. A sane person pitching a sane idea is just... a sane idea. There's nothing here that makes me think "that's fucking crazy." Routing payments through stablecoins instead of correspondent banks in 2026 is about as surprising as building a mobile app in 2012. The sanity-insanity ratio is miscalibrated in the boring direction.

Crebit is not creating a new primitive. International students have been paying U.S. tuition from abroad for decades — Flywire built a $623M TTM revenue business on exactly this flow using correspondent banking rails. Crebit swaps the settlement layer from SWIFT to stablecoins. That's a cost reduction on an existing behavior, not the birth of a new one. Compare this to Venmo, which created the behavior of mobile peer-to-peer payments — something that literally didn't exist before. Nobody will say "Crebit me" the way they say "Venmo me." Crebit is "better Flywire," and I invest in new verbs, not better adjectives. The stablecoin itself was the primitive, created by Tether and Circle and settled on protocols like Solana and Ethereum. Crebit is an application sitting on top of primitives others built — and I've said publicly that I invest in protocols, not dapps. This is the payments equivalent of a dapp.

The structural durability picture is mixed in a way that makes me uncomfortable. On one hand, Crebit passes the AI kill test — no LLM is going to commoditize actual money movement and multi-jurisdiction compliance. The local rail integrations (PIX, UPI, M-Pesa, PSE, SPEI) require real regulatory work per market. University portal embedding creates genuine switching costs once live. But the AI kill test isn't my only durability filter — the incumbent replication test matters too. Flywire will inevitably add stablecoin settlement to protect its 140+ currency, deeply embedded university business. When they do, Crebit's cost advantage evaporates, and Flywire's distribution advantage remains. Two people operating across seven countries also triggers my "multi-jurisdiction licensing" alarm — the compliance cost alone for Brazil, Mexico, Nigeria, Philippines, UAE, US, and Europe could consume Crebit's entire $2.5M raise.

The strongest bull case: what if Crebit's university distribution is stickier than I think, and what if Flywire's correspondent banking revenue dependency prevents them from cannibalizing their own margins for another 3-5 years? That's the Innovator's Dilemma argument — Flywire can't adopt stablecoins without destroying their spread-based revenue model. And in that window, Crebit locks in 500+ universities and becomes the default inbound stablecoin payment portal that universities never bother to switch away from. For that to work, Crebit needs to (a) actually have 45+ real university integrations and not just LOIs — this came from IssueWire, a paid press distribution service, not from a verified source — (b) sign universities at a pace that outstrips Stripe's ability to offer the same feature as a checkbox, and (c) avoid getting crushed by immigration policy changes that Flywire already flagged as a $30M revenue risk. That's a lot of things that need to be true simultaneously.

Capital discipline is the one dimension where Crebit looks reasonable — $2.5M raised, two-person team, $1M+ volume in three months, lean enough that the business might survive a funding drought. Jensen and Simmi aren't empire-building; they're shipping. But capital discipline alone doesn't save a bet where the core thesis — stablecoin payments are better — is already consensus and the application layer is about to get steamrolled by infrastructure players who can offer the same capability as a feature. My pass here is driven by two dimensions: consensus mispricing is terrible (everyone agrees stablecoins fix cross-border payments, so there's no asymmetric risk/reward), and primitive creation is absent (this is a cost-reduction play on an existing behavior, not a new category). Talented founders, wrong bet for my framework.
