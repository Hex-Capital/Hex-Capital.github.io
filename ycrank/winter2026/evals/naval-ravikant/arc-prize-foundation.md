# arc-prize-foundation -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The dual-entity structure is the first thing I see, and it's the structural question that dominates everything else. Chollet and Knoop run the nonprofit that defines the benchmark for AGI progress AND the for-profit company (Ndea, $43M from Coatue) that commercially exploits insights from that benchmark. This is like owning the ruler and the building simultaneously. In one frame, it's brilliant -- you own the measurement layer of the most important technology race in history. In another frame, it's a conflict so fundamental that it undermines the benchmark's credibility. The people writing the test also run the most well-funded test prep company. That structural tension doesn't resolve itself -- it compounds.

The specific knowledge here is undeniable and rare. Chollet created Keras -- 63.9K GitHub stars, the abstraction layer that made deep learning accessible to an entire generation of engineers. 71K citations. TIME 100 AI. But more importantly, he spent years at Google thinking not about how to build better models, but about what intelligence actually is. His "On the Measure of Intelligence" paper isn't a product spec -- it's a philosophical framework for evaluating cognition. That kind of thinking can't be hired or trained. It emerges from someone who sat inside the machine learning paradigm long enough to see its limits from the inside. Knoop brings complementary builder knowledge -- Zapier at $5B, profitable since 2014, is one of the cleanest demonstrations of automation leverage in the YC portfolio. But his transfer from workflow automation to AGI measurement is a hop, not a direct line. Kamradt's Needle In A Haystack benchmark shows he understands evaluation as infrastructure. The team's collective knowledge about measuring AI capability is probably the deepest in the world.

The contrarian positioning is genuine. Chollet's thesis -- that scaling transformers is not the path to AGI, that we need program synthesis and genuine abstraction -- runs directly counter to the $100B+ being deployed on the assumption that more compute and more parameters will get us there. Most of the AI industry is betting that scale is all you need. Chollet is saying: scale produces mimicry, not intelligence. If he's right, the companies aligned with his framework will be the ones that actually build general intelligence while everyone else builds increasingly impressive autocomplete. That's the kind of non-consensus position that generates asymmetric returns -- your losses are bounded, your upside is unbounded, and the crowd is looking the other way.

But here's where my framework breaks down: this is a nonprofit. I invest for leverage -- code, media, capital, protocol leverage that produces nonlinear output from minimal input. A nonprofit benchmark competition creates intellectual leverage for the entire AI field, but it doesn't create financial leverage for its backers. The standard I apply to every deal -- does each additional user, data point, or dollar make the company exponentially more valuable? -- doesn't apply when there's no equity, no revenue model, and no compounding financial mechanism. The value accrual happens at Ndea, which already raised $43M from Coatue and doesn't need my angel check. ARC Prize Foundation is a public good masquerading as a startup. Public goods are important -- I believe in open-source, I funded CoinList partly as infrastructure for open protocols -- but public goods don't return 1,000x.

The bull case I have to honestly confront: if ARC-AGI becomes THE standard benchmark for measuring progress toward AGI -- the way ImageNet defined computer vision progress for a decade -- then the Foundation controls the most important piece of evaluation infrastructure in AI. Every lab, every government, every safety researcher would reference it. That's Stack Overflow-level knowledge infrastructure leverage. And the founders who control that standard, through their for-profit entity Ndea, would be positioned to build the first systems that actually pass the test. The compounding would be extraordinary -- each competition cycle generates new approaches, new failure modes, new insights that flow directly to Ndea's research program. If I could invest in the combined entity -- the benchmark AND the commercial lab -- at reasonable terms, I'd write a check today. But I can't. The nonprofit structure separates the knowledge generation from the value capture, and the value capture entity is already funded by Coatue at terms I'm not seeing.

Extraordinary specific knowledge. Genuinely contrarian thesis. Wrong vehicle for angel investment. The founders built the right thing for advancing AGI, but they structured it in a way that makes the nonprofit a donation, not an investment. I need leverage and compounding equity to justify the risk, and I don't see it here.
