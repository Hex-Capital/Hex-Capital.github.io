# Gikl, Inc -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me first: two Meta directors who spent a combined 20 years building frontier AI -- one ran Media Generation, the other was in the Core Llama group -- leave to build a platform in the single hottest category in venture right now. AI for science. Sakana just raised $379M at $2.65B. FutureHouse pulled in $70M. Google is deploying AI Co-Scientist at all 17 DOE National Labs. Every LP deck I've seen in the last six months has "AI for scientific discovery" somewhere in the market map. So my first question isn't "is this interesting?" -- of course it's interesting. My question is: where's the mispricing? Because right now, I can't find it. When every sophisticated investor in the room is nodding along to your category thesis, the upside is already in the price. You're buying at the top of the consensus curve.

The pitch leans on an analogy that actually argues against them: "Coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers -- we're doing the same for science." OK, let's follow that thread. Who captured the value of coding assistants? GitHub Copilot, which is Microsoft. Cursor, which is a hot AI company with a known commoditization problem. The coding assistant wave wasn't disruptive -- it was sustaining. It made existing platforms more valuable. And Google is literally already doing the AI-for-science version of this, deployed at Stanford, Imperial College, and the national labs. When your own analogy points to incumbent capture, that's not a selling point -- that's a warning.

The founders are clearly impressive humans. Vajda has 6,500-10,000 citations and built text-to-image at Meta scale. Yamamoto published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, co-authored with a Fields Medalist, then went deep on LLM infrastructure. They worked together at Meta for nine years -- that's a real co-founder relationship, not some incubator match. I respect the credentials. But the sane-insane ratio is off for my framework. These are sane people pitching a... sane idea. "Let's use AI to help people contribute to science" is something every dean at every university would nod at approvingly. Nobody hears this pitch and says "that's absolutely nuts." Compare that to when I invested in Marina Mogilko -- $1.7M for 5% of a YouTuber's earnings for 30 years. People thought I was a lunatic. THAT's the ratio I'm looking for. The uncomfortable feeling that you might be wrong is where the returns live.

Now, the bull case I have to honestly engage with: what if ScienceSwarm becomes the Stack Overflow of scientific discovery? Stack Overflow built a community network effect that Google, despite having all the technical capability in the world, couldn't replicate for over a decade. If ScienceSwarm creates a dense, self-reinforcing community where open problems attract contributors, contributors build reputation, and accumulated approaches create a proprietary dataset of partial solutions to hard scientific questions -- that's a genuine flywheel. And these founders have the scientific credibility to seed the initial community in a way that random startup founders couldn't. Yamamoto can cold-email any physics department in the country with real standing. The "why now" is legitimate too: AI has crossed the threshold where a physics undergrad with the right tools can potentially contribute to problems that previously required postdoc-level domain mastery. If that behavioral shift is real and ScienceSwarm captures it, the primitive of "crowdsourced AI-augmented scientific discovery" could be as foundational as what Patreon did for creator monetization.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. The underlying capabilities -- literature synthesis, hypothesis generation, solution verification -- are all API calls to foundation models that get cheaper and better every quarter. The community is the only potential moat, and there's zero evidence it exists yet. No user counts, no revenue, no traction signals of any kind. The cold-start problem in scientific collaboration is brutal -- Polymath Project worked because Terry Tao personally rallied the math community around specific problems. That's not a scalable GTM. And the historical examples on the website (Foldit, Marjorie Rice) are beautiful stories that produced exactly zero venture-scale businesses. Grant-funded academic projects, sure. Billion-dollar companies, no. Without a visible business model, no monetization signals, and the full weight of Google and OpenAI building free tools in the same conceptual space, this feels like a feature waiting to be absorbed, not a company waiting to compound.

I respect these founders enough to say: if they came back to me with something that made my stomach drop -- something where the idea sounded genuinely insane and the market was running away from it -- I'd take that meeting in a heartbeat. Two people with their backgrounds and their relationship deserve serious attention. But this particular bet, in this particular category, at this particular moment? It's consensus-priced in a market where the incumbents are already moving. Pass.
