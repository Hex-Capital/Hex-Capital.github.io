# Didit -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most telling detail in this entire dossier is the GitHub organization name: `didit-protocol`. Didit started life as decentralized identity infrastructure for Gamium, a Web3 social platform with a token. That was closer to something I'd get excited about — identity as a protocol layer, a new primitive that other applications build on. Instead, the company pivoted away from the protocol vision and landed on... a cheaper KYC API. They went from potentially building the identity equivalent of Lightning Labs to building the identity equivalent of yet another SaaS vendor. That directional choice tells me a lot about how the founders think about their opportunity, and frankly, it's the wrong direction.

Let me run my primary filter. Alberto and Alejandro Rosas — identical twin brothers, professional tennis players turned ML engineers, co-built Gamium together, now Didit. The twin-founder relationship is actually a positive signal: deep relational foundation, shared cognitive framework, lifelong co-builders. Alberto's GitHub shows genuine ML chops — a StyleGAN2 implementation with 143 stars isn't tourist work. Alejandro built AI systems at Oracle. So: competent people. But where's the insane idea? "We'll build a cheaper, more consolidated identity verification API" is the definition of a consensus thesis. Every fintech investor on the planet knows KYC is expensive, fragmented, and friction-heavy. When I say "sane person, insane idea," I mean something that makes smart investors viscerally uncomfortable. Cheaper KYC makes investors nod approvingly. That's the wrong reaction. This is sane people with a sane idea — which in my framework is the most capital-destructive combination there is, because you're competing on execution in a market where everyone can see the opportunity.

Now the structural moat question, which is where this really breaks down for me. Didit is a software API company. Document OCR, face matching, liveness detection, AML screening — these are all AI-tractable capabilities that get cheaper by the month. Every major cloud provider already offers some version of identity verification. The "14,000 document types across 190 countries" sounds impressive, but it's a dataset, not a structural moat — it's a grind that any well-capitalized competitor can replicate, and some already have (Veriff covers 12,000+ types across 230+ countries). The free-tier pricing strategy is clever as a growth lever, but price-based differentiation in a software market is the exact opposite of a moat. When I wrote that "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore," this is what I meant. If your defensibility is "we're cheaper," you're one AI model improvement away from having your entire stack replicated at zero marginal cost. Persona has raised $417 million and specifically positions itself as "the verified identity layer for an agentic AI world." Veriff has $200 million. Sumsub built $85 million in revenue on $7.5 million raised. Didit has $2 million. The competitive dynamics here are brutal.

The bull case deserves serious engagement because the traction numbers, if accurate, are genuinely impressive for the capital deployed. Near-profitable on $2 million raised. 700+ B2B customers with 20% month-over-month growth. 300% net revenue retention at month six. Those economics echo Sumsub's capital-efficient trajectory, and if Didit can become the identity verification layer for SMBs and emerging-market fintechs the way Stripe became the payment layer for developers, the outcome space is large. The structural argument would be: deepfake-driven fraud creates an escalating arms race where verification becomes MORE critical infrastructure, not less, and Didit's aggressive pricing locks in a generation of customers before they grow into enterprise contracts. The free tier creates business model tension for incumbents who can't match it without cannibalizing existing revenue — the classic innovator's dilemma applied to pricing. If you believe identity verification is converging toward a utility with winner-take-most dynamics at the low end, Didit's position is actually strong.

But I don't buy it. The Stripe analogy fails because Stripe became a payment primitive with genuine network effects — more merchants using Stripe made the ecosystem more valuable for everyone. A KYC API is a vendor relationship. You can swap providers in a sprint. There are no compounding network effects from having more customers verify identities through Didit. The data flywheel argument — more verifications improve fraud models — applies equally to every competitor. And the "near-profitable" claim, while impressive, may reflect the constraints of competing at $0.03-$0.20 per check more than it reflects a structurally advantaged business. This is a software tool in a market that AI is making cheaper by the quarter, surrounded by competitors with 100x more capital, building an application on existing infrastructure rather than establishing a new primitive. It's the precise category of company I've spent the last two years publicly arguing against investing in.
