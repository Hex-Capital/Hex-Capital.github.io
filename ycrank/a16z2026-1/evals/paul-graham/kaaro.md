# Kaaro -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Two second-generation railroaders who already built and scaled AI agents for another phone-heavy, relationship-driven, legacy-system industry — that's the signal here. Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran were the first two engineers at Toma, where they built AI voice agents for car dealerships and scaled from roughly 20 to hundreds of customers before Toma raised a $17M Series A from a16z. Now they're doing the same thing for an industry they grew up inside. This is the pattern I trust most. When Patrick Collison built Auctomatic as a teenager and sold it, I didn't invest because of that specific company — I invested $15K in Stripe because I'd watched him build and knew the quality was real. Sai and Gautham aren't repeat founders in the traditional sense, but they're repeat builders. They've shipped the same category of product — AI agents automating communication workflows in a traditional industry — and demonstrated they can integrate with reluctant enterprise systems (CDK Global, Reynolds & Reynolds, Dealervault). The fact that they're 19 or 20 and chose to leave Toma to start Kaaro rather than riding the Series A upward tells me something about conviction.

The organic problem discovery is unusually strong for a pre-seed company. "Second-generation railroader" isn't a credential you manufacture through customer interviews or competitive analysis. They grew up watching railroad operations — the phone calls, the tribal knowledge, the manual paperwork — before they had the vocabulary to call it "workflow automation." Then they went to Toma, learned how to build AI agents for exactly this kind of problem in auto dealerships, and came back to the industry they already understood. This is the Dropbox origin structure — Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, and that personal frustration powered the company. Sai and Gautham grew up watching railroad operations run on phone calls and green screens, learned to build AI agents at Toma, and returned to the problem they'd always known. The discovery path is almost too clean: family domain knowledge plus technical execution experience converging on a specific opportunity.

Railroads are a masterclass in schlep blindness. When I talk about ideas that repel founders, I usually mean ideas that sound bad. Kaaro's idea doesn't sound bad — "AI for railroads" is obviously reasonable. What repels founders is the execution: integrating with Wabtec RailConnect's legacy green-screen terminals, navigating FRA regulatory requirements for incident reporting and billing, selling to CPKC and CN through 6-18 month procurement cycles, and earning trust in an insular industry where vendor relationships are measured in decades, not quarters. Optym has spent 20 years building relationships with Class I railroads. Railinc is literally owned by the Association of American Railroads. These aren't the kinds of incumbents you displace with a sharper pitch deck. You displace them by being a second-generation railroader who happens to have built this exact type of AI agent before. The schlep is the moat — and Kaaro has the unusual advantage of family relationships (CPKC engagement, CN conversations, CARS and NAMRC memberships) that no horizontal AI company can replicate by hiring a railroad consultant.

The risk I'd want to watch is the structural concentration of the railroad market. Seven Class I railroads dominate North American freight. Gulf & Ohio Railways, Kaaro's short-line customer, operates 200 miles of track with 30 locomotives — meaningful for a pilot, but the revenue ceiling per short-line is small. The real money is in Class I contracts, and "in late conversations with CN" could mean anything from a signed pilot agreement to a warm email thread. A two-person team burning through a $500K SAFE while waiting on CN's procurement process is a real scenario. If I were investing, I'd want to see either a signed LOI from CPKC or evidence that Kaaro's short-line deployments are generating enough revenue to extend their runway past the Class I sales cycle. The structural question is whether Kaaro can build a Stripe-like wedge — start with the small railroads (analogous to Stripe's early small-developer adoption), prove the product, and use that traction to sell upmarket. The ~600 short-line railroads could be that wedge.

What makes this a bet I'd take despite the concentrated market: the founders' path from Toma to Kaaro is the right kind of stubbornness — persistent about the goal (automating manual workflows in traditional industries), flexible about the context (dealerships to railroads). They've known each other for over a decade and built together under real production pressure. Gautham's VEX Robotics World Championship qualifications and mentoring of 100+ students, combined with his DMS integration work at Toma, suggest someone who builds compulsively — the "animal" quality where professional crosses into obsessive. The absent evidence that concerns me most is on resourcefulness stories: I don't have the cereal-box-selling equivalent, the specific narrative of creative survival under constraint. Their backgrounds suggest it exists — dropping out of Waterloo and UBC at 18 to join a pre-revenue startup is not a risk-averse move — but the dossier doesn't capture the anecdotes that would push my conviction higher. The two dimensions that most determined my score are Organic Problem Discovery, where "second-generation railroader" is among the strongest origin signals I've seen at pre-seed, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where the schlep factor is genuine but the idea itself reads as sensible rather than crazy. This is a founder bet in a domain that filters out everyone who didn't grow up in it.
