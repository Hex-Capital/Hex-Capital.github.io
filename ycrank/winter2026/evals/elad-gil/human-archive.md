# Human Archive -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the first thing that jumps out. Scale AI entered robotics data collection in September 2025, hiring hundreds of contractors globally for point-of-view demonstrations. Scale has $2B in revenue, existing relationships with Physical Intelligence, Generalist AI, and the other frontier robotics labs that constitute Human Archive's entire customer base. When a $13.8B incumbent moves into your market nine months before your YC batch starts, that's not a competitive concern -- it's a structural question about whether your category exists as a standalone business. The comparison isn't Scale versus Human Archive on data quality. The comparison is whether frontier robotics labs will buy multimodal training data from a pre-seed startup when Scale can bundle it with their existing labeling contracts and enterprise relationships. Distribution matters from day one, and Scale already has distribution into every major AI lab.

The structural moment in robotics is real -- I've been watching Physical Intelligence raise $1.1B, Goldman projecting 50K-100K humanoid shipments in 2026, and the capability curve of vision-language-action models crossing thresholds that make general-purpose manipulation feasible. There's genuine demand for real-world multimodal training data that can't be scraped from the internet the way LLM training data was. But here's where my framework breaks down for this company: this is not a non-obvious market. Robotics foundation models are the consensus hottest category in tech right now. Scale AI sees it. Generalist AI (Nvidia-backed) is collecting 10,000+ hours per week internally. RLWRLD just raised $41M. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody else wanted to fund operations alerting -- it seemed boring and niche. When I invested in Harvey, legal AI seemed impossible. Robotics data collection in early 2026 is the opposite pattern: every large investor and data company is circling this market simultaneously. The "why now" is obvious to everyone, which means the asymmetric returns I look for in non-obvious markets aren't structurally available here.

The "software-aware versus software-driven" distinction is the second concern. The dossier describes custom sensor hardware and operational infrastructure in India, with job postings for videographers and operations managers at $15K-$36K salaries. This reads more like a managed services operation than a technology company. The custom hardware is referenced repeatedly but never specified -- what exactly is custom about it? How does it produce data that a well-funded competitor couldn't replicate by deploying off-the-shelf sensors in similar environments? My experience building Color Health taught me that the moat in data-intensive operations comes from software automation that drives down per-unit costs along a learning curve, not from the physical collection infrastructure itself. I don't see evidence that Human Archive has that kind of software-driven cost advantage. If you removed the software layer from this business, you'd still have a data collection operation -- and that's the definition of software-aware rather than software-driven.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If robotics follows LLM scaling laws, the demand for training data will be insatiable -- orders of magnitude more data than any single company can collect internally. Aligned multimodal data (egocentric video, force feedback, teleoperation simultaneously) is genuinely harder to produce than labeled images, and custom hardware for capture could create a quality gap that contractor-recorded video can't match. The team's willingness to relocate to Asia and build collection operations from scratch shows the kind of operational intensity that matters in labor-intensive data businesses. And the vertical integration risk may be overstated -- model companies want to focus on model development, not building data collection infrastructure across thousands of residential and manufacturing environments. If Human Archive can establish itself as the quality standard for multimodal robotics data before Scale achieves comparable data fidelity through its contractor approach, there's a genuine business here. The parallel would be how specialized data providers in genomics carved out durable positions despite large incumbents, because domain-specific data quality mattered more than general data infrastructure.

But I keep coming back to the product-to-distribution trajectory, and it's weak. A dataset is a transactional product. You deliver it, the customer uses it, and they don't need you again until they need more data -- and by then, three other providers may exist. Stripe built a payment API that became a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Treasury, and dozens of other products flowing through the same developer integration. What's Human Archive's second product? What's their third? Data collection doesn't naturally compound into a platform the way infrastructure products do. The founders haven't articulated a multi-product vision in any public material, and the business model appears to be straightforward dataset licensing. That structurally caps the upside even if everything else works.

The founding team brings relevant technical backgrounds -- Samay's computer vision and robotic perception work at ML@B, Shloke's Stanford mechanical engineering and CHI '25 publication -- but none of them have deep robotics industry experience or prior startup experience at meaningful scale. Rushil's product role at Coinbase is useful operational exposure but doesn't map directly to building a robotics data business. These are sharp, young founders who've demonstrated willingness to do hard things by dropping out and relocating internationally. But in a market where Scale AI is deploying billions of dollars of infrastructure and Generalist AI has Nvidia's backing, execution velocity from a four-person team of undergrad dropouts faces a structural mismatch against well-resourced competitors who are moving fast in the same direction.
