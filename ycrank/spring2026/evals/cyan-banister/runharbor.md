# Harbor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

There's an invisible tax on clinical research that most people outside the industry never see. Medidata charges per-subject, per-site, per-CRF — pricing structures so deliberately convoluted that a small biotech running its first FDA study can barely predict what it'll cost before signing a contract. Eight-week setup timelines aren't a technical necessity — they're a revenue model disguised as customization, extracting professional services fees from sponsors who have no alternative. That opacity is the thing that caught my attention about Harbor. When I see a company offering flat monthly pricing against an incumbent whose entire economics depend on complexity-based billing, I recognize the pattern. It's the same structural dynamic as Affirm exposing what credit cards hide, or Ownwell making property tax assessments legible to homeowners who never questioned them. Harbor is saying: the emperor has no clothes, the 8-week build should take 10 minutes, and the pricing should be one number you can read on a webpage. That's a system being made visible.

Now, the founder story. Albert Cai spent four years at Biolinq — not in a strategy role watching from the balcony, but in clinical trial operations, shepherding a wearable biosensor from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization. He sat in the research sites. He watched coordinators spend hours manually entering data into systems that haven't fundamentally changed since the late '90s. He turned down medical school to stay in this world. That's meaningful — it tells me the problem found him through lived professional experience, not through a market sizing exercise. But I'd be lying if I said this is the kind of biographical forge that makes me lose sleep if I miss the deal. This is domain expertise born from career immersion, and it's credible. It's not someone whose entire life story bends toward this moment. Nathan Leung is a strong technical builder — employee number one at Jupiter, production systems at Google and Ramp — but he's the execution engine, not the domain compass. The CEO/CTO split is clean, which matters to me after HQ Trivia taught me what happens when it isn't.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. Five live clinical studies and six named customers at pre-seed, in a regulated health tech product that requires 21 CFR Part 11 compliance — that's not a landing page with a waitlist. The testimonial from Nova Genomics ("10-12 week timelines elsewhere, Harbor delivered in nine days at a fraction of the cost") is the kind of specific, verifiable customer love that matters at this stage. The free academic tier is smart distribution — it seeds the next generation of researchers on Harbor before they move to commercial sponsors. And the business model cannibalization dynamic is genuine: Medidata and Veeva can't easily automate away their own per-subject pricing without compressing the revenue that justifies their market caps. That's the innovator's dilemma in its textbook form. If Harbor executes, they could own the mid-market segment that incumbents are structurally incentivized to neglect.

What keeps me from writing a check is that this doesn't make anyone uncomfortable. Clinical trial efficiency is a consensus-good idea. AI in healthcare is the hottest investment thesis in venture right now. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we're making clinical trials faster with AI" — they nod and ask about your pipeline. Compare that to when I first heard about Flock Safety's surveillance cameras, or Contraline's male birth control, or Crusoe mining Bitcoin with flare gas. Those ideas provoked visceral reactions. Harbor provokes agreement, which is a different signal entirely. And the competitive timing concerns me — Veeva announced AI Agents across all applications launching through 2026, Medidata is embedding AI into study planning. These aren't hypothetical responses; they're announced product roadmaps from companies with thousands of existing customer relationships who can bundle AI features at zero marginal cost to the customer. A two-person team racing against that clock needs not just a better product but a fundamentally different architecture that incumbents can't replicate by bolting features onto existing platforms. The dossier suggests Harbor has that in their protocol-to-database automation, but general-purpose document extraction capabilities are becoming commoditized fast.

I respect what Albert and Nathan are building. The invisible system they're attacking is real, the early traction is genuine for this stage, and the impact on who gets to run clinical trials — especially through that free academic tier — connects to something I care about deeply. But my highest-conviction bets happen when the founder's biography and the market's discomfort converge in a way that feels almost inevitable, and this feels more like strong professional pattern-matching than biographical destiny. I'd want to meet Albert and hear the story behind the story — what happened at Biolinq that made him incapable of doing anything else? If there's a deeper forge there that the dossier doesn't capture, this could move for me. For now, it's a watch.
