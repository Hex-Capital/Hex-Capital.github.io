# Doomersion -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that immediately strikes me about Doomersion: everyone gets it. You say "doomscroll to learn languages" and every person in the room nods. Every investor smiles. It's clever, it's catchy, and it makes intuitive sense. And that's precisely the signal that makes me pull back. The most promising companies I've backed were the ones where people looked at me like I was crazy for writing the check. When I told people I invested in a company mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota, they thought I'd lost it. When I backed a male contraceptive gel, other VCs literally laughed. Doomersion doesn't provoke that reaction. It provokes the opposite -- immediate consensus that this is smart. And in my experience, when everyone agrees something is smart at the seed stage, the upside has already been priced into the round, or worse, it means the idea is accessible enough that larger, better-resourced teams will replicate it quickly. The original name was "Doomlingo" -- a pun on Duolingo -- which tells me this started as a riff on an existing category rather than an insight that arrived from a fundamentally different place.

Mostafa's domain connection is real, and I want to give him credit for that. Six years of teaching himself Japanese through YouTube immersion, the highest-rated post on r/LearnJapanese, a prior language learning startup, and a nonprofit serving 900 refugees through translation work -- that's a founder who has lived inside this problem. If I were sitting across from him, I'd want to hear more about the Healing Helpers work. Serving refugees through translation in Phoenix -- was that his own family's experience? Is there a story about being caught between languages, about what it feels like when you can't communicate and how that shapes your sense of belonging and possibility? Because that's the thread I'd be pulling on. What I can see from the dossier, though, is a talented Penn M&T student with genuine passion for language learning, not someone who has been through the kind of fire that creates the unbreakable founder. The Pokémon World Championships placement is charming and shows competitive intensity, but this isn't the biographical profile that makes me feel like this person will persist through the inevitable crises that come when Duolingo decides to add a short-form video mode and crush you with 52 million daily active users.

The bull case deserves serious consideration, because the early traction is genuinely impressive for a pre-seed consumer app: 15,000 downloads in two weeks, organically, with power users spending 3+ hours a day. That engagement number rivals TikTok itself, and if real and sustained, it's a signal of deep product-market fit around the scrolling habit. If Mostafa can build a content-difficulty matching algorithm that genuinely improves language outcomes -- not just engagement -- and if that algorithm creates a compounding data advantage, there's a path to building something defensible. The timing argument is legitimate too: TikTok trained a generation to scroll, and redirecting that trained behavior toward learning is a clever behavioral insight. If I squint, I can see a world where this becomes the on-ramp for a generation that would never have opened Duolingo, and where language acquisition unlocks economic opportunity for millions of young people. That's the version that would excite me.

But I keep coming back to the structural issues. The content appears to be sourced from existing platforms without licensing -- which means your entire product sits on someone else's terms of service. That's not technology risk, which I'm comfortable with; that's platform dependency, which is fragile in a way that can't be solved by persistence alone. The pedagogical efficacy is unvalidated -- users are scrolling for 3+ hours, but are they actually learning? If this turns out to be doomscrolling with a guilt-reduction wrapper rather than a genuine learning tool, the retention curve will look like every other novelty consumer app. And the commoditization risk is real: a short-form video feed graded by difficulty is technically reproducible by any team with a video corpus and a language classifier. There's no regulatory moat here, no structural barrier that keeps competitors out. This is a consumer app in one of the most competitive categories in mobile, going up against a $1 billion revenue incumbent that is already adding video and AI features.

The team opacity also gives me pause. Only one founder is named publicly despite a stated team size of four. After HQ Trivia, I probe co-founder dynamics explicitly before investing -- I need to understand how this team makes decisions under pressure, who holds what responsibilities, and whether they've stress-tested their working relationship. I can't do that when I don't even know who the other three people are. That's not a dealbreaker at the earliest stage, but it's a gap I can't ignore in my evaluation framework.

I respect what Mostafa has built here, and I think there's a version of this company that could work well in the right portfolio. But it's not my kind of bet. I'm looking for the founders and ideas that make other investors uncomfortable -- the ones operating in stigmatized spaces, building infrastructure at inflection points, or creating economic access where exclusion existed before. Doomersion is a clever consumer app with strong early engagement in one of the most consensus-attractive categories in tech. It's the kind of thing that will raise easily from investors who optimize for comprehensibility and market size. That's just not how I invest.
