# Oddpool -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Oddpool is a right-catalyst, wrong-curve company: Polymarket's $10–20M acquisition of Dome on Feb 19 2026 (theblock.co) genuinely opened the institutional venue-neutral slot — that's a specific 24-month re-opening, not steady-state — but Avi Arora and Ritesh Malpani are running at "several thousand dollars in MRR" (YC company page, May 2026), which is not the Harvey curve and not what "just working" looks like in dollars. Oddpool has the catalyst Anduril had and the curve Lyft had. That's the signature.

**Anduril** (2022) is the load-bearing analogue on catalyst. Ukraine made Pentagon software inadequacy legible inside a 24-month window; Polymarket buying Dome made the *neutrality vacuum* legible inside a four-month window, because the only YC-backed cross-venue aggregator just became a Polymarket-owned non-neutral feed (theblock.co, Feb 2026). Oddpool sits exactly where Anduril sat on the *category re-opening* axis — peers still think prediction-market data is a niche, the way peers thought defense software was a graveyard. Where Oddpool diverges from the Anduril rung is structural: Trae Stephens led contracts that compounded visibly inside the catalyst window; Oddpool's evidence of institutional pickup is one founder quote about "several thousand dollars in MRR" and 762 arb opportunities surfaced to Pro users last week (oddpool.com) — prosumer traction in an institutional thesis. The catalyst is real. The institutional buying behavior the catalyst implies isn't in the dossier yet.

**Lyft** Series C is the cautionary rung on revenue velocity, and not for the network-effect reason — for the *"if the curve were really there it would be in the deck"* reason. I passed on Lyft because the dossier couldn't show share inversion; I'd pass on most Oddpool-stage data plays for the same reason — "if you're an AI-adjacent company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss Show #863, April 2026). Oddpool's launch post leads with "several thousand dollars in MRR within months" and the Twitter alerts handle sits at ~233 followers, joined Oct 2025. Quant funds and prop shops do not buy market data on prosumer pricing tiers — they buy on enterprise contracts that show up as named logos, and the Oddpool launch page names none. The Harvey signature (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) was enterprise dollars compounding before the round closed; Oddpool's signature is $30/mo Pro seats and arb-spotter retail.

On founder shipping rhythm: the mechanism that compounds over 18 months is whether founders ship artifacts faster than competitors can copy them. Avi's prompt-injection model securing 20B Microsoft requests/year and Ritesh's 40%-faster Bloomberg buy-side systems are real shipped artifacts (YC company page), and the "Awesome-Prediction-Market-Tools" repo at github.com/aarora4 plus 700K+ indexed markets back to March 2026 (oddpool.com) are direct evidence of shipping into the Oddpool thesis itself. The compounding breaks on one fact: Ritesh's LinkedIn currently lists him as Co-founder of Benchspan (YC P26), a *separate* Spring 2026 YC company (ycombinator.com/companies/benchspan). NFX's *Unusual Signs* point about "every company needs a final decision maker" lands hard here — Oddpool has two founders, one of whom is also building a second YC-batch company. That's not a shipping-rhythm signal; that's a divided-attention signal on a two-person team.

Index position is the counterfactual question for Oddpool: what would have to be true for Oddpool to score a tier higher here? It would need three or more CFTC-licensed venues live and indexed, not two. Oddpool today covers Kalshi and Polymarket — the two venues every competitor (FinFeedAPI, Polymarket Analytics) also covers — and the "captured-history dataset that cannot be backfilled" moat (oddpool.com) only compounds if new venues keep arriving and Oddpool ingests them first. Founders cite "CFTC applications for new exchanges keep climbing" (YC company page) but the dossier names zero specific pending venue. **Stripe** was an index on e-commerce because every new merchant was a new Stripe account; Oddpool is currently an index on a two-venue oligopoly that already has a FinFeedAPI-shaped commoditizer covering Polymarket, Kalshi, Myriad, and Manifold (finfeedapi.com). The index thesis is plausible, not yet evidenced.

Single-miracle architecture is where Oddpool quietly does well, and it maps to **Color Genomics** (2015) as the single anchor on this axis. Color worked because the chemistry was commodity and the company built workflow on top; Oddpool requires no new science — symbology normalization and tick-history archival are pure engineering execution, and the venues themselves are the hard-tech layer Oddpool sits above. There is one miracle here, and it is *whether institutional buyers of prediction-market data exist at venture scale within 36 months*. Not multi-miracle. That's the cleanest part of the Oddpool dossier and the reason I don't dismiss it outright.

The 36-month forecast: Oddpool gets acquired by Kalshi or a CFTC-licensed venue operator for $15–40M inside 18 months — the Dome template, not the Coinbase template — or plateaus at $1–3M ARR as a prosumer arb-alert tool. The mechanism is the one Polymarket just demonstrated: venue operators cannot tolerate a neutral aggregator that surfaces competitor liquidity to their own institutional pipeline, so they either buy it or starve it of API access. Oddpool's 700K-market captured history is exactly the asset that makes the acqui-buy attractive and exactly the asset a venue starves by re-pricing its public API tier.

The strongest bull case against this forecast: the Coinbase (2013) template. Crypto looked niche; Coinbase became the basket. Prediction markets at Opinion's reported ~31% global share of $8B+ monthly volume by early 2026 (panewslab.com snippet) could be at the institutional inflection that Coinbase rode from 2013 — and Oddpool's un-backfillable tick history from March 2026 onward is precisely the kind of dataset that becomes load-bearing once Citadel, Jane Street, and DRW formally allocate to the venue category. If the CFTC genuinely licenses three or more new exchanges inside 12 months, Oddpool's two-venue limitation flips from weakness to land-grab position. The case stings because it's the same case I'd have made for Coinbase Series A. What dismantles it for Oddpool specifically: Dome already ran this exact play, built faster (months from Demo Day to acquisition), raised $5.2M (banklesstimes.com), and exited for an estimated $10–20M per Gabriel Jarrosson (X, Feb 2026). The comp is Dome, not Coinbase, and Dome is a venture-loss outcome at angel prices. The verdict flips to invest on one verifiable condition: a named institutional logo (Citadel, Jane Street, DRW, Susquehanna, or a top-10 hedge fund) signed to Enterprise tier within 90 days, disclosed in a follow-up update. Absent that, Oddpool is a competent acqui-hire bet at seed prices, which is not my model.
