# 9 Mothers -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive timing problem. Allen Control Systems is building the same product -- autonomous kinetic C-UAS turret -- with $30M from Craft Ventures, active Army and SOCOM contracts, and a broader capability envelope covering Groups 1 through 3. ACS won the xTechOverwatch competition in February 2026. 9 Mothers is entering a race where the most comparable competitor already has the government procurement relationships that take years to build. In defense, being second to contract is not like being second to market in software -- procurement slots close, budgets get allocated, and switching costs are structural. "Anyone in this business who's chasing hot markets gets killed" -- and counter-UAS in 2026 is as hot as markets get. Defense tech funding nearly doubled to $49.1B in 2025. This is not a contrarian bet. This is a well-identified opportunity where capital is already flowing aggressively.

Now, the specific knowledge question. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA to $41.2M raised and $25.7M in revenue -- he knows how to build and scale a venture-backed company. Roman Khomenko spent eight years doing production computer vision and ML at that same company, with prior time at DataRobot. Bogdan Pyzh brings cybersecurity and penetration testing from Leviathan Security Group. These are real, compounding skills. But here's the problem: they're skills in software QA, screenshot segmentation, and information security -- not in weapons systems, ballistics, military operations, defense procurement, or autonomous targeting under battlefield conditions. Computer vision for identifying UI elements on a screen and computer vision for tracking a drone moving at 80 mph through clutter in contested electromagnetic environments are separated by an enormous physics gap. The ML knowledge transfers at the framework level, but the domain-specific knowledge -- the hard-won understanding of what actually kills a drone in combat -- that has to be built from scratch. When I backed Evan Williams at Twitter, the non-teachable knowledge was his decade of thinking about public communication. I don't see the equivalent here: founders with adjacent technical capability applying it to a domain where they lack the years of immersion that produce genuine insight.

The leverage architecture is more interesting than a naive reading suggests. An AI-guided turret that autonomously detects, tracks, and engages cheap drones IS a leverage play -- it replaces what previously required either expensive human-operated weapon systems or even more expensive interceptor missiles with cheap kinetic projectiles guided by software. One engineer improving the detection algorithm improves every deployed unit simultaneously. That's real code-as-leverage. But it's leverage built on top of hardware that must be manufactured, shipped, and maintained for every deployment. Each unit has a marginal cost. The software intelligence compounds; the hardware doesn't. Compare this to Replit, where one improvement to the coding environment immediately serves millions of users with zero marginal cost. The leverage ratio here is better than a pure hardware company but fundamentally constrained by atoms.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If the founders can build a system that is meaningfully cheaper, smaller, and more portable than ACS's Bullfrog -- truly soldier-portable rather than vehicle-mounted -- they could own a distinct market segment that ACS's heavier weapon integration approach can't serve. The squad-level point defense niche might be large enough to build a substantial business, and the smaller form factor could be a genuine design insight that larger systems miss. Khomenko's Ukrainian background, while not documented as directly relevant to the conflict, may provide motivational authenticity that sustains the team through the brutal defense procurement cycle. The eight-year working relationship between Smith and Khomenko is a genuinely strong signal -- they've shipped production ML systems together, they know each other's capabilities. And Pyzh's security background is specifically relevant to hardening an autonomous weapon against adversarial attacks, which is a real and underappreciated challenge in deployed AI systems. If all three of these things compound -- a smaller form factor insight, deep co-founder trust, and security-first architecture -- the company could carve out a defensible position.

But I keep returning to the fundamental question: what do these founders know about killing drones that Allen Control Systems, with their defense contracts and military testing data, doesn't? The technology at the center -- autonomous kinetic engagement of small aerial targets -- is a systems integration challenge across optics, mechanical actuators, ballistics, and real-time ML inference. Building it well requires knowledge that accumulates through testing in realistic conditions, engagement data from military exercises, and iterative refinement against actual drone threats. ACS has access to that feedback loop through their Army and SOCOM contracts. 9 Mothers, with no disclosed government relationships, does not. In defense, the data moat is built through deployment, and deployment requires contracts, and contracts require relationships. The compounding advantage flows to whoever gets operational data first.

I respect the team's technical depth and the co-founder durability. This is a real problem in a real market. But the combination of hot-market consensus timing, a well-funded competitor with a meaningful procurement head start, and founders whose specific knowledge is adjacent rather than native to the defense domain makes this a pass for me. The asymmetric upside I look for requires either contrarian timing or irreplaceable founder knowledge -- ideally both. Here I see neither.
