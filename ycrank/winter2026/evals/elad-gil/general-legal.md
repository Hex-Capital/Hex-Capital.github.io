# General Legal -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural inversion here is what caught my attention. When I invested in Harvey, the thesis was clear: build AI tools that make lawyers more efficient, sell those tools to law firms. Harvey is a software company with software margins. General Legal flips that model -- instead of selling AI to lawyers, they ARE the law firm. They've looked at the Harvey playbook and concluded that the real value isn't in making existing law firms faster, it's in replacing them entirely. That's a genuine structural insight, and it's validated by Robin AI's financial collapse: pure-software contract review tools struggle because lawyers don't want to change their workflows. General Legal sidesteps that adoption problem by removing the lawyer-as-customer entirely. The question is whether this inversion creates a venture-scale business or a very efficient professional services firm.

The founding team is essentially the best possible assembly for this specific company -- and I don't say that loosely. All three built Casetext together, which became the first GPT-4-powered legal assistant (CoCounsel) and sold to Thomson Reuters for $650M in cash. Ryan Walker was CTO, Javed Qadrud-Din ran ML, J.P. Mohler deployed AI in federal investigations and judicial systems. Two Harvard Law JDs who practiced at Cooley and Fenwick -- the exact firms that represent growth-stage startups -- before pivoting to AI engineering. They've already built legal AI products, scaled them, and exited together. The co-founder relationship has been tested through a multi-year company-building cycle and acquisition integration. This is the kind of founding team where the execution question is almost a checkbox.

My core tension is the "software-driven versus software-aware" distinction. A law firm that uses AI to achieve 10x attorney efficiency is impressive, but it's still a law firm. Every $500 contract requires a barred attorney's involvement. Revenue scales with attorney headcount, not with server capacity. If you removed the AI, you'd have a flat-fee law firm with fast turnaround -- a better law firm, but structurally still a law firm. The counterargument is that if AI handles 90-95% of the cognitive work and the attorney performs validation, the margin structure starts approximating software over time as models improve. That's a real trajectory, and this team is positioned to ride it. But it's not there yet, and the gap between "attorney-assisted AI" and "AI-validated-by-attorney" is the difference between a $100M services business and a $10B platform.

The market timing creates a paradox. Legal AI was non-obvious when I backed Harvey in 2022 -- LLMs hallucinated too much, lawyers were skeptical, and the market looked impossible. That structural moment has passed. Legal AI is now one of the most consensus-hot sectors in tech: $6B in legal tech funding in 2025, Harvey at $8B, Spellbook at $350M, Lawhive raising $60M specifically to enter the US market. YC literally put "start your own AI law firm" in its Request for Startups. When YC publishes an RFS calling for exactly what you're building, you're no longer in non-obvious territory. The specific model -- being the law firm rather than selling to lawyers -- has a somewhat differentiated angle, but Lawhive has already proven the AI-native law firm model works at scale with $35M+ ARR and is expanding into the same geography.

The bull case requires you to believe three things: (1) the "be the firm" model for B2B commercial contracts is structurally distinct from Lawhive's consumer law focus, creating a separate category; (2) the attorney-efficiency multiplier improves with each model generation until margins approach software-like levels; and (3) the initial contract review relationship with growth-stage startups becomes a distribution channel for corporate governance, employment law, IP, M&A, and eventually full-stack legal services -- the Gusto trajectory where "just payroll" became the entire HR platform. If all three hold, this team is the best in the world to execute it, and the $500 price point creates a wedge that BigLaw can't match without cannibalizing their hourly billing model. That's a real structural moat. The product-to-distribution path is plausible: growth-stage companies whose legal needs multiply as they scale become captive customers if General Legal can expand its service offerings through that initial relationship.

I land at neutral-to-interested. The team is exceptional and the structural positioning against BigLaw is real. But the consensus nature of legal AI, the services-model margin constraints, and Lawhive's $60M US expansion create enough uncertainty that I'd want to see the unit economics prove out -- specifically, what does the actual attorney-time-per-contract look like at scale, and does it decrease with each model improvement? If the team can demonstrate that AI efficiency is compressing attorney involvement toward validation-only, the margin structure transforms and this becomes a different kind of bet entirely. Right now, I see a great team with a real insight building in a market that's no longer non-obvious.
