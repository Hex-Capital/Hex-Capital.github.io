# Dayjob -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Dayjob is what happens when an operationally-disciplined founder pair lands the right catalyst on the wrong-sized category: George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks have already shipped from launch to $496K ARR in seven months on top of waste-operator ERPs, but the underlying market they index against -- UK skip-hire and waste fleets dominated by AMCS ($273.1M revenue) and Routeware (1,000+ haulers) -- is not a compounding category in the **Stripe**-on-e-commerce sense. The high/low pairing is what defines Dayjob: real shipping rhythm anchoring a vertical that doesn't re-rate underneath them.

**Perplexity** (2022) is the founder-shipping template I keep coming back to -- Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing built, and that rhythm is the only legible pre-seed signal that matters. Dayjob earns the analogy on substance, not by analogy-flattery: Postlethwaite launched the UK grocery business at Deliveroo, ran sales at Otta, then he and Fooks (Oxford Engineering, Deloitte ESG/Supply Chain Analytics) co-built Gaea ERP from 2022 with depot ride-alongs before pivoting to Dayjob in January 2025 and hitting $359K ARR within seven months of the August 2025 product launch. Where Dayjob diverges from the **Perplexity** rung is the surface area of the shipping: Aravind was shipping into a category (consumer AI search) that was itself compounding 20%+ MoM around him, whereas Postlethwaite is shipping into UK waste depots where the underlying volume grows GDP-rate. The artifact rhythm is real; the substrate it lands on is not Perplexity's substrate.

**Lyft** Series C is the failure pattern I want to interrogate against Dayjob -- I passed because rideshare was a winner-take-all/oligopoly market where #2 captures a fraction of #1's economics, and the dossier never showed a credible share-inversion path. The waste-software market is structurally different (it's fragmented, not winner-take-all), but the closely-adjacent failure mode is incumbent absorption: AMCS already ships AI-driven routing inside its ERP per Tracxn, and Routeware acquired RUBICONSmartCity/Pro in 2024 (Waste Dive) explicitly to bolt fleet tech onto its existing 1,000-hauler base. Dayjob's "we overlay the ERP, we don't replace it" pitch is clever for switching cost but corrosive for index position -- they're a feature that AMCS's product roadmap will own a slide for at their next user conference. That's the Lyft pattern translated: not "beaten by competitor at scale," but "absorbed before they reach it."

On the catalyst dimension, Dayjob's "why now" is real but soft: agent frameworks reaching production reliability in 2024-2025 finally let you do exception-handling at the scheduler workflow layer that rules-based optimisers (RouteSmart, OptimoRoute) couldn't. Mechanism-wise, this catalyst compounds for Dayjob over 18 months only if the agent reliability curve outpaces AMCS's own internal LLM integration roadmap -- and AMCS has $225M+ raised to throw at exactly that question. The Ukraine-to-**Anduril** analogy doesn't load here; this is a horizontal capability catalyst applied to a sleepy vertical, not a vertical-specific re-opening with a named external shock.

Counterfactually, the revenue velocity score flips a tier if Dayjob discloses month-over-month growth above 20% rather than a launch-to-date $496K figure across three named customers (Allstone, Coastal Recycling, +1). Going from $359K to $496K in the months between launch and YC reads roughly mid-teens MoM, which is the **Decagon**-zone for vertical B2B but not the **Harvey** "$3B → $5B → $8B in months" curve where I pay up. Customer-reported 8%+ efficiency gains and the company-cited 11% revenue lift / 25% admin time reduction are credible operational metrics, but at three logos this is concentration risk masquerading as product-market fit -- one churn event resets the curve.

**Decagon** (2023) is the single-anchor parallel for Dayjob's index position: workflow infrastructure on a recently-catalyzed category, overlaying incumbents (Zendesk/Intercom) rather than replacing them. The difference is that customer support is itself a workflow every B2B SaaS company must touch -- it's an index on the entire enterprise-software universe. Dayjob's waste/skip-hire scheduler is not an index on industrial logistics until Dayjob proves it -- and every cited customer, metric, and case study in the dossier is waste/skip-hire. The "AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" framing on the YC page is positioning aspiration, not shipped surface area.

My 36-month forecast: Dayjob gets acquired by AMCS or Routeware for $40-90M as an "AI agent" bolt-on to their existing platforms, with the acquisition closing once Dayjob crosses $3-5M ARR and before they prove non-waste verticals. That's not a failure outcome -- it's a clean Postlethwaite/Fooks personal win and a credible YC mark -- but it's not the durability profile of "one of that dozen or two that are going to be really important 10 years from now," and at consensus YC pre-seed entry the math doesn't pencil for me.

The bull case worth taking seriously: this is the **Brex** (2017) / **Gusto** archetype -- operationally-disciplined CEO, deep prior-vertical exposure (three years of Gaea depot ride-alongs is the real moat, not the ERP integrations), and a category that looks small from the outside but expands as the workflow layer becomes the system of record. Postlethwaite at Deliveroo launched the UK grocery business -- that's the same shape of "build a vertical inside a horizontal" muscle that Henrique Dubugras showed at Pagar.me before Brex. If Dayjob ships into two non-waste industrial-logistics verticals within twelve months -- construction aggregates, port drayage, anything that isn't skip hire -- the index-position score rerates from 7 to 12 and the whole evaluation flips a tier. That is my single verifiable flip condition: two non-waste named customers by Q2 2027, disclosed publicly. Until then, Dayjob is a Pass-leaning Neutral I'd happily revisit at Series A if the curve confirms.
