# Modern Industrials -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Ventura, a YC W26 company, is building the identical product for the identical customer — AI-automated quoting for building materials distributors — with a CEO who already exited a wholesale distribution SaaS company. Modern Industrials has three talented engineers from xAI, Google, and McKinsey pursuing the same thesis at the same time with zero documented experience in lumber or distribution. This is the competitive landscape slide writing itself. When two accelerator-backed startups launch simultaneously into the same vertical with the same value proposition, the question is not which team is stronger. The question is whether either company possesses a secret. A secret, by definition, is something your competitor does not know. If Ventura is processing over one million quotes annually for a live customer while Modern Industrials announces it has "gone live with national players" without naming a single one, the secret — if one existed — has already been commoditized by the market's own recognition of the opportunity.

The canonical question yields nothing here. "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Modern Industrials' implicit answer is that LBM distribution is under-digitized and AI can automate the quoting workflow. Vatsal Bhargava's LinkedIn post calls it "one of the largest and most under-digitized industries in North America" — a description that functions as its own refutation. If everyone agrees the industry is under-digitized, the observation is not a secret; it is a market report. Flitch raised $3.16 million to build a broader operating system for lumberyards. DMSi, ECI, and Epicor have served this vertical for decades and will inevitably bolt AI capabilities onto their existing installed bases. The dossier itself identifies that "the core technical challenge — parsing unstructured quote requests and matching to product catalogs — is addressable by multiple well-funded teams." When your own research memo articulates why your moat does not exist, the analysis is already complete.

The founding team is the single genuinely compelling element. Austin Mao, Vatsal Bhargava, and Ankit Bhargava — two brothers and their childhood best friend of over twenty years — represent precisely the deep shared prehistory I consider structurally necessary. Ankit's tenure at xAI building agentic systems and Vatsal's work on Google Ads ML bidding at scale are credentials that demonstrate technical execution capacity. Vatsal built a side project to $10K MRR; Ankit generated $200K in algorithmic trading profits. These are not idle people. But my framework does not permit talented founders to override the absence of monopoly structure. A brilliant team competing against another brilliant team for the same customers in the same vertical is the definition of value destruction through mimetic rivalry. Girard would recognize this immediately — two startups, mirror images of each other, each believing they are differentiated while converging on identical positioning. The founders' capability makes this a better-executed commodity, not a monopoly.

The power-law calculation eliminates Modern Industrials from serious consideration. Vertical SaaS for building materials distribution is structurally fragmented — distributors are regional, relationships are local, and the market resists winner-take-all consolidation. Even the optimistic scenario where Modern Industrials defeats Ventura, outmaneuvers Flitch, and prevents ERP incumbents from adding native AI quoting produces a mid-scale B2B software company, not a fund-returner. The company's own $450B+ TAM claim is the red flag I warn against: leading with market size statistics rather than a specific insight about why one company will capture a disproportionate share of that market. Ramp reached $16 billion in valuation not because corporate spend management was a large market — everyone knew that — but because Ramp possessed a specific contrarian insight about prioritizing savings over rewards that created organic viral distribution through CFO networks. Modern Industrials has articulated no analogous insight about why their approach to LBM distribution creates compounding structural advantages that competitors cannot replicate.

The strongest bull case requires believing that Modern Industrials' AI engineering depth — particularly Ankit's xAI agentic systems experience — will produce a technically superior product that compounds data advantages faster than Ventura or any competitor. If their "national players" deployments generate proprietary pricing and catalog data that improves accuracy on every subsequent deployment, a data flywheel could emerge. The ERP vendors' business model conflict — AI quoting cannibalizes their professional services revenue from customization and training — creates a window where incumbents are structurally incentivized to move slowly. And the co-founder bond, forged over two decades rather than in an accelerator cohort, provides alignment that survives the grinding enterprise sales cycles this industry demands. For this to be a monopoly, Modern Industrials would need to demonstrate that cross-distributor learning creates accuracy and pricing intelligence that no single-customer deployment can match — a network effect in data rather than in users. Nothing in the dossier suggests this thesis has been articulated, let alone validated.

Modern Industrials is a pure software layer with no physical-world technology barrier, no regulatory moat, and no government customer pathway. The product sits atop existing ERP systems whose vendors control API access and could restrict it at will. This is the architecture of dependency, not dominance. My portfolio increasingly concentrates in companies that manipulate atoms — General Matter enriching uranium, Hadrian manufacturing precision defense components, Substrate reinventing chip fabrication — because physical-world barriers create replication timelines measured in years, not engineering sprints. A competing team could rebuild Modern Industrials' document-parsing and catalog-matching capabilities in months using the same foundation models available to everyone.

The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Return Magnitude. The simultaneous existence of Ventura pursuing the identical thesis with stronger domain credentials is dispositive evidence that no secret exists — an 8/30. The vertical SaaS ceiling in a fragmented, regional industry caps the realistic outcome well below fund-returner territory — an 8/20. The twenty-year co-founder bond prevents this from falling further, but founder quality without monopoly structure is a tragedy, not an investment.
