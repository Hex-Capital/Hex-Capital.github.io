# One Robot -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is genuine, and it follows a pattern I've seen pay off before. VLA models crossed a capability threshold in 2024-2025 -- Physical Intelligence's Pi 0.5, NVIDIA's Gr00t, Google's RT-2 -- and suddenly the bottleneck shifted from "can we build these models?" to "can we evaluate and train them fast enough?" The founders experienced this firsthand at Industrial Next: "evaluating a new policy started taking longer than training it." That shift from architecture-limited to data-limited is exactly the kind of inflection point I track. When I invested in Harvey, it was because I could see the GPT scaling curve bending toward legal-grade fidelity. Here, the capability curve for VLA models is bending toward industrial manipulation, and the evaluation infrastructure doesn't exist yet. One Robot is building picks and shovels for a gold rush that's just starting.

But the critical difference between this and the Harvey pattern is market depth. Legal AI serves a $900 billion industry with hundreds of thousands of practitioners who immediately benefit from automation. One Robot serves teams actively building VLA-based manipulation systems -- a customer base that numbers in the dozens today, maybe low hundreds. The bet isn't just that the product works; it's that VLA adoption in manufacturing and logistics follows a steep adoption curve over the next 3-5 years. That's a bet on a bet. The broader robotics simulation market projections ($35B growing to $275B by 2035) are real, but One Robot's specific segment -- learned world models for VLA training -- is a narrow wedge within that. If VLAs become the dominant paradigm for industrial manipulation, every robotics company becomes a potential customer. If VLA adoption stalls or fragments across approaches, this remains a niche tool for a niche audience. I don't have the same confidence in the VLA adoption curve that I had in the LLM scaling laws when evaluating Harvey.

The founder-market fit is the strongest signal in this dossier. Hemanth and Elton worked together at Industrial Next building the exact systems they're now building simulation infrastructure for -- they've been training robots together since 2020. Hemanth bootstrapped a prior company (Crescer AI) to profitability, has published robotics research, holds a NASA Early Career Achievement Medal, and brings ML depth from Georgia Tech and JPL. Elton spent five years at Tesla during the Model 3 ramp and on the Dojo team, then led software at Industrial Next. This isn't a team that pivoted into robotics because it's hot -- they've lived inside the problem for years. The co-founder relationship has been tested under production pressure at Industrial Next. Already running pilots with multiple robotics companies at pre-seed is good execution velocity for a two-person team.

What concerns me most is the competitive landscape. NVIDIA's Cosmos already has 3M+ downloads and is open-source. World Labs just raised $1.23B. Saturn is building in the same "world model as simulator" category. One Robot's differentiation is task-specificity -- learned world models trained on real robot data for contact-rich manipulation tasks like textiles and precision assembly, where general-purpose physics engines fail. That's a real technical wedge. NVIDIA's incentive is to sell GPUs through a horizontal platform, not to build bespoke simulation environments for textile folding. But the question is whether task-specific learned world models remain a specialized capability or become a feature that general-purpose platforms absorb as the technology matures. If world model architectures standardize (and open-source momentum suggests they will), differentiation shifts to task-specific data and integration depth -- which favors the company with the deepest customer relationships. One Robot could build that data flywheel, but a two-person team outrunning NVIDIA and a $1.23B competitor requires exceptional execution and a meaningful head start in customer trust.

The bull case I take seriously: if VLA adoption compresses to an LLM-like adoption timeline -- plausible because the compute and ML infrastructure already exists -- the number of teams needing this tool goes from dozens to thousands in three years. In that scenario, One Robot's task-specific expertise, early customer relationships, and accumulated world-model training data create a compounding advantage that horizontal platforms can't easily replicate. The "boring infrastructure for builders" pattern applies here -- this is tooling that every VLA team needs but nobody else will build at the required depth. Gusto looked like "just payroll" and PagerDuty looked like "just alerting" before the adoption curves revealed hidden scale. If robotics manipulation follows a similar trajectory, One Robot is positioned at the right layer. The founders have the exact background to execute on this, and the product-to-platform path exists: evaluation environments → synthetic training data → manipulation scenario marketplace → the default simulation layer for industrial VLAs.

I land on this as a genuine neutral -- not a dismissal but not a conviction bet. The structural timing is right, the founders are credible, and the market archetype ("too niche" today, potentially huge tomorrow) is one I've profited from before. But the single-miracle test isn't clean: they need their world models to achieve reliable sim-to-real transfer AND the VLA market to grow substantially AND to stay ahead of NVIDIA and World Labs. That's closer to three miracles than one. I'd want to see validation data on sim-to-real transfer quality and clearer evidence that the VLA adoption curve is steepening before writing a check.
