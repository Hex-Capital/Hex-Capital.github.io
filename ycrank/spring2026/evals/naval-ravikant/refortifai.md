# RefortifAI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the structural paradox at the center of this company. Software-only DRM has been defeated in every domain it has ever been deployed — music, film, games, enterprise software. The founders know this, which is why they frame it as "anti-cheat for AI models" rather than "DRM for AI models." But that framing tells you everything: game anti-cheat is a treadmill, not a flywheel. Easy Anti-Cheat and BattlEye employ hundreds of engineers in a perpetual arms race against modders and cheaters. Every update degrades. Every shipped binary gets reverse-engineered. This is labor leverage wearing a code-leverage costume. The product doesn't work while you sleep — it breaks while you sleep.

The specific knowledge question is where I'm supposed to start, and here the answer is: relevant composite, not native depth. Atman Kar's RTL design work at TI and Tenstorrent gives him hardware-level intuition about GPU memory architectures. Rithik Jain studied under Somesh Jha — a serious name in program analysis and adversarial ML — working on attack vectors and ROP gadgets. Sayan Mitra ran AI workloads at KLA's semiconductor inspection group. Combine them and you get a team that understands hardware, security, and ML inference. But none of them spent years inside a model provider agonizing over weight extraction. Nobody here built inference engines for a living. Nobody woke up at 3am because they discovered their company's $100M training run was being redistributed on a torrent. The knowledge was assembled for the opportunity, not the other way around. If you told me Rithik Jain had spent three years at Meta trying to secure Llama distribution and kept hitting the same wall, I'd score this differently. What I see instead is three early-career engineers who correctly identified that AI model IP protection is underserved — and that's market analysis, not specific knowledge.

The leverage audit concerns me most. My framework asks: does this scale with zero marginal cost? A runtime that obfuscates weights is code, yes. But the adversarial dynamic means the code's value decays continuously. Every new GPU architecture, every new inference optimization in upstream vLLM, every academic paper on model extraction creates work. Maintaining drop-in compatibility with vLLM and SGLang across NVIDIA and AMD while hardening the runtime against memory inspection attacks — for a 3-person team — is not leverage, it's a sprint on a treadmill that speeds up. Compare this to Notion, where every template a user creates makes the product more valuable without any engineering cost. Or Perplexity, where every query improves the answer quality. RefortifAI has no compounding mechanism. The moat must be actively rebuilt every quarter.

Now, the bull case — because I owe it honest consideration. The strongest version goes like this: training frontier models now costs hundreds of millions of dollars, and the number of companies that want to license and distribute proprietary weights for on-premise or edge inference is growing rapidly as regulation and latency requirements push computation away from the cloud. If RefortifAI can become the standard protection layer that sits between model provider and deployment environment, they're a toll booth on the most valuable digital assets ever created. The Protect AI acquisition at $500M+ and CalypsoAI's acquisition by F5 validate that enterprises will pay serious money for AI security infrastructure. The drop-in vLLM/SGLang replacement is a genuinely clever distribution wedge — low friction adoption for teams already using those engines. And the team's composite of hardware, security, and ML is rare enough that it would take a competitor meaningful time to assemble. If the on-prem AI deployment market explodes, this could be infrastructure.

But I keep returning to two structural problems that my framework cannot forgive. First, the compound interest test fails. DRM systems don't get stronger with usage — they get targeted. The more customers adopt the runtime, the more valuable it becomes to crack, and the more resources adversaries will dedicate. This is negative compounding. Second, my portfolio pattern systematically favors companies that expand access. AngelList gave founders access to capital. CoinList gave retail investors access to token launches. Polymarket gave everyone access to information markets. RefortifAI does the opposite — it restricts access to model weights. You could argue it *enables* model providers to distribute weights they otherwise wouldn't, expanding the set of deployable models. That's a real but secondary effect, and it's not the kind of structural access expansion that generates the power-law outcomes in my portfolio.

Three early-career founders with relevant-but-assembled knowledge, building a product whose core dynamic is an arms race rather than a flywheel, in a category that inverts my strongest unstated pattern. The market is real. The problem is real. The team is competent. But competent teams in real markets building arms-race products is not how I generate outlier returns. I pass.
