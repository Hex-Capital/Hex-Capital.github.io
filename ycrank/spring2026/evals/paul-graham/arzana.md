# Arzana -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Manufacturing front-office automation is a problem that's been hiding in plain sight for decades. Every manufacturer in America has a back office where someone is manually re-keying purchase orders from emails into Epicor, chasing down quotes in spreadsheets, and calling suppliers for updates. This is exactly the kind of ugly, multi-system mess that causes schlep blindness. Thousands of smart engineers at YC have pitched me AI products for developers, designers, and knowledge workers. Almost none of them want to build integrations across ten legacy ERP systems so that a distribution company in Wisconsin can stop manually entering orders. The schlep here is enormous — not one integration but ten, not one document type but dozens, not Silicon Valley buyers but Midwestern manufacturers who need to trust you before they'll let you touch their systems. That structural deterrent is real, and it's the most interesting thing about Arzana.

The organic discovery question is where I get both encouraged and uncertain. Alexander grew up in Okoboji, Iowa. Kools grew up in Neenah, Wisconsin — which anyone who knows manufacturing knows is a serious industrial corridor. Alexander describes himself as a "manufacturing nerd from Iowa." They maintain physical offices in Iowa and Wisconsin, and their founding field engineer is stationed in Okoboji, not San Francisco. This has the texture of founders who grew up watching this problem firsthand. But texture is not proof. Neither founder has actually worked in a manufacturing office. They're 21 and 24, straight from Stanford. Growing up near factories is different from sitting in the quoting room at 7am watching someone toggle between Epicor and Outlook for four hours. The geographic connection is suggestive — more suggestive than if two Stanford CS majors from Palo Alto had pitched me this — but I can't confirm the idea grew from lived experience versus smart market analysis by kids who happened to be from the right zip codes.

What concerns me most is that neither founder can build the product. Alexander studied Economics and CS at Stanford, but there are no public repos, no prior technical roles, nothing that suggests he's a hacker. Kools took "one coding class." The AI engine, the ERP integrations, the agent orchestration — all of it depends on Joe Politi, who is listed as "Lead Founding Engineer," not co-founder. I've seen this pattern before, and it worries me. When your company is fundamentally a technical product and nobody on the founding team built it, you've got a structural fragility. If Politi decides to leave, or if a hard technical decision needs to be made at 2am about how the quoting agent handles edge cases in SAP, the co-founders can't step in. Drew Houston was a solo founder, but he'd built a working Dropbox prototype himself — the product existed in his hands before I ever met him. Here, the product exists in someone else's hands.

The bull case is the customer list. Nine named logos including Schneider National and Zeiss — at pre-seed, with four people, that's striking if real. The Tier One Technologies testimonial is specific: their VP of Sales says quote turnaround went from five days to one and win rates jumped. That's not vague praise; that's a named person at a named company describing a measurable operational improvement. If Arzana genuinely has Schneider National and Zeiss as paying customers at $2,500-$7,000 per month, this is a company where real economic value is being created and someone should invest aggressively. But the dossier notes no independent verification of these relationships — no case studies, no press mentions from the customers, no third-party validation. At pre-seed, large enterprise logos can mean anything from a signed annual contract to a free pilot that one mid-level manager approved. The distance between those two realities is the distance between a strong invest and a pass.

There are background signals of the founders being tough and resourceful — Alexander self-taught discus to an Iowa state ranking from YouTube videos, started a pizza business as a teenager, learned Mandarin on his own. Kools was a four-time letterwinner in Division 1 wrestling and paid himself about ten thousand dollars all of 2025. These are not people who quit when things get hard. "There is no Plan B, because that assumes you will fail" is the kind of statement I've heard from founders who end up being relentless. But relentless alone isn't the test — it's relentless and resourceful together, and I haven't yet seen the moment where their plan failed and they invented a creative alternative. The determination signals are there; the resourcefulness under startup-specific pressure is still unproven.

I keep coming back to the schlep. The reason Conexiom raised $170 million and still only does order-to-cash document automation — not quoting, not procurement, not AR/AP — is because each of those functions requires deep, ugly integration work that nobody wants to expand into. Arzana is attempting to bundle all of it. That's either the right strategy because the manufacturing office needs a unified system, or it's a fatal overextension for a four-person team. At this stage I genuinely don't know which. If I could verify the customer logos and see a technical co-founder join the team, my conviction would shift significantly. As it stands, this is a company attacking a real schlep-blindness problem with authentic geographic access to the customer base, but without the technical founding DNA I typically need to write a check.
