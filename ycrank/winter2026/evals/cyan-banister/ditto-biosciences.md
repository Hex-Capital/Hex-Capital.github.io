# Ditto Biosciences -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Turning tick spit and parasitic worm proteins into autoimmune drugs. That's the kind of sentence that makes most venture investors physically recoil -- and it's exactly the reaction that tells me to lean in. Parasites have spent millions of years evolving proteins that hijack our immune systems, and Ditto wants to reverse-engineer those molecules into precision therapeutics. The last company that tried anything adjacent to this -- Coronado Biosciences -- fed people live pig whipworms and failed spectacularly in Phase 2 trials. That failure didn't just kill one company, it salted the earth. Which means Ditto is operating in a space that's not just unpopular -- it's actively discredited. When I first backed Contraline at Founders Fund, male contraception was similarly dismissed, partly because prior approaches had failed and partly because the category itself made people uncomfortable. Ditto has that same energy. The insight that you should isolate and engineer the specific immunomodulatory proteins rather than dump live organisms into patients is the kind of reframing that separates a dead category from a massive opportunity. And the claim that more than 98% of viral and parasite proteins remain uncharacterized means they're mining a chemical space that's essentially unexplored.

The scientific team here is formidable in a way I rarely see at pre-seed. Three PhDs spanning parasitology, evolutionary biology, computational genomics, and protein engineering -- the exact interdisciplinary convergence this approach requires. Borges's UCSF PhD in phage-CRISPR interactions, her Miller Fellowship studying alternative genetic codes, Sun's computational genomics training at Berkeley, Weiss's high-throughput capabilities from Illumina and DuPont. These aren't generalist founders who read a paper and got excited -- they've been studying the organisms and computational methods at the heart of this approach for years at Arcadia Science. But here's where I need to be honest about my own framework: I don't have the biographical story. When I ask "tell me how you got here -- not the business," I'm listening for the hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this specific problem. What I see in the dossier is intellectual evolution, not personal survival. These are credentialed scientists from elite institutions who identified an elegant scientific opportunity. That's meaningful, but it's not the same signal as a founder who experienced autoimmune disease firsthand, or who watched a family member suffer through immunosuppressive side effects, or who was told no fifty times and kept building. I can't score what I can't see.

What I can see clearly is the co-founder alignment, and after HQ Trivia burned me, this is the dimension I never skip. Three co-founders who spent three years together at Arcadia Science, co-authoring research publications, building shared scientific intuitions, and then choosing to leave together to start Ditto -- that's exactly the kind of pre-existing stress test I want. It's not a 50/50 split between two people who met at a conference. It's three scientists with complementary expertise who know how they work under pressure. The Fondo blog confirms they "collaborated for three years as early scientists at another startup." That history gives me real confidence that when the inevitable crises hit -- a lead candidate fails, funding gets tight, an immunogenicity problem surfaces -- they won't fracture.

I want to name the strongest bull case, because this company activates a pattern I've noticed in my own portfolio but rarely talk about: making invisible systems visible. Carta made cap tables transparent. Affirm made credit costs transparent. Flock Safety made neighborhood safety data transparent. Ditto is making visible a biological library that evolution assembled over hundreds of millions of years but that science has barely catalogued. If they're right that this protein space contains thousands of immunomodulatory molecules with nanomolar binding affinities, they're building a proprietary atlas of nature's immune-hacking toolkit -- and that atlas compounds in value with every protein they characterize. The 1 million proteins already analyzed, the tissue biobank mapping immune memory, the composition-of-matter patents they could file on novel therapeutic proteins -- these are the building blocks of a genuine moat. In a world where every other AI drug discovery company is competing over the same target space with similar computational methods, Ditto's differentiation isn't their algorithms, it's their source material. Nobody else is systematically screening parasite genomes because the category was poisoned by Coronado's failure and because, frankly, it sounds weird. That's a competitive moat built on stigma, and stigma moats are some of the most durable I've seen.

The hard truth is that drug development requires hundreds of millions of dollars and a decade of patience, and I'm looking at a three-person team with YC-level funding. The transition from "thousands of candidates" to a focused pipeline with a lead indication is where many computational discovery platforms stall. Immunogenicity -- the risk that the body attacks these foreign proteins before they can work -- is acknowledged but unsolved. These are real barriers. But I've made exceptions for capital-intensive bets before when the founding insight was extraordinary enough. SpaceX was my first angel check and it required billions. The question isn't whether this is expensive -- it's whether the founding team has the conviction and the scientific depth to attract the capital they'll need. On that front, the credentials are there, even if the personal story hasn't yet been told to me in the way I need to hear it.

I'd want a meeting. I want to sit across from Adair Borges and Dennis Sun and Emily Weiss and ask them to tell me their stories -- not the science, not the proteins, not the binding affinities. I want to know what brought each of them to this table, what they gave up to start this, and how they argue when they disagree about which indication to pursue first. The weirdness is real, the science is deep, and the co-founder dynamics look strong from the outside. But my primary signal -- the biographical thread that tells me this founder will persist through ten years of drug development hell -- I haven't felt it yet. This is a company I'd track closely and potentially invest in after that conversation.
