# noetic -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that YC funded Saphira AI in Summer 2024 to build "Vanta for Hardware" -- automated safety reports and traceability for TÜV, UL, and CE certification -- and Saphira already has $440K in revenue. Now, two batches later, YC has funded Noetic to build essentially the same thing with a slightly wider aperture. Two companies from the same accelerator, targeting the same customers, using the same underlying technology (LLMs parsing regulatory documents), competing for referrals from the same YC network. This is mimetic rivalry in its most literal form -- not even the creative kind that produces genuine innovation, but the kind where two companies stare at each other across a seminar table and converge toward identical products. When I see this pattern, I stop evaluating the market and start asking why no one involved seems troubled by it.

What is the secret here? The thesis appears to be: "AI can automate hardware compliance documentation faster and cheaper than human consultants." This is not a secret. It is the most obvious application of large language models to any document-heavy professional services workflow. Every compliance consultant alive can see this coming. Saphira saw it. Kite Compliance saw it. The only question is execution speed, and execution speed without structural defensibility is a treadmill, not a moat. The regulatory standards themselves -- ISO, UL, IEC, ANSI documents -- are copyrighted but commercially purchasable. There is no proprietary data advantage. There are no network effects in generating compliance documentation; one customer's risk assessment does not make the next customer's assessment meaningfully better. The lab-matching marketplace angle could theoretically create two-sided dynamics, but there is no evidence of lab partnerships, and testing laboratories already have established relationships with every compliance consultant in the industry.

The technology is an application layer on foundation models. Any capable engineering team with access to Claude or GPT-4 can build a regulatory document parser and gap analysis tool. The claimed 10x improvement -- weeks instead of months -- is a speed improvement enabled by generally available AI infrastructure, not by proprietary technology that compounds over time. SpaceX's reusable rockets created a cost advantage that widened with every launch because the learning was embedded in physical engineering that took years to replicate. Noetic's advantage, such as it is, can be replicated by a competent team in months. The underlying technology is not discontinuous; it is the predictable application of 2024-era language models to a domain that happens to involve dense text.

The strongest bull case runs through the UL 3300 timing. The standard was added to OSHA's NRTL Program list on December 31, 2025, creating an immediate compliance wave for every company deploying robots near humans. If Noetic could own UL 3300 compliance the way PayPal owned eBay power-seller payments -- total dominance of a specific, urgent, narrow wedge -- and then expand concentrically into adjacent standards, the monopoly playbook would apply. The structural business model conflict at TIC incumbents is real: UL Solutions generates $3 billion from testing fees and billable hours, and building software that cannibalizes their advisory revenue requires a self-disruption that large organizations almost never execute. This creates a genuine window. If Noetic had demonstrated obsessive focus on UL 3300 as their beachhead, with signed customers from the robotics deployment wave, I would find this more compelling. But the website lists UL 3300 alongside a dozen other standards -- FCC, CE, FDA 510(k), ISO 9001, ISO 26262 -- suggesting a broad, unfocused approach rather than the deliberate narrowness that creates small-market monopoly. Going after every standard on day one is the compliance equivalent of targeting a $263 billion TAM: it signals competitive thinking, not monopoly thinking.

The founders are Yale undergraduates -- Tony Gao and Jack Yin from the APOLLO robotics lab, Henry Zheng from AWS. I have backed teenage founders before, and age is not the disqualifying factor. What distinguished Zuckerberg at 19 was not his youth but his refusal to deviate from a specific vision that most people thought was insane. I see no evidence of that kind of conviction here -- no articulated vision of how compliance fundamentally changes, no refusal of easier paths, no indication that these founders believe something about the world that experts actively reject. The robotics lab background gives them exposure to the pain point, which is valuable, but exposure to a problem is not the same as possessing a secret about its solution. The quant finance internships at D.E. Shaw, Citadel, and Five Rings suggest intellectual horsepower, which I respect, but intellectual horsepower applied to a consensus thesis in a competitive market produces hard work, not monopoly.

This is a real problem with a real market, built by smart people who will work hard. It is also a company without a secret, without a monopoly mechanism, without proprietary technology, and with a funded competitor that has a year's head start and revenue. The compliance automation space for hardware is becoming what food delivery was five years ago -- multiple startups converging on the same insight, competing for the same customers, with no structural reason why any single player achieves dominance. I pass.
