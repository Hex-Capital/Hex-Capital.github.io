# Humwork -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Humwork is a serial-shipper-led MCP wedge into a marketplace that hasn't proven its demand curve -- Yash Goenka and Rohan Datta have both shipped production AI/voice systems before (Qualify.bot YC S25 and a >1M-minute voice platform respectively), yet the disclosed traction is 2,858 beta resolutions against 3,000+ experts, which is the inverse of the "just working" pattern. The signature pairing here is *founder shipping rhythm high, revenue velocity low* -- the kind of profile where I usually wait one cycle for the curve to either show up or not.

Perplexity (2022) is the load-bearing analogue for the founder-ships axis: Aravind Srinivas pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing we'd discussed built, and I funded the rhythm before the revenue. Yash Goenka has a comparable artifact trail -- Phonecall.bot, Instawrite, Qualify.bot (YC S25), a graphene-supercapacitor patent, first LLM startup in 2021 -- and Rohan Datta independently shipped an AI voice-calling system that did over a million call minutes. Humwork diverges from the Perplexity template in one specific way that matters: Yash is publicly listed as Co-founder & CEO of *both* Qualify.bot (YC S25) and Humwork (YC S26), and the dossier explicitly flags that "status of Qualify.bot relative to Humwork is not publicly clarified." Aravind shipped one company end-to-end; Humwork is asking me to fund a CEO who is two-timing across batches, which discounts the rung even though the shipping artifacts are real.

"If you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" -- I said it on Tim Ferriss #863 and the Humwork dossier is a near-clean test of the claim. 2,858 beta resolutions cumulatively divided across 3,000+ experts is roughly 0.95 questions per expert *for the entire pre-launch period*, which is the opposite of marketplace liquidity. Humwork cites 130% NRR for the Software Engineering domain, but on what revenue base? -- the dossier does not disclose a single MRR or ARR figure, only credit-package mechanics. Compare to Harvey (2023), where I sized up at $3B → $5B → $8B in months because the dollars were the thesis; Humwork is in the inverse posture, leading with supply count rather than dollars, and in this cycle silence on growth is itself the signal.

The MCP catalyst is real but it compounds asymmetrically over the next 18 months. Humwork's wedge -- a single MCP server routing across Claude Code, Cursor, Lovable, Replit, Cline, and OpenClaw -- works precisely because MCP standardized in the last 6-12 months, which is a genuine 24-month catalyst. The mechanism, though, runs both directions: if Anthropic or Cursor ships a first-party "ask a human" provider (and Anthropic has every incentive to, given the trust-and-safety surface area), the same MCP standardization that opened Humwork's door becomes the rail their replacement ships on. The catalyst is real; Humwork's *exclusive* claim on the catalyst is not.

Stripe (2010) was an index on e-commerce because every entrant in the category had to touch the payments rail, and there was no incentive for Shopify or Amazon to ship a Stripe-clone for everyone else. Humwork's equivalent claim is that every agent that hits the 80% wall has to touch a human-expert layer, and Humwork's MCP integration sits in that workflow path. The structural difference: Stripe's counterparty (merchants) had no leverage to build payments themselves; Humwork's counterparties (Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor) are exactly the firms with both the incentive and the capability to internalize the human-handoff layer if it becomes load-bearing. Humwork has a workflow-position claim but not an index claim in the Stripe sense.

Single-miracle math, counterfactual phrasing: Humwork would flip from a 5/12 to a 9/12 on this axis if the only required miracle were "agents need humans for the last 20%" and the rest were GTM. Humwork actually needs (a) sustained MCP openness from hyperscalers, (b) two-sided marketplace liquidity from a current 0.95-questions-per-expert ratio, (c) a credit-pricing model that clears against expert opportunity cost, and (d) sub-2-minute SLA holding under load across legal/marketing/strategy. That is at minimum three independent miracles compounding, which is the architecture I pass on by default per the NFX framework.

36-month forecast: Humwork pivots from open A2P marketplace to a vertical expert pool inside software engineering (the only domain where the 130% NRR signal is cited), lands in the $2-6M ARR range, and either gets acqui-hired by HumanLayer or one of the agent platforms (Anthropic, Cursor) for the integration footprint and Yash's shipping muscle, or limps as a single-vertical consultancy-marketplace hybrid. The mechanism is that the MCP catalyst commoditizes the integration layer before Humwork can convert the 3,000-expert supply pool into a defensible demand-side curve.

The strongest bull case against this forecast: Humwork is the **Decagon** (2023) of the agent-failure-mode market -- workflow infrastructure in a wide-open AI category I named in *AI Market Clarity*, first-mover on A2P framing, with two founders who have both shipped production voice/agent systems and a YC launch that already drew TestingCatalog, Analytics Drift, and an @ycombinator tweet quoting @theyashgoenka. If agent volume 10xs by mid-2027, "ask a human" becomes a default tool call, and the 3,000-expert head start compounds into the matching-data moat the dossier inferences. That case stings -- it's the exact "workflow infrastructure on top of a hard thing" pattern that worked for Color Genomics and Anduril. What dismantles it for Humwork specifically is the cold-start arithmetic (sub-1 question per expert pre-launch is not a marketplace, it's a directory) combined with Yash's split CEO status across Qualify.bot and Humwork -- I have not seen the Decagon-template work when the founder is running two companies simultaneously. The condition that flips my verdict: Humwork discloses $250K+ MRR with >20% MoM growth and Yash publicly clarifies that Qualify.bot is either wound down or has a separate operating CEO. Until then, pass and watch.
