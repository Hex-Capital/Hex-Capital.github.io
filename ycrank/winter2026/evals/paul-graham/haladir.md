# Haladir -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Haladir is that it used to be something else. The LinkedIn page described "an AI-powered mainframe modernization suite" for COBOL migration. Then the funding post emphasized "AI-led software development correctness." Now the website says "operational superintelligence" and RL for operations research. This kind of domain-pivoting at pre-seed isn't inherently disqualifying -- Reddit started as a mobile food ordering app -- but it tells you something important about the origin of the idea. When founders pivot the *approach* while keeping the problem fixed, that usually means they care deeply about the problem and are searching for the right solution. When they pivot the *problem* while keeping their technical toolkit fixed, it means they have a hammer and they're looking for a nail. Haladir has formal verification and RL expertise, and they're shopping for the right domain to apply it to. That's the pattern I described in "Why Smart People Have Bad Ideas" -- brilliant people generating ideas that emerge from their technical capabilities rather than from genuine need.

The problem they've landed on is real. LLMs achieving only 65% constraint satisfaction and 30.5% joint feasibility-and-optimality on operations research tasks is a genuine gap, and the ConstraintBench paper demonstrates that the founders understand it technically. Operations research involves exactly the kind of tedious, domain-specific formalization work that triggers schlep blindness -- translating business constraints into solver-checkable specifications across logistics, supply chain, crew scheduling. The schlep is there. But I don't see evidence that these founders ever worked in logistics or supply chain or wrestled with these constraints in the wild. They found this gap from inside a research lab, not from inside a warehouse or a dispatch center. The difference matters, because the founders who stumble into a problem through lived experience have conviction that survives the years of painful execution ahead. Founders who identified a gap through academic analysis can pivot away just as easily as they pivoted in -- which, based on their track record, they might.

The strongest bull case is that these are genuinely talented young technical people who have identified a structural gap that nobody else is filling. Harmonic has $295M and is doing formal proofs for pure mathematics. Code Metal has $161.5M and is doing formal verification for code translation. Nobody is building the bridge between mathematical solvers and RL training for operations research. That gap is real, the interdisciplinary expertise required is rare, and if Haladir owns this intersection, they could become the infrastructure layer that every AI lab uses to train models on constrained optimization. Susa Ventures, SV Angel, and Joshua Browder investing is a credible signal. And four co-founders who co-authored a research paper together at least suggests intellectual alignment. If the problem sticks -- if they stop pivoting and go deep on operational optimization -- the technical moat from accumulated domain formalization could be substantial.

But I keep coming back to what's missing. There are zero users. Zero revenue signals. A GitHub repo with 2 stars. A Twitter account with 8 followers and no posts. The public output is entirely academic -- a paper and a benchmark. "Make something people want" requires evidence that someone wants it, and there's nothing here. Not a waitlist, not a design partner, not a single enterprise conversation mentioned anywhere. At pre-seed I don't expect much, but I expect *something*. Even a founder saying "we talked to 30 logistics companies and 12 said they'd pay for this" would change my assessment. The absence isn't just thin evidence -- it's consistent with the manufactured-idea pattern. When an idea grows organically from lived experience, the founders usually already know who the first users are because they're those users themselves.

The team is young -- all current or recent university students with no prior startup exits or industry experience. Joseph Tso's Princeton CS background with a Google Scholar profile and Hutchins's CMU ML/statistics training demonstrate technical competence. They can clearly write research-quality code and reason about formal methods. But the GitHub footprint is nearly invisible, and I'm not seeing the hacker intensity where someone has built things compulsively, shipping projects because they couldn't help themselves. The HackCMU project (phone-to-Wii controller) is a positive data point for Hutchins, but it's thin. These founders are researchers first, and the open question is whether they'll become builders who obsess over what users need rather than what's technically interesting.

I'd want to revisit Haladir in six months. If they stop pivoting, find a specific vertical where they can demonstrate that solver-verified RL training produces measurably better optimization models than anything else available, and get a few enterprise design partners who depend on the output -- then the story changes completely. Right now, this is a technically interesting research project searching for a problem to own, built by smart people who haven't yet shown that they're building something anyone is pulling toward them.
