# InLoop Robotics -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$2.26 billion flowed into robotics startups in Q1 2026 alone, with over 70% going to warehouse and industrial automation. Nimble sits at a $1B valuation with $221M raised. Dexterity is at $1.65B on ~$300M. Every sophisticated investor I know is tripping over themselves to get into warehouse robotics right now. InLoop Robotics is entering the single hottest category in venture capital with the same human-in-the-loop teleoperation architecture that Nimble already deploys at scale — and they have $500K. When I look at InLoop Robotics, my first question isn't "is warehouse automation real?" Obviously it's real. My question is: where's the mispricing? And I genuinely can't find one. The market has priced warehouse robotics as a massive opportunity, bid it up accordingly, and InLoop Robotics is asking me to bet that a 4-person team from TUM can outrun companies with 400-600x their capital using the same architectural playbook. That's not contrarian. That's just underfunded.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Covariant's acqui-hire by Amazon in August 2024 removed a major independent player, creating whitespace. Amazon Robotics and Symbotic are captive to their anchor customers — Amazon and Walmart respectively — creating channel conflict that prevents them from selling to competing retailers and 3PLs. InLoop Robotics could be the Solana to Nimble's Ethereum: everyone thinks the winner is already decided, and a lean team with a subtly better learning architecture could compound faster per dollar. The RaaS model with zero upfront cost could penetrate mid-market warehouses that Nimble and Dexterity haven't bothered with. I genuinely tried to get excited about this framing. But Solana worked because Raj Dittmar wasn't building "cheaper Ethereum" — he was building a fundamentally different consensus mechanism. A new primitive. InLoop Robotics' confidence-aware AI with teleoperation fallback is architecturally straightforward and, per the dossier, "is already used by Nimble Robotics." InLoop Robotics is building better Ethereum at a cheaper price point. That's an execution bet in an existing category, not a new primitive — and execution bets against 400x-capitalized competitors are not where I make money.

Zakariea Sharfeddine did ML at Bosch and BMW. Stepan Feduniak was doing robot learning research at 18. Pasha Rizali founded RoboTUM and the European Student Robotics Association. Martin Mohammed interned at IBM on reinforcement learning at 16 and co-founded Eggi (YC-backed, $250K pre-seed). These are clearly smart, technically competent people — but I have zero multi-cycle observation of any of them. Zakariea got his BS from KIT in 2024. Pasha's LinkedIn headline reads "Aspiring Robotics Engineer." I backed Raj on Solana because I'd watched him through three companies over years and knew exactly how he operates under pressure. InLoop Robotics' founders are early-career engineers from TUM's robotics community — smart, probably sane, but I have no basis to assess the sanity-insanity ratio because the idea itself isn't insane. "Deploy warehouse robots that ask for help when they're confused" is a perfectly sensible, well-validated approach. Nobody hears that pitch and thinks "what you're doing is fucking crazy." They think "oh, like Nimble but smaller."

InLoop Robotics does have the physical-world element I usually love. The Metropolis parallel tempts me — AI-powered operations in physical space, seeded early, scaling over time. But Metropolis owns and operates parking infrastructure. The parking garages ARE the moat. InLoop Robotics deploys robots into someone else's warehouse. The physical asset belongs to the customer, not to InLoop Robotics. The claimed moat is the data flywheel — every teleoperator intervention generates labeled failure data that trains the next policy iteration. That's real in principle, but Nimble runs the same flywheel on $221M in capital with FedEx as a strategic investor generating vastly more deployment data. InLoop Robotics' structural durability depends on software differentiation in the AI layer — and I've said it a hundred times: "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI, you're like, this is perfect for Adobe." Replace Adobe with Amazon Robotics or Nimble. The physical robots save InLoop Robotics from the worst of the AI kill test, but the differentiation layer is exactly the kind of thing a well-funded incumbent replicates.

RaaS in warehouse robotics is the opposite of capital-light. InLoop Robotics is financing the hardware for their customers — zero upfront cost, flat monthly subscription — which means they're eating the capex and amortizing it over the subscription life. That requires significant working capital to scale. A 4-person team deploying bimanual robots at this model will need to raise continuously. No team member with visible hardware or mechanical engineering credentials was identified, which makes the manufacturing and field service challenge even steeper. InLoop Robotics will need to raise a Series A, then a B, then probably a C, all while competing against Nimble and Dexterity for the same warehouse customers with 1/500th the war chest. That's not building to survive — that's building to raise.

The single condition that would flip me to Invest: if InLoop Robotics showed me data proving their policy improvement rate per teleoperator intervention is 5-10x faster than Nimble's — that their architecture genuinely compounds faster despite less capital and fewer deployments. That would turn this from an execution bet into a genuine architectural advantage, the kind of thing where being small is actually better. But I see no evidence of this in the dossier, and the architecture is described as the same pattern Nimble already uses. Consensus Mispricing is the dimension that kills InLoop Robotics for me — this is a consensus-hot category where every other investor at Demo Day will be excited, which means the upside is already in the price. Primitive Creation is the second nail: InLoop Robotics is not creating a new behavior or category, it's competing in an existing one against massively better-capitalized players using the same approach. Pass.
