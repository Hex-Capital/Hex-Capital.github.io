# Kuli -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Michael Hodara managed $20M+ in annual marketing spend at Aircall and holds an EPFL MSc in Robotics. That intersection — buyer of influencer marketing services with ML engineering training — is real domain experience. But I need to ask the question I always ask: if Hodara disappeared tomorrow, would Kuli's core insight survive? The honest answer is yes. The insight that video content should be analyzed frame-by-frame rather than filtered by metadata is a good product observation, not a proprietary knowledge stack. Any competent ML engineer who has managed a brand's creator budget would arrive at the same conclusion. Compare to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, who spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact retrieval systems that make AI search possible — that's specific knowledge so deep it functions as the company's engine. Hodara's experience is the origin story for a good idea, but the idea doesn't require his continued presence to execute. The EPFL robotics degree is general ML training, not years of building multimodal video understanding systems specifically.

Kuli's leverage architecture triggers the question I use to separate companies from features: is this owned leverage or rented leverage? Kuli processes video "frame by frame using multimodal AI" — but GPT-4V, Gemini, and Claude all offer multimodal video understanding via API. The structured content index Kuli builds ("the world's largest analysed social media content database without storing any video," per EPFL Investor Day 2024) is the moat claim, but it's an index of outputs from commodity models applied to publicly accessible content. CreatorIQ has $80.8M in funding, $70.7M in revenue, and 300 enterprise customers. GRIN has $145M in funding. Both could integrate the same multimodal APIs into their existing platforms in a product cycle. Kuli is betting that the incumbents' metadata-architecture creates an innovator's dilemma — a fair bet structurally, but one that depends on the incumbents being slow. Code leverage exists here, but the zero-marginal-cost layer sits underneath Kuli, owned by the model providers. When I backed Notion, the leverage was fully owned — the product itself was the technology. Kuli's core AI capability is a rental.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Disney, Supercell, and Havas are using Kuli at pre-seed with a 3-person team — that's extraordinary output per person. Campaigns launching "4x faster" and monitoring dropping from 30 hours to under 1 hour per month for 10 creators are real value propositions, not slide deck claims. Hodara experienced this pain firsthand as a marketing budget owner. The $50K/month agency replacement positioning targets genuine waste in the system. And the content index could compound — every video analyzed makes Kuli's brand safety and creator matching more precise, creating a data flywheel that new entrants would need to rebuild from scratch. If I apply the Wish exception test: does marketplace-level leverage override the specific knowledge gap? Here the leverage is stronger than Wish's because the content index compounds in ways that Wish's product catalog did not. The Microsoft GenAI Studio selection and VivaTech 2025 showcase on Microsoft's stand signal institutional validation in France, though that's distribution support, not technology moat.

I dismantle the bull case on defensibility. The content index compounds, but only if Kuli's analysis outputs are materially better than what a competitor could generate by running the same multimodal APIs against the same publicly available videos. Nothing in the dossier evidences proprietary model training, custom fine-tuning, or analysis capabilities beyond what off-the-shelf multimodal models provide. The compound interest test requires that advantages accumulate in a way competitors cannot replicate by starting later — Google couldn't replicate Perplexity's citation-trust network overnight. But CreatorIQ could replicate Kuli's content index by running Gemini against the same TikTok and Instagram content, layering it onto their existing 300-customer distribution. The enterprise logos are impressive for pre-seed, but with no public revenue figures and custom pricing disclosed only "upon request," the depth of these relationships is unknowable. Jonathan Hassan's 6 years at KPMG M&A is solid enterprise sales training but carries zero influencer marketing domain knowledge — he's a competent agent brought in to sell, not a principal with skin in the specific problem.

Kuli's team-to-value ratio is genuinely strong: 3 people serving Disney and Supercell is the kind of leverage density I look for. Zero open job postings suggests they're not planning to solve their problems by hiring — that's a good instinct. On democratization, Kuli replaces $50K/month agencies with AI, which expands access to content-level creator analysis for brands that couldn't afford manual review. But Kuli's custom enterprise pricing and named-client focus suggest they're selling to the same large brands that already pay for agencies — they're reducing cost for existing buyers, not unlocking access for a new population. AngelList gave every accredited investor access to deals previously restricted to Sand Hill Road insiders. Kuli gives Disney a cheaper way to do what Disney was already doing.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Kuli has built proprietary analysis capabilities — custom-trained models, fine-tuned embeddings, domain-specific architectures — that produce measurably better creator-brand matching than running off-the-shelf multimodal APIs. That would transform the content index from rented leverage into owned leverage, and the compound interest dynamics would fundamentally change. Without that, Kuli is a well-executed application layer on top of commodity AI, competing against incumbents with 100x the distribution. Innovation without a durable moat flows to the customer and eventually to the infrastructure provider.
