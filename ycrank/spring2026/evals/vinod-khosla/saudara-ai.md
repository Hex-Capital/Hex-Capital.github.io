# Saudara AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice here is the policy dependency. This company's entire value proposition rests on the tariff differential between China (54%) and Indonesia (32%). I've been here before. I lost hundreds of millions on KiOR and Range Fuels partly because those businesses needed favorable policy environments to reach economic viability. I swore off that bet structure. When the dossier itself identifies "Indonesia tariff escalation risk" as a key risk factor, and the "why now" section is entirely about tariffs, I'm looking at a company whose success case requires a specific policy regime to persist. Tariffs can change with a single executive order. A company whose competitive position evaporates based on political decisions in Washington is not a technology bet -- it's a policy bet. I don't make policy bets anymore.

Let me ask the question I always ask first: if Saudara AI fully succeeds, what happens to the world? American brands source Indonesian goods slightly more efficiently. Indonesian factories get marginally more export business. The $30B US-Indonesia trade flow gets intermediated through a better agent. That's fine. That's a perfectly respectable business outcome. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. This is redistribution of existing trade spend through a marginally better channel, not creation of new demand or transformation of a cost structure. Compare this to what gets me out of bed -- fusion energy that powers civilization, AI that replaces the entire cost of healthcare delivery, synthetic protein that feeds ten billion people. "Easier sourcing from Indonesia" does not belong in the same conversation.

The technology here is the part that concerns me most. The dossier describes "an AI agent that handles factory discovery, supplier vetting, sampling coordination, and delivery tracking." That's an LLM wrapper applied to procurement workflows. There is no novel science, no proprietary architecture, no step-function improvement in cost or capability. The dossier itself acknowledges it: "The AI-sourcing layer (supplier matching, communication automation) is replicable by well-funded horizontal platforms like SourceReady or Accio." When your own competitive analysis concedes replicability, you don't have a technology business. You have a services business wearing a technology costume. The real asset is Jennifer Prasetyo's family manufacturing network -- and relationships, while valuable, are not technology moats. They don't scale non-linearly. Accio already has two million users. SourceReady covers 1.2 million suppliers across 100+ countries. Adding Indonesia depth to a horizontal platform is a product sprint for these companies, not a strategic impossibility.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: the China-to-Southeast-Asia reorientation is a generational supply chain shift, Indonesia is the anchor economy, and the first company to build trusted infrastructure for US-Indonesia trade captures a network effect that compounds. If Saudara AI processes enough transactions, the data on supplier reliability and pricing becomes a proprietary advantage. The family network provides a cold-start advantage that pure-tech competitors cannot replicate. In this scenario, capturing even 3-5% of the $30B trade flow creates a billion-dollar revenue business. I take this seriously as a business case -- but even this best case produces a successful marketplace, not a company that changes the world. I want to either lose my money or make fifty times my money. This is designed to generate moderate returns, and moderate returns are a waste of my attention.

The founders are competent professionals -- Amazon product management, Microsoft Quantum, Meta Reality Labs, Duke graduate degree. But competent professionals building a procurement tool is the exact profile I pass on every Demo Day. I see no evidence of outsider irreverence, no evidence of rapid learning or pivoting, no evidence that these founders are reasoning from first principles about what the world should look like. They're applying their professional experience to an adjacent problem. That's the resume-driven approach, not the learning-machine approach. Where is the evidence that something surprised them in the past three months? Where is the insight that made them see Indonesian sourcing differently from everyone else? The family manufacturing connection is genuinely relevant domain expertise, but domain expertise is what I deprioritize -- I'd rather fund a naive outsider who has figured out how to collapse the entire concept of cross-border sourcing, not someone who knows the existing system well and wants to optimize it by forty percent.
