# Maquoketa Research -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Anduril has raised $6.9 billion and is building a $1 billion factory to produce tens of thousands of autonomous systems annually starting July 2026. AeroVironment holds a $990 million Army Switchblade contract and is scaling to 1,200 units per month. Shield AI has $3.1 billion in equity and projects $540 million in 2026 revenue. Helsing has a €268 million German government contract and thousands of units deployed in Ukraine. Maquoketa Research is a 5-person pre-seed team with approximately $500K in YC funding building the same category of product. When I invested $15K in Stripe for 2%, payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled other developers — the Collison brothers' competitive landscape was Authorize.net and Braintree, not a $30.5 billion juggernaut that had already solved the problem and was manufacturing at scale. Maquoketa Research isn't facing schlep blindness from competitors. They're facing fully-awake, heavily-capitalized adversaries who are actively sprinting in their exact lane.

Evan Yeager comes from SpiderRock and Stylis.AI; Alejandro Hernandez from Berkeley's Wireless Research Center specializing in phased array systems and RF design; Aidan Williams brings mechanical engineering; David Muchow handles operations. Maquoketa Research has assembled the right technical disciplines — RF, software, airframe, ops — which shows organizational competence. Hernandez's phased array background is genuinely specialized and directly relevant to contested electromagnetic environments where GPS denial matters. But I evaluate founders through their stories of resourcefulness under constraint, and the dossier gives me nothing. No prior exits, no shipped products, no tales of creative workarounds, no evidence of the "animal" quality I look for — that slight excess of intensity that tells you someone will find a way through any wall. The GitHub organization contains 9 repositories, all forks of existing open-source projects like PX4-Autopilot and HackRF, with no original repos showing significant traction. Forking PX4 is pragmatic engineering, not evidence of obsessive building. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes worth telling; the absence of such stories from Maquoketa Research's founding team is itself a mild negative signal.

Maquoketa Research's origin doesn't pass my organic discovery test. None of the founders appear to have military backgrounds. Yeager came from trading and AI; Hernandez from academic RF research. There's no "I was deployed and watched soldiers die because their drones lost GPS lock" moment — no personal frustration that became a company. The Ukraine conflict is on the front page of every newspaper; a $5.36 billion market growing at 19.9% CAGR is the kind of number that appears in trend reports and investor decks. When someone tells me they identified a massive market opportunity, what I hear is that they sat down and brainstormed. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. Maquoketa Research looks like technically capable people who read about Ukraine, saw the market size, and decided to build. That's market research, not living in the future and building what's missing.

The idea-that-repels test is where Maquoketa Research scores worst. Post-Ukraine, autonomous attack drones are the single most consensus "good idea" in defense technology. Nobody reacts to this pitch with "that will never work" — they react with "oh, like what Anduril is doing." When I backed Coinbase in YC S12, cryptocurrency was a category that serious investors dismissed entirely. When I backed Airbnb, every VC had rejected the idea of strangers sleeping in your house. Maquoketa Research operates in a space where billions of dollars of smart money have already validated the thesis. If a good idea were obviously good, someone else would already have done it. Someone else has already done it — several someones, with combined funding north of $11 billion. The competitive moat theory — that traditional primes like Lockheed and Raytheon face business model cannibalization at the sub-$10K price point — is correct but irrelevant, because Anduril, AeroVironment, Shield AI, and Helsing are the actual competitors, and none of them face that constraint.

The strongest bull case for Maquoketa Research goes like this: Hernandez's phased array and RF expertise from Berkeley Wireless Research Center could produce a genuinely superior communications and navigation stack for GPS-denied environments. Vertical integration and Elk Grove Village manufacturing could undercut competitors on unit cost. Defense procurement is expanding so rapidly — the Army's fiscal 2026 budget proposes equipping infantry brigades with loitering munitions at scale — that multiple vendors will win contracts simultaneously, and a nimble 5-person team iterating faster than Anduril's 3,000-person organization could capture a beachhead. The "built entirely in the USA" positioning addresses real ITAR and supply chain security concerns. And defense procurement's 18-36 month cycle, while painful, creates a barrier that protects those who get through it. This is a plausible story. But it requires Maquoketa Research to survive years of capital-intensive hardware development and procurement cycles on pre-seed funding, competing for the same contracts as companies with three to four orders of magnitude more capital, without any evidence that they've found a structural advantage their competitors cannot replicate by hiring. RF expertise is valuable but acquirable — Anduril can hire 50 RF engineers from Berkeley's lab if phased array becomes the differentiator.

Maquoketa Research earns modest marks on earnest builder identity — the full-stack approach across flight control, communications, and ground station software suggests genuine builders rather than pitch-deck founders, and the Elk Grove Village location signals manufacturing seriousness over San Francisco optics. Founder learning rate is unobservable from this dossier: no growth metrics, no iteration history, no product evolution documented. I default to neutral on both dimensions because the evidence is simply absent, not negative. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Maquoketa Research demonstrated a proprietary autonomy or RF capability that produced measurably superior performance in contested environments — validated by DoD testing or a signed OTA contract — that would be evidence of a technical moat the well-funded competitors haven't matched. Without that, this is a pass. The two dimensions that drive the verdict are Organic Problem Discovery (market-identified opportunity rather than lived experience, scoring below my default) and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (the most consensus space in defense tech, actively triggering my red flag for ideas everyone agrees are good).
