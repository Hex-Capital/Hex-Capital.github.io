# Markov -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Harish Ashok founded Boldmoon Cloud at 13 and Zenith at 17, and that fact alone changes how I read the rest of the Markov dossier -- a person who has shipped two companies before legal drinking age is leaving the kind of tracks animals leave. The signature of Markov is exactly this pairing: a high score on raw animal resourcefulness from Harish's serial-shipping pattern, dragged down by a co-founder configuration where the dossier reports "no public data on co-founder history" between Harish and Dev Mandal -- a teenage hacker bolted to an IIT Madras Sarvam alum with no evidence they have built anything together before YC S26.

Brian Armstrong's hand-built Android wallet prototype after his **Coinbase** (2012) rejection is the canonical animal-resourcefulness rung, and what makes the analogy load-bearing for Markov is that Harish has already done the equivalent twice before this company existed -- Boldmoon Cloud at 13 operated for two years, and Zenith was a voice-based hardware engineering assistant covered by Startuppedia. Where Markov diverges from the Armstrong rung is that the dossier names no specific *Markov-era* stunt -- no shipped dataset, no public benchmark, no cereal-box equivalent that financed the seed. The @DevvMandal claim of "a human fleet in India that can capture thousands of hours of data per day" is the closest thing to a pre-funding artifact, and if true it is exactly the unscalable manual operation I look for, but the dossier flags it as an X-snippet rather than a verified shipped capability. Animals leave tracks; Harish has left them elsewhere, but Markov's tracks are thin.

Two MBAs pitching an AI-wrapper with a TAM slide is my canonical pass pattern for co-founder configuration, and the failure mechanism is that founders who met for the startup don't survive the year-two argument about equity or direction. Markov doesn't match the MBA-deck pattern -- both founders are technical, both India-origin, both relocated to SF -- but it does have the structural tell I worry about: the dossier finds no co-working history, no shared dorm, no prior project. The **Airbnb** (2009) trio had lived together; the **Reddit** (2005) pair were dorm roommates; the Collisons at **Stripe** (2010) were brothers. Markov gives me Harish (Chennai, 17) and Dev (IIT Madras, '27) and no described connecting tissue. That isn't disqualifying -- the YC interview surfaces chemistry I can't see from a dossier -- but it caps the score.

The schlep dimension is where Markov compounds or fails over 18 months, and the mechanism is asymmetric. If Dev Mandal's India fleet claim is real, Markov is doing the operational grind -- recruiting, training, and managing human computer-use annotators in Chennai or Bangalore -- that Surge AI ($1.2B 2024 revenue) and Mercor ($10B valuation) won't personally touch at their scale. That is structurally the **Coinbase** (2012) compliance pattern: unpleasant labor as moat. The compounding problem is that the schlep is no longer empty -- Surge, Mercor, Scale (Meta 49% at $30B), Applied Compute, Micro1, Fleet, Habitat, and Matrices are all named in the dossier as overlapping competitors. "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) requires the schlep to be unaddressed; here it is addressed by every well-funded vendor in the category.

Counterfactually, the organic-idea score flips a tier only if Dev or Harish had personally been the customer of computer-use training data -- if Dev had been the Sarvam AI engineer who couldn't find usable RL environments and built Markov to scratch his own itch. The dossier gets close: Dev was at Sarvam, an Indian foundation-model lab, where data pipelines are the daily grind. But the narrative as written is "frontier labs need this data" (a market observation) rather than "we needed this data and nobody made it for us" (the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick pattern). Markov reads as spotted-opportunity, not lived-pain, and the difference between those two framings is what separates the companies I back from the ones I pass on.

Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010) is the single anchor I'd compare Markov's hacker genealogy to -- not because the achievements are equivalent, but because Markov has the right *shape*. Harish built voice-based hardware tooling at 17 and a hosting company at 13; Dev maintains a GitHub as @ShadowDawg and worked at Sarvam, India's most technically credible foundation-model lab. Neither has the named OSS artifact (Aaron Swartz's RSS specification) that would put Markov on the high rung, but the genealogy is unambiguously hacker, not credential-by-degree. I lean above the default for Markov here.

My 36-month forecast for Markov is that it reaches $2-8M ARR with one frontier-lab anchor customer by mid-2028 -- most likely Anthropic given their stated $1B+ RL-environment spend -- and then gets acqui-hired by Mercor or Surge in the $30-80M range, because the same buyer concentration that lets two YC founders land a first contract also lets a $25B incumbent absorb them when procurement rationalizes vendors. The mechanism producing this outcome is Markov's high-animal/low-co-founder-history signature: Harish's resourcefulness gets them the first contract, but the absence of a long-relationship founder pair makes them less likely to refuse the eight-figure check when Mercor calls.

The strongest case against my pass-leaning verdict is that Markov fits the **Scale AI** template Alexandr Wang publicly attributed to my "Schlep Blindness" essay -- two technical founders, willing to run a human fleet in a low-cost geography, attacking data labor that software-only YC teams won't touch, at the exact moment Anthropic is reorganizing its $1B+ RL spend away from Meta-owned Scale. If that essay is the trigger Dev and Harish actually pulled, Markov is structurally Scale AI 2.0 and I should be writing the check. What dismantles the bull case is the dossier line that Markov is competing against Surge, Mercor, Scale, Applied Compute, Micro1, Fleet, Habitat, and Matrices simultaneously -- the schlep-blindness moat dissolves when eight funded players are already inside it. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a named signed contract with Anthropic or OpenAI at $1M+ ARR within twelve months of YC S26 demo day, which would prove Markov can clear the buyer-concentration hurdle the incumbents have already cleared. Until then, neutral.
