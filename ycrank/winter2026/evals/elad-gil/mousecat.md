# MouseCat -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The founder combination here is the first thing that caught my attention. Nicholas Aldridge spent 6.5 years as a Principal Engineer at AWS AI, where he helped launch Amazon Bedrock Agents, Knowledge Bases, and AgentCore — he literally built the production agent infrastructure that companies are now scrambling to use. He's also a core maintainer of MCP, the protocol that's becoming the standard for how AI agents interact with tools and data sources. Joseph McAllister spent four years at Coinbase building ML risk models for account takeover and ACH fraud, plus he has a prior exit (Roo Storage, acquired 2020). One founder built the agent plumbing, the other built fraud ML models in production. That's not a team that pivoted into fraud because AI is hot — that's a team whose combined experience maps precisely onto the product they're building. This level of founder-market specificity matters because in regulated enterprise infrastructure, domain credibility is the difference between a 6-month sales cycle and never getting past procurement.

The structural moment is real but requires parsing. "AI for fraud detection" is a consensus market — Sardine raised $70M in February 2025, Feedzai raised $75M in October 2025, and there's over $700M in aggregate competing capital across the landscape. That's a red flag under my framework. But MouseCat isn't building another fraud detection platform. They're building the operational toolkit that makes existing risk teams more productive by closing the investigation-to-production loop — automating the workflow from case research through feature extraction, rule generation, backtesting, and production deployment. Current platforms stop at alerts and recommendations. MouseCat closes the gap to deployable production rules. This distinction matters because it's closer to what PagerDuty did for operations teams — not replacing the monitoring stack, but making the response workflow dramatically more efficient. The "why now" is specific: LLM agent capabilities crossed the threshold for multi-step investigation reasoning in 2024-2025, and agent infrastructure standards (MCP, which Aldridge is defining) now provide the interoperability layer for connecting disparate data sources programmatically. Simultaneously, AI-enabled fraud attacks are spiking — 74% of senior fraud leaders cite it as a top threat — creating a volume problem that manual analyst teams can't absorb.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is promising but early. MouseCat already integrates with data infrastructure (Databricks, Snowflake) and identity/compliance vendors (Middesk, Sardine, Socure, Persona, Ekata, LexisNexis, Seon). Once embedded in a risk team's investigation workflow, the natural expansion path runs from fraud investigation to compliance workflows, AML monitoring, credit risk assessment, and onboarding decisions — all of which involve the same investigation-to-action loop across disparate data sources. The toolkit approach (augment existing infrastructure rather than rip and replace) reduces initial sales friction and creates a wedge that avoids direct competition with the full-stack platforms. But this is still a 2-person pre-seed team with no disclosed customers, and the multi-product vision is entirely theoretical at this point.

The competitive concern is the dominant risk. Sardine ($145M, 130% YoY ARR growth), Feedzai ($347M, ~$124M revenue, $2B valuation), and DataVisor ($94.5M+) all have existing customer relationships, proprietary data, and engineering teams that could build investigation agents as add-on features within quarters. The question is whether MouseCat's toolkit positioning — works with any infrastructure, doesn't require platform replacement — creates enough differentiation to establish a beachhead before incumbents respond. I've seen this dynamic play out both ways. Incumbents are generally slow to ship modular tools because their business model depends on platform lock-in, and their sales teams aren't incentivized to sell augmentation. But the barrier to building a competing investigation agent with LLM infrastructure is lower than it would have been two years ago, and these competitors have the fraud domain data that MouseCat will need time to accumulate.

If this investment works, here's why: the investigation-to-production loop creates a compounding data advantage — each automated investigation generates features, rules, and patterns that improve subsequent investigations, and this feedback loop is customer-specific, making it increasingly difficult to displace once embedded. Aldridge's MCP maintainer role gives MouseCat structural insight into how agent interoperability evolves, which is a timing advantage that competitors can't buy. The toolkit positioning avoids the enterprise platform sales motion and instead offers a faster land-and-expand path. And the on-premise deployment option — which shows the team understands financial institution data residency requirements — reduces the security review friction that kills most 2-person teams trying to sell into regulated buyers. The 2nd highest-rated company at YC W26's product showcase is a meaningful signal that the product resonated with a technical audience who could evaluate it. If they can convert that into 3-5 design partners before Demo Day, the thesis becomes much more compelling.

I'm neutral on this one. The founder-market fit is genuinely exceptional and the technology cycle timing is sound — agent capabilities are at the right point for this product. But the market is too consensus and too well-funded for me to get excited about the structural opportunity the way I do with markets that most investors dismiss. The toolkit positioning is the right strategic instinct, but I need to see evidence that risk teams will actually adopt an augmentation layer from a pre-seed startup rather than waiting for their existing vendor to ship the feature. The single miracle here is somewhat compound — they need both agent reliability at financial-grade accuracy AND enterprise distribution, and neither is trivially achievable. I'd want to revisit after Demo Day with customer validation data.
