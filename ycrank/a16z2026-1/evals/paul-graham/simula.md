# Simula -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Simula isn't the team or the product -- it's the competitive landscape they're walking into. Koah Labs has raised $26M+. ZeroClick launched with $55M and a founder who sold Honey to PayPal for $4B. OpenAI itself started running ads in ChatGPT in February 2026 and hit $100M+ ARR in under two months. Google has announced plans to bring ads to Gemini. When I hear "ad network for AI apps," I don't hear a contrarian bet -- I hear an idea that sounds good to everyone, which is exactly the tell that the return premium has already been competed away. The best startup ideas initially sound like bad ideas. "AdSense for AI" has never sounded like a bad idea to anyone in tech since ChatGPT crossed 100 million users. Simula is entering a space where the consensus is already formed, the checks are already written, and the platform owners are already building.

Yizhen Zhen's resume is impressive by every conventional measure -- Dartmouth, Google, Bain Capital, Blackstone, Harvard Business School -- and that's part of what makes me uneasy. When I look at that trajectory, I see someone who has optimized for credentials, not someone who has been obsessively hacking on a problem that won't leave them alone. The claim of building "dozens of games & apps since I was 10" is the most interesting line in the entire dossier, but 7 public GitHub repos doesn't substantiate it, and there's no story connecting that childhood building to discovering AI app monetization as a personal pain point. Compare this to Drew Houston, who built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive, or Patrick Collison, who was a teenage hacker frustrated by payment integration before Stripe existed. Yizhen's path from private equity to an MBA program to launching an adtech SDK reads like someone who identified a market opportunity through analysis -- exactly the pattern I've learned to be skeptical of. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas.

Logan Nicholson brings CRO experience and a gaming industry network, which is relevant for SDK distribution. But the absence of a visible technical co-founder on a team building what is fundamentally an SDK product gives me pause. The two founders share no university, no employer, no obvious origin story for their partnership. I don't see evidence of the "running back" quality -- that combination of persistence and flexibility where founders adapt creatively under pressure. What I see instead is polished positioning: "$15+ annual ad revenue per DAU," "ROAS beating Meta, AppLovin, and Google," "~$10M+ in net revenue at full rollout." That last phrase -- "at full rollout" -- is doing a lot of work. The distinction between "integrating into AI apps covering 5M+ DAUs" and "live and serving ads to 5M+ DAUs" is the kind of gap where marketing language fills in for honest uncertainty, and I've learned to treat that as a signal about the founders, not just about the metrics.

The structural comparison that keeps coming to mind is Stripe -- but inverted. Stripe worked because the Collison brothers were developers who had personally suffered through the misery of integrating payment APIs. They were building for themselves. The problem was a schlep that repelled everyone else, creating a natural moat. Simula is building for AI app publishers, but neither founder appears to have been an AI app publisher struggling to monetize. The schlep in adtech isn't the problem domain itself -- it's competing against companies with 10x to 100x your capital while OpenAI and Google build their own solutions for the same publishers you're courting. That's not the kind of schlep that creates a moat. That's the kind where you get crushed between the platforms above you and the better-funded startups beside you.

The strongest bull case for Simula is that interactive ad formats -- brand ambassadors, sponsored mini-games -- could genuinely outperform the static display ads that Koah sells and the reasoning-time insertion that ZeroClick offers. If Simula's "3X higher payout" claim holds up and publishers can earn materially more per DAU, the capital disadvantage becomes less relevant because publishers follow the money. Yizhen's lifelong game development background could provide real product taste for these interactive experiences. And the AI ad market is nascent enough that format innovation could matter more than funding -- just as Google beat much larger competitors in search advertising through a superior auction format. For this to work, Simula would need to prove that interactive formats generate meaningfully better outcomes than simpler alternatives, fast enough to lock in publishers before Koah and ZeroClick iterate toward similar formats. I don't see evidence that this is happening yet.

The dimensions that lock Simula into a pass for me are the consensus market and the absence of organic problem discovery. Every signal points to an idea chosen from a menu of obvious opportunities, not one that grew from a founder's lived frustration. The competitive landscape -- $80M+ in direct competitor funding, plus OpenAI and Google entering the space -- confirms that this idea sounds good to everyone, which in my framework means the expected returns have already been competed away. Yizhen Zhen may well be capable and driven, but capability channeled into a consensus bet against far better-resourced competitors is not how the best outcomes happen. The founders who end up building something enormous tend to be working on problems that most investors dismissed as weird or impossible -- not problems where $55M has already been deployed by a founder with a $4B exit.
