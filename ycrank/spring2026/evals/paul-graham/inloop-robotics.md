# InLoop Robotics -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that grabbed me about InLoop is that they're shipping robots that don't fully work -- and that might be exactly right. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." A warehouse robot that pauses and calls a human for help sounds, to most investors, like a confession of inadequacy. Every other robotics company in this space -- Nimble with $221M, Dexterity with $291M -- is racing toward full autonomy before deploying. InLoop is saying: we'll deploy now, imperfect, and convert every human intervention into training data. This is the robotics equivalent of the Collison installation. When the Stripe founders saw someone willing to try Stripe, they said "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot. InLoop is saying "Right then, give us your warehouse floor" and deploying a robot that will make mistakes and learn from them in production. That's doing things that don't scale in the most literal way imaginable -- paying human teleoperators to cover for your AI's gaps -- but it's also how you build an unassailable data advantage while competitors sit in simulation labs chasing the last 5% of autonomy.

This is also a textbook case of schlep blindness. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Deploying physical robots into messy, variable warehouse environments, then staffing a teleoperator team to babysit them 24/7, then building the ML pipeline to turn those interventions into model improvements -- that's an enormous operational schlep. It's "a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site." And that's precisely why this problem sat around despite being obvious. The well-funded competitors all chose the cleaner path: build in the lab, deploy when ready. InLoop chose the painful path, and painful paths tend to have fewer competitors on them.

Now, the founders. "The earlier you pick startups, the more you're picking the founders." Two of the three -- Rizali and Sharfeddine -- have genuine robotics DNA. They both came through RoboTUM at Technical University of Munich, which Rizali co-founded and which is described as Germany's largest student robotics initiative. Sharfeddine won the 2025 BEHAVIOR Challenge, worked at BMW and Bosch, and has public GitHub repos showing hands-on work with VLA models and teleoperation in IsaacSim. These are not people who read about robotics; they build robots. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." That checks out for two-thirds of the team. But the third founder, Stepan Feduniak, is essentially invisible -- no retrievable education, work history, or technical credentials. That bothers me. "The relationship between the founders has to be strong" and "if it can be pulled apart, it will be." Two founders clearly share a deep technical bond through RoboTUM. The third is a cipher. I'd want to understand what Feduniak brings and how this trio formed before writing a check. What I don't see yet is evidence of the "cockroach" quality -- the selling-cereal-boxes, sleeping-on-air-mattresses survival instinct that made me bet on the Airbnbs. These founders look technically capable. Whether they're the kind of people who simply refuse to die, I can't tell from the dossier.

The bull case for this being a massive winner rests on compounding. "If you do work that compounds, you'll get exponential growth." Every robot InLoop deploys generates failure data. Every failure a teleoperator resolves becomes a labeled training example. More deployments mean more edge cases mean faster model improvement mean fewer teleoperator interventions mean better unit economics mean more deployments. This is the kind of flywheel that, if it spins up, creates a data moat that even a $291M-funded Dexterity can't buy their way past because they don't have the production deployment hours. The RaaS model with zero CapEx removes the single biggest objection a small warehouse operator has to trying robots. And the TAM is real -- $6.5B growing to $13.5B by 2030, with RaaS growing at 18% CAGR. If InLoop captures even a modest slice of the small-to-mid warehouse segment that the big players are structurally misaligned to serve, the returns could be enormous.

But here's what keeps me honest: "I can lay out what I know to be the right thing to do, and still not do it." The capital disparity is extreme. Nimble and Dexterity have hundreds of millions and named enterprise customers like FedEx and UPS. Hardware is unforgiving -- you can't iterate on atoms as fast as bits. The teleoperator model only works if intervention rates drop fast enough to make the economics viable at scale. If they don't, you're running a staffing agency with expensive robot mascots. And the concept of human-in-the-loop recovery, while strategically clever, is not patentable and is conceptually straightforward for a well-funded competitor to replicate. The question is whether InLoop can accumulate enough deployment data, fast enough, to stay ahead. "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead." For a hardware RaaS company burning through units at pre-seed capital levels, the path to default alive is narrow.

I find myself genuinely torn, which in my experience is a signal worth paying attention to. The strategic insight is real. The domain expertise (for two of three founders) is real. The market pull from labor shortages is real. But the execution risk in hardware, the capital disadvantage against entrenched competitors, and the opacity around one founder and all financial metrics make this a bet I'd want to take only after a face-to-face meeting. "After minute number one or two, the probability of changing your mind is pretty high." I suspect meeting these founders in person would move me significantly in one direction or the other. On paper, this is a team with the right technical instincts attacking the right problem at the right time, but without enough evidence of the relentless resourcefulness that separates founders who have a good thesis from founders who make that thesis into a company.
