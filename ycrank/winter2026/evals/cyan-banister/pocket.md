# Pocket -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Pocket isn't the product or the founders -- it's that this is a hot market. AI wearables are on the cover of every tech publication. CES 2026 featured direct competitors. Meta bought Limitless, Amazon bought Bee, Plaud is sprinting toward $250M in revenue, and Anker's hardware distribution machine just entered. When I see a category where Big Tech is writing acquisition checks and incumbents are profitable and scaling, I know the consensus has already formed. The weirdness has been competed away. The companies I've done best with -- SpaceX when private rockets were a punchline, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when the pitch was literally "Bitcoin on gas flares" -- those were companies people laughed at. Nobody is laughing at AI note-taking devices. They're fighting over them. And that's precisely the signal that makes me cautious.

If Akshay Narisetti were sitting across from me, my first question would be: tell me your story. Not Pocket's story -- yours. And what I see in the dossier is a genuinely impressive young builder. Twenty-three years old, from Chennai, built a viral Arduino hack that got 19.9 million views, turned down a Google offer, shipped an open-source AI wearable to thousands of users, got into the Harvard/MIT Augmentation Lab as one of sixteen people globally, and now has 30,000 Pocket units in the field. That's remarkable execution. But what I'm listening for is the thread that connects this person to this specific problem -- the lived experience that means they'll keep going when everything breaks. Why conversation capture? Why this? I don't hear it. Akshay appears to be a gifted technologist who identified a market opening, and Gabriel brings solid enterprise and fundraising experience from DoxyChain. They're clearly capable. But capability and biographical necessity are different things. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund him. His commitment was forged by the work itself. Here, I see smart builders who could be building any number of things, and they chose this because the timing was right and the tech was ready. That's a fine reason -- it's just not the reason that makes me write checks.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement, because it's strong. $27M annualized revenue in five months with 30,000 units shipped is extraordinary by any measure. The competitive landscape just cleared -- two of three independent competitors were acquired, creating a gap for a standalone product, especially one that's HIPAA-compliant for healthcare. Akshay publicly declined acquisition offers, which mirrors the kind of conviction I respect deeply -- he believes he's building something bigger than an exit. And the price point ($99 versus Plaud's $179 and Anker's $160) gives them a real consumer wedge. If Pocket can lock down healthcare and other regulated verticals before Plaud or Anker figure out HIPAA, there's a defensible niche here. The open-source Omi community with nearly 8,000 GitHub stars shows this team knows how to build, ship, and iterate on hardware -- a skill set most software founders completely lack. For an investor whose framework weights execution and market timing over biographical narrative, this is a compelling bet.

But that's not my framework. What I also can't ignore is what my years at IronPort taught me about security in sensitive systems. Pocket records people's most private conversations -- sales negotiations, doctor visits, phone calls with family. And a Trustpilot reviewer reported receiving another user's transcripts. Another described their conversation being "automatically translated into Korean" and appearing in their account. For a product whose entire value proposition rests on trust with intimate personal data, these aren't bugs -- they're existential risks. One high-profile privacy breach in a healthcare setting and the HIPAA positioning collapses. I've seen what happens when security companies lose trust, and it doesn't come back easily.

I also don't see the economic access dimension that draws me to my best investments. Pocket serves professionals, salespeople, and healthcare workers -- people who already have economic opportunity. Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers who needed flexible work. Affirm gave transparent credit to people locked out of traditional finance. Pocket makes productive people more productive. That's valuable, but it's not the kind of structural impact I orient toward. It's a convenience tool, not a system that reveals hidden dynamics or creates new economic pathways for people who lack them.

The co-founder relationship has a positive data point -- they built Omi together before Pocket, which is a real stress test, and the "gift from god" characterization suggests genuine chemistry. But I'd want to understand how Akshay and Gabriel divide authority, how they argue, and what happens when the next crisis hits. Shipping hardware is inherently crisis-prone, and the Trustpilot signals suggest those crises are already arriving. My HQ Trivia experience taught me that even the best products can't survive misaligned founders, and I don't have enough visibility here to feel confident.

This is a well-executed company in a competitive, visible market being built by talented young founders who don't yet have the biographical connection to this problem that I look for. The traction is real and impressive, and I suspect many investors will fight to get into this round. That's exactly why it's not my kind of bet -- the best deals I've made were the ones nobody else wanted.
