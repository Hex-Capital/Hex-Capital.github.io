# RamAIn -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about RamAIn is that it is building a middleware layer in the exact space where the foundation model providers themselves are investing heavily. Anthropic launched Computer Use API in October 2024. OpenAI shipped its CUA. These aren't adjacent products -- they are the same capability RamAIn is selling, offered by the companies that control the underlying models. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router -- the incumbent was structurally unable to cannibalize itself. Here, the "incumbents" are Anthropic and OpenAI, and they are not only willing to build this capability, they are actively shipping it. RamAIn's pre-training layer sits between the model providers and the enterprise customer, and that is precisely the layer that gets compressed when the model providers decide to optimize for the same use case. Simular has $27M and a Microsoft partnership. Browser Use has $17M and strong open-source traction. This is a crowded, consensus category -- the opposite of where I deploy capital.

Let me address the consequence of success directly. If RamAIn fully succeeds -- if their pre-trained CUAs become the standard for enterprise workflow automation -- what changes? Back-office processes in procurement, insurance, and healthcare run faster. That is a real business. The RPA market is $35 billion and growing. But the world does not operate differently. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. No physical reality transforms. This is making an existing automation process incrementally better -- exactly the kind of 30% efficiency improvement I regularly pass on. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success meant reclaiming half the planet's arable land, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means unlimited clean energy. RamAIn's success means some insurance claims get processed without a human clicking through screens. The consequence of success is a solid business, not a civilization-scale outcome.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. Vansh Ramani's credentials are genuinely impressive for a pre-seed CTO -- an ICLR 2025 publication, research at CMU's ML department, and a nearest-neighbor algorithm integrated into Meta's FAISS library. That is not resume padding; that is real technical contribution to production ML systems at scale. If the pre-training approach creates a compounding data advantage -- where each new enterprise interface trained makes the next one faster and more reliable -- then the architectural moat could be real. The "10x faster" claim, if validated, would matter to enterprise buyers who need agents running at production speed across legacy desktop applications. And Shourya's experience bootstrapping Genoshi to six-figure revenue shows he can sell to enterprises, not just build. If these two can execute faster than Simular and Browser Use while Anthropic and OpenAI remain focused on general-purpose APIs rather than vertical enterprise deployment, there is a window. But that window requires three things to be true simultaneously: the pre-training approach must be genuinely architecturally superior, the foundation model providers must not prioritize enterprise-specific optimization, and RamAIn must out-execute competitors with 10-15x more capital. That is a lot of simultaneous low-probability requirements.

What I notice that a generic analyst might miss: these founders have calibrated their ambition to match the market, not to exceed it. They are building "super fast computer-use agents" -- an optimization of an existing paradigm. They are not questioning whether GUI automation is the right abstraction for enterprise workflow transformation. They are not asking whether the entire concept of humans-navigating-applications should be replaced by something fundamentally different. They have accepted the frame that the industry has defined -- RPA but with AI -- and are competing on speed within that frame. That is the hallmark of smart people working on a problem that is too small for their capability. The pricing is free, there is no revenue, no named customers, no LOIs. The competitive landscape includes four funded companies and two foundation model providers all pursuing the same vision. Everyone agrees this market is large and growing. When everyone agrees, the consequence of success for any individual company shrinks dramatically.

I respect the technical talent here, particularly Vansh's ML research depth. But talent applied to an incremental problem in a consensus market does not meet my threshold. These founders are too capable to spend their careers optimizing screenshot-to-action loops. I would tell them what I tell many talented teams: find a problem where success changes something fundamental about how the world works, not one where success means you captured share in a market that UiPath, Anthropic, and a dozen well-funded startups are all fighting over.
