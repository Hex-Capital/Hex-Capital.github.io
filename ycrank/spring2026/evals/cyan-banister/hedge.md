# Hedge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Hedge is a competent disintermediation of an opaque wholesale-broker market run by two operators with real domain reps — Luke Button's Fernstone (YC F25) and Luke Rosa's $20MM AI-agent work at Traba — but it is sitting in what has become the most consensus-attractive AI-fintech vertical of 2026, and that pairing of "real opacity, crowded vertical" is what kept me from leaning in. The dossier hands me 140+ retail agencies in 36 states and a CA surplus-lines license #6018729, and that's a respectable pre-seed shape. It just isn't a Long Journey shape.

The strongest dimension for Hedge is the invisible-system test, so let me put that ladder up. Uber is my high rung — the taxi medallion cartel was a hidden tax on every rider who didn't know the medallion auction prices. Carta is the middle rung — cap table opacity, real but B2B. HQ Trivia is the low rung — pure entertainment, nothing structural to disrupt. Hedge sits between Carta and Uber but pinned closer to Carta: the customer testimonial on hedgespecialty.com about a 4pm Thursday submission turning into a next-morning quote describes a real opacity — multi-day wholesale placement gatekept by relationship-driven CRC/Amwins/RT Specialty desks — but the people being un-gatekept are retail insurance agents, not the small-business contractor at the end of the chain. The hidden tax exists; it just doesn't fall on someone who looks like teenage me.

Hedge's weakest dimension is contrarian conviction, and that's where I lose the room. Anduril is my high rung — defense tech when SV wouldn't touch it. Affirm sits in the middle — contested fintech but well-funded. The pass example from my own playbook is the AI wrapper founder who tells me "I was at Google and saw the opportunity," and Hedge sits a notch above that but well below Affirm. The dossier names Federato, Sixfold, Cytora, Vouch, and Newfront in the same paragraph as Hedge, and Bloomberg ran a piece in April 2026 on hedge-fund money reshaping insurance. This isn't a space where VCs shift uncomfortably in their chairs; this is the space where they are leaning forward. AI-native specialty insurance is now a category with a TAM slide and a competitive set, not a magically weird bet.

The biographical forge is where I most want to score Hedge higher and most can't. Substrate's James Proud is my high rung — forged in fire, unbroken. Affirm's Max Levchin is the middle — credentialed and serial, strong but not biographically inevitable. The pass-example wrapper founder is the floor. Luke Button is a second-time founder in the exact same vertical, which is a real signal — but the Fernstone-to-Hedge transition is unexplained in public sources, with Fernstone's site still active per search snippets, and nobody has told me the story of why insurance found Luke Button rather than the other way around. Twelve to fourteen out of thirty. Domain depth without a crucible is career optimization with a head start, which is more than nothing but isn't what I write checks on.

On infrastructure-grade defensibility, Flexport is my high rung with its compounding logistics network, Carta is the middle with switching costs, and HQ Trivia is the floor with no structural moat behind a viral product. Hedge's CA surplus-lines license, NPN 22154671, and 30+ carrier appointments put it in the lower-middle — real regulatory friction state-by-state, real carrier-appointment slowness, but the dossier itself notes a funded Newfront or a CRC skunkworks could replicate the AI-intake layer. On cascading impact, Uber is the high rung for transportation access, Wonderschool is the middle for childcare access, and HQ Trivia is the floor. Hedge helps small contractors get coverage faster through their retail agent — that's real but indirect, B2B2B impact a layer removed from the actual life it changes.

My 36-month forecast for Hedge: $5–15M in commission revenue against a tuck-in acquisition by Amwins, CRC, or Newfront, or a pivot to a delegated-authority MGA model to widen the take rate. The mechanism is the dimensional signature — real opacity in a crowded vertical produces a real business that gets bought rather than a category-defining company that goes public. AM Best's November 2025 outlook revision from positive to stable, with rate softening through Q1–Q2 2026, compresses the commission window during the exact stretch Hedge would need to break out.

The bull case that almost moves me runs through Luke Button's Fernstone-to-Hedge sequence as a forge in disguise: a direct-to-insured AI brokerage that hit a structural wall, taught its founder that the lever is wholesale placement to retail agents, and is now back with the right structural insight at the moment AI-liability is forcing emerging risks into E&S. RTFKT is my exception precedent — cultural timing can substitute for moat when the window is genuinely narrow — and "AI-native E&S broker at the dawn of AI-liability coverage" is a narrow timing claim. The dossier doesn't deliver the win, though: it explicitly says the Fernstone-Hedge relationship is undisclosed and Fernstone's site remains active, which reads as confusion rather than pivot-discipline, and Luke Button has not led with a personal-stakes story about insurance the way founders I back almost always do. The condition that would flip me: a clean public account of Fernstone winding down into Hedge with a first-person founder story explaining why insurance specifically — not AI brokerage, not fintech — was the problem that wouldn't let Luke Button go. Without that, this is a good business that isn't my kind of bet.
