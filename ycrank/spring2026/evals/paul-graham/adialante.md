# Adialante -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Adialante is not the technology or the team but the structure of the problem. MRI has been largely unchanged for forty years. Everyone in medicine knows it costs too much and sits in too few places. Over a million men a year need prostate MRI and almost no urology clinic has one. This is the kind of problem I've written about -- not a hidden insight or a contrarian bet, but something obvious that has been sitting in plain sight for decades. The reason nobody has reimagined MRI from the physics up is that the schlep is genuinely terrifying: you need to invent new physics, build hardware around it, get FDA clearance for a novel imaging approach, and then somehow operate mobile units across regulatory jurisdictions. Each of those steps alone would make most founders unconsciously look away. The conjunction makes this one of the highest-schlep problems I've seen at YC.

The origin story passes my organic test convincingly. Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago and connects his work explicitly to bringing imaging to underserved communities. He didn't read about portable MRI in a market report and decide it was a good category -- he went to the University of Minnesota's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research, one of the best MRI labs on earth, and invented a fundamentally new approach that eliminates gradient coils entirely. The idea didn't come from a brainstorming session. It grew from the intersection of where he came from and what he spent years learning. That said, the discovery path was academic rather than the kind of visceral necessity that makes the best organic stories -- he didn't stumble into this because his family member couldn't get an MRI (or if he did, the dossier doesn't say so). It's organic, but it's the academic flavor of organic rather than the economic-desperation flavor that Airbnb had.

The technical team is the strongest signal here. Torres and Jenkins invented the FREE technology during their PhDs. The team includes five PhDs spanning MRI physics, image reconstruction, and computational physics. Sai Abitha Srinivas won the ISMRM Rabi Young Investigator Award -- that's not a participation trophy, that's the top prize for young MRI researchers worldwide. The core technology is published in peer-reviewed journals and patent-pending. These are not people assembling off-the-shelf components; they built the underlying physics. In my framework, I look for founders who are "animals" at their craft, and this team clearly qualifies on the physics and engineering side. The technology isn't a slide deck -- it's real and it works in a lab.

What concerns me is the gap between where they are and where they need to be, and I don't see evidence that they can cross it. The business model requires operating "imaging clinics on wheels" -- that's fleet management, staffing, scheduling, multi-state regulatory compliance, and maintenance. These are MRI physicists, not operations people. There's no visible prior startup experience on the team. The awards they've won -- Walleye Tank, Student Entrepreneur of the Year -- are academic pitch competitions, not evidence of the kind of creative survival under pressure that I look for when I ask whether founders are relentlessly resourceful. Selling cereal boxes to keep your company alive is relentlessly resourceful. Winning an NSF grant is good, but it's a different muscle. I can't tell from this dossier whether these founders have the commercial instinct and adaptive grit required to navigate FDA, build a mobile fleet, and outsell Promaxo -- which already has 69 FDA-cleared systems installed in urology clinics and $63-81M in funding. The competitive window is not infinite.

The strongest bull case: if FREE technology performs as described -- one-tenth the size, one-tenth the cost, one-tenth the power, completely silent -- then this is a genuine platform technology, not an incremental improvement. The per-scan mobile model creates a structural moat that incumbents cannot copy without cannibalizing their $1-3M hardware sales and service contracts. That business model conflict is real and powerful -- Siemens and GE literally cannot offer per-scan pricing without destroying their existing revenue streams. And if MRI becomes as ubiquitous as X-ray, Adialante built the physics that made it possible. This is the Boom Supersonic version of the argument: frighteningly ambitious, faces structurally less competition because the schlep scares everyone away, and if it works, the impact is enormous. The personal mission -- a kid from the south side of Chicago making cancer screening accessible everywhere -- is the kind of earnestness that sustains founders through the years of painful work ahead. If I had more evidence that these founders could execute commercially, not just technically, I'd be writing a larger check.

But today, I have a pre-FDA company with zero revenue, zero deployed units, and zero evidence of commercial traction, facing a competitor (Promaxo) that is already shipping exactly the product (in-office prostate MRI) that Adialante is years from delivering. The company needs at least two miracles -- novel physics that passes FDA scrutiny AND a viable mobile operations business -- and the founders have not yet demonstrated they can build anything beyond the physics. The technology is impressive. The schlep is real. The founders are earnest. But I don't have enough evidence of the resourcefulness and commercial instinct that would convert this from an impressive research project into a company that survives the crucible of commercialization.
