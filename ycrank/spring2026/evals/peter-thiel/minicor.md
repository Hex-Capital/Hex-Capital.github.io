# Minicor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking feature of this company is not what it has built but what it has built upon. In October 2024, Anthropic released its Computer Use API in public beta, giving any developer on earth the ability to make an AI agent see a screen and click buttons on a desktop application. Minicor is constructing its entire business on top of a capability that its model provider is distributing freely to everyone. This is the inverse of a proprietary technology advantage -- it is a dependency masquerading as a foundation. When I co-founded Palantir, we took PayPal's internal anti-fraud algorithms -- technology we had built ourselves, that we understood at the deepest level -- and adapted them for intelligence analysis. The secret was ours to exploit because the technology was ours. Minicor's "secret" belongs to Anthropic, and Anthropic has made it available to every competitor listed in this dossier.

The deeper problem is that there is no secret here at all. "Legacy desktop applications lack APIs, and AI can automate them through the UI" -- this is not a contrarian truth that most smart people reject. It is the founding premise of a $28 billion RPA industry. UiPath has a $13 billion market cap built on exactly this observation. Automation Anywhere is Gartner's number two. Microsoft Power Automate ships with every enterprise license. The mimetic dynamics are already fully expressed: Automat has raised $19.25 million pursuing the same thesis, Decisional is in YC pursuing the same thesis, and UiPath has already shipped its own AI Healing Agent feature. When I count five well-funded players in a market and the largest incumbent is actively incorporating the startup's claimed differentiator, I see a Girardian nightmare in formation -- companies converging on identical capabilities, imitating each other into margin compression. Minicor's claimed 93-96% click accuracy versus 80-85% for competitors is not a 10x improvement; it is a 15% improvement that will erode as models improve and competitors train on the same production workflows. A 15% accuracy advantage does not create a monopoly. It creates a feature comparison chart where you are one row among many.

The positioning as "infrastructure for AI companies" rather than direct-to-enterprise is the most interesting strategic choice, and it deserves a genuine examination. The bull case would go something like this: AI companies are flooding into healthcare, automotive, and supply chain. They all need to write data back into legacy EHRs and dealer management systems. None of them want to build their own Windows VM infrastructure, handle HIPAA compliance, or debug click-level failures at 3 AM. Minicor becomes the Stripe of legacy system integration -- an API call that handles the hard infrastructure so AI companies can focus on their product. If every AI company selling into legacy industries needs Minicor, the company could achieve the kind of centrality that creates genuine switching costs. The SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certifications -- unusual for a pre-seed company -- suggest the founders understand that compliance is the real barrier in healthcare, not technology. And "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" is a meaningful signal of production readiness. For this bull case to hold, three things would need to be true simultaneously: the AI-companies-in-legacy-industries market must grow rapidly, no incumbent must successfully serve that segment, and the managed infrastructure layer must prove harder to replicate than it appears. I find each of these plausible individually but their conjunction insufficient to overcome the structural competition problem.

The founders are competent builders -- university co-founders who have worked together since 2019, relevant enterprise experience at Ford and Morgan Stanley, a prior connection to YC through Phoenix. But competence and domain relevance are not what I invest in at the earliest stage. I invest in founders who see the world differently than everyone else, who have a specific vision of the future so compelling that they would refuse a billion-dollar acquisition to pursue it. I see no evidence of that kind of intellectual independence or definite vision here. The company's positioning reads like a thoughtful response to a market opportunity, not a founder-driven thesis about how the world must work. "Better RPA with AI for a specific customer segment" is a product strategy, not a civilizational claim. When Zuckerberg sat across from me and gave me one hour, I could feel that he had seen something about human social behavior that no one else had seen. I do not get that signal from this dossier.

The durability question is the one that settles it. In 10 years, will Minicor's managed VM infrastructure and self-healing orchestration still represent a structural moat? Frontier models will become cheaper and more capable. Anthropic or OpenAI could ship a turnkey "legacy system integration" product. UiPath could add an API-first mode for AI company customers. The compliance certifications can be obtained by any funded competitor within a year. The switching costs from on-premise deployment are real but not compounding -- they do not deepen with scale the way network effects do. Nothing about this business gets structurally harder to compete with over time. Studying the endgame, I see a market where Minicor is useful today but replaceable tomorrow. That is not a monopoly. That is a feature.
