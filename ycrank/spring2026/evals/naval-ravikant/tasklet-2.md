# Tasklet -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

By January 2026, Tasklet was adding more monthly revenue than Shortwave — Andrew Lee's previous company — had accumulated in its first four years. That's the most revealing data point in the entire dossier, and it has nothing to do with market size or competitive positioning. When a founder who spent four years building an AI email client watches a new product surpass that entire body of work in weeks, and pivots immediately — that's specific knowledge compounding in real time. The context engineering Lee and Jonny Dimond built at Shortwave wasn't wasted; it became Tasklet's foundation. Firebase taught them real-time infrastructure at scale. Google taught them cloud-grade reliability. Shortwave taught them AI context management. Each layer of specific knowledge fed the next. Tasklet's "cloud-native agent runtime" and "best-in-class context engineering" aren't marketing copy — they're the productization of a decade of infrastructure-building knowledge that starts at Firebase (now used by ~15% of all developers) and runs through Google Cloud Firestore to the present. If Lee disappeared, you'd lose the person who already won one infrastructure platform war and knows exactly which engineering decisions separate platforms that endure from platforms that collapse under load.

$5M ARR with >1,200% growth since January 2026, eight people. That's $625K in annualized revenue per person — real output, not projections. The leverage audit, though, reveals a structural tension. Lee himself told The New Stack: "it is super expensive to run." Tasklet's architecture requires Ubuntu VMs on Google Cloud, browser automation with computer vision, sandboxed code execution, and LLM API calls — each active user consumes real compute. Compare this to Notion, where every additional workspace costs effectively nothing. Tasklet has permissionless leverage in its distribution layer (software, self-serve SaaS, free tier) but resource-intensive leverage in its execution layer. Model costs will decline — that's physics — but the VM and browser infrastructure costs are structural. At $35–$250/month price points, the margin trajectory depends on how quickly the execution layer becomes cheaper per task. This is not the zero-marginal-cost machine I look for in my highest-conviction bets, but it's a defensible cost structure if the context engineering and agent reliability create enough switching costs to sustain pricing power.

Andrew Lee and Jonny Dimond have worked together for over a decade — Firebase, Google, Shortwave, now Tasklet. That co-founder history alone eliminates the single biggest failure mode I see in early-stage companies: co-founders who are agents to each other, performing trust rather than possessing it. The pivot from Shortwave is pure principal behavior — an agent would have rationalized staying. Lee followed the signal, not the sunk cost. The $20M Series A at $175M from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and Patrick and John Collison is a co-investor configuration that accelerates my conviction directly. Jeff Dean investing in Tasklet rhymes with Jeff Dean investing in Belfort — domain-level technical validation from someone who built Google's core infrastructure. The Collisons understand developer platform dynamics from building Stripe. When my trusted co-investor network independently converges on a deal, it functions as parallel diligence I don't need to replicate.

Tasklet's democratization pattern is clear and structural. Zapier has 100K+ paying customers who learned to build explicit workflows through drag-and-drop interfaces — a real skill that most knowledge workers never acquire. Lee's thesis is that the workflow itself is the gatekeeper: "what if the workflow goes away? And you just let the agent reason about what to do." Tasklet removes the construction step entirely. Natural language in, autonomous execution out. This is the AngelList pattern applied to automation — AngelList removed lawyers and connections as gatekeepers to startup investing; Tasklet removes technical workflow knowledge as a gatekeeper to automation. The free tier and self-serve pricing create the PLG flywheel that makes democratization economically real rather than aspirational.

The bear case is serious and I won't minimize it. Tasklet is building in the most consensus AI category of 2026. AI agents for knowledge work is exactly where every LP deck says to allocate. Lindy has $50M raised. Relevance AI has $37M. Google is building Gemini agents into Workspace. Microsoft is building Copilot agents into M365. OpenAI shipped Operator. The dossier states plainly: "The core technology (LLM-based agent with tool integrations) is reproducible." Multiple well-funded competitors and foundation model providers with hundreds of millions of existing users are converging on the same space. The Shortwave dual-product concern is real — sales@shortwave.com is still Tasklet's listed contact, and splitting an 8-person team across two products is the opposite of focus. And the planned hiring of 11 people nearly triples the team, suggesting Tasklet's growth plan requires headcount that erodes the team-to-value ratio I prize.

Here's why I invest despite the consensus risk: Firebase competed against Parse, Meteor, and Google's own offerings in a crowded real-time backend market. Lee won that war through infrastructure execution quality — the same advantage Tasklet is building with context engineering and agent reliability. The specific knowledge chain from Firebase through Firestore through Shortwave is not something Lindy or Relevance AI can replicate by hiring — it's accumulated through a decade of building infrastructure that millions of developers depend on. The $5M ARR at 1,200% growth is not a pitch slide; it's revealed demand. The co-investor signal (Dean, Collisons) validates the technical depth independently. If Tasklet's growth decelerates below 50% quarter-over-quarter by Q3 2026, or if gross margins remain below 50% as they scale past $10M ARR, I'd reassess — those would signal that the cost structure is fundamentally broken or that the market is commoditizing faster than Tasklet's context engineering can differentiate. But right now, a proven infrastructure founder with deep specific knowledge, real revenue traction, and the right co-investors, building the democratization layer for AI agents — that's a bet I make with my own money. The leverage architecture is imperfect, but the specific knowledge density and principal authenticity are in the top decile of what I see.
