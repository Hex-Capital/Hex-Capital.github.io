# Ventura -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that caught my attention about Ventura is the co-founder backstory. Swen Koller didn't read a Deloitte report about wholesale distribution and decide it needed AI. He built and sold a company called bravo that did B2B ordering for food wholesalers -- grew it to 1,000+ active restaurant users ordering CHF 20M+ in goods, then got it acquired by Sensational AG. He spent years watching wholesalers fumble through phone calls, emails, and fax machines to process orders, built software to fix it in one vertical, and now he's coming back to the broader industrial distribution market with AI tools that didn't exist when he built bravo. That's the kind of organic problem discovery I pay attention to. He's not attacking wholesale distribution because the TAM is large. He's attacking it because he already lived inside the problem once and knows where the bodies are buried.

The schlep here is real and substantial. Nine ERP integrations -- SAP, Oracle, Epicor, NetSuite, Microsoft Dynamics, Infor, Salesforce, DMSI Agility, RingCentral -- each with version-specific APIs, customer-specific configurations, and pricing rules that vary by account. Parsing multi-format inputs: emails, handwritten purchase orders, images, phone calls. Cross-referencing live inventory against customer-specific pricing. This is exactly the kind of tedious, ugly complexity that causes smart founders to unconsciously look away and go build another AI coding assistant instead. Industrial distribution is deeply unsexy, which is precisely why the opportunity persists. Thousands of distributors still have sales reps spending two to three hours a day on data entry, and nobody with venture backing has definitively solved it. The schlep is the moat.

The technical co-founder pairing strengthens my confidence. Jack Collins isn't a prompt engineer who discovered LLMs last year. He has nine years building production NLP and ML systems, created magentic -- an open-source Python LLM framework with 2.4K GitHub stars and 358 commits -- and was the founding engineer at Develop Health, which hit $7M ARR in fourteen months with five employees. That last fact is remarkable: it means Collins knows how to build AI products that reach revenue scale fast with a tiny team. The combination of Collins (builds the AI) and Koller (knows the customers and the sales motion from bravo) is structurally sound. One hacker, one domain expert with a prior exit in the same problem space. This is how I'd want a team composed.

Where I get stuck is the total absence of traction evidence. No named customers, no revenue, no waitlist, no organic sharing signal. Fifteen LinkedIn followers. The website makes marketing claims -- 3x productivity, 95% automation rate -- but attributes them to no one. At pre-seed I don't demand much, but I do want something: one distributor using it in production, one sales rep whose workflow actually changed, one signed LOI. The dossier shows nothing. It's possible they have customers and simply haven't publicized them -- B2B industrial sales don't happen on Product Hunt -- but the absence is a gap I can't fill with inference. Koller's track record building bravo to 1,000+ users gives me confidence he knows how to find and close distribution customers, but confidence in the founders is not the same as evidence of user pull for this specific product.

The competitive landscape introduces a second concern. Distro, another YC company from a prior batch, targets the same market with overlapping features. Parspec has $31.5M and 288 customers in the adjacent construction supply chain. Go Autonomous has $13.4M and enterprise customers including Grundfos and Saint-Gobain. Endeavor AI has $7M from Craft Ventures. These are not hypothetical future competitors -- they're funded, building, and selling now. The bull case is that industrial distribution is enormous enough to support multiple winners, the specific workflow focus (quote-to-order pipeline) is narrow enough to defend, and Koller's domain knowledge gives Ventura a faster path to product-market fit than competitors approaching from adjacent verticals. If Ventura can lock in a handful of mid-market distributors with deep ERP integrations before competitors consolidate the space, the per-customer data accumulation and switching costs become real. But "if" is carrying a lot of weight in that sentence.

The strongest case for investing is this: Koller tried to solve wholesale ordering before AI could fully automate it, and now the technology has caught up with the problem he already understands. Collins can build production AI systems that scale to millions in revenue with tiny teams. The schlep of ERP integrations will deter most AI startups chasing trendier markets. If they can land three to five distributors in the next six months and show the automation working in production, this becomes a much more compelling bet. The ingredients are present. What's missing is any evidence that the recipe is working.
