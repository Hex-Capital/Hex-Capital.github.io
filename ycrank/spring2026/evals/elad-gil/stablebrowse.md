# StableBrowse -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding targeting the same buyer persona tells you everything you need to know about how "nonobvious" this market is. Browserbase has a $300M valuation and 50M sessions processed. Browser Use raised $17M with 50K GitHub stars. Steel.dev raised $17M. Kernel raised $22M. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was a punchline -- nobody was funding it, which was precisely the signal. When I wrote Perplexity's seed check, "AI search" was a category investors associated with a decade of failures. StableBrowse enters a market where the structural shift -- AI agents needing web interaction infrastructure -- is the most consensus bet in AI infrastructure today. Every serious AI-focused fund has already placed a bet in this category. That's not a structural window I'm reading before others; that's a market where the window is already crowded with well-capitalized entrants.

StableBrowse's genuine strength is its infrastructure-layer positioning, and I want to give this full credit because it maps directly to how I think about value capture during platform shifts. Stripping the rendering engine entirely rather than wrapping Chromium is architecturally analogous to what made Stripe different from PayPal -- rebuilding the foundational layer rather than adding features on top of the existing stack. The claimed 130.6 MB per instance, 4ms cold start, and 85% token reduction versus conventional browsers represent a potentially 10x improvement if true at scale. CDP compatibility creates a migration path from existing Playwright/Puppeteer workflows, which is smart GTM design. The bull case writes itself: StableBrowse is building the engine while competitors are building wrappers, and if the rendering-free architecture proves web-compatible at scale, the performance delta could be decisive. At pre-seed, you're betting on that technical bet paying off, and if it does, the infrastructure positioning compounds in exactly the way I look for.

Here's why I can't get to conviction despite the infrastructure thesis fit. StableBrowse faces what I'd call a contested infrastructure layer problem -- the very layer they're building is being simultaneously contested by OpenAI (ChatGPT Atlas/Operator), Google (Disco/Gemini), and Anthropic, all building native browsing capabilities into their agent infrastructure. When LLM providers bundle browser infrastructure, the standalone third-party market could compress dramatically. I invested in Stripe when there was no risk that the foundation model providers would vertically integrate into payments. StableBrowse operates in a world where its upstream providers -- the LLM companies whose agents consume the browser infrastructure -- have explicit incentives to own this layer themselves. That's a structural platform risk that no amount of technical elegance resolves.

Somansh Shah (Purdue DS '25, Lenovo data science intern) and Deepit Shah (UIUC CS BS/MS, Amazon intern) bring relevant technical backgrounds but no scaling experience. The dossier flags that Deepit Shah's LinkedIn indicated he was returning to Google full-time, which raises commitment questions I can't resolve from public data. Two of the four founders -- Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta -- have common names with no publicly confirmed professional profiles linked to StableBrowse. A four-founder team with no clear CEO designation and two unverified members hits my "equal co-founder structure with no clear decision-maker" concern directly. I've seen great founders repeatedly get crushed by terrible markets, and I've also seen markets so strong that mediocre teams succeed -- but StableBrowse faces neither extreme. The market is real but crowded, and the team hasn't demonstrated the scaling-phase operator experience that would give me confidence they can out-execute Browserbase's presumably larger, better-funded engineering team.

StableBrowse's single-miracle test is more favorable than most of my concerns might suggest. The rendering-free browser engine either works at web scale or it doesn't -- that's a clean, singular technical bet. Distribution follows naturally via CDP compatibility and developer adoption if the performance claims hold. But web compatibility at scale is genuinely hard: sites using rendering-dependent anti-bot detection, canvas/WebGL, or dynamic CSS-based layouts may not function without the visual stack. That's a miracle that gets harder, not easier, as you move from controlled demos to the messy reality of the modern web. And the distribution miracle against $96M+ in competitor funding isn't zero -- it's a second, smaller miracle that compounds the odds. On margin architecture, no pricing is published, but the inferred usage-based API model is directionally correct and the cost advantages from the lighter engine should support healthy gross margins if the technology delivers. No evidence exists to score this dimension beyond the archetype.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: if StableBrowse demonstrated reliable web compatibility across a representative sample of the top 1,000 websites (including sites with aggressive anti-bot detection and rendering-dependent layouts), AND showed that LLM providers are partnering with rather than competing against third-party browser infrastructure providers, I'd revisit. The infrastructure positioning is real, and in a less crowded market with less platform risk, this would score meaningfully higher. But my framework is designed to identify nonobvious windows, and StableBrowse's window is the most obvious one in AI infrastructure today. The infrastructure-layer score of 17/25 is the highest dimension here and reflects genuine thesis fit; the nonobvious market timing score of 10/30 is the most decisive factor pulling this into Neutral territory.
