# ARC Prize Foundation -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing nobody in this room wants to say out loud: this is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit going through YC, and I'm being asked to evaluate it as an angel investment. I cannot buy equity in ARC Prize Foundation. There is no token. There is no revenue share. If I write a check, it's a tax-deductible donation, not an investment. François Chollet could become the most important figure in AI history and my financial return is exactly zero. So before I even run my usual filters, the basic mechanics of capital deployment are broken for someone investing their own money. That said, let me evaluate the underlying thesis honestly, because the intellectual exercise reveals something important about how I think about value creation.

The founder quality here is off the charts — and that's precisely what makes this frustrating. Chollet created Keras, which has 63.9K GitHub stars and is one of the most widely used deep learning frameworks on the planet. He was named TIME 100 Most Influential in AI. Knoop co-founded Zapier and scaled it to $310M in revenue at a roughly $5B valuation. These are two of the most accomplished technical builders alive. They are emphatically "sane" — and that's actually the problem. The idea of building better AI benchmarks is also completely sane. Every frontier lab wants this. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI have all adopted ARC-AGI as a reference metric. Sam Altman is doing a fireside chat at the launch event. When every major AI lab is publicly enthusiastic about your work, you have consensus validation, not a contrarian thesis. My framework specifically targets ideas that most investors consider crazy — this is an idea that most investors consider obviously important. Sane person, sane idea. That combination produces moderate outcomes at best, because the opportunity premium has been fully arbitraged.

The structural moat analysis is more interesting than it first appears. There's a legitimate argument that ARC-AGI functions as a measurement primitive — a neutral yardstick that the entire AI ecosystem calibrates against, creating network effects as more labs benchmark against it and more researchers compete on it. That's closer to protocol-layer positioning than most things labeled "platform." I can see the analogy to something like Solana establishing itself as the coordination layer — except benchmarks don't have the same compounding lock-in dynamics. Anyone can create a competing benchmark; the barrier is credibility and adoption, not technical switching costs. LMArena just raised $100M at a $600M valuation doing adjacent work, which proves there's real value in the benchmarking space but also proves the space attracts well-capitalized competitors. More critically, the conflict of interest between ARC Prize (nonprofit benchmark creator) and Ndea (Chollet and Knoop's for-profit AI lab that gets evaluated ON those benchmarks) is the kind of governance issue that could unravel the entire neutrality moat. The nonprofit's structural advantage IS its independence — compromise that, and you're just another benchmark.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if ARC Prize Foundation were structured as a for-profit — like LMArena — with the same founder caliber and lab adoption, this could easily justify a $500M+ valuation. The "why now" is perfect: existing benchmarks are saturating at absurd speed (SWE-bench went from 4.4% to 71.7% in a year), reasoning models like o3 are forcing new questions about generalization, and regulatory demand for credible AI measurement is growing. Chollet's theoretical framework on measuring intelligence gives ARC-AGI genuine intellectual depth that competitors lack. If they pivot the structure — create a for-profit arm, issue tokens for a decentralized benchmarking protocol, or find another mechanism to capture value — the underlying asset here (benchmark authority + research community + lab relationships) is genuinely valuable. But they haven't done that. They chose nonprofit, which is a philosophical decision I respect but cannot fund as an angel.

My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? Obviously not — the entire purpose is measuring AI progress. But the inverse test is relevant here: does AI progress make this MORE necessary? Yes, unambiguously. Every capability leap saturates existing benchmarks and increases demand for harder evaluations. That's a structurally attractive demand curve. However, the supply side has no real barrier — creating benchmarks is an academic activity that any well-funded research group can pursue. The moat is Chollet's personal authority and the community built around ARC-AGI, which is real but fragile and person-dependent. With both key founders splitting attention with Ndea, the operational continuity risk is meaningful for a four-person team where Greg Kamradt — talented but without the same gravitational pull — is running day-to-day operations.

This is a clear pass for me as an angel bet. Not because the people or the work aren't exceptional — they are — but because the structure prevents financial returns, the idea is consensus rather than contrarian, and the two founders with the most credibility are building a for-profit AI lab on the side that creates an unresolved conflict with the nonprofit's core value proposition. If Chollet and Knoop came to me with Ndea structured as a protocol-layer play with ARC-AGI benchmarking embedded as a credibility engine, I'd be deeply interested. That's the investment. This isn't.
