# Playabl.ai -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out here is that 530 startups are building at the intersection of gaming and AI. Five hundred and thirty. When I wrote about Black Swan Farming, the whole point was that the best investments look wrong to most people. Prompt-to-play game builders don't look wrong to anyone. They look obviously right — which is exactly the problem. Rosebud AI has $25M and actual revenue. CodeWisp and Nitrode are in YC too. This isn't schlep blindness territory where the opportunity is hiding because the work repels people. This is a gold rush where everyone showed up on the same Tuesday.

Now, the founders. There's something I find genuinely interesting here. Sanad was hacking AAA games and building viral mods. Omar built a six-figure game modding business at university. These aren't consultants who read a report about the UGC gaming economy being worth $2.2 billion and decided to "address the market." They grew up inside game engines, taking them apart and putting them back together in ways the original developers didn't intend. That's the hacker temperament — the slightly naughty, rule-breaking quality I describe in "What We Look for in Founders." Game modding is a genuine schlep: you're reverse-engineering proprietary code, navigating legal gray areas, building for communities that are technically sophisticated and brutally honest. The fact that they made real money doing this as students tells me something about their intensity.

But there's a disconnect I can't ignore. The product they're building — a conversational AI that converts natural language into playable 2D web games for people who can't code — isn't for people like them. Sanad doesn't need Playabl.ai. He can hack Unreal Engine directly. The deepest form of organic problem discovery is when you build the thing you desperately need yourself. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Octopart founders were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic parts. Here, the founders have authentic domain expertise in games, but their target user is someone with none. That gap between "I know game engines intimately" and "I'm building for people who've never touched one" is where a lot of products lose their way. You end up building what you think non-technical people want rather than what you know from your own frustration.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. Amjad Masad — the CEO of Replit, a company I personally invested in after discovering it on Hacker News — shared Playabl's beta and drove it from a planned 50-100 users to 10,000 in a week. That's not nothing. Amjad has taste; he doesn't amplify mediocre tools. And the pivot from NeonRain (cross-game UGC assets) to full game builder shows the founders are flexible, not stuck on their first idea. If the team can figure out quality — if they can make games that don't feel like "gameslop" — the combination of genuine game-engine depth and conversational AI could produce something meaningfully better than what generic AI teams build. The 40,000 beta users in a month suggests people do want this. The question is whether "want to try it once" becomes "use it every week."

What I keep coming back to is the commoditization problem. The underlying capabilities — LLMs generating game code, diffusion models generating art assets — are available to everyone. When the core technology is a commodity, differentiation has to come from taste, community, and distribution. The founders' modding background gives them taste. But I don't see evidence of community building (no Discord, no Product Hunt launch, 180 LinkedIn followers six months after beta), and the website returning HTTP 403 in March 2026 — five months after a 40,000-user beta — is a worrying signal. Where did those users go? What happened to the product? The absence of any revenue signal, any retention metric, any evidence of continued growth after the initial viral spike makes it hard to evaluate learning rate. The pivot shows they can change direction; I just can't see whether they're currently iterating at the speed this market demands.

I like the founders more than I like the bet. In a different market — something with more schlep, more regulatory complexity, more things that repel polished founders — this team's scrappiness and game-engine intuition could be devastating. But in a market where 530 other teams had the same idea at the same time, and where the best-funded competitor already has $2.7M in revenue, raw quality of founders has to be extraordinary to overcome the headwinds. I see good founders. I don't see extraordinary ones — not from what's in front of me.
