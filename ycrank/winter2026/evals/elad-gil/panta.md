# Panta -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Panta isn't the AI -- it's the market structure they've chosen to attack. E&S insurance brokerage is a $135 billion market growing at 12.5% annually for seven consecutive years, built on a workflow that takes 50 steps and two weeks to complete, staffed by an army of brokers doing manual data entry across carrier portals, email threads, and phone calls. This is the archetype of a "boring but critical" infrastructure market -- the same pattern I've seen with PagerDuty in operations alerting, Gusto in payroll, and Checkr in background checks. Most investors look at insurance brokerage and see a people business. What I see is a human API being replaced by a software one, at precisely the moment that LLM agents have crossed the capability threshold for navigating unstructured, multi-channel workflows. Pre-2024, you could automate structured data entry but not the judgment-intensive routing across carrier portals that defines specialty placement. That capability gap has closed.

The E&S specialty focus is strategically smart and reveals founder judgment I respect. Harper raised $47M and is going broad -- daycares, restaurants, dealerships -- targeting the simpler end of commercial insurance. Panta is going deep into the hardest placements: construction, hazmat trucking, oil and gas. This mirrors a pattern I've seen work: Stripe targeted developers when PayPal served consumers. The harder segment is where the most broker time is consumed, where commissions are highest, and where the data moat from carrier appetite intelligence compounds fastest. Insurance brokerage is not winner-take-all -- Marsh, Aon, WTW, and Lockton coexist at massive scale. Harper's funding lead is meaningful but not market-ending in a $135B segment with room for multiple winners differentiated by vertical expertise. The real question is whether Panta can establish density in E&S before Harper expands into it.

What makes me lean in is the regulatory moat pattern. Both founders obtained commercial insurance broker licenses. They claim licensing in all 50 states. Operating as a licensed brokerage -- not selling tools to brokers, but being the broker -- creates the kind of compliance barrier I've seen work at Coinbase and Color Health. Pure-software competitors can't bypass the licensing infrastructure. Traditional brokerages face the classic innovator's dilemma: automating the back-office would cannibalize their human headcount model. This structural position, combined with the "boring" market archetype, maps directly to my highest-conviction bet type. The Newfront acquisition by WTW at $1.3B validates the market while simultaneously demonstrating the ceiling of the human-heavy model Panta is disrupting.

The single-miracle test yields a clean answer: can autonomous AI agents reliably handle the full 50-step E&S placement process without human intervention? That's one miracle, and it's the kind where the foundation model labs are delivering the underlying capability improvement -- similar to how Harvey's miracle was being delivered by OpenAI. Panta can focus on workflow, carrier relationships, and distribution while model capabilities continue improving beneath them. The 99% placement rate claim, if real, suggests the miracle is already partially achieved. The 38% price undercut on a complex oil/gas placement is exactly the kind of anecdotal evidence that signals the approach works in practice.

Where I'm more cautious is on product-to-distribution trajectory and traction verification. The brokerage model inherently supports cross-selling -- a construction client needing general liability also needs workers' comp, builder's risk, umbrella, and surety bonds -- but I don't see evidence the founders have articulated this multi-product vision yet. The traction claims (127 reviews, "hundreds of businesses," 99% placement rate) come entirely from the company website with no third-party verification, and the product has only been in production for roughly three months. The founders -- Vincent Chen from Google ML (NotebookLM, Vertex AI) and Frank Wang from Apple with strong systems engineering credentials -- have backgrounds that map well to the core technical challenge, but neither has deep insurance industry relationships. In a carrier-dependent business, the question of whether carriers will extend binding authority to an AI-operated entity at scale remains open.

The bull case is genuinely compelling: if Panta can establish itself as the dominant AI-native brokerage in E&S specialty lines, it sits at the intersection of a $135B growing market, a structural technology inflection, and regulatory barriers that compound over time. The margin structure transforms from a service business (scaling linearly with headcount) to software economics (scaling with compute). That's the same structural shift that made Newfront worth $1.3B with a human-heavy model -- an AI-native version capturing even a small share of E&S premium with software-like margins could be extraordinarily valuable. I'd want to see verified carrier relationships, cohort-level retention data, and a clearer articulation of the multi-product distribution thesis before writing a larger check, but the structural moment and market archetype match my framework well enough to engage.
