# Ruma Care -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Christina Huang has been on biologics for seven years. She's navigated the insurance denial process herself -- as a patient, not a product manager researching a market. That kind of knowledge doesn't come from customer discovery calls or embedding yourself in a clinic for a few months. When you've personally experienced the system failing you while you're in pain, you understand the problem at a resolution that no competitive analysis can match. Her ML engineering at Apple and healthcare integration work at Medallion give her the technical muscle to build the solution, but the seven years as a patient is the non-teachable knowledge. Meng Fei Shen complements this with deliberate immersion -- he went and worked as a medical assistant at a rheumatology clinic, spending his days doing the actual paperwork. That's not how career optimizers build startups. It's how people who want to understand the problem at the workflow level prepare. Between Christina's patient perspective and Meng Fei's operational perspective, they've triangulated the problem from two angles that most founding teams wouldn't even attempt.

But here's where my framework starts to push back: what does Ruma Care create that's new? The company automates prior authorizations. It makes medical assistants 20x faster at existing work. That's valuable -- undeniably so at $80-150K per patient per year in biologics revenue. But it's efficiency, not leverage. When I backed Replit, it didn't make existing programmers faster -- it turned non-programmers into builders. When Uber launched, it didn't optimize the taxi dispatch workflow -- it created a new category of entrepreneur. Ruma Care takes a broken, manual process and runs it through software. The medical assistant is still doing prior authorizations, just with an AI co-pilot instead of a fax machine. The underlying activity remains linear -- each PA is a discrete transaction for a specific patient. The Formulary Navigator as a free tool hints at platform thinking, and the denial-pattern learning creates a compounding data layer, but the core business is workflow SaaS, not leverage infrastructure.

The timing problem compounds this. Prior authorization automation is not a secret. Congress has held hearings about it. CMS finalized FHIR-mandated PA APIs for 2027. Cohere Health has raised $200M. Waystar is a public company processing $1.8 trillion in claims annually and already offers PA modules. Forty-seven percent of physicians say automated admin is their top investment priority. When nearly half your target customers are already asking for your product and the government is mandating the infrastructure to build it, you're not contrarian -- you're consensus with a niche. The biologics focus is a smart wedge strategy, but "specialize within a hot market" is tactical positioning, not the kind of non-consensus bet where you get paid for being right first.

For the bull case to work, you'd need to believe three things: (1) the biologics niche creates enough depth that generalist PA platforms can't replicate the formulary intelligence without years of domain investment, (2) the denial-pattern data compounds into a genuine moat before EHR vendors build native PA automation or CMS standardization lowers the technical barriers, and (3) the 50% denial rate reduction and 20x time savings are real and sustainable, not an artifact of early pilots with favorable conditions. If all three hold, Ruma Care becomes the Stack Overflow of biologics prior authorization -- a knowledge graph that gets more valuable with every interaction, in a niche where each transaction is worth six figures. The Stanford Medicine symposium and Formulary Navigator as a free distribution tool show the founders are thinking about community-driven growth, not just SaaS sales. That's encouraging.

What holds me back is the leverage architecture. The best companies I've backed create new forms of capability, not faster versions of existing ones. The denial-pattern data is the most interesting technical asset here -- it does compound, and it could become genuinely difficult to replicate. But it's competing against a regulatory tide (CMS FHIR standardization) that's actively reducing the barriers to entry, and against an adversarial dynamic where payers can adjust criteria to counteract systematically optimized submissions. The founders have real specific knowledge. The market has real pain. But the company is solving a well-understood problem in a well-funded market with workflow automation rather than building a new category of leverage. That's a solid business. It's not the kind of asymmetric bet I'm looking for.
