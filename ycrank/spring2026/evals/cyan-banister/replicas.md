# Replicas -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that hits me about Replicas is that this is the hottest category in technology right now. AI coding agents, cloud orchestration, developer infrastructure -- every fund I know is writing checks into this space. Devin has raised nearly a billion dollars. Factory, Augment Code, even Imbue's Sculptor -- they're all attacking overlapping territory with hundreds of millions in the bank. And OpenAI's Codex already runs in a cloud sandbox natively. When I look at a space and see that every investor in the Valley is scrambling for exposure, that's not a signal that draws me in -- it's the opposite. The most promising companies I've backed were ones nobody else wanted to touch. SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy. Crusoe when they were trying to mine Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota. The pattern I trust is: unpopular, difficult to assess, weird enough that other investors walk away uncomfortable. Replicas isn't any of those things. It's a well-executed product in a category where consensus has already formed, which means the price of being right is high and the edge for an angel check is thin.

I always want to hear the founder's story before I hear the business. With Connor Loi, what I see is a talented University of Waterloo CS student who did solid work at Helicone for eight months, won a hackathon, and built several side projects with modest traction. That's a competent young builder. But I'm listening for something else -- I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this person the only one who could solve this specific problem. The thread that connects hardship to mission in a way that makes quitting impossible. I backed Contraline because its founder had spent years in academic research that everyone else ignored, and that commitment was the thing that made me believe he'd survive the FDA gauntlet. I don't hear that thread with Connor. The connection to Replicas appears to be professional proximity -- he worked on LLM infrastructure at Helicone, saw coding agents emerging, and built a cloud execution layer. That's a reasonable product insight, but it's not the kind of biographical stake that tells me this founder will endure the brutal stretch when Anthropic or OpenAI decides to build this feature natively.

The bull case deserves real engagement. If the major agent providers won't build orchestration for each other's tools -- if Anthropic won't feature Codex and OpenAI won't feature Claude Code -- then there's a genuine structural opening for a neutral platform. Think of it like the Stripe argument: payment processors won't integrate each other, so a neutral layer captures the market. Connor's Helicone experience is directly relevant to this -- he's built LLM API routing and observability at scale, which is adjacent infrastructure. And the timing is legitimately perfect: CLI-based coding agents that can run headlessly only became viable in mid-2025, creating sudden demand for exactly this kind of dispatch layer. If Connor can embed deeply into engineering workflows through Slack, Linear, and GitHub before incumbents expand, there's a version of this that becomes the default way teams assign work to AI agents. That's a big outcome. But the "if" is doing enormous work in that sentence. OpenAI already runs Codex in the cloud. The core technical operation -- spinning up a VM, cloning a repo, running a CLI agent -- is not complex enough to create durable differentiation. The integration stickiness is real but shallow.

What I don't see here is any dimension of economic access or making invisible systems visible -- the qualities that connect my best investments. Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Affirm made credit transparent for people being exploited by hidden fees. Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities that lacked it. Replicas makes life more convenient for software engineers who are already among the highest-paid workers in the economy. That's fine as a business, but it doesn't activate the part of my thesis where I feel the impact in my bones. And there's no regulatory complexity to navigate, no stigmatized category to enter, no hard space that deters other capital. The moat signals section of the dossier literally reads "No public data found." When I think about defensibility -- which is one of the first things we look for at Long Journey -- I see integration depth that any well-funded competitor can replicate in a sprint.

The solo founder dynamic is actually the one dimension where I feel some relief. After HQ Trivia, I've become deeply attuned to co-founder conflict as the thing that kills otherwise perfect companies. A solo founder eliminates that risk entirely. But it introduces a different vulnerability: one person handling product, engineering, sales, and support simultaneously, with no one to lean on when the inevitable crisis hits. At Crusoe, when the bottom fell out in 2020, Jonathan joined the company full-time -- that's what team resilience looks like in practice. Connor doesn't have that backstop, and the company isn't even hiring yet. For a category this competitive, with this many well-capitalized players, operating alone feels like bringing a knife to a gunfight.

I respect Connor's technical ability and his hustle. But when I sit with this honestly, Replicas lands in a space where consensus has already formed, built by a founder whose story -- while competent -- doesn't give me the biographical conviction I need to write a check from my own money against billion-dollar incumbents. If I can't feel the weirdness, if the idea doesn't make other investors uncomfortable, if the founder's path to this problem isn't forged in something deeper than professional adjacency -- then this isn't my bet to make.
