# Traverse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me before anything else: these founders were building AI tools for mortgage loan officers a few weeks ago. Clice AI — "the communication OS for loan officers" — is still listed on Y Combinator's site with their names on it. And now they're selling reinforcement learning environments for medicine and law to frontier AI labs. That's not a pivot born from conviction. That's a pivot born from reading TechCrunch and noticing that Mercor just raised $350 million and Surge AI is doing over a billion in revenue. I've learned to distinguish between founders who arrive at an idea because they can't NOT work on it and founders who arrive because the market is hot. The most promising companies are not popular. RL environments for AI labs are extremely popular right now.

When I sit down with founders, I want to hear the story that explains why this person, solving this problem. Lance Yan is 18 years old and dropped out of Waterloo — and look, I dropped out of high school, so I'm the last person to hold unconventional paths against someone. But dropping out to chase a trending AI infrastructure category is different from dropping out because you've discovered something you're burning to build. Zachary Yu worked at Mercor, which gives him genuine insider knowledge of how labs buy RL environments. That's useful market intelligence. But it's not the same as the kind of biographical commitment I saw in the Contraline founder, who spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund him. Neither founder has visible domain expertise in medicine, law, or any of the non-deterministic fields they claim to serve. They're two computer science students who understand the ML pipeline — that's real, but it's the easier half of what they claim is a two-part problem. The whole pitch is that you need "deep domain expertise combined with understanding of how models learn." Where's the domain expertise?

The bull case here is worth taking seriously. If the verification problem for subjective, taste-dependent work is genuinely the bottleneck preventing RL from unlocking the next wave of AI capabilities, then whoever cracks it owns an extraordinary position. Zachary's time inside Mercor means he saw exactly where the gaps are — he knows what labs are asking for and what current providers can't deliver. The insight that coding RL environments are commoditizing while non-deterministic ones remain unsolved is sharp and possibly correct. And these are clearly productive builders: Zachary shipped vibeornot.com to 25,000 users in 24 hours, and tunesform.com to 10,000 users. At 18 and early-twenties, with YC backing, the raw capability is there. If they hired domain experts in medicine or law and proved that their environments actually improved model performance on subjective tasks in a way labs couldn't achieve internally, this could be a real company. But "could be" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and right now every signal I look for is absent or inverted.

What I'm not seeing here is anything that activates my framework. This isn't unpopular — it's one of the most funded categories in AI right now. It doesn't create economic access for underserved populations; it's infrastructure sold to a handful of the wealthiest companies on earth. There's no invisible system being made visible, no regulatory moat to navigate, no stigma to overcome. The competitive landscape includes Mercor at a $10 billion valuation, Applied Compute with $100 million from ex-OpenAI researchers, and Surge AI doing over a billion in revenue. Two young founders with no domain expertise, no disclosed traction, no customers, and a weeks-old pivot are walking into that arena. I've backed David against Goliath many times — that's practically my specialty. But David needs a sling and a stone. David needs to know something Goliath doesn't. The specific thing Traverse claims to know — how to formalize taste — is acknowledged on their own website as "largely unsolved," and nothing in the dossier suggests they've made progress on solving it.

I respect the hustle and the willingness to pivot when something isn't working. That takes a kind of courage. But the pattern I see here — smart young engineers chasing consensus, pivoting to what's hot, positioning against competitors with orders of magnitude more capital — is exactly the pattern I've trained myself to pass on. My best investments were the ones everyone else thought were insane. This one, everyone else thinks is brilliant. That's the wrong signal for me.
