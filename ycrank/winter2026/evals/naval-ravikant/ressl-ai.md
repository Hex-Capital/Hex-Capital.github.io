# Ressl AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The pivot history tells you everything you need to know about specific knowledge. Ressl started as an AI consulting firm, then became "Devin for Salesforce," then pivoted to trades and home services. Salesforce-related content was still appearing in mid-2025. The $440K revenue figure -- the company's strongest traction signal -- may include revenue from the prior Salesforce product. When a founding team discovers their market through a month of consulting conversations rather than years of obsessive immersion, that's not specific knowledge. That's pattern recognition. Pattern recognition is teachable. Any smart team with access to GPT-4 and a month of customer discovery calls could arrive at the same conclusion: "trades businesses have glue work." The question I always ask first -- what does this founder know that can't be replicated by a capable generalist with six months of research? -- gets a weak answer here.

The leverage architecture is real but fragile. Software agents sitting on existing tools are code as leverage in principle -- one agent handling quoting and procurement that previously required two to four employees. That's genuine output multiplication. But the dossier surfaces a structural problem: each new customer runs a different software stack (CCCOne, QuickBooks, trade-specific ERPs, various phone and SMS systems), and there are no standardized APIs. This means each deployment potentially requires custom integration work. A business that needs proportional engineering effort for each new customer is not leveraged -- it's a services company wearing a software costume. The PE roll-up distribution channel is clever and suggests some commercial acumen from the CEO's prior CRM work with VC/PE firms. But clever distribution doesn't fix a fundamentally linear integration model.

This is a consensus bet in a hot market, which is the opposite of where I make money. Netic has $43M raised at a $450M valuation. Avoca has $48M. WorkHero just closed a $5M seed. ServiceTitan went public at $8.3B and launched its own AI division. When four well-capitalized competitors and a public incumbent are all building AI for the same customer base, the pricing already reflects the optimism. "AI agents for trades" is not a secret anyone is keeping. The only contrarian element is the overlay approach versus platform replacement, but that's a product architecture choice, not a non-consensus thesis about the world.

The strongest bull case: trades is genuinely under-digitized, and the $440K revenue with four people shows the founders can sell. If even half of that revenue is from the trades product, the unit economics are compelling for a pre-seed company. The PE roll-up channel could unlock batch deployments where a single relationship yields access to dozens of businesses. And the founders are young and technically capable -- Abhishek from IIT Bombay leading multi-agent architecture, Arushi winning a third of the hackathons she enters. The domain knowledge gap could close. Founders who embed deeply with customers for eighteen months sometimes develop the specific knowledge they lacked at inception. If Ressl develops proprietary workflow data -- estimating patterns, vendor pricing intelligence, insurance coordination protocols -- across hundreds of trades businesses, that accumulated knowledge could become a genuine moat. The bet would be that they learn faster than Netic and Avoca can spend.

But I keep returning to the founder-problem question. The trajectory -- consulting firm to Salesforce product to trades agents -- reads as a team that's passionate about building AI agents, searching for the right market to apply them to. That's passion for the technology, not for the problem. The best trades-tech founders I'd want to back would be someone who ran a collision repair shop for a decade and got so frustrated with the back-office waste that they taught themselves to code. Or an estimator who built internal tools and realized the problem was universal. Ressl's founders are smart builders who landed on trades through elimination. That's a fundamentally different energy than obsessive domain knowledge, and in a market where Netic and Avoca have $90M+ in combined funding, the team without deep domain roots is the one most likely to get outmaneuvered when the competition intensifies. I'd pass and watch whether they stay in trades for three years -- that would change my conviction about specific knowledge more than any other signal.
