# matforge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Akash Ramdas co-authored a paper in Science demonstrating niobium phosphide as an ultrathin conductor — 38 citations since January 2025 — and the YC page claims materials he discovered during his Stanford PhD have been adopted into Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. If that claim holds up, it's the rarest kind of founder credential: not someone who saw a market and assembled a team, but someone whose life's work IS the product, whose research is already inside the machines that power everything. I know the difference between a founder who chose a problem and a founder whose problem chose them. Akash's trajectory — IIT Madras metallurgy, Stanford Materials Computation and Theory Group, 8 papers, 101 citations — points toward the latter. But when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — the dossier gives me a CV, not a crucible. I see a brilliant researcher on an excellent trajectory. I don't see what happened to Akash that made him incapable of doing anything else. That absence weighs on me. The "best friends from IIT Madras" co-founder story with Advaith Sridhar is warm, and Advaith's background building autonomous AI agents at Persona AI (acquired by Luma Labs) means matforge has the rare pairing of deep materials domain and production AI engineering. But friendship is a relationship, not a forge.

Matforge sits in a space I recognize from my Substrate investment — semiconductor infrastructure at a national strategic inflection point. James Proud at Substrate was someone I described as "forged in fire and came out unbroken." Matforge occupies a parallel position: AI chip power consumption doubling annually, a materials bottleneck choking the scaling roadmap, ML interatomic potentials reaching the accuracy threshold validated in Nature Machine Intelligence in 2025. The timing convergence is real. And matforge's semiconductor-specific focus is genuinely narrower and harder than what CuspAI ($100M Series A, approaching $1B valuation) or Orbital Materials ($200M Series B, $1.2B valuation) are doing — those companies are playing broad chemistry and cleantech, not the exacting demands of fab-compatible interconnect materials. Matforge owns a harder vertical that the generalists haven't entered. That's not nothing. But "hard" and "contrarian" are different things. No sophisticated investor would shift uncomfortably in their chair hearing "AI for semiconductor materials discovery." It's ambitious and technically demanding, but it doesn't provoke the visceral discomfort I felt writing my SpaceX check while rockets were blowing up, or backing Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley. Matforge is hard. It's not weird.

The invisible system test is where matforge and I part ways most clearly. The 10+ year materials discovery cycle IS opaque — locked inside major fabs' internal R&D labs, mediated by incumbents like BASF and Entegris who optimize existing material families rather than discovering new ones. There is genuine information asymmetry here. But matforge isn't exposing that system to the people it disadvantages. Matforge is selling acceleration to Intel and TSMC — the very institutions that benefit from the opacity. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was making lending terms transparent to consumers who were being exploited by credit card companies. When I backed Ownwell, the company was giving ordinary homeowners a tool against an opaque property tax system. Matforge is building a better tool for the most powerful institutions on Earth to stay powerful. That's a valid business. It's not the kind of structural power shift that activates my deepest conviction.

Matforge's defensibility rests today on Akash Ramdas's research corpus and Stanford relationships — and that's both the strength and the fragility. The 15,000+ materials screened for interconnect applications (Wiley Small, 2024), the Science co-authorship, the reported Intel/TSMC roadmap inclusion — these form a genuine starting dataset advantage that CuspAI's broad molecule search and Orbital's cleantech sorbents don't replicate. The domain expertise required to integrate quantum-mechanical simulations with fabrication-compatibility constraints is a real barrier. But this is also key-person risk crystallized into a company. A 2-person team against competitors with 500x more capital, where the entire technical moat walks out the door if one founder leaves — that's a structure I've seen fail before. The data moat could compound if matforge accumulates proprietary experimental validation data from fab partners, but that requires surviving long enough to close enterprise deals with the world's slowest-moving customers. Multi-year procurement cycles at Intel or Samsung are not forgiving of two-person startups running low on runway.

The bull case an advocate would make: Akash Ramdas is not a market-opportunity founder — he spent years in the lab discovering the exact materials that the world's most important chipmakers need, his research is reportedly already on their roadmaps, and he's pairing that domain depth with Advaith's production AI agent expertise from Persona AI and CMU. The semiconductor materials bottleneck is a genuine infrastructure crisis with no AI-native solution, the well-funded competitors are pointed at different markets entirely, and the ten-year horizon is transformative — if matforge can compress materials discovery timelines by even 5x, they become the infrastructure layer beneath every next-generation chip. The CHIPS Act inflection, the AI compute scaling crisis, and the maturation of ML interatomic potentials all converge right now. This is Substrate's structural thesis applied to materials rather than manufacturing. I feel the pull of that argument. But the condition that would flip me: I need to hear Akash's story. Not his CV — his story. What happened to him that made semiconductor materials discovery feel like survival, not career advancement? If there's a biographical forge beneath the academic credentials that the dossier simply didn't capture, matforge moves from interesting to compelling. One conversation could change this.

The two dimensions that hold matforge in neutral territory are the ones I weight most heavily: Founder's Biographical Forge and Invisible System Made Visible. Akash's Science paper and domain depth are exceptional credentials — better than most pre-seed founders I evaluate — but credentials are not crucible, and matforge's value proposition serves existing power structures rather than disrupting them. The Contrarian Conviction score reflects a genuinely hard space that falls short of the "magically weird" threshold where I've made my best bets. Matforge is a strong team at a real inflection point, and if this were purely a credentials-and-timing evaluation, I'd be writing a check. But I invest my own money based on the founder's forge and the system being disrupted, and on both counts, matforge leaves me wanting.
