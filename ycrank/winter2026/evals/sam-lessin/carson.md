# Carson -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about Carson that jumped out at me immediately: the tagline is "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." That's not a startup pitch. That's a feature comparison chart. You're telling me, right in your positioning statement, that Anthropic and OpenAI's affiliated products define your category and you exist in the delta between them. That's the opposite of an insane idea -- it's the sanest possible idea in the hottest possible market. Every enterprise buyer wants better AI agents with better security controls. Every VC wants to fund the company that delivers it. When the room is nodding along, you're not buying a non-consensus bet. You're buying narrative at market price.

The founders are legitimately credible -- I want to be clear about that. Sid Menon as a Palantir tech lead understands enterprise security requirements from the inside. Milan Bhandari co-founded Bolto at YC S23 and got it past $1M in revenue, which is real operator evidence, not just pedigree. Ketan Agrawal's ML engineering work at Robust Intelligence -- an AI security company that Cisco acquired -- is directly relevant domain expertise. These are sane people. But the "sane person, sane idea" combination is exactly what I don't invest in. The idea needs to make people uncomfortable, and "enterprise-grade AI agent platform" makes every LP in America comfortable. It's the consensus trade of 2026.

Now let me run the kill test I actually care about: does this business get stronger or weaker as AI gets cheaper? Carson is pure software sitting on top of someone else's AI infrastructure. Its differentiation is enterprise security controls and dynamic UI generation -- both of which are product features, not structural moats. Anthropic has raised over $13 billion and has every financial incentive to make Cowork enterprise-ready; that's where the real revenue lives. Salesforce already has enterprise security baked into its DNA and is building Agentforce. When the model providers improve their agents -- and they will, relentlessly -- Carson's value proposition narrows with each release. The dynamic UI generation is a genuinely clever design choice, but a design choice is not a moat. It's a feature that gets copied in a product sprint. This is the Birchbox lesson I carry everywhere: narrative magnitude without structural defensibility creates a company that's too alive to kill quickly but too fragile to compound. Carson's defensibility story is "switching costs from embedded enterprise workflows" -- but that requires winning enterprise customers before the incumbents add the same features, and then those customers being too locked in to switch. That's a narrow window against opponents with a thousand times more resources.

The bull case I have to genuinely engage with: maybe Carson's wedge is real because large AI labs are structurally bad at vertical enterprise sales. Anthropic wants to be a horizontal platform, not a sales automation vendor. Salesforce has business model cannibalization risk -- autonomous agents threaten per-seat pricing. So maybe there's a window where Carson becomes the enterprise AI agent platform by focusing obsessively on sales workflows, building deep integrations, and accumulating enough workflow data to create genuine switching costs before the giants catch up. Bhandari's prior experience building a B2B SaaS company to $1M+ revenue suggests the team can execute a founder-led sales motion. And the AI agent category is genuinely exploding -- Gartner projects 40% of enterprise applications will have embedded agents by 2026. If Carson captures even a small slice of enterprise sales teams during this adoption wave, the numbers could work. That's a legitimate scenario. But it requires Carson to be faster than both the AI labs moving down-market and the CRM incumbents moving into agents -- and to be building in a space where "AI-powered sales tool" is already one of the most crowded categories in venture, with Clay at $3.1B valuation, Artisan at $46M raised, and 11x at $230M+. The competition isn't theoretical; it's already funded and shipping.

What I uniquely notice here is the absence of anything that could become a new primitive. Carson doesn't create a protocol that other applications build on. It doesn't establish a data standard or a new financial instrument or a foundational layer. It's an application on someone else's platform -- multiple someone elses, actually, since the underlying LLMs it orchestrates are controlled by its named competitors. When I backed Venmo, I saw a new payment primitive. When I backed Aleo, I saw a new privacy protocol. When I look at Carson, I see a well-executed integration layer that stitches together other people's infrastructure. The ceiling for that kind of company is a nice acquisition, not a defining platform. And the Bhandari situation adds a wrinkle -- he left an active, funded company that he co-founded to start Carson. Maybe that signals conviction in a bigger opportunity. But it also raises questions I'd want answered before writing a check.

The bottom line: strong founders, wrong category for my money. If the AI angle is obvious, the market has already priced it. Enterprise AI agents are the most obvious AI angle in 2026.
