# Proximitty -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Proximitty is that the space it's entering is crowded with well-funded companies already doing very similar work. Prodigal Technologies -- also YC-backed -- has $14.2M in funding and has been building AI for loan servicing and collections since 2018. Skit.ai is doing $22.4M in revenue with voice AI for collections. TrueAccord has raised $78 million. LoanPro closed a $100 million Series A. When I look at a market landscape like this, my internal alarm goes off -- not because competition is inherently bad, but because this is the opposite of the pattern I've learned to trust. The most promising companies tend to be unpopular and difficult to assess. AI for lending collections is neither. Every fintech-focused VC in the Valley can underwrite this thesis in their sleep. That tells me the weirdness has already been priced in, and what remains is an execution race against teams with years of head start and tens of millions in the bank.

When I try to find the founder's personal story -- the biographical thread that tells me why THIS person had to build THIS company -- I come up mostly empty. Wye Yew Ho went to LSE, consulted at McKinsey on credit risk, and then scaled growth at Taptap Send, which is a real credential. Zi Zhang spent five years at Bloomberg building security infrastructure for 300,000 terminals and then built ACI.dev into a 4.7k-star open source project for AI agent tooling. These are genuinely impressive resumes. But they're consultant-and-engineer resumes from elite institutions, and what I'm listening for is something different -- the hardship that forged an unbreakable connection between this founder and this specific problem. I backed Travis Kalanick at Uber not because of his resume but because of his gravitas, because years of obsessing over the taxi medallion racket had made him the inevitable person to dismantle it. I don't hear that kind of inevitability here. These founders identified a market opportunity, not a personal mission. McKinsey teaches you to see problems from the outside. I invest in people who've felt them from the inside.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to be honest about it -- is the CTO. Zi Zhang's ACI.dev work is directly relevant in a way that matters. Building an open-source tool-calling platform for AI agents that attracts 4.7k stars means he understands the plumbing of autonomous agent systems at a level most engineers don't. That's real technical credibility for the product they're building. And the "no-API, computer vision overlay" approach is a genuinely clever architectural bet. Most competitors require months-long API integrations. Proximitty says it can sit on top of existing browser-based tools using computer vision -- turn your SOPs into AI agents without engineering resources. If that works reliably, it's a powerful wedge into institutions that are stuck with legacy systems they can't or won't replace. For this to be a great investment, you'd have to believe that the computer vision overlay creates enough switching costs and data accumulation to build a moat before Anthropic and OpenAI commoditize browser automation, and that the compliance layer becomes genuinely defensible. It's a plausible path. It's just not one that activates my conviction.

What concerns me beyond the competitive landscape is the economic access question. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was building something that made credit transparent for borrowers -- replacing hidden fees with honest interest rates. That was making an invisible system visible for the people who needed it most. Proximitty operates on the other side of that equation. It makes collections more efficient for lenders and banks. It optimizes the institution's ability to contact borrowers and negotiate payments. The compliance layer might protect borrowers from regulatory violations, and that's not nothing, but it's a secondary effect. The primary value flows to the institution, not to the person in financial distress. I gravitate toward companies that create economic access for underserved populations, and I'm not seeing that here. This is infrastructure for the powerful, not a tool for the powerless.

I'll give credit where it's due on the regulatory dimension. FDCPA, CFPB, TCPA, and fifty state-level regulatory frameworks create genuine complexity that deters lazy competitors. Zhang's Bloomberg background in security infrastructure is relevant to building in regulated environments, and a real-time compliance monitoring layer across voice, SMS, and email is non-trivial engineering. That's the kind of regulatory moat I've seen work in other investments -- at IronPort, at Affirm, at Anduril. But a moat only matters if you're the one who gets to defend it, and with Prodigal Technologies already years ahead in the same space, the moat may already belong to someone else.

Ultimately, this is a competent team in a consensus category solving a real problem for institutions that can pay. On paper, it checks boxes. But I'm not a box-checker -- I'm looking for the jolt, the feeling that this company exists in a category of one, that these founders are solving their own deepest problem, that the rest of the market hasn't yet seen what I'm seeing. I don't feel that here. This is the kind of deal that a lot of very good institutional VCs will fund, and they should. It's just not my kind of bet.
