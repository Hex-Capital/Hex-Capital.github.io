# Prior Foundry -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The OECD partnership is what caught my attention before anything else about Prior Foundry. Signing a distribution agreement spanning 38+ member governments within two months of operating -- while simultaneously hitting $60K ARR -- is not a normal pre-seed trajectory for government tech. Government sales typically kill startups through procurement friction, but Shirin Abrishami Kashani's direct OECD analyst background means Prior Foundry didn't cold-call into the institution; she sold back into a network she already inhabited. That's the same pattern I saw with Palantir's early intelligence community relationships, and it's why Keshav Sivakumar's Palantir FDSE background as CTO isn't decorative -- he's deployed software into government environments and understands the compliance and integration surface area. The >$1M pipeline through a distributor at pre-seed suggests institutional pull, not founder push.

The market structure timing here maps directly to my GPT Ladder framework. Stanford's November 2024 paper on generative agent simulations demonstrated 85% accuracy in replicating individual survey responses using LLM agents anchored in qualitative interviews -- a specific capability threshold that did not exist prior to late 2024. Policy behavioral simulation has been attempted for decades through rule-based agent models (AnyLogic has been at this for years with $12-23M in revenue but zero behavioral granularity), and it never worked because you couldn't model individual human decision-making with sufficient fidelity. The LLM capability curve crossed the threshold that makes this feasible, and most investors categorize "AI in government" as chatbots and document processing, not synthetic population simulation. That's genuinely nonobvious. The consulting cannibalization dynamic strengthens the window: McKinsey and BCG's policy practices bill millions for the kind of behavioral analysis Prior Foundry aims to deliver on-demand, and building a product that replaces months of billable hours would directly undercut their revenue model. Incumbents structurally cannot respond -- the same dynamic I identified early with Harvey disrupting BigLaw associate work.

Where Prior Foundry breaks from my preferred pattern is infrastructure-layer positioning. Harvey embeds in legal workflows as a platform -- law firms build their document review processes around it, creating compounding switching costs. Prior Foundry, by contrast, is a vertical application that delivers simulation outputs and policy memos. Government agencies consume its deliverables; they don't build on top of its platform. There's no API-first architecture described, no developer ecosystem, no indication that other software companies would integrate Prior Foundry into their stack. The accumulated calibration data and customer survey integrations could compound over time, but right now this reads more like Palantir's consulting-adjacent deployment model than Stripe's infrastructure model. That's not fatal -- plenty of excellent companies are applications rather than infrastructure -- but it limits the defensibility ceiling, especially given that the foundational generative agent architecture is open-sourced on GitHub (joonspk-research/genagents) and built on publicly available survey datasets (GSS, WVS, ESS).

The miracle count concerns me. Prior Foundry needs the simulation technology to work for actual policy outcome prediction -- and the 85% accuracy benchmark measures survey response replication, not whether simulated population behavior predicts real-world policy impacts. That gap between survey accuracy and policy prediction accuracy is unvalidated and could be enormous. Even if the technology works, governments must trust AI-generated behavioral predictions for high-stakes policy decisions -- a cultural and institutional shift that requires its own breakthrough. The OECD partnership partially derisks distribution, but converting a partnership across 38 governments into 38 paying contracts is a fundamentally different challenge. I count two distinct miracles: technology validity for real policy prediction, and institutional adoption at scale. That's one more than I prefer, and neither is happening exogenously the way foundation model improvement was happening for Harvey.

The founding team's domain configuration is precisely right for this problem -- Shirin's OECD policy network, Jonne Kamphorst's authorship of the underlying Stanford behavioral simulation research, Keshav's Palantir government deployment experience -- but two structural concerns temper my enthusiasm. First, Jonne started as Assistant Professor at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026. The chief scientist of a three-person pre-seed startup holding a full-time academic appointment in a different timezone is a meaningful bandwidth constraint, and it creates ambiguity about commitment level. Second, none of the three founders has scaled a company past early stage; Keshav's prior startup Lumitics raised a $557K seed round in Singapore, which is operational experience but not scaling experience. The three-co-founder structure without a clear indication of decision-making hierarchy is a softer flag -- I've seen three-way co-founder dynamics work when roles are genuinely distinct (CEO/CTO/CSO is cleaner than three co-CEOs), but it requires one person unambiguously in charge of company decisions versus scientific versus engineering decisions.

The strongest bull case for Prior Foundry: if synthetic population simulation proves even directionally accurate for policy prediction, this becomes the default simulation layer for every government decision worldwide -- a category-defining company in an unglamorous, operationally essential market with no prestige-seeking competition. The OECD distribution channel could create a land-and-expand pattern across dozens of countries simultaneously, and the consulting cannibalization moat would protect against incumbents. $60K ARR in two months selling to government is a real signal of institutional demand. If the technology validity gap closes (as foundation models improve, accuracy likely improves too), the single-miracle concern collapses and Prior Foundry looks like Harvey at its earliest stage -- riding an exogenous technology curve into a market that was stuck for decades. That's the scenario where I'd write a much larger check at Series A.

The two dimensions that lock my tier: Nonobvious Market Timing is the strongest score -- the Stanford research threshold is concrete, timestamped, and genuinely not on most investors' radar, and the consulting cannibalization dynamic creates structural protection. Infrastructure-Layer Positioning is the weakest -- Prior Foundry is an application consuming foundation model capabilities, not a platform that other businesses build on, and the open-source exposure of the foundational methodology limits the technology moat. The net result is a company with compelling timing and founder-market fit, held back by application-layer positioning and a multi-miracle requirement that I can't fully discount.
