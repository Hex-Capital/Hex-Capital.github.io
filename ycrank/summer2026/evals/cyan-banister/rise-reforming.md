# Rise Reforming -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Rise Reforming is a lab-origin frontier-hardware bet — the category is exactly the "too unsexy" lane I fund, but the founder trajectory reads catalyst-bench, not dairy-farm. George Rose, Lucas Zubillaga Maharg, and Jona van Oord met in the same UChicago Pritzker Molecular Engineering undergraduate cohort and are pointing a shipping-container reformer at wastewater biogas. The category anchors high for me and the origin anchors low, and that pairing is the whole read.

Crusoe (~2018) is the load-bearing analogy for Rise Reforming on the frontier-hardware axis: a founder converting a wasted energy stream — flared methane in North Dakota — into a valuable output the incumbents were structurally incapable of chasing. Rise Reforming is *pitching* the same mechanism — ~60% of US biogas flared or dumped, containerized reforming on-site at each producer, $35bn/y of chemical potential per the YC page — and I love the shape. The place where Rise Reforming diverges from the Crusoe rung is not the technology, it's the *provenance* of the operator. Chase Lochmiller was of the oil patch; he could walk on a well pad. George Rose is of a molecular engineering lab at UChicago and a 776 Foundation Climate Fellowship, and I do not yet see who on the Rise Reforming team walks onto a Chicagoland wastewater digester and gets treated as one of the operators, not one of the vendors. The category rhymes with Crusoe; the founder biography does not.

HQ Trivia (2017) is what I think about when I see three co-founders who met in undergrad and split CEO/CTO/COO cleanly on paper — the failure mechanism is not the org chart, it's the absence of a documented tiebreaker when the pivot moment arrives. Rise Reforming names George Rose as CEO, which is more governance clarity than a lot of pre-seed dossiers give me, and it's not a 50/50 partnership. But the dossier also flags something quieter: earlier UChicago and 776 coverage described Rise Reforming as *plastic-to-DME*, and the YC S26 page is *biogas-to-DME/methanol*. A pivot already happened, from feedstock to feedstock, and I have no window into who broke the tie inside that room. Three UChicago classmates surviving one pivot together is fine; surviving the *second* pivot — when the Chicagoland wastewater pilot doesn't scale or Emvolon undercuts pricing — is where the HQ Trivia pattern actually gets tested.

Founder-lived-experience compounds over 18 months in one direction only: either the founder accumulates operator relationships that don't show up on a cap table, or they don't and the sales cycle stretches. Rise Reforming has seven signed biogas supply agreements per the YC page (or one binding plus six MOUs per PitchBook — I'll come back to that gap), and every one of those sites is a small municipal or agricultural operator whose trust is earned by showing up in boots, not decks. The Gagliardi Group and Patel Lab pedigree and the Argonne internship give Lucas and Jona the *catalysis* credibility, which is real, but catalysis credibility does not shorten the wastewater-plant sales cycle. Over 18 months I expect Rise Reforming to convert one or two of those seven agreements into actual containers on the ground, and the other five to slip into the "still working on permits" bucket.

The counterfactual on magically-weird: for Rise Reforming to move up a tier on non-consensus, I would need Emvolon not to exist. Emvolon has raised $10.5M with a $30M Series A in motion, a Montauk Renewables JV, DOE grants around $8.3M, and an MIT engine-as-reactor angle that reads *more* magically weird than Rise Reforming's containerized reformer. Modular Plant Solutions is already shipping MeOH-To-Go and DME-To-Go skids. Topsoe's SynCOR is the licensed incumbent. Rise Reforming is the *third* modular biogas-to-liquid-chemicals name I've seen this cycle, and "third into an unsexy category" is not the same as "first into an unsexy category" — Crusoe was first, and that mattered.

Physical-world verifiability, single-anchor to Flock Safety: Garrett Langley's cameras were bolted to poles in neighborhoods where I could drive past and see them. Rise Reforming has one pilot at a Chicagoland wastewater plant plus 1,800+ hours of stable syngas production — that is a tactile artifact, and I could in principle stand next to the container. But one pilot in Chicago is not the Flock-Safety-camera-on-every-pole density that lets me evaluate the product without founder testimony, and the $5M DME offtake is explicitly "conditional" while the marine methanol commitments are LOIs. Rise Reforming has *a* physical artifact; it does not yet have artifacts I can trip over in my own environment.

My 36-month forecast on Rise Reforming: stuck at two to four container deployments and under $5M realized ARR by mid-2029, with an acqui-hire outcome into a strategic like Montauk Renewables, Republic Services, or a European biogas operator in the $15-40M range while Emvolon consumes the marine-methanol channel. The mechanism is straightforward — the ARR figures already disagree by 6.5x between PitchBook ($14M) and the YC page ($92M), which tells me the pipeline is MOU-weighted, not binding-weighted, and MOU pipelines convert slowly when the on-site permitting and offtake logistics are this bespoke. Emvolon's Series A gives them the balance-sheet room to run pilots at a loss; Rise Reforming's $1.06M does not.

The bull case that stings is the DeepMind exception. Three credentialed UChicago Molecular Engineering grads with catalysis and polymer-lab depth is exactly the "polymath cross-domain compounding" profile that made me override my anti-pedigree bias on Hassabis, and if Rise Reforming's patent-pending reforming process turns out to be a genuine catalyst innovation rather than a systems-integration play, the moat compounds in a domain the modular-skid competitors (Modular Plant Solutions, Advanced Energy Materials) cannot chase. The dossier detail that keeps me from writing the check on that override is the plastic-to-DME → biogas-to-DME pivot: DeepMind's team was compounding *toward* their thesis for years before I invested; Rise Reforming has already switched feedstocks once as students, which reads less like polymath compounding and more like a team still finding their problem. Flip my verdict if Rise Reforming publishes a peer-reviewed catalyst result showing durable performance on real biogas (with H2S and siloxane contamination) beyond 5,000 hours, or converts two of the seven supply agreements into signed binding offtakes with revenue on the P&L before Demo Day.
