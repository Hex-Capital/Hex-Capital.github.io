# Fuchsia -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Tony Gao is a Yale robotics researcher running founder-led cal.com booking calls to sell FCC/FDA/CE documentation drafting -- that one sentence locates Fuchsia precisely in my framework: a labor-leverage consulting business dressed in AI-agent vocabulary, in a regulated workflow where each new customer requires bespoke human sign-off and a lab partner relationship that does not yet exist at scale. The leverage architecture is the first thing I check, and Fuchsia is on the wrong side of it.

UL Solutions at $2.9B revenue is the canonical capital/labor-leverage incumbent in this category, and Fuchsia's distribution mechanic -- "schedule a call with Tony at cal.com/tony-gao-fuchsia" -- is structurally the same motion at smaller scale, not a permissionless alternative to it. The Stack Overflow template is what the high rung looks like in a builder-tools-for-builders business: Atwood and Spolsky shipped code that developers found through organic search and contributed back into, with zero salespeople and zero gatekeepers. Fuchsia ships nothing that compounds without a sales conversation; the "AI agents map applicable standards" claim sits behind a closed website with no pricing page, no open-source corpus, no developer API, no community artifact. Code and media are permissionless leverage -- a SaaS dashboard sold via founder-led calls into regulated hardware buyers is the architecture I have spent twenty years explaining is the wrong shape.

The Pinterest pass is the failure mechanism I worry about on dimension four, not here -- here the failure mechanism is the orthodontist-SaaS pattern from my Red Flag list: a single vertical-specific niche with no documented adjacent pivot path. Fuchsia's $417.76B global TIC TAM is numerically large but compositionally narrow -- compliance documentation for FCC/FDA/FAA/CE/UL/ISO/RIA is one workflow shape, and the dossier names no adjacent pivot vector if AI-drafted technical files turn out to require human-expert sign-off anyway. The "first idea never works" axiom is the load-bearing concern: where does Tony Gao maneuver when Saphira AI (already 18 months ahead with $500K committed) locks the safety-critical sub-segment and UL layers AI documentation onto existing customer relationships? The dossier does not answer that, and I cannot answer it for him.

Tony Gao's specific knowledge is in robotics perception -- the ICRA 2026 paper on subsecond 3D mesh generation at Yale's Apollo Lab is real, non-trainable, and exactly the founder-product-market-fit signal I look for, except it does not match the product Fuchsia is shipping. Compliance is its own specific knowledge: the consultants at NOA Labs serving 10,000+ clients since 2012 know which FCC Part 15 clauses the test engineer at TÜV Rheinland actually cares about, and that knowledge lives in scar tissue, not in a Yale robotics lab. Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity was the inverse pattern -- Berkeley ML PhD shipping an ML retrieval product -- and the alignment of founder knowledge to product surface is exactly what made that bet work. Fuchsia inverts the alignment: a robotics researcher building consulting-automation software in a regulatory category he has not operated inside.

The "match with testing labs" workflow on getfuchsia.ai is technically a marketplace, which would normally activate my Uber-template instinct, but the dossier reports zero accredited lab partners, zero customer count, zero contracts -- 18 months from now Fuchsia either has supply density to make the matching surface load-bearing, or the marketplace remains a brochure feature beside a documentation-drafting SaaS. The compounding mechanism that made Uber work (Nth driver makes the (N-1) riders' wait time shorter) has no analog here unless the lab-partner side reaches density Fuchsia is not yet investing in. The three-person team with zero open roles tells me Fuchsia has not started building that supply side, and the cold-start economics for a regulated two-sided market are categorically harder than for ridesharing, where any licensed driver could onboard.

Fuchsia three years from now is acquired as a tools tuck-in by UL Solutions or Intertek at a sub-$25M outcome that returns capital but not the asymmetric upside angel math requires, OR it pivots to robotics-safety simulation tooling adjacent to Tony Gao's actual ICRA-grade specific knowledge and re-enters the deck as a different company. The mechanism producing the first branch is the services-revenue conflict the dossier already names: incumbents earn per test hour, so they buy the AI-documentation layer for $15-25M to defend their consulting margins long before Fuchsia compounds into a platform. The second branch is the only path where the Tony Gao robotics knowledge becomes load-bearing, but it requires abandoning the current GTM.

The strongest bull case is the Anchorage Digital exception: regulated category, permission-as-moat, three YC entrants in 18 months indicating category-timing pre-consensus, and LLM cost curves making thousands of pages of standards parseable for the first time. If I am wrong, the mechanism is that hardware certification documentation becomes a structurally permissioned moat -- regulators implicitly bless one AI-drafting vendor the way the OCC blessed Anchorage's charter -- and Fuchsia becomes the default filing layer for FCC submissions. The dossier dismantles this for me: Anchorage worked because I co-founded MetaStable in 2014 and held crypto-category conviction nobody else had, and because Diogo Mónica brought integrity-plus-specific-knowledge at the absolute top quartile. Fuchsia is the third YC entrant after Saphira (S24) and HardwareCompliance.com (March 2026 launch) -- this is not pre-consensus, it is mid-consensus, and Tony Gao does not bring compliance-industry specific knowledge to override that. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Fuchsia ships an open-source structured standards corpus or a public API used by 50+ hardware teams without sales touch within 12 months -- demonstrate permissionless code leverage, and I write the check.
