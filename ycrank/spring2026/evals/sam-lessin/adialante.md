# Adialante -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Efraín Torres is a PhD MRI physicist from UMN's CMRR with two issued patents on a gradientless architecture, $1.18M of NSF SBIR Phase II non-dilutive money, and $30k of actual deposits against $12.75M of LOIs — which makes Adialante a missionary-scientist-in-an-unsexy-corner bet whose entire problem is that the underlying asset wants to be a capex-heavy fleet operator and the round on offer is plain SAFE equity. The high/low signature here is non-consensus category + deep domain conviction colliding with a capital structure that is the opposite of venture-shaped — Adialante is Sublime Security's archetype trapped in Ro's cap table without Ro's regulatory moat yet proven.

**Sublime Security** is the load-bearing analogy for Adialante on the round-pricing axis: an unsexy enterprise security seed the SOC-SaaS crowd wasn't competing for, where I got to be early because the category was illegible to the consensus AI-agent buyer. Adialante is structurally similar — a hardware medical device with FDA risk and a $2.93M total cap base including NSF grant money is exactly the dossier a 2026 YC tourist scrolls past on the way to the next "AI agent for [vertical]" deck. The dossier shows no tier-1 bidding war, no oversubscribed signaling, no "five funds racing" language; the named co-investors are Brown Venture Group, Fogarty Innovation, Walleye Tank — not the Sequoia/Founders Fund stack. Adialante diverges from the Sublime rung only in that I can't yet name two tier-1 funds visibly passing on Adialante; the silence on the round dynamics reads as cold, not as deselection, which is the friendly version of non-consensus pricing.

**Teamshares** is what venture-shaped non-standard structure actually looks like at the bottom of my ladder — employee-ownership rollups that no other fund could underwrite because the instrument was the alpha. The failure mode it warns me about is the inverse pattern: a company that needs an unusual instrument but is being financed as if it were a software cap table. Adialante owns and operates physical MRI trailers across geographies, charges per-scan, and will need multi-million-dollar hardware per unit plus drivers, technicians, maintenance — the asset class is closer to imaging-center MSOs or a leasing book than to SaaS. Yet the dossier shows Adialante raising $2.93M of plain venture equity with no royalty, no equipment-financing tranche, no holdco wrapper around the trailer fleet. This is the Ro-exception I'd normally underwrite — regulatory complexity as moat — except Ro had drop-shipped prescription revenue from quarter one, and Adialante has the first physical system "scheduled operational by demo day."

Founder conviction compounds for Adialante on a 18-month mechanism: Torres and Parker Jenkins have been redesigning MRI physics from UMN's CMRR since 2022, two U.S. patents are issued not pending, and NSF SBIR Phase II is a peer-reviewed technical validation gate that consensus AI seeds cannot fake. The compounding case is that each successful clinical scan generates proprietary training data for the AI denoising layer, which is the only durable moat once Promaxo and Hyperfine notice the mobile per-scan model. Where Adialante stalls on this axis is the public-writing absence — Torres is named UMN Student Entrepreneur of the Year 2023 but I cannot find an opinionated long-form essay from him on why gradientless beats low-field-cryogenic, and "Writing is thinking" is not a slogan for me, it is a screen.

For the founder-as-brand dimension, the counterfactual is straightforward: Adialante would need Torres running a urologist-facing newsletter or a clinical-MRI podcast with thousands of named subscribers, or a pre-existing 2-year community of urology-clinic owners, for this score to flip a tier. Neither exists in the dossier — Adialante's distribution is six clinic sign-ups via a direct early-access program and a clinics@ inbox, which is conventional B2B medical-device GTM and means the Slow Creator Fund instinct does not activate here. That is acceptable for a hardware diagnostics play; it is not a thesis-fit signal.

The healthcare sleeve fit for Adialante echoes my **PillPack / Truepill / Ro** prescription-rail cohort without sitting cleanly inside it — Adialante is a hardware/imaging play, not a pharmacy rail, and the prostate MRI workflow lives in the urology-clinic channel that the AUA's pre-biopsy MRI guidance is dragging into existence right now. The "why now" is real: Hyperfine's 64mT FDA clearance proved the regulator will pass non-cryogenic low-field systems, and a $1.88B U.S. prostate imaging segment growing 9.64% CAGR is not a niche I dismiss. This is closer to a Hipcamp-style "category the SaaS crowd will not pattern-match" sleeve adjacency than a clean Solana-style sleeve home.

My 36-month forecast for Adialante: pre-revenue or sub-$2M ARR with one operational trailer, 1-2 of the six LOI clinics converted to paying per-scan customers, FDA 510(k) submission filed but not cleared, $15-25M Series A taken at flat-to-modest markup from a healthcare specialist (NEA Healthcare, 7wireVentures, or Catalyst Health) rather than a generalist — and a 25% probability of an early acqui-talks conversation with Hyperfine or Promaxo before clinical scale. The mechanism is that gradientless MRI physics will prove buildable but each trailer will cost more and utilize less than the deck implies, forcing Adialante into the Ro-shaped many-rounds path without Ro's revenue cushion.

The strongest bull objection is that Adialante is exactly the Ro-exception I've already conceded — regulatory complexity (FDA + 50-state imaging licensing + CPT reimbursement) IS the moat, and only venture capital can clear those schleps, so the capex-heavy non-default-alive structure is a feature rather than a bug. The sting is real: Promaxo has raised $99.1M across 13 rounds and is still selling capital equipment, which validates that the category absorbs sustained venture funding, and Torres's two issued patents plus CMRR pedigree are the kind of illegible technical asset I claim to specialize in pricing. The dossier evidence that wins for me is the $30k of actual deposits against $12.75M of LOIs — a 423x ratio between non-binding intent and committed cash is the classic medical-device sales-cycle warning that converting urology-clinic LOIs into per-scan revenue will take 24-36 months of validation work I am not getting paid to underwrite at this round. I flip to Invest if Adialante closes its first three paid commercial scans at a named urology clinic before Series A and discloses unit economics showing trailer payback under 30 months.
