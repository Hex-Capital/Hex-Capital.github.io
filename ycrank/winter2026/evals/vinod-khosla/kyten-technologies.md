# Kyten Technologies -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Kyten: these are SpaceX battery engineers who left SpaceX to build... a battery pack company. They deployed 6,000 packs to low Earth orbit, and their grand ambition is to do the same thing, but on the ground, faster. I have a visceral reaction to this pattern. It's industry insiders executing a refined version of the industry's playbook. They know exactly what the aerospace battery supply chain considers possible and impossible -- and that knowledge constrains them to pursuing what's possible. The claimed innovation is compressing timelines from 12 months to 3 weeks. That's a real operational improvement. It is not a technology disruption. Nobody's physics changes. No incumbent technology becomes obsolete. EaglePicher still makes battery packs. Amprius still makes advanced cells. Kyten makes them faster. When I ask "if this succeeds completely, does the world operate differently?" the honest answer is: the Pentagon gets its battery packs on time. That's a good government contracting business. It is not the kind of consequence that justifies the effort of a great team.

The timing, I'll admit, is nearly perfect. The U.S. military is planning to procure a million drones over the next few years. Ninety percent of the current drone battery supply chain runs through China, and NDAA compliance is forcing reshoring with real urgency. The Pentagon is literally writing a battery procurement strategy for 2026. This is a genuine supply gap accelerating right now -- a wave forming before most people see it. If I apply my Gretzky test -- skate to where the puck will be -- Kyten is positioned at the right place at the right time. That's a meaningful signal. But here's the distinction I'd make: positioning for a demand wave is not the same as building technology that creates a demand wave. Rocket Lab didn't succeed because the small satellite market was growing. Peter Beck succeeded because he invented a fundamentally new way to reach orbit -- the Electron rocket, the Rutherford engine, the carbon composite structures. He created capabilities that didn't exist before. Kyten is assembling known battery cell chemistries into known pack configurations using known engineering principles, with better scheduling. The supply gap is real, but supply gaps attract capital, and Packet Digital already has a $50 million DoD award. Defense primes can and will build internal capacity when the numbers justify it. Battery pack integration is, as the dossier itself notes, "a known engineering discipline."

The strongest bull case for Kyten -- and I want to engage with it honestly -- is that this is a category of company I sometimes undervalue: the infrastructure layer that enables a revolution without itself being revolutionary. If autonomous drone warfare truly scales to millions of units, the battery supply chain becomes as critical as ammunition supply chains. Whoever owns that manufacturing layer at speed and scale could become indispensable. The 3-week turnaround versus 12 months isn't merely incremental if it's the difference between a defense program deploying and not deploying. There's a scenario where Kyten's process advantage compounds through accumulated qualification data, platform-specific design libraries, and customer switching costs from requalification requirements. Aerospace programs hate requalifying components. Once you're locked into a platform, the relationship is sticky. If that flywheel spins up across dozens of drone, satellite, and submarine programs, Kyten could build a moat that's more valuable than any single technology. But that's a contract manufacturing moat, not a technology moat -- and I've seen how quickly those erode when larger, better-capitalized players decide the market is worth their attention.

My core concern is that the founders have de-risked their way into a space where the consequences of maximum success are a few hundred million in defense contracts. Both founders come from SpaceX with deeply homogeneous backgrounds -- electrical engineering, battery pack manufacturing, aerospace. No one on this team brings a perspective that the battery industry would find threatening or surprising. They haven't identified a technology trajectory that's exponential. They haven't proposed an approach that experts dismiss as crazy. They've identified a supply bottleneck and proposed to fill it with skills they already possess. That's the safest possible bet two SpaceX engineers could make. And safe bets, in my experience, produce exactly the outcomes they're calibrated for -- modest ones.
