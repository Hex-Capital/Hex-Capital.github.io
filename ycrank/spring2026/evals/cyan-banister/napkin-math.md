# Napkin Math -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Paper Star is simultaneously building three consumer products — Napkin Math, "Pooping With," and "Stampede: Bay Scouts" — with three people. That fact stopped me cold. When I back founders, I'm backing obsession. I'm backing someone who would be working on this problem if every investor on earth said no. Three MIT grads spreading across three unrelated consumer apps tells me something specific: Napkin Math isn't anyone's life's work yet. It's an experiment in a studio portfolio. Manuel Castro came from hardware product management at Verkada and Sonos. Claire Nord was an Apple OS security engineer. Jynnie Tang built multiplayer games and worked at Retool. These are talented builders — but when I ask my opening question, "tell me your story, not the business," I get MIT credentials and strong résumés, not a biographical crucible that made food journaling inevitable. Nobody on the Napkin Math team appears to have lived inside a broken relationship with food, survived an eating disorder, or spent years navigating the nutrition system's failures. The problem didn't find them; they found a market gap.

Napkin Math positions itself as "Stop counting calories" — explicitly rejecting the paradigm that earned Cal AI $40M in trailing revenue before MyFitnessPal acquired it in December 2025. I'll give credit here: that's a contrarian product thesis within the food-tracking category. Every other player — Cal AI, Foodvisor, PlateLens, Noom at its $1B ARR scale — competes on precision, coaching, or database depth. Napkin Math alone bets that the calorie number itself is the enemy. But contrarian product positioning in a consensus consumer market is not the same as operating in a hard space. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we're building a social food journal." Compare that to my Flock Safety bet — surveillance cameras that made civil liberties advocates nervous — or Anduril, where defense AI was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley. Napkin Math doesn't provoke discomfort. It provokes a polite nod and a question about retention metrics.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Cal AI's acquisition removed the leading independent AI food-tracking app, and vision-language models crossed a quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes food recognition accessible without purpose-built CV infrastructure. Napkin Math occupies the only positioning that MyFitnessPal structurally cannot copy — their entire acquisition strategy doubled down on calorie precision, creating a genuine business model conflict with an anti-calorie approach. Jynnie Tang's portfolio of social multiplayer projects (multiplayer trivia with 12 GitHub stars, collaborative pixel art, a majiang platform) maps directly to building the "Strava for food" social layer. The team has Apple, Verkada, and Retool DNA — they can ship. If the anti-calorie thesis is right, Napkin Math could define a new category the way Strava defined social fitness distinct from Nike+ and MapMyRun. That's a real argument. But it requires me to believe something I don't yet see evidence for: that lightweight food journaling without numerical goals can drive daily engagement and retention comparable to calorie targets. Cal AI's $40M proved consumers want *easier* calorie counting, not *no* calorie counting. Napkin Math must prove an engagement mechanism that doesn't yet exist in the data.

When I look for the invisible system beneath Napkin Math — the opaque, extractive structure being made transparent — I can't find one. Food tracking apps are transparent by design; they show you exactly what you're eating. The problem Napkin Math solves is UX friction, not information asymmetry. Compare this to how Affirm exposed credit card interest opacity, or how Ownwell attacks the property tax system that extracts value from homeowners who don't understand assessments. Napkin Math makes logging less tedious. That's a product improvement, not a structural revelation. The company that would excite me in this space would attack the *food system itself* — ingredient opacity, nutritional labeling manipulation, the hidden subsidy structures that make processed food artificially cheap. Napkin Math doesn't touch any of those hidden structures.

Napkin Math's defensibility profile maps uncomfortably to my failure pattern. The dossier states explicitly: "any team with access to vision LLMs and mobile development capability could replicate the feature set." No patents, no regulatory barriers, no proprietary data at this stage. The "Strava for food" social graph is theoretically compelling but faces a documented cold-start problem — the journal-sharing feature has near-zero value for solo users. I've been burned three times by consumer attention products without infrastructure moats: HQ Trivia had the right product and timing but no structural defensibility, and it was destroyed by dynamics unrelated to product quality. GameCrush and IRL followed the same pattern. Napkin Math's differentiation depends on "design execution, brand positioning, and community growth rather than proprietary technology" — those are exactly the factors that evaporate under competitive pressure. The name collision with Every's established "Napkin Math" newsletter compounds the organic discovery problem. The one condition that would flip me: show me 60-day retention data proving the anti-calorie journal model retains users at rates competitive with calorie-counting apps, combined with evidence that at least one founder has a deeply personal, biographical stake in this specific problem. Without those, Napkin Math is a well-designed consumer experiment by capable MIT engineers — but it's not a Long Journey bet.
