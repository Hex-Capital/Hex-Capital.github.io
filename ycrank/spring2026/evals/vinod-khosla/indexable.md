# Indexable -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Indexable is the gap between the sophistication of the engineering and the ambition of the target. A custom VMM on KVM, a patched Linux 6.19 kernel, RDMA transport, content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication — this is the kind of systems work that requires genuine talent and deep conviction. Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft engine in Rust that handles 10,000 concurrent players, which is structurally the same problem as forking stateful virtual machines at scale. The technical instincts are clearly there. But when I ask my first question — "if this fully works, does it materially change how millions of people live?" — the answer is indirect at best. Indexable is infrastructure for the infrastructure that supports AI agents that might change how people live. That's two layers of indirection from consequence.

Now, I believe AI agents will transform most knowledge work within this decade. I've said publicly that 80% of jobs will be done by AI. If that's true, the substrate those agents run on matters enormously. The bull case for Indexable is that stateful forking — the ability to snapshot an entire running environment including databases and processes in 26 milliseconds — enables a qualitatively new class of agent behavior. Agents that can branch, explore, and roll back are fundamentally more capable than agents constrained to linear execution. If Indexable becomes the operating layer for autonomous agents the way Linux became the operating layer for cloud compute, the consequence is massive. I take this seriously. The problem is that six well-funded competitors and every major cloud provider are converging on the same insight simultaneously. E2B has 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. Daytona crossed $1M ARR within two months of relaunch. Modal is at $50M ARR. And Morph Labs — which my own firm backed at $5.75M — is building "Infinibranch" environment branching in the same space. The category will produce winners. The question is whether Indexable's technical edge — 26ms vs. 90ms vs. 150ms — is the kind of discontinuity that compounds into market dominance, or a benchmark advantage that competitors close within 18 months.

The technical approach is where Indexable scores highest in my framework. Building a custom hypervisor instead of wrapping Firecracker or Docker is the right kind of decision — it's how Rocket Lab built custom engines instead of brokering launches on existing rockets. The full-stack integration of VM forking, database state, process memory, and deduplicated storage represents a genuinely non-linear approach. Every competitor I see is assembling commodity components; Indexable is building from the metal up. That matters. But I want to distinguish between technical non-linearity and consequence non-linearity. A 10x improvement in sandbox cold-start time is technically impressive. Whether it unlocks a step-function change in what agents can do — rather than a smoother developer experience — is the question I can't answer from this dossier.

Gazelka has the profile of a strong systems engineer, not a domain expert, which I generally prefer. xAI, Speechify, a prior acquisition, deep Rust expertise, and the Hyperion project all signal someone who builds from first principles rather than following playbooks. But I see a single named founder on a 3-person team building one of the most complex systems stacks I've encountered at pre-seed. Custom VMMs, custom kernels, and RDMA transport layers are not maintained by small teams for long without something breaking. My "gene pool" principle says the early team is everything — and here, the gene pool is effectively one person. The job posting for a founding engineer at $150K-$175K with 0.75-1.5% equity tells me the team is still assembling, which at this stage of technical complexity is a genuine risk factor.

What would make me write a check? If Gazelka showed me that the 26ms stateful fork enables agent architectures that literally cannot exist on any competitor's infrastructure — not faster versions of existing workflows, but new capabilities that require atomic state branching as a primitive. If I saw evidence that agent platform builders had tried E2B and Daytona and hit a wall that only full-state forking could solve. That would shift this from "better plumbing" to "new foundation." I don't see that evidence yet. The website returned a 403. There are no public users, no press, no community. The "early access DM" distribution model is fine for pre-seed, but at this level of competitive intensity, stealth is not a strategy — it's a timer counting down while E2B and Daytona lock up enterprise contracts.

This is a technically impressive company building in a real and growing market, but it doesn't activate my framework at the level where I write checks. The consequence magnitude is bounded and indirect. The ambition is engineering-deep rather than world-reshaping. There is no path to making an expensive necessity free — this makes developer tools incrementally better, not radically cheaper. I respect the technical craft, and I think the category will be important. But when I invest, I'm either losing my money or making 50 times my money. A sandbox infrastructure company competing against six funded startups and every cloud provider, with one named founder and no visible users, has a high probability of losing my money and a moderate probability of building a successful business — but not the asymmetric consequence profile I need.
