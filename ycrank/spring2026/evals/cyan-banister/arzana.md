# Arzana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

William Alexander is 21 years old, grew up working at a manufacturing sourcing company in Okoboji, Iowa, and went back there after Stanford. Marshall Kools is 24, worked manufacturing jobs in Wisconsin, wrestled D1 at Stanford, and is pulling about $10,000 a year in personal salary while embedding engineers in factory offices across the Midwest. When I read that in the New York Times piece — "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype" — something real flickered. These two didn't sit in a San Francisco apartment and decide manufacturing was a good market. They grew up inside it. Arzana maintains offices in Okoboji and Appleton alongside San Francisco, and their forward-deployed model means they physically show up at customer facilities before they write a line of configuration code. That's not how AI wrapper founders operate. Marshall's $10K salary tells me he's building because he has to, not because it's his best career option after Stanford. The biographical connection to manufacturing is authentic — but I have to be honest about its depth. Working at a sourcing company and seeing inefficient quoting processes is genuine domain exposure. It's not the same as the Crusoe founders watching energy literally burn in North Dakota oil fields and feeling compelled to stop the waste. Arzana's founders saw a slow process and decided to fix it. That's real, but it's closer to "I knew this world and spotted the gap" than "this problem found me and I couldn't walk away."

Eight named customers — Tecton, Tier1, Koike, Milltown Paper, Iowa Mold, Kuebler, Givi, and Zeiss — at the pre-seed stage is meaningful traction for Arzana. Trent Duerr at Milltown Paper saying quotes went from 15 minutes to one click is the kind of specific, embodied testimonial I pay attention to. And the clear CEO/COO split between William and Marshall is exactly right — I broke my rule on that once with HQ Trivia and watched the right product at the right time get destroyed by founder conflict. Arzana doesn't have that problem. But when I run my invisible system test — is there an opaque, extractive structure being made transparent? — Arzana comes up short. Manufacturing front offices are inefficient. Everyone inside them knows they're inefficient. The 15-minute quoting process isn't hiding anything from anyone; it's just slow. Compare that to what Affirm did to credit card opacity or what Ownwell does to the property tax system — those companies exposed structures that were actively extracting value from people who didn't understand the mechanics. Arzana is making a visible, understood process faster. That's valuable. It's not the same thing.

Conexiom has raised $170M and processes $100B+ in B2B transactions annually. Paperless Parts has $51M raised and 500+ customers. Esker was acquired by Bridgepoint and General Atlantic with 3,000+ enterprise customers including NVIDIA and Sony. Arzana is entering a market where well-funded incumbents already exist and are shipping their own AI capabilities — Conexiom launched an AI Platform, Paperless Parts released AI-powered quoting, DigiFabster has an AI Quote Agent. The underlying technology — LLMs extracting structured data from RFQs and POs and writing to ERPs — is accessible to any well-resourced competitor. Where I see defensibility for Arzana is in the per-customer AI models trained on historical job costs and the ERP integration switching costs that accumulate after their forward-deployed onboarding. That's a real but moderate moat. When I think about Flexport — which built compounding logistics infrastructure where each new customer made the network more valuable — Arzana has some of the same switching-cost dynamics but lacks the network effects. Each Arzana customer relationship deepens independently; it doesn't make the next customer's product better in the way that Flexport's freight data or Flock Safety's camera network compounds across the installed base.

The contrarian question is where I struggle most with Arzana. "AI for manufacturing" is becoming a recognized category — $170M into Conexiom alone proves investors are already comfortable here. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we're automating quoting for manufacturers." Compare that to when I wrote my check into Anduril, when defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley, or Contraline, when male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Arzana's founders going back to Iowa and Wisconsin factories is charming and authentic, but it's not contrarian — it's just unfashionable, which is different. The NYT ran a warm profile. YC accepted them. Tom Blomfield is their partner. This isn't a company being told "no" by 300 investors. The space is underserved, not stigmatized.

Here's the strongest bull case an advocate would make: Arzana's forward-deployed model and Midwest manufacturing roots create an authentic distribution advantage that Conexiom and Esker — selling from enterprise sales offices to procurement departments — can never replicate. William and Marshall speak the language of job shop owners in Iowa and Wisconsin because they grew up there. The per-customer training data creates a compounding accuracy advantage. Manufacturing is at a genuine LLM inflection point where document extraction finally works reliably enough for production use, and Arzana is positioned as the AI-native entrant against legacy vendors who are bolting AI onto old architectures. The ITAR compliance signal shows they're building for defense manufacturing, which could be a genuine moat. And at pre-seed, comparing them unfavorably to Conexiom's $170M is unfair — Conexiom started somewhere too. I take this seriously. The authenticity is real. The traction is real. The timing is defensible. But the condition that would flip me: I'd need to see evidence that Arzana is exposing something genuinely hidden and extractive — that manufacturers are losing deals or getting exploited because pricing information is trapped in opaque systems controlled by ERP vendors and implementation consultants, and that Arzana's transparency creates structural power shifts, not just time savings. If the pitch is "we freed this job shop from a system that was costing them deals they didn't even know they were losing," that's an invisible system story. Right now, the pitch is "we made quoting faster," which is a different, smaller thing.

Arzana lands in the space where I respect the founders, believe in the market, and see real early traction — but where my framework doesn't light up the way it does when I encounter something that disrupts a hidden structure or enters a space that makes people uncomfortable. The two dimensions that hold this score down are the absence of an invisible system being made visible and the lack of genuine contrarian conviction — Arzana is solving a real problem in a recognized market, not exposing an extractive structure in a stigmatized one. William and Marshall's biographical connection to manufacturing earns genuine points, and the clear CEO/COO delineation, the sacrifice signals, and the eight named customers at pre-seed all register. But when I imagine where Arzana ends up in ten years, I see a strong vertical SaaS company — not the kind of infrastructure that reshapes what's possible. I'd watch from the sideline and revisit if the system-disruption thesis emerges.
