# Kita -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking thing about Kita is the CTO's credentials. Rhea Malhotra's Firestone Medal -- Stanford CS's highest thesis honor -- combined with publications at ICRA, Humanoids, and RSS represents genuine technical firepower. Paired with Carmel Limcaoco's Manila roots and product shipping experience at Apple, the founder combination is unusually well-matched to the problem. If I were evaluating the founders in isolation, I would note this as a team with real capability. But capability deployed in service of a consensus thesis without a path to monopoly is capability wasted. The most talented engineers in the world built cleantech companies in 2008, and their talent did not save them from the structural dynamics of competitive markets.

The question I always ask first: what is the secret? Kita's implicit thesis is that VLMs can process messy emerging-market financial documents better than legacy OCR, and that linking document signals to repayment outcomes creates a compounding data advantage. Neither half of this thesis is contrarian. Every AI researcher knows VLMs outperform OCR on unstructured documents -- this is the consensus view at every machine learning conference. And every fintech investor knows that emerging markets are underbanked and document-heavy. Combining two consensus observations does not produce a secret. A secret is something most intelligent people would actively argue against. No one argues against "AI can read documents better than humans" or "the Philippines needs better lending infrastructure." This is a problem hidden in plain sight -- a schlep, in Paul Graham's language -- but it is not a truth that the world rejects. The absence of a genuine contrarian insight means the company is structurally exposed to competition from anyone who makes the same obvious observation.

The competitive dynamics confirm this diagnosis. Ocrolus has $252 million in revenue and $127 million in funding. Docsumo, Vaultedge, and Zest AI occupy adjacent positions. Kita's differentiation is geographic -- "we do what Ocrolus does, but for Southeast Asia and Latin America." This is a 1-to-n move: taking an existing category and adapting it to new markets. It is not creating something that did not previously exist. More critically, the underlying technology -- vision-language models -- is commodity infrastructure. OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are making these capabilities available through APIs that any lender could use directly. When your core technology is an API call to someone else's model, the "10x" advantage you claim over manual review has nothing to do with your proprietary technology and everything to do with the model provider's. The hyperlocalization layer -- training on Filipino e-wallet formats and Indonesian payslips -- is real work, but it is the kind of data collection and fine-tuning that a well-resourced regional fintech company could replicate in a year. This is not the kind of proprietary technology that compounds over decades.

The strongest bull case requires believing that the data flywheel -- document signals linked to repayment outcomes -- creates a self-reinforcing monopoly in emerging-market credit intelligence. If Kita processes enough loans and accumulates enough outcome data, it could transition from document processing (a commodity) to credit scoring (a monopoly). This would be analogous to how Palantir's intelligence platform became indispensable to government agencies not because of any single algorithm but because of the accumulated integrations and institutional knowledge embedded in the system. The six-figure pilot and reported 30% month-over-month growth across multiple Southeast Asian markets suggest real demand. If Kita could own the credit intelligence layer for the Philippines the way Palantir owns intelligence analysis for the CIA, the market could expand concentrically -- Indonesia, Mexico, eventually thin-file populations in the US. The beachhead strategy is actually sound: start with Philippine lenders, monopolize that specific niche, then expand. But this bull case requires several things to be true simultaneously: that the data flywheel generates meaningful predictive power (unproven), that regional incumbents and local digital banks don't build this in-house, that VLM inference costs fall fast enough to support the thin margins of high-volume, low-ticket emerging-market lending, and that regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions doesn't fracture the flywheel into country-specific silos that never reach critical mass. Each of these is uncertain; together they make the monopoly outcome improbable rather than inevitable.

The market arithmetic also troubles me. The Philippines alternative lending market is $1.64 billion in total lending volume. Kita captures a processing fee on that volume -- perhaps 50 to 100 basis points at best. That is a $8-16 million revenue opportunity in a single country, growing at 14% annually. Even across all of Southeast Asia, the lending-specific document processing TAM in emerging markets is likely sub-$100 million today. This is not a power-law opportunity. The best investment in a fund must have the potential to return the entire fund. A document processing company serving emerging-market lenders, even if it dominates its niche completely, does not have the structural capacity for the kind of outcome that justifies an angel investment from my portfolio. The company could become a solid, profitable business -- and that is precisely the problem. I do not invest in solid, profitable businesses. I invest in companies that will either fail or reshape civilization.

Kita has competent founders solving a real problem in a market they understand personally. By most conventional venture standards, it is a reasonable bet. But my framework is not conventional. The absence of a genuine secret, the commodity nature of the underlying technology, the competitive market structure, and the limited power-law potential all point in the same direction. This is a company built to compete, not to monopolize. And as I have said many times: every moment spent competing is a moment not spent building a monopoly.
