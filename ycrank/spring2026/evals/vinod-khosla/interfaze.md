# Interfaze -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Interfaze published its hybrid DNN/CNN routing architecture as a peer-reviewed paper at IEEE CAI 2026 (arXiv:2602.04101), which tells me two things simultaneously — the team has genuine technical chops, and the architecture is now public knowledge. That's the central tension. The approach of routing perception tasks through specialized modules before forwarding distilled context to LLMs is real systems engineering, not a wrapper bolted onto OpenAI's API. But publishing the blueprint means Firecrawl (already at $16.2M in funding), Google Document AI, or any motivated team at Amazon can study the exact routing mechanism and replicate it. I've learned from decades of investing that defensibility lives in compounding advantages — data flywheels, network effects, proprietary processes that improve with use. Interfaze's architecture paper is an academic credential, not a moat.

Interfaze's consequence of success is where my framework breaks against this company. If Interfaze fully succeeds — captures the unified perception API market, achieves the claimed 98–99% structured output accuracy at $1.50/M input tokens — what changes? Developers have a better API to call for OCR, scraping, speech-to-text, and classification. Their pipelines are cleaner, cheaper, more deterministic. That's useful. It's a real product solving a real pain point. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. Compare this to what I asked myself before writing the OpenAI check: "What if artificial general intelligence actually works?" The answer was: everything changes. Compare to Commonwealth Fusion Systems: "What if fusion works?" Answer: limitless energy, the entire cost structure of civilization shifts. Interfaze's answer is: developer workflow efficiency improves. I regularly see YC companies building better tooling for technical professionals, and the pitch always sounds reasonable — large TAM ($14.66B Document AI market, $10.2B scraping market), real inefficiency, technical differentiation. But a moderately profitable API company is not what I invest my own money for. I want to either lose the money or make 50 times.

Yoeven Khemlani moved from Singapore to San Francisco, built a prior startup (Stayr) that claimed $1M ARR in year one with a potential acquisition, and rebranded from JigsawStack to Interfaze — all signals of a founder who iterates and adapts. Harsha Vardhan Khurdula brings Purdue ML research credentials and co-authored the architecture paper, giving Interfaze genuine research depth in computer vision and NLP. The team of five has production engineering on one side and ML science on the other, which is the right composition for building a hybrid model system. But I have no evidence of the kind of first-principles reasoning velocity that separates the founders I back from competent technologists. The rebrand itself is ambiguous — it could signal sharp strategic thinking or a company still searching for identity (their email is still interfaze@jigsawstack.com). Interfaze was in the Vercel AI Accelerator and secured a Groq partnership, which are real distribution signals, but they're validation from the ecosystem, not evidence of the outsider irreverence I look for. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand building rocket engines in his garage — that's an outsider with an unreasonable belief. Interfaze's founders are skilled engineers solving a known problem with a well-understood value proposition.

The bull case for Interfaze deserves honest construction: the hybrid architecture is a genuine non-linear insight — separating perception from reasoning is architecturally distinct from both monolithic LLMs and fragmented point solutions. The IEEE CAI 2026 acceptance validates the approach is novel, not incremental. The consumption-based pricing ($1.50/$3.50 per million tokens) could undercut GPT-4 by an order of magnitude for perception-heavy tasks, making Interfaze the default perception layer for every AI application. The OpenAI-compatible API creates zero-friction adoption. And the TAM is legitimately large and growing at 13.5–23.5% CAGR. A bull investor would argue Interfaze is to perception tasks what Stripe was to payments — the unified API that collapses fragmentation. But Stripe's consequence magnitude was enabling an entire generation of internet commerce; Interfaze's is making OCR cheaper. Stripe's moat was regulatory complexity and financial partnerships that compounded over time; Interfaze published its architecture in a paper. I checked my Known Exceptions — the DoorDash override required planetary-scale consequence (transforming food access for hundreds of millions), and the Oscar Health override required radical affordability of a basic human need. Interfaze serves developers, not end consumers of basic services. No exception applies.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Interfaze demonstrated that its perception layer was being adopted not just by developers building pipelines, but as foundational infrastructure for applications that make a basic human need dramatically cheaper — AI healthcare systems using Interfaze for medical document processing at 1/10th the cost, or educational platforms using it for universal content accessibility. Evidence of that downstream consequence would change my calculus entirely. Right now, Interfaze is a well-engineered developer tool. The technical approach is its strongest dimension — genuine architectural innovation, peer-reviewed, with real systems complexity. But the consequence magnitude and ambition level are what I weight most heavily, and both point to the same conclusion: Interfaze is designed to build a successful company, not to change the world. The gap between those two things is where my decision lives.
