# Rhizome AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Rhizome AI: this company is making an opaque, Byzantine system visible. The FDA regulatory landscape is one of those invisible power structures that determines which drugs reach patients and which don't, and the knowledge required to navigate it has been locked inside expensive legacy databases and the heads of a small priesthood of regulatory consultants. That pattern -- taking something hidden and making it transparent -- is one I keep recognizing in my own portfolio. Carta did it for cap tables. Affirm did it for credit costs. Flock Safety did it for public safety data. Rhizome is doing it for regulatory intelligence, and the $400/month entry price means a small biotech startup can access the same regulatory knowledge that used to require a Clarivate subscription and a team of specialists. That resonates with me on a fundamental level. When a regulatory affairs manager tells you "I would cry if I lost access," that's real product love, not polished testimonial language.

But I have to be honest about what I don't see here, and my process starts with the founder's story -- not the product. Chetan Mishra has an impressive professional trajectory: IBM Research, employee number sixteen at Instabase where he closed $7 million as a technical lead, founding product engineer at EvolutionaryScale where he co-authored the ESM3 protein language model paper in Science. That's a genuinely strong technical career. What I can't find is the biographical thread that explains why *this* person, *this* problem. How did he arrive at regulatory affairs? The dossier suggests he noticed a gap while working at EvolutionaryScale inside the life sciences AI ecosystem. That's a professional observation, and it's legitimate founder-market fit in the way most VCs evaluate it. But it's not the kind of story I build conviction around. I'm listening for the hardship that forged the commitment -- the thing that means this founder won't quit when the enterprise sales cycle stretches to eighteen months and Clarivate starts bolting AI onto their existing platform. I'm not saying it doesn't exist. I just can't see it from here, and that's where my conviction has to start.

The other thing I notice is that this isn't a weird company. It's a smart company. AI applied to document-heavy regulatory workflows is exactly the kind of opportunity that every enterprise-focused fund understands immediately. That's not how my best investments have worked. SpaceX was dismissed as insane. Crusoe Energy was mining Bitcoin on gas flares. Contraline was building male contraceptive gel. Those companies looked bad to most investors at the time I wrote my check. Rhizome AI looks *good* to most investors -- it's a clear RAG application over a specialized corpus with verifiable citations, serving an enterprise market with obvious pain. That clarity makes it a reasonable bet for someone else, but it means the non-consensus premium I look for isn't really there. The competitive landscape confirms this: Sorcero has raised $59 million in adjacent territory, incumbents are adding AI capabilities, and this is becoming a recognized category, not a contrarian one.

Now, the bull case is real, and I want to give it full weight. A solo founder generating six-figure revenue in his first year with 20+ paying customers in *pharma* -- one of the hardest enterprise sales environments on earth -- that's extraordinary execution. The 2.5 terabyte normalized corpus across 30 datasets and 6 markets is meaningful infrastructure that took real engineering effort to build. The zero-hallucination claim matters enormously in a regulated context where bad guidance could sink a drug submission. And the regulatory moat itself is something I'm drawn to -- my IronPort years taught me that complexity others avoid creates defensibility for those who embrace it. If Chetan can build a small team around him and survive the inevitable incumbent response, this company could become the intelligence layer that every regulatory affairs team depends on. The customer testimonials suggest that future is already starting to emerge.

What holds me back is that this company asks me to invest on execution quality in a comprehensible market rather than on founder conviction in an incomprehensible one. Those are two different kinds of bets, and I've learned that my edge is in the second kind. The solo founder question also weighs on me -- not because of co-founder conflict risk (there is none, which actually removes my biggest red flag), but because building enterprise sales into pharma companies as a team of one requires superhuman capacity. He's not hiring, and I don't see how you serve 20+ regulated-industry customers, maintain a data pipeline across six global markets, and run a sales motion simultaneously without something breaking. When Crusoe Energy hit crisis, we could send Jonathan in full-time. Here, there's no team to absorb the shock.

This is a good company building a real product in a hard space, and someone should fund it. The transparency-and-access pattern hits something genuine in my thesis. But my conviction comes from founders whose biography and mission are inseparable, building things that look ridiculous to everyone else. Rhizome AI looks sensible to everyone else, and the founder's connection to the problem, while professional, doesn't give me that visceral pull I felt with Crusoe or Niantic. I'd want to meet Chetan and hear the story behind the story before moving forward.
