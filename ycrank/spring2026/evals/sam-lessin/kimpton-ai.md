# Kimpton AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the fundamental problem with Kimpton AI: the idea isn't insane. "Build an AI-powered Bloomberg Terminal for the rest of us" is the single most consensus pitch in fintech right now. The dossier lists five funded competitors — AlphaSense at $4 billion, Reflexivity backed by Druckenmiller, Composer, Mezzi, FinChat — and those are just the ones big enough to show up in a search. Every accelerator cohort in 2025 and 2026 has some version of this. When I run my sane-person-insane-idea filter, I get an uncomfortable configuration: credible founders chasing an idea that every other VC in San Francisco is already excited about. That's a sane person with a sane idea, and it means the returns are already getting priced in at the formation stage.

The founders themselves are genuinely credible — I don't want to undersell that. Jack Zumwalt and Mauricio Ortiz overlapped at Goldman Sachs, left to build Level III Capital as a quant fund, and spent four years building the trading infrastructure that became Kimpton. Adrian Del Bosque brings SpaceX and cloud engineering depth. The co-founder relationship is real — forged in the trenches of running actual trading operations, not at a co-founder matching event. The product originated as internal production infrastructure at their own fund, which means they were their own first users and the tool has been battle-tested against real markets. That's operator credibility I respect. But operator credibility in service of a consensus idea is how you get a competitive knife fight, not a venture-scale outcome.

My bigger concern is structural, and it's the one that dominates my thinking about everything right now: this business fails the "cherry on top" test completely. Strip away the AI and there is no business. The AI doesn't amplify an existing operation — it IS the operation. That's the exact category I've been arguing is uninvestable for startups. Apple, Google, and every major brokerage platform are better positioned to ship agentic financial research features than a five-person startup, because the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution, and Robinhood has 23 million users. When I praised OnlyFans, it was because the business was a killer financial machine independent of AI, and AI was a non-obvious accelerant. Kimpton is the inverse — a product that only exists because of AI and will get absorbed when platforms with distribution build the same capabilities natively, which Interactive Brokers and Robinhood are already doing.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: Cursor proved that an AI-native IDE can build real market position even against incumbents, because developers form deep workflows around their primary tool. If Kimpton becomes where investors live — where their portfolio data, research history, watchlists, and analytical context accumulate — switching costs compound and the product becomes sticky enough to defend. The Level III Capital origin gives a genuine head start over teams building from scratch, and the 27-tool orchestration layer plus sandboxed Python execution represents real technical complexity that's harder to replicate than a simple chat wrapper. Maybe portfolio-aware personalization creates a data flywheel where recommendations improve with usage. If all of that is true, this could be a real business. But I don't buy it, because Cursor's analogy breaks down on a critical dimension: developers spend eight-plus hours a day in their IDE, making it the gravitational center of their work. Most investors — even active ones — check their portfolio episodically. Bloomberg's real moat isn't the terminal software, it's the chat network and the fact that traders live in it all day as social infrastructure. A $200/month prosumer tool doesn't generate that kind of habitual lock-in, and the Plaid integration that enables brokerage connectivity is a dependency on someone else's primitive, not your own.

The protocol-layer test is where this falls hardest. Kimpton is building an application on top of other people's infrastructure — LLMs from OpenAI or Anthropic, brokerage data from Plaid, market data from existing vendors. It creates no new primitive that other businesses build on. There's no ecosystem effect where Kimpton's success makes adjacent products better. Compare this to Venmo, which established a new payment primitive, or Aleo, which created zero-knowledge privacy infrastructure. Kimpton is a very capable application sitting on layers it doesn't control, and that caps the ultimate outcome even in the best case. The "Cursor for investing" tagline is aspirational, but Cursor at least has extension ecosystems and language server integrations that create some platform dynamics. I don't see the equivalent here.

I respect these founders and I think they'll build a product people use. The quant fund background is real, the co-founder chemistry is real, and the technical execution looks solid. But this is a subscription software product in the hottest AI category, dependent on foundation models it doesn't own, competing with well-funded startups and massive incumbents, with no structural moat that survives the next two years of AI capability improvements. It's the kind of company I'd have invested in before I learned the Birchbox lesson — narrative magnitude without structural defensibility. I'm passing.
