# Foaster -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what Foaster calls itself: "AI-native consulting firm." That self-description tells me almost everything. A consulting firm -- even one with exceptional AI tooling -- has consulting economics. Engagements are scoped by humans, delivered to specific clients, and priced per project. The AI accelerates the data collection and synthesis, but the business model is fundamentally the same one McKinsey has run for a century: sell expertise on an engagement basis, staff up to grow. When I evaluate whether a company is software-driven or software-aware, the test is whether removing the software breaks the competitive model or just slows it down. If you replaced Foaster's AI interview agents with a team of twenty junior analysts conducting the same interviews over three weeks, you'd get roughly the same output at higher cost. That's a smart toaster -- faster and cheaper, but still a toaster. The potential escape hatch is the persistent operational map, which could become a platform asset rather than a consulting deliverable. But that transition itself requires a miracle, and there's no evidence the founders are building toward it yet.

The market structural moment here is real but entirely consensus. "AI disrupts management consulting" is one of the five most discussed AI application theses in venture right now. HBR published pieces on consulting firms cutting graduate hiring 6-30% as AI automates analyst work. Every investor I know is looking at this category. When the structural moment is this visible, the returns get competed away -- which is exactly what the funding landscape confirms. Distyl AI has raised $202M. Aily Labs has $101M and $28.6M ARR. PromptQL sits on $136M+ with seven-figure deals. Skan.ai has $57.6M and $22.2M in revenue. When I backed PagerDuty, nobody else wanted to fund operations alerting -- that's why the returns were asymmetric. When I look at AI consulting in 2026, I see a market where five well-capitalized competitors are already building overlapping products. The "why now" is obvious to the entire market simultaneously, which means timing isn't an edge -- it's table stakes.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. The innovator's dilemma for McKinsey, BCG, and Bain is structurally real -- they cannot offer 3-5x cheaper delivery without cannibalizing the leverage model that funds their partnerships. That's a genuine opening for a new entrant. The operational map, if it becomes a persistent, queryable asset that clients update continuously rather than rebuild per engagement, could create switching costs analogous to how Gusto turned payroll into embedded HR infrastructure. Combes built a system handling 60,000+ AI calls at Repondia, which maps directly to Foaster's core mechanism. And the Werewolf benchmark work -- 300K+ views, shared by OpenAI's president, noticed by xAI's co-founder -- demonstrates genuine depth in designing and evaluating AI agent interactions. If Foaster executes the transition from consulting-with-AI to platform-as-operational-intelligence, the upside case becomes compelling. But that's two miracles: first, get enterprises to trust AI agents interviewing their employees about sensitive operational dynamics (a hard sales and compliance challenge), and second, convert engagement-based revenue into a persistent platform model. Two miracles compound low-probability events.

The founders are young -- both ~23, still completing degrees at ENSAE Paris -- which is not inherently disqualifying but compounds the enterprise sales challenge. Selling AI-native consulting to senior executives requires trust-building that typically depends on industry credibility. Combes's Repondia experience is the strongest execution signal, but it has no independent web presence, press coverage, or verifiable traction data. For the most directly relevant prior experience in the pitch, that absence of verification matters. Dabadie's community-building trajectory -- 245K followers at age 11, 400K+ users across tennis communities, recruited as an agent for Nadal's Academy at 18 -- shows unusual hustle and sales instinct, which is genuinely relevant for a founder-led consulting GTM. But there are no public traction signals for Foaster itself: no revenue, no named customers, no LOIs, no waitlist data. At pre-seed, absence of traction isn't penalizing, but the presence of even one enterprise pilot would have meaningfully changed my assessment.

The technology moat concern is real. LLM-based interview agents, data synthesis, and recommendation generation are built on generally available foundation models. Any of the well-funded competitors could add an interview-based collection layer. Dialogue AI already conducts AI interviews at scale and would only need to pivot from external to internal interviews to compete directly. The accumulated methodology and prompt engineering for organizational analysis create some know-how barrier, but it's thin compared to the regulatory moats I typically look for -- the kind of structural barriers that made Coinbase or Anduril defensible. There's no CLIA certification, no ITAR compliance, no financial licensing creating a multi-year barrier to entry. The moat here, if one develops, would come from the proprietary operational maps and client lock-in -- but that requires the platform transition that hasn't been articulated.

This is a competent team in a real market with correct timing -- but the market is consensus, the technology is reproducible, the business model skews services over software, and the plan implicitly requires multiple breakthroughs. I pass.
