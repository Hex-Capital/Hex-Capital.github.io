# Compresr -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Compresr is the deflation problem at the center of the business. They're building a cost-optimization layer for LLM tokens, and LLM token prices are dropping roughly 10x per year. That's like building a better long-distance phone compression system in 1999. The product works. The engineering is real. But the underlying unit you're optimizing is racing toward zero faster than almost anything in the history of technology. The founders seem to recognize this -- they cite an accuracy improvement (72.3% to 74.5% on FinanceBench) alongside the cost savings. But the company's positioning, website copy, and value proposition all lead with "up to 200x compression" and "76% cost savings." If cost savings is the hook, and the cost is evaporating, you have a shrinking market for your product. That's not necessarily fatal -- the accuracy angle could become the real business -- but it means the company needs to evolve its thesis, and I don't see evidence they've started that evolution yet.

The origin of this idea is academic, not organic. Ivan Zakazov is a PhD student at EPFL's Data Science Lab researching LLM context compression. He wrote a paper benchmarking 25 models. Then the team productized the paper. I've seen this pattern many times -- researcher discovers something technically interesting, sees a startup opportunity, builds it. Sometimes it works (Google was a Stanford research project). But the strongest founder-problem connections I've seen come from practitioners who hit the problem while building something else entirely. Drew Houston didn't research file synchronization algorithms -- he forgot his USB drive on a bus. The Collisons didn't study payment infrastructure -- they were building web apps and hated integrating payments. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the answer here is "it was their dissertation topic." That's not the worst answer, but it means the motivation is intellectual rather than economic necessity, and intellectual motivation tends to be more fragile when the company hits the hard, boring parts of building a business.

The structural argument that LLM providers won't build compression because it cannibalizes their token revenue -- this is the kind of insight that sounds brilliant in a pitch and falls apart on closer examination. Companies cannibalize themselves all the time when competition forces their hand. Google's ADK already documents context compaction features. Anthropic and OpenAI compete ferociously on price. If one of them offers native compression as a differentiator, the others will follow within months, and a third-party compression API becomes redundant. The dossier itself notes this risk without mitigation. Meanwhile, Microsoft Research has LLMLingua freely available, already integrated into LlamaIndex and Azure. When your main competitor is an open-source tool backed by Microsoft's research budget, your moat had better be something more than "our compression ratios are higher on one benchmark."

Here's the bull case I want to take seriously: if agentic AI workloads grow as projected -- 74% of startups running majority inference workloads -- then context management becomes essential infrastructure. Not just for cost, but for quality. Agents that accumulate conversation history, tool outputs, and retrieved documents need intelligent context curation to avoid degraded accuracy. The Compresr team has genuine technical depth: a peer-reviewed paper, a proprietary post-trained model, and they've shipped real product (SDK at pip install, open-source gateway at v0.4.4, working API). If the accuracy benefit proves to be the durable value proposition -- if compression makes agents genuinely smarter, not just cheaper -- then this could become the caching layer that sits between every agent and every LLM. Four EPFL ML engineers who've published together and already shipped product is a real team. Ivan's prior startup failure with Iterate shows willingness to try again. These are smart, capable people working on a real technical problem.

But the signals I look for aren't here. No evidence of users pulling the product toward them -- 117 GitHub stars after YC is modest, and there's no revenue data, no user testimonials, no organic adoption stories. The category is crowded enough that I can name four competitors from the dossier alone. The problem is visible to everyone building with LLMs, which means it's not hiding in plain sight due to some schlep that scares people off -- the schlep here is "train better models," which is exactly what every ML team in the world is set up to do. The co-founders all met at EPFL, which passes my co-founder relationship test, but the team's public footprint is thin (Ivan's GitHub has 24 total stars across his personal repos; two other founders have no confirmed public GitHub). For a developer tools company selling to engineers, your own engineering output is your resume. I want to see hackers who can't stop building, and the evidence here is more "researchers who published" than "builders who shipped obsessively."

This is a competent team with real ML expertise working in a space where the problem is genuine but the market dynamics work against them. Token deflation shrinks the cost-savings pitch. Platform-native compression threatens the third-party model. Open-source alternatives provide a free baseline. And the origin is academic rather than organic. I'd want to see evidence that developers are reaching for this tool on their own -- that the compression actually changes how they build agents, not just what they spend -- before I'd put my own money in.
