# Rubric AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that arrests my attention, because it tells you something about the nature of the bet. Scale AI is a $29B company. Surge AI is doing $1.2B in revenue bootstrapped. Mercor just raised at $10B. Labelbox -- which has already shipped "rubric evaluation" tooling that overlaps directly with this company's name and stated focus -- sits at $1B. Rubric AI is a two-person team walking into a room where the incumbents have, collectively, billions in revenue and tens of billions in enterprise value. That's not automatically disqualifying -- Uber was illegal in most cities when I invested, and every taxi lobby in America was the incumbent. But Uber had a technology-enabled leverage mechanism that fundamentally restructured the unit economics of transportation. The question for Rubric AI is: what is the equivalent structural advantage? I don't see one in the dossier.

The specific knowledge test is where this falls apart for me. Pragya Saboo is clearly a capable product manager -- shipping Time Tracking at Asana from inception demonstrates she can scope and execute. The Climate Capital principal role shows she understands venture dynamics. But "post-training research and product lab" is a positioning that demands a very particular kind of knowledge: the kind you get from spending years inside a training pipeline, understanding at a visceral level how reward signals shape model behavior, where RLHF breaks down, what makes an evaluation rubric produce meaningful gradient signal versus noise. That knowledge lives in maybe a few hundred people globally -- researchers at Anthropic, DeepMind, OpenAI who've spent years watching models respond to different training methodologies. Product management at a productivity tool and health insurance company doesn't produce that knowledge. It's not adjacent; it's a different domain entirely. The unnamed second team member is a genuine unknown -- if they're a former frontier lab researcher with deep RLHF expertise, the picture changes materially. But I can't invest on the hypothesis that an invisible co-founder fills the gap. The absence of a named technical co-founder triggers one of my hardest red flags: "If you don't have a builder or product to evaluate, how are you supposed to invest?"

The leverage architecture concerns me almost as much. Selling evaluation services to fewer than twenty frontier labs globally is not leverage -- it's enterprise consulting. Each lab has a unique training pipeline, unique model architectures, unique security requirements. Customizing evaluation rubrics and training environments for each customer sounds like proportional effort per contract. Software tooling can create leverage, but the "research and product lab" framing suggests a significant services component. Compare this to my infrastructure investments: Stack Overflow built a developer knowledge graph where each answer made the platform more valuable for every subsequent user. Alchemy built a blockchain API layer where one engineer's work served thousands of dApp developers. Rubric AI, as described, builds bespoke evaluation frameworks for a handful of very large, very sophisticated buyers. That's a consulting business with a software wrapper -- not a leverage engine.

The strongest bull case requires taking seriously that Meta's $14.3B acquisition of 49% of Scale AI genuinely fragmented the market. If Google, OpenAI, and xAI are pulling sensitive training data away from Scale over data-sharing concerns, there's a real window for a trusted, independent evaluation partner. A small, focused team could become the go-to for a lab that doesn't want its proprietary training methodology flowing through a Meta-affiliated vendor. And the paradigm shift from static benchmarks to dynamic rubric-based evaluation is real -- academic papers show 13% performance gains from rubric-based reward signals in domains where frontier models still fail 22-30% of the time. If Rubric AI can build proprietary evaluation methodology that demonstrably improves model performance better than what labs can build internally, the concentrated customer base becomes an advantage: you only need three contracts to build a meaningful business. But this bull case requires the unnamed second team member to be exceptional at ML research, the evaluation methodology to be genuinely novel rather than standard RLHF practice, and the team to win trust with frontier labs despite having no public track record in the space. That's three simultaneous unknowns resolving favorably.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. "The era of evals" is literally a competitor's blog post title. Everyone in AI infrastructure agrees that evaluation is important and underinvested. When everyone agrees something is a good idea, the pricing has already adjusted. I invest in things smart people think are wrong -- Twitter when microblogging seemed trivial, Bitcoin when it was dismissed as digital toy money. "Evals are important" is not a contrarian thesis; it's a slide in every AI infrastructure pitch deck I've seen in the last eighteen months.

I'm passing. The founder's publicly documented knowledge doesn't match the domain. The leverage mechanics look linear, not exponential. The competitive landscape is brutal. And the timing is peak consensus, not early contrarian. Any one of these I could overcome -- I invested in Opendoor despite capital-intensity because the founders had extraordinary specific knowledge. But all four together, with an unidentified co-founder as the load-bearing assumption? That's not an asymmetric bet. That's hope.
