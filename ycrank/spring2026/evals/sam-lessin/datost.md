# Datost -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Datost is building exactly the kind of company I tell founders not to build. An AI data analyst inside Slack -- the entire value proposition is that an LLM generates and executes SQL for you in a chat window. Strip away the AI, and there is no business. There is no painting crew, no wireless hardware, no employee ownership structure, no physical-world operation that software amplifies. The software IS the business. And I've said this publicly enough times that I shouldn't need to repeat it: when the software is the business, AI doesn't create your moat -- it destroys it. The dossier itself acknowledges the core capability is reproducible by "any Slack bot developer." That's not a competitive analysis; that's an obituary.

The founder-idea pairing fails my primary filter in both directions. Maceo and Jason are two 21-year-old software engineers whose deepest experience is building production code at Traba, a staffing company. They're technically capable -- I don't doubt that. But there's nothing in their backgrounds that gives them unusual insight into how enterprise data teams operate, how BI procurement works, or what breaks when a non-technical marketing VP tries to self-serve on a data question. That's the "sane" half missing. The "insane" half is worse: this idea isn't remotely insane. It's the most consensus application of LLMs to enterprise software I can imagine. Microsoft shipped Copilot in Power BI. Salesforce has Einstein in Tableau. Google has Looker AI. When I look for ideas that provoke genuine skepticism from smart people, "AI answers your data questions" provokes the opposite -- everyone nods along, which means the opportunity is already priced in and the competition is already here.

The platform dependency is what makes this particularly unattractive through my lens. Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. The product Datost is building -- an AI data analyst that lives in Slack and connects to your databases -- is the single most obvious product integration Salesforce should ship. You're building a feature for a company that owns both the distribution channel and the adjacent analytics product. This is worse than typical platform risk; it's building in the exact gap that the platform owner is structurally incentivized to close. I think about this the way I think about anyone who built a Facebook feature as a standalone app in 2012 -- you're one product review away from obsolescence, and the platform owner doesn't even need to acquire you because the capability is trivially reproducible.

The strongest bull case I can construct goes something like this: conversational data access becomes the new primitive for how organizations interact with information, and Datost's accumulated context -- the organizational knowledge graph built from ingesting docs, Slack history, databases, and code -- creates genuine switching costs that make it sticky even when incumbents ship competing features. Salesforce is famously terrible at internal product integration, so the Slack-Tableau unification could take years, giving Datost a window to entrench. If the "own computer" execution environment achieves meaningfully higher reliability than prompt-to-SQL competitors, enterprise customers might pay a premium for accuracy on production data. In this scenario, Datost looks less like a SaaS tool and more like an Airtable-style exception -- a product that appears to be software but is actually establishing a new interaction primitive. I'd need to see that the execution environment produces dramatically better results than competitors (not incrementally better), that enterprise customers are already trusting it with production data (not just demos), and that the founders have discovered something non-obvious about how organizations actually use data that the Tableaus and Power BIs of the world are missing. None of that evidence exists in the dossier.

The narrative ceiling is theoretically large -- "democratize data" could stretch into "conversational interface to all enterprise systems" -- but it's also the same narrative every BI company has used for two decades. When your tagline could be swapped onto fifteen competitors without anyone noticing, you don't have a narrative; you have a category description. There's no revenue, no public traction of any kind, and a single GitHub repo with zero stars. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of scale, but I do look for any signal of real demand -- a waitlist, a design partner, a paying pilot. Nothing here provides even a minimal downside cushion.

I keep coming back to the test I wish I'd applied to Birchbox: would this business be defensible without the software layer? With Datost, there is no layer beneath the software. There are no crews to dispatch, no hardware to install, no regulatory positions to hold. Every dollar of value creation depends on an LLM capability that gets cheaper and more accessible every quarter -- for Datost AND for every competitor simultaneously. AI doesn't make this company stronger as software gets cheaper; it makes the competition infinite. This is a clear pass.
