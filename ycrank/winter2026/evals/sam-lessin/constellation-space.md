# Constellation Space -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The founder credential here is legitimately compelling -- Kamran Majid literally built the Starlink ground network optimization tools at SpaceX, and Raaid Kabir wrote embedded flight software on New Glenn at Blue Origin. This is the pattern I love: infrastructure engineers who've shipped at the companies defining the frontier, then leave to build the vendor-neutral version of what they built proprietary. It's exactly how the Mysten Labs team emerged from Meta's crypto division to build Sui. The "sane" side of the ledger is strong. But when I ask myself the second half of the question -- where's the insane part? -- I struggle. "Satellite constellations are scaling massively and need better predictive monitoring" is not an insane idea. It's correct. It's consensus. Every space industry analyst will tell you the same thing. Nobody is going to hear this pitch and say "you're crazy." They're going to say "that makes sense, can you actually build it?" And that's a fundamentally different bet -- an execution bet in a consensus market, not a conviction bet on something non-obvious.

Here's where my AI skepticism gets activated in a specific way. ConstellationOS isn't an LLM wrapper -- the physics-informed neural network approach using ITU atmospheric standards, orbital propagation models, and space weather data is genuinely domain-specific work. I respect the engineering. But the product IS AI. The value proposition IS prediction. And my core question remains: does this business get stronger or weaker as AI tooling gets cheaper and more accessible? Kratos is a $1.3 billion revenue company with deep satellite ground infrastructure. Lockheed Martin has Horizon C2. As foundation models for scientific computing improve -- and they will -- what stops these incumbents from bolting on a prediction layer to their existing hardware-integrated, customer-locked platforms? ITU standards are public documents. Orbital mechanics aren't proprietary. The domain knowledge barrier is real today, but it's a knowledge barrier, not a structural one, and those erode. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is physical painting crews and operational logistics that no amount of AI improvement can replicate from a laptop. Constellation Space's moat ultimately lives in software, and I've spent the last two years arguing that software moats are dissolving.

The federated learning architecture is the most interesting structural element -- the idea that each constellation trains local models on its own data without sharing raw telemetry, creating cross-orbit transfer learning that improves for all participants. If that works at scale, it's genuinely a primitive-like dynamic: an intelligence layer that other operators plug into, where each additional participant makes the network more valuable for everyone. That's the path to a protocol position rather than just an application. But the math troubles me. How many meaningful constellation operators exist globally? Maybe fifteen to twenty. Federated learning network effects need density to compound. Helium needed millions of hotspot deployments for its decentralized wireless protocol to achieve critical mass. Constellation Space needs maybe a dozen operators to cover the market, and each one is a twelve-to-twenty-four month enterprise sale with defense procurement complexity. The network effect could work, but it'll be slow to manifest and the participant count may never be large enough for the compounding dynamics I look for in protocol-layer bets.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: someone is going to build the intelligence layer for the satellite communications industry as it scales from ten thousand to seventy thousand nodes. The founders who built the proprietary version at SpaceX and Blue Origin are among the most credible people to build the universal version. The $2.5 billion per year in network failure costs is a quantifiable pain point. And the multi-operator federated learning approach is a genuinely differentiated architecture that neither Cognitive Space (focused on tasking) nor Quindar (focused on ground systems) is pursuing. If ConstellationOS becomes the standard routing intelligence layer that all constellation operators depend on, this is infrastructure -- not an application. The universe where this works is one where the vendor-neutral, cloud-native approach wins over incumbent proprietary stacks, similar to how AWS won over on-premise. That's a real possibility.

What keeps me from leaning in harder is the structural positioning. Two of the four founders -- Omeed and Laith -- come primarily from Capital One, not space. All four overlapped at Capital One, which is good for co-founder cohesion but doesn't deepen the domain bench beyond the SpaceX and Blue Origin credentials of the other two. There's no revenue, no LOIs (contrast with competitor Apolink's $140 million in signed LOIs at their seed), no named customers. The design partner phase is the right place to be at pre-seed, but there's nothing providing a downside cushion if the narrative stalls. And the narrative itself, while large, is bounded by the satellite operations market -- this isn't an "infinity story" that could plausibly encompass an entire economic category. It's a critical tool for a specific and growing but finite industry.

I respect the engineering and the team's relevant experience, but the founder-idea pairing doesn't produce the non-consensus signal I need for my highest conviction. This is a sane person with a sane idea -- which, in my framework, means the market has already priced the opportunity and the returns will accrue to whoever executes best in a competitive field, not to whoever had the structural insight earliest.
