# Return Signals -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I'd want to ask Ilya Valmianski is how someone who built one of the largest patient-facing machine learning applications at Kaiser Permanente — helping real patients triage real medical symptoms across all of Southern California — ended up sending AI text messages to ask people if their new jeans fit. That's not a dismissive question. It's the most important question. Because the biographical thread between the founder and the problem is where I start, always. Ilya clearly has the technical chops — a PhD in physics, deployed conversational AI at massive scale in healthcare, took a prior startup to $40M ARR. That's a real builder. But I don't see the lived experience connection to e-commerce returns. When I backed the Contraline founder, his years of dedicated research in male contraception told me everything about whether he'd persist through the inevitable hard moments. Here, I see a skilled engineer who identified a market opportunity and applied existing capabilities to it. That's a fine reason to start a company. It's not the reason I write checks.

The honest truth about Return Signals is that "AI-powered customer engagement for e-commerce" is about as popular a category as exists right now. Every investor in the Valley is looking at AI applied to SaaS workflows. The claimed metrics — 61-80% response rates, 70% return-to-exchange conversion — are genuinely impressive numbers if they hold at scale. And the timing angle is real: LLMs crossed the quality threshold for natural SMS conversations, and Emotive getting absorbed into Privy in July 2025 removed the most direct standalone competitor. But this is exactly the kind of deal where consensus forms fast. Attentive is at $500M ARR. Klaviyo is at $1.2 billion. Postscript has $117 million in funding. Any of them could build a "post-delivery conversational check-in" feature in a quarter. The defensibility pitch — proprietary sizing data and return-reason patterns — doesn't exist yet at pre-seed, and the core technology is LLM API calls that any competent team can replicate. I've learned to trust my instinct when something feels like a feature rather than a company, and this triggers that instinct.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I take that seriously. If Ilya can prove the ROI attribution — that his SMS conversations actually caused customers to keep items they would have returned — that's a powerful wedge into a massive pain point. Apparel return rates run 20-30%, and every percentage point matters to brands operating on thin margins. The 5-10% absolute margin uplift claim, if validated with named enterprise customers, would make this a must-have. And Ilya's specific background in medical conversational AI, where the stakes of getting conversations wrong are literally life-and-death, means he's probably overqualified for the technical challenge here. If the data flywheel kicks in — learning brand-specific sizing curves, quality patterns, exchange preferences — there could be real switching costs over time. The path to a strong business exists. But it's a path that requires surviving incumbent attention in a space they're already watching closely.

What I don't find here is the economic access story that pulls me in. Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Postmates opened up delivery work. Affirm gave transparent credit to people locked out of traditional finance. Return Signals helps brands protect their margins. That's legitimate business value, but it's not the kind of positive-sum outcome that makes me feel like capitalism is doing its best work. The customer on the other end of that SMS isn't gaining economic access — they're being persuaded to keep a purchase they might genuinely want to return. I can see how good post-purchase engagement creates a better experience, but I struggle to locate the broader impact that I need to feel before writing a check from my own money.

The co-founder dynamic is the other piece I can't evaluate from the outside. Ilya and Alejandro come from completely different worlds — healthcare AI and proptech business development — with no visible shared history. That's not inherently bad. Complementary skills matter. But I learned the hard way with HQ Trivia that you need to understand how co-founders fight, not just how they divide labor. Two people who met to start a company together haven't been tested yet. I'd need a conversation with both of them in the same room to get a read on this.

This is a competent team building a reasonable product in a real market. That sentence is exactly why I'm passing. I'm looking for the unreasonable, the weird, the thing that makes other investors squirm. Return Signals doesn't make anyone uncomfortable — it makes them nod and say "that makes sense." In my experience, the investments where everyone nods are the ones where the returns are already priced in.
