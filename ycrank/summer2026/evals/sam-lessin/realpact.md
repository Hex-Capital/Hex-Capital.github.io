# RealPact -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

RealPact is the canonical Pass Example I wrote into the persona file, almost word-for-word: "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], a few months out of YC, Dartmouth CS undergrads, $500K from YC plus LeapYear, four design-partner brokerages." The dimensional signature is a high-execution founder pair (Ranvir Deshmukh and Erik Peterson shipped CourseMe to 90% of Dartmouth) crossed with the lowest-conviction category on my board (vertical AI agents for real estate transaction management) — which makes RealPact a capable builder team pointed at the exact YC S26 sleeve I de-rate by construction.

**Solana at $0.05/coin** is the high rung on price/non-consensus, and the reason it loads here is that nobody else was bidding the category — RealPact is the opposite. The dossier puts RealPact inside the MarketScale "YC 2026 RE-and-construction cohort bets big on AI agents" roundup, which is shorthand for "this is the batch theme this season." There is no tier-1 pass named in the dossier, no pricing reset, no strategic ambiguity — it's a $500K YC SAFE plus LeapYear Fund pre-seed at presumed standard YC terms, into a vertical Andreessen, Bain, and Greylock will all chase at Series A if the logo count doubles. I cannot generate alpha from this SAFE cap; I'm paying retail for an asset the market has already named.

**Teamshares** is what venture-shaped-but-non-standard actually looks like — an employee-ownership rollup of small businesses, an instrument no other generalist fund could underwrite, and the reason RealPact's structure registers as failure mode is the absence of any such wrinkle. RealPact reads as per-seat brokerage SaaS (no pricing page, "OS for Real Estate Brokerages" framing on the YC page) on a standard equity cap, which means five more rounds to default-alive against Dotloop and SkySlope. The interesting structural move RealPact does *not* make — and I'd be in the meeting if they did — is taking equity in the brokerages themselves, or a revenue share on closed transactions instead of a per-seat license. The Sotheby's franchise customer is the kind of counterparty where a deal-fee bps instrument would actually price the asset; RealPact instead is selling them a $X/seat/month subscription.

Counterfactual on conviction: for Dimension 3 to flip from "pass" to "neutral," I'd need Ranvir Deshmukh to have published one essay — newsletter post, Twitter thread, anything — laying out a non-obvious belief about why Dotloop (a Zillow Group subsidiary handling ~50% of US transaction management) structurally cannot ship deed-parsing AI on top of its own pipes. The dossier shows 222 Twitter followers on @ranvirdeshmukh_, 22 GitHub followers, no real estate operating background, no Erik Peterson Twitter account at all, and CourseMe — a Dartmouth course advisor — as the only prior shipping evidence. RealPact's founders are clearly capable builders (Magnuson $20K, CourseMe acquired, 4 brokerage customers signed pre-YC-Demo-Day is real), but capable-builder is not the same signal as missionary-on-this-problem, and the dossier contains zero artifacts of pre-pitch obsession with brokerage workflow.

Mechanism analysis on owned distribution: the path from 4 NH brokerages to category default in transaction management compounds through reference selling brokerage-to-brokerage, and that compounding requires either a named-founder distribution channel (RealPact has none in real estate) or a structural wedge incumbents won't copy (RealPact's wedge is auto-filling contracts from county registry parsing, which Dotloop can ship in two quarters on top of existing distribution). Eighteen months out, RealPact is either at ~$1-3M ARR with NH/New-England concentration and a Dotloop AI-autofill announcement compressing its differentiation, or it has eaten the cost of expanding the county-registry parser to a second state and discovered that each new state's deed/tax format reset is closer to "ground-up rebuild" than "config file."

Single-anchor parallel on category sleeve: **Sublime Security** is the recent enterprise-security check I made because the category was pre-consolidation and the larger SOC SaaS funds had ignored it; RealPact sits on the opposite side of that comparison — real estate transaction management has a $2-11B market with Dotloop already at ~50% share and SkySlope serving 400,000+ professionals, which is the textbook definition of post-consolidation. "AI is just more internet" applies cleanly: layering LLM autofill on a category Zillow already owns the rails for is consensus exposure dressed as a YC seed.

36-month forecast: RealPact reaches $1.5-4M ARR across NH and one or two adjacent New England states, Dotloop or Lone Wolf ships competing AI auto-fill on top of existing distribution within 18 months, and RealPact gets acqui-hired by SkySlope, Lone Wolf, or a Compass-tier brokerage for sub-$40M — or pivots to a narrower title/escrow workflow product that doesn't directly compete with Dotloop. The mechanism is straightforward: county-registry parsing is a logistical schlep, not a regulatory moat, and a logistical schlep against an incumbent with national distribution resolves the same way every time.

The strongest bull case here is the Ro override — Ro was a high-burn DTC pharma rollup that violated capital-efficiency because regulatory complexity across 50-state prescription rails made capital intensity itself the moat, and a reasonable person could argue that RealPact's per-state contract-form-and-county-registry expansion is the same shape of schlep, and that getting to default-alive across 10 states is genuinely worth $500K of YC money. I don't buy it for RealPact: Ro's schlep was prescription rails where the licensing barrier itself excluded software competitors, whereas RealPact's schlep is data-pipeline engineering Dotloop and SkySlope can fund out of existing ARR the moment RealPact's logo count hits 50. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is RealPact disclosing a non-standard instrument — a transaction-fee bps share with brokerage customers, broker-equity, or a holdco model rolling up small NH brokerages a la Teamshares — at which point this becomes a different asset class and I'd take the meeting.
