# Sherpa -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Optimizely does $400M in ARR. VWO just sold for $200M. Mutiny raised $72.6M. The CRO market is big, validated, and crowded — and every one of these incumbents has an AI roadmap. When I see Sherpa entering this space with "AI that automates A/B testing and increases conversions," my first reaction is: this idea sounds good to everyone. And ideas that sound good to everyone have already had their returns competed away. Nobody hears "AI-powered conversion optimization" and says "that's crazy, it'll never work." They say "obviously." That's the tell. The sweet spot is the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Sherpa sits firmly in the "seems like a good idea" and "might be a good idea" quadrant — which is where returns go to die.

Ethan Kinnan and Norbu Sonam both worked at Lindy, an AI agent platform, where Ethan handled partnerships and Norbu built onboarding optimization features. Sherpa's origin story is two people who got good at building AI agents and then looked for a problem to point them at. That's the wrong direction. The way to get startup ideas is not to take your skills and search for applications — it's to notice problems so painful you can't ignore them. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, he didn't sit down and think "what market could I apply file synchronization to?" He just built the thing he needed. Sherpa's founders have real AI engineering experience — Norbu's Microsoft Copilot work and 29 GitHub repos confirm he can build — but nothing in the dossier suggests either founder was personally tortured by the inadequacy of existing CRO tools. They were tortured by nothing. They identified an opportunity, which is a polite way of saying they chose this idea from a menu.

Sherpa's primary traction proof — 40 experiments launched in 30 days for Lindy, yielding +30% total conversion lift — is simultaneously the strongest number in the dossier and the most suspect. Lindy is their former employer. Everett Butler, Head of Marketing at Lindy, gave the testimonial. Getting your old colleagues to use your product is not evidence of product-market fit; it's evidence of a warm relationship. Gauge and Yeti Co appear as named customers on Sherpa's website but without case studies or lift numbers, which suggests those engagements are thinner. The 4.3M+ website visits analyzed sounds impressive until you realize that's aggregate traffic across all clients, not a measure of depth. I need to see Sherpa acquire and retain customers who have no idea who Ethan and Norbu are. Until then, the traction is a reading of room temperature, not signal.

Ethan's YC bio claims he "automated every job he's ever had," and his 8,488 LinkedIn followers plus the 260-reaction launch post show he has distribution instincts. Those are real skills. But distribution instincts and the animal quality are different things. The animal quality is the Airbnb founders designing Obama O's cereal boxes to fund their company because every investor on earth had turned them down. It's Patrick Collison shipping Auctomatic as a teenager because he couldn't stop building. I see no such stories for Sherpa's founders — no creative workarounds, no evidence of building this before thinking about fundraising, no moment where they did something resourceful enough to become an anecdote. Ethan graduated in 2024 with a finance degree. Norbu's GitHub shows 29 repos with a top project at 10 stars. These are competent early-career people, not the obsessive builders who generate the stories I use to diagnose determination. The absence of anecdotes is itself mildly negative, because truly resourceful founders produce them involuntarily.

The strongest bull case for Sherpa is timing: LLM capabilities crossed the quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes autonomous variant generation credible for the first time, and CRO incumbents are distracted by consolidation — Intellimize absorbed by Webflow in April 2024, VWO and AB Tasty merged under Everstone in January 2025. Sherpa's single-line-of-code integration could undercut bloated enterprise platforms the way Stripe undercut payment processors. But Stripe worked because payments infrastructure was a genuine schlep — PCI compliance, bank integrations, regulatory complexity across jurisdictions. The schlep created the moat. CRO has no comparable schlep. Sherpa's core capability — feeding session data into an LLM to generate page variants — runs on foundation models available to everyone. The commoditization risk the dossier flags is real: the technology layer has no barrier to replication. Optimizely's AI team could build this feature in a quarter. What Stripe had that Sherpa lacks is a domain so painful that incumbents couldn't copy the approach without rebuilding their entire stack.

One condition would flip me: if Sherpa showed five or more cold-acquired customers — companies with no prior relationship to either founder — each achieving measurable conversion lift and retaining for 90+ days, I'd reconsider. That would demonstrate the product works outside the Lindy greenhouse and that the switching costs are real. Right now, Sherpa is a competent team applying AI agents to a consensus market, with traction concentrated in a warm relationship. I pass on companies where the idea sounds obviously good, the founders chose it from a menu, and the primary validation comes from people who already liked them. Sherpa's decisive weaknesses are Organic Problem Discovery — this was chosen, not discovered — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — nobody thinks this is a bad idea, which in my framework means nobody will get rich from it being right.
