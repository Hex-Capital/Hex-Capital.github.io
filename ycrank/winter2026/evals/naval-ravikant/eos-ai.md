# Eos AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The infrastructure positioning catches my eye before anything else. Eos wants to be the data harmonization layer underneath every clinical AI model -- the Alchemy of healthcare, if you will. When I backed Alchemy, the thesis was simple: every blockchain application needs an API layer, so own the layer and let a thousand apps bloom above you. Eos is making the same structural bet: radiology AI, pathology AI, clinical decision support -- they all need harmonized, ML-ready data as a prerequisite. That's the right architecture. Picks-and-shovels plays at the data layer of an emerging wave are precisely the kind of infrastructure bet I gravitate toward. The federated indexing approach -- keeping data in place while building a reasoning layer on top -- is a genuinely interesting design choice that avoids the compliance nightmares of centralized health data warehouses.

But architecture without specific knowledge is just a whiteboard. Arya Khokhar has a Caltech CS+Math degree, Stanford research, and a published paper on physics-aware neural operators for medical imaging. The ONCOVATE cancer triage project piloted with Stanford Medicine shows engagement with clinical environments. This is real technical talent, and the imaging ML research is directly relevant to the VERA harmonization engine. Yet when I apply my core filter -- what does this founder know that couldn't be acquired by any brilliant ML researcher given a year of focused study? -- I don't find a satisfying answer. Healthcare data fragmentation isn't a problem you understand from research papers. It's a problem you understand from spending years inside hospital IT systems, watching why HL7 integrations break at 3am, learning which radiology departments will never adopt a third-party preprocessing layer, knowing why Epic's Cosmos initiative makes your value proposition harder to articulate to CIOs who just signed seven-figure Epic contracts. That's the kind of domain knowledge that can't be taught. What I see instead is a technically gifted early-career builder who has identified the right problem from the outside.

The leverage question is where healthcare brutally diverges from software infrastructure. Alchemy serves developers through APIs -- one engineer's work scales to millions of API calls. Healthcare data harmonization requires touching each institution's specific EHR configuration, imaging system stack, compliance framework, and clinical workflows. Every hospital is a bespoke integration project. The federated architecture mitigates this somewhat -- you're not moving petabytes of data -- but the harmonization engines (VERA for imaging, LUCIA for text) almost certainly need per-deployment calibration across scanner manufacturers, documentation practices, and coding conventions. A 2-person team selling into health systems with 12-18 month procurement cycles, where the second team member isn't even publicly identified, faces a structural capacity constraint that no amount of architectural elegance resolves. The website's claimed metrics -- 3x administrative productivity, 37% revenue recovery -- are unattributed and unverified, which is normal at pre-seed but means the leverage mechanics remain theoretical.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If CMS interoperability mandates (HTI-1, TEFCA) force health systems to standardize data exchange, and if clinical AI adoption accelerates as expected, then the demand for an AI-native data harmonization layer will be enormous and urgent. The incumbents -- Epic, Oracle Health -- face genuine cannibalization risk in offering truly vendor-neutral harmonization, since their business models depend on ecosystem lock-in. Commure at $6B is building broadly across healthcare AI, not specifically owning the data preprocessing layer. There's a window where a focused infrastructure player captures the chokepoint. If Khokhar's imaging harmonization research is genuinely ahead of what cloud providers offer (AWS HealthLake, Google Health), and if the Stanford connection converts into a lighthouse customer, this could compound rapidly once the first few deployments prove the harmonization quality. The comparison to Stack Overflow's early days isn't crazy -- developer knowledge infrastructure that seemed niche before becoming indispensable.

But I keep returning to the same structural concern: healthcare is where leverage goes to die. The problem is real, the timing is plausible, the architecture is thoughtful -- but this isn't a domain where code leverage scales cleanly. It's a domain where regulatory compliance, institutional politics, and integration complexity impose friction that grows with each new customer segment. The contrarian angle is weak -- healthcare data interoperability is one of the most consensus problems in health tech. Everyone agrees the data is fragmented. What's contrarian is believing a 2-person team of recent graduates can solve it where hundreds of well-funded companies have struggled. That's either audacious insight or naive ambition, and without deeper evidence of domain-specific knowledge that goes beyond technical research, I can't distinguish between the two. I'd want to meet Khokhar and understand what they learned from the Stanford pilot that changed their model -- what broke, what surprised them, what they know now that they couldn't have known from the outside. Without that conversation, the specific knowledge signal is promising but insufficient for my framework.
