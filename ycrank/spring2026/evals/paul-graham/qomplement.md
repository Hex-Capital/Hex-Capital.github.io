# qomplement -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about qomplement is the pivot. A year ago this was a general-purpose computer-using agent -- "the smartest AI built right into your operating system." Now it fills out PDF forms and spreadsheets. That narrowing tells me something. Either they discovered, through actually shipping a broad product, that document filling was the use case where users kept coming back -- in which case the pivot is a healthy organic discovery. Or they narrowed because the CUA space got impossibly crowded and they needed a story for their YC application. I can't tell which from the public evidence, and the difference matters enormously.

The document filling angle has a genuine whiff of schlep blindness, and that's what keeps me from dismissing this outright. Every well-funded document AI company -- Nanonets at $100M revenue, Rossum with $104M raised, Reducto with $108M -- focuses on extraction. Pulling data out of documents is the glamorous problem. But in actual enterprise workflows, the extracted data has to go somewhere: into PDF forms, into spreadsheet templates, into regulatory filings. That reverse direction is tedious and ugly. It involves parsing fillable PDF field structures, handling Excel template quirks across three different fill modes, connecting to nine database types. Nobody with $100M in funding wakes up excited to build that. The fact that this niche exists essentially uncontested despite the extraction market being well-funded suggests the unconscious filtering I write about -- smart people see the document AI space but their minds skip right past the filling side because it's unglamorous grunt work.

But here's my concern: is the schlep deep enough to sustain a company, or is this a feature that any extraction vendor bolts on in a quarter? Stripe's schlep was regulatory -- banking relationships, fraud systems, compliance across dozens of countries. That gets harder over time. Document filling's schlep is technical integration -- PDF parsers, Excel modes, database connectors. That's real work, but it's reproducible work. If Nanonets decides their customers want a fill step after extraction, they ship it. The defensibility has to come from workflow stickiness and template coverage breadth, which are slow accumulations rather than structural moats. I'd feel better if the schlep here involved something like regulatory compliance for specific industries that takes years to navigate, rather than integration engineering that a funded team could replicate.

Kerim Taray's background at Contalink -- a YC S21 accounting fintech serving Mexican businesses -- gives me a plausible organic thread. If you've worked inside Latin American enterprise accounting, you've watched people manually transfer data between PDF forms hundreds of times a day. His second company Growsphere dealt with manufacturing sourcing, another document-heavy domain. The thread connecting accounting fintech to manufacturing documents to document-filling AI is coherent. It's not as clean as Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's far better than "we brainstormed AI applications." The one customer testimonial reinforces this: a transport company automating 5,000-10,000 invoices monthly and saving $15K/month. That's real economic dependency, not hypothetical demand. But it's one customer, and it predates the current product positioning by months.

If I construct the bull case: these founders genuinely understand Latin American enterprise document pain from the inside. The filling niche is real, underserved, and protected by the same dynamic that kept extraction vendors from looking down -- why build the boring reverse side when extraction alone is a $12B market? The local-first architecture solves a genuine compliance problem for healthcare and financial services that cloud competitors structurally can't address without cannibalizing their own business model. They lock in mid-market enterprises with workflow automation, compound switching costs through template libraries, and expand to U.S. regulated industries through YC's network. In that world, being the specialist in filling while everyone else fights over extraction is a smart wedge. For this bull case to work, though, they need to demonstrate that filling is a standalone buying decision, not a feature request tacked onto an extraction purchase. I don't see that evidence yet.

The team is young -- both graduated around 2024 from Tec de Monterrey. They've clearly built a working product with meaningful technical scope (the docs describe detailed features across Excel, PDF, workflows, and database connectors). But the CTO has no visible prior technical footprint, no public GitHub, no prior ventures. I can't apply my "animal test" without evidence. The $200K development investment and $60K/month AWS infrastructure costs tell me they're building seriously, but also that they're burning through capital without visible revenue. No pricing page, no revenue signal, listed as "free" in directories. At pre-seed I don't penalize thin traction, but the combination of meaningful burn and no revenue signal means the default-alive question looms.
