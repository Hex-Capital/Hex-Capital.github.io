# Hub.xyz -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the structural mismatch between what Hub.xyz tells YC it is and what it actually appears to be. The YC tagline is "API for real-world training data" -- a B2B enterprise positioning. But the actual product is a DePIN bandwidth-sharing network with IQ Points, a planned $HUB token generation event, airdrop campaigns, and SwissBorg as lead investor providing access to its crypto community. The enterprise API is the narrative layer; the token economy is the operating layer. When I see a company whose go-to-market is airdrops and Discord quizzes but whose pitch is "enterprise AI infrastructure," I apply my software-driven versus software-aware test. Strip away the token mechanics and what remains is a residential proxy network that collects web data through distributed nodes -- something companies like Bright Data have done for years without blockchain. A smart proxy is still a proxy. The AI training data framing doesn't change the underlying economics or defensibility of bandwidth aggregation.

The market tells me everything I need to know about thesis fit. AI training data is one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture capital right now. Scale AI is at $29 billion. Grass, which runs an almost identical DePIN model, is at roughly $1 billion with $17.5 million raised. There are 600-plus DePIN projects and the sector has a $16 billion market cap. This is the opposite of a non-obvious market -- it's the kind of category where every investor at every stage has an opinion and a bet. My best returns come from markets that look too boring, too niche, or too weird. PagerDuty, Gusto, Checkr -- nobody was excited about those markets. When I see 600 competitors and a dozen funded bandwidth-sharing projects, I know the asymmetric returns have already been competed away or concentrated in the earliest movers.

The "why now" case is entirely inferred in the dossier -- the founders haven't articulated a specific structural moment. The inferred thesis is that rising AI training costs, DePIN infrastructure maturation, and regulatory friction around web scraping converge to create opportunity. But examine each: rising training costs are driving demand for synthetic data and better curation, not more raw web scraping. DePIN "maturation" at 600+ projects signals late-cycle crowding, not early inflection. And regulatory friction around scraping applies equally to distributed scraping through user proxies -- the legal exposure doesn't disappear because you route through residential IPs. In fact, it might increase scrutiny. None of these add up to a capability threshold being crossed. Compare this to when I invested in Harvey: the GPT scaling curve gave me a specific, trackable signal that model fidelity would cross the legal-grade threshold in a defined timeframe. I can't identify an equivalent signal here -- what specific technology improvement in the last 12 months makes distributed bandwidth collection viable when it wasn't before?

The strongest bull case is that Hub.xyz's 500,000 node network represents real supply-side scale, and if the team can crack the "structuring" layer -- converting raw web data into enterprise-grade multimodal training datasets -- they could build a genuine data moat. The SwissBorg partnership with 1M+ KYC-verified community members is a real distribution asset for supply acquisition. And YC backing does provide enterprise credibility that pure crypto DePIN projects lack, potentially bridging the gap between crypto-native supply and enterprise demand. If the AI training data market truly reaches $16 billion by 2033, even a small share captured through a differentiated API could produce significant returns. But this bull case requires multiple sequential miracles: the structuring technology must produce enterprise-grade quality, the team must navigate evolving token regulations, they must outcompete Grass and Nodepay which have more funding and comparable scale, and they must build enterprise sales capability from a crypto-native base. That's at least four low-probability events compounding.

The team composition reinforces my concern. Armin has venture LP experience and early crypto involvement but no visible deep technical background in AI, distributed systems, or data engineering. Tim's background is HEC Paris, Berkeley, and financial data systems at Tiffany -- operational capability, but not the domain depth I'd want for building AI training infrastructure. Léonie, the CBO, was appointed CEO of Meey Group in October 2025 -- her current involvement is unclear, but a co-founder taking a CEO role at another company during the pre-seed stage is a significant signal. No public GitHub repos are visible for any founder. The website itself returned a 403 error during research. For a company selling an API product to enterprise AI teams, these are not the execution signals I look for. I think about the contrast with Color Health, where our founding team included genomics PhDs and lab automation engineers building in the exact domain where we needed to win. Founder-market fit here is thin.

I'm passing. This company sits in a consensus-hot market with well-funded direct competitors running nearly identical architectures, requires multiple miracles to reach its enterprise vision, and has a fundamental identity tension between its crypto DePIN reality and its enterprise AI positioning. The token economy may generate community engagement, but community engagement and enterprise training data quality are different problems that require different teams and different go-to-market motions. When those motions conflict rather than reinforce each other, the company ends up serving neither audience well.
