# Tepali -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the story before anything else. Boulevard at $800M valuation, Zenoti at $1B+, Vagaro at $1B -- these aren't sleepy incumbents waiting to be disrupted. They've collectively raised over $400M, serve tens of thousands of locations, and Boulevard's $80M Series D in July 2025 explicitly earmarked AI investment. When I see a 2-person pre-seed team entering a market where three well-funded competitors have already crossed the billion-dollar threshold and are actively pursuing the same AI thesis, the burden of proof for "why this team wins" becomes very high. This isn't the structural vacancy I saw with Anduril after Google's Project Maven cancellation -- this is a contested market where the incumbents are awake and spending.

The "why now" doesn't hold up under scrutiny. The argument is that LLM capabilities now enable an AI-native architecture that legacy platforms can't replicate without significant re-engineering. That was a compelling timing thesis in 2023. By early 2026, it's the pitch deck slide of every vertical SaaS startup I see. Boulevard's explicit AI investment, Mangomint's automation focus, and Zenoti's enterprise AI features all signal that the capability gap is closing from the incumbent side. When the structural advantage you're banking on is "we built on modern architecture" and the incumbents are spending $80M to modernize theirs, your window is measured in quarters, not years. Harvey worked because legal AI required GPT-4 fidelity that didn't exist yet -- the miracle was being delivered externally by the foundation model labs. Here, the AI capabilities Tepali needs already exist and are equally available to Boulevard's engineering team.

The market itself concerns me. U.S. medspa management software is a $99.8M market today. Even at 13.9% CAGR reaching $1.3B by 2034, this is structurally small. When I backed Gusto, "payroll" looked boring but served every employer in America -- the hidden scale was enormous. When I backed PagerDuty, "operations alerting" seemed niche but applied to every company running software. Medspa management software serves 30,000 locations in the U.S. That's not a boring market hiding massive scale -- it's a genuinely narrow vertical. The broader medspa services market at $18.6B is large, but capturing meaningful software revenue from that requires a different thesis than "replace their SaaS tools." I don't see evidence that this market is 10x larger than it appears when you account for adjacent disruption -- it's roughly the size it looks.

The strongest bull case is this: medspas are growing 20%+ annually, generating a wave of greenfield locations making first-time software purchases with no legacy lock-in. If Tepali's marketing-to-LTV attribution feature -- connecting ad spend to patient lifetime value -- is genuinely superior to anything incumbents offer, it could become the default choice for new locations. That attribution feature is the most interesting technical wedge in the dossier, because it requires the unified data model that point-solution competitors structurally can't match. From there, switching costs compound as patient records, charting history, and inventory data accumulate. Vishnu's experience building exactly this type of all-in-one platform at Dripos is directly relevant technical founder-market fit. If the team executes fast enough to establish a beachhead in new locations before Boulevard and Zenoti capture them, there's a path to a meaningful vertical SaaS business. But "meaningful vertical SaaS business" in a $100M software market with three unicorn-class competitors is not the 10x outcome profile I need to write a check.

The possible pivot from "Lotus" to "Tepali" during the batch is a signal I'd probe in a meeting. It could reflect rapid iteration -- testing a thesis, getting data, pivoting quickly -- which I'd respect. Or it could indicate that the medspa focus is weeks old rather than born from deep domain conviction. The dossier can't distinguish between these, but the absence of any public traction, customer metrics, or even a pricing page suggests the product is very early. Combined with the competitive dynamics, I'd need to see evidence that the AI-native approach delivers a step-function improvement in medspa operations -- not just incremental convenience -- before I'd get comfortable with the market entry risk.
