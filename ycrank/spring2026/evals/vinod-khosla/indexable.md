# Indexable -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see here is Andrew Gazelka's Hyperion project — a Minecraft game engine that processes 1,000 players in 455 microseconds and targets 170,000 concurrent players in a single world. That's not a game mod. That's someone who looked at an infrastructure problem everyone considered solved — "Minecraft handles a few hundred players, that's the limit" — and rebuilt the architecture from zero to achieve three orders of magnitude improvement. That same instinct drives Indexable: E2B gives you 200ms cold starts on Firecracker microVMs, and Gazelka didn't try to shave 20% off that number. He built a custom VMM on KVM from scratch — custom Linux kernel, custom Nix fork in Rust, content-addressable storage engine, libfabric transport — and achieved 26ms full-state forking. Files, processes, memory, databases. That's a 7-8x improvement built on a fundamentally different architecture, not incremental engineering on someone else's stack. This is an outsider move: a game engine developer attacking cloud infrastructure because he doesn't know what the cloud establishment considers impossible.

The consequence-of-success question is where I pause. If Indexable succeeds completely, the world gets better sandbox infrastructure for AI agents. That's genuinely valuable — E2B's 500 million sandbox sessions prove this is a required primitive, and 44-46% CAGR in agentic AI means the demand curve is accelerating. But "better plumbing for AI agents" is not "the world operates differently." The world operates differently because of the agents themselves; Indexable makes the agents' workspace faster and more capable. I invested in Juniper because TCP/IP routers would make the legacy telecom architecture structurally obsolete — Juniper didn't just make routers faster, it replaced the paradigm. Indexable's 26ms forking does enable qualitatively different agent behaviors — parallel exploration, backtracking, version-controlled execution — that are impractical at 200ms. That's a real capability gap, not just a speed improvement. But the transformation it enables is one step removed from Indexable itself. The company that captures civilization-scale value from agentic AI will be the company building the agents, not the sandbox they run in.

What concerns me most is the competitive structure. E2B has $43.8 million raised, more than $12 million in implied ARR, 88% of the Fortune 100 as customers, and two million monthly SDK downloads. Daytona has $31 million and already offers fork-and-snapshot capabilities — the same core value proposition — at 90ms on containers. Modal Labs is at $1.1 billion-plus valuation. Google is open-sourcing Agent Sandbox. Indexable is pre-revenue with a team of three entering a market where four well-funded competitors and a hyperscaler are already established. Now, I've backed companies against much worse odds — Juniper against Cisco, NexGen against Intel. The question is whether Indexable's architectural advantage is durable enough that E2B can't close the gap by investing in custom VMM technology of their own. I believe the argument that rebuilding Firecracker from scratch is a multi-year effort that E2B's existing customers and revenue make politically difficult — incumbents rarely cannibalize their own architecture. But "difficult" is not "impossible," and E2B doesn't need to match 26ms; they need to get close enough that switching costs outweigh the performance delta.

The strongest bull case is this: if autonomous AI agents become the dominant mode of software execution over the next five years — and the trajectory unmistakably suggests they will — then the infrastructure that enables their most powerful capabilities becomes as critical as cloud compute itself. Full-state forking in sub-30ms isn't a feature; it's an enabling primitive for an entirely new computing paradigm where execution branches like code branches. The company that owns that primitive at the architectural level, the way AWS owned elastic compute in 2006, captures enormous value. Gazelka is building the right technology at the right moment, and his technical profile — Hyperion, xAI, deep Rust/KVM/systems expertise — is precisely calibrated to the problem. If you believe agents will need to fork, backtrack, and explore parallel paths millions of times per day, and that 26ms versus 200ms is the difference between viable and impractical for that workload, then Indexable's custom VMM is the only architecture that works. That's a real thesis. It just requires the agent workflow paradigm to evolve specifically toward branch-heavy execution patterns rather than simpler sequential workflows.

Gazelka himself is the strongest signal. An engineer who builds systems that handle orders-of-magnitude more concurrent state than anyone thought possible, who jumps from game engines to xAI to custom VMMs — that's someone whose learning rate is extraordinary even if I can't measure it through pivot history. The BountyBot acquisition shows he's shipped commercially before. But the team composition worries me: three people, one public identity, one open engineering hire, zero visible commercial capability. For infrastructure targeting enterprise buyers, someone needs to sell. Right now this looks like three systems engineers building extraordinary technology with no one to bring it to market. That's fixable — I spend more time helping portfolio companies recruit than anything else — but it's a real gap at a moment when the market window is defined by how fast competitors are moving.

I'd want to meet Gazelka and watch him think through a problem he hasn't seen before. The technical depth is undeniable. The timing is favorable. The architectural moat against E2B is genuine. But the consequence magnitude keeps me from full conviction — this is important infrastructure, not a problem too important not to work on. I'd write a small check to stay close to this and see how the agent paradigm evolves, which is exactly my option-value framework: a million-dollar bet that either expires or becomes transformational as the market clarifies whether full-state forking is a nice-to-have or the critical primitive.
