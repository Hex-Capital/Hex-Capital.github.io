# OctaPulse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The schlep here is extraordinary. Deploying delta robots with compliant grippers to handle live fish in corrosive, humid hatchery environments, running edge inference on Jetson hardware connected via Starlink in remote locations, navigating food-safety regulations across multiple countries -- this is exactly the kind of problem that triggers what I call schlep blindness. Thousands of engineers know aquaculture is ripe for automation. But when they imagine actually building robots that grab slippery juvenile trout in a wet, corrosive facility in rural Idaho, their unconscious minds quietly redirect them toward building another SaaS dashboard. The competitive landscape confirms this: every funded aquaculture AI company -- Aquabyte, Umitron, Observe Technologies, GoSmart -- chose the clean problem of pointing cameras at fish in pens. Nobody wanted to build the robots that physically handle individual fish in hatcheries. That's a strong structural signal.

But here's where I hesitate. The founders met at a CMU entrepreneurship happy hour to discuss ocean technology. That's not organic discovery -- that's two smart people at a university scanning for interesting problem domains. Grech comes from Bloomberg and an MBA program. Singh comes from ASML, Nvidia, Tesla, and Toyota. Neither one was working in a fish hatchery when they stumbled into this. Grech has since earned recognition from the National Fisheries Institute, but that came after founding the company, not before. The "coastal community heritage" framing feels like post-hoc narrative construction around what was fundamentally a brainstorming-derived idea. This matters because the question isn't whether the problem is real -- it clearly is -- but whether these specific founders have the deep domain intuition that comes from years of living inside the problem. When Tony Xu at DoorDash personally delivered food for months, he was building intuition about logistics that no market research could provide. I don't see the equivalent here.

What partially compensates is Singh's technical profile. This is someone who worked at four of the most demanding hardware engineering organizations on the planet -- semiconductor lithography at ASML, GPU systems at Nvidia, manufacturing at Tesla, and automotive robotics at Toyota -- then went to CMU for robotics and AI. That's not a generic "technical co-founder." That's someone with the exact stack of skills this company needs: mechanical engineering, edge compute, robotics, and ML inference. The fact that they've already built a working system with delta robots, custom grippers, TensorRT-optimized vision models, and Starlink connectivity as a two-person team is impressive. Singh passes the animal test for hardware engineering.

The strongest signal in this dossier is the Riverence contract. A six-figure annual deal with the largest trout producer in North America is not a pilot or an LOI -- it's a real customer paying real money because the product solves a real problem. At pre-seed, with a two-person team, that's meaningful evidence of wanting. Riverence didn't sign that contract because OctaPulse had a good pitch deck. They signed it because inspecting fish by hand at five minutes per fish is genuinely painful, and automating it to thirty seconds at ninety percent accuracy changes their economics. That's the kind of pull signal I watch for. Two additional deployments planned for early 2026 suggests the first deployment worked well enough to generate referrals or repeat interest.

The bull case is that this is a DoorDash-type situation: a problem everyone underestimates because the schlep is enormous, where execution intensity in the physical world creates a moat that software-only competitors can't replicate. Each deployment generates proprietary labeled data for new species. Each deployment teaches the team how to harden robots in harsh environments. Each deployment builds switching costs as farms accumulate phenotypic records for their breeding programs. If OctaPulse can expand from trout to salmon to sea bass, they could own the hatchery automation layer globally -- and hatcheries are the chokepoint of the entire aquaculture supply chain. The timing is genuinely good: edge AI hardware finally enables real-time inference in power-constrained environments, and aquaculture has crossed the inflection point of surpassing wild catch.

The bear case is that this is fundamentally a hardware-services business with long sales cycles, high deployment costs, and species-specific retraining requirements that make scaling painful. The founders don't have deep aquaculture domain expertise -- they're learning the industry while building the product. And the co-founder origin story (entrepreneurship mixer, not organic pain) means their commitment hasn't been tested by the kind of adversity that reveals whether founders will persist through the inevitable brutal stretch of selling robotics to conservative fish farmers in rural Norway. I'd want to see how they respond when the third deployment hits unexpected species-specific challenges and the delta robots corrode faster than expected.

Overall, the schlep magnitude and the Riverence contract pull this above the neutral range. Singh is the right technical founder for this problem, and the competitive vacuum at the hatchery layer is real. But the manufactured origin of the idea and the absence of deep domain experience before founding the company keep this from being a strong conviction bet. This is interesting enough that I'd want to meet them -- specifically to understand whether they've developed genuine domain intuition through the Riverence deployment, or whether they're still operating from the outside looking in.
