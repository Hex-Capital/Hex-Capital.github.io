# Didit -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know. Persona has raised $417.5 million and generates $141.2 million in revenue. Veriff has raised $200 million at a $1.5 billion valuation. Sumsub has built $85.6 million in revenue on just $7.5 million in funding. Entrust acquired Onfido for $650 million. And now Didit enters this arena with $2 million and a free tier. This is not a market waiting for a disruptor — it is a Girardian arena where multiple well-funded players offer nearly identical feature sets (document OCR, biometrics, liveness detection, AML screening) and are imitating each other into margin destruction. Didit's response is to accelerate that destruction by making core KYC free. A company whose primary differentiation is being cheaper is a company that has already conceded it is competing, and competing on price is the most brutal form of competition there is.

What is the secret here? I ask this about every company, and the answer matters more than any metric. Didit's implicit thesis is: "Identity verification is overpriced, and a free-tier product-led growth model can capture the market from the bottom up." But this is not a secret — it is an observation about pricing that any competitor could replicate tomorrow. Persona could launch a free tier. Veriff could cut prices. Sumsub, which has already demonstrated extraordinary capital efficiency, could undercut Didit while generating real revenue. A secret must be something that most intelligent people actively disagree with, something that creates a structural monopoly if correct. "KYC should be cheaper" is a pricing tactic, not a truth about the world that reorganizes a market. There is no visible mechanism — no network effect, no proprietary technology moat, no scale economy exclusive to Didit — through which early price leadership converts into an unassailable position. Every customer Didit wins through pricing can be lost through pricing.

The company's origin story deepens my concern. Didit began as decentralized identity infrastructure for Gamium, a metaverse/SocialFi platform with a $GMM token. The GitHub organization is still named "didit-protocol." Token holders were offered airdrops tied to Didit's growth. The company then pivoted from this Web3 vision — which, whatever its merits, was at least contrarian — to conventional centralized B2B KYC SaaS, which is the opposite of contrarian. They moved from a position that challenged orthodoxy to one that embraces it. This trajectory suggests adaptive founders, not definite optimists. A founder with a concrete vision of how the world must change does not start with decentralized metaverse identity and end up selling document verification APIs at three cents per check. That is iteration — exactly the "lean startup" pattern I view as code for unplanned.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. The traction numbers, if accurate, are noteworthy: 700+ B2B customers, 20% month-over-month growth, 300%+ net revenue retention by month six, near-profitability on $2 million raised. That capital efficiency approaches Sumsub's remarkable trajectory. The 300%+ NRR in particular suggests that customers who begin with the free tier expand dramatically into paid features — which would validate the freemium strategy as a wedge. The founders have real ML credentials: Alberto's StyleGAN2 implementation with 143 stars on GitHub shows genuine technical depth, and Alejandro built AI systems at Oracle. The "why now" around deepfakes and AI-generated fraud is compelling — as synthetic identity fraud becomes trivial to execute, detection becomes existentially important. If Didit's AI models for deepfake detection proved genuinely superior, and if the free tier created enough integration switching costs to hold customers, you could construct a path to a large, profitable business. But a large, profitable business in a competitive market is not a monopoly. Sumsub already demonstrates that this exact trajectory — capital-efficient identity verification growth — is achievable, and Sumsub has 40x the revenue. The endgame here is not dominance; it is market share in an increasingly commoditized category.

The technology is competent but not discontinuous. Didit offers the same capabilities as its competitors — document verification, biometrics, liveness, AML screening — consolidated into a single API at lower prices. Supporting 14,000+ document types across 190+ countries represents real work but is not unique: Veriff covers 12,000+ types across 230+ countries. The drag-and-drop workflow engine and MCP server for AI agents are good product decisions but do not constitute a 10x improvement over what exists. Persona specifically positions itself as "the verified identity layer for an agentic AI world" — directly overlapping with Didit's messaging. When your competitor with $417.5 million in funding is making the same claim about AI agents, your thesis is not contrarian.

I would pass. This is a team with genuine technical ability building a well-executed product in exactly the kind of market I avoid — multiple funded players, commoditizing technology, no structural path to monopoly, and a differentiation strategy (low pricing) that any competitor can replicate. The founders may build a real business, perhaps even a profitable one. But the power law demands that an investment have the potential to return the entire fund, and a company competing on price in a crowded identity verification market — no matter how efficiently — does not have that potential.
