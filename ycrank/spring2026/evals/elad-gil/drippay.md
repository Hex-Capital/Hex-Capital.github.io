# Drip -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Stripe paid roughly $1B for Metronome in January 2026 (Lago blog, 2026 via search snippet), and Drip launched into YC Spring 2026 pitching essentially Metronome-plus-settlement three months after the consolidation event. That single fact reorders my entire evaluation of Drip. The usage-based billing market went from "interesting standalone category" to "consensus category the dominant payments platform just locked up" inside a single quarter. When I read the timestamp on Drip's market window, I'm not looking at a nonobvious opening -- I'm looking at a market where the consensus formed, cleared, and got acquired before the company left YC.

Mike Levin built metered billing infrastructure at GitHub before founding Drip (search snippet, 2026), which is the exact adjacent-domain experience I'd want to see. That's real founder-market fit on the technical axis. But Drip lists a team size of 0 on its YC profile (YC profile, via WebFetch), a CS-2026 new-grad CEO, and a co-founder "Lucas He" whose background couldn't be verified against the Drip entity in public sources. This is not Parker-Conrad-rebuilding-after-Zenefits. It's a strong engineer who worked on the right backend team pitching me against Stripe's combined distribution and Metronome's rating engine, with an unverified CTO and no operating experience north of 5 people. My scaling architecture test -- can this team navigate from 10 to 10,000 without structural redesign -- can't even be run yet.

Drip's single-miracle count concerns me more than the team. To win, Drip needs: (1) a high-throughput metering engine that clears the ~1,000 events/sec ceiling Lago calls out on Stripe Billing (Lago blog, 2026 via search snippet), (2) a prepaid-balance settlement layer that almost certainly triggers state-by-state money-transmitter licensing or stored-value regulatory obligations, and (3) bottom-up developer distribution against Stripe+Metronome's combined install base. That's three miracles. Anduril got an exception from me for multi-miracle risk because bipartisan defense modernization was a generational structural shift; Drip does not have that kind of tailwind. AI-era event volumes are real, but they're not a Coinbase-style market opening -- they're a scaling requirement that every incumbent is racing to meet.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Drip actually scores well. Drip is API-first, embedded in customer billing flows, with prepaid float sitting on-platform creating real switching costs once integrated (drippay.dev via search snippet). This is the kind of layer I like -- it's picks-and-shovels for the AI-agent economy, it's outcome-priced (per event) rather than per seat, and the prepaid-balance batching model captures margin that Metronome structurally can't because Metronome settles through downstream card processors on every charge. If the market timing were two years earlier, Drip would look like a Stripe-trajectory bet on a platform layer. My closest portfolio parallel here would be the infrastructure thesis behind Checkr or Infisical -- API-first, compliance-adjacent, workflow-embedded -- but both of those entered markets where the category infrastructure layer was unclaimed. Drip is entering after the claim was staked.

Margin architecture on Drip is the most interesting design choice in the dossier. Batching thousands of micro-charges against a prepaid balance to collapse per-transaction card fees is exactly the "selling units of cognition, not seats" model I've been writing about -- revenue scales with customer usage value rather than headcount. Drip also earns float on the prepaid balance, which is a secondary monetization mechanism that strengthens the retention equation if customers keep balances loaded. But no pricing page is public (drippay.dev returned 403), and Lago's open-source metering primitive (Lago GitHub, via search snippet) means the commoditization pressure on the rating layer is immediate. Drip has to defend on settlement economics that Stripe can replicate with a memo.

Bull case I have to honor: AI-agent traffic genuinely broke Stripe Billing at the event-rate layer, Mike Levin worked on the exact problem inside GitHub, and Stripe+Metronome integration realistically takes 12-18 months during which a purpose-built high-throughput settlement API could win the AI-first cohort that doesn't want to wait. If Drip locks in the top 20 agentic-AI companies before Stripe ships the integrated product, the prepaid-balance moat compounds. That's a real window -- I just don't think it's wide enough or nonobvious enough to clear my timing bar, and the money-transmitter exposure makes the single-miracle math worse than the pitch suggests.

One verifiable signal would flip me to Invest: signed integrations or paid usage from two or more named agentic-AI companies processing >10M events/month each, plus a money-transmitter licensing path (either a partner bank, a BIN sponsor, or a specific MSB/MT licensure plan) confirmed in writing. That would tell me Drip has cleared the distribution miracle AND the regulatory miracle, leaving only the engineering miracle -- which Mike's GitHub background actually addresses. Absent that, the dimensions that lock my verdict are Nonobvious Market Timing (the January 2026 Metronome acquisition is the opposite of a nonobvious window) and Single-Miracle Viability (three compounding breakthroughs required). Pass at neutral tier, not strong pass, because the infrastructure-layer positioning and the prepaid-balance margin model are genuinely interesting design choices I'd want to revisit if traction appears.
