# Valence -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Valence is where it sits in the stack. Kalshi is a protocol -- a CFTC-regulated exchange that creates the legal and technical primitive for event contracts in the United States. Polymarket is a protocol -- a decentralized prediction market on Polygon that creates permissionless global access to event trading. Valence is an application sitting on top of both of them, routing orders between someone else's infrastructure. I have spent the last decade learning, sometimes painfully, that the hierarchy between primitives and applications is the single most important structural question in any technology market. Venmo created a new payment primitive. Helium created a new wireless primitive. Aleo creates a new privacy primitive. These are the bets where compounding network effects produce generational returns. An aggregation layer that depends on maintained API access to two primary exchanges is not a primitive -- it's a feature those exchanges can build, restrict, or absorb whenever they choose. I know what platform dependency looks like from the inside. I spent four years at Facebook watching third-party applications live and die based on API access decisions made in a conference room. Valence's entire business evaporates the day Kalshi decides to restrict third-party order routing, and Kalshi -- sitting at an $11 billion valuation with $260 million in estimated 2025 revenue -- has every structural incentive to keep volume on its own interface.

The founders are genuinely impressive, and I want to be clear about that. Daniel Kasabov-Nouvion did hardware engineering at Optiver and IMC Trading -- these are firms where you learn how to build low-latency execution infrastructure at the physical layer, down to FPGA programming. Neo Wang worked on latency-sensitive strategy research at DRW and interned at Cubist and Citadel Securities. He also co-created the USACO Guide with 70K+ registered users and 1,800 GitHub stars, which shows he can build products that people actually use. Arthur Zhou is a USAMO qualifier and USACO Platinum -- genuine competitive programming elite. This team has exactly the technical credibility to build cross-venue trading infrastructure. The "sane" part of my framework scores very high. The problem is the "insane" part scores near zero. Building a smart order router across fragmented prediction market venues is exactly what three quantitative trading engineers would build. It's the obvious play. Every quant who trades Kalshi and Polymarket simultaneously has either built internal tooling for this or wishes someone would. There's nothing non-consensus here -- this is a sane person with a sane idea, and that combination is how you get a solid business that never produces venture-scale returns because every other competent team in the space sees the same opportunity.

The claimed traction deserves attention: 1B+ contracts traded all-time, $200M+ monthly volume, 50% peak market share on individual events. For a three-person pre-seed company, these numbers would be extraordinary. But a customer testimonial describes a single quant firm trading "$150M+ monthly" through the platform. If that's real, one client may represent 75% of total monthly volume -- which isn't a distribution flywheel, it's a key-man risk wearing a trenchcoat. The numbers are entirely self-reported with no independent verification. I've seen enough pitch metrics to know that "contracts traded" can be inflated by counting both sides, by including paper trading, or by measuring notional value generously. I'd want to see exchange-verified fill data before weighting these claims heavily. If the volume is real and diversified across many clients, the revenue cushion could be meaningful. If it's concentrated in one or two institutional relationships, it's fragile.

Here's the bull case I take seriously: prediction markets are genuinely fragmenting. Kalshi, Polymarket, Robinhood, DraftKings, FanDuel, and Crypto.com all launched or expanded prediction products in 2025-2026. More venues means more fragmented liquidity, which means more value in aggregation. The Bloomberg terminal analogy is the strongest version of this argument -- Bloomberg built a $12 billion revenue business as an aggregation and execution layer across traditional financial markets. If prediction markets grow from $2 billion to $10 billion in annual revenue by 2030, the aggregation layer captures real value. And the founders have the technical DNA to build it better than anyone. What would have to be true for this to be a great investment: prediction markets would need to fragment across 10+ meaningful venues (not 2-3), Kalshi and Polymarket would need to keep APIs open and not build their own cross-venue tools, regulatory classification of intermediaries would need to remain favorable, and Valence would need to evolve from a routing layer into something closer to infrastructure that other applications depend on. That's a lot of "would need to" for my taste.

The comparison I keep returning to is 1inch, the DEX aggregator in crypto. 1inch aggregates liquidity across decentralized exchanges -- same structural play as Valence. But there's a critical difference: DEXs are permissionless protocols. Nobody can revoke 1inch's access to Uniswap's smart contracts. Kalshi is permissioned. Kalshi can shut off Valence's API with a phone call. This makes Valence's aggregation model fundamentally more fragile than the crypto DEX aggregator model, which is ironic given that Valence bridges crypto and regulated markets. The structural fragility isn't a technical problem the founders can engineer around -- it's a business model constraint inherent to aggregating permissioned venues. And as AI makes software development cheaper (my whole thesis), building exchange integrations and order routing becomes easier for any well-capitalized competitor, which further erodes whatever execution lead Valence has today.

I respect the team's capability, and I think prediction market infrastructure is a real category. But I invest in primitives, not applications. I invest in businesses where AI makes the moat deeper, not businesses where cheaper software makes the competition easier. And I invest in non-consensus ideas that most investors think are crazy -- not consensus plays that every quant in the target market would build themselves given a free weekend and API documentation. This is a pass for me.
