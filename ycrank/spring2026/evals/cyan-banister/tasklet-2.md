# Tasklet -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$5M ARR and over 1,200% growth since January 2026 — those numbers demand respect. Tasklet is executing at a pace that makes most pre-seed companies look like science projects. Andrew Lee built Firebase, which 15% of developers now rely on, took it through YC S11, sold it to Google, and then recognized when his second company Shortwave was generating more signal in agent demand than in email. That pivot — killing your darling to follow the stronger current — is a founder instinct I value. The $20M Series A from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and both Collison brothers confirms what the revenue already says: Tasklet is a real company with real momentum. But momentum and thesis fit are different questions, and when I apply my own framework, the picture shifts.

Andrew Lee's story is a builder's story — Rose-Hulman to Firebase to Google to Shortwave to Tasklet. Jonny Dimond walked that same path alongside him: early Firebase, Google Cloud Firestore tech lead, Shortwave co-founder. Their decade-plus working relationship through an acquisition, a corporate tour, and two co-foundings is one of the strongest co-founder bonds I've seen, which neutralizes my usual anxiety about partnership dynamics. But when I ask my opening question — "tell me your story, not the business" — I'd hear about Firebase and infrastructure patterns. That's a career narrative, not a biographical forge. Where is the moment that made knowledge-work automation inevitable for Andrew Lee personally? The pivot from Shortwave reads as sharp pattern recognition: "we saw agent demand emerge from our user base." That's intelligence, not destiny. I've learned to distinguish between founders who are running toward a problem because their life left them no choice and founders who are running toward a market because their skills qualify them. Tasklet's founders are the latter — deeply qualified, genuinely talented, but not forged in the specific fire that makes me write a first check.

Tasklet operates in what might be the single most consensus space in technology right now. AI agents for productivity. Every investor I know is chasing this category. Google is building it into Workspace. Microsoft has Copilot embedded across M365. OpenAI launched Operator. Anthropic is integrating agent capabilities. Zapier has $310M in revenue and 7,000+ integrations to defend from. Lindy raised ~$50M. Relevance AI raised $37M. The $20M Series A from marquee investors isn't a contrarian bet — it's a consensus validation that every top firm competed for. My biggest wins came from writing checks when 300 investors had already said no, into spaces that made rooms of VCs shift in their chairs. Rockets blowing up. Defense AI when Silicon Valley considered it politically toxic. Male birth control. Tasklet makes no one uncomfortable. It's the kind of company that gets enthusiastic nods at a dinner party, which is precisely the signal that tells me someone else's pattern, not mine, is being activated.

Tasklet attacks a real inefficiency — knowledge workers toggling between dozens of SaaS apps — but inefficiency is not opacity. When I backed Affirm, credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing behind minimum payment psychology. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessments were buried in bureaucratic fog that homeowners couldn't navigate. Tasklet's target, workflow friction, is visible to everyone who experiences it. Zapier and Make aren't extracting value through information asymmetry — they're just clunky tools that require manual construction. Tasklet replaces "drag-and-drop" with "describe in natural language." That's a UX improvement, not a structural revelation. CEO Andrew Lee framed the thesis as "what if the workflow goes away?" — but the workflow was never hidden. It was always right there on your screen, annoying you. There's no invisible tax being exposed, no gatekeepers being disintermediated, no power shifting from institutions to individuals. This dimension is where my framework most sharply diverges from conventional analysis: Tasklet's traction proves the product works, but my question isn't whether it works — it's whether it cracks open something that was previously sealed shut.

Tasklet's defensibility argument is its strongest card for my framework, and it connects to the Firebase parallel that makes the bull case compelling. Andrew Lee built infrastructure that developers embedded so deeply they couldn't rip it out — that's how Firebase survived Google offering competing products. Tasklet's cloud-native agent runtime, context engineering from the Shortwave pivot, and integration layer through Pipedream plus MCP plus HTTP APIs echo that infrastructure playbook. But the parallel has a structural flaw: Firebase was developer infrastructure with code-level embedding and brutal switching costs. Tasklet is an end-user chat interface where users describe tasks in natural language. If a better agent appears tomorrow, a user types the same plain-English instruction into the new system. There's no code migration, no SDK swap, no database restructure. The Pipedream dependency compounds this — Tasklet's integration breadth runs through a third-party layer it doesn't control. And the CEO's own podcast framing, "always bet on the models," concedes that value accrues upstream to foundation model providers. That's the opposite of the infrastructure moat thesis. Crusoe Energy built physical data centers at stranded energy sites — you can't replicate that with an API call. Tasklet's infrastructure, while genuine, sits in a layer that foundation model providers are actively collapsing into their own platforms.

The strongest bull case for Tasklet is execution velocity from founders who've done this before. Andrew Lee took Firebase from YC S11 to ubiquity. Tasklet is adding more monthly revenue than Shortwave accumulated in four years. The agent runtime with Ubuntu VMs, browser automation via computer vision, and sandboxed code execution is more than an AI wrapper — it's genuine systems engineering. If I check my Known Exceptions, RTFKT shows I've overridden infrastructure concerns for cultural timing. But Tasklet's timing isn't narrow — it's a broad wave that every major company is riding simultaneously, which means the timing advantage is shared rather than proprietary. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Tasklet's users are building critical business processes on the platform that create genuine data and switching-cost moats — that the agent runtime is becoming infrastructure rather than interface. If enterprise retention numbers showed that companies using Tasklet for six months are structurally unable to leave, that changes the defensibility story from Firebase-like aspiration to Firebase-like reality.

Tasklet is a well-built product from proven founders in a space that activates none of my distinctive investment instincts. The contrarian conviction dimension and the invisible system dimension are what separate my framework from conventional venture analysis, and Tasklet scores low on both — not from absence of evidence but from clear evidence pointing the wrong direction. Andrew Lee and Jonny Dimond are builders I'd want at the helm of almost any infrastructure company, but the biographical forge that makes me write first checks into uncomfortable spaces isn't present here. This is a Long Journey pass — not because Tasklet won't succeed (it may succeed enormously), but because the shape of the bet doesn't match the shape of my conviction.
