# Oddpool -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Avi Arora trained the prompt-injection model that secured 20B requests a year at Microsoft and Ritesh Malpani made Bloomberg's buy-side trading systems 40% faster — and they are now selling a $30/month prosumer tier and an arbitrage API for "762 opportunities last week" on top of two venues, Kalshi and Polymarket. That is the signature of **Oddpool** before any further analysis: real operator pedigree pointed at a category whose revealed acquihire ceiling was set three months ago when Polymarket bought Dome for an estimated $10–20M after roughly four months of existence. **Oddpool**'s highest dimension is founder lineage; its lowest is the definiteness of the plan. That pairing names the company — competent infrastructure engineers building a feature-shaped business in a category where the incumbent has already demonstrated it will absorb the neutral layer rather than let it compound.

**Affirm** (2014, Levchin) is the load-bearing analog on founder lineage: an operator who had shipped payments-grade infrastructure at PayPal turning that pedigree into a regulated-fintech moat that compounded over a decade. The shared mechanism is that financial data and financial rails reward operators who have already debugged latency, correctness, and counterparty trust at institutional scale — exactly what Bloomberg buy-side work and Microsoft production-ML work proxy for. Where **Oddpool** diverges from the Levchin pattern is the LinkedIn fact the dossier surfaces: Ritesh Malpani is concurrently listed as co-founder of Benchspan, a separate Spring 2026 YC company. A two-person team where one founder runs two YC-batch companies is not the Levchin signature; it is the signature of a founder hedging which bet matters. Operator pedigree is real here, but it is not anchored.

**Quora** (2010) is the failure mechanism on the definite-plan axis: a roadmap that resolves to "we will see what users find valuable" with no 2-to-5 year architectural commitment the founder refuses to pivot from. **Oddpool**'s dossier has the same shape — a tiered SaaS pricing page ($30/$100/Enterprise), a freemium funnel, "CFTC applications keep climbing" as the why-now, and no named technical milestone the founders have committed not to abandon. The Quora override was Adam D'Angelo's Facebook CTO pedigree plus a knowledge-graph bet. **Oddpool** has neither a comparable pedigree override nor a comparable network-effect compounding hypothesis; the captured-history dataset back to March 2026 is real but linear, not network-effect compounding. Indefinite optimism in a category with a demonstrated $15M ceiling is the canonical configuration I was taught by the Founders Fund manifesto to refuse to fund.

On the contrarian secret, the mechanism analysis matters. **Oddpool**'s stated secret is that venue-neutral aggregation is structurally impossible for Polymarket or Kalshi to build because cross-listing competitor liquidity cannibalizes their own order flow. That is a real market-structure observation — Polymarket's Feb 19 2026 acquisition of Dome rather than build-neutral is the revealed-preference evidence. But the secret does not compound over 18 months; it inverts. The same observation that says exchanges cannot build neutral also predicts that exchanges will *acquire* every neutral aggregator that reaches institutional scale. **Oddpool**'s secret is therefore an acquihire thesis dressed as a monopoly thesis, and Dome (raised $5.2M, exited in months) is the proof.

On the monopoly trajectory, the counterfactual: what would have to be true for **Oddpool** to score in the Palantir-shaped tier? Three things — a third and fourth CFTC-licensed venue would need to come online with **Oddpool** as the default ingest layer, the captured-history dataset would need a switching cost rivaling Bloomberg terminal lock-in (named customers, enterprise contracts, S3-dump dependencies), and Polymarket would need to fail to acquire or replicate. The dossier surfaces none of these — only Kalshi and Polymarket are live venues, the enterprise tier has no named counterparties, and FinFeedAPI from the CoinAPI team already ships the normalized REST product. The trajectory is not absent; it is bounded.

On atoms-heavy or regulated moat, the single-anchor parallel is **Palantir** (2003) — the company I keep returning to when the question is whether a regulated customer base produces structural pricing power. Palantir's moat was integration depth into intelligence-grade workflows where the customer's switching cost was career-ending. **Oddpool** sits one layer abstracted from regulation: the CFTC licenses the venues, not the data layer, so **Oddpool** captures regulatory tailwind without the regulatory moat itself. That is the Bloomberg-on-top-of-exchanges pattern, which has produced real businesses, but not the Palantir pattern.

My 36-month forecast for **Oddpool**: acquired by Kalshi, a CoinAPI/FinFeedAPI rollup, or a fintech-data incumbent in the $8–25M range, with the Dome February 2026 deal as the explicit comparable; if no acquisition, **Oddpool** settles at $1–3M ARR as a prosumer-plus-small-enterprise data business. Not zero, not a fund-returner, and not a power-law shape — the category's revealed ceiling and the two-person team with one founder split across Benchspan both pin the outcome to the same tier.

The strongest opposing case: prediction markets become the institutional asset class the Opinion ~$8B monthly volume figure suggests, CFTC licenses proliferate to five-plus venues by 2028, and **Oddpool**'s captured-history dataset back to March 2026 becomes irreplaceable because no later entrant can backfill tick-level orderbooks that did not exist at the time. The Bloomberg-of-prediction-markets bull case is real, and the operator pedigree at Bloomberg itself is the right pedigree for it. What dismantles it is the precedent: Polymarket already revealed it will pay $10–20M to remove a neutral aggregator from the board after four months, and FinFeedAPI from the CoinAPI team is already shipping normalized Polymarket/Kalshi/Myriad/Manifold REST data — the dataset gap closes from two directions. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a named enterprise contract with a top-twenty quant fund or prop shop, disclosed in dollar terms, where **Oddpool**'s tick-level history is the cited reason for the procurement. Absent that, **Oddpool** is a feature company in a $15M-ceiling category, and the right check size is zero.
