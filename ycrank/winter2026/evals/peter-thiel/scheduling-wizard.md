# Scheduling Wizard -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Scheduling Wizard is the competitive landscape surrounding it, and it tells me almost everything I need to know. QGenda was acquired for $2.5 billion by Hearst eight months before this company entered YC. Lightning Bolt has won the #1 KLAS rating for physician scheduling five separate times. Amion serves 200,000 providers across 11,000 departments. YouShift, literally one YC batch ahead, is building the same AI-powered scheduling product with Bain Capital Ventures money. This is the signature of a market where everyone can see the opportunity -- where the thesis is consensus, the dollars are flowing, and multiple well-resourced players are converging on the same customers. Girard would recognize this immediately: mimetic rivalry, where companies imitate each other into mutual margin destruction. When a $2.5 billion acquisition validates a market, every entrepreneur and every VC fund sees the same signal and rushes toward it. That is not a market with a secret hiding inside it. That is a market where the secret has already been found, exploited, and sold to Hearst.

What is the claimed secret here? The implicit thesis is that hospitals don't want better scheduling software -- they want someone else to schedule for them entirely. Every competitor sells a tool; Scheduling Wizard sells a service. This is presented as differentiation, and at first glance it sounds contrarian: the entire industry went one direction, and these founders went the other. But a business model preference is not a secret about the world. "Customers prefer outsourcing to operating software" is an observation that consulting firms have been monetizing for decades. Accenture and Deloitte have built hundred-billion-dollar businesses on exactly this insight. The deeper problem is that managed services do not create monopolies. They create linear businesses where revenue scales with headcount, margins compress under competition, and no structural moat compounds over time. Three people serving nineteen departments is already near operational capacity. To reach a hundred departments, they hire proportionally -- or they automate, at which point they become the same self-serve software company that every competitor already is.

The strongest argument for this company is the Scheduling Programming Language and the founders' domain fit. Oberly's background -- Cambridge master's in applied mathematics, Department of Defense war games modeling, Minor League Baseball scheduling -- maps almost perfectly to healthcare constraint optimization. The team landed fourteen hospitals including Mass General, Johns Hopkins, and UCSF at pre-seed, which is nontrivial customer validation. If the Scheduling Programming Language genuinely encodes institutional scheduling logic in a way that accumulates and compounds -- creating an ever-growing library of department-specific rules that makes each new client faster and cheaper to onboard -- then the managed service is a Trojan horse for a software platform. The bull case requires believing that this domain-specific language becomes the operating system for physician scheduling, that the service layer is temporary scaffolding, and that the encoded knowledge creates switching costs deep enough to resist QGenda's 694-person engineering organization. For this to work, the founders would need to articulate a definite vision for that transition -- from service to platform -- with the specificity and conviction I look for. I see no evidence of that vision. Instead, the company is already expanding scope into "patient triaging" and "provider management systems," which signals indefinite thinking: when you don't know exactly where you're going, you go in every direction.

The founders are recent Johns Hopkins graduates, class of 2024, with no prior startup experience and no evidence of the extreme conviction I associate with exceptional founders. This is not disqualifying at pre-seed -- Zuckerberg was nineteen, Buterin was nineteen -- but those founders articulated a specific, non-consensus vision of the future and held it against enormous pressure. The Scheduling Wizard team articulates a market opportunity, not a worldview. The technology, while competent, is applied constraint satisfaction -- a well-studied field where the innovation is in application, not in fundamental capability. A domain-specific scheduling language is useful engineering, but it is not the kind of technological discontinuity that makes the old approach structurally obsolete. QGenda, with its resources and installed base, could build or acquire equivalent optimization capability within eighteen months.

The small-market entry strategy is actually the strongest element here. Residency and fellowship program directors at academic medical centers represent a tightly defined niche -- roughly 1,100 ACGME-accredited institutions -- where the founders have direct relationships through Johns Hopkins. The department-by-department land-and-expand within hospitals is a sensible wedge. But dominating a small market requires being the unchallenged incumbent within that niche, and Scheduling Wizard shares this exact customer segment with Amion (already in 11,000 departments), Lightning Bolt (already #1 KLAS in physician scheduling), and YouShift (pursuing the same AI thesis one batch ahead with venture backing). You cannot monopolize a niche that four other companies already serve. The endgame is not defensible: in ten years, QGenda's parent company Hearst will still have more resources, Lightning Bolt will still have deeper hospital integrations, and whoever builds the best AI scheduling engine will commoditize the optimization layer entirely. I see a competent team solving a real problem in a market that has already attracted the competition that destroys returns.
