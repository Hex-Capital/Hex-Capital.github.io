# Antihero Studios -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Antihero Studios presents an unusual case for my framework because everything about it is designed to sound good -- and that's precisely the problem. "Games Worth Sharing." "ARC Raiders meets Brawl Stars on your phone." "Leads on 8 different $1B/year games." A16z co-leading the seed. This is the kind of company that wins consensus approval from every investor in the room, and I've learned to treat consensus as a contra-indicator. The extraction shooter genre is already validated on PC with ARC Raiders generating $500M in revenue. Mobile gaming is a $121B+ market. Gen Z wants social games. No one hears this pitch and says "that will never work." And when no one says that, the returns have usually already been competed away. The best startup ideas are the ones where knowledgeable people's first reaction is confusion or dismissal -- Antihero Studios gets neither.

Frank Yan's credentials are the strongest signal in this dossier. He was lead designer on Brawl Stars and engineered its revenue recovery from an all-time low to 8.8x growth -- that's not a line on a resume, that's a demonstration of craft at billion-dollar scale. His GDC talk on removing loot boxes from Brawl Stars shows someone who understands the intersection of game design and monetization at a level few people on earth do. Brice Laville Saint-Martin directed art on Clash Royale, and Andre Parodi ran technical infrastructure at King for a decade and has two prior startup co-founds. These people know how to build mobile games. But knowing how to build something inside Supercell's machine and knowing how to build a startup are fundamentally different skills. The question I keep returning to is whether Antihero Studios has the running back quality -- the ability to find openings when the obvious path is blocked -- or whether they're running plays from Supercell's playbook on a field with different rules.

The origin story troubles me. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he started with a USB drive he kept forgetting -- a personal frustration so specific it was almost embarrassing. When the Octopart founders built their electronic parts search engine, they were physics grad students who couldn't find components. Antihero Studios starts with "Gen Z players use video games primarily to socialize, yet mobile gaming lacks games built around social mechanics." That sentence reads like the output of a market analysis deck, not a frustrated builder's complaint. Frank Yan lived inside Brawl Stars' design space for years and presumably felt its limitations firsthand -- that's genuine domain knowledge, and it pushes this above pure trend-chasing. But the framing is opportunity-first, not problem-first. The A24 and Liquid Death brand analogies compound this: brand-building vocabulary belongs to marketing departments, not to founders who can't sleep because their prototype isn't right yet.

The pre-alpha metrics deserve attention. Seventy thousand players with 50-minute average daily playtime across three sessions, power users averaging 10 hours per day, and eliminated players 6% more likely to restart than successful extractors -- that last stat is the kind of counter-intuitive finding that reveals genuine understanding of game psychology. The 750-creator network generating 250 content pieces during pre-alpha is a clever post-IDFA distribution hack that shows Brice understands how mobile game distribution has shifted. These are real learning signals, and they push my assessment of founder learning rate above the default. But pre-alpha testers are self-selected enthusiasts; the gap between 70K engaged early adopters and the mass-market retention required to make cosmetic-only monetization work at scale is enormous, and Tencent -- which owns both Arena Breakout and Supercell -- can respond to any proof of concept with overwhelming resources and existing distribution.

The strongest bull case for Antihero Studios is a ClassDojo-style exception to my usual pattern. ClassDojo violated my "hackers building for hackers" preference but succeeded because the founders had extreme domain expertise in an unsexy space with genuine schlep. Antihero Studios has extreme domain expertise -- arguably the best mobile game design credentials I've seen at pre-seed. The schlep is real: building a new genre on mobile with organic distribution in a post-IDFA world while competing against Tencent's capital. If Frank Yan's design instincts translate from Brawl Stars to extraction mechanics, and if the creator network compounds into a genuine distribution moat, this could become a breakout studio. What would flip me: evidence that the founders encountered a specific, personal frustration with existing games that they couldn't ignore -- not "we saw a market gap" but "we kept trying to play extraction games on mobile and nothing existed that worked." That story might be there, but the dossier doesn't surface it.

My pass comes down to two dimensions. Antihero Studios' idea actively fails the Black Swan test -- it sounds good to everyone, the genre is pre-validated, and multiple gaming-specific VCs immediately funded it, which means the insight is already priced in. And the problem discovery is market-gap analysis rather than organic frustration, which in my experience predicts a company that will execute competently but won't find the desperate, irrational product-market fit that defines the biggest outcomes. These are experienced operators building intelligently within a known framework, and experienced operators building within known frameworks is what venture capital funds every day. It's not what I fund with my own money.
