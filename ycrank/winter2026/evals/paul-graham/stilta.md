# Stilta -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Stilta is that it's entering a market where five competitors raised a combined $117 million in the last twelve months alone, and at least 25 AI patent tools already exist. One of those competitors — &AI — is also YC-backed and builds an almost identical agentic AI product for patent attorneys. Solve Intelligence already has eight-figure ARR and 400+ customers. When I see a category this crowded and this well-funded, my instinct is not "big market, room for everyone." My instinct is that the timing window everyone identified is the same timing window, and the idea has already been priced. This is the pattern I've warned about repeatedly: when every VC is excited about a category, the consensus upside is probably gone. The companies that win from here will win on execution, not insight.

The organic test is where Stilta loses me. Four engineers from McKinsey and QuantumBlack decided to build AI for patent attorneys. But none of them are patent attorneys. None of them appear to have worked in IP law or at a patent search firm. The path from "we build enterprise AI systems at McKinsey" to "we'll build AI for patents" runs through market analysis, not lived experience. This is exactly the failure mode where smart people with strong skills pick a vertical because it looks lucrative and underserved rather than because they personally couldn't stand the way things worked. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the honest answer seems to be: they analyzed the market and saw an opportunity. Compare this to Stripe, where the Collisons had personally experienced the pain of integrating payment processing as developers, or Dropbox, where Drew Houston was literally carrying a USB drive. The McKinsey background is almost anti-organic — consultants are professionally trained to identify market gaps through frameworks, which is the opposite of stumbling into a problem through your own work.

The schlep here is real — patent databases, legal-grade auditability, regulatory requirements, conservative enterprise buyers. That's exactly the kind of tedious complexity that would normally filter out competition and create structural advantage for the team willing to wade through it. But the filtering mechanism has broken down. When 25+ companies are all willing to do the same schlep because agentic AI made it technically tractable, the schlep stops being a moat. Schlep blindness works as an investment thesis only when most people are still blind. Here, everyone's eyes are wide open. The company even says their approach "has not been technically possible until a few months ago" — which means every other team with LLM expertise reached the same conclusion at the same time.

The bull case rests on the team's execution capacity. Oskar Block bootstrapped two sports betting startups to over $1M ARR — that's a real commercial signal, not a theoretical one. The CTO built scalable AI systems at AWS and QuantumBlack. They have four engineers, which means they can move fast. And they claim paying customers at "leading firms" in both Europe and the US, which at pre-seed with a four-person team suggests they can both build and sell. If the patent AI market consolidates to two or three winners — which markets like this often do — a lean, technical, fast-moving team with YC backing could plausibly be one of them. The product-market timing is genuinely right. The question is whether timing plus execution beats the massive capital advantage held by Solve Intelligence, Patlytics, and others who are already building the same thing with 20-100x more money.

Ultimately I'd pass. Not because the team is weak — they're clearly capable — but because the idea's origin and the competitive dynamics both point the wrong way. This reads like a team that sat down and asked "what vertical should we apply AI to?" rather than a team that couldn't stop thinking about how broken patent search was. The absence of any patent domain experience on the founding team, combined with a market where the most directly comparable competitor (&AI) has already raised 13x more capital with First Round leading, makes this a bet on pure execution speed in a race where others have a significant head start and more fuel. I want to invest in companies solving problems that nobody else wants to touch. This is a problem that everybody wants to touch.
