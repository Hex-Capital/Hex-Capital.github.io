# qomplement -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me: this company started as a general-purpose computer-using agent — "the smartest AI built right into your operating system" — and pivoted to filling out PDF forms. They had the bigger idea and retreated from it. That pivot tells me everything about the competitive reality they walked into. The general CUA space got crowded fast, so they narrowed to a niche within document processing. But narrowing within a consensus-hot AI category doesn't create mispricing — it just means you're playing a smaller game in a market where Nanonets is doing $100M in revenue and Reducto just raised $108M. You're not contrarian. You're just late and small.

The document processing space is exactly the kind of AI application layer I've been saying is a terrible place to deploy capital. Every extraction vendor — Nanonets, Rossum, Reducto, Extend — already processes the same document types qomplement touches. Adding a "fill" step downstream of extraction is a feature sprint for any of them. It's maybe two quarters of engineering work, not a company. And the bigger kill shot: Microsoft Power Platform already has form-filling capabilities baked into a product with distribution to every enterprise on Earth. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about — every time someone pitches me "AI for [workflow incumbent already owns]," I know exactly who's going to capture the value, and it's not the startup.

The bull case — and I want to be honest about it — is that the "filling" niche really is underserved, and the local-first on-device architecture creates a genuine compliance wedge in regulated industries where data can't touch the cloud. Healthcare, financial services, transportation in Latin America — these are sectors where data residency matters and where Silicon Valley incumbents don't prioritize because the deal sizes are too small to justify enterprise sales teams. If qomplement nails workflow stickiness with their 9+ database connectors and visual builder, and if the LatAm enterprise market adopts AI tooling faster than VCs expect, there's a scenario where they build a defensible mid-market business. The Contalink experience gives the CEO real exposure to Latin American enterprise document pain. I don't dismiss the market knowledge. But "defensible mid-market LatAm document tool" is not a venture-scale outcome, and it's definitely not the kind of asymmetric risk/reward I'm looking for.

Nothing about this is insane in the way I need it to be. The founders are young — graduated 2024, first real venture for the CTO, brief prior startup experience for the CEO. That's fine, everyone starts somewhere. But the idea itself is completely sensible. "AI fills out forms for operations teams" is the kind of thing that makes every VC nod along and say "that's a real problem." When everyone's nodding, I'm already bored. I want the pitch where I think "this person is clearly smart and what they're describing sounds impossible." Document filling automation is the opposite — it sounds inevitable and obvious, which means the upside is already priced in. There's no narrative mispricing to exploit. The $60K/month AWS infrastructure spend for model training at pre-seed with no visible revenue signal also bothers me — that's burning through your YC capital on commodity compute before you've found product-market fit.

The structural durability here is close to zero. Pure software, no network effects, no protocol layer, no physical-world moat. The local-first deployment is a smart architectural choice but it's a configuration, not a moat — anyone can ship a quantized model that runs on 16 gigs of RAM. Compare this to Metropolis, where we seeded AI-powered parking management that sits on top of physical parking infrastructure no one can replicate with code. Or Teamshares, where the moat is literally owning small businesses. qomplement's moat is "we fill forms instead of extracting from them." That's a product positioning choice that any competitor can copy in a quarter. Pass.
