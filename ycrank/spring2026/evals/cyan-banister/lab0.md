# Lab0 -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Rocketlane — a $105M-funded incumbent with 750+ customers — banned Lab0, a three-person pre-seed team, from their Propel conference on April 19, 2026. That's the most striking fact in this entire dossier. Incumbents don't gatekeep against companies they don't fear. When I found Flock Safety through a WiFi network name at a YC event, part of what excited me was that the surveillance space made established players nervous — discomfort from incumbents is signal. But the nature of the discomfort matters. Rocketlane isn't uncomfortable because Lab0 occupies a stigmatized or "weird" space; they're uncomfortable because Lab0 threatens to eliminate the human consultant layer that Rocketlane's entire $60M Series C business model serves. That's competitive fear, not the kind of categorical rejection that signals contrarian territory. Three major PSA vendors — Rocketlane Nitro, Certinia Veda, Kantata Expertise Engine — all launched AI agent products within six months of each other. Lab0 is swimming into consensus waters where well-capitalized fish are already circling.

Lakshya Gupta, Sujay Srivastava, and Onkar Borade are all IIT graduates — Gupta and Borade from IIT Bombay, Srivastava from IIT Madras. When I sit down with founders, my first question is always about their story, not the business. What I'm looking for is biographical inevitability — did the problem find them, or did they find the problem? Gupta co-authored a research paper benchmarking LLM performance on ServiceNow workflows (arXiv 2601.22130, January 2026), which shows genuine intellectual engagement with enterprise complexity. Srivastava previously founded Ravvio in BFSI AI and scored in the 99.94th percentile on CAT 2024. Borade built production systems at Oracle and Mudrex. These are capable people. But capability isn't forge. Lab0's founding story reads as "we identified a large market and applied our technical skills to it" — and that's the exact narrative that doesn't activate my pattern recognition. I don't see evidence that any of these founders spent years as forward-deployed engineers themselves, enduring the months-long implementation grind, feeling the frustration in their bodies. The Crusoe founders witnessed gas flaring in North Dakota oil fields before they built anything. The Uber founders disrupted a system they'd personally been exploited by. Lab0's founders appear to have researched their way into the problem, which is respectable but fundamentally different from being forged by it.

Lab0 targets the $451.6 billion system integration market — Accenture, Deloitte, Wipro billing consultant hours to configure and integrate enterprise software. There is an opaque structure here: implementation scoping is notoriously unpredictable, cost overruns are endemic, and customers often can't evaluate whether they're getting value until months into an engagement. But when I compare Lab0 to Affirm attacking credit card opacity, or Ownwell attacking property tax opacity, the information asymmetry is qualitatively different. CIOs who buy Accenture engagements know they're paying a premium — they're not victims of a hidden tax they can't see. They're sophisticated buyers trapped by a lack of alternatives and high switching costs. Lab0's "0-day Enterprise Software Implementation" vision would certainly help these buyers, but it doesn't carry the structural justice dimension that drives my deepest convictions. Lab0 is making an expensive system cheaper, not making an invisible system visible.

The bull case for Lab0 is real, and I want to steelman it honestly. Gupta's "World of Workflows" paper identified "dynamics blindness" in frontier LLMs — their inability to predict cascading side effects in enterprise systems — which means Lab0 may have research-informed technical depth beyond what a wrapper company possesses. The incumbent business model creates a structural barrier: Accenture can't automate implementations without cannibalizing its own billable-hour revenue model, the same way taxi companies couldn't build Uber. Lab0's claimed "continuous learning from every rollout" data flywheel, if real, could create compounding defensibility over time. And the market is vast — even a sliver of $451.6 billion is enormous. If Lab0 can truly compress months-long implementations into weeks, the value per deployment dwarfs typical SaaS pricing. Srivastava's prior founding experience with Ravvio provides some evidence of startup resilience.

But when I stress-test Lab0 against my red flag checklist, several concerns emerge. The commoditization risk is severe — ServiceNow acquired Moveworks in late 2025 and sits on top of exactly the enterprise workflow data Lab0 needs. Salesforce, Microsoft, and Google could all build implementation agents natively into their platforms. Lab0's three-person team faces enterprise sales cycles that require SOC 2 compliance, proof-of-concept periods, and reference customers — a brutal mismatch with pre-seed runway. The three co-founders' equity structure and leadership hierarchy aren't disclosed, which I always notice after what happened with HQ Trivia. And while Gupta's research paper demonstrates intellectual depth, I can't distinguish Lab0's approach from what any team with access to frontier models and enterprise domain expertise could build. Lee Jacobs would call this adjacent to "AI wrapper" territory — not because Lab0 lacks engineering substance, but because the defensibility against platform vendors building this natively is unclear at best.

The one condition that would flip me to Invest: if I sat down with one of Lab0's founders and learned they'd spent three years as a forward-deployed engineer at Palantir or a system integrator, watching implementations fail, feeling the weight of that broken system on their back — and then showed me a paying customer whose six-month Salesforce implementation Lab0 compressed to two weeks. That would change everything. Biographical inevitability plus demonstrated product-market fit would transform this from a market-opportunity play into a mission-driven infrastructure bet. Without that, Lab0 is a competent team chasing a real market, but not a Long Journey bet. The founder biographical forge dimension is most decisive here — scoring 11/30 reflects that Lab0's founders researched their way to the problem rather than being forged by it. The contrarian conviction dimension at 12/25 confirms the second concern: Lab0 operates in a space where three well-funded incumbents just launched competing AI agent products, which is the opposite of "making sophisticated investors shift in their chairs."
