# Bubble Lab -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did the founders find this problem? With Bubble Lab, the answer is conspicuously absent. Selina Li and Zach Zhong were building gymii.ai, a consumer nutrition tracking app, before pivoting to B2B operations automation. Neither founder has documented experience in BizOps, RevOps, or as a Chief of Staff -- the exact roles they're building for. The dossier explicitly confirms this: "neither founder has documented prior experience in BizOps or RevOps roles specifically." This is the pattern I wrote about in "How to Get Startup Ideas" -- when founders decide to start a company and then go hunting for an idea, rather than stumbling into a problem they can't stop thinking about. Ops teams wasting hours on cross-tool busywork is a real problem, but it's a problem you'd identify by reading TechCrunch, not one that kept either of these founders awake at night. The organic backstory isn't just weak here. It's absent.

The schlep question makes this worse, not better. What's the hard, ugly work that deters competitors? API integrations across 25 tools? That's engineering labor, not institutional complexity. Stripe's schlep was banks, fraud departments, and financial regulation -- structural barriers that took years to navigate and that every programmer unconsciously avoided. Bubble Lab's schlep is connecting to Jira, Notion, and HubSpot APIs, which is tedious but well-documented. The dossier itself identifies the core capability as "technically reproducible." When the founders' own competitive landscape confirms that the work can be replicated, you don't have schlep-driven defensibility. You have a feature that n8n -- sitting on $253 million in funding and $40 million in ARR -- can add in a quarter.

The platform dependency compounds this. Building your core product inside Slack while Salesforce actively launches Agentforce 360, positioning Slack as an "agentic OS," is building on land the landlord has announced plans to develop. Salesforce's incentives are clear: keep AI capabilities as an upsell for premium Slack tiers. That might create short-term pricing space for Bubble Lab's freemium model, but it also means the company's entire distribution advantage -- Slack-native virality -- exists at Salesforce's discretion. This isn't a death sentence; plenty of companies build successfully on platforms. But it means this company needs multiple things to go right simultaneously: the LLM agent has to be reliable, the integrations have to stay ahead, Salesforce has to not crush them, and n8n and Gumloop and Dust all have to stumble. That's too many miracles.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. These are young, technical founders who shipped fast. A working product with 25+ integrations, an open-source engine with 1,100 GitHub stars, SOC 2 Type 1 certification, and 4,000 claimed users -- all at pre-seed with a two-person team -- is genuinely impressive execution velocity. Their prior startup gymii.ai won at Cornell Tech's Startup Awards, showing they can build and ship. Zach Zhong is described as a "3x founding engineer," and the product exists, which puts them ahead of most pre-seed pitches I see. If I squint, the consumer-to-B2B pivot could be a feature: they learned that consumer is brutal and brought real engineering chops to a market with clearer revenue. And the Slack-native positioning is a smart wedge for bottom-up adoption. If they can accumulate enough users and long-term memory data before the incumbents move, there's a plausible moat in switching costs.

But execution velocity on a manufactured idea is running fast toward a cliff. The 4,000 users number comes from the company website -- it's a marketing claim, not independently verified traction. The named customers (Dyna Robotics, PeakMojo, Hattrick Ventures) appear to be early-stage companies themselves, not the fast-growing mid-market operations teams the product targets. And the open-source engine cuts both ways: 1,100 stars builds some community, but Apache 2.0 licensing means anyone can fork and self-host the core technology. You've essentially published your own competitive moat for free.

What I keep coming back to is the category itself. AI agents for workflow automation is the consensus hot idea right now. Every VC is excited about it. Multiple well-funded competitors are building adjacent products. This is exactly the kind of crowded, trending space where I've observed that "the two most impressive companies I've seen so far are not working on AI." The founders didn't discover an invisible problem through lived experience -- they entered an already-visible market where the idea was obvious to everyone. When the problem is obvious and the schlep is moderate, you need extraordinary founder-market fit or extraordinary resourcefulness to win. I see competent engineers here, but not the kind of organic, almost obsessive connection to the problem that distinguished the best YC companies in their earliest days.
