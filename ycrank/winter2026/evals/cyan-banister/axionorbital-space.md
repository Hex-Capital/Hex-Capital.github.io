# AxionOrbital Space -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about AxionOrbital is what's missing from the story — and I don't mean traction or revenue, because at pre-seed that's expected. What's missing is the *why*. Dhenenjay worked at ISRO as an ML engineer, Atharva did a PhD in computer vision at CU Boulder and time at Harvard's Visual Computing Group. Those are genuinely impressive credentials, and the technical pairing is strong — someone who handled real satellite radar data paired with someone pushing the frontier on diffusion models. But when I look at a company, the first thing I want to know is how these founders got here, not professionally but personally. What hardship or obsession led them to this specific problem? The dossier gives me a clean credential narrative — elite business school, elite research lab, competition wins — but no survival story, no biographical thread that makes this problem *theirs*. Maybe it exists and I just can't see it from the public record, but the signal I depend on most to evaluate pre-seed founders is absent here.

The technical work itself is legitimately interesting. Translating SAR radar imagery into photorealistic optical output — making an invisible data stream visible and usable — maps onto a pattern I've seen in my own portfolio. Carta made cap tables visible. Affirm made interest rates transparent. Flock Safety turned surveillance data into something communities could act on. AxionOrbital is doing something analogous: taking data that's unintelligible to human eyes and standard computer vision pipelines, and making it readable. Their ORION model claims state-of-the-art benchmark results with sub-100ms latency, which matters for the real-time applications they're targeting. But here's where my pattern breaks: the people benefiting from this transparency aren't underserved populations gaining economic access. They're defense agencies, hedge funds, and intelligence analysts. Disaster response and agriculture are listed as target markets, and those could create genuine impact, but they're not where the money is or where the company seems to be pointing first. The economic access story is indirect at best.

What gives me pause on the "weird factor" is that SAR-to-optical translation isn't actually unpopular or dismissed — it's an active academic research area with a systematic literature review published in November 2025 cataloging numerous competing approaches. The most promising companies in my experience are the ones where other investors think the idea is *crazy*, not where they think it's technically sound but niche. ICEYE has $600 million in funding and could build this. Large AI labs could replicate it with sufficient paired training data. The algorithmic approach, while well-executed, doesn't have the kind of structural non-consensus quality I look for. This feels more like a smart application of current AI capabilities to a known problem than a non-obvious insight that others are missing. The pivot from a no-code geospatial IDE to foundation models — sometime between mid-2025 and early 2026 — reinforces my sense that the founders are searching for the right wedge rather than arriving with the kind of inevitable conviction I saw with Crusoe Energy or SpaceX.

Now, the strongest bull case here deserves real engagement. This could be an infrastructure play at an inflection point — exactly the kind of bet that, when I'm honest with myself, I've made repeatedly despite telling people I back scrappy outsiders. SpaceX, Anduril, Crusoe Energy, Together.ai — these are capital-intensive structural bets at technological turning points. If one-step diffusion models just became production-viable, if SAR constellation data just became abundant, and if defense budgets are expanding globally, then AxionOrbital could be building the translation layer that makes the entire SAR ecosystem dramatically more useful. The ISRO-to-Harvard pipeline between these two founders is genuinely rare. If they secure a defense or intelligence contract early, the security clearance and procurement complexity becomes a moat that protects them from academic replicators and big tech. That path is real. But it requires a two-person team with $500K to navigate one of the hardest sales cycles in technology — defense procurement — without any visible government relationships, security clearances, or LOIs. That's not impossible, but it requires the kind of relentless persistence that I usually see rooted in something deeper than professional interest.

The co-founder dynamic is a question mark. No shared prior employer, no shared university, no visible prior collaboration. Complementary skills — absolutely. Dhenenjay's space domain and business sense, Atharva's deep CV research expertise. But complementary skills don't predict whether two people can survive the crisis that's coming, and at pre-seed, the crisis is always coming. After what I learned from HQ Trivia, I'd want to sit with these two founders and understand how they argue, how they make decisions when they disagree, how they found each other. The public record gives me nothing here.

This is a technically competent team building a logical product at a plausible inflection point. But it doesn't activate my core investment instincts. I don't feel the biographical grit, the non-consensus weirdness, or the economic access mission that defines my best investments. I'd want to meet these founders and hear their personal stories before forming a final view — there might be something beneath the credential surface that the dossier doesn't capture. But based on what's visible, this is a pass for me.
