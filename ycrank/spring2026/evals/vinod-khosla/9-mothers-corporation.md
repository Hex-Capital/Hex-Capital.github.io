# 9 Mothers -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that grabs me — and not in a good way. When I backed Juniper to challenge Cisco, there was no credible startup competitor building TCP/IP routers for telecom. When I backed OpenAI, no one else was writing $50M checks into AI. Counter-drone is not that situation. Anduril just landed a $20B Army framework contract and sits at a $60B valuation. DroneShield is a public company doing A$216M in revenue growing 276% year-over-year. Epirus has $550M+ in funding. This is a category that has already been created — by other people. The outsiders have already stormed the castle. 9 Mothers isn't inventing a new market; they're showing up to an existing fight where the competitors have billions in capital and deployed systems generating real revenue. That changes the entire risk calculus.

Now, the consequence-of-success question. If 9 Mothers genuinely succeeds — builds a radically cheaper, portable, autonomous counter-drone system deployable at the squad level — that matters. Ukraine proved that $500 FPV drones can destroy $10M tanks, and right now the defensive response costs orders of magnitude more than the offensive weapon. Democratizing drone defense the way cheap drones democratized drone offense is a real transformation. The counter-UAS market projections ($69B by 2034) reflect a genuine, growing problem. But here's what I keep coming back to: the consequence of 9 Mothers' success specifically is that they become a defense contractor selling units into a procurement system, competing against Anduril's Lattice platform and DroneShield's 70-country distribution. They're not making the incumbent approach obsolete — they're offering a different form factor within the same procurement ecosystem. That's meaningful but not civilization-scale.

The strongest bull case, and I want to take it seriously: there may be a genuine gap at the bottom of the market. Anduril sells large integrated platforms. The defense primes sell expensive systems with long procurement cycles. Nobody is building the $50K counter-drone unit that every vehicle and forward operating base needs. The defense primes won't build it because it cannibalizes their high-margin platform sales — the same business model cannibalization dynamic that let Square eat the payment processors' lunch. If 9 Mothers is the Square of tactical C-UAS, occupying the space that's too small for Anduril to care about and too technical for cheap commercial solutions, that's a real wedge. And the acoustic AI approach has genuine differentiation potential — passive detection that doesn't emit signals, hard to jam, low power. Roman Khomenko's Ukrainian background means the team understands this threat from lived reality, not from reading defense white papers. That personal proximity to the problem reminds me of founders who attack problems because they experienced them, not because a market analysis told them to.

But the team composition gives me pause. Russell Smith ran a QA testing SaaS company for a decade — he raised $42M but I see no exit event. Khomenko was an ML data scientist at that same company. Pyzh comes from security consulting. None of them have built defense hardware, navigated DoD procurement, or shipped a physical weapon system. I generally prefer outsiders — Peter Beck had no aerospace pedigree when I backed Rocket Lab. But Beck was building rockets from the ground up, not competing against a $60B defense company with established military relationships. In defense procurement, relationships and certifications aren't incumbency bias you can route around with better technology — they're structural requirements. The 14 job postings suggest they're building a real engineering team, and the acoustic ML roles indicate serious technical work, but I see no defense industry hires who know how to get a weapon system through the DoD approval pipeline. That's not a nice-to-have — for an autonomous weapon system, it's existential.

The timing trajectory is where I see the most tension. On one hand, the fundamentals are undeniable: Ukraine created urgent demand, DoD budgets are responding with $49.9B in defense tech spending, and edge AI inference has made portable autonomous systems technically feasible. These are real tailwinds. On the other hand, by the time the Army is awarding $20B framework contracts, you're not skating to where the puck will be — you're skating to where it already is, surrounded by faster, better-funded skaters. The exponential curve in counter-drone has already inflected into mainstream awareness. When I invested in cleantech in 2004 or AI in 2019, I was years ahead of consensus. 9 Mothers is entering counter-drone in 2025-2026, after the category has been validated by the largest defense contracts in history. That's not a timing advantage — it's a timing tax.

I respect the ambition of attempting autonomous weapon systems as a pre-seed startup. These founders haven't de-risked their way into irrelevance; they've taken a running leap at one of the hardest problems in defense. The acoustic AI approach is technically interesting and could offer real differentiation. But the honest assessment is that this is a company entering a validated category with well-funded competitors, led by founders pivoting from a completely different domain, with no disclosed traction, no performance data, and no defense procurement experience. The consequence of success is a strong defense business, not a reinvention of how warfare works. I need either a technology breakthrough that makes Anduril's approach structurally obsolete, or evidence that the founders are learning at a rate that compensates for their domain inexperience. I see neither yet.
