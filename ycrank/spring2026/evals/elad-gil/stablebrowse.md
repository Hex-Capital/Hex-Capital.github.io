# StableBrowse -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Four founders, most recently out of undergrad, are attempting to build a custom browser engine from scratch. I want to sit with that for a moment. Browser engines are one of the three or four hardest sustained engineering efforts in software -- Google maintains Blink/Chromium with hundreds of engineers, and even Microsoft gave up on EdgeHTML and switched to Chromium in 2019. StableBrowse is proposing to strip the rendering pipeline out while retaining correct JavaScript execution, network handling, and web compatibility across the full surface area of the modern web -- SPAs, Shadow DOM, iframes, WebSocket-driven UIs. The performance claims are impressive on paper (85MB RAM, <80ms cold start, 95% token reduction), but I see no public benchmarks, no GitHub repos, no customer testimonials, and no evidence that this engine exists at production quality. The gap between a demo that works on ten websites and infrastructure that handles the actual web is enormous, and I've seen that gap kill technically ambitious companies many times.

The market structure question is where this evaluation gets interesting but ultimately doesn't work for me. Browser infrastructure for AI agents is a real and rapidly expanding category -- I don't dispute that. But it's one of the most consensus bets in the current AI infrastructure wave. Over $180M has been deployed across Browserbase, Kernel, Airtop, TinyFish, and Browser Use. Browserbase alone has $67.5M raised, a $300M valuation, and 1,000+ paying customers generating $4.4M in revenue. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was a wasteland that serious investors wouldn't touch. When I wrote the Perplexity seed check, AI-powered search was considered a failed category. This market is the opposite: it's crowded, well-funded, and every major VC has already placed bets. There's a subtle timing argument that the scale inflection -- where millions of concurrent agent sessions make Chromium's rendering overhead a binding constraint -- hasn't fully arrived yet, but that's a known trajectory, not a nonobvious insight. Investors are already pricing in that future.

Where StableBrowse genuinely excites my infrastructure-layer pattern recognition is the depth of the architectural bet. The competitors are operating at the hosting and orchestration layer -- Browserbase manages fleets of standard Chromium instances, Browser Use wraps existing browsers with automation APIs. StableBrowse claims to operate at the engine level, which is structurally a different layer of the stack entirely. If it works, this would be the kind of architectural divergence that creates real switching costs: customers building pipelines around 85MB instances and semantic markdown output can't easily revert to 500MB+ Chromium sessions with pixel parsing. This maps to how Stripe wasn't just another payment processor but a fundamentally different API layer that made the prior approach feel antiquated. The CDP compatibility is a smart on-ramp -- drop-in replacement lowers adoption friction. As infrastructure positioning, this is genuinely strong.

The single-miracle test is structurally favorable but practically daunting. The market demand miracle is already happening exogenously -- agent frameworks are proliferating, enterprises are deploying web agents, and the cost curve of running Chromium at scale is a real constraint. StableBrowse needs one miracle: prove that a rendering-free engine can handle the full diversity of the modern web at production quality. That's one miracle, which is the right count. But it's an extraordinarily difficult one, and the anti-bot detection risk compounds it -- a non-standard engine fingerprint is paradoxically easier for Cloudflare and DataDome to detect and block than actual Chromium, which could undermine the product's reliability for the exact use cases (scraping, automation) where customers need it most.

The bull case requires two things to be true. First, that the rendering-free architecture is fundamentally correct -- that as agent workloads scale to millions of concurrent sessions, the cost and latency overhead of full Chromium becomes untenable, and the market naturally migrates toward StableBrowse's approach. This would make Browserbase's $67.5M in Chromium fleet infrastructure a stranded asset, similar to how companies that invested heavily in on-premise server management got disrupted by cloud-native architectures. Second, that this team can actually ship a production-grade engine that handles real-world web complexity. If both are true, the category outcome could be massive -- the company would own the infrastructure layer that every AI agent connects through, with unit economics 10x better than the competition. I can see the path to a Gusto-like outcome: boring, essential infrastructure with compounding switching costs and no prestige-seeking competition at this specific layer. But the "if" on execution is enormous given the team's visible profile.

On the founders: I'm not seeing the evidence I need for a project of this technical ambition. Somansh Shah graduated from Purdue in 2025 with relevant coursework but no visible industry track record. The potential Amazon connection for Deepit Shah is unconfirmed. No founder has a public presence in browser engineering, systems programming, or infrastructure at scale. Four co-founders with no clear decision-making hierarchy raises my standard concern about equal founding structures. I've said repeatedly that strong markets compensate for some execution gaps -- "I've actually seen some pretty mediocre people do incredibly well if there's very strong product-market fit." But this isn't a case where the market carries you. Building a browser engine requires deep, rare systems engineering talent. The market doesn't do that work for you.

I'm passing. The infrastructure positioning is real and the architectural thesis is intellectually compelling, but the market is consensus, not nonobvious, and the execution risk against well-funded incumbents with a founding team that hasn't demonstrated the specific technical depth required for browser engine development is too high. If I saw evidence of a working engine handling 10,000+ diverse websites at production quality, or a founder who'd previously shipped browser or rendering engine code at scale, the calculus would shift significantly.
