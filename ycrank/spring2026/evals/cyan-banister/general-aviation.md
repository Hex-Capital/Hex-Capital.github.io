# General Aviation -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ben Frank is a licensed pilot, an aircraft mechanic, an MIT aerospace engineer, and a two-time aviation-software CEO (Rotabull, Seer Aerospace -- the latter acquired by Aviation Week in 2023), and he is sitting alone at team size 0 saying he is going to fix the $32B FAA modernization program with a satellite network. General Aviation is the cleanest "founder-is-the-artifact, product-is-still-vapor" pre-seed I have seen this batch. The biography is overbuilt for the lane; the website is a placeholder. That pairing is the entire analytical signature, and it is the exact pairing that produced my SpaceX (2008) check before there was a single Falcon on a pad.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the load-bearing analogue on lived experience. Luckey did not discover defense in a memo -- he shipped Oculus to scale from his parents' garage, then went at unmanned systems with the social license that prior product gave him. General Aviation has the same shape: Ben Frank did not read about ATC in a Bain deck, he holds a pilot's license and a mechanic's certificate, he sold Rotabull-grade aviation SaaS into the parts ecosystem, he sold Seer Aerospace into Aviation Week's stack, and he then went to Rotor Technologies to put autonomous code into a flying helicopter. Where General Aviation diverges from the Luckey rung is consequence-scale of the prior exit -- Aviation Week is not Facebook-buying-Oculus -- so the conviction sits just shy of HIGH. But the test is whether he was *of* the place or *visiting* it, and Frank is unambiguously of the place. The dossier even tells me he can talk to a roughneck of the sky -- he is the roughneck.

The "consensus YC AI-agent darling" failure mode is the inverse of General Aviation. That archetype fails on physical verifiability because the product is a pure software abstraction the founders cannot hand you to install, photograph, or ride; the moat lives in the model, not the human. General Aviation has the opposite physical-verifiability failure: I cannot touch the satellite, I cannot install the avionics, the website at generalaviation.com is marketing copy, and the founder Twitter @benjaminsfrank reads 85 followers. There is no Ingress to play, no Postmates burrito to receive, no Flock camera to spot on a pole. What I *can* touch is the founder -- Frank flies the airplane the system would route -- but the product itself is the lowest-verifiability artifact in this batch.

Solo founder, on this dimension, is the inverse of HQ Trivia (2017) -- no Yusupov/Kroll tiebreaker problem because there is no second name on the cap table to fight with. General Aviation passes the unambiguous-CEO test by structural default; the same fact, viewed differently, becomes the capacity risk the dossier flags (certified avionics + satellite network + ANSP interop is not a one-person job for long). I will take that trade. The HQ Trivia and GameCrush lessons were about *unresolvable* leadership, not insufficient headcount, and General Aviation has the former pinned even if the latter has to be solved with the next four hires.

What would have to be true for General Aviation to flip from a 19-tier on non-consensus to a 22-tier? Aireon, freshly acquired by Iridium for $366.7M in May 2026 with ~$100M annualized revenue, would need to be publicly cited by two sophisticated investors as the reason General Aviation is uninvestable -- "the surveillance lane is closed." That is the rebuttal-not-hard-no shape I look for. The dossier does not yet show two named passes on aesthetic grounds, but the FAA Common Automation Platform shortlist already names only Thales, Collins, L3Harris, Indra, Leidos, Frequentis -- six incumbents, zero startups -- which is itself a deselection field that a pre-seed solo founder is structurally outside of. That outsider posture, against six primes for $32B in federal procurement, is exactly the "too hard or too unsexy" lane I funded Mindbloom and Vigil Labs (August 2025) into.

Crusoe (~2018) is the single anchor for the frontier-hardware axis. Crusoe stapled three uninvestable things together -- crypto + flared gas + rural industrial -- and the moat compounded because the founder was geographically and socially native. General Aviation staples four: certified safety-of-life avionics + FCC/ITU spectrum + FAA SWIM integration + LEO constellation economics. Every one of those is a regulatory or capital chokepoint that compounds with time once cleared, and every one of them is the kind of thing that makes a generic Founders Fund associate's spreadsheet go red. The Iridium/Aireon comp at $366.7M for surveillance-only is actually a *floor*, not a ceiling -- it tells me a networked-replacement architecture, if it certifies, prices into a fundamentally larger envelope than overlay ADS-B.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation does not ship a global satellite ATC network. General Aviation lands an FAA SBIR Phase II or a foreign ANSP pilot in the UTM/drone-corridor adjacency, grows to 8-15 people, and is either operating at $1-5M ARR as an aviation-IoT/UTM company by Spring 2029 or has been acqui-hired into Iridium, Anduril, or a defense prime hungry for an FAA-fluent CEO. The mechanism is the high/low signature: the founder is too capable to fail outright, the product is too vaporware to clear FAA certification on a pre-seed timeline, so reality routes through a smaller adjacent procurement where Frank's pilot/mechanic biography and his Rotor Technologies autonomy experience cash in.

The bear case stings here and I want to name it: the Red Flag in my own file says "technology risk, but not insurmountable technology risk," and a solo founder with zero teammates building certified avionics plus a satellite constellation plus global ANSP interop is the closest thing in this batch to *insurmountable*. Aireon spent a decade and $500M+ to put surveillance-only payloads on Iridium NEXT; General Aviation proposes a more ambitious architecture from team size 0. The bear says: this is SpaceX-without-the-Musk-PayPal-balance-sheet, which is a different company. My answer is that I am not underwriting the global network -- I am underwriting Ben Frank, who has already exited one aviation company and shipped autonomous-helicopter code at Rotor, into a $32B procurement window the GAO has publicly flagged urgent. The verifiable condition that flips me to pass: if by demo day Frank still has team size 0 and no signed LOI from a single ANSP, drone-corridor operator, or DoD program office, then the "capacity" half of the solo-founder trade has lost, and I walk. Until then, this is a magically weird, founder-of-the-place check.
