# Smartbase -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Three ventures across three completely unrelated domains in roughly two years — Sphinx Labs (smart contract DevOps, $2M raised), Anytrace (AI support engineering, YC S25), and now Smartbase (manufacturing PO automation, YC S26). Sam Goldman and Taira Fujioka have pivoted twice, which tells me something important: they haven't found the thing yet. I invest in repeat relationships where I've watched a founder demonstrate consistent judgment across cycles — Raj through three companies before Solana, Kortina from Venmo through Fin. But "consistent judgment" means something different from "new domain every ten months." The pivot pattern here looks less like iteration and more like search, and I'm not the right investor to fund someone else's search for founder-market fit when they openly admit they were doing AI support engineering six months ago and now they're sleeping on factory floors.

Conexiom has $170M in funding, roughly $75M in annual revenue, and serves 16 of the top 20 industrial distributors. Smartbase's pitch is essentially "Conexiom for SMBs" — a cheaper, lighter-weight version of an existing enterprise product aimed downmarket. That's not mispricing. Nobody in the market is saying "automated PO entry in manufacturing can't work." Conexiom literally proves it works. The argument is that Conexiom's enterprise sales motion and pricing are misaligned with small manufacturers, and Smartbase can fill that gap. Fine. But "the incumbent is too expensive for the low end" is the most consensus SaaS thesis in existence. Every vertical SaaS company tells this story. There's no asymmetry here — no category that sophisticated investors have dismissed, no discomfort creating mispricing. Smartbase is competing for a known segment of a known market against a known incumbent. The risk isn't in the price because the price already reflects reality: this is a modest outcome bet.

Smartbase's core technology — AI extraction from heterogeneous PO formats plus ERP API integration — is the exact thing I warn people about. The dossier states outright that "the core technology is technically reproducible." Conexiom launched an "AI-Powered Ideal Order Platform" in January 2025. Every ERP vendor with an API ecosystem — SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics 365 — could build native PO ingestion as a feature. The "why now" argument in the dossier is that LLMs got accurate enough and ERP APIs got open enough, which means the enabling conditions that make Smartbase possible make it possible for everyone simultaneously. When I say "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI," this is what I mean — Smartbase is pitching SAP for AI. SAP will eventually do this themselves, or Conexiom will drop a lighter-weight tier, and Smartbase's differentiation evaporates. Nine ERP integrations at a two-person team is ambitious engineering, but it's not a moat — it's a maintenance burden.

The bull case an advocate would make: Smartbase has five-figure annual contracts at pre-seed, which is genuinely strong early traction for a two-person team. Taira Fujioka was the youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference, building scalable ML infrastructure for JP Morgan and Salesforce — real technical credibility. Manufacturing is physical-world operations that software amplifies but cannot replace, fitting my Metropolis pattern. The ERP switching costs are real. And the founders' hands-on approach — literally working on factory floors — shows missionary commitment to understanding the customer. Maybe this is the next Procore, a boring vertical SaaS compounder that nobody gets excited about at demo day but quietly compounds to a multi-billion outcome. I take the physical-world argument seriously — Metropolis proved that AI layered on physical infrastructure can produce enormous outcomes. But Metropolis owns the parking infrastructure itself. Smartbase doesn't own the factory, the ERP, or the PO formats. Smartbase is a software layer sitting on top of other people's systems, processing other people's documents, pushing data into other people's databases. That's a feature, not an infrastructure play. The condition that would flip me: if Smartbase demonstrated that their per-customer AI models create a genuine compounding accuracy advantage that makes them measurably better with each deployment — something no generic LLM API call can replicate — and they showed 80%+ gross margins on those five-figure contracts with zero customer churn, I'd revisit the durability question.

Smartbase is a sensible team building a sensible product for a real problem, and that's exactly why I'm passing. Goldman and Fujioka aren't pitching me something insane — they're pitching me workflow automation, which is the most predictable AI application category in 2026. A calm person with a calm idea doesn't activate my framework. The capital discipline looks reasonable for pre-seed — two people, early revenue — but discipline on a small bet doesn't change the ceiling of the bet itself. Smartbase's outcome distribution, even in the best case, looks like a $50-100M ARR vertical SaaS company that gets acquired by Conexiom or an ERP vendor. That's a fine business. It's not my kind of bet. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Consensus Mispricing — there is zero narrative mispricing in "AI automates data entry for manufacturers" — and Structural Durability — Smartbase sits in the exact kill zone where incumbents add AI to their existing product and replicate the value proposition within 18 months.
