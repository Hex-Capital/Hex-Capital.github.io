# General Astronautics -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The leverage architecture here is the dealbreaker, and I want to address it directly. General Astronautics is building robotic hardware for sale to approximately five customers on the planet -- NASA, Axiom, Vast, Blue Origin's Orbital Reef, maybe JAXA. That's not a startup. That's a defense subcontractor without the lobbying infrastructure. Every unit requires custom integration with a specific station's physical configuration, power systems, and operational protocols. Revenue scales linearly with robots shipped. There are no network effects, no protocol dynamics, no code reaching millions of users. The company's own positioning as "platform-agnostic" -- able to work on any orbital facility -- sounds like flexibility but is actually an admission that they don't own the platform. Varda owns its spacecraft. Redwire owns its ISS manufacturing hardware. General Astronautics is proposing to be a vendor to other people's platforms, which is the structural opposite of the infrastructure plays I gravitate toward. When I invested in Alchemy, it was because every blockchain developer needed their API layer. When I backed Replit, it was because every aspiring programmer was a potential user. Here, the total addressable customer count fits on a Post-it note.

On specific knowledge: Schork has genuine robotics obsession -- winning Harvard's soft robotics design competition twice as a high schooler, publishing in *Soft Robotics* before college, and choosing SpaceX over easier paths. That trajectory signals authentic curiosity rather than career optimization, and I respect it. Zhou brings CMU's robotics program, which is world-class. But the domain they're entering -- autonomous laboratory operations in microgravity -- demands knowledge that neither founder appears to have accumulated yet. Zero-g fluid dynamics is its own physics. Biological sample handling protocols are their own discipline. NASA procurement is its own political economy. SpaceX Starlink laser testing and soft robotic actuators are adjacent to space robotics but not to this specific problem. The founders have general robotics capability applied to a space context, not deep experiential knowledge about microgravity science or orbital laboratory operations. Compare this to when I backed Extropic: Guillaume Verdon had spent years inside Google's quantum computing program before recognizing that thermodynamic approaches to computation could unlock fundamentally different leverage at the hardware layer. His knowledge was years ahead of any competitor's starting point. Here, I see talented young engineers who could learn this domain but haven't lived inside it yet.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If microgravity manufacturing scales -- and Varda's successful pharmaceutical reentry missions suggest it might -- then the labor bottleneck at $130K per astronaut-hour is a real constraint that someone will solve. General Astronautics could become the standard automation layer for orbital labs, the way Opentrons became standard for terrestrial wet labs. First-mover advantage in microgravity fluid handling could compound as operational data accumulates -- every pipetting protocol optimized for zero-g makes the system harder to replicate. And the founders' youth is a potential asset for a market that won't fully materialize until 2030+. They're building for a decade-out reality. If I squint, there's a version of this that looks like my ePlane Company investment -- a small bet on a physics-enabled disruption far outside my comfort zone, treated as optionality rather than a thesis investment.

But the ePlane analogy breaks down on leverage. Electric aviation, if it works, serves billions of potential passengers. Microgravity lab robots serve a handful of station operators. The contrarian timing angle is also weaker than it appears -- the idea that space manufacturing needs automation is consensus within the industry, not a secret insight. The real contrarian question is whether microgravity manufacturing itself will scale beyond niche pharmaceutical applications, and General Astronautics is downstream of that bet without controlling the answer. They're dependent on Axiom, Vast, and Orbital Reef hitting their timelines, on launch costs continuing to fall, and on microgravity manufacturing producing results valuable enough to justify the infrastructure. That's three layers of dependency before a robot purchase order gets signed. I make bets on founders who control their own destiny, not on companies whose timeline is gated by other companies' capital expenditure decisions.

I pass. The founders show real technical spark, and I'd watch them closely if they pivoted toward something with genuine leverage mechanics. But investing my own money in a hardware company with five potential customers, consensus market positioning, and founders whose specific knowledge -- while promising -- hasn't yet reached the depth this domain demands? That's not how compound interest works.
