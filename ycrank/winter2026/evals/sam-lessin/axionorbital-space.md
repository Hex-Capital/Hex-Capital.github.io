# AxionOrbital Space -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem I can't get past: the entire value proposition of AxionOrbital is a diffusion model. Remove the AI and there is literally no company. This is the anti-thesis of what I look for. My "cherry on top" test asks whether the business would be good if AI didn't exist — and the answer here isn't just "no," it's "there would be nothing." A foundation model that translates SAR radar imagery to optical-equivalent photos is technically impressive, but it's software doing software things, and the dossier itself acknowledges that "the core technique is reproducible by teams with deep learning and remote sensing expertise." A November 2025 systematic literature review catalogs numerous competing approaches. This is an active academic research area, not a proprietary breakthrough. When I said "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore," this is exactly the category I meant.

The founders clear my credibility bar without clearing my non-consensus bar. Atharva Peshkar's CV — PhD at CU Boulder, Harvard Visual Computing Group, "Best in Physics" at AAPM 2023, two-time Microsoft Imagine Cup champion — is genuinely strong for this specific problem. Dhenenjay Yadav's ISRO ML engineering background gives him direct radar domain knowledge. These are sane people. But the idea itself isn't insane — it's an obvious application of modern diffusion models to a well-known domain gap. Every remote sensing research group in the world knows SAR data is hard to interpret and that cross-modal translation is valuable. The sane-person-insane-idea pairing requires that second element to be genuinely non-consensus, the kind of pitch that makes most investors uncomfortable. "Use AI to make radar images look like photographs" doesn't provoke skepticism; it provokes a nod and a question about defensibility. That's a consensus idea, and consensus ideas have their returns priced in before the seed round closes.

The competitive dynamics are what really concern me. ICEYE is sitting at a $2.8 billion valuation with €600 million raised, owns 40+ SAR satellites, is already profitable at ~$250 million revenue, and has the exact customer relationships AxionOrbital needs. If SAR-to-optical translation is genuinely valuable — and I think it is — ICEYE builds it, acquires it, or partners for it. They have the training data (their own satellite constellation generates it continuously), the customer distribution (defense, intelligence, commercial), and the capital. Maxar/Vantor at $1.6 billion in revenue controls the optical side and might view this translation layer as threatening their core business — another competitive complexity a two-person team can't navigate. This is the Birchbox pattern I learned from the hard way: narrative magnitude without structural moat. The subscription box model was trivially replicable, and so is a diffusion model trained on publicly describable paired datasets.

The bull case would require AxionOrbital to establish ORION as the standard translation layer — a primitive that every SAR customer routes through, the way Stripe became the primitive for payments. If they could achieve protocol-like lock-in where defense agencies and hedge funds build their optical analysis pipelines on ORION's output format and API, switching costs would compound. The open-source MCP tool with 185 GitHub stars gestures at this developer-ecosystem strategy. And defense procurement does create real switching costs once you're embedded. If Dhenenjay's ISRO connections open doors to defense customers before the field catches up, and if the 19% FID improvement over state-of-the-art reflects a durable architectural advantage rather than a fleeting benchmark lead, this could work. But that requires maintaining a performance edge against an entire field of active research, landing defense contracts without disclosed clearances or LOIs, and outrunning incumbents who have every structural advantage except the model itself. That's too many simultaneous breakthroughs for my framework.

The product pivot also gives me pause. An Invstt listing approved November 2025 describes a "no-code IDE enabling geospatial developers to quickly download imagery, detect changes, and generate new insights" — a completely different product from the SAR-to-optical foundation model now being pitched to YC. Pivoting at pre-seed is normal, but pivoting TO an AI-first product during peak AI hype suggests building toward what investors want to fund rather than what customers want to buy. I look for founders with "rizz and tizz" — charisma plus relentless operational focus — and rapid repositioning toward the hot narrative is a signal I've learned to be cautious about. There are no disclosed customers, no LOIs, no revenue, and no evidence of anyone paying for this output yet. The benchmark numbers are real but benchmarks are academic currency, not customer currency.

I respect the technical depth here and the genuine domain expertise of both founders. In a different structural configuration — if they owned a SAR data source, or had embedded hardware in ground stations, or held exclusive data partnerships with constellation operators — I'd score the moat dimension entirely differently. But as it stands, this is a model company in a category where models get commoditized. I'd rather fund the company that owns the satellites and hires a team to build the translation layer than the team that builds the translation layer and hopes the satellite companies don't.
