# Cumulus Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the story before anything else. Cumulus is entering serverless GPU inference -- a market where Modal has raised $111M at a $1.1B valuation (now reportedly in talks at $2.5B), Baseten has raised ~$585M at a $5B valuation, Together AI has raised $533.5M with ~$300M in annualized revenue, and RunPod has crossed $120M+ ARR with 500K+ developers. That's over $1.3 billion in aggregate competitor funding. When I look for asymmetric returns, I look for markets that most investors dismiss as too boring, too niche, or too small -- PagerDuty was operations alerting nobody cared about, Gusto was payroll nobody found exciting. Serverless GPU inference in early 2026 is the exact opposite of that pattern. It's the most consensus-hot infrastructure category in venture capital right now. Everyone sees the inference market growing. Everyone is writing checks into it. That consensus pricing eliminates the asymmetric return structure I invest against.

The structural "why now" is real -- open-source LLMs (Llama, Mixtral, DeepSeek) created massive demand for self-hosted inference, and inference workloads overtook training as the dominant GPU spend category in 2025. The hyperscaler business model tension is also genuine: AWS and GCP profit from persistent GPU reservations and idle capacity, creating a structural disincentive to build true scale-to-zero billing. That's the kind of incumbent misalignment that creates openings for startups -- analogous to how PayPal's consumer model gave Stripe room to build a developer-first payments API. But this insight has already been captured. Modal, Baseten, and RunPod are all exploiting this exact tension with hundreds of millions in capital and established developer ecosystems. Cumulus isn't building ahead of a capability curve the way Harvey built legal AI before GPT-4 existed. They're entering after the market has crystallized around well-funded leaders.

The founders have genuinely strong domain credibility. Suryaa built a distributed GPU marketplace as Lead Engineer at TensorDock -- that's not adjacent experience, that's direct operational knowledge of GPU supply-side dynamics, pricing, and infrastructure orchestration. Veer ran production ML workloads for Space Force and NASA, providing customer-side understanding of the performance constraints Cumulus aims to solve. The fact that they've known each other since third grade mitigates one of my three startup killers (co-founder conflict). Their complementary supply-side and demand-side expertise is the strongest signal in this dossier, and it's the kind of founder-market fit that gets my attention in less crowded markets.

Here's what would have to be true for this to work: Ion, the proprietary inference engine, would need to deliver a durable and compounding performance advantage over both commercial competitors and the rapidly improving open-source stack (vLLM, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM). If Ion's optimizations accumulate customer-workload-specific intelligence that improves with scale -- creating a data flywheel similar to how Google's search quality improved with query volume -- that could be a genuine moat. The 4x cold start improvement over Modal (12.5 seconds vs. ~60 seconds on Flux 2) would need to hold as competitors invest in their own cold start optimization. And the market would need to be large enough to support multiple winners at scale, which at 35%+ CAGR is plausible. In that scenario, Cumulus could be the "better infrastructure" wedge that captures a meaningful segment of developers who want performance without the managed API provider's margin. But this requires sustained R&D investment against teams with 50-100x more engineering resources, and open-source inference engines are improving on release cycles measured in weeks, not years.

The single-miracle test is where I struggle most. Cumulus needs to simultaneously: (1) maintain a performance edge with their inference engine against much larger and better-funded engineering teams, (2) secure competitive GPU supply without significant capital for procurement, and (3) win developers away from platforms with established ecosystems and extensive documentation. The dossier notes the company's messaging has shifted between aggregating idle GPU capacity from distributed sources and positioning as a proprietary inference engine platform -- different architectures, different value propositions. That pivot signal combined with the multi-miracle requirement pushes this outside my comfort zone. This is a competent team in a real market, but the market structure rewards capital scale over technical elegance, and the opportunity is consensus rather than contrarian. I pass.
