# Parasma -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Sean Cole made 200,000 human brain cells play Doom, got Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, and Popular Science to write it up between February and April 2026, and then incorporated the company. That sequence -- artifact first, press second, company third -- is the dimensional signature Parasma carries: magically weird up top, software-on-someone-else's-wetware at the bottom. The pitch isn't "we built a model"; it's "we wrote the algorithm that taught living neurons to learn faster than the algorithm alone." I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, so I'm not investing in biocomputing the category -- I'm investing in the solo founder who is currently the most-cited name in the category before YC has even printed the batch list.

Crusoe burning North Dakota gas flares to mine Bitcoin is the HIGH rung on "magically weird" -- three uninvestable things stapled together, ignored by every credible fund on aesthetic grounds, and the founder was *of* the place. Parasma loads on the same axis: human neurons playing Doom in a Petri dish reads as either a sideshow or a Black Mirror episode to anyone who hasn't watched the video, which is exactly the deselection signal I want. The dossier shows zero competing VCs circling -- no oversubscribed seed, no Twitter froth, no "hot deal" tells -- and the named competitors are Cortical Labs ($11.6M total raised) and FinalSpark, both of which are wetware vendors, not algorithm shops. Where Parasma diverges from Crusoe is that Chase Lochmiller had a commodity moat *under* him -- flared gas is a physical input no software vendor can re-implement -- whereas Sean Cole's substrate is rented from the same Cortical Cloud Python interface that Cortical Labs sells to everyone else.

Cortical Labs ships the CL1 wetware at $35K per unit and the Python interface Sean Cole used for his Doom demo. That's the failure mechanism that sinks the Capital-Intensive dimension: the Pass Example I keep in my head -- the consensus YC AI agent wrapper where "the moat is the model, not the founder" -- is structurally adjacent to Parasma's risk. The wetware vendor can hire three ML engineers and absorb the algorithm layer natively, and the dossier flags exactly this ("Cortical Labs already ships the Cortical Cloud Python interface used by the founder for the Doom demo"). Parasma isn't *pure* AI-wrapper -- the substrate is exotic enough that the polymath generalist can't rebuild it in a quarter -- but it sits on someone else's well pad. Anduril owns its hardware. SpaceX owns its rockets. Parasma owns its training protocols and nothing else, which is why this dimension caps below the frontier-hardware rung even though the category screams hardware.

Sean Cole's biography is the load-bearing question on lived experience: an MS in Artificial Intelligence at Sussex plus, per ZME Science, "little experience in biological computing" prior to the Doom project. Counterfactually, what would push this score from "Invest" into "Strong Invest" territory is one more prior -- a year inside Cortical Labs, a published paper on spike-timing dependent plasticity, a Sussex thesis on the same substrate. What I *do* have is the Palmer Luckey pattern in miniature: Parasma's founder shipped the publicized artifact in the category before anyone else, which is the move that put Luckey in front of Founders Fund in 2017 after Oculus. Sean Cole's lived experience is one week deep, not ten years deep, but it is autobiographical -- he built the thing, he didn't memo it.

University of Sussex Facebook posted the Doom demo. The GitHub repo "doom-neuron" exists at github.com/SeanCole02. The Twitter bio at @vytalow reads "Made some brain cells play Doom." This is the Niantic-Ingress test inverted: I cannot install Parasma's product on my phone tonight, but I can watch the neurons stumble through Doom on YouTube via Scientific American's embed, and a normal human can comprehend what they are seeing in under thirty seconds. That tactile-via-video registers higher on physical-world verifiability than any developer-tools abstraction; the artifact is observable in the wild even if it isn't deployable in the wild.

The solo founder structure -- team size 1 per the YC page -- inverts the HQ Trivia failure I keep on the shelf. No 50/50 partnership, no unresolved tiebreaker, no Yusupov/Kroll feud waiting to detonate at Series A. The risk on this axis isn't governance; it's execution capacity, because Cortical Labs runs a wet lab in Melbourne and FinalSpark runs one in Vevey, and Sean Cole runs a laptop. Eighteen months out, this dimension compounds or fails on whether he can recruit a wet-lab co-conspirator without diluting CEO authority -- a knife edge, but the knife is sharper than the co-CEO trap on the other side.

My 36-month forecast: Parasma is either acquired by Cortical Labs for $8-25M in a talent-and-IP deal that lets the wetware vendor own the full stack, or Sean Cole raises a Series A on the strength of a second viral demo (something harder than Doom -- a control task, a robotics loop, a real-time inference benchmark) and Parasma becomes the "Cursor for biocompute" -- the algorithm layer that lives independently of any one substrate vendor. Dead is possible but unlikely on this timescale because the press distribution alone keeps the optionality alive for two cycles.

The bear case stings: the Hustle Fund profile flags founders without lived domain priors as exactly the replaceable-operator pattern I avoid, and Sean Cole acquired his biocompute context in approximately one week. Cortical Labs has a Series A closed in March 2026, a $35K hardware SKU, a Python SDK, and the institutional muscle to hire the next three Sean Coles before Parasma raises a Series A. The dossier says "no patents, contracts, or proprietary data assets found in public sources" -- meaning the moat today is Sean's head, and DARPA's O-CIRCUIT program is funding the public-good version of the same research directly into academia. What dismantles this bear case is the press distribution: Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, Popular Science, Interesting Engineering, ZME Science, and 80.lv all chose Sean Cole's name, not Cortical Labs', as the protagonist of the story -- and that authorship is harder to acquire than a wet lab. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict to pass is if Cortical Labs publishes its own "Cortical Cloud Algorithms" SDK in the next nine months; if that ships, the algorithm layer is commoditized at the platform, and Parasma's narrative moat collapses with it. Until then, this is the magically weird, solo, tactile-artifact bet I write checks into.
