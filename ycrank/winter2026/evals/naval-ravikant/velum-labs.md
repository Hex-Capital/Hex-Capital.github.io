# Velum Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Three product framings before launch — homomorphic encryption firewall, then "operating system for data quality," then "ontology engine for enterprise AI" — is the loudest signal in this dossier. Not because pivoting is inherently bad. Every great company iterates. But at the pre-revenue, pre-customer stage, the question isn't "have they found the right product?" It's "do these founders possess specific knowledge that will guide them to the right product?" When a team pivots from cryptographic access control to data observability to enterprise ontology construction, I'm watching someone search for a problem worth solving, not someone who has spent years living inside a problem and finally has the tools to solve it. The distinction matters. Searching founders find markets. Obsessed founders create them.

The specific knowledge test comes back thin. Benjamin brings physics and quantum computing from Stanford — strong quantitative foundations, but nothing that maps to the messy, unglamorous world of enterprise data pipelines breaking at 3 AM because a Salesforce schema drifted. Alen is more interesting: principal ML engineer at Dataroot, research in topological neural networks, a pinned GitHub repo called "ontology" that suggests genuine intellectual interest in graph-structured knowledge representation. That's real technical depth. But technical depth in ML is not the same as specific knowledge about how enterprises actually manage data quality. The people who know data quality in their bones are the ones who've spent years inside data engineering orgs watching Monte Carlo alerts fire on false positives, or manually writing Great Expectations suites across thirty microservices. I don't see that lived experience here. What I see are two talented young researchers applying powerful ML techniques to a category they've identified as important — which is how consultants think, not how domain experts think.

The leverage architecture is incremental. Velum automates the writing of data contracts that data engineers currently write by hand. That's genuine productivity improvement. But it doesn't create a new form of leverage — it doesn't enable a person who couldn't previously manage data quality to suddenly do so. Compare this to what Replit does for coding: it takes someone who has never written a line of code and gives them the ability to build software. That's leverage creation. Velum takes someone who already writes data contracts and makes them faster. That's optimization. The distinction between creating leverage and optimizing existing workflows is the difference between building a category and competing in one. Enterprise data engineers already have tools. Monte Carlo has raised $236M. Atlan has raised $206M. Great Expectations has significant open-source adoption. This is a crowded, well-funded, consensus category — the opposite of the asymmetric setup I need.

The strongest bull case: LLMs have crossed a capability threshold that makes automatic ontology derivation from live data traffic genuinely feasible for the first time. If that's true, and if Velum's ontology-first architecture is fundamentally incompatible with the anomaly-detection architectures of incumbents, then there's an architectural disruption play here — the incumbents can't retrofit, and Velum captures the next generation of data governance. The 50+ pre-built extractors, if real, represent meaningful integration work that compounds. And if enterprise AI adoption elevates data quality from an engineering concern to a board-level requirement, the market could expand faster than current estimates suggest. For this to work, though, the ontology derivation needs to be accurate enough to generate trustworthy contracts automatically — and I see no benchmarks, no case studies, no evidence that this works at enterprise scale. The core ML components (NER, relationship extraction, schema mapping) are available to anyone with a strong engineering team. Snowflake and Databricks are already building governance features natively. The bull case requires Velum to stay ahead of both well-funded startups and platform incumbents with a two-person team and $500K. The math doesn't favor them.

Two technically gifted founders building in a consensus category where the incumbents are well-funded and the platform players are closing in. No contrarian angle — everyone agrees data quality matters. No new form of leverage — just automation of existing workflows. No deep domain-specific knowledge that would take a competitor years to accumulate. Smart people, wrong setup for the kind of asymmetric returns I invest for.
