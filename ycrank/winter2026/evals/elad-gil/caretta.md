# Caretta -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing I see, and it tells me almost everything. Gong has $332 million in revenue, $584 million raised, and just closed a $250 million Series F in October 2025. Cresta has $276 million in funding. Salesken and Balto are both funded at scale and already doing real-time call assistance. This is not a non-obvious market -- it's one of the most consensus categories in enterprise software. When I look at the best investments I've made, they share a common feature: most other investors thought the market was wrong. PagerDuty seemed boring. Coinbase seemed speculative. Airbnb seemed weird. Nobody thinks "AI for sales calls" is weird or boring or niche. Every enterprise software investor I know has a thesis on conversation intelligence. That's a structural problem for a pre-seed company trying to compete here, because consensus markets attract consensus capital, and consensus capital funds well-resourced competitors who can move fast.

The "real-time vs. post-call" distinction is the core of Caretta's thesis, and the critical question is whether that's a category or a feature. When Gong decides to add real-time assistance -- and with $250 million fresh in the bank, the question is when, not if -- Caretta's primary differentiator becomes a checkbox on Gong's feature page. I've written about this dynamic as "market-ending moves": when the incumbent has the distribution, the customer relationships, and the capital to absorb your innovation into their platform, your differentiation evaporates. Gong already owns the buyer relationship with heads of revenue at thousands of companies. Adding real-time overlays to their existing call recording infrastructure is an engineering project, not a strategic pivot.

The bull case would require me to believe several things simultaneously. First, that real-time intervention is so architecturally different from post-call analytics that Gong would have to fundamentally re-architect to compete -- and the dossier's inference about this is plausible but unproven. Second, that the "compounding knowledge base" flywheel creates genuine switching costs before competitors replicate it. Third, that three TU Delft graduates with no enterprise sales experience and whose most notable prior work is a Discord bot with 30,000 users can navigate enterprise procurement cycles against incumbents with established sales organizations. That's at least three things that all need to break right. I'm counting miracles, and I'm getting past one.

The team shows technical capability -- wrapping NVIDIA's Parakeet model for transcription, building RAG-based retrieval, the VoysAI-to-Caretta iteration suggests they're learning and adapting. YC acceptance is a real signal. But founder-market fit concerns me. Enterprise B2B sales is a domain where buyer psychology, procurement workflows, and champion-building matter enormously. The founders don't appear to have operated inside a sales organization or sold into one. Building a Discord bot and building enterprise sales infrastructure are fundamentally different challenges. When I invested in companies like Gusto, the founders had lived inside the problem -- they understood payroll not just as a technology problem but as a compliance, HR, and trust problem. I don't see equivalent domain depth here.

On the product-to-distribution axis, I struggle to map the expansion path. Stripe started as payments but the developer integration became a channel for Atlas, Billing, Treasury, and Radar. What does Caretta distribute through its real-time call assistant? The knowledge base creates some switching costs -- customer-specific data that wouldn't transfer -- but switching costs are not the same as a distribution channel. This looks like a single-product company in a market where the product could be absorbed as a feature by any of four well-funded incumbents. The Slack integration is a sensible distribution tactic but not a platform strategy.

The technology timing is reasonable in the abstract -- LLM quality and speech-to-text latency have crossed thresholds that make real-time assistance viable. But unlike Harvey, where building ahead of the GPT-4 capability curve meant there were no credible competitors when the models caught up, Caretta is building at a moment when every conversation intelligence company has access to the same models and the same latency improvements. The structural moment isn't unique to Caretta. It's available to Gong, Salesken, Cresta, and any well-funded team that decides to build here. When the technology inflection benefits incumbents as much as entrants, it's not a startup advantage -- it's a market condition.
