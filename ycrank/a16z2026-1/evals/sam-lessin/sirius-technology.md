# Sirius Technology -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Sirius Technology is the platonic ideal of what I mean when I say AI seed investing has been a washout. An "autonomous revenue retention platform" that sits on top of Stripe and Chargebee, intercepts cancellation requests, and deploys AI-generated win-back offers. You know who has more data on subscription cancellations than Sirius Technology will ever accumulate? Stripe. Stripe processes the cancellation event. Stripe knows the payment history. Stripe has the billing relationship. Every time I hear "we integrated with Stripe to do X," my first question is: why won't Stripe just do X? And retention at the cancellation moment is not some weird edge case Stripe would never touch — it's a direct monetization opportunity for their platform. Chargebee already markets "revenue recovery" as a feature. Sirius Technology is building a feature for someone else's platform and calling it a company.

The $3M ARR claim with 200% NRR and 75% month-over-month growth — if those numbers are real, that's genuinely impressive execution. I'm not dismissing the hustle. But impressive execution in a commoditizable category is a trap, not a signal. You can grow fast when you're the first AI-native player in a niche, and then Stripe launches a native retention feature and your growth curve inverts overnight. LiveX AI, Subsets, Churned.io, QuadSci, ChurnZero, Gainsight — there are at least six funded competitors already targeting overlapping churn/retention use cases. This is not a market where the consensus is wrong. Every investor in 2026 understands "AI reduces churn" as a value proposition. There's no mispricing here. The risk isn't in the price — the risk IS the price, because everyone's already bidding.

The primitive test kills Sirius Technology cleanly. Churn reduction is not a new behavior. Customer success teams have been doing cancellation interception since the first subscription business existed. Gainsight built a billion-dollar company around it for B2B. What Sirius Technology offers is automation of an existing function — doing the same thing with less human labor. That's valuable, sure, but it's not a primitive. Compare this to Venmo: before Venmo, the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments didn't exist. Nobody was "doing Venmo manually." Sirius Technology is doing what customer success managers already do, faster and cheaper. That's an efficiency play, not category creation. Efficiency plays accrue to the platform with distribution, not the startup with the wrapper.

Azamat Khamidov and Benazir Toleubekova co-founded PointAI together before pivoting to Sirius Technology, which is a genuine positive — I care about co-founder relationships forged through actual shared work, not incubator matchmaking. Benazir's claimed experience architecting Alibaba's recommendation engine serving 500M daily users is exactly the kind of background you'd want for building personalization at scale, though the dossier notes this couldn't be independently verified. The problem isn't the founders — it's that even competent founders can't overcome structural category dynamics. A sane person pitching a sane idea in a consensus-hot category is just... fine. There's no insanity here. "Use AI to reduce subscription churn" is the kind of idea that gets a polite nod from every investor in the room. Nobody's telling these founders they're crazy. That's the tell.

The bull case I have to honestly consider: what if the data flywheel is real and defensible? Millions of daily interactions generating proprietary training data on cancellation-moment behavior, compounding model accuracy over time, creating a moat that's hard to replicate even for Stripe. And the "why now" has teeth — RevenueCat reported AI-powered apps churn 30% faster than traditional apps, meaning the pain is acute and growing. If Sirius Technology can lock in the consumer subscription vertical before billing platforms react, maybe the switching costs from deep Amplitude/Mixpanel/Salesforce integrations hold. But I keep coming back to the Solana contrast: Solana was a new consensus mechanism at the protocol layer — definitionally a new primitive that couldn't be absorbed by Ethereum as a feature. Sirius Technology is an application sitting on top of other people's protocols (Stripe's API, Chargebee's API). Applications get commoditized. Protocols compound. I invest in protocols.

The two dimensions that lock this as a pass are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability. On mispricing: a16z backed Sirius Technology, five-plus competitors are funded in overlapping retention/churn, and "AI reduces churn" is consensus-obvious — there is zero asymmetric risk/reward here. On durability: pure software wrapping billing platform APIs with no physical-world operations, no protocol-level lock-in, and direct platform risk from Stripe and Chargebee building native retention features. Sirius Technology may be a good business for a while, but it's not my kind of bet.
