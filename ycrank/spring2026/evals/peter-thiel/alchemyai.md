# Alchemy -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

CellProfiler is free. ImageJ is free. napari is free. Cellpose is free. Alchemy's entire competitive landscape on the research lab side consists of open-source tools maintained by the Broad Institute and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative — organizations with functionally unlimited funding horizons and no need to monetize. On the enterprise side, PathAI has raised $490 million and secured FDA approval for AISight Dx, Proscia has $130 million and 400% year-over-year case growth, and Leica's Aivia ships 22 built-in applications backed by Danaher's global distribution network. Alchemy occupies the precise position that destroys companies: squeezed between free tools that researchers already cite in publications and enterprise incumbents with regulatory moats and institutional sales forces. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?", Alchemy's implicit answer is "natural-language interfaces can replace scripting for microscopy pipelines." Every AI company in every vertical is making this identical claim. That is not a secret — it is the most consensus thesis in technology today.

Alchemy's core capability — LLM-driven orchestration of computer vision models for microscopy — relies on what the dossier explicitly identifies as "generally available foundation models and open-source segmentation tools." The barrier to replication is described as "moderate," which in practice means low. Any team with access to GPT-4 or Claude and familiarity with Cellpose can build a natural-language microscopy pipeline assembler. Alchemy has no proprietary model architecture, no unique training dataset, no physical-world technology component. Compare this to General Matter, where I took a personal board seat: enriching HALEU uranium requires centrifuge infrastructure, NRC licensing, DOE contracts, and SpaceX-grade engineering talent — barriers that compound over decades. Alchemy's barrier is typing speed. A well-resourced incumbent like Leica could integrate LLM-based natural-language workflows into Aivia in a single product cycle, and Alchemy would have no structural response because the technology is commodity.

Phillip Baek holds a BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin and an M.Ed. in Psychological Counseling from Columbia — a background that signals breadth, not the obsessive domain depth that definite conviction requires. Neither Baek nor co-founder Liam McBride has published research in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis; no Google Scholar or PubMed results were found for either founder. McBride's prior venture was BoBo, a shared-living mobile app that raised $20,000 in non-dilutive funding. Alchemy's founding team met through a student app project, not through years of shared intensity in a research lab or a prior high-stakes venture. The co-founder relationship was forged building a Flutter app, not surviving the crucible that produces the alignment my framework demands. I do not hear a founder who can describe exactly what the world looks like when Alchemy wins — I hear a team that identified a genuine pain point (a UC Santa Cruz PhD student's 3-hour analysis reduced to 10 minutes) and is iterating toward product-market fit. That is lean-startup thinking, not definite optimism.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Alchemy targets a real gap: academic and biotech researchers who cannot afford Aivia's enterprise licensing and lack the scripting skills for CellProfiler. A 98% reduction in analysis time is a genuine value proposition, and bottom-up PLG through individual researchers could create organic institutional expansion — the Slack playbook applied to lab software. If Alchemy builds a proprietary model library from researcher contributions, network effects could emerge where each new user makes the platform more valuable. The $3.13 billion live cell imaging market growing at 8.68% CAGR provides a real addressable category. But this bull case collapses under the monopoly test. Alchemy's serviceable segment — AI-powered research microscopy software for labs that won't pay enterprise prices and can't use free tools — is a fraction of $3.13 billion. Even complete monopolization of that niche produces a modest outcome, not the power-law magnitude that justifies the opportunity cost of the investment. And the network-effects thesis remains purely speculative at pre-seed — the dossier marks it "[Inferred]" and "unproven at this stage."

No Known Exception rescues Alchemy from my framework. The Stemcentrx override required a genuine contrarian scientific insight that the establishment actively rejected — Alchemy's thesis is not rejected, it is consensus. The Lyft override required a category generating hundreds of billions in total value where even the number-two player produces fund-relevant returns — the research microscopy software market has no such magnitude. The Clearview override required an unreplicable proprietary dataset — Alchemy has no dataset moat. I would reconsider if Alchemy demonstrated one specific condition: a proprietary foundation model for biological image analysis trained on data no competitor can access, combined with institutional contracts (NIH core facilities, DOD biodefense laboratories, or top-20 pharma companies) that create the kind of operational embedding I value at Palantir and Anduril. Without either proprietary technology or institutional lock-in, Alchemy is a UX layer on commodity infrastructure competing with free alternatives — and competition, as I have said, is for losers.

Alchemy scores lowest on the two dimensions that matter most: Monopoly Secret and Physical-World Technology Moat. The absence of a contrarian insight — combined with generally available technology, free open-source competitors, and well-funded enterprise incumbents — places Alchemy in the category of companies swept up by the AI trend rather than built on hard analysis of what specifically they know that the market does not. The 719 LinkedIn followers, single PhD endorsement, and absent revenue signal a company at the earliest possible stage of validation, which at pre-seed I would not penalize — but I cannot invest in a structural position that execution alone cannot remedy. Pass.
