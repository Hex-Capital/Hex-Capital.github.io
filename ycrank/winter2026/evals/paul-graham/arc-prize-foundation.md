# ARC Prize Foundation -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about ARC Prize Foundation is a structural conflict that, left unaddressed, could unwind everything else that's right about it. François Chollet and Mike Knoop co-founded this nonprofit to create the definitive benchmark for measuring AI progress toward general intelligence. They also co-founded Ndea, a for-profit AI lab building program synthesis systems — systems that would be evaluated on the very benchmarks ARC Prize creates. This is like creating the SAT and the test prep company simultaneously. No public governance policy separating the two entities was found in the dossier. For an organization whose entire value proposition rests on neutrality and independence, this is a load-bearing omission.

Set that aside and the organic problem discovery here is about as deep as it gets. Chollet didn't read about benchmark saturation in TechCrunch and decide to start a company. He published the theoretical framework for why existing benchmarks are inadequate ("On the Measure of Intelligence," 2019), created the ARC-AGI benchmark itself, and built the organization around his own intellectual work. This is the founder-problem equivalent of what Drew Houston had with Dropbox — except Chollet didn't just notice a broken USB drive, he wrote the paper explaining why all USB drives are broken. The schlep is real but unusual: it's not banking regulations or logistics. It's maintaining benchmark difficulty against exponentially improving AI systems (ARC-AGI-1 SOTA jumped from 33% to 55.5% in a single competition year), coordinating competing labs who each have incentives to game the evaluation, and sustaining a nonprofit that burns through $1M+ prize pools annually with no revenue model. Most researchers would publish their paper and move on. Building the institution around it is the schlep that makes this defensible.

The evidence of wanting is genuinely strong for a pre-seed nonprofit. OpenAI used ARC-AGI to evaluate o3 before its public announcement. Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI have adopted it as a reference benchmark. These are not casual endorsements — the biggest AI labs in the world are voluntarily measuring their systems against Chollet's yardstick. The competitions attracted 1,400+ teams in back-to-back years. Eight organizations with $1M+ in funding are specifically working on ARC-AGI solutions. Sam Altman is doing a fireside at the ARC-AGI-3 launch event. When the people building frontier AI systems choose your benchmark as their measuring stick, that's the nonprofit equivalent of economic dependency — they need credible third-party evaluation, and they've chosen this one.

On the founder quality axis, Chollet is one of those rare technical founders who build things the whole field depends on. Keras has 63.9K GitHub stars and was the default high-level API for TensorFlow. His Xception paper has 18,000+ citations. He's a hacker who made something people want at global scale — twice, if you count ARC-AGI separately. Knoop built Zapier to $310M in revenue. These are not founders who need to prove they can execute. But the attention split worries me. Both Chollet and Knoop are simultaneously building Ndea. The person actually running ARC Prize day-to-day is Greg Kamradt, who has relevant skills (Salesforce, YouTube community) but is not the intellectual architect. This creates a fragility: if Chollet's attention shifts primarily to Ndea, the foundation loses the person whose credibility IS the moat.

The bull case is that ARC Prize Foundation becomes the NIST of AI — the standard-setting body that every lab, regulator, and researcher relies on to measure progress toward general intelligence. LMArena raised $100M at a $600M valuation for crowdsourced preference voting, which is a much simpler problem. If independent AI evaluation becomes as critical as credit rating agencies became to finance, the organization with Chollet's theoretical authority and existing lab adoption has a structural advantage that's genuinely difficult to replicate. The nonprofit structure, usually a limitation, is actually the right architecture here: labs won't trust a for-profit evaluator (as Epoch AI's controversy over undisclosed OpenAI funding demonstrated). The fact that Andy Fang, Dharmesh Shah, and Aaron Levie are already donors suggests the philanthropic funding model can work at meaningful scale.

But I keep coming back to two things. First, the nonprofit structure means there's no financial return for an angel investor — this is a donation, not an investment, and my framework is built for evaluating bets with asymmetric upside. Second, the Ndea conflict creates a credibility time bomb. If Ndea's systems start scoring well on ARC-AGI benchmarks, every competitor and every skeptical researcher will question the independence of the evaluation. The founders need to either separate these entities cleanly with published governance policies, or choose one. You can be the referee or the player, but not both. Chollet's authority is the single most valuable asset this organization has, and the Ndea dual role puts it at risk.
