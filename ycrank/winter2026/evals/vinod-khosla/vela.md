# Vela -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Vela: these are genuinely exceptional young founders building something whose consequence of success is a moderately useful SaaS business. Gobhanu Korisepati founded a microfinance institution at fourteen that reached 77 countries and disbursed $400K+ in microloans. His brother completed Carnegie Mellon's CS program in two years with highest honors and shipped production code on AWS's ML Supercompute team. That kind of talent and learning rate is rare. And they're applying it to... making interview scheduling fifteen minutes faster. When I see founders this capable choosing a problem this small, my instinct isn't to evaluate the product -- it's to tell them they're wasting their best years on the wrong target.

Let me be direct about the consequence magnitude. If Vela completely succeeds -- captures the entire AI scheduling coordination market, automates every recruiter and sales team's calendar -- the world doesn't operate any differently. Interviews still happen. Recruiters still exist. The hiring process is slightly more efficient. The company's own value proposition is framed as saving 43.5 hours per month per recruiter. That's a workflow optimization metric, not an industry transformation metric. Compare this to what I look for: Impossible Foods was about reclaiming billions of acres of arable land. Commonwealth Fusion was about replacing fossil fuel power generation entirely. Square didn't just make payments faster -- it made the entire traditional POS infrastructure economically obsolete for small merchants. Vela makes scheduling faster. The ceiling on this is a well-run B2B tool, not a company that changes how the world works.

The technology question makes this harder, not easier. The dossier itself identifies the core vulnerability: "Any team with LLM API access and calendar integrations could build a basic scheduling agent." When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router -- that dismissal was the signal that the technology represented structural disruption, not incremental improvement. The situation with Vela is the opposite. Google is actively integrating Gemini into Calendar. Microsoft is building Copilot into Outlook. Apple Intelligence is being woven into their calendar stack. The incumbents aren't dismissing this approach -- they're shipping it. When every major platform company is building your product as a feature, you're not disrupting an incumbent system. You're racing companies with a billion existing users and unlimited engineering resources. That's a red flag in my framework, not a validation signal.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to engage with it honestly -- goes something like this: Vela isn't really a scheduling company. It's building the first autonomous AI agent that negotiates across every communication channel on your behalf. Scheduling is the wedge into a much larger coordination platform. The enterprise logos (Netflix, LinkedIn, AWS, Scale AI) at pre-seed suggest genuine product-market pull. "Thousands of interviews per week" is real volume, and the accumulated scheduling context data creates a compounding advantage. If you squint, this is what Square was early on -- a tiny hardware dongle for credit card processing that turned into a $40 billion financial services platform. The sibling founders with a decade of shared operational history could be the kind of high-trust team that executes through the inevitable platform expansion. For this bull case to work, though, I would need evidence that the founders see scheduling as a narrow beachhead for something structurally larger, and the dossier frames the product squarely as a scheduling tool. The market they're in -- AI meeting assistants at $2.76 billion, growing at 9.4% CAGR -- isn't the kind of exponential curve I chase. It's a mature category being incrementally improved.

What troubles me most is the pattern recognition. This has all the signatures of what I warn founders about: they've reduced the probability of failure by choosing a well-understood problem, a proven approach, established buyer personas, and a market where customer willingness to pay is obvious. The result is that the probability of building a decent business is relatively high -- maybe they reach $10-20M ARR, maybe they get acquired. But they have also reduced the consequence of success to something that doesn't justify the talent in the room. The traction signals are real, the team is capable, and the product apparently works. None of that changes my fundamental concern. I have no interest in funding a company where the best outcome is a feature that Google ships for free in eighteen months.
