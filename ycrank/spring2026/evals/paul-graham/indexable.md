# Indexable -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft game engine that handles 10,000 concurrent players in a single world. That's the kind of project nobody builds for market reasons. You build Hyperion because the problem of synchronizing state across ten thousand concurrent connections at low latency is the kind of thing that keeps you up at night — because you find it genuinely beautiful to make work. The 1,000+ GitHub stars, the #1 spot on r/rust, the 221 repositories — this is someone who has passed right through professional into obsessive. When I look at Indexable, the founder signal is the strongest thing in the dossier. Gazelka was Member of Technical Staff at xAI, co-founded BountyBot (acquired), and is now building a custom hypervisor from KVM up with a modified Linux 6.19 kernel and a Nix build system reimplemented in Rust. The technical ambition here is not normal. What I can't find, though, are the stories. I want to know what happened when BountyBot almost died. I want to know what broke at xAI that made him quit to build Indexable. The dossier gives me the resume but not the narratives of creative survival under constraint, and those narratives are what separate the technically excellent from the relentlessly resourceful.

Indexable's approach — building a custom VMM rather than wrapping Firecracker like E2B, Daytona, and Blaxel — reminds me of the decision Patrick Collison made with Stripe. In 2010, there were payment processors. PayPal existed. Braintree existed. Everyone else was building wrappers. The Collisons decided to build the infrastructure layer from scratch because they understood that the abstraction everyone else was wrapping was itself the problem. Indexable is making the same kind of bet: that Firecracker microVMs are not the right primitive for AI agent sandboxes, and that millisecond forking of full VM state — files, processes, memory, databases — requires a purpose-built hypervisor. This is textbook schlep blindness. Most founders in the AI sandbox space look at Firecracker and say "good enough." Gazelka looked at it and said "wrong abstraction." The custom kernel patches, the content-addressable storage engine with cross-tenant deduplication, the libfabric storage fabric — Indexable is doing the painful, unglamorous systems work that creates genuine technical moats. I'd estimate 12-18 months for a competitor to replicate this stack from scratch.

E2B has $21M in funding and claims 88% Fortune 100 adoption. Daytona raised $24M and hit $1M forward ARR in under three months. Modal is valued at $1.1B. Blaxel raised $7.3M with 25ms cold starts. Indexable has approximately $500K from YC, a website that returns HTTP 403, a GitHub organization with zero stars, and no public users. The competitive landscape is the obvious bear case, and it's a real one. But the market itself being hot is not what concerns me — AI agent infrastructure is genuinely exploding, E2B processed "hundreds of millions" of sandbox sessions since October 2024, and I've said publicly that the most impressive YC companies don't have to be about AI. What concerns me is that Indexable is entering this market as a functionally solo founder. The YC page lists only Gazelka despite a team of three. I have a hard time funding solo founders, and I've written about why: starting a startup is too hard for one person, you need someone to talk you out of stupid decisions. A custom hypervisor with a modified kernel maintained by one decision-maker with no co-founder to challenge architectural choices — that concentrates risk in a way that makes me uncomfortable.

Indexable's origin story is absent from the dossier, and that bothers me. Gazelka worked at xAI, which makes it plausible he encountered the need for forkable sandboxed environments firsthand — AI training and inference pipelines generate exactly this kind of infrastructure pain. But plausible is not the same as demonstrated. When Drew Houston pitched Dropbox, he told the USB drive story in the first thirty seconds. The best founders can't help telling you about the moment of frustration that started everything. The dossier captures no such moment for Indexable. Maybe Gazelka has one and it simply wasn't captured in public sources — pre-seed companies with no press coverage don't generate much narrative data. But the absence is itself mildly negative, because truly organic discovery usually produces a story that founders share at every opportunity.

The bull case for Indexable is compelling and specific: Gazelka has the exact technical DNA required (high-concurrency Rust systems programming, kernel-level competence, prior exit), the approach creates a genuine moat that Firecracker wrappers cannot replicate incrementally, the market is growing at 45% CAGR, and the schlep of building a custom VMM functions as a natural competitor filter. If Indexable demonstrates that millisecond full-state forking enables agent workflows that are impossible on Firecracker — branching exploration, speculative execution, instant rollback — the switching cost argument inverts: developers won't switch away from the only platform that supports their architecture. This is the Stripe playbook: build the right primitive, and the ecosystem builds on you. The condition that would flip me to Invest is evidence that Gazelka has found a genuine co-founder (not an employee, but someone with equity and decision-making authority who challenges his architectural choices) combined with even a handful of developers using Indexable's fork semantics in production and refusing to go back. Those two signals would address both the solo-founder risk and the organic-demand question simultaneously.

Indexable scores highest on earnest builder identity — Gazelka's 221 repositories, the Hyperion project, the nushell contributions, and the decision to build a VMM from scratch rather than wrap Firecracker all point to someone who builds compulsively and cares about getting the abstraction right. The schlep-blindness dimension of the idea is genuinely strong, pushing the "repels before attracts" score above neutral. But the solo-founder structure and the absence of resourcefulness narratives or organic-discovery evidence keep this in mixed-signal territory. The decisive dimensions are Founder Resourcefulness (where the resume is strong but the survival stories are missing) and Organic Problem Discovery (where the xAI background is suggestive but no origin narrative exists). I'd watch Indexable closely and revisit after Demo Day.
