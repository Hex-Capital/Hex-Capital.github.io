# Hexa -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Hexa's signature is a clean single-miracle architecture married to a founder duo I can't verify shipped anything before -- Ishaan Makkar and Auri Nayak surface in search only as LinkedIn headlines and a hackathon listing ("Aurideep Nayak, Hexa YC X26" on Luma), with no GitHub, no prior product named, no Twitter. That pairing -- right-shaped thesis, unknown executors -- is what I have to price.

The market-re-opening test is where Hexa loads most of its weight. The catalyst the dossier names is agentic tool-use crossing a usability threshold for multi-step ERP workflows, with SupplyChainBrain citing "76% of supply chain professionals believe autonomous AI agents are ready to manage core operational tasks" -- that's a real 24-month window, not a steady-state TAM pitch. **Decagon** (2023) had the same shape: post-LLM customer support was a catalyzed re-opening, and the workflow layer on top compounded. Where Hexa diverges from the Decagon rung is sharpness: Decagon's catalyst was a single discontinuous capability (LLM dialog), whereas Hexa's catalyst is the softer "agents now reliable enough for ERP plumbing" -- credible, but the same window is funding Didero ($30M Series A, Feb 2026), Traza ($2.1M), and every ERP incumbent's native agent roadmap. The window is open; it's also crowded.

The Founder-Ships rung is where Hexa is weakest, and the failure pattern I worry about is the **Lyft** Series C pass mechanism inverted: not winner-take-all, but pedigree-without-artifact. Aravind at **Perplexity** (2022) earned my check by showing up a week after every conversation with a built thing -- that rhythm was the legible signal at pre-seed. The Hexa dossier offers no prior-startup, no shipped side project, no GitHub repo for either Makkar or Nayak; the only authored artifact is a LinkedIn snippet quoting "54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets." That's a thesis statement, not a shipped product. In a Spring 2026 batch where the founders who will become Aravinds are already publicly shipping demos on X, this opacity is itself the signal -- not absent data, but a quiet data point.

Hexa's $9,000 MRR from 3 customers in 6 weeks is what "just working" looks like at the lowest possible resolution -- real dollars, real buyers, ~$3K/mo ACV that implies SMB-distributor budget tier rather than enterprise. **Harvey** (2023) at the comparable point was unmistakably compounding; Hexa has no MoM curve disclosed and 33% revenue concentration per logo. Over 18 months this dimension compounds two ways: if Hexa adds a fourth and fifth customer at the same 2-week cadence, it's a $30K MRR pre-seed story that gets marked up; if churn hits any one of the three before logo five lands, it's a $6K MRR story that doesn't raise. The velocity is plausible, not yet confirmed.

Hexa's index-position claim is "OS for Manufacturers and Distributors" spanning sales+procurement+finance+CS, versus Didero's procurement-only wedge -- and the counterfactual that would flip my read here is whether Hexa actually sits in the *quote-to-cash* workflow path of every distributor, or whether the breadth is a positioning slide that fragments into four mediocre features. **Stripe** (2010) earned the index label because every e-commerce entrant had to touch payments; for Hexa to earn it, every industrial distributor would have to touch RFQ-and-PO automation, and the SAP/NetSuite/Epicor integrations listed on hexaagents.com would have to become genuinely sticky data graphs rather than thin connectors. Today the dossier names breadth without depth evidence.

Hexa's single-miracle scoring is the cleanest part of the analysis -- the architecture maps to **Color Genomics** (2015): assume one validated capability (LLM extraction + tool-use accuracy on structured documents and ERP APIs) and the remaining work is GTM and integration breadth. No new physics, no regulatory miracle, no consumer-behavior miracle. This is the *workflow-infrastructure-on-top-of-hard-thing* pattern I run, and on this axis alone Hexa screens cleanly.

My 36-month forecast on Hexa: acqui-hired by an ERP-adjacent buyer (NetSuite/Oracle, Epicor, or Didero itself) at sub-$50M, or dead. The mechanism: Didero's $30M war chest at ~37x Hexa's capitalization, targeting the same "manufacturers and distributors who need to source raw materials" ICP (TechCrunch, Feb 2026), wins the enterprise distributor logos while Hexa stays stuck in the $3K/mo SMB tier where 3-customer concentration kills you on any single churn.

The bull case that stings: Didero is procurement-only and Hexa's broader OS scoping -- sales-side quoting plus procurement plus finance reconciliation plus CS -- might actually be the correct wedge because industrial distributors are sales-and-procurement businesses, not procurement businesses. If that's right, Didero is mis-scoped and Hexa's $9K MRR across full-stack workflows is a more durable signal than Didero's bigger raise on a narrower surface. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway is founder opacity -- the OS-scope thesis is harder to execute than the procurement-only thesis, and I have zero shipping artifacts from Makkar or Nayak to underwrite the harder execution. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest: Hexa publishes a shipped-artifact trail (a public changelog, a GitHub, a Loom demo cadence) AND clears $30K MRR with logo five by month four, at which point founder opacity stops mattering and I'll write the check at consensus seed.
