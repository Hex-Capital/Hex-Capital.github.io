# ZeroSettle -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about ZeroSettle: both founders spent their careers inside Apple, building the very operating systems that enforce the 30% commission, and now they're building a tool to route around it. That's not an outsider attacking a broken system — that's an insider exploiting a regulatory crack in their former employer's wall. I've said repeatedly that industry insiders carry "a set of biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing." These founders know Apple's StoreKit APIs cold, which makes the SDK technically sound. But their imagination is bounded by the system they came from. They're not asking "why does Apple control distribution?" — they're asking "how do we help developers keep 25 more cents on the dollar?" That's an optimization, not an invention.

Let me be direct about the consequence magnitude. If ZeroSettle succeeds completely — captures a dominant share of the alternative billing migration market — what changes? Developers retain more revenue. Apple's commission pool shrinks. Consumers might save a few dollars on subscriptions. The world still works exactly the same way: people download apps, subscribe through paywalls, and pay monthly fees. Nobody's life is materially different. Compare this to Square, which didn't just save merchants money on processing — it enabled millions of businesses that couldn't accept cards to participate in the economy for the first time. That's a structural transformation. ZeroSettle is a toll reduction on an existing toll road. The $27 billion in Apple commissions sounds massive as a TAM, but ZeroSettle's role is redistributing margin from Apple to developers, not creating new value. I've backed companies because the problem was too important not to work on. "App developers pay too much commission" is a legitimate business problem, but it's not that kind of problem.

The strongest case for ZeroSettle is timing. The April 2025 contempt ruling, the EU Digital Markets Act, Japan's new law — there's a genuine multi-jurisdictional regulatory wave opening a market that was literally illegal to serve eighteen months ago. That's real. And the structural conflict is clever: Apple can't offer this solution without cannibalizing $27 billion, and RevenueCat can't offer it without endangering its Apple relationship. There's a genuine window where a focused startup can move before incumbents adapt. If I squint, this looks like the early days of Juniper — exploiting a structural opening the dominant player can't close. But the analogy breaks down on magnitude. Juniper's TCP/IP routers made Cisco's legacy architecture structurally obsolete — the entire telecom industry had to migrate. ZeroSettle doesn't make Apple's billing system obsolete; it offers a cheaper alternative alongside it. And the window could close: the Ninth Circuit remanded the question of whether Apple can charge a "reasonable fee" on linked-out purchases. If Apple imposes even a 10-12% commission on alternative billing, the savings gap between 30% and ZeroSettle's 5% narrows to maybe 15 points, and the hassle of migrating subscribers may not be worth it for most developers.

The commoditization risk here is severe, and it's the kind of risk I learned about the hard way with KiOR — technology that works but lacks durable economic advantage. The dossier itself notes the "core SDK technology is replicable." Stripe already published a guide for iOS developers to accept payments outside the App Store. Paddle raised at a $1.4 billion valuation and explicitly cited Apple's ecosystem opening as a growth driver. Neon Pay has $14 million from Thrive and a16z. RevenueCat, with $119 million in funding and 70,000+ apps, could build this in months if the Apple relationship risk subsides. ZeroSettle's value-add layer — the "Switch & Save" campaigns, retention flows, A/B testing — is product craft, not technology moat. When Stripe decides to wrap those features around its payment infrastructure, ZeroSettle becomes a feature, not a company. The MoR switching costs provide some stickiness, but that's a defensive moat, not a transformational one.

The bull case I have to genuinely consider: ZeroSettle could become the default payment layer for mobile commerce. If they move fast enough during this regulatory window, sign thousands of developers through the free Founding Partner Program, and accumulate enough optimization data on conversion and retention, they could build network effects that matter. The MoR position means every migrated subscriber is a switching cost. And if the regulatory trajectory continues globally — more countries forcing Apple to open billing — ZeroSettle's jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction compliance infrastructure becomes genuinely valuable. A world where 30-40% of iOS subscription revenue flows through alternative billing and ZeroSettle owns the infrastructure for that flow — that's a multi-billion-dollar business. But even in that best case, it's a very good payments company. It's not Square. It's not changing who can participate in an economy. It's changing the fee structure of an existing transaction.

I pass on companies where the founders have narrowed the problem enough that success is plausible but the consequence of that success doesn't justify the effort of my attention. Two smart Apple engineers building a well-timed regulatory arbitrage product with replicable technology, facing well-funded competitors converging on the same opportunity, in a market whose size depends on the outcome of ongoing litigation — that's a venture bet, just not my venture bet. The problem isn't big enough, the approach isn't contrarian enough, and the technology isn't disruptive enough.
