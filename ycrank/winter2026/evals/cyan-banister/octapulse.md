# OctaPulse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What catches me first about OctaPulse isn't the founders or the market — it's that they're building robots that physically handle live fish. Delta robots with soft-grip compliant actuators, picking up juvenile trout in a wet, corrosive hatchery, while a vision system inspects each one in under thirty seconds. That's the kind of specific, unglamorous, hard-to-replicate work that most investors scroll right past. Nobody at a demo day gets excited about fish hatchery robotics. And that's actually the part I find most promising — it reminds me of the pattern I saw with Crusoe Energy, where the initial reaction from almost everyone was "why would anyone do that?" The competitors in aquaculture AI are all pointing cameras at fish swimming in ocean pens and calling it innovation. OctaPulse is the only team I can find that's actually combining physical manipulation with vision at the hatchery stage. That's a genuinely different problem, and it's the kind of unsexy niche where durable companies can grow before anyone notices.

But when I try to hear the founder's story — the thing I always listen for first — I get a gap I can't quite fill. Paul Grech comes from Bloomberg, did an MBA at CMU Tepper, has a Robotics Institute fellowship. Rohan Singh worked at ASML, Nvidia, Tesla, Toyota, and is doing graduate robotics at CMU. These are impressive credentials. They met at an entrepreneurship happy hour to discuss ocean technology. The dossier mentions "personal motivation rooted in growing up in coastal communities" — Grech from Beacon, New York; Singh from Goa, India. I can see how coastal upbringing creates awareness of the ocean, but I'm not hearing the biographical thread that tells me these founders will stay in the trenches of fish farming through the brutal middle years. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else ignored — his biography and the problem were inseparable. Here, I see two talented CMU grad students who identified a smart market opportunity. That's different from someone who can't imagine doing anything else.

The "making invisible systems visible" pattern is here, and it's real. Right now, a trained technician eyeballs each fish by hand — five minutes per fish, subjective judgment, no data trail. OctaPulse creates a digital record of every fish's phenotype, every deformity, building a dataset that becomes the backbone of a farm's selective breeding program. That's transparency where opacity existed. It's the same structural insight as Carta replacing spreadsheet cap tables or Affirm showing you your actual interest rate. And once a farm's genetics program depends on your historical data, switching costs get genuinely high. I find that moat logic more compelling than the vision models themselves, which use standard architectures any well-funded team could replicate.

The bull case I keep turning over: this could be an infrastructure play at a genuine inflection point. Aquaculture just passed wild-catch globally. Edge AI hardware finally enables real-time inference in a power-constrained barn in rural Idaho. They already have a six-figure annual contract with Riverence — the largest trout producer in North America — which is meaningful traction for a pre-seed company founded in 2025. If they nail species generalization and prove the system works on salmon, sea bass, and other commercial species, the land-and-expand path into Chile, Norway, and Scotland is real. The competitors were all acquired or pivoted, leaving a genuinely open lane. And the hardware-plus-software combination in harsh environments creates the kind of deployment know-how that's hard to commoditize. If I squint, I can see a world where OctaPulse becomes the operating system for hatchery production the way AKVA Group became the infrastructure layer for ocean pens.

But I keep coming back to my core filter. My best investments have come from founders where the problem was personal — where I could feel in the room that this person had been shaped by the exact pain they were solving. The most promising signal here is the weirdness of the niche and the early commercial traction, not the founder-problem bond. I'd want to sit with Paul and Rohan and hear the version of their story that the dossier doesn't capture — maybe there's something deeper about Goa's fishing communities or Beacon's connection to the Hudson River ecosystem that would change my read. As it stands, this is a smart bet by capable people in a genuinely underserved market, and I respect the execution. But it doesn't give me the jolt I felt with Niantic or the conviction I had writing that first check to SpaceX. It's a pass for now, though I'd take the meeting and listen for what I might be missing.
