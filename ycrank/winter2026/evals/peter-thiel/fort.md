# Fort -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Whoop acquired Push -- the most direct predecessor to Fort's product -- in 2021 for its wrist-based velocity tracking technology, and then killed the hardware. This is the single most important fact in the entire dossier, and it cuts both ways. The optimistic reading is that Whoop's subscription-oriented business model couldn't accommodate a niche strength product, leaving a gap Fort can exploit. The pessimistic reading -- and the one I find more persuasive -- is that a company with $454 million in capital and 260 million dollars in recurring revenue evaluated this exact product concept from the inside and concluded it wasn't worth pursuing as standalone hardware. When a well-funded incumbent acquires your closest analog and shelves it, you should have a very clear theory of why you know something they don't. I don't see that theory here.

The first question I always ask is: what is the secret? Fort's implicit thesis is that heart rate is the wrong primary metric for fitness wearables, and that movement kinematics will create the next great platform. But this is not a secret -- it is a known limitation that every serious lifter, every exercise physiologist, and every product manager at Whoop and Garmin already understands. The fact that Whoop's own data shows a 122% year-over-year increase in strength workouts means the entire industry is watching this trend. A secret must be something most intelligent people actively disagree with. "Heart rate doesn't capture muscular effort well" is something everyone agrees with. The deeper question -- whether this known limitation creates a monopoly-scale business opportunity in a standalone wearable -- is where the real debate lies, and I find the monopoly case unconvincing. IMU-based exercise detection is published in academic literature. Push Band demonstrated velocity-based training measurement years ago. The technology is competent engineering, not proprietary discontinuity. If Fort proves the market, Whoop already owns the acquired IP to respond within a product cycle, and Apple or Garmin can add comparable features as software updates to hardware they've already shipped to millions of wrists.

The founders deserve genuine respect. Three Tesla and SpaceX engineers who have shipped complex hardware at scale -- battery cells for Cybertruck, sensing systems for Semi and Robotaxi, satellite structures at SpaceX -- represent exactly the team you'd want building a consumer electronics product. Miranda Nover's personal connection to the problem (she met Zac at a gym) suggests authentic domain engagement, not consultant-derived market analysis. This is not a team of "men in suits." But strong execution capability is necessary, not sufficient. I've seen brilliant engineers build excellent products in markets that structurally prevent monopoly returns. The question is not whether they can build it -- they clearly can -- but whether building it creates something that compounds into an unassailable position. A wearable at $289 with an $80 annual subscription, competing against Whoop's zero-hardware subscription model and backed by no network effects, no platform lock-in, and no structural switching costs beyond data history, does not describe a monopoly. It describes a good product company.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: first, that strength training is not just growing but becoming the dominant exercise modality such that a strength-first wearable captures a market comparable to what Whoop built for recovery; second, that the per-rep kinematics data Fort collects creates a compounding data advantage that makes its algorithms structurally better over time, analogous to how Palantir's government integrations deepen with deployment; and third, that Fort's multi-placement form factor (wrist, magnetic equipment attachment, body strap) creates a hardware moat that pure wrist-worn competitors cannot replicate. If all three are true, Fort could own the strength-training data layer for a generation. But I assign low probability to the conjunction. The strength market, while growing, remains a subset of fitness. The data moat is theoretical -- no evidence of compounding accuracy improvements exists yet. And the multi-placement design, while clever, is a mechanical engineering challenge, not a decade-long barrier. Stripe's expansion from payments to treasury to atlas to billing created reinforcing platform effects; Fort's expansion from strength tracking leads where, exactly? Sleep and recovery tracking where Oura and Whoop have multi-year data advantages and hundreds of millions in revenue?

This is ultimately a 1-to-n company -- a better wearable for a specific segment -- not a 0-to-1 creation. The category "fitness wearable" already exists. The subcategory "strength tracking device" has been attempted (Push Band) and absorbed by an incumbent. The technology is real but not discontinuous. The market is growing but competitive, with four well-funded players and a public company all converging on broader feature sets. Fort is entering the mimetic arena, not escaping it. The founders are capable enough to build a good business, possibly one that gets acquired by Whoop or Garmin for its engineering talent and validated product. But I invest for power-law outcomes -- companies that return the entire fund -- and a consumer hardware startup in a competitive wearable market at a $289 price point does not describe that return profile. I would pass.
