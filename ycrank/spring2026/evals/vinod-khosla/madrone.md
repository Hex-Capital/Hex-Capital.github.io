# Madrone -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Madrone is a hardware-real, consequence-thin bet — its highest dimension is technical/capital risk (dew-point cooling units that no software VC will underwrite) and its lowest is consequence magnitude (a claimed "30% less power and water" versus chiller + cooling-tower stacks, per madrone.cool). That pairing is the exact inverse of the pattern that gets my check. **KiOR** (~2008) had three falsifiable miracles AND a civilizational redirect of the liquid-fuels market; Madrone has hardware risk attached to an optimization play. A 30% efficiency improvement on cooling does not collapse an incumbent regime — it makes Johnson Controls' chillers 30% less compelling, which is a feature war, not a category death.

On consequence magnitude, my ladder is **OpenAI** (2019) at the top — the $50M first check went in because the success-state rewrote what cognitive labor costs across the $15T U.S. labor base. **DoorDash** (2013) sits in the middle — capital-light but it reshaped consumer-commerce flow into a $72B IPO. At the bottom sits the GPT-wrapper pitch from my Pass Example — a 20-30% workflow efficiency gain that any Salesforce product team ships in twelve months. Madrone's "30% less power and water consumption versus conventional chiller + cooling tower" (madrone.cool) is structurally the same shape as that GPT-wrapper: a percentage delta against a named incumbent stack, not the collapse of the stack. Madrone sits between the GPT-wrapper rung and DoorDash, closer to the wrapper. The dossier does not name an incumbent regime that disappears — chillers still exist, cooling towers still exist, hyperscalers still build in Texas. The world-state at maximum Madrone ambition is "data centers cost 30% less to cool." That is a Trane product roadmap, not a civilizational variable.

On technical/capital risk, the ladder runs **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) at the top — $115M Series A into physics miracles consensus VCs refused to underwrite — through **QuantumScape** (early, IPO 2020) at mid, where solid-state battery science required real but bounded miracles. At the bottom sits the GPT-wrapper, all software, zero capital risk. Madrone sits between QuantumScape and the wrapper, closer to QuantumScape: dew-point cooling is bounded by ambient wet-bulb temperature (which the dossier flags can climb in humid Texas summers), modular hardware manufacturing has real capex, and field deployment at hyperscale loads is genuinely unproven. This is the one dimension where Madrone earns honest credit — Tom Blomfield's batch is full of GPT-wrappers and Madrone is not one. But Corintis at ~$58M and Accelsius at ~$50M show that the cooling-startup category is already capital-funded by consensus VCs at chip-level. Facility-side dew-point is less crowded, but the absence of competition is not yet evidence of a miracle.

On founder defiance of expert consensus, **Impossible Foods** (2011) anchors the top — Pat Brown, a Stanford biochemist, publicly contradicting the food industry's premise that animal protein was irreplaceable. **Physical Intelligence** (2024) sits mid with Levine and Finn — academic-pedigree outsiders to robotics commercialization. The bottom rung is career-domain-expert continuity. Akshay Trikha (ex-QuantumScape ML, UC Berkeley scaling laws research, Harvey Mudd) and Erik Meike (ex-Apple iPhone wireless power, Harvey Mudd) sit between the mid and bottom rungs — they are not domain-orthodoxy continuers because neither has HVAC or refrigeration-cycle experience, but they are also not defying any expert consensus in cooling because they have not publicly staked a contrarian thermodynamic claim. The dossier shows zero published efficiency data, zero pilot validation, and a marketing-grade description of "a different thermodynamic process." That is outsider energy without an instigator stance.

On learning rate, **Slash** (2024) at the top with its documented Kanye-pivot, **DoorDash** (2013) at mid for the multi-pivot consumer-logistics evolution, and at the bottom the linear-plan founder who has shipped one thing one way. Madrone launched 2025 with no documented pivot, no public thesis update, and no founder-prior-exit pattern. I apply the absent-evidence default — Trikha and Meike are too early in Madrone's life for a real signal. On required-capital, **Commonwealth Fusion**'s $115M Series A and **Genesis AI**'s $105M co-led seed anchor the top; the GPT-wrapper at the bottom needs $5M to $1M ARR. Madrone sits mid-low — modular cooling units need real manufacturing capital, but the dossier's silence on a multi-hundred-million roadmap suggests this is a $20-40M-to-pilot business, not a $200M-to-fleet business. I am over-resourced for Madrone, and any Tier-1 generalist with a climate-hardware appetite can fund it.

My 36-month forecast: Madrone gets acquired by Johnson Controls, Carrier, or Trane for under $80M after one or two named hyperscaler pilots, or it pivots into a chip-level liquid-cooling adjacent product where it dies competing with Corintis (~$58M, 10,000+ deployed systems) and Accelsius. The mechanism producing this forecast is the same one the dossier already names — Johnson Controls' May 2026 acquisition of Alloy Enterprises and Flex's November 2024 acquisition of JetCool establish the M&A path explicitly. Hyperscale procurement cycles are multi-year; a two-person Harvey Mudd team running parallel hyperscaler evaluations against incumbent HVAC sales forces gets acquired before it gets to standalone scale. That is a fine outcome for a YC pre-seed. It is not a Khosla outcome.

The strongest opposing case is the Texas-buildout consequence argument: if AI data center capex actually compounds toward the $1T-by-2030 figure Trellis cites, and if wet-bulb-constrained sites become the binding constraint on where compute can physically land, then a 30% cooling efficiency unlock is not "feature war" — it is the difference between Texas hosting the next 50 GW of AI compute or losing it to Quebec hydropower. That is a real bull case and it stings, because the labor-displacement frame I push (Fortune 2026-03-06, "$15 trillion of U.S. GDP is labor") runs THROUGH cooling-constrained compute. But it does not survive the dossier's specifics: Madrone has no patents, no published efficiency data, no pilot deployment, no third-party validation of dew-point performance at hyperscale loads in actual humid-Texas wet-bulb conditions, and two founders with zero disclosed HVAC or facility-engineering background. The "different thermodynamic process" is a marketing phrase, not a falsifiable miracle count. **Commonwealth Fusion** I could write because the miracles were named and the PIs had published the physics. Madrone has named neither. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: a signed pilot with a named hyperscaler (Microsoft, Meta, CoreWeave, Crusoe) at a Texas site with third-party-measured PUE or water-usage data showing the 30% claim holds in 35°C+ wet-bulb conditions. Without that, this is a pass and Tom Blomfield can take it.
