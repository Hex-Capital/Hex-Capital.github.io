# Kita -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Kita: it's making an invisible system visible. In the Philippines, Indonesia, Mexico -- the financial lives of hundreds of millions of people exist only in messy documents. E-wallet screenshots, scanned payslips, utility bills in Tagalog. That data represents someone's creditworthiness, their ability to get a loan, to start a business, to pull their family into a different economic bracket. And right now, some credit analyst is squinting at a blurry PDF trying to decide if this person deserves a chance. That's the kind of opacity I've seen companies like Affirm and Carta dissolve -- taking a system where power lives in opaqueness and flooding it with transparency. Kita sits squarely in that pattern, and I feel the pull of what this could mean for economic access in markets where the majority of people have been locked out of formal credit.

But when I try to find the founder story underneath -- the biographical thread that tells me these are the only two people on Earth who could build this -- the signal gets thinner than I'd like. Carmel is from Manila, and that matters. She carries local context that no one flying in from San Francisco will ever have. She knows what a GCash statement looks like, she understands the social mechanics of informal lending in the Philippines. But the dossier reads more like an impressive resume -- Stanford Symbolic Systems, Apple internships, UN recognition at 16, a campus marketplace -- than a survival story. I don't see the moment of personal hardship that fused her identity to this specific problem. Maybe it's there and I just can't see it from the outside. I'd want to sit across from her and ask: why this? Not why is it a good market -- why does this keep you up at night? Rhea Malhotra's Firestone Medal is extraordinary -- that's the highest honor Stanford CS gives for a thesis, and her published work in vision and robotics maps cleanly onto the technical challenge. But again, her connection to emerging market lending feels like a pivot of skill rather than a calling born from lived experience.

The honest truth is that this company doesn't make me uncomfortable, and that's actually a problem for how I invest. Document processing for lenders is a well-understood category. Ocrolus has been doing this in the US for years. The insight that VLMs can now handle noisy multilingual documents better than legacy OCR is correct, but it's also the kind of observation that a hundred smart Stanford CS grads could arrive at simultaneously. The emerging market focus is smart differentiation -- Ocrolus has no incentive to build for Filipino e-wallet formats -- but it's logical, not contrarian. Nobody in a pitch meeting is going to say "that's insane, why would you do that?" They're going to nod and say "yeah, that makes sense." And in my experience, the deals where everyone nods are the deals where the upside has already been priced in. The most promising companies I've backed were the ones where people told me I was crazy.

Now, let me be fair about what the bull case looks like, because it's real. A six-figure paid pilot the week the prototype was built is not nothing -- that's a customer pulling the product out of the founders' hands, which is one of the strongest demand signals you can get at pre-seed. Thirty percent month-over-month growth across multiple Southeast Asian markets, if it holds, puts them on a trajectory that would make any early-stage investor take notice. The data flywheel connecting document signals to actual repayment outcomes is a genuine moat mechanism -- if Kita processes enough loans and learns which document patterns predict default versus repayment, that becomes a compounding advantage no new entrant can replicate without years of data. And the regulatory fragmentation that makes this hard is also what keeps incumbents out. Ocrolus isn't going to learn Philippine SEC rules for a small TAM. That structural disincentive for incumbents is a real moat if Kita can execute across these markets. If I'm wrong about this company, it'll be because Carmel's local knowledge and Rhea's technical depth created a data asset that became the credit infrastructure layer for emerging market lending -- and I missed it because it looked too sensible.

The co-founder dynamic is actually one of the brighter signals here. They met before Stanford and have been building together ever since -- that pre-existing relationship that predates the business is the kind of foundation I've learned to value after watching HQ Trivia implode from co-founder conflict. Two Stanford women who chose each other before the startup, with complementary skills (product/market + deep technical), operating as a two-person team in YC -- that's a relationship that's already been tested by proximity and shared work. I don't have evidence of how they handle real crisis, but the structure gives me more comfort than a team that met at a co-founder matching event last quarter.

Ultimately, Kita sits in an interesting tension for me. The economic access thesis is strong -- this could genuinely unlock credit for millions of people who are currently invisible to formal financial systems, and that's the kind of impact I care about deeply. But my highest-conviction bets have always been on founders whose biographies are inseparable from their mission and on ideas that the consensus considers ridiculous. This company has strong founder-market fit through Carmel's Manila roots and exceptional technical capability through Rhea's research credentials, but it doesn't trigger the intuitive jolt I felt with Niantic or Crusoe. It's a well-reasoned bet in a logical market with good early traction -- and I respect that, even as I recognize it's not the profile that has historically produced my best returns.
