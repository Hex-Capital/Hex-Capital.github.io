# Voltair -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that grabbed me about Voltair is the sheer physical weirdness of the idea — a drone that lands on a live power line, clamps on, and drinks electricity from the grid to recharge itself. That's the kind of thing that makes most investors recoil. Safety liability? What if something arcs? What about the FAA? And that instinctive recoil is exactly the signal I've trained myself to notice, because the best companies I've backed made people deeply uncomfortable before they made them rich. When I heard about Crusoe wanting to burn waste gas to mine Bitcoin, people thought it was insane. Drones perching on power lines like robotic hawks has that same quality of being grounded in obvious physics — power lines carry electricity, drones need electricity — yet nobody wanted to do it because it sounds terrifying. That's where I start to lean in.

But when I try to hear the founders' story — not the business, the story — I find something competent rather than compelling. Hayden Gosch worked at Seattle City Light for two years, saw the inspection problem firsthand, and brought it to his childhood friend Ronan Nopp, who had drone engineering chops. That's a clean founder-market fit. Ronan turned down SpaceX to pursue this, which is a real conviction signal — SpaceX was my very first angel check, and I know the gravitational pull of working for Elon. Walking away from that orbit means you believe deeply in what you're building. But I'm listening for the kind of biographical thread where the founder's life story and the company's mission are inseparable — where they've been forged by adversity that makes them the only person who could solve this problem. What I hear instead is two talented UW engineering graduates who identified a good problem through professional experience. That's respectable, but it's not the survival narrative that gives me the deepest conviction. I don't sense that this problem chose them in the way the best founder-problem bonds work.

What I do notice — and this is something a more analytical investor might miss — is how cleanly Voltair fits my pattern of companies that make invisible systems visible. America's power grid is literally out of sight, strung across rural corridors that nobody looks at until a line sags into dry brush and burns a town down. Eighty percent of major outages caused by extreme weather. Utilities cycling through full inspections every five to eleven years. That's an invisible system failing in catastrophic, uninsurable ways. Voltair compresses that cycle to sixty days. It's the same structural dynamic I saw in Flock Safety — Garrett Langley was making neighborhood safety data visible that had been invisible — and in Carta, which replaced opaque spreadsheets with transparent cap tables. The question is whether this visibility serves underserved communities directly or primarily serves utility balance sheets. Wildfire prevention undeniably protects vulnerable rural communities, but the economic access angle is indirect. This isn't Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm making credit transparent for consumers. It's infrastructure sold to institutional buyers who may or may not pass savings along.

The competitive landscape gives me real pause. Nomadic Drones in Munich independently arrived at the same core concept — charging drones on power lines — with European utility partnerships already in place. When two teams on different continents converge on the same weird idea simultaneously, it suggests the insight isn't as contrarian as it appears. It may be more of a timing convergence enabled by FAA BVLOS expansion and advances in compact power electronics than a singular founder insight. The existence of well-funded incumbents like Skydio ($562M raised) and Percepto ($120M+) also means that if the concept proves viable, larger players could integrate similar capabilities. Voltair's patent-pending clamp is meaningful, but patents at pre-seed are intentions, not moats. The real defensibility will come from execution speed, accumulated inspection data, and utility relationships — and on that front, having only one pilot with a small rural cooperative in Washington State against competitors with billions in pipeline isn't the position I want to see.

The four co-founder dynamic is worth examining. Nopp and Gosch have been friends since middle school and studied together at UW — that's the kind of tested relationship I prize after watching HQ Trivia implode from co-founder conflict. But then there are two more co-founders, Gotskind (CGO, GWU international affairs background) and Weissbluth (COO, Rice operations research), whose connection to the core pair isn't documented anywhere I can find. Four co-founders is complex territory. How do they make decisions? Who breaks ties? I'd want to sit with all four of them and understand how they argue before writing a check. The Gotskind aerospace GTM experience is genuinely useful for navigating utility procurement and FAA regulatory environments, but I need to understand whether this team gels under pressure or whether it fragments when the first real crisis hits.

If this were going to be a great investment, here's what would have to be true: the self-charging mechanism works reliably at scale with a clean safety record, FAA BVLOS regulation continues opening up on a favorable timeline, Voltair signs two or three more utility pilots in the next six months proving the sales motion works, and the patent-pending clamp creates enough of a technical moat to stay ahead of Nomadic Drones and potential fast followers. All of that is plausible — the 2,000 poles inspected and five flying prototypes show real engineering execution — but it requires sustained execution across hardware manufacturing, regulatory navigation, and enterprise sales simultaneously, by a team of recent graduates with no prior startup experience. I've seen young founders pull off extraordinary things, but this particular combination of hardware complexity, regulated industry, and long enterprise sales cycles stacks the execution challenge higher than most pre-seed companies face.

I respect the weirdness and I respect the domain alignment between these founders and this problem. But I invest my own money based on conviction, and my conviction comes from the founder's story more than the technology. I don't feel that biographical inevitability here — the sense that these are the only people on earth who could solve this problem because their lives demanded it. They're smart, they're capable, they saw a real opportunity, and they're executing. That puts them ahead of most pre-seed teams. But it doesn't give me the jolt I felt with Niantic or the certainty I had writing that first SpaceX check. I'd want to watch them for another six months, see how they handle the utility sales grind, and most importantly, sit down with all four founders and hear how they've navigated their hardest moment together.
