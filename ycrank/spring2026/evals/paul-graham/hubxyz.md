# Hub.xyz -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that nobody on this founding team can build the product. The three co-founders have titles of CPO, COO, and CBO — not a single technical builder among them. No public GitHub repos. And they're actively posting a job for a "Founding Full Stack AI Engineer," which means the person responsible for the core technology hasn't joined yet. This is an AI data infrastructure company — an API for training data that runs on a distributed P2P mesh network with Ethereum smart contracts — being built by people whose backgrounds are industrial engineering, luxury retail finance, and supply chain logistics. I've said it many times: you need someone on the founding team who can build the product. When nobody can, you don't just lose iteration speed — you lose the ability to make the thousand small judgment calls per week that determine whether a technical product is actually good.

The deeper problem is that this doesn't feel like an idea born from personal frustration. It feels like an idea born from reading CoinDesk and trend reports simultaneously — "DePIN is hot, AI training data is hot, let's combine them." Armin is an LP at venture funds. Tim came from Tiffany & Co. Léonie was running supply chains for fashion brands. Where in that collective experience did anyone personally struggle to collect real-world training data for an AI model? The way to get startup ideas is not to sit down and think about which markets are growing. When someone says they're building "the API for real-world training data" and their background is venture fund LP and luxury goods, what I hear is that they identified the intersection of two hype curves and built a company there. That's the kind of reasoning that produces ideas everyone agrees are good — which is precisely why they're usually bad investments.

And everyone does agree this is a good idea. There are over 600 DePIN projects. Grass has raised $17.5M and sits at roughly a billion-dollar valuation doing almost exactly the same thing — distributed bandwidth sharing for AI data. Nodepay raised $7M and has 1.5M active users with the same model. The "AI training data" market is a consensus bet with Fortune Business Insights publishing bullish projections and Scale AI running toward $2B in revenue. This is the opposite of the kind of idea I look for. The ideas I want to fund are the ones where smart people's first reaction is skepticism or confusion. Nobody is confused by "help AI companies get training data using distributed nodes." It's immediately legible, immediately plausible, and therefore immediately competitive.

What's the bull case? If you squint, there's something here. The 500K-node network is real — that's not nothing, even if those users were acquired through token incentive mechanics rather than genuine product pull. The SwissBorg partnership gives access to a million KYC-verified users for supply-side growth. The AI training data market is genuinely expanding. And the YC acceptance provides a credibility bridge to enterprise customers. If Hub.xyz could build a truly differentiated data structuring layer — turning raw bandwidth contributions into high-quality, multimodal training datasets that are measurably better than what Grass or Scale AI offers — and if they could hire an exceptional technical lead who effectively becomes the real builder of this company, there's a version of this that works. The distributed collection model theoretically accesses long-tail internet data that centralized scraping misses, which is a real insight about where the next generation of training data will come from.

But I keep coming back to the things that would have to go right simultaneously: they'd need to hire a world-class technical leader who is effectively a co-founder joining late, they'd need to differentiate from better-funded DePIN competitors doing the same thing, they'd need to navigate token regulation without getting burned, and they'd need to convert crypto-incentivized node runners into a data product that Fortune 500 AI teams would pay for. That's four independent breakthroughs required, and the founding team hasn't demonstrated they can build the first one. There's also the Léonie Nguyen situation — she became CEO of Meey Group in October 2025, and she's not listed on the YC page. When a co-founder leaves to run an entirely different company, that's not always fatal, but it's never a good sign. It suggests either the team wasn't as committed as you'd want, or there were disagreements serious enough to cause a departure.

What this company really needs is a technical founder who's obsessed with data quality — someone who has personally felt the pain of training models on stale, poorly structured datasets and who built the first version of the data pipeline themselves because they couldn't stand using anything else. That person would anchor the team, give the product real taste, and provide the kind of organic problem discovery that makes the difference between a company built on conviction and one built on market analysis. Without that person at the center, Hub.xyz is a business plan searching for a builder. I've learned to bet on the builders first and let the plans take care of themselves. When the builders aren't there, neither am I.
