# Adialante -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Forty years. MRI machine design has been fundamentally unchanged for forty years. Siemens, GE, and Philips sell multi-million-dollar systems that weigh tens of thousands of pounds, require dedicated shielded rooms, specialized technicians, and months of installation — and 90% of the global population has never been inside one. Adialante's Efraín Torres didn't optimize within that paradigm. He eliminated gradient coils entirely — a core hardware component present in every MRI machine ever built — using new physics he invented during his PhD at the University of Minnesota. The FREE technique (Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes) produces a system one-tenth the weight, one-tenth the power, one-third less hardware, and silent. Published in Magnetic Resonance in Medicine in 2022, patent-pending. When I backed Rocket Lab, the signal was the same: Peter Beck didn't negotiate cheaper launches on existing rockets — he built new engines with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers. Adialante didn't build a better MRI. They built a fundamentally different one.

The consequence question is what grabs me first, and here Adialante delivers. If this technology reaches diagnostic-grade clinical performance, cancer screening becomes available to the billions of people currently locked out by cost and infrastructure. MRI is the gold standard — no radiation, highest fidelity — yet it's rationed by geography and capital. Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model means a truck shows up at a urology clinic, patients get scanned, the clinic pays a flat per-scan fee, and nobody writes a $2M purchase order. That's not a 20% efficiency gain for existing MRI users. That's creating access where none existed. Adialante starting with pre-biopsy prostate MRI is smart — it's a specific clinical workflow where urologists currently refer patients out, creating friction and delay. But the real consequence is the platform: prostate, then breast, renal, orthopedic, neuro. If the physics works across anatomies, the installed base of $6.5 billion in MRI systems becomes obsolete.

Adialante's technical approach is where I get genuinely excited and where most investors will get genuinely nervous. FREE is not an AI wrapper on existing hardware — that's what Ezra does before getting acquired by Function Health for what I'd guess was a modest outcome. FREE is not a low-field compromise — Hyperfine's 64mT Swoop system is FDA-cleared for brain imaging only and produces images that radiologists tolerate, not prefer. Adialante is attempting diagnostic-grade cancer screening from new physics. The comparison I keep returning to is Impossible Foods: Pat Brown didn't source better soybeans, he synthesized heme protein. Torres didn't shrink an existing MRI, he eliminated the component that made MRI large. The peer-reviewed publication, the $1.18M NSF SBIR Phase II, the Fogarty Innovation accelerator — these are validation signals that the physics is real, not vaporware. But the gap between "published physics" and "FDA-cleared diagnostic device that radiologists trust for cancer screening" is enormous, and Adialante has not yet bridged it. No clinical trial data, no FDA submission announced, no revenue.

Torres himself is a complicated signal for my framework. First-generation college graduate from Little Village on Chicago's south side, who ended up inventing new MRI physics at one of the world's top imaging programs. That personal trajectory — the distance traveled — is exactly the kind of learning rate evidence I look for. But he's also a domain expert: PhD under Michael Garwood, a leading MRI researcher, with a team of five PhDs in MR physics, systems engineering, and computational physics. I typically prefer the naive outsider who doesn't know what can't be done. Here I apply the same exception I applied to OpenAI: when the founder IS the core technology — when Torres literally invented the physics — domain expertise isn't a credential being cited, it's the source of the non-linear advantage. Parker Jenkins, co-founder and COO, met Torres on the first day of undergrad at Marquette and followed him to UMN. That's a tight founding relationship, but the team profile is heavily technical with overlapping MR physics backgrounds. Where's the operational gene pool diversity for running a mobile fleet? Adialante lists Kyle King as CFO and seven total team members, but I see no evidence of healthcare operations, logistics, or commercial leadership — and the mobile-as-a-service model requires all three.

The bull case an advocate would make: Adialante is Impossible Foods for medical imaging — new science that makes a fundamental human need radically affordable, built by the inventor of the technology, with patent protection and a published physics foundation that took years to develop and would take competitors years to replicate. Hyperfine spent five years and over $200M to bring a limited portable MRI to market; Promaxo has raised $72-81M for a single-application office system. Adialante's approach, if it works, leapfrogs both. The mobile-as-a-service model creates an operational moat on top of the physics moat. The market is not speculative — prostate MRI before biopsy is increasingly standard of care per NCCN guidelines. And the affordability thesis is directly on my portfolio's deepest pattern: making the expensive free. That's a strong bull case, and I mostly believe it.

The bear case deserves equal weight. Adialante has ~$3M in total funding and is targeting a $3.5M seed. Building MRI hardware and operating a mobile fleet could require $50-100M before meaningful revenue. The FDA pathway for a novel-physics MRI with no predicate device is likely De Novo classification — multi-year timeline, substantial regulatory cost. No clinical validation data comparing Adialante's diagnostic accuracy to conventional MRI exists publicly. Radiologists are conservative adopters; without published sensitivity and specificity data, clinical uptake will be slow regardless of how elegant the physics is. And the competitive landscape includes Openwater with $100M raised pursuing an entirely different approach (optoelectronics) that could obsolete magnetic resonance altogether. The near-term evidence that would flip me to Pass: if clinical testing reveals that FREE imaging cannot achieve diagnostic equivalence to conventional MRI for prostate cancer detection — if the physics works in a lab but not at the sensitivity thresholds oncologists require — then consequence magnitude collapses regardless of how beautiful the engineering is.

Adialante earns an Invest from me on the strength of two dimensions: consequence magnitude (cancer screening for the 90% of humanity without MRI access) and non-linear technical approach (new physics that eliminates a core MRI component unchanged for four decades, published and peer-reviewed). The affordability thesis is as clean as anything in my portfolio — per-scan pricing with zero capital outlay, enabled by a 10x reduction in weight and power. The founder profile is strong but not ideal for my framework — Torres is a domain expert, not a naive outsider, though his personal trajectory shows extraordinary learning capacity. FDA risk is real and could kill the company before it ever scans a paying patient. I'm treating this as an option: the technology either works at clinical grade or it doesn't, and if it does, the consequence is that cancer gets caught earlier for hundreds of millions of people. That's worth the option price.
