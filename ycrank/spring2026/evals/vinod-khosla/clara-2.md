# Clara -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

George Favvas built Circle Medical to $100M+ annualized revenue and then walked away to start over. That's the single most interesting fact in Clara's dossier. Most founders who achieve $100M in healthcare revenue spend the next decade optimizing it — they don't torch the playbook and return to YC as a pre-seed company. The question I have to answer is whether Clara represents genuine learning — Favvas saw the AI discontinuity and realized the old model was structurally obsolete — or whether this is Circle Medical 2.0 with a chatbot bolted on. The reunited team (Ahmed designed Circle Medical's EHR for 200+ providers, Swift ran its clinical operations) has undeniable execution credentials. But execution credentials are what I look for in a COO, not what I look for in a founder attacking a frontier problem. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand — not a veteran of Lockheed's launch division starting a slightly better rocket company. Clara's founders are the domain experts I explicitly deprioritize: "When I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I've always picked better thinking."

Clara's consequence magnitude is where my framework gets genuinely interested. If AI primary care works — truly works, not as a triage bot but as the primary interface for hundreds of millions of patients — something that costs $300+ per visit becomes near-free at the margin. I've said publicly that AI will deliver "free healthcare, free education, free legal services." Clara is building toward one-third of that vision. I backed Curai Health on exactly this premise: AI primary care at marginal cost approaching zero. The U.S. AI telemedicine market is projected at $72.64B by 2034. Primary care holds the largest segment share. The consequence of success here is not a profitable SaaS company — it's a fundamental restructuring of how 330 million Americans access basic medical care. That consequence earns Clara's attention from me. But consequence magnitude alone doesn't write checks. OpenAI earned the override because the consequence was civilizational and singular. Clara's consequence is significant but shared — Counsel Health (100,000+ members, $36M from a16z and GV), Galileo ($108M+ raised, 50 states), and every major health system's patient portal team are chasing the identical consequence.

Clara's technical approach is where the bet breaks down for me. Clara imports records from 150,000+ facilities, layers an AI chat interface over them, and routes clinical decisions through licensed human clinicians for approval. This is competent integration engineering — it is not a step-function technical advance. The 21st Century Cures Act information-blocking rules (effective 2024) made that 150,000-facility interoperability available to anyone willing to build the pipes. The AI reasoning is off-the-shelf LLM capability applied to medical records. Counsel Health has the same architecture. Galileo has the same architecture. Clara's dossier contains zero evidence of a proprietary model, novel training methodology, unique dataset, or technical insight that would create a 10x improvement over competitors with identical approaches and 10-100x more capital. When I invested in Impossible Foods, the core technology was synthesized heme protein — a genuine scientific breakthrough that no competitor could replicate without years of biochemistry work. Clara's core technology is "we connected an LLM to medical records," which is what every well-funded health tech company in America is doing right now.

Here is the strongest bull case for Clara: the Oscar Health exception. I backed Oscar despite it facing massive regulatory risk because the affordability thesis was powerful enough and the industry was sufficiently calcified. Clara faces similar regulatory complexity (state-by-state licensing, evolving FDA stance on AI clinical tools) but the founding team has already navigated that complexity at Circle Medical for a decade. They know clinical hiring, compliance, malpractice — all the operational machinery that makes healthcare startups fail. Forward Health burned $650M on a hardware-heavy approach; Clara's asset-light model avoids that trap entirely. Favvas's decision to leave $100M+ revenue could signal the highest form of learning: recognizing that AI makes your own successful company's architecture obsolete before the market does. The DoorDash exception is also instructive — I overrode the technical novelty requirement because consequence magnitude was planetary-scale and Tony Xu's learning velocity was extraordinary. Could Clara be a DoorDash-style exception where execution + consequence override technical novelty?

No. The exception doesn't hold. DoorDash was creating a new market — on-demand food delivery at scale didn't exist. Clara is entering a market where Counsel Health already has 100,000 members and Galileo operates across 50 states. DoorDash's consequence was unique at the time of investment; Clara's consequence is shared across multiple well-funded competitors pursuing identical outcomes. Oscar was entering health insurance as an outsider with a technology-first approach to an industry that had zero digital infrastructure. Clara is entering digital primary care as an insider, building what their previous company already built, with an AI layer that every competitor is also adding. The override signals — unique consequence, outsider energy, market creation — are absent. What I see instead is a capable team with a 2-3x improvement in a crowded category. That's a good business. It is not the asymmetric option I'm buying.

Clara earns a neutral assessment from me. The consequence magnitude is real — AI primary care could restructure healthcare access for hundreds of millions — and that pulls the score up. The founding team's $100M+ Circle Medical track record is exceptional execution evidence, rare at pre-seed. But Clara fails my non-linearity test: the technology is application-layer integration, not a step-function breakthrough. The ambition level is achievable, not improbable — no informed observer says "that can't be done" when multiple companies with $36M-$108M+ in funding are already doing it. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Clara has developed a proprietary clinical reasoning system that demonstrably outperforms general-purpose LLMs on diagnostic accuracy or treatment recommendation — a genuine technical moat, not an integration advantage. Without that, Clara is competing on execution in a capital-intensive category where it is drastically outgunned. I don't invest to win execution races. I invest to fund consequences that require the world to be different.
