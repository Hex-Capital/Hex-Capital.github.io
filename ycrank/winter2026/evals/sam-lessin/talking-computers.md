# Talking Computers -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: this is the single hottest consensus category in enterprise AI right now. Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries in a single year. Microsoft is merging AutoGen into a unified Agent Framework with enterprise GA targeted this quarter. Google launched the A2A protocol. Anthropic shipped MCP. CrewAI has raised $24.5 million, Relevance AI just closed $24 million from Bessemer, Lindy pulled in $35 million. When I say "if every VC wants in, the insane-idea premium has been arbitraged away," this is what I'm describing. Building multi-agent orchestration in early 2026 is not insane — it's the most consensus play in the most consensus category. My framework specifically screens for ideas that provoke genuine skepticism from most investors. "AI agents need a workplace to coordinate" provokes zero skepticism. It's on every enterprise AI roadmap at every Fortune 500.

The founder-idea pairing runs exactly opposite to what I look for. Parsa and Zayaan are University of Waterloo CS graduates with internship-level experience — Parsa at TextQL and prefix.dev, Zayaan doing inference work at Modular and a stint at Yutori. The technical skills are real and relevant: Rust systems programming, GPU optimization, compiler development. But "sane" in my framework means the kind of deep operational credibility that anchors a non-consensus bet — Anatoly Yakovenko's years at Qualcomm building systems before proposing Solana, or the Mysten Labs team shipping crypto infrastructure at Meta before building Sui. Early-career engineers with intern-level experience at small startups don't provide the credibility ballast needed to underwrite a genuinely contrarian position. And since the idea itself isn't contrarian, the pairing collapses: competent young engineers chasing a hot market is the most common configuration in YC, not the exception. The possible pivot from "Speedtrain" to "Talking Computers" mid-batch adds noise — it suggests the specific thesis is still crystallizing.

Now let me run the test that dominates my current framework: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? The answer is devastating. This business IS software. Remove the AI component entirely and there's nothing left — no physical infrastructure, no operational complexity, no regulatory moat, no proprietary data generating compounding advantages. The inferred business model is SaaS subscription, which is precisely the category I've been arguing publicly is dissolving. Multi-agent orchestration tooling has a low technical barrier — the dossier itself flags this — and every major cloud platform is building it as a bundled feature. Microsoft can ship agent coordination inside Copilot Studio at marginal cost to their existing enterprise customers. That's the distribution advantage I keep pointing to on CNBC: Apple, Google, Microsoft control the AI ecosystem because they have the relationships and the installed base. A two-person startup with a placeholder website is competing against that.

The strongest bull case — and I want to be honest about it — is the protocol-layer angle. If Talking Computers built a genuinely vendor-neutral communication standard for AI agents, something like an HTTP for agent coordination that every framework and every cloud provider needed to interoperate through, that would be a new primitive. That's the kind of investment I love — Venmo as a payment primitive, Aleo as a privacy primitive, Helium as a wireless primitive. A neutral agent interoperability layer could create network effects and winner-take-most dynamics. The founders' systems backgrounds (compilers, package resolvers, inference engines) are arguably the right toolkit for building protocol infrastructure. For this to work, though, you'd need several things to be true simultaneously: the incumbents' A2A and MCP protocols would need to fail at true interoperability, the founders would need to be building at the protocol layer rather than the application layer, and two early-career engineers would need to establish an industry standard against Google and Anthropic. The "workplace" framing suggests they're building an application, not a protocol. And even if they pivoted to protocol, the window may have already closed — the big players are already defining the standards.

The pre-product stage compounds every concern. The website is a title page. No demo, no customers, no revenue signal, no LOIs. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of traction per se, but when the market is this crowded and this consensus, I need to see something that demonstrates the founders have an angle no one else has spotted. There's no evidence of that here. This is a pass — not because the founders lack talent, but because the structural position is exactly wrong for my framework. An AI-first product in a consensus market with a SaaS model and no physical-world moat, competing against incumbents with unlimited distribution. If I wanted to fund multi-agent coordination, I'd look for a painting company that happens to use AI agents to schedule crews, not a company whose entire existence depends on the AI agent ecosystem fragmenting in precisely the right way.
