# Chert -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Chert sits in a genuine schlep zone -- unofficial Apple iMessage protocol, line-health checks, anti-spam ban risk that scares off the AI-wrapper crowd -- yet the founders, Gary Gao (Penn CS/Wharton, neuromorphic chip research) and Ian Fong (NYU Math, incoming JPMorgan), did not personally suffer this problem. That asymmetry is what Chert is: a real schlep being attacked by founders who spotted it rather than lived it. The cereal-box stunt of **Airbnb** (2009) is not here. The USB-stick-at-MIT origin of **Dropbox** (2007) is not here either. What is here is six months of grinding through iMessage integrations for leasing companies and home-service agencies, which is closer to a consulting wedge than to an organic itch.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the schlep analogue I keep returning to when reading Chert -- Brian Armstrong took the compliance grind nobody else in his batch wanted, and the regulatory unpleasantness compounded into the moat. Chert is doing the equivalent on a smaller protocol: Apple-controlled iMessage with no official business API, anti-spam line-banning that punishes naive outbound, an SMS/RCS fallback layer that has to be hand-wired per CRM. The mechanism is real -- Sendblue has been grinding this for five years and the category still feels unfinished, which is exactly the shape of a problem mainstream founders route around. But Chert diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) analogy on the regulatory dimension: Apple is not a regulator with rulemaking timelines, it is a counterparty that can ship an official Business iMessage API at any WWDC and instantly compress the category to zero. That is a fragility Armstrong did not face.

The MBA-deck-and-TAM-slide pass I wrote about -- no founder ever used the product, no shipped artifact pre-funding -- is the failure mode I check Chert against on the organic-idea axis. Chert is not that company; Gao and Fong did ship, and they shipped to ten named logos including Whop and Rubbrband before Launch HN. But the dossier never says either founder reached for an iMessage agent because they themselves needed one. The narrative is "we built projects on iMessage for leasing companies, DTC startups, and home service agencies" -- that is customer development, not USB-stick frustration. "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) put it plainly: the best ideas are something the founders themselves want. Chert reads as something the founders' customers want, which is a different and weaker signal.

Ian Fong walking away from an incoming JPMorgan offer to build Chert is the closest thing in the dossier to an animal moment -- not as vivid as cereal boxes, but a real opportunity cost paid in public. The eight hackathon wins across UPenn, MIT, Harvard, and Princeton compound this: someone who repeatedly ships under time pressure tends to ship under fundraising pressure. The Chert team also produced ten paying-or-piloting logos and a Launch HN before the institutional check cleared, which is the demo-first pattern I learned to trust from Houston. What is missing is the stunt -- the unscalable, slightly absurd thing that proves Chert founders will do whatever the moment requires. Hackathons reward cleverness on a fixed clock; the **Airbnb** (2009) cereal boxes rewarded improvisation when the clock was running out and the bank account was empty.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is sharp: if either Gao or Fong had a visible GitHub trail -- one non-trivial open-source library, one Lisp-adjacent artifact, one piece of infrastructure other hackers cite -- Chert would jump a tier on this axis. The dossier instead says "No public repos found" for both, twice. Fong's Courant fast-algorithm research and Gao's neuromorphic chip work are real technical signals but they are credential-shaped, not artifact-shaped. Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize predated **Stripe** (2010) and was a tribal marker I could read; Chert offers no equivalent marker, only schools and employer logos. This is the dimension where Chert most resembles the kind of team I would have passed on in 2007.

The co-founder configuration is fine but not load-bearing -- Gao and Fong have been building together since June 2025, hit "random hackathons together for fun," and split technical/business coverage cleanly. They did not room together at MIT for four years like Huffman and Ohanian; they did not survive a prior failed YC company like Kan and Shear coming out of **Kiko** (2005). Chert is an eleven-month-old partnership of two competent technical people from different schools, which is the modal YC pair and produces neither alarm nor excitement.

My 36-month forecast for Chert: still alive, $1-3M ARR, stuck between Sendblue's feature breadth and Blooio's $39/mo undercut, most-likely outcome is an acquihire by HubSpot or Attio in the $15-30M range when Apple ships official Business iMessage at WWDC 2027 or 2028 and collapses the unofficial-API category. The mechanism is the schlep-without-organic-pain pairing from the signature: Chert founders will work hard on this, but when Apple changes the rules they will not have the Houston-level personal conviction that keeps a founder fighting through a platform-shift extinction event.

The bull case that stings: **Stripe** (2010) was the fifth or sixth developer-payments company when I funded it, the Collisons had not personally suffered "I wish payments were easier" the way Houston had suffered the USB stick, and being late to a crowded API category did not stop them from becoming the default. If Chert founders are animals on the Stripe template rather than the Airbnb template -- quiet, technical, relentless on developer experience -- then category crowding and Apple risk are exactly the things they out-execute. The dossier evidence that wins the argument against this read: the Collisons had Auctomatic and a $5M exit at ages 19 and 17 behind them when I wrote the Stripe check; Chert founders have hackathon trophies and no shipped open-source. The condition that would flip my verdict to Invest: Gao or Fong publishing a non-trivial technical artifact on the iMessage protocol itself -- a reverse-engineered spec, a deliverability research post that Hacker News picks up -- within the next six months. That would convert "they spotted the schlep" into "they own the schlep," and the Coinbase analogy would start to load.
