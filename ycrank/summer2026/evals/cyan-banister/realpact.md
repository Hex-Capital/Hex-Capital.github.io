# RealPact -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ranvir Deshmukh is a Dartmouth CS undergrad who just shipped a campus course advisor called CourseMe, and now RealPact is the "AI-native OS for Real Estate Brokerages" -- which tells me everything about the analytical signature here. RealPact is well-governed: a named CEO, a named CTO, four logo customers including Four Seasons Sotheby's, and a tidy $500K YC plus LeapYear pre-seed round. RealPact is also a textbook case of founders who discovered real estate the same way I imagine they discovered enterprise SaaS -- by reading about it, not by sitting in a closing room watching a transaction coordinator re-key a deed at 11pm. The high score is on governance. The low score is on lived problem ownership. That pairing produces a specific kind of company in my book: two prodigies cosplaying as a back-office operator, with the org chart of a real startup and the founder-market scar tissue of none.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril shipped Oculus from his parents' garage *before* he built a defense company, and that prior-product compounding into a domain he had personally obsessed over is the mechanism that made the 2017 check write itself. RealPact's prior-product is CourseMe -- 4,000 MAU and 90% of Dartmouth students -- which is a real shipping credential and gets Ranvir and Erik well past the kid-with-a-deck threshold. But CourseMe is an undergraduate course advisor, and the dossier discloses no real estate, title, escrow, or brokerage operating background for either Ranvir Deshmukh or Erik Peterson. They didn't run a transaction desk; they didn't watch their parent lose a sale because the disclosure packet was three hours late; they didn't grow up in a Sotheby's office the way Chase Lochmiller grew up adjacent to the oilfield. The Anduril mechanism is *autobiographical contact predating the company.* RealPact's biography stops at Hanover, NH, which is also where its customers happen to be -- the beachhead is geographic convenience, not lived domain.

The pass example I keep on file -- the consensus YC AI-agent darling with two ex-FAANG PMs, $200B TAM slide, oversubscribed seed -- is what RealPact pattern-matches to on the "magically weird" axis, and the failure mechanism is the same: I cannot find the audience that has *already passed on aesthetic grounds.* RealPact is in YC Summer 2026's Real Estate cohort that MarketScale wrote up as the AI-agent vertical of the year. Voice agents for county registries is not transgressive; it is the consensus configuration of vertical AI in 2026. Dotloop is owned by Zillow Group and runs roughly half of US transaction management; SkySlope serves 400,000 professionals; the entire competitive set is buildable by incumbents the moment they care to ship LLM autofill on their existing distribution. RealPact is not too hard or too unsexy -- it is exactly sexy enough that six other YC teams are probably pitching adjacent variants this batch.

Mechanism check on CEO authority over 18 months: RealPact has Ranvir Deshmukh named CEO and Erik Peterson named CTO on a co-founding pair that previously shipped together at Dartmouth. The shipped-together history is the strongest governance signal in the dossier -- HQ Trivia's Yusupov/Kroll collapse happened because the partnership had no pre-conflict pressure-test, and CourseMe is that pressure-test for these two. Veer B Patel sits in the org with an unconfirmed role and an unconfirmed equity slice, which I flag as a small unresolved seam, and the realpact.ai footer reading "SignPact, Inc." while the product is RealPact is the kind of branding rename that usually trails an entity restructuring I have not been told about. Neither of those is fatal. The tiebreaker is named; it is just not battle-tested in adversity.

Counterfactual on capital intensity and regulatory moat: RealPact would have to flip from "OS for brokerages" to "regulated escrow or title operator" to crack into the Vigil Labs / Mindbloom tier of unsexy-regulated swings I write checks into. The dossier shows county-registry data extraction pipelines and contract autofill, both of which are software abstractions that Dotloop, SkySlope, Brokermint, or any new entrant can replicate given general LLM availability -- the Paperless Pipeline comparison surface from 2026 makes the commoditization risk concrete. If RealPact were taking title or escrow license per state, parsing 3,000+ county recording offices into a proprietary dataset, or building hardware at the closing table, the score on this dimension flips a full tier. It is not doing that. It is selling AI-flavored transaction management on top of public records that anyone can scrape.

Single-anchor parallel on physical-world verifiability: I bet on Postmates in 2011 because the delivery happened in front of me at a deli counter, and I could photograph the bag. RealPact is sold to brokerage staff and runs as voice agents inside a brokerage's back office, so the only way a normal human encounters the product is by being a real estate agent in New Hampshire signing a contract whose autofill happened upstream and invisibly. Four Seasons Sotheby's and Black House are real logos, and I take them at face value -- but the artifact is workflow software, not something I can install on my phone tonight, ride, or photograph. The dimension defaults below its midpoint for a reason: when the artifact is invisible to me, I cannot do my work, which is to observe before I read the deck.

My 36-month forecast: RealPact reaches the $300K-$1.2M ARR band, remains concentrated in New England brokerages because re-mapping each new state's county registries and contract forms is wetter and more boring than the agentic-AI narrative suggests, and either gets acqui-hired by Dotloop / Zillow Group for low-eight-figures or pivots Ranvir and Erik into an adjacent vertical-AI product where their CourseMe distribution playbook actually transfers. The mechanism producing this outcome is the same one I described for the consensus-AI pass: founder origin is analytical rather than autobiographical, the moat lives in the model layer rather than the founder, and a polymath generalist with better distribution -- Zillow Group already has it -- can rebuild the surface area in a quarter.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the DeepMind exception: Hassabis was a chess prodigy, neuroscientist, and game designer compounding across disciplines, and I overrode my anti-pedigree default because the polymath cross-domain compounding was real. Ranvir and Erik are young, did ship CourseMe to 90% of Dartmouth, won the $20K Magnuson prize, and could plausibly be early-stage polymaths whose campus-AI fluency compounds into real-estate-AI fluency. The case stings because I have written this exception check before. It does not survive contact with the specific dossier: course advising and real estate transaction management share no domain primitive, the four customers are geographic-proximity sales rather than evidence of category insight, and a polymath compounding pattern requires *two* domains the founder has gone deep in, not one prior product and one whitespace they entered last spring. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: RealPact lands a paying customer in a second state -- say California or Texas, where contract complexity, MLS politics, and county recording vary materially from New Hampshire -- without rebuilding the product from scratch, within nine months. If Ranvir can ship that, the polymath thesis becomes load-bearing and I take a second meeting.
