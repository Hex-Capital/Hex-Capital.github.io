# Sellara -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Spencer Secord built workflow automations at Citi and integrated them into core risk systems. That detail stopped me. Most founders selling AI to banks observed the problem from the trading desk. Secord was already writing the proto-solution inside the institution before he left. That's the difference between someone who identified a market and someone who couldn't stop solving a problem even when no one was paying them to. Jolly covered 180 institutional clients across Macro Credit Derivatives, IG Credit, and Short Term Credit Sales — he knows every buyer by name, knows their workflows, knows their pain tolerance for new vendors. Two of Sellara's three founders have specific knowledge that a competitor would need years on a Citi desk to replicate. The question is whether that specific knowledge is about the *problem* or the *solution*. Knowing which workflows break is valuable. Building AI that outperforms leading LLMs by 60% on financial tasks requires a different kind of specific knowledge — and that's where Ahmad Roumie's domain pivot from robotics and nutrition-tracking at BabylonAI (YC W23) introduces uncertainty. His ML engineering transfers, but the claimed performance edge would need to originate from deep financial domain data expertise that his background doesn't obviously contain.

The leverage audit is where Sellara loses me. "Custom AI agents deployed within financial institutions" with a three-step process — identify a workflow, configure integrations, validate outputs, deploy with monitoring — is consulting enhanced by code, not code that works while you sleep. Each institutional deployment requires human effort proportional to the institution's complexity. Beam AI's numbers tell the story: $4.5M in revenue with 57 employees works out to roughly $79K per employee — that's services-company economics, not software economics. If Sellara's comparable market requires similar headcount-to-revenue ratios, a four-person team will need to grow linearly with customers. The "deploy in weeks, not quarters" claim is better than FIS or Bloomberg's implementation cycles, and the principal-agent problem at those incumbents is real — their professional services revenue makes them agents of their own P&L, structurally unable to ship a product that deploys in weeks. But Sellara exploiting the incumbents' agency problem doesn't mean Sellara itself has permissionless leverage. When I built AngelList, my specific knowledge from angel investing created a platform with network effects — each new startup listing made the platform more valuable to every investor without additional cost per user. Sellara's deployed agent at Goldman doesn't make the agent at JPMorgan better. There's no compounding. Each deal restarts.

The principal signals from Jolly and Secord are genuinely strong. Leaving a Citi sales desk covering 180 clients — that's walking away from a high-probability income stream to pursue a low-probability startup. Secord's six years across Cross Asset, Fixed Income, and Convertible Sales desks, combined with his internal automation work, reveals someone who was already a builder trapped inside a bank. But Roumie's trajectory — robotics at ETH Zurich, nutrition tracking at BabylonAI, now financial trading operations — is three domains in three acts. That pattern often signals a talented technologist who joins founding teams rather than a principal pulled inexorably toward one problem. The SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed are an unusual commitment signal; these take months and real capital to obtain, suggesting Sellara may already be in procurement conversations with institutions that require them. That's revealed behavior, which I weight more than stated ambition.

The strongest bull case requires two things to be true: first, that the 60%+ performance claim reflects proprietary model training on financial domain data (creating a data moat that deepens with each deployment), not prompt engineering on commodity models (easily replicated by Beam AI or any consulting firm with access to GPT-4). Second, that Sellara evolves from custom deployments to a platform — where the configurations, integrations, and validated workflows from each institution become reusable components that reduce the marginal cost of the next deployment toward zero. If both are true, Sellara compounds. The timing supports it: 44% of finance teams projected to use agentic AI in 2026, and a Forrester study found one firm already running 60 agents with plans for 200 more. But "if validated" is doing heavy lifting in both claims, and Beam AI at $4.5M revenue with Accenture's FinTech Innovation Lab backing is already establishing the category with more resources and traction.

Sellara has real specific knowledge on the business side — Jolly and Secord are not tourists in institutional trading. The principal signals from two founders leaving Citi are meaningful. But the leverage architecture points toward linear scaling, not exponential. The access democratization angle is absent — Sellara sells efficiency to institutions that already have access to these workflows, not capability to populations that lack it. My framework rewards specific knowledge productized into permissionless leverage. Sellara has the first half of that equation clearly. The second half — the leverage that compounds — remains unproven, and the comparable evidence from Beam AI suggests this market may structurally resist it.
