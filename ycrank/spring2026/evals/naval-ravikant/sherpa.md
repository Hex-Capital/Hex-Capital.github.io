# Sherpa -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sherpa is that it maps uncomfortably close to the archetype I pass on most frequently: the AI wrapper. Two founders who worked at an AI agent company identify that marketing teams need automated A/B testing, plug into foundation model APIs to generate copy variants, deploy via JavaScript snippet, and show 20% lift for their former employer. The underlying capability — generating marketing copy with an LLM and injecting it into a webpage — is reproducible by any engineer with an afternoon and an API key. Mutiny has $72.6 million in funding and already does AI-powered website optimization for B2B. Optimizely has $400M ARR. The question isn't whether the product works. The question is whether anything here compounds into a position that can't be replicated.

Specific knowledge is where this falls apart for me. Neither Kinnan nor Sonam has years of accumulated, non-transferable expertise in conversion rate optimization. Kinnan was Head of Partnerships at Lindy with a finance degree from 2024. Sonam was an engineer at Microsoft building Copilot image generation, then at Lindy. What they have is knowledge of how to build AI agents — which is exactly the kind of knowledge that society is training thousands of people to have right now. When I backed Perplexity, Srinivas had spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the systems that make AI search work. That knowledge would take a competitor years to accumulate. Here, the relevant knowledge is "how to call an LLM API and manage A/B test infrastructure." That's engineering competence, not specific knowledge. If both founders disappeared tomorrow, the company's core insight — "automate CRO with AI agents" — survives perfectly well with any replacement team. That's the test, and Sherpa fails it.

The leverage architecture has the right shape but the wrong ownership. Code leverage through a SaaS product that scales via a JavaScript snippet — structurally correct. Two people shipping a working product to production — that's the team density I look for. But they're renting their leverage from foundation model providers. The moment OpenAI or Anthropic or Google ships better variant generation natively, or Mutiny adds automated test orchestration to their existing personalization engine, Sherpa's leverage evaporates. The data flywheel from one million visits sounds promising until you realize Optimizely has billions of data points and VWO has six thousand customers generating test data continuously. At this scale, the data advantage is aspirational, not defensible.

The principal-agent diagnostic is where I'd want to be proven wrong. There IS a genuine principal-agent problem at the incumbents: Optimizely profits from the complexity of manual test creation — their enterprise sales motion and professional services revenue depend on CRO being hard. Automating it fully would cannibalize their own business model. That's structurally similar to Google's inability to cannibalize search ads, which is what made Perplexity investable. If Sherpa's founders were CRO practitioners who had spent years running thousands of A/B tests and developed proprietary intuition about what converts — specific knowledge accumulated through obsessive practice — I could see this as a principal with unique knowledge attacking agents trapped by their own incentives. But the founders' connection to CRO appears to be "we worked at an AI agent company and identified this as a good vertical to apply agents to." That's opportunity identification through market analysis, not specific knowledge driving toward an inevitable product.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the data flywheel from aggregated test results across customers creates compounding advantage faster than incumbents can replicate it, that Mutiny's $72.6M war chest and existing B2B distribution won't allow them to ship automated testing before Sherpa scales, and that the founders' AI agent expertise from Lindy translates into execution speed that constitutes a durable advantage. If all three hold — and particularly if the cross-customer learning produces genuinely superior variant recommendations that no single-tenant system can match — this could become the default CRO layer for growth-stage companies. The principal-agent trap at Optimizely and VWO is real. But I need the founder to BE the moat, not just the first mover. First-mover advantage without specific knowledge is a head start in a race where the other runners are faster.

I pass. The product works, the market is real, the team is lean. But I'm looking at general-purpose AI engineering skills applied to a vertical where the core technology is commoditized, the only customer reference is the founders' former employer, and well-funded competitors are one feature sprint away from equivalent capability. Competent founders building a reproducible product in a competitive market is the median YC outcome, not the outlier structure I write checks for.
