# Andustry -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Johann Stürken's family background in trading goods and supplier sourcing is the detail I locked onto first. When I ask "tell me your story," I want to hear that the problem was already inside the founder before the company existed. Johann grew up watching the world of sourcing — RWTH Aachen mechanical engineering, McKinsey, Airbus, Capgemini Invent — and his family traded goods. That's proximity. It's not nothing. But proximity isn't the same as being forged. The founder who watched the system from a comfortable vantage point reads differently to me than the one who was crushed by it and rebuilt themselves around fixing it. Het Dave, Andustry's technical co-founder, brings CS from IIT Jodhpur and software development skills, but no visible biographical thread connecting him to industrial procurement. These two share no prior employer, no university overlap — the dossier doesn't tell me how they found each other or what bonds them beyond the business opportunity. I don't see the biographical inevitability I need. I see career-informed opportunity recognition, which is competent but not the same thing.

Andustry's strongest signal for my framework is the invisible system it's attacking. Industrial procurement for SMEs with 20–1,000 employees is genuinely opaque: contacting hundreds of suppliers, waiting weeks for a first quote, manually comparing non-standardized responses, and paying brokers who charge upfront commissions for the privilege of navigating confusion. That's an extractive information asymmetry. The small manufacturer in Germany trying to source injection-molded components is paying a hidden tax — in time, in broker fees, in suboptimal pricing — because the system is designed to benefit the people who already know how it works. Andustry's AI agents that normalize quotes side-by-side and eliminate upfront commissions through pay-on-delivery genuinely make this opacity visible. This reminds me of what Flexport did for freight forwarding — taking a deeply opaque, relationship-driven, paper-heavy logistics system and making it legible. Ryan Petersen, though, had lived inside that system since he was importing motorcycles from China. The biographical connection was visceral, not professional. Andustry has the structural parallel without the founder intensity.

€1M+ in transaction volume during beta with a two-person team across 100+ countries is real signal for a pre-seed company — Andustry has product in market and money moving through it. The 500k+ verified companies claimed in the supplier network, if genuine, would represent meaningful early infrastructure. But "verified" is doing heavy lifting in that sentence, and the dossier can't tell me how many of those companies are actively responding to RFQs versus passively scraped into a database. Xometry acquired Thomasnet for $300M and has 500k+ sellers generating $687M in FY2025 revenue. The incumbent response risk is specific and credible: if Xometry adds an AI-agent sourcing mode alongside its manufacturing marketplace, Andustry's value proposition gets replicated with an existing supplier base at massive scale. The dossier notes channel conflict would make this difficult for Xometry — being a manufacturer-of-record conflicts with being a pure sourcing marketplace — and that's a legitimate structural argument. But it's the kind of argument that buys you time, not permanent safety.

Andustry operates in a well-funded, understood category — AI procurement is not a space that makes sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs. Leverage AI, Zip, Fairmarkit, Laserhub, Hectool — the competitive landscape is populated. This isn't defense tech or male birth control or Bitcoin mining with flare gas. Nobody is going to look at you funny at a dinner party for saying "I'm building an AI-powered procurement platform." That's consensus-comfortable. The core AI-matching technology uses LLM-based agents applied to procurement, which the dossier correctly identifies as technically reproducible. When I run the ten-year question — where does Andustry end up in a decade? — I can see a useful company, a good business, maybe a solid outcome. But I can't see the infrastructural surprise hiding beneath a weird surface. The current product is more or less what the company will be, made bigger and better. Crusoe looked like a crypto mining operation and was secretly building distributed energy infrastructure. Andustry looks like an AI procurement marketplace, and I believe it is one.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Johann's family trading background IS the biographical thread — he grew up inside this supply chain, and the €1M+ beta traction proves the market is pulling. Industrial procurement opacity is a textbook "invisible system made visible" — exactly the Affirm or Carta pattern, but for physical goods at global scale. The timing is genuinely right: LLMs crossed the autonomous-agent threshold in 2024–2025, making AI negotiation and quote normalization feasible for the first time, and post-COVID supply chain disruption created SME demand for diversified cross-border sourcing. The two-sided marketplace, if it reaches network density, creates compounding defensibility that a purely vertical player like Xometry can't replicate without cannibalizing its own model. At pre-seed with €1M+ already transacting, Andustry is further along than most companies at this stage. I take this bull case seriously — the invisible system angle is genuine, and it's what keeps Andustry in neutral territory rather than a clean pass. But the biographical forge isn't deep enough for the conviction I need, the space isn't contrarian enough to generate the asymmetric returns I look for, and the AI moat is thin against incumbents and the wave of procurement startups entering this exact space. The one condition that would flip me to invest: if I sat across from Johann and heard a story about watching his family get exploited by the very sourcing system he's now disrupting — if the problem broke something in him before he decided to build — that changes the entire calculus. The biographical forge is the gateway to my conviction, and right now the gate is only partway open.

Andustry is a competent team with real domain knowledge building in a genuinely opaque market. The invisible system dimension — the strongest fit with my framework — holds it above a pass. But the absence of biographical inevitability in the founders, the consensus-comfortable competitive landscape, and the reproducibility of the AI layer against well-capitalized incumbents like Xometry at $687M revenue keep me from writing a check. The two dimensions that locked this tier: Founder's Biographical Forge (Johann's family background provides proximity without crucible) and Contrarian Conviction (AI procurement is crowded, not stigmatized). Andustry may build a successful business, and I'll watch from here. But this isn't a Long Journey bet.
