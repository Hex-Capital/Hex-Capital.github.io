# Aurorin CAD -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the central paradox that jumped off the page at me: Jared Friedman — a YC partner I respect — made the pitch for Aurorin by saying "This would have been impossible before AI coding got good, because Solidworks is 10M lines of code. But it is now plausible." He's right. And that's exactly the problem. If AI coding has crossed the threshold where a solo founder can build a multi-million-line CAD kernel, then the kernel itself is not a durable moat — because the next talented engineer with Claude Code and a weekend can start compressing that same gap. This is literally my thesis playing out in real time: software is a business tool, not a business model. The technology that makes Aurorin possible is the same technology that ensures Aurorin's output can be replicated. The enabler is the eroder. I've been saying this about SaaS for two years and it's jarring to see a YC partner inadvertently confirm it in the pitch.

Now, the sane-person-insane-idea filter. Michael Baron has three SpaceX internships — Raptor combustion sim, Dragon GNC, Starshield — plus Apple GPU driver optimization. These are legitimately hard positions to land, and the combination of aerospace CAD exposure plus GPU systems programming is almost surgically relevant to building a GPU-accelerated geometric kernel. The idea is genuinely insane: nobody has successfully built a new production-grade CAD kernel from scratch and displaced incumbents. Parasolid, ACIS, and CGM have dominated for decades. SolidWorks has 3.2 million licensed seats. Onshape tried the modern approach and sold to PTC for $470M rather than fight the distribution war independently. The insanity checks out. But "sane" requires more than raw talent — it requires operational credibility proportional to the ambition. Baron was an intern at these companies, not a senior systems engineer who shipped production infrastructure. Compare to Anatoly Yakovenko when we backed Solana at five cents — Yakovenko had years of deep systems work at Qualcomm, building the kinds of distributed systems that informed Solana's architecture. Baron has potential, not track record. And he's solo on a project that historically required hundreds of engineers over decades. The calibration is off — the idea is sufficiently insane, but the "sane" anchor isn't heavy enough to hold it.

The structural moat question is where my framework generates the sharpest verdict. Aurorin is building software. Good software, potentially groundbreaking software, but software. It has no physical-world operations, no installed hardware, no regulatory position, no proprietary dataset that compounds with usage. My whole thesis — the thing I've been hammering on CNBC, on X, in the WTF VC deck — is that software moats are dissolving. The question I always ask is: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? For Aurorin, the answer is explicitly no. The YC partner's own framing says it was "impossible" before AI. This isn't cherry-on-top AI. This is AI-as-the-cake. That's the category I avoid.

Let me steelman the bull case, because there's a real one here. CAD kernels are not normal software. There are essentially three geometric kernels serving the entire global engineering industry. A new, production-grade parametric B-Rep kernel would function as a genuine primitive — the kind of foundational layer that other applications build on, creating the compounding dynamics I look for. This is the Airtable exception in my framework: something that looks like a software tool but actually establishes a new computational substrate. If Baron's kernel reaches production quality and gets adopted not just as a CAD application but as the geometric engine that other tools integrate with, this becomes a protocol-layer investment with winner-take-most dynamics. The incumbents face real innovator's dilemma — Dassault can't rebuild ACIS without cannibalizing SolidWorks, Siemens can't rebuild Parasolid without disrupting NX. PTC's $470M Onshape acquisition proves incumbents would rather buy than build. For this bull case to materialize, three things have to be true simultaneously: Baron has to be a generational engineering talent (possible but unproven), the kernel has to reach production-grade geometric precision for real engineering workflows (sheet metal, surfacing, tolerance analysis — not just demo parts), and Aurorin has to position itself as infrastructure, not just an application. Right now, there's zero evidence of any of these beyond a demo video and a free download.

The competitive landscape adds another wrinkle. Adam (YC W25) already raised $4.1M and generated 10M+ social media impressions. Zoo has $5.12M and is building open-source text-to-CAD infrastructure. These are AI-layer plays, not full-stack kernel builds, but they're capturing mindshare in the "AI for CAD" narrative right now. Baron's full-stack approach is technically more ambitious, but ambition without execution is just a harder way to lose. The market is getting crowded enough that "AI-native CAD" is trending toward consensus rather than contrarian — every smart engineering-background founder can see that legacy CAD is ripe for disruption. When the pitch deck category is hot, the non-consensus premium evaporates.

I keep coming back to the structural contradiction. The narrative says "we can build this because AI makes massive software projects feasible for small teams." My framework says "if AI makes massive software projects feasible for small teams, then massive software projects aren't defensible moats." You can't have it both ways. Either the kernel is impossibly hard — in which case a solo intern-level founder can't build it — or AI has made it plausible — in which case others will build competing kernels too. Baron may be genuinely exceptional. The SpaceX and Apple backgrounds suggest he might be. But I need more evidence than intern tenure to bet my own money on someone attempting one of the hardest software engineering projects in existence, solo, against well-funded competitors and entrenched incumbents with 30 years of file format lock-in and enterprise relationships.
