# Laurence -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Laurence is that its core analogy -- advertising bidding is really quantitative trading -- actually undermines its own investment thesis. Leo Gierhake came from Jump Trading, where he led research overseeing billions in live trading volume. The founders' insight is that Amazon PPC optimization should be treated as a stochastic decision problem, with reinforcement learning replacing the spreadsheet jockeys and rule-based automation that dominate the status quo. This framing is intellectually coherent and probably correct. But consider what happened when quantitative methods entered financial trading. Did one firm achieve monopoly? No. The quant revolution produced Citadel, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Renaissance Technologies, Jane Street, and Jump itself -- an ecosystem of brilliant, competing firms that drove margins toward zero through mutual imitation. The very industry the founders draw their analogy from is one of the most Girardian competitive landscapes in economic history. If ad bidding truly is like trading, the structural prediction is not monopoly but perpetual, margin-destroying competition among technically sophisticated firms chasing the same signals.

What is the secret here? The founders believe that reinforcement learning applied to Amazon PPC -- treating keyword bids as continuous stochastic optimization rather than discrete rule-based decisions -- will deliver structurally superior returns. But this is not a truth that most intelligent people disagree with. The application of quantitative trading techniques to advertising has been discussed and practiced for over a decade. Google's own ad auction system was designed by economists and attracts quant talent. Programmatic advertising has been described as "trading" since the emergence of real-time bidding in 2009. The specific application to Amazon PPC is a narrower claim, and it may be that the Amazon ad-optimization market is technically underserved relative to Google or Meta's ecosystems. But that makes this a market-timing observation about a gap that will close, not a secret about the structure of the world. Five funded competitors -- Pacvue at $50.8M in revenue, Quartile managing $2B+ in ad spend, Teikametrics with $65M raised -- are already addressing the same customers. Everyone can see that Amazon's $60B ad business needs better optimization tools. That is consensus, not insight.

The team is genuinely strong for this particular market. A Jump Trading quant researcher paired with a Google/Meta ML engineer represents an unusual talent stack among Amazon PPC tool builders, who tend to be e-commerce software engineers rather than stochastic optimization specialists. Matthew Chen's deliberate three-month immersion building tools for Amazon Ads agencies before founding the company reflects the kind of pre-research I respect -- he studied the domain rather than abstractly theorizing about it. The advisory relationship with Aaron Apple, who built and exited a top-75 Amazon 3P seller and ran a PPC consultancy managing eight figures of annual ad spend, provides authentic distribution access. The early traction -- $10M in managed ad spend, 15-20% advertising profit improvements, 40% ACoS reduction -- is real and meaningful at pre-seed. These are competent founders executing well. But competent execution in a competitive market produces a good business, not a monopoly.

The bull case would require believing that reinforcement learning creates such a dramatic performance gap over rule-based and simpler ML approaches that Laurence achieves something like what Renaissance Technologies achieved in trading -- sustained alpha that competitors simply cannot replicate. If RL-driven bid optimization consistently delivers 40%+ ACoS improvements and 2-5x ROAS gains that no incumbent can match, brands would consolidate around Laurence, and the data flywheel would compound the advantage. At $60B+ in Amazon ad spend, even a small percentage of managed spend at a reasonable take rate becomes a substantial business. If the founders could then expand this engine to Walmart, Instacart, and eventually Google and Meta ad ecosystems, the platform becomes the quantitative trading desk for all digital advertising. That is a genuinely large outcome. But this scenario requires two things I do not see evidence for: first, that the performance gap is 10x rather than 20-40% (their own reported results suggest meaningful but not discontinuous improvement); and second, that well-funded incumbents with $50M+ in revenue and thousands of customers cannot hire quant talent and build RL-based systems of comparable quality within 18-24 months.

This is a "1 to n" company. The founders are taking techniques that exist in quantitative finance and applying them to a domain where they have not yet been fully exploited. That is smart engineering and may produce a profitable business. But it is not going from zero to one. They are not creating a new category -- they are entering the existing Amazon PPC optimization category with a better algorithm. "Better" is not a monopoly. When the improvement is 20-40% rather than 10x, when the market has five funded competitors, and when the underlying technique (reinforcement learning) is replicable by any well-resourced team, the structural prediction is competition, not dominance. I would rather invest in a company creating an entirely new market with no competitors than a company entering a crowded one with a superior approach that will be mimicked.
