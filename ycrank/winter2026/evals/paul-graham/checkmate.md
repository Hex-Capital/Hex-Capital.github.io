# Checkmate -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Checkmate is that AlphaSense -- a company with $500M in ARR and a $4B+ valuation -- launched its own AI Interviewer in 2025, built on the 200,000+ expert transcripts it acquired when it bought Tegus for $930M. InsightAgent is also in market. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness. This is a problem that every well-capitalized player in financial data infrastructure can already see clearly. When everyone can see the opportunity, the question shifts from "will someone build this?" to "why will *these* founders win?" And that's a harder question to answer when you're a two-person startup against a $4B incumbent that already has distribution to 6,500+ enterprises.

How did these founders find this problem? The dossier gives me no answer. Neil Garde spent eight years as a PM at Google and then built Monzo's US operation. James Sha built a fitness tech company, worked on NLP at Google, and did AI at Meta. Neither of them has ever been an analyst at a hedge fund. Neither has apparently spent years sitting through expert network calls, experiencing the specific pain of junior analysts missing follow-up questions or timezone barriers making international research impossible. The idea looks like what happens when two smart technical people scan the market landscape, notice that expert networks are a multi-billion dollar category ripe for AI disruption, and decide to build something. That's the pattern I've warned about in "How to Get Startup Ideas" -- sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than stumbling into a problem through lived experience. The absence of any organic origin story is itself a signal.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about it. Checkmate's network-agnostic positioning is structurally clever. AlphaSense's AI Interviewer only works within its own Tegus library. But hedge funds use GLG, AlphaSights, Guidepoint, Third Bridge, and direct expert relationships simultaneously. If Checkmate becomes the AI layer that sits across all of them -- the way Stripe sat across all the banks -- that's a genuinely more valuable position than any single network's captive tool. The compliance infrastructure (SOC II, MNPI guardrails, consent collection) creates switching costs once embedded. And the founding team is not weak: Sha had a prior exit with Onyx (300K+ users, acquired by CureFit), published AI research with Pieter Abbeel at Berkeley, and has exactly the NLP background you'd want for building conversational AI agents. Garde's experience building Monzo US from zero is directly relevant to the enterprise sales motion this company needs. These are competent, credentialed founders.

But competent and credentialed is not the same as relentlessly resourceful in service of a problem they can't stop thinking about. I see no evidence of the founders doing things that don't scale -- no stories of personally conducting mock expert interviews to understand the nuances, no indication they embedded themselves in a hedge fund's research workflow to learn the compliance pain firsthand. The anonymous testimonials on their website ("$10B+ L/S Equity Hedge Fund," "Mid-Market PE Firm") suggest some early pilots, but anonymous quotes on a marketing page are weak evidence of genuine pull. Real demand at this stage looks like funds calling you because word spread from another fund's research team. What I see instead is a "Request Access" form, which is just a waitlist with better branding.

The technical dimension is where Checkmate scores highest. Sha is a legitimate hacker-founder -- published CV/robotics researcher, prior CTO with an acquisition, NLP experience at Google Search. He can build this product himself. But the core technology concern is real: LLM-powered voice agents conducting structured interviews is increasingly accessible. The hard part isn't the technology -- it's earning trust with the most paranoid buyers in the world (hedge fund compliance officers) and navigating the relationship dynamics with expert networks whose business model you're partially threatening. That's a schlep, but it's one that AlphaSense has already proven it can handle, and they have a nine-figure head start.

The multiple brand names bother me more than they should. Checkmate Research. Expert Insights. Labyrinth Technologies Inc. Three names for one pre-seed company. When I funded Stripe, it was Stripe. When I funded Dropbox, it was Dropbox. Naming confusion at this stage usually reflects strategic confusion -- founders who haven't yet achieved the clarity about what they are that makes everything else easier. It's a small thing. But small things at the beginning tend to become big things later.

I'd pass on this one. The founders are strong enough that I'd pay attention to what they build next, especially if they pivot toward a problem they discovered through genuine experience rather than market analysis. But right now this is a consensus AI opportunity in a category where a dominant incumbent is already executing, built by founders without visible connection to the problem they're solving. Smart people building something that sounds good is one of the most common failure modes in startups.
