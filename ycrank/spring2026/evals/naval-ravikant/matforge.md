# matforge -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Akash Ramdas discovered novel interconnect metals during his Stanford PhD that ended up in Intel and TSMC process roadmaps. Not cited in a paper. Not referenced in a footnote. Adopted into the roadmaps of the two largest logic foundries on the planet. At pre-seed. That's not domain expertise -- that's specific knowledge in its purest form. You cannot teach someone how to identify which compound metals will replace ruthenium at sub-3nm nodes. That understanding emerged from years inside the Reed Group at Stanford, running first-principles calculations on thousands of candidates, developing intuition about which material properties coexist and which trade off. A well-funded competitor can hire materials scientists, but they cannot purchase the trust that comes from having your discoveries already embedded in a foundry's process integration plans. When TSMC evaluates a novel thermal interface material, they don't want a demo -- they want to know who ran the simulations and whether they trust the methodology. Akash has that trust. That's a decade-long head start compressed into a credential.

The leverage architecture here is real but incomplete, and that's the honest tension. A swarm of AI agents screening materials computationally -- compressing ten-year discovery cycles into months -- represents genuine code leverage applied to physical science. Akash's published methodology screened 15,000+ materials using multi-objective optimization. That's one researcher producing output that would have required dozens of experimentalists and years of lab time. But MIT Technology Review is right that there has been no "ChatGPT moment" in materials discovery, and the bottleneck isn't computational prediction -- it's bridging prediction to physical synthesis and validation. Matforge's leverage chain has a gap: they can screen at scale, but they appear to lack wet-lab capabilities to close the loop. This means they're either dependent on fab partners to validate candidates (which is actually how the industry works and could be a feature, not a bug, given Akash's existing relationships) or they'll need to build or partner for synthesis infrastructure. The leverage ratio from prediction alone is enormous, but the value capture depends on the last mile.

The competitive landscape is where most investors would flinch, and they'd be wrong to weight it the way it looks on paper. Periodic Labs raised $300M. Lila Sciences has $550M. CuspAI sits at $154M with Samsung Ventures. These are real companies with real capital and physical lab infrastructure. But they're all going broad -- AI for science writ large, hitting multiple verticals, optimizing for optionality. Matforge is doing the opposite: going absurdly narrow on semiconductor materials for datacenters and fabs. That narrowness is the contrarian bet. The semiconductor materials discovery problem isn't a software problem that general AI capabilities can solve by brute force. It requires understanding fab-compatible process integration, knowing which material properties the ITRS roadmap cares about at each node, and having the relationships to get candidates evaluated. CuspAI can list semiconductors as a focus area, but listing a focus area and having your materials in TSMC's roadmap are different universes of credibility. The structural insight is that incumbent materials suppliers (Entegris, Shin-Etsu) sell existing chemicals at scale -- they have no incentive to discover replacements that cannibalize their product lines. The fabs outsource exploratory materials research. Matforge is positioning to be that outsourced research partner, but with AI-driven throughput instead of academic timelines.

The CTO pairing is exactly right for this problem. Advaith Sridhar built long-horizon autonomous agents at Persona AI before it was acquired by Luma Labs, then worked on foundation model post-training at Luma. He's not a materials scientist, and he doesn't need to be. The product thesis -- autonomous AI agents orchestrating multi-step scientific workflows -- requires someone who has shipped agentic systems in production, not someone who understands crystallography. The founding team maps cleanly: deep domain knowledge on the CEO side, frontier AI systems engineering on the CTO side, and a relationship that predates the company by seven years (IIT Madras 2019 batchmates who co-authored a book together). That shared history matters. Play long-term games with long-term people -- and these two have been playing together since undergrad.

This is infrastructure for infrastructure. Every AI chip at every advanced node needs interconnect metals, dielectrics, and thermal interface materials. The customers are fewer than twenty companies, but those companies represent the entire advanced semiconductor manufacturing base of the planet. The concentrated buyer set is a risk in the traditional sense -- long sales cycles, single-customer dependency -- but it's also a leverage mechanism. If you become TSMC's trusted materials discovery partner, that relationship compounds at every process node. Each new node creates new materials requirements. Each validated candidate builds your proprietary dataset. Each successful engagement deepens the trust moat. This is the same compounding dynamic that made Stack Overflow's developer knowledge graph impossible to replicate -- not because the technology was unique, but because the accumulated knowledge and trust were. The solo-domain-expert risk is real: Akash is the only person on a two-person team with semiconductor materials knowledge. But at pre-seed, that concentration is the norm, not a disqualifier. The question is whether the compounding machine gets built before the key-person risk materializes.

The bull case is that compute scarcity is the defining constraint of the AI era, and semiconductor materials are the upstream bottleneck of that constraint. If AI chip power and heat really are doubling annually, foundries will pay almost anything for materials that solve thermal and electrical performance at advanced nodes. Matforge, with Akash's existing roadmap relationships and Advaith's agentic AI systems, could become the de facto materials discovery engine for the entire advanced semiconductor industry. The TAM isn't the $170M materials informatics software market -- it's a royalty on the $67-81B semiconductor materials market, captured through IP licensing on novel compounds. That's asymmetric upside of the kind I look for: limited downside on a small check, uncapped upside if the founder's specific knowledge turns out to be the scarce resource in a market desperate for solutions.
