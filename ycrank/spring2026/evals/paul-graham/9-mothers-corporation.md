# 9 Mothers -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that immediately grabs my attention about 9 Mothers isn't the product or the market -- it's the pivot. Russell Smith spent a decade building no-code QA testing tools at Rainforest QA. Roman Khomenko spent eight years as a data scientist at the same company. Now they're building autonomous weapon systems. That is one of the most dramatic domain leaps I've seen. And it forces a direct confrontation with something I've said many times: "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." None of these founders have built defense hardware, worked in military procurement, or designed weapon systems. If I take my own advice seriously -- and I should -- that's a real problem. But there's another framework that also applies here, and it points in the opposite direction.

I wrote about schlep blindness because I kept noticing that some of the best startup opportunities sat unsolved for years because founders' unconscious minds wouldn't even let them see ideas that involved painful, tedious work. "It's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site." Building an autonomous counter-drone weapon system for the U.S. military, navigating ITAR, surviving government procurement cycles that can stretch years -- that's about as painful a schlep as exists in startups. Most software founders would never attempt it. The fact that Smith and Khomenko are willing to charge into this space is either a sign of exceptional determination or a failure to understand what they're getting into. I genuinely don't know which, and the dossier doesn't give me enough evidence to distinguish between the two. What I do know is that Khomenko is Ukrainian, with a computer science degree from Kharkiv National University. Ukraine has been the proving ground for exactly the Group 1 drone threat that EDDA is designed to counter. That's not domain expertise in the traditional sense -- he hasn't built weapon systems -- but it's something potentially more powerful: a genuine, personal understanding of the problem that money can't buy. "A genuine interest in something is a very powerful motivator -- for some people, the most powerful motivator of all."

The co-founder relationship is one of the strongest signals here. Smith and Khomenko worked together for eight years at Rainforest QA. I've always said that startups do to the relationship between founders what a dog does to a sock -- if it can be pulled apart, it will be. Eight years of shared startup hardship is about as strong a test as you can get. Pyzh is the unknown -- no public overlap with the other two, though his security consulting background at Leviathan is at least directionally relevant to defense systems. Smith being a second-time YC founder (S12) also matters. He's been through the fire. Rainforest QA raised $42.3M from Bessemer and others, and while there's no public exit event, running a venture-backed company for a decade teaches you things you can't learn any other way. He knows what he doesn't know -- and "you can recognize genuinely smart people by their ability to say things like 'I don't know.'"

What gives me pause is the hiring pattern. Fourteen active job postings at $150K-$250K salaries with no visible revenue, no public customers, and no evidence of a deployed product. "Hiring too fast is by far the biggest killer of startups that raise money." Are these founders default alive or default dead? "Half the founders I talk to don't know whether they're default alive or default dead." This is a hardware defense company that will need to survive long procurement cycles on limited capital. The investors -- YC, Rsquared, Sterling Road, Theory Forge Ventures -- provide some runway, but I'd want to understand the burn rate before writing a check.

The bull case is genuinely compelling, and I want to lay it out honestly. The market timing is nearly perfect -- Ukraine proved that cheap drones change warfare, the DoD is pouring money into counter-UAS ($49.9B in defense tech investments), and Anduril's $20B Army contract ceiling proves institutional commitment to this category. "Worrying that you're late is one of the signs of a good idea," and you could argue the massive incumbents actually validate rather than threaten the opportunity. Anduril is building expensive, integrated platforms. 9 Mothers is going after something Anduril might consider too small to pursue -- portable, low-cost, tactical-edge systems. The acoustic ML approach is interesting because passive detection doesn't emit signatures the way radar does, which matters in contested environments. If these founders can actually build what they're describing, the form factor and price point differentiation could carve out a niche that defense primes would find uneconomical to attack. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." A portable counter-drone system is deceptively small compared to Anduril's platform play, but it's exactly what a dismounted infantry squad needs.

But I keep coming back to the gap between ambition and evidence. There's no indication of a working prototype, a field test, a government relationship, or a single customer conversation. The website returned a 403 error. The GitHub repos are a Rust UDP proxy tool. I don't see the equivalent of the Airbnbs selling cereal boxes -- that scrappy, cockroach-like evidence that these founders will find a way no matter what. Maybe it exists and just isn't public, which is plausible for a defense company. But from where I sit, I'm being asked to invest in a massive domain leap on the strength of a strong co-founder relationship, a repeat founder, and excellent market timing. That's enough to keep me interested, but not enough to write a check from my own money today.
