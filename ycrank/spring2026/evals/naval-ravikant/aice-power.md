# AICE Power -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been deploying clip-on CT sensors with AI disaggregation since 2011, has raised $41.8M, and targets the same core problem AICE Power is pursuing. When a well-funded competitor has a fourteen-year head start using nearly identical technology, the question becomes: what does AICE Power know that Verdigris doesn't? The answer, based on everything in this dossier, is a market segment — small-format multi-site commercial buildings — not a technology. Segment targeting is strategy, not specific knowledge. Any competent team with the same sensors and the same NILM papers could make the same observation. AICE Power's differentiation is "we go downmarket and charge less." That's a business model choice, and business models change. The founder's specific knowledge is what survives when everything else shifts.

Grégoire Chomette's background is MIT Aeronautics, NASA asteroid risk assessment, and one EPFL publication on biogas supply chain optimization from 2018. Rémi Bouteiller designed PCBs and BLE firmware for medical wearables at Amira Health, managed manufacturing in Shenzhen, and won a French robotics championship. Both are clearly gifted engineers. But neither has spent years obsessing over how commercial buildings waste electricity. Chomette wasn't monitoring supermarket HVAC systems before founding AICE Power — he was modeling asteroid impact trajectories. Bouteiller was building hearing aids. Their skills transfer to building a sensor-to-cloud pipeline, but transferable engineering skills are precisely the kind of knowledge that society can train someone else to replicate. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had been inside the exact neural architectures that make AI search work for years at Google Brain and OpenAI. AICE Power's founders identified an energy monitoring opportunity; they didn't emerge from one.

The leverage architecture presents a structural ceiling that my framework catches immediately. AICE Power's software layer — AI disaggregation, weather-adjusted baselines, multi-site dashboards, real-time anomaly alerts — is genuine code leverage with near-zero marginal cost per additional dashboard user. But every dollar of new revenue requires manufacturing physical sensors, shipping a 4G gateway, and someone spending thirty minutes at an electrical panel. That's hardware leverage layered under software leverage, and hardware is permissioned — it requires supply chains, inventory, logistics, field support. Notion scales to millions of users without anyone touching a circuit board. AICE Power scales to the next supermarket only after deploying metal on a panel. This mixed leverage profile means the team-to-value equation deteriorates as AICE Power grows: two founders serving 10+ sites tracking 5+ MW is impressive density today, but reaching hundreds of sites will force proportional hiring in manufacturing, deployment, and field support. Bouteiller's Shenzhen manufacturing experience partially offsets this risk but doesn't eliminate the structural physics of hardware scaling.

The bull case an advocate would make is compelling on paper. AICE Power is attacking a classic Christensen disruption position: Honeywell and Siemens sell through specialized integrators whose economics require high-margin, complex installations, making it structurally irrational for them to serve a site that costs 5% of their current price point. EU EPBD regulation is creating mandatory monitoring requirements in commercial buildings, generating demand that didn't exist two years ago. E.Leclerc and Accor as named enterprise customers for a two-person pre-seed team demonstrates real procurement credibility. The data moat compounds — every deployed site generates load signatures that improve disaggregation models, and multi-site comparison within a chain creates benchmarking value that single-site competitors can't match. If AICE Power captures thousands of small commercial sites across European chains, the data flywheel becomes genuinely defensible. This is the compound interest case, and it's real. But compound interest requires a principal at the center who cannot be replaced, and the technology requires a leverage architecture that scales without linearly scaling humans. AICE Power has neither. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Chomette has been studying building energy waste for years before founding AICE Power — unpublished projects, prior tools built for himself, domain fluency that takes a competitor years to acquire. That would change the specific knowledge score dramatically and shift the entire evaluation.

AICE Power does score well on access democratization — arguably its strongest thesis-fit dimension. Small commercial buildings "have no monitoring at all," and AICE Power claims deployment at 5% the cost of traditional BMS with same-day installation versus weeks. This follows the AngelList pattern of removing a structural gatekeeper: just as angel investing was locked behind accredited investor networks and AICE Power's BMS monitoring was locked behind $50K+ integrator projects, AICE Power gives thousands of supermarkets and retail stores access to energy visibility they could never previously afford. The 6-month ROI claim, if validated, means the product pays for itself faster than the customer's budget cycle — a rare self-selling characteristic. But access democratization alone has never been sufficient for my highest-conviction bets. AngelList worked because I had decades of specific knowledge about what the angel market needed. AICE Power's access democratization is real but built on transferable engineering rather than irreplaceable insight.

One red flag my framework specifically watches for: the co-founder relationship. No shared employer or university was found between Chomette (MIT/EPFL/NASA) and Bouteiller (ENS Paris-Saclay/IP Paris/ESSEC/Amira Health). The dossier infers a connection "through French engineering school networks or the Paris/Station F startup ecosystem." Co-founders without pre-existing deep trust and shared history create internal principal-agent problems before the company launches. This isn't disqualifying — it's a data point — but combined with the opportunity-identification origin story rather than an obsession-driven one, it reinforces the pattern of agents assembling around a market opportunity rather than principals who couldn't stop building together.

AICE Power is a competent, capital-efficient hardware-software team attacking a structurally underserved market with a clear Christensen disruption playbook. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation are specific knowledge productization and permissionless leverage architecture. On specific knowledge: neither Chomette's NASA asteroid modeling nor Bouteiller's medical wearable firmware constitutes the non-replicable energy domain expertise that would make AICE Power's core insight survive founder replacement — and at 10/30, this falls below even my absent-evidence default, because I have evidence of adjacent rather than domain-specific knowledge. On leverage: the hardware dependency creates a structural ceiling on scaling efficiency that pure-software companies in my portfolio don't face. These two dimensions together define a company that can build a solid business but not the kind of leveraged, knowledge-moated outcome that justifies angel risk. I pass.
