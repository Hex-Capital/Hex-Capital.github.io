# Beyond Reach Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The $175M in LOIs from a two-person pre-seed team jumps off the page -- until you remember that in space hardware, non-binding letters of intent are the industry's cheapest currency. Every defense and space startup I've seen can produce impressive LOI numbers because procurement officers sign them as "we'd be interested if this works." The real question isn't whether people want more power in orbit. Of course they do. The question is whether the demand curve they're projecting -- 50 MW today to 500 GW by 2035 -- represents a structural inflection I can map, or a forecast that requires its own set of miracles to materialize. Orbital datacenters and lunar outposts are the demand drivers cited. Neither exists today in any commercial form. When the market you're selling into also needs multiple miracles to exist, you're compounding low-probability events -- exactly the pattern I avoid.

Let me separate what's real from what's speculative in the "why now." Starship reducing launch costs by an order of magnitude -- that's happening. DARPA funding large-scale deployable structures through NOM4D -- that's real government validation of the technology category. Multiple commercial space station programs in development -- real, but years from needing football-field-scale solar arrays. The current spacecraft solar panel market is roughly $600M, growing at 8% CAGR. That's not a market at an inflection point. That's a market growing linearly. The inflection to 500 GW would require the emergence of entirely new categories of space infrastructure that are themselves pre-revenue, pre-validation ventures. Compare this to when I looked at Coinbase: blockchain transactions were already happening at scale, the market was messy but demonstrably real. Here, the market Beyond Reach Labs needs doesn't yet exist. They're building for a structural moment that may be correct but is likely 7-10 years away -- the Webvan timing problem.

The founder-market fit is genuinely exceptional, and I want to be clear about that. Collins spent seven years at SpaceX leading Dragon parachute engineering across 30+ missions -- he's designed, qualified, and flown human-rated space hardware. Fogelson invented the core technology during his PhD, holds the patents, and has already tested in microgravity on NASA's dime. They've known each other thirteen years from undergrad at Penn. This is not a team that wandered into space hardware because it's trendy. But exceptional founders don't override a multi-miracle problem. Their path to meaningful revenue requires: (1) successful orbital demonstration in Q2 2027, (2) flight qualification -- a multi-year process with failure modes that ground testing can't fully capture, (3) conversion of LOIs against incumbents who have flight heritage they don't, and (4) manufacturing scale-up from a 2-person prototype shop to aerospace-grade production. That's four sequential low-probability events. When I funded Anduril, the structural vacancy was immediate -- Google had walked away from Project Maven and there was no credible startup competitor. Here, Redwire has flight-proven arrays on the ISS right now, and Northrop Grumman has UltraFlex on Mars. The incumbents haven't left the field.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If Starship delivers on cost reduction and commercial space stations actually get built, the power bottleneck becomes the binding constraint on an entire industry. The length-stiffness-mass tradeoff is a real physics problem, and metamaterial-based deployment is a genuinely different structural paradigm -- not an incremental improvement on roll-out or fan-fold designs. If the 50x expansion ratio holds up in orbit, this could be the enabling technology for everything from orbital manufacturing to deep-space missions. The ITAR/regulatory complexity creates exactly the kind of compliance moat I've been comfortable with at Color and Anduril. And the patented IP originating from years of PhD research isn't easily replicated -- you can't hire two engineers and reproduce this in six months. If I believed the demand curve would arrive within 3-5 years rather than 7-10, this would be a different conversation. The team is right, the technology is real, and the market thesis is directionally correct. But "directionally correct with uncertain timing" is how I'd describe Webvan, not Instacart.

The product-to-distribution dimension is where space hardware fundamentally differs from the software infrastructure I typically back. Stripe's API became a distribution channel for twelve additional products. A deployable solar array is a hardware sale -- each unit is a discrete transaction, not a relationship that compounds. There might be expansion into deployable antennas, radiators, or other structures using the same metamaterial platform, but that's speculative, and each new application requires its own qualification cycle. This is a project business, not a platform business. The margin structure and revenue predictability look more like traditional aerospace than software-driven infrastructure. This isn't "software aware vs. software driven" exactly -- the metamaterial design optimization is genuinely software-enabled -- but the business model is hardware procurement with long sales cycles and lumpy revenue.

I'm passing, but with the caveat that I want to revisit after the orbital demo. If the Q2 2027 flight works, one miracle is eliminated and the risk profile changes materially. The founders are exactly the kind of operators I respect -- deep domain expertise, real technical IP, long-standing working relationship. But at pre-seed, I'm being asked to underwrite four sequential miracles in a market that itself needs miracles to reach the projected scale. That's too many compounding low-probability events for my framework, regardless of how impressive the team is.
