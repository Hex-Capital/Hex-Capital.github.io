# Dayjob -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Dayjob is a competent-operator company without a secret: George Postlethwaite launched Deliveroo's UK grocery business and spent three years inside Gaea building ERP for e-waste recyclers, which is real founder-market depth, but the thing he is selling -- an "AI agent that overlays AMCS and Routeware" to re-optimize ~100 daily waste-truck jobs -- is exactly the consensus 2026 YC shape with operator pedigree bolted on. The high dimension is lineage, the low dimension is atoms-heavy moat, and that pairing names the file: vertical SaaS feature for a regulated-adjacent customer, not a regulated-customer business.

The Pass Example I wrote against -- "AI-powered platform for [vertical X], no named incumbent failure mode, moat hypothesis terminates at 'we have a better model'" -- is approximately the Dayjob YC page. Stripe (2010) had a contrarian secret with the incumbent named and the blind spot specific: PayPal's stack could not be addressed by developers because the company had architected itself around merchant-account sales, not API consumers. Dayjob names AMCS and Routeware, which is more than most, but the dossier's own framing of the wedge -- "overlays the ERP rather than replacing it" -- is not a secret, it is a sales objection-handler. AMCS already "ships AI-driven routing built into the platform" per the dossier's own Tracxn line, and Routeware acquired RUBICONSmartCity in 2024 specifically to add this surface. The Straussian read of Postlethwaite's positioning is not "the incumbents structurally cannot do this," it is "the incumbents have not yet bothered to ship the v2 module." That is a feature gap, not a Lockheed-Raytheon-cost-structure gap.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the right contrast on the lowest dimension, atoms-heavy regulated-sector moat: defense gave him a customer whose switching cost is integration depth and a procurement clock that punishes new entrants but rewards the one that gets in. Dayjob's customer -- Allstone, Coastal Recycling, one unnamed UK skip-hire fleet -- sits adjacent to environmental regulation but is not itself the regulated entity in any way that confers Dayjob a moat. The atoms in this picture (skips, trucks, depots) belong to the customer; Dayjob's product is a pure-software scheduling layer. There is no certification depth, no hardware capex, no government procurement gate. The "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" critique applies in miniature: this is bits dispatching atoms someone else owns.

On monopoly trajectory, the 18-month mechanism is unfavorable. Dayjob's moat thesis is "ERP integration into operator back-ends" plus "depot/scheduler workflow data from Gaea." Both compound only if Dayjob captures enough fleets before AMCS ($273M revenue) or Routeware (1,000+ haulers) ships a native module priced at zero-marginal-cost inside an existing license. The customer-reported "8%+ efficiency gains from day one" is feature-grade improvement, not category-grade -- exactly the magnitude an incumbent absorbs by adding a checkbox. Pricing power five years out requires a story the dossier does not contain.

Counterfactual for the definite-plan dimension: to flip a tier, Dayjob would need a multi-year roadmap that commits to a specific architectural bet Postlethwaite will not pivot from -- say, "we are building the autonomous depot operating system; routing is year one, predictive maintenance year two, dynamic pricing year three, and we will not sell horizontally into food logistics." Instead the dossier shows a January 2025 pivot from Gaea, an August 2025 product launch, and the "AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" positioning that quietly hedges the vertical. That hedge is the tell. Definite-optimism founders narrow as they grow; Dayjob is already widening before it owns waste.

On lineage, the single anchor is Dustin Moskovitz at Asana (2010), the known exception where pure-SaaS-without-a-secret got a check because the founder had shipped Facebook to scale. Postlethwaite launched Deliveroo's UK grocery business and ran sales at Otta -- meaningful, but Deliveroo grocery is not Facebook, and Otta is not a Thiel-portfolio operator role. Fooks at Deloitte ESG and Capgemini is solid technical adjacency, not the Levchin or Luckey override I would need to fund through a missing secret. The 13-year Oxford friendship and shared Gaea wreckage is genuine co-founder bond and genuine domain time. It is enough to clear a lineage floor; it is not enough to override the structural absence on dimensions one, two, and five.

Thirty-six month forecast: Dayjob reaches $2-4M ARR across roughly 15-25 UK and US waste fleets, then is acquired by AMCS or Routeware for $15-30M as a feature tuck-in once one of them decides building the agent module in-house is slower than buying the team that already integrated with their API. This is a fine outcome for Postlethwaite and Fooks. It is not a power-law outcome and it is not what my own capital is for.

The bull case that stings: depot-level workflow data captured over three years of Gaea ride-alongs is genuinely tacit knowledge that AMCS's product managers in Limerick do not have, and if Dayjob compounds that into a proprietary scheduling dataset across fifty fleets before incumbents react, the integration becomes the moat -- this is the Stripe-developer-experience argument transposed to waste. The bear answer is the dossier's own competitive line: AMCS already ships AI routing and Routeware bought RUBICONSmartCity in 2024 precisely to own this surface, so the reaction window is months, not years, and the cannibalization conflict I would normally cite as Dayjob's protection only holds if the incumbent's license revenue actually drops -- which it will not, because routing is a module sold inside the license, not a separate SKU. Verdict flips to invest if Dayjob signs a named US enterprise hauler (Waste Management, Republic Services, GFL) at six-figure ACV within twelve months, because that would prove the wedge is reachable above the AMCS ceiling rather than below it. Absent that, pass.
