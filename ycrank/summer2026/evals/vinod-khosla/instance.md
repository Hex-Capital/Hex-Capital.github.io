# Instance -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Instance is a learning-rate signal attached to a consequence-magnitude void -- Claire Mao and Lucy Cai pivoted on the record from "AI data analyst for databases" in January 2026 to a "physics-aware quality layer" for synthetic video by their YC Summer 2026 listing, which is the cleanest pivot evidence in the dossier and also the only dimension where Instance scores above my floor. The success-state at maximum ambition is "world-model labs have a better eval harness," and that is not a sentence I have ever underwritten a check on.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the wrong analogy and the right ladder rung. When I wrote the $50M into a no-revenue, unconventional-structure company, the success-state was a civilizational redirection of cognitive labor -- the $15 trillion frame I gave Fortune (2026-03-06). Instance occupies the opposite position on this axis: it is infrastructure *for* someone else's OpenAI bet. If Instance wins maximally, NVIDIA Cosmos and the Genie/Veo class of world models train on cleaner synthetic data and ship six months earlier; the consequence accrues to the model lab, not to Instance. The dossier names no incumbent regime that collapses when Instance succeeds -- not diesel long-haul, not animal protein, not human radiology reads. A benchmark company at best becomes MLPerf, and MLPerf is a non-profit. That is the entry-point failure.

The **GPT-4-sales-call-summarizer** pass pattern is the failure mode to test Instance against -- a thin layer on top of someone else's foundation breakthrough where any Tier-1 generalist can fund the round and the success-state is a 20-30% workflow improvement at one persona. Does Instance fit? Partially. The buyer set is narrower and more technical -- frontier video-world-model labs versus HubSpot users -- but the structural shape is the same: four published academic benchmarks (T2VPhysBench, WorldModelBench, VideoPhy-2, LikePhys, all 2024-2025 arxiv) already cover what Instance proposes to commercialize, and Encord ($110M raised, serving Toyota Woven and Skydio per TechFundingNews 2025) and Meta-owned Scale AI have both the labor force and the customer relationships to bolt on a physics module in a quarter. Instance is closer to this rung than to anything I have funded.

On technical and capital risk, the mechanism analysis matters more than the rung. Reliable open-domain physics-violation detection is unsolved -- LikePhys (Oct 2025) explicitly frames it as open research -- so there is *some* science risk here. But the capital profile compounds the wrong way over 18 months: a physics-eval product hits a wall not at $50M of training compute but at the moment a frontier lab decides to fold the capability in-house, which is the rational build-versus-buy outcome when the buyer count is in the single digits. The Commonwealth Fusion analogy fails -- there is no tokamak here, just a labeling workflow against a moving target.

Counterfactual on founder-defies-expert-consensus: for Instance to clear my threshold on this axis, Cai or Mao would need a public position -- a paper, a thread, a stated thesis -- that the field is wrong about how synthetic video should be evaluated. The dossier surfaces an MIT MEng in AI and a Lincoln Lab spotlight on 2D-to-3D point clouds for Cai, and an MIT Math + CS profile plus MIT Council for Math Majors presidency for Mao. These are credentials, not contrarian commitments. The TED-talk-Berkeley study (28,000 predictions, "only slightly more accurate than chance") applies symmetrically: there is no expert-defying instigator profile in Instance's founder dossier, and there is also no expert-credential heavyweight of the Arvind Jain / Glean exception variety to override on consequence scale.

Compared on capital profile to **Cognition** ($175M Series B April 2024 on AI software engineers), Instance is in a different solar system. A two-person team building an eval product against a buyer set of a dozen labs does not absorb $50M to first commercial milestone -- it absorbs $3-5M to figure out whether any of those labs will pay, and then either gets pulled into one of them or doesn't. I am over-resourced for this deal and so is every generalist on Sand Hill.

My 36-month forecast: Instance is acqui-hired by Encord, Surge AI, or Scale/Meta for under $40M in 2027-2028 as a physics-eval module bolted onto an existing labeling platform, *or* pivots again -- the third thesis in 24 months -- into a vertical robotics simulation play. The mechanism is straightforward: a two-person MIT team with a documented pivot history and a narrow buyer set converges on whichever incumbent extends a term sheet first, and the physics-violation dataset they accumulate is worth more inside Encord's pipeline than as a standalone benchmark.

The bull case stings and I want to name it honestly: the same Jan 2026 pivot I am treating as a yellow flag is *exactly* the **Slash** (2024) pattern that justified my $100M Series C -- founders demonstrating learning rate by abandoning a worse thesis for a better one, and if NVIDIA Cosmos (Jan 2025) is the inflection that makes synthetic video the default robotics training substrate, then a physics-eval standard becomes load-bearing infrastructure and Instance is the first commercial entrant. NVIDIA acquires for $200M+, the academic benchmarks become reference implementations, and the proprietary violation dataset compounds. What dismantles this case is that Slash's pivot was *inside* consumer fintech with a documented user-traction signal at the pivot point; Instance's pivot leaves zero transferred traction and lands in a category where four open-source benchmarks already exist and the buyer set is a research-grade frontier-lab oligopoly that builds eval in-house by default. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: two named frontier video-model labs (NVIDIA Cosmos, Google DeepMind Veo, Runway, Pika, or a robotics foundation-model startup) sign six-figure paid contracts with Instance before the Demo Day check clears. Absent that, this is a pass.
