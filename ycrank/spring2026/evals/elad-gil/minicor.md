# Minicor -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is precise and identifiable: Anthropic shipped Computer Use in public beta in October 2024, crossing the capability threshold where AI agents could reliably interact with desktop application UIs. That's the technology event. But the demand-side event is what makes this interesting -- AI companies expanding into healthcare, automotive, supply chain, and financial services are hitting a wall that has nothing to do with model capability. Their customers' systems of record are Windows desktop applications with no writable APIs, and in some cases vendors are actively restricting third-party access. This creates a bottleneck that looks boring from the outside -- "clicking buttons on old software" -- but is actually the production-blocking constraint for an entire category of AI deployment. When I was at Google working on mobile, we encountered a version of this same problem: new platform capabilities were useless if you couldn't connect them to the systems where the data actually lived. Legacy integration isn't glamorous, but it's where enterprise value chains get stuck.

What caught my attention is the compliance posture at this stage. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certification for a five-person pre-seed team is operationally expensive and time-consuming -- Type II typically requires six to twelve months of demonstrated controls. Most pre-seed founders either don't know they need this or push it to Series A. These founders did it first, which tells me two things: they understand that compliance is the gate to production deployment in healthcare and financial services, and they're building compliance-as-moat intentionally. I've invested repeatedly in companies where regulatory barriers deter software-focused investors but create structural defensibility -- Color Health required CLIA certification, Coinbase navigated financial regulation, Anduril operates under ITAR. The pattern holds: if you can get through the compliance gauntlet early, every subsequent competitor faces the same months-long process while you're already in production.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has a Stripe-like structural possibility that I find compelling. Stripe started as a payments API but became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury -- because once developers integrated payments, Stripe owned that relationship. Minicor's MCP interface positions it similarly: if AI companies integrate Minicor to access one legacy system (say, Epic for healthcare), Minicor becomes the natural path to access additional systems (dental PMS, supply chain, automotive DMS) as those AI companies expand. Each new legacy system Minicor supports isn't just a product improvement -- it's a distribution expansion into an adjacent vertical of AI company customers. The API-first model and managed infrastructure (VMs, Citrix, observability) create integration depth that makes switching costly once embedded in production workflows. The founders are thinking about distribution from day one, which is exactly the signal I weight heavily.

The commoditization question is the central risk and deserves direct treatment. Anthropic's Computer Use API is public. Any team can build desktop automation on top of it. This surfaces the "smart toaster" test: is Minicor software-driven or merely software-aware? I think the answer is software-driven, but only because of the managed infrastructure layer -- the VMs, the scaling orchestration, the compliance certifications, the self-healing recovery loops, the observability with video recordings and screenshot-level debugging. The raw model API can see a screen and click buttons. Making that reliable at 25,000 patients per day in a HIPAA-compliant environment with deterministic code paths and AI-only-for-recovery architecture is a genuinely different engineering problem. UiPath's AI Healing Agent feature is the more serious competitive threat -- they have enterprise distribution and an installed base. But UiPath sells to enterprise IT buyers through traditional sales cycles. Minicor sells to AI companies through developer-oriented APIs. These are different customers reached through different channels, which gives Minicor a window before incumbents adapt their GTM.

The founders' background maps well to the problem. Saheed spent three years as a senior engineer at Ford Motor Company -- one of the archetypal legacy enterprise environments -- and worked at Morgan Stanley, another. Faizaan built at Phoenix (YC W21), which involved system integration work. They've been building together since 2019 through Software For Love and started Minicor in 2023, which means the co-founder relationship has been tested across multiple projects over six years. The traction claims -- "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" and "thousands of workflows a week" -- suggest real production usage, though the phrasing "at same architecture scale" is ambiguous about whether this is actual patient volume or theoretical capacity. Either way, for a team founded in 2024, the velocity from concept to compliance-certified production infrastructure is notable.

The single miracle here is achieving reliable enough accuracy that AI companies can deploy legacy system integrations in production without human oversight. The 93-96% click accuracy claim (versus 80-85% for competitors) matters because at 25,000 operations per day, the difference between 93% and 96% is hundreds of daily failures in healthcare workflows. The architectural decision to store automation as deterministic code and use AI only for recovery and edge cases directly attacks this miracle -- it constrains the problem space rather than hoping the AI model handles everything. The miracle is being partially delivered externally (model capability improvements from Anthropic and others will push accuracy higher), which means Minicor can focus on infrastructure reliability rather than model research. That's the Harvey pattern applied to a different domain.
