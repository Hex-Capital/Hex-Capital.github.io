# Lamina Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Knowlify raised $3M last year, already has Google and Amazon teams as customers, and is building in the same prompt-to-explainer-video space that Lamina Labs is entering with a waitlist and zero disclosed traction. Synthesia sits at $4B with $146M in ARR. Colossyan took $22M. The dossier itself states the quiet part plainly: "The barrier is accuracy and speed at scale, not conceptual novelty." When I hear that the conceptual approach is openly replicable using open-source Manim plus any commercial LLM, and the only claimed differentiation is execution speed from a single Twitter testimonial, I'm looking at the exact pattern that Lee and I have talked about avoiding — a layer on top of existing infrastructure that a well-resourced competitor can replicate in a quarter. Lamina Labs may have built something genuinely proprietary under the hood, but the dossier gives me no evidence of it, and the product has already been renamed from Pictor to Simi, which at pre-seed reads like founders still searching for what this actually is.

Kartikesh Mishra and Sudip Rokaya are clearly sharp — MIT CS, CSAIL research under Daniela Rus on visual-language models, coursework in ML and computer vision. Sudip left MIT to build Lamina Labs, which shows real conviction. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I get back is academic credentials and a market observation about 3Blue1Brown-style education videos being hard to make. I don't hear a biographical crucible. I don't hear what happened to Kartikesh or Sudip that made educational animation *their* inevitable problem. Sudip is from Nepal, which hints at a story about educational access that could be deeply compelling — growing up in a system where great visual teaching was inaccessible, where the quality of your education depended entirely on the luck of your teacher assignment. But Lamina Labs doesn't tell that story, and I can't infer it into existence. The absence matters to me. When founders lead with credentials and TAM rather than the personal wound that drove them here, I register it as career optimization, not mission inevitability.

Lamina Labs sits in a space — EdTech AI, projected at $32B by 2030 — that every VC in the world is comfortable funding right now. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "AI for education." Compare that to when I wrote my check into Anduril, and half of Silicon Valley thought defense tech was morally disqualifying. Or Contraline — try pitching male birth control at a partner meeting and watch the room get quiet. Lamina Labs is building in a consensus-attractive category with well-funded competitors already present. The "magically weird" signal I look for — the idea that provokes genuine discomfort in sophisticated investors — is entirely absent here. This is a sensible application of generative AI to a proven demand pattern. Sensible doesn't make me lean in.

The invisible system question is where Lamina Labs almost has something. Education *is* one of the most opaque, extractive systems I know — who gets access to great teaching, what determines whether a kid in rural Nepal or rural Alabama gets a clear explanation of calculus, why visual intuition remains locked behind either coding expertise (Manim) or production budgets (After Effects). If Lamina Labs were positioned as the company that makes every student's teacher as clear as Grant Sanderson, that would resonate with the part of me that sees hidden structures everywhere. But "visual infrastructure for EdTech AI applications" is a B2B tool positioning, not a system disruption. Lamina Labs is making content creation faster for people who already create content. That's efficiency, not transparency. The extractive structure — the gatekeeping of quality visual education — remains unaddressed by the stated go-to-market.

Here is the strongest bull case an advocate would make: 3Blue1Brown's 6M+ subscribers prove that visual math education has enormous demand, but the creation side is catastrophically broken. Manim requires Python fluency. After Effects requires design training. Lamina Labs' claimed 11-second generation time versus 15+ minutes for competitors, if real, implies a custom rendering pipeline that constitutes genuine technical infrastructure — not a wrapper. Kartikesh's CSAIL research on visual-language models is precisely the skillset required to build that pipeline. The Serve Robotics parallel applies: take a category with proven demand (delivery → visual education), make it autonomous, and the first mover with the best engine wins. And at pre-seed, you bet on technical founding teams in large markets, not current traction. The condition that would flip me: if I sat with Sudip and Kartikesh and heard a personal story about educational access that made this problem biographical — and if they showed me the rendering architecture proving this is infrastructure, not an API wrapper — I could see myself writing a check. One verifiable proof point: demonstrate that Lamina Labs' animation engine works without calling any external LLM API for the core rendering step, proving proprietary depth rather than orchestration.

But I can't get there today. Lamina Labs fails my two highest-weighted criteria — Founder's Biographical Forge and Contrarian Conviction — and triggers my explicit "AI wrapper" red flag pattern. The founders present credentials where I need crucible. The market is consensus where I need discomfort. The product improves creation efficiency where I need structural disruption. And the competitive landscape — Knowlify's $3M and enterprise customers, Synthesia's $4B valuation, multiple open-source alternatives — creates commoditization pressure that the dossier acknowledges and cannot refute. The dimension that locks this verdict is Founder's Biographical Forge: without a story connecting these founders to the problem at the level of lived experience rather than market observation, Lamina Labs remains a technically capable team chasing a well-identified opportunity. I wish them well, but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
