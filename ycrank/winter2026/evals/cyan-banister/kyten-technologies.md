# Kyten Technologies -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Kyten is that this is a popular deal. Defense tech reshoring, NDAA compliance, domestic drone battery supply chains — every investor I know is looking at exactly this category right now. The DoD is publicly announcing plans to procure a million drones, the Pentagon is developing a dedicated battery procurement strategy, and 90% of the current supply chain runs through China. That's not an insight — that's a headline. And in my experience, when the "why now" reads like a briefing deck that any analyst could assemble, the weirdness has already been arbitraged away. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy, Crusoe when they wanted to mine Bitcoin on gas flares — were companies that made people uncomfortable. Kyten makes people nod. That's a meaningful difference for how I invest.

If I could sit down with Cooper McBride and Lucas Maddox, my first question would be the same one I always ask: tell me your story, not the business. And from what I can see in the dossier, what I'd hear is a career narrative — Tesla internship, SpaceX progression, Shield AI, leading 30-person teams, 6,000 battery packs to orbit. That's genuinely impressive engineering credentialing, and I don't dismiss it. But it's a resume, not a survival story. I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem — the hardship that forged an obsession. What I see instead is two accomplished aerospace engineers who identified a market gap through their professional vantage point and decided to fill it. That's a rational founding story. It's just not the kind of story that makes me write a first check from my own money. When I backed Contraline, it was because the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception while everyone else thought the idea was absurd. There was a biographical inevitability to it. I don't feel that here.

Now let me make the strongest case for why I might be wrong, because I owe the founders that honesty. The domain expertise is not just relevant — it's the exact job. These two didn't study batteries in a lab; they manufactured and deployed 6,000 of them to orbit at one of the most demanding companies on the planet. The claim of compressing 12-month timelines to 3 weeks, if real, is a genuine capability moat rooted in their SpaceX process knowledge. The timing is extraordinary — the DoD procurement surge is happening now, and domestic capacity doesn't exist at the scale needed. Aerospace qualification creates real switching costs once you're locked into a platform. A defense-focused investor would probably look at this deal and see exactly the kind of founder-market fit they want: proven operators attacking a structural supply gap with a speed advantage incumbents can't match. If Kyten executes and captures even a fraction of the procurement surge, the economics could be compelling. This is a real company solving a real problem with real expertise.

But through my specific lens, it doesn't activate enough of what I look for. It doesn't create economic access for people who lack it — it serves defense OEMs and aerospace primes. It doesn't make an invisible system visible — battery packs are a well-understood component; Kyten just makes them faster. The competitive landscape is already well-capitalized: Packet Digital has a $50M DoD award, Lyten has over $425M, Amprius is publicly traded. And the underlying product — battery pack integration — is a known engineering discipline. The moat is execution speed, which is real but replicable given sufficient talent and investment. I've backed capital-intensive infrastructure plays before — SpaceX was my very first angel check — but that was because Elon had a gravitational intensity that made the impossible feel merely difficult, and because private space was a generational bet nobody else would make. Kyten is a solid manufacturing startup in a hot category. It's the right company for someone, but it's not my kind of bet. I need the jolt, and I don't feel it here.

The one dimension where Kyten genuinely shines through my framework is navigating complexity. Aerospace-grade battery manufacturing involves real qualification barriers, thermal management challenges, compliance requirements, and the cell supply chain constraint is non-trivial. These founders have proven they can operate at that altitude — literally. My IronPort background gives me genuine respect for founders who choose hard, regulated, technically demanding sectors over easy software plays. If everything else aligned — if the founding story had more biographical weight, if the category were less consensus, if the company created broader economic access — this complexity navigation would be the foundation of a strong investment thesis.
