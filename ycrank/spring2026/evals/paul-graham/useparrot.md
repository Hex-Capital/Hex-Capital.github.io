# Parrot -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Phillip Baek and Liam McBride are biomedical and computer engineers who previously co-founded a life-sciences computer-vision company called Alchemy AI, and now they are pitching Parrot as an AI operating system for collision repair shops — supplement turnaround dropping from 5 days to 2 at one pilot shop. The signature here is unusual and worth naming directly: Parrot scores highest on schlep zone (calling adjusters, parts vendors, state-by-state supplement regs) and lowest on organic founder-problem fit (neither founder has any documented exposure to a body shop). Parrot is a tourist in a schlep market — a profile I do not see often, because most tourists pick clean SaaS, and most schlep-chasers picked the schlep because they lived in it.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the high rung on the schlep ladder I keep returning to — Brian Armstrong walked toward bitcoin compliance because everyone else in his batch flinched. Parrot is doing the analogous thing in collision: insurance supplement negotiation, parallel calls to parts suppliers, write-back into CCC ONE and Audatex and Mitchell 1. No clever YC kid wants to spend their twenties on the phone with a State Farm adjuster about a labor-rate dispute. Parrot picked that work, and the pilot evidence — $34K of supplement dollars processed monthly, $2,800 in parts savings — suggests they have actually done it, not just slide-decked it. That said, the schlep is not yet a moat the way Armstrong's compliance grind became one; Parrot is one shop in, and Rote (YC) is already inside the highest-value slice of the same schlep, which is the part I cannot wave away.

**Dropbox** (2007) is the canonical organic-idea case — Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT and built the thing for himself before anyone asked. Parrot inverts that. Phillip Baek's prior role was EP Mapping Specialist at Boston Scientific; Liam McBride was at Atsign and CivitasAI. Nothing in the dossier says either founder spent a summer at a body shop, has a parent who runs one, or hit this problem in their own life. The Alchemy AI → Parrot pivot reads as market-spotted, not lived. The failure mode this exposes — and the reason "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) put organic origin at the center — is that founders who did not feel the pain underestimate which corners of the workflow are non-negotiable to the shop owner, and they pivot away from the unpleasant parts under pressure. The 90-day pilot is real evidence against that worry, but it is one shop.

The animal-resourcefulness dimension compounds over the next 18 months in a specific way for Parrot: the pilot shop's +20% revenue lift and 100% inbound answer rate are exactly the kind of unscalable-thing-that-doesn't-scale that should let Parrot hand-sell shop #2 through shop #10 in person, the way the Airbnb founders went door-to-door in New York. If Parrot is still showing one pilot in six months, the absence of a second shop is the tell — animals leave tracks across multiple sites, not one. The pilot is more than nothing, but it is less than Houston's Hacker News demo, because Parrot has not yet shown they can repeat it.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is what would have to be true to flip this tier. Liam McBride has a UIUC MS in Computer Engineering and prior stints at Stanford and Atsign, which is competent — but no GitHub footprint, no shipped open source, no competitive programming wins, no Hacker News handle surfaced in the dossier. To move Parrot from "technical-by-credential" to the Collison-brothers-Lisp tier, I would need to see something like a published latency benchmark for the voice-agent stack or an open-source release of the CCC ONE integration layer. Absent that, this is the dimension where I apply my default lean-down, because hackers leave trails and the silence is itself a signal.

On co-founder configuration, Parrot is closest to the **Stripe** (2010) Collison-brothers pattern in shape — two founders, prior shared venture, multi-year working relationship at Alchemy AI before pivoting into Parrot. That is the part of the dossier I weight most positively without qualification. It is not a hackathon pairing; it is a second at-bat together, and second at-bats are where Kan and Shear went from Kiko to Twitch.

My 36-month forecast for Parrot: pivoted or acquihired into CCC ONE / Solera Audatex / Mitchell for $15M–$40M, with a small probability of $2M–$5M ARR as an independent vertical voice-agent vendor, and a non-trivial probability of dead-or-merged-with-Rote. The mechanism producing this is the high/low signature — Parrot picked a real schlep without organic founder ownership of it, which is the configuration where the founders' conviction wavers at month 18 when a second-shop deployment turns out to be three times the supplement-regulation work of the first, and the natural exit is into the incumbent whose API they already integrate against.

The strongest case against this forecast is that Armstrong himself was not a bitcoin native — he was an Airbnb engineer who picked the schlep because he saw it, and the wallet prototype was what rescued him after the written rejection. Parrot's pilot numbers are the analogous artifact. If the +20% revenue lift replicates at shops two through five, and Parrot ships a public benchmark against Rote on supplement turnaround within the next six months, the verdict flips to invest — that is the verifiable condition. What dismantles the bull case today is the LinkedIn residue (both founders still list Alchemy as their company), the single-pilot evidence base, and the direct overlap with Rote, who is one batch ahead inside the supplement slice that is the densest dollar density in the workflow. Parrot is interesting. It is not yet a check.
