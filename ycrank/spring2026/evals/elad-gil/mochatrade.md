# Mochatrade -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Utkarsh Sinha scaled Martian Wallet to 2M+ installs before Pontem acquired it in May 2025 -- that's a real crypto distribution exit, and at pre-seed it's the strongest signal in Mochatrade's dossier. But the product Mochatrade is building sits in a fundamentally different position than Martian Wallet did. A self-custodial wallet during Aptos's early growth was infrastructure -- the on-ramp layer that every user of the ecosystem needed. Mochatrade is a consumer trading application built on top of someone else's infrastructure. Hyperliquid's HIP-3 framework, launched October 2025, already enables third-party builders to deploy stock perps, and Trade[XYZ] has crossed $100B cumulative volume since then. Ostium raised a $20M Series A from General Catalyst and Jump Crypto in December 2025 and has processed $25B in cumulative volume. Mochatrade is entering a market where the infrastructure layer is contested and commoditizing simultaneously -- a pattern I explicitly avoid. When I invested in Stripe, I was backing the infrastructure layer itself, not an application consuming Stripe's API. Mochatrade is on the wrong side of that ledger.

On-chain RWA perps volume surged 162% month-over-month in January 2026, and the CFTC directed staff to explore rulemaking to permit perpetual contracts onshore in March 2026. These are real structural shifts, but they fail my nonobviousness test. General Catalyst is already in this market through Ostium. Jump Crypto is in it. Hyperliquid generates roughly $55M per month in platform revenue. This market has passed the point where an early entrant captures a structural window that consensus missed -- the window is wide open and well-capitalized players are already through it. Mochatrade's India-first angle adds geographic specificity, and India's 200M+ demat accounts represent genuine demand for US equity exposure beyond the LRS limit of $250K/year. But geographic distribution focus is a go-to-market choice, not a structural moat. If Ostium or Hyperliquid ecosystem builders decide to build INR on-ramps, Mochatrade's differentiation compresses to brand and UX in a market where traders are notoriously price-sensitive and disloyal. Compare this to when I wrote the seed check for Coinbase in 2012: crypto infrastructure was structurally emerging, consensus dismissed it entirely, and Coinbase was building the default on-ramp layer for an entire country. Mochatrade is building an application in a market that's already hot, which is closer to the pattern I pass on.

SEBI data showing 91% of Indian retail F&O traders lose money is not just a factoid -- it's a regulatory vector. Mochatrade offers up to 20x leverage on US stock perps to the exact population that Indian regulators are actively trying to protect from leveraged products. The company needs regulatory survival across at minimum three jurisdictions: SEBI and RBI in India (for crypto transactions, forex restrictions, and the 30% crypto gains tax plus 1% TDS), and SEC/CFTC in the US (for the underlying tokenized equity derivatives). That's not one miracle -- it's a compounding regulatory gauntlet. Add the fiat on-ramp dependency, where any disruption to INR-to-stablecoin conversion channels directly kills user acquisition, and Mochatrade requires at least two independent breakthroughs: regulatory navigation across hostile jurisdictions AND distribution against better-capitalized competitors. My "single miracle" framework exists precisely to filter this pattern. The CFTC's movement toward onshore perps regulation could paradoxically hurt Mochatrade -- if US-regulated exchanges start offering perps, the structural gap that makes Mochatrade's offshore model viable could close from the supply side.

The bull case deserves construction. Mochatrade's founding team is genuinely strong for pre-seed: Utkarsh Sinha has a verifiable exit (Martian Wallet acquired by Lightspeed-backed Pontem), Parth Maheshwari co-built the same product at the technical layer, and Chetan Manda brings product management depth from Sprinklr, a public enterprise SaaS company. The co-founder relationship between Utkarsh and Parth -- IIT Kharagpur through Columbia through a startup exit together -- is the kind of battle-tested partnership I weight heavily. Their stated motivation maps to lived experience: Indian traders who wanted leveraged access to US equities and couldn't get it through traditional channels. If Mochatrade can nail India-specific distribution through crypto-native channels the way Martian Wallet acquired 2M+ wallet users, the 200M+ demat account base represents a massive addressable audience. The on-chain RWA perps volume explosion ($31B in January 2026 alone) suggests the product category has product-market fit at the macro level. A focused India-first execution with local-currency on-ramps could build a localization moat that global platforms deprioritize. I take this case seriously -- the founder credentials alone put Mochatrade above median for YC pre-seed.

But the bull case collapses against my framework on two dimensions. First, Mochatrade is a consumer application, not infrastructure. There's no API layer, no platform for other builders, no compounding switching costs beyond habit. Consumer trading products compete on fees and leverage ratios, both of which compress toward zero over time -- Morpher already offers zero-fee synthetic trading. Mochatrade has no structural pricing power. Second, the market Mochatrade targets is not "boring but critical" infrastructure that prestige-seeking founders avoid -- it's a flashy consumer trading product in a category attracting significant capital and attention. My highest-conviction pattern is the Gusto or Checkr archetype: unglamorous, operationally essential, deeply embedded in workflows, facing little competition from ambitious founders chasing shinier markets. Mochatrade is the opposite. The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Mochatrade demonstrated it was building the infrastructure layer -- the oracle network, the settlement protocol, or the on-ramp API that other emerging-market trading products build on -- rather than the consumer application itself. That would change the layer positioning score entirely.

Mochatrade's outcome hinges on two dimensions where it scores poorly: infrastructure-layer positioning and single-miracle viability. The founding team (Utkarsh Sinha's prior exit, the Columbia/IIT KGP co-founder bond, Chetan Manda's Sprinklr product experience) is the company's genuine asset, scoring well above the pre-seed default. But strong operators in a market where they're building at the wrong layer, facing multi-jurisdiction regulatory compounding risk, and entering after well-funded competitors have already established volume -- that's a pattern where even good founders get crushed by market structure. I've seen this before: "I've seen great founders repeatedly get crushed by a terrible market." The market isn't terrible here, but Mochatrade's positioning within it is unfavorable.
