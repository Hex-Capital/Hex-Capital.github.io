# AutoSitu -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about AutoSitu is the competitive density. Five funded competitors, one with $91M in the bank, all converging on AI-powered plan review. PermitFlow is scaling 10x year-over-year. CivCheck got acquired. Blitz AI claims to be the most widely adopted in American local government. Archistar has a $2.7M government grant and partnerships with cities AutoSitu also lists. When I see five companies with capital attacking the same problem from slightly different angles, the asymmetric upside has already been competed away. This is not a bet where I'm right first -- this is a bet where I'm joining a crowded table.

Now, the specific knowledge question. Xuanshu Lin studied urban planning at Michigan and Harvard GSD and did project work in Champaign-Urbana and Detroit. George Zhai built autonomous systems at GM and McLaren, holds 7 patents, and has production AI deployment experience. This is credentialed, pipeline knowledge -- the kind you acquire through elite institutions and corporate R&D labs. It's real expertise, but it's the kind that can be taught. A different urban planner paired with a different ML engineer would arrive at a substantially similar product concept. Compare this to what I look for: knowledge that emerges from years of obsessive personal immersion that produces insights surprising even to experts. Lin hasn't spent a decade inside a planning department watching reviews break -- he studied planning. Zhai hasn't built regulatory AI systems from first principles -- he built autonomous driving systems at car companies. The founder-problem connection is logical rather than inevitable.

The leverage architecture concerns me more. Every municipality structures its code differently -- that's not a feature of the opportunity, it's a constraint on the leverage ratio. Each new jurisdiction requires ingesting unique ordinances, mapping unique departmental workflows, and calibrating compliance checks against unique interpretations. This is closer to a consulting business with software tooling than a pure code-as-leverage play. One engineer's work does not serve a million users -- one engineer's work serves one jurisdiction's workflow. The 1,500+ plans checked across 11 named cities is traction, but it's traction that required jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction customization. I'm looking for companies where the marginal cost of serving the next customer approaches zero. Here, the marginal cost of serving the next city is substantial.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If AutoSitu cracks the municipal-side integration -- embedding deeply enough into city workflows that switching becomes operationally painful -- they could own the regulatory layer of the built world. The political tailwind is genuine: housing crisis pressure, staffing shortages in planning departments, Seattle's executive order mandating AI review. And their positioning on the municipal reviewer side, rather than the developer side, is a differentiated wedge -- cities are the bottleneck, not applicants. If jurisdiction-specific regulatory intelligence compounds into something like a knowledge graph of how American cities actually interpret their own codes, that dataset becomes a defensible asset. The question is whether this compounding happens fast enough to matter before PermitFlow's $91M builds the same capability from the other side of the transaction.

I keep returning to the leverage test. The companies I back create new categories of capability for individuals -- Notion gave one person the output of five SaaS tools, Replit gave one person the power to build software without a CS degree. AutoSitu gives one city planner faster reviews. That's valuable, but it's optimization of an existing workflow, not creation of new leverage. The product makes a municipal employee 2-3x more productive. It doesn't enable a fundamentally new actor or a fundamentally new action. And the government sales cycle -- 6 to 18 months of procurement -- means this two-person team is burning runway while navigating RFPs and council approvals. That's renting time to bureaucracy, not compounding code.

The founders seem competent, the problem is real, and the timing tailwinds exist. But this is a consensus opportunity in a crowded category where the core technology is reproducible, the go-to-market requires patient government sales, and the leverage architecture scales with jurisdictions rather than with code. I'd pass.
