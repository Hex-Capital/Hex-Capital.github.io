# Crebit -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Forty-five university integrations and a million dollars in FX volume inside three months, built by two teenagers who met at a Bank of America scholarship program — that's the number that stopped me. Jensen Coonradt is nineteen, Pell Grant-eligible, from Aurora, Illinois. Simmi Sen founded a nonprofit providing vocational training to women in Bangladesh and India before she could legally drink. When I ask my question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is two kids who grew up inside financial constraint, who navigated scholarship systems and aid applications, who understood what it feels like when hidden fees eat into money your family scraped together. That's real proximity to the problem. Jensen's prior company Lendora being acquired by BankSocial and scheduled for a Federal Reserve pilot tells me he can ship in regulated fintech, not just win hackathons. But proximity is not the same as inevitability. The Crusoe founders didn't just notice gas flaring — they'd spent years in the energy industry watching it burn. Jensen and Simmi felt the friction of financial systems as students, then identified a market opportunity adjacent to that experience. The forge is genuine but young. I'd want to know: would they be working on cross-border payments even if stablecoins hadn't become a viable settlement layer? Or did the GENIUS Act passing in July 2025 create the opportunity that found them?

The hidden system beneath Crebit is the one that activates me hardest. Correspondent banking is a textbook extractive structure — international students pay 2–10% markups on tuition payments, and they can't see the markup because it's buried inside FX spreads across multiple intermediary banks. Flywire charges 2–3% above live exchange rates. PayMyTuition charges 1–2%. These aren't fees on a receipt — they're invisible taxes extracted from students who are already financially vulnerable, often sending money from families in Brazil, Nigeria, the Philippines. When I was fifteen and navigating systems designed to extract value from people who didn't understand them, nobody explained the hidden costs. Crebit's stablecoin settlement layer bypasses the correspondent banking chain entirely, making the true cost visible and compressible. That structural revelation — turning an opaque multi-intermediary chain into a transparent stablecoin bridge — is exactly the pattern I look for. This is closer to what Affirm did to credit card opacity than most fintech pitches I see.

But here's where my conviction fractures: Affirm was contrarian. Max Levchin was building a new financial product that lenders didn't want to exist because it made their hidden economics visible. Crebit is using stablecoins for cross-border payments in 2026, which is about as contrarian as suggesting people might want faster internet. Stripe launched stablecoin payouts across 100+ countries. Mastercard unveiled end-to-end stablecoin transaction capabilities. Brex announced stablecoin payments. Latitude raised $8M from NEA for adjacent stablecoin payment infrastructure. The GENIUS Act didn't just enable Crebit — it enabled everyone. When I invested in SpaceX, no other serious investor would touch private rockets. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in my world. Crebit operates in a space where the largest payment companies on earth are racing to do the exact same thing. The "magically weird" window for stablecoin payments closed sometime around 2024. By the time Jensen and Simmi started building in summer 2025, the thesis was consensus.

The university portal embedding is the strongest defensibility argument. Once Crebit is integrated into a bursar's payment system, switching costs are real — universities move slowly and hate changing payment vendors. Forty-five integrations in three months suggests either extraordinary hustle or a distribution insight I'm not seeing. Multi-country local rail compliance (PIX, UPI, M-Pesa, SPEI) adds another layer of friction for competitors starting from zero. But Flywire has $623M in TTM revenue and deep incumbent relationships with hundreds of universities. Flywire's structural barrier to adopting stablecoin settlement is business model cannibalization — their margins come from the very FX spreads Crebit eliminates — and that cannibalization dilemma is a real moat for Crebit. The question is whether that moat holds long enough. Flywire flagged $30M in potential revenue pressure from immigration policy alone, which means they're already under margin pressure and might be forced to innovate on settlement rails sooner than Crebit needs them to.

The strongest bull case for Crebit is this: if Jensen and Simmi can lock in 200+ university integrations before Flywire adapts its settlement layer, they become the stablecoin-native Flywire — cheaper rails, faster settlement, same institutional switching costs. The $1M+ volume in three months, the 45+ partners, the iOS app already live across seven countries — for a two-person team, this is remarkable execution. The immigration policy risk cuts both ways: if student flows are pressured, Crebit's expansion into non-tuition use cases ("someone just bought a home through Crebit," per Simmi's LinkedIn) suggests the payment rails are more general than the university wedge implies. And Jensen's age works in his favor on one dimension — he'll be building this for the next decade, and infrastructure companies reward persistence.

Crebit scores highest on my invisible system criterion — correspondent banking opacity exploiting vulnerable international students is exactly the extractive structure I've built my career identifying. It scores meaningfully on impact — reducing a 2–10% hidden tax on tuition payments directly expands financial access for underserved populations. But the absence of contrarian conviction is the gap I can't bridge. The best investments I've made required courage to articulate at a dinner party. Telling someone you're backing stablecoin payments for international students in 2026 provokes no discomfort whatsoever. The founders show genuine resourcefulness and biographical proximity to the problem, but not the kind of biographical inevitability that makes me write a check from my own money before anyone else will.
