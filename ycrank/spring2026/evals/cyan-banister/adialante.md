# Adialante -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

There's something hiding inside MRI that most people never see. Not the images — the system around the images. A million men a year in the U.S. need prostate MRI, and almost no urology clinic can give it to them. Not because the technology doesn't exist, but because MRI machines cost millions, need specialized rooms with magnetic shielding, require months of installation, and lock clinics into recurring service contracts with three companies that control the global market. If you're a patient in an underserved community and your urologist says you need an MRI before a biopsy — which is increasingly what guidelines recommend — you're navigating an invisible toll system. You don't know why it costs what it costs, why the wait is what it is, or why the machine can't just be in your doctor's office. That opacity is where I start paying attention.

Efrain Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago. When he tells me his mission is bringing MRI to communities that can't access it, I hear something specific — he didn't discover this problem in a market analysis deck. He grew up surrounded by the consequences of that access gap. He went and got a PhD at the University of Minnesota's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research, one of the premier MRI labs in the world, and while he was there, he co-invented a fundamentally new physics approach — FREE, Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes — that eliminates the gradient coils that make conventional MRI machines enormous and expensive. He published it in peer-reviewed journals, won Student Entrepreneur of the Year, and is now building a company to deploy it. That trajectory is not someone who saw a market gap. That's someone the problem chose. I believe the biographical connection, and it matters to me. What gives me pause is that both co-founders come entirely from academia — no visible prior startup experience, no evidence of navigating the messy commercial realities of healthcare operations. The forge is real but it's been academic so far. The question is whether it hardens when it hits the market.

The invisible system here is one of the clearest I've seen in a while. MRI access is gatekept by capital expenditure that only large institutions can absorb, by infrastructure requirements that exclude smaller clinics, and by an incumbent oligopoly (Siemens, GE, Philips) whose entire business model depends on selling $1-3M machines with lucrative service contracts. Adialante's per-scan mobile model — imaging clinics on wheels where the clinic pays a flat fee with zero capital outlay — doesn't just reduce cost. It structurally redistributes access. The incumbent manufacturers cannot easily offer this without cannibalizing their own hardware sales and service revenue. That business model conflict is a form of structural protection that I find compelling. It reminds me of the dynamic I saw with Crusoe — they brought portable data centers to where the stranded energy was, instead of requiring the energy to come to the data center. Adialante brings the MRI to where the patients are, instead of requiring patients to navigate the institutional radiology maze.

Now, the hard truth: Promaxo already has FDA clearance for in-office prostate MRI, has sold 69 systems, and has raised $63-81M. Hyperfine is a public company shipping portable brain MRI with $13.6M in revenue. Adialante is pre-FDA with $2.43M raised. The regulatory path for novel MRI physics is uncertain and could take years. By the time Adialante gets clearance, Promaxo could have hundreds of installed systems with clinical evidence and switching costs embedded in urology practices nationwide. This is the kind of timing risk that keeps me honest — the technology could be genuinely superior but arrive too late to matter. The strongest bull case is that FREE, if it delivers clinical-grade images at 1/10th the size and cost, creates unit economics that Promaxo simply cannot match. Promaxo still requires clinics to buy hardware. If Adialante can offer the same scan at a fraction of the per-unit cost because the machine itself costs a fraction, the mobile per-scan model becomes structurally unbeatable on access. And the ISMRM Rabi Award winner on their team — Sai Abitha Srinivas, the first from Vanderbilt to receive it — gives me real confidence that the physics is not vaporware. Patent-pending technology backed by peer-reviewed publications and an NSF SBIR Phase II award is serious IP, not a slide deck.

The impact dimension here is where my conviction deepens. Prostate cancer disproportionately affects Black men and communities with the least access to advanced imaging. If Adialante works, it doesn't just make MRI cheaper — it creates screening access where none existed. That's not the kind of impact investing that I call a marketing trick. That's structural expansion of who gets to live longer because they caught cancer early. Every form of investing is impact investing, and this one wears it honestly.

Where I land: this is a genuine thesis fit on the invisible system and impact dimensions, with a founder whose biographical connection to the problem I believe. The contrarian angle is moderate — medical devices at pre-seed is hard enough that most investors won't touch it, but low-cost MRI is becoming a recognized category rather than something that makes VCs shift in their chairs. The defensibility question is real but not disqualifying — the patent-pending physics plus eventual FDA clearance plus a business model that incumbents can't replicate without self-harm is a defensible architecture if they execute. The biggest risk is the team's ability to navigate the commercial and operational transition from academic lab to mobile healthcare fleet. That's a gap I'd want to see addressed, and it's why this is a lower-end Invest rather than a high-conviction bet. But the bones are here — a founder shaped by the problem, a genuinely novel technology, and a hidden system waiting to be cracked open.
