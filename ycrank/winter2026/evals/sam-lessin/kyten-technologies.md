# Kyten Technologies -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Six thousand battery packs deployed to low earth orbit. That's the number that grabbed me. Cooper McBride and Lucas Maddox aren't pitching a PowerPoint about aerospace battery manufacturing -- they lived it, at SpaceX, at Starlink, building and shipping the actual hardware that powers over half the satellites currently orbiting Earth. When I see founders who built critical infrastructure at one of the most demanding engineering organizations on the planet, my "sane person" detector pegs. These two are deeply, verifiably sane. McBride progressed from Tesla intern to leading 30+ person engineering teams across SpaceX and Shield AI. Maddox spent seven years in the guts of Starlink's test and manufacturing operation. This is the exact founder profile that makes me lean forward -- people who've shipped at infrastructure scale and now want to do it independently.

But here's where my framework starts to strain: the idea isn't insane enough. Domestic defense manufacturing is one of the hottest consensus plays in venture right now. Every fund with a defense thesis is looking at exactly this category. The Army wants to build a million drones. NDAA compliance is forcing reshoring. Chinese supply chain dependency is front-page news. When I described Coinbase as a "sane person, insane idea" bet, the insane part was that most of Silicon Valley thought Bitcoin was a joke. Nobody thinks defense battery manufacturing is a joke in 2026 -- it's a national priority with bipartisan support. The only "insane" element here is the audacity of thinking a two-person pre-seed startup can compete in capital-intensive manufacturing against EaglePicher ($297M revenue), Lyten ($425M+ raised), and Packet Digital (sitting on a $50M DoD award). That's either founder courage or founder naivety, and I genuinely can't tell which from the dossier alone.

Where Kyten maps beautifully to my thesis is the physical-world moat dimension. This is a Craftwork play in aerospace -- a manufacturing operation where software is the cherry on top, not the business itself. Their "software-defined automated manufacturing" compresses timelines from 12 months to 3 weeks, but the moat isn't the software. The moat is the manufacturing process knowledge, the in-house qualification capability, the accumulated design library from building packs across programs. AI makes this business stronger, not weaker -- it can optimize dispatch, quality control, design iteration -- but no LLM is going to manufacture an aerospace-grade battery pack with integrated BMS and thermal management. That's physical-world complexity that software amplifies but cannot replace. And once your pack is qualified into a defense platform, requalification costs create genuine switching friction. This structural positioning is exactly what I look for.

The bull case that would make this exceptional: if Kyten becomes the de facto rapid-prototyping-to-production partner for the next generation of defense drone companies -- the Anduril, Shield AI, and dozens of smaller OEMs racing to fill DoD procurement orders -- they could build something resembling a platform position. Not a protocol in the crypto sense, but a manufacturing layer that an ecosystem depends on. The 3-week turnaround versus 12-month incumbents is a genuinely different value proposition that could capture the high-mix, low-volume segment that EaglePicher's cost structure can't serve. If they nail the execution, qualification data compounds, design libraries become proprietary, and customer relationships lock in. The SpaceX network gives them warm introductions to exactly the right buyers. In a world where the DoD is procuring hundreds of thousands of drones and the domestic supply chain doesn't exist yet, being the fast, flexible, American-made option could be a very real business.

What holds me back from conviction is the absence of any primitive or protocol position. Kyten is a manufacturing services company -- an excellent one potentially, but a services company nonetheless. There are no network effects. Growth scales with capital expenditure and headcount, not with compounding adoption. Every new customer requires custom engineering work. This isn't Helium building a decentralized wireless protocol where every new node makes the network more valuable -- it's a factory that needs to build more factory to serve more customers. The narrative can expand across verticals (drones, satellites, submarines, EVs), but the expansion requires proportional capital investment each time. In my "infinity story" framework, the ceiling is visible. It's a big ceiling -- multi-billion dollar TAM in a growing market -- but it's bounded by physics and capex in a way that protocol-layer investments aren't.

The capital intensity question is the one that nags me most. Two founders with YC-scale funding competing against companies with hundreds of millions in backing, in a business that requires manufacturing facilities, equipment, inventory, and qualified personnel. The "regatta model" of fundraising helps -- they don't need to follow the factory-farmed unicorn playbook -- but they do need to raise significant capital to build manufacturing capacity before the window closes. If Lyten or Packet Digital or a defense prime captures the domestic battery pack market while Kyten is still scaling, the opportunity evaporates regardless of how talented the founders are. And there's zero Kyten-specific traction data in the dossier -- no revenue, no customers, no LOIs. The 6,000 packs to orbit is pre-Kyten SpaceX experience. I'm investing in a team and a thesis, not a business with a downside cushion.

Exceptional founders in a consensus market with a capital-intensive path and no protocol position. I respect the physical-world moat thesis deeply -- it's my thesis -- but the combination of consensus timing, capital asymmetry against funded competitors, and lack of compounding dynamics keeps this in neutral territory for my personal check.
