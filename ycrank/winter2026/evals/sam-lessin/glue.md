# Glue -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: Glue is building a design tool where AI coding agents are the primary user. Strip away the positioning and the YC badge and ask my favorite question -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer isn't "it would be worse." The answer is it literally would not exist. There is no product without Claude Code and Codex and Cursor running underneath it. That's not a cherry on top of a sundae. That's a cherry floating in mid-air with no sundae beneath it. When I wrote that the investable AI play is businesses that would be great pre-AI but where AI non-obviously accelerates them, this is the precise inverse -- a business that is entirely constituted by AI, sitting on top of platforms controlled by companies with billions in resources and every incentive to build this feature natively.

The sane-person-insane-idea calibration is off in both directions. Perbhat Kumar (Amazon Kindle Fire launcher, Microsoft Copilot Studio RAG agents) and Tejas Priyadarshi (Meta SWE, Microsoft Azure DNS) are competent engineers from big tech. But competent-engineer-from-big-tech is the median YC founder profile, not the signal that makes me trust someone with a contrarian bet. Compare this to Anatoly Yakovenko's deep Qualcomm systems architecture experience before Solana -- that was a person whose specific technical credibility made you believe he could build a new consensus mechanism. These founders built products at large companies, which is fine, but nothing in their backgrounds suggests they have unique insight into how to become the standard interface layer between humans and AI agents. And the idea itself -- building AI-native dev tools -- is the single hottest consensus category in technology right now. Lovable just raised $330 million at $6.6 billion. Cursor is at $29.3 billion doing $2 billion in revenue. Every VC on Sand Hill Road wants exposure to this category. When every investor wants in, the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged away and you're buying narrative at market price. The pivot signal is also worth noting -- Tejas's GitHub bio references "Founder @ Willow (YC W26)," not Glue, suggesting a recent product change that means whatever validation existed for the prior concept may not transfer.

Let me steelman the bull case, because there's a version of this that could be interesting. If Glue becomes the canonical visual interface protocol that every coding agent integrates with -- the way Figma became the standard canvas for human designers -- then you're looking at a new primitive, not an application. A universal design surface that Claude Code, Codex, Gemini, and every future agent must render through would have compounding network effects and winner-take-most dynamics. That's the Airtable exception from my portfolio: something that looks like a SaaS tool on the surface but functions as infrastructure that other workflows build on. The open-source strategy could accelerate this -- if the repo existed. But the dossier found no public GitHub repository despite the company describing itself as open source. The distribution strategy hasn't been activated. And even if it were, Figma's moat was built over a decade of compounding designer-to-designer collaboration workflows. Glue is trying to establish a standard in a market where Cursor alone has more engineering resources than most public companies, and where the major AI labs have direct incentive to own the visual feedback loop for their own agents. The structural barriers to becoming a standard here are enormous, and I see no evidence of the kind of community traction or ecosystem development that would indicate protocol-layer potential.

The moat analysis is where this falls apart most severely for my framework. I've been saying publicly for two years that software companies that had moats are discovering they aren't moats anymore. Glue's defensibility is purely software -- an open-source canvas that renders UI and passes feedback to coding agents. There's no physical infrastructure, no regulatory position, no proprietary data flywheel, no protocol-layer lock-in. The core technical capability -- a live preview canvas that syncs with code and accepts agent inputs -- is described in the dossier as "non-trivial but not unprecedented." Any of the five named competitors with their combined billions in funding could build this as a feature. And the open-source strategy, while good for distribution in theory, also means anyone can fork the codebase. This is Birchbox's structural vulnerability in a different costume -- narrative magnitude without defensibility. The subscription box was trivially replicable; the AI design canvas is trivially replicable. The category that AI creates, AI can also commoditize overnight.

I'm passing. The founders are capable but not distinctively credentialed for this specific challenge, the idea is peak consensus in the hottest market in tech, the product has zero value independent of AI platforms it doesn't control, and there are no traction signals, no community, and no activated open-source distribution despite the claim. The competitive landscape features multiple companies with hundreds of millions to billions in funding attacking adjacent problems. For this to work, Glue would need to thread an extraordinarily narrow needle -- becoming the universal standard canvas for all coding agents before any of those well-funded competitors build the same feature. I need to see a sane person with an insane idea. What I see here is a reasonable person with an extremely obvious idea in the most crowded market in technology.
