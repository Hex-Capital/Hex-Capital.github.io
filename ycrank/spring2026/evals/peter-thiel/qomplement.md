# qomplement -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Rossum has raised $114.5 million and generates $44.9 million in annual recurring revenue processing documents for PepsiCo, Bosch, and Siemens. Extend AI raised $17 million and is already cash-flow positive serving Brex, Square, and Checkr. BrowserUse accumulated 50,000 GitHub stars building open-source browser automation. qomplement enters this arena with a $200,000 proprietary model, a placeholder website, and 16 Twitter followers. The competitive landscape here is not a footnote — it is the diagnosis. When I can name five funded competitors pursuing the same thesis in adjacent configurations, there is no secret. There is a market. And markets, by definition, are competitive. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. qomplement has not articulated what it knows that Rossum, Extend AI, and BrowserUse do not — because the insight "legacy desktop applications can be automated by an AI agent that mimics human actions" is precisely what every major AI lab is building into its foundation models. Anthropic's computer use, OpenAI's CUA capabilities — these are not future threats, they are present realities being integrated into platforms with billions in R&D budgets.

The advocate's case for qomplement centers on local-first execution in regulated industries. The argument runs: healthcare, financial services, and transportation companies in Latin America cannot send data to the cloud, US-centric competitors neglect this market, and qomplement's on-device processing creates a structural wedge. A logistics customer processing 10,000 invoices monthly and saving $15,000 per month demonstrates genuine economic value. If qomplement can embed deeply enough in Mexican and Latin American enterprise systems — industries running on software from the 1990s — it could build switching costs reminiscent of how Palantir embedded in intelligence agencies. The co-founders, Kerim Taray and Andrés Fernando Garza García, both from Tecnológico de Monterrey, have shared regional origin and university overlap — the kind of prehistory I value in founding teams. This bull case is not absurd. But it collapses under the monopoly test: even if qomplement captures every regulated Latin American enterprise running legacy desktop software, the outcome is a regional workflow automation business — not a monopoly. Palantir's government embedding worked because intelligence analysis is a winner-take-all domain where one platform processes all the data. Document processing is inherently fragmented — there is no structural reason why one company must process all the invoices.

qomplement's $200,000 model training investment — described as a moat in the Expansión coverage from September 2025 — illustrates a category error about defensibility in AI. A model trained for $200,000 is not a moat; it is a cost that any Series A competitor can replicate in a weekend. The "15x cheaper than cloud" positioning qomplement claims against "$3 per interaction" cloud models is a temporal arbitrage, not a structural advantage. Foundation model inference costs have declined by orders of magnitude annually since 2023 — qomplement's cost edge erodes with every model release from Meta, Mistral, or any open-weight competitor. The $60,000 monthly AWS burn rate against undisclosed pre-seed funding compounds the problem: qomplement is spending capital to maintain a cost advantage that the market is eliminating for free.

Kerim Taray describes himself as a "contrarian" on his personal website, but qomplement's positioning reveals no contrarian thesis. The product description shifts across sources — "OS-level AI agent for legacy software automation" on Semilla Ventures, "AI Agents for Document Filling" on YC's page, "Turn invoices and POs into system-ready data" on X/Twitter. This variation is not strategic versatility; it is the signature of a company still searching for its thesis through iteration rather than executing against a definite conviction. A founder with a genuine secret does not need three different descriptions of what they are building — the secret itself provides the organizing principle. qomplement's founders may be competent engineers, but the dossier reveals no evidence of the kind of bold, specific multi-year plan that distinguishes definite optimists from lean iterators. No irreversible commitments, no audacious vision of what the world looks like when qomplement wins — just incremental automation of document workflows.

The power-law arithmetic confirms the pass. The intelligent document processing market projects to $43.92 billion by 2034, but qomplement's serviceable segment — OS-level desktop automation for legacy systems in regulated industries — is a fraction of that already-fragmented category. Rossum's $44.9 million ARR after $114.5 million in funding demonstrates the ceiling: even well-capitalized document processing companies produce mid-scale outcomes, not fund-returners. qomplement is not positioned to become the last mover in document automation — it is positioned to compete with better-funded companies for a niche within a niche. There is no physical-world technology barrier here, no atoms-based moat, no hardware that takes years to replicate. qomplement is a software layer running someone else's cloud infrastructure, automating desktop clicks — a capability that frontier model providers will bundle as a feature, not license as a platform. The one condition that would flip my assessment: if qomplement secured a locked-in government contract — a Mexican federal agency mandating on-premise document processing with multi-year exclusivity — that would create the kind of institutional embedding that transforms a software tool into an infrastructure monopoly. Without that structural lock-in, qomplement is competing, and competition is the one thing my framework cannot forgive.
