# Datost -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Datost is not the product -- it's the platform dependency structure. Salesforce owns Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. Salesforce has Einstein AI. The company's entire distribution channel, data integration surface, and competitive moat are controlled by a single entity with every structural incentive to build exactly this product. This isn't the generic "what if a big company competes" risk I hear in every pitch. This is the specific case where the platform owner already possesses the distribution layer (Slack), the data layer (Tableau), the AI engine (Einstein), and the enterprise relationships -- and has a $27.7 billion acquisition thesis predicated on making Slack the center of enterprise workflows. Building an AI data analyst as a Slack bot when Salesforce owns Slack is like building a browser extension when the browser maker is shipping the same feature natively. I've seen this pattern destroy startups.

The "why now" here is that LLMs crossed the reliability threshold for SQL generation and code execution. That's real -- I've been tracking these capability curves since GPT-2. But this is the most consensus "why now" in venture capital today. AI data analytics is the default demo at every LLM API launch, the first project at every hackathon, and the pitch deck for dozens of funded startups. Julius AI already has 2 million users and $10 million from Bessemer. Defog.ai has open-source traction. Equals raised $23 million from a16z. Microsoft, Google, and Salesforce are all shipping AI analytics features inside their existing platforms. When I look at the markets where I've found asymmetric returns -- PagerDuty in operations alerting, Gusto in payroll, Checkr in background checks -- those were markets that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors. Nobody was excited about operations alerting in 2013. "AI data analyst" in 2026 is the opposite of that pattern. It's a market where everyone is already excited, which means the opportunity for asymmetric returns from non-obvious positioning has been competed away.

The product-to-distribution question is where this gets structurally capped. Stripe's payment API became a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury because Stripe owned the developer relationship and the integration point. Datost's Slack integration is a distribution tactic, not a distribution channel it controls. Slack owns the relationship. If Datost succeeds in making "ask your data questions in Slack" a behavior, Slack can replicate that behavior with a native feature and distribute it to every workspace for free. The company can't become a distribution channel for additional products because it doesn't own the surface where users interact with it. Compare this to a company like Figma, which owned the canvas and could expand into FigJam and developer handoff because it controlled the user relationship. Datost is renting its distribution from its most dangerous competitor's parent company.

The strongest bull case: Salesforce's business model is dashboard seats. An AI chat agent that eliminates the need for dashboards cannibalizes Tableau revenue, creating a classic innovator's dilemma where Salesforce can't aggressively pursue this without undermining its own economics. If that cannibalization paralysis holds, a startup could capture the market before Salesforce responds. And the "own computer" execution environment -- a sandboxed compute layer for autonomous query execution -- could create reliability and trust advantages over simpler prompt-to-SQL tools. If Datost nails enterprise security (SOC 2, permissions, audit trails) early, the organizational context it accumulates becomes a genuine switching cost. For this to work, though, the cannibalization paralysis would need to last 3-5 years, the founders would need to sprint through enterprise security certifications as a two-person team, and they'd need to outexecute funded competitors with 10-50x their resources. That's at least three sequential miracles, which is exactly the pattern I avoid.

On the founders: two 21-year-old software engineers who worked together at Traba, a staffing tech company. They can clearly build software. But the enterprise data analytics market requires selling to data teams, navigating security reviews, and understanding the specific workflows of analysts, data engineers, and business stakeholders. I see no evidence of domain depth in enterprise analytics -- no prior experience on a data team, no advisory relationships in the space, and a LinkedIn headline that still reads "Software Engineer Intern." The single public GitHub repo has zero stars. At pre-seed, I don't expect significant traction, but I do expect to see signals of rapid building and early user engagement. The absence of any public traction signal -- no Product Hunt launch, no beta users mentioned, no early customer conversations surfaced -- makes it hard to assess execution velocity.

This is a pass. The market is consensus-hot with massive incumbent attention, the distribution is rented from the most structurally threatening competitor, the product is a single-function tool without a clear platform trajectory, and the plan requires multiple miracles to succeed. Competent founders, real technology timing, but the wrong market structure for asymmetric returns.
