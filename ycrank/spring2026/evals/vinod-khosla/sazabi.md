# Sazabi -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Datadog generates $2.7 billion in ARR selling logs, metrics, and traces as three separate products billed on three separate meters. Sazabi's bet is that AI can derive metrics and traces from logs alone, collapsing that entire paradigm into a single ingestion point. That is a genuine innovator's dilemma setup — Datadog cannot cannibalize its own per-signal revenue model without destroying the economics that Wall Street values it on. I respect the structural insight. But I have to start where I always start: if Sazabi fully succeeds, what happens to the world? Engineering teams at startups debug production faster and pay less for observability. That is a useful outcome for thousands of software teams. It is not a consequence that changes how millions of people live. Sazabi is building a better developer tool, not reshaping a basic human need. When I backed Impossible Foods, the end-state was feeding billions without animal agriculture. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, the end-state was limitless clean energy. Sazabi's end-state is cheaper monitoring dashboards. The consequence magnitude simply does not activate my framework.

Sherwood Callaway's trajectory is the strongest signal in Sazabi's dossier. History degree from Davidson College, coding bootcamp in 2014, frontend QA at Crunchbase, infrastructure engineering at Brex where he started the observability team from scratch as employee ~70, then co-founded Opkit — a healthcare voice AI company through YC S21 that got acquired by 11x in September 2024. He rebuilt an agentic AI sales system at 11x, then launched Sazabi with a team of 10 and SOC 2 Type II certifications already obtained. That is a real learning curve — non-traditional entry point, rapid skill acquisition across domains, a completed startup cycle including acquisition, and now applying accumulated insight to a domain he knows firsthand. I give genuine credit for the rate of learning here. The jump from history major to infrastructure engineer to two-time YC founder with an exit shows the athlete profile I look for. But Callaway is not an outsider to observability — he is a domain expert who started Brex's observability team. My framework actually prefers the naive outsider who does not know what cannot be done. Callaway knows exactly what the industry does and is proposing a better version. That is competence, not irreverence.

Sazabi's logs-only architecture is contrarian relative to the OpenTelemetry standard that Dash0 ($155M raised, $1B valuation, 600+ customers), SigNoz, and the CNCF ecosystem have adopted. Rejecting the three-pillars paradigm is a real bet — the dossier notes the industry could standardize on OpenTelemetry and leave Sazabi fighting adoption friction. I like contrarian bets when they are backed by a proprietary technical insight that creates a step-function improvement. But the dossier is explicit: "The logs-only-plus-AI approach is architecturally reproducible. Any team with LLM infrastructure expertise could attempt a similar product." Sazabi is applying commodity LLM capabilities to log processing. Dash0 already ships AI agents for observability. Datadog is adding AI features on top of 47,000 existing customers. The approach is a simplification, not a discontinuity. When Rocket Lab built the Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers, that was a non-linear technical achievement — novel engineering that competitors could not replicate quickly. Sazabi's core insight — "logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events, traces are correlated events" — is an architectural observation, not a technology breakthrough. The moat depends on execution speed against competitors with hundred-million-dollar war chests, which is exactly the kind of race I prefer not to fund.

The bull case an advocate would make: Sazabi is attacking a proven multi-billion-dollar market with an architectural wedge that incumbents cannot adopt without destroying their own revenue model. Callaway is a two-time YC founder with a completed acquisition, direct domain expertise from building Brex's observability infrastructure, and the AI-agent observability angle — monitoring code generated by Claude Code, Cursor, Codex — positions Sazabi for a genuinely new market category that did not exist eighteen months ago. The team of 10 at pre-seed with SOC 2 Type II, SOC 1, ISO 27017, and ISO 27001 certifications already obtained shows unusual execution velocity. Angel backing from leaders at Anthropic, Vercel, Replit, LangChain, and Browserbase creates organic distribution into exactly the developer communities most likely to adopt. I considered the DoorDash exception — where consequence magnitude at planetary scale overrode the lack of technical novelty. But DoorDash's consequence was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. Sazabi's consequence is transforming observability for engineering teams. The populations affected differ by orders of magnitude.

Sazabi's path to affordability exists but operates in the wrong domain for my framework. Making observability cheaper for startups is cost reduction within a professional tooling category. My highest-conviction affordability bets — Curai Health, Impossible Foods, Commonwealth Fusion Systems — target basic human needs: healthcare, food, energy. Observability is a line item on an engineering budget, not a necessity being held hostage by legacy cost structures. Sazabi could cut Datadog bills by 5x and the world would not notice.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Sazabi demonstrates within six months that its logs-only approach creates a genuinely new capability — not cheaper monitoring, but something categorically impossible with three-pillars architectures — specifically in AI-agent observability, and shows that this capability expands the market rather than redistributing existing spend. If Sazabi can prove that autonomous AI systems require a fundamentally different observability paradigm and that paradigm affects the reliability of AI systems used by millions of consumers, the consequence magnitude changes entirely. Until then, Sazabi is a well-executed developer tool in a competitive market — exactly the kind of company that generates 3-5x returns, which is a perfectly fine outcome for most investors but not a bet I need to make with my own money.
