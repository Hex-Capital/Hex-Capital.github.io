# Adialante -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see is the miracle count. Adialante needs to prove that FREE physics produces clinically adequate prostate images -- a novel, unvalidated approach to MRI encoding. Then they need FDA clearance for that novel physics, which likely requires a De Novo pathway since there's no obvious predicate device for gradient-coil-free MRI. Then they need to build and operate a mobile imaging fleet, a logistics and compliance challenge entirely orthogonal to MRI physics. Three sequential miracles, each with independently low probability. When I wrote about the single-miracle framework, this is the pattern I was warning against: compounding small odds. Airbnb needed marketplace liquidity -- one miracle. Color needed lab automation to drive down genetic testing costs -- one miracle. Adialante needs to chain together a physics breakthrough, a regulatory approval, and an operations buildout, in sequence, before generating a dollar of revenue.

The market itself is not non-obvious. Affordable MRI is a well-understood problem with over $400 million in venture capital already deployed against it: Promaxo at $63-81M with FDA clearance and 69 systems sold, Hyperfine at $90M+ with $13.6M in revenue and growing, Synaptive at $260M with $35M annual revenue, Chipiron at $22M+ pursuing a different quantum detection approach. When I look at a market and see five funded competitors with a combined quarter-billion-plus in capital, this is not the "too boring" or "too niche" archetype where I find asymmetric returns. Nobody is dismissing portable MRI -- investors and incumbents are actively pursuing it. The prostate-specific focus and mobile delivery model are differentiated, but the broader market is consensus-hot. Compare this to PagerDuty when I invested: nobody was funding operations alerting because it seemed boring and small. Here, everybody sees the opportunity.

The bull case deserves a full accounting because the Color Health parallel is real, and it's the kind of bet I've made before. If FREE technology works at clinical grade, the regulatory barrier becomes a structural moat that could protect them for years -- FDA clearance for novel MRI physics would be extremely difficult for fast-followers to replicate. The per-scan mobile model is genuinely innovative: it creates a business model conflict where Siemens, GE, and Philips cannot easily match without cannibalizing their $1-3M hardware sales and service contract revenue. That's the innovator's dilemma playing out in medical imaging. The team credentials are exceptional for a pre-seed -- CMRR is one of the top three MRI research labs globally, the Rabi Award winner (Srinivas) signals world-class talent, and the FREE technology is published in peer-reviewed journals. If the physics works, if the FDA clears it, and if they can operate mobile units efficiently, this could be a massive business serving every urology clinic in America. My experience founding Color taught me that making cancer screening accessible at population scale through a different delivery model can work. The question is whether the path there requires one miracle or three.

What tips this toward a pass for me is the technology cycle positioning combined with the competitive landscape. The structural moment for low-field MRI already happened -- Hyperfine got FDA clearance in 2020, Siemens launched Free.Max, clinical guidelines have been shifting for years. Adialante's FREE technology is genuinely novel physics, but it's pre-clinical-validation and pre-FDA. Promaxo is already selling FDA-cleared prostate MRI systems into the exact same urology clinics Adialante is targeting. Building ahead of a capability curve works when the curve is being delivered by external forces -- that's what made Harvey's bet so elegant, because OpenAI was delivering the model fidelity miracle for them. Here, Adialante has to deliver its own miracle internally, with $2.4M raised against competitors with 20-40x more capital. The team is entirely academic with no visible commercial or operational experience, and the mobile fleet model requires an execution skillset -- fleet logistics, multi-state regulatory compliance, staffing -- that has nothing to do with MRI physics research. I need to see that the founders have identified which of their three miracles is THE miracle and organized the entire company around it. Right now, the dossier suggests they're trying to solve all three simultaneously.

I have genuine respect for the science here -- patent-pending physics that eliminates a third of conventional MRI hardware is not a wrapper or an increment. But respect for the science and conviction to deploy capital are different things. The gap between a published paper in Magnetic Resonance in Medicine and a mobile MRI unit scanning prostate patients in a urology clinic parking lot is measured in years and tens of millions of dollars. With Promaxo already commercially deployed and Hyperfine growing revenue, the window for a pre-FDA entrant with novel physics is narrowing, not widening.
