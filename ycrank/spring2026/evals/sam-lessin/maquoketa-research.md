# Maquoketa Research -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Anduril has raised $6.9 billion and is building a $1 billion, five-million-square-foot factory to produce tens of thousands of autonomous systems annually starting mid-2026. AeroVironment holds a $990 million Army contract for Switchblade and is scaling to 1,200 units per month. Shield AI has $3.1 billion in equity and projects $540 million in 2026 revenue. Helsing has a €268 million German government contract and has already shipped thousands of units to Ukraine. Maquoketa Research has five people in Elk Grove Village, Illinois, nine forked GitHub repos, and a website returning a 403 error. I don't care that it's pre-seed — I care about what the pricing tells me. And what it tells me is that defense tech autonomous drones is one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture capital right now. Every sophisticated investor on the planet watched Ukraine and drew the same conclusion: cheap autonomous drones change warfare. That's not a contrarian thesis. That's a New York Times headline. When the market has already priced in the category thesis, you need an extraordinary edge to justify the entry — and Maquoketa Research hasn't shown me that edge.

The primitive test fails cleanly. Maquoketa Research is building a one-way attack drone in a market where one-way attack drones already exist, are being procured at scale, and have named competitors with billion-dollar contracts. MarketsandMarkets sizes the loitering munition market at $5.36 billion in 2025 growing to $13.26 billion by 2030 — this is a defined, measured, actively competed market, not a category being birthed. When I backed Venmo, peer-to-peer mobile payments wasn't a market — it was a behavior nobody had named yet. When I seeded Solana, the thesis wasn't "better Ethereum" — it was a fundamentally different architectural bet on consensus mechanisms. Maquoketa Research is offering "cheaper, more autonomous Switchblade" — that's execution competition in an existing market, not primitive creation. The Metropolis parallel is instructive here: I seeded Metropolis when nobody gave a shit about parking technology. The mispricing was extreme because the category was boring. Autonomous attack drones in 2026 are the opposite of boring — they're the sexiest category in defense, which means the upside is already in the price.

Evan Yeager brings UC Berkeley EECS and software experience at SpiderRock and Stylis.AI. Alejandro Hernandez has genuine RF and phased array specialization from Berkeley Wireless Research Center — that's real, hard engineering, and contested electromagnetic environments are a legitimate technical frontier. Maquoketa Research covers the right disciplines across its founding team: software, RF/communications, mechanical engineering, operations. But I can't calibrate the sanity-insanity ratio without more signal. There are no prior exits, no observable multi-cycle track record, no evidence of the calm-under-pressure rationality I look for. Taking on Anduril at $30.5 billion with five people and pre-seed capital is certainly insane, but it's the wrong kind of insane — it's not "this category doesn't exist yet and I'm going to create it," it's "I'm bringing a knife to a gunfight with four well-armed opponents." The co-founder relationships trace to UC Berkeley, which is fine but doesn't give me the depth of signal I got watching Raj Dittmar across three companies before writing the Solana check.

Here's the bull case, and I want to be honest about it because there's a structural argument that's genuinely interesting. Lockheed, Raytheon, and Northrop face real business model cannibalization — their margins depend on $100K+ precision munitions, so they have economic disincentive to flood the market with $6,000 expendable drones. That cannibalization dynamic is textbook Christensen, and it's real. Maquoketa Research's vertical integration claim and "built entirely in the USA" positioning address legitimate DoD procurement priorities around supply chain security. The ITAR regulatory barriers create a moat against non-US competitors. And the hardware itself — the physical drone, the custom phased array electronics, the manufacturing infrastructure — passes my AI kill test beautifully. You cannot download a drone. You cannot prompt an LLM to manufacture a phased array antenna. Maquoketa Research scores highest on structural durability precisely because hardware companies with physical-world operations and regulatory barriers are the kind of thing AI cannot commoditize. If this were a software-only autonomy stack, I'd have already stopped reading.

But the bull case collapses when you look at who Maquoketa Research is actually competing against. The cannibalization argument applies to traditional primes — it does not apply to Anduril, which was purpose-built for this exact market and is constructing Arsenal-1 to produce at massive scale. It does not apply to AeroVironment, which is already the incumbent in small loitering munitions. The relevant competitors don't face the innovator's dilemma because they are the innovators. And Maquoketa Research faces a brutal capital discipline problem: hardware manufacturing at scale requires exactly the kind of multi-round, factory-farm fundraising model I've said publicly is dead. This company cannot survive if this is the last money it ever raises — it will need significant capital for manufacturing infrastructure, security clearances, ITAR compliance, and the 18-36 month DoD procurement cycles before any meaningful revenue. The Allbirds parallel haunts me here: capital-intensive physical product that needs the VC conveyor belt to scale, competing against established players with distribution advantages.

One verifiable condition would flip me: if Maquoketa Research has already secured an OTA contract, a Replicator program slot, or a signed letter from a DoD program office — something that demonstrates the procurement relationship is real, not aspirational. The dossier shows zero revenue, zero contracts, zero traction signals. In defense, relationships and clearances are the moat, and I see no evidence Maquoketa Research has either. The structural durability from hardware is real — that's the one dimension where Maquoketa Research genuinely fits my thesis. But structural durability alone doesn't overcome a consensus-priced category, no primitive creation, uncertain founders, and a capital-intensive path that requires exactly the fundraising treadmill I believe is broken. Pass.
