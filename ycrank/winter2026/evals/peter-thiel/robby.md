# Robby -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask about any company is whether it possesses a secret -- a truth about the world that is important, non-obvious, and that most intelligent people would actively argue against. Robby's implicit thesis is that home services businesses leave enormous revenue on the table during technician visits, and that an AI layer can systematically capture it. This is not a secret. It is an observation so universally acknowledged that ServiceTitan -- a $9 billion public company that owns the data platform Robby depends on -- has already announced native AI features targeting the exact same revenue-generation use case. When the dominant incumbent, the startup, and at least one other competitor (Arch) all share the same thesis, you are not looking at a secret. You are looking at a consensus dressed in AI clothing. Every participant in this market sees the same opportunity, which means no participant has a structural advantage, which means the market will be competed away.

The structural dynamics here are worse than ordinary competition -- they are dependency masquerading as partnership. Robby syncs to ServiceTitan. It reads ServiceTitan's data. It writes back to ServiceTitan's records. ServiceTitan controls the API, the customer relationship, and the platform roadmap. This is the Zynga problem: building your house on someone else's land. When ServiceTitan ships Atlas Campaign Recommendations and its SMS Booking Agent -- features that overlap directly with Robby's lead generation and technician assistance -- Robby faces an existential choice between competing with its platform provider or accepting a diminishing niche. I have seen this pattern destroy companies. The startup that integrates with the platform rather than becoming the platform is, almost by definition, building a feature, not a company. Features get absorbed. Companies endure.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Robby has 9+ named paying customers at pre-seed, with testimonials claiming six-figure weekly revenue opportunities uncovered per customer and $40,000 closed within the first hour of launch. If those numbers hold under scrutiny, the ROI is extraordinary -- a home services business paying a few hundred dollars per month for a tool that generates six figures weekly would never churn. The team's decision to ride along with technicians and pursue EPA 608 certification reflects a level of domain immersion that most SaaS founders never achieve. Schwarzmann's Bain background in PE-backed home services acquisitions gives him pattern recognition across dozens of businesses, and Jammalamadaka's experience as founding engineer at Cloud Health Systems demonstrates he can build data platforms from zero. If I believed the revenue-generation layer could maintain structural independence from ServiceTitan -- if, for instance, Robby's data flywheel from accumulated technician visits created such superior lead quality that ServiceTitan's native features could not replicate it -- then the traction signals would be compelling. But that conditional requires Robby to build a proprietary data asset that is 10x better than what ServiceTitan can build on its own platform with its own data. I do not see evidence that this gap exists or can be sustained.

The founding team's trajectory raises a separate concern. Jammalamadaka explored AI customs brokerage (Importrix) before pivoting when tariff dynamics shifted. Mohideen and Schwarzmann co-founded Lifelong Health before pivoting to Robby. Three distinct product directions across the team in under a year is not the behavior of definite optimists with a singular vision for the future -- it is the behavior of lean startup practitioners searching for product-market fit. I do not invest in search processes. I invest in founders who have already found their truth and refuse to abandon it. The domain immersion is admirable, but arriving at home services through elimination rather than conviction is a meaningful distinction. Zuckerberg did not iterate his way to social networking. Musk did not pivot into rockets after trying three other industries. The best founders are unreasonably committed to their specific vision before any market validates it.

The technology itself is competent vertical application of large language models -- combining CRM data with third-party data sources to generate talking points and automate documentation. This is solidly in the category of "1 to n" rather than "0 to 1." The dossier itself acknowledges that the core technology is "technically replicable." A well-funded team at ServiceTitan, Jobber, or Housecall Pro could build comparable features within 12-18 months. There is no proprietary algorithm, no fundamental research breakthrough, no 10x technological discontinuity that makes the prior approach structurally obsolete. The company is building a better tool, not creating a new category. Better tools get commoditized. New categories create monopolies.

I will pass. Robby is a well-executed vertical SaaS product with genuine early traction, built by capable founders who are deeply embedded in their market. In a portfolio optimized for base rates and steady returns, it might be a reasonable bet. But my portfolio is optimized for the power law, and the power law requires monopoly, which requires a secret. The home services AI revenue-generation thesis is shared by the incumbent platform, at least one direct competitor, and the general zeitgeist of "AI for SMBs." When everyone sees the same opportunity, no one earns monopoly profits. Competition is structurally guaranteed here, and I invest on the other side of that equation.
