# Terranox AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what grabbed me immediately: nobody else is doing this. KoBold has raised over a billion dollars for AI-driven mineral exploration. Earth AI is doing it. GeologicAI is doing it. They're all chasing copper, nickel, cobalt, lithium. Not one of them is touching uranium. That absence is either a signal that the opportunity is structurally different in some way that makes AI exploration unworkable for uranium -- or it means everyone else is flinching from the nuclear stigma. And I've learned over the years that when smart, well-funded companies collectively avoid a space, it's usually not because they've done rigorous analysis. It's because the category makes them uncomfortable. That's the same dynamic I saw with Contraline -- male contraception that nobody wanted to fund because the category felt weird and regulatory-heavy and hard to explain at a partner meeting. The fact that AI-powered uranium exploration is a genuinely open lane with no direct competitor is the strongest signal in this dossier.

But here's my problem: I can't find the founder's story. When I sit down with someone, the first thing I want to hear is how they got here -- not the business plan, not the technology, but the personal thread that makes this specific founder the person who has to solve this specific problem. Jade Checlair has a PhD in geophysical sciences from UChicago, did research at NASA Ames on exoplanet habitability, then went to BCG as a project leader. Leeav Lipton spent eight years building AI systems at RBC's research institute. These are genuinely strong credentials for the technical challenge -- a geophysicist and an ML engineer is exactly the team composition you'd design in a lab for this problem. But I'm not seeing the biographical thread that connects them to uranium, to mining, to nuclear energy. How does someone studying snowball states on tidally locked planets end up wanting to find uranium deposits in North America? There might be a beautiful answer to that question, but I can't see it from what's available. And without that story, I can't assess whether these founders will persist through the inevitable crises of uranium exploration -- the failed drill campaigns, the permit denials, the capital crunches -- or whether this is two brilliant people who identified an attractive market gap and applied their transferable skills to it. Those are very different founders in my experience.

The vertical integration model is what makes this structurally interesting to me, and it's where the Crusoe Energy parallel becomes hard to ignore. Crusoe started by wanting to mine Bitcoin on waste gas -- an idea that sounded completely absurd -- and combined a technology insight with physical assets in a way that created a structural moat. Terranox is doing something similar: they're not selling software to miners, they're building the AI AND owning the mineral claims. If their model works and they discover a significant uranium deposit, they capture the full economic value of that discovery rather than earning a licensing fee. That's the kind of structural bet I find compelling. And the timing is genuinely interesting -- AI data centers are driving nuclear energy demand, uranium supply is constrained with prices at $86/lb, and 15 new reactors are expected to come online this year. This isn't hype-driven timing; it's physics-driven demand meeting geological scarcity.

One thing I'd want to be true for this to be a great investment: the 70+ years of exploration outcome data they claim to have assembled is actually proprietary and hard to replicate. If they've done the unglamorous work of aggregating, cleaning, and structuring fragmented geological records from government agencies, academic institutions, and private companies into a training dataset that nobody else has built for uranium specifically, that's a real data moat. Every drill result they generate through their own exploration adds to that dataset in a compounding way. But "trained on 70+ years of exploration outcomes" is a claim on a YC page -- I'd need to understand the provenance, the quality, and whether this data is truly inaccessible to KoBold if they decided to pivot to uranium next quarter with a billion dollars in the bank.

The co-founder relationship is the one dimension where I have genuine confidence. They met in first-year physics and have worked together for over a decade. After watching HQ Trivia collapse -- the right product at the right time destroyed by co-founder conflict -- I now weight this heavily. Ten years of collaboration means they've already navigated disagreements, ego dynamics, and the inevitable friction of long-term partnership. That's not a guarantee, but it's the kind of tested relationship I want to see. What I don't see is how the economic access dimension plays out. Uranium exploration is important for energy infrastructure, but Terranox is several layers removed from creating economic opportunity for underserved people. This isn't Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm giving transparent credit to people shut out of the financial system. It's a resource extraction play, and while clean energy matters enormously, I can't draw a direct line from "two people finding uranium deposits" to the kind of positive-sum economic outcomes that anchor my thesis.

I'm landing in a genuinely mixed place. The weirdness factor is real -- AI-powered uranium exploration by a two-person team is exactly the kind of thing that most investors would politely pass on while privately thinking it's insane. The regulatory complexity of uranium creates the kind of moat I look for. The co-founder relationship is tested. But my primary filter -- the founder's biographical connection to the problem, the personal stake that tells me they'll push through when everything goes wrong -- isn't visible to me here. And this is an extraordinarily capital-intensive path for a pre-seed company with no uranium-specific operating experience. I could see myself getting excited about this if Jade sat across from me and told me the story I can't find in this dossier. But based on what I can see, this is a competent team in a compelling space without the biographical signal I need to write a conviction check.
