# Loops AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Hakan Baş co-founded Peak Games and sold it to Zynga for $1.8 billion. That's a real credential -- one of the strongest exit signals I've seen in a pre-seed dossier. But the first question I ask isn't about the founder, it's about the market structure: what structural change is making this possible now that wasn't possible two years ago, and does consensus underestimate the window? For Loops AI, the answer is uncomfortable. AI-powered commerce agents are the single most consensus AI application category in 2026. OpenAI has launched agentic checkout directly in ChatGPT. Google has launched agentic commerce in Search and Gemini. Gorgias, with $114 million raised and roughly $80 million in ARR, is actively shipping AI Agent 2.0. Gartner forecasts 75% of online shopping interactions will involve AI-driven conversation by 2026. Every investor, every platform company, and every e-commerce incumbent is looking at this exact category right now. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed as fringe. When I backed Perplexity, AI search was considered a dead category. Loops AI is the structural opposite -- a company entering a market where the structural shift has been fully recognized and is being actively captured by platform-scale players.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Loops AI fails most decisively for my framework. Stripe became the payments API that an entire generation of businesses built on -- that's compounding, irreversible lock-in. Loops AI deploys AI agents on top of LLM infrastructure built by OpenAI, Google, and others. It integrates into e-commerce platforms but doesn't become a platform itself. There's no API layer other companies depend on, no developer ecosystem, no compounding network effect. The multi-modal product stack -- semantic search, virtual try-on, image search, sales agents -- creates breadth, but breadth without depth is a feature bundle, not infrastructure. When the foundation model providers verticalize into commerce (which they're actively doing), Loops AI's application layer is directly in the blast zone. This is my explicit red flag: a startup building an application on a contested infrastructure layer where the platform winners haven't been determined yet.

The single-miracle test reveals compounding challenges. The technology miracle -- LLMs becoming good enough for conversational commerce -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable. But Loops AI needs at least two additional breakthroughs: cracking U.S. distribution (all 50+ current brand customers are concentrated in Turkey, MENA, and Europe per The SaaS News) and sustaining differentiation against Gorgias, Rep AI, and the platform companies building identical capabilities. Enterprise e-commerce sales cycles in the U.S. are structurally different from Turkish and MENA markets, and Gorgias already has 12,000+ merchants with deep Shopify integration. That's two additional miracles beyond the technology working, and compounding small odds is how I've seen strong teams get crushed.

The founder profile deserves careful examination. Baş's $1.8 billion Peak Games exit and serial founding track record (Lidyana.com, Ace Games, Bitlo) represent genuine operator-scaler credentials -- he's navigated hypergrowth and knows what the transition from 10 to 1,000 looks like. Ari Nazir brings White House NEC and investment management experience, and the team has clearly leveraged Turkish business networks effectively to land 50+ brands quickly. But four co-founders with Baş listed only as "Co-Founder" without an operational title (not CEO, not CTO, not COO) raises questions about decision-making hierarchy. One of my explicit red flags is ambiguous authority structures -- at four co-founders, someone needs to be unambiguously in charge, and the dossier doesn't clarify who that is beyond Nazir holding the CEO title. The $500K ARR and claimed 2x growth in 45 days are impressive numbers if verified, but they're entirely self-reported with no third-party confirmation, and zero churn across 50+ enterprise customers would be extraordinary at any stage.

The strongest bull case: Loops AI has built a working product generating real revenue faster than most pre-seed companies, and Baş's exit history means the team knows how to build and sell companies. The ~$10K average ACV (inferred from $500K ARR across 50+ brands) could expand significantly with U.S. enterprise customers, and the multi-modal stack could create enough switching cost friction to resist individual feature replication. If Loops AI can establish itself as the default AI commerce layer for mid-market brands before platform companies fully vertically integrate, there's a path to meaningful scale. But "before platform companies fully vertically integrate" is doing enormous work in that sentence -- OpenAI and Google are already there. The comparison that haunts me is Wish: a commerce-layer company where growth velocity masked the absence of structural defensibility, and when the competitive environment tightened, there was no infrastructure moat to fall back on.

My verdict is Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. Loops AI enters the most consensus AI application category of 2026, building at the application layer on a contested infrastructure stack where OpenAI and Google are actively shipping competing products. Baş's $1.8 billion exit and the team's efficient customer acquisition partially offset these concerns, but my framework is market-structure-first for a reason: I've seen strong founders repeatedly get crushed when platform companies decide to build the same product as a feature. The $500K ARR is real progress, but progress in a market where the structural timing advantage has already been captured by larger players isn't the same as progress in a market others haven't noticed yet.
