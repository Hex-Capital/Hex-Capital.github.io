# Alt-X -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that jumps out, and it's not encouraging. Shortcut has $78M from Sequoia and scores above 80% on Excel World Championship cases. Endex has $14M from the OpenAI Startup Fund with access to frontier reasoning models. Datarails just raised at a $550M valuation and is approaching $100M in revenue. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where multiple players are already well-capitalized and shipping product, I apply my "market-ending moves" framework: can a horizontal platform with 50-100x more capital add your vertical specialization as a feature? In this case, the answer is almost certainly yes. Shortcut or Endex adding alternative-asset KPI templates is a product sprint, not a structural barrier. Alt-X's positioning as the "alternative-asset-specific" Excel AI tool is a marketing choice, not a moat.

The underlying market does have qualities I find interesting. Private-market underwriting is genuinely boring -- analysts spending 30-50 hours per deal copying numbers from PDFs into spreadsheets is exactly the kind of high-repetition, high-cost workflow where AI creates real value. The $25-30B diligence spend figure is plausible directionally. And this workflow has the "too boring for most AI investors" quality that I've historically found attractive -- PagerDuty alerting, Gusto payroll, Checkr background checks all had that same dismissive-seeming surface. But here's the critical difference: the broader category of "AI for Excel" is decidedly not boring to investors in 2026. It's consensus. The non-obvious slice would be the alternative-asset vertical specifically, but even there Apers already exists. When a market thesis is non-obvious, you don't see $78M in Sequoia funding already deployed against it.

The strongest bull case for Alt-X is a Gusto-like trajectory: start with a specific, underserved vertical (family offices and mid-market PE/RE firms), build deep domain expertise in alternative-asset KPIs, and expand from there into portfolio analytics, fund reporting, and LP communications. Ryan Samadi's background at Citadel, BofA, and in PE means he actually lived inside this pain point -- he's not guessing about the workflow, he watched analysts do this manually. If Alt-X can capture the underwriting workflow for even a meaningful fraction of mid-market private funds, it becomes the data ingestion layer for those firms' entire deal pipeline. That's a distribution channel worth something. For this bull case to work, though, you'd need the vertical specialization to compound faster than horizontal competitors can add vertical features -- and you'd need to move upmarket from $30/month to meaningful ACV before the horizontal players squeeze you out. I don't see enough evidence that this is the plan.

The architecture decision -- building inside Excel rather than replacing it -- reveals a tension I keep coming back to. On one hand, it's smart: institutional investors will never abandon Excel for IC approvals, so meeting them where they are reduces adoption friction. On the other hand, building your entire product inside someone else's platform caps your distribution trajectory. You can't become a distribution channel for additional products when you're an add-in. Microsoft's own Copilot for Excel is expanding steadily, and any changes to Excel's add-in architecture could undermine the product overnight. Compare this to Stripe, which created its own integration surface (the API) that became the distribution channel for dozens of additional products. Alt-X's Excel dependency means the product could be excellent at what it does but structurally unable to compound beyond it. A smart toaster problem, in a different form -- the software makes Excel better, but Excel remains the platform, not Alt-X.

On execution, Samadi's domain credibility is real but the signals are thin. The website returned a JavaScript-only page with no rendered content. No public product, no named customers, no revenue disclosed, no GitHub presence. The CTO's background couldn't be independently confirmed. The co-founders have no visible prior working relationship. These aren't disqualifying at pre-seed -- many great companies started with less -- but when you're entering a market with $78M-funded competitors, execution velocity is existential, and I see no evidence of it yet. The $30/month price point also concerns me: institutional finance sales don't work at that ACV. Compliance review, IT security approval, and procurement processes at even a small family office cost more to navigate than the annual contract is worth. Either the real monetization is at a much higher tier that isn't disclosed, or the GTM model hasn't been pressure-tested against actual institutional buying behavior.

I'm passing. The market insight is directionally correct but no longer non-obvious. The competitive landscape is hostile. The product architecture limits platform potential. And the evidence of execution is insufficient given the pace required to win against dramatically better-funded competitors.
