# Lumius -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Junjie Yao has 11,000 citations and an H-index of 50 in the exact imaging modality Lumius is built on. That's not a founder who "identified an opportunity" — that's someone who spent a career creating the science that makes Lumius possible. And Luca Menozzi, the CTO, didn't adopt 3D diffractive acoustic tomography from a paper — he developed it as his PhD research at Yao's Duke lab, presenting it at BMES in 2023. When Stripe came along, part of what made the bet compelling was that the Collisons understood payment infrastructure at a level that couldn't be replicated by reading documentation. Lumius has that same quality of founder-technology lock-in: the team IS the technology, and anyone trying to replicate 3D-DAT would either need to hire them or reinvent years of photoacoustic physics independently. The scientific moat here is real, and it's the kind of depth that makes me lean forward.

Lumius sits squarely in what I'd call a schlep canyon. FDA clearance for a novel imaging modality. Hospital procurement cycles measured in quarters. Manufacturing tolerances for medical-grade hardware. Sales conversations with anesthesiology department heads who've used GE and Philips for decades. Most YC applicants hear "medical device" and their unconscious steers them toward building another SaaS dashboard. The fact that 50% of vascular access attempts fail on the first try — that's a problem hiding behind so many layers of regulatory and institutional friction that it stays invisible to anyone who hasn't lived inside clinical imaging. Butterfly Network raised $353.5 million and Exo raised $428 million, yet neither has shipped real-time 3D. Lumius is attacking the gap those war chests haven't closed, and the execution path is ugly enough to keep most competitors from following. When I invested in ClassDojo — K-12 classroom software, about as far from Hacker News demographics as you can get — it was because the schlep blindness was so severe that it functioned as a moat. Lumius has that same structural advantage.

Tri Vu, Luca Menozzi, Chenhang Li, and Junjie Yao are four deeply credentialed scientists with zero publicly documented startup experience, zero prior exits, and zero evidence of the scrappy commercial instincts I weight heaviest. I've funded thousands of teams, and the pattern I keep returning to is that academic brilliance and startup determination are orthogonal axes. You can be extraordinary on one and mediocre on the other. When the Airbnb founders were selling cereal boxes to fund their company, that wasn't intellectual — it was animal. Where are the Lumius founders' cereal box stories? I don't see a single anecdote of creative workaround, revenue generation before fundraising, or manual customer acquisition. Their public profiles are uniformly academic: publications, fellowships, lab appointments. Tri Vu is listed as a Research Assistant Professor at Oklahoma. Menozzi is an NSF Graduate Research Fellow. Li's work is in epidermal electronics and soft robotics — adjacent but not identical to ultrasound devices. The transition from Duke's Photoacoustic Imaging Lab to a company shipping FDA-cleared hardware into hospital supply chains is one of the hardest jumps in startups, and nothing in the dossier tells me these founders have practiced jumping.

Lumius's idea — affordable real-time 3D ultrasound — doesn't fail my Black Swan test in the way I'd want. "Better, cheaper imaging" is the kind of idea that sounds good to everyone. No one hears "we're making 3D ultrasound affordable and portable" and says "that will never work." They say "that sounds amazing, good luck with the FDA." The absence of skepticism is itself a mild warning sign. What does repel people is the execution path — building a medical device against incumbents with hundreds of millions in R&D budgets — and that's where the contrarian signal lives. But it's a different kind of repulsion than, say, Coinbase launching into cryptocurrency when serious investors dismissed the entire category. Lumius is tackling an obviously good idea through an obviously hard path, which is a weaker form of the "seems bad, is good" dynamic I look for.

Yao's omission from the YC listing — he appears only on the company website as "President, Scientific Advisor & Co-Founder" — raises a question I'd ask directly if this were a 10-minute YC interview. Is he full-time? A tenured Duke associate professor with active lab responsibilities, affiliated with the Duke Cancer Institute and Institute of Brain Sciences, likely cannot be. If Yao is part-time, the operational team is three PhD students with no commercial experience running a medical device company through FDA clearance and hospital sales. The advisory board includes clinical MDs (Jeffrey Lawson, Keith Hemmert) and a business advisor (Barry Myers), which helps — but advisors advise, they don't execute. Lumius needs someone who has navigated 510(k) submissions, managed contract manufacturing, and sold capital equipment to hospital systems. I don't see that person on the team.

The bull case for Lumius is genuinely strong: the deepest possible technical moat (invented the core science), massive schlep blindness keeping competitors away, a quantifiable clinical problem (50% first-attempt failure rate), and a market where the POCUS segment alone is projected to reach $5.56 billion by 2034. If I apply my Boom Supersonic exception — where ambition scale and missionary conviction override normal frameworks — I could argue that bringing 3D imaging to every bedside is transformative enough. But Boom had Blake Scholl, who'd already built and sold companies before attempting supersonic flight. Lumius has four first-time founders from academia. The exception requires not just ambition but demonstrated commercial determination, and Lumius shows the former without evidence of the latter. What would flip me to Invest: if the founders told me they'd already run Lumius prototypes on 50 patients in a Duke clinical setting and could describe specific iteration cycles from clinician feedback — evidence that they've crossed from lab to bedside and started learning at startup speed.

Lumius has the scientific foundation of a potentially important company trapped inside the profile of a university lab spinout that hasn't yet proven it can become a real business. The technology is genuine, the moat is defensible, and the problem matters. But I weight founder resourcefulness at 30% of my evaluation for a reason — it's the single most predictive signal I've found across thousands of YC companies, and Lumius gives me nothing to work with on this dimension. The two criteria that most determine my verdict are the absence of any commercial resourcefulness evidence across all four founders and the genuine strength of the organic technology-to-problem connection. These pull in opposite directions, landing Lumius in the zone where I'd want a working session with the founders before committing capital.
