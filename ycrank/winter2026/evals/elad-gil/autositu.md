# AutoSitu -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Municipal plan review is one of those markets that makes most investors' eyes glaze over -- zoning compliance, building code checking, cross-department coordination across 19,000 U.S. municipalities, each with its own ordinance structure. It's the kind of market I've consistently found my best returns in. When I backed PagerDuty, "operations alerting" triggered the same dismissive reaction. When I backed Gusto, "payroll software" did too. The pattern is a market that appears small and boring but turns out to be universal infrastructure -- every city that issues permits needs this, and every developer building housing needs it to work faster. What makes this moment structural rather than incremental is a triple convergence: LLMs crossed the threshold for reliable regulatory document reasoning in 2023-2024, the U.S. housing crisis created acute political pressure to accelerate permitting (Seattle's mayor signed an executive order in June 2025 directing AI review of all development applications), and municipal planning departments are severely understaffed post-COVID. That's not a static TAM slide -- that's an adoption curve inflecting.

The competitive density here, though, demands serious scrutiny. PermitFlow has raised $91M with 10x revenue growth. CivCheck got acquired by Clariti. Blitz AI claims to be the "most widely adopted AI plan review platform" across five states. CodeComply.Ai and Archistar are also funded and growing. When I see five-plus funded competitors in a space, my first instinct is that this is no longer a non-obvious market -- it's becoming consensus. But there's a critical distinction AutoSitu is making that I think most observers are collapsing. PermitFlow serves developers and contractors navigating the permit process from the outside. AutoSitu embeds inside the municipal reviewer's workflow -- the cross-department coordination where a plan gets bounced between zoning, fire, public works, and planning. These are fundamentally different customers with different procurement processes, different integration requirements, and different value propositions. It's the difference between building tools for taxpayers versus building tools for the IRS. The municipal side is harder to sell into but creates dramatically higher switching costs once embedded.

The founder-market fit here is better than it looks on paper. Xuanshu Lin has a Harvard GSD urban planning degree and hands-on experience with zoning and site plan compliance -- he understands the regulatory substrate at a level that pure software founders cannot fake. George Zhai brings production AI engineering from GM and McLaren's autonomous systems group with 7 patents. This is the exact pairing I look for in regulated-market plays: deep domain knowledge of the regulatory complexity paired with the engineering capability to automate it. My Color Health experience taught me that the hardest part of building in regulated markets isn't the technology -- it's encoding domain-specific knowledge that lives in practitioners' heads, not in textbooks. For a 2-person pre-seed team to have 1,500+ plans checked and 11+ named city engagements including San Jose, Seattle, Los Angeles, Boston, and San Francisco is genuinely impressive execution velocity. Those aren't small municipalities running casual pilots -- those are major U.S. cities with sophisticated procurement processes.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this gets interesting. If AutoSitu becomes the regulatory intelligence layer inside municipal review workflows, the initial plan review product becomes a distribution channel for adjacent compliance products -- inspection scheduling, ongoing code enforcement, environmental review, historic preservation review. Every municipality has dozens of regulatory review workflows that follow the same pattern: complex local codes, understaffed departments, paper-based processes. The jurisdiction-specific regulatory knowledge AutoSitu accumulates -- 19,000 municipalities each with unique codes -- creates a data moat that compounds. This is analogous to how Stripe's payment API became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. The initial wedge (plan review) is narrow enough to be achievable but connected to a much larger regulatory infrastructure opportunity.

The single miracle here is municipal adoption at scale -- can a 2-person team navigate government procurement cycles that typically run 6-18 months? The AI technology works; the foundation model labs are delivering the capability improvements. The regulatory knowledge encoding is being solved through the founders' domain expertise. What remains is distribution into government. This is a real obstacle, but the structural tailwinds are strong -- political pressure on housing, staffing shortages, and executive-level mandates for AI adoption in permitting (Seattle's executive order is a leading indicator, not an anomaly). My concern is that PermitFlow's $91M war chest could allow them to attack the municipal side too, collapsing AutoSitu's differentiation. But incumbents in government sales rarely pivot customer segments easily -- selling to developers and selling to city planning departments require completely different relationships, trust dynamics, and integration approaches.

The bull case is that AutoSitu becomes the Gusto of municipal regulatory infrastructure -- starting with the "boring" wedge of plan review and expanding into the full regulatory stack for every municipality in America. If the housing crisis continues to intensify political pressure for faster permitting, and if LLM capabilities continue improving, the adoption curve could be steeper than anyone expects. The bear case is competitive commoditization -- if the core technology is reproducible (LLM + regulatory documents), well-funded competitors could build equivalent products faster. I lean toward the bull case because the jurisdiction-specific data moat and municipal workflow integration create real switching costs, and the founders have the right domain-technical combination for this market. This is a regulated-market infrastructure play with the kind of "boring but critical" profile where I've historically found strong returns.
