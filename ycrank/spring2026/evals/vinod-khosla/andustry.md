# Andustry -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Andustry is how many companies are already doing variations of this. Matchory raised $8.86 million for AI supplier discovery. LightSource Labs raised $33 million for AI-powered sourcing. Cavela raised $8.6 million for AI procurement. MESH Works has processed $884 million in RFQs. When I backed Juniper Networks to challenge Cisco, nobody else was building TCP/IP routers for public telecom -- Cisco's own CTO said they'd never do it. When I backed OpenAI in 2019, there wasn't a crowded field of AI labs with venture backing. The contrarian signal was the emptiness of the field. Andustry is entering a field that is already filling up, with competitors holding ten to sixty times more capital. The absence of contrarian positioning is itself a signal.

Let me start where I always start: if Andustry succeeds completely -- captures the entire off-the-shelf industrial machinery procurement workflow globally -- what changes? Buyers get quotes in days instead of weeks. Suppliers get more inbound leads. The $810 billion industrial machinery market continues to exist exactly as it does today, except the middlemen are software agents instead of trade show booths and cold emails. This is not the disruption of animal agriculture. This is not the replacement of fossil fuels. This is not even the displacement of traditional payment infrastructure the way Square displaced POS terminals. This is making an existing process run more efficiently. The consequence of success is a good marketplace business -- perhaps a billion-dollar one -- but not an outcome that changes how the world operates. Nobody will look back and say industrial civilization was fundamentally different because procurement agents automated RFQ follow-ups.

The technology layer concerns me most. LLM-based RFQ generation, specification extraction, and supplier matching -- the dossier itself flags this as "reproducible." That's not my word, that's the factual assessment. There are no patents. The 500K+ supplier database might be aggregated from public directories. The "Best Offer Engine" is comparison logic on top of commodity AI. Where is the structural technology advantage that makes the incumbent approach -- manual sourcing, Thomasnet directories, trade relationships -- economically unviable? When I invested in Juniper, the technology created a fundamentally different architecture. When I backed Impossible Foods, heme protein engineering was a genuine scientific breakthrough. Bolting GPT onto procurement workflows is not a technology disruption -- it's a feature that Thomasnet, Xometry, or any well-funded competitor can build in a quarter.

I should engage with the bull case honestly. Johann Sturken has genuine domain credibility: mechanical engineering from RWTH Aachen and TUM, McKinsey aerospace consulting, Airbus strategy work, a family steeped in industrial goods trading. The EUR 1M+ beta transaction volume is a real signal -- actual money moving through the platform, not just sign-ups. And marketplace businesses, when they achieve liquidity, build real network effects. The argument would be: industrial procurement is a relationship-driven, opaque market where trust matters enormously, and the first platform to aggregate 500K+ suppliers with trust scores and certification checks could become the category default. If Andustry achieves liquidity in even one vertical of industrial machinery, the flywheel could compound. This is the Xometry story for off-the-shelf goods. That's a real business. But a real business and a transformational outcome are different things, and I optimize for the latter.

The founders are competent insiders, not contrarian outsiders. A McKinsey consultant from the aerospace and defense practice, building a procurement platform for the industry he consulted to. A technology management graduate who worked at a SaaS transformation company. Both from CDTM, a selective but conventional entrepreneurship program. This is exactly the profile I warn about: experienced people who know the industry's pain points and are building a sensible solution within the industry's existing framework. They are not attempting something experts consider impossible. They are attempting something experts would call obvious -- "of course procurement should be automated." When experts validate your approach rather than dismissing it, the innovation is usually incremental enough to be non-threatening. I look for irreverence and foolish confidence. I see disciplined execution of a well-understood playbook.

The timing is fine but not prescient. AI adoption in procurement is expected to hit 86% by end of 2026 -- this is consensus, not contrarian. Andustry isn't skating to where the puck will be; the puck is already there, and several better-capitalized players are fighting over it. Compare this to my OpenAI investment in 2019, when AI was widely considered "laughable" and I was tracking the rate of improvement in models before most VCs even had an AI thesis. Andustry is building into an accelerating trend, but they're building alongside a crowd, not ahead of it. The exponential curve in LLM capability is real, but it benefits every competitor equally -- it's infrastructure, not moat.

I pass on this one. The team is smart, the market is real, and the product makes sense. But I've spent forty years learning that "smart team, real market, sensible product" produces modest outcomes when the ambition is calibrated to efficiency rather than transformation. Andustry has reduced the probability of failure -- pick a proven model, target a known pain point, use available AI tools -- to the point where the consequence of success is, in my framework, insufficient. I tell founders like this: you're too talented to spend the next decade making procurement 10x faster. Go find the problem in industrial manufacturing that terrifies everyone and that nobody else will touch. That's where the real returns hide.
