# One Robot -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem I can't get past: One Robot is an AI company selling AI capabilities to AI-powered robotics teams. Every layer of that stack is exactly the category I think produces the worst risk-adjusted outcomes for investors right now. The company's differentiation is "our learned world models are better than physics-based simulators for contact-rich manipulation" -- and that may well be true today. But the entire value chain is software. There are no painting crews, no parking garages, no installed hardware. If I apply my own test -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? -- the answer isn't just "no," it's "this business literally cannot exist without AI." That's not cherry on top. That's the whole sundae.

The founders are legitimately impressive, and I want to be clear about that. Elton Shon spent five years at Tesla building production AI infrastructure during the Model 3 ramp and on Dojo. Hemanth Sarabu has NASA JPL, Google, Georgia Tech, and -- critically -- bootstrapped a previous company to profitability, which tells me he's not just a researcher cosplaying as a founder. They worked together at Industrial Next, where they directly experienced the evaluation bottleneck they're now solving. The "sane" half of my equation is fully satisfied. But the "insane" half? Building better robotics simulation is the most consensus idea in the robotics AI ecosystem right now. NVIDIA launched Cosmos in January 2025, World Labs raised $1.23 billion, Saturn is pursuing the same general direction. When NVIDIA is giving away world foundation models with three million-plus downloads, "we build task-specific versions" is a positioning choice, not a non-consensus insight. This is a sane person with a very sane idea, which in my framework is actually one of the weaker configurations -- the returns are already priced into the market's expectations.

The moat question is where this gets uncomfortable. One Robot's defensibility rests on task-specific world model expertise and a potential data flywheel from customer engagements. Both of those are software knowledge moats -- exactly the kind that dissolve as AI commoditizes development. NVIDIA's structural incentive is to make robotics simulation as accessible as possible to drive GPU sales, which means every capability One Robot builds is something NVIDIA has reason to replicate and give away free. The Birchbox lesson haunts me here: Birchbox's data on customer preferences never compounded into genuine lock-in because the model was trivially replicable. Task-specific world model training, once the architecture patterns are established and open-sourced (which is already happening with RoboCasa and MimicGen), could follow the same trajectory. A two-person team trying to outrun NVIDIA and a $1.23 billion-funded World Labs on model quality is a race where the physics of capital work against you.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because it's not trivial. If VLA-based manipulation becomes the dominant paradigm for industrial robotics -- and Physical Intelligence, Google DeepMind, and NVIDIA Gr00t are all pushing hard in this direction -- then evaluation and training infrastructure becomes critical. The specific wedge of contact-rich manipulation (textiles, deformables, sub-millimeter assembly) requires domain knowledge that horizontal platforms genuinely struggle with. NVIDIA doesn't want to build custom simulation environments for every textile folding task at every factory. There's a version of this where One Robot becomes the evaluation layer that every VLA team relies on, creating a primitive for robotics policy development the way Stripe became a primitive for payments. If that happens, the task-specific data flywheel compounds and the narrow wedge expands into a platform. The founders have exactly the right backgrounds to execute on this -- they've shipped at Tesla-scale infrastructure and published in IEEE robotics. What would have to be true: (1) the VLA ecosystem matures fast enough to create a large customer base before One Robot runs out of capital, (2) task-specific world models remain too specialized for NVIDIA to commoditize, and (3) sim-to-real transfer works reliably enough that customers trust simulation over physical testing. All three are plausible individually but their conjunction is where I lose confidence.

The primitive-versus-application question is the most interesting analytical dimension. Could learned world models for specific manipulation tasks become a new foundational layer? There's a structural argument: if One Robot's simulation environments become the standard way that robotics teams validate policies before deployment, that's infrastructure other companies build their development workflows around. But I keep coming back to the fact that the primitive here is really the world model architecture itself, which is being developed and open-sourced by NVIDIA, academic labs, and well-funded startups. One Robot is building task-specific applications of that primitive, not the primitive itself. That's an important distinction -- it's the difference between being Algorand (Layer 1 blockchain protocol) and being a DApp that runs on someone else's chain.

I'm passing on this one. The founders have the credentials and the operational depth I look for, but the structural positioning runs counter to almost everything in my framework. The business is pure software with no physical moat, AI is the product not the cherry on top, the idea is consensus rather than contrarian, and the competitive landscape includes players with literally a thousand times more capital. In the world where AI makes software cheaper and more commoditized, building a software tool for AI teams is swimming directly into the current.
