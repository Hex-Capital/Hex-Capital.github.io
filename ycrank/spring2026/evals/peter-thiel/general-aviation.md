# General Aviation -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Ben Frank is the right operator in the right sector pointed at the wrong layer of the stack ? General Aviation is an MIT aero engineer, pilot, and mechanic with three aviation companies behind him pitching satellite-native ATC in a regulated sector I have spent two decades arguing VCs systematically misprice, and yet General Aviation does not own a constellation, does not own spectrum, does not own a certified avionics stack, and does not name a multi-year architectural commitment that would prevent Starlink or Iridium from building the same application themselves. The signature is sector-correct, founder-correct, plan-empty. That is a different failure mode from the "AI for X" pass, and it deserves a real read rather than reflexive dismissal.

The contrarian-secret ladder runs from **Anduril** (2017) at the top ? Luckey naming Lockheed and Raytheon's cost-plus structure as the specific reason prime contractors cannot ship software-defined defense ? down through **Lyft** (2010), where the secret was duopoly stability rather than a hidden truth, down to the generic "AI for X" pitch where the secret terminates at "better model." General Aviation sits below the **Anduril** rung and above **Lyft**: the dossier names Verizon's $2.4B FENS contract, Peraton's 52-vendor integrator role, and Aireon's surveillance-only Iridium architecture as specific incumbent failure modes, which is more than most pre-seed dossiers offer. But General Aviation's secret is no longer hidden ? DOT publicly announced an ATC overhaul in May 2025, FAA is already trialing Starlink at 17 sites per avitrader.com, and Iridium just paid $366.7M to consolidate Aireon. When the Wall Street Journal and the Secretary of Transportation agree with you, you do not have a secret; you have a thesis the market has already priced.

The atoms-heavy / regulated-sector ladder is where General Aviation places highest ? above **Asana** (2010), which I funded on Moskovitz lineage despite zero structural moat, and approaching **Anduril** (2017), where the hardware capex and FAA-equivalent certification depth created the integration trust I weight heavily. General Aviation is unambiguously in the right sector: FAA-certified ATC is exactly the kind of regulated, sovereign-customer category Silicon Valley has refused to touch for fifteen years, and RTCA/DO standards plus TSO approvals are exactly the credential moats invisible to leverage-only analysis. The pin, though, is that **SpaceX** (2008) earned its rung by building the rockets. General Aviation has not committed to building the constellation, the avionics, or the certified comm channel ? the dossier says "satellite-based ATC" without naming whose satellites. That is sector-right and layer-wrong. The moat lives one level below where General Aviation has chosen to operate.

Monopoly trajectory ranks below sector. **Palantir** (2003) sits at the top of this ladder for the same reason General Aviation cannot yet claim the rung: Karp's team built integration depth into intelligence-grade workflows that took fifteen years to defend. General Aviation faces Aireon's existing NAV CANADA contract (~$20M/year recurring per aireon.com), Verizon's incumbent FENS position, and Peraton's prime-integrator chair ? three different vectors of incumbent capture and zero disclosed pricing-power hypothesis. The category will produce a monopoly; the dossier does not establish that General Aviation will be it.

Definite-plan is General Aviation's lowest dimension and the one that worries me most. **SpaceX** (2008) is the high rung because Musk named reusability as a fifteen-year commitment he would not pivot from; the low rung is the "iterate to product-market fit" framing Zero to One was written against. General Aviation publishes a homepage claim of "50% lower ATC costs" and a sweep across "aircraft, drones, rockets, and ground vehicles" with no disclosed roadmap, no certification timeline, no constellation partner, and a team size of zero with hiring set to false. That is not a definite plan ? it is a thesis statement. Founder-lineage saves some ground here: Ben Frank is closer to the **Anduril** / Luckey mid-rung than the first-time-founder low rung, with Rotabull, Seer Aerospace (acquired by Aviation Week per the YC page, though Tracxn flags deadpool ? a Straussian read worth holding), and Rotor Technologies' CCO seat where he spoke with 200 helicopter operators. The pedigree is real; the team behind him is not yet visible.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation does not win an FAA prime contract, pivots from infrastructure to an application/data-services layer atop Starlink or Iridium, raises a $4-8M seed in the next eighteen months on the strength of one signed FAA cooperative research agreement or a NAV CANADA pilot, and exits via acquihire to Iridium, Aireon, or a Peraton-adjacent integrator at a sub-$50M outcome ? not a fund-returner. The mechanism is exactly the high/low pairing: right sector, no architectural commitment, so the value accrues to whoever owns the layer below.

The strongest bull case ? and I want to steel-man this ? is the **Anduril** override: Luckey looked subscale and politically polarizing in 2017, and the secret was that the regulated customer would reward a software-defined entrant precisely because the primes structurally could not ship. General Aviation could be that bet if Ben Frank's pilot-plus-mechanic-plus-aerospace-engineer-plus-B2B-aviation-operator profile is the unusual credentialed-outsider signal I reward, and if the DOT May 2025 mandate creates a five-year procurement window Peraton's 52-vendor integration cannot fill. The bear answer is that **Anduril** built the hardware ? Lattice runs on Anduril-owned sensors and Anduril-owned compute. General Aviation does not own the constellation, and SpaceX and Iridium are not Lockheed: they will ship the application themselves at startup velocity, because that is how they got here. The bull case fails on layer choice, not on founder. I would flip to invest on one verifiable condition: a signed FAA cooperative research agreement or a named ANSP pilot (NAV CANADA, NATS, ENAV) within twelve months ? evidence that General Aviation has converted Ben Frank's aviation operator network into the regulated-customer integration depth that is the actual moat.
