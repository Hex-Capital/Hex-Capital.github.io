# 9 Mothers -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 9 Mothers is that counter-drone is one of the most consensus defense categories in venture capital right now. The U.S. Army just handed Anduril a $20 billion framework contract for C-UAS in March 2026. Epirus has raised over $550 million. DroneShield is a public company doing A$216 million in revenue, up 276% year-over-year. Every defense-focused fund and their LP is hunting for counter-drone exposure. When I say I'm looking for a sane person with an insane idea, the insane part means the idea provokes genuine skepticism -- investors should be confused or dismissive. Nobody is confused by "we need to shoot down cheap drones." That thesis is on the front page of every defense publication and validated by a hot war in Europe. The opportunity is real, but the returns from identifying it at seed have already been arbitraged by the time it's this legible.

The founder configuration compounds the problem. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA, a no-code testing platform, over a decade. That's legitimate operating experience -- a second-time YC founder who raised $42.3 million and shipped a real product. Roman Khomenko spent eight years doing AI and ML at that same company. But the "sane" in my framework requires domain-specific credibility for the particular idea. When Anatoly Yakovenko brought Qualcomm systems engineering to building Solana, the expertise mapped directly -- clock synchronization and hardware-level performance optimization were the actual technical insight. Here, the skill transfer from QA automation to autonomous weapon systems is indirect at best. Building defense hardware that passes ITAR, survives contested electromagnetic environments, and navigates DoD procurement is a fundamentally different operating domain than building a SaaS testing tool. The CTO's Ukrainian background and the COO's security consulting pedigree at Leviathan add texture, but I don't see the deep defense industry relationships or weapons systems engineering experience that typically precede success in this space.

Now let me steelman this, because the physical-world angle genuinely activates my framework. This is the kind of business I keep telling people I want to fund -- hardware with operational complexity that software cannot replicate, where AI is the cherry on top rather than the whole sundae. EDDA's acoustic sensing approach, the ITAR regulatory barriers, the hardware integration challenge, the need for physical deployment and field maintenance -- none of this gets commoditized by a better language model. If anything, cheaper AI inference makes the onboard detection better while the physical moat stays intact. That's exactly my Craftwork thesis applied to defense. And the government procurement cycle, which everyone identifies as a risk, is actually a moat once you're through it -- certified vendors in defense enjoy lock-in that would make any SaaS company weep. If 9 Mothers can get EDDA deployed, tested, and certified before the primes decide this form factor matters, they could own a niche that's structurally difficult to dislodge.

But that bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the founders can learn defense procurement faster than Anduril can scale down to portable form factors, that the acoustic sensing approach creates genuine technical differentiation rather than a replicable feature, and that a pre-seed startup's runway can survive 12-to-36-month government procurement cycles. Anduril has Lattice OS, $4 billion in new funding, and existing DoD relationships across every service branch. DroneShield already operates in 70+ countries. The incumbents aren't sleeping on portable C-UAS -- they're actively building it with billions in capital and established distribution. In defense, government relationships and contract vehicles ARE the distribution moat, and 9 Mothers has no public evidence of either. This isn't a market where you can move fast and break things -- it's a market where certification timelines and procurement officers determine your trajectory regardless of how good your prototype demos.

The primitive-versus-application test seals it for me. EDDA is a product -- a specific weapon system for a specific threat class. It's not a new layer that other defense systems build on. It doesn't create compounding network effects or winner-take-most dynamics. The best outcome I can construct is acquisition by a defense prime that wants the acoustic sensing capability or the portable form factor. That's a fine outcome, but it's a bounded outcome. There's no infinity story here where 9 Mothers becomes the foundational infrastructure for autonomous defense -- Anduril's Lattice is already making that play at a scale 9 Mothers can't match. The narrative expands from "counter-drone" to "autonomous defense" in theory, but in practice, that expansion requires resources and relationships this team hasn't demonstrated.

I respect the urgency of the problem and I believe low-cost tactical C-UAS will be a massive category. But a consensus idea pursued by founders pivoting from an adjacent domain into a brutally competitive market dominated by well-funded incumbents with established government relationships -- that's not my kind of bet. I'd rather fund the painting company.
