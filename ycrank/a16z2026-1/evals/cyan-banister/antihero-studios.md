# Antihero Studios -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Antihero Studios has assembled perhaps the most credentialed founding team I've seen at this stage in gaming — Frank Yan led the Brawl Stars revenue recovery that drove 8.8x growth, Brice Laville Saint-Martin was the Art Director behind Clash Royale's visual identity, and Andre Parodi spent a decade building King's technical infrastructure underneath Candy Crush. Eight billion-dollar-per-year games across their collective resumes. That's not a pitch deck claim — those are verifiable product credits at the highest-grossing mobile franchises ever made. And yet, when I ask my first question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I hear back is career progression. Senior roles at Supercell, Blizzard, King. A market gap identified. A team assembled to fill it. That's smart entrepreneurship, but it's not a biographical forge. Nobody's life broke open in a way that made mobile extraction shooters an inevitability. The problem didn't find these founders; they found the problem through industry expertise. I respect the craft, but I can't score what I don't see.

The HQ Trivia comparison haunts me here, and I think Antihero Studios needs to hear why. Colin Kroll had built Vine — world-class product credentials. The early engagement metrics were electric. The cultural moment was real. And I invested, breaking my own rules, because the product was so clearly right for the moment. Antihero Studios' 70,000 pre-alpha players averaging 50 minutes of daily playtime across three sessions — with eliminated players 6% more likely to restart than winners — those are genuinely compelling engagement signals. But I've learned that consumer entertainment engagement at pre-alpha, with self-selected enthusiasts, is not the same as mass-market retention. HQ Trivia taught me that attention-dependent products without infrastructure moats are the most seductive and the most dangerous bets in my portfolio. The dossier itself states plainly: "The top-down extraction shooter format on mobile is technically reproducible. Any well-funded studio with multiplayer netcode expertise could build a similar product." That sentence alone would give me pause.

Where's the invisible system? I look for opaque structures that extract value from people who don't understand the extraction — taxi medallion cartels, credit card fee opacity, property tax bureaucracies. Mobile gaming has predatory monetization practices, and Frank Yan's GDC talk on removing loot boxes from Brawl Stars suggests he understands that extraction. Antihero Studios' "no pay-to-win, cosmetic-only" pledge is a nod toward fairness. But rejecting exploitative monetization is a product philosophy, not a structural disruption. Antihero Studios isn't making an invisible system visible — it's making a better game within the existing system. The App Store still takes 30%. The creator economy still operates on the same attention dynamics. The market structure remains intact.

The contrarian case is weak by my standards. Mobile gaming attracted $121-165 billion in 2025. a16z co-led the $4.5M seed. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors squirm — it's a space they're actively competing to fund. The "contrarian" angle — that extraction shooters haven't cracked mass-market mobile yet — could mean opportunity or could mean the genre doesn't translate. Arena Breakout generates $50.5M with 95% from China's hardcore audience, which suggests the casual translation is genuinely unproven, not merely untried. And Tencent owns both Arena Breakout and Supercell. If Antihero Studios proves the concept, Tencent can redirect resources from either franchise with a speed and distribution advantage that no 13-person Barcelona studio can match.

The strongest bull case: these founders don't just understand games — they understand live-service games at the billion-dollar scale. Frank Yan rescued Brawl Stars from revenue decline. That's not abstract market knowledge; that's the specific operational skill required to run a free-to-play title through its lifecycle. The 750-creator program generating 250 content pieces during pre-alpha, anchored by KairosTime (a major Supercell community creator), is a genuine early distribution moat. The IDFA disruption creating an opening for organic-first studios is a real timing thesis. And the "A24 for games" brand strategy — if it works — would be a compounding distribution asset across future titles. If Antihero Studios ships MISFITZ, retains a mass audience, and proves the organic growth model works at scale, the second and third games benefit from a brand flywheel that incumbents can't easily replicate. That's a real bull case, and I don't dismiss it.

But my framework asks different questions than the bull case answers. I don't evaluate whether the founders are talented — they clearly are. I evaluate whether their biography made them incapable of working on anything else. I don't evaluate whether the market is large — mobile gaming obviously is. I evaluate whether there's a hidden structure being exposed. I don't evaluate whether the product is good — 50-minute daily sessions suggest it might be. I evaluate whether the company is building infrastructure that compounds. Antihero Studios is a talented team making a well-designed game in a massive, well-understood, consensus-funded market. The decisive dimensions are the biographical forge (strong credentials without biographical inevitability) and the absence of an invisible system to disrupt. This is not a Long Journey bet.
