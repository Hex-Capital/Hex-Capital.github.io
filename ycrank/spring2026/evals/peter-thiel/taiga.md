# Taiga -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Candid Health has raised $99.5M with approximately 250% revenue growth in 2024. AKASA has raised $205M targeting enterprise health systems. LunaBill, from YC's own Fall 2025 batch, already reports $764K in contracted ARR. Medbill AI has $16.3M. Taiga enters this market as a two-person pre-seed team with no disclosed revenue, no disclosed customers, and no disclosed traction of any kind. I do not need to proceed much further. When I can count four well-funded competitors pursuing the identical thesis -- "AI can now parse clinical notes into billing codes" -- I am not looking at a company built around a secret. I am looking at a company swept up in a trend. The dossier's own "why now" section states the catalyst plainly: "maturation of large language models capable of parsing unstructured clinical notes." Everyone knows this. Every healthcare AI investor knows this. The existence of a competitive landscape slide comparing five similar startups is itself the diagnosis. Taiga has no secret because "AI will automate medical billing" is the consensus view of every participant in this market.

Taiga's claimed differentiation -- positioning as a "full-service agency" rather than a SaaS platform -- is a go-to-market choice, not a monopoly-creating insight. An agency model versus a software model is a decision about where to draw the service boundary, not a contrarian truth about how the world works. Candid Health chose SaaS; AKASA chose enterprise automation; LunaBill chose voice agents for insurance calls. Taiga chose full-stack agency. These are tactical variations on the same consensus thesis, each fighting for share in a market that everyone agrees is ripe for AI disruption. Recall Wish: a company operating in a massive market ($14B IPO valuation) with no structural monopoly advantage, competing on execution against Amazon and Alibaba. Taiga's $163B TAM figure is precisely the kind of enormous addressable market statistic that signals competitive thinking rather than monopoly thinking. Where is the small, specific beachhead that Taiga can dominate completely before expanding? "Small and independent practices across the U.S." is not a beachhead -- it is 200,000+ fragmented customers with no network effects connecting them.

Nanda Guntupalli (Penn CS) and Adam Wax (Lehigh CS and Mathematics) bring technical credentials and a genuine personal connection to the problem -- both founders' parents are physicians who own independent practices. The co-founder relationship since third grade is the single strongest signal in this dossier; founding teams with deep shared prehistory survive stress that destroys recently assembled teams. But personal motivation and co-founder alignment are necessary conditions, not sufficient ones. Neither founder has prior healthcare industry work experience, prior startup founding experience, or a prior exit. Adam Wax's most recent professional experience includes a Goldman Sachs summit acceptance and a field engineering internship with Weeks Marine. These are the credentials of talented students beginning their careers, not founders who have made irreversible commitments to a definite vision for transforming revenue cycle management. I see no evidence of a bold, specific multi-year plan -- no articulated theory of how Taiga becomes the last mover in medical billing, no irreversible technical or organizational bets that would only make sense if their vision is correct.

The bull case deserves explicit construction. An advocate would argue: incumbents like Waystar and R1 RCM structurally cannot serve small practices profitably -- their cost structures require large-contract, high-touch delivery. Taiga's AI-native approach could achieve unit economics impossible for legacy players, creating a monopoly in the underserved small-practice segment through structural cost advantage. The seven EHR integrations (Athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, AdvancedMD, Tebra, Elation, Epic) create switching costs that compound with each integration. The data flywheel from processing claims across multiple payers and specialties could build a denial-prediction engine that improves with volume, eventually creating a proprietary intelligence layer no new entrant could replicate. And the agency model -- handling everything from clinical notes to payment collection -- creates deeper lock-in than any point solution. I check my Known Exceptions: Lyft violated "competition is for losers" because the ride-sharing category was generating such enormous total value that even the number-two player would produce significant returns. Does the RCM market qualify? No. RCM is a service market, not a platform market with winner-take-all dynamics. There is no network effect connecting one practice's billing to another's. Taiga's theoretical data advantage is buildable by any of its four funded competitors processing the same payer rules and denial patterns. The exception does not apply.

Taiga's core technology -- LLM-based medical coding, claims scrubbing against payer rules, automated appeal generation -- runs entirely on foundation models accessible to any competitor with API access. The dossier states this directly: "LLM-based medical coding and claims scrubbing are technically reproducible." There is no physical-world barrier, no hardware component, no regulatory moat beyond baseline HIPAA compliance that every participant must meet. A well-funded competitor -- and Candid Health with $99.5M qualifies -- could rebuild Taiga's capabilities and outspend it on distribution simultaneously. This is the pure software commodity pattern I have warned about since the Founders Fund manifesto: wrapping someone else's model is the most exposed competitive position in technology.

The one verifiable condition that would change my assessment: if Taiga demonstrated that its denial-prediction engine achieves measurably superior claim acceptance rates -- say, a 15-20 percentage point improvement over the industry baseline -- documented with specific payer data across at least three specialties, that would constitute evidence of a genuine proprietary insight about payer behavior rather than generic LLM application. That would be a secret worth investigating. Until then, Taiga is a consensus bet in a crowded market with no structural path to monopoly. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret (no contrarian insight, four funded competitors sharing the same thesis) and Physical-World Technology Moat (pure software layer on commodity foundation models). Both score near the bottom of their ranges, and together they define a company I cannot invest in.
