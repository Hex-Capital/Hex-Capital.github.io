# Adialante -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Efraín Torres invented new physics. Not applied existing research to a new domain, not combined two known techniques in a novel way — he created a fundamentally new method of encoding magnetic resonance signals during his PhD at the University of Minnesota, published it in *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine* in 2022, and is now building a company around it. The FREE technique eliminates gradient coils entirely from MRI machines, producing a system that's one-third less hardware and one-tenth the weight (Fogarty Innovation, Aug 2024). When I think about what separates founders who discover problems organically from those who brainstorm them in a conference room, Adialante sits firmly on the right side of that line. Torres didn't read a market report about MRI access gaps and decide to start a company. He spent years deep in MR physics, saw something no one else had seen, and realized it could make MRI available to the 90% of the global population that currently can't access it (MNSBIR, Jul 2025). That's the genuine version of "live in the future, then build what's missing."

Torres grew up first-generation in Little Village on Chicago's south side, got himself to a PhD at Minnesota under Michael Garwood — one of the leading MRI researchers in the world — and built a team of five PhDs to pursue this (Adialante website; UMN CTSI, Mar 2024). Parker Jenkins, his co-founder and COO, met Torres on literally the first day of college at Marquette; they've been collaborating through undergrad and graduate school (Fogarty Innovation, Aug 2024). That's the kind of co-founder relationship where the hard conversations have already happened — they've had a decade to learn each other's failure modes. Adialante's team depth is unusual for pre-seed: Sai Abitha Srinivas (Rabi Award winner, PhD from Vanderbilt), Michael Mullen (Physics PhD, UMN), Daniel Pizetta (Computational Physics PhD, São Paulo). You don't assemble a team like that with a pitch deck. These people joined because the science is real and the mission compels them.

Adialante is building in a domain that triggers every instinct most founders and investors have evolved to avoid. Hardware. Medical devices. FDA clearance with no predicate device — potentially requiring De Novo authorization rather than a 510(k). Mobile fleet logistics. Clinical validation studies. Reimbursement negotiations. Each of these alone makes smart people run away; combined, they create the kind of schlep barrier that functions as a natural moat. Hyperfine spent over $200M and five-plus years to get FDA clearance for their portable brain-only MRI (Hyperfine investor relations, 2025). Promaxo has raised $72-81M for their in-office prostate MRI (PitchBook; Tracxn). Adialante has raised roughly $3M including a $1.18M NSF SBIR Phase II grant (MNSBIR, Jul 2025). The capital gap is real and potentially fatal. But this is exactly the Stripe pattern: Patrick and John Collison were willing to wade into PCI compliance and bank integrations — the regulatory plumbing that repelled every other developer — and that willingness *was* the competitive advantage. Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model, charging a flat per-scan fee with no capital expenditure required by the clinic, adds an operational schlep on top of the hardware and regulatory schleps. Most founders can't even see this idea. The ones who can and still pursue it are either delusional or right, and the published peer-reviewed physics tips toward the latter.

The bear case is serious and I won't pretend otherwise. Adialante has no revenue, no FDA submission, no clinical trial data comparing FREE-based imaging to conventional MRI for prostate cancer screening (Key Risks section). The $3.5M seed target (UMN CTSI, Mar 2024) won't get them to commercialization — they'll need $20-50M more before generating meaningful revenue, which means dilution risk and execution dependency on fundraising. Four years from founding (2022) to YC batch (Spring 2026) without a commercial product is within normal range for medical devices but leaves open the question of whether this is a company or a research project. And the competitors aren't asleep: Promaxo already builds compact prostate MRI systems, and Ezra (acquired by Function Health for its AI layer on existing MRI) is approaching the access problem from the software side. The strongest version of this objection is that Adialante's technical moat doesn't matter if they run out of money before FDA clearance — and at $3M raised against a multi-year regulatory timeline, that's a plausible outcome.

But I keep returning to the Boom Supersonic pattern. Boom was pre-revenue, capital-intensive, regulatory-dependent, and building hardware most investors considered insane to attempt. I made it my largest startup investment ever because the founder's conviction was genuine, the ambition was "frighteningly ambitious" in the way I describe in my essays, and the technical vision was specific enough to be credible. Adialante shares this DNA. Torres didn't choose this problem from a menu of attractive markets; he invented the physics that makes it solvable. The company name — "Adialante," Spanish for "forward" — connects to Torres' cultural identity in a way that signals missionary motivation, not mercenary calculation. When I evaluate earnestness, I look for founders who would be working on this problem even if no one gave them money, and Torres' decade-long arc from Little Village to publishing novel MR physics to YC reads that way.

What I lack — and what keeps Adialante from scoring higher — is the specific startup-style resourcefulness data I use as my primary signal. I don't have Adialante's version of the cereal box story. Torres and Jenkins have demonstrated extraordinary intellectual resourcefulness in inventing and developing FREE, and genuine institutional resourcefulness in winning NSF SBIR Phase II funding ($1.18M) and getting into both Fogarty Innovation and YC. But I haven't seen them do the scrappy, unscalable, slightly-crazy things that separate founders who will make a company work from founders who have built impressive technology. The learning rate evidence is similarly thin — the progression from SBIR Phase I to Phase II to Fogarty to YC shows forward motion, but I can't see the weekly growth curve or the rapid iteration cycles because there's no product in market yet. The condition that would flip me from Invest to Pass: if I learned that Torres and Jenkins are waiting for FDA clearance before pursuing any commercial engagement — no LOIs, no pilot partnerships, no paid feasibility studies with urology clinics. The condition that would push me toward Strong Invest: if they showed me a signed LOI from even one urology practice, or evidence of diagnostic-quality images from a working prototype, or a creative pre-clearance revenue path that proves they're building a company, not a lab.

Adialante earns an Invest verdict driven primarily by two dimensions: organic problem discovery — Torres invented the science, which is the deepest possible form of authentic problem engagement — and the idea's natural repulsion, which creates a schlep-blindness moat that competitors need $100M+ and half a decade to overcome. The earnest builder identity is strong and genuine. The founder resourcefulness and learning rate scores are limited by available evidence rather than negative signals, and I weight the difference: absence of data is not the same as absence of the quality.
