# Booko -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask about any company is: what is the secret? What does this founder know about the world that most intelligent people would actively dispute? With Booko, I cannot find one. Dynamic pricing is not a secret -- it is a technique that American Airlines pioneered in the 1980s, that every hotel chain adopted in the 1990s, and that Uber made a household concept with surge pricing. The proposition that fitness studios and medspas should price their time slots dynamically is not contrarian; it is obvious. Mindbody already offers it. The only claim Booko makes is that it can deliver the same capability at a lower take rate than Mindbody's 20% fee. Undercutting an incumbent's pricing is a competitive strategy, not a secret. It is the definition of what I mean when I say competition destroys value -- Booko is positioning itself to fight over margin in a category the incumbent has already defined.

The deeper structural problem is that Booko is a textbook "1 to n" company. Dynamic pricing exists. Machine learning demand prediction exists. API integrations with booking platforms exist. Booko applies known techniques to a new vertical. This is the kind of work that consulting firms do -- take a solution from one industry and adapt it to another. There is nothing here that makes the previous approach structurally obsolete. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "The core technology -- demand prediction for time slots using historical booking data -- is not novel at an algorithmic level. Any ML team with access to booking data could build a similar model." When your own competitive analysis concedes that the technology is replicable, you have answered the monopoly question. There is no proprietary technology moat, no network effect (the company explicitly does not share data across clients), no economies of scale in a per-client model architecture, and no brand power. Zero of the four monopoly characteristics are present.

The bull case deserves examination, because the Mindbody dynamic is genuinely interesting as a business opportunity -- even if it fails my investment criteria. Mindbody earns 20% plus 3.5% on every dynamically priced booking, which creates a structural conflict: lowering that fee to compete with Booko would cannibalize a growing revenue stream. This is the classic innovator's dilemma applied to a feature. If Booko can establish itself as the default pricing layer across multiple booking platforms -- Mindbody, Mariana Tek, Zenoti, custom systems -- before any incumbent adjusts, it could build a meaningful SaaS business. The claimed 20% revenue uplift and fill-rate improvement from 67% to 89% represent a compelling ROI for individual merchants. In the best case, Booko becomes the "Beyond Pricing" of appointment-based businesses, a profitable vertical SaaS company serving tens of thousands of locations. But "profitable vertical SaaS" is not what I invest in. The power-law math does not work. A pricing optimization layer for yoga studios and medspas, even at scale, does not have a path to returning the fund. The ceiling is an acquisition by Mindbody or Zenoti for a modest multiple -- and a company built to be acquired is a company that never achieved monopoly.

The founders are essentially invisible. Neither has a documented background in dynamic pricing, machine learning, booking platforms, or the fitness and wellness industry. The product works -- SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance require genuine engineering effort, and the API integrations suggest technical competence -- but I cannot assess conviction, vision, or intellectual depth when I cannot see the founders at all. At the Facebook investment, Zuckerberg was a teenager nobody had heard of, but within an hour of conversation his vision was unmistakable. Booko's founders may possess extraordinary conviction, but nothing in the public record reveals it, and the company's positioning as "dynamic pricing but cheaper than Mindbody" suggests tactical rather than visionary thinking. The absence of a definite vision for how this transforms the world -- rather than marginally improves a yoga studio's Tuesday 5pm class -- is precisely what separates a competent startup from a company I would fund.

The beachhead strategy is the one dimension where Booko shows reasonable instincts. Starting with fitness and wellness, where Mindbody and Mariana Tek concentrate the market, and expanding concentrically to medspas, salons, tutoring, and eventually all service businesses with perishable inventory -- this is the right tactical shape. But the correct market-entry strategy cannot compensate for the absence of a secret, the absence of a technology discontinuity, and the absence of any structural path to monopoly. Many companies execute the playbook correctly and still end up as features rather than platforms. When the incumbent can replicate your offering by adjusting a fee schedule, you are not building a monopoly -- you are renting a temporary arbitrage.
