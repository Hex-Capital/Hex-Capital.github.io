# Cascade -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that hits me about Cascade is that everyone wants to be in this market. AI agent observability and safety is one of the most actively pursued categories in early-stage venture right now — Braintrust just raised $80M at an $800M valuation, Raindrop pulled in $15M from Lightspeed with what sounds like nearly the same technical approach (training small custom models per customer to monitor agent behavior), Arize has $131M in the bank, and ClickHouse acquired Langfuse, Snyk grabbed Invariant Labs, and Anthropic acqui-hired HumanLoop. When I see that kind of activity — every serious investor and infrastructure player converging on the same thesis — my instinct is to walk the other direction. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Cascade is in a category that is popular and, at this point, not that difficult to assess. Everyone can see that production AI agents need monitoring. The question is whether a two-person pre-seed team with no visible product can outrun competitors with 50-100x more capital in a space that's consolidating through M&A before the category is even mature.

Here's what I'd normally do: I'd sit down with Adam and Haluk and ask them to tell me their stories — not the pitch, not the BAIR Lab credentials, just how they got here. And the dossier gives me nothing to work with on that front. Adam is a UC Berkeley CS grad who did research at BAIR on graph reasoning and agentic safety, consulted for NASA, built embedded systems for medical devices. Haluk studied CS and Math at Berkeley, built production monitoring infrastructure at Netflix and Amazon, researched failure mode taxonomies at BAIR. These are genuinely impressive technical backgrounds, and the combination is relevant — one person who understands agent safety research paired with someone who's built production monitoring at scale. But where's the biographical thread that connects them to this problem at the level of identity? I'm not hearing about a personal crisis caused by an AI agent failure, or years of frustration watching systems break in ways nobody understood, or some formative experience that made this problem feel like a calling rather than an opportunity. It reads like two talented researchers who correctly identified that AI agent safety is the next big market. And they may be right about the market. But "correctly identifying the next big market" is consensus investing, not conviction investing.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. The founder-market fit on technical credentials alone is strong — BAIR Lab research specifically in agentic safety and failure mode taxonomies, combined with production-grade monitoring experience at Netflix and Amazon, is a rare and genuinely relevant pairing. The custom evaluator model approach, if executed well, could create meaningful switching costs as models learn company-specific behavioral norms. The market timing is undeniable — agents are entering production now, and the reliability gap is real. If Cascade can ship something exceptional in the next six months and land a few meaningful enterprise customers through the YC network, they could establish a data flywheel that makes them an attractive acquisition target even in a consolidating market. And sometimes being small and focused in a crowded space is an advantage — you can move faster and build deeper on one wedge while the well-funded players try to be everything. If I believed the founders had the kind of bone-deep commitment that makes someone fight through a market where Braintrust has $80M and Datadog is extending its platform, I might see a path.

But I don't have that signal. What I have is a two-person team in the hottest category in infrastructure, with no visible product, no public customers, a website that didn't render content at the time of research, and a company name that competes with a dozen other products called "Cascade." There's no economic access story — this is enterprise tooling for engineering teams at companies wealthy enough to deploy AI agents at scale. It doesn't make an invisible system visible for people who need it; it makes agent failures visible for teams that could probably build internal tooling to do the same thing. And when Raindrop — with $15M from Lightspeed and functionally identical positioning around custom models trained on production data — is already in market, the window for a differentiated entry is narrowing, not opening. I need founders who've been told "no" fifty times and kept building because they couldn't not build it. Here, the market is saying "yes" to everyone in the category, which is exactly the signal that tells me the consensus has already formed and the value has been arbitraged away.
