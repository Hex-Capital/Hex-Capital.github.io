# FullSeam -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's the irony: I backed this team before. TaxProper raised from Khosla Ventures, and Tom Dowling and Geoff Segal built it into a real company — $1 billion in annual property tax payments, 115,000 properties, acquired by Opendoor. That was a good bet. So when I see these same founders come back with their next company, my instinct is to lean in. But then I ask my first question — if FullSeam succeeds completely, what changes? — and the answer stops me cold. Finance and accounting teams at growing companies process their invoices 3x faster. Some AP clerks get redeployed. CFOs save on headcount. That's a real business. It is not a transformation of anything. The consequence of success is a SaaS company doing $50-100M in revenue in a $9.5 billion market that already has Stampli ($148M raised), Vic.ai ($52M raised), and Auditoria.ai ($60.5M raised) fighting over the same back-office workflows. These founders are too talented to spend their next decade making accounts payable 30% more efficient.

What concerns me more than the market size is the pattern I see. These founders experienced accounting pain at TaxProper as it scaled — "drowning in invoicing, reconciliations, customer questions." So they built the obvious solution. This is the classic trap of domain expertise: you see a problem you personally suffered, and you build the thing that would have solved it for you. But that's not how transformational companies start. That's how incremental companies start. The founders are insiders executing the insider's playbook. Pat Brown didn't start Impossible Foods because he had personally struggled to find good plant-based burgers — he started it because animal agriculture was destroying the planet. Pradeep Sindhu didn't start Juniper because he was frustrated with his office router — he saw that TCP/IP would restructure global telecommunications. The founding impulse matters because it determines the ceiling of ambition. When the impulse is "our accounting team was overwhelmed," the ceiling is a better accounting tool.

The technology layer confirms this reading. The dossier states it plainly: "The core capabilities (invoice OCR, GL coding, payment matching) are technically reproducible using commercially available LLMs and standard API integrations." This is wrapper technology. The "AI employee" framing is marketing — underneath, it's API integrations to nine platforms plus LLM-driven document processing. There is no technology here that makes the incumbent approach structurally obsolete. QuickBooks, Xero, and NetSuite are all actively shipping native AI features. When Intuit decides to make invoice processing autonomous inside QuickBooks, FullSeam doesn't just lose a competitor — it loses its integration surface. The middleware layer between accounting platforms is exactly where you don't want to build when the platforms themselves are adding intelligence.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. These are proven founders — second YC batch, prior exit, team that's worked together for six years through a build and an acquisition. The timing on agentic AI for financial workflows is genuinely favorable — LLMs just crossed the reliability threshold for structured data extraction from messy financial documents, and the multi-system agent paradigm is maturing. If FullSeam can capture the SMB and mid-market segment before Auditoria.ai moves down from enterprise, the breadth play (AP + AR + cash application + reconciliation in one platform) could create real switching costs. And I know from backing them at TaxProper that this team ships product and closes customers. If you believe the AP/AR automation market grows to $13-14 billion by 2031, capturing even 2-3% of that is a meaningful outcome. For most investors, this is a reasonable bet on a strong team in a growing market. But I am not most investors. A reasonable bet on a growing market is precisely the kind of investment where you've reduced your probability of failure until the consequences of success no longer matter.

The competitive dynamics seal it. FullSeam has $500K against $250M+ in aggregate competitor funding. Auditoria.ai — the closest comparable — just raised a $60.5M Series B in February 2025 and is processing $2.4 billion in collections and $9 billion in invoicing annually. That's not a hypothetical competitor; that's a company with 120x FullSeam's capital doing the same thing at scale. Capital advantages compound in enterprise SaaS — more integrations, more customers, more training data, more sales reps. The founders would need to find a wedge so sharp that capital can't blunt it, and I don't see that wedge in the dossier. Breadth of coverage across AP and AR is a feature roadmap, not a moat.

I respect Tom, Aaron, and Geoff. I'd back them again — on a problem whose consequence of success is worth their ambition. This isn't that problem.
