# Protent -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Protent: the AI IS the product. Strip away the machine learning layer and you have... a camera feed. There's no business underneath the software. When I run my "cherry on top" test — would this company be valuable if AI didn't exist? — the answer is unambiguously no. That's the single most disqualifying signal in my framework right now, because every major camera hardware company, every cloud provider, and every well-funded video analytics startup is racing to build exactly this intelligence layer. Verkada has $700M in funding and $1B in annualized bookings. Lumana just raised $64M building vision-language models for the same basic value proposition. Protent is bringing a $500K pre-seed budget to a knife fight where the other combatants are armed with heavy artillery. The SaaS tag is literally on this company's YC page, and I've been saying for two years that SaaS moats are dissolving faster than founders realize.

The sane-person-insane-idea calibration doesn't fire here. The founders have relevant technical DNA — Srihan Balaji deploying RL-optimized video intelligence at Lockheed Martin is the right kind of sane, and Abhisheik Sharma's NLP research maps cleanly to the sentiment-analysis component. But the idea itself isn't insane. AI-powered surveillance is a $5 billion market growing at 22% CAGR. Dozens of companies are already operating in this space. Choosing law enforcement as the customer segment is controversial, sure — the YC LinkedIn post drew predictable dystopian commentary — but controversial isn't the same as non-consensus. There's no structural insight here that most investors think is crazy. It's more like "AI video analytics, but for cops," which is a go-to-market positioning choice within a consensus market, not a genuinely contrarian thesis. And the founders appear to be very early in their careers — Srihan attended Thomas Jefferson HS with a graduation year suggesting he's 22 or 23 — which means they lack the government sales networks and procurement experience that this particular go-to-market requires. Selling to municipal police departments through multi-year RFP cycles is a fundamentally different skill than building ML models.

The strongest bull case is the Palantir analogy, and I want to engage with it seriously because it's the version of this story that could produce a massive outcome. Palantir built an enduring franchise by being willing to serve government and intelligence customers that made other tech companies squeamish. If the political radioactivity of law enforcement surveillance genuinely thins the competitive field — if Verkada and Lumana stay in commercial security because their boards don't want the PR risk — then Protent could own a government vertical with deep switching costs built on procurement compliance (CJIS, FedRAMP), officer training, and dispatch system integration. The data flywheel from real escalation patterns in live law enforcement environments would be genuinely hard to replicate without similar access. And the deployments in Atlanta, Chicago, and St. Louis metro areas at pre-seed stage suggest these founders can actually get in the door. If that Palantir path materializes, the narrative expands from "video analytics for police" to "the real-time intelligence layer for all public safety" — which is an infinity story. The problem is that Palantir had Peter Thiel's network, Alex Karp's singular intensity, a team of Stanford cryptographers, and hundreds of millions in capital to survive the decade it took to reach escape velocity. The gap between three metro deployments and becoming government infrastructure is vast, and I don't see evidence that these founders have the relational depth or operational seasoning to navigate it.

What I keep coming back to is the structural moat question. Protent builds software that sits on top of other people's cameras. They don't own physical infrastructure. They don't control a protocol. They aren't creating a new primitive that other applications build on — they're an application on existing surveillance infrastructure. As AI models continue to improve and drop in cost, the video intelligence layer commoditizes. Genetec or Hikvision can integrate equivalent analytics into their hardware stack. Google or AWS can offer real-time video analysis as a cloud service. The escalation-detection models trained on law enforcement data are the most defensible component, but that defense requires achieving enough deployment scale to generate proprietary training data before better-capitalized competitors enter the same vertical — and the capital asymmetry here is brutal. This is the Birchbox lesson applied to a different domain: if the software IS the business and the barrier to replication is just "we got there first," the moat erodes exactly when you need it most.

I'm passing. The founders have genuine domain relevance and impressive early traction for pre-seed, but the structural logic works against them on every dimension I care about. Pure software moat in a hot market with massive incumbents, application-layer positioning with no path to becoming a primitive, and a consensus idea wrapped in controversy rather than genuine non-consensus insight. The company that wins law enforcement video intelligence will likely be whichever existing platform player with hundreds of millions in capital decides to pursue the government vertical — not a two-person pre-seed startup competing on ML capabilities that are commoditizing by the quarter.
