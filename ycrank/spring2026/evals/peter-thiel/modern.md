# Modern -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

ServiceNow paid $2.85 billion for Moveworks, and that acquisition is the single most important fact in Modern's dossier -- not because it validates the market, but because it reveals that the last mover has already moved. ServiceNow now owns both the dominant ITSM platform and the leading AI-native resolution layer. When the monopolist absorbs the challenger, the war is over; what remains is mopping up. Modern enters a market where the structural endgame has already been played. The bull case would argue that ServiceNow faces a cannibalization conflict -- its revenue model depends on ticket volume, so fully deploying autonomous resolution undermines its own pricing architecture. This is a real tension, and it mirrors the classic innovator's dilemma. But the dilemma buys Modern a window, not a moat. A window closes. And Automation Anywhere's parallel acquisition of Aisera for approximately $1.5 billion means that two separate incumbents have now internalized the AI-ITSM thesis. Modern is not exploiting an insight the market has missed; Modern is entering a thesis the market has priced at $4.35 billion in acquisition currency within a single year.

Modern's dossier reveals no secret -- no important truth that most informed observers would dispute. "AI agents can autonomously resolve IT, HR, and Finance tickets" is the purest consensus thesis in enterprise software today. The dossier itself acknowledges that Ravenna raised $15 million from Khosla and Madrona on the same thesis, Workativ offers a nearly identical feature set, and the core technology is "buildable by well-funded competitors." When I ask "what do very few people agree with Modern on?" the dossier provides no answer. Modern's claimed differentiation -- overlay deployment, multi-channel support, broader HR/Finance coverage, and "live in under a day" onboarding -- describes execution preferences, not a contrarian insight about how the world works. This is the cleantech pattern I warned about in Chapter 13: entrepreneurs swept into a category by social momentum rather than possessing a specific, non-obvious understanding of why they will win where others cannot.

Seb Poole's two prior exits -- a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in 2023 -- demonstrate that Modern's CEO can build and sell companies. The ~20 million AUD raised for his Hong Kong e-commerce venture shows fundraising capability. These are real credentials. But prior exits in adjacent B2B categories do not constitute the kind of irreversible commitment to a definite vision that distinguishes founders I back at the earliest stage. Modern's pricing page displays "Coming soon" in April 2026. The "Modern Managed" offering -- where the company operates the customer's IT service desk directly -- is the most revealing signal in the dossier: it is a services business embedded inside what claims to be an AI automation company. If Modern's AI agents truly resolve tickets autonomously, the managed-services tier is unnecessary. If it is necessary, the AI does not work well enough to stand alone. Either way, this hedging suggests a company discovering its value proposition rather than executing against a predetermined plan. Aleks Tomovski's engineering background across DevOps and SRE is competent but unremarkable; no evidence of a shared prehistory with Poole beyond a Melbourne geographic overlap was identified.

Modern's power-law arithmetic fails. The ITSM market is approximately $15 billion, growing to $32 billion by 2031 -- a meaningful market, but one where the ceiling has already been established by Moveworks' $2.85 billion acquisition price. Modern's realistic best case is acquisition by a remaining ITSM incumbent (Zendesk, BMC, or a platform player like Salesforce) at a fraction of the Moveworks price, since Moveworks had $150 million in funding, years of enterprise deployments, and hundreds of customers when it sold. A $500 million exit for Modern would be extraordinary success; a $5 billion independent company is structurally implausible given that the category leader already sold for less. This is not the shape of a fund-returner. Compare this to Palantir, which at its earliest stage was building software for an intelligence community with effectively unlimited budgets and no competing product -- the structural ceiling was invisible because the customer's willingness to pay scaled with national security needs. Modern faces a well-defined, well-priced market with well-resourced incumbents who have already made their AI bets.

Modern's distribution story has one genuinely interesting element: the claim that "most teams are live in under a day" inverts the enterprise ITSM sales cycle, which typically involves months of implementation and professional services. If true, this could compress the go-to-market timeline dramatically. The SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, and GDPR certifications for a two-person team suggest serious preparation for enterprise procurement. But 218 LinkedIn followers, no confirmed customers, no public revenue, no Product Hunt listing, and no press coverage as of April 2026 suggest that Modern has not yet found the organic pull that signals genuine market demand. When eBay sellers added PayPal logos to their auctions without being asked, that was the signal. Modern has no equivalent signal visible in this dossier -- only projected customer outcome metrics on a website. On the technology moat dimension, Modern is pure software wrapping foundation model capabilities with integration connectors -- the dossier explicitly states that the barrier is "execution speed, integration depth, and accumulated workflow data rather than proprietary technology." There are no atoms here, no physical-world manipulation, no multi-year replication barrier. A well-funded team could rebuild Modern's integration surface in months.

The strongest bull case for Modern rests on two pillars: the ServiceNow cannibalization conflict creates a structural window where the incumbent is incentivized to slow-walk autonomous resolution, and Poole's repeat-founder profile with two exits demonstrates the execution discipline to exploit that window. An advocate would argue that Modern's independence is the product advantage -- non-ServiceNow shops need an AI-ITSM vendor that is not captive to ServiceNow's roadmap. This is a real market dynamic. But it does not constitute a secret, because every investor and every ITSM buyer can see the same dynamic. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Modern has discovered a specific, non-obvious insight about how ticket resolution actually works -- perhaps a proprietary approach to workflow learning that produces 10x better resolution rates than Moveworks' approach, demonstrated with named enterprise customers generating measurable revenue. Without that, Modern is a competent team riding a consensus wave into a market where the monopolist has already consolidated.

Modern scores low on the two dimensions that matter most in my framework. The absence of a monopoly secret -- combined with five named competitors pursuing identical approaches and two multi-billion-dollar incumbent acquisitions validating the consensus thesis -- places Modern squarely in the competitive market I avoid. The power-law arithmetic, capped by the Moveworks acquisition price in a $15 billion market with no government-monopoly pathway, cannot produce a fund-returner outcome. Poole's execution history and Modern's compliance certifications prevent the score from collapsing further, but execution advantages in a competitive market produce modest outcomes, not monopolies.
