# Regbase -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy built regulatory research at Paul, Weiss -- one of the top law firms in the world -- and experienced the exact pain point they're now attacking: a cross-border regulatory tracking project across 50 countries that had no adequate tooling. That founder-market fit is genuine. But I've seen dozens of companies where the founders lived the problem and still got crushed because the market structure wasn't moving in their favor fast enough. So the question I need to answer for Regbase isn't "do they understand the problem?" -- it's "what structural shift is making an AI-native regulatory intelligence platform viable NOW in a way it wasn't two years ago, and does the market underestimate the window?"

The structural catalyst for Regbase is real but not paradigm-shifting. LLM capabilities have crossed a threshold that makes parsing, translating, and summarizing regulatory documents across languages feasible at scale for the first time. And regulatory volume is exploding -- EU CSRD, SEC climate disclosure rules, state-level AI legislation, PFAS requirements -- creating a fragmentation problem that scales beyond what manual processes can handle. The regulatory intelligence market is growing at 11.6-16.6% CAGR depending on the source. But here's what limits my conviction: the regtech category is well-established with funded competitors. CUBE Global has $22M raised and 1,000+ clients. Thomson Reuters Regulatory Intelligence covers 750+ regulatory bodies across 130+ countries. AscentAI raised $27M before being acquired. "AI makes regulatory monitoring better" is consensus -- every one of these incumbents is integrating AI into their workflows. Regbase's subnational and multilingual long-tail focus is a genuine gap in incumbent coverage, but it's an incremental positioning advantage within a known market, not a structural opening of the kind I saw when I backed Coinbase in a market that most investors considered nonexistent. When I compare Regbase to Harvey -- my closest portfolio parallel -- Harvey entered a market where every prior AI legal tool had failed, creating deep investor skepticism that masked a genuine capability threshold crossing. Regbase enters a market where competitors have already raised collectively over $50M and Thomson Reuters has a public-company-scale operation. The window exists but it's not underestimated.

Regbase's positioning on the infrastructure-layer test lands in the middle. The company indexes hard-to-find subnational and non-English regulatory sources -- "obscure local websites and content behind QR codes" per their HireTop profile -- which creates a potential data moat that compounds as each new source requires custom ingestion and validation. This is operationally similar to the "boring but critical" pattern I've seen work with Gusto and Checkr: unglamorous, compliance-essential, clear payer with budget authority. Compliance teams can't stop monitoring regulations, which creates natural retention. But Regbase appears to be a monitoring and intelligence product -- closer to a data feed -- rather than a platform that other businesses build on. There's no evidence of API-first architecture, no developer ecosystem, no indication that Regbase is becoming the infrastructure layer that other compliance tools integrate with. Stripe became the payments API; Regbase, as described, is a regulatory monitoring application. That distinction matters for long-term defensibility and margin expansion.

Ethan Shea's technical background -- Microsoft engineering, Citadel tech lead, Vercel Staff Engineer leading agentic AI projects -- is directly relevant to building the AI ingestion and analysis pipelines Regbase needs. That's a strong CTO hire for this specific problem. The three-founder structure with clear role separation (CEO/CTO/CBO) avoids the equal co-founder deadlock red flag I watch for. But none of the three founders have operated at a company during a scaling phase from tens to thousands of employees, and none have prior startup exits. Aresta-Katz's Columbia MBA and domain expertise from Paul, Weiss give her credibility with enterprise buyers, and the Paul, Weiss network provides an initial distribution channel into law firms. But Regbase claims to be "trusted by Fortune 500 companies, global consultancies, and leading law firms" without naming a single customer, disclosing any revenue metrics, or publishing case studies. At pre-seed, unverifiable enterprise claims aren't damning -- but they're not signal either.

The single-miracle test is where Regbase scores best in my framework. The technology miracle -- LLMs that can parse and translate regulatory documents at quality -- is happening exogenously; Regbase doesn't need to solve that. The distribution channel exists through the founders' law firm network and compliance teams are proven buyers. No regulatory approval is needed to operate. Regbase needs exactly one thing to go right: building and maintaining comprehensive, accurate coverage of thousands of fragmented subnational sources faster and more reliably than competitors can replicate. That's a real operational challenge -- the dossier flags the maintenance burden of monitoring thousands of government websites that change formats unpredictably -- but it's a single execution miracle, not compounding risks. The human verification layer Regbase employs adds quality assurance but also limits scalability, creating a tension the team will need to resolve as they grow beyond three people.

The bull case for Regbase is legitimate: two domain-expert founders from a top-tier law firm, a technically strong CTO with agentic AI experience at Vercel, attacking the fastest-growing segment within regtech (16.6% CAGR per Precedence Research) at a moment when regulatory proliferation is accelerating beyond what incumbents optimized for financial services can serve. The $400M school safety grants case study and the 80-country greenhouse gas mapping example demonstrate real value delivery. If Regbase can establish the definitive subnational regulatory dataset -- the Bloomberg Terminal equivalent for the regulatory long tail -- incumbents would face a structural mismatch trying to replicate it, just as legacy defense contractors couldn't replicate Anduril's software-first approach. But the Anduril exception doesn't apply here: that override required a generational structural opening (bipartisan defense modernization consensus), not an incremental market growth story. Regbase's market is growing but it's established, populated, and consolidating -- CUBE just acquired Thomson Reuters' regulatory intelligence division. The commoditization risk from improving general-purpose LLMs is real: the technical barrier to parsing government websites is moderate and declining, which means Regbase's moat depends on the operational barrier of maintaining thousands of source connections, not a technology breakthrough competitors can't match. The verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: named enterprise contracts with expansion revenue data showing that Regbase's subnational coverage is generating retention and pricing power that competitors can't match -- proof that the data asset is compounding, not just growing.

Regbase earns a Neutral verdict driven by two dimensions. Nonobvious Market Timing is the primary constraint -- the structural tailwind is real but the market is established, and "AI + regulatory monitoring" is consensus, not contrarian. Infrastructure-Layer Positioning is the secondary gap -- Regbase is an application, not a platform that other businesses build on, which limits the compounding switching cost dynamic I look for. The single-miracle structure and founder-market fit are genuinely strong, but they can't overcome the fundamental positioning question: in a known, funded, consolidating market, Regbase needs to demonstrate that its subnational data coverage creates a defensible asset, not just a feature that an incumbent could build or acquire.
