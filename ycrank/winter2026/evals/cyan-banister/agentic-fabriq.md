# Agentic Fabriq -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Agentic Fabriq: this is a popular idea. "Okta for Agents" -- it's clean, it's legible, every VC in the room nods when they hear it. And that's exactly the problem for someone like me. Arcade.dev raised $12 million with a former Okta executive at the helm. Composio has $29 million. OASIS Security has $75 million. Opti has $20 million. SailPoint, a public company, has already shipped agent identity as a feature. When I look at this landscape, I don't see a pocket of dreamers and weirdos building something nobody else believes in -- I see a crowded room where everyone arrived at the same insight simultaneously. The companies I've done best with are the ones where I'm writing the first check because nobody else wants to touch it. SpaceX was considered delusional. Uber was illegal. Crusoe was burning gas in North Dakota. This? This is the opposite. Everyone can see that AI agents need identity management. That's not conviction investing -- that's consensus investing at a pre-seed price against competitors with sixty times more capital.

When I try to find the founder story -- the biographical thread that tells me why these two people are the ones who should solve this problem -- I come up empty. Paulina and Matthew are both MIT students who dropped out before their second year to build this. They have AI research backgrounds at impressive labs -- Kavli, Haystack, CSAIL, FutureTech. They're clearly smart. But dropping out of MIT to do a YC startup is the most well-worn path in Silicon Valley at this point. It's not adversity; it's the playbook. I don't see any personal experience with enterprise security operations, with the pain of managing agent permissions at scale, with the frustration of watching an AI agent access data it shouldn't have. I spent years at IronPort living inside security operations before Cisco bought us for $830 million -- I know what it looks like when someone has been burned by a security failure and is building out of that scar tissue. These founders seem to have identified a market opportunity through research, not through lived experience. And at pre-seed, when the product barely exists and the market is forming, the question I'm always asking is: will this person keep going when the company is in crisis, when the market shifts, when the bigger competitors start cutting off oxygen? The biographical signal that answers that question isn't here.

The strongest bull case, and I want to be honest about it, is that agent identity really is a new infrastructure category emerging at a genuine inflection point. I've backed infrastructure at inflection points before -- SpaceX for private space, Crusoe for energy-to-compute, Together.ai for AI infrastructure. If AI agents proliferate the way cloud services did, someone will be the identity layer, and being purpose-built could matter more than being an incumbent bolt-on. Okta's architecture was designed for human-speed interactions, not machine-speed per-action authorization. That's a real architectural gap. And if Paulina and Matthew have the technical depth from their MIT AI research to build something genuinely agent-native, there's an argument that product quality could overcome the capital disadvantage. The co-founder relationship also has something going for it -- they've been friends since MIT admit weekend and made the high-stakes decision to drop out together, which suggests alignment. I tend to favor family-run businesses and close personal bonds between founders, so that's a small positive.

But I keep coming back to the same problem: nothing about this company is hard to assess, unpopular, or difficult to explain. Those are the three characteristics I've learned to look for in my best investments. Agentic Fabriq is easy to assess (clear market need, understood technical components), popular (five well-funded competitors, incumbent attention), and easy to explain ("Okta for Agents" -- four words). The underlying technical building blocks -- OAuth flows, RBAC, token management -- are well-understood engineering. The differentiation is in product positioning, not in technology risk. And this is an enterprise sales game, where credibility, relationships, and capital for long sales cycles matter enormously. A former Okta executive with $12 million is going to walk into enterprise IT meetings with a very different reception than two nineteen-year-old MIT dropouts. I don't see the moat developing before the competition closes in. This is a pass for me -- not because the problem isn't real, but because this is a consensus bet in a crowded space, and that's never been where I find my edge.
