# Heavi -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari is an ASE T8 Certified Diesel Mechanic who scaled WayCool Foods to $250M+ ARR and raised $366M across 28 rounds. His father ran Ashok Leyland, one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers. In a different framework — Paul Graham's, for instance — this combination of domain credibility and proven scaling ability would be the primary investment signal. But the question I ask is not whether the founder is impressive. The question is whether Heavi possesses a secret — an important truth that the market disputes — and whether that secret creates a structural path to monopoly. On both counts, the answer is no.

Heavi's thesis is that AI can replace the institutional knowledge of retiring diesel technicians, starting with parts procurement. This is not a secret. The diesel technician shortage — 65.5% of shops understaffed, a projected 67,000-technician deficit by 2026 — has been documented in industry reports, ATRI studies, and Heavy Duty Trucking publications. Every stakeholder in the heavy-duty ecosystem knows this. "AI can help find parts faster" is the precise kind of consensus insight dressed in contrarian clothing that I warn against. If the thesis appeared in a McKinsey report on workforce automation — and functionally equivalent analyses have — then the conditions under which a real secret exists are absent. Compare this to Palantir, where the secret was that the intelligence community needed software that made analysts structurally more capable, at a time when every defense contractor believed hardware procurement was the only path to government embedding. The CIA's In-Q-Tel backed Palantir because the thesis was genuinely disputed by the defense establishment. Heavi faces no equivalent institutional resistance — everyone agrees the problem is real, which means everyone will build toward the same solution.

The monopoly structure test fails more definitively than the secret test. Fullbay already serves 5,000+ shops with $19.4M in revenue and acquired Pitstop for AI capabilities in March 2026. Decisiv holds $63.2M in funding and manages 14,000+ daily repair events across 74,000+ fleet owners. FleetPride and TruckPro, as large parts distributors, possess the vendor relationships and catalog data that Heavi must access as a dependent intermediary. The dossier acknowledges that "the parts data itself is not proprietary" and that defensibility depends on "speed of domain-specific data accumulation" — which is another way of saying there is no structural moat, only a head start. A head start is not a monopoly. Heavi's competitive landscape slide — comparing itself to four funded competitors pursuing AI-enhanced heavy-duty repair — is itself the clearest red flag in my framework. The existence of that comparison means the market is competitive by definition.

The power-law arithmetic compounds the problem. The US heavy-duty truck parts dealer market is $25.5B and the repair services market is $18.1B, growing at 3-4% CAGR. Heavi captures value as a software intermediary taking a transaction fee or SaaS subscription — it does not manufacture parts, own fleet relationships, or control physical infrastructure. Even granting dominant market share in AI-assisted procurement, the realistic ceiling is a mid-single-digit-billion outcome. This is the kind of company that could become a "nice $200M business" or perhaps a $1B acquisition by Fullbay or a distributor. It is structurally irrelevant to power-law portfolio construction. My Wish investment — a no-moat marketplace in a fragmented market that IPO'd at $14B and crashed over 90% — taught the most expensive version of this lesson: marketplace software without proprietary technology or structural lock-in in a market where incumbents have distribution advantages is a recipe for value destruction, not monopoly creation.

The strongest bull case for Heavi rests on Dasari's extraordinary founder-market fit and early commercial traction: $100,000 contracted from five design partners, with claims of 30% time savings and 40% margin improvement. If those efficiency gains are real and compound as Heavi's data accumulates shop-specific knowledge, switching costs could emerge organically — the Palantir playbook of operational embedding applied to independent repair shops. The physical-world proximity of the problem (trucks break down, parts are tangible, downtime costs real money per day) gives Heavi more gravitational pull than a pure-software productivity tool. For this bull case to hold, Heavi would need to achieve what no company in this dossier has demonstrated: a self-reinforcing data flywheel where each transaction makes the next one structurally harder to replicate, creating the kind of compounding advantage that transforms a procurement tool into an indispensable operating system. I see no evidence that this flywheel exists yet, and the absence of proprietary data sources makes its emergence speculative rather than architectural.

Heavi is a well-credentialed team applying consensus AI capabilities to a documented workforce problem in a competitive market with active incumbents and no structural path to monopoly. The Monopoly Secret dimension — scoring 9 against a default of 10 because the competitive landscape is explicitly populated — and the Power Law Magnitude dimension — capped by a market that lacks explosive growth dynamics and a business model that captures thin intermediary economics — are the two criteria that lock Heavi into a Pass. Dasari's credentials elevate the Definite Founder Conviction score above default, but a brilliant founder pursuing a competitive market remains, in my framework, a founder who has failed to escape competition before the race has begun.
