# Mango Medical -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Mango Medical isn't the technology or the LOI -- it's that this is a company built inside institutions, not against them. Academic spinoff from the Technical University of Munich, through XPRENEURS incubator, through Stanford Ignite, through Plug and Play, and now into YC. Every step in this company's story follows an established pathway for turning research into startups. That's not a criticism of the founders' intelligence or capability -- it's an observation about what kind of company this is. And what it is, fundamentally, is a credentialed team executing a known playbook in a recognized market category. When I look at my best investments, they almost never look like this. The most promising companies are difficult to assess and unpopular. AI-powered surgical planning software in 2026 is neither.

When I ask myself "tell me the founder's story," I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this person the only human being on earth who should be solving this problem. Adrian Kilian was a dentist in Berlin. Dentistry shares some DNA with orthopedic surgery -- you plan procedures, you work in 3D space -- but he wasn't an orthopedic surgeon who watched patients suffer through weeks of delayed planning. He wasn't someone who broke his femur and experienced the system's failures from the gurney. The connection is adjacent, not autobiographical. Jorge Padilla Pérez is an AI engineer from TU Munich with strong technical credentials in deep learning and XR. Together they make a competent founding team, and six years of working on this problem since 2020 shows real persistence. But I don't feel that jolt I felt with Crusoe, where the founders had independently stumbled onto something nobody else could see. This team found their opportunity through academic programs designed to surface exactly this kind of opportunity.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because there's one signal here that's hard to ignore: a $10M ARR letter of intent from a "top orthopedic company" at pre-seed stage. If that LOI converts, it would be one of the fastest paths to meaningful revenue I've seen in medtech. And the fracture reconstruction niche is smart positioning -- while Formus Labs chases hip replacement planning and Enhatch builds broader surgical instrumentation platforms, Mango Medical is focused on trauma and fractures, which are less planned, more urgent, and less well-served by existing tools. The "Invisalign for bone fractures" framing reveals real ambition -- standardizing what's currently bespoke. If they nail the FDA pathway and convert that LOI, the acquisition math from a Stryker or Zimmer Biomet could be very attractive. The regulatory moat is real once obtained, and I'm comfortable with FDA complexity from my IronPort days where I learned to see compliance burdens as competitive advantages.

But here's where I get stuck. Five funded competitors are already further along -- Formus Labs has FDA clearance, $2.9M in revenue, and a Zimmer Biomet distribution deal. Enhatch has clearance and partnerships with Smith+Nephew and 3D Systems. Mango Medical is pursuing 510(k) with no public record of even submitting. That LOI is almost certainly contingent on clearance they don't have. In medtech, the gap between "pursuing FDA clearance" and "obtained FDA clearance" can be measured in years and millions of dollars. And the underlying technology -- CT scan segmentation, 3D reconstruction, surgical simulation -- uses well-documented deep learning techniques. This is execution risk in a competitive landscape, not the kind of technology risk I find investable, where there's a real frontier challenge that most people think is impossible.

The economic access dimension is where this falls furthest from my thesis. When I backed Uber, the insight wasn't just logistics -- it was that hundreds of thousands of people could earn a living driving. When I backed Affirm, it was about making credit transparent and accessible to people the system excluded. Mango Medical's primary beneficiaries are orthopedic surgeons -- among the highest-compensated medical specialists -- and large device companies. There's a real downstream benefit to patients in faster, better surgical outcomes, and you could argue that automating planning helps address surgeon shortages, getting more patients treated. But that's an indirect, second-order effect. This company makes an invisible system more efficient, which I notice and appreciate, but it doesn't create economic access for people who currently lack it.

The co-founders have worked together for six years through what appears to be a long journey from Munich to San Francisco, which is a meaningful signal of durability. But the geographic split -- Kilian in Menlo Park, Padilla Pérez still in Munich -- introduces the kind of distributed stress that I've seen fracture teams under pressure. I'd want to understand how they resolve disagreements across nine time zones before writing a check.

This is a capable team building a real product in a real market. But it doesn't activate my framework. The founders are credentialed rather than battle-scarred. The category is populated rather than contrarian. The impact is operational rather than democratizing. I respect the persistence and the technical depth, but this is the kind of investment where consensus will form eventually and later-stage investors with medtech expertise will compete for the deal -- if the FDA pathway clears. It's not a bet I need to make before anyone else sees it, and those are the only bets I make.
