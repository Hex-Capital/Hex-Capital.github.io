# Didit -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Didit is the gap between impressive execution and modest ambition. Here are founders who pivoted from a decentralized identity protocol -- which, if it had worked, could have given every human on earth sovereign control over their own identity data -- into a company whose pitch is "KYC but 70% cheaper." They moved *away* from the transformational vision *toward* the safer, proven one. That's exactly backwards from how I evaluate companies. They reduced the probability of failure by choosing a commodity market with known demand and competing on price. And in doing so, they compressed the consequence of success into something that doesn't justify my kind of bet. If Didit succeeds fully, businesses pay less for identity verification. That's a useful service. It is not a change in how the world works.

The traction is genuinely notable -- 700+ B2B customers, claimed 300%+ NRR, near-profitable on $2M raised. If those numbers are real, this is capital-efficient growth in a way that would make most SaaS investors salivate. But I'm not most SaaS investors. The market they're competing in has Persona at $2B valuation with $141M in revenue, Sumsub at $85.6M revenue on just $7.5M raised, and Entrust acquiring Onfido for $650M. Those are the ceiling outcomes. A $500M acquisition, a $1-2B company. That math doesn't work for my portfolio construction -- I need the possibility of 100x outcomes, not the certainty of 5-10x outcomes in a competitive commodity market.

The technology doesn't displace the incumbent system; it *is* the incumbent system at a lower price point. Document OCR, face matching, liveness detection, AML screening -- every competitor offers these. Didit built it from scratch, which shows engineering capability, but the stack is converging across the industry. The free-tier pricing model creates structural tension for incumbents at the low end, but it doesn't protect the enterprise segment where real revenue concentrates. Persona is already positioning itself as "the verified identity layer for an agentic AI world" with $417M in capital to execute. When your differentiation is price in a market that's consolidating around well-funded players, you're playing a game where scale and capital win, not technology breakthroughs.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that AI-driven identity fraud becomes so severe that verification volume grows 10-50x (expanding the TAM dramatically), that Didit's free-tier funnel captures the majority of new market entrants who then expand into paid tiers, and that their AI fraud detection models improve faster than competitors' because of data flywheel effects from processing "millions of monthly verifications." If all three hold, you get a platform company that becomes the default identity layer for every new fintech, marketplace, and AI agent globally -- and the consequence of success becomes meaningful. But that requires the technology AND the GTM AND the competitive moat AND the market expansion to all work simultaneously, and the founders haven't articulated which risk they're eliminating first with their limited capital.

Alberto's ML credentials are real -- a StyleGAN2 implementation with 143 stars on GitHub shows hands-on depth, not resume padding. The identical twins who were professional athletes bring competitive intensity and tight coordination. The pivot from Gamium's Web3 identity infrastructure to standalone B2B KYC demonstrates learning -- they found the valuable kernel inside a broader project and extracted it. But this is the learning of pragmatic operators, not the contrarian courage of someone attacking what experts declare impossible. Nobody declared identity verification impossible. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never happen in telecom -- *that's* the kind of dismissal that signals transformational potential. The identity verification industry is welcoming Didit's entry because cheaper competition validates the market category; it doesn't threaten to make the category obsolete.

I pass on this. These are capable founders building a real business in a growing market, and they may well succeed -- Sumsub's trajectory on minimal capital shows it's possible. But I invest my own money in companies where success means the world operates differently, not companies where success means an enterprise saves 70% on compliance costs. The problem Didit solves is real but not "too important not to work on" in the sense I mean it. Go find the version of this that makes identity verification itself obsolete -- self-sovereign identity, zero-knowledge proofs that let you prove you're over 18 without revealing who you are, cryptographic attestation that eliminates document-based KYC entirely. *That* would be a consequence worth the risk of total failure.
