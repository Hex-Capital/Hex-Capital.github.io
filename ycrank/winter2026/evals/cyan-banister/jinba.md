# Jinba -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Jinba isn't the product or the team -- it's the competitive landscape. AI workflow automation is one of the hottest categories in enterprise software right now. n8n just closed $180 million at a $2.5 billion valuation. Workato has $415 million in funding and $150 million in revenue. Microsoft is weaving Copilot into Power Automate, which already sits on the desktop of nearly every Fortune 500 company. When I see a pre-seed company with $500K entering a market where incumbents have hundreds of millions in capital and established distribution, I need an extraordinary reason to believe. And the reason I usually find -- the thing that tells me this team sees something nobody else does -- is the founder's story. That's where I always start. Tell me how you got here. Not the business. You.

What I find in Shoya Matsumori's background is a Ph.D. in deep learning from Keio, publications at ICCV, a string of ML engineering roles, and a prior startup called BLUEM. Takuya Norisugi has an INSEAD MBA and came up through McKinsey. These are genuinely strong credentials, and I don't dismiss them -- but they're establishment credentials. I don't hear a survival story. I don't feel the biographical thread that connects these specific people to the specific pain of enterprise workflow automation in banking and insurance. When I backed Uber, I'd spent years arguing with Scott about the taxi medallion racket. When I found Contraline, the founder had devoted years of his academic life to male contraception because he believed the world needed it. The connection between founder and problem was visceral, not analytical. Here, I see smart people who identified a large market and built a technically competent product to address it. That's a fine way to build a company -- it's just not the signal I invest on.

The other problem is that this doesn't feel weird at all. "Automate any enterprise workflow through chat" is, in 2026, about as consensus as it gets. Every venture fund in the Valley is actively looking for AI-native enterprise tooling plays. The category isn't difficult to assess -- it's well-understood, well-mapped, and heavily capitalized. My best investments have been in companies that other investors found ridiculous or impossible to evaluate. SpaceX when private space was a joke. Crusoe when they wanted to burn gas flares for Bitcoin. Mindbloom when psychedelic therapy made VCs uncomfortable. Jinba is doing something that every VC understands and most are actively seeking. That means the price is likely wrong, the differentiation gets commoditized quickly, and the weirdness -- which is where the real returns hide -- has already been arbitraged away.

Now, the strongest case for Jinba is one I want to take seriously: 40,000 enterprise users at Fortune Global 500 companies in banking and insurance. If that number is real and represents active engagement on the current product, that's extraordinary pre-seed traction and signals genuine product-market fit in one of the hardest customer segments to crack. Japanese enterprise buyers in particular are famously difficult to win and famously loyal once won -- the switching costs are enormous. Suntory is a legitimate global conglomerate. The on-prem deployment capability combined with AI-native workflow generation is a genuinely rare combination -- most AI tools are cloud-only, and most on-prem tools are legacy. If Jinba has built a real beachhead in Japanese regulated enterprise and can use YC to bridge into Western markets, there's a version of this story where the Japan-first strategy is the moat. The regulatory complexity of banking and insurance -- SOC II, RBAC, audit logging, on-prem deployment -- does create the kind of hard-space advantage I respect. This is where Jinba scores best against my framework: willingness to operate in regulated environments that deter casual entrants. But I have real uncertainty about that 40,000 number given the prior product pivot from Jinbaflow (a data analytics tool) to the current workflow automation product. Self-reported metrics with no third-party verification, especially after a pivot, make me cautious.

Ultimately, Jinba is a competent team building in a legitimate market, but it sits in almost exactly the wrong place on every axis I care about most. It's popular, not unpopular. It's easy to assess, not difficult. The founders are insiders from elite institutions, not outsiders forged by adversity. The product creates enterprise efficiency, not economic access for people who need it. I don't feel the jolt -- that intuitive energy I felt with Niantic, that sense of seeing something nobody else sees. What I see is a well-structured company that would make sense in many portfolios, but doesn't belong in mine.
