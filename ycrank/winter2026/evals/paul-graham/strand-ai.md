# Strand AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Strand AI is where the idea came from. Both founders were inside the machine — literally building petabyte-scale spatial biology infrastructure at Enable Medicine, and in Yue Dai's case, training foundation models on multimodal patient datasets at Pathos/Tempus. When you spend your days wrangling spatial proteomics, transcriptomics, and H&E data at that scale, you see the holes. You see the patient who has histology but no proteomics, the cohort that shrinks from 500 to 200 because you have to throw away everyone with incomplete profiles. This idea wasn't manufactured in a brainstorming session about "how to apply AI to healthcare." It emerged from professional frustration at the data layer, which is the biotech equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive — you keep encountering the same missing data problem until you finally decide to solve it.

The schlep here is significant and worth naming precisely. Generating biological modalities that were never measured is not the kind of product you casually add to your roadmap. It requires access to scarce paired multimodal training data — samples where both H&E and proteomics were actually measured on the same patient — which is expensive to collect and difficult to access. It requires deep expertise at the intersection of spatial biology and ML. And then there's the truly terrifying part: if a pharmaceutical company uses your imputed proteomics data to select patients for a trial, and your predictions are wrong, the downstream consequences are real. This is regulatory and liability schlep of a kind that makes most AI teams unconsciously look away. Everyone else in this space — Owkin, Nucleai, Bioptimus — analyzes data that already exists. Strand AI is generating data that was never collected. That's a categorically different kind of scary, and that's why they might actually have a window.

The technical credentials are real. Oded Falik has been programming since age 8 and built Enable Medicine's spatial biology platform end to end. Yue Dai comes from Yoshua Bengio's lab at Element AI, then Microsoft Research, then managed GPU clusters at scale. They claim to have beaten state-of-the-art cross-modal prediction performance in under six weeks. I can't validate that claim from the outside, but their backgrounds make it plausible rather than aspirational. The GitHub organization shows active model development, including a VariantFormer implementation. These are builders, not deck-makers.

What I can't see is any evidence that anyone outside the founding team wants this yet. No named pharma partner. No LOI. No pilot. Not even an anecdote about a conversation with a drug development team that got excited. At pre-seed this isn't unusual, but it matters more here because of the market they're selling into. Pharma sales cycles are brutal — 12 to 18 months is normal, and for a product category that doesn't have regulatory precedent, it could be longer. Two people trying to simultaneously build cutting-edge foundation models and navigate enterprise pharma sales is a genuine structural challenge. This is the kind of company where I'd want to see evidence that at least one potential customer has said "yes, I need this" before I feel confident the demand side is real rather than theoretical.

The bull case is compelling if you squint right. If cross-modal biological imputation works at clinical-grade accuracy, the value unlocked is enormous — you're essentially turning every incomplete cohort into a complete one, rescuing statistical power that currently gets thrown away. The scarce paired training data creates a compounding moat: whoever trains on the best paired datasets first builds models that attract more pharma partnerships, which generate more paired data, which improve the models. And the schlep itself is the moat — Owkin has $300M+ and Bioptimus has $76M, but neither has chosen to go here precisely because the validation and liability burden is daunting. If Strand AI can land even one validation partnership with a mid-tier pharma company, that precedent becomes a competitive asset that money alone can't buy quickly. The founders' prior relationships from Enable Medicine and Tempus/Pathos give them plausible warm introductions to exactly the right buyers.

But I keep coming back to the absence of pull. The problem is real, the schlep is genuine, the founders are technically strong and have organic domain knowledge. What's missing is any signal that the bridge from "this should work" to "someone is using this" has been crossed or is close to being crossed. Strong founder-problem fit and a real schlep put this ahead of most AI companies I see, where the idea was basically "AI is hot, let's apply it to X." But without even a single data point of external validation — a pilot, a LOI, a named design partner — I'm left evaluating potential rather than trajectory.
