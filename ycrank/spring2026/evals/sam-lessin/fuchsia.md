# fuchsia -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Saphira AI shipped this exact pitch — "Vanta for hardware" — out of YC S24, has a team of six, is hiring a Founding Account Executive, and covers the same UL, CE, ISO, and TÜV standards Fuchsia targets. YC funded Saphira over a year ago. Reglyr launched an AI compliance platform in April 2026. UL Solutions generates $400 million a year in software and advisory revenue on top of $3 billion in total revenue. So when I look at Fuchsia and ask my first question — "is the market wrong about this?" — the answer is no. The market is extremely right about hardware compliance being painful. Every sophisticated investor can see the TAM ($36B compliance software, 12.67% CAGR to $65.7B by 2030). Nobody has dismissed this category. Nobody is uncomfortable writing this check. That means the upside is already in the price, and what you're buying is execution risk against a funded competitor with a twelve-month head start. That's not my game.

Tony Gao's robotics background — perception research, humanoid deployments — gives Fuchsia genuine domain credibility on the hardware certification pain point. Jack Yin's résumé reads like a quant trading all-star team: D.E. Shaw, Citadel Securities, Five Rings. These are clearly smart, capable people. Yale math and physics, Yale CS and math — fine. But the sane-person-insane-idea ratio is inverted here. The founders are sane, and the idea is... also sane. "Let's use AI to parse regulatory documents and auto-generate compliance paperwork" is the single most obvious application of LLMs that any hardware founder would come up with after spending a week on FCC filings. When I backed the Solana seed, people told me I was an idiot. Nobody is telling Tony Gao he's an idiot for building Fuchsia. They're telling him Saphira already exists.

Fuchsia fails the AI kill test in the most textbook way possible. The dossier says it plainly: "The core AI capability (parsing regulatory documents, generating documentation) relies on general-purpose LLMs." That's the whole product. UL Solutions — the actual standards body — already has a software platform called GCM. The dossier notes UL's business model conflict: building a self-serve AI tool would cannibalize their consulting revenue. That's the strongest bull case for Fuchsia, and it's real — incumbents with misaligned incentives sometimes let startups eat their lunch. But here's the problem: this isn't a one-startup-vs-one-incumbent fight. Saphira, Reglyr, Compliance & Risks ($29.8M revenue, 188 employees), and any competent team that can prompt an LLM against a regulatory corpus are all converging on the same wedge. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the moat was physical parking infrastructure — you can't replicate garages with software. Fuchsia's "moat" is a regulatory document database that any funded competitor can build and that LLMs make cheaper to construct every quarter.

The primitive test seals it for me. Fuchsia does not create a new behavior, a new financial instrument, or a new category. It makes an existing workflow faster. That's valuable! But it's not a primitive. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments — a behavior that didn't exist before. Patreon created direct creator monetization as a category. Fuchsia creates... faster compliance paperwork. The lab-matching marketplace is the most interesting feature because it has potential network effects — a two-sided market connecting hardware teams to testing facilities — but there's no evidence of any lab network built, and marketplace businesses at pre-seed with no supply-side traction are just PowerPoint slides. The regulatory data maintenance burden (dozens of standards bodies, different update schedules, liability for accuracy) is cited in the dossier as a risk, but a bull would argue it's a moat. I'd argue it's a cost center that scales linearly and doesn't compound.

Let me steelman Fuchsia as hard as I can. The bull case: hardware compliance is a $36B market with no dominant software player for the specific product-certification workflow. UL won't cannibalize its own consulting. Saphira focused on industrial safety and heavy machinery while Fuchsia positions broadly across hardware categories. Tony Gao has lived the certification pain firsthand in robotics. The team is lean — two Yale quantitative founders at pre-seed, burning almost nothing. If Fuchsia nails the lab-matching marketplace, it could become the two-sided network that locks in both hardware companies and testing facilities. I've checked my Known Exceptions: Slack was consensus-hot but created a genuine new behavioral primitive. Fuchsia is consensus-hot but does not create a new primitive — it automates an existing one. The Slack exception doesn't apply. The condition that would flip me: if Fuchsia showed me $50K+ in contracted revenue from three or more hardware companies that tried Saphira first and switched, demonstrating that Fuchsia's approach to broad hardware coverage (not just industrial safety) is a genuinely different wedge with better product-market fit. That's a verifiable signal I could underwrite.

Fuchsia lands as a Pass driven by two dimensions: Consensus Mispricing scores near the bottom because every investor in the room can see the compliance TAM, YC already funded Saphira in this exact category, and the risk-reward is symmetrical rather than asymmetric. Structural Durability scores near the bottom because the core product — LLM-powered document parsing and generation — is the definition of what I mean when I say "this is perfect for the incumbent." Smart founders, real problem, no contrarian edge, no primitive, no moat AI can't eat. That's a feature, not a company.
