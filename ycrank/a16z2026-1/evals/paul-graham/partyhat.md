# PartyHat -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Jarret Cuisinier spent years as VP of Marketing & Operations at Avast, which had 400 million users and sold to Gen Digital for $8.1 billion. He saw the consumer security market from the inside — the pricing, the distribution channels, the aging brand equity — and concluded the opportunity was to repackage it with TikTok-native humor and a $2.99/month price point. This is exactly the origin story that makes me nervous. The way to get startup ideas is not to identify market gaps from inside an incumbent's strategy team. When someone who ran marketing and M&A at Avast launches a consumer security bundle, what I'm looking at is not organic problem discovery — it's a corporate playbook executed as a startup. Cuisinier didn't live in the future and build what was missing. He lived inside Norton's acquirer and built what his competitive analysis suggested.

PartyHat bundles VPN, dark web monitoring, scam call blocking, and an AI security advisor into a single app — every component commercially available via white-label providers and monitoring APIs. The dossier itself notes this: "VPN infrastructure is widely available via white-label providers. Dark web monitoring APIs are sold by multiple vendors." The differentiation is branding, not technology. "Always Wear Protection" is a clever line, but clever marketing copy is not a moat. Gen Digital generates $4.73 billion in annual revenue. Aura raised $898.8 million and has deep credit bureau integrations. NordVPN has an estimated 15 million subscribers. PartyHat has 1,000 users and 106 App Store ratings after four months. The question I keep asking is: what does PartyHat know that these companies don't? And the answer appears to be that Gen Z thinks Norton is uncool. That's an observation, not a secret.

The idea fails my Black Swan test completely. "Better cybersecurity with fun branding for young people" — tell that to any room of investors and no one recoils. No one says "that will never work." Everyone nods. And when everyone nods at a startup idea, the returns have already been competed away. The best ideas provoke the reaction "that's crazy" before they provoke "that's obvious." PartyHat provokes neither. It sits in the comfortable zone where the concept is sensible and the execution challenge is distribution against entrenched, well-funded incumbents — which is not the kind of problem that rewards scrappy two-person teams. It rewards marketing budgets.

The strongest bull case for PartyHat is Cuisinier's genuine domain knowledge. He understands consumer security acquisition economics at a level very few founders do, and Myneni shipped a cross-platform product — iOS, Android, macOS, Windows — in roughly four months from founding. Myneni's background building Age of Rivals (featured on multiple "Best of 2017" lists) shows he can ship consumer products solo. If PartyHat's TikTok-first distribution catches a viral loop among college students, the $2.99 entry price could generate rapid organic growth that incumbents can't match because Norton can't pivot to irreverent branding without alienating its existing 75 million paying customers. That structural brand lock-in for incumbents is real. But "maybe TikTok will go viral" is a hope, not a strategy, and the referral program (3 months free with annual plan) is standard SaaS mechanics, not evidence of product-market fit.

I don't see evidence of resourcefulness under genuine constraint — no stories of creative workarounds, no doing-things-that-don't-scale moments, no signal that these founders solved problems in ways that surprised anyone. Cuisinier is a polished executive with a Stanford MBA, Berkeley engineering degree, and VP titles at multiple companies. Myneni has 25 years of software experience. These are competent, experienced operators, but the "animal" quality I look for — the obsessive intensity that crosses from professional into something slightly unhinged — doesn't show up anywhere in the dossier. The version history (1.0 to 1.3.2 in three months) shows steady iteration, but 1,000 users in four months is not the 5-7% weekly growth curve that tells me something is working.

PartyHat is a Pass. The two dimensions that lock this are Organic Problem Discovery, where Cuisinier's Avast tenure makes this explicitly a market-analysis-driven idea rather than a lived-frustration idea, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where consumer cybersecurity with better branding is a consensus-good concept that generates no controversy and no contrarian return premium. The founders are capable, but capability applied to a commoditized product in a market dominated by billion-dollar incumbents, without a technical moat or a non-obvious insight, is not the kind of bet I make with my own money.
