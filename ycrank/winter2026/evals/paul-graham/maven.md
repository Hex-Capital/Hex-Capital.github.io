# Maven -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Maven is the schlep. PCI DSS compliance is exactly the kind of regulatory, bureaucratic, soul-crushing work that causes smart engineers to unconsciously look away. Every team building a voice agent knows that at some point, the caller needs to pay. And every one of those teams quietly decides that's someone else's problem. "We'll just transfer them to a human." "We'll send a link after the call." The avoidance is so consistent it's structural. That part — the schlep itself — is real. Voice AI platforms have raised over $145 million in the past year alone, and not one of them has built PCI-compliant payment collection as a core feature. When well-funded companies systematically avoid a problem, it's usually because the problem is genuinely ugly.

But here's where I get stuck: how did these founders find this problem? Both are recent Berkeley CS graduates with junior engineering stints at Amazon and Meta. There's no evidence they were building voice agents themselves and kept hitting the payment wall. There's no story of personal frustration. One founder's LinkedIn still says "Building Invoke" — suggesting Maven may be a rebrand or pivot from something else entirely. The dossier shows two smart engineers who appear to have surveyed the voice AI landscape, identified an infrastructure gap, and decided to fill it. That's market analysis, not organic discovery. The distinction matters. When the Collison brothers built Stripe, they weren't analyzing the payments market — they were hackers who kept getting frustrated trying to accept money online. The problem found them. I don't see evidence that this problem found these founders.

The bull case is worth taking seriously, because the structural setup is genuinely good. Maven is positioned at a chokepoint in the voice AI stack: between the conversation layer (VAPI, Retell, LiveKit) and the money layer (Stripe, Adyen, Braintree). Neither side wants to deal with the other's complexity. The voice platforms don't want PCI compliance headaches. The payment processors haven't built voice-optimized collection flows. If Maven embeds itself as default middleware in these platform SDKs — the way Stripe embedded itself in every web framework — it could become the connective tissue of a very large market. The single-API-call design philosophy mirrors early Stripe almost exactly. And two founders with low burn gives them time to find product-market fit before running out of money. If one of these voice platforms starts shipping Maven as their recommended payment integration, this company could move very fast.

What concerns me beyond the origin story is the absence of any signal that this is working. Free early access with no reported users, no customer logos, no waitlist numbers, no community, no social media presence. At pre-seed I don't expect much — but I expect something. When Stripe launched, Patrick Collison was literally grabbing people's laptops and setting them up. Where's the equivalent unscalable intensity here? The dossier describes integrations with four voice platforms and four payment gateways, which suggests breadth of engineering work. But breadth of integration is not evidence of demand. I want to see a founder who has personally onboarded their first five customers, not a founder who has connected four gateways and is waiting for developers to show up.

The platform dependency risk is also real and worth naming. Maven's entire distribution depends on voice AI platforms remaining independent and not building payments themselves. VAPI and Retell are both YC-backed. Bland AI has $65 million. Any of them could decide that payment collection is a natural feature extension — especially if they see Maven gaining traction in their ecosystem. The PCI compliance barrier is real but temporary. It takes months, not years, for a well-funded team to get certified. Maven needs to achieve enough integration depth and transaction volume before a platform decides to build rather than buy. That's a race, and two junior engineers without deep fintech experience are not the obvious favorites.

The founders have the right technical credentials — Berkeley CS, Amazon infrastructure experience — but I see nothing that distinguishes them as hackers in the way that matters to me. No public GitHub repos. No technical blog posts. No open source work. No evidence of building things compulsively. They may be excellent engineers. But "excellent engineer from a big company" is a different animal from "hacker who stays up all night finishing code because they can't stop thinking about the problem." I have no evidence either way on the resourcefulness question — they haven't yet faced the kind of adversity that reveals whether founders are relentlessly resourceful or merely competent. Getting into YC is a signal, but a modest one for this criterion.

This is a well-identified schlep in a growing market, built by competent technical founders. But the idea feels constructed from the outside rather than discovered from within. The absence of any organic backstory, combined with zero traction evidence and significant platform dependency risk, makes this a pass for my own money — even though I acknowledge the structural opportunity is genuine. If I saw evidence that these founders had been obsessively building voice agents for two years before starting Maven, or if they had five paying customers who'd found them organically, my evaluation would shift significantly.
