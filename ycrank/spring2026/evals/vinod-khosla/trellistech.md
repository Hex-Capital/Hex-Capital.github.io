# Trellis -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Trellis claims "software is being commoditized" and boasts feature parity with competitors achieved in two weeks. Stop and think about what that actually means. If Trellis can replicate Guesty's functionality in fourteen days, Guesty — sitting on $409M in capital and thousands of existing customers — can bolt an AI agent layer onto its platform in the same timeframe. Hostaway, now valued at $1B after raising $543M, has the engineering headcount to do it by next quarter. Boom is already there with its "BAM" agentic features across 20 countries. Conduit, which came out of YC W24, is running AI guest messaging across 77,000+ properties. Trellis's core argument — that AI makes software trivially reproducible — is a weapon pointed at its own head. When your thesis is that moats are dead, you'd better have an extraordinary reason why you're the exception. I don't see one.

Trellis operates in the $8.6B vacation rental software market, targeting professional operators managing 10–150 units. Let me apply my standard test: if Trellis fully succeeds — every STR property manager in the world uses their autonomous agents — what happens? Property managers save 20 hours a week. Reviews improve. Operations get smoother. That's fine. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. Travelers don't get radically cheaper stays. Housing doesn't become more accessible. The consequence of total success is a very profitable SaaS company in a competitive vertical. I regularly see YC companies building AI copilots for specific enterprise verticals — AI for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for property managers. The pitch is always "large TAM, underserved, we automate X% of workflow." An 84.5% automation rate sounds impressive as a dashboard metric, but the consequence of that automation is incremental efficiency in a niche business. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Trellis is designed to generate the kind of 3–5x return that wastes my time and attention.

Lodovico Benvenuti brings a genuinely interesting trajectory — he scaled a property management company to ~$2M revenue and 150+ listings, then built AI agents at Conduit (YC W24), and now founded Trellis. That's operator-to-builder evolution, which shows adaptability. Jan Sahagun has sales stints at Besty AI, Guestway, and Boom — three STR tech companies — and founded STR Tech Report, a media platform that doubles as a distribution channel into the target buyer persona. The problem is that both Trellis founders are deep insiders in a small industry. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand with no formal aerospace degree — an outsider who didn't understand what couldn't be done. Lodovico and Jan understand exactly what can and can't be done in STR operations. That's domain expertise, and when I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I pick better thinking every time. Experts extrapolate the past. There's no evidence in the dossier of either founder surprising themselves, pivoting based on new information, or reasoning from first principles about why the STR industry should look fundamentally different.

The bull case an advocate would make: Trellis's "AI that builds your software" could be a trojan horse. If autonomous agents can truly run a PM company "with 1 person" — as their YC launch post claims — then the barrier to professional property management collapses. Anyone with a spare property operates like a 150-unit company. That's a supply-side explosion that could create a new market, not just optimize an existing one. The DoorDash exception in my portfolio proves I'll override the technical novelty requirement when consequence magnitude is planetary-scale — Tony Xu's food delivery logistics weren't technically novel, but transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people was consequential enough. Can Trellis claim the same? No. DoorDash served hundreds of millions of consumers directly. Trellis serves professional property managers — a B2B operations tool for a niche within the hospitality industry. The causal chain from "better AI agents for STR operators" to "the world visibly changes" has too many links and too little force. No Known Exception applies here.

Trellis's technical approach — orchestrating LLM-powered agents across 200+ existing integrations including Airbnb, VRBO, Guesty, Hostaway, Breezeway, and QuickBooks — is classic API orchestration, not a technology discontinuity. There's no novel architecture, no proprietary data advantage at this stage, and no science breakthrough. The cross-company knowledge network they describe — "every workflow that works, every SOP that survives contact with guests" feeding back into the system — is aspirational, not demonstrated. Trellis has one customer. Conduit has 77,000+ properties generating that exact same data flywheel. The gap between "aspirational data network effect" and "demonstrated moat" is where hundreds of millions of my dollars have gone to die in cleantech. I learned that lesson.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if Trellis demonstrated that its AI agents could autonomously manage 500+ listings across multiple PM companies with measurable revenue uplift (not just time savings) — proving the cross-company knowledge network actually compounds — that would shift the consequence magnitude from "efficiency tool" to "intelligence layer that makes professional property management a commodity." That's a verifiable milestone, and I'd want to see it before writing a check.

The decisive dimensions are Consequence Magnitude and Non-Linear Technical Approach. Trellis scores low on both because the world doesn't visibly change if it succeeds, and the technical approach — LLM agents orchestrating existing APIs — is a wrapper, not a wedge. Competent founders, real market, zero asymmetry in the outcome distribution. Pass.
