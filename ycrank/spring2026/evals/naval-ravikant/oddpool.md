# Oddpool -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Oddpool is two infrastructure engineers — Avi Arora from Microsoft's prompt-injection model and Ritesh Malpani from Bloomberg's buy-side trading stack — selling a $30/month and $100/month REST API for prediction-market data through a `founders@oddpool.com` inquiry channel to quant funds. The dimensional signature here is competent-infrastructure-team on top, permissioned-distribution on bottom. That pairing names what Oddpool is in my portfolio system: a serviceable B2B data feed built by people who know how to ship one, distributed through exactly the labor-and-capital channels I do not fund at angel size.

**Twitter** (2007) is the high rung on the leverage axis: media, zero marginal cost of replication, anyone can post, the platform charges nothing — the architecture that runs through **Stack Overflow** (2008), **Notion** (2013), **Replit** (April 2023), and **Airchat** (April 2024). Oddpool diverges from that rung in the load-bearing way. The GTM line in the dossier reads "outbound to quant funds, prop shops, market makers, hedge funds via founders@oddpool.com" — that is labor leverage requesting permission from a small set of institutional buyers, the structural inverse of nav.al/product-media. The free tier at 1K req/mo is not a permissionless distribution channel; it is a sales funnel for the Enterprise S3-dump tier where the actual revenue lives. Oddpool is selling data to allocators who already operate inside capital-leverage businesses, which is the most permission-gated buyer set in the economy.

The 50/50-cofounder-with-unresolved-authority pattern — Venture Hacks' "most important decision is still pending" — is what I look for in the founder-trio rung's failure mode, and Oddpool surfaces a variant of it. Ritesh Malpani's LinkedIn lists him as Co-founder of Benchspan, a separate Spring 2026 YC company, while also co-founding Oddpool with a two-person team. That is not an integrity flag; Avi and Ritesh have been roommates since freshman year at Georgia Tech, which is the kind of long-arc relationship signal I weight positively. It is an energy-allocation flag. On a two-person team where the founder originally responsible for the buy-side-trading specific knowledge is splitting attention with a second YC company, the 10-year-partner test gets weaker because I cannot tell which of the two YC bets is the one Ritesh wakes up thinking about. Notion's Ivan Zhao did not run a second startup in parallel; the integrity bar held because the focus bar held.

The specific-knowledge dimension is where Oddpool earns its highest placement, and it works through an 18-month compounding mechanism. Bloomberg buy-side trading systems and Microsoft production ML at 20B requests/year are not McKinsey-PM credentials; they are shipped infrastructure at scale. The "Awesome-Prediction-Market-Tools" GitHub repo from `@aarora4` predates the round, which is the **Stack Overflow** (2008) and **Replit** (April 2023) pattern of the founder's artifact shipping before the equity story. The gap is that neither founder was a prediction-market trader before Oddpool — they brought adjacent market-data infrastructure expertise into a domain they did not live inside as users. That distinguishes them from Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD at **Perplexity** (2022) and puts Oddpool in the "credible operators in an adjacent domain" bucket rather than the "founder-as-leverage-user" bucket.

On the network dimension, Oddpool is a single-sided data API where each customer's value is independent of the next; 700,000 indexed markets is a content moat, not a Uber-style two-sided compounding mechanism. The score on this axis would flip a tier only if Oddpool re-architected the free tier as a public dataset with community contributions — annotated markets, crowd-tagged resolution dates, normalized event taxonomies that prosumers extend — converting the Pro and Premium tiers from data resale into network-augmented data. The dossier shows no such mechanic; the 762 arb opportunities flagged last week are a feature, not a network.

The market dimension is best read against **Uber** (2010), which started in a niche (black-car-only in San Francisco) and survived because the adjacent vector (UberX) was already inside the founder's field of view. Prediction-market institutional data is narrow today — the dossier names only Kalshi and Polymarket as live venues, and "CFTC applications keep climbing" is founder narration, not regulatory fact. The pivot vectors I can name from the dossier are sports-betting data, event-derivatives data, and broader fintech market-data infrastructure. Those are real, but they are crowded by Bloomberg and Refinitiv as the commoditization-risk section concedes. Pivot-room exists; it is not Twitter-shaped pivot-room.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool is acquired by Kalshi or Polymarket for $8M–$25M inside 18 months, mirroring the Dome → Polymarket Feb 2026 deal at $10M–$20M after roughly four months post-Demo Day. The mechanism is structural — the dossier itself names "acquirer-substitution risk" where Polymarket bought neutral aggregation rather than building it, and a two-person team with several-thousand-dollars MRR and 700K indexed markets is exactly the acqui-hire shape that incumbents resolve by writing a check. That outcome is consistent with the high/low signature: competent infrastructure team produces a sellable asset; permissioned distribution caps the ceiling at the buyer's valuation.

The bull case stings, and I have to address it honestly. Dome raised $5.2M and exited at $10–20M in four months — that is a clean 2–4x in the seed slot and meaningfully better at the pre-seed slot Oddpool occupies; a $500K check into an $8M acqui-hire is a 16x on capital with a sub-two-year hold, which is not the math I dismiss. The Anchorage Digital exception sits adjacent: regulated crypto infrastructure where permission-as-moat overrode the permissionless-leverage rule. I do not extend that exception here. Anchorage cleared on category-timing pre-consensus and on the regulatory moat itself becoming structural leverage; Oddpool clears on neither — prediction markets are now consensus post-Polymarket-election-cycle, and the "moat" is a content dataset that FinFeedAPI's CoinAPI team can replicate from the same public venues. The 1x-downside / 10,000x-upside math does not work when the upside is structurally capped at a $20M acqui-hire by one of two buyers. The verdict flips to Invest if Oddpool ships an open-source SDK or public symbology standard that gets organic adoption from independent quant developers outside the institutional outbound channel — that is the one verifiable condition that would convert this from labor leverage to code leverage, and the dossier shows no such artifact today.
