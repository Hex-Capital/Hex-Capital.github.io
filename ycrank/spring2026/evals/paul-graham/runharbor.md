# Harbor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Albert Cai spent four years inside clinical trials at Biolinq, helping take a wearable biosensor from prototype through first-in-human studies to FDA De Novo authorization. He wasn't reading about EDC systems on TechCrunch. He was sitting at research sites watching coordinators spend hours per subject per day transcribing data from source documents into software designed in the 1990s. Then he declined medical school to build the replacement. That's not a manufactured startup idea. That's what organic problem discovery looks like -- the founder was literally embedded in the pain for years before deciding to fix it.

The schlep here is what makes me pay attention. Clinical trial data capture sits at the intersection of every kind of complexity founders unconsciously avoid: FDA audit trail requirements, 21 CFR Part 11 validation, HIPAA, GDPR, and the fact that any extraction error in a pivotal trial could torpedo an entire drug approval. This is why the incumbents -- Medidata, Veeva, Oracle -- have been sitting on architectures from the late '90s with effectively no disruption for twenty-five years. Everyone in health-tech knows EDC systems are terrible. Every clinical research coordinator complains about them. But the regulatory infrastructure required to replace them is so grinding that smart engineers unconsciously look the other way. Harbor is doing what Stripe did with payments: not working around the compliance burden, but building it into the product as a core feature. They ship a full Verification & Validation package with every license. They aren't trying to avoid the schlep. They're charging through it.

The user pull evidence is unusually strong for pre-seed. Six named customers. Five live clinical studies. A 1,600-subject randomized trial configured in one week versus the legacy vendor quote of months. Specific customer quotes with names attached -- not anonymous testimonials, but CEOs of biotech companies saying "this was a two-month project and Harbor did it in two days." Once a trial's EDC is configured and data collection begins, mid-study migration is operationally impossible because of regulatory audit trail requirements. These aren't users who might churn. They're locked in by the physics of their own regulatory obligations. That's the kind of structural demand you can't manufacture.

The team composition works. Albert brings the domain expertise -- biomedical engineering degree, four years of clinical ops, understands FDA regulatory pathways firsthand. Nathan was employee number one at Jupiter, a YC S19 company, where he built production systems from scratch. Seventy-four public GitHub repos. Google and Ramp internships. The pair met at Michigan, which means the co-founder relationship predates the company -- they aren't strangers who teamed up for Demo Day. As a two-person team maintaining regulatory compliance infrastructure while serving six customers across five live studies, they're already demonstrating the kind of resource efficiency that keeps you default alive. But the bear case is real: Medidata and Veeva are both announcing AI features in 2025-2026. If the incumbents' AI additions are good enough, Harbor's window narrows. The counterargument is that bolting AI onto a 25-year-old architecture is fundamentally different from building AI-native from day one -- and more importantly, the incumbents can't match Harbor's flat monthly pricing without cannibalizing their own per-subject, per-site revenue model. That's a structural constraint on incumbent response, not just a speed advantage.

The pricing disruption deserves its own note. Legacy EDC vendors charge per subject, per site, per CRF -- complexity that makes it nearly impossible for small sponsors and academic sites to predict costs. Harbor charges a flat monthly fee. Free for academics, $2,000/month for commercial. This is the insurgent pricing model that creates distribution leverage: the free tier gets you into academic medical centers, where researchers become familiar with the product, then carry that preference to commercial sponsors. It's the same playbook that worked for Slack, Dropbox, and every other bottom-up enterprise tool. The question is whether clinical trial buyers follow the same adoption patterns as software teams. My instinct says yes, because the decision-makers are increasingly the researchers themselves, not just procurement departments.

What would make this a great investment? If Harbor can accumulate enough regulatory validation artifacts and domain-specific extraction accuracy across therapeutic areas before the incumbents' AI features mature, the switching costs create a moat that compounds over time. The flat pricing model could make them the default choice for the thousands of small-to-mid-size trials that Medidata and Veeva don't serve well. And the free academic tier could become a distribution channel that no incumbent can replicate without destroying their own economics. The bones are here. Organic discovery, massive schlep, real users, technical founders who build, and a pricing wedge into an underserved segment of a multi-billion dollar market.
