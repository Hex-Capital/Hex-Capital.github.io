# Superset -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Superset: every major AI coding tool shipped multi-agent features in the same two-week window in February 2026. Cursor's cloud agents, Claude Code's Agent Teams, Codex CLI, Gemini, Grok Build — all of them, at once. That's not a market being born. That's consensus crystallizing in real time. And Superset is building an orchestration layer on top of it. I've spent my entire career looking for the companies that are not popular, that are difficult to assess, that make people uncomfortable. This is the opposite of that. AI developer tools is the most competed, most funded, most consensus category in technology right now. Cursor just raised at a $29.3 billion valuation. Cognition is at $10.2 billion. When I see a space where every major platform player is racing to absorb the exact capability you're building — and doing so as a bundled feature of products developers already pay for — that's not a contrarian opportunity. That's a timing trap.

When I evaluate founders, I always start with the story. Not the business, the person. How did you get here? What broke you and what did you build from the rubble? The dossier tells me these are three ex-YC CTOs with strong resumes — Amazon, Google, Meta, ServiceNow — who met through the YC network and decided to build together. That's a perfectly respectable origin. But it's not the kind of biographical thread I look for. Two of them left YC W25 companies to start this, and one of those companies — Onlook — has 23.4k GitHub stars and appears to still be active. When I see founders leaving a working thing for a hotter thing this quickly, my pattern recognition fires in the wrong direction. The founders I back are typically solving a problem that's been gnawing at them for years, something forged through personal experience that makes them the only person who could build this specific company. I don't see that biographical inevitability here. I see talented engineers chasing an emerging market window, which is exactly the kind of founder motion I've trained myself to distinguish from genuine conviction.

The bull case deserves real engagement, because it's not nothing. Kiet Ho has demonstrated a rare skill: the ability to build open-source developer tools that achieve massive organic adoption. Getting 23.4k stars on Onlook and 6,500 stars on Superset within weeks of launch is genuinely impressive distribution instinct. If AI coding agents fragment across many providers — which is plausible — a neutral, agent-agnostic orchestration layer could become the Kubernetes of this space: the standard that persists precisely because it belongs to no single vendor. The team ships fast, they understand developer distribution, and the open-source Apache 2.0 approach builds community goodwill that proprietary tools can't replicate. If Cursor's cloud agents remain locked to their IDE, and Claude Code's Agent Teams remain locked to Claude, and every developer ends up using three or four different agents, Superset's neutral positioning becomes structural rather than cosmetic. That's a real path to something meaningful.

But I can't get past the structural issues. The core product — running multiple terminal sessions in isolated Git worktrees with a monitoring dashboard — is a thin convenience layer, not deep infrastructure. Any IDE, any terminal emulator, any agent provider could absorb this. Claude Code already did, in February. The product is free with no revenue model, which means there's no signal that anyone would pay for this when the underlying agent providers are incentivized to bundle equivalent functionality. And the competitive landscape isn't a few underfunded startups — it's Anthropic, Microsoft, and Anysphere, companies with billions in resources and direct control over the platforms Superset depends on. Being one native feature release away from partial disintermediation is not the kind of moat I look for.

There's also nothing here that connects to economic access or making invisible systems visible — the through-line that runs from Uber to Affirm to Carta to Flock Safety in my portfolio. Superset makes already-productive software engineers marginally more productive. That's fine as a product, but it doesn't create opportunity for people who currently lack it, and it doesn't expose any opaque system to transparency. When I'm writing my own check, I need to feel that the company is doing something that matters beyond the immediate market — that it's creating positive-sum outcomes for people who need them. I don't feel that here.

This is a competent team building a well-executed product in a space that doesn't fit how I invest. The founders are talented but trend-following rather than conviction-driven. The market is consensus, not contrarian. The moat is thin. The impact dimension is absent. I'd pass, and I'd tell them honestly: you're building for a market that will either be absorbed by platforms or fragment in ways that make standalone orchestration tools unnecessary. If they prove me wrong — if agent fragmentation persists and Superset becomes the neutral standard — I'll tip my hat. But I'm not writing a check on that thesis from my own money.
