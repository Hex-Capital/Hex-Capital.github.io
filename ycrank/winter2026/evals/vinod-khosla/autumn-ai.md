# Autumn AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The tagline tells you everything: "Stalk your prospects at scale." This is not a company trying to replace how an industry works. It's a company trying to make sales reps slightly faster at something they already do. If Autumn succeeds beyond anyone's wildest expectations — captures massive market share, becomes the default signal intelligence platform — the result is that B2B outbound emails get sent with better timing and more personalization. Sales cycles shorten by some percentage. That is the ceiling. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The consequence of success is a solid SaaS business in a $5 billion market that already has four incumbents each doing over $150 million in revenue. I have spent forty years investing, and the pattern I see most often is talented engineers who've reduced their probability of failure to the point where even winning doesn't matter much.

What concerns me more than the market size is the absence of any structural disruption thesis. The dossier is unusually candid: "No structural barrier identified at this stage that would prevent incumbents from replicating this approach as an add-on feature." ZoomInfo has already launched AI-driven prospecting via Copilot. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, the structural insight was that TCP/IP routing was architecturally incompatible with Cisco's legacy switching infrastructure — Cisco's CTO publicly said they'd never build a TCP/IP router. That architectural incompatibility was the moat. Here, ZoomInfo, 6sense, Apollo, and Demandbase can all add real-time public signal monitoring as a feature layer tomorrow without cannibalizing their core business. The core technology — monitoring public APIs, applying LLMs to extract intent — is described in the dossier itself as "technically reproducible" and "increasingly commoditized." When incumbents can replicate your innovation without disrupting their own business model, you don't have a disruption — you have a feature.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. LLMs have genuinely crossed a capability threshold that makes unstructured public signal extraction fundamentally different from what static contact databases provide. If Autumn builds something closer to a real-time intelligence graph — where the compounding effect of monitoring millions of public signals across LinkedIn, GitHub, X, filings, and conferences creates a living map of buying intent that static databases structurally cannot replicate — there's a world where this becomes a new category rather than a feature. Vishnu's experience building sourcing technology at Sierra Ventures gives him direct exposure to the problem, and Shiv's NeurIPS-level research chops could translate into genuinely superior signal extraction. At option-value scale, if they discover that incumbents' data-licensing business models create a structural conflict with open-signal aggregation — similar to how Kodak couldn't embrace digital photography because it would destroy film revenue — there's a path. But the dossier provides no evidence this structural conflict exists. The incumbents are already adding AI signal features without any business model tension.

Both founders are technically strong — Columbia CS, NeurIPS presentations, SandboxAQ AI residency, contributions to LMCache. These are good athletes. But good athletes executing a safe playbook is exactly what I warn against. Where is the contrarian courage? Nobody is telling them this is impossible or crazy. The sales intelligence market is well-understood, the buyers are known, the workflow is mapped. They're bringing better AI to an existing process. That's the definition of incremental. I look for the signal where experts actively dismiss the approach — Cisco's CTO saying TCP/IP routing would never happen, the food industry saying plant-based meat was a joke. Here, the market is welcoming them with open arms, which means the innovation is non-threatening to the established order. Additionally, both founders share identical backgrounds — Columbia CS, AI/ML research — with no go-to-market co-founder on a team building a product whose entire value proposition is sales workflow. That's a gene pool gap.

The timing argument — that LLMs make this newly feasible — is real but not proprietary. Every B2B AI startup in this YC batch is riding the same LLM capability wave. When the timing thesis belongs to everyone, it belongs to no one. I want to see a company positioned at the leading edge of a curve that hasn't reached mainstream awareness. AI-powered sales tools reached mainstream awareness approximately two years ago. These founders are smart enough to work on something where success would reshape how the world operates. This isn't it.
