# General Aviation -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ben Frank is a pilot, a licensed aircraft mechanic, an MIT aero '14, and the CCO who talked to ~200 helicopter operators at Rotor Technologies -- he is *of* aviation in exactly the way I look for, and yet General Aviation is pitching me the single most consensus narrative in transportation policy right now: "fix ATC by connecting aircraft to the internet" in the same month the DOT publicly announced a new ATC system (CNN, May 8, 2025) and Iridium consolidated Aireon for $366.7M (FlightGlobal, May 2026). The signature pairing here is a domain-native solo operator pointed at a market the whole world is already loudly pointing at -- right founder, wrong moment for my thesis.

The Founder-Surfaced-From-Lived-Experience ladder runs from Palmer Luckey at the top -- Oculus shipped to scale before Anduril, then defense from a personal patriotic conviction -- through Demis Hassabis in the middle, the polymath who got the Known-Exception override despite the credential stack -- down to the consensus YC AI-agent darling at the bottom, where ex-FAANG PMs discovered the problem in a memo. Ben Frank sits firmly between Luckey and Hassabis, closer to Luckey: pilot license + A&P mechanic ticket + three aviation companies (Rotabull, Seer Aerospace acquired by Aviation Week, Rotor Technologies CCO on the R550X autonomous helicopter) is the kind of biography I will sit outside the door for. The dossier specific that pins General Aviation high on this rung is that Frank can both fly the airplane and turn a wrench on it -- that's the Luckey-in-the-garage signal, not the McKinsey-deck signal.

The Magically-Weird ladder is where General Aviation collapses, and this is the dimension that decides the check. The HIGH rung is Crusoe burning North Dakota gas flares for Bitcoin in 2018, a sentence that made every credible VC cringe before it anchored Stargate; the MID rung is Anduril, unsexy but with the Luckey halo; the LOW rung is the YC AI-agent darling with six tier-1 funds circling. General Aviation places near that LOW rung -- Starlink is already running FAA trials at 17 sites (avitrader.com, Mar 5, 2025), Verizon holds a $2.4B FENS contract (CNN, Feb 27, 2025), Iridium just paid $366.7M for the rest of Aireon, and Peraton is the FAA's prime integrator across 52 vendors (FedScoop, 2025). When the President, Musk, the DOT, and three public-company incumbents are all in the same room talking about "satellite ATC," I am not finding General Aviation before it becomes consensus -- I am arriving last.

Single-CEO authority is where General Aviation maxes out -- the SpaceX/Musk HIGH rung versus the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll feud LOW rung, with Anduril's Luckey-Schimpf duo in the middle; Frank is a solo founder, the cleanest possible answer to the "who's the tiebreaker" question I've quoted out of Tim Ferriss #780 a hundred times. Capital-Intensive-Unsexy-Regulated places General Aviation high too, between the SpaceX HIGH rung (capital-intensive frontier hardware) and the Crusoe MID rung (energy infrastructure) -- DO-178/DO-254 avionics assurance, RTCA standards, FAA TSO approval, sovereign ANSP procurement is exactly the lane that scares off the Stanford/Harvard-Goldman-McKinsey deck-makers. But Physical-World Verifiability is the second collapse: against the Flock Safety HIGH rung (I read the Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons café) and the Niantic Ingress MID rung (I played the game for three years before I wrote the check), General Aviation has no app, no constellation, no transponder I can hold, no signed LOI, and a team size of zero with Hiring listed as False -- it sits near the dev-tools-abstraction LOW rung.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation does not displace Aireon or Starlink; Frank pivots General Aviation into an application/protocol layer reselling Iridium or Starlink capacity to second-tier fleet operators and one or two smaller ANSPs, ends 2028 between $0.5M and $2M ARR, and is acquired by Peraton, Iridium, or a Verizon FENS subcontractor for $10-30M as a regulatory-positioning tuck-in -- a respectable outcome for Frank, a flat outcome for a seed check. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a domain-native solo founder in a maximally regulated category gets the meetings and earns the certification, but the constellation economics and procurement concentration force partnership with exactly the incumbents he was trying to leapfrog.

The strongest bear case against my own pass is that General Aviation is an Anduril analogue I am pattern-failing to recognize -- a regulated, capital-intensive, founder-domain-native swing at a sovereign-procurement market that the smart money refuses on aesthetic and capital-intensity grounds. Frank is genuinely a Luckey-shaped founder in an industry I respect, and the Anduril check in 2017 had its own "everyone is talking about defense modernization" backdrop. The reason this bear case doesn't win is that Anduril in 2017 had no Aireon-equivalent incumbent -- Palmer was building hardware from a blank field, whereas General Aviation is entering a board already covered by Iridium's $366.7M consolidated piece, Starlink's active FAA trials, and Verizon's $2.4B FENS award. The flip condition I'll watch for: if General Aviation announces a named LOI or pilot contract with a sovereign ANSP -- NAV CANADA, NATS, ENAV, or a Gulf-state authority -- inside 12 months, I'll re-open the file, because that would prove Frank's regulatory social license is converting into procurement traction that Aireon/Iridium can't match.
