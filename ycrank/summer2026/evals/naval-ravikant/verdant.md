# Verdant -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Verdant sells AI plan-review software to municipal permitting departments through Calendly demos and RFP responses against Accela and Tyler — which means Aidan Ng and Jason Yi are building the exact business architecture I have spent twenty years explaining I do not fund. The high-score dimension here is the founder-trio default; the low-score dimension is leverage architecture, and that pairing names Verdant as a labor-and-capital-leverage govtech SaaS dressed in AI vocabulary, structurally opposite the code-and-media businesses my ledger compounds on.

**Twitter** (2007) is the load-bearing anchor on leverage architecture — anyone could post, the platform charged nothing, marginal cost of replication was zero, and the distribution required permission from no gatekeeper. Verdant's distribution requires permission from every single 19,000 US municipality individually, each gated by a multi-vendor RFP that the dossier itself documents — City of Astoria evaluated five vendors per the Civic IQ Blog, and Accela's average contract is $382K signed on multi-year cycles. The only contact mechanism on verdantapp.com is a Calendly link to aidan@verdantapp.com. That is labor leverage — humans selling to procurement officers — wrapped around a code product. The leverage check fails at the first question, before the founder section loads.

**Pinterest** is my standing reminder that "I don't get it" is the wrong tiebreaker when leverage architecture and asymmetric math both pass — but the inverse rule holds here: when specific knowledge is conspicuously absent in a non-leveraged business, the absence is the signal, not a tiebreaker against my taste. Aidan Ng is a Georgetown undergraduate Government major affiliated with the Global Lab; Jason Yi's LinkedIn headline could not be retrieved and a different Jason Yi works in Talent Acquisition at the adjacent competitor PermitFlow. Neither founder has documented planning-department operating experience. Verdant's own site claims "backgrounds in public service" without naming a single prior role. Compared against Atwood and Spolsky shipping for the developers they were, or Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley ML PhD specifically in retrieval, Verdant's founder-product-market fit is policy-curious undergraduate, not lived practitioner.

On marketplace mechanics: each Verdant municipality is economically independent of the next — Pasadena's deployment does not make Tulsa's more valuable, the way the Nth Uber rider made the (N-1) drivers more valuable. The asymmetric upside math that justifies one-X downside requires a compounding mechanism Verdant's single-sided per-jurisdiction SaaS architecture does not have. **GovWell**'s 130+ municipal customers across 34 states with $25M from Insight Partners in May 2026 demonstrates the ceiling — a horizontal SaaS govtech business, not a 10,000x outcome.

On market size and pivot room: the Dataintelo $1.85B North America SAM across 19,000 municipalities is real but narrow and vertical-locked — for the score to flip to Invest tier on this dimension, Verdant would have to credibly describe expansion into adjacent permission-issuing markets (environmental review, federal grants, insurance underwriting) that the dossier does not mention. The "first idea never works" axiom means I need to see the second idea's runway, and on a 2-person team with no disclosed customers selling into 18-month RFP cycles, the runway to a second idea expires before the first contract closes.

On the founder trio: integrity reads neutral by default — no contested exits, no self-promoting honesty claims, just a Georgetown student page and an unverifiable second LinkedIn. The intelligence-and-energy legs cannot be assessed from the dossier. **Notion**'s Ivan Zhao had been rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence before any check; Verdant gives me no comparable artifact of multi-year obsession. I default to 7/14 and move on.

Forecast, 36 months: Verdant is either dead or pivoted out of municipal plan-review into applicant-side permit prep (the PermitFlow lane) at sub-$500K ARR, because GovWell's 130+ customers and $34.5M total raised compound RFP wins and reference accounts faster than a 2-person team can close a single jurisdiction; the procurement-cycle math is what kills it, not the technology. The exit, if any, is an acqui-hire into GovWell or PermitFlow for the AI tooling, not a standalone outcome.

The bull case worth taking seriously is **Anchorage Digital** — I funded a permissioned, regulated, sales-led business when category-timing conviction outweighed leverage architecture, and one could argue Verdant is the AI-permit-review version: AI plan-review is technically real, incumbent contracts are churning per the YC description, and GovWell's Insight-led round corroborates the timing. The case stings because the category window may be real. But Anchorage cleared on extreme specific knowledge (Diogo Mónica, ex-Square security) and on regulatory moat that itself became leverage; Verdant offers Georgetown undergraduate Government coursework against a $25M-Series-A competitor with 130+ deployed customers already accreting the jurisdictional code-coverage dataset that would have been Verdant's only future moat. The category-timing exception requires the founder to be the rare practitioner who can compound inside the regulatory permission structure — Aidan Ng's dossier does not establish that, and the competitor is two years ahead inside the same window. Verdict flips to Invest only if Verdant publishes three named municipal contracts within six months — until then, this is the labor-leverage govtech bet I structurally pass on.
