# Avoice -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

"Harvey for Architecture" is a pitch that tells you everything you need to know about where this company sits in the consensus spectrum -- and it's not where I want to be. When a founding team leads with a comparison to a hot category-defining company in an adjacent vertical, they're explicitly signaling that the idea is validated, patterned, and legible to every VC in the room. That's the opposite of what I look for. My best investments -- Venmo, Solana, Teamshares -- were ideas that made smart people squint and ask "why would anyone want that?" An AI documentation tool for architects in 2026 makes everyone nod. The market has already priced this in. Every professional services vertical is getting its "Harvey for X" company right now. The fact that Swapp AI has $21.3M, Togal.AI has $22.6M, and Cove.tool has $36.6M tells you this is a crowded battleground, not an undiscovered frontier.

Chawin Asavasaetakul is clearly a capable operator -- Stanford MS&E, serial founder with Yindii hitting 700K+ users and profitability in Hong Kong, an edtech exit at $1M+ revenue, a coatings business at $15M. He spent two years building architecture sales teams before founding Avoice. That's the "sane" part, and it's legitimately strong. Multiple ventures across multiple geographies with demonstrated ability to scale and exit -- this is someone who can build. But a sane person with a sane idea produces a very different expected return than a sane person with an insane idea. The pairing here gives me a competent founder executing in a consensus category, which means I'm competing with every other investor for returns that will be competed down to market rates.

Here's the structural problem that dominates my analysis: remove AI from Avoice and you have nothing. There is no painting crew. There is no parking garage with installed hardware. There is no protocol layer. The software IS the business, and that's exactly the category where moats are dissolving fastest. The dossier itself names the risk clearly -- "Large language model capabilities are broadly available, and a well-funded competitor with architecture domain expertise could build competing features." Autodesk has every architect on the planet already using Revit and AutoCAD. When Autodesk ships AI documentation features -- and they will -- they reach the entire market overnight without a single sales call. The "Studio Library" that learns from a firm's past projects creates modest switching costs, but this is Birchbox-era thinking about data moats. Accumulated project data is useful but not the kind of compounding lock-in that survives a platform incumbent deciding to enter your space. My Birchbox lesson haunts this evaluation: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: architecture is uniquely specialized -- building codes, MasterFormat specifications, cross-sheet coordination protocols -- and this domain knowledge is genuinely hard to replicate. Small firms (110,000+ worldwide) are desperate for help competing with large studios, creating a massive fragmented market hungry for vertical solutions. If Avoice achieves deep workflow integration and becomes the operating system for architectural practice, the switching costs become real over years of accumulated institutional knowledge. The $300M+ in active projects managed on the platform suggests genuine usage. And the founders' multi-venture track record proves they can execute on GTM in fragmented markets. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) domain specialization creates a durable moat against both Autodesk and general-purpose AI, (b) small architecture firms will pay enough per seat to support venture-scale economics despite tight budgets, and (c) Avoice can win the race against well-funded competitors like Swapp AI who are already deeper in the Autodesk ecosystem. I don't believe all three simultaneously, but I respect the argument on domain depth.

The company also fails my "primitive or application" test. Avoice isn't creating a new layer that others build on -- it's an application sitting on top of existing LLMs, existing CAD tools, and existing architectural workflows. Compare this to Helium, which created a decentralized wireless protocol that IoT applications build on top of, or even Venmo, which created a new peer-to-peer payment primitive. There's no compounding network effect here. Each new architecture firm using Avoice doesn't make the product fundamentally more valuable for other firms. The firm-specific learning is siloed by design. This caps the "infinity story" -- at best, Avoice becomes a dominant vertical SaaS tool for architects, which is a good outcome but not an unbounded one, and frankly it's an outcome more likely captured by Autodesk adding features than by a startup doing enterprise sales to price-sensitive small firms one demo at a time.
