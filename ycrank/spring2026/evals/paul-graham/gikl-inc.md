# Gikl, Inc -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about ScienceSwarm is that the idea sounds wonderful. "Accelerating scientific discovery with AI." "Turning millions of people into scientists the way coding assistants turned them into developers." It's the kind of thing where everyone in the room nods and says yes, we need that. And that's exactly what makes me uneasy. The best startup ideas provoke confusion or dismissal on first contact. When I hear an idea and my first reaction is "obviously good," my second reaction is to wonder why it hasn't already been competed away. Google has already deployed AI Co-Scientist at 17 DOE National Labs. OpenAI is building an automated researcher. Elicit has 400K monthly users and $33M in funding. The space is crowded with well-resourced players, and the consensus that AI-for-science is important is already priced in. ScienceSwarm's specific angle — crowdsourced citizen science augmented by AI — is the one unclaimed position, which is interesting. But an unclaimed position in a hot market is different from an idea that repels people. Nobody hears this and thinks it's crazy. They think it's noble.

The founders are technically formidable in ways that matter. Vajda ran Media Generation at Meta for over a decade — Emu, Movie Gen, the generative AI infrastructure behind Instagram and AR/VR. Yamamoto was Senior Staff in the Core Llama group at Meta Superintelligence Labs, and before that published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters and co-authored with a Fields Medalist. These are not people cosplaying as AI researchers. They spent a combined twenty years at the frontier of AI at one of the world's most capable organizations. And Yamamoto went through the full scientific training pipeline — undergrad physics at Stanford, PhD at Rice, postdoc at the National High Magnetic Field Lab — so he knows firsthand what the 12-year path to becoming a contributing scientist looks like. That's real. What I can't tell from the dossier is whether this company emerged from a specific moment of frustration — Yamamoto hitting the wall of literature review during his postdoc, say, or watching brilliant non-physicists get turned away from problems they could have helped solve — or whether two senior Meta researchers sat down and asked "what important thing should we build with AI?" The framing on the website reads like a thesis statement, not a war story. And the analogy to coding assistants, while clever, papers over a fundamental difference: code gives you instant feedback, science often doesn't give you feedback for years.

What I'd want to see in a 10-minute interview is whether these founders have the running back quality — not just the ability to build sophisticated AI systems, which they clearly have, but the ability to do the unglamorous work of building a community from zero. The cold-start problem for a platform like this is brutal. You need problems worth solving, contributors capable of solving them, and a verification mechanism that gives the solutions credibility — and none of those things exist without the other two. The historical precedents (Foldit, the Polymath Project) succeeded through extraordinary curation and the personal involvement of famous scientists. Can Vajda and Yamamoto recruit those first fifty contributors one by one? Can they personally moderate the quality of submissions? Can they figure out which of the six scientific domains will produce the first breakthrough and focus relentlessly on it? Those are the questions that matter, and the dossier gives me no signal either way. There are no user counts, no growth metrics, no anecdotes about early adopter obsession. The platform is live, which shows they shipped — that's a real positive. But a live platform without visible usage is a prototype, not a product.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because it's not trivial. If LLMs have genuinely crossed a capability threshold — and Yamamoto and Vajda would know better than almost anyone whether they have — then the citizen-scientist-plus-AI model could work for the first time in history. Every incumbent in this space is building either a search tool for existing researchers or an autonomous AI scientist that replaces researchers. Nobody is building the community platform that treats AI as an amplifier for human scientific intuition rather than a substitute for it. If ScienceSwarm nails this, the network effects could be extraordinary — every solved problem validates the platform, attracts more contributors, generates training data that makes the AI better, which enables harder problems to be tackled. It's the Stack Overflow flywheel for scientific discovery. And the 9-year co-founder relationship at Meta dramatically reduces the interpersonal risk that kills most startups. These are two people who've worked closely together through multiple product cycles and organizational changes. That's worth something real.

But here's where I land: the idea was chosen, not discovered. The pitch is a thesis about what the world needs, not a story about a problem the founders couldn't stop thinking about. The competitive moat is theoretical — community network effects that don't yet exist, a data advantage that hasn't been accumulated. And the founders, for all their extraordinary technical depth, are senior corporate research leaders stepping into a domain (community platform building, consumer growth, marketplace dynamics) that requires a completely different set of instincts than directing a research team at Meta. When I backed the Collisons at Stripe, they were building infrastructure they personally needed as developers, tackling a schlep that repelled everyone else. The schlep here — convincing the scientific establishment that crowdsourced AI-augmented contributions from non-experts have value — is real, but I don't see evidence the founders have started grappling with it yet. I'd want to track this company through a batch and see if the founders demonstrate the learning velocity their credentials suggest they're capable of. But I'm not writing a check today.
