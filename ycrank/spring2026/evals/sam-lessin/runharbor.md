# Harbor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about clinical trial EDC software — every health-tech investor I know thinks AI is going to transform it. Every single one. This is not a contrarian bet. This is the exact opposite of a contrarian bet. "AI automates manual data entry in a regulated industry" is probably the most consensus pitch in B2B health tech right now. Castor raised $65M doing roughly the same thing. Clinion is doing it from India at a fraction of the cost. And then the real kicker: Veeva announced AI Agents rolling out across all applications starting December 2025, expanding across R&D in 2026. Medidata announced AI-driven insights embedding into study planning and execution. The incumbents aren't asleep. They're cloud-native, subscription-based companies with thousands of customers who will get AI features bundled into their existing contracts at marginal cost. This is the exact pattern I keep screaming about — every time you get pitched "better [incumbent product] with AI," you have to ask: is this perfect for the incumbent? Veeva is a $35-billion-plus public company. This is perfect for Veeva.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Medidata and Veeva make their money from complexity — per-subject, per-site, per-CRF pricing, plus fat professional services fees for database builds that take 8-12 weeks. Harbor's flat $2K/month pricing and 10-minute protocol-to-CRF generation directly attacks that revenue model. The innovator's dilemma argument says incumbents can't adopt disruptive pricing because it would cannibalize their own base. And Albert Cai isn't some outsider — four years at Biolinq doing actual FDA-track clinical work, watching coordinators waste hours on manual data entry, is real domain pain. The 1,600-subject trial configured in one week versus the competitor's multi-week timeline — that's a genuine 10x. If Harbor can get enough trials running on their platform before Veeva's AI catches up, switching costs mid-study create a real wedge. But here's why I don't buy it: Veeva isn't Oracle. Veeva IS the disruptor that killed Oracle Clinical. They're not some dinosaur that can't ship product. They can layer AI as a premium feature without destroying their pricing model, and their distribution advantage is insurmountable at enterprise scale. Harbor gets the scraps — small biotechs and academic sites that can't afford Medidata — and that's a fine business, but it's not a venture-scale outcome from where I sit.

The primitive test is where this really falls apart for me. Harbor isn't creating a new behavior. It's making an existing behavior faster. Clinical data capture existed before Harbor and will exist in roughly the same form after — someone collects data at a research site and puts it into a database. The AI makes the transcription step faster. That's a feature, not a category. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a social behavior. Patreon created direct creator monetization. Harbor creates... faster form filling? When I ask "does this become 100x larger," the answer involves capturing more of the existing EDC market, not creating a new market that didn't exist. Market share capture, not category creation. The ceiling is known.

Structurally, this is a software tool that wraps document extraction AI — the exact kind of business I've been saying publicly will get commoditized. The regulatory compliance layer (21 CFR Part 11, HIPAA, GDPR) is real work and real barrier, but it's table stakes for anyone serious about this market, not a proprietary moat. General-purpose document extraction models improve every quarter. The dossier itself flags this: "any well-funded health-tech startup or incumbent could build AI-assisted data capture." Correct. The founders seem solid — Albert has the domain experience, Nathan has the engineering chops from Jupiter and Google — but the ratio is wrong. These are competent, sensible people with a competent, sensible idea. I'm looking for sane people with insane ideas, and "automate clinical data entry" is about as sane as ideas get. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "that's obviously a good idea," which means the upside is already in the price.

Credit where it's due: six named customers and five live studies at pre-seed is real signal. The testimonials from BioDynamik and NOVA Genomics describe genuine pain relief. Two-person team keeping burn tight and pricing transparently — that's capital discipline I respect. But capital discipline and early traction don't override the fundamental problem: this company is swimming directly into the teeth of two multi-billion-dollar incumbents who are building the same thing, in a category every health-tech VC is already excited about. There's no mispricing here. The market sees exactly what Harbor is. Pass.
