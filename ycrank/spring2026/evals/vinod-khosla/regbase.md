# Regbase -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Regbase is a regtech wrapper on agentic crawling whose lowest dimension is exactly the one I weight 30 of 100 — consequence magnitude — and whose highest is a mid-grade capital-risk profile dragged up only by Ethan Shea's ex-Vercel agentic infrastructure pedigree; that pairing makes Regbase a workflow-automation play for in-house counsel at Fortune 500s, not a regime-collapsing bet, and Natalie Aresta-Katz's own pitch ("Fortune 500 companies, global consultancies, and leading law firms") is a TAM description, not a civilizational redirection.

Consequence first, always. Set the **OpenAI** (2019) rung at the top — the success-state was a civilizational rewrite of cognitive labor, which is why I wrote $50M into a no-revenue company. The mechanism that makes that analogy load-bearing isn't AI per se; it's that OpenAI's win-state names a system that disappears (human-priced cognitive work at $15T of GDP, per my Fortune 2026-03-06 framing). Regbase's win-state is that compliance officers at Paul, Weiss-style firms catch a Brazilian draft bill three weeks earlier than a LexisNexis alert would have surfaced it. That is not a regime collapse; that is a recall-rate improvement on an existing workflow at one persona inside one cost center. Nothing in the Regbase dossier names an incumbent system that goes to zero — FiscalNote does not die because a startup indexes Portuguese statutes better, it dies because its ARR is already down 22% YoY on its own. The consequence ceiling here is a better tracker, not a vanished category.

The **sales-call-summarizer pass example** in my own pass library is the failure mechanism to watch: a domain expert from Company X automates the workflow they personally ran at Company X, leads with persona-specific efficiency gains, and is funded by any Tier-1 generalist because the capital ask is small and the success-state is a feature in a Salesforce roadmap. Regbase fits that pattern almost cleanly — Aresta-Katz oversaw ESG regulatory research at Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison and is now building software for the ESG regulatory researcher persona at Paul, Weiss-shaped firms. The dossier's own "Concentration on Paul, Weiss network for distribution" risk note concedes the loop. My TED Talk warning was specifically that experts extrapolate the past; Regbase's founder-market fit is built entirely on extrapolating one BigLaw research workflow forward, which is the wrong direction on this axis even though it reads as a strength in a generic founder-market-fit framework.

Technical risk in Regbase compounds the wrong way over 18 months. The wedge is "multilingual + sub-national + draft-bill" ingestion, but every legal-AI peer with capital — Legora at a $5.6B valuation (TechCrunch, April 2026), Harvey, FiscalNote with its new PolicyNote AI — can extend a crawler into Portuguese municipal websites in two engineering quarters. There is no falsifiable physics miracle here, no catalyst-yield problem like **KiOR** (~2008), no plasma-confinement problem like **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019). Shea's Vercel-grade agentic infrastructure is real engineering, but the moat compounds only if Regbase ships a citation-accuracy benchmark that FiscalNote and Legora cannot match — and the dossier shows zero published recall study as of May 2026.

Counterfactual on founder-defies-expert-consensus: for Regbase to flip a tier on this dimension, Aresta-Katz would need to have publicly contradicted the BigLaw orthodoxy she came from — argued, on record, that in-house ESG research itself should not exist, or that Paul, Weiss-style compliance review is structurally obsolete. Instead the dossier shows the inverse: Regbase exists to make that workflow faster for the same buyers. That is conformity tooling, not the instigator pattern I cited in "World Needs 12 Good Instigators to Address Climate Change."

Single-anchor parallel on founder learning rate: compare Regbase to **Slash** (2024), the YC-backed fintech where I wrote a $100M Series C specifically because the founders had a publicly documented Kanye-pivot story — the kind of "we were wrong about X" moment that proves learning rate above the noise floor. Regbase's three founders show none of that. There is no prior pivot, no public thesis update, no sold-company-and-pivoted-into-something-harder arc for Aresta-Katz, Shea, or Isiah Godoy. Absent evidence, I default to 7/15 — not punitive, but it cannot rescue the consequence score.

Capital profile is the cleanest tell. Regbase's ~$500K YC check and SaaS-economics roadmap mean any Tier-1 generalist can fund this; I am structurally over-resourced for the deal. Compare the $115M Series A I led into **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019), or the $105M co-led seed into **Genesis AI** (July 2025) — those checks existed because the capital risk repelled consensus VCs. Regbase has no equivalent wall. Sequoia, a16z, or Bessemer would write this; that is the test it fails.

36-month forecast: Regbase reaches $1–4M ARR serving ~15–30 mid-market law firms and ESG consultancies in the Paul, Weiss adjacency, then gets tuck-in acquired by FiscalNote, Thomson Reuters, or Wolters Kluwer for $25–70M between late 2027 and mid-2029 — a perfectly respectable YC outcome and a complete miss for my fund's math. The mechanism is straightforward: the multilingual crawler is a feature an incumbent buys cheaper than they build, the Paul, Weiss network produces enough revenue for the acquirer to underwrite, and no labor category gets displaced along the way.

The strongest bear-side objection to my pass — actually, the bull case I have to dismantle — is that FiscalNote's ARR is already down 22% YoY in its SEC 10-Q (FY2025), which proves the regtech incumbent layer is genuinely disruptable and a competent agentic-native team could ride that collapse to a $50M+ ARR business. Shea is a Staff Engineer from Vercel; Aresta-Katz lived the manual workflow; Godoy has the ESG consulting wedge. The Glean exception in my own portfolio says I will tolerate domain-expert founders when the consequence on Fortune-500 information asymmetry is large enough. The dismantle: Glean's consequence surface is every knowledge worker at every Fortune 500 — Aravind Jain's product unifies all enterprise information for tens of millions of seats. Regbase's consequence surface is the regulatory-research analyst seat, a five-figure-headcount global market, not seven-figure. The Glean exception requires consequence scale Regbase cannot produce even at maximum ambition. Verdict: pass. The single condition that flips me to invest: Regbase publishes a third-party-audited recall benchmark showing it catches 3x the sub-national/non-English regulatory developments versus FiscalNote and Legora within a 90-day window, AND signs three Fortune 500 General Counsels at $250K+ ACV with measurable headcount displacement — not augmentation — of in-house regulatory research staff. That is the labor-displacement frame from my Fortune 2025-07-01 "80% of jobs by 2030" thesis. Without it, this is a feature.
