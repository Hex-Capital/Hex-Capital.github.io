# Keyframe Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Parth Radia built "the world's first 3D call over production networks" at AT&T Labs and then ran a 30-person team at Ericsson on voice/video foundation models across a petascale human dataset before co-founding Keyframe Labs with his brother Kaahan -- that is the rarest thing in the dossier, and it is sitting inside the most crowded category I have looked at this batch. Keyframe Labs is a deep-founder bet wearing a consensus-category jersey, and that pairing is exactly the place where my framework gets uncomfortable in both directions at once.

Anduril/Luckey is the high rung on lived founder experience -- Palmer shipped VR to scale before he ever pointed a sensor at a battlefield, and that prior product is what made the defense pivot legible. Keyframe Labs has a real version of that pattern: Parth Radia's AT&T 3D-telepresence research and his Ericsson voice/video foundation-model leadership are the exact two prior chapters that should precede a real-time photoreal avatar API, and brother Kaahan's five years at Zipline shipping perception for autonomous drones is a credible real-time-systems complement. Where Keyframe Labs diverges from the Luckey rung is that the prior shipping was research and big-company infrastructure rather than a consumer artifact that detonated in the wild -- Parth led a team inside Ericsson, he did not ship the Oculus of talking-head video to a million users before this. That puts Keyframe Labs above the DeepMind-Hassabis polymath-pedigree midpoint and below the Luckey-after-Oculus ceiling.

The Y Combinator AI-agent darling I pass on every cycle is the low rung on "magically weird," and the failure mechanism is always the same: every credible VC is already in the room, the round prices in the outcome, and the moat reduces to the model. Keyframe Labs's dossier wears that pattern unmistakably -- five named direct competitors (Tavus at $24.1M raised, HeyGen at $95-100M ARR, Synthesia at $146M ARR, D-ID with 280K developers, Anam shipping the same real-time API positioning), a feature in YC "Launches of the Week," and a category projected at 30.73% CAGR that every generalist deck in this batch is quoting. "Find them before it becomes consensus" is the line, and Keyframe Labs is launching into a room that became consensus eighteen months ago. The $0.06/minute consumption price against $29/month subscription tiers is a real wedge, but a wedge is not a weirdness.

(Counterfactual on CEO authority.) The dossier names Parth as CEO and Kaahan as CTO, which is title-clear, but they are brothers and there is no public mention of equity split, vesting, or tiebreaker protocol. For the score to flip a tier here, I would need to see the operating agreement language or hear one of them describe a real disagreement they resolved -- the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll feud and the GameCrush activist-investor unwind both started inside relationships that looked clear on the org chart on Day 1. Brothers can be the best founding pair on earth or the worst; the dossier does not tell me which Keyframe Labs is.

(Mechanism on capital intensity over 18 months.) Keyframe Labs ships in-house foundation models -- Cosmo 1.5 in October 2025 and Persona-1 in January 2026 -- which means real GPU spend and a real training pipeline, not a thin wrapper. The compounding question is whether that spend converts into a data flywheel from accumulated multi-party video calls before HeyGen or Synthesia decide to release a real-time tier funded by their existing ARR. Keyframe Labs is meaningfully more capital-intensive than the average YC AI app I see, but it is structurally less defensible than the Crusoe-class energy-arbitrage swing that earns the top of this dimension.

(Single anchor on physical-world verifiability.) Niantic I bought because I had been playing Ingress since 2012 and felt the pull in my own hands; Keyframe Labs offers a free tier and an embed-code install, which means I can put a photoreal avatar in a browser tab this afternoon and judge the 500ms latency claim against my own ear. That is genuinely tactile -- more tactile than the average B2B API in this batch -- and Keyframe Labs gets real credit for it, with the caveat that "I can see it work" is the floor for a consumer-adjacent video product, not the ceiling.

My 36-month forecast for Keyframe Labs is acqui-hire by Tavus, HeyGen, or a foundation-model lab in the $30-80M range for the model weights and the Radia brothers' talent, rather than independent escape velocity past $20M ARR. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a deep founder pair in a category where the moat is the model and the incumbents have the ARR to fund a real-time tier the moment Keyframe Labs proves the wedge works. Parth and Kaahan get bought before they get to compound.

The strongest bear-killer I can build against my own pass is the HandUp exception logic inverted -- Parth Radia is not a memo founder, he is the rare case of a researcher who spent his actual professional decade on photoreal talking-head video, and "I am gifted with the ability to make these connections" should reward the founder who built the literal prior art for what he is now commercializing. The bear-killer stings. But HandUp earned the exception because the founder's biography mirrored my own homelessness origin, and DeepMind earned it because Hassabis compounded across four genuinely separate disciplines; Keyframe Labs's founder fit is deep but in-lane, and the in-lane category is consensus. The verdict flips to Invest if Parth surfaces two signed named-Fortune-500 LOIs at $50K+ ACV sourced through his Ericsson telco-channel relationships -- distribution that no Tavus or HeyGen can replicate would convert "deep founder in consensus category" into "deep founder with private distribution," which is the Anduril-shaped check I am paid to write.
