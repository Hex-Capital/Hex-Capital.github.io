# Sherpa -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Sherpa is ex-Lindy operators automating the growth-experiment workload they personally ran, dropped into a CRO category where the distribution-advantaged incumbents -- VWO, Optimizely, Kameleoon -- already ship LLM variant generation. Norbu Sonam tripled paywall conversion at Lindy running 50+ experiments by hand; that is real specific knowledge. But Sherpa is a JS snippet sold to marketing teams against a $100M+ Everstone rollup of Wingify and AB Tasty announced January 2026, which is the inverse of the leverage geometry I write checks on. The signature is high specific-knowledge / low permissionless-distribution -- an operator-automation play in a consolidating, capital-leveraged category.

**Twitter (2007)** is what permissionless media leverage looks like on the top rung -- anyone posts, the platform charges nothing, marginal replication cost is zero. Sherpa shares the surface trait that the product is code (one-line JS snippet, free 30-second audit as top-of-funnel), but the GTM motion underneath is B2B SaaS sold to growth teams at SMBs, with three named logos (Lindy, Insforge, Gauge) all sitting inside the founders' YC-adjacent network. The free audit is a clever acquisition hook, not a media-leverage distribution architecture -- nothing about Sherpa's snippet compounds without the founders paying capital or labor for the next customer. When VWO already ships GPT-3.5 copy generation and Kameleoon markets "AI for A/B Testing," Sherpa's code-leverage edge is parity, not asymmetry, and the actual scaling vector is enterprise sales motion -- the exact "labor and capital require permission" pattern (nav.al/product-media) I avoid.

The pattern I watch for at this rung is vertical SaaS without compounding user-added value -- the Nth customer leaves the (N-1)th customer's product experience unchanged. Sherpa's 7,338,896+ sessions analyzed is data-network, not user-network: a cross-customer model that might predict winning variants better, but each Sherpa customer's value does not increase when the next one installs the snippet. Lindy's +30% conversion lift does not make Gauge's 2.5x demo bookings more likely -- they share a model, not a network. This is precisely the single-sided architecture that lacks the 10,000x upside math from Knowledge Project #18, and it's why my **Perplexity** (2022) exception doesn't extend here -- Perplexity cleared three of five gates emphatically including extreme specific knowledge from Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD, while Sherpa clears one.

Norbu Sonam ran 50+ growth experiments and tripled paywall conversion at Lindy; Ethan Kinnan was Lindy's first forward-deployed engineer and Head of Partnerships. This is the dimension Sherpa earns. The mechanism that compounds over 18 months: every variant Sherpa ships against real production traffic teaches the model what works for SMB SaaS landing pages, and Norbu has the lived calibration to distinguish a junk lift from a real one. Where Sherpa diverges from **Stack Overflow** (2008) -- Atwood and Spolsky shipped a developer product to developers and the audience self-recruited -- is the buyer: Sherpa's user is a growth marketer at an SMB, not a builder, which means Norbu's specific knowledge translates into product quality but not into permissionless distribution among peers who would adopt without sales touch.

CRO software is $1.7B in 2025 projected to $5.0B by 2035 at 11.6% CAGR, A/B testing $1.5B to $4.82B at 11.2%. For Sherpa's market-size score to flip a tier upward, the pivot vector would need to be platform-shaped -- autonomous-agent infrastructure for any website action, not just conversion experiments. The dossier doesn't describe that adjacency; it describes a focused autonomous-CRO product. Pivot room exists into broader marketing automation or personalization, but with Everstone's Wingify+AB Tasty consolidation closing the enterprise channel, Sherpa's pivot has to happen inside SMB before a Series A, which is tight runway for the "first idea never works" axiom (Venture Hacks 2007-10).

The Buffett trio reads clean on Sherpa -- two Lindy alumni, no contested exits, no honesty-volunteering, no partner-equity ambiguity disclosed. Norbu's GitHub @norbusonam exists; Ethan's @ethan_kinnan ships on X. This is the dimension that defaults neutral when the dossier is factual rather than scarred -- nothing surfaces to penalize, nothing surfaces to trigger above-default. I would happily spend 10 years with these two; the question is whether the architecture they chose lets the next 10 years matter for an angel ledger.

My 36-month forecast: Sherpa gets acquired by Everstone (VWO/AB Tasty) or Optimizely for $20-50M as an autonomous-variant feature bolted onto the incumbent suite, with revenue plateauing in the $1-4M ARR band before the acquisition closes. The mechanism is straightforward -- the founders have real operator credibility and will ship faster than the incumbents internally, but the incumbents have the distribution and the seat-based revenue to absorb a feature acquisition before Sherpa accumulates enough cross-customer dataset to flip from feature to platform.

The bull case that stings: Norbu's "tripled paywall conversion" at Lindy is not a generic operator credential -- it is the rare specific-knowledge signal that, paired with the 7.3M-session dataset accumulating, could produce a model so much better than VWO's bolted-on GPT-3.5 wrapper that Sherpa becomes the developer-API default for autonomous CRO, the way **Perplexity** (2022) became the AI-search default despite single-sided architecture. Three of my five gates are not clearing the way Perplexity's did -- Sherpa's distribution is B2B SaaS to marketing teams, not API-to-developers; the specific knowledge is operator-deep but not PhD-rare; the category is consensus, not pre-consensus. The condition that flips my verdict to invest: an enterprise LOI from a non-YC customer (Fortune 1000 marketer) plus an open-source release of the variant-generation core with organic GitHub adoption by growth engineers outside the Lindy network -- both, not either, because together they would re-architect Sherpa from capital-leverage SaaS to code-leverage developer infrastructure.
