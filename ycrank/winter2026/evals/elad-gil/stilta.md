# Stilta -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the story before anything else. Solve Intelligence has $55M in funding, eight-figure ARR, and 400+ IP teams on six continents. Patlytics raised $21M with Google Gradient backing. &AI -- also YC-backed -- raised $6.5M from First Round for what is essentially the same product: an agentic AI agent for patent attorneys. Ankar pulled in $20M from Atomico. DeepIP raised $15M. At least 25 AI patent tools exist across the category. Stilta enters with $500K against competitors carrying 20-100x more capital and, in Solve Intelligence's case, a profitable business with hundreds of customers. When I see five well-funded competitors all attacking the same market within the same 12-month window, that's not a non-obvious market at a structural inflection -- that's a consensus bet. And consensus bets are where my framework breaks down, because there's no asymmetric information advantage left to exploit.

My entry point is always the structural moment: what changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this market possible now? The answer -- agentic AI architectures maturing in 2024-2025 -- is correct. Stilta explicitly says their approach "has not been technically possible until a few months ago." But when I backed Harvey in 2022, legal AI felt impossible to most investors. The structural moment was visible only if you tracked the GPT scaling curve. Patent AI in early 2026 is the opposite: the structural moment is visible to everyone, and the funding explosion proves it. A correct timing thesis that's also a consensus timing thesis doesn't generate the returns I'm looking for. The asymmetric returns live in the gap between what the market sees and where the adoption curve is actually heading -- that gap has already closed here.

The bull case deserves engagement because the team has real qualities. Four McKinsey/QuantumBlack engineers with direct experience building and deploying enterprise AI systems is exactly the profile that can navigate complex sales cycles. Oskar Block bootstrapping two ventures to $1M+ ARR demonstrates commercial execution, not just consulting polish. They already have paying customers described as "leading firms" in both Europe and the US -- at pre-seed, with a four-person team, that's a genuine execution signal. The auditability emphasis is structurally correct: patent work is high-stakes, regulated, and hallucination-intolerant, which creates a trust barrier that weaker entrants can't clear. If I squint, this resembles the "boring but critical" enterprise infrastructure pattern I've seen work at PagerDuty and Gusto -- deeply embedded workflow tools that become switching-cost-protected. If Stilta could establish deep integration with a dozen major firms before consolidation hits, they'd have defensible position regardless of competitor funding.

But the "boring but critical" pattern works precisely because other investors don't fund boring markets -- that deterrence IS the moat. PagerDuty faced funding resistance despite solving universal operations needs. Here, every growth-stage fund is writing checks into patent AI. The market structure that makes "boring" valuable -- investor apathy creating space for underfunded companies to build distribution quietly -- doesn't exist. Additionally, the product-to-distribution trajectory is constrained by the narrow user base. Patent attorneys are a small professional population compared to developers or designers. The "Cursor for patent practitioners" analogy understates this: Cursor's addressable user base is every software developer in the world. Stilta's is patent practitioners at law firms and corporate IP departments. There's a real platform expansion path from search to analysis to drafting to portfolio management, but Solve Intelligence is already executing on the full-stack version of this vision with 400+ customers providing compounding workflow data.

The plan also looks like it requires multiple things to go right simultaneously: building agentic AI reliable enough for legal-grade patent work, winning enterprise sales against funded competitors with established customer bases, and securing access to patent databases where incumbents like Clarivate and Questel control proprietary data assets. That's not a single-miracle company -- it's a multi-miracle bet where the team must outexecute on technology, distribution, AND data access, all with 20-100x less capital than their nearest competitors. The McKinsey network provides warm introductions, but enterprise legal sales cycles are measured in quarters, and a four-person team can only run so many concurrent enterprise deals while building product.

I respect the execution velocity and the founder profiles here. But my framework is clear: a well-understood market with obvious sizing, a consensus "why now," and 20+ funded competitors pursuing similar approaches is the low-score anchor on my primary evaluation criterion. The structural moment is real, but the information advantage is gone.
