# CellType -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most interesting thing about CellType isn't the technology — it's the structural contradiction at its core. David van Dijk invented Cell2Sentence, published it at ICML, open-sourced it under Apache 2.0, co-developed the scaled version with Google DeepMind, and then started a company to commercialize it. Meanwhile, Google has a $600 million drug discovery spinout called Isomorphic Labs and access to the exact same methodology. This is the kind of situation where you have to ask: if the technology works, who captures the value? Van Dijk built the frontier, but he published the map for anyone to follow. That's either scientific nobility or a competitive vulnerability that will define the company's trajectory. I've seen both outcomes.

Let me set that aside and start where I always start: the consequence of success. If CellType's approach works — if you can genuinely simulate human biology at the single-cell level and predict drug outcomes computationally — you're not making drug discovery 15% cheaper. You're making animal models obsolete. You're collapsing a process that currently takes a decade and a billion dollars into something fundamentally different. The $1.7 trillion pharmaceutical industry runs on a process that fails 90% of the time in clinical trials despite passing preclinical tests. That failure rate is the problem statement, and it's enormous. The specific result CellType has demonstrated — computationally identifying a compound that turns immunologically cold tumors hot, then validating it in human tumor microenvironments — is exactly the kind of signal that says "this isn't a toy." The consequence magnitude is real. But I have to be honest: there are half a dozen well-funded companies — Recursion at $461M raised, Insilico with drugs in clinical trials, Isomorphic with $600M — all claiming to revolutionize the same process. The question isn't whether AI drug discovery matters. It's whether CellType's specific paradigm — biology as language, cellular-level simulation rather than molecular-level prediction — is the one that breaks through.

The timing trajectory is where this gets compelling. Foundation model capabilities are on an exponential curve, and single-cell sequencing data is exploding thanks to projects like the Human Cell Atlas. CellType is building at the intersection of two accelerating trends — the kind of convergence I look for. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, I wasn't evaluating GPT-2's performance; I was tracking the slope of improvement and the flow of talent. Cell2Sentence went from a research paper to a 27B-parameter model in collaboration with Google DeepMind in roughly a year. Sundar Pichai called it "an exciting milestone for AI in science." That's not validation of the company — it's validation of the trajectory. The paradigm of treating gene expression as language and applying transformer architectures to cellular biology is early enough that CellType could define the category, but late enough that the technical prerequisites clearly exist. This is the window I want to be in.

Van Dijk presents an interesting case against my usual outsider thesis. He's a Yale Associate Professor with 11,589 citations — the consummate insider to computational biology. But he's a genuine outsider to the pharmaceutical industry. He's a computer scientist and mathematician who has never run a drug development program, never navigated an FDA submission, never managed a pharma sales force. In that sense, his outsider status is precisely where it needs to be: he doesn't know what pharma considers impossible, which frees him to attempt computational drug discovery in a way that pharma's own AI teams — constrained by organizational inertia and established R&D workflows — have struggled to achieve. The fact that he turned down Google to build CellType is a real conviction signal. But I notice the Yale tenure provides a safety net that reduces my confidence in the all-in commitment I prefer to see.

The bull case is genuinely strong, so let me articulate it clearly. CellType's approach — modeling entire cellular programs through language models rather than individual protein-ligand interactions — could represent a paradigm shift analogous to Juniper's TCP/IP routers versus Cisco's legacy architecture. Structure-based approaches (Atomwise, Isomorphic) predict molecular binding but miss the complexity of how cells actually respond. Phenomic screening (Recursion) captures cellular responses but through imaging, not computational simulation. CellType's LLM-native approach could integrate both — predicting not just "does this molecule bind?" but "what happens to the entire cellular state when it does?" If that's right, the other approaches become structurally incomplete, not just incrementally worse. The Senhwa MOU and claimed Top 10 pharma engagement with 100% inbound deal flow suggest the market is already pulling on this. And the compounding data moat — each pharma partnership generating proprietary validation data that improves the models — could eventually overcome the open-source vulnerability of the core methodology.

The risks are structural, not cosmetic. The open-source Apache 2.0 core means the methodology is replicable. Google DeepMind, with vastly greater compute and talent resources, co-developed the technology and operates Isomorphic Labs in the same broad space. A two-person team with no pharma commercial, regulatory, or business development experience faces the exact challenge that killed many of my cleantech companies — the technology worked, but the commercial translation didn't. I learned from KiOR that technology achievement without the operational machinery to deliver economic impact is a trap. Drug discovery validated computationally still requires wet-lab confirmation and clinical trials, and that transition demands capabilities this team hasn't yet assembled. If I write this check, I'm betting that Van Dijk can recruit the operational talent to bridge this gap — and that Google won't simply outrun him with the tools he built.
