# Coasty -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Coasty is the exact pattern I pass on inside thirty seconds: a two-person team -- Prateek Jannu and Nitish Kovuru -- selling $19-to-$99/month subscriptions for a vision-based computer-use agent whose entire moat is a 10-point lead (82% vs. 72%) over Anthropic Claude Computer Use on a public OSWorld benchmark. The dimensional signature is unmistakable -- Coasty borrows the rhetoric of my $15T labor-displacement thesis while running commodity SaaS economics in a market where the frontier-model labs themselves ship the substitute. That pairing -- consequence frame borrowed, capital and technical risk profile absent -- is the optimization-play silhouette my Outlook Business essay warned against.

On consequence magnitude, the **HIGH** rung is **OpenAI** (2019), where the $50M first check went in because the success-state was the displacement of labor across the global economy, not "we beat Salesforce Einstein at summarization." Coasty's pitch lands closer to the rung my Pass Example occupies: "we use vision models to replace UiPath/Blue Prism/Automation Anywhere scripts." The dossier names the incumbents Coasty wants to displace -- UiPath, Blue Prism, Automation Anywhere -- and that is the tell. UiPath being collapsed is a $30B market-cap re-allocation, not a civilizational variable. The founders did not write "knowledge work itself disappears"; they wrote "RPA that doesn't break when the UI changes." That is a 30% improvement frame on an existing regime, not the Impossible Foods (2011) frame of collapsing animal-protein production. Coasty sits between my MID and LOW rungs, closer to LOW.

On technical and capital risk that repels consensus VCs, the failure mechanism to study is the **sales-call summarizer wrapper from my Pass Example** -- no science risk, no capital depth, fundable by any Tier-1 generalist, indistinguishable from a feature any frontier lab ships in twelve months. Does Coasty's dossier escape that pattern? It does not. The product is a `/v1/predict` wrapper around vision-language models Coasty does not train, running on sandboxed VMs Coasty does not manufacture. There is no catalyst-yield miracle as in **KiOR** (~2008), no Z-pinch confinement question as in **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019), no protein-engineering decade as in **Impossible Foods** (2011). Coasty's $19/month Starter tier is the dossier's own admission that the capital required to reach commercial milestone is rounding error against my typical check size.

On founder defies expert consensus -- mechanism analysis. The compounding question is whether Jannu's ex-Microsoft ML Researcher and ex-TruGen AI Lead MLE credentials, and Kovuru's dev.to-author posture, generate over 18 months any public position the field would reject. Nothing in the Coasty dossier shows a controversial public stake -- no "RPA is dead," no "frontier labs are wrong about agent architecture," no instigator pattern from my TED Talk "World Needs 12 Good Instigators." The founder profile is domain-expert continuity -- ML engineer applies ML to RPA -- which my TED Talk explicitly flagged as the McKinsey-1986-cellphone-forecast posture. Coasty compounds toward consensus, not away from it.

On founder learning rate -- counterfactual. For Coasty to flip from a 6 to a 12 on this dimension, Kovuru's "AI agent deleted my production database" origin story (dev.to) would need to be a documented prior-company pivot rather than a debugging anecdote that motivated a sandboxed-VM design choice. The Slash (2024) Kanye-pivot pattern that justified my $100M Series C was a public, company-level thesis reversal -- Coasty's analogue is a personal incident that produced a feature decision. The signal is real but small; it is the kind of detail I note and do not act on.

On required-capital profile -- single-anchor parallel. Compare Coasty against **Genesis AI** (2025), where I co-led a $105M seed with Eclipse because the foundation-model-for-robotics roadmap demanded training compute, hardware integration, and trajectory data collection at a scale no $5M seed could fund. Coasty's pricing page -- $19/$50/$99 monthly tiers, 2-person team in SF -- is the dossier's own evidence that the company's roadmap fits in a standard $500K YC pre-seed plus a $3-5M seed. I am over-resourced for Coasty by an order of magnitude, which is the exact mismatch the criterion penalizes.

The 36-month forecast: Coasty is acquired for under $50M by UiPath or Automation Anywhere as a vision-grounded feature inside their existing RPA suite, or absorbed into an Anthropic/OpenAI agent-ops bundle once the OSWorld delta closes -- the alternative being quiet shutdown when the $99/month Unlimited tier fails to clear seed-stage burn. The mechanism is straightforward: Coasty's only moat is a benchmark percentage that compresses on every frontier-model release, and the named comp set -- Browserbase ($67.5M), Kernel ($22M Series A), Browser Use (~$17M), Anchor ($6M) -- is already capitalized ahead of Coasty in adjacent ground, so the acquihire price is set by competitors, not by Coasty.

The strongest objection is the **Glean** exception in my own portfolio -- ex-Google-search Arvind Jain, a paradigmatic domain-expert profile, where I overrode the founder-defiance prior because consequence magnitude on Fortune-500 information asymmetry justified it. The bull case for Coasty would run: Jannu's Microsoft ML pedigree mirrors Jain's Google pedigree, the RPA market is similarly Fortune-500-shaped, and 82% OSWorld is the proprietary-data flywheel in seed form. The bear evidence wins decisively. Glean raised at capital scale -- the override required check sizes that made the consequence magnitude real -- whereas Coasty's $99/month ceiling and 2-person team cannot absorb a Khosla check. And Glean's moat was enterprise-data integration depth, not a public benchmark any competitor can target. The verdict flips only on a single verifiable condition: Coasty signs two named Fortune 500 enterprise contracts at six-figure ACV by month 12 with audited workflow-completion metrics in production, not OSWorld. Absent that, Coasty scores where the framework puts it.
