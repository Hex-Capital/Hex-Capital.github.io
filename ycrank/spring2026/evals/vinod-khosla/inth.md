# Inth -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Inth is the canonical shape I trained myself to pass on: a competent dev-tools founder with real OSS distribution attacking a governance category I have actively avoided for forty years. The signature is "high relative founder execution, low everything that matters to me" — Christopher Burns shipped c15t to 2.6M npm downloads and Vercel/Expo/Zed production apps, but the success-state of Inth at maximum ambition is "GDPR/CCPA evidence generation runs inside the repo instead of inside a OneTrust dashboard." That is the **sales-call summarizer pass** from my own framework wearing a developer-tools costume.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence magnitude because the success-state was "machine cognition becomes a substitutable input to $15T of labor." The shared mechanism a bull would invoke for Inth is "AI agents writing code that moves user data" — Inth's own rebrand language. But the consequence Inth captures if it wins completely is that engineering teams stop filling out Vanta-style questionnaires and instead ship c15t plus an evidence pack to satisfy regulators in Quebec and Brussels. No incumbent regime collapses. OneTrust's $5.1B valuation gets compressed; Transcend's $40M Series B (Axios, May 2024) eats a slower lunch; the global privacy-ops category gets cheaper. That is a margin transfer, not a civilizational redirection — closer to my pass example than to **Impossible Foods** (2011) displacing animal protein.

Required-Capital Profile is where Inth sits closest to the LOW rung — the **sales-call summarizer** pattern where any Tier-1 generalist is over-resourced for the deal. The failure mechanism at that rung is not technical; it is that the company never needs the kind of capital that makes me useful. Inth's three-person team in London, OSS-led GTM, and developer-installed SDK with 6.5K downloads per day reaches its first $5M ARR on a seed and an A from Index or Accel — there is no plant, no fleet, no foundation-model training run, no Mississippi $75M state loan analog. The pattern is conspicuously present in Inth's dossier: pricing page, freemium funnel, managed DSAR platform "in development." This is exactly the deal I should be over-resourced for.

On technical risk that repels consensus VCs, the mechanism analysis matters more than a ladder. Consent SDKs compound on integration switching cost, not on physics — and switching cost in a headless, tree-shakable Apache 2.0 package is exactly as deep as the next developer's afternoon. The shadcn/c15t fork on GitHub is not a footnote; it is a live demonstration that the wedge is reproducible. Over 18 months, either Inth's repo-scanning agent becomes a genuine data-lineage graph with real customer-specific accumulation, or Privado ($17.5M, Series A, Insight + Peak XV) ships the same with more capital. The dossier admits the scanning product is "the company's next layer beyond c15t, not yet a shipped paid SKU."

Founder defiance of expert consensus is the dimension where Inth would need to flip a tier for me to keep reading. The counterfactual: Burns would need to have publicly contradicted the privacy-ops orthodoxy in a documented way — a thesis essay arguing CMPs are a dead category, a Heavybit podcast where he commits to a falsifiable position other privacy founders reject. Instead the dossier gives me a Jamstack Radio episode about cookie banners. Burns is a thoughtful builder, not an instigator in the sense of my TED Talk "12 Good Instigators" framing. The Everfund-to-Inth path is domain continuity — GDPR-regulated UK donation platform → GDPR consent SDK. That is the McKinsey-1986-cellphone-forecast pattern of extrapolating the past, not breaking it.

Founder learning rate is the single dimension where Inth earns a real-but-modest signal — comparable on this axis only to **Slash** (2024), where the documented Kanye pivot justified a $100M Series C into a YC fintech against my consequence-magnitude prior. The parallel breaks because Burns does not have a Slash-equivalent pivot story; he has Everfund winding down and a Consent.io → Inth rebrand in October 2025. The wind-down plus the OSS traction signals a founder who can read the market and re-aim, but it is not the Kanye-grade reversal that overrode my framework for Slash. And Slash's override required YC pedigree plus consequence-magnitude indifference; Inth has the YC pedigree but I am not granting the override.

My 36-month forecast: Inth is acquired by Transcend, Ketch, or a Vercel-adjacent infrastructure buyer for between $40M and $120M before reaching $10M ARR, with c15t persisting as the de facto OSS consent default while the Inth-branded managed platform never differentiates from Privado's roadmap. The mechanism producing this is the asymmetry between the OSS layer (genuinely loved, 1,691 stars, real production usage) and the paid layer (undisclosed pricing, "in development," fork-vulnerable). This is the c15t-acquihire outcome — a respectable result for Burns, a non-event for a $3.5B fund.

The strongest bull case I owe a hearing: Inth is the next **Glean** exception. Glean violated my Founder-Defies-Expert-Consensus criterion (Arvind Jain is ex-Google search, paradigmatic domain expert) but cleared on capital scale and Fortune-500 consequence. The bull argues Inth's "AI agents writing code" thesis maps to my Fortune (2026-03-06) labor-displacement frame — the labor being displaced is legal/privacy ops — and that 2.6M npm downloads at Vercel/Expo/Zed is a Glean-comparable distribution wedge into enterprise. The case stings because the distribution is real and the AI-agent-data-flow thesis is genuinely well-timed. It loses on scale: Glean's consequence is "knowledge work inside the Fortune 500 stops being information-asymmetric"; Inth's is "privacy evidence generation gets cheaper." The flip condition I can name precisely: if Inth signs a named seven-figure ARR contract with a hyperscaler or top-10 SaaS vendor for the managed DSAR platform within 18 months, demonstrating that the OSS-to-paid funnel produces enterprise pricing power Privado does not have, I revisit. Until then, pass — and I write the pass without regret, because **KiOR** (~2008) is the price of holding this discipline and Inth is the kind of capital-light governance bet that would prove the discipline meaningless.
