# Button Computer -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The graveyard here is the story. Humane raised $230 million and sold its carcass to HP for $116 million. Rabbit R1 shipped to brutal reviews. Limitless got absorbed into Meta. Bee got absorbed into Amazon. The wearable AI hardware category has now chewed through over a quarter billion dollars of venture capital and produced exactly zero independent, sustainable businesses. And into this graveyard walk two ex-Apple Vision Pro engineers saying, "We'll build the same thing, but simpler and cheaper." The founders are clearly competent — Ryan Burgoyne co-invented the gaze-and-tap UI for visionOS, holds three patents from Apple, shipped Skyglass to 95K downloads, graduated Techstars. Chris Nolet is a staff software engineer from the same Vision Pro team with a prior startup through 500 Startups. These are sane people. But the idea isn't insane — it's the most consensus play in consumer hardware right now. Apple is reportedly building an AI wearable. Every major platform company wants this category. When I describe my framework as "sane person, insane idea," the insane part is the crucial differentiator. A brilliant person building what everybody already agrees should exist is the kind of bet where returns get competed away before you cash the check.

The structural moat problem here is severe — and it's not the usual SaaS moat erosion I talk about. It's worse. Button is a hardware shell around someone else's API. The dual-mic array, speaker, Bluetooth chip, and physical button are commodity components. The software pipeline — voice-to-LLM-to-speech — runs on OpenAI or Anthropic infrastructure that Button doesn't control. The $7.99/month subscription has to cover API costs that will fluctuate based on upstream pricing decisions Button has zero leverage over. They even offer a bring-your-own-key option, which is an honest acknowledgment that their software layer adds minimal proprietary value. When I evaluate AI-era defensibility, I look for businesses where AI makes the moat deeper. Here, as AI voice capabilities improve, they improve identically for every competitor and every incumbent. Apple could ship this as an AirPods firmware update. Google could build it into Pixel Buds. The improvements in the underlying AI don't accrue to Button — they accrue to the API providers and the platforms with distribution.

This is the Birchbox pattern I know too well: big narrative, no structural moat. "Wearable AI for everyone" sounds expansive, but the narrative is entirely dependent on someone else's technology. Button doesn't create a new primitive — it's an application-layer consumer device that accesses commodity intelligence. There are no network effects. Other businesses don't build on Button's success. The "infinity story" is capped because the moment this category proves viable, the platform owners eat it. The comparison Button wants is the iPod Shuffle — the simplest version of a complex category. But the iPod Shuffle worked because Apple controlled iTunes, the music ecosystem, and the entire hardware stack. Button controls none of the analogous layers. They're building a Shuffle for someone else's iTunes.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: Humane failed because it tried to replace the phone at $699. Rabbit failed because it shipped a half-baked OS. What if the insight is that wearable AI should be radically simple — a companion device, not a phone replacement, at a price point where impulse purchasing kicks in? The founders' Vision Pro experience means they understand hardware interaction design at a level most startups can't match, and nailing the form factor and latency could create genuine consumer love before Apple ships its own version. If Button achieves real traction in 2026, it becomes an acquisition target for every platform company that just acqui-hired its way into the category. A $179 device with strong early adoption could produce a Nest-like or Ring-like outcome. For this to work, though, they'd need to ship on time (end of 2026), achieve meaningful unit volume before Apple's AI wearable materializes, and build enough brand affinity that consumers choose them over the platform-native option. That's a narrow window requiring flawless execution across hardware manufacturing, supply chain, and consumer marketing — all at pre-seed scale with a two-person team. I don't see a path to an independent, compounding business here. I see, at best, an acquisition lottery ticket.

My framework is built to find businesses that get stronger as software gets cheaper. Button gets weaker. Every improvement in voice AI makes it easier for the next competitor to match Button's experience. Every dollar spent on hardware tooling is a dollar that doesn't compound. The founders are impressive operators building in a category that structurally punishes startups. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech company that's just a layer.
