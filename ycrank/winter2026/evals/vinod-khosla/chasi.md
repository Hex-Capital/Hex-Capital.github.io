# Chasi -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is a team that built AI deployments at Cummins, Harley Davidson, Boeing, and Tesla -- real industrial DNA, not pitch-deck cosplay -- and they've chosen to point all of that capability at making equipment dealers answer the phone faster. That's the central tension. Akash and Sarman have the kind of hard-won industrial credibility that takes years to accumulate. They watched Axion Ray grow from pre-seed through a $64 million Series B serving Fortune 500 manufacturers. Sarman built supply chain orchestration software as a founding engineer at Tesla. These are people who understand how physical-world industries actually operate, at the system level. And they've deployed that understanding to build... a vertical AI sales agent for forklift dealers. I find myself wanting to grab them by the shoulders and say: you are too capable to spend your careers on a problem this small.

Let me be precise about the consequence of success. If Chasi wins completely -- every equipment dealer on Earth runs their AI agents -- fleet utilization moves from 60% to perhaps 75%. Dealers capture revenue they were losing to slow follow-ups. Response times drop. Margins improve. This is a real business improvement, and the dealers would pay for it. But nothing fundamental changes about how equipment gets distributed, manufactured, or utilized. The $1 trillion equipment distribution industry continues to operate on the same structural logic. No incumbent system becomes obsolete. The consequence of total success is a well-run SaaS company in a vertical market -- exactly the kind of outcome I consider inconsequential relative to the effort required to build a great company. When I backed Square, it wasn't because merchants needed slightly faster checkout. It was because mobile payments would structurally replace the entire POS infrastructure -- banks, terminals, merchant accounts, the whole stack became irrelevant. Chasi doesn't make anything irrelevant. It makes an existing system incrementally better.

The technology concern compounds this. The dossier itself states the quiet part: "The core technology (LLM-powered agents handling sales/service inquiries) is broadly available. Multiple companies could build similar agents." Quipli has already launched Quinn with the advantage of proprietary transaction data from millions of equipment rentals. BigRentz is pivoting toward AI with $500 million in sales data. The technology moat here isn't a moat -- it's domain-specific prompt engineering and integration work, which is valuable but doesn't create the kind of structural displacement I look for. When Juniper built TCP/IP routers, Cisco's CTO literally said they would never do it. Nobody is saying AI agents for equipment sales are impossible -- everyone is building them simultaneously. When incumbents and competitors validate your approach rather than dismiss it, that's a warning sign in my framework, not a comfort.

The strongest bull case requires believing this is a Trojan horse: start with AI agents, accumulate proprietary data on equipment demand and pricing across thousands of dealers, and eventually become the intelligence layer that controls equipment commerce globally. If Chasi processes enough transactions, it could theoretically predict demand, optimize pricing across the entire market, and become indispensable infrastructure rather than a point solution. The founders' industrial network from Axion Ray -- which served major OEMs -- could provide distribution leverage that a generalist AI startup couldn't replicate. And equipment dealers are genuinely underserved by technology; the 60% utilization rate persists precisely because existing solutions don't solve the problem. If you believe Chasi can lock in dealers before the commoditization wave hits, build a data flywheel that competitors can't replicate, and expand from sales automation into full operational intelligence, this could be a multi-billion-dollar vertical platform. That's a real path. But I see no evidence in the dossier that the founders are articulating this larger vision -- what I see is "AI agents that handle sales inquiries," which is the starting point, not the destination.

The timing is adequate but not visionary. LLMs reaching production quality for customer-facing interactions in 2024-2025, cloud DMS adoption at 54%, construction boom driving demand -- these are real tailwinds. But they're consensus tailwinds. Every AI startup serving every vertical is riding this same wave. The founders aren't skating to where the puck will be; they're skating to where the puck obviously is, alongside a dozen other players. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was laughable to most people. When I backed cleantech in 2004, most VCs thought I was crazy. Chasi is entering a space where the enabling technology is mature, the market need is well-understood, and competitors are already shipping products. That's not a timing advantage -- that's a foot race.

I respect the founding team's composition. A mechanical engineer with GTM experience paired with a Waterloo-trained software engineer who built systems at Tesla -- complementary, battle-tested, with a shared working history at Axion Ray. That's a solid foundation for execution. But solid execution of an incremental thesis is precisely the trap I warn about. These founders have reduced their probability of failure to the point where the consequences of their success are, in my judgment, modest. They're building a good business, not attempting to transform an industry. I pass -- not because the team is weak, but because the ambition is calibrated to produce a comfortable outcome rather than a consequential one.
