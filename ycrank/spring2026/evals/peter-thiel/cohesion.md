# Cohesion -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

AlphaSense generates over $500M in annual recurring revenue by letting analysts search filings and transcripts. Hebbia raised $130M at a $700M valuation to analyze 10-Ks. Trata graduated YC W25 to distribute anonymized analyst research. Standard Signal is in Cohesion's own Spring 2026 batch building autonomous AI hedge funds. YC itself listed "AI-Native Hedge Funds" as a formal Request for Startups category. Over 35% of new hedge fund launches in 2025 branded themselves as AI-driven or AI-enhanced. When I ask Cohesion the canonical question — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — the answer appears to be: AI agents can autonomously monitor earnings, podcasts, and social media better than human analysts doing it manually. But nobody disagrees with this. Every investor, every fund manager, every fintech founder nods along. A truth that makes the room nod is not a secret. It is consensus wearing a lanyard.

Cohesion's dossier reveals the precise pattern I have warned against repeatedly: an "AI-powered" version of an existing software category where the only insight is "apply AI here." The open-source ai-hedge-fund repository on GitHub demonstrates that basic investment research agents can be assembled from commodity LLM APIs. Cohesion's core technology — LLM agents monitoring public data sources — has what the dossier itself acknowledges are "low technical barriers." This is not a 10x proprietary technology breakthrough. This is not reusable rockets when aerospace consensus said they were impossible. Cohesion is wrapping someone else's foundation models around a workflow that AlphaSense, with its $650M in funding and $4B valuation, could replicate as a product extension any quarter it chooses. The acquirer can add the feature faster than the startup can build the platform. That is not a monopoly structure — it is a feature request.

Devon Krapcho spent five years as a fundamental equity analyst at Long Path Partners covering software companies. Matt Munns built AI for investors at T. Rowe Price, a $1.5 trillion asset manager. Matthew McBrien engineered AWS Lambda at Amazon. The strongest bull case for Cohesion centers on this team: Krapcho is the target user persona incarnate, Munns has built ML systems inside institutional finance, and McBrien knows infrastructure at planetary scale. They are already live with 10+ fundamental equity funds managing a combined $10 billion in AUM. For a pre-seed company, that is genuine early pull — founder-led distribution leveraging Krapcho's network, timed intelligently to earnings season when the pain is sharpest. If I were Paul Graham, I might invest on the strength of this founder-market fit and the visible organic demand. But my framework requires that founder quality serve a monopoly structure, and no amount of domain expertise creates a monopoly when the underlying technology is commodity and the market is a crowded battlefield. A brilliant, determined founder pursuing a competitive market is still a loser — because all failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition.

Cohesion's co-founders do not share overlapping employers or universities. Krapcho came from Notre Dame and Long Path Partners; McBrien from Georgia Tech and AWS; Munns from T. Rowe Price. The dossier found "no public data on co-founder history." Founding relationships forged under prior shared intensity — a previous startup, military service, a research lab — predict survival under the stress of company-building. Relationships formed for the purpose of starting a company lack that structural alignment. Cohesion's team was assembled for this venture, not tempered by a prior crucible together. On definite conviction, Cohesion offers "limited early trial access" during earnings season — language that suggests iterative market testing rather than an irreversible commitment to a specific vision. No pricing page exists. The business model is inferred, not declared. The company is not hiring. These are signals of a team still discovering what to build, not a team that has already decided what the world looks like when they succeed.

Wish is the portfolio anchor that haunts this evaluation. Wish entered e-commerce — a massive TAM — without proprietary technology, without a monopoly structure, without a genuine secret about why it would win rather than merely compete. It IPO'd at $14 billion and crashed over 90%. Cohesion's SAM — fundamental equity funds willing to pay for AI research tooling — is "likely in the low hundreds of millions" by the dossier's own inference. Even in the optimistic case where Cohesion captures significant share of that narrow market, the ceiling is a modest acquisition, not a power-law outcome. The AI-in-investment-management TAM of $7.4 billion by 2030 is structurally fragmented across dozens of players serving different workflows. No winner-take-all dynamics are visible. Cohesion cannot return a fund from this position — the mathematics of the power law make this category structurally irrelevant to how I construct a portfolio.

Cohesion is a pure software layer with zero physical-world technology moat — no hardware, no regulatory barrier, no deep infrastructure integration that would create multi-year replication barriers. Palantir embedded its intelligence software so deeply into CIA and Army operations that displacement became structurally impossible. Cohesion monitors public podcasts and X/Twitter posts — data sources controlled by third parties whose API policies have changed multiple times since 2023. The dependency runs in the wrong direction: Cohesion depends on platforms it does not control, rather than platforms depending on Cohesion. The switching costs the dossier identifies are "weak at current stage" by its own admission. Nothing in Cohesion's architecture creates the compounding lock-in that converts a product into a monopoly.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Cohesion demonstrated that its agents generate proprietary signal — measurable alpha attribution showing that funds using Cohesion outperform on specific trades traced to Cohesion-surfaced insights — that would constitute a genuine secret about which data sources matter for which sectors, creating a data flywheel no competitor could replicate without the same usage base. That evidence does not exist today. Distribution earned the highest marks in this evaluation because 10+ live funds at pre-seed is real demand, but distribution without a monopoly structure is a consulting business with software margins. Cohesion's fatal dimension is the absence of a secret: what Cohesion knows, everyone knows, and what everyone knows cannot be the foundation of a monopoly.
