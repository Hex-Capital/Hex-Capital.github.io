# Miraka -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The fact that jumps off the page about Miraka is the $300K annual run rate with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments. At pre-seed, most companies are showing me prototypes and waitlist numbers. Nolan Abeyta, Kazuo Nakamura, and Jesse Abeyta are showing me insurance reimbursements — money flowing from CMS billing codes into a bank account. That's not a vanity metric. You can't fake billable appointments to Medicare. And the trajectory matters more than the absolute number. If 71% monthly growth in appointments holds for even a few more months, the revenue curve starts to look like something.

What I keep returning to is the Overwatch Health origin. These three built a remote cardiac rehabilitation platform together starting in 2021 at Princeton's eLab, when Nolan was a sophomore and Kazuo was a junior. They spent years learning hospital sales cycles, clinical workflows, CMS billing structures — and then they came back to build it again. This is the pattern I trust most. Patrick Collison built Auctomatic before Stripe. The AppJet team built EtherPad during YC. Founders who have already shipped something in the same domain and return to do it better are operating with a map that first-time founders don't have. The claim that Miraka is "the youngest team to sell into a top-5 heart hospital" — and the fact that they've gotten a patient enrolled at NewYork-Presbyterian — is evidence that the map works. Hospital procurement is one of the most punishing sales environments in existence. Doing it at 23 is not normal.

The schlep here is staggering, and it's the right kind of schlep. Cadence raised $144M to license RPM software to hospitals. Biofourmis raised $465M for FDA-cleared analytics. Both chose the comfortable side of the problem: build technology, sell it, let someone else deal with patients. Miraka chose the uncomfortable side — actually being the care team. That means credentialing clinicians, managing insurance billing, integrating with hospital EHR systems, and delivering measurable clinical outcomes. Most founders' unconscious minds won't even let them see this version of the opportunity. It's the cardiac care equivalent of what Stripe did with PCI compliance: everyone knew the problem existed, but the work required to solve it properly was so grinding that only founders with a specific kind of stubbornness would attempt it. The structural parallel to ClassDojo is instructive — I invested there despite K-12 being far from my typical sector because the schlep of selling to school districts created a natural moat. Miraka's clinical delivery model creates a similar barrier: a pure-software competitor can't replicate the integrated care team without fundamentally changing what they are.

Where I hesitate is the scaling question. Stripe's marginal cost of processing an additional payment approaches zero. Each additional Miraka patient requires clinical staff time. The dossier confirms no public data on their clinical staffing model, and this gap matters because it determines whether Miraka is building a startup or a very good medical practice. If the AI predictive models genuinely work — if continuous wearable and EHR data creates a feedback loop where each patient makes the system smarter, reducing clinician time per patient and catching deterioration earlier — then superlinear returns are possible. The >90% readmission reduction and 94% patient capture rate versus the 10% industry baseline would be extraordinary if validated. But these numbers are self-reported from the a16z Speedrun portal, not peer-reviewed or third-party audited. The difference between "our internal data shows 90% reduction" and "a published study at NewYork-Presbyterian confirms 90% reduction" is the difference between a pitch and proof.

The strongest bull case for Miraka is that CMS is actively expanding RPM reimbursement — the 2026 fee schedule lowered billing thresholds — which means the regulatory tailwind is real and growing. If the clinical outcomes hold, hospitals face HRRP penalties for readmissions and Miraka offers a turnkey solution that captures 94% of eligible patients versus the 10% industry baseline. That's not an incremental improvement; it's a category difference. A hospital administrator looking at those numbers doesn't care that the founders are 23. And the data advantage compounds: every patient monitored feeds the predictive models, which should improve intervention timing, which improves outcomes, which wins more hospital contracts. The question is whether the compounding happens fast enough relative to Cadence's $144M war chest and existing hospital relationships.

Against my instated patterns, I notice Miraka falls outside my "hackers building for hackers" bias — this is healthcare delivery, not developer tools. No founder has visible GitHub repos. Nolan studied Economics, Kazuo studied Politics. Jesse is the technical founder with Amazon AI experience and military exoskeleton biometrics work, which is genuine technical depth, but I can't evaluate his code or systems thinking from the dossier. The organic problem discovery signal is strong in duration — five years in the same domain — but weak in origin narrative. I don't know why three Princeton undergrads became obsessed with cardiac rehabilitation. The best founders can tell you the exact moment of frustration that started everything. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive. The Octopart founders couldn't find electronic parts. The dossier gives me no equivalent moment for Miraka's founders. What I can see is that they kept coming back — Overwatch Health, then whatever Paralign was, then Miraka — and persistence across multiple attempts in the same domain is itself a form of organic discovery. You don't grind through CMS billing codes for five years on a thesis you found in a market research deck.

The two dimensions that most determine my score are Founder Resourcefulness and Organic Problem Discovery. The resourcefulness evidence is concrete: $300K ARR, NewYork-Presbyterian partnership, the youngest team to sell into a top-5 heart hospital, real insurance revenue at pre-seed. These are not achievements you stumble into. The organic discovery evidence is circumstantial but cumulative — five years, three company iterations, same domain. The linear scaling concern is real but not disqualifying at this stage; the evidence that would flip me is a published clinical study validating the outcome claims, or a demonstration that the AI layer genuinely reduces clinician time per patient as the dataset grows. Without that, Miraka might be building an excellent cardiac care practice rather than a startup. With it, the combination of schlep moat, regulatory tailwind, and data compounding could produce something Cadence's SaaS-only model can't match.
