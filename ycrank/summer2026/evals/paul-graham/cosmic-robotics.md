# Cosmic Robotics -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Cosmic Robotics is a hardware-schlep company with aerospace-grade cofounders whose highest dimension is the sheer unpleasantness of the work — dusty utility-scale solar sites, terrain-adjusted panel handling, mobile electric robotics at 30–40 seconds per panel — and whose lowest is a software-hacker tribal trail I usually rely on to confirm founder taste. James Emerick and Lewis Jones have already shipped Cosmic-1A onto "some of the largest solar farms in the US" for "tens of thousands of panel installs" (YC page) before the YC check cleared, which is the artifact-first pattern I care about; what's missing is the GitHub-and-Hacker-News residue that told me the Collisons were the Collisons.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy for Cosmic Robotics on the schlep axis. Brian Armstrong won because he was willing to grind bitcoin compliance while every other applicant in the batch wanted to build a consumer app; the regulatory ugliness *became* the moat. Cosmic Robotics is playing the physical-world version of the same game: EPCs with margins up ~40% and labor costs up 15% annually (SEIA / IREC 2025) cannot in-house a robotics OEM without cannibalizing their own labor billings, which is exactly why SunRobi (Sunstall's division) became the "first certified operator" rather than a competing internal build (Solar Power World, Feb 2026). Where Cosmic Robotics diverges from the Coinbase rung is that the schlep here is capital-intensive and mechanically contested — Terabase Energy has raised $130M for the field-factory approach (pv magazine, Apr 2026) — so the schlep protects the category but not necessarily this specific company's share of it.

The **AI-wrapper MBA pass** is the failure mode I test the hacker-genealogy dimension against, and Cosmic Robotics does not fall into it — but it also doesn't clear the Collison bar. Neither Emerick nor Jones has a public GitHub, no Twitter, no open-source lineage, no Lisp-adjacent prize (dossier: "No public repos found"). What they have instead is Jones's work at Relativity Space helping launch the first 3D-printed rocket plus stints at SpaceX and NASA JPL, and Emerick's early-employee run at Built Robotics — hardware-hacker genealogy, not software-hacker genealogy. I've historically funded the latter and read the former only through Wozniak-era analogies. Cosmic Robotics is closer to a Woz bet than a Swartz bet, and that's real signal, just quieter than the tribal markers I'm calibrated on.

**Mechanism analysis on animal resourcefulness:** Cosmic Robotics shipped a working mobile robot that installs panels every 30–40 seconds (Electrek, Mar 2026), won the DOE American-Made Solar Prize, secured a JLL Foundation grant, and got a paying-adjacent partner in SunRobi to certify as operator — all on a $4M pre-seed (TechCrunch, Apr 2025). That compounds because each additional certified operator is a distribution lever and each install adds terrain data. What it *isn't* is a cereal-box stunt; the resourcefulness here reads as competent DOE-grant chasing plus disciplined field deployments, not a story I'd retell at dinner. Animal, yes. Feral, no.

**Counterfactual on organic-idea fit:** Cosmic Robotics scores where it does because Emerick has "four-generation family construction background" plus Built Robotics (Samson Rose, 2025). To flip this into the Dropbox tier, I'd need Emerick to have personally installed solar panels on a jobsite and gotten so annoyed at bending over 4,000 times a day that he built the robot for himself — the USB-stick moment. The dossier doesn't say that. It says he *studied* the space and *worked* in adjacent robotics. Organic, but one biographical layer removed from the pain.

**Single-anchor parallel on cofounder configuration:** the Collison brothers at **Stripe** (2010) are the ceiling — two people, decade-plus of working together, complementary within a shared culture. Cosmic Robotics has Emerick (Penn/Built Robotics/Autodesk) and Jones (Relativity/SpaceX/JPL) with "no public data on shared prior employer or university" surfaced. Two technical cofounders with complementary construction-plus-aerospace coverage, but no evidence they'd built anything together before Cosmic Robotics. Neutral placement.

**36-month forecast:** Cosmic Robotics gets acquired by Terabase Energy or a major EPC (Blattner, Bechtel) in a $80–200M range within 36 months, having raised a $25–50M Series A along the way and expanded to 3–5 certified operators without hitting the standalone-OEM escape velocity that would justify staying independent. The producing mechanism is that the $130M Terabase raise (pv magazine, Apr 2026) sets the capital tempo for the category, and Cosmic Robotics's mobile-robot approach is complementary enough to Terabase's field-factory that consolidation is the natural exit — a good outcome, not a Stripe outcome.

**The strongest bull case against my forecast** is the NASA lunar contract plus the "Earth and beyond" thesis on the YC page: Cosmic Robotics is not a solar-installation company, it's an autonomous-construction platform with terrestrial revenue funding a lunar wedge that nobody else can credibly ship on. If Jones's SpaceX/JPL/Relativity lineage lets Cosmic Robotics win a real Artemis-adjacent contract (not just the unverified line item), the ceiling reframes from "solar consolidator" to "the SpaceX of construction robotics" — **Airbnb** (2009) territory where the idea looks dumb ("robots on the moon") and the founders shipped anyway. What dismantles this bull case in the dossier: the NASA lunar contract is "not independently verified" and "no matching award appears in NASA CLPS, LSII, or SBIR public listings," while ICON has the actual $57.2M lunar SBIR. Until Cosmic Robotics produces a verifiable NASA award ID, the lunar wedge is marketing, not moat. The verifiable condition that would flip me from Invest to Strong Invest: a named NASA award (CLPS, Tipping Point, or SBIR Phase II with public solicitation number) landed within 12 months of the YC batch, paired with a second certified operator beyond SunRobi.

I'd write the check. The founders are animals in the hardware sense, the schlep is real, and consolidation-tier outcomes still return the fund at pre-seed prices.
