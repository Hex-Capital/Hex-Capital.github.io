# SideKit -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the story before anything else. PostHog has raised $180M+, is open-source, bundles analytics with feature flags and session recording, and is growing at 138% year-over-year. Statsig was just acquired by OpenAI for $1.1B. LaunchDarkly has $330M in funding and $60M in revenue. Firebase Remote Config is free and integrated into 72% of iOS apps and essentially all Android apps. This isn't a market with a structural gap waiting to be filled -- it's a market where well-funded winners are actively consolidating. When I look at SideKit's positioning -- a 244 kB SDK bundling analytics, version gating, and AI feedback for indie mobile developers -- I see a product entering after what I'd call market-crystallizing moves have already occurred. PostHog is already executing the "bundle everything into one open-source product" strategy with 10x the resources and broader platform coverage.

The "why now" thesis is the weakest link. SideKit's implicit argument is that AI coding tools (Cursor, Copilot, Claude Code) are creating a wave of new indie developers who can build apps but can't operate them. That's a plausible observation, but it's speculative -- there's no data showing this cohort is large enough to constitute a market, and it's unclear whether these AI-assisted developers represent a durable segment or a transient phenomenon. The privacy angle is even less compelling as a timing signal: GDPR passed in 2018, Apple's App Tracking Transparency shipped in 2021. Privacy-first analytics has been a positioning option for years, and several companies already occupy it. When I invested in Harvey, the timing thesis was anchored to something concrete -- I could map the GPT scaling curve and predict when model fidelity would cross the threshold for legal work. Here, I can't point to a specific capability threshold or cost curve that unlocked this market in the last 12-24 months.

The strongest bull case goes like this: Stripe entered payments when PayPal already existed because it served a different persona -- developers who needed an API, not merchants who needed a checkout button. SideKit could similarly serve indie mobile developers who find Firebase too heavy and LaunchDarkly too expensive. Ashish Selvaraj lived this problem directly -- he built an iOS app (Not Evil Sudoku, 50K installs), needed analytics, found existing solutions unsatisfying, and built his own. That's genuine founder-market fit at the problem-identification level, and the free version-gating wedge is a smart acquisition strategy. If AI coding tools genuinely create millions of new app builders who need production infrastructure, SideKit is positioned in the right corridor. But for that bull case to work, you need multiple things to be true simultaneously: the indie developer cohort has to be large and growing, the bundled approach has to be meaningfully better than stitching together free tiers from Firebase and PostHog, the AI self-healing feature has to actually work well enough to justify the $99/month tier, and Android support has to ship before Firebase's dominance makes the iOS-only positioning a dead end. That's three or four sequential challenges, not one miracle.

On the product-to-distribution dimension, I'm struggling to see where this goes beyond the current bundle. Analytics plus version gating plus AI feedback -- that's already the full product vision at three price tiers. Contrast with Stripe, where the payments API was a wedge into a developer relationship that enabled Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury. Or PostHog, which started with analytics and expanded into feature flags, session recording, A/B testing, and surveys. SideKit's SDK is lightweight by design (244 kB), which is a selling point for adoption but structurally limits how much additional functionality you can distribute through it. I don't see the path from "lightweight SDK for indie developers" to "platform that compounds."

Ashish's background shows the right instincts -- he built and shipped an app, identified a real pain point, and started building a solution. The Databricks internship gives him relevant data infrastructure exposure. But 7 GitHub stars and 12 commits on the SDK, with no visible customer traction, no community, and no Product Hunt launch, suggest the product hasn't yet found even early resonance. The unverifiable credentials claim (Jane Street, Citadel on the website without confirmable profiles for the second co-founder) is a minor but unnecessary credibility risk. At pre-seed I wouldn't penalize minimal traction, but I also don't see the execution velocity signals -- the rapid iteration cycles and user feedback loops -- that would give me confidence in the team's ability to outrun well-funded incumbents in an already-competitive market.

This is a pass. The market isn't non-obvious -- developer tooling is consensus-hot. The structural moment isn't clear -- nothing has changed in the last 24 months that specifically enables bundled mobile analytics. The competitive encirclement is real -- PostHog and Firebase occupy adjacent positions with vastly more resources. And the product-to-distribution trajectory is capped by the inherent limitations of a lightweight SDK. The founders have some relevant experience, but this looks like a convenience product in a market where convenience alone won't create a moat.
