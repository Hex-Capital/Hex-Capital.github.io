# Voxel Energy -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The thing that immediately catches my eye about Voxel is the intersection of two exponential curves. The second-life EV battery market is growing at 65% CAGR — from roughly 25 GWh today to 330-350 GWh by 2030 — while AI compute demand is overwhelming grid capacity so severely that the average interconnection wait has stretched to five years. These two curves are about to collide, and Voxel is standing at the intersection. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, I tracked two rates of change: model improvement and talent inflow. Here I'm tracking battery availability and power demand. The trajectories are clear. But — and this matters — the trajectories are clear to everyone. Exowatt has $140M from a16z and Sam Altman. Crusoe has over $2 billion at a $10 billion valuation. This is not a space where Cisco's CTO is telling me it will never work. The market has already validated the problem, which means I'm evaluating an execution race, not a contrarian insight.

The consequence of success is substantial but not transformational in the way I find most compelling. If Voxel fully succeeds, they unlock some meaningful fraction of $1.5 trillion in stalled data center construction and change where computing infrastructure can be built — anywhere with sun and land, not just near available grid capacity. That's a large outcome. But data centers will still exist, the grid will still matter, and Voxel becomes a very large infrastructure company rather than a force that makes an entire industry structurally obsolete. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success meant reclaiming half the planet's arable land, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means fossil fuel power generation becomes a historical footnote. Voxel's success means data centers get built faster with cleaner power. Important, yes. Civilization-redefining, not quite.

The DC-native microgrid architecture is the most technically interesting claim in the dossier. Traditional data centers lose up to 30% of energy through AC-DC conversion stages; Voxel claims 4%. If that holds at scale, it's not a marginal improvement — it's a fundamental architectural advantage that grid-connected competitors cannot match without rebuilding their entire power distribution stack. That's the kind of economic impact I look for: technology that makes the existing approach structurally inefficient. The 5,400% overhead capacity claim for handling AI training power surges also suggests they've thought through the specific electrical characteristics of GPU-dense workloads, not just generic data center power. But I've learned from KiOR that technology achievement without validated unit economics is a trap. The dossier contains no pricing data, no revenue signal, no disclosed PPA terms. The question isn't whether the DC architecture is more efficient — it's whether second-life batteries at scale deliver power at a cost that makes this economically compelling after accounting for battery management complexity, degradation variance, and replacement cycles. That's unproven.

The team is competent and well-matched to the problem — Pfeiffer actually built and shipped electric vehicles at Maxwell Vehicles, which tells me he can move atoms, not just electrons on a whiteboard. Schmidt has managed data center construction projects for a decade. Spencer has founded three hardware companies. But these are industry-adjacent insiders, not outsiders. They came from Tesla, from construction management, from EV manufacturing — they know what's possible in this domain, which is both an asset and a constraint. My best investments have been in people who didn't know what the industry considered impossible. Peter Beck didn't know you couldn't build a rocket company in New Zealand. Pat Brown didn't know you couldn't replace meat with plants. This team knows exactly how hard battery management and data center construction are, and they're doing it anyway, which earns respect. But it doesn't produce the outsider advantage I weight most heavily.

The strongest bull case: Voxel's specific bet on second-life EV batteries is genuinely differentiated within the off-grid data center space. Exowatt heats rocks. Crusoe burns gas. Enchanted Rock runs natural gas microgrids. None of them are riding the 65% CAGR wave of retiring EV batteries. If Voxel locks up battery supply relationships and land positions now — and the dossier says they're already amassing batteries and have thousands of acres under contract — they could build a procurement moat that well-funded competitors can't easily replicate because those competitors chose different storage technologies. The fact that Redwood Materials is already running a 63 MWh second-life battery microgrid powering a data center validates the technical approach. If I squint, there's a version of this where Voxel becomes the infrastructure standard for the next generation of AI data centers — deployed in months, fully renewable, structurally more efficient — and the speed advantage creates a land-grab dynamic where they fill the market before Exowatt's thermal storage or Crusoe's gas approach can scale. That's a real scenario, and it's why this crosses my threshold for an option-value check.

I'd write a small check here — a million dollars to watch the rate of change over the next six months. The timing convergence is real, the team has the right technical DNA, and the DC-native architecture could be a genuine structural advantage. But this is an execution bet in a recognized market, not the kind of contrarian, consequence-of-success bet that defines my best investments. The problem is important enough to work on. The question is whether Voxel can outexecute competitors with 100x their capital, and that's a question I can't answer from a dossier — I need to see how fast these founders learn when things go wrong.
