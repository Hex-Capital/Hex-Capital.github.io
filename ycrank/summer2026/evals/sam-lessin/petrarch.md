# Petrarch -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Petrarch is a structurally-curious rollup-shape wrapped inside the single most consensus-priced sleeve of the 2026 batch — AI training data — sourced by three undergraduates with zero public distribution and no disclosed licensing counsel. That pairing is the signature: the *idea* of acquiring orphaned datasets from wind-downs is the kind of illegible-asset play I say I love, and the *execution stack* (Samuel Hahn and Sudhish Swain both current students, Ian Lee's prior life at CBRE building lab campuses for Google/Apple) is the wrong stack to clear the schleps the idea actually requires. Petrarch shows up on my desk as a structure play attached to the wrong founders in the hottest sleeve on the board.

**Solana at $0.05/coin** is the anchor for what a non-consensus round looks like — I bought when every generalist had quit crypto post-ICO crash, and the alpha was the pricing, not the eventual thesis validation. Petrarch is the mechanical opposite: "specialized training datasets for AI companies" is the 2026 YC consensus sentence, sitting one clause away from my Pass Example canonical pitch. Scale AI at $1.5B ARR growing 97% YoY and Snorkel's $100M Series D at $1.3B in May 2025 mean every tier-1 fund has an AI-data thesis and a check-writing muscle already primed for anything with "training data" in the tagline. Petrarch's petrarch.co page discloses nothing about round size or price, but silence on round dynamics in a category this hot is exactly when I assume consensus terms — I punish silence here because the entire alpha lives in the price.

**The Pass Example founder with no public writing** — that's the failure mode of the founder-as-brand dimension, and Petrarch reproduces it cleanly. The dossier is explicit: "No public Twitter/X account found" for Samuel Hahn, Ian Lee, and Sudhish Swain. No GitHub confirmed for any of the three. No newsletter, no essays, no Discord, no organic distribution channel of any kind. Petrarch is going to have to cold-outbound to bankruptcy trustees on one side and to AI-lab procurement on the other — a two-sided outbound motion with a $25/hr Business Ops Intern job posting as the visible sourcing apparatus. This is the anti-Bookshelved: John Fish walked in with a YouTube audience I could underwrite directly; Petrarch's founders walk in with LinkedIn URLs whose follower counts the dossier could not even retrieve.

Mechanism analysis on the capital structure dimension: if Petrarch were self-aware about what it actually is, this could look like a **Teamshares**-adjacent rollup — a holdco acquiring orphaned data assets from wind-downs at cents on the dollar, quality-certifying, and licensing them out on a royalty stream. That's a structure I would genuinely underwrite off-cap-table. But the dossier gives me zero evidence Petrarch is structured that way — no disclosed acquisition vehicle, no royalty model, no capital pool for asset purchases separate from operating equity. What I most likely get offered is a standard SAFE on a marketplace-take-rate business, which is the wrong instrument for an asset-acquisition motion and burns founder equity funding what is really working capital.

Counterfactual on founder conviction: for Petrarch to flip a tier here, one of the three founders would need to have been quietly acquiring or brokering datasets for 18+ months before the batch — a paid or unpaid Wizard-of-Oz run I could verify. Sudhish Swain's ISEF 2023 grand award for computational modeling of low-dose chemotherapy on cancer metabolism is a real technical signal, but on cancer biology, not dataset provenance. Ian Lee's CBRE / Lincoln Property / DPR Construction experience building healthcare and data-center campuses for Apple and UCSF is a real operator signal, but on physical construction sourcing, not IP chain-of-title diligence. Petrarch has real credentials pointing at adjacent problems — I don't see a single one pointing at *this* problem.

Single-anchor parallel on category sleeve: **Sublime Security** is the closest thing in my portfolio to what Petrarch *thinks* it is — an enterprise-picks-and-shovels play in a category the consensus overpaid for the flagship (CrowdStrike, Wiz) but under-invested in the sourcing layer. The disanalogy is that Sublime's founders had spent years inside email-security detection engineering before the round. Petrarch's founders are three months from a landing page that says "Coming 2026," and the sleeve isn't crypto or DTC — it's AI infrastructure with a data-brokerage wrapper, which "AI is just more internet" specifically re-weights away from.

**Forecast, 36 months:** Petrarch is dead or has quietly pivoted into a single-vertical dataset acquisition play — most likely medical imaging (Sudhish's biology background) or construction/property-tech records (Ian's CBRE network) — at under $500K in booked revenue, absorbed as an acqui-hire into a Scale AI or Snorkel sourcing pipeline for high-single-digit millions. The mechanism producing this is the collision between the asset-acquisition motion (which needs a licensing/legal ops apparatus and patient capital for illiquid asset diligence) and the SAFE-shaped marketplace cap table three undergraduates will have taken from the batch — the structure and the execution stack will fight each other until the runway ends.

**The bull case that stings:** distressed-dataset acquisition is, in isolation, exactly the illegible-asset play I keep publicly saying I love — nobody else is bidding for the data locked inside shutdowns, provenance diligence is a moat other funds can't underwrite because they don't understand the asset class, and one exclusive license on a valuable orphaned dataset (think a shuttered radiology-AI startup with a labeled imaging corpus) could pay for the whole fund. Petrarch is invoking the **Ro** exception in miniature — the regulatory schlep across chain-of-title and GDPR anonymization is the moat, not the failure mode, if the founders can clear it. What dismantles this: Ro cleared licensing schleps by design, with named regulatory counsel and a capital plan built around the schleps. Petrarch's dossier discloses zero legal apparatus, zero compliance disclosure, and a Business Ops Intern at $25/hr as the visible sourcing layer — the schleps are being stumbled into, not underwritten. One verifiable condition would flip me: Petrarch discloses a named GC or licensing partner plus one closed exclusive-license acquisition from a Series-B-or-later shutdown, at a disclosed price. Absent that, I pass and revisit when the regatta tacks show me who these founders actually are.
