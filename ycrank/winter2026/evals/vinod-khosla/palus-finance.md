# Palus Finance -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: a company that has reduced the probability of failure to the point where the consequences of success are inconsequential. If Palus succeeds beyond anyone's expectations -- captures every dollar of idle startup treasury in the country -- the result is that founders earn an extra hundred basis points on their cash. Nobody's life changes. No industry gets disrupted. No incumbent system becomes obsolete. The world operates exactly as it did before, except some bank accounts are slightly fatter. I tell founders: you're too talented to spend your best years optimizing a yield spread.

The founders have clearly identified a real inefficiency. Startups do overpay for liquidity they don't need. The innovator's dilemma framing is structurally valid -- Mercury and Brex can't easily cannibalize their MMF fee-sharing revenue to offer a bond portfolio product. That's a genuine insight. But a genuine insight about a small problem is still a small problem. At $10 billion in AUM, which would require capturing a massive share of U.S. startup idle cash, Palus generates $25 million in annual revenue on a 0.25% fee. That's not a venture-scale outcome. That's a lifestyle business wearing a fintech costume. When I backed Square, the consequence wasn't "merchants save 50 basis points on payment processing" -- it was "millions of people who literally could not participate in the digital economy suddenly can." That's a binary transformation. A yield bump is a rounding error.

What concerns me most is the absence of any technology here. The "innovation" is applying floating-rate agency MBS -- an asset class that Fortune 500 treasury teams have used for decades -- to a smaller customer segment. They're currently routing through an existing ETF managed by someone else. There is no proprietary algorithm, no novel instrument, no data advantage, no compounding technology moat. Any well-funded competitor can replicate this tomorrow. Mercury could hire two fixed-income analysts and launch a bond-portfolio tier next quarter. The fact that they haven't done it yet reflects prioritization, not impossibility. When Juniper built TCP/IP routers to challenge Cisco, Pradeep Sindhu was building something Cisco's architecture fundamentally couldn't accommodate. What Palus is building, Mercury's architecture can accommodate with a product update.

I'll give the founders credit where it's due. Sam Lushtak's background building investment research platforms for managers handling $3 trillion in assets provides legitimate domain exposure to the institutional fixed-income world -- he understands the asset class from the inside. The pivot from consumer savings to startup treasury within their YC batch shows real-time learning and intellectual honesty. They felt the pain themselves and redirected. That's the right instinct. But learning rate without contrarian ambition channels energy into optimizing known problems rather than attacking ones the consensus considers impossible. Nobody laughed at these founders. Nobody told them this was crazy. Every other YC company in their batch understood the pitch instantly -- and that's the problem. When everyone gets it, the upside is bounded.

The strongest bull case would require Palus to be a wedge into a much larger platform. Manage the treasury, then own the financial stack: lending against the portfolio, automated cash-flow forecasting, CFO-as-a-service, eventually replacing the bank relationship entirely. If the agency MBS product is just the entry point into becoming the financial operating system for startups, the consequence magnitude expands dramatically. For that to work, the founders would need to articulate that this yield product is chapter one of a story that ends with displacing Mercury entirely -- not optimizing the piece Mercury does poorly. I don't see that ambition in the current pitch. The company is positioned as a better mousetrap, not a world without mice. If Sam came to me and said "we're going to make traditional startup banking obsolete by owning the entire treasury-to-operations financial stack, and this MBS product is how we get in the door," I'd lean in harder. But the current framing is "better yields" -- and better yields is a feature, not a company.

I pass. The problem isn't that Palus will fail -- it might well succeed. The problem is that success at scale looks like a $25-50 million revenue business in a competitive market, not a category-defining transformation. Some problems are too important not to work on. This one isn't important enough.
