# Expanse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ismaeel Bashir built the multimodal HPC resource predictor at EPCC -- Edinburgh's Parallel Computing Centre -- as research, then walked it into YC. That's Expanse's signature: a HIGH on founder-lived-experience pinned against a LOW on physical verifiability. Expanse is the "researcher-of-the-place built the artifact in academia, but the product is invisible to anyone who doesn't run a Slurm cluster" company, and the gap between those two dimensions is the entire bet.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the rung I keep reaching for on the founder axis -- the kid who shipped Oculus from a garage before pivoting to defense had *touched* the hardware before he pitched it. Expanse passes that test in the academic register: Bashir's EPCC predictor "beat every published baseline" per the YC blurb, and Yafet Melake was a software engineer at G-Research running the exact quant-fund workloads the product is built to optimize. The divergence from Luckey is that Expanse's prior artifact is a research paper's worth of benchmarks, not a consumer product 2M people strapped to their faces. Lived, yes -- shipped to scale in an adjacent domain, not yet. That's why I'm in the upper-middle of the ladder on this dimension, not at the top.

The pass example -- the consensus YC AI-agent darling whose moat is "pure software abstraction I cannot install, photograph, or hand to a friend" -- is the rung Expanse sits closest to on physical verifiability. Expanse is an intelligence layer that ingests passive cluster telemetry and surfaces predictions to HPC schedulers; the only thing a non-cluster-admin can touch is the free OS Wastage Scanner at wastage.expanse.sh. I cannot ride Expanse the way I rode Uber in 2010, I cannot scan Expanse's camera on a pole the way Flock Safety put devices in the physical world. The pattern doesn't quite collapse to the pass example because at least the wastage scanner gives me one tactile artifact -- but it's a marketing funnel, not the product, and that distinction matters to me.

NVIDIA Run:ai (acquired 2024, now bundled into NVIDIA's GPU stack), Rafay Systems ($89M+ raised), and WoolyAI are all named in Expanse's own dossier as adjacent competitors -- which mechanically means the magically-weird filter fails. Over 18 months, the compounding question on this dimension is whether HPC research clusters and quant funds are *structurally* underserved by the hyperscaler-aligned vendors (Run:ai has channel conflict -- it sells to people who profit from more GPU consumption, not less) or whether NVIDIA simply ships prediction as a Run:ai feature in 2027. Expanse's bet that on-prem data sovereignty is the wedge is plausible, but it isn't "burn gas flares in North Dakota" weird -- it's an infra-optimization category with named giants already inside it.

Counterfactual on single-CEO authority: Bashir is named CEO, Nikodem Bieniek CTO, Melake COO, Eren Mendi CPO, all with distinct functional titles -- which would push Expanse into the upper rung if equity splits were disclosed. They are not. To flip this dimension a full tier, the dossier would need either a 4-1-1-1 founder split with documented tiebreaker, or Bashir naming the operating-agreement structure on a podcast. Four UK-and-Edinburgh-adjacent co-founders is the structure that occasionally produces the HQ Trivia (2017) feud pattern I track for; the structure is clean on paper, untested under stress.

Anduril (2017) is the single anchor on the capital-intensive / unsexy axis. Anduril was unsexy defense hardware most VCs declined on moral or capital-intensity grounds. Expanse is unsexy in customer profile -- national supercomputing centres and quant funds, both slow-procurement -- but it is *software* sitting on top of Slurm and Kubernetes, with no hardware capex of its own. The unsexiness is in the lane, not the build, which is why this dimension scores at the lower-middle. The $320B in FY2025 hyperscaler AI capex Expanse cites is the right tailwind framing, but Expanse isn't building any of that infrastructure -- it's selling a prediction layer on top.

My 36-month forecast: NVIDIA acquires Expanse to fold the predictor into the Run:ai stack at $80M-$200M, OR Expanse grinds to $2-5M ARR with 5-10 named national-lab and quant-fund logos and stalls in the slow-procurement valley. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: Bashir's EPCC research is acquisition-grade IP exactly because the product is invisible to anyone but cluster admins -- a hard standalone scale story but a clean tuck-in for the GPU incumbent who already bought Run:ai. The thing that's HIGH (founder built the predictor) and the thing that's LOW (no tactile artifact, no consumer pull) converge on "acqui-hire by the channel that already owns the customer."

The strongest case against my forecast is that Bashir is a Hassabis-class polymath override -- a research-credentialed founder with a technical artifact that "beat every published baseline," and that's exactly the DeepMind (~2013) exception I've made before for pedigreed founders whose cross-domain compounding outweighs the outsider-trajectory bias. If Expanse becomes the Datadog of HPC -- sticky telemetry layer that expands from prediction into orchestration -- the NVIDIA acquisition window closes and Expanse becomes the standalone. I'm not buying it: the bear is named in the dossier itself -- Run:ai bundled with NVIDIA's GPU stack is the channel-conflict killer, and Expanse's prediction wedge is precisely the feature NVIDIA's product team will ship in their next release. I'd flip my verdict on one verifiable condition: a publicly disclosed paid contract with a named tier-1 HPC center (NERSC, Oak Ridge, Argonne, or EPCC itself) before end of 2026. Until then, Expanse is the rare pre-seed where I respect the founder's lived experience but cannot find the tactile artifact or the magically-weird angle that makes me write the check. Neutral.
