# Fort -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Fort: this is three Tesla engineers who met at a gym and decided to build a better fitness tracker. Miranda designed 4680 battery cells for the Cybertruck, Paul architected sensing systems for the Semi and Robotaxi, Zac built satellites at SpaceX and powertrain structures at Rivian. That's genuinely exceptional hardware talent -- possibly the strongest hardware team I've seen come through YC in a while. If my primary question were "can this team manufacture and ship a complex consumer electronics product," I'd feel great about it. But that's never my primary question. My primary question is always: tell me how you got here, and why this problem chose you. And what I hear from Fort is that three elite engineers from elite institutions like lifting weights and wanted a device that tracked their workouts better. That's authentic user-founder alignment, and I respect it. But it's not the kind of biographical inseparability I look for -- where the founder's life story and the company's mission are the same thread. Compare this to the Contraline founder who spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would fund it, or the way my own discussions about the taxi medallion system with Scott preceded my conviction about Uber by years. Fort's founders are solving a personal annoyance, not a biographical imperative.

The bigger issue for me is that this is one of the most consensus markets I can imagine. Fitness wearables. Whoop is at $3.6 billion valuation. Oura just raised at $11 billion. Garmin's fitness segment alone does $2.36 billion a year. Everyone -- and I mean everyone -- agrees that wearables are a massive growing market and that strength training is trending. The "strength tracking gap" Fort identifies is real, but it's a feature gap inside a well-mapped market, not the kind of insight that makes other investors uncomfortable. Nobody at a dinner party is going to tell Miranda she's crazy for building a better fitness tracker. And that's precisely the problem for my framework. The most promising companies I've backed were ones where other investors thought the founders had lost their minds. SpaceX when private rockets were considered delusional. Crusoe Energy when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded like a fever dream. When Fort raised from YC, Afore, Weekend Fund, Theory Forge, and angels from OpenAI and Tesla -- that's not a company struggling to find believers. That's consensus capital flowing to a consensus thesis.

The competitive dynamics concern me in a specific way. Whoop acquired Push -- literally the closest prior product to what Fort is building -- in 2021, and then discontinued the standalone hardware, integrating only a simplified muscular load feature into their platform. The charitable read is that Whoop's subscription model incentivizes broad-audience features over niche strength tracking, leaving a gap Fort can exploit. The less charitable read, and the one I keep coming back to, is that the team that built Push couldn't make wrist-based velocity tracking accurate or compelling enough to justify a standalone product even with Whoop's resources and data behind them. Fort hasn't published any independent accuracy benchmarks. For a device that promises per-rep velocity, proximity to failure, and per-muscle stimulus categorization from a wrist-worn sensor, that's a significant absence. The physics of inferring barbell velocity from wrist motion is genuinely hard -- your wrist doesn't move the same way as the bar -- and I haven't seen evidence that Fort has solved what Push couldn't.

For the bull case to work, you'd have to believe three things: that the longevity movement has permanently shifted consumer demand toward strength training in a way that creates a dedicated-device market (plausible -- Attia and Huberman have genuinely moved culture), that Fort's multi-placement form factor and ML models can achieve accuracy that Push couldn't (possible but unvalidated), and that incumbents like Whoop and Garmin won't prioritize this feature as strength training grows (unlikely given Whoop's 122% YoY growth in strength workouts). If all three are true, Fort's Tesla-grade hardware team could build a category-defining product with a compounding data moat. That's a real upside case. But it requires the incumbents to stay asleep while a startup with no shipping product and no validated accuracy takes the market. That's execution-risk investing, not conviction-against-consensus investing.

What I don't see here is economic access -- Fort is a $289-$349 device for affluent people who care about longevity and already have gym memberships. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or create opportunity for people who lack it. It doesn't operate in a stigmatized or regulated space that deters other investors -- consumer electronics has regulatory requirements, but nothing like the FDA pathways or defense procurement complexity that create real moats. These are the dimensions where my portfolio companies tend to shine and where Fort simply doesn't register. This is a well-executed product idea from impressive engineers in a crowded, well-understood market. Any investor could evaluate this on its merits. It doesn't require the kind of conviction-over-consensus judgment that defines my best bets, and that's how I know it's not for me.
