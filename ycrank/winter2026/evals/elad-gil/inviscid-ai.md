# Inviscid AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Inviscid AI is that this is not a non-obvious market. Building automation is a $100B+ category with $125M going to PassiveLogic, $103M+ to BrainBox AI (acquired by Trane), $50M to EkkoSense, $45M to 75F -- all in the last 18 months. Data center cooling is a consensus bet that every infrastructure investor is tracking. When I look at a market and see five well-funded competitors with clear category leaders already emerging, the structural moment has already been recognized. The opportunity for asymmetric returns comes from seeing a market inflection before capital pours in, not after. This is the opposite of what I saw with PagerDuty, where operations alerting seemed boring and niche to most investors. Everyone understands that data centers need better cooling and buildings waste energy. The TAM slides write themselves. That's the problem.

The specific technical angle -- replacing traditional CFD solvers with physics-informed neural networks for real-time building simulation -- is genuinely interesting as a capability unlock. Traditional CFD taking days per simulation run versus Inviscid's claimed 1000x speedup is a meaningful threshold crossing. But PINNs are well-published academic research, NVIDIA has made their Modulus framework available specifically for physics-informed ML, and Cadence already markets a "Reality Digital Twin Platform" that combines CFD with AI for data center digital twins. When the underlying methodology is open and the dominant compute platform provider is commoditizing the inference layer, the technical approach alone isn't a moat. It's a starting position.

The case studies on their website -- HVAC vent optimization, coastal infrastructure, storm surge forecasting -- expose a deeper concern. These are three fundamentally different verticals with different buyers, different sales cycles, and different domain expertise requirements. For a 2-person team, this scatter pattern is the opposite of operational clarity. The single-miracle test asks whether the founders can name their one critical obstacle in one sentence and organize everything around it. Spreading across building HVAC, coastal engineering, and weather forecasting suggests they haven't yet figured out where their physics simulation technology has the sharpest product-market fit. Compare this to how Harvey laser-focused on legal AI -- one vertical, one buyer, one workflow, one miracle (model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy). Inviscid AI appears to be searching for its market, which is normal at pre-seed, but compounds the risk when the broader category is already well-capitalized.

The strongest bull case: if AI workload density continues doubling every 12-18 months, data center operators will face thermal management challenges that pure sensor-driven approaches can't solve -- you need physics-based models to predict airflow dynamics in novel rack configurations, not just optimize against historical patterns. A real-time CFD engine that integrates with BMS could become genuinely critical infrastructure, and the BrainBox AI acquisition validates that HVAC OEMs will pay $100M+ for this kind of capability. If Inviscid focuses exclusively on data center thermal management and builds deep integrations with hyperscale operators, the acquisition path alone could generate meaningful returns. But that's a $50-200M exit trajectory, not a platform company trajectory, and it requires the team to narrow focus to one vertical and out-execute well-funded competitors in a recognized market -- neither of which the current evidence suggests is happening.

On the founding team: Ziming Qiu is a PhD student in ECE at NYU with published ML research in computer vision and image analysis, which is adjacent to but not directly in computational fluid dynamics or building science. Kabir Jain's background is essentially undocumented beyond a stated interest in "physics and AI." Neither founder appears to have operational experience in building management, HVAC systems, or data center operations. The best deep-tech companies I've backed had founders whose domain expertise was the product -- not researchers applying general ML tools to an unfamiliar domain. The gap between building accurate PINN benchmarks in a research environment and deploying reliable autonomous building control in production is enormous, and domain expertise in the operational environment matters as much as the ML capability.

I'm passing. The market is real but consensus. The technology is interesting but commoditizing. The team hasn't demonstrated domain depth in their target verticals. And the multi-vertical spread at a 2-person stage signals that the company hasn't found its wedge. When I invest in "boring but critical" enterprise infrastructure, the founding team typically has deep operational scars in the exact domain they're building for -- that's what makes the insight non-obvious and the execution credible. I don't see that signal here.
