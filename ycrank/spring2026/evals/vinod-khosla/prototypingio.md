# Prototyping.io -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that nobody would laugh at this idea. AI-powered manufacturing quoting and DFM analysis in 2026 -- Xometry is doing it, Protolabs is doing it, RMFG raised $4.5 million for it, Jiga raised $19 million for it, Toolpath raised $10 million for it. When I wrote the check for Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, people thought I'd lost my mind investing in fake hamburgers. The signal I've always chased is the dismissive laugh from experts -- it means you're in territory where the outsiders haven't yet crowded in and the incumbents are too proud to look. Here, the incumbents are already building this. Five well-funded startups launched in the same 18-month window. This is a consensus bet dressed up as innovation. When everyone validates your approach, you're probably optimizing an existing system by 20%, not replacing it.

Let me apply my foundational question: if Prototyping.io succeeds completely -- captures the entire AI-native manufacturing ordering market -- what changes in the world? Hardware engineers get DFM feedback in minutes instead of days. They upload one CAD file instead of emailing three vendors. That is genuinely useful. It is also genuinely inconsequential at the scale I invest for. The online CNC machining services market is $1.5 billion, growing to $3.2 billion by 2033. Even the broader CNC services market is $7.28 billion. The consequence of total success is a good business in a modest market -- not an industry rendered obsolete, not a resource constraint lifted, not a fundamental change in how the physical world operates. Compare that to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means replacing fossil fuel power generation entirely, or to OpenAI, where success means machine intelligence surpasses human capability across domains. The magnitude gap is not subtle. These founders are too capable for a problem this contained.

The strongest bull case requires me to imagine that AI-native DFM analysis doesn't just accelerate ordering -- it transforms how physical products are designed from first principles, compressing hardware development cycles so dramatically that it triggers a Cambrian explosion of physical products. If the feedback loop between design intent and manufacturing constraint becomes instantaneous and intelligent, you could argue the entire $402 billion global machining market restructures around whoever owns that intelligence layer. And the founder pairing has surface appeal: Revanth's IIT silver medal and Schlumberger manufacturing experience provides the domain knowledge, while Prerit's Microsoft AI background and distributed systems publications provide the engineering capability. But here's where I get stuck -- this bull case requires Prototyping.io to evolve from a marketplace intermediary into something categorically different, and nothing in the current positioning suggests that ambition. The website says "Request Early Access" for what appears to be a quoting platform. The advertised specs (±0.0001" tolerances, 34 ft parts, six manufacturing processes) describe a marketplace breadth play, not a technology transformation play. You don't disrupt manufacturing by building a better front door to the same supply chain.

The team composition reinforces my concern about ambition calibration. Revanth is a mechanical engineer from Schlumberger -- a classic industry insider. He knows what DFM problems look like because he's lived them. That's valuable domain expertise, but it's exactly the kind of experience that, in my observation, produces 5% year-to-year improvements rather than whole new markets. The founders who built my best investments didn't know what their industry considered impossible, which freed them to attempt it. Pat Brown didn't know the food industry. Peter Beck didn't know aerospace. Pradeep Sindhu came from Xerox PARC, not Cisco. An IIT-trained mechanical engineer building a better mechanical parts ordering system is the definition of an expert executing within the established playbook. I see no evidence of contrarian courage -- no signal that they're attempting something the consensus deems crazy.

On timing: the underlying technology curve is real. Vision models and LLMs crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 that makes automated CAD geometry interpretation feasible. But being right about a technology trend is not enough -- you also need to be early. Five funded competitors in the same window means the timing advantage has already been arbitraged away. RMFG has shipped 100,000+ parts. Jiga has NASA and Siemens as customers. Xometry processes millions of parts through AI quoting. Prototyping.io is pre-launch with no disclosed traction, entering a race where the starting gun fired two years ago. The rate of change in underlying AI capability benefits everyone in this cohort equally -- there is no proprietary data flywheel, no patent portfolio, and no evidence of a technical approach that would compound faster than competitors' approaches.

I pass. The consequence of success is a solid business in a $3 billion market segment, not a transformation of how manufacturing works. The category is consensus, the positioning is incremental, and the founders -- while technically capable -- are industry insiders running a known playbook in a crowded field. If they want my attention, they need to tell me which trillion-dollar system they intend to make obsolete and why every expert thinks they're crazy for trying.
