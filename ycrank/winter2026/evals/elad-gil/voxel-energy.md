# Voxel Energy -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural collision here is genuinely interesting: two independent adoption curves are converging at the same moment. AI compute demand has overwhelmed grid capacity for the first time, creating a binding power constraint with 5-year interconnection queues. Simultaneously, first-generation EV batteries are retiring at meaningful volume -- the second-life battery market is projected to grow from ~25-30 GWh today to 330-350 GWh by 2030 at 65% CAGR. Neither of these conditions existed three years ago. This is the kind of timing convergence where I've seen asymmetric returns -- two cost curves crossing at the same moment, unlocking an approach that was impossible in 2022. The specific insight that second-life EV batteries at 70-80% remaining capacity can power data centers at a fraction of new battery cost is non-obvious. Most investors in the data center power space are thinking about nuclear, new solar, thermal storage, or natural gas. Nobody is looking at the used battery supply chain from retiring Teslas and Leafs as a strategic asset. That's the kind of arbitrage I pay attention to.

But here's my concern: data center power is not a non-obvious market. It's one of the most consensus investment themes in 2025-2026. Exowatt has $140M from a16z and Sam Altman with a 90 GWh pipeline. Crusoe has over $2 billion at a $10 billion valuation. Enchanted Rock has a 100 MW contract with Microsoft. The Washington Post is writing about off-grid data center power. When the newspaper of record covers your market thesis, the structural moment is real but the asymmetric returns may already be priced in. The non-obvious element isn't the market -- it's the specific approach within it. Second-life batteries plus DC-native architecture with 4% vs 30% conversion losses is a genuine technical differentiation. But a differentiated approach within a consensus market is a very different bet than a differentiated approach within a non-obvious market. The former competes against well-funded incumbents; the latter has room to build before incumbents notice.

The team composition is notably well-matched to this exact problem -- almost suspiciously well-matched, which is actually what I want to see. Pfeiffer built and shipped electric vehicles at Maxwell Vehicles since 2019, designed Tesla electric truck prototypes, led power electronics at Washington Hyperloop, and earned Forbes 30U30. Spencer project-managed Tesla Autopilot and founded three hardware companies. Schmidt brings a decade of data center construction management. This isn't a software team pivoting into energy; it's an energy-and-hardware team that understands the physical constraints of the problem. They already have a working prototype running off-grid compute, thousands of acres under contract, and are amassing battery inventory. For a pre-seed company, that's real execution velocity -- hardware shipped, not decks polished. The Sift monitoring partnership shows they're thinking about operational reliability from day one.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if the DC-native architecture and second-life battery sourcing create genuinely better unit economics -- lower $/MW delivered, faster deployment timeline -- then the competitive moat is in cost structure, not in technology novelty. Crusoe uses fossil fuels. Exowatt uses experimental thermal storage. Enchanted Rock is bridge-to-grid, not permanent. Voxel could be the only fully renewable, fully off-grid, permanently deployed solution with the cheapest storage medium available. If hyperscalers care about speed-to-power AND renewable credentials AND cost -- and ESG pressure suggests they increasingly do -- then Voxel's approach could win despite the capital disadvantage. The Instacart-to-Webvan pattern applies: maybe the prior attempts at off-grid data centers failed or compromised because the supply chain for cheap storage didn't exist yet. Now it does.

Where I get stuck is the single-miracle test. The obvious miracle is proving second-life battery reliability at data center SLAs -- 99.999% uptime with heterogeneous battery packs of varying degradation, chemistry, and remaining cycle life. That's a hard but identifiable engineering problem. But the business also needs to secure consistent battery supply from OEMs and recyclers, navigate off-grid permitting in jurisdictions that lack frameworks for large-scale battery installations, and out-execute competitors with 100x more capital. That's three or four miracles, not one. And unlike Harvey, where the critical miracle (model fidelity) was being delivered by foundation model labs while Harvey focused on workflow, here every miracle is Voxel's own problem to solve. The "software-aware vs. software-driven" question also nags at me -- at its core, this is a power infrastructure and construction company with a software monitoring layer, not a software company with a power component. The margin structure and capital requirements will reflect that reality regardless of how the pitch frames it.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is limited in ways that cap my excitement. Once you've sold powered data center capacity to a hyperscaler, the customer relationship is sticky but narrow. You're a power provider, not a platform. Stripe's API created a developer relationship that enabled distributing Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through the same integration point. Voxel's data center creates a landlord-tenant relationship. You can expand capacity, maybe add cooling or monitoring services, but you're not becoming a distribution channel for multiple product lines. The ceiling on this business is more Equinix than Stripe -- and that's a fine business, but it's not the generational-compounding-distribution-engine I look for at the earliest stages.

I respect what this team has built in a compressed timeline, and the timing convergence of AI power demand with second-life battery supply is structurally sound. But the market is consensus, the competitive dynamics are brutal, the capital requirements are immense, and the business model is physical infrastructure with limited platform dynamics. This is a well-constructed company in a real market -- but it's not my kind of bet.
