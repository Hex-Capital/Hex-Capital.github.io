# Dispatch -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The structural positioning here is exactly right. A logistics infrastructure layer for space manufacturing — reentry vehicles as a service — is the picks-and-shovels play in a market where everyone else is trying to be the gold miner. When I backed Alchemy, the thesis was identical: don't own the dApps, own the developer platform underneath them. Dispatch is trying to be the return-trip infrastructure that every orbital manufacturer needs but shouldn't have to build themselves. That instinct maps to how I think about where value accrues. The problem is that picks-and-shovels logic works when the shovel is software and the gold rush creates millions of miners. Here, the "shovels" are physical reentry capsules requiring tens of millions of dollars each, and the "miners" number fewer than a dozen companies worldwide. The leverage ratio is inverted.

The founder-specific-knowledge question yields a complicated answer. Case spent time in the Office of the CTO at Astranis doing spacecraft architecture and worked thermal engineering on Lunar Gateway. Mello led avionics at Astranis and did hardware-software integration at Zoox and Apple. These are directly relevant credentials — thermal protection and avionics are the two hardest subsystems in a reentry vehicle. But this is institutional knowledge acquired through good career choices, not the kind of non-transferable insight that emerges from years of obsessive personal curiosity about a problem nobody else was thinking about. Hundreds of engineers at SpaceX, Rocket Lab, and Astranis have comparable or deeper experience with these exact systems. When I invested in Twitter, what struck me about Ev Williams wasn't that he'd worked at a communications company — it was that he'd spent a decade thinking about the compression of public expression in a way no job title could teach. I don't see that depth of obsession reflected here. The 100x cheaper heat shield claim is the most interesting data point — if it reflects a genuinely novel approach to thermal protection materials, that could indicate knowledge the industry hasn't absorbed yet. But the claim is unverified, and a ground test with rocket exhaust is categorically different from surviving Mach 20+ atmospheric reentry. Inversion Space had $125M and their first orbital attempt failed due to a propulsion short circuit.

The leverage architecture is where this investment breaks down for me. Every form of leverage I've invested in — code, media, capital, protocol, network effects — shares one property: marginal cost approaching zero as usage scales. AngelList serves a thousand more founders with the same codebase. Replit serves a million more programmers without building a million more IDEs. A reentry vehicle serves one mission, returns one payload, and must be physically refurbished before the next flight. Even with refurbishability, this is a linear-scaling physical operations business with massive capital requirements per unit of capacity. The dossier shows competitors spending $40M to $329M before reaching first flight or operational status, and Dispatch has approximately $500K. That's not a funding gap — that's a three-order-of-magnitude capital chasm. This company will need to raise $50M+ just to attempt its first orbital mission, and at each round, it will be competing for capital against Varda (five successful missions, $329M raised) and Inversion ($125M with DoD contracts). The physics of fundraising in capital-intensive hardware are brutal: each dilutive round erodes the asymmetric upside that makes angel investing work.

The contrarian timing test fails cleanly. Space manufacturing was contrarian in 2020. In 2026, Varda has completed five successful reentry missions, Scientific American is writing about semiconductors in space, and at least seven funded startups are building reentry vehicles. Reditus Space — a YC W25 company — is pursuing an almost identical "Microgravity-as-a-Service" model at a similar stage. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I bought Bitcoin, it was dismissed as nerd money. Dispatch is entering a market that venture capital has already validated with over half a billion dollars in combined competitor funding. The consensus has already shifted. That doesn't mean the company can't succeed, but it means the pricing of optimism has already occurred, and the asymmetric upside that justifies angel risk is diminished.

The strongest bull case: if the 100x cost reduction in heat shield fabrication is real and transferable to flight-grade hardware, Dispatch could be to reentry vehicles what SpaceX was to launch — the cost-structure breakthrough that expands the market by an order of magnitude. A world where reentry costs drop 100x is a world where thousands of experiments get returned from orbit instead of dozens, and the picks-and-shovels provider at that cost point captures the infrastructure layer of an entirely new manufacturing paradigm. The Astranis pedigree is real, the co-founder relationship is forged in actual spacecraft work, and building a full-scale heat shield in an apartment demonstrates the scrappy resourcefulness that sometimes predicts greatness. If I could verify the cost claim independently and see a credible path to first orbital demonstration within the current funding trajectory, this would move from neutral to interesting. But the gap between "interesting engineering project" and "investable company with leverage mechanics" remains wide.

I respect the founders and the structural instinct. But I invest in leverage, and this company's physics don't create it. Every additional customer requires another physical vehicle, another launch, another recovery operation. The technology is real engineering, not a new form of leverage. I'd want to see either a verified cost breakthrough that fundamentally changes the market's economics, or a software/data layer that compounds with each mission — something that turns operational experience into a scalable asset rather than just institutional knowledge. Without that, this is a capital-intensive hardware race in a crowded field, and my money compounds better elsewhere.
