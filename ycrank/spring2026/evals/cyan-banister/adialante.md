# Adialante -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Efrain Torres isn't the novel physics or the mobile MRI business model -- it's that he grew up on the south side of Chicago and built his entire PhD around the question of how to bring cancer screening to communities like his. That biographical thread is what I listen for before anything else. This isn't a founder who saw a market report about portable MRI and decided to jump in. This is someone who spent years in one of the world's premier MRI research labs at the University of Minnesota, developing genuinely new physics -- eliminating an entire subsystem of the MRI machine that's been standard for forty years -- because he understood viscerally what it means when your community doesn't have access to a million-dollar scanner. I don't have his full story yet, and I'd want to sit down and hear it without any slides in front of us, but what I can see reminds me of the Contraline founder -- someone who emerged from years of dedicated academic work that other investors couldn't see the value in, solving a problem rooted in something deeply personal.

What makes Adialante genuinely interesting to me isn't just the technology, though the FREE approach -- eliminating gradient coils entirely through a novel frequency-modulation method -- is the kind of physics-level innovation that creates real defensibility. What I find compelling is the business model architecture. MRI has been locked behind million-dollar installations, specialized facilities with reinforced floors and RF shielding, and months-long installation timelines. That's an invisible system of exclusion. Most people never think about why their local clinic doesn't have an MRI machine, but the answer is structural: the economics don't work for any facility smaller than a major hospital. Adialante's mobile per-scan model eliminates all of that friction -- zero capital expenditure, no infrastructure requirements, just a truck that shows up. This is the same pattern I've seen work before: making an opaque, exclusive system transparent and accessible. Affirm did it for credit. Carta did it for equity. Flock Safety did it for public safety data. When you take something that was gatekept by cost and complexity and bring it to everyone, that's where the real returns live -- financial and human.

The competitive landscape is the strongest objection, and I take it seriously. Promaxo already has FDA clearance and 69 installed systems for prostate MRI. Hyperfine is public and shipping portable brain MRI. These aren't hypothetical competitors -- they're generating revenue. But here's what I think the spreadsheet analysts miss: Promaxo sells hardware to urologists at a capital cost, which means their growth is gated by each clinic's willingness to make a six-or-seven-figure purchase decision. Adialante's per-scan mobile model is structurally different -- it's the difference between selling taxis medallions and building Uber. And the incumbents -- Siemens, GE, Philips -- cannot easily replicate a per-scan mobile service without cannibalizing their core hardware and service-contract revenue. That business model conflict is a moat that doesn't show up in patent filings but is just as real. The question is whether this team, which comes entirely from academic backgrounds with no visible commercial or operational experience, can actually execute the mobile healthcare operations layer -- fleet management, staffing, multi-jurisdiction compliance. That's a genuinely different skill set from building MRI physics, and it's the gap that keeps me from being fully convicted.

The bull case here is significant: if the FREE technology achieves clinical-quality imaging at one-tenth the size and cost of conventional MRI, this isn't a niche prostate screening company -- it's a platform that could reshape how and where all cancer imaging happens. Prostate is the wedge, but the physics doesn't stop there. The NSF SBIR Phase II award provides independent validation that the technical approach is sound, and the team's credentials -- five PhDs from world-class programs, an ISMRM Rabi Award winner -- represent genuine depth. If Torres can pair that technical firepower with operational leadership for the mobile deployment side, this could be a company that saves thousands of lives while building a multi-billion dollar business. That combination of impact and return is exactly what I look for.

What gives me pause beyond the operational execution question is the FDA timeline. This is pre-submission with novel physics, and the path to clearance is uncertain. Competitors with conventional approaches have already cleared that hurdle. Every month of regulatory delay is a month Promaxo is building installed base and clinical evidence. But I've invested through regulatory uncertainty before -- Contraline faced the same dynamic, and the founders who persist through that process build moats that fast-followers can't easily cross. The $2.43M raised against competitors with $60-260M in funding doesn't scare me either. I take extraordinary risk, and sometimes the most underfunded company in a category is the one that's actually right about the approach. Torres has been told this is too hard by enough people to prove he's not going away. I'd want to meet him and hear the full story -- every detail of how a kid from the south side of Chicago ended up reinventing MRI physics -- before writing the check. But the signal is strong enough that I'd take that meeting tomorrow.
