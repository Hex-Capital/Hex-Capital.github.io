# Axis -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Axis is that the problem space genuinely has schlep. Physical commodity trading is one of those domains that most AI founders would unconsciously skip right over. The information environment is a mess — broker commentary arriving as PDFs, geopolitical analysis scattered across a dozen news sources, refinery throughput data in proprietary formats, OTC deal flow communicated by phone. Every smart person building an AI startup right now is reaching for the clean-data problems: code generation, document search, sales automation. Almost nobody wants to wade into the murky world where a metals trader at Trafigura is synthesizing Indonesian export restrictions, LME warehouse reports, and a text from a broker in Singapore — all before lunch. That's a schlep that should produce structural competitive advantages for whoever endures it.

But here's my problem: I can't tell whether these founders actually stumbled into this pain or manufactured the idea from the outside. Eric Zhu was a quantitative trading intern at what appears to be TransMarket Group, a prop trading firm focused on derivatives. Ian Wang was at Yale and is described as "interested in trading." Quantitative trading and physical commodity trading are cousins, not siblings. A quant trader works with structured data, systematic models, and electronic markets. A physical commodity trader works in a world of relationships, unstructured intelligence, and bilateral deals negotiated over the phone. The analytical workflow that Axis claims to improve — synthesizing broker data, macro narratives, and geopolitical events — is the daily reality of physical traders, not quant interns. When I ask the central question — "how did they find this problem?" — the honest answer is: I don't know. And the absence of that story is itself a signal. When Drew Houston pitched Dropbox, the origin was obvious — he kept forgetting his USB drive. When the Collisons pitched Stripe, they'd been personally frustrated by payment APIs. Here, two recent graduates are building "AI for commodity trading desks" and the path from their experience to this specific problem is unclear.

The complete absence of any visible product compounds the concern. No GitHub repos for either founder. The website returns a page title and nothing else. No prototype, no demo, no user signals, no waitlist. For an AI company that should be building models to parse unstructured market data, I'd expect to see at least some evidence that the founders can build. Zhu's math background at UChicago suggests technical capability in the abstract, but capability and building are different things. I want to see code, not credentials. Reddit existed because Huffman and Ohanian sat down and built it. Stripe existed because the Collisons wrote the code themselves. At this stage, the most important evidence a two-person team can present is a working thing — and I see nothing.

The strongest bull case goes like this: physical commodity trading is exactly the kind of domain where schlep blindness operates at full force. The buyers are rich, the problem is acute, and nobody else in the YC ecosystem is seriously attacking the analytical layer for physical trading desks. CommodityAI (W24) went after operations — shipment tracking, document processing — which is the logistics schlep, not the analytical schlep. Kpler and Vortexa sell structured cargo data, not AI-driven synthesis of unstructured intelligence. If these founders have been quietly building relationships with trading desks through Zhu's quant network, and if there's a working product behind that empty website, the opportunity is real. Commodities make up a third of global goods exports. The trading houses that move physical oil, metals, and grain generate enormous revenue and would pay serious money for tools that actually work. But "if" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that paragraph.

The go-to-market challenge is the other wall. Institutional commodity trading houses — Vitol, Glencore, Cargill, the energy majors — are among the most difficult enterprise customers on earth. They are paranoid about information security, slow to adopt new vendors, and deeply relationship-driven. Two recent graduates without visible industry connections face an enterprise sales cycle that could easily consume their entire runway before they close a single desk. This isn't a developer tool that spreads through GitHub stars. This requires walking into trading floors and earning trust from people who've been doing this for twenty years. That's a second schlep on top of the technical schlep, and I see no evidence the founders have a plan for it beyond the inferred "existing relationships from their quantitative trading backgrounds."

I'm passing. The problem has genuine schlep and the market is real, but I need to see organic founder-problem fit and evidence of building. Two recent graduates who may have observed trading from an adjacent perch, with no visible product, no traction signals, and a brutal enterprise go-to-market ahead of them — this doesn't cross my threshold. If Zhu had spent three years on a physical commodities desk and could describe the specific moment he realized traders were drowning in unstructured data, that would change things. If they had a working prototype and one trading desk using it, even informally, that would change things. Right now this reads like a smart market analysis — AI is powerful, commodity trading is data-intensive, therefore build AI for commodity trading — rather than a problem that grew organically from the founders' lives.
