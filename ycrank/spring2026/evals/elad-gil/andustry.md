# Andustry -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Andustry is the competitive landscape, and it tells me something important about market timing. LightSource Labs raised $33M. Matchory raised EUR 6M. Cavela raised $8.6M. MESH Works has processed $884M in RFQs. When I see four or five funded companies in overlapping space, my question isn't "which one wins" -- it's "has the nonobvious window already closed?" AI-powered procurement automation isn't a thesis you need to explain to anyone in 2026. The dossier itself cites an expectation that 86% of procurement teams will adopt AI by end of year. That's not a structural shift most investors haven't recognized -- that's front-page consensus. The underlying catalyst is real (LLMs crossing the accuracy threshold for specification extraction and multi-parameter RFQ generation), but the window where you could enter this market with a structural timing advantage was 2023-2024, not Spring 2026. Andustry is entering a market that has already attracted significant capital from investors who read the same structural shift a year or two earlier.

The vertical focus on industrial machinery is the most interesting strategic choice here, and it's where I'd construct the bull case. Industrial goods procurement -- specifically off-the-shelf machinery, not custom parts -- sits in a gap between Xometry's custom manufacturing marketplace and Alibaba's general trade platform. German Mittelstand companies buying CNC machines, industrial robots, and packaging equipment still rely on trade shows and manual outreach because the existing platforms don't serve their specification complexity. Johann Sturken's family background in industrial goods trading is authentic domain signal -- he's seen this workflow firsthand, not just researched it. The EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume, if real, suggests actual buyer-supplier matching is occurring, not just sign-ups. If Andustry can build density in specific machinery subcategories before the horizontal AI procurement platforms move down-market, there's a path to a defensible vertical marketplace. The Xometry analog is instructive: Xometry hit a $2B+ public market cap by owning custom manufacturing transactions. An "Xometry for catalog industrial goods" is a legitimate multi-billion dollar outcome. But that outcome requires winning a marketplace liquidity race against better-capitalized competitors while simultaneously proving that vertical specialization matters more than horizontal breadth -- and I'm not convinced vertical focus in an AI-agent layer creates a durable moat when any horizontal platform can add "industrial machinery" as a category.

The infrastructure-layer test is where Andustry struggles most in my framework. This is an application sitting on top of commodity LLM capabilities, not a platform that other businesses build on. The 500K+ verified supplier database could be a genuine data asset -- but only if it's proprietary. If it's aggregated from public directories (Thomasnet, Alibaba, Kompass), any competitor with a weekend of web scraping can replicate it. The dossier explicitly flags this: "no evidence this data is proprietary vs. aggregated from public sources." When I invested in Stripe, the infrastructure thesis was clear -- every internet business would build payments on top of Stripe's API, creating compounding switching costs with every integration. I don't see that dynamic here. Andustry processes transactions; it doesn't become a layer that other software depends on. The "Best Offer Engine" and automated RFQ generation are features, not infrastructure. Features get copied. Infrastructure gets embedded.

The single-miracle test is unfavorable. The technology miracle is free -- LLM capabilities are improving on their own curve, and Andustry benefits from this exogenously. But they still need at least two independent breakthroughs: building marketplace liquidity on both sides (buyers AND suppliers) in a category with notoriously long sales cycles, AND differentiating against competitors with 4-10x more capital. Industrial procurement buyers don't switch platforms quickly -- these are high-value, high-trust transactions where a two-person team from Munich competing against established players with in-person audit capabilities (MESH Works) and enterprise sales teams (LightSource Labs) faces a structural disadvantage. Long sales cycles plus a thin team plus well-funded competition is a compounding problem, not a single hurdle.

The founders bring real domain context -- Johann's mechanical engineering pedigree (RWTH, TUM, Cambridge) and family exposure to industrial trading, plus McKinsey A&D consulting, give him credibility with industrial buyers in a way that a pure software founder wouldn't have. Het Dave's SaaS-to-AI transformation experience at Aries Global is relevant to the product build. But neither founder has operated inside a scaling company. They've advised and consulted, not built and shipped at scale. For a marketplace business where the entire challenge is operational execution -- supplier onboarding velocity, buyer activation, transaction processing -- I'd want to see evidence of prior operational intensity, not strategic advisory experience.
