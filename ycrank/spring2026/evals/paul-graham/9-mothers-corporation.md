# 9 Mothers -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most important thing about 9 Mothers is what's missing from the dossier: any explanation of how these founders found this problem. Russell Smith spent a decade building QA automation software. Roman Khomenko spent eight years as an AI engineer at the same QA company. Bogdan Pyzh was a cybersecurity consultant. And now they're building autonomous weapon turrets. When I ask the question I always ask first -- "how did you find this problem?" -- the answer appears to be that counter-drone defense became the hottest category in defense tech after Ukraine, defense-tech funding nearly doubled to $49.1B in 2025, and smart engineers noticed the opportunity. That's the startup idea machine at work, not organic discovery. The best I can do is note that Khomenko studied in Kharkiv and might have a personal connection to the Ukrainian conflict. But even personal proximity to a war doesn't automatically generate insight into weapons engineering. The idea could have been conceived by anyone reading defense industry news.

This matters more than usual because the category is emphatically not a schlep-blindness opportunity. When I talk about schlep blindness, I mean problems everyone knows about but unconsciously avoids because the work is ugly. Payments before Stripe. File sync before Dropbox. Counter-drone defense in 2026 is the opposite: Allen Control Systems has raised $30M from Craft Ventures, won a U.S. Army xTechOverwatch competition, secured a SOCOM maritime contract, and tripled its Austin operations. Anduril is raising $4B at a $60B valuation. Epirus has over $550M in funding. DroneShield is a public company doing AU$216.5M in annual revenue. The schlep hasn't kept anyone away -- it's attracted billions in capital. When I noted in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies "are not working on AI," the broader principle applies: when every investor is excited about a category, the upside has largely been priced in. 9 Mothers is entering a race where the starting gun fired two years ago and several runners are already rounding the first turn.

The bull case is real, and I want to state it clearly. Smith is a proven YC founder -- Rainforest QA (S12) raised $41.2M, reached roughly $25.7M in revenue, and scaled to 141 employees. That's not a fluke. He knows how to build a company, hire, fundraise, and navigate YC's network. Khomenko has genuine production ML and computer vision chops -- PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX, eight years shipping AI systems -- which is precisely the technical capability EDDA's core targeting system requires. The two of them worked together for eight years, which is the kind of co-founder relationship that survives the crucible. If I were constructing the ideal software team to build the AI brain of a kinetic turret, these two would be on the shortlist. And EDDA's positioning as smaller, cheaper, and more portable than Bullfrog is a plausible wedge -- going after the individual-soldier segment that larger systems can't serve. There's a version of this story where their "small and cheap" design philosophy wins the same way that startups always beat incumbents: by being too small to notice until it's too late.

But that version requires multiple things to go right simultaneously. The founders need to master defense procurement -- a domain none of them have touched. They need to build and manufacture defense-grade hardware with a team whose background is entirely in software. They need to navigate ITAR and autonomous weapons policy. And they need to do all of this while Allen Control Systems, which is building essentially the same product with a two-year head start in government relationships, races ahead. Each of these is a separate miracle. When a startup requires more than one miracle to succeed, the probability of all of them converging gets very small very fast.

The co-founder relationship between Smith and Khomenko is genuinely strong -- eight years of working together is rare and valuable. But Pyzh's connection to the other two has no documented history, and I've seen what happens when co-founders who met recently are bolted onto a team. His cybersecurity background is relevant to hardening the system, but the team still has a glaring gap: nobody on this founding team has publicly documented experience in hardware engineering, defense contracting, or weapons systems integration. The AI targeting software is only one component of a kinetic turret. Someone has to build the physical mechanism, the precision actuators, the ammunition feed system, and make it all work reliably in battlefield conditions. That's a fundamentally different kind of engineering from what these founders have done before.

I respect the ambition. Autonomous weapons are frighteningly ambitious in exactly the way I've written about -- they scare people, and scared-away competition creates opportunity. That's the Boom Supersonic pattern. But Blake Scholl had been obsessed with supersonic flight for years before starting Boom. The organic connection between founder and problem was visible. Here, I see capable software engineers who pivoted into the hottest venture category of the moment. The technical talent is real, especially Khomenko's CV work. The execution history is real, especially Smith's track record. But when I apply my sequence of tests -- organic origin, schlep blindness, relentlessly resourceful, doing things that don't scale, growth rate -- this company struggles most on the first two, which are the ones I weight most heavily. The problem wasn't discovered; it was selected. And the schlep isn't keeping anyone away.
