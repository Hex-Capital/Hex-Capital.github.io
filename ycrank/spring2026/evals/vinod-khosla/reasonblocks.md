# ReasonBlocks -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

ReasonBlocks is the YC archetype I built my Pass Example around, dressed in agent-infra clothing instead of vertical-copilot clothing. Sajeev Magesh and Rohan Vij are pitching a runtime that catches mid-run failures and compresses context — 52% token reduction, 42% accuracy lift on SWE-bench Pro. Real numbers, real engineering. But ReasonBlocks's signature is exactly what makes me uninterested: highest dimension is a credible founding pair with applied-AI research output, lowest dimension is improbable ambition, because "infrastructure layer for smarter and cheaper AI agents" is a known problem shape with a known solution shape, and the world looks essentially the same whether ReasonBlocks wins or LangSmith ships parity.

On Improbable Ambition — my lowest rung for ReasonBlocks — the high rung is Hermeus, a Mach 5 passenger aircraft that provokes industry experts into saying "that can't be done." The mid rung is Rocket Lab, where Peter Beck attacking SpaceX with electric turbopumps was openly improbable in 2013. The low rung is the YC vertical-AI-copilot I pass on every batch: a known problem solved with a well-understood architecture. ReasonBlocks sits on the low rung, pinned there by its own positioning — the YC page calls it "infra layer for smarter and cheaper AI agents" and slots into a competitive set (LangSmith, Braintrust, Langfuse, Helicone, Latitude) where five named players already exist. No expert hears "runtime mid-run intervention plus reasoning library" and says that's impossible. They say Braintrust will ship it next quarter.

On Founder as Learning Machine — ReasonBlocks's highest rung — the high rung is the 19-year-old MIT dropout at Mach Industries building weapons systems with an irreverent outsider's contempt for Lockheed. The mid rung is Peter Beck, self-taught New Zealander welding rocket engines in his garage. The low rung is the founder with a scripted resume answer who defers to expert consensus. Sajeev — Stanford CS, USAMO qualifier, *Nature Sustainable Agriculture* author — and Rohan — CMU AI, ENGIE, UC Davis molecular dynamics — sit above the low rung but well below Peter Beck. They are credentialed insiders to AI, not naive outsiders. The 11-years-since-2nd-grade co-founder relationship is genuine and rare; the absence of a prior exit, a public pivot story, or any visible "what surprised us in three months at YC" puts ReasonBlocks in the credentialed-young-team default, not the learning-machine bucket.

On Consequence Magnitude, the high rung is OpenAI — civilization-scale, worth an apology letter to LPs. The low rung is the vertical AI copilot that makes accountants 40% faster. ReasonBlocks lands closer to the low rung: if it fully works, AI agents in production cost half as much and fail 70% less often on coding tasks. That is useful infrastructure, not a consequence explosion. The dossier offers no path from token compression to a basic-need cost collapse. On Non-Linear Technical Approach, the high rung is Commonwealth Fusion's HTS magnets and the mid rung is Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine; ReasonBlocks's "private reasoning library that grows with every run" is plausible engineering but the dossier itself flags that "two-engineer teams at AI-observability incumbents or model labs can plausibly build runtime-intervention layers." That is a 2x story, not a 10x story.

On Path to Radical Affordability, the high rung is Impossible Foods collapsing the cost of protein and Curai Health collapsing primary care to marginal cost. The low rung is premium-positioned SaaS. ReasonBlocks sits in the middle — a 52% token reduction is real cost compression, but it compresses the cost of running AI agents, not the cost of healthcare, education, energy, or legal access. It is one layer removed from the affordability endgame I actually care about.

ReasonBlocks in 36 months is acquired by Braintrust, LangChain, or Clickhouse-Langfuse for $40–90M as a runtime-intervention SKU, or shuts down quietly when an incumbent ships parity using the trace data they already own. The mechanism is simple: ReasonBlocks's defensibility depends on compounding a per-customer reasoning library faster than five well-funded observability incumbents can ship the same feature on top of distribution they already have, and a two-person team with no SRE coverage, no GitHub presence, no Product Hunt launch, no public Twitter, and an unverifiable "90% repeat usage" claim does not win that race.

The strongest opposing case: the "private reasoning library" is a genuine compounding data asset, and if Sajeev and Rohan are actually live with top Series B companies at 90% repeat usage, ReasonBlocks could become the default runtime layer before incumbents react — DoorDash is my known exception for execution-led wins where consequence magnitude was planetary. That case stings until you check the dossier: DoorDash's consequence was food access for hundreds of millions of people, not 52% cheaper coding-agent runs. The Series-B traction is a search snippet from an unnamed source, the benchmark generalization beyond SWE-bench Pro is unproven, and the reasoning library is per-customer — it does not aggregate across customers into a network effect. The condition that flips my verdict: signed, named contracts with three frontier labs or top-five AI agent companies showing the reasoning library demonstrably compounds *across* customers and produces a cost or capability gap incumbents cannot close in twelve months.
