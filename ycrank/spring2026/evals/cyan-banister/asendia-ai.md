# Asendia AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Asendia AI: this is a company building tools to make staffing agencies faster. Staffing agencies. The entities that sit between workers and employers, taking a 20-50% markup that most candidates never fully understand, operating in a world of opaque fee structures and information asymmetry. When I look at a company, I'm always searching for the hidden system underneath — who's being extracted from, and is this company exposing that extraction or reinforcing it? Asendia is selling weapons to the gatekeepers. It's making the intermediary more efficient, not making the system more transparent. Affirm attacked credit card opacity. Ownwell attacks property tax opacity. This company helps the people who profit from opacity move faster. That's a fundamental misalignment with how I think about where durable, world-changing value gets created.

When I ask "tell me their story," I get Rihab — a year at Google Cloud, stints at Microsoft doing cloud architecture and Xbox marketing in the DACH region, some time at Siemens. Badis — six years as a product application engineer at Infineon, electrical engineering at TU Munich, built an AI interview prep tool called FAANG AI. These are capable, technically credentialed people. But when I look for the biographical inevitability — the thing that happened in their lives that made them incapable of working on anything else — I can't find it. Neither founder has worked inside a staffing agency. Neither appears to have been on the wrong end of a broken recruiting process in a way that left a mark. The CTO built an AI interview coach, which shows interest in the space, but "I built a tool and saw an adjacent market" is how you find a startup opportunity, not how a problem finds you. The pivot tells the story honestly: they started with a B2C job seeker product, launched it twice on Product Hunt, and then shifted to B2B staffing agencies. That's iteration, which I respect, but it's market-seeking behavior, not mission-driven inevitability.

The competitive landscape here is the opposite of what gets me excited. AI recruiting has absorbed hundreds of millions in venture capital — Paradox raised over $250M before Workday acquired them, hireEZ has $76M, Humanly $24M, Fetcher $40M. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic and VCs wouldn't touch it. When I wrote my check into Contraline, male birth control made investors physically uncomfortable. AI for recruiting makes absolutely nobody uncomfortable — it's one of the most consensus categories in enterprise software right now. Every accelerator demo day has three of these. There's no dinner party where mentioning this idea provokes a reaction. The Paradox acquisition creates a temporary gap, sure, but it also signals that the major HCM platforms are actively consolidating this space. Bullhorn, which dominates staffing agency ATS, could build or acquire these capabilities tomorrow. A three-person team competing against that gravitational pull needs something structurally unusual, and I don't see it.

The strongest case for Asendia is this: the staffing agency vertical is genuinely underserved by current AI tools, most of which target in-house recruiting teams. If Asendia can own the agency-specific workflow — the multi-client, high-volume, speed-dependent world that agencies operate in — the 50+ ATS integrations create real switching costs over time. The Paradox acquisition left a vacuum. The full-stack approach (sourcing, outreach, video screening, phone screening in one platform) could be genuinely differentiated if executed well. And staffing is a $169B market that's technologically unsophisticated relative to its size. If the founders can build deep agency-specific workflow intelligence and the data flywheel kicks in, there's a version of this that becomes the operating system for mid-market staffing agencies. That's a real business. But "real business" and "Long Journey bet" are different things. I need to see a founder who would be doing this without funding, a system being cracked open, and an idea that makes smart people nervous. I see none of those here.

The compliance claims also give me pause — SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA for a three-person pre-seed team are extraordinary claims that warrant verification. And the brand confusion with Asendia, the logistics company with 25K LinkedIn followers, is the kind of practical obstacle that signals the founders may not have done the "living in the world" research that precedes building. These aren't fatal, but they compound the overall picture of a technically competent team chasing a consensus opportunity without the biographical depth or structural insight that would make me reach for my checkbook.
