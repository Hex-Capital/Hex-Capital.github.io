# ProjectX -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that three of four original co-founders are gone. The July 2024 press lists Rounak Adhikari, Subhrakamal Das, Ronit Banerjee, and Suraj Kuncham. The YC Spring 2026 page lists Rounak Adhikary, Bishal Karmakar, and Sourya Majumder. That's a complete team replacement except for the CEO. I wrote years ago that cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate, and I once reported that nine companies in a single batch added unknown co-founders and all nine fell apart within a year. Now, this isn't quite the same -- Rounak didn't add strangers right before Demo Day; he apparently rebuilt the team over eighteen months. That could be a sign of dysfunction, or it could be a sign that he had the determination to keep going when his original team walked away. The distinction matters enormously, and I don't have enough information to know which it is. But the pattern is a yellow flag I can't ignore.

The schlep here is real, and it's the kind that makes most technical people unconsciously look away. Streaming GPU-accelerated applications -- Blender, Unreal Engine, DaVinci Resolve -- through a browser tab with sub-3-second cold starts requires solving problems at every layer simultaneously: GPU virtualization, video encoding pipelines, container orchestration, network latency optimization, and billing granularity down to per-app compute. This is the unsexy, painful infrastructure work that thousands of CS graduates could theoretically attempt but almost nobody does, because each layer is its own multi-year problem. The closest analogy in my experience isn't Stripe exactly -- it's more like what the early cloud providers did, except compressed into a consumer-accessible interface. The fact that they've built a working beta that apparently does this is itself evidence of schlep willingness.

The origin story is plausible but not fully confirmed. Rounak is from Kalyani, West Bengal -- not Bangalore, not Mumbai -- and the dossier cites that over 75% of Indian students lack access to high-quality computing devices. He studied Interaction Design, which means he likely needed GPU-intensive tools like Blender or Adobe Creative Suite and couldn't easily access them. That's the shape of an organic discovery: a young person in a tier-2 Indian city who personally experienced the frustration of not having the hardware to run the software his coursework required. But the dossier doesn't give me the moment -- the specific story of when this went from personal annoyance to startup idea. Without that, I'm inferring organicity from circumstantial evidence rather than observing it directly.

The bull case is compelling enough that I want to lay it out honestly. If the sub-3-second cold start claim is real -- and having a CTO who works in Golang, Rust, and C++ suggests they're building at the right level of the stack -- then ProjectX has solved a genuinely hard systems problem that hyperscalers haven't prioritized because it would cannibalize their VM revenue. The $2.4M in signed LOIs and the IIT Bombay pilot suggest institutional demand exists. And the "why now" is legitimate: WebGPU is maturing, GPU cloud costs are falling, and the gap between what a $300 Chromebook can do natively versus what it can access through streaming has never been wider. If Rounak can convert those LOIs to revenue and demonstrate that Indian educational institutions will actually pay for this, the company is attacking a market where the need is obvious and the competition is structurally deterred by schlep.

But I keep returning to the numbers that don't add up. The LOIs are from July 2024 -- almost two years ago -- and there's no public evidence they've converted to revenue. Thirty thousand community members is claimed, but the Discord has 116. The total funding is $204K, which is remarkably thin for a company that needs GPU infrastructure to function. Shadow PC raised over $100M. CoreWeave has billions. ProjectX is trying to compete in GPU streaming with essentially no capital. The grants from Microsoft and AWS help with infrastructure costs, and YC's funding will help, but the unit economics of streaming GPU compute to users who can't afford local hardware -- meaning they're likely price-sensitive -- is a fundamental tension I don't see addressed. You can't serve the students who need this most if they can't afford to pay enough to cover GPU costs.

Rounak shows the hustle I associate with founders who might be relentlessly resourceful -- founding a consulting company at 19 to fund this, winning awards at Princeton and IIT Bombay, getting into Draper University and Stanford ASES and now YC. That's an impressive string of door-opening for someone born in 2002 in a small West Bengali city. And Bishal Karmakar looks like a genuine systems hacker with exactly the right technical stack for this problem. But I want to see the conversion from LOIs to revenue, I want to understand why three co-founders left, and I want evidence that the product works at the level claimed rather than just at demo quality. This is a company with a real schlep, a plausibly organic problem, and a technical team that appears capable -- but with enough unanswered questions about execution and team stability that I'm not writing a check today.
