# PartyHat -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Jarret Cuisinier spent years as VP of Marketing & Operations at Avast — 400 million consumer users, ultimately acquired by Norton/Gen Digital for $8.1 billion. He saw the inside of the incumbent machine at massive scale. And what he came away with is: "I can do this with better vibes for young people at $2.99 a month." I want to respect the domain knowledge — he genuinely knows how consumer security distribution works at a level almost nobody in YC does. But knowing the machine and having a thesis that beats the machine are two different things. The thesis here is a branding play against a $4.73 billion revenue company. That's not insane. That's just hard.

Let me run the mispricing test first, because it kills this fast. Consumer cybersecurity is one of the most consensus-validated markets in tech. Aura has raised $898.8 million at a $1.6 billion valuation. NordVPN is a unicorn. Gen Digital prints $4.73 billion a year. Every sophisticated investor on Earth knows consumer security is a real, profitable market. PartyHat isn't in a category the market has dismissed — it's in a category the market has correctly priced as winner-take-most. When I backed Eaze, every top-tier VC thought cannabis delivery was legally radioactive. When I seeded Solana, the consensus was Ethereum had already won. Where's the mispricing with PartyHat? There isn't one. "Norton but for TikTok users" is a pitch that makes every VC nod along — and that's exactly the problem. If everyone nods, the upside is already in the price.

The primitive test is where PartyHat completely stalls out. VPNs exist. Dark web monitoring exists — Have I Been Pwned has been doing it for free for years. Scam call blocking exists. Malware protection has existed since the 1990s. PartyHat bundles these commoditized components with an "AI security advisor" and playful branding. That's product marketing, not primitive creation. Nobody is going to say "PartyHat me" the way they say "Venmo me." Compare this to what made Venmo interesting at the earliest stage: peer-to-peer mobile payments literally did not exist as a consumer behavior. Kortina and Magdon-Ismail created a new verb. PartyHat is wrapping existing verbs in a party hat. The dossier's own defensibility section states it plainly: "No defensibility signals found in public sources at this stage."

The AI kill test is brutal here, and I don't say that with any pleasure. The dossier lists it explicitly: "VPN infrastructure is widely available via white-label providers. Dark web monitoring APIs are sold by multiple vendors. Scam call databases are commercially licensable." Every single feature in PartyHat's stack can be assembled by any well-funded company in weeks. Gen Digital already acquired MoneyLion for $1 billion in April 2025 to bundle financial wellness with cybersecurity — they are actively expanding their bundle. Apple is building more security features into iOS every cycle. This is the Adobe problem I keep coming back to: every time someone pitches me "the incumbent product but with AI and better UX," I think about how trivially the incumbent can ship that update. Gen Digital could launch a youth sub-brand for less than their quarterly marketing budget. PartyHat's technology stack is literally for sale off the shelf.

The bull case, and I want to be honest about it: Cuisinier knows the distribution playbook at Avast-scale and may understand exactly where incumbents are structurally unable to acquire younger users. Gen Digital's brand equity is built on trust and seriousness — Norton literally can't pivot to irreverent TikTok marketing without alienating their 75 million paying boomer customers. If PartyHat can achieve viral social distribution at a CAC that incumbents structurally cannot match, there's a generational brand transition thesis. The 4.7-star rating with 106 reviews on the App Store and 1,000+ users after three months suggests some early consumer resonance. Vijay Myneni shipped across iOS, Android, macOS, and Windows with a two-person team — that's real engineering output. And the $2.99/month price point is aggressive enough to drive trial behavior. But brand-led consumer plays in commoditized categories are the Allbirds trap. Allbirds had genuine material innovation in wool footwear, better branding than Nike for a younger audience, and it still collapsed 97% from peak because DTC scaling requires capital that eventually demands the factory-farm VC model I've watched die. PartyHat's path to meaningful revenue requires massive user acquisition spend against incumbents with 1,000x their budget.

Two scores drive this to a pass. Consensus Mispricing scores low because consumer security is a validated, well-funded, incumbent-dominated category with zero narrative mispricing — this is the opposite of the discomfort-driven bets where I find asymmetry. Structural Durability scores low because every component of PartyHat's product stack is commercially licensable commodity infrastructure with no physical-world moat, no protocol layer, and no network effect that survives a single incumbent product update. Cuisinier's Avast pedigree is real and I respect the domain expertise, but sane founders need insane ideas to activate my framework, and "better branding in consumer security" is the sanest idea in the room.
