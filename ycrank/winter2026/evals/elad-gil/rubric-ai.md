# Rubric AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing I notice, and it's the dominant signal. Rubric AI is entering a market where Scale AI ($29B, now 49% owned by Meta), Surge AI ($1.2B revenue, seeking a $25B valuation), Mercor ($10B valuation, ~$450M ARR), and Labelbox ($1B valuation with "rubric evaluation" tooling already shipped) are all aggressively competing for the same ~20 frontier lab customers. This is not a "too boring" or "too niche" market that incumbents are ignoring -- it's one of the most visibly attractive infrastructure markets in AI right now. When I backed PagerDuty, no one else wanted to fund operations alerting. When I backed Gusto, payroll seemed unsexy. The asymmetric returns in my portfolio have come from markets where the incumbents weren't looking. "Evals for frontier labs" is a market where every major data infrastructure company is already looking, already building, and already generating hundreds of millions in revenue. That's the opposite of non-obvious.

There is a real structural moment here, and I want to be fair about it: Meta's $14.3B acquisition of 49% of Scale AI in June 2025 genuinely fragmented the market. Competing frontier labs -- Google, OpenAI, xAI -- have legitimate reasons to seek alternative vendors for sensitive training data work. This is structurally similar to Google's Project Maven cancellation creating the defense AI vacuum that Anduril filled. But the analogy breaks in a critical place. When Project Maven created that vacuum, there was no credible startup competitor in defense AI -- Anduril filled a structural absence. The Scale AI disruption didn't create an absence; it reshuffled demand among four or five well-capitalized, already-positioned competitors who immediately moved to capture displaced volume. Surge AI was already serving every major frontier lab. Mercor had 30,000 contractors and $450M ARR. This is a market reshuffle, not a market opening.

The "research and product lab" positioning raises a specific concern I apply to every investment: is this company software-driven or software-aware? The dossier describes Rubric AI as building "purpose-built training environments" and combining "expert human judgment" with evaluation infrastructure. When the core value proposition is organizing expert human judgment through rubrics, I'm looking at a services business with software tooling -- not a software-driven company that compounds. Surge AI charges 10x competitors precisely because expert evaluation is fundamentally a labor-intensive, relationship-driven business. The margin structure of "experts evaluating AI outputs" looks more like consulting than infrastructure. That's the "smart toaster" pattern: technology layered onto a fundamentally non-tech business model.

The strongest bull case would require three things to be true simultaneously. First, that the unnamed second team member is a top ML researcher who brings the technical credibility that frontier lab buyers demand -- this would close the founder-domain gap that Pragya Saboo's PM background doesn't address. Second, that Rubric AI has developed genuinely proprietary evaluation methodology that produces measurably better training signals than what Scale AI or Surge AI deliver -- not better tooling, but better science. Third, that the concentrated customer base (~20 frontier labs) becomes an advantage rather than a constraint, because you only need 3-5 contracts to build a meaningful business. If all three are true, the Meta/Scale AI disruption creates a real window to land those initial contracts. But that's three miracles, not one -- and compounding low-probability events is exactly the pattern I reject.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is unclear. "Evals for frontier labs" is a specific service for a specific customer set. I don't see the path where this initial product becomes a distribution channel for additional products. Stripe started with payments and distributed Atlas, Billing, and Treasury through the same developer integration. What does Rubric AI distribute through its evaluation relationships? The customer base of fewer than 20 organizations globally caps the distribution surface area. And those customers are among the most sophisticated technology companies on earth -- they have massive internal teams, they build their own tooling, and they have enormous buyer power. The pricing power asymmetry runs entirely in the wrong direction for a two-person startup.

Pragya Saboo's product management experience at Asana -- particularly leading Time Tracking from inception to launch -- demonstrates she can scope and ship. Her Climate Capital investing background shows market analysis capability. But the publicly documented profile doesn't include ML research, RLHF, model training, or AI infrastructure experience. When you're positioning as a "post-training research lab" selling to the most technically demanding buyers in the industry, the founder credibility bar is exceptionally high. The unidentified second team member may entirely fill this gap, but I can't evaluate what I can't see, and the absence of any public identification is itself an unusual signal at the YC stage.
