# Belong -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Holmstén built Tunigo, sold it to Spotify, and spent six years as Spotify's Global Head of Music shaping how hundreds of millions of people discover artists. Pournouri managed Avicii through a career that generated hundreds of millions in revenue and co-created the Brilliant Minds conference with Daniel Ek himself. These are not generic music industry credentials — these are two people who operated at the absolute apex of the intersection between technology platforms and artist monetization. Paul Graham would look at this founder pairing and see an invest signal. I look at it and ask: what is the secret? Because my framework demands that the idea's monopoly structure come before the team's biography, and brilliant founders pursuing a competitive market are still pursuing a competitive market.

Belong's claimed insight — that fans generate billions in economic activity around artists but receive no recognition or value back through financial products — fails the secret test decisively. Goldman Sachs published a research note estimating the "superfan" segment would drive $4.2B in music industry revenue by 2030. That estimate was, per the dossier's own language, "widely cited in music industry press." When Goldman Sachs publishes your thesis and it becomes a talking point at every music industry conference, there is no secret — there is consensus wearing a new brand name. The structural gap Belong targets — between streaming platforms that own content relationships and banks that own financial rails — is real, but identifying a real gap is not the same as holding a contrarian truth. Every music executive, every fintech operator scanning for verticals, and every VC reading the Goldman report sees the same gap. The existence of Fanprime ($2.47M raised), FanMaker (operating since 2007), FanHub (70K users), and most damningly Fanatics ONE (10M existing FanCash members backed by $4.85B in total Fanatics capital) confirms that multiple funded players are already converging on fan monetization from different angles. When I can list four competitors and a Goldman Sachs report, the canonical red flag is triggered: there is no secret, and therefore no monopoly.

The product architecture reinforces this diagnosis. Belong is a branded debit card issued through Middlesex Federal Savings on Mastercard rails, wrapped in a fan identity layer — FanScore, KYF, Dropblock, Belong Passport. These are trademarked feature names, not proprietary technology. The "compliance barrier" of SOC 2 certification and a Mastercard BIN represents months of lead time, not years. The entire Banking-as-a-Service infrastructure that makes Belong viable — sponsor banks, BIN programs, card issuance platforms — is the same infrastructure that makes Belong replicable. The dossier's own "why now" argument undercuts the moat: "BaaS infrastructure has lowered the cost and time required to launch a branded card product." What lowers barriers for you lowers them for everyone. Compare this to Palantir, where I co-founded a company that embedded proprietary intelligence software so deeply into CIA and military operations that switching costs became structurally infinite over seventeen years. Belong's switching cost is a FanScore that a fan accumulated over months — a loyalty point balance, not an operational dependency. If Spotify decided tomorrow to launch a fan rewards card, they would bring 600 million users and every artist relationship Belong is trying to build from scratch.

The strongest bull case requires believing that Holmstén and Pournouri's personal networks can lock up exclusive artist partnerships so rapidly that Belong becomes the de facto fan identity standard before incumbents respond — the Facebook-at-Harvard density play applied to individual artist fanbases. Troy Carter's involvement as an angel (former Lady Gaga manager, Venice Music CEO) adds another distribution node into the artist management ecosystem. If Belong achieved 100% penetration of, say, three global artists' superfan bases, the network effects and data moat would begin compounding. The fan identity graph — financial transaction data crossed with engagement data — would become a dataset no streaming platform or bank independently possesses. For this to work, Belong needs artist exclusivity commitments creating content and experiences unavailable anywhere else. No such partnerships have been announced. And the friction of adopting a new debit card — a banking relationship, not an app download — makes the density play structurally harder than Facebook's frictionless campus expansion. The cold-start problem on a two-sided marketplace where one side requires opening a bank account is severe.

The power-law arithmetic seals the pass. The fan engagement platform market is $6.5-8B growing at 16-21% CAGR — a market that could support a successful company but not a fund-returning monopoly. Interchange revenue on a debit card is a low-margin business. Even in the optimistic scenario where Belong captures significant share, the realistic ceiling is a meaningful acquisition by Spotify, Apple, or a major fintech — not independent market dominance producing 100x returns over decades. Companies built for acquisition rather than monopoly are companies that never achieved the structural position my framework requires. Belong's best outcome — a $500M-$1B acquisition by a platform that wants its fan identity data — is a fine outcome for the founders and a structurally irrelevant outcome for a power-law portfolio.

Belong has no atoms leverage whatsoever — pure software abstraction on existing financial rails, no hardware, no physical-world manipulation, no multi-year replication barrier that capital cannot shortcut. This places it firmly in the category of companies I have been systematically moving away from since 2011, when I wrote that "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters." A fan rewards debit card is closer to the 140 characters end of that spectrum than the flying cars end. The founders are exceptional operators working in a domain they understand deeply — but exceptional operators in a market where Goldman Sachs has already published the thesis, four competitors are already funded, and Fanatics has 10M existing loyalty members are exceptional operators headed into competition, and competition is the structural condition under which all companies fail the same way.
