# ZeroSettle -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me about ZeroSettle: the two biggest players who should be solving this problem structurally cannot. Apple won't build a tool that cannibalizes $27 billion in annual commission revenue, and RevenueCat — which powers 70,000+ apps — can't offer an App Store billing bypass without torching its relationship with Apple and potentially losing StoreKit API access. That's a genuine structural gap, and it's the kind of thing I learned to recognize at IronPort — when the incumbents are structurally conflicted, someone outside the castle walls has a window. Two former Apple systems engineers who understand the billing infrastructure from the inside are building in that window. On paper, the positioning is sharp.

But when I try to get underneath the positioning to the founders themselves, I hit a wall. My first question is always "tell me your story — not the business." And what I see here are two Cal Poly graduates who worked together at Apple on operating systems, spotted a regulatory opening created by the Epic v. Apple ruling, and built a technically competent SDK to exploit it. Ryan has a patent from Apple. Gabe previously started a smart appliance company. These are capable engineers making a rational bet. But I don't feel the biographical thread — the hardship, the obsession, the reason THIS problem won't let them sleep. Working at Apple and then building a tool to route around Apple's commission is clever, but it's not the same as someone who lost their company to the 30% tax and vowed to fix it. I need to feel that the founder and the problem are inseparable, and I don't feel it here.

The deeper issue for me is that avoiding the Apple tax is not a weird or unpopular idea — it's one of the most consensus grievances in the entire developer ecosystem. Epic Games spent hundreds of millions litigating this exact point. Stripe published its own guide for iOS developers to accept payments outside the App Store in May 2025. Paddle explicitly cited Apple's ecosystem opening as a growth driver. Neon Pay raised $14 million from Thrive Capital and a16z to do something similar in gaming. This isn't a "magically weird" bet where I'm backing a pocket of dreamers before consensus forms. This is a well-understood market opportunity that multiple well-funded players are already circling. The most promising companies I've invested in were ones nobody else wanted to touch — SpaceX when private space sounded delusional, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded like a joke. Helping developers keep more of their App Store revenue sounds like common sense to everyone.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because it's real. If Apple is permanently barred from charging any commission on linked-out purchases (which is still TBD after the Ninth Circuit remand), and if ZeroSettle locks in developers as Merchant of Record during this regulatory transition window, the switching costs compound meaningfully. Once subscribers are migrated to ZeroSettle-managed billing, reversing that migration is painful. The Founding Partner Program — free for two years — is a smart way to build a base before competitors react. And there's a version of this that rhymes with what Carta did for cap tables: taking something opaque and making it transparent, giving developers real visibility into what they're losing and the power to change it. That "making invisible systems visible" pattern has been one of my most consistent investment themes. If these founders can move fast enough to become the default infrastructure before Stripe builds a turnkey competitor, there's a real business here. But "move fast before Stripe catches up" is an execution race, not a moat — and that's a very different kind of bet than the ones I like to make.

The regulatory complexity dimension is genuinely strong. U.S. law, EU Digital Markets Act with a 5% Core Technology Commission, Japan's new Mobile Software Competition Act — each jurisdiction has different rules, different fee structures, and different compliance requirements. Navigating that as a Merchant of Record handling tax, fraud, and entitlements across all of them is hard, and it's exactly the kind of regulatory complexity that deters lighter-weight competitors. The founders' Apple insider knowledge gives them a real advantage in understanding how App Review works, where the compliance landmines are, and how to build SDK integrations that don't get rejected. This is the dimension where ZeroSettle most fits my pattern of investing in regulated spaces others avoid.

But I keep coming back to the question I ask myself with every company: would I drop everything to work for these founders? And without a personal story that explains why this problem owns them — without the grit narrative, without the years of rejection, without the felt sense that this is their life's work — I can't get to yes. This is a well-constructed arbitrage play by competent engineers, and I wish them well. It's just not the kind of bet where I feel the jolt.
