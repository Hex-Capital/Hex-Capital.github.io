# Alike -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Alike is what's missing: a builder. I look at three founders and I see two consecutive Oxford Students' Union Presidents and a tenured Associate Professor who maintains his full-time faculty position while serving as CTO. No public GitHub repos for any of them. Addi Haran Diman started university at 13 and finished a PhD at 21, which is genuinely impressive — but it's impressive in the way that winning a math olympiad is impressive. It tells me about raw processing power, not about the specific obsessive need to make things that characterizes the founders I back. When I funded Patrick Collison, he was a teenager, yes, but he was a teenager who had already shipped Auctomatic and sold it. When I found Replit on Hacker News, Amjad Masad had built a working product that programmers were already using. Alike's founders have a demo day prize from a university incubator and a website published eight days ago. The question I keep asking is: who is writing the code?

Max Van Kleek's 9,012 Google Scholar citations in human-AI interaction and privacy-preserving systems represent real intellectual capital — I don't dismiss that. Twenty-five years thinking about how humans and AI systems interact is exactly the kind of deep domain knowledge that could matter for building a coordination layer between agents and people. But he's simultaneously an Associate Professor at Oxford's CS department. I've seen this pattern before: the academic who wants to keep one foot in the university and one foot in a startup. It almost never works at the pace a startup demands. Dust has $21.5 million from Sequoia and is already at $7.3 million ARR. CrewAI has $18 million from Insight Partners. Slack is adding 30 native AI agent features. You cannot compete with that velocity while grading papers on Tuesdays and Thursdays.

The deeper problem is that Alike's idea is consensus. "AI agent coordination for enterprises" is not something that repels before it attracts — it's the sentence that appears on every pitch deck at every demo day this quarter. Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries. Google launched A2A with 50-plus partners including Salesforce, SAP, and Workday. When I talk about the sweet spot being the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea," Alike is firmly in the other circle — the intersection of "seems like a good idea" and "is a good idea," which is where the returns have already been competed away. The Collison brothers succeeded with Stripe because nobody wanted to deal with PCI compliance and bank integrations. Everyone wants to deal with AI agent coordination right now.

I also don't see organic problem discovery. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas — it's to look for problems you have yourself. Alike's problem framing is "coordination fatigue" — a coined marketing term, not the language of someone who was personally drowning in miscoordinated AI agents and built something to save themselves. Addi's PhD studied social networks analytically; Max researched human-AI interaction from a lab. These are adjacent academic interests, not the lived frustration that Drew Houston had when he forgot his USB drive. The origin story reads as: researchers at Oxford noticed the AI agent trend, had relevant academic backgrounds, and assembled a team through their university network. That's identifying an opportunity, not discovering a problem.

The bull case for Alike would require three things to be true simultaneously: that the AI agent coordination market fragments rather than consolidates around existing platforms, that Max Van Kleek's privacy-preserving architecture becomes a genuine differentiator in regulated industries, and that the team's academic depth in HCI translates into a product so meaningfully better at understanding coordination patterns that Dust's $21.5 million head start and Slack's installed base don't matter. The 10 live team deployments and named design partners — Exus Renewables with its $1 billion backing from Partners Group, K-ID with $51 million from Lightspeed and a16z — suggest the product exists and someone is using it. That's real. But design partners using a free coordination tool is a long distance from enterprises paying for it, especially when Google is giving away the interoperability layer for free through A2A.

I'm passing on Alike. The two dimensions that lock this are Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — this is a consensus play in the hottest category in enterprise software, which is almost exactly the pattern I warn against — and Organic Problem Discovery, where the absence of any personal frustration narrative, combined with the academic-research-to-startup pipeline, signals a team that chose this problem from a menu rather than discovering it in the wild. Addi's intellectual precocity is real, and Max's research depth is rare, but these are the ingredients for a successful research lab, not necessarily for the kind of relentlessly resourceful startup that can outrun Dust, CrewAI, Slack, and Google's A2A protocol simultaneously.
