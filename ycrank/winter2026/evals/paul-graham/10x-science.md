# 10x Science -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is about as clean as I see in deep tech. David Roberts has spent seven-plus years doing top-down mass spectrometry -- first at UW-Madison, then as a postdoc in Carolyn Bertozzi's lab at Stanford. Andrew Reiter built mass spectrometry methods at the Broad Institute's proteomics platform. These are people who have personally sat through weeks of manual data curation using the exact legacy tools they're now replacing. They didn't read a market report about protein characterization bottlenecks. They lived inside the bottleneck. When a founder has 37 publications in the precise analytical technique their software automates, the idea didn't come from a brainstorming session. It grew from years of accumulated frustration with tools that should have been automated a decade ago.

The schlep here is real and layered in a way that should scare off most founders. You need FDA 21 CFR Part 11 compliance. You need GxP validation. You need to understand the edge cases of protein chemistry well enough that your ML models don't produce results that get a drug rejected by the FDA. And you need to sell into biopharma teams that have already validated their workflows around Thermo Fisher or SCIEX instruments with bundled software -- meaning every sale requires the customer to go through a revalidation process. This is the kind of regulatory-technical-commercial schlep stack that makes smart engineers unconsciously filter the idea out before it even registers. Meanwhile the incumbents are getting acquired into large instrument conglomerates -- Genedata into Danaher, Protein Metrics into Dotmatics -- which is exactly the consolidation pattern that slows innovation and creates openings for focused startups.

The team composition is the most interesting structural element. Tejus is the piece that makes this work mechanically -- he was the second employee at Nooks, a YC-backed company that raised a $43M Series B, so he's built production software at startup speed before. He was CTO of CMU's RoboTutor XPRIZE finalist. He brings ML depth and shipping experience. Roberts and Reiter bring domain knowledge that would take an outside team years to acquire. The three connected through Stanford's ChEM-H ecosystem, which is a natural meeting point rather than a co-founder-matching exercise. That matters -- organic co-founder relationships outperform arranged ones consistently.

What concerns me is the commitment question. Two of three founders appear to still hold active academic positions at Stanford. Roberts is a postdoc. Reiter is a PhD student. The company isn't hiring. At some point you have to choose: are you building a company or finishing your degree? I've seen this pattern before where academic founders try to hedge, keeping one foot in the lab, and the startup suffers because it doesn't get the intensity it needs. The best signal would be Roberts and Reiter having already left their positions. The absence of that signal is not disqualifying -- plenty of academic founders transition successfully -- but it introduces uncertainty about whether these founders will be relentlessly resourceful when the company needs fourteen-hour days for months straight, or whether they'll be pulled back into lab obligations.

The traction evidence is thin even by pre-seed standards. "Currently onboarding customers" tells me almost nothing -- I want to know if any biopharma team has actually run their real characterization data through the platform and gotten results they trusted enough to act on. The $150,000 time savings claim is marketing, not evidence of pull. The US HUPO conference sponsorship shows initiative but conferences are outputs, not inputs. At this stage I'm looking for even one story about a proteomics team that tried the tool and refused to go back to their old workflow. That story may exist but it's not in the evidence.

The bull case is compelling if you zoom out. AI-powered drug discovery is genuinely flooding the pipeline with candidates. If Recursion, Absci, and a dozen other platforms are each generating ten times more candidates than traditional discovery, the characterization bottleneck becomes physically impossible to clear with manual tools. That's a forcing function that could override switching costs -- when you literally can't process your pipeline fast enough, you'll adopt new software regardless of revalidation pain. If 10x Science becomes the vendor-neutral analytical software layer for AI-era biotherapeutics, disconnected from any single instrument company, the value creation could be very large. The Veeva analogy -- narrow vertical SaaS in regulated pharma that expanded into a dominant platform -- is the right frame for the upside.

This is a company where the idea origin and schlep profile are genuinely strong, but I need more evidence of full commitment and real user dependency before writing a personal check. The organic discovery is excellent. The team composition is rare and well-fitted to the problem. But I'm scoring this as a company I'd watch closely through the batch rather than invest in today.
