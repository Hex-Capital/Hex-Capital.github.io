# Mantis -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out is that Georgia Witchel was CTO and co-founder of Louiza Labs -- a YC S25 company that simulates human biology for medical device testing -- and within roughly six months pivoted to founding Mantis as CEO, a separate YC W26 company with deeply overlapping technology themes. That's not automatically disqualifying, but it demands explanation. Was this an amicable spin-out? A founder disagreement? An IP fork? The dossier can't answer this, and I won't pretend it doesn't matter. When I see a founder leave one YC company to start another in an adjacent space that quickly, I want to understand the relational dynamics before I write a check. The best founding stories have deep pre-existing trust at their core. This one has an unexplained rupture at its core.

Setting that aside and running my primary filter: is this a sane person with an insane idea? Witchel is credibly sane -- Harvey Mudd CS, computational math at Johns Hopkins, bioengineering MS from UW, hands-on engineering roles, and a genuine prior company in the exact domain. The technical credibility is real. But here's the problem: "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data" is not an insane idea. It's a perfectly rational idea that dozens of smart people are already pursuing with significantly more capital. Unlearn.AI has $130 million and EMA-qualified methods. Medidata sits on 30,000+ clinical trials and 9 million patient records. When I described Brian Armstrong as the archetype of this framework, the key was that virtually nobody in Silicon Valley believed in regulated cryptocurrency infrastructure in 2012. Clinical data infrastructure in 2026? Every healthcare VC on Sand Hill Road has this thesis on a slide somewhere. This is a sane person with a sane idea, and sane-sane combinations don't produce venture-scale returns because the opportunity has already been priced.

Now let me apply the test that dominates my analysis post-Birchbox: does this business get stronger or weaker as AI commoditizes software? The answer is uncomfortable. AI doesn't just threaten this company's moat -- AI literally IS this company. The product combines LLMs with physics simulations. Strip out the AI and you have a clinical data integration tool, which is what Medidata, Veeva, and Oracle Health Sciences already sell to every major pharma company on earth. The "cherry on top" test asks whether the business would be good if AI didn't exist. The answer here is no -- without AI, there is no differentiated product. That's the exact category I've been warning about since my "SaaS era is over" essay. Every incumbent with distribution and proprietary clinical data will build equivalent AI capabilities. Medidata, backed by Dassault Systèmes, has the data and the R&D budget to embed physics-enhanced AI into their existing platform as a feature update, not a new company.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to be honest about it -- is that the intersection of physics simulation, LLMs, and clinical domain knowledge is genuinely narrow and hard to replicate. If Mantis can encode biological semantics into a data layer that becomes the canonical standard for how clinical trial data is structured, validated, and queried, it could create real switching costs and potentially function as infrastructure others build on. The FDA and EMA are moving toward accepting digital twins and simulation-based evidence, which creates a regulatory tailwind. And Witchel's specific technical stack -- computational math plus bioengineering plus CS -- is authentically rare. If this were positioned as a new data primitive for clinical evidence generation rather than "Databricks for X," the protocol-layer argument would be stronger. But that's not how it's positioned, and the product description spanning "digital twin company," "Databricks for Biomedical Data," "domain-aware data platform," and "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform" suggests the founders haven't yet found the primitive they're building. When I backed Aleo, the team knew they were building a zero-knowledge privacy protocol -- the primitive was crystallized from day one. Here, the primitive is still searching for itself.

The narrative optionality isn't zero -- clinical data infrastructure could theoretically expand into all biomedical data, all regulatory submissions, all drug development workflows. But "Databricks for X" narratives are structurally capped in a way that protocol narratives aren't. Databricks itself already exists and could extend vertically. The ceiling is visible, which means the infinity story isn't there. Combine that with no public revenue signal, no confirmed customers, a team of three facing enterprise pharma sales cycles measured in quarters if not years, and well-funded competitors with massive data advantages -- this is a structural mismatch with how I deploy capital.
