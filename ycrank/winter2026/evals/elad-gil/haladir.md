# Haladir -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Haladir is the positioning trail: "AI-powered mainframe modernization suite" for COBOL migration, then "AI-led software development" correctness, now "operational superintelligence" with RL for operations research. Three framings in what appears to be a matter of months. At pre-seed, pivoting is normal -- I've backed companies that changed direction early. But the pattern here isn't a single strategic pivot based on customer signal. It's a team searching for the right way to describe what they're building, which usually means they haven't yet found the market that pulls them forward. When I backed Harvey, the team wasn't pivoting between verticals -- they were building legal AI workflow tools before the models were ready, grinding against actual law firm buyers. The clarity of their market target preceded the product. Haladir's rapid reframing suggests the opposite sequence.

The structural observation underneath is genuinely interesting: RLVR has proven itself for math and code, operations research offers natural verifiability through existing solvers, and nobody has yet formalized OR constraints as reward signals for RL training. That's a real gap. But I need to ask my standard question -- what has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this possible now? The answer is that RLVR became a proven paradigm with DeepSeek R1 and OpenAI o3. Fair. But here's what concerns me: RLVR for math and code was built by the foundation model labs themselves. When a training methodology proves valuable, labs absorb it -- that's what labs do. The argument that OpenAI or DeepSeek won't extend RLVR to operations research domains requires believing they'll permanently ignore a natural expansion of their own core methodology. That's not a bet I'd take. Harvey worked because workflow software for lawyers is a different business than building foundation models. Training infrastructure is not a different business from what labs do -- it IS what labs do.

The market archetype test exposes a tension. The end market -- logistics, supply chain, fleet optimization -- fits my "boring but critical" pattern perfectly. Every company with a supply chain needs constrained optimization. PagerDuty succeeded because operations alerting was boring but universal. But Haladir isn't building optimization tools for logistics companies. They're building RL training infrastructure -- which sits squarely in the "AI infrastructure" category, the single most consensus-hot market in venture capital right now. That's the opposite of non-obvious. And the specific segment -- "RLVR training infrastructure for operations research" -- has no clear buyer. AI labs do this in-house. Enterprises aren't training their own RL models for constrained optimization. The dossier cites a $58.78B AI infrastructure TAM, but that generic number tells me nothing about whether Haladir's specific offering has a market measured in millions or billions.

The strongest bull case would be this: RLVR extends beyond math and code into dozens of verifiable domains, each requiring domain-specific reward formalization. Operations research is just the first vertical. Haladir becomes the platform for formalizing reward signals across multiple domains -- logistics today, scheduling tomorrow, resource allocation next. The ConstraintBench benchmark becomes a standard, creating a gravitational pull similar to how MLPerf shaped hardware evaluation. The team's interdisciplinary expertise (OR + formal methods + RL) is genuinely rare, and the paper demonstrates they can execute at the research frontier. For this bull case to work, two things must be true: (1) reward formalization needs to be a standalone business, not a feature that labs build internally, and (2) the team needs to translate research artifacts into a product with clear buyers and pricing. I don't see evidence for either. The ConstraintBench paper has 2 GitHub stars. The Twitter account has 8 followers and no posts. There's no product demo, no pricing, no customer names, no LOIs. The research is intellectually credible but commercially unvalidated.

My single-miracle framework reveals the core problem. I count at least three sequential breakthroughs required: (1) formalized OR rewards must meaningfully improve model performance beyond what traditional solvers already deliver, (2) this must translate to commercial demand from identifiable buyers, and (3) the capability must resist absorption by the labs who already own the RLVR training stack. When I have more than one miracle, I have compounding small odds. The team -- all current or recent university students with no prior exits, no industry operating experience, and credibility resting on a single paper -- would need to navigate all three simultaneously. Young founders can be extraordinary, but the operational complexity of building a commercial product in enterprise AI infrastructure, while defending against lab absorption, while the positioning keeps shifting, is a steep climb.
