# ProjectX -- Elad Gil Evaluation

ProjectX's website returned a minimal loading page at the time of research -- nearly two years after announcing a public beta in June 2024. That single fact tells me more than anything else in this dossier. When I evaluate companies, I start with market structure timing, but I need to flag the execution evidence gap first: ProjectX has been building since 2022, raised $204K from Google Cloud, won competitions at Princeton and IIT Bombay, and yet has zero public revenue signal, zero customer logos, zero user counts, and a non-functional website. Two of three original co-founders (Ronit Banerjee, Suvrakamal Das) departed before YC admission. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of metrics, but I do penalize the absence of metrics *after four years of development*. ProjectX isn't a fresh start -- it's a company with runway history and no visible traction artifact to show for it.

The market structure question is where ProjectX loses me. Desktop-as-a-Service is a USD 4.5-9.8B market in 2025 growing at double-digit CAGR -- but it's the opposite of nonobvious. Microsoft (Azure Virtual Desktop, Windows 365), Amazon (WorkSpaces), and Citrix have been building in this space for over a decade. ProjectX's differentiation -- per-app isolated compute with dedicated GPU and cross-OS support in a single browser session -- is a technical architecture choice, not a response to a structural market shift. The dossier's "why now" case rests on three inferred catalysts: GPU cost drops, AI agent demand, and container orchestration maturity. All three benefit incumbents at least as much as a pre-seed startup. When I backed Coinbase, the structural shift was the emergence of a fundamentally new asset class with no incumbent infrastructure -- crypto had no Citrix equivalent. ProjectX is entering a market where Microsoft, Amazon, and Google already have massive distribution and can replicate a per-app isolation model with a product team smaller than ProjectX's entire company.

ProjectX's infrastructure-layer positioning is ambiguous in a way that concerns me. The company is described as a "cloud-based operating system," which sounds like infrastructure, but functionally it's an end-user application that sits on top of AWS, GCP, and Azure GPU infrastructure. There's no API, no developer platform, no evidence of other businesses building on ProjectX. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments API that thousands of companies integrated into their core stack -- the switching costs compounded because Stripe was embedded in codebases. ProjectX's switching cost is browser tab closure. Worse, ProjectX is building on a contested infrastructure layer: Google Cloud simultaneously gave them a $204K grant and competes with them via Google Cloud's own DaaS offerings. This is one of my explicit filters -- when the infrastructure layer beneath you hasn't been won, and your supplier is also your competitor, you're playing a game with structurally unfavorable odds.

ProjectX fails my single-miracle test cleanly. The company needs at least three independent breakthroughs: (1) the technology miracle of building production-quality per-app GPU isolation with sub-second cold starts across Windows and Linux simultaneously, (2) the unit economics miracle of making dedicated per-app GPU allocation profitable when hyperscaler compute is the dominant cost input, and (3) the distribution miracle of acquiring enterprise customers against Microsoft, Amazon, and Citrix, all of whom have existing IT procurement relationships. Compounding those odds is exactly the failure mode I warn about. The Anduril exception -- where I accepted multi-miracle risk because the market timing signal was overwhelming -- doesn't apply here because ProjectX's market timing is not overwhelming; it's a well-served market with incremental architectural innovation.

Rounak Adhikary is 23 with a BSc from Lovely Professional University, a web development agency (Woldix) founded at 19, and accelerator stints at Stanford ASES and Draper University. I've seen young founders build extraordinary things -- age alone isn't disqualifying. But ProjectX's ambition requires the kind of systems engineering depth and enterprise distribution capability that typically comes from operating at scale. Rounak has no scaling experience at a hypergrowth company, no enterprise sales background, and the co-founder turnover raises questions about team stability. CTO Bishal Karmakar has relevant Rust/Go/Kubernetes skills but a thin public footprint (23 GitHub stars, 3 followers). COO Sourya Majumder's strongest signal is scaling IT consulting to ~$120K MRR -- real revenue, but a different magnitude from what ProjectX requires. The YC page mentions expertise from "CISCO, NVIDIA, Wharton, and IITs" but the dossier couldn't verify which team members this refers to. I give founders the benefit of the doubt on execution, but when the market is this competitive, the founder profile needs to compensate -- and here it doesn't.

The strongest bull case for ProjectX: AI agent workflows (2024-2026) will create demand for a fundamentally new computing paradigm where multiple agents and humans operate simultaneously in isolated environments, and ProjectX is the only company building this natively. Traditional single-cursor desktops can't serve a world where five AI agents each need independent GPU-backed compute alongside a human operator. If the agent revolution makes the single-desktop model obsolete the way mobile made desktop-only websites obsolete, ProjectX could be the infrastructure layer for agent-era computing -- genuinely nonobvious because most investors still think of DaaS as a VDI replacement category. The competitions won (Tiger Launch Global Finals at Princeton, WTC Innovation Award among 17,000 startups at IIT Bombay) suggest external validators have seen something compelling in the technical demo. The condition that would flip me: if ProjectX showed me three enterprise customers actively running AI agent workloads on Infinity with measurable productivity gains and expanding usage -- proof that the agent-native computing paradigm has real demand that traditional DaaS cannot serve -- I would revisit. That would convert a crowded market into a structural shift and reduce the miracle count from three to one (distribution, with technology proven and unit economics validated by customer willingness to pay).

ProjectX scores lowest on the two dimensions I weight most heavily: nonobvious market timing and infrastructure-layer positioning. The DaaS market is obvious, well-served by incumbents with orders-of-magnitude more resources, and ProjectX's technical differentiation -- while genuine engineering -- doesn't constitute a structural moat. The company is building an application on a contested infrastructure layer rather than being the infrastructure layer itself. Multiple simultaneous miracles are required, the founding team lacks scaling experience, and four years of development have produced no publicly visible traction. Pass.
