# Alchemy -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Alchemy's core capability — LLM-driven orchestration of open-source computer vision models for microscopy — relies on "prompt engineering and model orchestration rather than proprietary model architectures," per the dossier's own assessment. Every component in Alchemy's stack is available to anyone: foundation models from OpenAI or Google, open-source segmentation tools like Cellpose and CellProfiler maintained by the Broad Institute and Chan Zuckerberg Initiative, and natural-language interfaces that any competent AI engineer can build. Alchemy is assembling rented leverage into a workflow product. The question is whether the assembly itself becomes proprietary before someone else assembles the same pieces. Leica could integrate an LLM front-end into Aivia's 22 built-in applications and 20 pre-trained deep learning models tomorrow. The dossier's hypothetical data flywheel — researchers contributing trained models to a growing library — is the only structural path to defensibility, and it is exactly that: hypothetical, with zero evidence of compound accumulation.

Phillip Baek holds a BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin and briefly accepted a role at Boston Scientific as an EP Mapping Specialist. Liam McBride has an MS in Computer Science from UIUC and previously built a shared living app called BoBo that raised $20,000 in non-dilutive funding. Neither founder has published research in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis — no Google Scholar results, no PubMed results. Alchemy's product serves researchers whose specific knowledge in imaging science almost certainly exceeds the founders' own. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact systems that power AI search — his specific knowledge WAS the company's engine, not an accessory to it. Alchemy's founders identified a workflow bottleneck, which is useful pattern recognition, but it is not the non-replicable, non-trainable expertise that makes a company irreplaceable. A competitor with a founding team drawn from the CellProfiler core development group or a microscopy-focused computational biology lab would possess domain knowledge that Alchemy's founders would need years to accumulate.

Alchemy's leverage architecture has the shape of permissionless software leverage — a two-person team serving researchers at near-zero marginal cost per analysis, SaaS economics, no proportional headcount requirement. A UC Santa Cruz PhD student described reducing H. pylori microscopy analysis from three hours to under ten minutes. That is genuine workflow compression. But the leverage is rented from foundation model providers and open-source tools, not owned. Compare Alchemy to Notion, where the team rebuilt their product from scratch after the first version failed — Notion's leverage was entirely proprietary: custom block architecture, proprietary data model, permissionless scaling through templates that users created and shared. Alchemy's natural-language-to-pipeline orchestration sits on top of components that any well-resourced competitor can access. The 98% time reduction claim matters, but it describes the leverage of the underlying AI models, not leverage that Alchemy uniquely created.

Baek and McBride's prior collaboration on BoBo — a shared living mobile app using Dart, Flutter, and Firebase — demonstrates co-founder trust built through a shared project, which avoids my red flag on co-founders who met at startup weekends. But the pivot from shared living apps to biotech image analysis signals founders searching for a fundable problem rather than founders whose identity is fused with a specific domain. Alchemy was founded in 2024 and the founders have persisted through YC acceptance in 2026, which shows commitment over an eighteen-month span. Persistence is necessary but not sufficient for principal authenticity. I look for founders who would be working on this problem without venture funding — founders for whom the work feels like play. The BoBo-to-microscopy trajectory reads more like agent behavior: competent operators applying general AI skills to a market opportunity they identified through analysis.

The strongest bull case for Alchemy: it democratizes access to sophisticated image analysis for the long tail of researchers locked out by enterprise pricing (PathAI at $490M raised, Proscia at $130M raised, both focused on clinical diagnostics) and intimidated by open-source tools requiring scripting expertise. Alchemy gives a graduate student with no coding skills access to capabilities previously restricted to labs with dedicated computational biologists or enterprise software budgets — my access democratization pattern maps cleanly. The team of four people shipping a working product with at least one verified user endorsement is the lean structure I reward. And the incumbent principal-agent problem is real: Leica, PathAI, and Proscia are agents of their enterprise sales models, serving hospital pathology labs and top-20 pharma companies, structurally unable to chase individual academic researchers at low price points. Alchemy could own a segment the agents cannot profitably serve.

The bull case is real but insufficient. Alchemy's access democratization removes a UX barrier, not a structural one — CellProfiler and ImageJ are already free and already cited in thousands of publications. The barrier being lowered is expertise, which is valuable but fragile: the moment a foundation model provider ships microscopy-specific capabilities natively, or the moment CellProfiler adds an LLM interface (which the Broad Institute could fund through grants), Alchemy's value proposition compresses. "Innovation without a moat eventually becomes consumer surplus" — the value flows to the researcher and eventually to the foundation model provider. What would flip me to Invest: evidence that Alchemy has built a proprietary model library with contributions from 50+ research labs, creating a data network effect that compounds with each new user. That would transform rented leverage into owned leverage. Without it, Alchemy is a competent workflow tool built by competent engineers in a domain where they lack the specific knowledge to stay ahead of the researchers they serve or the incumbents who may eventually notice them.
