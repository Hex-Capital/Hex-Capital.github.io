# Cardboard -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking fact about Cardboard is not what the founders are building but how many other companies are building it simultaneously. Descript has raised $101 million and generates roughly $55 million in annual revenue. OpusClip has $50 million in funding and ten million users. Kapwing, Vizard, CapCut, and Adobe are all layering AI capabilities onto video editing workflows. Hacker News commenters on Cardboard's own launch thread observed "lots of players in the market doing this" -- including other companies in the same YC W26 batch targeting adjacent video editing problems. When the accelerator itself is funding multiple entrants in a single category, you are not looking at a secret. You are looking at a consensus thesis being simultaneously pursued by dozens of well-funded actors, which is the precise market structure I avoid. The mimetic dynamics here are textbook Girardian: every player is imitating every other player, competing for the same marketing teams, the same content creators, the same workflow. The result is margin compression and mutual destruction, not monopoly profits.

What is the secret? The founders' implicit thesis is that video editing can be collapsed from a skilled manual craft into a natural language instruction -- "footage plus goal equals first cut." This is not contrarian. It is the stated roadmap of every AI video company and every incumbent simultaneously. Adobe is adding generative AI to Premiere Pro. CapCut already has AI-assisted editing for hundreds of millions of users. The specific implementation choice -- a browser-native NLE built on WebGPU and WebCodecs combined with an agentic AI layer -- is an engineering bet, but an engineering bet is not a secret about the world. A secret must be a truth that most intelligent people actively reject. No intelligent person in 2026 rejects the premise that AI will transform video editing. They simply disagree about which company will win, which is not a disagreement about the nature of reality -- it is a competitive horse race, and I do not bet on horse races.

The strongest bull case for Cardboard is the compound technical moat: building both a professional-grade browser NLE and a reliable agentic editing layer simultaneously, which the founders correctly note "most startups do one; incumbents can't rebuild their stack without breaking everything." If both systems reach production quality and reinforce each other -- the AI agent generating edits directly into a timeline that users can refine in-browser without downloading software -- the integrated experience could be genuinely 10x faster than the alternatives. Ishan Sharma's five years building performance-critical browser applications at HackerRank is directly relevant technical experience for the rendering challenge, and the WebGPU/WebCodecs timing argument has some validity: these APIs matured recently enough that no incumbent has built their core architecture around them. The pricing structure (flat SaaS at $60-$150/month rather than usage-based credits) suggests the founders understand that predictable pricing reduces adoption friction for marketing teams. For this to work as an investment, you would need to believe that the dual-system complexity creates a sustainable engineering moat -- that no competitor with ten or fifty times the capital can replicate both systems within two years. I do not believe this. The core AI capability runs on commercially available foundation models. The browser rendering, while technically demanding, is engineering work, not proprietary science. A well-resourced competitor -- Descript, Adobe, or a ByteDance subsidiary -- could assign a team of fifty engineers to replicate both systems faster than two founders can build them.

The founders show execution velocity -- thirteen product releases since November 2025 is impressive for a two-person team, and the 1,000+ early users and named customers like PostHog represent real signal at pre-seed. Saksham Aggarwal's content creation experience (250,000 views per month) gives him genuine exposure to the pain point. But I see competent builders pursuing a consensus thesis, not visionary founders holding a contrarian conviction against social pressure. Saksham's previous company, Iterate AI, was shut down -- not disqualifying, but it does not demonstrate the pattern of extreme persistence against adversity that characterizes the founders I back most aggressively. There is no evidence of refusing an easier path, no indication of a definite plan for how the world changes if Cardboard succeeds beyond "video editing gets easier." The vision is product-level, not civilizational.

The market entry strategy is also insufficiently narrow. "Growth and marketing teams" at startups is a segment, not a monopoly beachhead. When Facebook launched, it was Harvard and only Harvard -- a community so small and dense that the product was immediately indispensable to every member. Cardboard is targeting a broad category of potential users across industries from day one, competing with established tools that already serve those users. The XML export to Premiere Pro and DaVinci Resolve is a feature that reduces switching costs in both directions -- it makes adoption easier, but it also makes departure frictionless. There is no lock-in mechanism, no network effect, and no structural reason why a marketing team that adopts Cardboard today cannot switch to a competitor tomorrow.

I pass. The absence of a contrarian secret in a crowded competitive market is the primary reason. Two talented founders building a well-executed product in a category where a dozen well-funded companies share the same thesis is the definition of what I avoid. The technology is competent but built on commodity foundations. The market structure ensures that even if Cardboard builds a superior product, the returns will be competed away. This is not a zero-to-one company -- it is a one-to-n improvement in a field where the "n" is already large and growing.
