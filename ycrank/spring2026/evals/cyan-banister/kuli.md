# Kuli -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Michael Hodara ran AI allocation on a $20M paid-ads budget at Aircall and Jonathan Hassan spent six years inside KPMG underwriting M&A for consumer brands — Kuli is what happens when two competent operators who *advised* the buyer decide to build the tool for the buyer. The signature here is a well-governed pair shipping the most consensus-shaped product in the Spring 2026 batch: "AI coworker for marketing teams." The clearest thing about Kuli is who holds the pen (CEO/CRO, ten years of side hustles together); the weakest thing about Kuli is that I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game everyone is playing right now is exactly this one.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage before he ever pitched a defense company — the Anduril check loaded because the founder's prior product proved he could *manufacture an artifact* in the adjacent domain. Kuli's founder origin diverges on the substance, not the shape. Hodara operated AI systems that *bought* creator-style media at Aircall and Logitech; he didn't run the creator side of a consumer brand, didn't ship a creator-marketing tool to scale before Kuli, and didn't surface this problem because he *felt* it as a marketer drowning in eight hours a week of TikTok scrubbing. He surfaced it because he watched marketing budgets get allocated and saw a category of spend. That is competent founder-adjacency, not the autobiographical contact I look for. Hassan's KPMG M&A pedigree is the exact "advised the industry from a deck" trajectory I tend to discount. Operationally credible, biographically thin.

The pass example I keep on a sticky note reads: "two ex-FAANG PMs, $20M seed oversubscribed, opens with a TAM slide and a four-logo team page." Kuli is the European-flavored variant of that exact deck — EPFL and St. Gallen instead of Stanford, Aircall and KPMG instead of FAANG and Goldman, and the four-logo wall is Disney/Nivea/Havas plus a Fortune 100 entertainment anchor. The pattern is identical: pedigreed operators, named-brand logo proof, AI-agent positioning, a saturated competitive set (CreatorIQ at $35K–$200K/yr, Aspire at $2,499/mo, Modash at $299/mo). Kuli is the Spring 2026 consensus YC AI-coworker pitch with marketing as the vertical wrapper. Find them before it becomes consensus is the rule — this one is already consensus on the day of launch.

On Magically Weird, Kuli scores against itself. The YC launch tweet went out May 4, 2026, the category has three priced incumbents, the product description ("watches every video on social platforms") will be a Gemini or Sora API call by mid-2027, and the "free trial available, custom enterprise pricing" model is identical to every CreatorIQ alternative on the Syncly comparison page. Eighteen months of mechanism: the "we actually watch the content" wedge compresses as foundation models commoditize frame-level understanding, the data flywheel Kuli implies is structurally weak because campaign outcomes belong to the brand and don't aggregate across customers, and CreatorIQ bundles video analysis into existing seats. The differentiation evaporates from both ends.

On Capital-Intensive / Unsexy-Regulated, the counterfactual is what would have to be true for Kuli to score a tier higher: Kuli would need to own a proprietary creator-payment, compliance, or FTC-disclosure rail — the regulated plumbing underneath the campaigns — not just the discovery and reporting layer. The Vigil Labs or Mindbloom lane requires Kuli to take on something other investors flinch at. The dossier shows nothing of that: pure software, pure SaaS, pure discoverability and reporting. The free utility calculators (engagement, EMV, pricing) on kuli.one are SEO bait, not regulatory moat.

On Physical-World Verifiability, the single-anchor parallel is HQ Trivia — the exception I made because the product was so tactile I could feel the viral pull on my own phone before any metric existed. Kuli inverts HQ Trivia. Kuli's product output is enterprise dashboards behind a Calendly demo gate; the artifact a normal human encounters in the wild is the creator video Kuli helped surface, not Kuli itself. I cannot install Kuli at the Four Seasons, cannot scan it on a hotel Wi-Fi list, cannot play it at a dinner table. The Fortune 100 "3x campaigns" testimonial is founder-mediated metric; it is not a Kalanick-at-The-Lobby observation.

The 36-month forecast on Kuli: tucked acquisition by CreatorIQ, Aspire, or Sprout Social at $30–80M after reaching $3–8M ARR, or dead as foundation-model video understanding collapses the wedge — most likely the tuck. Mechanism: Hodara and Hassan are operationally good enough to close a handful of enterprise contracts on the strength of the Aircall paid-ads narrative and the KPMG relationships, which is exactly the profile that produces a competent acqui-hire when the incumbent's product team needs the video-LLM workflow shipped a year earlier than they could build it.

The strongest case against my pass is that Disney, Nivea, Havas, and a Fortune 100 entertainment customer in five months is *not* normal pre-seed logo wallpaper — those are real procurement cycles, and Hodara's $20M-budget operational fluency at Aircall is the rare profile that can actually sell into a CMO without flinching. Biz and Tiz check; the question is whether the Rizz of a "watch every video" agent compounds into workflow lock-in fast enough to outrun incumbent bundling. The dossier evidence that wins my pass is the competitive page: CreatorIQ at $35K–$200K/yr with enterprise distribution already inside the same consumer brands Kuli is naming as customers. Kuli is selling a wedge into accounts that already have a seat-based contract with the entrenched player, which means the bake-off Kuli has to win is feature-against-incumbent-roadmap, not greenfield. One verifiable flip condition: if Kuli publishes a signed enterprise contract north of $250K ACV with a brand that *cancels* its CreatorIQ seat to do so, that is workflow-lock-in beating incumbent bundling, and I would re-open the file.
