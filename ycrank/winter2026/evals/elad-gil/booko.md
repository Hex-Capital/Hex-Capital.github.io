# Booko -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most interesting structural question here isn't whether dynamic pricing works for fitness studios -- it obviously does, airlines and hotels have proven this for decades. The question is why a two-person startup should be the one to deliver it when Mindbody, the dominant booking platform with ~$500M in revenue, already ships its own dynamic pricing feature. The dossier highlights Mindbody's 20% + 3.5% take rate as creating pricing headroom for a cheaper alternative, and that's a real observation -- incumbent fee structures do create third-party opportunity. But it also highlights the existential platform dependency: Booko integrates via API with Mindbody, meaning Mindbody can restrict access, degrade the API, or simply lower its fee and eliminate the arbitrage. When your entire distribution channel runs through a company that competes with you directly, you don't have a distribution moat. You have a temporary pricing spread.

The market itself is real but I struggle to identify a structural inflection point. What changed in the last 12-24 months to make this possible now? The dossier's "why now" section is entirely inferred, and the signals are weak: cloud ML infrastructure has been mature enough for per-client demand prediction models for at least five years, booking platform APIs have been available for years, and post-COVID demand variability is a 2021 story, not a 2025 structural shift. Mindbody launching its own dynamic pricing feature in 2024 is being framed as market education, but I'd read it the other way -- the incumbent is moving into this space with the advantage of already owning the customer relationship and booking data. When the platform you depend on ships the feature you sell, that's a market-ending move in slow motion. This is fundamentally different from the Gusto pattern: Gusto built payroll as a wedge into a platform it owned end-to-end. Booko is building a pricing overlay on top of someone else's platform -- it's a feature, not a foundation.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this falls furthest short of what I look for. Stripe started with payments but owned the developer integration point, which became the channel for Atlas, Billing, Treasury. Figma owned the design file, which became the channel for FigJam and developer handoff. Booko sits between the booking system and the pricing decision -- it doesn't own either the customer relationship (that's Mindbody's) or the end-consumer interaction. Where does the second product go? You could imagine expanding into demand forecasting, marketing automation, or business intelligence for service businesses, but all of those require deepening a customer relationship that structurally runs through someone else's platform. An overlay that optimizes one variable is a feature, not a distribution channel.

To genuinely engage the bull case: the "boring but critical" archetype partially applies. Every business that sells time slots leaves revenue on the table, and a 20% uplift is a compelling ROI story if it holds across customer types. The perishable inventory framing is sharp -- an empty Tuesday 5pm yoga slot genuinely is gone forever, and that urgency could drive rapid adoption. If Booko could expand beyond Mindbody to become the pricing intelligence layer across every booking system (Zenoti, WellnessLiving, GlossGenius, custom platforms), it would reduce single-platform dependency and build a cross-platform data advantage that no individual booking system could replicate. The company achieving SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance as a two-person team shows operational seriousness, and the early customer results ($4,280/week recovered, fill rates from 67% to 89%) demonstrate the product works. If this team can navigate the platform dependency risk and build integrations broad enough to become platform-agnostic, the market of every time-slot-based service business is genuinely large. But "if we can get broad enough distribution to become platform-agnostic" and "if the incumbents don't capture this feature" are two separate miracles on top of the core miracle of proving the pricing model consistently works -- and I pass on multi-miracle plans.

The founder opacity is unusual even for pre-seed. I don't require famous pedigrees, but I need some signal of why this team is uniquely positioned to solve this problem. Neither founder's background in ML, dynamic pricing, booking systems, or the fitness/wellness industry could be confirmed. The product itself demonstrates technical competence, which counts for something -- shipping per-client ML models with enterprise compliance certifications as a two-person team is non-trivial. But I can't evaluate founder-market fit when I can't see the founders' backgrounds at all. When I backed Harvey, I could see a team with deep legal domain knowledge building ahead of a capability curve. Here I see a competent product with no visible connection between the builders and the domain.

The technology cycle positioning doesn't help either. This isn't a case where a new technology threshold has been crossed that makes something possible for the first time. Demand prediction using historical booking data is well-established ML. The enabling infrastructure -- cloud ML, booking APIs -- has been available for years. There's no GPT-2-to-GPT-3 moment here, no cost curve crossing a critical threshold. The company is applying mature technology to a vertical that hasn't adopted it yet, which is a valid business but not the kind of structural timing bet that generates asymmetric returns. I look for companies positioned at the exact moment a technology capability crosses a threshold that unlocks a market. Booko is positioned at the moment when... nothing in particular has changed except that a competitor launched the same feature at a high price point.
