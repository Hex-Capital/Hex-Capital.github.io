# Minicor -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is specific and real. Anthropic shipped computer-use capabilities in late 2024, OpenAI and Google followed, and for the first time, vision-based AI models can reliably read and interact with arbitrary desktop GUIs. That capability threshold makes self-healing desktop automation possible in a way it simply wasn't 24 months ago -- UiPath and Automation Anywhere built empires on brittle scripts precisely because there was no alternative. What Minicor is doing is taking that capability jump and wrapping it in the deployment infrastructure enterprises actually need: Windows VMs, Citrix environments, HIPAA compliance, horizontal scaling. Desktop automation for healthcare EHRs is exactly the kind of market most AI investors walk past -- everyone is building browser agents right now, competing for the same surface area. Minicor is targeting the Windows desktop and Citrix environments that run the actual back-office of American healthcare. That's a "too boring" market archetype I know well.

The production traction claim is what makes me lean forward. Processing 25,000 patients per day with SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certification at pre-seed, with a four-person team -- that's not a demo. SOC 2 Type II alone requires months of documented operational processes and a formal audit. Getting that done while building the product and entering YC tells me something about execution velocity. When I was building Color Health, I learned firsthand that healthcare compliance isn't a checkbox -- it's a moat that compounds. Every healthcare organization that deploys Minicor in production accumulates switching costs that script-based RPA never created, because the compliance certification travels with the vendor relationship. The enterprise logos on the website (unnamed, but present) and the healthcare-first positioning suggest they understand that regulated industries are where compliance creates structural defensibility.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where I need to see more. The API-first architecture is the right foundation -- a single API call triggering a full desktop workflow is how you become infrastructure rather than a point solution. Healthcare is the wedge, but financial services firms running legacy desktop applications, accounting organizations on QuickBooks and Sage, any enterprise stuck on Citrix-hosted apps -- these are all natural expansion verticals once you've proven reliability. The pattern would look like Gusto: start with the boring, critical function (payroll, or in this case, desktop automation), then distribute additional products through the same enterprise relationship. But I don't see evidence the founders are thinking this way yet. The pivot from Laminar -- a low-code integration platform -- to desktop automation suggests they found a market pull, which is good, but the sign-in portal still resolving to app.laminar.run tells me the pivot is recent enough that the infrastructure hasn't fully transitioned. That's not disqualifying, but it makes me want to understand whether they're building for the desktop automation market specifically or whether this is another iteration in a search for product-market fit.

The question I keep returning to is whether the differentiation layer is thick enough. Harvey built legal AI ahead of the capability curve, but Harvey's miracle was being delivered by the foundation model labs -- model fidelity improving to legal-grade accuracy -- while Harvey itself could focus entirely on workflow, enterprise trust, and distribution. Minicor has a similar structure: the computer-use capability is improving externally, and Minicor focuses on deployment infrastructure and compliance. But Harvey's application layer is deep -- legal workflows, citation verification, firm-specific knowledge bases. Minicor's application layer is thinner: VM orchestration, compliance certifications, Citrix support, observability. Those are real but potentially replicable by a well-funded competitor in 6-12 months. If Anthropic or OpenAI decides to ship a turnkey desktop automation product with enterprise compliance, how much of Minicor's differentiation survives? The desktop-specific deployment surface and healthcare compliance create more distance from the model providers than a typical AI wrapper, but less distance than Harvey has in legal.

The bull case is compelling if you weight the market structure correctly. Enterprise RPA is a $35 billion market (or $5 billion depending on whose estimate you trust) built on script-based tools that everyone hates. The incumbent response dynamic favors Minicor: UiPath moving to AI-first self-healing agents would mean cannibalizing the scripted automation business their entire sales force and customer base is built around. That's the innovator's dilemma playing out in real time. Healthcare migration to cloud-native SaaS is glacially slow -- these EHR systems and Citrix environments will exist for another decade or more. A team that already has production healthcare deployments with HIPAA compliance, building on foundation model capabilities that are improving every quarter, in a market that most AI-focused investors dismiss as boring legacy infrastructure -- that's a pattern I've invested in before. If the founders develop a clear multi-product vision and the differentiation layer thickens with accumulated operational data across customer deployments, this could become the infrastructure layer for enterprise desktop automation the way PagerDuty became the infrastructure layer for operations alerting.

What holds me back is the accumulation of moderate risks rather than any single disqualifying factor. The pivot from Laminar is recent. The foundation model dependency creates a structural ceiling on defensibility. The founders have solid enterprise engineering backgrounds but no deep healthcare domain expertise. The single miracle isn't crisply defined -- is it agent reliability, enterprise distribution, or compliance moat? The market is "boring" in a way I like, but "AI agents" is simultaneously a consensus-hot category, which creates noise. I'd want a direct conversation to understand: what's the one thing that has to work, and how thick is the moat getting with each customer deployment? For now, this lands as a company where the structural moment is real and the market archetype fits my thesis, but the execution evidence and strategic clarity haven't yet reached the threshold where I'd write a check from my own money without additional diligence.
