# Indexable -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft engine in Rust that hit 1k stars on GitHub and topped r/rust for 24 hours before he ever pitched Indexable, and that single fact is the analytical signature of this company: Indexable is a hacker-genealogy bet with a co-founder fog. The highest signal in the dossier is technical lineage; the lowest is that two of three founders at Indexable are not named anywhere public, and the dossier carries no evidence Gazelka himself ever lost an afternoon to a broken agent sandbox.

The hyperion repo is what registers first when I read Indexable. Patrick Collison won Ireland's 41st Young Scientist for Lisp work in 2005, and that prior artifact -- not a market thesis -- is what made the **Stripe** (2010) check easy years later. Indexable's analog is hyperion-mc/hyperion at 1k stars plus contributions to nushell (39.6k stars, not his), plus xAI tenure on high-performance systems, plus the Rust subculture flex. The divergence from the Collison rung is that Patrick's prize predicted Patrick's commerce work because he had already shipped **Auctomatic** (2007) in the exact category; Gazelka's hyperion proves he can write a multiplayer game engine in Rust, which proves systems chops but not that he has shipped anything in the sandbox category before Indexable. The hacker tracks are real. They are not yet category-loaded the way the Collisons' were.

The pass-example MBA team in my own writing fails on the cofounder dimension by being two strangers who paired at a hackathon weeks before applying -- no shared history, no demonstrated chemistry under load. Indexable triggers a softer version of the same pattern: the YC page lists team size 3, but the dossier explicitly states "only Andrew Gazelka is named on the YC company page; the other two members are not publicly identified on YC, GitHub indexable-inc org, or website." That is not a solo founder, which would be a hard red flag, but it is opacity where I want a roommate story. **Reddit** (2005) was Huffman and Ohanian from a UVA dorm; **Twitch** (2007) was Kan and Shear continuing from **Kiko** (2005). Indexable's two unnamed engineers may be either of those, or they may be hires Gazelka is calling co-founders. The dossier cannot tell me which, and the YC interview is where I would settle it.

KVM-based hypervisor with millisecond fork of running process memory plus content-addressable cross-tenant dedup is the kind of work other YC applicants in Spring 2026 are unconsciously routing around -- I would bet most of Indexable's batch is shipping LangChain wrappers. The schlep dimension at Indexable compounds over 18 months in the way **Coinbase** (2012)'s compliance grind compounded: the longer Gazelka stays inside the virtualization stack, the harder it is for a Modal engineer to catch up by reading docs, because the bugs live in the kernel and the tenancy model, not the API. That is a real moat candidate, weaker than payments-rails but stronger than what most AI infrastructure pitches arrive carrying.

The organic-idea dimension at Indexable would flip a full tier if the dossier contained one sentence: "Gazelka built Indexable because at xAI he watched Grok's coding agent silently corrupt sandbox state on rollback." That sentence is not there. What is there is xAI tenure plus a category that an xAI engineer would plausibly have hit, and I am asked to infer the rest. I will not infer it. Drew Houston had the USB stick story; the Collisons had been hacking commerce flows since teenagers. Indexable gets the absent-evidence default plus a small bump for plausible adjacency, not the full organic mark.

Modal raised $355M in May 2026 at a $4.65B valuation, E2B already runs inside Hugging Face / Perplexity / Groq / Manus, and Daytona shipped sub-90ms container create in February 2026 -- compared against those numbers, Indexable is a hypervisor team competing with a docs repo at 11 stars. **DoorDash** (2013) faced GrubHub and Seamless and won on the last-mile schlep that the incumbents had not industrialized; the parallel at Indexable is that none of E2B, Modal, or Daytona currently forks running memory plus DB state, only filesystem. If Gazelka actually ships that primitive at production latency, the incumbents either copy it (commodity) or acquihire him to skip the 18 months (the more likely path).

My 36-month forecast for Indexable: acquihired by Modal or E2B before mid-2029 for under $60M, with Gazelka becoming a staff engineer running their fork-snapshot subsystem -- the hypervisor talent is the asset, the company brand is not. The mechanism is the capital gap times the commoditization clock: Modal's $466M total raised buys 18 months of parallel hypervisor R&D, and Indexable's YC standard check does not buy the same runway against three funded incumbents in the same Modal-blog comparison post.

The strongest case against my acquihire forecast is the **Coinbase** (2012) precedent: Brian Armstrong looked structurally outgunned by Coinbase Pro's competitors in 2012, and the schlep he ran toward -- regulatory compliance -- turned into the moat that nobody could copy by hiring one engineer, because compliance is cumulative trust, not code. The bull argument is that KVM fork-of-running-memory at production multi-tenancy is the same shape of moat: it requires accumulated bug-fix scar tissue that one Modal hire cannot import. I find this argument real and partly dismantled by the dossier itself: Coinbase had Armstrong's hand-built Android wallet as the pre-funding animal artifact, and Indexable's equivalent for THIS company -- not for hyperion -- is 11 stars on indexable-inc/index and 11 on indexable-inc/docs. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a named design-partner customer (Cursor, Cognition, Replit, Vercel) running Indexable in production on a workload Modal cannot serve. That fact would tell me the moat compounded faster than the capital gap closed. The dossier does not contain it today.
