# Smartbase -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Three ventures across three unrelated domains in roughly two years. Sam Goldman went from Sphinx Labs (smart contract DevOps, $2M raised) to Anytrace (AI support engineering, YC S25) to Smartbase (manufacturing PO automation, YC Spring 2026). I start every evaluation at specific knowledge, and Smartbase presents the clearest absence of it I've seen in this batch. Specific knowledge takes years of obsessive engagement to accumulate — it feels like play to the person building it. Goldman and Fujioka describe sleeping at a factory and working on factory floors. That's due diligence, not specific knowledge. If I dropped a different pair of competent engineers into those same factories, they'd identify the same pain point within a week. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had years at Google Brain building the exact systems underneath AI search — the company WAS his accumulated expertise, productized. Smartbase is a market opportunity identified through field research, which is the opposite pattern. "If you're more passionate about founding a business than the business itself, you can fall into a ten year trap."

Taira Fujioka's AWS SageMaker Inference background — youngest SDE 2, built inference infrastructure for Salesforce and JP Morgan — is legitimately relevant to building document extraction systems. But it's general AI infrastructure expertise, transferable across any domain. Any senior ML engineer at AWS could build Smartbase's extraction pipeline. The knowledge that would actually constitute a moat here is deep understanding of how manufacturers negotiate PO exceptions, how different ERP systems handle partial shipments, why certain business rules exist in precision machining versus food manufacturing. That knowledge lives in the heads of people who've run manufacturing operations for decades, and neither founder has it.

Conexiom at $170M raised and ~$75M annual revenue with 600+ clients has been solving this problem for twenty years. Smartbase's bull case is structural: Conexiom serves 16 of the top 20 industrial distributors with enterprise pricing and sales motions that make downmarket expansion unattractive — the same principal-agent dynamic that lets startups exist. Conexiom's sales team are agents of their enterprise P&L, unable to justify $10K contracts when their average deal is likely 10x that. Smartbase positioning against this gap is sound, and "onboarding first customers onto five-figure annual contracts" at pre-seed is a real signal — manufacturers don't sign contracts for things they don't need. The access democratization pattern is genuine: thousands of SMB manufacturers currently hire multiple full-time employees to key in purchase orders because Conexiom's solution was built for Fortune 500 distributors. Smartbase gives small manufacturers access to automation previously gated behind enterprise pricing. This maps to the AngelList pattern — removing a gatekeeper between people and something they want.

Smartbase's leverage architecture is mixed. The AI extraction layer is genuine permissionless leverage — zero marginal cost per document processed, LLM-powered parsing that improves with volume. But nine named ERP integrations (Acumatica, Epicor, Infor, Dynamics 365, NetSuite, QuickBooks, SAP, SYSPRO, Sage X3) for a two-person team is not leverage — it's a maintenance burden that scales linearly with the number of supported systems. Each ERP vendor updates APIs, deprecates endpoints, changes schemas. Custom business rules per customer add another layer of non-zero marginal cost. Smartbase is part AI leverage, part integration consulting. The commoditization risk is real: Conexiom launched an "AI-Powered Ideal Order Platform" in January 2025, and Workist (€9M Series A, 50+ enterprise customers) already automates B2B documents for Deutsche Bahn and PepsiCo. The core technology — OCR/LLM extraction plus ERP API calls — is technically reproducible by anyone with API keys and documentation.

The team-to-value ratio concerns me. Two founders is lean, which I like — Midjourney proved you can do extraordinary things with a tiny team. But Smartbase's product surface forces headcount growth. Nine ERP integrations means nine separate API surfaces to maintain, test, and debug. Custom business rules per manufacturer means onboarding is high-touch. Smartbase's five-figure contracts suggest $10K-$50K ACV — to reach $10M ARR, they need 200-1,000 customers, each requiring configured integrations and business rules. This is not the topology of a company that stays at five people. Compare this to Notion, which built a general-purpose tool that scales to millions of users without per-customer configuration — that's the leverage architecture I look for.

The strongest argument for Smartbase is the access democratization combined with legitimate switching costs. Once Smartbase is integrated with a manufacturer's ERP and configured with their business rules, the switching cost is high — workflow dependency, employee retraining, risk of order processing errors during migration. This is real defensibility, even if it's earned through labor rather than through network effects. The "why now" is also credible: LLM document understanding became cheap enough for SMBs in 2024-2025, and ERP API ecosystems matured simultaneously. One verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Goldman or Fujioka had spent two or more years working inside a manufacturing operation — or if Smartbase hired a manufacturing operations veteran as a third co-founder who brought the specific knowledge the technical founders lack. The technology is sound. The market gap is real. What's missing is the founder whose knowledge IS the company, rather than founders whose knowledge is building software applied to whatever problem they found last quarter.
