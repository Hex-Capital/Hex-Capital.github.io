# Chamber -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Chamber is that it's four Amazon veterans doing exactly what you'd predict four Amazon veterans would do: take the infrastructure cost optimization playbook they ran inside Amazon and productize it for GPU clusters. There's nothing wrong with that -- it's competent, it's timely, the domain expertise is real. But this is precisely the pattern I warn founders about. Industry insiders executing the industry's playbook. They know what Amazon built internally, they know the pain points, and they're building a slightly better version for everyone else. That's experience-driven innovation, not first-principles invention. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a guy from New Zealand who didn't know what aerospace experts considered impossible. Chamber's team knows exactly what's possible and what's not -- which constrains them to the possible.

Let me ask my first question: if Chamber succeeds completely, does the world operate differently? They claim $240B in wasted GPU compute. Let's take that at face value. If Chamber captures a slice of that waste -- brings utilization from 40% to 80% across enterprise GPU clusters -- the consequence is that companies spend less on their AI infrastructure. That's a cost savings story. Nobody's life changes. No industry gets disrupted. GPUs still exist, Kubernetes still exists, NVIDIA still sells the hardware, enterprises still run the same workloads. The existing system operates more efficiently, but the system itself is untouched. Run:ai -- the category leader -- was acquired for $700M. That's the demonstrated ceiling for the dominant player in this exact space. Chamber would need to be dramatically better than Run:ai to exceed that outcome, and there's no evidence of a step-function improvement -- it's the same concept (GPU orchestration) with a different AI layer on top. This is the definition of a consequence of success that doesn't justify the ambition I look for.

The timing argument is the bull case, and I want to take it seriously. NVIDIA acquired Run:ai and is open-sourcing it, which simultaneously validates the category and creates a genuine structural opening. Every enterprise running mixed GPU environments -- hybrid cloud, multi-vendor -- now needs a vendor-neutral orchestration layer, and the incumbent just got absorbed into the hardware vendor's stack. GPU spending is on an exponential curve: $120 billion in data center GPUs in 2025, projected $228 billion by 2030. The convergence of these factors -- exploding spend, vendor vacuum, optimization urgency -- is real. If I were evaluating Chamber purely on market timing, it's well-positioned. The agentic AI layer could theoretically create a data flywheel: more deployments mean better demand forecasting models, which attract more deployments. In a world where GPU capacity remains constrained for years and enterprises are spending at unprecedented scale, the orchestration layer could become critical infrastructure worth multiples of what Run:ai achieved. That's the scenario where I'd be wrong to pass.

But here's what I notice that a generic infrastructure investor might miss: I already backed vCluster through Loft Labs at $24M Series A in April 2024. That company operates in virtual Kubernetes clusters for GPU efficiency -- adjacent territory. The dossier even lists vCluster as a competitor. So I'm not evaluating Chamber in isolation; I'm evaluating whether I need a second bet in GPU infrastructure optimization when I already have one. More fundamentally, the technology here is incremental improvement to scheduling -- smarter queuing, better fault detection, demand forecasting. It makes existing GPU infrastructure work better by 2-3x. It doesn't make existing approaches structurally obsolete. Compare that to Square, which didn't optimize traditional POS systems -- it replaced them entirely with a fundamentally different delivery mechanism. Chamber is optimizing the POS system.

The team composition reinforces my concern. Four co-founders from the same company, all infrastructure engineers, all with the same mental model. Charles Ding is a second-time founder with an exit, which signals execution capability. Jason Ong has specific GPU scheduling tooling experience from Amazon. Shaocheng Wang brings nine years of AWS observability. These are strong credentials for building this specific product. But the homogeneity is a risk I track -- where's the go-to-market founder? Where's the person who doesn't think like an Amazon engineer? The team is engineered to build the product, not to challenge the assumptions embedded in the product's design. They haven't assembled a team to address the venture's critical risks (enterprise sales cycles, NVIDIA ecosystem gravity, open-source commoditization) -- they've assembled a team to write the code. That's not gene pool engineering; that's hiring your former coworkers.

I pass on companies like this regularly. Strong team, real market, working technology, good timing -- and the consequence of success is a solid infrastructure business that makes enterprise AI cheaper to run. That's worth building, but it's not worth my kind of bet. Some very good investor should fund this. It's not the kind of problem that's too important not to work on.
