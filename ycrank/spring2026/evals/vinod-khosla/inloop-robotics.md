# InLoop Robotics -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Nimble Robotics has $221 million in the bank and a $1 billion valuation deploying the same human-in-the-loop teleoperation architecture that InLoop Robotics describes as its core differentiation. Dexterity has $300 million and is already embedded at FedEx and UPS. InLoop Robotics has $500K from YC and four people. I have funded companies into brutal competitive gaps before — I wrote a $28 million check into Rocket Lab when everyone assumed SpaceX had consolidated the launch market. But Rocket Lab had genuinely novel technology: electric turbopumps, 3D-printed combustion chambers, a non-linear engineering approach that made dedicated small-sat launch economically viable for the first time. InLoop Robotics' confidence-aware Safety Module and teleoperator fallback loop are smart engineering, but the dossier itself acknowledges the architecture is "architecturally straightforward and already used by Nimble Robotics." When your technical approach is the same as a competitor with 440 times your capital, you need a discontinuous advantage somewhere. I do not see it here.

InLoop Robotics' consequence magnitude — the question I always start with — lands in an uncomfortable middle ground. 2.1 million unfilled warehouse jobs by 2030 is a real labor crisis, and automated fulfillment at scale would transform e-commerce economics for consumers worldwide. That category-level consequence is genuine. But InLoop Robotics is not uniquely positioned to deliver it. Amazon Robotics, Symbotic ($31.3 billion market cap, exclusive $23 billion Walmart deal), Nimble, and Dexterity are all converging on this outcome through different vectors. When I backed Impossible Foods, it was the only credible path to protein synthesis without animal agriculture. When I backed OpenAI at $1 billion, no other organization was seriously pursuing AGI at that scale. InLoop Robotics is one entrant in a category that absorbed $2.26 billion in Q1 2026 alone. The consequence belongs to the category, not to this company.

Zakariea Sharfeddine's ML work at Bosch and BMW, Stepan Feduniak's robot learning research at 18, and Martin Mohammed's IBM reinforcement learning internship at 16 — these are real signals of early technical capability. I funded Mach Industries when the founder was a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems, and these InLoop Robotics founders share some of that youthful technical ambition from TUM's robotics ecosystem. But Mach's founder had a specific, contrarian technical insight about defense hardware manufacturing that Lockheed couldn't replicate. InLoop Robotics' founders show competence in the relevant technical stack — computer vision, manipulation policy training, cloud infrastructure — without evidence of a non-obvious insight that their better-funded competitors have missed. Pasha Rizali's LinkedIn headline reads "Aspiring Robotics Engineer | Computer Science Student with a Passion for Innovation," which is a student's profile, not a founder's declaration of war against an industry. I look for rate of learning, and Martin Mohammed's prior YC experience with Eggi and hackathon wins suggest adaptability, but the dossier contains no evidence of how the team has iterated, pivoted, or been surprised during their YC batch — the specific signal I use to assess learning velocity.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: InLoop Robotics' RaaS model with zero upfront capital cost is the exact affordability play I get most excited about. Traditional warehouse automation requires $100K+ and months of integration — InLoop Robotics claims deployment in hours with a flat monthly subscription. This is the pattern I see in my highest-conviction bets: making the expensive radically accessible. The Covariant acqui-hire by Amazon in August 2024 removed the leading independent warehouse AI company, creating genuine market space. Foundation model advances in 2023-2025 mean a four-person team can now build manipulation policies that would have required fifty people two years ago. These young German immigrants building robots in San Francisco fit my revealed preference for outsider founders. The data flywheel — every teleoperator intervention generates labeled training data — creates a compounding advantage that could accelerate faster per deployment dollar than Nimble's more capital-intensive approach. An advocate would cite my DoorDash exception: when I overrode the "non-linear technical approach" criterion because the consequence magnitude was enormous and the founding team demonstrated extraordinary learning velocity. But DoorDash was creating a new behavioral category — on-demand food delivery — not entering a market where $221M-funded competitors already deployed the same architecture. Tony Xu's learning rate was visible and extraordinary. InLoop Robotics' founders have not yet demonstrated that level of adaptive velocity in the evidence available.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if InLoop Robotics can show that their autonomous success rate is improving at 2-3x the rate of Nimble's published benchmarks per unit of training data, that would constitute evidence of a non-linear technical advantage masked by the architectural similarity. A faster learning curve per dollar would transform the capital disadvantage from fatal to irrelevant — the same way Rocket Lab's superior cost-per-kg to orbit made SpaceX's scale advantage moot in the small-sat segment. Without that data, I am betting on a team executing the same playbook as a competitor with 440 times more resources, and I do not make bets where the upside case is "we execute better" without a technical wedge. The RaaS affordability angle is real but insufficient on its own — it is a business model innovation, not a technology discontinuity.

InLoop Robotics occupies a category I care about — physical-world transformation, labor automation, radical affordability — but without the non-linear technical approach or the improbable ambition level that distinguishes my best bets from competent companies in competitive markets. The ambition of automating warehouse fulfillment is consensus in 2026, not contrarian. Nobody says "that can't be done" — they say "fifty companies are trying." I need to either lose my money or make fifty times my money, and InLoop Robotics' current positioning suggests a third outcome: a capable team ground down by better-capitalized competitors pursuing identical architectures.
