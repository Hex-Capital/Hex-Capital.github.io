# Hyper -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Hyper is a two-person company with strong team-to-value density and weak principal authenticity — a lean autonomy-engineer duo (Shalin Shah, Kanyes Thaker, both ex-Matic Robots) that pivoted from a March 2026 Product Hunt iOS voice notetaker to a "shared memory for humans and AI" cross-tool layer eight weeks later. That signature — efficient builders chasing the fundable wedge in AI context — is the analytical fingerprint, and it tells you most of what you need to know before reading the rest of the dossier.

Specific knowledge is where I always start, and Hyper sits below the midpoint. The HIGH rung is Perplexity — Aravind Srinivas spent years at Google Brain and OpenAI building exactly the systems AI search requires; the company IS his stack productized. The MID rung is Notion, where the team had no prior enterprise-knowledge resume but rebuilt the product from scratch on conviction. The LOW rung is the AI-wrapper MBAs from my pass example — anyone could build it. Hyper's founders are autonomy and perception engineers from a home-robotics company; multi-source sensor fusion is a real conceptual cousin to fusing signal across Notion, Cursor, and email, but it is adjacency, not domain density. Neither founder has shipped enterprise SaaS, neither has lived inside the team-knowledge problem, neither has prior public writing on this space. Hyper sits between MID and LOW, closer to LOW.

Permissionless leverage is where Hyper looks best on paper. The HIGH rung is Twitter — zero marginal cost per user, hundreds of millions scaled with a small team. The MID rung is Uber, where leverage came through marketplace network effects but required real operations. The LOW rung is Poshmark, where social commerce dragged in marketplace operations headcount. Hyper is pure code-and-AI: webhooks in, embeddings, retrieval injected per chat turn — that scales to a million teams with the same two people. But the leverage Hyper is building is rented, not owned. The integration surface (Notion, Claude Code, Cursor, ChatGPT) belongs to other companies, and each of those incumbents is shipping native memory. Hyper sits between HIGH and MID, dragged down by the commoditization of the underlying primitives.

Principal authenticity is where Hyper fails my hardest test. The HIGH rung is Sahil Lavingia, who kept coding Gumroad solo after the Series A collapsed. The MID rung is the Notion founders, who scrapped a working product because they weren't satisfied. The LOW rung, again, is the AI-wrapper team chasing what's fundable. Hyper launched on Product Hunt in March 2026 as an iOS voice memory app for in-person conversations, hit Day rank #18, and by the YC May 2026 page had become a B2B cross-tool company brain. Two months. No public statement explaining the change. That is not a principal who couldn't stop building this — that is two engineers testing wedges. Signals are what people do, despite what they say. Hyper sits between MID and LOW, closer to LOW.

Team-to-value leverage is Hyper's strongest dimension. The HIGH rung is Midjourney — ~163 employees, $500M revenue — and Cursor at <50 people and $500M ARR. The MID rung is early Notion. The LOW rung is any company whose plan requires 50+ engineers in year two. Hyper is two people, not hiring, building on Claude Code and Supabase. That is the shrinking-company thesis in the flesh, and it sits firmly between HIGH and MID. Access democratization is weakest — AngelList and CoinList are the HIGH rung because they removed structural gatekeepers from restricted resources; Stack Overflow is MID; Wish is LOW. Hyper serves AI-first teams who already have Glean, Dust, and Notion AI available. There is no gatekeeper being unlocked. Hyper sits between MID and LOW.

The 36-month forecast: Hyper either acqui-hires into Notion, Glean, or a foundation-model lab for under $30M, or dies, with a small probability of pivoting again to a vertical agent product where the autonomy-fusion specific knowledge becomes load-bearing. Glean grew from $100M to $200M ARR in nine months and has 100x the integration leverage; Notion AI is shipping the same connectors; per-tool memory is being natively absorbed by every model vendor. Hyper has no proprietary data, no exclusive integration, no community flywheel — only a clever positioning sentence and two strong builders. The leverage it produces flows to whoever owns the surfaces it reads from.

The strongest bull case: Matic Robots is genuinely about fusing noisy, multi-source signal into coherent state under latency and cost constraints — that IS the technical core of a cross-tool memory layer, and the autonomy-team archetype ships infrastructure other people can't. If Hyper turns out to have a non-obvious indexing or freshness algorithm that enterprises feel through better AI tool answers, two builders with that specific knowledge could compound into a real $20-50M ARR business before incumbents catch up. The bear answer is the pivot. A founder with deep specific knowledge in cross-tool team memory does not spend Q1 2026 building an iOS voice notetaker. Hyper would flip my verdict if within six months it shipped a proprietary capability — a teams-knowledge-graph benchmark, a named-customer LOI from an AI-first company over 50 people, or organic adoption inside YC W26 — that demonstrated the autonomy stack actually translates. Absent that, this is a competent team renting someone else's leverage in a market with $7.2B-valued incumbents already three years ahead. Pass at neutral conviction.
