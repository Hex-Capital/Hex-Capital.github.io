# Eos AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Healthcare data fragmentation is one of the most textbook schlep blindness problems I've ever seen in enterprise software. Every programmer who has tried to build a clinical AI model knows this. You pull imaging data from one hospital's PACS system and it looks nothing like the data from another hospital's system -- different scanners, different protocols, different metadata conventions. You try to match a patient across two facilities and discover their records are split across incompatible formats with inconsistent identifiers. Everyone in health IT has been complaining about this for twenty years. But actually fixing it means wading into HIPAA compliance, dealing with Epic's deliberate lock-in strategy, normalizing free text written by thousands of clinicians who each have their own shorthand, and standardizing imaging distributions across scanner manufacturers who have no incentive to cooperate. The schlep is genuinely enormous, and that's precisely why the problem persists. Commure has raised $820 million and still hasn't fully solved it. Redox raised $98 million and focused on the easier routing problem instead. The fact that this problem remains open despite all that capital is itself evidence of how painful the work is.

The question I keep returning to is: how did Arya Khokhar find this problem? The trail of evidence suggests a plausible but not definitive organic discovery. He was at Caltech studying CS and mathematics, did research at Stanford, published work on photoacoustic computed tomography using physics-aware neural operators -- which means he was actually in the weeds of medical imaging ML, not reading about it in TechCrunch. Then the ONCOVATE project: building an AI tool for cancer patient triage, piloted with Stanford Medicine. When you're trying to build a cancer triage model and you hit the wall of fragmented, unstandardized clinical data, the idea of building a harmonization layer emerges naturally. That's the kind of organic path I look for -- you start trying to solve problem A and discover that problem B is blocking you, and problem B turns out to be bigger than problem A. Dropbox happened because Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive. Eos may have happened because Arya kept discovering that you can't build reliable clinical AI when every hospital's data looks different. It's plausible. But I don't have the founder's own account of this origin story, and "plausible" is weaker than "obvious."

What concerns me most is the solo founder situation. The YC page lists only Arya Khokhar as founder, with a team size of 2. I've been explicit about this: co-founders are for a startup what location is for real estate. I forced Drew Houston to find one before accepting Dropbox. Healthcare enterprise sales with a 2-person team is already an extreme challenge -- you need someone simultaneously building the product, navigating 12-18 month procurement cycles, doing security reviews, and managing pilot deployments. When one person gets sick or burns out, the whole company stops. The exception I made for Houston was because his technical demonstration was so obviously strong that the organic problem-founder fit overrode the structural concern. Here, I don't have a public demo or GitHub repos to evaluate. The published research is a positive signal of technical depth, but I can't assess whether the actual Eos platform exists as working software or as an ambitious architecture diagram.

The bull case for this company is genuinely interesting. If the federated indexing approach works -- keeping data in place while building a searchable central index -- that solves the compliance problem that kills most centralization attempts. The timing is structural rather than hype-driven: CMS interoperability mandates and TEFCA are forcing health systems to open up data access, creating a narrow window where a newcomer can insert itself before Epic builds its own version. And this is precisely the kind of unglamorous infrastructure play I tend to prefer -- picks and shovels for clinical AI, not another chatbot. A company that makes every hospital's data ML-ready would be the foundation layer for thousands of clinical AI applications. That's a legitimate platform opportunity.

But the evidence of actual user pull is thin. The Stanford Medicine pilot was for ONCOVATE, the precursor project, not for Eos AI itself. The website claims metrics -- 3x administrative productivity, 37% revenue recovery, 18% model performance improvement -- but these aren't attributed to named customers or independently verified. At pre-seed I don't demand much traction, but I want at least one user who found the product organically and can't live without it. The TechCrunch Disrupt participation and Bill Gross competition are markers of ambition, not demand. Against competitors who collectively have raised over a billion dollars, a 2-person team needs some evidence that their specific approach is working where others haven't -- and that evidence isn't visible yet.

This is a company sitting in a genuine schlep blindness gap with a technically credentialed founder, but missing the evidence that would convert my intellectual interest into a check. The problem is real and hiding in plain sight. The founder's path to it is plausibly organic. The schlep creates natural competitive protection. But I don't see the working product, I don't see the co-founder, and I don't see the early users pulling the product toward them. If Arya showed me a working deployment at a real health system and had a co-founder who complemented his technical depth with healthcare operations experience, this evaluation would look very different.
