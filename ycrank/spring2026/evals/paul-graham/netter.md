# Netter -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Netter is a technically credible Paris team — Amin Hamzaoui at Mines Paris and ENS Ulm, two of the most selective filters France has — pointed at the most consensus problem space of 2026: a chat-driven data platform for mid-market companies, with Carrefour, Tesco and Auchan posted as logos on the homepage. The signature is upside-down for me: the highest dimension is the founders' apparent ability to land enterprise meetings, and the lowest is the idea itself, which sits squarely in the part of the Venn diagram where everyone agrees the idea is good. That's almost the opposite of how I want a pre-seed company to look.

The schlep test is where Netter fails hardest. The HIGH rung on this ladder is Stripe — Patrick Collison willingly walked into PCI compliance, bank integrations, and reams of regulatory paperwork that repelled every other developer; the LOW rung is the consultant team from my pass example, identifying a "massive opportunity in B2B workflow automation" through customer interviews. Netter is closer to the consultant rung than to Stripe. The competitive list in the dossier — Fivetran ($565M raised), Airbyte (550+ connectors, open source), Hex ($96M), Palantir Foundry ($2.8B revenue), Workato (~$100M ARR) — isn't a competitive landscape, it's a wall. "Data Science Autopilot for the real economy" is a phrase that could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece next Tuesday. Nobody is going to tell Samuel Ladroue this idea is crazy. That's the tell.

Founder Resourcefulness is where Netter scores best, and the ladder runs from Airbnb's cereal boxes at the top, through Replit's slow Hacker News-discovered grind in the middle, down to founders waiting on funding to make something happen. Netter sits in the lower-middle: getting Carrefour and Auchan on the homepage at pre-seed is not nothing — those are not logos a four-person Paris team lands by accident — but the dossier flags that we cannot tell whether these are paid deployments or unpaid pilots, and there are no anecdotes of scrappiness, no manual onboarding stories, no Drew-Houston-USB-drive moment. Hamzaoui's Mines + ENS pedigree gives me confidence Netter can build; Ladroue's pre-Netter operating history is opaque enough that I can't underwrite GTM execution.

Organic problem discovery is the second-weakest dimension. The HIGH rung is Dropbox — Drew Houston forgot his USB drive — and the LOW rung is, again, the McKinsey-style "we identified five verticals." Netter is closer to the latter: Hamzaoui is a student, Ladroue's prior life is unverified, and the public materials describe Netter's wedge across retail, logistics, healthcare, manufacturing, and real estate. Five verticals at a four-person company is a sign that nobody on the team lived in any of them. The nursing-home receivables story and the empty-trucks logistics story read like cases gathered from prospect calls, not frustrations the founders carried in from a previous job. Founder learning rate I'll mark at the default — no evidence of iteration cadence either way, but Hamzaoui's profile suggests slope. Earnest builder identity is muted; the homepage copy ("Data Science Autopilot," "~4 hours from concept to deployed application," "30 minutes to running systems") relies on borrowed framing rather than a Netter-specific point of view, and that lands closer to my pass-example consultants than to the Replit team's obvious obsession with their craft.

My 36-month forecast for Netter: raises a €8–15M Series A from a French or pan-European fund (Eurazeo, Serena, or Partech), reaches roughly €1–3M ARR concentrated in EU retail and logistics, gets squeezed between Palantir Foundry pushing downmarket and Airbyte/Fivetran bolting on LLM-driven build layers, and by 2029 either narrows to a single vertical — most likely retail loyalty, given the Carrefour/Tesco/Auchan logos — or is acquired by an ELT/iPaaS incumbent for a flat-to-modest multiple. The mechanism is straightforward: a non-schlep problem in a fashionable category attracts well-capitalized competitors faster than a four-person Paris team can build moat, and "120+ connectors" is not a moat when Airbyte ships 550+ for free.

The case against my pass deserves a real hearing. ClassDojo is the precedent — I broke my "hackers building for hackers" pattern there because the K-12 schlep was real and the founders were extraordinary, and one could argue EU mid-market sales is a comparable schlep that American competitors won't touch. If Hamzaoui's Mines + ENS technical bench builds a vertical ontology for European retail before Fivetran notices, Netter could become the European Foundry-for-mid-market. I don't buy it. ClassDojo's wedge was a sector VCs actively avoided; Netter is in the most-funded category of 2026, and the bull case requires both that the named logos are paid (unverified) and that an undifferentiated chat-on-data layer holds against incumbents who have hundreds of millions in capital and existing customer relationships. The verifiable condition that would flip me: a disclosed paid contract with one of the named retailers — Carrefour, Tesco, or Auchan — at ≥€200K ACV with a named buyer and seat count, paired with one founder having prior operating time inside a mid-market retailer or logistics operator. Until then, Netter is a competent team building what their frameworks suggested rather than what they lived through.
