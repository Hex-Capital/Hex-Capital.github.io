# Caretta -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Caretta is tagged on its own YC page as "SaaS, Sales, Sales Enablement, AI Assistant." I genuinely cannot think of a combination of four words more precisely calibrated to trigger every filter I've built over the past two years. This is a company whose entire value proposition is an AI capability -- real-time knowledge retrieval during sales calls -- built on commodity infrastructure (they wrap NVIDIA's Parakeet model for transcription, use standard RAG for retrieval, and sit on top of LLMs they don't control). Strip away the AI and there is no business. That's not a "cherry on top" -- that's a sundae made entirely of cherry. When I say "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" the answer here is that the business literally cannot exist without AI. It IS the AI layer. And that means every improvement in AI infrastructure that makes Caretta better also makes it easier for Gong, Salesforce, Microsoft, and a dozen other incumbents with actual distribution to build the same thing.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't hit my primary filter from either direction. Three TU Delft CS graduates whose most notable prior achievement is scaling a Discord bot to 30,000 users -- that's a fine technical credential for building software, but it doesn't confer specific credibility for understanding B2B sales workflows, enterprise procurement, or revenue team dynamics. The "sane" half of my equation requires that the founder's background makes you believe they uniquely understand the problem space. Nothing in this team's history suggests they've lived inside a sales organization, run a revenue team, or experienced the pain they're solving. And the "insane" half? Real-time AI assistance during sales calls is not an insane idea. Salesken has $41M raised doing exactly this. Balto has $57M. Cresta has $276M. Gong just raised $250M in Series F and has every incentive and resource to add real-time features to their platform that already has thousands of enterprise customers and years of accumulated sales conversation data. This is a consensus play in a hot market with well-funded incumbents -- the opposite of what I look for.

The moat story centers on a "self-improving knowledge base" -- essentially, the system gets smarter about each customer's sales context with every call it processes. I've heard this pitch a hundred times. Customer-specific RAG with accumulated transcripts creates some switching cost, but it's the weakest form of data moat because the data is generated by the customer's own activity, not by network effects across customers. Each customer's knowledge base is siloed. There's no cross-customer compounding, no protocol position, no physical infrastructure. Compare this to my investment in Craftwork: if you want to compete with Craftwork, you need to recruit, train, and manage painting crews across markets. If you want to compete with Caretta, you need an API key to an LLM and a speech-to-text model. The structural moat asymmetry is profound.

The strongest bull case I can construct: real-time inference during live conversation is genuinely harder than post-call analysis, and if Caretta solves the latency problem elegantly enough, they could build a wedge before incumbents retool. The data flywheel could theoretically evolve from "customer-specific knowledge base" into something resembling an "organizational sales intelligence primitive" -- a layer that CRMs and other sales tools integrate with rather than compete against. If you squint at it through an Airtable lens (which I backed early despite it looking like "just SaaS"), there's a version where Caretta becomes the knowledge layer underneath all sales workflows. But Airtable was genuinely creating a new primitive for non-technical database creation -- a category that didn't exist. Caretta is building an application in a category where Gong has $332 million in revenue and four years of head start on the exact same underlying data asset. The analogy breaks down precisely where it matters most.

The enterprise sales cycle problem compounds everything. A four-person team selling to B2B revenue organizations -- the buyers with the longest procurement cycles, the most security reviews, and the strongest incumbent relationships in enterprise software. Gong's sales team has been building these relationships for years. Caretta needs champions inside these organizations willing to bet their deal pipeline on a pre-revenue startup with no track record. That's not impossible, but it requires the kind of founder charisma and sales DNA that I don't see evidence of in a team of Dutch CS graduates whose prior product (VoysAI) pivoted into this without visible traction under either name.

I keep my money in my pocket here. This is a well-executed technical product in exactly the category I believe AI is about to commoditize out of existence. The subscription model is the business model. The AI is the moat. The idea is consensus. Every dollar of improvement in foundation models makes it easier for someone with actual distribution to replicate this. I'm looking for businesses that get stronger as software gets cheaper -- Caretta gets more vulnerable.
