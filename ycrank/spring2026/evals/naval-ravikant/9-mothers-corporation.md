# 9 Mothers -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this is a consensus bet dressed in hardware clothing. Counter-drone is the single hottest category in defense technology right now. Anduril just landed a $20B Army framework contract. DroneShield grew revenue 276% year-over-year to A$216 million. The DoD is pouring $49.9 billion into defense tech procurement. Everyone -- every analyst, every defense procurement officer, every LP allocating to defense funds -- agrees that counter-UAS matters. You don't get paid for agreeing with the crowd. You get paid for being right when the crowd is wrong. The crowd isn't wrong here; they're just early. And "early consensus" is still consensus. The asymmetric upside that comes from non-consensus timing is absent. When twenty well-funded companies are racing into the same market, pricing reflects the optimism.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Russell Smith spent a decade building Rainforest QA -- a no-code testing platform. His documented skills are Ruby, AWS, DevOps, CI/CD. Roman Khomenko spent eight years at the same company doing computer vision for screenshot segmentation and QA automation ML. Bogdan Pyzh was a cybersecurity consultant. These are competent software people. But building an autonomous weapon system that integrates acoustic ML, real-time tracking, hardware actuators, and military-grade reliability is a fundamentally different domain. The tell is in the job postings: they're hiring for Senior Applied Audio ML Engineers, DSP Engineers, acoustic systems specialists. The founders don't possess the core technical knowledge their product requires -- they're recruiting for it. When I backed Evan Williams at Twitter, he'd spent years on Blogger understanding public communication at a cellular level. When I backed the Replit team, Amjad Masad had been building coding tools obsessively. Here, the founders identified a market opportunity and are assembling a team to pursue it. That's the difference between specific knowledge and market analysis.

The one genuinely interesting signal is Khomenko's Ukrainian background -- educated at Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics, a city that has experienced drone warfare firsthand. There's potential lived understanding of the threat environment that can't be acquired from reading defense reports. And his ML production experience, while in a different domain, provides real engineering foundations. But "radioelectronics" in a university name and proximity to conflict don't substitute for years of building acoustic sensing systems, defense hardware, or autonomous weapons. The gap between QA automation and autonomous weapon systems is vast, and hiring senior engineers to fill that gap means the specific knowledge lives in the employees, not the founders. That's a fragile foundation.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If you believe compute-at-the-edge has crossed a threshold where fully autonomous, portable counter-drone systems are newly feasible at low cost -- and I do think that's plausible -- then there's a genuine gap at the tactical edge that defense primes won't fill. Raytheon and Lockheed are optimized for large platform sales; a $50K portable system cannibalizes their business model. Anduril is building the integrated platform, not the handheld device. The soldier who needs a man-portable counter-drone system today has limited options. If 9 Mothers can build EDDA and get it into field testing, the procurement dollars are waiting. The acoustic-sensing approach offers passive detection without emitting RF signatures -- a real tactical advantage. And the data flywheel from deployed systems could compound detection accuracy over time. My ePlane Company investment shows I'll make small hardware bets outside my comfort zone when the physics-enabled disruption thesis is compelling. But ePlane was a $1M option on electric aviation physics. The question here is whether the founders can execute in a domain where they have no prior operating history, against competitors with billions in capital and established government relationships.

The leverage architecture concerns me structurally. This is hardware sold to government through procurement contracts. Each EDDA unit requires manufacturing, deployment, maintenance, and support. Revenue scales roughly linearly with units produced and contracts won. There's no network effect, no marketplace dynamic, no protocol-layer leverage. The AI component adds some software-like scalability -- models trained on field data can improve across all deployed units -- but the core business remains physical goods sold through twelve-to-thirty-six-month procurement cycles. That's not the kind of leverage I look for. It's closer to Opendoor than to Notion. Defense hardware companies can build valuable businesses, but they don't create the nonlinear output-per-input ratio that generates asymmetric returns at the angel stage.

The long co-working relationship between Smith and Khomenko -- eight years at the same company -- is a genuine positive. I weight founder relationships heavily, and a decade of shared professional history means they know each other's strengths and fault lines under pressure. Smith's willingness to go back to YC as a second-time founder, in an entirely different domain, shows either exceptional adaptability or insufficient attachment to a specific problem. I'm inclined toward the latter interpretation. The best founders I've backed couldn't NOT work on their problem. They'd been thinking about it for years before starting the company. Here, the founders appear to have identified an opportunity and pivoted into it. That's market analysis, not obsession.
