# Lexius -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me immediately: Veesion has raised fifty-three million euros. Everseen has ninety million. Spot AI has ninety-three million. ArcadianAI literally uses the same tagline -- "AI Security Guards." When I look at a company, one of the first things I ask myself is whether this feels unpopular and hard to assess, because in my experience those are the investments that turn into something extraordinary. Lexius is entering a space where the consensus has already formed. Institutional money has poured into AI-powered retail loss prevention from multiple directions. That's not the signal I'm trained to follow. When everybody already sees the opportunity, the price of being right goes up and the odds of being the winner go down.

I want to hear the founder's story before anything else -- not the TAM, not the case studies, not the competitive positioning. David Elskamp founded a media agency at fourteen with Adidas and Heineken as clients, which is genuinely impressive hustle. Liam Webster grew up in a small town on the Oregon coast, went through community college, and worked his way into Berkeley EECS, which tells me something about persistence and resourcefulness. But when I search for the thread that connects these founders to *this specific problem* -- why retail theft, why security cameras, why shoplifting detection -- I come up short. There's no story I can find about a family store that was stolen blind, or a security guard job that showed them how broken the system was, or a personal experience with the consequences of shrinkage in their community. The founding looks like a smart market read, not a biographical imperative. That doesn't mean they can't build a great company. But it means I'm relying on execution signals rather than the kind of deep founder-problem fusion that gives me conviction before the data arrives.

The bull case here is real and I want to be honest about it. Landing 7-Eleven, Erewhon, and Prada as named customers with a three-person pre-seed team is not nothing -- that's extraordinary distribution velocity. The FineFare case study showing sixty-three percent theft reduction in thirty days, Erewhon reporting three thousand dollars in monthly savings per store -- those are concrete, measurable outcomes that suggest the product genuinely works. The software-only approach with sub-ten-minute remote installation is a legitimate distribution wedge; it means a retailer can pilot this during a single meeting. And the cross-store offender database has the bones of a real network effect -- every store that joins makes the system more valuable for every other store. If I were evaluating this purely on traction-to-stage ratio, I'd be more excited. The team has clearly built something that retailers want.

What I do recognize from my own portfolio pattern is the "making invisible systems visible" quality. Flock Safety took neighborhood crime data that was opaque and made it actionable. Carta took cap table information trapped in spreadsheets and made it transparent. Lexius takes the massive volume of unwatched security footage -- cameras that exist everywhere but that nobody has enough eyes to monitor -- and makes the theft patterns visible in real time. The privacy-first approach of analyzing behavior rather than faces is a smart regulatory positioning choice, and Liam's Berkeley research on AI and privacy suggests this isn't just marketing but a genuine technical commitment. That resonates with me. I also appreciate that they're navigating the EU AI Act landscape and U.S. biometric privacy laws -- regulatory complexity can become a moat if you're thoughtful about it early.

But I keep returning to the core problem for my framework: this is a competitive, well-funded, consensus category. The founders are capable but young, without the kind of biographical stake in the problem that makes me believe they'll persist through the inevitable brutal stretch when a competitor with ninety million dollars in the bank undercuts them on pricing or out-hires them on sales. The path from here requires winning a distribution race against better-capitalized opponents, and while their product and install speed give them a wedge, that's fundamentally an execution bet in a known market -- not the kind of non-obvious, "everyone thinks this is crazy" conviction bet that defines my portfolio. I'd be watching this company with genuine interest, but the check doesn't come from me at this stage.
