# Replicas -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what stops me cold: this company is building a thin orchestration layer on top of other people's AI agents, in a space where Devin has raised $900 million, Factory and Augment Code have hundreds of millions more, and OpenAI's Codex already runs natively in a cloud sandbox. The core product — spin up a VM, clone a repo, execute a CLI agent — is commodity infrastructure. Open-source alternatives already exist. Every major AI lab could build this as a feature, not a company. When I look at the competitive landscape and ask myself whether a solo student founder with no prior founding experience can outrun organizations with a thousand times more capital and established enterprise customer bases, I don't see the structural advantage that would make that possible. This isn't a space that's being avoided — it's a space being stampeded into by every well-funded player in technology.

When I ask my first question — tell me Connor's story, not the business — I get: University of Waterloo CS student, eight months on the core team at Helicone, hackathon winner, built some side projects. That's a talented young builder. But it's professional adjacency to the problem, not biographical inevitability. He worked at an AI infrastructure company and saw an adjacent opportunity. I've described this exact pattern before as the kind of founding story that doesn't activate my conviction — "I was at [company] and saw the opportunity" reads as career optimization, not a life that was shaped by forces that made this problem impossible to walk away from. I don't see the forge here. I don't see the survival story, the personal encounter with a broken system, the biographical thread that makes Connor the one person on earth who must build this. That absence matters more to me than it does to most investors, because I believe the forge is what carries a founder through the valley of death — and in a space this competitive, the valley will be deep.

There's no invisible system being made visible here. The "problem" is that running AI coding agents locally is inconvenient — it ties up your machine, requires environment setup. That's friction, not extraction. Nobody is being exploited by the current arrangement. There's no hidden tax, no information asymmetry, no opaque structure that profits from people's ignorance. When I backed Affirm, credit card companies were burying real costs in fine print that consumers couldn't decode. When I backed Ownwell, property tax systems were systematically over-assessing homeowners who lacked the resources to appeal. Replicas is solving a workflow annoyance for engineering teams at funded startups — people who already have power, resources, and information. The problem is real, but it's not the kind of problem that activates my deepest conviction.

The strongest bull case I can construct: we're at a genuine inflection point where multiple CLI-based coding agents just became capable enough to run autonomously, and neither Anthropic nor OpenAI has incentive to build a neutral multi-agent platform that showcases competitors' models. If that structural opening holds, and if Replicas becomes the standard dispatch layer, the Slack and Linear and GitHub integrations could create real workflow stickiness. The Helicone experience is genuinely relevant — Connor built LLM request routing and observability infrastructure, which is directly adjacent to agent orchestration. And there's a version of this story where Replicas is to coding agents what Helicone is to LLM observability: the neutral infrastructure layer that all the agents pass through. If the agent landscape stays fragmented — three or four competing models, none dominant — the multi-agent orchestration thesis has legs. But that "if" is load-bearing. If one agent wins (and the funding dynamics suggest consolidation), the neutral-platform value proposition collapses. And even in the fragmented scenario, those Slack and GitHub integrations are weeks of engineering work for any competitor, not years. The switching costs are thin.

What I keep coming back to is the absence of what I look for in my highest-conviction bets. No biographical forge connecting the founder to the problem at a level deeper than professional experience. No contrarian conviction — AI developer tools in 2026 is the most consensus-attractive space imaginable, the opposite of defense tech or male birth control or Bitcoin mining with flared gas. No invisible system being disrupted. No structural moat that compounds with scale. Connor is clearly a capable builder, and the timing insight about multi-agent orchestration isn't wrong. But capable builder plus correct timing insight, in a space with billions of competing capital and no defensibility beyond execution speed — that's not a Long Journey bet. I wish Connor well, and I'll be watching to see if the forge develops as the competitive heat rises, but I can't write a check here based on what I see today.
