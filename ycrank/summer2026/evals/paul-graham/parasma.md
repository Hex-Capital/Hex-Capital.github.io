# Parasma -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Sean Cole made 200,000 human neurons play Doom in roughly one week using Cortical Labs' CL1, and Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, Popular Science, Interesting Engineering, and ZME Science all wrote it up before he ever filed a YC application. That is the dimensional signature of Parasma: an unusually high pre-funding artifact paired with an unusually weak co-founder configuration. Cole is one person who shipped something the internet wanted to look at, and that is the entire company today.

Drew Houston's pre-seed **Dropbox** (2007) demo video on Hacker News is the right rung to anchor Parasma against on animal resourcefulness, because the mechanism is identical -- founder ships a working artifact, the artifact does the marketing, the institutional check arrives after the demo is already viral. Parasma's Doom-on-neurons video is plausibly the strongest pre-funding artifact in the S26 batch in raw press-pickup terms, and Cole did it in a week with no prior biocomputing background per ZME Science, which is the animal tell -- the man stayed up nights until the neurons learned to shoot. Where Parasma diverges from the Houston rung is that Houston's demo solved a problem Houston himself suffered (lost USB stick); Cole's demo solved a problem nobody had, which is a different category of resourcefulness, closer to a hacker stunt than to product-market resonance. I still score this dimension high. Animals leave tracks, and Parasma has tracks in six publications.

The LOW rung on idea-organic-to-founder-pain is the polished AI-wrapper team from my own Pass Example -- founders who spotted a market opportunity rather than scratched their own itch. Parasma is not that pattern exactly, but it is adjacent in a way that matters: ZME Science explicitly notes Cole had "little experience in biological computing" before this project, and the YC page frames the company around "AI compute" energy economics rather than around any problem Cole personally hit in his own workflow. Cole did not lose his USB stick. Cole found a cool platform (Cortical Cloud) and made it do something photogenic. That is curiosity-driven idea-generation, which is honorable but is not the **Dropbox** (2007) / **Stripe** (2010) organic-pain pattern, and "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) is fairly explicit that the strongest ideas come from founders who wanted the thing for themselves first.

On schlep blindness: the mechanism that makes biocomputing a real schlep zone is that the wetware demands wet labs, cell culture protocols, and stimulation-protocol tuning that no SaaS founder voluntarily picks up -- this is the **Coinbase** (2012) compliance grind translated into Petri dishes. Over 18 months this compounds for Parasma only if Cole personally accumulates proprietary stimulation/training-protocol data across many tasks; if he stays a thin algorithm layer on top of Cortical Cloud, the schlep is being absorbed by Cortical Labs, not by Parasma, and the moat goes to the wetware vendor. The dossier's commoditization-risk note that Cortical Labs already ships the Python interface Cole used is exactly this failure mode pre-articulated.

Hacker genealogy: Cole's GitHub repo "doom-neuron" and his MS in AI from Sussex would have to be paired with an open-source footprint older than the Doom stunt for this to clear the Patrick-Collison-Young-Scientist-2005 bar -- which it doesn't, since the dossier surfaces no prior shipped artifact before the neurons project. The score flips a tier upward only if Cole's GitHub history shows a multi-year hacker trail predating Sussex; the dossier doesn't tell me that, so I sit one notch below the Collison rung. The Doom demo itself is hacker culture in its purest form (the canonical "does it run Doom" meme), and that pulls the score up from the absent-evidence default.

Compare Parasma's co-founder configuration to **Loopt** (2005) -- Sam Altman was effectively the load-bearing founder, and I funded it anyway because Altman's animal-test result overrode the structural preference. Parasma is solo (YC page, team size 1), and Cole has no disclosed cofounder pipeline, no advisor list, no wet-lab partner beyond renting Cortical Cloud. "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) was unambiguous that the big successes have two or three; the Loopt exception exists but it cost me. Parasma needs a biology cofounder by Demo Day or this dimension stays a structural drag.

Forecast, 36 months: Parasma is acquihired by Cortical Labs or FinalSpark for $3-8M as an algorithm-layer talent grab, or Cole pivots to a research-services / consulting model billing universities and DARPA O-CIRCUIT-adjacent grants. The mechanism is the high/low signature -- the demo got the press, the solo configuration plus vendor dependency means the value accrues to whoever owns the wetware substrate, and absent a biology cofounder Parasma cannot extend downward into hardware. This is not the **Airbnb** (2009) shape. This is the **Kiko** (2005) shape -- talented founder, photogenic artifact, eBay-sized outcome.

The strongest case against my forecast is the **Coinbase** (2012) parallel: Brian Armstrong was effectively solo when he hand-built the Android Bitcoin wallet that rescued his rejected application, and the schlep he was running toward (crypto compliance) looked unattractive to every other founder in the batch. Parasma's biocomputing schlep is structurally similar -- mainstream AI-infra founders are routing around wetware because it's gross and slow, and Cole is the one S26 applicant who walked toward it with a working demo. If Cole recruits a biology cofounder in the first six months and Cortical Labs fails to ship its own training-algorithm layer by mid-2027, Parasma compounds into the algorithm-default for the category. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway is that Cortical Cloud's Python interface already exists -- the platform is one engineer-quarter away from making Parasma redundant, and Armstrong owned his stack while Cole rents his. The flip condition is clean: if Cole announces a technical cofounder with wet-lab credentials before Demo Day, I write the check.
