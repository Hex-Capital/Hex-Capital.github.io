# Asendia AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Asendia AI sells voice AI screening tools to staffing agencies — the very entities that sit at the center of a $600 billion industry built on information asymmetry between employers, candidates, and temp workers who often don't understand the margin structure extracting value from their labor. When I look at staffing, I see one of the most opaque systems in the American economy: agencies marking up workers 30-50%, candidates with no visibility into rates or processes, fragmented information deliberately kept fragmented because the fragmentation IS the business model. This is exactly the kind of hidden, extractive structure I've spent my career backing companies to dismantle — Affirm against credit card opacity, Ownwell against property tax systems, Carta against cap table mystery. But Asendia AI isn't dismantling the staffing system. Asendia AI is selling efficiency tools to the staffing agencies themselves, making the gatekeepers faster without shifting any power to the candidates on the other side of those phone screens. The opacity stays intact. The margin structure stays hidden. The AI just processes more people through it per hour.

Rihab Lajmi left Tunisia at 18, built a career at Microsoft Munich and Google Cloud in Germany, and walked away from Google at 27 to start this company. Badis Zormati made the same leap from Tunisia at the same age, earned an EE from TU Munich, and spent six years building AI systems at Infineon. Both founders are immigrants who rebuilt their lives in a foreign country — I recognize what that takes, because resourcefulness under constraint is something I understand from the inside. Rihab becoming the first Tunisian woman admitted to Y Combinator is a genuine milestone. She conducted 35+ recruiter interviews and went through dozens of hiring processes herself at Google, Microsoft, and Amazon. That's real research, real immersion. But when I ask my first question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I hear is a cloud architect who researched the recruiting problem thoroughly, not someone whose biography made this problem inescapable. Rihab wasn't a temp worker navigating the staffing system's opacity. She wasn't a recruiter ground down by the industry's dysfunction. She saw a market opportunity from her technical vantage point. That's competence, not inevitability.

Asendia AI operates in what is, candidly, one of the most consensus-attractive spaces in AI right now. Paradox raised $253M before Workday acquired it in August 2025. HireVue pulls in $89M in annual revenue. Humanly just closed a $25M Series B. Sense HQ hit a $500M valuation. Fetcher raised $41M. No sophisticated VC shifts in their chair when you pitch "AI for recruiting" — they lean forward and ask about your wedge. Asendia AI's wedge is staffing-agency-specific voice screening, which is a real differentiator against Paradox's text-based chatbot and HireVue's asynchronous video, but it's a product feature, not a structural moat. The dossier itself states the quiet part clearly: "Voice AI screening agents can be built on top of commodity LLMs and telephony APIs. The core technical components — speech-to-text, LLM-based evaluation, text-to-speech — are widely available." When Paradox's enterprise customers at Workday don't need standalone screening tools, and Humanly is processing 9,000 interviews per day with $52M behind it, Asendia AI's 3-person team and ~10 paying clients face a capital asymmetry that product features alone won't overcome.

The couple co-founder dynamic at Asendia AI directly invokes my most painful investment lesson. A Tunisian press article refers to Rihab and Badis as "ce jeune couple tunisien" — they are romantic partners as well as co-founders. I broke my own rule once on co-founder dynamics with HQ Trivia, where Colin Kroll and Rus Yusupov had no clear CEO delineation, and it destroyed a company that had the right product at the right time. Romantic couples as co-founders amplify every vector of that risk: professional disagreements become personal betrayals, personal friction becomes professional paralysis, and if the relationship fails, the company dies regardless of product-market fit. Asendia AI does appear to have clear role delineation — Rihab as CEO, Badis as CTO — which mitigates somewhat. But this remains a structural risk I've been burned on before, and I take it seriously.

The bull case an advocate would make is real and I want to name it honestly. Asendia AI has ~10 paying clients running thousands of automated screens in healthcare, IT, and blue-collar verticals across US and European markets — that's genuine B2B traction for a pre-seed company that pivoted recently. The Workday acquisition of Paradox creates a legitimate structural opening: Workday will integrate Olivia into its enterprise HCM suite and likely deprioritize standalone staffing agency customers who don't use Workday's core platform. Asendia AI's 50+ ATS integrations including Bullhorn (the dominant staffing ATS) and SOC 2/GDPR/HIPAA certifications represent meaningful execution from a 3-person team. The pivot from B2C ("FAANG") to B2B shows founders who listen to the market and adapt — the same adaptability I prize in founders who survive near-death moments. If Asendia AI captures the staffing-agency-specific niche that Workday's acquisition leaves underserved, they could build real switching costs through integration depth and compliance infrastructure. This is a legitimate execution story in a large market. But none of my Known Exceptions apply here — this isn't a narrow cultural timing window (RTFKT), it isn't "magically weird" in a genre-defying way (Superplastic), and it isn't a transformative product moment that overrides governance concerns (HQ Trivia). It's a competent team in a consensus space selling automation to existing power structures. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Asendia AI built a candidate-facing transparency layer — showing temp workers the agency markup, the employer's actual rate, the placement economics — THAT would be an invisible system made visible, and I'd want to talk to Rihab about what forged that conviction.

Asendia AI scores low on the two dimensions that drive my highest-conviction bets. Contrarian conviction: AI recruiting is the opposite of stigmatized or uncomfortable — it's a space where competitors have raised hundreds of millions and where the idea provokes enthusiasm, not discomfort. Invisible system disruption: the staffing industry's opacity is real, but Asendia AI automates within it rather than exposing it. The founders have genuine biographical resilience — leaving Tunisia at 18 is real forge — but the connection to this specific problem is professional, not inevitable. Defensibility through ATS integrations and compliance certifications provides moderate switching costs, but the commodity nature of the underlying technology limits how much infrastructure moat can compound. Asendia AI is a well-executed company in a real market, but it is not a Long Journey bet.
