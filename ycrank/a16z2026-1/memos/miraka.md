## The Idea

**Problem:** Cardiac patients face high rates of hospital readmission after discharge. The industry standard captures only ~10% of eligible patients for post-discharge cardiac care (company description, a16z Speedrun portal). Existing cardiac rehabilitation is primarily clinic-based, creating access and compliance barriers. The Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program (HRRP) penalizes hospitals financially for excess readmissions, creating institutional demand for solutions (CMS policy).

**Approach:** Miraka provides an always-on cardiac care team delivering clinician-led preventative care informed by continuous patient data. The platform integrates health records and wearable data to build predictive risk models that identify emerging cardiac risk, enabling clinical intervention before emergencies occur (company description, a16z Speedrun portal). All services are covered by insurance (company description). [Inferred]: The model likely uses remote patient monitoring (RPM) billing codes under CMS guidelines, given the insurance-covered structure and billable appointments metric.

**Differentiation:** Unlike technology-only RPM platforms (e.g., Cadence, Health Recovery Solutions) that provide monitoring tools licensed to health systems, Miraka operates as the care team itself — combining AI-driven risk prediction with direct clinician intervention. Cadence uses a B2B SaaS model licensing its platform to health systems (Cadence website). Health Recovery Solutions provides telehealth tablets and monitoring technology (HRS website). Miraka claims >90% readmission reduction vs. industry benchmarks of 8–28% readmission rates for RPM-equipped patients (AJMC study on HRS) and 70% reduction claimed by Biofourmis (Biofourmis website). [Inferred]: The direct care delivery model (vs. SaaS licensing) may create higher margins per patient but requires clinical staffing.

**Business Model:** Insurance-reimbursed clinical services with billable appointments (company description). The CMS 2026 Physician Fee Schedule lowered RPM billing thresholds, making device supply and data transmission billable for 2–15 day or 16–30 day periods within a 30-day cycle, down from the previous 16+ day minimum (search snippet, industry sources). $300K annual run rate with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments (company description, a16z Speedrun portal). [Inferred]: Revenue is driven by per-patient fee-for-service billing to insurance rather than software licensing.

## Defensibility

- **Data advantage (developing):** Continuous patient monitoring generates proprietary clinical datasets combining EHR and wearable data, which can improve predictive model accuracy over time (company description). This advantage is nascent.
- **Clinical outcomes as switching cost:** If the >90% readmission reduction claim (company description) holds under scrutiny, partner hospitals face risk in switching providers once clinical workflows are integrated.
- **Regulatory complexity:** Operating as a clinical care provider (vs. pure software) requires compliance with healthcare regulations, credentialing, and payer contracts, creating barriers to entry for technology-only competitors.

**Market structure:** Large incumbent health systems and payers (e.g., UnitedHealth's Optum) could build in-house cardiac monitoring. However, [Inferred]: hospital systems typically outsource specialized post-discharge programs rather than staffing them internally, due to the operational complexity and the gap between acute-care and outpatient incentive structures. RPM vendors like Cadence sell tools but do not provide clinical staff, creating a structural gap Miraka occupies.

**Commoditization risk:** RPM technology is increasingly commoditized — Cadence, HRS, Biofourmis, and others offer monitoring platforms with hundreds of device integrations. [Inferred]: Miraka's differentiation rests on combining technology with clinical delivery, which is harder to replicate but also harder to scale. Other clinical-service RPM models could emerge.

## Key Risks

**Clinical outcome validation:** The >90% readmission reduction and 94% patient capture rate claims (company description) are self-reported without peer-reviewed validation or third-party audit. Hospital partners will likely require clinical evidence before expanding contracts. Mitigation: early results at NewYork-Presbyterian (LinkedIn, Feb 2026) may provide third-party data.

**Scaling clinical operations:** Unlike SaaS competitors, Miraka must recruit, credential, and manage clinical staff alongside technology development. This creates linear cost scaling and potential talent bottlenecks in a healthcare labor shortage. No public data on clinical team size or staffing model found.

**Payer reimbursement dependency:** Revenue depends on insurance reimbursement under RPM and related CMS billing codes. CMS billing rules change annually; adverse changes to RPM thresholds or reimbursement rates could directly impact unit economics. The CMS 2026 fee schedule was favorable (lowered thresholds), but future rule changes are uncertain.

**Pivot history and naming:** The team previously operated as Overwatch Health (2021–2022, Princeton eLab) and Nolan Abeyta's LinkedIn currently lists "Paralign" as his company title (LinkedIn). The relationship between Overwatch Health, Paralign, and Miraka is unclear from public sources. Multiple name changes may indicate strategic pivots or could create confusion in hospital procurement processes.

**Competitive pressure from funded incumbents:** Cadence ($144M raised, $1B valuation per Fierce Healthcare) and HRS (~$85.5M raised) have significant capital advantages and existing hospital relationships in the same target market.
