# Ekpa -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ekpa is two MIT undergrads — Yuga Patel ("CS/AI + Econ @ MIT") and Anant Asthana ("Student - Massachusetts Institute of Technology") — building "autonomous research agents for trading," and I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but neither is Ekpa: the game Ekpa *appears* to be playing is the most consensus YC Summer 2026 sentence I can imagine, and the game Ekpa is *actually* playing is the same one. The dimensional signature here is a company where the highest score is a weak, incidental brush with regulated finance and the lowest is the "magically weird" test — which is to say, Ekpa is a pedigree-analytical AI-agent wrapper wearing a fintech disclaimer, the exact shape of my Pass Example.

Palmer Luckey shipping VR from his parents' garage before Anduril is the load-bearing analogy for founder-lived-experience, because Luckey had already *touched* the object and the community around it long before the pitch — his biography put him in front of defense hardware, not a memo. Ekpa's dossier gives me Patel's coursework line and Asthana's blog post "Autonomous AI Agents and Financial Trading: What's Real Today vs What's Hype" — a *survey* of the space, which is the analytical mode I flag, not the autobiographical one. No hedge-fund seat, no prop-desk seat, no father-was-a-broker moment, no personal blow-up account that made Ekpa necessary. The divergence from the Luckey rung is total: Ekpa's founders discovered the problem the way most 2026 MIT juniors discover problems, by reading the discourse about them.

The Pass Example I wrote for exactly this shape — "two ex-FAANG PMs, both Stanford CS, $5M pipeline pre-launch, deck opens with a $200B TAM slide" — is the failure mechanism I want to trace onto Ekpa. That template dies because the moat is the model, not the founder, and a polymath generalist rebuilds it in a quarter. Ekpa's public artifact is a waitlist page, its differentiation from Composer (rule-based drag-and-drop), Numerai ($21.5M raised, institutional), AlphaSense ($350M at $7.5B, enterprise), and LinqAlpha ($22M Series A, 70+ hedge-fund customers) is [Inferred] in the dossier itself, and the incumbents most likely to eat Ekpa are Public.com and Robinhood shipping the same LLM summary natively into an app 30 million retail accounts already use. That is not a Crusoe. That is the darling I pass on before slide 3.

Ekpa's regulated-swing dimension is the one place I lean forward slightly, and the mechanism to test is how it compounds over 18 months: "share count recommendations" tied to user holdings sits inches from the Investment Advisers Act line, and the site disclaimer that Ekpa is "not a broker-dealer or investment adviser" is a legal position, not a moat. In the Mindbloom or Vigil Labs mold, regulatory friction is the wall competitors can't climb; in Ekpa's mold, regulatory friction is the wall Ekpa's own founders will hit first without a securities lawyer on the cap table. Compounding negatively, not positively.

Ekpa's co-founder governance is the counterfactual dimension: the dossier names two people, no CEO, no tiebreaker, no equity split. For this to flip a tier I would need one of Patel or Asthana explicitly named as CEO with majority operating authority and a documented vesting protocol — the structural inverse of the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll feud I've cited a hundred times. Without it, Ekpa gets my absent-evidence default and I read the silence as "they haven't had the conversation," which at a 2-person MIT-dorm stage is a coin flip on which semester the friendship ends.

Ekpa's physical-world verifiability compares directly to Niantic on a single axis and loses: I played Ingress in 2012 for three years before Hanke took my meeting, meaning the artifact was in my hand and the addiction was in my body before the check. Ekpa is pre-launch, waitlist-only, no Product Hunt, no app-store presence, no Discord — a normal human cannot install, ride, scan, or photograph anything Ekpa has built. The website even contradicts itself between "portfolio aggregation dashboard" and "hold S&P 500 above long-term trend, cash below," which tells me Ekpa hasn't decided what the artifact is yet, let alone shipped one.

My 36-month forecast on Ekpa is that Ekpa is either dead or acqui-hired by a brokerage-adjacent aggregator (SnapTrade, Plaid Investments, or a second-tier broker like Public.com) for under $10M in equity-value, with sub-$500K ARR at the acquisition moment, because the two mechanisms killing Ekpa are incumbent-native AI features shipping into apps with existing retail distribution and the FINRA/SEC posture on personalized AI recommendations tightening through 2027. That forecast is consistent with the signature — a consensus-category AI wrapper with no lived-experience anchor cannot outlast the incumbents' quarterly release cycles.

The strongest bull case on Ekpa borrows the DeepMind exception: elite-credential young founders whose polymath compounding across CS, econ, and finance-adjacent writing (Asthana's blog) prefigures the "age of the polymath" I've named as the reweighting force for AI-era founders, and AlphaSense's $7.5B July 2026 mark proves the market pays for AI-over-filings. That case stings because I *have* written the DeepMind-shaped check before. It loses on the dossier: Hassabis was already a chess prodigy, neuroscientist, and shipped-game-designer when I met that deal — cross-domain artifacts, plural, already built. Ekpa's Asthana has one blog post and Patel has a course load. The verifiable condition that flips me: Ekpa ships a working read-only dashboard I can connect my own Fidelity account to within 90 days AND names one of the two founders as CEO with a public equity split. Absent both, Ekpa is the pass I wrote the template for.
