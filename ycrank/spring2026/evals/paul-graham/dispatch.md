# Dispatch -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me from this dossier isn't the founders or the idea — it's the competitive landscape. Seven funded companies building reentry vehicles. Varda already at five successful missions with $329M behind them. Reditus Space, another YC company one batch ahead, doing the exact same "Microgravity-as-a-Service" play. When I wrote about Black Swan Farming, the whole point was that the best investments look wrong to most people. When seven companies are all chasing the same thesis, it's stopped being contrarian. The in-space manufacturing return-vehicle idea has moved from the "seems bad, is good" quadrant into the "seems good, everyone knows it" quadrant — which is the worst quadrant for returns. That doesn't mean the market isn't real. It means the returns have already started getting competed away.

Now, the founders. Case and Mello both come from Astranis, which is the right kind of origin. When you're building spacecraft for a living and you decide the real bottleneck is getting payloads back from orbit, that smells like organic problem discovery rather than trend-surfing. Case specifically did thermal engineering on the Lunar Gateway program, then oversaw spacecraft architecture at Astranis. Mello led avionics there. Between them, they cover the two hardest disciplines in reentry vehicle design — thermal protection and guidance systems. This is the kind of technical founder-market fit that actually means something. You don't wake up one morning and decide to build a heat shield because you read a market report. You build one because you spent years watching payloads go up with no good way to come down, and that gnawed at you until you couldn't ignore it anymore.

The heat shield story is the single strongest signal in the entire dossier. They built a full-scale reentry heat shield in an apartment and tested it in the Mojave using rocket exhaust to simulate Mach 20+ conditions, claiming it cost 100x less than competitors. That's the kind of thing that makes me lean forward. It reminds me of what I look for in YC interviews — not the polished pitch, but the story about the thing they built that they shouldn't have been able to build. An apartment heat shield is absurd in exactly the right way. It says these two have the scrappy engineering instinct that aerospace companies normally beat out of people with process and compliance reviews. The 100x cost claim is unverified, and extraordinary claims do need scrutiny, but the fact that they built and tested hardware before raising real money — that's the right sequence. Build first, raise second.

Here's what worries me though. The gap between a ground test with rocket exhaust and actual atmospheric reentry at Mach 20 is not a gap — it's a chasm. Inversion Space had $125M and their first orbital attempt still failed from a propulsion short circuit. Space Forge has $40M+ and hasn't demonstrated reentry yet. The path from "tested a heat shield in the desert" to "returning payloads from orbit" requires $50-100M in capital, successful orbital integration, guidance and navigation systems, recovery operations, regulatory clearances, and the kind of systems-level reliability that only comes from iterative flight testing. Two people with ~$500K are standing at the base of a very tall capital mountain. That's not disqualifying — every ambitious company starts with an absurd gap between where they are and where they need to be. But in a market with seven funded competitors, several years ahead and hundreds of millions richer, the question isn't just "can they build it?" but "can they build it fast enough to matter?"

The bull case is this: if their heat shield technology genuinely is 100x cheaper and the "refurbishable" claim holds, they could become the low-cost provider in a market where everyone else is spending tens of millions per vehicle. The "picks and shovels" positioning — being the logistics layer rather than owning the manufacturing payloads — is strategically smart. Varda is vertically integrated, which means they're both a potential customer and a competitor. If in-space manufacturing fragments into dozens of companies making different materials, someone needs to be the neutral return service. That's a better strategic position than owning one vertical. And the founders have exactly the technical depth to execute on it. If I were betting purely on founder-problem fit, this would score higher. But I've seen enough capital-intensive hardware companies to know that technical excellence alone doesn't survive a funding desert, and with this many competitors vacuuming up the available capital in the space, raising $50M+ as the eighth entrant is a genuinely hard problem — one that requires a different kind of resourcefulness than building a heat shield in an apartment.

I'd want to meet them. The dossier gives me one good resourcefulness story but I need more. I need to hear how they think about the capital problem, whether they've got DoD interest (that's where the big non-dilutive money is in this space), and whether they can articulate why they win in a market where Varda has a three-year head start and 600x their funding. The technical credentials are right. The schlep is real. But the contrarian edge has eroded, and I don't yet see the evidence that these specific founders can outrun the field.
