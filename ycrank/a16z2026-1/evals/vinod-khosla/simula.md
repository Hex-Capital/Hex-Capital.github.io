# Simula -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Simula is entering a market where ZeroClick has $55M from Ryan Hudson — the man who built Honey and sold it to PayPal for $4 billion — and Koah Labs has $26M with 35 million ad impressions already served. Those are not early-stage competitors. Those are well-capitalized companies with proven operators racing to own the same integration points Simula is pursuing. When I see a pre-seed company with five people competing against $80M+ in aggregate competitor funding for what amounts to SDK distribution slots in AI apps, the question I ask is: what does Simula know that ZeroClick and Koah do not? The dossier gives me no answer. The differentiation is ad format innovation — brand ambassadors and mini-games versus contextual display or reasoning-time insertion. That's a product design bet, not a technology insight. Product design bets can be copied in a quarter by any team with competent designers and engineers. This is a distribution race, and Simula is starting with the least capital and the smallest team.

The consequence question comes first, always. If Simula fully succeeds and becomes the dominant ad network for AI applications, what changes? AI app developers have a better monetization option. Advertisers reach users in conversational interfaces. That is a financial rearrangement, not a transformation. Nobody's healthcare gets cheaper. Nobody gains access to energy or education or legal services they didn't have before. The world looks essentially the same, except advertising dollars flow through AI surfaces instead of mobile banners. OpenAI's own ad pilot hit $100M+ ARR in under two months — which validates demand but also demonstrates that the platforms themselves can capture this value. Simula's pitch is that third-party AI publishers need an independent ad network, and that's probably true for the long tail. But the consequence of serving the long tail of AI app monetization is a successful AdTech business, not a changed world. I don't invest to build successful AdTech businesses.

Three red flags from my framework fire simultaneously. First, Simula is targeting redistribution of existing advertising spend into new surfaces — not creation of new demand. My highest-conviction bets created entirely new categories. Second, the improvement is incremental: Simula claims 3X higher payout versus traditional ads and 10X more engagement than banners. Those are format-level gains, not discontinuous. When I backed Impossible Foods, the thesis wasn't "30% better veggie burger." It was protein synthesis from molecular biology — a step-function change in how food is produced. Simula's SDK is a 30-minute integration with no patents, no proprietary models, and no technical moat identified in any public source. Third, the pitch leads with financial projections: "$15+ annual ad revenue per DAU," "~$10M+ in net revenue at full rollout." Spreadsheets are fiction. Believing in what you're building is what matters. When the lead metric is projected revenue per DAU rather than a technology thesis, the company is optimized for the wrong thing.

Yizhen Zhen's biography — Dartmouth '24, HBS MBA, Google SWE, Bain Capital PE — is the kind of resume that signals competence but not the outsider energy I look for. First-generation college student who built games since age 10 is a genuine signal of resourcefulness, but the career path (Google, Blackstone, Bain, Harvard Business School) is the establishment playbook executed well, not the irreverent outsider trajectory of a Peter Beck building rocket engines in a New Zealand garage or a teenage Mach Industries founder who doesn't know what can't be done. Logan Nicholson's gaming industry network provides commercial coverage, but I see no evidence of what either founder learned during their accelerator batch, what surprised them, or how they adapted. A static founder is a red flag in my framework. The prior venture — Fledge, a recruiting CRM at Dartmouth — is about as incremental a startup idea as exists. No evidence of the rate-of-learning signal I weight heavily.

The strongest bull case for Simula: every major computing platform spawns a dominant independent ad network. Google AdSense for web, Meta for social, AppLovin for mobile gaming. AI is the next platform, and the long tail of AI publishers will need someone other than OpenAI or Google to monetize their apps. If Simula's interactive formats (games, brand ambassadors) turn out to be the native ad unit for AI — the way video ads were native to mobile — then format innovation becomes the wedge that wins publisher loyalty. The claimed 5M+ DAUs, if real and growing, would represent meaningful early distribution. For this bull case to work, Simula would need its format thesis to be decisively right, its competitors' approaches (contextual display, reasoning-time insertion) to be wrong, and its tiny team to out-execute $80M+ in competing capital. That is three simultaneous conditions, each uncertain. I prefer technology risk to execution risk, and Simula offers only execution risk.

The scores that lock this verdict are consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach — the two dimensions that carry 55 of 100 points in my framework and are my primary filters. Simula scores below default on both. The consequence of success is a profitable AdTech company serving AI publishers, which does not materially change how millions of people live. The technical approach is a product-design bet on ad formats with no proprietary technology, no patents, and a replicable SDK model. When I size a bet, I need the consequence to justify the option price. Simula's consequence doesn't clear that bar.
