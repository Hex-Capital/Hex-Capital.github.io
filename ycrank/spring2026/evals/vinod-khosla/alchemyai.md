# Alchemy -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's the thing about AI copilots for professional verticals: I see fifty of these every YC batch. AI for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for radiologists, and now AI for microscopy researchers. The pitch structure is identical — "professionals spend X hours on tedious task Y, our AI reduces it to minutes." Alchemy claims 20 hours to 5 minutes. Fine. That's an efficiency gain for a population of researchers. But I don't invest in efficiency gains. I invest in consequences. If Alchemy fully succeeds — every life science researcher on Earth uses it — what happens? Scientists annotate images faster. That's useful. It's not civilizational. The indirect argument, that faster image analysis accelerates drug discovery and therefore saves lives, requires you to believe that microscopy annotation is the binding constraint on pharmaceutical progress. It isn't. Regulatory timelines, clinical trial design, funding cycles, and basic biology are all larger bottlenecks. Removing one step in a twenty-step process doesn't change the throughput of the system.

The technical approach compounds my concern. This is an LLM interface over existing open-source models — Cellpose, StarDist, the usual segmentation toolkits — assembled into pipelines via natural language. The dossier itself states the quiet part: "Any ML-capable team with life science domain knowledge could build similar pipeline-assembly tooling." When the competitive moat is described as "curation of domain-specific validated nodes and end-to-end researcher UX," you're telling me the advantage is product polish, not technology. That's an execution play, not a technology wedge. I funded Impossible Foods because heme synthesis was a genuine scientific breakthrough that no competitor could replicate by hiring two good engineers and calling an API. Alchemy's core technology is available to anyone with a Hugging Face account and domain knowledge. Foundation model improvements will continuously lower the barrier for new entrants, and microscope manufacturers like Leica and Zeiss have every incentive to bundle this capability for free with hardware sales — exactly the way they absorbed DRVISION's Aivia in 2021.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Alchemy nails the researcher UX and builds a genuine data flywheel — where each pipeline interaction improves model selection for the next researcher — it could become the default tool that displaces both expensive enterprise platforms and clunky open-source scripts. The addressable market in microscopy image analysis software is projected at $5.9B by 2031, and there's a real structural gap: hardware vendors can't easily unbundle and sell standalone SaaS, and open-source projects lack resources for polished agent workflows. If Alchemy captures even a meaningful fraction of that, it's a solid business. The affordability angle is real too — making Visiopharm-grade analysis available to grant-funded academic labs that can't afford enterprise pricing does fit my pattern of making the expensive accessible. But "solid business" and "accessible software for researchers" isn't what I'm looking for. I want to either lose my money or make fifty times my money. This company is designed to generate moderate returns in a mid-sized market, and that's a waste of my attention.

The founders are early-career — Baek with biomedical engineering from UT Austin and medical device experience at Boston Scientific, McBride with a CS master's from UIUC and a patent at 19. The credentials are relevant and the pairing of bio domain knowledge with ML engineering makes sense. But I have no evidence of learning velocity, no visible iteration trajectory, no public signal of how these founders think when challenged. Neither has a prior startup, neither has public technical work I can examine. McBride's early patent is a positive data point. But what I look for is the founder who can articulate what surprised them, what they got wrong, what changed in their thinking over the three months at YC. That signal is entirely absent here. Credentials tell me where someone has been. Learning rate tells me where they're going.

I keep coming back to the fundamental question: is this a problem too important not to work on? Microscopy image annotation is tedious. It is not too important not to work on. The world doesn't visibly change if this company succeeds. It changes incrementally for a niche population of researchers who save time on one step of their workflow. That's the kind of bet where success means a $200M SaaS company — perfectly good for most investors, but not for someone who needs the consequence of success to justify the option price.
