# Touchmark -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Touchmark is a competent infrastructure schlep wrapped around a founder pair I cannot yet describe as animals — Ilia Bolgov and Roman Yanushevskyi shipped a TypeScript SDK called @touchmark/sdk and a clean thesis about quality-adjusted AI billing, but the dossier contains no cereal-box moment, no Hacker News demo flip, no rejection-rescue prototype, just a YC S26 logo and a marketing line that says cross-customer price adjustments "average to zero." That last phrase is the tell I keep returning to.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the rung I want to compare Touchmark against on schlep, because Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind was the whole asset — the unpleasantness *was* the moat. Touchmark sits in genuinely schleppy territory: LLM-as-judge evals that customers will dispute the moment a refund hits their invoice, integration work at every AI platform's billing path, and the regulatory-adjacent posture of telling a vendor you are going to lower their revenue line. Touchmark diverges from the Coinbase rung in that Armstrong personally absorbed the schlep as identity, whereas Touchmark's own copy preemptively neutralizes the schlep by promising vendors "your top-line revenue is preserved" — which is the sound of founders flinching at the exact moment the work gets unpleasant. Still schleppy enough to clear mid; not Coinbase-grade conviction.

The lowest rung is animal resourcefulness, and the canonical failure pattern there is the polished-deck team with no pre-funding stunt — the MBA-AI-wrapper profile I described in my own pass example. Touchmark's dossier shows no Product Hunt launch, no press, no signed LOI with a named AI platform like Lovable (where Yanushevskyi interned and presumably has warm intros), no revenue, no waitlist, not even a retrievable Twitter follower count for either founder. An SDK on npm is an artifact; it is not the **Dropbox** (2007) demo video on Hacker News that made me reach for the checkbook. Bolgov spent six years and nine months in Product at Revolut, which is a serious tour, but Product at a unicorn is not the same as the unscalable hustle I am hunting for.

On organic founder-pain — the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick test — Touchmark earns a partial credit through Yanushevskyi's Lovable AI Engineer internship, because Lovable is precisely the kind of product whose billing is a mess when users generate ten broken apps versus one shipping one. The dossier does not say he was annoyed by it. It does not say either founder personally cut a check for a $5,000 surprise AI bill and decided to fix it. The mechanism by which this dimension compounds — founders re-deriving product decisions from their own continued use — requires a story the dossier never tells, and I have learned to read silence here as silence rather than as modesty.

Hacker genealogy is where Touchmark gets its most underrated signal: Yanushevskyi is an ICPC 2025 competitor and a two-time Citadel Securities QR intern, which is exactly the Patrick-Collison-Lisp-prize shape I unconsciously over-index on. Counterfactual: if either founder had a visible GitHub trail with a starred eval library or a Hacker News post explaining the math of output-quality scoring, Touchmark moves a full tier on this axis alone. Neither does. The competitive-programming pedigree is real but disembodied from a shipped public artifact, which is the difference between hacker-by-credential and hacker-by-trail.

Co-founder configuration is where Touchmark's structure worries me most relative to the **Stripe** (2010) Collison-brothers anchor: two founders is the right count, but the dossier explicitly states "no public data on co-founder history" — no shared employer across Revolut and Citadel, no shared university across Imperial and NUS, no documented years of building together. That is the Reddit-Huffman-Ohanian-dorm-roommates pattern absent. It does not disqualify Touchmark, but it removes the chemistry-buffer I usually grant when other signals are thin.

My 36-month forecast: Touchmark gets absorbed by Orb or by Stripe-via-Metronome as a sub-$15M acqui-product within 24-30 months, or it quietly dies — because the "average to zero" revenue-neutrality promise structurally caps Touchmark's own take-rate, and Stripe Billing already sits inside Vercel, Replit, and Perplexity through Orb's customer list and through the $1B Metronome acquisition in December 2025. The eval-scoring layer is a feature on the incumbent roadmap, not a company.

The bull case that stings: Yanushevskyi's Citadel QR pedigree is exactly the quantitative-eval DNA needed to build a defensibly accurate output-scoring library while incumbents ship a checkbox version, and Bessemer's 2026 AI pricing playbook documenting per-seat SaaS dropping from 21% to 15% in twelve months is a real tailwind. Touchmark could become the eval-library-of-record before Orb wakes up. I dismantle it on one piece of dossier evidence: Lago is already AGPLv3 open-source with OpenAI/Mistral templates, meaning the commoditization clock on eval-billing started before Touchmark shipped — Touchmark needed to be the open-source default and isn't. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed paid deployment at a named AI platform with disclosed ARR above $10K MRR before the S26 demo day, or Yanushevskyi publishing an open eval library that lands on Hacker News front page. Absent either, this is a neutral pass.
