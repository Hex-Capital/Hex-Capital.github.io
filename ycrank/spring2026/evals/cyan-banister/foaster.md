# Foaster -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what caught my attention first: there are already hundreds of millions of dollars pouring into AI-augmented consulting. Distyl AI has $202M. Aily Labs has $101M. PromptQL has $136M. This is a space where VCs are not shifting uncomfortably in their chairs — they're leaning forward with open checkbooks. When I hear "AI consulting" at a dinner party, nobody flinches. Nobody says "that's crazy." They say "obviously." And in my experience, when something is obvious to everyone, the window for an outsized return has already narrowed dramatically. My biggest wins — SpaceX, Anduril, Crusoe — all started in spaces where people thought the founders were out of their minds. Foaster is entering a space where people think the founders are smart, which is a very different signal.

Now, tell me the founders' stories. Raphael built a massive Rafael Nadal fan community starting at eleven years old — 400,000 users by his teens — and then got recruited as an agent for Nadal's Academy at eighteen. That shows early resourcefulness and the ability to build something from nothing, which I always notice. Alexandre built Repondia, where AI agents handled 60,000+ customer calls for French restaurant groups. That's directly relevant technical execution — he's already built the machine that conducts conversations at scale. Their LLM behavioral benchmark got shared by OpenAI's president and drew attention from xAI's co-founder. These are clearly gifted technical builders. But when I listen for the forge — the part of the story where the problem found THEM, where their biography made them unable to walk away from this specific thing — I don't hear it. They saw a market gap. They have the tools to fill it. That's career optimization dressed up as a company, and it reads differently to me than someone who lived inside the dysfunction they're trying to fix. The founders who taught themselves to survive inside broken systems before deciding to dismantle them — that's what I'm listening for, and it's not here.

What IS here, and what a generic investor might miss, is that management consulting is genuinely one of the most opaque, extractive industries in business. McKinsey charges half a million dollars for an engagement where a handful of twenty-five-year-old analysts interview a sample of employees and extrapolate to the whole organization. The client has almost no ability to evaluate the methodology, the pricing is deliberately mystified, and the billable-hours model creates incentives that are structurally misaligned with the client's interest. That's exactly the kind of hidden tax I look for — information asymmetry weaponized against people who don't understand what they're buying. Foaster's "100% of teams included" approach is an implicit attack on the sampling opacity at the heart of traditional consulting. The innovator's dilemma is real: McKinsey cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper product without destroying its own leverage model. That structural opening is where something interesting could happen.

Let me run the ten-year question, because that's where the bull case lives. If Foaster builds a persistent operational map of every client organization — a living, continuously updated picture of how a company actually works — that stops being a consulting engagement and starts being infrastructure. It becomes a process intelligence layer that companies rely on for every strategic decision, not just periodic consulting projects. The switching costs compound with each update. The data moat deepens with each engagement. In that world, you're not an AI wrapper on top of McKinsey's methodology — you're building the operational nervous system of mid-market enterprises. That's an infrastructure bet at an inflection point, and it's the version of this company that would excite me. But I'm not seeing evidence that the founders are thinking about it this way. Their current framing is "faster, cheaper consulting" — which is an efficiency play, not an infrastructure thesis. And when I look at the competitive landscape, any of the well-funded players in adjacent spaces could add an employee-interview layer to their existing platform. Dialogue AI already conducts AI interviews at scale and would need only a pivot from external to internal interviews to be in Foaster's exact lane.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The founders would need to discover — through the crucible of building — that they're not actually building a consulting firm but a permanent operational intelligence infrastructure. They'd need to survive the competitive convergence from well-funded players by moving faster and going deeper on the interview methodology. And they'd need the biographical forge to come from the building itself — sometimes the company forges the founder rather than the other way around. Combes's Repondia experience is the seed of something real; if the consulting engagement data starts compounding into something proprietary and irreplaceable, the moat could emerge. But I'm investing my own money, and at this stage, I need to see either a founder whose life made this inevitable or a space that makes other investors deeply uncomfortable. I see neither. Smart builders in a consensus market with well-funded competitors — I wish them well, but this isn't where I place my bets.
