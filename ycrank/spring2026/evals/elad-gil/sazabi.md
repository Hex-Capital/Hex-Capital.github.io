# Sazabi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check when evaluating an observability startup in 2026 is whether the market still qualifies as non-obvious -- and this one doesn't. Arize raised $70M. LangSmith hit a $1.25B valuation. ClickHouse acquired Langfuse. Laminar came out of YC S24 with a nearly identical thesis. Datadog, a $50B public company with $2.1B in revenue, is actively building AI observability features into its existing platform. When I invested in PagerDuty, "operations alerting" was a market that triggered dismissive reactions from other investors -- it seemed boring and small. AI agent observability in 2026 triggers the opposite reaction: every investor sees it, every incumbent is building it, and capital has already been deployed across half a dozen well-funded competitors. My best returns come from markets where the adoption curve is inflecting but the opportunity still looks wrong to most people. This market looks right to everyone. That's the core problem.

The structural moment is real -- I want to be clear about that. AI coding agents crossed a meaningful adoption threshold in 2024-2025, and a material share of production code is now AI-generated. That creates genuine new failure modes that traditional APM tools weren't designed to surface. The "why now" is legitimate. But a legitimate "why now" that's obvious to the entire market creates a fundamentally different competitive dynamic than one only a few people have mapped. When I backed Harvey in 2022, the structural moment (GPT scaling laws pointing toward legal-grade fidelity) was visible to me but invisible to most investors because the current-state capability seemed disqualifying. Sazabi's structural moment is visible to Datadog's product team, to LangChain's leadership, to every infrastructure-focused VC. That distinction matters enormously for whether a pre-product startup with ~$500K can capture the market.

The most technically interesting claim in the dossier is "logs are all you need" -- that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces from logs, giving you "three pillars for the price of one." If validated, this would structurally undercut Datadog's per-host and per-trace pricing model. Datadog can't easily cannibalize its own billing to offer a logs-first architecture -- that's the kind of business model barrier that creates room for an attacker. This is similar to how Stripe's developer-first API attacked a structural weakness in PayPal's merchant-first model. But here's the problem: that thesis is itself a miracle. Can log-derived metrics and traces actually match the fidelity of purpose-built instrumentation at production scale? That's an unvalidated technical claim, and it sits on top of the distribution miracle of winning against well-funded competitors. Two miracles is one too many. The single-miracle test exists precisely for situations like this: compounding low-probability events create structurally low odds even when each individual bet seems plausible.

Callaway's founder-market fit is the strongest signal in this dossier and the part that makes me pause before passing. He built observability infrastructure at Brex -- the exact problem domain. He founded Opkit, got it acquired by 11x in September 2024, then managed engineering on Alice, which the dossier describes as "one of the largest agentic systems in production." That's an unusual combination: someone who has built both production observability systems and large-scale AI agents. The angel investor list reinforces this -- Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, Replit, Brex. These are the ecosystem companies whose products generate the telemetry Sazabi would ingest. That's not just social proof; it's a potential distribution channel. The SOC 2 and ISO certifications for a 10-person team signal operational seriousness about enterprise readiness. If the market were less crowded, this founder profile would push me toward a check.

The bull case I'd want to believe: observability is being rebuilt from the ground up, not patched, and a purpose-built architecture designed for AI-generated code will outperform incumbent feature additions the same way Datadog itself outperformed the previous generation of monitoring tools. Callaway's unique combination of observability and AI agent experience positions him to build something the incumbents can't easily replicate because their teams lack that dual expertise. The ecosystem angels become distribution partners, and the "logs are all you need" thesis validates, creating a pricing advantage that structurally undercuts Datadog's model. In that scenario, Sazabi is the attacker entering through a wedge that incumbents can't easily follow due to business model constraints. I've seen this pattern work -- it's how Stripe entered payments. But Stripe entered when online payments infrastructure was genuinely broken and underserved. Observability for AI agents already has multiple well-funded solutions, a recently acquired competitor (Langfuse), and an incumbent that has proven it can extend its platform into adjacent categories. The window of structural opportunity may already be closing by the time Sazabi ships.

I pass. The founder is strong, the timing is real, but this is a consensus market with heavy competition -- not the non-obvious, seemingly-too-boring category where I find asymmetric returns. The technical thesis requires its own miracle before the distribution challenge even begins, and the competitive landscape is consolidating faster than a pre-product startup can establish position. I'd want to see Sazabi with shipped product and enterprise design partners before reconsidering.
