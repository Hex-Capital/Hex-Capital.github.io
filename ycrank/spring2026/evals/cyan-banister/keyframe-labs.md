# Keyframe Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Keyframe Labs isn't the technology -- it's the competitive landscape. Tavus just raised $40M from CRV and Sequoia to build exactly the same thing. HeyGen is doing $95M in annual revenue. Synthesia is worth $4 billion and has publicly announced real-time video agents on their 2026 roadmap. This is not an unpopular idea that's difficult to assess. This is one of the most actively funded categories in AI right now, with well-capitalized incumbents converging on the exact same product surface. When I look at Keyframe, I see a two-person team entering a market where consensus has already formed and the capital has already flowed. That's the opposite of what I'm looking for. The most promising companies are the ones nobody else wants to fund.

Parth Radia's technical credentials are genuinely impressive -- five years leading a 30-person generative AI team at Ericsson building photorealistic digital humans, shipped production products, built what he describes as a petascale human dataset. That's deep, real domain expertise, and it explains the 5-6x cost advantage over Tavus. This isn't a founder who read a paper and spun up a model over a weekend. He has spent nearly a decade at the intersection of real-time video synthesis and production infrastructure. Kaahan's complementary perception engineering work at Zipline -- building camera-based detect-and-avoid for autonomous drones -- adds a serious real-time systems dimension. The technical founder-market fit here is as strong as I've seen at pre-seed. But I keep coming back to the question I always start with: tell me your story, not the business. And the dossier gives me a professional resume, not a biographical thread. I don't hear why these founders feel called to this problem beyond having worked on it at big companies. The path from Ericsson VP to YC startup in the same category reads more like a career optimization than a mission forged by personal experience.

The family dynamic is actually the part that gives me the most comfort. The Radias appear to be siblings, and I've learned -- painfully, through HQ Trivia and GameCrush -- that the most dangerous configuration is a 50/50 partnership between people who haven't already navigated real conflict together. Family co-founders have stress-tested their relationship in ways business partners never have. They've argued over the Thanksgiving table and survived. Parth and Kaahan bring complementary technical expertise from different domains, which means they're less likely to step on each other's territory. If I were going to invest in this company, the sibling founding team would be a significant reason why.

Here's the bull case I have to honestly engage with: if the $0.06/min pricing reflects a genuine, durable architectural advantage -- not a loss-leader strategy -- then Keyframe could become the Twilio of visual AI agents. Voice agent platforms like LiveKit and Vapi have created a massive installed base of audio-only agents that need a face, and the cheapest, lowest-latency option wins in API businesses. Synthesia's entire business model is high-margin per-seat enterprise licenses, which creates a real channel conflict that would slow their pivot to commodity per-minute pricing. Tavus is 5-6x more expensive. If Keyframe's two-stage flow-based architecture gives them a structural cost floor that well-funded competitors can't easily match, this could be the wedge that wins the infrastructure layer. That's a real thesis. But it requires believing that model architecture moats persist as foundation video models rapidly improve and open-source alternatives proliferate -- and my experience watching infrastructure commoditize across computing cycles makes me skeptical that any two-person team can maintain a cost moat against Sequoia-backed competitors with 50x the engineering headcount.

What I don't see here is economic access. Keyframe's customers are enterprise training platforms, B2B SaaS companies, and sales enablement teams. These are legitimate use cases, but they don't create income for drivers, or transparent credit for the underbanked, or safety data for underserved neighborhoods. The cheaper-API argument -- that at $0.06/min, interactive video becomes accessible to smaller companies -- has some merit, but it's a stretch to frame a B2B developer API as poverty alleviation. I back companies where I can trace a clear line from the product to real people whose lives improve. I can't draw that line here. This is a capable team building a technically impressive product in a consensus market without the biographical urgency or economic impact that drives my highest-conviction investments.
