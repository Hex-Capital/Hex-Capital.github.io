# Hyper -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Shalin Shah and Kanyes Thaker are competent Berkeley 2021 engineers who shipped autonomy at Matic Robots, and Hyper is them pointing that skill at the most-consensus wedge in the Spring 2026 YC batch -- a cross-tool AI knowledge layer sitting between Glean's $200M ARR and Dust's $40M Sequoia-led Series B. The signature pairing here is real technical chops paired with the polar opposite of magically weird: Hyper is a tidy build in the loudest software category, and that mismatch is the entire evaluation.

Anduril's Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus out of his parents' garage before pivoting to defense, and the load-bearing analogy is that the founder had *already* built the adjacent thing at scale before claiming the new problem. Hyper's founders did ship -- Kanyes was founding data engineer on Snorkel AI deals and Shalin led autonomy at Matic -- but the problem Hyper claims (employees re-explaining context to Cursor and Claude Code) is not a problem either of them lived in any biographical sense; it is a problem they noticed as recent users of those tools. The Anduril rung requires the founder to be *of* the domain, not adjacent to it via job title, and the Matic-to-knowledge-management jump is the same kind of analytical migration I down-weight when ex-FAANG PMs discover SaaS opportunities in a memo. Hyper sits between the consensus-darling rung and the DeepMind-polymath rung, and the dossier gives me no autobiographical "moment of contact" that would push it up.

The HQ Trivia post-mortem with Yusupov and Kroll is the warning I cite most often -- two co-founders, no designated tiebreaker, and the feud took the company down. Hyper is a two-person team of roommates and former co-workers from Matic with no CEO named anywhere in the dossier, equity split undisclosed, no operating agreement referenced. That is not yet HQ Trivia; that is the conversation Hyper has not had. Roommate-and-coworker pairings produce the warmest founding dynamic and also the slowest tiebreaker escalations, because the cost of a hard call is the friendship. I am applying the absent-evidence default here, but the absence is itself loud for a YC batch where the partner should have asked.

Mechanism on magically-weird: Glean is at $200M ARR with a $7.2B valuation, Dust is at 41k MAU with Sequoia behind it, Guru is at $63M ARR, Notion AI and Slack AI are ecosystem-native, and Hyper proposes to be the tool-neutral inject layer across all of them. The compounding question over 18 months is whether Anthropic or Cursor ship native memory first -- if they do, Hyper's channel narrows to zero, because the entire value proposition is injection into someone else's chat turn. This is the inverse of Crusoe's "magically weird" -- Crusoe was ignored because the category was uninvestable; Hyper is in a category where ten VCs are already bidding on every adjacent deal. The thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus, and Hyper is the consensus.

Counterfactual on capital intensity: to flip this from a 4/15 to a 9/15, Hyper would need to be doing something the dossier does not describe -- shipping a regulated-data-handling moat for healthcare or defense knowledge graphs, or running an on-prem appliance for classified environments, or owning the connector pipeline as proprietary infrastructure rather than RAG glue. None of that is present. Hyper is pure software abstraction in a category where the technical pattern (connectors plus retrieval) is, per the dust.tt and capacity.com comparisons in the dossier, broadly buildable. The capital-intensity dimension is anti-thesis at baseline.

Niantic earned the highest physical-verifiability score in my portfolio because I had been playing Ingress obsessively since 2012 before John Hanke ever pitched me. Hyper is invisible by design -- "silent learning agents" is the dossier's own phrase -- heyhyper.ai returned only a tagline on fetch, there is no Product Hunt launch, no public demo, no downloadable artifact a normal human can install or photograph. I cannot ride this, scan this, or hand it to a friend at dinner. The product is a background process you only experience by trusting the founder's testimony that it works, which is exactly the developer-tools abstraction failure mode in my pass example.

Hyper's most likely 36-month outcome is an acqui-hire by Cursor, Anthropic, or Notion for the integration engineering at $10-40M, with the secondary path being a pivot into agent infrastructure after failing to escape Glean and Dust's gravity on enterprise sales -- the mechanism is that the wedge (injection into third-party AI tools) is exactly the wedge those third parties will eventually close themselves with native memory features, and the Berkeley/Matic engineering pedigree is acquirer-attractive even when the standalone company isn't.

The strongest bull case is that Hyper is structurally different from Glean and Dust in a way the dossier actually supports -- Glean is a destination UI, Dust is an agent-building workspace, and Hyper is the only player proposing to be a *passive pipe* into tools developers already live in. If bottom-up Cursor and Claude Code adoption compounds, Hyper could be the "tool-neutral memory layer" that everyone else's UI assumption blocks them from becoming, and the Snorkel enterprise-deals experience Kanyes brings is non-trivial GTM rigor for a two-person team. The HQ Trivia exception is the precedent that explosive tactile traction can override governance concerns -- but Hyper has zero tactile artifact for me to feel pull from, and the DeepMind polymath exception doesn't apply to two focused engineers in their mid-twenties. I would flip to Invest if Hyper shipped one signed enterprise pilot with a named AI-native company or hit 1,000+ Cursor installs with retention data, neither of which exists in the dossier.
