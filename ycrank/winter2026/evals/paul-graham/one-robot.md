# One Robot -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is the part that gets my attention. Both founders were at Industrial Next -- a YC W22 company -- literally training robots for precision manufacturing assembly, and they hit a wall: "evaluating a new policy started taking longer than training it." This is not an idea someone conceived after reading a TechCrunch article about robotics simulation. This is two people who spent years wrestling with the specific problem of getting robots to fold textiles and assemble parts at sub-millimeter tolerance, who discovered that the bottleneck had shifted from training the model to evaluating it. The problem found them. That's the strongest signal I look for, and it's unambiguously present here.

What makes this particularly interesting through my schlep blindness lens is that building task-specific world models for contact-rich manipulation is exactly the kind of work that causes smart robotics researchers to unconsciously look elsewhere. Everyone in the VLA space knows simulation environments are inadequate -- physics engines can't handle deformable objects realistically, and general-purpose world models aren't fine-grained enough for manipulation. But actually fixing this requires collecting real robot data for each specific task domain, training world models that capture the messy physics of textiles and precision assembly, and validating that rollouts transfer back to reality. That's tedious, domain-specific, unglamorous work. It's the robotics equivalent of the Collisons walking into banks to build Stripe's payment infrastructure while everyone else just complained about payments being broken. The schlep is the moat -- NVIDIA won't build task-specific world models for individual manipulation workflows because it conflicts with their horizontal platform business. This is picks-and-shovels for the VLA ecosystem, and the people selling picks and shovels during a gold rush usually do well.

The founding team is technically formidable in a way I find genuinely reassuring. Hemanth has dual master's degrees from Georgia Tech, published robotics research, NASA JPL work, and time at Google -- but more importantly, he previously bootstrapped Crescer AI to profitability, which tells me something about financial discipline that credentials alone don't convey. Elton spent five years at Tesla during the Model 3 production ramp and on the Dojo supercomputer team, then served as Head of Software at Industrial Next. These are builders, not presenters. They've been working together training robots since 2020, which means the co-founder relationship predates the startup by years -- the opposite of the "met at a co-founder matching event" pattern that kills companies. They have a working demo on YouTube. When I see two technical founders who've been collaborating for six years, who both have deep domain expertise in the exact problem they're solving, who've already shipped a prototype -- that's the kind of team where the product actually gets built.

The honest concern is that they're building infrastructure for a market that barely exists yet. The number of teams actively training VLA models for industrial manipulation is small today. If VLAs don't become the dominant paradigm for robot manipulation -- if some other approach wins -- the entire premise weakens. And the competitive threat from NVIDIA is real: Cosmos has 3M+ downloads, and NVIDIA has the resources to add task-specific capabilities if they choose to. A two-person team outrunning NVIDIA requires that the domain specificity and customer intimacy create a structural advantage that scale cannot easily replicate. I think that's plausible -- building world models from a customer's actual robot data for their specific manipulation tasks is inherently a high-touch, narrow-wedge business that doesn't fit NVIDIA's platform model -- but it's not certain. The "pilots with multiple robotics companies" language is encouraging but frustratingly vague: I'd want to know who these companies are and whether they'd be genuinely disappointed if One Robot disappeared tomorrow.

If this works, the bull case is compelling. Every robotics company building VLA-based manipulation systems will need realistic simulation environments for evaluation and training data generation. As the VLA ecosystem expands -- and Physical Intelligence, NVIDIA, Google DeepMind are all pouring resources into it -- the demand for task-specific simulation grows with it. One Robot could become the default simulation layer for industrial manipulation, with a data flywheel where each customer engagement makes their world models better. The starting-narrow-and-expanding pattern is right: textiles and precision assembly today, broader manipulation categories tomorrow. This is how infrastructure companies get built.

The organic discovery, the schlep depth, and the technical founder quality all point in the same direction. What's missing is concrete evidence of user pull beyond vague pilot mentions, and the market itself needs to mature. But the founders have been living in this problem for years, the work is genuinely hard in ways that deter competition, and the team can clearly build. That combination, at pre-seed, is enough for me to write a check -- knowing that the biggest risk isn't the team or the idea, but the timing of the VLA market beneath them.
