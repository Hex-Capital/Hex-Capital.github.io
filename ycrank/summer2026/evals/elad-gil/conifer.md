# Conifer -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Conifer is a real-catalyst company with no revenue curve to confirm the catalyst is actually monetizing — the exact profile my "false signal vs. just working" test (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) was built to filter. Michael Jeffords is running at a genuine inflection (Ollama went from ~100K to 52M monthly downloads by Q1 2026 per programming-helper), but the dossier's revenue line reads "No public data found" and the marketplace is "Coming soon" — so the loudest thing about Conifer is the silence where the growth curve should be.

**Anduril** (2022) is the load-bearing analogy on the catalyst axis, and it's where Conifer is most interesting. Ukraine revealed a 24-month software gap in defense procurement; open-weight parity plus Ollama's 52x download curve is a similar step-function in inference economics that most enterprise buyers haven't priced in yet. The mechanism that made Anduril work — a specific recent event forced a category that peers had written off — does partially rhyme in Conifer's setup, because "local-first because tokens are expensive and financials can't leave the box" is a real 2026 buyer conversation. But Conifer diverges from the Anduril template on the critical detail: Anduril's catalyst had a specific dollar counterparty (Pentagon budgets shifting) and shipping contracts already compounding; Conifer's catalyst is a developer-download curve on a *different* product (Ollama), and the dossier gives me zero named enterprise buyers who have moved budget because of it. A catalyst without a buyer signature is a hypothesis, not a re-opening.

**Lyft** Series C is the failure I compare Conifer to on distribution position, and the mechanism is uncomfortably close. Lyft's problem was being #2 in a winner-take-all market where the leader captured disproportionate economics (20VC). Conifer sits squeezed between OpenRouter — which just closed $113M from Google and NVIDIA per TechTimes (May 2026) at implied gateway-default status — and Ollama at 174K GitHub stars owning the free local runtime. Conifer's 303 stars on `ConiferKit/sage` and 57 org followers don't move the needle against either flank. The Lyft pattern shows up here: when the #1 in your category has strategic capital from the compute vendors themselves, "we route smarter" isn't a wedge, it's a feature bullet in OpenRouter's next release.

Revenue velocity is where Conifer fails hardest, and the analytical mode is mechanism-over-18-months. Harvey (2023) traced $3B→$5B→$8B in months (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) because the underlying enterprise buying was compounding weekly — that's what "just working" looks like in dollars. Conifer's dossier discloses no ARR, no design partners, no named logos, no MoM anything. In the AI cycle my Tim Ferriss #863 (April 2026) line applies literally: silence on growth is negative signal because real growth is easy to disclose. Over 18 months, a routing product either accumulates a proprietary prompt→cheapest-model-that-works dataset (the "live intelligence index" Conifer references) or it doesn't — and the dossier gives me no evidence that dataset is compounding, which means the moat is theoretical and the clock is a gateway incumbent's clock.

Single-miracle math on Conifer counterfactually pencils only if the score flips to "the kernels are the miracle and everything else is GTM." Today it doesn't: Conifer requires (1) hand-written Metal/CUDA kernels genuinely beating cloud on quality-adjusted cost, (2) enterprises trusting a three-person team with routing decisions on sensitive data, and (3) OpenRouter and LiteLLM not adding local execution before Conifer wins distribution. That's three independent miracles compounded — the exact structure NFX's *Unusual Signs* piece warns against. Color Genomics (2015) worked because the workflow layer isolated the miracle to software on top of commodity sequencing; Conifer would need to isolate its miracle similarly, and the dossier doesn't do that isolation for me.

On founder shipping rhythm, Conifer sits below where I need it. The Perplexity (2022) template requires a founder who ships between meetings — Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and returned a week later with the thing built (Tim Ferriss #863). Jeffords has shipped `sage` v1.2.14 by Jun 2026, which is real artifact evidence, but the dossier explicitly notes "no verified education, prior company, or exit data" and no confirmed LinkedIn/GitHub/Twitter. I can see the repo cadence; I can't see the operator underneath it, which is the input to whether this compounds.

My 36-month forecast: Conifer either acqui-hires into a gateway (most likely OpenRouter, given its $113M war chest and strategic need for on-device credibility) at sub-$30M, or winds down after the "live intelligence index" fails to differentiate from LiteLLM's next release. Sub-$5M ARR at 36 months, no Series A from a top-tier fund. Mechanism: the squeeze between a strategically-capitalized gateway and a 174K-star OSS runtime doesn't leave room for a paid middle layer without a compliance-vertical wedge that Conifer hasn't signaled.

The strongest opposing case is that Conifer's proprietary Metal/CUDA fused kernels are actually the wedge — a regulated buyer (banks, healthcare) needs on-device inference for financials-can't-leave-the-box reasons, values Conifer's cross-provider consolidation over Ollama's bare runtime, and Jeffords converts that into $2-3M ARR from three Fortune 500 design partners inside 12 months. That's a real path and it maps to my own *AI Market Clarity* (blog.eladgil.com) call that compliance-heavy verticals are still wide open. What dismantles it: the dossier shows zero named design partners, zero regulated-industry logos, and no compliance certifications (SOC 2, HIPAA, FedRAMP) that would be table stakes for that buyer — the wedge is available in theory and unclaimed in evidence. Flip condition: two named Fortune 500 or top-20 regulated-industry design partners disclosed with usage data by the end of the YC batch, and I re-underwrite.
