# Andustry -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I look at with Andustry is the competitive landscape, because it tells me something important about the structural moment. When I see Matchory ($8.86M raised), LightSource Labs ($33M at $130M valuation), Cavela ($8.6M), and MESH Works all building AI-powered procurement tools -- and all funded within the last 18 months -- that's not a non-obvious market anymore. That's a consensus forming. The AI procurement wave is real, but when five-plus funded companies are pursuing overlapping segments simultaneously, the question shifts from "is this market interesting?" to "does this specific team have a structural advantage that compounds?" And I'm not seeing that structural advantage here.

The "why now" is genuine: LLM capabilities crossed the threshold for reliable specification parsing and RFQ generation in 2024-2025, enabling automation of workflows that previously required domain-expert humans. Industrial machinery procurement is legitimately one of the least digitized segments of the global economy -- an $810B market where buyers still contact hundreds of suppliers manually and wait weeks for a first quote. This pattern -- "too boring, deeply manual, massive TAM" -- is exactly the archetype I've done well with. PagerDuty seemed like niche alerting software; Gusto seemed like "just payroll." Both became embedded infrastructure. Industrial procurement has that same quality of seeming boring while being universal. But there's a critical difference: PagerDuty's software layer was proprietary and deeply embedded in engineering workflows. Andustry's AI layer is built on commodity LLMs that any competitor can access. The RFQ generation, supplier matching, and quote comparison features are reproducible by a competent team in months, not years. The defensibility has to come from the marketplace side -- supplier network density, data quality, transaction history -- not the technology side.

This creates a multiple-miracle problem. Andustry needs to simultaneously: build a supplier database that's genuinely proprietary rather than aggregated from public directories (the 500K+ claim is unverified by third parties), achieve marketplace liquidity in a market where industrial buyers make high-value, relationship-dependent purchasing decisions, and outcompete teams with 10-20x more capital. Each of those is hard individually. Combined, they're compounding low-probability events. When the single miracle is marketplace liquidity -- which is already one of the hardest miracles to achieve -- adding "also build a proprietary data moat" and "also survive against well-funded competitors" makes this a three-miracle company. That's where I get uncomfortable.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. Johann Sturken's family background in industrial goods trading gives him insider pattern recognition that most software founders lack -- he's seen how procurement actually works from the inside, not from a pitch deck. His mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM, combined with McKinsey Aerospace & Defense experience, provide both technical credibility with industrial buyers and enterprise sales context. And EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is a real signal at pre-seed -- it means actual economic activity is flowing through the platform, which is more than most pre-seed marketplaces can claim. If the supplier data is genuinely proprietary and the network effects start compounding, this could become the embedded procurement layer that industrial buyers can't switch away from. The product-to-distribution potential exists: own the buyer-supplier transaction, then expand into trade financing, logistics coordination, quality inspection, insurance -- similar trajectory to how Stripe went from payments to a full financial services platform through a single developer integration. For this to work, though, Andustry would need to demonstrate that their supplier network is uniquely deep and verified in ways that public directories aren't, and that transaction data creates a compounding advantage in quote quality and supplier matching.

What keeps me from getting to conviction is that the software layer here looks more like a feature than a platform -- a well-executed AI wrapper on top of a procurement workflow that could be replicated by Matchory adding a transactional layer, by LightSource Labs narrowing to industrial verticals, or by Xometry extending beyond custom manufacturing. The founders haven't articulated a distribution strategy beyond "book a call," which at pre-seed is fine for most companies, but in a marketplace business where distribution IS the product, I need to see evidence that they're thinking about this from day one. Long industrial sales cycles with a 2-person team in Munich compound the challenge. Johann's McKinsey background gives him the vocabulary for enterprise conversations, but consulting and selling are fundamentally different muscles, and I've seen plenty of ex-McKinsey founders struggle with the transition from advising buyers to becoming a vendor they'll trust with procurement.

I'd watch this company. If they can demonstrate that the 500K+ supplier database is genuinely proprietary and that repeat transaction rates are high -- meaning buyers come back because Andustry's matching quality improves with data -- the marketplace flywheel could start turning. But right now, the structural moment is shared across too many competitors, the software layer is commodity, and the path to defensibility requires multiple things to go right simultaneously. The market fits my "too boring" archetype, but boring markets only work as investments when the company building in them has a compounding advantage that makes the next competitor's job harder, not easier.
