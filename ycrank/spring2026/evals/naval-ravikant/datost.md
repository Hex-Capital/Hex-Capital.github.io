# Datost -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Salesforce owns Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. Datost is building an AI data analyst inside Slack that replaces the need for Tableau. Read that sentence again. You're constructing a house on land owned by your most natural competitor, and your entire value proposition is to make their other product unnecessary. This is not a platform risk -- it's a structural impossibility. Salesforce has $30 billion in annual revenue, thousands of engineers, and a CEO who talks about AI at every earnings call. The question isn't whether they'll build this. The question is whether they've already started.

The specific knowledge test fails at the threshold. Both founders are 21 years old, software engineers who worked together at Traba -- a staffing technology company. Their LinkedIn profiles reflect engineering internships, not years spent inside data teams watching analysts drown in ad-hoc SQL requests. What do they know about enterprise data workflows that can't be learned in six months? What non-obvious insight about how organizations actually consume data emerged from building staffing software? I can't find it. When I invested in Stack Overflow, Jeff Atwood and Joel Spolsky had spent a decade writing about developer knowledge sharing -- they understood something about how programmers learn that nobody else did. That's non-transferable. Building a Slack bot that generates SQL is something any two engineers at a YC hackathon could prototype in a weekend. The capability is in the LLM, not in the founders.

The leverage architecture here is thin. Yes, a non-technical person querying a database through Slack instead of filing a ticket with the data team is a friction reduction. But it's not a new form of leverage -- it's a better interface to existing capability. Looker already lets people self-serve. Metabase already democratizes queries. The innovation is "do it in Slack" -- which is a distribution choice, not a leverage mechanism. Compare this to Replit, which created a fundamentally new capability: people who couldn't program before can now build software. That's leverage creation. Datost makes something slightly easier for people who already had access to the answers through existing tools. The marginal leverage per user is modest, and the total addressable leverage is bounded by how many Slack workspaces have connected databases.

The strongest bull case: Salesforce is bureaucratic enough that a fast-moving startup could own the Slack-native analytics experience for three to five years before the mothership ships something competitive. If Datost accumulates deep organizational context -- each company's schema, naming conventions, tribal data knowledge -- that context becomes switching cost. The "AI analyst with its own computer" framing suggests they understand that text-to-SQL without execution is useless, which is a real insight most competitors miss. And young technical founders with no career baggage sometimes develop specific knowledge through sheer velocity of iteration -- they learn the domain by building, not by studying. If they can get into fifty enterprise Slack workspaces in the next twelve months and build context depth that makes the product irreplaceable, the commoditization risk diminishes. But "if they execute perfectly and the giant doesn't move" is a bet on the field, not on specific knowledge. I need more than that.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. "AI for data analytics" is perhaps the single most crowded application category in the current LLM wave. Julius AI already has two million users and $10 million from Bessemer. Defog.ai exists. Every BI vendor is shipping AI features. Microsoft has Copilot in Power BI. Google has AI in Looker. Nobody smart is dismissing AI-powered analytics -- everyone agrees it's coming. The only question is who captures it. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, people thought I was throwing money away. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was literally illegal. Where is the disagreement here? What informed skeptic thinks AI data analysis is a bad idea? The absence of contrarian positioning means the pricing already reflects the opportunity, and the competition is already funded.

I pass. Two capable young engineers, no domain-specific knowledge I can identify, building in the most consensus AI category on a platform owned by the company with the greatest incentive to build exactly this product. The technology doesn't compound in any distinctive way -- the value is in the LLM, which they don't own, and the platform, which they don't control. I want founders who know something that can't be taught, creating leverage that didn't exist before, at a time when the crowd thinks they're wrong. Datost is smart people building an obvious thing at the obvious time.
