# Maquoketa Research -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Anduril Industries -- a company I backed from seed through a record $1 billion check at $30.5 billion -- is currently constructing Arsenal-1, a $1 billion, five-million-square-foot factory designed to produce tens of thousands of autonomous defense systems annually starting mid-2026. AeroVironment holds a $990 million Army Switchblade contract and is scaling to 1,200 units per month. Shield AI has raised $3.1 billion at a $12.7 billion valuation. Helsing has deployed thousands of loitering munitions in Ukraine on a €268 million German government contract. Maquoketa Research, a five-person pre-seed team in Elk Grove Village, Illinois, proposes to enter this exact market with autonomous one-way attack drones. The most important question about Maquoketa Research is not whether their technology works -- it is whether entering a market with four heavily-funded competitors pursuing identical theses constitutes a secret or a suicide note. I have a specific rule: when you can draw a competitive landscape slide with five companies on it, you have already answered the monopoly question in the negative.

Maquoketa Research's stated insight -- that autonomous capabilities quadruple mission success rates compared to operator-guided systems -- is the defining consensus of the post-Ukraine defense world. Every investor in every defense-tech pitch meeting has heard this thesis. Every company in the competitive landscape is building autonomy stacks. The U.S. Army's $70 million fiscal 2026 loitering munition budget request exists precisely because this is no longer a contrarian view but settled doctrine. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is something that MarketsandMarkets projects at 19.9% CAGR and that four multi-billion-dollar companies are already executing against, there is no secret. Maquoketa Research's vertical integration and domestic manufacturing claims are table stakes in ITAR-regulated defense procurement, not differentiators. The one potentially non-consensus element -- Alejandro Hernandez's phased array and RF design specialization from Berkeley Wireless Research Center -- could matter in contested electromagnetic environments, but this is a thin thread of possible differentiation, not an articulated monopoly thesis.

The bull case deserves construction before dismissal. Maquoketa Research operates in atoms, not bits -- custom hardware spanning airframes, propulsion firmware, RF communications, and phased array systems requires integration across multiple engineering disciplines that no software company can replicate quickly. Defense procurement deliberately maintains multiple suppliers; the DoD will not sole-source loitering munitions to Anduril regardless of Anduril's scale. Ukraine consumes drones at rates that exceed any single manufacturer's capacity, creating structural demand for additional producers. Hernandez's Berkeley Wireless Research Center background in phased array systems addresses a genuine technical frontier -- GPS-denied, EW-contested environments where most commercial drone architectures fail. And the market is growing fast enough ($5.36 billion in 2025 to $13.26 billion by 2030) that even a minority position could produce meaningful returns. I checked whether the Lyft exception applies -- where the total category value is so enormous that even the number-two player produces fund-relevant returns. It does not. The loitering munition market is large but not the hundred-billion-dollar category that justified backing Lyft against Uber. And Maquoketa Research would not be number two in a duopoly -- they would be number five in a fragmented field, which is precisely the competitive structure that destroys value for everyone.

Evan Yeager's background -- UC Berkeley EECS, SpiderRock, Stylis.AI -- demonstrates technical competence but not the kind of prior high-stakes execution that characterizes my highest-conviction checks. Compare the Maquoketa Research founding team to Palmer Luckey building and selling Oculus before co-founding Anduril, or to General Matter's team drawn from SpaceX, Tesla, and national laboratories. Maquoketa Research's founders are Berkeley graduates with no visible defense industry relationships, no security clearances mentioned, no prior exits, and no network provenance in my ecosystem. Their GitHub organization contains nine public repositories, all forks of existing open-source projects -- PX4-Autopilot, QGroundControl, MAVLink, HackRF -- with no original repositories showing significant development. A definite founder would have already built proprietary technology that makes the vision irreversible; forks of existing open-source tools signal an iterative discovery process, not a company organized around a specific conviction about what the world requires.

Maquoketa Research has no visible distribution architecture -- no DoD contracts, no LOIs, no named government customer relationships, no evidence of security clearances that would enable classified procurement discussions. Defense sales cycles run 18 to 36 months at minimum, and a five-person startup burning pre-seed capital cannot survive that timeline without either extraordinary capital efficiency or a patron relationship with a government buyer. Palantir had CIA backing from inception; Anduril had Palmer Luckey's public profile and Trae Stephens's defense network from Founders Fund and the Pentagon. Maquoketa Research has none of these distribution advantages, and as I have written, if you have invented something new but you have not invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business regardless of the engineering.

The physical-world technology dimension is Maquoketa Research's relative strength and the reason this evaluation does not score lower. Hernandez's phased array and RF expertise represents genuine atoms-level technical depth, and the full-stack integration challenge -- airframe, propulsion, flight control, RF, computer vision, ground control -- creates real complexity barriers. But complexity barriers without proprietary breakthroughs are merely difficulty, not defensibility. Anduril's Lattice autonomy platform and Arsenal-1 manufacturing scale will create the kind of structural last-mover advantage I look for -- the system designed to make all previous approaches obsolete. Maquoketa Research is not positioned as the last mover in this market; it is a late entrant competing against companies with three to four orders of magnitude more capital. The one verifiable condition that would flip my assessment: a signed OTA contract or LASSO program selection from the DoD, demonstrating that Maquoketa Research possesses a specific technical capability -- likely in the RF/phased array domain -- that the incumbents cannot replicate, creating a government embedding pathway independent of Anduril's. Without that, this is a competent team entering a competitive market, and competition remains what it has always been.
