# Hex Security -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hex Security is the competitive landscape, and it tells the whole story. XBOW has $117 million. Novee has $51.5 million. Aikido is at a billion-dollar valuation with $13 million in ARR. Casco and MindFort are YC peers doing the same thing. Open-source pentesting agents are proliferating. When I see five-plus well-funded companies and a growing open-source base converging on identical positioning — "AI agents that automate penetration testing" — that is the market telling me this idea is consensus. And consensus is the death of alpha. The entire premise of my investing approach is finding bets where most people think you're crazy. Nobody thinks autonomous pentesting is crazy. Every cybersecurity conference, every VC newsletter, every enterprise CISO is talking about it. This is the opposite of an insane idea — it's the most obvious application of AI agents to a services market anyone could draw on a whiteboard.

Ahmad Khan's decade at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM doing real application security and black-box pentesting is the one legitimate signal here. That's a credible operator who understands the problem from the inside. But "sane person, sane idea" is not my bet. It's a formula for fighting a resource war you've already lost. When your primary competitor has 230x your capital and is already ranked on HackerOne with enterprise bank customers, your domain expertise isn't a moat — it's table stakes. Novee's ex-IDF elite cyber unit founders arguably have deeper offensive security credentials. The founding team is competent but not uniquely positioned to win this particular knife fight.

Here's what really kills this for me: the AI IS the business. Strip away the reinforcement learning agents and there is no Hex Security. That is the precise category I've been publicly arguing is structurally uninvestable. "Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore" — and a company whose entire moat IS the AI model is the most extreme version of this problem. Every model generation reshuffles the competitive deck. The "data flywheel from deploying across more environments" story sounds compelling until you realize every competitor tells the identical story, and the open-source community is building the same capability for free. This isn't a painting company with a tech layer. This isn't a parking infrastructure business with installed hardware. There are no physical-world operations, no protocol position, no regulatory fortress. It's pure software automating a workflow — the exact category where AI commoditization hits hardest and fastest.

The bull case deserves honest engagement: the penetration testing market has genuine structural dysfunction. Companies test once a year, there are 3.5 million unfilled cybersecurity positions globally, and the cost of breaches keeps climbing. Continuous autonomous testing addresses a real and growing pain point. If Hex Security's RL approach produces genuinely superior exploit-chaining that competitors can't match, and if the YC network provides a wedge into hundreds of startups who become enterprise customers later, there's a path to meaningful revenue. The team demonstrated real capability by finding critical vulnerabilities — SQL injection exposing billions of records, proof-of-concept worms — across dozens of YC companies. That's not nothing. For a different investor who believes AI-native companies can build durable advantages through deployment-derived data, this could be a reasonable seed bet in a large expanding market. But that investor's framework is fundamentally different from mine.

The narrative could theoretically expand from "pentesting" to "autonomous offensive security platform" to "the AI that secures everything" — but every competitor in the space is telling the same infinity story, which means the narrative premium has already been arbitraged across the category. When six companies are raising money on the same future vision, you're not buying a unique narrative — you're buying a consensus position at market price. And there's no revenue, no pricing page, no public customer count to provide a cushion. The $3B in "prevented damages" is a clever marketing number based on IBM breach cost benchmarks, not business fundamentals. This is a pre-revenue company entering a resource war against competitors with 100x-200x their capital, armed with the same general-purpose technology that gets cheaper and more available every quarter. That is structurally the wrong setup for my money.
