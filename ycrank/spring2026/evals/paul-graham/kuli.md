# Kuli -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Michael Hodara managed $20M+ in annual marketing spend at Aircall, a French unicorn, and came out the other side knowing exactly what was broken about influencer marketing workflows. That's the strongest thing in Kuli's dossier. He didn't read a MarketsandMarkets report about a $14.2B TAM and decide to build a startup. He sat in the chair, opened the spreadsheets, reviewed the creator content manually, and built the thing he wished existed. When I evaluate how founders arrived at their idea, the question I care about is whether they lived in the future and built what was missing or whether they identified an opportunity from the outside. Hodara's Aircall experience puts him firmly in the first category. His EPFL Robotics MSc gives him the technical machinery to actually build the multimodal video analysis system Kuli's pitch depends on. That combination — domain buyer who can also write the code — is rarer than people think and explains how Kuli landed Disney, Supercell, and Havas as named users with a three-person team at pre-seed.

Jonathan Hassan's six years of KPMG M&A advisory work is the part that gives me pause about Kuli. I don't mind a non-technical co-founder handling enterprise sales — someone has to — but KPMG is exactly the kind of background that produces people who "identify market opportunities" rather than feel problems in their bones. Hassan is the CRO, not the CEO, so the company's center of gravity is Hodara's technical vision, not Hassan's deal-making. But I notice the dossier contains no stories about either founder. No evidence of creative workarounds when things went wrong, no "we built the prototype over a weekend" anecdote, no sign of the cereal-box desperation that told me the Airbnb founders could convince anyone of anything. Landing enterprise clients within four months of inception is a real signal — you don't get Disney to use your product by being mediocre — but it reads more like competent execution than the slightly-too-intense obsessiveness I look for when I'm deciding where to put my own money.

Kuli's core problem is that "AI for influencer marketing" sounds good to everyone. The TAM is growing at 33-37% CAGR. TechCrunch writes about this space constantly. CreatorIQ has raised $80.8M and generates $70.7M in revenue. GRIN raised $145M at a $910M valuation. When I apply the framework I laid out in "Black Swan Farming" — that the best investments sit at the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea" — Kuli falls outside that intersection entirely. Nobody hears "we use AI to analyze influencer video content for brands" and says "that will never work." They say "oh, that's smart." Which means the returns have already been competed away, or will be soon. The multimodal video analysis angle is a genuine technical differentiator today, but OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic all offer video understanding APIs that lower the barrier quarterly. CreatorIQ already uses AI for database curation. Kuli's dossier itself acknowledges the commoditization risk — the defensibility lies in the application layer, not the raw capability. That's a thinner moat than Stripe's PCI compliance and bank integration schlep, which repelled competitors for years because the work was genuinely miserable, not merely technically challenging.

Kuli's learning rate is hard to assess from the available evidence. The EPFL Investor Day 2024 pitch, the VivaTech 2025 Microsoft showcase, and the Spring 2026 YC batch show steady progression through institutional programs, but I see no evidence of product pivots, weekly growth metrics, or the kind of rapid iteration that signals a team learning faster than its competitors. The product appears to be the same concept from inception — video analysis for influencer vetting — which can mean either that they got it right the first time or that they haven't tested hard enough alternatives. Hodara's 4.8/5 rating on Influencer Marketing Hub and Microsoft's GenAI Studio selection validate product quality, but validation from industry programs is a different signal than validation from users who can't stop using your product even in its crappiest form.

The bull case for Kuli is real and I want to be honest about it. Hodara's organic domain pain is genuine — not manufactured. The enterprise traction with Disney, Supercell, and Havas at pre-seed with three people is objectively impressive. The timing alignment between multimodal AI viability (2023-2024) and the widening gap between creator video volume and manual review capacity creates a legitimate window. And there's a version of the ClassDojo exception here: Kuli operates in a domain (brand marketing) that pure hackers avoid, which could mean less technical competition. If Kuli is the company that defines "content-level intelligence" as the new standard for influencer platforms — replacing the metadata-and-filter architecture that CreatorIQ and GRIN are built on — the innovator's dilemma dynamics could protect them while incumbents struggle to rearchitect. That's a plausible story.

But I don't invest on plausible stories. I invest on founder quality that makes me believe a team will find a way through whatever the market throws at them. Kuli's dossier gives me one strong signal — Hodara's organic problem discovery — and otherwise neutral-to-concerning evidence. The idea is consensus-good in a consensus-hot market. The co-founder team includes a KPMG consultant. The competitive landscape is crowded with well-funded players. And I have no stories, no evidence of the "relentlessly resourceful" quality that distinguishes the founders who make it from the ones who merely have good credentials. The condition that would flip me: if Hodara showed me specific examples of Kuli doing things incumbents couldn't replicate — not because of technology, but because of the willingness to do painful, unscalable work that their video analysis enables — that would change the calculus. The problem isn't the idea. The problem is that I can't see the animal.
