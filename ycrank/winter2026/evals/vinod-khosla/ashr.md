# Ashr -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Ashr: at least four funded startups and multiple open-source projects are building essentially the same thing — testing infrastructure for AI agents. Janus at YC, Coval with $3.3M from General Catalyst, Maxim AI, LangWatch, Langfuse open-source. When I backed Juniper Networks to challenge Cisco, nobody else was crazy enough to try it. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routers in telecom would never happen. That dismissal was the signal. With Ashr, I see the opposite — universal consensus that agent testing is needed, and a crowd racing to fill the gap. Consensus is the enemy of transformational returns. When everyone agrees on the problem and the approach, you get a crowded market with compressed outcomes, not a 2,500x return.

Let me start where I always start: if Ashr succeeds completely — if they become the dominant testing platform for AI agents — what changes in the world? Companies ship slightly more reliable agents. Fewer bugs reach production. QA teams save hours. This is a legitimate pain point, and I don't dispute the market exists. But the consequence of success is a solid SaaS business serving developer teams, not a restructuring of how an industry operates. The AI-enabled testing market is projected at $4.6 billion by 2034. That's a real market, but it's not a trillion-dollar system being made obsolete. Nobody's reclaiming farmland, achieving fusion, or replacing a fundamental human activity. They're making an existing workflow — testing — marginally better for a new category of software. I've spent forty years separating "useful business" from "world-changing company," and this sits firmly in the first bucket. Some problems demand a solution regardless of commercial uncertainty. Agent testing is commercially sensible but does not keep me up at night with the urgency of unsolved necessity.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. AI agents are proliferating on an exponential curve — the agentic AI market projected to grow at 44.6% CAGR to $93 billion by 2032. Only half of organizations even run offline evaluations today, meaning the testing gap is real and growing. If Ashr captures the evaluation layer for this entire category, they could become the Datadog of the agent era — and Datadog is a $30+ billion company. Rohan Kulkarni has a prior exit as CTO of Ask Geri, showing he can build and ship product. The multimodal scope (voice, image, file generation) is broader than most competitors. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) the testing layer consolidates to one or two winners rather than fragmenting, (b) incumbents like Datadog and LangChain don't simply absorb this functionality, and (c) Ashr specifically wins despite starting with no pricing, no visible revenue, and academic customers. Each of those is a real bet, and their conjunction is unlikely — particularly given that Coval's founder was a tech lead at Waymo bringing simulation methodology, and Janus's founders have Microsoft eval framework experience. This is a field where the competitors have equally compelling founder-market narratives.

The founders are technically capable Berkeley graduates building in a domain they know well — Shreyas built AI agents for two years, Rohan shipped an AI product that got acquired. But in my framework, domain insiders executing the playbook everyone agrees on is not the signal I invest behind. I look for outsiders who don't know what the industry considers impossible. These founders know exactly what the industry considers the problem, and they're building exactly what the industry expects as the solution. There's no contrarian courage here — no one is laughing at them, no expert is telling them it can't be done. That absence of dismissal tells me the ambition has been calibrated to what's comfortable rather than what's transformational. The team is also homogeneous — two Berkeley engineers with overlapping backgrounds — with no evidence of the deliberate "gene pool engineering" I look for: identifying the top risks and recruiting specifically to address them.

The technology itself — synthetic user journey generation for agent testing — is a reasonable product but not a structural disruption. It makes testing faster, not obsolete. It doesn't create a 10x cost advantage or a capability that didn't previously exist. The dossier itself notes "no defensibility signals found in public sources," and the product is listed at $0 with no visible paid tiers. When a company's differentiation rests on "multimodal support and SDK simplicity" — features any well-funded competitor can replicate in a quarter — I don't see technology moat. I see a feature race in a commoditizing space. These founders are too smart to spend their careers making agent QA 30% faster. I'd rather see them aim at something that terrifies them.
