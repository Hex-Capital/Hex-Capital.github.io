# Harbor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Albert Cai ran clinical trials and regulatory strategy at Biolinq through FDA De Novo clearance for the Shine intradermal CGM, and that single line is the most interesting sentence in the Harbor dossier. The signature here is a founder who genuinely lived the EDC pain on a regulated medical-device program, paired with a product I cannot install, ride, scan, or photograph this weekend — a high-conviction founder bet wrapped inside a B2B clinical SaaS I am structurally bad at touching. Harbor sits in the lane where my lens works hardest on the founder and weakest on the artifact.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the rung I hold against Harbor on lived experience: someone who shipped a hard thing in an adjacent domain before pitching the current company, with the social license to walk into the room. Albert Cai on the Biolinq Shine program is not Palmer in scale, but it is the same shape — he was the person filling out the eCRFs, fighting Medidata-style per-subject pricing, and shepherding a De Novo through FDA, not an ex-FAANG PM who discovered EDC pain in a McKinsey memo. Where Harbor diverges from the Anduril rung is consequence magnitude: Palmer's prior product was Oculus-at-scale, while Albert's prior was a single device clearance, so the credential is real but smaller-caliber. This is the criterion Harbor most clearly wins, and it is why I am not in the Pass tier.

Crusoe in 2018 was three uninvestable things stapled together and almost nobody understood the energy-arbitrage thesis underneath, which is why the round was underpriced and the founder had social license on the well pads. The Harbor pitch is closer to the opposite failure mode: clinical EDC is a category every YC batch in the last four years has produced an attempt at, the "LLM parses a PDF" mechanism is widely available, and Medidata, Veeva, Oracle, Castor, Florence, Medrio, and Medable are all named competitors before I even start naming the AI-clinical entrants. Harbor is unsexy, which I like, but unsexy is not the same as non-consensus — flat-fee pricing against Medidata's per-subject model is the one genuinely contrarian wedge in the deck, and it is also the one a Veeva product manager could copy in a quarter.

Harbor's governance reads clean on paper: Albert Cai is named CEO, Nathan Leung is named CTO, two co-founders with a shared University of Michigan overlap, no third co-founder muddying the equity, no public language about top-grading or CEO succession. The mechanism that compounds here over 18 months is whether the CEO/CTO split survives the first enterprise pharma RFP where the CTO wants to rebuild the extraction stack and the CEO has already signed an MSA promising a 1-week build — and on a two-person team the tiebreaker conversation has either happened or it has not. The dossier shows no evidence either way, which under my default reads as "they haven't had it yet," but the clarity of the CEO title earns Harbor partial credit I would not give a 50/50 partnership.

The Harbor product sits in 21 CFR Part 11, HIPAA, and GDPR with an included validation package, which is the unsexy-regulated lane that produced Mindbloom and Vigil Labs in my book and the lane other investors refuse to enter on aesthetic grounds. The counterfactual that would flip this dimension a full tier is hardware — if Harbor were shipping a site-deployed capture appliance with a regulatory submission attached, this would score like a frontier swing, but it is software-only, and the moat compounds in audit trails and customer switching costs rather than in atoms. Regulated, yes; capital-intensive, no.

Harbor's customer list — Biolinq, BioDynamik, NOVA Genomics, Apollon, Attuned HealthTech, Kalevala Therapeutics, University Hospitals — is the most tangible evidence in the dossier, but I cannot do my Flock Safety move on this product. I cannot install an EDC in my own life over a weekend, I cannot read a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons that tells me a trial coordinator is using Harbor, and the only person who can tell me whether "Magic Capture" actually hits clinical-primary-endpoint accuracy is a sponsor under NDA. This is the developer-tools failure mode of my sensory apparatus — Harbor exists, real sites use it, but the artifact lives behind a regulated firewall I cannot reach into.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Harbor reaches roughly $2-5M ARR across 30-60 small device and academic studies, the flat-fee pricing wins them the SMB and Biolinq-adjacent biotech segment but never penetrates top-20 pharma, and Harbor gets acquired by Veeva, Medable, or a Dassault-Medidata tuck-in in the $40-80M range as a Magic-Build feature graft. The mechanism is that Albert's De Novo credential opens the medical-device door but does not open the Pfizer door, and the LLM-extraction wedge is too commoditizable to outrun a Veeva product team with three years of runway and the existing Vault Clinical contract.

The bull case that stings: Albert Cai is exactly the founder profile I claim to fund — outsider trajectory, lived the problem on a specific regulated device program, attacking incumbents whose per-subject pricing creates real channel conflict against any flat-fee response, in a domain where 21 CFR Part 11 validation is a real switching-cost moat once a sponsor goes live mid-trial. The Anduril precedent says I should weight the lived-experience signal heavily even when the round looks like consensus YC vertical SaaS, and the Vigil Labs precedent says regulated finance/health compounds in ways spreadsheet investors miss. The dossier evidence that wins the argument back for me is the absence of a tactile artifact and the presence of seven named direct competitors with $5.8B and $2.7B revenue behind them — this is a fight where Harbor's founder edge has to carry against capital I cannot out-patient. The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict to Invest: a signed pilot with a top-20 pharma sponsor (not an academic site, not a Biolinq-adjacent device startup), because that is the evidence the De Novo credential travels beyond Albert's existing network.
