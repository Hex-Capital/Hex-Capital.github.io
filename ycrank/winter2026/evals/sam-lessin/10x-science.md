# 10x Science -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The founder credentials here are genuinely remarkable — a postdoc from Carolyn Bertozzi's lab (2022 Nobel laureate in Chemistry), 37+ peer-reviewed publications in the exact analytical technique the platform automates, a Damon Runyon Cancer Research Fellow, paired with a Broad Institute proteomics alumnus and a CTO who was the second employee at Nooks and has already been through YC once. On the "sane" axis, this team maxes out. Trained under a Nobel laureate in the precise domain you're building software for? That's about as credentialed as it gets. But here's my problem: the idea isn't insane. It's obvious. "Use AI to automate slow, manual mass spectrometry analysis in biopharma" is what every scientifically literate ML engineer thinks of when they look at this workflow. The tags on the YC page say it plainly — SaaS, B2B, Biotech, Enterprise Software, AI. This is a sane person pursuing a sane idea, and that combination rarely produces the returns I'm looking for. The best investments come from rational operators chasing things that make other investors uncomfortable. Nothing about "automate protein characterization with AI" makes anyone uncomfortable.

The structural moat question is where this really unravels for me. Strip away the biotech terminology and what you have is an enterprise software product whose entire value proposition is AI-powered analysis of data generated by someone else's instruments. The company's moat IS the AI. That's exactly the category I've been arguing is dissolving. Would this business exist without AI? No — it would be a consulting shop. My "cherry on top" test asks whether removing the AI layer leaves a still-viable company, and here removing AI leaves nothing. Meanwhile, the companies that own the instruments — Thermo Fisher at $45 billion in revenue, SCIEX under Danaher, Waters — own the customer relationships, the data formats, the validation history, and the distribution. When these incumbents decide to integrate equivalent ML capabilities into their bundled software (and they will, because the AI is getting cheaper by the month), 10x Science's standalone position becomes extremely precarious.

The bull case I genuinely respect is the consolidation timing argument. Genedata got swallowed by Danaher in August 2024. Protein Metrics got absorbed into Dotmatics in 2021. When analytical software gets folded into conglomerates, innovation dies — the acquirers optimize for hardware attach rates, not software quality. That creates a real window for a vendor-neutral, AI-native challenger. If 10x Science can become the cross-platform standard that works with every manufacturer's mass spectrometers, they could occupy an infrastructure position that the instrument vendors can't replicate without cannibalizing their own hardware lock-in. The $150K/team/month savings claim, if it holds up in production, represents the kind of value creation that makes enterprise buyers break through switching costs. And the regulatory validation requirements in biopharma — once you're validated in a customer's GxP workflow, you're extremely sticky. For this to be a great investment, all three things have to be true simultaneously: the vendor-neutral positioning holds, the regulatory moat gets built before incumbents wake up, and the domain-specific data from customer runs compounds faster than general-purpose AI catches up. That's three conditional bets stacked on each other.

Two of three founders still hold academic positions at Stanford — Roberts as a postdoc, Reiter as a Ph.D. student. I look for the combination of personal magnetism and operational obsession in founders. Academic moonlighting is a signal of hedging, not conviction. The company isn't hiring yet. They launched in February 2026 and are "onboarding customers" — which could mean anything from signed contracts to free pilots. In regulated biopharma, the distance between "onboarding" and validated deployment generating revenue is measured in quarters, not weeks. This is a team that needs to decide whether they're building a company or running a research project, and nothing in the dossier tells me they've fully crossed that threshold.

I also can't ignore the brand confusion with 10x Genomics, a publicly traded company. In a world where your early growth depends on SEO, conference presence, and word-of-mouth in a niche scientific community that already associates "10x" with genomics, you're starting with a self-imposed marketing handicap. It's a small thing, but it signals something about commercial instinct — or the lack of it.

The narrative can expand — from protein characterization to all proteomics analysis to all mass spectrometry workflows — but it's bounded by the analytical chemistry universe. This isn't an infinity story. It's a large but finite niche within biopharma tooling. And without confirmed revenue, real customer commitments, or evidence of organic adoption, the downside cushion is essentially the team's credentials and their Stanford network. That's meaningful socially but not economically. Bottom line: extraordinary founders, consensus idea, software moat in a world where software moats are evaporating. I'd want to see them before passing definitively — the team quality earns that — but the structural fit with how I invest just isn't there.
