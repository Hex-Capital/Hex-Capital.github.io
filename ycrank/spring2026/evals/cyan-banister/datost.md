# Datost -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Datost: Salesforce paid $27.7 billion for Slack. Salesforce owns Tableau. Datost is building an AI data analyst that lives inside Slack and replaces the need for Tableau dashboards. You are building the product that the company who owns your distribution platform has every reason and every resource to build themselves. That's not contrarian risk — that's structural vulnerability dressed up as a wedge. When I backed Uber, the taxi commissions couldn't build their own ride-hailing app because they were bureaucracies, not technology companies. When I looked at Affirm, the credit card companies couldn't easily unbundle their opacity because transparency destroyed their business model. But Salesforce is a technology company that already has AI teams, already has Slack APIs, and already has Tableau's data connectors. The question isn't whether they'll build this — it's when.

When I sit down with founders, the first thing I want to hear is their story. Not the pitch, not the TAM slide — the biographical crucible that made this problem inescapable for them. What I find in this dossier is two twenty-one-year-old software engineers who worked together at Traba, a staffing technology company, and built a Slack bot for querying databases. There's no story here about being trapped on the wrong side of a data bottleneck that cost something real — a family member denied a loan because an analyst was too busy to pull the numbers, a business decision that went wrong because the data was locked behind a SQL query nobody could write in time. The founding story reads as "we saw a market opportunity in AI analytics," which is exactly the pattern I've learned to recognize as career optimization rather than mission inevitability. Maceo's LinkedIn headline still reads "Software Engineer Intern." I'm not judging youth — I was rebuilding my life at their age. But I need to see what forged you, and I'm not finding it.

The deeper problem is that there's no invisible system here. The company's tagline is "democratize data," which sounds like it should activate my pattern recognition — I've spent my career backing companies that tear down walls between people and the information or services they need. But enterprise BI isn't an opaque, extractive structure. Nobody is being harmed by Tableau's existence. Data analysts running ad-hoc queries for their teammates is a workflow inefficiency, not a hidden tax on vulnerable people. Compare this to Ownwell, which attacks a property tax system that literally overcharges homeowners who don't know how to appeal, or Affirm, which exposed how credit card companies bury the true cost of borrowing. Datost makes it slightly easier for a product manager at a well-funded startup to check last week's conversion numbers without pinging their data team. That's convenience, not liberation.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because I try to find the thread that could prove me wrong. If these founders nail the "AI analyst with its own computer" — a true sandboxed execution environment that doesn't just generate SQL but runs complex multi-step analyses autonomously — they could be building something closer to infrastructure than a wrapper. The Slack-native distribution creates genuine viral mechanics: one person asks a question in a channel, the answer appears, and suddenly everyone wants to talk to the bot. If organizational context accumulates — schema understanding, terminology mapping, institutional knowledge about what metrics actually mean — that could create real switching costs. And the timing is genuinely right: LLMs crossed the reliability threshold for autonomous code execution in the last eighteen months. In a world where these founders turn out to be unexpectedly resilient and the execution environment becomes genuinely deep, this could be a meaningful company. But even in that scenario, I can't answer the Salesforce question. When your landlord can build your product, you need a moat they can't cross, and I don't see one.

This lands squarely in the space that Lee Jacobs and I explicitly avoid — an AI application layer on top of foundation models without proprietary data, infrastructure depth, or domain moats. The core capability, natural language to SQL to results, is available to any developer calling the Claude or GPT API. Julius AI already has two million users and $10 million from Bessemer. Equals has $23 million from a16z. The incumbents are shipping. This isn't a hard space that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable — it's a consensus-attractive category where capital is actively competing for deals. Nothing about this idea would make a room of VCs shift in their chairs. It would make them nod and reach for their checkbooks, which in my experience is often the more dangerous signal.
