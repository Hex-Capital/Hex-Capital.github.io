# Modern Industrials -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Modern Industrials is who they're building for -- and who they are. Austin Mao was top 5% at McKinsey. Vatsal Bhargava ran ML for Google Ads Bidding. Ankit Bhargava built agentic AI at xAI. These are genuinely strong technical people. And they're building quoting automation for lumber distributors. That mismatch is the entire story of this company. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas -- it's to look for problems you have yourself. When I look at these three founders, I don't see anyone who has ever worked in building materials distribution, processed a quote request, or argued with a distributor's ERP system. I see three smart people who identified an under-digitized market. That's the McKinsey move, not the Dropbox move. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Where is the equivalent story for Modern Industrials? The dossier doesn't contain one, and in my experience, that's because it doesn't exist.

Now, LBM distribution is a genuine schlep. Faxes, PDFs, legacy ERPs built in the 1990s, relationship-driven sales cycles with gruff purchasing managers who've been ordering 2x4s the same way for thirty years -- this is exactly the kind of domain that makes most AI founders reach for a more comfortable problem. So Modern Industrials gets real credit for pointing themselves at something unglamorous. The $450B+ distribution industry with minimal software penetration is a real opportunity. But here's what I keep coming back to: Ventura, a YC W26 company, is building essentially the same product for the same vertical, and Ventura's CEO has a prior exit in wholesale distribution SaaS. That's the founder-market fit I look for. That's someone who lived in the future of this industry and built what was missing. Modern Industrials is competing against organic discovery with analytical selection. I've seen that contest before, and the founders who felt the pain usually win.

The co-founder dynamics deserve attention. Vatsal and Ankit Bhargava are brothers, and Austin Mao has been their best friend for twenty-plus years. That's a strong foundation -- co-founder conflict kills startups, and these three have a relationship that predates any business ambition. And the individual builder signals are real: Vatsal shipped an NBA injury intelligence platform to $10K MRR with 500+ users as a side project. Ankit generated $200K+ in algorithmic trading profits on Kalshi and Polymarket. These are people who build things that work. But building things that work and building things that lumber distributors desperately want are different challenges. The team has left prestigious positions -- Vatsal left Google, Ankit left xAI -- which shows commitment. What it doesn't show is the obsessive relationship with this specific problem that separates the Stripe-level outcomes from the acqui-hires.

The strongest bull case for Modern Industrials requires believing that technical AI capability matters more than domain expertise in this particular race. If parsing unstructured quote requests -- faxed spec sheets, emailed PDFs, phone orders -- is primarily an ML problem rather than a domain-knowledge problem, then the xAI/Google Ads/Uber ML pedigree is the right weapon. And Vatsal's claim that they've "already gone live with national players" suggests fast execution, though without customer names, revenue, or retention data, I can't distinguish a real deployment from a friendly pilot. The timing argument is legitimate: LLMs crossed a document-understanding threshold in 2024-2025 that made this technically feasible for the first time. If Modern Industrials can learn the domain faster than domain experts can build the AI, they win. That's plausible but not the way I'd bet.

Against my current lean, I should test the ClassDojo exception -- building for a non-technical audience in a schlep domain that other founders avoid. ClassDojo reached unicorn status selling to teachers, a customer segment as far from Hacker News as lumber distributors. The override required exceptional execution quality and deep commitment to the customer. Modern Industrials could follow that path if Austin's McKinsey operational background translates into enterprise sales effectiveness and the Bhargava brothers' ML capabilities produce a genuinely superior product. But ClassDojo's founders were deeply embedded in education before they started. The exception still requires domain intimacy; it just relaxes the "hackers building for hackers" constraint. Modern Industrials doesn't clear that bar yet.

My verdict lands on the Organic Problem Discovery dimension. Modern Industrials has the technical talent to build excellent AI quoting software and the co-founder relationship to survive the hard years. What's missing is the origin story -- the moment of personal frustration with LBM distribution that would tell me these founders understand the problem at the level Stripe understood payments. Austin Mao identified an opportunity; he didn't stumble into a calling. Vatsal's LinkedIn post describes the $450B+ market opportunity, not a specific distributor whose broken workflow kept him up at night. Combined with Ventura's superior founder-market fit in the same vertical, Modern Industrials reads as a capable team that chose a good market rather than a team that was chosen by an irresistible problem.
