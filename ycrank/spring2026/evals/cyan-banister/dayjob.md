# Dayjob -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$450K in ARR seven months after first sales, from a five-person team selling AI scheduling to skip hire operators — that's the number that stopped me from flipping past Dayjob's dossier. In waste management, where technology adoption is glacially slow and planners still run fleets off spreadsheets, that ramp is evidence that George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks found a genuine nerve. The pivot from their earlier product Gaea — a waste ERP that generated $150K in revenue — to Dayjob's scheduling agent shows they did something I respect: they sat inside the industry for eighteen months, listened to what operators actually needed, and rebuilt around the real pain point. That's not nothing. But execution velocity and biographical inevitability are different animals, and my framework starts with the latter.

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching their UK grocery business — directly relevant logistics experience. Fred Fooks did supply chain analytics at Deloitte. Both attended Oxford. When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," what I'd hear is: "We were at Deliveroo and Deloitte, saw how logistics scheduling could be modernized, started a waste management ERP, learned the industry, and found the scheduling problem." That's a smart career path. It's rational domain entry. But it's not the biographical forge I build conviction around — nobody in this founding story was broken and rebuilt by the waste industry, nobody grew up watching their family's hauling business drown in manual routing, nobody came to this problem because their life left them no alternative. Dayjob's founders found the problem through professional exposure, not personal compulsion. The Gaea pivot and Deliveroo tenure prevent this from being empty credentialism, but they don't transform it into inevitability.

Dayjob operates in skip hire logistics — a vertical that is deeply unsexy but not remotely contrarian. Nobody shifts in their chair when you describe AI scheduling for waste collection. Compare this to Flock Safety, where surveillance cameras provoked genuine civil liberties anxiety, or Contraline, where male birth control was too stigmatized for most VCs to even take the meeting. Dayjob's space is under-served, but under-served and contrarian are structurally different signals. No investor is saying "that's crazy" or "that's dangerous" — they're saying "that sounds niche," which is a different kind of rejection entirely. The competitive landscape confirms the consensus nature: Routific raised $1.8M for general-purpose routing, AMCS raised $225M for full-stack waste ERP, FarEye raised $152M for last-mile delivery. Dayjob isn't entering a space other investors fear; it's entering one they find too small.

Dayjob's claim to making an invisible system visible is the weakest dimension in my framework for this company. The website states that planning teams lose 65 hours weekly to admin, and Coastal Recycling's transport manager reports a "5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour." These are real operational improvements — but operational inefficiency is not the same as structural opacity. When I backed Affirm, the hidden system was credit card interest rates that deliberately confused consumers. When I backed Ownwell, it was a property tax assessment apparatus that extracted value from homeowners who didn't know they could appeal. Dayjob's customers — waste management operators — aren't being exploited by a hidden system. They know their scheduling is manual. They know they lose hours to admin. They're choosing between bad tools. There's no information asymmetry being shattered, no extractive gatekeeper being disintermediated. Dayjob makes waste operators more efficient; it doesn't expose a power structure that was invisible to the people harmed by it.

The strongest case for Dayjob maps to my infrastructure-at-inflection pattern, and it's worth building the bull case honestly. Three named ERP integrations — Weighsoft, PurGo, Wastelogics — create real switching costs once deployed. The VWS partnership, announced January 2026, formalizes co-marketing into PurGo's installed base. If Dayjob becomes the scheduling intelligence layer across every waste management ERP, each new integration makes the platform harder to displace — the Flexport pattern of logistics network effects applied to waste collection. The ten-year imagination exercise yields: Dayjob starts with skip hire, expands to general waste collection, then to bulk haulage, then becomes the real-time scheduling backbone for all industrial fleet operations. That's a path to infrastructure. But the technology itself — AI route optimization — is not proprietary. Routific, FarEye, or AMCS adding a real-time rescheduling module could narrow Dayjob's differentiation to ERP-agnostic deployment, which matters less for AMCS's own installed base. The moat is forming around integration depth and domain tuning, not around the core technology — and integration moats in a narrow UK vertical are thin ice for a decade-long bet.

The condition that would flip me: if George Postlethwaite told me a story about why waste logistics broke something in him personally — or if Dayjob's scheduling layer had already crossed into a second vertical with the same ERP-integration playbook, proving the horizontal infrastructure thesis before I had to imagine it. Either the biographical inevitability or the infrastructure evidence would change my calculus. Right now, Dayjob is a well-executed niche B2B SaaS company with impressive early traction, competent founders who found product-market fit through disciplined iteration, and a distribution strategy through ERP partnerships that could compound. But it lacks the biographical forge, the contrarian charge, and the invisible-system disruption that my highest-conviction bets require. The decisive dimensions are Founder's Biographical Forge — where smart domain exposure falls short of the inevitability I need — and Invisible System Made Visible — where operational efficiency improvement does not constitute structural revelation.
