# OctaPulse -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

What immediately catches my attention here is the physical infrastructure angle -- delta robots with compliant grippers operating in corrosive fish hatchery environments, handling live animals one at a time. This is the kind of business that makes pure-software VCs physically uncomfortable. You can't replicate a robot in a wet, corrosive facility by shipping better code. That operational ugliness is exactly what I look for -- it's the Craftwork pattern applied to aquaculture. The vision models are commodity (YOLO variants, off-the-shelf Luxonis cameras, Nvidia Jetson edge compute), but the integrated system -- robots that safely grip a live fish, inspect it, and sort it in under 30 seconds in an environment that destroys electronics -- is genuinely hard to copy from a desk in San Francisco. Every deployment teaches them something about gripper durability, camera calibration in humid conditions, and species-specific anatomy that doesn't transfer to a competitor without similar field time.

Where my framework gets less enthusiastic is the founder-idea calibration. Singh's engineering background -- ASML, Nvidia, Tesla, Toyota, plus CMU robotics -- is legitimately strong for building physical automation systems. That's the "sane" part, and it's real. Grech brings commercial chops from Bloomberg and an MBA, which matters for selling to conservative aquaculture operators. But neither founder has deep aquaculture domain experience. They discovered this market through a CMU entrepreneurship happy hour, not because they grew up on fish farms or spent a decade in the industry. The "Future Leader" recognition from the National Fisheries Institute is a positive signal but wasn't independently verified. More importantly, the idea itself -- automating a manual inspection process in a growing industry -- isn't truly non-consensus. It's niche and overlooked, which is different from insane. Nobody thinks automating hatchery QA is a bad idea; they just haven't thought about it. That's a lower-voltage version of the signal I look for. Compare this to Teamshares proposing to buy hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership -- people actively argued that was impossible. Fish inspection robots provoke indifference, not skepticism.

The structural moat question is where OctaPulse scores best against my framework. This business gets stronger, not weaker, as AI arrives. Better vision models improve their inspection accuracy without enabling a pure-software competitor to eliminate the need for physical robots on-site. The proprietary labeled dataset compounds with each deployment and each species -- though with only one production customer (Riverence, the largest North American trout producer), that dataset is embryonic. The switching cost logic is interesting: once a hatchery's selective breeding program depends on OctaPulse's historical phenotypic data, moving to a competitor means losing that genetic intelligence. But this lock-in is theoretical until they have multi-year data across multiple species.

The bull case I have to take seriously: aquaculture is the fastest-growing food production sector on earth, hatchery QA is a genuine bottleneck that nobody else is automating with robotics (competitors like Aquabyte and Umitron focus on in-water pen monitoring, a completely different technical problem), and OctaPulse has a six-figure contract with a major producer at the pre-seed stage. If they can prove species generalization from trout to salmon to sea bass, the deployment playbook could scale across thousands of hatcheries globally. The "land in hatchery QA, expand into full hatchery automation" path has real logic. And the M&A activity in the space -- Aquabyte acquired by Vitruvian, Observe Technologies by AKVA Group -- validates that acquirers are paying real money for aquaculture AI companies, which provides a floor on outcomes even if the infinity story doesn't materialize.

But I keep coming back to the protocol test. OctaPulse is building an application -- a very good, very hard-to-replicate application -- but not a new primitive that others build on top of. Fish hatchery inspection robots don't create an ecosystem. The narrative can stretch from "hatchery QA" to "aquaculture automation" to maybe "food production automation," but each step requires building fundamentally new capabilities rather than naturally expanding the existing platform. That's linear scaling, not compounding. And the co-founders meeting at a startup networking event to pursue a market neither had deep ties to -- that pattern doesn't match the founder-market intimacy I see in my best bets. It's not disqualifying, but it means the domain credibility has to be earned through execution rather than brought to the table.

Net: OctaPulse has genuine structural moat characteristics -- physical operations, harsh environments, compounding data, and AI as a tailwind rather than a threat. The six-figure contract at pre-seed is meaningfully above the baseline. But the founder-idea pairing is "competent people with a logical niche idea" rather than "sane people with an insane idea," and the company is building a vertical application, not a new primitive. It's a real business with a defensible position in a growing market, which makes it interesting -- but it doesn't trip the non-consensus wire that drives my highest-conviction bets.
