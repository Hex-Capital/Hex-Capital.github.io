# Polymath -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Polymath is not the founders or the technology but the competitive field they're entering. Mechanize already has Nat Friedman, Daniel Gross, Patrick Collison, and Jeff Dean as backers and is working with Anthropic. Prime Intellect has raised $70M. Scale AI is building Agent-RLVR on top of their existing relationships with every frontier lab. Idler -- another YC company -- has already closed a multimillion-dollar contract with a leading foundation lab. And Wing VC projects this market consolidates to 3-5 winners by 2030. When I see a 2-person pre-seed team entering a space where this much capital and talent has already assembled, the question becomes very specific: what do you know that all of these people don't? The dossier doesn't answer that.

The RL environment space became a recognized market category in 2025 when The Information reported Anthropic was planning to spend over a billion dollars on environments. That's the kind of signal that spawns dozens of companies in months. When I said the most impressive YC companies I'd seen recently were not working on AI, this is exactly the pattern I was cautioning against -- a category that feels urgent because it's in the news, not because you stumbled into it through your own work. The YC page says the founders have "years of experience post-training frontier models in industry," which is relevant. But there's a difference between having worked in a domain and having organically discovered a problem that nobody else sees. Every engineer who has post-trained models knows that environment generation is slow and labor-intensive. That's not a secret. It's common knowledge that has attracted a half-dozen well-funded teams. The schlep here -- building complex multi-service sandbox environments -- is real, but it's no longer hiding in plain sight. It's hiding in broad daylight with a spotlight on it.

To be fair to Polymath, they've shipped something real. Horizon-SWE is a substantive piece of engineering: a sandboxed production-grade environment with a 20,000+ commit monorepo, MCP servers for Linear, Notion, Slack, Prometheus, Grafana, Sentry, a running full-stack application with databases and caches and queues, and a synthetic traffic generator. They benchmarked eight frontier models against it. That's not a slide deck -- that's a working system built by people who know what they're doing. Dylan Ma's ML background at Hume AI and Naren Yenuganti's infrastructure experience at Amazon and Plaid is a reasonable combination for this problem. The technical founders-who-build signal is present. If I had to evaluate just the benchmark in isolation, I'd say these are competent engineers who can ship.

Here's the bull case I have to take seriously: if Polymath's generative approach to environment creation actually works -- producing realistic, diverse environments from text descriptions alone -- that's a fundamentally different cost structure than what Scale AI or Surge AI can offer with their contractor armies. There's a genuine innovator's dilemma at play: incumbents making billions from human labor won't easily cannibalize their own model. And the market is massive and growing at 28% CAGR. If Polymath can demonstrate that automated generation produces environments of comparable quality to hand-built ones, they could be the low-cost producer in a market where the largest buyer may spend a billion dollars next year. The timing argument is strong. The technical argument has potential. But "could" and "if" are doing a lot of work in that paragraph.

What worries me is that there are no disclosed customers, no revenue signals, no LOIs -- while a direct competitor in the same YC batch already has a multimillion-dollar contract. The total addressable buyer pool is maybe five frontier labs. In markets with this few customers, relationships and reputation matter enormously, and Mechanize already has the Anthropic relationship locked down. The path from "published a benchmark" to "won a contract against Mechanize and Scale AI" requires a specific story about why these two founders will beat teams with ten times their resources and a head start. I don't see that story yet. The benchmark is clever as a credibility play -- it gets Polymath's name in front of the labs evaluating their own models -- but a benchmark is a calling card, not a business. Building one impressive environment by hand is fundamentally different from automating the generation of thousands, which is their stated end goal. That gap is unvalidated.

I keep coming back to the category dynamics. The buyers are concentrated. The competitors are well-funded and ahead. The idea is consensus. Everyone who reads TechCrunch in 2025-2026 knows that RL environments are the next big infrastructure play. That doesn't mean Polymath can't win -- Stripe entered a space with existing payment processors. But the Collisons had a genuinely novel insight about developer experience, and they were the first ones willing to deal with the banks. Polymath's insight -- automate environment generation with generative models -- is the same insight every competitor has. When the idea is shared and the market is small, execution alone isn't enough. You need an unfair advantage, and I don't see one here.
