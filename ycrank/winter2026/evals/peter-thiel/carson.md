# Carson -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

"More capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." This is the company's tagline, and it tells you everything you need to know. Carson defines itself through its competitors. This is not positioning -- it is mimetic surrender. When a company's identity is constructed as a negation of two rivals, you are watching Girard's mimetic triangle play out in real time: the company desires what its models desire, competes where its models compete, and becomes indistinguishable from the thing it opposes. A company built on "better than X and safer than Y" has no secret. It has a comparison chart.

The question I always ask first -- what important truth does this company see that most intelligent people reject? -- yields nothing. The implicit thesis is: "Enterprise sales teams need AI agents with better security controls and task-specific interfaces." This is not a secret. It is the single most consensus observation in B2B software. Every enterprise buyer wants security. Every CTO wants audit trails. Every vendor promises SOC 2 compliance. The thesis that enterprises will pay more for secure, vertical AI tools is shared by Clay ($3.1 billion valuation), Artisan ($46 million raised), Salesforce's Agentforce, and every other player converging on this exact space. When a dozen funded companies share your thesis, you do not have a thesis -- you have a market report. The AI sales automation space is a Girardian catastrophe: companies copying each other's feature sets, burning capital to acquire the same customers, and competing away all the margins that would justify the investment. This is the food delivery problem applied to enterprise software.

The team presents a genuine tension. Two founders -- Menon and Bhandari -- worked at Palantir, a company I co-founded. I know what Palantir teaches people: how to build technology that becomes structurally indispensable to institutions with existential stakes. Intelligence agencies cannot rip out Palantir's integration after years of deployment; the switching costs are civilizational. But Carson is not building for institutions with existential needs. It is building sales automation -- prospect research, slide generation, lead qualification. These are workflows where switching costs are measured in days, not years. The Palantir pedigree creates the illusion of enterprise depth without the underlying structural necessity. Bhandari's prior founding experience at Bolto (YC S23, $1M+ revenue) demonstrates execution capability, and the team's combined Harvard CS, Stanford ML, and enterprise engineering credentials are strong on paper. But execution capability in a competitive market is not the same as vision in a monopoly-creating one. Bhandari leaving an active, revenue-generating company to start something new in a different vertical raises a question about definite conviction -- is this a founder with a singular vision for the future, or a capable builder searching for the next opportunity?

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously. First, that dynamic UI generation -- creating task-specific interfaces from natural language rather than forcing work through a chat window -- represents a genuine product discontinuity that enterprises will pay substantially more for. This is the most interesting technical claim, and if the execution is extraordinary, it could create a meaningfully different user experience. Second, that Anthropic, despite having raised over $13 billion and owning Cowork, will choose not to add enterprise security controls -- an assumption that defies everything we know about how platform companies expand. Third, that Carson can achieve enough distribution to build switching costs before Clay, Salesforce, or Anthropic close the feature gap. Each of these beliefs is individually plausible; together, they require the kind of compounding optimism that usually ends in competitive destruction rather than monopoly.

The technology does not pass the 10x test. The dossier states it plainly: "The integration of adaptive interfaces with enterprise security in a single platform is a product design choice rather than a deep technical moat." Design choices are reproduced in quarters, not decades. Core components -- LLM orchestration, dynamic UI rendering, sales data enrichment -- are individually available and combinable by any competent engineering team. There is no proprietary model, no unique dataset, no physical-world technology barrier. This is a 1-to-n product improvement: a better wrapper around capabilities that already exist. When I backed SpaceX, the secret was that reusable rockets were physically possible but the entire aerospace industry believed otherwise. When I co-founded Palantir, the secret was that PayPal's fraud algorithms could transform intelligence analysis. Carson's implicit claim -- that enterprises want secure, well-designed AI tools -- is not something the industry rejects. It is something the industry is already building.

I pass. The company sits in a competitive market with no visible secret, no path to monopoly, and technology that is incremental rather than discontinuous. The founders are capable, but capability deployed in a mimetic market creates hard work, not outsized returns. A company that names its competitors in its own tagline has already conceded the frame to those competitors. The best companies do not compare themselves -- they render the comparison irrelevant.
