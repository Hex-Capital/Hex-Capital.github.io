# Hlabs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Hlabs isn't the product or the market -- it's that Paul Hetherington spent six years building Mystic AI, an ML infrastructure company, and then turned around and started a hardware supply chain business for robots. That pivot tells me something. Going from software to atoms is the hard direction. Most founders run the other way. I want to understand what happened at Mystic -- did it work, did it fail, did he get bored? The dossier is silent on this, and that silence matters because it's exactly the biographical thread I'd pull on in a meeting. What I do know: MEng in integrated mechanical and electrical engineering, UAV research, embedded systems work, commercial drone certification. He has the technical bones for this. But I don't have his story. I don't know what hardship forged his commitment to building a US robotics supply chain specifically. The credentials are relevant, but relevance isn't the same as inevitability -- and at seed stage, I'm looking for the founder who seems inevitable for this problem.

Forty orders from robotics companies in roughly four months, from a solo founder selling physical hardware, is genuinely impressive early traction. That's not signups on a waitlist -- those are customers who paid for circuit boards and put them into robots. When I think about the parallels in my portfolio, this maps to the infrastructure-at-inflection-point pattern I've seen work at Crusoe Energy, at Anduril, at Together.ai. The humanoid robotics wave is creating dozens of teams that all need the same core components -- actuators, controllers, compute modules -- and right now they're either sourcing from China or building custom from scratch. Hlabs sits at that exact pain point with a plug-and-play stack. The US manufacturing angle creates a real structural advantage for defense customers who can't source from Beijing, and ITAR compliance is the kind of regulatory moat I've learned to appreciate from my IronPort days. That part of the thesis genuinely resonates.

But here's where I get stuck: building components for the robotics boom is not a weird idea. It's a sensible idea. It's the kind of idea that, when you describe it to other investors, they nod along and say "yeah, that makes sense." And in my experience, the best investments don't make people nod -- they make people uncomfortable. SpaceX made people think Elon was out of his mind. Crusoe Energy sounded like a fever dream. Even Uber was considered borderline illegal. Hlabs is a logical picks-and-shovels play in what is currently one of the hottest categories in tech. Humanoid robots are on the cover of every magazine. Every major VC is writing checks into robotics companies. That consensus energy around the broader category makes me cautious, because my returns have come from being early to things that look ridiculous, not things that look smart.

The solo founder question is significant for a hardware business. I learned from HQ Trivia that co-founder conflict can kill a perfect product, but I've also learned that the long journey of building something hard requires people around you. Hardware means managing manufacturing, inventory, logistics, quality control, customer support, documentation, and product development -- simultaneously. Paul isn't hiring. He has $500K and a roadmap that includes four new product lines by the end of Q1 2026. That's ambitious for a team of ten, let alone a team of one. The bull case would be that Paul is the kind of founder who gets to revenue fast, proves the model, and attracts a team and capital on the strength of momentum. The 40+ orders suggest he might be exactly that. And if the robotics wave plays out the way the market expects, and if US manufacturing policy holds, and if he can hire fast enough after raising, Hlabs could become the default component supplier for an entire ecosystem of robot builders. That's a real business with real defensibility. The open-source software creating developer lock-in, the documentation site as a community hub, the hardware-software integration that traditional actuator companies can't replicate -- these are legitimate moat signals.

If I'm being honest with myself, the infrastructure pattern is there and it's the part of my portfolio I'm sometimes surprised by, because I think of myself as backing scrappy outsider founders but I keep writing checks into capital-intensive structural bets. This has that shape. But it's missing the biographical connection and the contrarian energy that typically accompany those bets. Paul may well be a magically weird person -- I just can't see it from what's in front of me. And the market he's serving is too popular right now for me to feel that jolt of conviction that tells me to move before the data exists. The data here actually looks decent. Which, paradoxically, makes me less certain rather than more.
