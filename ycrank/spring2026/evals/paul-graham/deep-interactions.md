# Deep Interactions -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Deep Interactions is the research-pedigreed UX team — Sruthi Viswanathan's Oxford DPhil in Human-AI Interaction is the load-bearing fact in the dossier — building a product they studied academically rather than one they coded into existence at 3am. That's the signature. The high dimension is organic-idea fit (she literally researched human-AI collaboration before pitching one); the low dimension is hacker genealogy (two Oxford DPhils, zero public GitHub trail). I have seen this shape before. It is the academic-to-startup translation problem, and the question is whether Sruthi turns into Drew Houston or whether Deep Interactions becomes a polished thesis demo with a Calendly link attached.

Dropbox (2007) is the load-bearing analogy for the organic-idea axis: Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT and built the thing he wished existed. Deep Interactions has a structurally similar origin — Sruthi spent years at Oxford and Naver Labs Europe studying exactly how humans and AI fail to share understanding, then built a tool for that failure mode. The mechanism is the same: founder lived inside the problem before naming it. Where Deep Interactions diverges from Dropbox is the form of the living-inside. Houston's pain was personal and physical (a missing thumb drive); Sruthi's is professional and observed (95% of enterprise AI pilots fail, per the launch page). Observed pain converts to product more slowly than felt pain, because the founder is one translation step removed from the user.

The pass example in my own file — the two-MBA AI-wrapper team with the glossy deck and no shipped artifact — is the failure mechanism I keep watching for on the hacker-genealogy axis. The dossier says explicitly: "GitHub: No public repos found" for Sruthi, "No public repos surfaced in search" for Neil Natarajan. Both are Oxford DPhils in human-centred computing, which is credential-coded, not hack-coded. UX research at Naver Labs and a DPhil advised by Sir Nigel Shadbolt is not the Patrick Collison Young Scientist Lisp prize; it is the polite version of technical lineage. Deep Interactions does not match the pass-example profile — they shipped a real product with 50K usage hits across 12 businesses — but the absence of any Hacker News footprint, open-source artifact, or named technical co-founder for an engineering-heavy product is the tell I cannot unsee. Hackers leave tracks. These founders left papers.

On animal resourcefulness, the dossier gives me 12 businesses live and 50,000+ "real-world usage hits," which is more than most P26 companies will show at launch but is not the cereal-box pattern. The compounding question is what happens over 18 months: does Sruthi convert design-partner usage into named Fortune 500 logos with disclosed contract values, or does the Calendly+Typeform funnel cap out at sub-$10K pilots while Lovable's $400M ARR engine eats the category? The funnel mechanics matter because hand-sold pilots scale on founder energy, and Deep Interactions has not yet shown the kind of unscalable stunt — door-to-door photography, Obama O's, an Armstrong-grade prototype rescue — that tells me the founders will outrun their own GTM ceiling.

On the schlep axis, the counterfactual is sharp. For Deep Interactions to flip into Coinbase (2012) territory — schlep-as-moat — the integration grind across Slack, Gmail, Chrome, Miro, Figma, GitHub, Vercel, Supabase, and CLI would need to be the moat, not the feature list. Today the dossier presents it as breadth; that breadth has to become a context layer competitors won't replicate because the per-client wiring is too tedious. If the YC Launch page's "shared understanding of customer, product, and codebase" becomes a real proprietary data asset by 2027, the schlep score doubles. As currently described, it is integration plumbing — work, yes, but not work Lovable's 146 employees couldn't replicate in two quarters.

Stripe (2010) is the single anchor on cofounder configuration. Patrick and John Collison had a decade of shared hacking and a prior $5M Auctomatic outcome at ages 19 and 17 before Stripe. Deep Interactions has Sruthi and Neil sharing Oxford CS DPhil lab proximity in human-centred AI — real, but institutional rather than collaborative — plus Beni Ngwamah from WEKA and Virgin Media O2 Moonshots in an "undefined founding role," and a fourth team member. The dossier says only Sruthi is publicly listed as "Founder." That ambiguity on roles for an engineering-heavy product is the part that does not match the Collison shape.

My 36-month forecast for Deep Interactions: sub-$30M acquihire by Lovable, Notion, or Vercel by Q2 2029, with the team absorbed into a multi-stakeholder collaboration feature inside the acquirer's existing app builder, after failing to break $5M ARR against Replit's parallel-agent product and Lovable's distribution flywheel. The mechanism: research-grade founders without hacker velocity get out-shipped in a category where the incumbent added $100M of revenue in a single month.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that Sruthi's Oxford Human-AI Interaction research is itself the contrarian secret — that Lovable, Bolt, and Replit are all locked into the single-developer prompt-to-app frame and structurally cannot pivot to multi-stakeholder workflow because their products are built around solo-chat UX. If that frame-lock is real, Deep Interactions owns the team-collaboration wedge by default, and the 12 design partners with 50K hits is the early Airbnb-grade signal. I do not buy it, because Replit Agent 4 already supports parallel agents on a single project (Replit blog, March 2026) — the wedge is closing in real time, and Deep Interactions has no disclosed technical moat to slow it. The condition that flips my verdict: Sruthi posts a demo to Hacker News that hits the front page, or Deep Interactions discloses a named Fortune 500 design partner with contract value above $250K, within the next six months. Either would prove the animal test in a way the dossier currently does not.
