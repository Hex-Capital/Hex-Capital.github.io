# Congruent -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what's genuinely interesting about Congruent: they've identified a structural gap that almost nobody is talking about. The entire autonomous driving industry is shifting toward end-to-end neural networks -- Tesla proved it works with FSD v12, Wayve raised a billion dollars to chase the same approach -- and radar, the cheapest depth sensor already sitting in 90% of cars, is locked out of this revolution because existing radar hardware throws away 99% of its raw data before any AI model ever sees it. No raw radar simulator exists for driving scenes. Camera has one. Lidar has one. Radar doesn't. That's the kind of "difficult to assess" opportunity I've trained myself to notice -- most investors can't even evaluate whether this gap matters because you need to understand both radar signal processing and the architecture of end-to-end training pipelines to see the problem clearly.

But here's where I get stuck: I don't know these founders' stories. I have their credentials -- Clement Barthes, PhD from Berkeley, was CTO at SafeHub and lead perception engineer at Zendar, one of the few radar startups targeting autonomous vehicles. Evan Carnahan, PhD from UT Austin, researched radar attenuation for planetary bodies and built perception pipelines. These are people who've spent years inside radar engineering, and they independently arrived at this insight about the missing infrastructure layer. That's real. But when I sit across from founders, I'm not asking about their resumes. I'm asking how they got here -- what happened in their lives that makes this the problem they have to solve, not just a problem they're technically equipped to solve. The dossier gives me domain expertise. It doesn't give me biographical urgency. And at pre-seed, when I'm writing a check from my own money, the founder's relationship to the problem matters more than their technical qualifications.

The timing thesis is where this gets most compelling. Congruent is making a second-order bet: not on autonomous driving itself, but on a specific architectural transition within autonomous driving that creates demand for an entirely new sensor infrastructure stack. That reminds me, structurally, of how Crusoe Energy wasn't really betting on Bitcoin -- they were betting on stranded energy becoming computation, which is why they survived the crypto crash and became an AI infrastructure company. If end-to-end training becomes the standard approach for autonomous vehicles and the industry decides it needs radar in those stacks (because lidar costs twenty times more and cameras can't see through fog), then someone needs to build this, and these two might be first. That's a legitimate contrarian position. But the "if" in that sentence is doing enormous work. Tesla is currently running its end-to-end system with cameras only. If the industry converges on camera-only architectures, Congruent's entire value proposition shrinks to a niche of winter-weather autonomy developers.

For the bull case to work, you need to believe three things simultaneously: end-to-end training wins over modular perception stacks, cameras alone aren't sufficient for safety-critical autonomy, and incumbents like NXP, Texas Instruments, and Infineon don't simply add raw data output to their next-generation radar chipsets. The first two feel plausible -- I'd lean toward yes on both. The third is the real threat. When I backed companies operating in hard, regulated spaces -- Contraline in male contraception, Anduril in defense -- the regulatory complexity itself was the moat. Here, the moat is more technical than regulatory: can incumbents with hundred-million-dollar radar businesses easily expose raw data and build generative simulators? The simulator piece, trained on proprietary real-world radar data, is harder to replicate. The hardware piece is more vulnerable. A 2-person pre-seed team building automotive-grade hardware that needs AEC-Q qualification and years of validation before any OEM will touch it -- that's a long, capital-intensive road that doesn't match the resources they have today.

What I don't see here, and what I'd need to feel that jolt of conviction, is the human element that transforms a technically sound thesis into a company I'd drop everything to support. This isn't an outsider story. These are accomplished research engineers who spotted a gap in their field. That's how most deep-tech companies start, and many of them build real value. But it's not the pattern that activates my framework most powerfully. I'm not sensing the "magically weird" quality -- the idea is technically non-obvious, but it's not the kind of thing that makes other investors uncomfortable. It's not stigmatized or dismissed as crazy. It's just hard to evaluate, which is different. And the company doesn't create economic access for underserved people -- it sells infrastructure to OEMs and AV developers. That's a legitimate business, but it's not the kind of impact that makes me lean forward in my chair.
