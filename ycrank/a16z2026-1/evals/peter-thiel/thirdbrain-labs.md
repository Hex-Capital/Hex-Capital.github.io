# Thirdbrain Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape table in Thirdbrain Labs' dossier is the evaluation. Scale AI at $29 billion. Together AI at $3.3 billion. Labelbox past $100 million in ARR. Snorkel AI at $1.3 billion. Plus Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, LLaMA-Factory — all free. When I see five well-capitalized incumbents and three open-source alternatives listed in a single dossier, I am not looking at an investment opportunity. I am looking at a market that has already been competed into commodity economics. Every one of these players is pursuing the same thesis: make fine-tuning accessible to teams without ML engineers. Thirdbrain Labs enters this field with no customers, no revenue, and no publicly documented technical artifact. The question is not whether the team is capable. The question is whether the market structure permits monopoly — and the answer is visible in the competitive table itself. All failed companies are the same.

Where is the secret? Thirdbrain Labs describes itself as capturing "expert decisions directly" as the training signal — an approach the dossier's own analyst marks as "[Inferred]: RLHF-adjacent or preference-learning approach." RLHF is not a secret. It is the consensus methodology of the entire post-training industry. Scale AI built Scale Labs around it. Labelbox added RLHF workflows in 2024. Every foundation model provider offers preference-tuning APIs. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is "domain experts should be able to fine-tune models without ML teams" — that is not a contrarian insight. That is a McKinsey slide. The absence of a genuine secret is not merely a missing signal at pre-seed; it is the structural condition that guarantees competition, because without a non-obvious insight, every competent team converges on the same approach.

Margaret Zhang's prior venture is the most analytically revealing signal in this dossier — and it cuts against investment. She co-founded Coalition Systems, a defense AI startup building "secure AI coordination software for allied defense," backed by a16z Speedrun. She traveled to Kyiv to meet with Ukraine's Ministry of Defence. That company had structural characteristics I value: government embedding, defense sector switching costs, a physical-world application of AI that creates monopoly through operational indispensability. Instead, she left that trajectory to build a software infrastructure layer in the most crowded corner of the AI stack. The dossier does not clarify whether Coalition Systems was abandoned, pivoted, or is running in parallel — and that ambiguity is itself a red flag. A founder with definite conviction does not leave an architecturally superior market position to enter a commodity one. David Huang's credentials — UC Berkeley Regents' Scholar, Two Sigma Halite top 1%, Stanford NLP coursework — demonstrate technical competence, but competence without monopoly structure is what produces Wish, not Palantir.

The comparison to Wish is structurally precise. Wish entered a massive e-commerce market with no proprietary technology, no network effects, and no monopoly mechanism — competing on price against Amazon and Alibaba. It reached a $14 billion IPO and then collapsed over 90% because there was never a structural reason it had to win. Thirdbrain Labs enters the $3.2 billion LLM fine-tuning orchestration market with no proprietary technology documented in the dossier, no network effects, and no monopoly mechanism — competing on UX against Scale AI's $2 billion revenue machine and Together AI's 450,000+ users. The potential data flywheel from accumulated expert feedback is entirely hypothetical and marked "[Inferred]" — it requires customers to generate, and no customers exist. Predibase, the closest structural analogue (LoRA-based fine-tuning platform), was acquired by Rubrik for $100-500 million — a respectable outcome for its investors but structurally irrelevant to the power law. That acquisition ceiling is the realistic best case for Thirdbrain Labs, and it falls below fund-returner threshold.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the "expert decision capture" UX is sufficiently differentiated from standard RLHF tooling to create genuine switching costs, that Meta's disruption of Scale AI in mid-2025 opened a structural gap in the market that a two-person pre-seed company can fill before Together AI and the cloud providers close it, and that the robotics tag in Thirdbrain Labs' profile indicates a physical-world application layer that the dossier does not document. If all three were true — if Zhang and Huang have built something technically novel that embeds in robotics and manufacturing workflows with proprietary feedback loops — there would be a path to the kind of operational indispensability that makes Palantir's contracts structurally irreplaceable. But none of these conditions has evidence in the dossier. The website shows "Book a demo" and "Advanced Benchmarks." There are no public repos, no technical papers, no customer testimonials, no named design partners. The bull case is a series of inferences stacked on inferences, and my framework demands demonstrated secrets, not hypothetical ones.

I pass on Thirdbrain Labs. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Physical-World Technology Moat. The market is structurally competitive — five funded incumbents and free open-source alternatives pursuing identical theses — which is the precise condition under which my framework predicts value destruction regardless of team quality. The company operates as a pure software layer with no atoms-based barrier, no government customer pathway, and no documented proprietary technology. Margaret Zhang's departure from a defense AI company with superior monopoly structure to build commodity fine-tuning infrastructure is the biographical signal that crystallizes the structural concern. Competition remains what it has always been: a condition to escape, not a market to enter.
