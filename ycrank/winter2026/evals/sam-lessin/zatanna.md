# Zatanna -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing that jumps off the page is the pivot history. Search engine caches show "a lawtech company focused on jury trials," then "Adaptive Healthcare Admin Agents," and now "Request Based Scraping Infrastructure" — all under the same legal entity, NoPoll Inc. Three product identities before or during a YC batch is not iteration, it's searching. My best investments — Solana, Venmo, Teamshares — come from founders with deep structural conviction about one specific insight they've been obsessing over for years. Anatoly Yakovenko didn't pivot from legal tech to healthcare agents to blockchain; he spent years at Qualcomm thinking about clock synchronization and consensus mechanisms, then built Solana because that specific insight demanded to exist. When I see a team cycling through unrelated categories, I see founders optimizing for YC acceptance rather than pursuing something they'd build regardless of external validation. That's a "tizz" problem — where's the maniacal operational obsession?

On the sane-person-insane-idea calibration, the pairing doesn't land. Alex Blackwell's anti-bot bypass experience at Pikkit is genuinely relevant domain expertise — that's the "sane" part working. But protocol-level HTTP scraping is a well-known technique that sophisticated data shops have used for a decade. Bright Data is doing $300M in annual revenue. Firecrawl just raised $14.5M with 81K GitHub stars. Apify has $13.3M in revenue. This market is hot, crowded, and consensus. When I describe an "insane idea," I mean something that makes most investors uncomfortable — the way Coinbase made people uncomfortable in 2012 or Teamshares' plan to buy hundreds of small businesses made people laugh. "We scrape websites faster" does not provoke discomfort. It provokes a shrug and the question "why you and not the ten other companies doing this?" The founders are also early-career — one is still finishing his undergraduate degree in 2026. No shared prior employer or institution identified among the three co-founders, which raises the question of how this team formed.

Now, here's where my distinctive lens matters most: run the AI kill test on this business and the answer is brutal. Zatanna's value is engineering labor — reverse-engineering HTTP requests, TLS fingerprints, and authentication flows for each target site. That's exactly the category of work that AI is automating fastest. An LLM that can analyze network traffic and generate optimized HTTP clients will commoditize the per-integration work that constitutes Zatanna's entire moat. The company's defensibility is a "library of reverse-engineered integrations" — but each integration breaks when the target site updates its auth flow, creating linear maintenance costs. There are no network effects, no physical infrastructure, no regulatory position. This is software whose moat is engineering effort, and engineering effort is exactly what AI makes cheap. If I apply my framework — does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? — the answer is unambiguously weaker.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I want to be honest about what would have to be true for this to work. If Zatanna could establish itself as the default "data access layer" for AI agents — a protocol-level primitive that every agent framework routes through for web data — that's genuinely interesting. The AI agent explosion creates massive demand for structured web data, and browser-based scraping at 3-10 seconds per request with 200MB per instance is genuinely too slow for real-time agent workflows. Sub-500ms at <1MB is a real performance advantage. If the team could build broad enough coverage fast enough and lock in as infrastructure before Firecrawl or Bright Data adapts, there's a path to becoming a new primitive. But that path requires (a) the custom-per-site approach somehow scaling faster than general-purpose browser solutions, which is structurally disadvantaged, (b) AI not automating the reverse-engineering that constitutes the core product, which contradicts all available evidence about AI capability trajectories, and (c) winning against competitors with 10-100x more resources and distribution. All three conditions need to hold simultaneously. I don't bet on triple-conjunction scenarios.

The ~$1B TAM is small by venture standards, and the SAM for HTTP-request-based scraping specifically is a fraction of that. The narrative could theoretically expand to "data infrastructure for the AI era," but the per-site custom integration model structurally caps the story. A true infinity narrative requires compounding dynamics — each new customer or integration making the next one easier or more valuable. Zatanna's model scales linearly: each new target site requires fresh reverse-engineering work, independent of previous integrations. That's a services business wearing an infrastructure costume. No revenue, no traction, no pricing page — which I don't penalize harshly at pre-seed, but combined with everything else, there's no downside cushion to complement the limited upside narrative.
