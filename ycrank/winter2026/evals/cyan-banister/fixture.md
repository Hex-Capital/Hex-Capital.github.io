# Fixture -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Fixture: this is the most consensus category in enterprise software right now. AI-native CRM. Every fund I know has looked at this space. Attio has raised $116 million and uses almost identical positioning. Clay just closed at a $3.1 billion valuation. When I walk into a room of investors and say "I'm looking at an AI-native CRM for startups," nobody flinches -- they nod and show me three others in their pipeline. That's the opposite of the signal I've spent my career learning to trust. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Fixture is popular and straightforward to assess, and that tells me something important about what kind of return profile this is likely to have. The weirdness has already been arbitraged away by competitors with hundred-million-dollar war chests.

When I think about the founders, Matt Riley and Quin Hoxie have an impressive professional resume -- Swiftype through YC, the Elastic acquisition, senior leadership roles at a public company. Matt's time as a YC Visiting Partner means he's seen the CRM pain point firsthand across dozens of startups. But when I imagine sitting across from them and asking my first question -- "Tell me your story, not the business" -- I don't think I'd hear the kind of answer that makes me lean forward. I'd hear about successful careers in search infrastructure and a logical observation that startup CRM is broken. That's valid. It's smart. But it's not biographical urgency. It's not someone who was forged by this problem. The founders who persist through the inevitable crises of building a company in a hyper-competitive market are usually the ones who can't imagine doing anything else because the problem is personal. I don't see that thread here, and in a space where Fixture will be competing against Attio's $116M and HubSpot's free tier, that kind of irrational founder commitment isn't optional -- it's load-bearing.

The one thing that genuinely excites me is the co-founder relationship. After HQ Trivia -- which had the right product, the right timing, and still imploded because two co-founders couldn't resolve their differences -- I've become almost obsessive about this dimension. Matt and Quin built Swiftype together, navigated a YC batch together, raised venture capital together, sold the company together, and then worked side by side at Elastic for years afterward. That's over a decade of shared context, shared stress, and presumably shared conflict resolution. You don't survive an acquisition integration and years at a large public company without learning how to fight productively. If I were evaluating Fixture purely on team resilience, this would be one of the strongest signals I've encountered.

But here's the bull case I want to be honest about: if you believe -- really believe -- that AI agents are about to become the primary interface for all go-to-market work, then building a CRM that's agent-native from day one is a structural bet, not an incremental one. The Claude Code integration, the "use from any AI tool" positioning -- that's a bet that the CRM of the future isn't a dashboard at all, it's an API that agents query. If that paradigm shift happens fast enough, legacy CRMs (including Attio) might find themselves retrofitting agent capabilities onto architectures designed for human-first workflows, the way mobile-first companies had structural advantages over desktop-first companies that bolted on responsive design. And Matt and Quin's deep search and data infrastructure background at Elastic is genuinely relevant to building that kind of context-aggregation layer. The technical founder-market fit is real. If I could see evidence that their agent-native architecture is meaningfully differentiated from what Attio is building -- not just the same LLM wrapper everyone else is shipping -- I'd be more interested.

Ultimately, though, Fixture doesn't create economic access for people who lack it. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to the underserved. It doesn't operate in a regulated or stigmatized space where complexity becomes a moat. It makes sales workflows more efficient for startup founders who already have YC backing and venture capital. That's a real business -- probably a good one -- but it's not the kind of investment that gets me out of bed. And the "zero data lock-in" positioning, while philosophically admirable, is strategically at war with building the kind of defensibility I look for. They're explicitly marketing against their own moat. I respect the principle, but I invest in companies that become harder to leave over time, not easier. This is a well-credentialed team building a solid product in a space I'd pass on regardless of who was building it.
