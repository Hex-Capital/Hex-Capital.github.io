# Fenrock AI (YC W26) -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Fenrock isn't the product or the market -- it's that "AI agents for financial crime compliance" is one of the least weird ideas I've seen in a while. McKinsey just published research on exactly this. The FCA is running AI sandboxes for AML. Unit21, which has $92 million in funding, is already advertising AI agents for fraud and anti-money-laundering on its homepage. When I look at my best investments -- SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy, Crusoe Energy when people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was a joke -- the defining feature was that nobody else wanted to fund them. Fenrock is swimming directly into a current that every compliance vendor and their investors are already riding. The most promising companies are not popular, and this company is building exactly what the market is asking for. That's usually the signal that the opportunity has already been priced in.

When I think about Charu Sharma's story, there are real elements of resilience. She shut down NextPlay.ai -- her HR tech company that served Fortune 500 clients for mentorship programs -- and that's a crucible I don't take lightly. Closing a company you built is one of the hardest things a founder can do, and it tells me something about her honesty with herself. She has an impressive set of credentials: VentureBeat's AI Entrepreneur of the Year, an MBA from Chicago Booth, enterprise sales experience at LinkedIn, 600-plus speaking appearances. But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- how did this founder arrive at *this* specific problem? -- I can't find the biographical thread. There's no evidence she ever worked inside a compliance team, sat across from a regulator, or felt the pain of reviewing false positive alerts at two in the morning. Her prior company was in a completely different domain. The credentials are strong, but they're generalist credentials, and in a space where regulatory nuance can make or break you, I need to see that the founder has lived inside the problem. I've learned that the founders who persist through the inevitable crises in regulated markets are the ones who can't imagine doing anything else because the problem is personal to them.

The bull case here is real, and I want to engage with it honestly. The AML market is enormous and growing -- $3.4 billion today heading toward $8 billion by 2030, with the broader compliance market potentially reaching $70 billion. The timing for agentic AI in compliance is genuinely strong: LLMs can now handle the kind of complex reasoning chains that previous machine learning approaches couldn't touch, and regulators are signaling openness to AI-driven compliance. If Charu's enterprise sales instincts from LinkedIn translate to navigating bank procurement, and if she can hire deep domain expertise to complement her AI and go-to-market skills, this could work. A founder who has already been through the fire of shutting down a company and who understands the investor landscape from her time at SignalFire and Afore Capital has real advantages. The question is whether those transferable strengths are enough in a market where her competitors have hundreds of millions in funding and years of regulatory track record.

What I can't get past is the combination of three signals that, together, push me away. First, the consensus nature of the play -- this doesn't trigger the intuitive jolt I felt with Niantic or Crusoe. Second, the founder's profile reads more like polished performance than raw obsession with a specific problem. Six hundred speaking appearances, TEDx conferences, Forbes columns -- I'm not saying any of that is bad, but it's the curated narrative pattern that makes my instincts pull back. I'm looking for the founder whose phone is tethered to hotel WiFi because they're too busy building to care about optics, like Garrett Langley at Flock Safety. Third, there's a ghost on this team -- the second member is completely unidentified in any public source. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder dynamics are everything, I can't evaluate what I can't see. A two-person company where I only know one person is a team I can't underwrite.

Financial crime compliance is a legitimately hard, regulated space, and I respect anyone who enters it. The audit trail emphasis in their positioning is smart -- explainability is where most AI compliance tools stumble, and if Fenrock actually solves that, it's meaningful. But I don't see Fenrock creating economic access for people who currently lack it, or making an invisible system visible to those who need transparency most. This is an efficiency tool for compliance teams at financial institutions -- valuable, but not the kind of positive-sum impact I gravitate toward. My best investments have put money in the pockets of drivers, delivery workers, and tradespeople, or have brought transparency to systems that were deliberately opaque. Fenrock helps banks process alerts faster. That's useful. It's just not what gets me out of bed.
