# AICE -- Paul Graham Evaluation

AICE is the inverse of the pattern I keep my checkbook open for: it sits on a genuine schlep — subsea defense hardware manufactured in France, ITAR exposure, naval procurement cycles — but the two founders behind AICE, Grégoire Chomette and Rémi Bouteiller, pivoted into it roughly two weeks before this dossier was written, from an energy-sensor product that was itself unsuccessful. A schlep nobody else wants is only an asset when the founders are the kind of animals who will eat the schlep. AICE shows me the schlep. It does not yet show me the animals.

Coinbase (2012) is the rung I want AICE to climb. Brian Armstrong saw the compliance grind that every other YC applicant in his batch was unconsciously routing around, and when I rejected his first application he came back with a hand-built Android Bitcoin wallet — the schlep was the asset, and the animal moment was the artifact. AICE's category genuinely qualifies on the schlep axis: naval ministries, subsea acoustics, ITAR certifications, hardware manufacturing in Paris, the France AI underwater acoustics program (Defense News, May 2024) as a procurement opening. This is the part of the dossier I lean toward. What AICE does not have is the Armstrong-style post-rejection artifact. The dossier shows a pivot from "sensors to cut energy bill by 30%" to submarine swarms two weeks before research, with no announced demo, no signed LOI with a named navy, no working 600m-rated unit on the water. Coinbase had the wallet before the check. AICE has the pitch before the prototype.

The MBA AI-wrapper team from my standard pass — glossy deck, $50B TAM slide, no founder who personally suffered the problem — is the failure mechanism I want to test AICE against on organic-idea fit. The pattern there is that the founders adopted the problem from a market scan rather than from their own life. AICE's dossier reads exactly that way on this axis: Chomette's MIT AeroAstro thesis is on asteroid risk assessment for NASA (2019-2021); Bouteiller built embedded energy sensors; neither has a documented naval, undersea, or marine-systems role in their public history. The pivot did not come from a USB stick Drew Houston kept losing on the MIT shuttle — it came from reading that the AUV market is going from $3.13B to $4.64B by 2030 (MarketsandMarkets, 2025). Drew Houston was Dropbox's customer before he was its founder. Chomette and Bouteiller are not yet AICE's customer. That is the smell.

AICE's hacker-genealogy reading runs through a single GitHub handle. Bouteiller's @rbouteiller carries an airplay-esp32 repo at 282 stars — a real, non-trivial embedded-systems artifact, the kind of thing Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize (2005) signaled before Stripe (2010), though at lower amplitude. Chomette's MIT AeroAstro thesis is credentialed work but not a hacker-tribal marker; no GitHub presence under his name, no open-source footprint, no Hacker News trail. AICE clears the technical-by-credential-only bar — Bouteiller is plausibly an embedded hacker — but the team does not radiate the lineage that made Reddit (2005) and Stripe (2010) self-evident on this axis. One half-hacker, one credentialed aerospace researcher.

The 18-month compounding mechanism on animal resourcefulness is where AICE worries me most, because the dimension carries the largest weight in how I actually decide. The pivot two weeks pre-research is not by itself a disqualifier — Airbnb (2009) pivoted through AirBed & Breakfast — but the dossier names no pre-funding stunt, no unscalable revenue improvisation, no equivalent of the Obama O's and Cap'n McCain's cereal boxes that flipped my Airbnb skepticism in 2008. In a category where Anduril just raised $4B at $60B and Helsing is closing $1.2B at $18B, AICE needs the founders to be doing something nobody told them to do — knocking on the door of the French DGA, sleeping on a fishing boat off Toulon to demo a 600m unit, anything. The dossier is silent on it. Animals leave tracks. I do not yet see tracks for AICE.

Co-founder configuration is the dimension where AICE would have to clear a counterfactual to flip a tier: if the dossier surfaced that Chomette and Bouteiller had built together for two-plus years before AICE Power — dorm roommates the way Huffman and Ohanian were at Reddit (2005), or brothers the way the Collisons were at Stripe (2010) — the team would clear the strong-pair bar. The public record shows only that they worked together on the failed energy-sensor product and the pivoted defense product. Two co-founders, plausibly complementary on paper (CEO with autonomy/ML research, CTO with embedded), no documented long history. Neutral on this axis, leaning down.

My 36-month forecast for AICE: pivots a second time or acquihires into a defense prime (Thales, Anduril after another Dive Technologies-style tuck-in) for under $10M, does not exceed $3M ARR, and is dominated in the small-swarm-AUV segment it described by Anduril's post-Dive-Technologies roadmap and Helsing's post-Blue-Ocean Australian stack. The mechanism is straightforward: founders without organic pain in a category, entering against incumbents with two-orders-of-magnitude capital advantages, after a two-week-old pivot, tend to either pivot again when the first naval procurement cycle stalls at month nine or sell themselves into the larger platform that already has the contract relationships.

The strongest bull case against my forecast invokes the Loopt (2005) exception — Sam Altman force-of-personality before mobile location was usable infrastructure — and the Airbnb (2009) exception, where I funded a team whose idea I had mentally rejected because the cereal-box stunt overrode my skepticism. The bull case for AICE is that subsea grey-zone is a 2026 inflection (France AI underwater acoustics program, May 2024; sabotaged cables; UUV market projected $5.58B → $25.9B at 16.6% CAGR), and a modular small-form-factor swarm is plausibly the right architectural bet against Anduril's monolithic Dive-LD the way distributed beat mainframe. That case stings — the category is real, the architectural thesis is defensible, and incumbents are visibly buying their way in (Anduril/Dive Technologies, Helsing/Blue Ocean). What dismantles it is that both Loopt and Airbnb passed the animal test for me before the check — Altman's force-of-personality in the interview, Chesky and Gebbia's cereal-box revenue — and the AICE dossier names no equivalent moment for Chomette or Bouteiller. The one verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a signed LOI or paid pilot with a named navy (DGA, US Navy NAVSEA, Royal Navy) within six months of YC Demo Day, plus a 600m-rated unit on the water in publicly verifiable footage. Until then, AICE is a category bet without a founder bet, and category bets are not what I do. Pass.
