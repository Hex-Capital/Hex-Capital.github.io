# Standard Signal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Michael Royzen left Phind — an active company that raised $10.4M in December 2025 and processed 150M+ developer searches — to launch Standard Signal roughly four months later. That's the fact I can't stop circling. When I evaluate founders, I'm looking for people whose biography made them incapable of working on anything else. Royzen is genuinely talented: building iOS apps at twelve, "Geek of the Week" in GeekWire at sixteen, UT Austin Turing Scholar, ML stints at Lyft, Cloudflare, and Microsoft. He proved he can train production LLMs that millions of people use. But when I ask my question — tell me your story, not the business — I hear "I built excellent coding models and realized I could apply them to trading." That's career optimization wearing the costume of conviction. Standard Signal's founding narrative reads like a capability in search of a problem, not a problem that found its founder and wouldn't let go.

Royzen has zero documented quantitative finance experience — no time at a fund, no personal encounter with the extractive mechanics of Wall Street, no biographical scar tissue from the financial system. Standard Signal's job listings for ML Research Engineer and Quantitative Researcher confirm the gap: the domain expertise hasn't been hired yet, let alone lived. Compare this to Crusoe Energy, where the founders had literally stood in North Dakota oil fields watching gas burn off — the problem was physical, visceral, impossible to unsee. Or compare it to my Affirm investment, where Max Levchin's entire career had been building toward transparent financial systems after watching the credit card industry extract value through deliberate opacity. Royzen's forge is real, but it was forged in AI infrastructure, not in finance. That matters when you're asking LPs to hand you $100K minimums and trust an autonomous system with their capital.

Standard Signal operates in what is, as of 2026, one of the most consensus-attractive investment spaces imaginable: AI applied to trading. Numerai has $550M in AUM and a $500M valuation. Rebellion Research has been running AI-only strategies since 2007. Earthian AI is positioning as an "AI-native hedge fund." Renaissance Technologies, Two Sigma, and Citadel have spent decades building data infrastructure that dwarfs what any pre-seed company can assemble. The dossier itself notes that open-source AI hedge fund frameworks already exist on GitHub. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic — VCs would literally leave the room. When I wrote my check for Contraline, male birth control made seasoned investors physically uncomfortable. Standard Signal's pitch — "AI can trade better than humans" — is a dinner party conversation that everyone nods along to. There is no courage required to articulate this thesis. The 300-investor-pass test fails in reverse: too many investors would say yes for this to carry contrarian signal.

Standard Signal's $100K minimum LP investment tells me exactly who this product serves: the already-wealthy. My strongest portfolio pattern is backing companies that make invisible, extractive systems visible to ordinary people — Uber cracked open the taxi medallion cartel, Ownwell exposed property tax opacity, Affirm revealed what credit cards actually cost. Standard Signal isn't exposing the hedge fund industry's opacity to the people it exploits; it's building another hedge fund inside that same opaque structure, just with a different engine. The fund's claimed Sharpe ratio of 2.0+ is unaudited, with no time period or third-party verification — institutional LPs will scrutinize this, and the performance claims on standardsignal.com carry no more weight than a pitch deck slide until an auditor signs off. There's no structural shift in who gets access to what. Hedge fund returns stay behind the same $100K gate, flowing to the same class of investors.

The bull case is real and I want to state it honestly: Royzen is a second-time YC founder who has demonstrably shipped production AI systems at scale. The timing window for LLM-native trading is genuinely narrow — inference costs dropped 10-100x over 2023-2025, making continuous autonomous trading across 2,847+ pairs economically viable for the first time. If Standard Signal's proprietary model training compounds into a performance track record over 12-24 months, that audited record becomes a genuine moat — institutional LPs weight track records heavily and won't easily switch. The solo founder concern is mitigated by the pre-seed stage, and the "full trading autonomy within strict risk parameters" framing is legitimately novel. I checked my known exceptions: RTFKT was a cultural-timing bet that defied my defensibility criterion, but Standard Signal lacks the narrow cultural window that justified that override. There's no exception in my framework that overcomes the missing biographical forge and absent invisible-system disruption.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Royzen had a deeply personal encounter with financial system opacity — a family bankruptcy caused by predatory lending, years working inside a fund and witnessing how the system exploits information asymmetry, or evidence that Standard Signal's roadmap includes democratizing access to institutional-quality returns below the $100K threshold. The biographical connection to the problem domain is what's missing, and without it, I'm watching a brilliant engineer build in a space where Renaissance Technologies has a $130B head start and no structural barrier prevents them from adopting the same LLM approach with vastly more data. Standard Signal scores lowest on the two dimensions I weight most heavily — Founder's Biographical Forge and Invisible System Made Visible — and those are the dimensions that separate my portfolio's defining companies from the ones I wished well and watched from the sideline.
