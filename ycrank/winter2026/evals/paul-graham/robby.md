# Robby -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three Harvard MBA graduates are studying to become certified HVAC technicians. That's the detail I keep coming back to. The EPA 608 certification isn't something you pursue as market research -- it's a 60-hour commitment to understanding refrigerant handling, system diagnostics, and ductwork. When I hear that the founding team also spent months riding along in service vans and shadowing every office role, the question sharpens to one I've seen before: is this the DoorDash pattern, where consulting-derived founders prove themselves through execution intensity, or is this the MBA project pattern, where smart people rotate through problem spaces until they find one that raises money?

The origin of this idea is clearly consulting, not lived experience. Joseph Schwarzmann found this problem while advising PE firms on home services acquisitions at Bain. He saw across dozens of portfolio companies that technicians were observing revenue opportunities -- aging equipment, upgrade candidates, safety issues -- and none of it was being captured systematically. That's pattern recognition from the outside, not a founder who couldn't make rent discovering Airbnb or a programmer forgetting his USB drive and building Dropbox. When I ask "how did they find this problem?" the honest answer is "spreadsheets at Bain." That puts a ceiling on my conviction. But the schlep itself is real and substantial. Building AI tools for blue-collar workers in the field, integrating with ServiceTitan's APIs, understanding the actual workflow of a technician crawling through an attic -- this is exactly the kind of tedious, unglamorous work that causes most AI founders to build another developer copilot instead. The schlep blindness here is structural: thousands of AI engineers know this industry exists, but almost none want to spend months in service vans learning the trade.

What saves this from the "MBA project" bucket is the traction. Nine named paying customers at pre-seed is not nothing. More importantly, the revenue attribution claims suggest these customers are experiencing genuine economic pull. "$40K in new business within the first hour of launch" for one customer, and multiple customers each uncovering six figures in weekly revenue opportunities -- if even half of that is real, it means the product is generating value that dwarfs whatever Robby is charging for it. That's the kind of asymmetry that creates stickiness. When your tool is directly attributable to five or six figures in new revenue per week, it becomes the last subscription you cancel. The question I can't answer from the dossier is what fraction of "revenue opportunities surfaced" actually close, and what Robby's own revenue is. The gap between opportunities identified and deals closed is where a lot of sales intelligence products go to die.

The technical team is solid if not exceptional. Two of three founders are genuine engineers -- Vineet was employee #1 at Cloud Health Systems and built data platforms at Uber, Feroze was summa cum laude in ECE at Duke and engineered at Ironclad and Amazon. The product exists and nine companies are paying for it. That's the bar at pre-seed. I'm not seeing the "hacker building something for himself" signal I got from the Collisons or Drew Houston, but the technical capability to execute is clearly present. The pivot history concerns me more than it should excite me. Importrix (AI customs brokerage), Lifelong Health, and now Robby -- three different directions in under a year between the founding team. Pivoting is normal, but the velocity here suggests they're still searching for conviction rather than executing on a problem they can't walk away from.

The bull case is the DoorDash analogy taken seriously. DoorDash was "probably bottom half of the batch" at Demo Day, the idea wasn't contrarian or hidden, multiple companies had tried food delivery before, and investors were openly skeptical about unit economics. Tony Xu's intensity -- personally delivering food, understanding every step of the logistics -- created the moat that market analysis said shouldn't exist. If Schwarzmann's PE pattern recognition gave them the map, and the team's field immersion gives them the territory, and if ServiceTitan's ecosystem creates a distribution channel the way restaurant aggregators created one for DoorDash, then this could compound quickly. The home services industry is enormous, underserved by technology, and consolidating through PE -- exactly the conditions where a revenue-generation layer could become indispensable. If Robby's data flywheel from technician visits compounds the way I'd hope, the ServiceTitan dependency becomes a feature (distribution) rather than a risk (platform lock), at least until they're large enough to matter.

But I keep returning to the origin. The best companies I've funded were built by people who would have built the product even if they never started a company. I don't think these founders would be studying for EPA 608 certification if they hadn't decided to start a startup first. That's the difference between organic discovery and manufactured commitment -- the commitment here is real, impressive even, but it was chosen rather than inevitable. Combined with the ServiceTitan platform risk and the fact that the core technology (combining CRM data with third-party data for lead scoring) is technically replicable, I land in the territory where the execution is strong enough to be interesting but the foundation isn't deep enough for me to write a check with full conviction.
