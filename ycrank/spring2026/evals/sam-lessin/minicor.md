# Minicor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Minicor is that the "why now" section of their story tells you everything you need to know about the structural problem: "Anthropic shipped Claude computer use capabilities in late 2024, and OpenAI and Google followed with similar offerings." The company exists because foundation model providers built the core capability. That's not a founding insight -- that's a feature request. When your business only becomes possible because Anthropic released an API, you are building on a platform that has every incentive and ability to absorb your functionality. This is the LLM wrapper problem applied to desktop automation, and it's the category I pass on fastest. The question I always ask -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? -- has a clear answer here: no. Without vision-model computer use, Minicor is just another script-based RPA tool competing against UiPath's $1.43 billion in revenue. The AI isn't the cherry on top. The AI is the whole dessert.

The sane-person-insane-idea filter produces a mismatch in the wrong direction. Faizaan and Saheed are credible enterprise engineers -- Microsoft, Ford's Android Auto, Morgan Stanley -- with a real co-founding relationship predating this company through Software For Love at University of Ottawa. That's the "sane" part, and I give them credit for it. But "AI-powered self-healing RPA" is not an insane idea. It's the single most obvious application of computer use models. Every RPA incumbent has this on their roadmap. Every browser automation startup (Skyvern, Browserbase, Browser Use -- three YC companies already) is adjacent. When your idea makes every VC in the room nod enthusiastically, the non-consensus premium has already been arbitraged away. The pivot from their prior product Laminar -- a low-code integration platform -- to desktop automation right after computer use APIs shipped reinforces my concern that they're following a wave rather than pursuing deep structural conviction. The sign-in portal still resolves to app.laminar.run.

Let me steelman the bull case, because there is one worth articulating. The argument would be: desktop and Citrix environments are genuinely ugly infrastructure. Foundation model providers ship raw computer use APIs, but someone needs to build the production-grade, HIPAA-compliant, horizontally-scalable deployment layer for enterprises that actually run these agents at scale in regulated environments. The 25,000 patients per day claim suggests real production usage. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certification represent genuine time and capital investment that creates switching friction. Healthcare organizations are notoriously slow to change vendors. In this reading, Minicor is the Craftwork of RPA -- operational complexity in the physical (or at least legacy-system) world that pure software competitors won't bother replicating. If this were true, I'd be more interested. But the analogy breaks down: Craftwork's moat is that you literally need painting crews with brushes. Minicor's "operational complexity" is deploying and managing Windows VMs -- infrastructure work that any well-capitalized competitor can replicate, and that cloud providers are actively commoditizing. UiPath already has healthcare-specific offerings and compliance certifications with ten thousand existing customer relationships. The compliance moat creates switching cost, not structural defensibility.

On the primitive-versus-application question, Minicor sits clearly on the application side. They're not creating a new protocol or foundational layer that an ecosystem builds on. They're wrapping foundation model APIs in deployment infrastructure for a specific use case. Compare this to my investment in Helium, which created a genuinely new wireless primitive that other applications built on top of, or Aleo's zero-knowledge protocol layer. Minicor doesn't enable other companies to build new categories of products -- it enables the same legacy workflows to run with less maintenance. That's valuable, but it caps the narrative ceiling. The "infinity story" requires Minicor to perpetually be the best infrastructure wrapper for someone else's improving capability, which is a race where the platform provider always has the inside lane. The RPA market TAM numbers are large, but market size without structural position is just a denominator that makes your share look small.

The founding team deserves respect for shipping something real in a regulated vertical -- HIPAA compliance and production healthcare deployments are not trivial. But the combination of consensus idea, foundation model dependency, application-layer positioning, and a recent pivot toward whatever's hot doesn't match how I allocate my own capital. I need to see a founder whose specific background makes them uniquely credible for this exact problem -- someone who built EHR systems or ran RPA operations at scale -- pursuing an approach that makes other investors uncomfortable. What I see instead is two capable generalist engineers building the most obvious product in an already-crowded space.
