# Mercury -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Mercury is building a no-code orchestration layer on top of other companies' AI agents — Claude Code, Devin, gstack — and that architecture tells me almost everything I need to know before I even get to the founders. My biggest wins have been infrastructure bets where the company *is* the thing underneath. SpaceX builds the rockets. Crusoe builds the data centers. Substrate builds the chips. Mercury builds the schedule for someone else's trains. When I run the ten-year question — where does this go if everything works? — I see a coordination layer that gets thinner every quarter as Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft bake multi-agent orchestration directly into their platforms. OpenAI already ships an Agents SDK with native multi-agent capabilities. Mercury's position in the stack is the most fragile one: above the models, below the user, dependent on every layer it doesn't control.

When I ask to hear a founder's story — not the business, the story — the dossier gives me Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma, both Forward Deployed Engineers at Palantir, both Stanford-connected, both with relevant deployment experience. And I respect the Palantir FDE role — those are the people who sit at the seam between powerful technical systems and the humans who need to use them, which maps directly onto Mercury's no-code-for-non-technical-users thesis. But "I was at Palantir and saw the opportunity" is career optimization, not biographical inevitability. I don't hear what happened to Stefano or Naveen that made them incapable of *not* building this. Stefano's unusual double in Religious Studies and Electrical Engineering at Stanford — that combination makes me curious, but the dossier never explains it. And the third co-founder, "Jayson," has no last name, no role description, no public presence whatsoever. For a five-person company where co-founders are the entire leadership structure, that opacity troubles me — especially given my rule about clear CEO delineation and co-founder dynamics.

Multi-agent AI orchestration is the precise opposite of a space that makes other investors uncomfortable. CrewAI raised $24.5M. Relevance AI raised $37M. Lindy raised approximately $50M. Dust pulled in $21.5M. VCs are not shifting in their chairs here — they're writing checks as fast as they can. Mercury is entering one of the most consensus-attractive spaces in AI, competing against companies with 5-10x their funding and existing user bases. Lindy already has $5.1M in revenue; CrewAI has $3.2M. Adding multi-agent orchestration to an established no-code agent builder is an incremental feature for Lindy or Relevance AI. For Mercury, building the full product *and* acquiring users from scratch against those incumbents is a structural disadvantage, not a contrarian edge.

The strongest bull case for Mercury: Palantir FDEs really are the perfect archetype for this problem. They've spent years watching non-technical operators struggle with powerful tools, and they understand the deployment gap viscerally. The heterogeneous backend approach — orchestrating across different agent providers rather than being locked to one — could be genuinely differentiated if the market fragments further. And the a16z Speedrun acceptance, roughly 70 from 19,000+ applicants, is a real selectivity signal. If Mercury achieves density with early users before platforms consolidate, the switching costs from accumulated agent configurations and persistent memory could harden into a real moat. But every element of this bull case depends on a timing window that I believe is narrowing, not widening — the agent providers are all converging on orchestration because that's where the value capture sits. The pattern reminds me of what happened with HQ Trivia: a genuinely compelling product experience riding a moment that was already closing. Right product, right time, no structural moat to survive when the platforms caught up.

What's absent here is what I look for most viscerally: there is no invisible system being made visible. Mercury addresses tool fragmentation — twenty browser tabs, nine disconnected tools — which is a real productivity problem for knowledge workers who are already sophisticated enough to use AI agents. Nobody is being exploited by the current state of multi-agent complexity. There's no hidden tax being extracted from people who lack information or power. Compare this to Affirm making credit card terms transparent, or Ownwell cracking open property tax opacity for homeowners who didn't know they were being overcharged. Mercury makes already-empowered users more efficient. That's valuable, but it doesn't move me the way a structural redistribution of access and information does. The cascading impact stays contained within the population that already has agents to orchestrate.

Mercury has a team with relevant deployment credentials and a real market need they've identified from direct experience. I wish Stefano and Naveen well. But this is a consensus play in a well-funded space with no biographical forge, no contrarian edge, no hidden system to crack open, and a thin position in the stack that the underlying platforms are already moving to absorb. The dimensions that most decisively place this outside my framework are the absence of contrarian conviction — this is the most consensus category in AI right now — and the absence of an invisible system, which is where my deepest conviction lives.
