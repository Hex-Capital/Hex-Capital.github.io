# Kimpton AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Kimpton is that it wasn't conceived as a startup idea. It was built as production trading infrastructure inside Level III Capital, the quant fund the founders ran together starting in 2021. When I ask my first question -- "how did they find this problem?" -- the answer is clean: they were their own users for years. They built tools to manage portfolios and run quantitative research because they needed those tools to trade. That's organic. Dropbox happened because Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive. Kimpton happened because three quant fund operators got tired of stitching together Bloomberg terminals and spreadsheets. The founder-problem backstory is real and specific, which puts them ahead of the median AI company I see.

But organic discovery alone isn't enough. The second test is schlep -- is this problem painful enough that most smart people unconsciously filter it out? And here's where Kimpton's story weakens. Financial data integration and AI-powered research tools are not hiding in plain sight. They're in plain sight and everyone is running at them. AlphaSense has $500 million in ARR. Reflexivity raised $30 million backed by Druckenmiller. Composer, Mezzi, FinChat -- the list keeps going. When I noted in early 2025 that the most impressive YC companies weren't working on AI, I was describing exactly this pattern: when an entire category becomes consensus, the returns get competed away before the companies even launch. Schlep blindness works as a moat precisely because the schlep scares people off. Here, the schlep -- brokerage APIs, financial data normalization, regulatory complexity -- isn't scaring anyone. It's attracting them.

The founders themselves are solid. Three computer science graduates, two from Goldman Sachs engineering, one who interned at SpaceX. They've worked together for four years at Level III Capital. That co-founder relationship predates the startup by a wide margin, which matters -- I've seen too many teams disintegrate when co-founders met just to start a company. And they're technical. They built the platform. Mauricio Ortiz is rated as a top AWS developer on Toptal. Adrian Del Bosque was doing cloud engineering at Vistra Corp. These aren't founders who need to hire engineers to build their product; they are the engineers. On the technical hacker dimension, they score well. But I notice none of them have public GitHub repos, which is a minor gap -- I like to see code in the open because it signals the kind of hacker who builds for the joy of building, not just for the job.

What concerns me most is the absence of user pull after four months in market. The product launched November 2025. By March 2026, they have 571 Twitter followers and 1,251 on LinkedIn. The launch post got 81 likes. There's a single reference to someone on the "$200 plan" maxing out credits, which is the thinnest possible signal that anyone is paying. Compare this to what I look for: users who found the product organically, who tell others without being asked, who would be upset if it vanished. I don't see that evidence. At pre-seed I don't demand it, but after four months with a live product, some organic pull should be visible if the product is hitting a nerve. The "Cursor for investing" positioning is clever, but Cursor succeeded because developers genuinely wanted it from day one -- the waitlist and word-of-mouth were immediate. The absence of similar energy here is notable.

The strongest bull case goes like this: Bloomberg charges $24,000 a year and the product hasn't meaningfully changed in decades. There's a massive price gap between Bloomberg and nothing. If Kimpton can deliver 60% of Bloomberg's analytical capability for $200 a month, augmented by agentic AI that actually runs backtests and Monte Carlo simulations against your real portfolio, that's a genuine value proposition. The founders' quant fund experience means they understand what investors actually need, not what demos well. And the Level III Capital infrastructure gives them years of head start over teams building from scratch. If the agentic computation layer -- sandboxed Python, 27 integrated tools, portfolio-aware trade proposals -- proves genuinely difficult to replicate, the "consensus category" concern fades. The question is whether execution quality in this specific niche can outrun both well-funded startups and the inevitable features that Robinhood and Interactive Brokers will ship natively.

I'd give the bull case maybe a 25% chance of being right. The organic origin and technical team are real. But the crowded market, minimal user signal, and commoditization risk from both foundation model providers and incumbent fintech platforms create too many ways this doesn't work. The company doesn't need multiple miracles -- that's good -- but it does need to win a street fight against well-funded competitors in a category where everyone can see the opportunity. The best startup ideas are the ones everyone unconsciously avoids. This one, everyone is consciously pursuing.
