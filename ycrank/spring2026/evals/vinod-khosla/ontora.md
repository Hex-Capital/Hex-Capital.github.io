# Ontora -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what immediately strikes me about Ontora: the technology underneath it is available to everyone. LLM-based interviewing, retrieval-augmented generation over enterprise documents, conversational agents -- these are capabilities that any team with API access to a frontier model can assemble in weeks. The dossier says it plainly: "broadly available foundation models" with "moderate barriers to replication." When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu was building custom ASICs and a fundamentally different router architecture that Cisco couldn't replicate without dismantling their own product line. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown was engineering plant-based heme at the molecular level -- deep science that took years of R&D to get right. Ontora is an application layer. Application layers get commoditized. Celonis, with $1.77 billion in capital and $771 million in revenue, is already expanding into AI-powered process intelligence. This is not a technology moat; it's a head start measured in months, not years.

Now let me address the consequence of success. Ontora frames itself against the $1.06 trillion management consulting market. That number is real, but it obscures what Ontora actually does. The product automates stakeholder interviews and process mapping -- one phase of a consulting engagement. McKinsey doesn't charge $500 million a year because they're good at interviewing employees. They charge because they provide strategic frameworks, executive relationships, political cover for hard decisions, and implementation oversight. If Ontora succeeds brilliantly, it automates perhaps 15-20% of what consulting firms deliver -- the diagnostic labor. That's a real business, maybe a billion-dollar business at scale. But it doesn't make consulting obsolete. It doesn't change how industries operate. The Vertiv testimonial is telling: "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon." Faster diagnosis is valuable. It is not transformational. When I think about consequences that justify a bet, I think about reclaiming half the planet's arable land, or achieving nuclear fusion, or democratizing financial services for 2 billion unbanked people. "Organizational diagnostics delivered faster" doesn't clear that bar.

The founders present a pattern I've seen many times and almost always pass on. Maximilian Arnold built CoAnalyst at Porsche -- an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data. Now he's building Ontora -- an agentic RAG system for enterprise operational data. This isn't an outsider attacking an industry with fresh eyes. This is an insider applying the same technical approach to an adjacent domain. It's competent engineering, not contrarian invention. Leon Iwanowitsch comes from HSG, a top European business school -- the kind of institution that produces management consultants, not the kind that disrupts them. Nobody is laughing at this idea. Nobody is telling them it's impossible. The experts and incumbents are nodding along and building the same thing. That's the wrong signal. My best investments were in companies that incumbents mocked -- Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never work, the food industry told Pat Brown that plant-based meat was a joke. When Celonis and Gartner are actively publishing about AI-powered consulting automation, you're not ahead of the curve. You're on it, which means the curve's consequence of success has already been bounded by consensus.

Let me give the strongest bull case its due. If you believe that AI agents will eventually handle 80% of what junior and mid-level consultants do -- not just interviews but analysis, recommendation generation, implementation tracking -- then Ontora's interview-and-process-mapping tool is a wedge into something much larger. The timing is legitimately good: LLMs crossed a quality threshold for open-ended conversational interviewing in the past 18 months, and being the first productized solution with enterprise integrations (CRM, email, documents, meeting transcripts) could build switching costs and data advantages over time. The Vertiv reference from a strategy manager at a public company is meaningful at pre-seed -- it demonstrates that at least one enterprise buyer found real value. If you squint, this is the beginning of autonomous consulting at scale. But the "if you squint" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The path from "faster interviews" to "obsoleting McKinsey" requires multiple leaps that the current product architecture doesn't suggest, and the commodity technology base means any well-capitalized competitor can make those same leaps.

I pass on this. It's the kind of B2B efficiency play where the team is capable, the market exists, the technology functions, and the consequence of full success is a solid enterprise software company in a competitive category. The founding team hasn't identified a problem so important that the attempt itself is justified regardless of outcome. They've identified a workflow that can be accelerated -- and they've reduced their risk of failure by targeting a well-understood pain point with well-understood technology. That's precisely the trade-off I warn against. You've made the bet safe enough that winning it doesn't change anything fundamental about how the world works.
