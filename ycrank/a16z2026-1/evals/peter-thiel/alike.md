# Alike -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Alike's CTO Max Van Kleek has 9,012 Google Scholar citations across 25 years of research in human-AI interaction and privacy-preserving systems at MIT, PARC, Nokia, IBM, and Oxford. That is a genuinely formidable academic record — the kind of credential that makes Paul Graham's pattern-matching instinct fire. But here is the problem: Van Kleek is still an Associate Professor at Oxford. He has not made an irreversible commitment. A full-time tenured professorship and a startup CTO role are structurally incompatible at the execution speed this market demands, particularly when Dust has $21.5 million from Sequoia and CrewAI has $18 million from Insight Partners and both are building in the same territory. The academic credentialing, paradoxically, becomes a signal of indefinite thinking — a hedge against the possibility that the startup doesn't work, which is exactly the mental posture that guarantees it won't.

The monopoly secret test produces a null result. What important truth does Alike know that the market denies? "Enterprise coordination is wasteful" — everyone agrees. "AI agents need to communicate with each other" — Google launched the A2A protocol in April 2025 with Atlassian, Salesforce, SAP, and Workday as partners, and placed it under Linux Foundation governance. Fifty-plus technology companies are building on this premise right now. Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025. This is not a secret — it is the loudest consensus in enterprise software. Alike's positioning as the "agent collaboration layer" is precisely the kind of thesis that appears in a dozen pitches in the same week, each using the same foundation models, each claiming a slightly different angle on the same obvious insight. The cleantech lesson applies with surgical precision: entrepreneurs swept up by a category trend rather than driven by a specific, non-obvious understanding of the domain.

The competitive landscape is the most damning exhibit. Dust has $7.3 million in ARR and Sequoia's backing. CrewAI has processed 1.4 billion agentic automations. Moveworks was acquired by ServiceNow for $2.85 billion — establishing both a ceiling and a proof that incumbents will absorb this capability rather than cede it. Salesforce is auto-provisioning AI-powered Slack for every new customer and announced 30 new agent features. Against this array, Alike brings 20 self-reported design partners — K-ID ($51 million raised), NewtonX ($47 million raised), Exus Renewables — none of which are the kind of Fortune 500 anchor customers that create the switching-cost monopoly I look for. The dossier notes that Alike's named partners are mid-stage startups, not the institutional buyers whose embedded usage creates structural lock-in. Compare this to Palantir's origin: backed by In-Q-Tel and the CIA from inception, embedding into intelligence workflows where displacement becomes operationally unthinkable. Alike has no equivalent institutional anchor, and "word of mouth" waitlists among startup networks do not constitute a distribution architecture.

The strongest bull case requires a specific belief: that enterprise privacy requirements will create a wedge that open protocols and incumbent platforms cannot serve. Van Kleek's research in privacy-preserving systems is directly relevant, and Alike's per-person, per-channel granular privacy controls could matter in regulated industries — finance, healthcare, defense — where Slack's data practices and Google's open protocol create compliance risks. If Alike repositioned around this insight — not "agent collaboration" but "the only coordination layer that meets enterprise privacy and compliance requirements for autonomous agent communication" — there would be something closer to a secret. Regulated enterprises genuinely struggle with this, and compliance requirements create the kind of switching costs that compound over time. But Alike does not position itself this way. The a16z Speedrun description leads with "autonomous agent to agent communication," not with a specific claim about what the market gets wrong about privacy in multi-agent systems. The privacy architecture is mentioned as a feature, not articulated as the foundational insight that renders competing approaches structurally inadequate.

The Wish anti-anchor is instructive. Wish entered a massive market — e-commerce — without a monopoly structure, competing on price against Amazon and Alibaba. It IPO'd at $14 billion and crashed over 90%. The pattern: large addressable market, obvious value proposition, no structural defensibility. Alike enters the AI agent coordination market — projected at $35 billion by 2030 — with an obvious value proposition, no proprietary technology barrier, an open-source protocol from Google commoditizing the interoperability layer, and multiple funded competitors building the same thesis. The absence of a genuine secret combined with the presence of well-funded competition is the structural signature of a company that has failed to escape competition before it has even started competing. The founding team's Oxford connections produced early design partners, but Oxford network effects do not compound the way Palantir's intelligence community embedding compounded — they are a one-time distribution advantage that flattens as the company scales beyond its origin network.

I pass on Alike because the monopoly criterion fails decisively. There is no visible contrarian insight, the interoperability layer is being commoditized by an open protocol with 50+ partners, and the competitive landscape features multiple funded players pursuing the same thesis simultaneously. The CTO's academic distinction and the CEO's precocious PhD are real credentials, but credentials in a competitive market produce the same outcome as no credentials in a competitive market — because all failed companies are the same.
