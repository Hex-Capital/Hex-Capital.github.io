# Bylaw -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Bylaw is what happens when three Waterloo CS first-years notice a real failure mode in agent infrastructure and arrive at the same conclusion Galileo's $68M-funded team published as Apache 2.0 open source in March 2026. The dimensional signature is a company that scored highest where it had at least to *name* incumbents — Guardrails AI, Lakera, NeMo, Galileo — and lowest where it could not generate any atoms-heavy or regulatory anchor against a layer that integrates with LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI, and Braintrust. That is the shape of a feature, not a company.

The Stripe (2010) comparison is what Bylaw is implicitly reaching for. Stripe named the incumbent failure mode precisely: PayPal had "won" payments by optimizing for merchants, and the secret was that developers were the actual buyer no one was serving. The mechanism that made it load-bearing was that Patrick Collison could articulate why Braintree, Authorize.net, and PayPal *structurally* could not pivot — their org charts had no developer relations function and their integration surface was a 200-page PDF. Bylaw names the analogous failure mode — "tool-permission ≠ evidence verification" — and that distinction is real. But where Stripe's secret was actively rejected by the market in 2010, Bylaw's secret was *agreed with* by Galileo's product team and shipped as open source by March 2026 with AWS, CrewAI, and Glean as launch partners. A secret the consensus has already adopted before your seed round is, by Zero to One's own definition, no longer a secret.

Pure-software-only in a commoditizing market with no network-effect or switching-cost hypothesis is the canonical Founders Fund manifesto failure mode — "we wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters." Bylaw is software-only by construction: an SDK that sits between an agent runtime and a write call. There is no hardware, no defense customer, no regulated certification depth. The EU AI Act high-risk obligations applying August 2, 2026 are gestured at, but Bylaw sells to AI engineering teams, not to the regulated end-users — the regulatory tailwind benefits incumbents with Fortune 50 distribution (Galileo: Comcast, Twilio) far more than a three-person SDK vendor with no disclosed customers. The atoms-heavy criterion is not just absent; it is structurally unreachable from where Bylaw stands.

The monopoly trajectory compounds backwards over 18 months for Bylaw rather than forwards. The customers Bylaw needs — teams already using LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI, and Braintrust — are exactly the platforms positioned to ship native evidence-verification primitives in the next two model-platform release cycles. Each integration Bylaw ships is a tutorial for its eventual replacement. Switching costs accrue only if the policy library and conflict-telemetry corpus become large enough to be painful to recreate, but no customer count, deployed-policy volume, or pilot is disclosed on usebylaw.com as of June 2026. Pricing power at year five depends on a moat that does not exist at month zero.

For the definite-plan dimension, the dossier is silent — no multi-year technical roadmap, no published latency budget for the per-write evidence reconstruction, no false-block rate target. The counterfactual is concrete: if Bylaw had published a "100ms p99 evidence-gate at 10K policies, with formal verification of deterministic policy compilation" commitment with a 24-month horizon, that would shift this dimension a full tier and signal definite optimism rather than indefinite iteration. Without it, Bylaw reads as feature-discovery mode in a category that is already discovering the same feature publicly.

Mazin Al-Ani, Farhan Ur Rehman, and Gurshabd Singh Varaich are Waterloo CS undergraduates with internships at Optiver, Meta, BitGo, theScore, and Ford. Compared against Dustin Moskovitz at Asana (2010) — the canonical override where operator-pedigree weight overrode missing structural moat — the gap is the entire premise of the exception: Moskovitz had shipped Facebook to a billion users before Asana. Bylaw's founders have shipped internship tickets at adjacent firms. The Optiver and BitGo experience is the most interesting data point — environments where unverified writes have direct financial consequence — but internship-as-thesis-origin is not the same as having built and shipped the failure mode at scale. No Known Exception applies; these are not credentialed-outsider profiles in the Collison or Luckey sense.

The 36-month forecast: Bylaw is acqui-hired by Galileo, Langfuse, or Braintrust for under $25M, with the team folded into the acquirer's agent-control product line, or it pivots into agent-eval consulting; not a fund-returner trajectory, not a write from Founders Fund, not a check from my personal capital. The mechanism is straightforward — Galileo Agent Control (Apache 2.0, March 2026, AWS/CrewAI/Glean partners, $68M raised, 834% revenue growth, six Fortune 50 customers) is the substitution event, and the integration partners (LangSmith, Langfuse, OpenAI, Braintrust) are the absorption channel. Three Waterloo students with an SDK do not out-distribute that asymmetry.

The strongest opposing case is the EU AI Act bull: high-risk system obligations effective August 2026 create compliance demand for runtime evidence verification, and a deterministic-policy-engine architecture with signed audit logs is exactly what auditors will ask for. A regulated financial customer — the BitGo intern connection is the load-bearing dossier fact here — could anchor a wedge that Galileo's developer-positioned OSS does not serve. The bear case wins on math: Galileo already has Comcast and Twilio, and regulated enterprises buy from vendors with $68M and Fortune 50 logos, not from three undergraduates with no disclosed pilot. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed enterprise contract with a Tier-1 regulated financial institution at $250K+ ACV before March 2027, naming Bylaw as the runtime-evidence-gate of record. Until that condition lands, this is the indefinite-optimism feature-as-startup pattern Zero to One was written against. Pass.
