# Adialante -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Efraín Torres grew up in Little Village on Chicago's south side, a first-generation college student from a working-class Latino neighborhood, and during his PhD at the University of Minnesota he didn't optimize an existing system — he invented entirely new physics. Adialante's FREE technique (Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes) eliminates gradient coils from MRI, a component that has defined the architecture of every scanner built in the last four decades (Torres et al., *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine*, 2022). That's not iteration. That's someone who looked at a forty-year-old design and said "this is wrong at the foundation." The company name itself — derived from *adelante*, forward — carries his heritage into the mission. When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," Torres's answer is embedded in the physics: a kid from an underserved community who built the tool that could bring cancer screening to communities like the one he came from. Adialante's biographical thread isn't as viscerally forged as, say, a founder who watched a family member die from a missed diagnosis — and the dossier doesn't give me that story. But the path from Little Village to peer-reviewed MRI physics at twenty-six is real forging, real resourcefulness, and the kind of trajectory I recognize because I've lived a version of it.

Adialante's core bet stacks four hard things that make most investors physically recoil: novel hardware, medical device regulation, FDA clearance with no predicate device, and mobile fleet logistics. Hyperfine spent over $200M and five-plus years to get FDA clearance for brain-only portable MRI — a different technology, a different clinical target, and they're still doing only $13.5M in annual revenue as a public company. Promaxo has raised $72-81M for an in-office MRI that clinics must purchase. Openwater has $100M for an optoelectronics approach that isn't MRI at all. Every one of these competitors has ten to thirty times Adialante's capital and none of them has built what Adialante is building: a diagnostic-grade MRI that fits in a truck, costs the clinic nothing to adopt, and charges a flat per-scan fee. The compounding of difficulty here is the point — it's what clears the field. When I wrote my SpaceX check, rockets were blowing up on launch pads and every reasonable person said private space was insane. Adialante's "MRI in a truck with new physics and no FDA clearance" sounds exactly as uncomfortable, and that discomfort is what I invest into.

90% of the global population lacks access to MRI (MNSBIR, July 2025). That statistic hides an extractive system that most patients never see: hospitals sink $2-3M into a machine, build a shielded room around it, hire specialized technologists, and then the economics of that capital expenditure determine who gets scanned, when, and at what price. The scheduling bottleneck, the referral chain, the geographic concentration in urban hospital centers — none of this is designed around the patient. It's designed around the machine. Adialante's mobile model — a truck shows up at your urologist's office, you get scanned, the clinic pays per scan — strips out the entire gatekeeper layer. This is the same structural play as Uber against the taxi medallion cartel or Affirm against credit card opacity: an invisible tax on access, made visible by showing it was never necessary. Adialante doesn't just make MRI cheaper; it reveals that the entire hospital-imaging-center infrastructure was a consequence of hardware limitations that no longer need to exist.

Adialante's defensibility architecture reminds me most of Crusoe Energy — not the energy part, but the structural pattern. Crusoe looked like a Bitcoin mining company but was secretly building portable data center infrastructure; Adialante looks like a medical device company but is secretly building healthcare imaging infrastructure. The patent-pending FREE technique, published in a peer-reviewed journal, represents years of R&D under Dr. Michael Garwood at one of the world's top MRI research programs. The team includes five PhD-level engineers across MR physics, systems engineering, and computational physics (Adialante website lists seven team members total). Replicating this requires not just the physics breakthrough but the reconstruction algorithms, the RF hardware, the regulatory pathway, and the operational fleet — each layer compounds the moat. But I have to be honest: this moat is potential, not proven. Adialante has no FDA submission, no clinical trial data comparing diagnostic accuracy to conventional MRI, and roughly $3M in total funding against a problem that will require orders of magnitude more capital to reach commercial scale. The $3.5M seed target (UMN CTSI, March 2024) is a fraction of what's needed. This is a company that will need to survive multiple near-death funding moments, and the question is whether Torres and Jenkins — co-founders since their first day at Marquette, bound by a decade-long collaboration — have the survival instinct to navigate them.

The bull case an advocate would make is powerful: Adialante has invented genuinely new physics with no analog in the competitive landscape, operates in a space so hard that capital can't easily follow, attacks an opaque healthcare system that gatekeeps cancer screening from billions of people, and is led by a founder whose biographical trajectory from an underserved community to the invention of the underlying technology creates natural alignment with the mission. The bear case is equally real: no FDA clearance, no clinical data, no revenue, capital requirements that dwarf current funding, and first-time founders with academic backgrounds running a hardware-plus-logistics company. If I'm wrong about Adialante, it will be because the FDA pathway takes seven years instead of three, or because the capital markets close before the clinical validation arrives. But I've watched Crusoe Energy nearly die in 2020 and come back to $10B+ — the thesis didn't change, so the conviction didn't either. What would flip me to Pass: if the clinical data, when it arrives, shows FREE imaging has materially lower diagnostic fidelity than conventional MRI for the prostate cancer application. The physics must work clinically, not just in a journal. Everything else — FDA timeline, capital intensity, competitive dynamics — is navigable if the core science holds.

Adialante earns an Invest from me on the strength of two dimensions: the contrarian difficulty stack that clears the competitive field (four compounding hard problems that most capital won't touch), and the invisible system being dismantled (hospital-based MRI as a gatekeeper structure that extracts value from patients and clinics through capital equipment economics). The biographical forge is meaningful — Torres's journey from Little Village to novel MRI physics is genuine — but falls short of the top tier because the dossier doesn't show the deeply personal, biographical *inevitability* that I look for in my strongest bets. The impact dimension is nearly maximal: cancer screening for the 90% who currently lack it is as clear an access expansion as anything in my portfolio. Adialante is a long journey bet — the kind where you write the check knowing it might be years before you know if the physics, the regulation, and the operations all converge. I've made this bet before. The best ones always feel like this at the beginning.
