# Asendia AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Asendia AI is a competent execution-machine team — Rihab Lajmi out of Microsoft Munich and Google Cloud, Badis Zormati out of TU Munich and Infineon — building exactly the vertical AI agent product the Spring 2026 YC batch is going to ship forty variants of, and the dossier line that fixes my read is "$500K+ revenue" against a category where Mercor just closed a $350M Series C at a $10B valuation in October 2025. Asendia AI's highest dimension is founder conviction (real public artifacts, real customers in 35 countries); its lowest is category-sleeve fit (vertical AI agents are the canonical "AI is just more internet" trade I de-rate). That high/low pairing is the whole evaluation: missionary operators pointed at a consensus sleeve at what will almost certainly be a consensus price.

The Pass Example I wrote — "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $25K MRR, three months out of YC, $30M post-money seed, four tier-1 VCs already in" — is the rung directly below Asendia AI on the round-pricing ladder, and the only meaningful divergence is that Asendia AI has 20x the revenue of the archetype ($500K vs $25K) and six no-name pre-seed investors (Angel Invest, Super Angels, Scrape Ventures, NV Capital, EWOR, YC) rather than four tier-1 brands. That is a real divergence on traction but not on the underwriting frame: the YC P26 round will be priced against AI-agent comps where Mercor anchors the ceiling and a dozen sourcing-AI startups (per SitePoint's 2026 list) anchor the floor, and Asendia AI's wedge — "50+ ATS integrations including JobDiva, Lever, Manatal" — is the kind of integration-count moat that markets routinely pay up for and that I cannot generate alpha on at the SAFE cap. The Solana comp at $0.05/coin worked because nobody else was bidding; here everyone with an AI fund is bidding.

Teamshares is the failure-mechanism rung I want to invoke on capital structure, because the whole point of that employee-ownership rollup was that no other VC could underwrite the instrument — and Asendia AI's cap table is the inverse: a generic enterprise SaaS SAFE selling seat-priced software to staffing agencies, no pricing page published (WebFetch, May 2026), no unusual instrument on offer, no holdco wrapper around the ATS-integration IP. Asendia AI will need the standard five-tack regatta — pre-seed, seed, A, B, C — to reach default-alive against Mercor's war chest, and the $500K revenue base is too thin to fund the GTM hire (Founding GTM Lead, $100-150K + 0.5-1% equity per the YC page) without another round inside twelve months. Nothing in the Asendia AI dossier suggests a structure Slow could write that Sequoia couldn't.

Rihab Lajmi is the first Tunisian woman accepted into Y Combinator and has the Slush 2025 speaker slot to prove the missionary arc; Badis Zormati publishes at badiszormati.substack.com on "AI and the evolution of technical hiring" — these are the public artifacts of conviction I screen for, and Asendia AI clears the bar by more than the median P26 founder. The compounding question over 18 months is whether that conviction is pointed at the right asset: Asendia AI is "cloning recruiters' workflows" rather than rebuilding the staffing-agency P&L, and neither founder has documented domain time inside a staffing agency before Microsoft and Infineon. Conviction without operator-domain depth tends to lose to Paradox.ai-style incumbents who knew which compliance bodies (NYC Local Law 144, EEOC) would actually shape the buyer's spec — so the conviction score holds, but it doesn't translate to category-defining founder-market fit.

Counterfactual on founder-as-brand: for Asendia AI to flip a tier on this dimension, Badis Zormati's Substack would need to be the canonical reference staffing-agency CTOs forward each other (it's not — there's no readership signal in the dossier), or Rihab Lajmi would need to have built a Tunisian-engineering-diaspora pipeline that competitors structurally cannot replicate (the OK Reporter and fwdstart.me press hints at this but doesn't document it). Asendia AI's distribution is direct enterprise sales (SGF Global, FSTAFF) plus an active "Founding GTM Lead" hire — that is the paid-acquisition shape John Fish/Bookshelved was specifically the opposite of, and it pins Asendia AI on the bottom half of the founder-brand ladder regardless of the personal press cycle.

Mercor at $10B is the single-anchor parallel for sleeve fit, and the comparison is brutal: Mercor proved the category is real and simultaneously proved that AI-recruiting infrastructure consolidates around whoever raised first and hardest. Asendia AI's positioning ("staffing agencies and BPOs with ATS-deep integration") is a genuine wedge against Mercor's marketplace shape, but it lands inside the exact sleeve — vertical AI agents on top of LLM voice/SMS rails — that I publicly called "consensus-priced commodity exposure, not a venture moment." This is not Lightning Labs in 2018 or Aleo at seed; this is the sleeve I'm explicitly underweighting at Slow, and Asendia AI doesn't carry the offsetting structure (creator distribution, holdco, royalty) that would buy a sleeve override.

My 36-month forecast: Asendia AI reaches $5-12M ARR on the staffing-agency wedge, raises a Series A from a generalist AI fund at a price I cannot make money on, and gets acquired between 2028 and 2029 by a staffing-platform incumbent — Bullhorn is the obvious bidder, JobDiva the second — for a modest revenue multiple that returns the pre-seed cleanly but does not return a Slow fund. The mechanism producing this is ATS-incumbent channel logic, not founder failure: Bullhorn and JobDiva already sell to the same staffing agencies, and the cheapest way for them to neutralize a recruiter-cloning AI layer is to buy Asendia AI before Mercor's enterprise motion arrives in the agency channel.

The bull case that actually stings: Asendia AI did $500K+ in months with a 3-person team, SGF Global runs across 35+ countries, and the Wizard-of-Oz frame — cloning a specific recruiter's manual workflow per tenant — is exactly the manual-process-before-automation template Andrew Kortina and I lived through at Fin. If recruiter-workflow data accumulates inside each tenant fast enough, Asendia AI compounds into a per-customer switching cost the marketplace shape (Mercor) structurally cannot replicate, and a $50M ARR profitable outcome on a thin cap table is real. I lose that argument on price: Asendia AI's P26 round will be marked to the Mercor comp, not to the Fin comp, and "missionary engineers in a consensus sleeve at consensus pricing" is the trade I've stopped making. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: round prices at sub-$8M post with a non-standard instrument — revenue share on placement fees collected through the agent, or an explicit IP-royalty sleeve on the per-tenant recruiter-clone model — at which point Asendia AI becomes a structure-first deal I can underwrite without paying the Mercor tax.
