# Kimpton AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Jack Zumwalt and Mauricio Ortiz ran a $10M digital-asset systematic fund called Level III Capital for roughly four years and then turned their internal research stack into Kimpton AI -- which means Kimpton AI is the rare pre-seed where the founders are the literal user, but built a labor-leverage business to serve themselves. That is the signature: specific knowledge is the highest rung here, leverage architecture is the lowest, and the gap between those two is the whole evaluation. Operator-built tooling delivered through founder discovery calls and FactSet licensing is what Kimpton AI is. That is not what I fund.

**Stack Overflow (2008)** is my anchor on the leverage axis -- Atwood and Spolsky shipped code on the open web, developers pulled the product through links and Google, the marginal cost of the Nth user was zero and nobody had to grant permission for the next one to arrive. Kimpton AI's distribution architecture is the inverse on every leg: kimpton.ai routes prospects to "discovery calls through co-founder Jack Zumwalt," there is no Product Hunt launch, no public GitHub, no founder X presence on either Zumwalt or Ortiz, and the data backbone is a FactSet licensing agreement that can be repriced or revoked. That is capital-and-labor leverage wearing an AI sweater. The Anchorage Digital exception does not apply because Kimpton AI is not entering a pre-consensus category where regulation becomes the moat -- Rogo just priced at $750M post-money in January 2026, AlphaSense raised at $4B, Hebbia took $159M, and Microsoft already acquired Fintool. The narrative has fully arrived.

The Pinterest pass haunts me whenever I see a single-sided product that I'm tempted to score down for taste reasons, so I'll be precise about why Kimpton AI's network score is genuinely low rather than aesthetically low: there is no mechanism in the dossier by which the Nth hedge fund using Kimpton AI makes the (N-1) prior funds more valuable. The mandate.md and strategy.md files are by design private to each portfolio manager -- they encode confidential risk profiles -- which means switching costs may eventually form but cross-user compounding cannot. Each PM seat is an economic island. Uber's 4,000x and Twitter's 400x both turned on user-to-user value compounding; Kimpton AI's payoff geometry is bounded by enterprise SaaS seat math against incumbents already at $1.39B and $750M valuations.

Specific knowledge is where Kimpton AI does real work. Zumwalt and Ortiz met at a Goldman Sachs intern event, joined full-time in February 2020, left in 2021 to co-found Level III Capital, and ran it for four years before productizing the tools they "couldn't buy" -- that is the **Notion (2013)** pattern of Ivan Zhao rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence, transposed onto buy-side research. This is not an ex-McKinsey PM identifying TAM. The integrity leg of the Buffett trio reads clean enough -- no contested exits, no self-promotion about ethics, Adrian Del Bosque followed them from Level III which is a small loyalty signal -- but neither Zumwalt nor Ortiz has the public writing, open-source contribution, or media footprint that makes me confident I want monthly contact for ten years. The dossier is silent rather than negative. I apply my default.

Market and pivot-room: the headline TAM number of $107.7B growing to $1.168T by 2035 is the kind of figure that reads like a slide-one TAM grid -- one of my listed red flags. The honest read is that "AI for portfolio managers" is a verticalized buy-side workflow, narrower than "developer tooling" or "consumer media," and the dossier names only one adjacent pivot vector implicitly: from buy-side PMs to sell-side analysts to RIAs. That is pivot-room, but it is not Uber-pivoting-from-black-car-to-UberX pivot-room. Kimpton AI has 18 months of agentic-finance commoditization risk because LLM + FactSet API is, as the dossier flatly states, replicable by any team with data licensing -- and several YC-batch peers are doing exactly that in P26 alongside Kimpton AI.

The 36-month forecast: Kimpton AI reaches between $1.5M and $4M ARR across 12-30 hedge fund and family office logos, hits the agentic-research commoditization ceiling that Rogo and Hebbia are already pricing in at the top of the market, and gets acqui-hired by FactSet itself, S&P Global, or a Bloomberg-adjacent vendor for $25-70M -- a fine outcome for the founders and a 2-4x for whoever wrote the seed, which is structurally below my 10,000x upside math. The mechanism: when your product runs on a vendor's data, the vendor eventually owns the customer relationship or buys the layer, and FactSet has done this before.

The bull case that actually stings: the Perplexity exception. Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity violated my marketplace criterion, but I waived it because specific knowledge, permissionless distribution, and pre-consensus AI timing all cleared at top quartile. Could Kimpton AI clear three legs the same way? Specific knowledge -- yes, four years running a systematic fund is real. Pre-consensus timing -- no, Rogo at $750M and AlphaSense at $4B means consensus has arrived loudly. Permissionless distribution -- no, founder discovery calls and FactSet licensing are the opposite. Two of three exception gates fail, so the Perplexity carve-out does not extend to Kimpton AI. The condition that flips my verdict: if Zumwalt publishes an open-source version of the mandate.md/strategy.md framework on GitHub and a developer community of quant researchers and emerging-manager PMs starts contributing strategy templates without Kimpton AI signing them as customers -- that would convert this from a labor-leverage business into a code-leverage business and I would write the check. Until that exists, Kimpton AI is a pass.
