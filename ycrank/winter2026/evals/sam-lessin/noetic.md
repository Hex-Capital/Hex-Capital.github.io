# noetic -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the problem with Noetic in one sentence: a direct YC competitor — Saphira AI, batch S24 — is already generating $440K in revenue doing essentially the same thing, with more experienced founders, and a year-plus head start in a market where trust and accuracy are the entire product. When I see two YC companies occupying the same niche within eighteen months, my immediate question isn't "which one wins?" — it's "if this idea is obvious enough that YC funded it twice, is there any non-consensus premium left?" The answer here is no. "Use AI to automate expensive compliance consulting" is an idea that every investor immediately understands. It passes the pitch test but fails my filter, because the clarity of the opportunity is precisely what makes it dangerous.

Let me run my sane-person-insane-idea calibration. The "sane" component is mixed. Tony Gao and Jack Yin both worked at Yale's APOLLO robotics lab, which gives them firsthand exposure to the compliance pain hardware teams experience. Jack Yin's stints at D.E. Shaw, Citadel, and Five Rings suggest raw intellectual horsepower. But all three founders are current Yale undergrads — BS 2024–2028 — meaning they're building this company during college. I've backed young founders before, but the "sane" in my framework requires credibility specific to the domain. Regulatory compliance is a trust-heavy, relationship-dense, error-intolerant field. Knowing that compliance is painful from your robotics research is a different thing from having spent years inside the regulatory apparatus understanding how standards bodies think, how testing labs operate, how compliance officers make decisions. The "insane" component is completely absent — there is nothing about this idea that provokes confusion or skepticism from investors. It's a clean, legible pitch in a hot category. That combination — thin domain credibility plus consensus idea — is the weakest quadrant of my framework.

The AI kill test here has a recursive quality that I find almost darkly funny. The company's value proposition IS AI parsing regulatory documents and generating compliance artifacts. So when I ask "does AI commoditize this business or strengthen it?" the answer is: AI literally IS the business, and therefore the next model upgrade from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google that makes regulatory document parsing trivially accurate commoditizes Noetic's core offering overnight. The dossier explicitly states that "regulatory content itself (standards documents from ISO, UL, IEC) is publicly purchasable, so there is no proprietary data lock." That means any competitor with an LLM and the same purchased standards documents can replicate the core functionality. Compare this to Craftwork in my portfolio — you can't replicate painting crews with a model upgrade. You can't commoditize Metropolis's installed parking hardware with a better prompt. The entire category of "AI agent that reads documents and generates outputs" is precisely the thing I've been writing about when I say software moats are dissolving. It's not just that this company lacks an AI-resistant moat — it's that the product itself is the moat erosion I've been warning about.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. The UL 3300 timing is genuinely interesting — a new safety standard for autonomous robots was formally added to OSHA's program on December 31, 2025, creating an immediate compliance wave for every robotics company deploying commercial systems. If Noetic positioned itself as the go-to tool during this specific regulatory moment, it could build relationships and institutional knowledge that compound. The structural argument about TIC incumbents (SGS at $3B revenue, UL Solutions with 14,000 employees) being unable to self-cannibalize their consulting fees to build software is real disruption theory. And the lab-matching component could theoretically create a two-sided marketplace dynamic with genuine network effects. If the founders accumulated a structured knowledge graph of regulatory mappings — which standard applies to which product category in which jurisdiction — that gets better with every customer engagement, there's a version of this that looks like infrastructure. But that's a lot of "ifs" and "coulds" stacked on a team that has never built or scaled a company, competing against a funded rival that already has revenue in the same market.

The narrative can stretch — from robotics compliance to all hardware compliance to the universal compliance platform — and the underlying TIC market is enormous. That gives it some narrative elasticity. But "we automate what consultants do manually" is a services-replacement narrative, not an infinity story. It doesn't create a new economic primitive or enable an ecosystem. Nobody builds their business on top of Noetic's platform the way applications build on protocols. It's useful — potentially very useful — but it's an application on existing regulatory infrastructure, not a new layer. And at the end of the day, the physical testing that makes hardware compliance hard still has to happen in a physical lab. Noetic automates the documentation upstream, but the actual bottleneck — getting your product tested and certified — remains firmly in the hands of the incumbent TIC firms. That limits both the moat and the ultimate scope.
