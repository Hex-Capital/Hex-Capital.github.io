# Incandor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Incandor is a behavioral-biometrics API sold to bank fraud teams by two Stanford CS founders — Matthew Yekell out of Vista Equity and Cboe, Luc Rosenzweig out of NVIDIA, Apple, Epic Games — and that one-sentence description tells you the whole problem. The highest dimension here is the market: behavioral biometrics is a $3.45B 2026 category compounding at 26.95% with room to pivot adjacent into device intelligence, identity, payments-fraud. The lowest is the one that gates the check: the distribution architecture is enterprise sales to regulated banking buyers under SR 11-7 model-risk review and SOC 2 procurement. That pairing — real market, permissioned distribution — is what kind of company Incandor is. It is a Feedzai-shaped business shipped by a two-person seed team, not a Stack Overflow-shaped one.

Twitter in 2007 is the leverage-architecture HIGH rung — media, anyone posts, zero marginal cost, no gatekeeper. Incandor is the structural opposite. The incandor.com surface is "Request early access," there is no self-serve, no SDK on npm, no open-source repo on Rosenzweig's GitHub beyond one listed snippet, no developer docs anyone can read without a sales call. The buyer is a bank fraud officer whose procurement requires SOC 2, a DPA, and model-risk attestation Incandor has not published. This is labor-and-capital leverage in the nav.al/product-media taxonomy — it requires permission from a JPMorgan, a Chime, a Cash App. The Anchorage exception exists for exactly this shape, but Anchorage's "permission-as-moat" came from a Treasury OCC charter granted at the federal-regulator level; Incandor has no charter, no regulatory wedge, no asymmetry against BioCatch's 60+ patents that lets a permission-required business become a moat instead of a tax. The leverage architecture is wrong for an angel check off my balance sheet.

Pinterest was the canonical pass that taught me network-with-compounding-user-value matters even when I don't aesthetically get it — the inverse pattern here is a single-sided API where each bank's integration does not make the next bank's session-one detection materially better, because the behavioral baselines are per-user not cross-population at any verified scale. Incandor's "cross-customer behavioral baselines and population-level ring detection" is gestured at in the dossier as [Inferred] future moat, not shipped. The Perplexity exception waived this dimension because criteria #1 and #3 cleared at top quartile; Incandor clears neither, so the waiver does not apply. The N+1 bank does not make the Nth bank's product better in any way the dossier documents.

Specific knowledge compounds when the founder lived the problem — Atwood and Spolsky writing Coding Horror and Joel On Software for a decade before Stack Overflow, Ivan Zhao rebuilding personal productivity since adolescence before Notion. Incandor's CTO Rosenzweig brings NVIDIA/Apple/Epic Games systems-and-input-handling depth that is genuinely relevant to capturing mouse, keystroke, and touch telemetry at the kernel layer — that is real, and rare among YC fraud bets. But neither founder has a fraud-domain artifact: no prior fraud team at a bank, no published research on unsupervised behavioral models, no GitHub history of anomaly detection, no blog at Cboe about exchange-side fraud patterns. The leverage-user identity is partial — Incandor's CTO has builder-knowledge for the capture layer, not for the fraud-detection layer where BioCatch's $239M of accumulated label data is the actual moat being attacked.

Eighteen months of compounding on the market dimension is what saves Incandor's score from the pass-floor. Behavioral biometrics is the rare YC category where the "why now" — deepfake social engineering, GenAI bot traffic, authorized-push-payment mule fraud — is genuinely a step-change in the last 24 months per biometricupdate.com, and where the unsupervised label-free architecture Incandor claims is a coherent technical bet against the supervised incumbents. Pivot vectors exist: into account-opening fraud, into agentic-AI session detection, into employee-insider-threat. Incandor has room to be wrong twice on the wedge and still land somewhere.

Yekell and Rosenzweig read clean on the Buffett trio — Stanford CS, no contested exits, no self-promotion about ethics in the dossier, no integrity tells in either direction. The counterfactual that would flip this from MID into a 10-year-partner HIGH is published technical writing, an X presence demonstrating fraud-domain thinking, or a GitHub trail showing the unsupervised approach being built in public before the YC batch. None of that exists — Incandor's founders have no Twitter accounts found, the LinkedIns are sparse, the GitHub is one repo. That is not a red flag, it is silence, and silence at the founder-leverage layer is what stops Incandor from being the kind of bet I size up on.

Thirty-six months out: Incandor is either acquired for $20–60M by Sift, LexisNexis (which already absorbed BehavioSec in 2022), or a payments processor looking to bolt unsupervised behavioral signals onto a supervised stack — or it is sub-$2M ARR with two enterprise pilots and a 2028 down round, killed not by technical failure but by the 9-to-18-month banking sales cycle outrunning the seed-extension runway. The dimensional signature — real market, permissioned distribution, partial specific knowledge — produces a tuck-in acquisition outcome, not a Notion outcome.

The strongest case against my pass is the Anchorage precedent: a regulated-buyer infrastructure business where permission flips from cost into moat once the founder owns a credential incumbents cannot replicate. The bull mechanism is that Incandor's label-free architecture is structurally hard for BioCatch and Feedzai to copy without cannibalizing supervised-model contracts already booked — a genuine innovator's-dilemma wedge. I'd buy that argument if Incandor had one signed top-20 US bank pilot with a published case study validating the >99% account-takeover claim, or a Visa/Mastercard distribution deal that turned the permissioned channel into a wedge instead of a tax. Neither is in the dossier. The flip condition is concrete: a named bank pilot disclosed publicly with third-party benchmark within twelve months. Until then Incandor is not my check — the leverage architecture is wrong, the network compounding is unproven, and the asymmetric-payoff math does not punish me for waiting to see the wedge before sizing in.
