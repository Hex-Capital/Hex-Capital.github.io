# Clawvisor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for $70M in 2023 after running Airbnb Trust & Safety engineering from 2012 to 2017, and now Clawvisor is the second swing of an identity-and-trust operator at the AI-agent layer -- this is what specific knowledge looks like when it stacks. The problem with Clawvisor is the shape of the product underneath that specific knowledge. Clawvisor is a B2B authorization gateway with `sales@clawvisor.com` as its public contact and 202 GitHub stars as its distribution proof. The highest dimension here is the founder; the lowest is the network math. That pairing -- deep specific knowledge wrapped around a single-sided middleware -- is the **Perplexity** (2022) silhouette without the API-as-distribution explosiveness, which means the Known Exception that saved Perplexity does not automatically extend to Clawvisor.

**Stack Overflow** (2008) is the load-bearing analogue for criterion #3 here -- Atwood and Spolsky were developers writing the product they themselves needed, and Clawvisor's Eric Levine ran Trust & Safety engineering at Airbnb for five years and then built and sold an identity verification company. Where Clawvisor diverges from the Stack Overflow rung is that the specific knowledge sits in the *founder*, not in the *artifact*: Atwood had already shipped Coding Horror to a community before the equity story; Clawvisor has 202 stars and 27 forks on `clawvisor/clawvisor` (May 2026) against Composio's 100K developers, which means the founder's specific knowledge has not yet metabolized into community-evident specific knowledge. That gap is recoverable -- the Berbix → Socure $70M outcome tells me Levine knows how to convert identity-domain depth into a buyer-relevant artifact -- but it caps the upper bound until the OSS repo behaves like Stack Overflow did pre-equity, not like a marketing surface.

**Perplexity** is also the Known Exception I have to test against on criterion #2, because Clawvisor is similarly single-sided -- a customer's policy stack does not make the next customer's policy stack more valuable, in the way an Uber rider made the next Uber driver more valuable. Perplexity got the waiver because criterion #1 (API-as-distribution), criterion #3 (Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD), and pre-consensus AI category timing all cleared at top quartile. Clawvisor clears criterion #3 (Berbix exit, Airbnb T&S) and clears category timing (agent auth pre-consensus), but does *not* clear criterion #1 at top quartile -- the OSS repo is a real signal at 202 stars but the GTM has `sales@clawvisor.com` and competitors like Arcade.dev ($12M seed, March 2025) and Composio ($1M+ ARR, 200+ paying customers, July 2025) are already operating at materially larger code-leverage scale. Two of three Perplexity gates, not three. The waiver does not extend.

Permissionless leverage at Clawvisor is mixed, not absent (mechanism analysis). The Go codebase is open source, the 14+ adapter library compounds with every new SaaS integration, and any developer can fork `clawvisor/clawvisor` without a sales call. Eighteen months from now, this either becomes a Nango-shaped OSS-to-paid funnel where the adapter library is the moat, or it becomes a Permit.io-shaped policy-engine sales motion where Clawvisor is hiring SDRs against Okta-class incumbents -- and the second outcome is structurally a capital/labor leverage business, which is below my bar. The 202-star repo at month T+0 needs to be ~5,000 stars and forks contributing adapters by T+18 for the leverage architecture to register as permissionless rather than as marketing-with-a-license-file.

The agent-auth market for Clawvisor is platform-shaped on paper but operationally crowded (counterfactual). Composio at $29M raised with 500+ integrations and 200+ paying customers, Arcade.dev at $12M seed with hundreds of pre-built tools, Permit.io at $14M raised on OPA/OPAL -- this is not a quiet pre-consensus category, it is a contested one with the narrative already arriving. For Clawvisor's market score to flip a tier, the dossier would need to show that "purpose-based per-request verification" is a meaningfully different SKU from "agent tool layer with built-in auth" -- a customer LOI with a named buyer choosing Clawvisor *over* Arcade.dev for that specific enforcement primitive would do it. The dossier does not contain that artifact. The pivot room is real -- agent firewall, MCP enforcement, audit logs for compliance -- but the market is already pricing competitors for those adjacencies too.

Levine reads clean on the Buffett trio (single-anchor parallel to **Notion's** Ivan Zhao on the integrity leg). The Berbix → Socure $70M exit closed without flagged litigation, the YC Visiting Group Partner role is a community-trust signal Garry Tan does not hand out to people who burn teams, and Levine is not running around advertising his honesty. Solo founder at team size 1 is a real execution discount against Composio's 25+ engineers, but Naval's framework does not punish solo founders -- it punishes unresolved 50/50 co-founder splits, which is the opposite problem. The 10-year-partner test passes on Clawvisor without strain.

Thirty-six-month forecast on Clawvisor: acqui-hired by an identity incumbent -- Okta, Auth0/Twilio, or Cloudflare -- or by a winning agent-tool platform like Composio, for $40M-$120M, with Levine running an "agent identity" product line inside the acquirer; standalone $50M+ ARR by 2029 is unlikely given the capital gap to Composio and Arcade.dev. The mechanism producing this forecast is the high/low signature: Clawvisor's specific-knowledge ceiling is high enough that Levine's *next* job is valuable to an acquirer, but the network-compounding floor is low enough that the standalone math does not generate the 10,000x upside that justifies a pre-consensus angel check at angel-stage portfolio construction.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that Levine's Berbix playbook is exactly the playbook -- he built identity infrastructure, sold it to a strategic for $70M in five years, and is now running it back with a larger TAM and a category that did not exist when Berbix started. The bear-case rebuttal lives in the Red Flags: Clawvisor's primary public contact is `sales@clawvisor.com` not a public-facing developer-relations identity, Levine's X account is at 419 followers (May 2026) which means the founder-as-leverage media channel is dormant, and the OSS repo at 202 stars is an order of magnitude below where Composio's developer community sits. Code-leverage and media-leverage are both visibly underweighted at Clawvisor relative to capital/labor-leverage signals -- and that is the structural read I cannot waive. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Clawvisor reaches 3,000+ GitHub stars with 30+ external contributors to the adapter library inside 12 months. Hit that, and the leverage architecture has flipped from "OSS as marketing" to "OSS as distribution," and I size in. Until then, Clawvisor is a watch with respect, not a check.
