# Grove Tax -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Grove Tax is building AI automation for tax preparers at a moment when 300,000 CPAs have walked away from the profession since 2020 and exam candidates have dropped 37%. That workforce crisis is real, and I don't dismiss it. But when I ask my first question -- tell me the founders' stories, not the business -- what I hear back is career trajectory, not crucible. Uday Nandam was a PM at Intuit across ProConnect, Lacerte, and Intuit Link, and claims Head of Product for UltraTax at Thomson Reuters. That's a decade inside the two dominant tax software platforms. Gaurav Mathur engineered at Indeed, Airbnb, and Cloud Kitchens. Both are credentialed operators who identified a market gap from inside the machine. That's exactly the founding story that doesn't activate my pattern recognition: "I worked at the incumbents and saw the opportunity." I'm looking for the person who was broken by this system and came back to fix it -- the tax preparer drowning in manual work who taught herself to code, the immigrant family that got destroyed by a preparation error nobody caught. That biographical inevitability isn't here. Nandam's most recent verified role before Grove Tax was Product Management Lead at Raise Commercial Real Estate, a non-tax company, and his Thomson Reuters credentials couldn't be independently verified. These are gaps I'd want closed before writing a check.

The deeper problem for Grove Tax from my framework is that tax preparation is not an invisible system. Compare this to Ownwell, which I backed because property tax assessments are genuinely opaque -- homeowners don't understand how their properties are valued, don't know they can appeal, and the system extracts billions through that ignorance. Tax preparation is the opposite: everyone knows they need a CPA, everyone sees the bill, the workflow is well-understood by practitioners. Grove Tax is automating a visible process -- intake forms, document categorization, e-signatures -- not exposing a hidden structure that extracts value from people who don't understand it. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity, it revealed a system designed to confuse. Grove Tax's $25-per-return Collect product and $10-per-return Deliver product are efficiency tools for a market that already knows what it needs. That's a real business, but it's not the kind of structural revelation that drives my highest-conviction bets.

I also can't find the contrarian signal. AI for tax preparation is consensus-attractive right now -- TaxGPT raised $5.22M, Canopy has $292M in funding and is layering AI features into its practice management platform, Taxnova is literally in the same a16z Speedrun cohort, and Thomson Reuters announced AI-powered compliance solutions in 2025. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "AI for accountants." This is the kind of space where investors compete for deals, not the kind where they run from them. My biggest wins -- SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically toxic, Contraline when male birth control made VCs physically squirm -- came from founders operating in zones of active discomfort. Grove Tax operates in a zone of active enthusiasm, which historically means the market is efficient enough that execution alone won't produce venture-scale returns.

The bull case I want to be fair to: the per-return pricing model with no seat fees is clever because it directly conflicts with how Intuit and Thomson Reuters make money. Their revenue scales with headcount; Grove Tax's revenue scales with automation replacing headcount. That business model tension could give Grove Tax a structural opening if incumbents can't cannibalize their own seat-based revenue. The data flywheel potential is real -- each processed return could improve document recognition and form classification models. And the 7-year document storage creates genuine switching costs once firms adopt the system. If Grove Tax ships the Prepare & Review module (currently "Coming Soon") and achieves the accuracy required for production tax returns across thousands of form variants and state-specific rules, this could compound into something defensible. What would have to be true: Nandam's unverified depth at Thomson Reuters is real and translates into accuracy advantages that commoditized LLM extraction can't match, the Prepare module launches before Canopy or Thomson Reuters ship equivalent capabilities, and small firms adopt fast enough to build a data moat before the funding gap ($30/return versus Canopy's $292M war chest) becomes insurmountable.

Grove Tax lands as a Pass for me. The two dimensions that lock it: the absence of biographical forge in the founding story (career optimization where I need biographical inevitability) and the absence of an invisible system being disrupted (workflow efficiency where I need structural revelation). Uday Nandam and Gaurav Mathur may build a successful tax automation company -- the workforce crisis provides real demand pull, and the domain expertise is relevant. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. It's a competent team automating a visible process in a consensus market where well-capitalized competitors are already moving. I wish them well, but my check goes to the founders who couldn't stop themselves from building, not the ones who saw the opening.
