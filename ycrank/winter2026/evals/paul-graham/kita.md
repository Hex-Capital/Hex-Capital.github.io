# Kita -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Kita is the schlep. Parsing Filipino e-wallet statements, Indonesian payslips, and Mexican utility bills to help lenders underwrite loans in countries where banking APIs don't exist — this is the kind of problem that makes smart engineers in San Francisco unconsciously look away. Not because it's technically impossible, but because the work is ugly. Every document format is different. Every country has different regulations. The languages are different. The financial instruments are different. Ocrolus has $252 million in revenue processing US documents and has zero rational incentive to figure out how GCash statements work in the Philippines. That structural disincentive is exactly what creates the opening. When a problem persists for years despite being obvious to everyone in the industry, the explanation is almost always schlep blindness — and the schlep here is enormous.

The organic origin is real but not the visceral kind I find most compelling. Carmel Limcaoco is from Manila. She grew up in a country where most of the population is unbanked and a borrower's entire financial life lives in paper documents and e-wallet screenshots. That's not market research — she didn't read a report about emerging market fintech and decide it was a big TAM. She comes from the market. But I'd feel stronger if the dossier showed her working at a Philippine lender, or personally struggling to get credit, or watching a family member navigate this exact pain. Being from the country gives you cultural context and local networks. It doesn't automatically mean you felt this specific problem in your bones. Still, the connection is genuine enough that I believe she understands something about this market that a team of Stanford engineers picking from a list of emerging market opportunities would not.

The technical half of this founding team is where I get genuinely excited. Rhea Malhotra won the Firestone Medal — Stanford's highest honor for a CS thesis — and her research is in computer vision and vision-language models. That's not adjacent expertise. That IS the core technology. She has published at ICRA, Humanoids, and RSS, and she has 25 public GitHub repos. When I evaluate technical founders, I'm looking for people who could build the thing themselves, and whose technical instincts are good enough to make elegant architectural choices under constraints. Rhea is clearly that person. The pairing works: Carmel brings the Philippine market knowledge, product sense from Apple, and prior founding experience; Rhea brings deep, directly relevant technical capability. They met before Stanford and have been building together since — that's the kind of co-founder relationship I want to see, not two people who matched on a co-founder dating site.

The most surprising data point is the six-figure paid pilot secured the week the prototype was built. If that's accurate, it tells you something important about how badly lenders in the Philippines need this. You don't write a six-figure check for a week-old prototype unless the alternative — manual document review capping your lending volume — is genuinely painful. The dossier notes this expanded to customers across multiple Southeast Asian markets at roughly 30% month-over-month growth, though the source couldn't be directly verified. I'd want to confirm those numbers, but even the paid pilot alone is a strong signal of real demand at pre-seed. Lenders are economically rational actors. They're paying because this solves a problem that costs them real money every day.

The risk I think about most is whether the per-country markets are large enough to build something venture-scale. The Philippines alternative lending market is $1.6 billion total — the document processing slice is a fraction of that. Indonesia and Mexico add more, but each requires its own localization work, its own regulatory navigation, its own set of document formats to train on. The question is whether the platform generalizes across emerging markets efficiently or whether each new country is nearly as much work as the first. If each country is a new schlep, growth is linear with effort. If the VLM architecture transfers and only the training data changes, growth compounds. I suspect the truth is somewhere in between, and the data flywheel — linking document signals to repayment outcomes — is what tilts it toward compounding. But this is the key strategic question the founders need to answer with execution, not slides.

The commoditization risk from general-purpose VLMs is real but I think misunderstood. As models get cheaper, Kita's input costs go down. They're not selling access to a vision model — they're selling the localized signal extraction layer, the fraud detection calibrated to actual repayment outcomes in specific markets, and the integration into lender underwriting workflows. That's the picks-and-shovels play I've seen work repeatedly: let the underlying technology commoditize while you own the domain-specific application layer. The moat isn't the VLM. The moat is knowing what a legitimate GCash statement looks like versus a doctored one, and having the outcome data to prove your signals predict repayment.

This is my kind of bet. The schlep is real and structural. The founder-market connection is genuine. The technical co-founder has directly relevant, world-class capability. And there's already a paying customer. It's not Airbnb-level conviction — I don't have the visceral organic discovery story or the adversity-tested resourcefulness evidence — but the pieces fit together in a way that makes me want to write a check.
