# Maquoketa Research -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier has nothing to do with Maquoketa itself: Stark Defence won a €268 million German contract despite failing all four test strikes. Let that sink in. A company that couldn't hit a target won a quarter-billion-euro deal. That tells you everything about the procurement dynamics in this market — relationships, timing, and sovereign urgency currently matter more than technical superiority. For a pre-seed company with zero disclosed contracts, no SBIR awards, and a website returning a 403 error, that should be deeply sobering. You can build the best drone in the world and still lose to the company that filed the right paperwork six months earlier.

I backed Mach Industries — a 19-year-old building weapons systems against Lockheed Martin. I understand the outsider-in-defense thesis viscerally. The question I ask isn't "can a tiny team compete with Raytheon?" — it's "what happens if precision strike becomes so cheap that every infantry squad has autonomous munitions?" The consequence of that world is genuinely significant. Military power redistributes. Expensive manned platforms become liabilities. Nations that can mass-produce cheap autonomous strike drones gain asymmetric advantage over nations that rely on exquisite, expensive platforms. That consequence is real — and it's already materializing. Ukraine demonstrated it. Germany is buying it. The U.S. Army's PBAS program is procuring it. But here's what separates this from my OpenAI bet: the consequence of this *category* succeeding is enormous, while the consequence of *this specific company* succeeding versus Anduril or Helsing or AeroVironment succeeding is indistinguishable. The world doesn't change differently depending on which drone company wins. That matters in my framework — I want to fund the company whose success creates a different world, not a company competing for share in a world that's already changing.

The technical angle is the strongest card in Maquoketa's hand. Hernandez's affiliation with Berkeley Wireless Research Center — one of the premier RF and mixed-signal research labs globally — and his phased array specialization is genuinely atypical for a pre-seed drone startup. In contested electromagnetic environments where GPS is denied and communications are jammed, the drone that can still navigate and acquire targets autonomously wins. That's not a software layer you bolt onto a DJI frame. Phased array design is hard physics and hard engineering — the kind of capability that takes years to build and can't be replicated by a coding bootcamp graduate with API access. If the team can deliver EW-resistant autonomous guidance as a vertically integrated hardware-electronics stack, that's a real technical moat against the commodity FPV assemblers. That "if" is doing enormous work in that sentence, though. I have no evidence of a prototype, no test data, no demonstration — just a YC page description and a LinkedIn profile. The entire technical thesis is [Inferred], not demonstrated.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Hernandez's phased array capability creates an autonomous guidance system so robust in contested environments that military customers specifically select Maquoketa over better-funded competitors. The team's Berkeley engineering roots give them iteration speed that larger organizations can't match. The large primes face genuine innovator's dilemma — their margins depend on expensive, low-volume platforms, structurally preventing them from building cheap expendable munitions. YC's defense network compresses the procurement timeline from 24 months to 6. And radical affordability emerges naturally — if their in-house electronics replace what incumbents source from expensive mil-spec suppliers, the cost per unit drops by an order of magnitude, making precision strike available to allied militaries that currently can't afford Switchblades. That's a path to what I'd call "democratized defense" — precision strike too cheap to ration. If all of that is true, this could be a significant company.

But I can't invest on inference alone. I have no evidence of founder learning rate — no public writing, no press, no GitHub, no Twitter, nothing that shows me how these founders think or how they've adapted since starting. The competitive field is brutal: five companies with $100M+ in funding each and active military contracts. The "autonomous" framing may be constrained by DoD Directive 3000.09 before it even reaches a battlefield. And the market, while large in aggregate, has TAM estimates varying by an order of magnitude ($640M to $4B in 2025), which tells me nobody actually knows how big this is yet. I need to see a working system, a customer conversation, or at minimum a founder who's telling me something about this problem that I haven't heard from the last ten defense-drone pitches. Right now this is a credentialed team in a hot space with no distinguishing evidence of execution. That's a pass from my portfolio, though I'd take the meeting to assess what I can't see in the dossier.
