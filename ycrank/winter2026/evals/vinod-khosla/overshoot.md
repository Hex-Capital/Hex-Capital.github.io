# Overshoot -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Overshoot: these are two cousins who spent their careers at Uber, Meta AI, and Intel building exactly the systems they're now rebuilding as a startup. Zakaria wrote GPU inference kernels at Meta. Younes built a CV training and serving platform at Cosmonio that Intel acquired. And what did they build together? A faster inference API for serving open-source vision models. This is the textbook pattern I warn about -- industry insiders executing the industry's playbook. They know what Fireworks AI and Together AI look like from the inside, so they built a slightly more specialized version. Their experience has given them a precise understanding of what's possible, which is exactly what constrains them from attempting what seems impossible.

Let me start where I always start: if Overshoot succeeds completely, what changes? Developers get sub-200ms inference on video feeds through a clean API. That's useful infrastructure. Security cameras get smarter. Robotics apps process vision faster. But Overshoot isn't the security company or the robotics company -- it's the plumbing underneath. And plumbing that serves open-source models (Qwen, InternVL) with no proprietary technology, no data moat, no network effects, is plumbing that somebody with $554 million in the bank -- that's Together AI -- can replicate the moment the market segment gets large enough to notice. The consequence of success here is a developer tools business with 10,000 or maybe 100,000 API customers. That's a company, not a transformation. Nobody's food system changes. No trillion-dollar industry gets disrupted. The world operates the same way, just with slightly faster video inference calls.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. VLMs applied to real-time video genuinely didn't exist as a practical capability eighteen months ago. The rate of improvement in vision-language models -- Qwen, InternVL, the Gemini family -- is on an exponential curve, and we're early. If real-time video understanding becomes as foundational as text-based AI, the specialized infrastructure layer could become enormously valuable. Zakaria's combination of Uber-scale low-latency systems engineering and Meta's GPU kernel optimization is legitimately rare technical DNA for this exact problem. The "3 lines of code" developer experience could create adoption velocity that outpaces incumbents focused on text. And the dossier notes that Younes "witnessed firsthand customers abandoning traditional Computer Vision" for VLMs -- that's a real market transition happening now. If Overshoot captured the default developer on-ramp for video AI the way Stripe captured payments, the outcome would be substantial. But Stripe had a regulatory moat and merchant-side lock-in. Overshoot is proxying open-source models through an optimized streaming layer. The switching cost is literally three lines of code -- their own marketing copy makes this point.

The technology story confirms my concern. The differentiation is "codec, streaming protocols, and inference engines specific to video." That's engineering optimization, not structural disruption. KiOR taught me that technology achievement without unit economics is a trap, and Overshoot teaches a related lesson: engineering optimization without structural defensibility is a feature, not a company. Fireworks AI at $331 million in funding could hire five engineers to build video-specific inference optimizations in a quarter. The 10x latency advantage is real today and evaporates the moment an incumbent decides this niche matters. There is no technology here that makes the existing approach uneconomic -- it makes it marginally slower by comparison, which is a different and far less durable advantage.

The timing dimension is where Overshoot looks strongest, and I'll give credit: they're building into a genuine inflection point. VLMs reaching practical capability on video is a 2024-2025 phenomenon, and being the specialized platform when developers first need this capability is strategically sound. But good timing benefits everyone, including the companies with hundreds of millions in capital. Being early to a trend matters when you can build structural advantages during the window -- network effects, proprietary data, regulatory capture, ecosystem lock-in. I see none of those mechanisms here. 300 developers and 14 GitHub stars is early traction consistent with a hackathon-launched API, not evidence of compounding adoption.

I keep coming back to the core issue: these founders are too talented to spend their careers making video inference 10x faster for a few hundred developers. The problem they've chosen is real but small relative to their capability. When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu wasn't building a slightly faster router -- he was replacing the entire architecture of how telecom networks operated. When I backed Square, Jack Dorsey wasn't optimizing point-of-sale latency -- he was making every person with a phone a merchant. Overshoot is optimizing inference latency. The ambition is calibrated to what they know is achievable, not to what would matter if they achieved it.
