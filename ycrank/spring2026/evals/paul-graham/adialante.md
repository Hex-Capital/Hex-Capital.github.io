# Adialante -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

"Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." I wrote that about Stripe -- about why payment processing sat broken for a decade while smart people built recipe sites instead. Adialante is attacking exactly this kind of problem. MRI hardware is the ultimate schlep: physics, manufacturing, FDA clearance, clinical workflows, and now they're adding mobile fleet operations on top. Most founders' unconscious minds would steer them away from this at step one. The fact that Torres and Jenkins are attempting it tells me something. But what it tells me is incomplete -- I need to know whether they're attacking this schlep because they're relentlessly resourceful, or because they spent five years in an academic lab and this is the natural next step from a PhD. Those are very different animals.

The FREE technology is real science, not vaporware. Peer-reviewed publications in Magnetic Resonance in Medicine. An ISMRM Rabi Award winner who left an academic career to join as principal systems engineer -- that's the kind of signal that matters more than pitch competition wins. When someone who could be faculty at a top program instead joins your four-person startup, that person has seen the technology working in the lab and believes it can work in the world. And the approach itself has the shape of an idea I'd want to fund: eliminating a third of conventional MRI hardware through novel physics sounds like it should be impossible, which is exactly why nobody else is doing it this way. "The biggest startup ideas are terrifying. You wonder if you'd have enough ambition to carry them through."

The mobile per-scan model is the part that shows these founders are thinking beyond the lab. Promaxo sells hardware to urologists -- that's the obvious play. Adialante instead says: we'll bring the MRI to you, you pay per scan, zero capital expenditure. This is a genuine strategic insight. It means clinics face zero risk to try it, which solves the adoption problem that kills most medical device startups. And it creates a conflict for incumbents -- Siemens can't offer per-scan mobile MRI without cannibalizing their multi-million-dollar equipment sales. The business model, not just the technology, is the moat. That said, running a fleet of mobile imaging units is operationally brutal. Staffing, scheduling, maintenance, multi-jurisdiction regulatory compliance -- it's a whole second company. I see no evidence anyone on this team has run operations at any scale, let alone mobile healthcare logistics.

Here's what gives me genuine pause: the competitive landscape. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors" -- I still believe that. But Promaxo has FDA clearance, 69 installed systems, and $80M+ in funding, specifically in prostate MRI. That's not a theoretical competitor; that's a company with paying customers and clinical data while Adialante is pre-FDA. The regulatory gap alone could be two to three years. And Hyperfine is a public company generating $13.6M in annual revenue from portable MRI. Adialante's technology may be better -- the FREE approach might produce superior images at lower cost -- but in medical devices, better technology routinely loses to earlier clearance. The question isn't whether Adialante's physics is novel. It's whether the regulatory timeline destroys the window.

The bull case is this: clinical guidelines are expanding to recommend MRI before prostate biopsy, which means the market is growing faster than the installed base can serve it. If Adialante gets FDA clearance in 2027 or 2028, the market they enter could be meaningfully larger than today's. The per-scan mobile model could scale faster than Promaxo's capital-sale approach because it eliminates the purchasing decision for clinics. Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago and is building this to bring cancer screening to underserved communities -- "the startup founders who end up richest are not the ones driven by money. They're usually trying to improve the world." That kind of earnestness, combined with world-class domain expertise from the best MRI research center in the country, is the combination I find hardest to bet against. If they can make the leap from academic to commercial -- if they can find their equivalent of the Airbnbs' cereal box moment -- this could be enormous.

But I haven't seen that moment yet. No deployed units. No revenue. No LOIs mentioned. No evidence of a prototype being tested on patients. What I see is impressive science, smart people, and a good plan. Plans are worth very little. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off." I'd want to sit across from Torres and Jenkins for ten minutes. If they have the energy the Airbnbs had -- if they're cockroaches who won't die -- this is a bet I'd make despite the regulatory risk and the competitive gap. The technology and market are right. The team has the domain expertise. What I can't evaluate from a dossier is whether they have the determination to grind through the commercial schlep that's coming. Without meeting them, I'm on the fence -- and on the fence means I don't write a check.
