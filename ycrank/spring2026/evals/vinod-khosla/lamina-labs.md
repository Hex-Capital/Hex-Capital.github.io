# Lamina Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I look at is what happens if this company wins completely. Every educator on earth uses Pictor. Every Khan Academy video, every university lecture supplement, every STEM explainer is generated through Lamina's pipeline in eight seconds. And the honest answer is: animation creation gets faster. That is a real improvement to a real workflow, and I do not dismiss it. But it does not make an industry structurally obsolete. It does not reclaim land or replace a power grid or eliminate a trillion-dollar incumbent system. "Fastest deterministic animation infrastructure for EdTech" contains three qualifiers, and each one narrows the consequence of success. The relevant market is not the $110 billion EdTech number -- that includes everything from Coursera subscriptions to classroom hardware. The actual addressable slice is explainer video animation software at $3.83 billion, and within that, they are targeting mathematically precise STEM content specifically. If they capture substantial share of that niche, they build a good business. But a good business is not why I write checks.

The founders are genuinely interesting, and I want to be clear about that. Two Nepalese undergraduates who left MIT and Caltech to build deep tech -- that is exactly the outsider profile I gravitate toward. Rohan Bhattarai has a Bronze Medal from the International Computer Olympiad, Honorable Mention at the International Olympiad on Astronomy, and published astrophysics research from Caltech. Sudip Rokaya built ghostclaw in C++ with 30+ AI provider integrations. These are not people optimizing someone else's playbook. The combination of AI systems engineering and mathematical physics maps precisely to the core technical challenge of converting natural language into deterministic, frame-accurate animations. If I were evaluating the founders independent of the problem, I would want to find them something bigger to work on. That is the tragedy of this pitch -- the athletes are better than the game they have chosen to play.

The technology approach has genuine merit. Deterministic rendering that is mathematically precise and hallucination-free is a fundamentally different architecture from the diffusion-based generative video that Synthesia, Runway, and HeyGen produce. That architectural incompatibility creates real short-term defensibility -- those companies cannot pivot to mathematical precision without rebuilding from scratch. But the other side of the moat is shallow. Manim has 35,800 GitHub stars and an active community. An AI wrapper that converts natural language to Manim code could approximate Lamina's value proposition within months, and multiple teams will attempt this as LLMs improve at code generation. The barrier is execution speed and the quality of text-to-scene conversion, not the rendering engine itself. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, the technological gap was structural -- TCP/IP routing required a fundamentally new architecture that Cisco's installed base could not easily adopt. Here, the gap between Lamina's pipeline and an LLM-generated Manim script is a matter of engineering quality, not architectural incompatibility.

Let me steelman the bull case, because it exists. Square started as a small white dongle and became the payment infrastructure for millions of businesses. Could Lamina start with STEM animations and expand into all deterministic visual communication -- engineering schematics, medical imaging explanations, scientific publications, technical documentation? If text-to-precise-visual becomes a platform rather than a feature, the consequence magnitude scales dramatically. The rate of improvement in LLM capabilities is an exponential tailwind for this category, and these founders -- with their specific combination of physics rigor and AI engineering -- might be uniquely positioned to ride it. The fact that they are building before the category is crowded counts for something. But here is where I apply my KiOR lesson: the technology has to achieve economic impact that displaces the incumbent approach. Square displaced expensive POS terminals with a free dongle and 2.75% swipe fee. Lamina has to displace what, exactly? Manual animation? Educators already use Manim for free, or skip animation entirely. The economic displacement mechanism is unclear.

The timing is reasonable but not revelatory. LLMs crossing the threshold for reliable scene understanding is a real enabling moment, and AI in Education spending is accelerating at 42.8% CAGR. But this is not a case where I am seeing something two years before the market -- everyone sees that LLMs will transform content creation. Knowlify raised $3 million six months ago in the same general space and has already generated 200,000-plus videos. The window for being the outsider who arrives before the crowd has largely closed in AI-for-education. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was genuinely laughable to most investors. When I backed Impossible Foods, plant-based meat was a joke. Nobody is laughing at AI-generated educational content in 2026 -- which means the contrarian premium is gone.

These founders deserve a problem whose consequence of success terrifies them and matters to the world. Deterministic rendering from natural language is a real technical capability. But wrapping it in an EdTech animation tool shrinks the ambition to fit a manageable market rather than stretching it to transform an important one. I would tell Sudip and Rohan what I tell every smart founder aiming at a manageable niche: you are too talented to spend your careers on a problem this contained. Find the version of this technology that makes people say you are crazy for attempting it.
