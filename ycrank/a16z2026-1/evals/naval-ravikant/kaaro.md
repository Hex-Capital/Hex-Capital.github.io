# Kaaro -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Two twenty-year-olds left a company that raised $17M from a16z to go build AI agents for their parents' industry. That sentence contains the entire evaluation -- the rest is just unpacking which parts compound and which parts don't.

Sai Surisetti and Gautham Venkateshwaran were founding engineers one and two at Toma, where they built AI voice agents that automated phone workflows for car dealerships, scaling from roughly twenty to hundreds of customers. Toma is the prior art here, and it matters. The specific knowledge Kaaro is productizing isn't railroad operations per se -- it's the transferable expertise of building AI agents that replace phone calls, emails, and tribal knowledge in relationship-driven industries resistant to digitization. Auto dealerships and freight railroads share a structural DNA: legacy green-screen systems, workflows held together by human memory and phone calls, and incumbents (CDK Global for dealers, Wabtec for rail) whose revenue models depend on the very manual processes AI would eliminate. Both founders claim "second-generation railroader" status through family ties, which provides the domain layer on top of the AI-agent-building layer. Neither layer alone would be sufficient -- a railroad insider without AI agent experience would build a consultancy, and an AI agent builder without railroad roots would spend two years learning what FRA compliance means before shipping anything. The combination is where the specific knowledge lives, though I'd want to probe the depth of the railroad exposure in person. At twenty years old, "grew up around it" and "operated inside it" are different things. I'd score this above the absent-evidence default but below the top quartile -- the Toma track record is real evidence, the railroad heritage is plausible but unverified.

The leverage architecture is where Kaaro maps cleanly to my framework. AI agents processing railcar tracking, idle-car alerts, shipment notifications, and billing workflows are pure zero-marginal-cost code leverage -- once the agent handles one railroad's exception-handling patterns, serving the next similar workflow costs essentially nothing. The compound interest test is the key question: every workflow Kaaro automates accumulates institutional knowledge that currently lives in phone calls and tribal memory. That data flywheel -- operational patterns, billing exceptions, contact networks, exception-handling protocols -- compounds in ways that a competitor entering in year three would need to replicate from scratch. Wabtec's RailConnect faces the same principal-agent trap I saw Google facing against Perplexity: Wabtec generates revenue from per-railroad TMS licensing and the manual data-entry interfaces that feed those systems. Building AI agents that eliminate the manual workflows would cannibalize their own product's usage patterns. Wabtec's engineers are agents of their P&L. Sai and Gautham, as principals with zero legacy revenue, can move where Wabtec's twenty-five-billion-dollar market cap makes movement structurally impossible. The partial drag on Kaaro's leverage score is integration friction -- each railroad runs different legacy systems with limited APIs, meaning some labor-intensive customization is required per deployment. That's not fatal, but it means this isn't Notion-style pure software leverage.

The principal authenticity signal is genuinely interesting but ambiguous. The founders left Toma after it raised a $17M Series A -- walking away from a funded, growing company to start something harder in a less glamorous industry is a principal's move, not an agent's. They've known each other for over a decade and share VEX Robotics backgrounds dating to high school. The co-founder relationship passes the pre-existing-trust test cleanly. But I notice a pattern that could cut either way: "build AI agents for underserved vertical X" is a repeatable playbook. Did they choose railroads because their families are in it and they couldn't stop thinking about the waste -- or because they identified it as the next fundable vertical for their proven AI agent architecture? The family connection suggests the former, but at their age, I can't fully distinguish principal obsession from pattern-matching a successful formula. The strongest bull signal: they joined a16z Speedrun, not YC, which means they leveraged their Toma-era a16z relationship rather than seeking validation from a new network. Principals leverage existing relationships; agents seek new credentialing.

Kaaro's team-to-value leverage ratio is exactly what my shrinking-company thesis predicts. Two technical builders engaging CPKC -- Canada's largest railroad with $14B+ in annual revenue -- is the kind of asymmetry that only exists when AI is the primary workforce multiplier. If Kaaro closes CPKC and CN with a team of two, that's a revenue-per-employee ratio that validates the entire thesis about AI collapsing the efficient company size. The risk that makes this interesting rather than obvious: Class I railroad procurement cycles run six to eighteen months, and "working with CPKC" and "late conversations with CN" are not signed contracts. A two-person pre-seed company burning through a $500K SAFE while waiting for enterprise procurement to complete is structurally fragile. The Gulf & Ohio Railways relationship -- four short-line railroads, thirty locomotives, sixty-four industrial customers -- is the smarter near-term play. Short-lines have lower procurement friction, faster decision cycles, and less IT infrastructure to integrate with. If Kaaro can show measurable ROI at Gulf & Ohio before the CPKC cycle closes, the cash-flow risk is manageable.

The access democratization angle is secondary but real. North America has roughly six hundred short-line railroads that lack IT budgets for full TMS deployments. Kaaro's AI agents could give a thirty-locomotive operation the same operational intelligence that CPKC gets from a dedicated IT department -- that's structural access expansion, not incremental improvement. The pattern maps to AngelList giving individual angels access to deal flow previously restricted to Sand Hill Road insiders, though the scale of democratization here is narrower. The strongest bear case I need to address: this could be an AI wrapper on top of existing railroad systems that a well-funded horizontal AI agent platform (or Wabtec itself, overcoming its principal-agent problem) eventually commoditizes. The defense is domain specificity -- FRA regulatory requirements, interchange rules, railroad-specific terminology, and the relationship capital embedded in CARS and Canadian Railway Club memberships create a customization barrier that generic AI agents would need years to overcome. But "years" in AI time is compressing. Kaaro needs to accumulate enough proprietary operational data and railroad-specific workflow patterns in the next twelve to eighteen months to make the switching costs prohibitive.

The dimension that most determines my verdict is the specific knowledge layering -- Toma-proven AI agent architecture combined with inherited railroad domain exposure. Neither is world-class alone, but the combination creates a founder-market fit that no other team I can identify currently possesses for this problem. The dimension that most tempers my conviction is the depth of the railroad-specific knowledge at their age. If the "second-generation railroader" claim means they grew up watching their parents navigate the exact workflows Kaaro automates -- the phone calls, the billing disputes, the tribal knowledge about which contact to call for a stuck railcar -- then this is a genuine specific-knowledge company. If it means their parents work in an adjacent part of the railroad ecosystem, the knowledge density drops significantly.
