# Oxus -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Oxus isn't the product or the market -- it's that Janet and Melinda Liu are sisters. After HQ Trivia taught me that even perfect timing and product can't survive co-founder dysfunction, I started paying deep attention to how founding teams handle conflict. And I've said publicly that I have a preference for family-run businesses, because families have been stress-testing their conflict resolution for years before they ever write a line of code. These two co-authored children's economics books through Scholastic, co-founded a nonprofit together, went through the same high school and the same university. That's a relationship forged over a lifetime, not a Y Combinator matching event. Christine Watts as the third co-founder adds technical depth from a shared MIT network. If I could evaluate this company purely on co-founder alignment, I'd be interested.

But here's where I get stuck: when I imagine sitting down with this team and asking my first question -- "Tell me your story, not the business" -- I don't see the biographical thread that connects these founders to the specific suffering of SOX compliance. Janet had a summer internship at the Federal Reserve on CBDC research. She worked in investment strategy at D.E. Shaw. Melinda was in finance at JPMorgan. These are impressive credentials for people fresh out of MIT, but none of them involve sitting in a conference room at 2 AM reviewing control documentation, or watching your audit team burn through a million dollars a year on Big Four consultants who produce inconsistent workpapers. The connection to this problem is analytical, not personal. They identified a market opportunity -- a smart one -- but I need to feel that the founder would keep building this company even if nobody funded them, because the problem is *theirs*. I don't feel that here.

And then there's the weirdness problem, which is really an absence-of-weirdness problem. "AI automates enterprise compliance workflows" is possibly the single most consensus category in venture capital right now. Every seed fund in San Francisco would take this meeting. Every AI startup accelerator has three companies doing adjacent things. The approach -- upload documents, run them through an LLM pipeline, generate review-ready outputs -- is the most obvious application of large language models to a known pain point. I'm not saying it won't work. I'm saying this is the opposite of what I look for. The most promising companies are unpopular and difficult to assess. Oxus is popular and easy to assess. There's nothing here that makes other investors uncomfortable, which means the price will be wrong by the time it matters, and the competitive moat will be shallow because every well-funded incumbent and horizontal AI platform is heading in the same direction. AuditBoard was just acquired for $3 billion and is actively integrating AI. Workiva has launched "Workiva AI." The window for a standalone entrant is narrowing, not opening.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I want to be honest about what could make this work. The regulatory complexity of SOX compliance is real -- PCAOB oversight, personal liability under Section 302/906, the professional judgment required to assess control effectiveness. That's the kind of hard space I normally lean into. And there's a structural channel conflict where the Big Four firms that dominate outsourced internal audit have no incentive to build a product that cannibalizes their $200-$800/hour billing rates. If Oxus can navigate the enterprise sales cycle and build deep domain expertise through customer intimacy, they could own a wedge that neither the incumbents (who are workflow tools, not substantive automation) nor the Big Four (who are conflicted) will aggressively pursue. The pricing headroom against $300K-$1M in annual outsourcing spend is enormous. And the sister co-founders have the kind of tested relationship that could survive the brutal enterprise sales grind. If Janet or Melinda had spent five years as an internal auditor and was building this out of fury at the status quo, I'd probably be writing a check.

But they didn't, and I'm not. This is a competent team building an obvious product in a consensus category for customers who are already well-served by well-funded incumbents. It doesn't create economic access for people who lack it -- it saves money for CFOs at public companies. It doesn't make an invisible system visible -- SOX compliance is already highly visible to the people who care about it. The founders are smart and clearly driven, but the biographical signal that tells me they'll persist through the years of enterprise sales rejection and regulatory uncertainty just isn't there yet. I wish them well, but this isn't my kind of bet.
