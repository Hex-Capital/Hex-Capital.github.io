# Korso -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me before anything else: Uptool raised $6 million from Khosla Ventures, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins — my own firm already placed a bet in this exact space. When I see a YC company entering a market where five funded competitors are building functionally identical products using the same underlying LLM technology, that tells me something important. This is not a technology problem waiting for a breakthrough. This is an execution race in a commoditizing space. I don't invest in execution races unless the consequence of winning is enormous. The consequence of winning the AI-quoting-for-manufacturers race is... a profitable vertical SaaS company. That's fine for somebody. It's not fine for me.

Let me apply my standard test: if Korso fully succeeds — captures dominant market share, automates every manufacturer's quote-to-order workflow — does the world visibly change? No. Manufacturers process RFQs faster. Their back offices shrink. Margins improve by some percentage points. This is useful. It is not consequential. Nobody's healthcare becomes free. No basic human need gets radically cheaper. No new market gets created. The end-state is that a $17 billion software market gets partly captured by a new entrant. I have said many times that I see AI copilots for specific enterprise verticals and my response is always the same: a 40% efficiency gain in a single workflow is an incremental improvement. The consequence of success is a moderately profitable company, and I would rather lose my money entirely than tie it up in a moderate outcome.

The technical approach confirms this. RAG-powered document extraction over manufacturing RFQs is standard architecture in 2026. Every competitor in the dossier — Paperless Parts, Uptool, Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI — is applying the same class of LLM capabilities to the same document types. Korso's website claims "frontier AI research" but provides no specifics, no patents, no novel architecture. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown had spent years synthesizing heme protein — a genuine scientific breakthrough that no competitor could replicate by calling an API. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck designed electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — novel engineering that created a step-function cost reduction. Korso is applying generally available models to a domain. That is a wrapper, not a wedge. The technology risk I prefer to take is the risk that a novel approach won't work — not the risk that five other teams with more capital will execute faster on the same approach.

The strongest bull case I can construct: manufacturing is genuinely underdigitized, the "why now" around LLM capability for unstructured document extraction is real, and the multi-channel ingestion angle (email plus WhatsApp) suggests the team understands how manufacturers in emerging markets actually communicate. If Korso could accumulate enough proprietary pricing data across hundreds of manufacturers, the data flywheel could create real switching costs — the AI assistant becomes more valuable over time as it learns each customer's pricing history. Martin Pan's General Motors experience and the Penn Electric Racing background give the team direct exposure to manufacturing workflows. And the end-to-end bundling of RFQ ingestion through PO tracking is a reasonable product strategy against point solutions. If one of these young founders turns out to be exceptional — if Alex Liu's dropout-and-read-papers trajectory reveals genuine intellectual velocity — this could compound into something meaningful. But "could" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. I see no evidence of it yet. No traction, no customers, no iteration, no signal of what they've learned or how they've adapted. At pre-seed I don't penalize absent traction, but I do need to see something in the founders that signals escape velocity, and I don't see it here.

The founding team are current or recent UPenn undergraduates with internship-level experience. I've backed young founders — Mach Industries was started by a 19-year-old dropout building weapons systems, which is exactly the kind of improbable ambition that gets my attention. But that founder was attempting something the defense establishment said was impossible. Korso is attempting something five other companies are already doing. There is no evidence of first-principles thinking that reframes the problem, no evidence of rapid adaptation, no evidence of the irreverence and naivety I celebrate when it's pointed at an impossibly ambitious target. The target here is not impossible. It's a known market with known approaches and known competitors with more capital. This is the kind of bet designed to generate 3-5x returns, which in my framework is not worth the attention.
