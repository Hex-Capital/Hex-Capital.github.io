# AutoSitu -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: this space is crowded. PermitFlow has raised $91 million. CivCheck got acquired. Archistar has $6.7 million in revenue. CodeComply and Blitz AI are both deploying across municipalities. When I see five-plus funded competitors converging on the same category, my instinct is to walk the other way -- because the weirdness has been arbitraged out. The most promising companies are not popular, and AI-powered permit review is very, very popular right now. Every city council member in America is talking about using AI to fix their housing crisis. That's consensus. And consensus-stage investing at pre-seed prices means you're buying into a crowded race where someone with $91 million in the bank is already running hard.

Now, what I can't get from this dossier is what I need most -- the founders' stories. Not their resumes, their stories. Xuanshu Lin went to Michigan and Harvard GSD for urban planning. George Zhai worked at GM and McLaren on autonomous systems, has seven patents. These are impressive credentials, genuinely. But credentials tell me what someone has accomplished inside existing systems. They don't tell me what drove them to leave those systems and bet everything on this particular problem. Did Xuanshu watch a community he cared about get strangled by permitting delays? Did George experience something at GM that made him realize bureaucratic review processes were fundamentally broken? I don't know. And without that biographical thread connecting founder to problem, I'm evaluating a well-credentialed team rather than a mission-driven one. That's a meaningful difference in how I think about who persists through the inevitable crises.

What I do notice -- and what a generic analyst might miss -- is that AutoSitu sits on one of my patterns. This is a company making an invisible system visible. Municipal code review is one of those opaque bureaucratic processes where power operates behind closed doors, where a planner's subjective interpretation can add months and millions to a housing project, and nobody outside the system can see why. That's the same structural dynamic I recognized in Carta making cap tables transparent, or Affirm making credit costs visible. When you embed AI agents inside the city's own review workflow and surface compliance issues in minutes instead of weeks, you're exposing the machinery of government to sunlight. And in the middle of a housing affordability crisis, accelerating the path from plans to permits has real economic impact for the communities that need housing most desperately. That matters to me.

The bull case is genuinely compelling if I set aside my thesis filters. Eleven named cities as a two-person pre-seed team is extraordinary govtech traction -- municipal procurement usually takes 6-18 months, so getting San José, Seattle, Boston, LA, San Francisco, and others engaged at this stage suggests either exceptional hustle or an acute enough pain point that cities are pulling the product toward them. The 1,500+ plans checked demonstrates real usage, not just LOIs. And the jurisdiction-specific data accumulation creates a moat that deepens with every new city -- each municipality's unique code interpretations become proprietary knowledge that competitors would need to rebuild from scratch. If AutoSitu can convert these city engagements into durable contracts before PermitFlow or the incumbents like Accela move upstream into the reviewer workflow, there's a real infrastructure company here. The "why now" is also strong: LLM capabilities, housing crisis pressure, and municipal staffing shortages are all converging simultaneously.

But I keep coming back to the question I ask myself about every deal: is this unpopular, difficult to assess, and does it have real but surmountable technology risk? AutoSitu is popular -- the whole category is flooded with capital. It's not especially difficult to assess -- the value proposition is straightforward and every competitor is telling the same story. And the technology risk feels more like execution risk than genuine technical frontier -- LLM-based document analysis against regulatory text is reproducible, as the dossier itself notes. The differentiation is operational depth, not technological breakthrough. That's a fine business, but it's not the kind of bet that gives me that jolt of intuitive energy I felt with Niantic or Crusoe. This feels like a competent team executing well in a space that's already been discovered by the market.

The co-founder pairing makes structural sense -- planning domain expertise plus production AI engineering is exactly what you'd draw up on a whiteboard. Their shared UIUC connection suggests prior familiarity, and complementary skills reduce the risk of territorial overlap. But I have no signal on how they handle conflict, how they've been tested together, or what happens when a major city contract falls through and they're burning runway with no revenue. At two people, the team risk is concentrated but at least the dynamics are simpler than a 50/50 split between three co-founders.

I respect what AutoSitu is building, and I think AI-powered municipal review is going to be a real category. But this isn't my kind of bet. I look for the misfits solving problems nobody else sees, in spaces nobody else wants to enter. AutoSitu is a well-credentialed team solving a problem everyone sees, in a space everyone wants to enter. I wish them well, and I'd love to hear their personal stories someday -- there might be something there that changes my mind. But based on what I see today, this is a pass for me.
