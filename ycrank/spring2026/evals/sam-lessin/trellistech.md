# Trellis -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Trellis claims they reached "feature parity with competitors in two weeks" and that "software is being commoditized." Stop right there. That's Trellis making my argument for me. If an AI-native startup can replicate Guesty's features in fourteen days, what happens when Guesty — $409M raised, $900M valuation, thousands of existing customers — points its engineering team at building an AI orchestration layer? Hostaway, sitting at $543M raised and unicorn status, has the same playbook available. Trellis is telling you that software moats are dead while simultaneously asking you to invest in a software company. The internal contradiction is the whole story.

Lodovico Benvenuti built AI agents at Conduit, a YC W24 company doing AI for STR operations. Jan Sahagun sold software at Boom, another AI-first STR competitor, and at Besty AI before that. Both left direct competitors to build a version of what their former employers are building. I don't see the sane-person-insane-idea ratio here — I see two credible industry people with a credible industry idea. "Use AI to automate short-term rental operations" is what literally every company in this space is already doing. Conduit has 77,000+ properties on its AI messaging platform. Boom launched "BAM," their agentic AI manager, across 20 countries. Trellis is running one property management company's operations. The idea isn't crazy enough, and the founders' provenance from Conduit and Boom raises the question of whether Trellis is differentiated or derivative.

The consensus mispricing test is where Trellis falls apart for me. AI agents for vertical SaaS operations is the single most consensus-hot category in venture right now. Every YC batch has a dozen of these. "We built AI agents that orchestrate [industry X] workflows across fragmented tools" — I hear this pitch weekly. The STR market specifically has Guesty, Hostaway, Boom, and Conduit all investing heavily in AI capabilities. There is no mispricing here. The market has priced AI-for-STR-operations as valuable, and capital has flooded in. When I backed Solana at four cents, the entire smart money consensus said Ethereum had won — that's mispricing. When I looked at Eaze, every mainstream VC was running from cannabis. Trellis is swimming in the same pool as every other VC's portfolio company. The upside is already in the price.

The primitive test kills it too. Trellis is not creating a new behavior — property managers already manage properties using software. Trellis is offering a better version of existing PMS tools with AI bolted in. Nobody is going to say "Trellis me" the way they say "Venmo me." When Venmo launched, peer-to-peer mobile payments didn't exist as a behavior. When Patreon launched, direct creator monetization wasn't a category. Trellis's "AI that builds your software" framing is a technical architecture choice, not a new social or financial primitive. The cross-operational orchestration across 200+ integrations sounds impressive until you realize integrations are commodity infrastructure — any well-funded competitor can build the same connectors.

Let me steelman Trellis, because I owe every company the strongest version of their own argument. Lodovico scaled a real property management company to ~$2M revenue and 150+ listings — he's not a pure software person theorizing about operators, he lived the pain. Jan's STR Tech Report is a genuine distribution moat into the exact buyer persona. The innovator's dilemma argument has teeth: Guesty and Hostaway built per-listing subscription models and cannibalizing that revenue to go AI-native is structurally hard. And the cross-operational data network effect — every SOP, every resolution pattern feeding back across customers — could compound into something defensible if Trellis gets enough customers on the platform. The physical-world angle is real too: STR operations involve cleaning crews, maintenance, smart locks, physical inspections. Could Trellis become more like Metropolis — where AI amplifies physical operations that can't be commoditized? The problem is Trellis doesn't own or operate the physical infrastructure. Metropolis manages actual parking garages. Trellis is the software layer on top of operators who manage actual properties. That's the crucial distinction. They're the app, not the asset. And in my framework, the app layer is exactly what gets commoditized. The condition that would flip me: if Trellis showed me they were becoming the operational backbone that actually dispatches and manages physical workforce (cleaners, maintenance crews) with real-world lock-in — not just software orchestration but operational infrastructure — that changes the durability calculus entirely. But that's not what the dossier shows.

Trellis scores low on the two dimensions that matter most to me: consensus mispricing and primitive creation. The AI-for-STR-operations category is peak consensus with $1B+ of competing capital already deployed across Guesty, Hostaway, Boom, and Conduit. Trellis is not creating a new behavior but optimizing an existing one. The structural durability question — Trellis's own argument that software commoditizes in weeks — is the company's most damning self-own. The founders are domain-credible but the idea is domain-obvious. Pass.
