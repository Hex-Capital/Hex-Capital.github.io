# Interfaze -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

"Deep Learning, Developer Tools, Generative AI" — those are the three tags on Interfaze's YC page, and they're also the three most crowded investment categories in Silicon Valley right now. When I look at a company, I need to feel something that makes the room uncomfortable. I need the idea to sound a little crazy at first. Interfaze bundles OCR, web scraping, speech-to-text, and document classification behind a single API at $1.50 per million input tokens. That's a solid engineering product. It's not a bet that makes anyone shift in their seat. Firecrawl just raised $16.2M for scraping alone. Google Document AI, Amazon Textract, Azure Document Intelligence — the biggest companies on earth already sell these exact capabilities. Every VC fund in San Francisco has a developer tools thesis right now, and Interfaze sits squarely in the middle of that consensus. My biggest wins have come from spaces where other investors wouldn't go — SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive, Crusoe when people thought Bitcoin mining with flare gas was either a scam or a joke. Interfaze doesn't have that quality. Not even close.

Yoeven D Khemlani graduated from Imperial College London, worked as a Senior Technical Lead at UOB, co-founded a prop-tech startup called Stayr that claims $1M ARR in year one and a possible acquisition, then moved to the US in 2025 to build JigsawStack, which became Interfaze. Harsha Vardhan Khurdula has an M.Sc. from Purdue with CV and NLP research credentials. When I sit down with founders, the first thing I ask is: tell me your story, not the business. What I'm listening for is whether the problem found the person — whether their life left them unable to ignore this specific thing. Yoeven's path reads like a capable engineer who identified a market gap in AI APIs after working in banking and prop-tech. Harsha's reads like a talented ML researcher looking for applied problems. Those are fine backgrounds. But I don't hear the forge. I don't hear someone who would be working on deterministic AI perception tasks even without funding, someone whose biography made this problem inevitable rather than opportunistic. The absence of that story isn't disqualifying at pre-seed — but it's the single most important data point I evaluate, and Interfaze doesn't have it.

Interfaze's architecture paper — accepted at IEEE CAI 2026, documented on arXiv — describes a hybrid DNN/CNN routing system that shifts computation from large transformer models to smaller specialized components. That's genuinely non-trivial engineering, and I respect the intellectual rigor of putting it through peer review. But here's the problem: it's public. The architecture is on arXiv for anyone to read and replicate. When I backed Crusoe Energy, their moat wasn't a paper — it was physical infrastructure sitting in North Dakota oil fields, portable data centers that required operational knowledge in harsh conditions no competitor wanted to acquire. Interfaze's custom web engine for bot-protected sites is a real moat signal, but it's a continuously evolving arms race against anti-bot systems, not a compounding infrastructure advantage. The OpenAI-compatible API makes Interfaze easy to adopt — and equally easy to abandon when a competitor matches the routing approach. I don't see the switching costs or data network effects that make Carta or Flexport harder to displace with every new customer.

The bull case someone would make to me: Interfaze has done more than most pre-seed companies. They've raised $1.5M from Antler and Ada Ventures, secured a Groq partnership, participated in the Vercel AI Accelerator, and produced peer-reviewed research. Yoeven has a prior startup with claimed traction and an exit. The Document AI market is $14.66B growing to $27.62B by 2030, and the web scraping market is $10.2B at 23.5% CAGR. Bundling multiple specialized capabilities behind one unified model with 98-99% structured output accuracy could become the default developer platform if they execute before incumbents notice. And maybe the "ten-year question" leads somewhere — if small specialized models keep improving, the routing architecture could become foundational infrastructure for how AI systems compose multiple perception tasks. That's a real argument. But I keep coming back to what Lee Jacobs said: "Not some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper." Interfaze's architecture is more sophisticated than a wrapper — routing through specialized perception modules before forwarding distilled context to LLMs is a real technical contribution. But the business model, the pricing, the competitive positioning? That's a commodity API play in a market where Google and Amazon already operate, and where Firecrawl has $16.2M to attack just the scraping piece alone.

Interfaze doesn't attack an opaque, extractive structure. Developers choosing between multiple AI APIs for OCR and scraping aren't being exploited by information asymmetry — they're experiencing fragmentation in a well-functioning market. When I backed Affirm, it was because credit card companies were hiding the true cost of lending from consumers who couldn't calculate compound interest. When I backed Ownwell, it was because property tax systems were opaque bureaucracies that extracted value from homeowners who didn't know they could appeal. Interfaze makes developer workflows more efficient. That's valuable. But it doesn't make a hidden system visible, and it doesn't shift power from gatekeepers to the people they exploit. The company's impact is narrowly contained to making existing developers more productive — it doesn't create new economic participants or expand access to people previously shut out.

What would flip me? If Yoeven told me a story about being trapped inside a system that Interfaze specifically exists to dismantle — if the founding wasn't about market opportunity but about personal necessity. Or if Interfaze demonstrated a proprietary data flywheel that compounds with usage in a way competitors structurally cannot replicate, something beyond the published architecture. A signed enterprise contract with a Fortune 500 company that locks in switching costs would change the defensibility calculus entirely. But based on what's in front of me — a capable team, a real product, a public architecture, a consensus market, no biographical inevitability, no invisible system, no contrarian edge — Interfaze is a pass. The two dimensions that lock this: Contrarian Conviction scores near the bottom because developer AI tools are the single most consensus investment category in venture today, and Founder's Biographical Forge sits at default because neither Yoeven nor Harsha demonstrate the kind of lived-experience connection to this problem that separates mission-driven founders from market-opportunity founders in my framework.
