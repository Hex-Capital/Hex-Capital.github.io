# Bidflow -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here are two exceptionally talented young engineers -- USACO Platinum, Jane Street at eighteen, ICML preprints before they could legally drink -- and they've aimed all that firepower at making electrical contractors fill out bid spreadsheets 5x faster. Let me be direct: if Bidflow succeeds beyond anyone's wildest expectations, the outcome is a dominant position in a $200-500 million electrical estimating software sub-segment. That's a good business. It's not a transformed industry. Nobody wakes up in a different world because electrical takeoffs got automated. The construction estimating software TAM is $1.5-2.7 billion, and electrical is maybe 15-20% of that. When I look at this, I see exactly the pattern I've warned hundreds of founders about: they've found a niche small enough to be defensible and scoped their ambition to fit it. The probability of building a viable company went up. The consequence of success became irrelevant.

The outsider dynamic here is instructive but cuts against the founders in a way that matters. When I backed Pat Brown at Impossible Foods, the outsider advantage was specific -- his molecular biology expertise was the paradigm shift the food industry couldn't generate internally. When I backed Pradeep Sindhu at Juniper, his packet-switching architecture from Xerox PARC was the disruption Cisco's telecom mindset couldn't conceive. Bidflow's founders bring generic AI/ML capability to a vertical. That's not an outsider paradigm shift; it's a horizontal skill applied to a vertical problem. And their direct competitor, Drawer AI, has $5 million, a 25-year construction veteran founder, and a multi-year head start in the identical niche. In construction, where relationships and domain trust determine purchasing decisions, the insider with AI capability beats the AI expert without domain credibility. The 83.5% accuracy number on their website -- compared to Togal.AI's 97% claim on broader construction takeoff -- tells you where the domain data gap manifests. Getting from 83.5% to production-grade accuracy in an industry where a counting error directly costs money on a bid requires exactly the kind of proprietary training data that comes from decades of estimating relationships, not from competitive programming prowess.

The bull case deserves honest examination. These founders have demonstrated genuine velocity -- pivoting from Ghostship to Bidflow and landing three named customers (Bass Electric, Infinity Electric, Checkmate Electric) with a testimonial citing 5x time reduction, all within roughly six months. That's real learning rate. The data center construction boom is creating unprecedented demand for electrical infrastructure bids, and the skilled estimator shortage means contractors are desperate for productivity tools. If Bidflow's model accuracy improves rapidly through customer data, and if the data center wave creates a wedge into a broader electrical estimating market, and if the founders' technical iteration speed outpaces Drawer AI's domain advantage -- then you could see a path to a $500M+ outcome. The founders are clearly exceptional athletes in the technical dimension. But every conditional in that sentence is doing heavy lifting, and even the upside scenario describes a successful vertical SaaS company, not an industry transformation.

What I can't get past: "an assistant not a replacement" is their own positioning language. They've pre-committed to incrementalism. They're making the existing estimating workflow 5x faster rather than reimagining how electrical infrastructure gets designed, bid, and built. Compare that to what these two could be attempting with their skills -- something in AI that changes how physical infrastructure gets planned at a systems level, or something in construction that makes the entire bid-estimate-build cycle obsolete. Instead, they've found a safe harbor. The pivot from Ghostship (a crowded, ambitious space) to electrical estimating (a small, specific niche) reads less like contrarian courage and more like retreat to safety. I've seen this in young founders before -- the instinct to find the most winnable market rather than the most consequential one. These two are too technically gifted to spend their careers on a problem this contained. My advice would be: find a problem where the consequence of success terrifies the incumbents and thrills you. This isn't it.
