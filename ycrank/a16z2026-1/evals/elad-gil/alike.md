# Alike -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking feature of Alike is that its CTO, Max Van Kleek, holds a full-time Associate Professor position at Oxford while serving as the company's technical leader. I've backed academic founders before -- my biology PhD means I understand the pull of research -- but speed is the only advantage a startup has against an incumbent, and Alike is racing against Slack (Salesforce), Google's A2A protocol with 50+ enterprise partners, Dust ($21.5M raised, already at $7.3M ARR), and CrewAI ($18M from Insight Partners). A part-time CTO in a four-person company facing that competitive set is a structural handicap, not a scheduling inconvenience. Van Kleek's 9,012 Google Scholar citations in HCI and privacy-preserving systems represent genuine domain depth -- that's the kind of credential that, in a different configuration, would make me lean forward. But research credibility and shipping velocity are different currencies, and Alike needs the latter right now.

The market structure question: is there an exogenous shift making agent-to-agent coordination feasible NOW that wasn't feasible two years ago? Yes -- Gartner's 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025 confirms that enterprise AI agent proliferation is creating a real coordination problem. But here's where my framework diverges from a generic "big market" analysis: this shift is entirely consensus. Google launched A2A under the Linux Foundation with Salesforce, SAP, Atlassian, and Workday as partners. Slack announced 30 new AI agent features and auto-provisioning for all new Salesforce customers. When the incumbents are already building the thing you want to build, the market timing isn't nonobvious -- it's the most obvious play in enterprise AI right now. Compare this to when I backed Coinbase in 2012: crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors, and that dismissal was the alpha. Nobody is dismissing agent coordination in 2026.

Alike's infrastructure-layer positioning triggers one of my explicit red flags: building an application on a contested infrastructure layer. Google's A2A protocol is providing the interoperability standard for free, which means Alike's value proposition has to live entirely in the coordination intelligence layer above the protocol -- the ability to learn team patterns, automate check-ins, and manage cross-functional alignment. That layer has real potential switching costs (accumulated organizational knowledge compounds over time, per alike.work's own positioning), but it's also precisely what Slack is incentivized to build natively into its platform. Alike is caught between commoditization from below (open protocol) and feature absorption from above (Slack's installed base). The structural position reminds me less of Stripe -- which defined the API standard -- and more of application-layer plays that got squeezed when the platform owner decided to compete.

The single-miracle test yields an unfavorable result. Alike needs enterprise buyers to adopt agent-to-agent coordination as a standalone category (not just a feature of existing tools), AND it needs to build coordination intelligence demonstrably superior to what Slack and Google will ship with their existing distribution. That's two miracles with compounding odds. The design partner traction -- 10 live deployments, 20 signed partners including named companies like Exus Renewables ($1B backing), K-ID ($51M from Lightspeed and a16z), and NewtonX ($47M raised) -- is a genuine signal at pre-seed, but design partnerships in a hot category often reflect curiosity rather than commitment, especially when no pricing or revenue has been disclosed.

The strongest bull case for Alike: the privacy architecture (per-person, per-channel granular controls with full traceability) could be a real differentiator in regulated enterprises where Slack's approach to AI data handling creates compliance friction. If European regulatory frameworks around AI workplace monitoring tighten -- which is plausible given EU AI Act trajectory -- Alike's Oxford-rooted privacy-by-design approach and Van Kleek's decades of privacy research could become a genuine moat. The CEO Addi Haran Diman's PhD in social data science, studying group behavior and social networks quantitatively, is directly relevant to building coordination automation. For this bull case to work, Alike needs the regulated enterprise segment to value privacy-preserving coordination enough to adopt a standalone tool over Slack's native features -- a plausible but narrow path that still requires winning against better-funded competitors in a consensus market.

The founder profile gives me the most pause beyond the market timing. None of the three founders have operated at a company during a scaling phase. Addi finished a PhD at 21, which signals raw intellectual horsepower, but Oxford Students' Union presidency -- even managing a 30,000-member constituency -- is a fundamentally different operational challenge than scaling a B2B enterprise product from 10 to 1,000 customers. Danial's growth experience at an unnamed Sequoia-backed startup is the closest thing to operator DNA on the team, but without knowing the company, the role, or the outcomes, I can't credit it heavily. Three co-founders connected as "best friends" through Oxford, with consecutive SU presidencies, raises the question of clear decision-making hierarchy that I've seen trip up otherwise talented teams. I've seen mediocre operators do incredibly well in strong markets with strong product-market fit, but Alike doesn't yet have evidence of either.

The decisive dimensions here are Nonobvious Market Timing and Single-Miracle Viability. The structural shift in agent coordination is real but fully consensus -- Salesforce, Google, and multiple well-funded startups are all attacking it with vastly more resources. Alike needs multiple independent breakthroughs to win from this position, with an academic team that hasn't demonstrated the execution speed required to outrun incumbents. The privacy angle and Van Kleek's research depth are the most interesting signals, but they aren't sufficient to override the structural positioning problems.
