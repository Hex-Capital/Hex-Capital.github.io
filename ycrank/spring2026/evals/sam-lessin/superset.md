# Superset -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with Superset in one sentence: the platforms whose agents this company orchestrates are already building the orchestration themselves. Anthropic has shipped native multi-agent orchestration for Claude Code. That's not a hypothetical competitive threat I'm speculating about -- it's in the docs right now. When your value proposition depends entirely on a platform remaining incomplete, and the platform is actively completing itself, you're building a feature with a countdown timer. I've watched this movie before. The Birchbox lesson taught me that narrative without structural moat produces the worst venture outcomes. Superset is worse than Birchbox on this dimension because at least Birchbox's platform partners (beauty brands) had no incentive to replicate the box model. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google have every incentive to make their agents work better together natively.

Let me apply my sane-person-insane-idea filter. The founders are genuinely credible on the "sane" axis -- three ex-YC CTOs, and Kiet Ho shipping Onlook to 25,000 GitHub stars demonstrates real ability to build developer tools that people actually use. That's operator credibility, not just credential signaling. But the idea isn't insane. It's the opposite of insane. Every developer running Claude Code in a terminal has felt this exact pain. When I describe Superset to anyone in the industry, nobody's confused or skeptical -- they nod immediately and say "yeah, obviously someone should build that." That universal head-nod is exactly the signal I run from. If everyone agrees the idea makes sense, the returns are already priced in, and you're buying into a commodity race where execution speed is the only differentiator. The "insane" ideas that produce venture returns -- Coinbase, Helium, Teamshares -- generate genuine confusion or dismissal, not immediate recognition. There's also the co-founder formation pattern: three CTOs who met through the YC network, not through years of working together. My strongest investments come from founding teams with deep pre-existing relational capital. YC-batch-formed teams haven't been stress-tested through genuine adversity together.

The AI kill test here doesn't just reveal weakness -- it reveals an existential dependency. My framework asks "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" If AI didn't exist, Superset literally does not exist. There is no business, no product, no customer, no use case. That's the most extreme possible failure of the cherry-on-top test. The company IS the AI layer, with nothing underneath. Compare this to Craftwork, where the painting crews and customer relationships exist independent of any technology -- AI just makes dispatch and scheduling more efficient. Superset has zero gravity without AI agents, and the agents it depends on are controlled by companies with orders of magnitude more resources and every strategic incentive to subsume this functionality. Cursor at $29.3 billion and $1.2 billion ARR can add multi-agent orchestration as a Tuesday afternoon feature release. The dossier notes that the "barrier is UX polish and ecosystem integration, not deep technical complexity." That's the company's own positioning telling me there's no moat.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously: Superset could become the neutral orchestration layer precisely because incumbents won't build it. Cursor won't build agent-agnostic orchestration because it commoditizes their proprietary agent. OpenAI won't optimize for Claude Code workflows. There's a structural gap where a Switzerland-positioned player could thrive as the number of competing AI agents proliferates. If the AI agent landscape fragments rather than consolidates -- ten viable CLI agents from ten providers -- the orchestration layer becomes genuinely valuable infrastructure, like Docker became for containers. The 8,000 GitHub stars in five months is legitimate developer pull, not manufactured hype. And three technical co-founders who each built and shipped real products is a team that can iterate fast. For this to work, though, you need to believe that CLI-based agents remain the dominant interaction pattern (rather than IDE-integrated agents winning), that no single agent platform achieves sufficient dominance to dictate the workflow, and that orchestration grows complex enough to warrant a standalone product rather than remaining a feature. I'd put each of those at well below 50% probability, and you need all three simultaneously.

Superset occupies no protocol position whatsoever. It's an Electron application wrapping Git worktrees and terminal sessions. Git worktrees are the primitive -- and they've existed for years. The CLI agents are the primitive -- built by Anthropic, OpenAI, Google. Superset is an application sitting on top of other people's applications sitting on top of other people's protocols. That's three layers removed from anything that compounds. My biggest returns -- Venmo, Solana, Aleo, Helium -- all came from investing in new primitives that became foundational layers for ecosystems. Superset creates no new primitive. It doesn't establish a standard others build on. It doesn't generate network effects where each new user makes the product more valuable for existing users. The narrative ceiling is visible from here: "tool that helps developers manage multiple AI agents" doesn't expand into an economic category. It contracts as agent platforms absorb the functionality.
