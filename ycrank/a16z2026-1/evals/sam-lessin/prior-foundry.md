# Prior Foundry -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The foundational research behind Prior Foundry -- LLM agents that replicate human survey responses at 85% accuracy -- is sitting on a public GitHub repo right now. Anyone can clone it. The Stanford genagents repository is open-source, the probability surveys (GSS, WVS, ESS) are publicly accessible, and the methodology was published in a paper anyone can read on arXiv. So the first question I ask is: what exactly am I buying here? When the core technology is a published academic paper and a public repo, what's left is execution, relationships, and domain workflow -- and those are exactly the things an incumbent like Palantir, with its existing government contracts and forward-deployed engineering model, can replicate in a quarter. Keshav Sivakumar literally came from Palantir. He knows this. The AI kill test doesn't just apply to Adobe and Salesforce -- it applies to every government-tech incumbent that already has the procurement relationships Prior Foundry is spending years trying to build.

Here's where Prior Foundry gets interesting, though, and why I can't dismiss it entirely: the OECD partnership spanning 38+ member governments is a genuinely unusual distribution asset for a three-person pre-seed company. Shirin Abrishami Kashani's direct OECD experience -- she was a junior analyst in their International Migration Division -- creates the kind of institutional access that cold outreach never produces. $60K ARR within two months and a reported >$1M pipeline through a distributor suggests this isn't vaporware. Real money from real institutions. That's signal. The govtech category also provides mild consensus mispricing -- most VCs hear "government procurement" and run the other direction because the sales cycles are brutal. So there's a small gap between how the market prices this and what the actual distribution leverage might be worth.

But I keep coming back to the primitive test, and Prior Foundry doesn't pass it. Governments have been modeling policy impacts since the dawn of econometrics. AnyLogic is doing $12-23M in revenue selling agent-based simulation software. What Prior Foundry is doing is making existing policy modeling faster and cheaper by swapping LLMs for traditional rule-based agents. That's a technology upgrade, not a new behavior. Nobody will say "Prior Foundry me that policy" the way people say "Venmo me." This isn't creating a verb -- it's automating a consulting engagement. When I ask myself "is this ten times larger than what exists?" the answer requires the government simulation market to explode, and even then Prior Foundry is competing for share of an existing practice, not defining a new one. Consulting firms like McKinsey could build this too, though they face business model cannibalization -- replacing months of billable work with a software tool is suicidal for their revenue model. That cannibalization dynamic is real, but it protects Prior Foundry from incumbents who won't self-disrupt, not from new entrants who will.

The sanity-insanity ratio is miscalibrated in the wrong direction. These are clearly sane, accomplished people -- a Knight-Hennessy Scholar, a Palantir engineer, a researcher whose work is published in The Journal of Politics. But "use AI to help governments make better decisions" is the opposite of an insane idea. It's the kind of thing everyone nods along to. There's nothing here that makes me think "what you're doing is absolutely crazy." And then there's the Jonne Kamphorst situation -- the Chief Scientific Officer, who owns the core research methodology, started as Assistant Professor at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026. His primary appointment is at a university in a different timezone from a San Francisco-based startup. That's not a commitment signal I love. The CSO of the company whose entire value rests on scientific methodology credibility has his name on a faculty page at Sciences Po. At pre-seed, I need founders who are burning the boats, not hedging with tenure-track positions.

The strongest bull case: Prior Foundry is positioned where consulting firms won't self-cannibalize, traditional simulation tools (AnyLogic) lack behavioral science depth, and legislative trackers (FiscalNote, now delisted and effectively dead at $18M market cap) don't simulate at all. If "simulate before you legislate" becomes a regulatory norm across OECD nations, this company is sitting on a distribution channel to 38 governments with zero competition in the specific product category. That's a version of the world where Prior Foundry matters enormously. What would have to be true: the 85% survey-replication accuracy needs to translate to actual policy prediction accuracy (currently unvalidated), government buyers need to trust AI simulation for high-stakes decisions (a cultural shift that takes years), and no well-funded competitor needs to build on the same open-source foundation faster. Those are a lot of "ifs" -- and they're sequential, not parallel.

Prior Foundry's decisive weakness is structural durability. This is a software-only AI application built on published research with no physical moat, no protocol-level lock-in, and no network effect that compounds. The OECD relationship is a sales channel, not a structural moat. Capital discipline is actually the bright spot -- small team, early revenue, institutional buyers who pay real money -- but discipline doesn't overcome the fundamental problem that this is a feature dressed as a company. The category isn't mispriced enough, the primitive isn't new enough, and the idea isn't crazy enough.
