# MouseCat -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about MouseCat is that one of its founders is literally writing the rules of the game — Nicholas Aldridge is a core maintainer of the Model Context Protocol, the emerging standard for how AI agents interact with tools and data sources. That's a genuinely rare credential. When I invested in Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu understood packet-switching architecture at a level that let him see what Cisco's CTO declared impossible. Aldridge has that kind of infrastructure-level vision for AI agents. The question I keep circling back to is: why is someone with that vantage point building a better fraud investigation tool?

Let me be direct about what bothers me. MouseCat's own positioning is that they "augment existing risk team workflows rather than replacing them." That sentence is a disqualifying signal in my framework. When you explicitly choose to augment rather than replace, you've calibrated your ambition below the problem's potential. If AI agents can truly automate the full fraud investigation lifecycle — from case research through production rule generation — then the consequence of success isn't "fraud analysts work faster." It's that the current model of staffing 50-person investigation teams becomes as obsolete as manual telephone switching. But MouseCat isn't saying that. They're positioning as a toolkit. That's the safe bet, and safe bets produce inconsequential outcomes. The fraud detection market is $32 billion — real money, but this company isn't proposing to restructure it. They're proposing to sell picks and shovels to the people already working in it.

The bull case is worth engaging with honestly. AI-enabled fraud is accelerating exponentially — 74% of senior fraud leaders cite it as their top threat. Every financial institution is drowning in investigation volume that human analysts can't process fast enough. The timing convergence is genuinely strong: AI agents just crossed the capability threshold for complex multi-step reasoning, agent protocols like MCP are maturing (with Aldridge at the center), and the attack surface is expanding faster than defenses. If I apply my rate-of-change framework, the trajectory here is favorable — this looks like early 2019 AI, where current tools are barely adequate but the slope of improvement is steep. And the team is deliberately assembled: Aldridge brings the agent infrastructure depth from AWS Bedrock, McAllister brings direct fraud ML experience from Coinbase plus a prior exit. That's smart gene pool engineering for hire numbers one and two. Being rated 2nd at YC W26's product showcase suggests the product already works in a way that impresses technically sophisticated peers. If the real vision is "we start with fraud investigation but become the platform for automating expert judgment across all regulated industries" — legal, healthcare compliance, financial auditing — then the consequence of success gets substantially larger. But that's not what they're articulating, and I've learned to evaluate the ambition founders state, not the ambition I project onto them.

The competitive landscape crystallizes my concern. Sardine has $145M, 130% ARR growth, and is actively building AI automation for the same buyer persona. Feedzai has $347M and processes 70 billion events per year. DataVisor holds patents on unsupervised ML for fraud. These aren't sleeping incumbents — they're well-funded, domain-expert competitors who are already shipping AI investigation features. MouseCat's differentiation — closing the loop from investigation to production rules — is a feature, not a structural moat. The dossier itself notes that "open-source LLM agent frameworks lower the barrier to building investigation agents." When your core capability can be replicated by incumbents who already have the customer relationships, the data, and the sales teams, you need either a 10x technology advantage or a fundamentally different business model. I don't see either here.

What I see is two capable engineers who've reduced their probability of failure by picking a well-understood market with clear buyers and obvious pain points — and in doing so, they've reduced their consequence of success to building what could be a solid $500M company. That's fine. Lots of people will fund that. But I invest my own money in bets where, if the founders are right, the world works differently afterward. If MouseCat wins, fraud analysts investigate cases 5x faster. If they lose, nothing changes. Either way, the financial system operates exactly the same. Some problems are too important not to work on, and some solutions are too incremental to justify the founders' talent.
