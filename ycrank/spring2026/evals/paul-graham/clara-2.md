# Clara -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Clara is also its most dangerous feature: George Favvas already built this company once. Circle Medical, YC S15, grew to $100M revenue run rate with profitability. He ran a telehealth primary care operation. He hired the clinicians, navigated the state licensing, figured out insurance billing, and built the clinical workflows. Now he's back, with two of the same people, to do it again — except this time with AI handling the clinical reasoning and humans serving as reviewers rather than primary actors. This is the pattern I find most compelling in my own portfolio. I backed Patrick Collison at Auctomatic when he was a teenager, then backed him again at Stripe. The second time around, the founder knows where the bodies are buried. They've already made the expensive mistakes.

And Favvas's team composition is exactly right for this problem. Caitlin Swift is a nurse practitioner who did primary care at Circle Medical — she's not advising on healthcare, she practiced it. She knows what a 15-minute visit feels like from the clinician's side, which interactions are rote enough for AI to handle, and where human judgment remains irreplaceable. Zeeshan Ahmed designed consumer products at Careem (40M users) and Instagram before doing EHR interfaces at Circle Medical. The three of them have worked together. They chose each other with full information. That's the kind of co-founder relationship where the hard conversations about equity and roles have probably already happened — they've seen each other under pressure. This is a genuine "second company together" team, and that matters enormously. The organic problem discovery here is as strong as I've seen: they didn't read a McKinsey report about physician shortages. They operated a telehealth company for years and saw, from the inside, that the human clinician bottleneck was the binding constraint. They lived in the present, understood its limitations, and are building what the future requires.

But here's where I get uncomfortable: I cannot figure out who thinks this is a bad idea. "AI doctor" is one of the hottest categories in technology right now. K Health has raised $441M. Hippocratic AI is valued at $3.5B. Forward Health burned through $546M. When I look at the competitive landscape, I see massive capital already deployed by people who believe exactly what Favvas believes. My framework says the best startup ideas sit at the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." An idea where everyone immediately nods and says "yes, AI will transform healthcare" has already had its return premium competed away. The specific inversion — AI-first with human review, rather than human-first with AI assist — has a contrarian edge, but it's a tactical distinction within a consensus category, not a genuinely repulsive idea. Nobody recoils from this pitch.

There is a bull case I take seriously, though. The competitors have raised enormous sums precisely because they're attacking different wedges. K Health does AI triage paired with human clinicians — still human-first. Hippocratic AI sells B2B to health systems — not consumer. Forward Health built expensive hardware pods. None of them have Favvas's specific playbook: DTC telehealth with insurance billing, which is exactly what Circle Medical does. Clara's real moat isn't the AI — it's the clinical operations wrapper that Favvas has already built once. State-by-state licensing, insurance credentialing, prescription workflows, HIPAA compliance — this is the deepest healthcare schlep, and most AI companies try to avoid it by going B2B or staying in the "wellness" lane. If Clara can combine Circle Medical's operational infrastructure knowledge with genuinely good AI clinical reasoning, the unit economics could be transformative: same quality of care, fraction of the clinician time per patient. The $100M Circle Medical playbook, re-run with AI leverage, could produce something that scales in a way the original never could. Carbon Health's Chapter 11 filing in February 2026 validates that the old capital-intensive model is breaking — which is exactly the opening an asset-light AI-first approach needs.

What keeps this from being an obvious yes for me is threefold. First, the dual CEO question: Favvas is listed as CEO of both Circle Medical and Clara. Circle Medical is a WELL Health subsidiary doing $100M+ in revenue. I've seen founders try to run two companies, and it rarely works during the critical early stage when you need total commitment. Second, there's zero traction evidence — no users, no revenue, no launched product. For a pre-seed YC company that's not disqualifying, but paired with a nine-person team and experienced founders, I'd expect to see something shipped by now. Third, and most fundamentally, this team is experienced operators executing a well-understood playbook in a hot category. That's a fine venture bet but it's not the kind of bet I'm typically best at. My portfolio skews toward younger founders with raw technical brilliance attacking problems nobody else sees. This feels more like a Series A investment by a healthcare-focused fund than an angel bet by me.

I'd write a check here primarily on the strength of the founder-market fit and the organic problem discovery. Favvas has earned the right to bet on — he's done the schlep, he's shipped the product, he's scaled the revenue. The team knows each other and complements each other well. But the consensus enthusiasm around AI healthcare, the capital asymmetry with competitors, and the ambiguity around Favvas's Circle Medical commitment temper my conviction. This is a bet on execution superiority in a crowded field, not a bet on insight that others are missing.
