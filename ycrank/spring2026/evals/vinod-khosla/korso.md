# Korso -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Korso's own dossier states it plainly: "The core capability — LLM-based document parsing, email triage, and ERP API integration — uses generally available AI infrastructure." That sentence is the entire evaluation. I don't need to read further to know where this lands in my framework, but I will, because I want to be fair to the team. The manufacturing operations space is a $34 billion market growing to $155 billion by 2030 — real money, real problem. But Korso is building an AI agent layer over existing ERP systems to automate RFQ processing, quote generation, and purchase order tracking. This is the definition of making an existing job 40% easier rather than making the job disappear. I see a dozen YC companies every batch with this exact architecture: take a vertical, wrap an LLM around the workflow, integrate with the incumbent software stack, sell efficiency. The consequence of Korso's full success is that mid-market manufacturers spend less time on email and spreadsheets. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The world doesn't look different.

Paperless Parts has raised $45-51 million doing AI-supported quoting for job shops. Leverage AI has $14.9 million for PO automation. Turian in Berlin is building agentic AI for procurement across industries with 17 employees. Epicor has already launched Prism — an AI agent specifically for RFQ workflows baked into their ERP. Korso is entering a market where funded competitors with head starts are converging, and where incumbents with existing distribution can bundle AI features at marginal cost. The "ERP-agnostic layer" positioning is Korso's best strategic argument — manufacturers running legacy or multi-ERP environments can't use Epicor Prism — but that's a niche within a niche, not a category-creating insight. When I backed Juniper Networks, the technical architecture of the M40 router was a genuine discontinuity — purpose-built silicon for packet forwarding that made Cisco's general-purpose approach obsolete overnight. Korso's long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing is solid engineering, but it's not a step-function breakthrough. It's the kind of integration work that any well-funded team can replicate within 18 months.

Martin Pan's General Motors experience gives Korso genuine domain exposure — he's seen manufacturing operations from inside an OEM, which matters for credibility in founder-led sales. Daichi Hiraoka built an ML model that reduced radiotherapy dose prediction discrepancies by 97%, which demonstrates real engineering capability. Alex Liu brings research depth from Notre Dame. The team traveled across Asia talking directly to clients after YC acceptance, which I respect — that's the kind of ground-level learning orientation that tells me the founders are not sitting in a San Francisco apartment hallucinating about their market. But I have no evidence of pivots, no evidence of what surprised them, no evidence of rapid adaptation during the batch. No public GitHub repos, no product demos, no customer testimonials, no revenue signals. At pre-seed, I don't penalize the absence of traction — but I need to see something that tells me these founders reason from first principles rather than following the "AI agent for X" playbook. The dossier gives me nothing to evaluate their learning rate, which means I default to moderate benefit of doubt but not conviction.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: manufacturing is a $13 trillion global industry where office inefficiency is the last major untouched cost center, the ERP integration moat is real because each deployment requires deep customer-specific configuration that creates lock-in, Korso's "tribal knowledge into data" thesis means accumulated context compounds per account creating a data advantage, and the team has the domain credibility (Pan at GM) combined with technical execution (Hiraoka's ML work) to out-integrate pure-play AI startups. The DoorDash exception might apply — Tony Xu and team had no technical discontinuity either, but the consequence magnitude of transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people overrode the technical novelty requirement. Does Korso have that level of consequence magnitude? It does not. DoorDash created new demand — people who never ordered delivery suddenly could. Korso redistributes existing spend — manufacturers who already process RFQs do it faster. The exception doesn't hold because the prerequisite condition — planetary-scale human impact — is absent.

Korso's affordability thesis is weak by my standards. If Korso scales, manufacturers save on operations headcount and cycle time. Those savings accrue to corporate margins, not to making a basic human need 10x cheaper. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success means protein cheaper than animal agriculture — feeding billions sustainably at lower cost. Or Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means energy too cheap to meter. Korso's success means mid-market manufacturers have slightly better EBITDA. I don't invest my own money for that outcome. What would flip me to Invest: if Korso demonstrated that its autonomous agent layer could reduce manufactured goods costs by 10x — not 10x the operations efficiency, but 10x the end-product cost to the consumer — by collapsing the entire procurement-to-fulfillment cycle in a way that eliminates entire categories of intermediaries. That would be consequence magnitude worth betting on.

Korso is a competent team pursuing a real problem with generally available technology in a crowding market. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are consequence magnitude — manufacturing office automation does not change how millions of people live — and non-linear technical approach — LLM wrappers over ERP APIs are not a technology discontinuity. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Korso is designed to generate 3-5x returns in a competitive SaaS market, which is a fine business for someone else's portfolio. Pass.
