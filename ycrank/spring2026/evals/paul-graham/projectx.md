# ProjectX -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about ProjectX is that three of the four original co-founders are gone. The July 2024 press coverage names Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham. The YC application two years later lists Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder — only Rounak survived. I've written about co-founder conflict as one of the top startup killers, and losing three out of four co-founders before you've raised a real round is the kind of thing that either destroys a company or reveals something about the remaining founder. The question with Rounak Adhikary is which one it is.

There's a version of this story where losing three co-founders is the signal, not the noise. Rounak was 19 when he started this. He founded a consulting company (Woldix) to bankroll the early development — the kind of bootstrap-from-nothing move I find encouraging. He then secured grants from Microsoft, AWS, and two Indian government ministries, scraped together $204K, got a pilot running at IIT Bombay, and reportedly accumulated Rs 20 Cr+ (~$2.4M) in signed LOIs. All of this while rebuilding his founding team entirely. He replaced the original co-founders with a systems engineer who actually has the right toolchain for this problem (Rust, Go, C++ — exactly what you need for GPU virtualization and low-latency streaming) and an operator who'd scaled a consulting team to $120K MRR. That's the running back quality in action — he didn't just plow ahead with a broken team, he found new openings. But I'm reconstructing this narrative from fragments. I don't have Rounak in front of me telling me the stories, and the stories are what I use to X-ray a founder's character. The raw facts are compatible with both resourcefulness and instability.

The schlep here is real and worth noting. Building sub-3-second cold starts for GPU-accelerated applications streamed in browser tabs is genuinely hard systems engineering. It's the kind of unglamorous infrastructure work — GPU memory management, container orchestration, latency optimization across unreliable networks — that makes most founders' eyes glaze over. They'd rather build another AI wrapper. The fact that Shadow PC raised over $100M and still delivers a full persistent VM rather than per-app streaming tells you something about how hard the per-app approach is. If Infinity actually works as described, the technical achievement is substantial. But I have a nagging concern about the capital math. GPU infrastructure is expensive. $204K in funding is rounding error against what it costs to run GPU instances at scale. Shadow and CoreWeave raised hundreds of millions and billions respectively. ProjectX is bringing a slingshot to a tank battle. The LOIs help this story — if $2.4M in committed revenue materializes, it funds real infrastructure. But those LOIs are from July 2024, and there's no public information about whether any converted. Stale LOIs are not revenue. They're polite interest.

What I don't see is the organic discovery story I look for. The problem — students in India lacking access to powerful computing — is real and Rounak grew up in West Bengal, so there's a plausible personal connection. But the pitch is framed in market terms: "$4.31B TAM," "75% of students lack access," "Fortune Business Insights projections." That's the language of someone who identified an opportunity through research, not someone who spent three years frustrated that their laptop couldn't run Blender. The best startup origin stories start with a specific moment of personal frustration. Maybe Rounak has that story — but it's absent from everything I can see, and founders with genuine organic discovery usually can't stop themselves from telling you about it.

The bull case I want to take seriously: a young, determined founder from a non-elite background in India, tackling a genuine schlep that repels well-funded competitors, who has already demonstrated scrappy capital efficiency by stacking grants and LOIs far beyond what his funding would predict — and who got into YC despite obvious red flags. Andrew Miklas presumably saw a demo and met these founders. If the sub-3-second cold start claim is real and not vapor, and if the team restructuring was Rounak upgrading rather than the ship sinking, this could be a Bindwell-shaped bet: young founders, unsexy domain, underestimated by everyone. What would have to be true is that the LOIs convert, the technical claims hold, and the new co-founding team is actually stronger than what it replaced. That's a lot of "ifs," but the shape of the opportunity — making GPU computing accessible through a browser for a massive underserved market — is genuinely compelling if the execution is there.

But my honest assessment is that the execution signals are mixed. The company has been in development since at least late 2023, with a public beta in June 2024, and the community metrics are thin — 116 Discord members after two years doesn't suggest the kind of obsessive early usage that tells me people desperately want this thing. The accelerator-collection pattern (Draper U, IIM Bangalore, Stanford ASES, now YC) could be strategic relationship-building, but it can also indicate a founder who's optimizing for the appearance of progress rather than shipping product. And while "cloud OS" sounds technically contrarian to anyone who remembers Stadia's failure, it's actually quite consensus in the current GPU-infrastructure boom — 95 companies tracked, $20.8B raised collectively. This isn't an idea that makes smart people say "that's crazy." It's an idea that makes smart people say "that's obvious." Obvious-sounding ideas in hot markets are exactly where the return premium has already been competed away.
