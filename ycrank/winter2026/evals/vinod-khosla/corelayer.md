# Corelayer -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: two Goldman Sachs data infrastructure engineers have built a tool that helps on-call engineers at companies like Goldman Sachs debug production data faster. Their ROI calculator frames the value proposition precisely -- 100 engineers at $200K/year, reduce their support time from 20% to 6%, save $2.8M annually. That's an honest and useful product. But when I read that calculator, I see a company that has already told me the ceiling. If Corelayer succeeds completely -- dominates its category, wins every Fortune 100 financial institution -- the consequence is that banks save money on production support. Nobody's world changes. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The existing monitoring stack (Datadog, PagerDuty) still exists. The data pipelines still exist. Engineers still exist. They just get paged less often. I have a visceral reaction to investments where the consequence of total success is an efficiency improvement measured in percentage points. Some problems are too important not to work on -- and some problems, however real, are too operational to justify the opportunity cost of a founder's career.

The competitive landscape reinforces my concern. incident.io raised $62M in April 2025 explicitly to build AI agents that resolve incidents. PagerDuty ($441M ARR) and Datadog ($30B market cap) are both layering AI capabilities onto platforms that already sit inside every target customer. Shoreline.io, which was doing automated incident remediation, was acquired by Nvidia. BigPanda was acquired. FireHydrant was acquired by Freshworks. The space is consolidating rapidly around well-funded players, and the core technology -- applying LLMs to logs, metrics, and code -- is not proprietary. Corelayer's confidential compute and on-prem deployment options are real differentiators for regulated industries, but these are implementation features, not technology moats. Any well-resourced competitor can achieve SOC 2 certification and offer on-prem deployment when the market demands it.

The founders are the opposite of the outsider pattern that has produced my best returns. Mitch and Shipra built data infrastructure together at Goldman Sachs and are now building data infrastructure tools for financial services companies. Their domain experience is precisely the kind that creates what I call "a set of biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing." They know exactly what's possible and what's needed in their domain -- which means they'll execute well on the current problem but are unlikely to reimagine the category entirely. Compare this to Peter Beck building rockets from New Zealand with no aerospace pedigree, or Pat Brown applying biochemistry to the meat industry. Those founders didn't know what the established players considered impossible, which freed them to attempt it. Mitch and Shipra know exactly what Goldman considers impossible -- which is why they've built a compliance-first, integration-heavy tool that fits neatly into existing enterprise procurement workflows. Smart strategy, but it's playing the game rather than changing it.

The strongest bull case deserves genuine consideration. If I project the trajectory forward aggressively: AI agents go from "assisting" on-call engineers to fully replacing first-line production support teams. That $100M+ per year that Fortune 100s spend on production support doesn't shrink by 70% -- it goes to near zero. The entire concept of a human on-call rotation becomes obsolete for most incident categories. In that scenario, you're looking at a genuine transformation of how software engineering organizations operate, and the regulated-industry wedge gives Corelayer a beachhead that less compliance-ready competitors can't easily attack. The Goldman alumni network provides distribution into exactly the right early buyers. And the timing is genuinely favorable -- LLM capabilities crossed the debugging threshold in the last 12-18 months, and the M&A consolidation of older players has created real space. For this bull case to materialize, though, Corelayer needs to outpace incident.io's $62M war chest, Datadog's platform advantage, and every other team building AI debugging agents -- with two people and YC funding. The path from "better on-call tool" to "eliminates the on-call function" requires a leap in ambition that the current positioning doesn't suggest.

The timing is adequate but not exceptional. This isn't skating to where the puck will be -- it's arriving at the rink when everyone already sees the game. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was laughable to most investors. When I backed cleantech in 2004, VCs thought I was insane. Corelayer is entering a category that every major observability and incident management company is already pursuing with AI features. The underlying technology trend (LLMs for code understanding) is real, but it's consensus. Five acquisitions in the space in 2024-2025 tell me the market has already been discovered. I prefer to invest before the market validates the category, not after.

This is a competent team attacking a real pain point with reasonable timing and legitimate domain expertise. It will probably become a real business. But I don't invest in real businesses -- I invest in attempts to change how industries fundamentally operate, where either the money expires or you build something civilization-scale. Corelayer has calibrated its ambition to the size of a department budget, not the size of a systemic transformation.
