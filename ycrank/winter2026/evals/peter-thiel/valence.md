# Valence -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The question I always ask is: what is the secret here? What important truth does Valence see that most intelligent people would actively dispute? The implicit thesis is that prediction markets will fragment across enough venues to make a cross-venue aggregation layer essential infrastructure. But this is not a secret -- it is a pattern so well-established that it has its own Wikipedia page. Smart order routing exists in equities, options, and crypto. 1inch aggregates decentralized exchanges. The entire concept of "aggregate liquidity across fragmented venues" is a known playbook in financial markets, executed by dozens of firms over decades. Nobody disputes that fragmentation creates aggregation opportunities. The contrarian claim would be the opposite -- that prediction markets will *not* fragment, that one venue will monopolize, and that aggregation is therefore unnecessary. Valence is building on consensus financial infrastructure logic applied to a new asset class. That is definitionally "1 to n."

The structural problem runs deeper than the absence of a secret. Aggregation layers in financial markets are historically thin-margin, competitive businesses. They do not create monopolies. There are no network effects -- one trader using Valence does not make the platform more valuable for another trader. There are no meaningful economies of scale beyond basic server infrastructure. And most critically, the platforms Valence depends on are also its most dangerous competitors. Kalshi has raised $1.7 billion and has every incentive to make its own interface the best execution venue. Polymarket has raised $2.3 billion. Robinhood has 24 million brokerage customers and has already traded 12 billion prediction contracts. DraftKings is spending $400 million in 2026 alone on prediction markets. Valence is a three-person startup sitting between massively capitalized exchanges that control the APIs and the liquidity. An exchange can revoke API access with a single configuration change. This is not a position from which monopolies emerge.

There is a deeper irony in the go-to-market. Valence targets professional and systematic traders -- quantitative firms that trade $150 million monthly across dozens of instruments simultaneously. But the firms these founders interned at -- Citadel, DRW, Optiver, Cubist -- do not use third-party execution infrastructure. They build their own. The very sophistication that makes a trader valuable enough to target is what makes that trader capable of building the tooling internally. The addressable market for a third-party prediction market aggregation layer is, by construction, the set of traders too small to build their own infrastructure but too sophisticated for Robinhood. That segment exists, but it is narrow and does not compound into dominance.

The strongest bull case requires you to believe that prediction markets are at a true inflection -- that the growth from 4,000 monthly active users to 600,000 in eighteen months is the beginning of an exponential curve, that fragmentation will accelerate to five or ten venues rather than two, and that Valence can evolve from middleware into something with proprietary data or network effects that create structural defensibility. The Bloomberg analogy is tempting: Bloomberg started as a terminal for bond traders and became the nervous system of global finance. But Bloomberg's moat was never routing -- it was proprietary data feeds, analytics, and the social network embedded in Bloomberg messaging. Valence's aggregation generates no proprietary data that exchanges don't already have, and no social graph. For this to become a great investment, the founders would need to discover a secret *after* building the initial product -- to find, through their position in the execution layer, some non-obvious truth about prediction markets that transforms the business into something with genuine monopoly characteristics. That is possible but speculative, and I do not invest in the hope that a secret will reveal itself later.

I will note that the founding team is technically formidable. Three UT Austin students with internships spanning Optiver, IMC, DRW, Cubist, Citadel, and Citadel Securities -- this is not a team of MBAs. Daniel Kasabov-Nouvion built an order book in Rust. Neo Wang co-created the USACO Guide with 70,000 registered users and previously built an event-contract exchange. Arthur Zhou is a USAMO qualifier and USACO Platinum competitor. These are engineers with genuine low-latency systems expertise and competitive programming depth. The founder-market fit is real. But founder quality without a structural path to monopoly produces, at best, a good business -- not a power-law outcome. Plenty of brilliant engineers have built competent, competitive, thin-margin infrastructure companies. The power law demands that I invest only in companies where success produces an unassailable market position, and nothing about Valence's structure -- its dependence on exchange APIs, its replicable technology stack, its absence of network effects -- suggests that outcome.

The prediction market space itself is interesting. The regulatory unlock from Kalshi's CFTC lawsuit, the Polymarket breakout during the 2024 election, the entry of Robinhood and DraftKings -- genuine catalysts are creating a real market. But the right investment in prediction markets is either an exchange (where you own the liquidity and set the rules) or a company built on a non-obvious insight about what prediction markets make possible beyond trading. An aggregation layer captures neither the exchange economics nor any novel insight. It is infrastructure for a market that may or may not mature, built on technology that is established rather than discontinuous, with no path to the kind of dominance that justifies the risk.
