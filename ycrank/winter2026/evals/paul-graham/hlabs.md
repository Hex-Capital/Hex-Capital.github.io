# Hlabs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Hlabs is that it's a picks-and-shovels play for the robotics boom, built by a founder who deliberately walked toward hardware manufacturing after spending six years in software. That's moving toward the schlep, not away from it. Thousands of engineers see that the US robotics supply chain is fractured and China-dependent. They all know the problem exists. Almost none of them want to deal with PCB manufacturing, inventory management, shipping logistics, and quality control for physical components at volume. That's textbook schlep blindness — the problem is obvious, the pain is real, and the work required to solve it is so unglamorous that most technical founders unconsciously filter it out and go build another SaaS product instead.

The organic discovery signal here is plausible but not airtight. Paul Hetherington has an MEng in integrated mechanical and electrical engineering, did UAV control and embedded systems research, holds a commercial drone certification, and then spent six years running Mystic AI, an ML infrastructure platform from YC W21. That biography traces a path through exactly the world where you'd encounter broken robotics supply chains firsthand. But the dossier doesn't give me the specific moment — the equivalent of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. I suspect the story exists; someone with that background who pivots from ML software to manufacturing actuator control boards in Austin probably had a specific catalyzing experience. The absence of the explicit narrative costs points, but the background coherence partially compensates.

What I find genuinely compelling is the 40+ robotics companies ordering since the October 2025 launch. That's roughly four months of selling a physical hardware product, pre-YC, as a solo founder. For a hardware company at this stage, that level of pull is unusual. Nobody orders circuit boards out of politeness. These are robotics teams that need components and found Hlabs. The breadth of customer segments — humanoids, household robots, quadrupeds, robot arms, defense — suggests the product solves a real horizontal pain rather than a niche vertical need. This isn't Wufoo-level virality, but for hardware it's a meaningful signal that people actually want what he's making.

The solo founder question is the structural concern I keep returning to. I've said cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. For a software company, a solo technical founder can sometimes pull it off — Drew Houston was effectively solo when he applied, and I made the co-founder a condition rather than a rejection. But hardware is fundamentally harder to run alone. You're simultaneously designing products, managing manufacturing, handling logistics, supporting customers, and — if the Q1 2026 roadmap is real — developing four product lines at once. On $500K. That's not a resource allocation I'd choose. Hetherington is clearly technical enough to build the product himself — the GitHub repos, the engineering degree, the embedded systems research all confirm that — but one person running a hardware supply chain company is stretched thin no matter how talented.

The bull case, and it's a strong one, is timing combined with structural advantage. US industrial policy now actively favors domestic manufacturing. Defense procurement requires US-made components. Dozens of new robotics startups need standardized parts and cannot wait months for custom designs or navigate Chinese import restrictions. If you believe the humanoid robotics wave produces a broad ecosystem of builders rather than consolidating around three vertically integrated giants, then the picks-and-shovels supplier with open-source developer tools and US manufacturing wins by default — because nobody else wants to do the work. The comparison isn't Stripe exactly, but there's a structural parallel: an infrastructure layer that every builder needs, hidden behind a schlep that deters competition. If Hetherington finds a co-founder and the robotics ecosystem diversifies as expected, this could be a foundational company in the American robotics supply chain.

Where I land: this is a bet I'd make, but with the explicit expectation that the solo founder situation gets resolved. The schlep is enormous and real. The founder has the right technical DNA and the prior YC experience to navigate what's coming. The early traction is genuine. The timing is structural rather than hype-driven. Hardware from a solo founder on $500K is scary, but the problem is big enough and ugly enough that most competitors will continue to unconsciously look away.
