# Sherpa -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Optimizely does $400M in ARR. VWO was just acquired for $200M. Mutiny raised $72.6M. AB Tasty pulled in $38.9M in revenue. And now Sherpa wants to automate what these companies already do, but with LLMs generating the variants instead of human designers. When I see a space this well-funded and well-served, with multiple incumbents who already own the customer relationship and are all building AI roadmaps, I don't see an opening — I see a closing window. Lee Jacobs and I have talked about this pattern at Long Journey: the AI company that looks like a platform but is really a beautiful interface sitting on top of foundation model capabilities that every competitor can access. Sherpa's core technology — using generally available LLMs to generate headlines, CTAs, and page layouts — is exactly the kind of capability Optimizely or VWO ships as a feature update in a quarter. The dossier itself flags this: "Barriers to replication are low at the technology layer." That's not my inference; that's the structural reality of building on someone else's infrastructure.

Ethan Kinnan holds a BS in Finance from the University of Washington, worked as Head of Partnerships at Lindy. Norbu Sonam has a BS in Computer Science from RPI, interned at Microsoft and IBM, then joined Lindy as a software engineer. When I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not your business — what I hear is: two smart young professionals who worked at an AI agent company and spotted a market opportunity in conversion rate optimization. Ethan says he "automated every job he's ever had," which is a nice line, but it's a productivity instinct, not a biographical crucible. Nobody here was broken and rebuilt by the CRO problem. Nobody lived inside a system that exploited them through conversion opacity. Sherpa's founding story is "we were at Lindy and saw the opportunity" — and I've passed on that origin story enough times to recognize it. The founders who build things I fund tend to be people who couldn't stop working on the problem even if nobody gave them money. Sherpa's founders built relevant skills at a prior employer and parlayed them into an adjacent product. That's smart career execution, but it's not the forge I look for.

Sherpa's best bull case is the traction: 4.3 million website visits analyzed, 40 experiments launched in 30 days for Lindy yielding +30% total conversion lift, and a claim of 15-20% lift within 30 days for customers. Everett Butler at Lindy says Sherpa enabled them to "ship dozens of tests a week." That's a real result, and an advocate would argue the data flywheel — each experiment improving variant generation quality — creates compounding defensibility over time. The "why now" is genuine: LLMs crossed a quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes autonomous copy and layout generation credible, and incumbent consolidation (Intellimize acquired by Webflow April 2024, VWO and AB Tasty merging under Everstone January 2025) creates distraction. I'll grant all of that. But the flagship case study is Lindy — their former employer. Both founders worked there. Gauge and Yeti Co appear on the website without detailed results. When your best proof point is your last boss, I need to see Sherpa win cold prospects who have no prior relationship with the team before I believe the product sells itself. And even if it does, the data flywheel at 4.3 million visits is modest compared to what Optimizely accumulates in an afternoon across its enterprise customer base.

A/B testing and CRO is one of the most transparent, well-understood markets in software. Every marketing team knows what these tools do, how to evaluate them, and what they cost. There is no invisible tax being extracted, no opaque system exploiting information asymmetry, no hidden structure that Sherpa is exposing for the first time. Compare Sherpa to Affirm, which attacked credit card opacity — a genuinely extractive system where consumers didn't understand the real cost of borrowing. Or compare Sherpa to Ownwell, which automates property tax appeals against a system designed to be impenetrable to ordinary homeowners. Sherpa makes an existing, visible workflow faster. That's valuable but it's not structural disruption. It's the kind of company that serves already-sophisticated marketing teams at growth-stage SaaS companies — people who already have budgets, tooling, and expertise. The cascading impact stops at the business's bottom line; there's no mechanism by which Sherpa's success expands access to a resource or service previously gatekept from underserved populations.

The condition that would flip me: if Ethan or Norbu revealed a biographical connection to this problem that goes beyond workplace proximity — something that made them incapable of ignoring CRO dysfunction — and if Sherpa demonstrated proprietary conversion models trained on data that incumbents cannot access, with repeatable results across ten or twenty cold customers in different verticals. That would move Sherpa from "smart AI application" to something with the structural depth I need. Right now, Sherpa scores lowest on the two dimensions I weight most heavily — the founders' biographical connection to the problem and the contrarian nature of the space — and the dimensions where Sherpa performs best (traction, data accumulation) don't compensate for what's missing at the foundation. The decisive factors are the absence of biographical forge in a market I explicitly identify as consensus and already-transparent, compounded by the AI wrapper commoditization risk that Long Journey has stated publicly we avoid.
