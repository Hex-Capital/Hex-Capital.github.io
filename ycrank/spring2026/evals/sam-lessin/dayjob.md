# Dayjob -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$450K ARR in seven months selling AI route scheduling to skip hire operators in the UK -- that's real money from a real pain point, and I respect the hell out of it. George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo, launched their grocery business, then spent eighteen months building Gaea (a waste management ERP that did $150K in revenue) before pivoting Dayjob into the specific scheduling pain point he'd watched planners struggle with firsthand. Fred Fooks brings Oxford Engineering Science and Deloitte supply chain analytics. These are competent, grounded operators who found a gap by living inside the industry. But here's my problem: competent operators executing well in a sensible vertical is not what gets me out of bed. Dayjob is a reasonable team building a reasonable product for a reasonable market. That's the profile of a company that returns 3-5x if everything goes right -- not the asymmetric mispricing I hunt for.

Dayjob's consensus positioning is where this falls flat for me. Skip hire route optimization in the UK is not a category investors are wrong about -- it's a category investors correctly identify as small and niche. There's a difference between "the market has dismissed this because of narrative bias" (which is how I got Solana at four cents, or Eaze when cannabis made every top-tier VC uncomfortable) and "the market has dismissed this because it's a subsegment of a subsegment of UK waste management." The route optimization software market is $8.5B globally, but Dayjob's actual addressable market -- skip hire operators running enough vehicles to justify SaaS scheduling -- is a fraction of that. Nobody is irrationally scared of waste management software. Nobody thinks this can't work. The price isn't wrong; the ceiling is just visible.

AMCS, sitting on $225M in funding with an existing waste management ERP suite that already includes route optimization, is the elephant in every room Dayjob walks into. This is exactly the pattern I warn about constantly -- every time someone pitches me "AI for [industry X]," I ask: who is the Adobe here? Who is the incumbent that's going to look at Dayjob's demo and say "we should add that feature by Q3"? AMCS is that incumbent. Dayjob's counter-positioning -- ERP-agnostic, integrates with Weighsoft, PurGo, and Wastelogics instead of replacing them -- is smart tactically. The VWS/PurGo partnership announced January 2026 gives them channel access to PurGo's installed base. But channel partnerships with ERP vendors are not moats. They're distribution agreements that last until the ERP vendor decides to build native scheduling themselves or signs a deal with AMCS's module. Dayjob's structural durability depends on integration depth and domain tuning accumulated over deployments, and at seven months in with two named customers (Allstone, Coastal Recycling), that accumulation is barely begun.

The primitive test is where Dayjob definitively fails my framework. Route optimization is not a new behavior. AI scheduling is not a new behavior. Waste management software is a $11.13B market that already exists. Dayjob is a better version of an existing product in an established category -- vertically specialized, sure, but "Routific for skip hire" is an improvement, not a primitive. When I backed Venmo, Andrew Kortina was creating a behavior that literally didn't have a name yet. When I invested in Patreon, direct creator monetization was a concept, not a category. Dayjob's customers aren't doing something they couldn't describe five years ago -- they're doing route planning faster. That's valuable. It's just not the kind of value that compounds into something 100x larger. Coastal Recycling's testimonial -- "5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour" -- tells you the exact shape of the upside: operational efficiency gains. Meaningful to the customer. Not the foundation of a new market.

Here's the bull case an advocate would make, and I want to be honest about it: Dayjob looks a lot like Metropolis at seed stage -- AI applied to a physical-world operation (parking vs. waste fleets), sold into a fragmented industry of local operators, with the potential to become the scheduling layer for an entire vertical. Metropolis seeded early and is now worth ~$5B. And $450K ARR in seven months from a 5-person team in an industry that historically moves at glacial speed is genuinely exceptional capital efficiency. The Gusto parallel also works -- boring vertical, unsexy product, massive switching costs once embedded in payroll/scheduling workflows. Maybe Dayjob expands from skip hire to general waste collection to recycling to bulk haulage, and each vertical adds ERP integrations and domain knowledge that compound. But the Metropolis parallel actually reveals the gap: Metropolis owns and operates parking infrastructure. Dayjob is a software overlay on someone else's physical operations. The moat at Metropolis is physical. The moat at Dayjob is integration depth and domain tuning -- both of which an AI-native incumbent can replicate. What would flip me to Invest: if Dayjob showed me they were building toward owning a piece of the transaction itself -- taking a cut of each job scheduled, or becoming the marketplace connecting waste operators with demand -- something that shifts them from SaaS tool to infrastructure primitive. Revenue-share per skip moved would be a fundamentally different business than subscription per seat.

Dayjob's capital discipline is the one dimension that genuinely impresses me. $450K ARR from $150K in prior Gaea revenue, pivoted cleanly in January 2025, shipping product by August 2025, five people, and presumably running near breakeven given the ARR relative to a lean London team. George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks aren't burning cash waiting for product-market fit -- they found customers fast and are scaling revenue faster than most Series A companies I see. If this were the last money they ever raised, they'd probably survive. That earns real points in my framework.

The two dimensions that lock my verdict are Primitive Creation and Consensus Mispricing. Dayjob scores low on both because it's a better route optimizer in an unsexy-but-not-mispriced vertical -- there's no narrative the market is wrong about, and no new behavior being created. The founders are sane, the idea is sane, and sane-sane is exactly the configuration that produces solid small outcomes, not the asymmetric returns I need to justify writing a check with my own money. I'm not saying Dayjob won't work. I'm saying the upside is already priced into what it is: a good vertical SaaS business in UK waste management. Pass.
