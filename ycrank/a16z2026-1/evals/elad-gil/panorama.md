# Panorama -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Panorama's biggest structural problem is one I flag before anything else: it's building an application on a contested infrastructure layer. Slack, Notion, Linear -- the workplace platforms Panorama reads data from -- are owned by companies (Salesforce, Microsoft ecosystem, Linear itself) that have native access to the same signals Panorama must retrieve via API. Microsoft Copilot Studio already observes M365 work patterns and suggests automations. Google AgentSpace does the same across Workspace. When the platform owners can replicate your core capability with a feature release rather than a product build, you're not in an infrastructure position -- you're in a feature position. I've written about this explicitly: I'd rather invest in the infrastructure layer itself than in applications sitting on top of someone else's platform. Panorama is doing the inverse of what Stripe did. Stripe became the API that other businesses built on; Panorama consumes APIs that other businesses control. That asymmetry matters enormously at scale.

The market structure timing here fails my primary test. "AI-powered workflow automation" is not a nonobvious market -- it's arguably the single most consensus category in venture capital right now. Every major platform player is building it. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks for $2.85 billion specifically to expand into AI workflow automation beyond IT. Zapier, already at $310 million in revenue, is layering AI onto its existing automation graph. n8n raised $254 million. The structural shift -- LLMs enabling observational automation from unstructured data -- is real, but it's been obvious to every investor and platform company since ChatGPT launched in late 2022. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by consensus. When I wrote a check to Perplexity, AI search was considered a graveyard. Panorama enters a market where consensus is already enthusiastic and capital is already deployed at scale. That's the opposite of the nonobvious window I look for.

Panorama's "TikTok-like recommendation engine" framing -- observing work patterns and proactively surfacing automations rather than requiring users to configure them -- is a genuine product insight. But product insight without structural positioning is a feature, not a company. The dossier confirms the cold-start dependency: Panorama needs 1-2 weeks of workplace data observation before generating meaningful recommendations. That delayed time-to-value creates a conversion problem during evaluation periods, and it compounds the platform risk -- a team already inside Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace gets instant AI suggestions from their existing vendor with zero onboarding friction. Distribution against an incumbent with native data access is a distinct miracle from building the recommendation engine itself, which means Panorama needs at least two independent breakthroughs: the technology must be materially better than what platform vendors ship, AND the distribution must overcome the native-access advantage. That's compounding odds.

The bull case deserves serious consideration because the founding team is genuinely strong by my operator-scaler criteria. Jingwei Hao progressed from Senior Engineer to Staff to Tech Lead Manager at Lyft, owned key infrastructure at Cash App, and has experience across Google and Twitter -- exactly the scaling-company operator profile I weight heavily. Jaclyn Lunger brings an MIT PhD in ML and was the first employee at Lila Sciences, which reached $1.3 billion valuation with Nvidia backing. This is a team that could execute at the 1-to-100 transition. SOC2 Type I certification at pre-seed shows enterprise seriousness most early companies skip. For this to work, Panorama would need the cross-platform observation angle to be structurally superior to any single-platform native solution -- the argument being that Microsoft can only see Microsoft data, but Panorama sees Slack AND Notion AND Linear simultaneously, creating recommendations no single-platform copilot can match. If that cross-platform synthesis proves dramatically more valuable than single-platform automation, and if enterprise buyers prefer a neutral third party over vendor lock-in, there's a path. But "if the multi-platform play wins" is a bet on buyer behavior, not a structural moat.

I'm passing. The two dimensions that drive this decision are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. Panorama enters the most consensus AI category with an application-layer product dependent on platforms actively building competing capabilities. Strong founders don't override broken market structure -- I've seen that movie enough times. The $23.77 billion workflow automation TAM is real, but TAM without a structural entry point just means well-funded competitors will capture most of it. If Panorama can demonstrate that cross-platform workflow discovery is categorically superior to single-platform native solutions -- with retention data showing teams don't churn back to incumbent tools -- I'd revisit. That evidence doesn't exist yet, and the structural headwinds are significant enough that I'm not willing to fund the experiment at this stage.
