# Madrone -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Madrone is a right-category, wrong-biography company: the strongest signal is that Akshay Trikha and Erik Meike are building modular industrial cooling hardware -- the unsexy, capital-intensive frontier-hardware lane where I write checks -- and the weakest signal is that neither of them has lived a single day inside the problem they're solving, with the dossier explicitly noting "neither founder has disclosed prior experience in HVAC, refrigeration cycle design, or data center facility operations." That pairing is the whole evaluation.

On the lived-experience ladder, the HIGH rung is Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017), who shipped Oculus out of a garage before pivoting to defense -- a founder *of* hardware before he was *of* defense. The MID rung is Demis Hassabis at DeepMind (~2013), pedigreed but compounding across neuroscience, chess, and game design in a way no memo could have produced. The LOW rung is the consensus YC AI-agent darling: two ex-FAANG PMs who discovered the problem in a market-map. Madrone sits between LOW and MID. Trikha was an ML engineer at QuantumScape doing computer vision on solid-state batteries and graduate work at UC Berkeley on ML interatomic potentials -- adjacent to materials, not adjacent to a hyperscaler facility team in West Texas. Meike worked on iPhone wireless power at Apple, which is real thermal-electrical hardware but at the watts scale, not the megawatt refrigeration-cycle scale Madrone claims to displace. Neither Trikha nor Meike has the Chase Lochmiller "I can walk on the well pad and talk to the roughneck" social license that made Crusoe (~2018) bankable. They are Harvey Mudd kids who *read about* the 40% cooling-power statistic, not operators who *watched* a chiller fail in 45°C ambient.

On the capital-intensive frontier-hardware ladder -- Madrone's highest dimension -- the HIGH rung is SpaceX (2008), my first-ever angel check into a category every conventional VC called uninvestable. The MID rung is Flock Safety (~2018), tactile hardware in a regulated-adjacent category. The LOW rung is undifferentiated AI-app SaaS. Madrone clearly sits between MID and HIGH: modular dew-point cooling units, a claim of "20°C supply water without mechanical chillers," 30% less power and water than incumbent chiller + cooling tower stacks, directly targeting Texas hyperscale buildout. This is exactly the lane HVAC incumbents Johnson Controls, Carrier, and Trane structurally cannot move into without cannibalizing installed-base service revenue, which is precisely the kind of incumbent-paralysis moat that compounds for outsiders.

On magically weird, the HIGH rung is Crusoe burning gas flares in North Dakota; the LOW rung is the consensus AI-agent round with six VCs chasing. Madrone sits at MID -- liquid cooling is a $4.07B market growing 31.5% CAGR, hardly laughed at, but facility-side dew-point cooling is less crowded than the chip-level pile-up of Corintis (~$58M raised), Accelsius (~$50M), JetCool (acquired by Flex November 2024), and LiquidStack. Not magically weird, just unfashionably tangential. On single CEO authority, Trikha is named CEO and Meike is named Co-Founder on the YC page -- a clear delineation, which is above my default for a two-person team, but no operating agreement or vesting protocol is disclosed and these are college co-founders from the same Harvey Mudd cohort, the exact configuration where the tiebreaker has often never been tested. Sits between MID and the HandUp-class HIGH. On physical-world verifiability, the HIGH rung is Niantic (~2015) where I played Ingress before writing the check, and Flock Safety (~2018) where I read the founder's Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons café. The LOW rung is a B2B API I can only evaluate via founder testimony. Madrone sits firmly at LOW -- no pilot, no deployed unit, no third-party performance validation, no Texas data center I can drive to and photograph. The "different thermodynamic process" is marketing language in the dossier, not an artifact.

Forecast, 36 months: Madrone is acquired by Johnson Controls, Carrier, or Trane for under $50M as an acqui-hire after struggling to convert any hyperscaler pilot into a multi-unit deployment, OR Madrone pivots to selling cooling-control software / retrofit-services to mid-tier colocation operators where the procurement cycle is shorter than hyperscale facility teams require. The Alloy Enterprises acquisition by JCI on May 13, 2026 and JetCool's absorption by Flex in November 2024 are the template -- the exit floor for credible cooling teams is real, but so is the ceiling, because incumbents acquire to neutralize, not to scale.

The strongest bull case against my pass: Meike's Apple iPhone wireless power work was genuinely hard thermal-electrical hardware at iPhone production volumes, which is not nothing, and Trikha's QuantumScape ML-on-materials background plus Berkeley scaling-laws research could be exactly the unfair advantage if Madrone's cooling system uses ML for real-time control optimization -- the polymath-compounding override that bailed out DeepMind despite the pedigree. NVIDIA Inception is a non-equity program but it does signal Madrone has talked to people who care, and the JCI/Alloy precedent guarantees a soft landing even in the bear case. That case stings because the polymath exception is a real exception in my book. But it doesn't win: Madrone is selling capex hardware into hyperscaler facility teams whose procurement language is refrigeration tonnage and ASHRAE compliance, not ML cleverness, and Apple consumer-thermal at watts is a different planet from data-hall thermal at megawatts. The flip condition is concrete: one signed pilot LOI with a named hyperscaler or top-tier colocation operator -- Meta, Microsoft, Equinix, Digital Realty, or Crusoe itself -- within 12 months, with a deployed unit I can fly to and stand in front of. Until then Madrone is a thesis without a roughneck.
