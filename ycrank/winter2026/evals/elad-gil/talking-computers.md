# Talking Computers -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive dynamics here are the first thing I see, and they're unfavorable. Multi-agent orchestration is not a non-obvious market -- it's arguably the most consensus-hot category in AI right now. Microsoft merged AutoGen into its unified Agent Framework with enterprise GA targeting Q1 2026. Google launched the A2A protocol. Anthropic defined MCP. OpenAI ships Swarm and Assistants API. CrewAI has $24.5 million in funding and reports 450 million agents per month. Relevance AI raised $24 million and uses nearly identical "workforce" positioning. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed boring and nobody was building there. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto seemed too weird for most software investors. This market is the opposite -- every large platform company and dozens of well-funded startups are actively building the agent communication layer. The 1,445% surge in Gartner multi-agent inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025 tells me the market is real, but it also tells me every enterprise buyer's existing vendor (Microsoft, Google, AWS) is racing to own this layer. A two-person pre-seed team doesn't have a structural advantage when the incumbents are the ones defining the protocols.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. If enterprises adopt agents from multiple vendors -- OpenAI for one function, Anthropic for another, open-source for a third -- they'll need a vendor-neutral orchestration layer. Incumbents are structurally incentivized to lock customers into their ecosystems, which could create an opening for a neutral "workplace" that sits above proprietary frameworks. This is the HTTP analogy: nobody owned the protocol, so middleware companies captured enormous value. If that pattern repeats in agent communication, the "Facility" concept could be well-positioned. The founders' complementary technical backgrounds are relevant -- Parsa's systems programming in Rust and compiler work combined with Zayaan's GPU/inference optimization at Modular cover the right stack for building agent infrastructure. And being at YC during the agentic wave means they'll have access to early enterprise adopters who are experimenting with multi-agent deployments.

But I don't see evidence that overcomes the structural headwinds. First, the product doesn't appear to exist yet -- the website returned a title page, no demo, no documentation, no pricing. At pre-seed that's not automatically disqualifying, but combined with the signal that the company may have pivoted from "Speedtrain" during the YC batch (Parsa's GitHub still lists "CTO @ Speedtrain (YC W26)"), it suggests the team is still searching for their specific product thesis rather than executing against one they've identified. That search phase is normal -- but in a market this competitive, with CrewAI and Relevance AI already shipping product and acquiring customers, the clock matters. Second, I can't identify the single miracle. If it's "build a better orchestration platform than CrewAI," that's not a miracle -- it's an execution grind against better-funded competitors. If it's "become the vendor-neutral standard," that's closer to a miracle but it requires both technical excellence AND distribution at enterprise scale, which looks like two miracles for a two-person team. Third, the founders are early-career -- internship-level experience at TextQL, prefix.dev, Modular, and Yutori. Those are relevant technical environments but none suggest the kind of enterprise sales or distribution experience needed to compete in this market. When I look at the founders who've won in competitive infrastructure categories -- Stripe, Figma, Coinbase -- they combined technical depth with an insight about distribution that their competitors missed. I don't see that distribution insight here.

The technology cycle timing is real but doesn't advantage this company specifically. Agent communication IS becoming a practical requirement as enterprise AI adoption matures. MCP and A2A emerging in 2024-2025 created genuine infrastructure for building on. But unlike Harvey, where the founding team was building ahead of a capability curve that most investors couldn't yet see, Talking Computers is entering a capability moment that everyone can see. When the structural moment is obvious, the returns accrue to whoever has the best distribution -- and in enterprise software, that's usually the incumbent with existing customer relationships. I'd need to see a specific, non-obvious wedge that the large platforms structurally cannot serve before I'd get conviction here.
