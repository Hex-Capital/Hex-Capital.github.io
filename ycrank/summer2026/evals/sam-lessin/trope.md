# Trope -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Trope is the pass example I wrote down before the batch dropped — "we're building an AI agent for [enterprise vertical], team from Tesla/Amazon/Zipline, YC Summer 2026, no revenue disclosed, no pricing page, demo-only CTA." Matthew Chow shipped Cybertruck software and Zipline autonomous delivery code, which is the highest-signal fact in the dossier and the one thing keeping Trope out of the bottom quartile on founder conviction; every other dimension collapses toward the canonical AI-vertical-SaaS pattern I've said out loud I cannot generate alpha inside of.

Solana at $0.05/coin is the round I want to compare Trope against — pre-consensus, in a category most funds had quit, priced for the illegibility. Trope sits at the opposite pole: ERP-AI is the exact 2026 YC consensus sleeve, with CoPlane already at $14M raised (PYMNTS, 2025) and Rewst at $104M cumulative (SiliconANGLE, 2024) doing structurally identical work on adjacent surfaces, and Microsoft Finance Copilot going GA in October 2025 as the vendor-native version. The dossier surfaces no tier-1 pass, no price discount, no strategic ambiguity — it reads like every other "AI agents for enterprise workflow" pitch I've deselected this year, which is the point of the pass example: I don't need to see the term sheet to know the SAFE cap is priced against a factory-model demand curve, not against the illegible-asset curve where Trope would actually be underwritable.

John Fish / Bookshelved is the ceiling on founder-as-brand — a YouTuber with owned distribution I'll write a check into on the strength of the audience alone. Trope's failure mode is the mirror image: Chow's LinkedIn shows 2K followers, Vannara has no retrievable Twitter, no public GitHub, no essays on ERP implementation economics, no newsletter, no Discord, no Cansbridge-adjacent community that pre-existed the company. When the founder-brand dimension is empty at pre-seed, the deal has to make it up on structure or category — and Trope doesn't. The Slow Creator Fund exists precisely because owned distribution is a step-function feature at seed, and Trope has none of it to price against.

Trope's capital-structure profile is the SaaS default I keep telling founders not to force-fit — enterprise partner sales motion, no exposed pricing, demo-only funnel aimed at implementation partners whose gross margin literally comes from the billable hours Trope claims to compress. Over 18 months this compounds badly: the SAFE round funds a partner-channel GTM that requires Trope to convince Accenture and Deloitte practice leads to buy a tool that shrinks their own book, which is why every attempt at this since the mid-2010s has needed 3+ rounds to find the buyer that isn't structurally hostile. This is a five-tack regatta before Series A and I don't see how the first two tacks close.

Sublime Security is where I actually paid for pre-consensus enterprise B2B — a security category where I could name what the incumbents wouldn't build and why. For Trope's category score to flip a tier, the dossier would need to show that Microsoft, SAP, and Oracle have a structural reason not to ship partner-side implementation copilots — and the opposite is true: Microsoft already released the end-user Copilot in October 2025, the partner-tooling version is a quarter's worth of roadmap away, and vendor-embedded distribution beats a two-person UBC startup selling into the same practice leads. "AI is just more internet" (Joe Lonsdale #113, 2024) is exactly the frame here — Trope is more internet layered over an ERP suite that already has the internet.

Fin — my own company — is the single-anchor parallel on founder conviction. Kortina and I ran a Wizard-of-Oz personal assistant manually for months before automating, and the manual operation was the underwriting proof. Trope's dossier shows zero evidence of Chow or Vannara having sat inside an ERP implementation project as consultants, developers, or PMs — the Tesla and Zipline pedigree is production embedded software, not SAP fit-gap workshops or NetSuite cutover plans. Without the manual-operation phase or the domain-embedding phase, the founder input to the "does this structure work" question is a competent generalist team building a category they haven't lived inside, and that's the profile the ERP-vendor Copilots will beat on distribution before Trope hits $1M ARR.

My 36-month forecast for Trope: acqui-hired by a mid-market ERP implementation partner (Syntax, Rimini Street, or a Deloitte-tier practice) or by Microsoft/Oracle for sub-$20M in talent-and-tuck terms, with revenue never crossing $2M ARR before the Series A window closes as Microsoft Finance Copilot and SAP Joule expand into partner tooling in 2027. The mechanism is straightforward — channel-incentive misalignment prevents partner-side traction, vendor-native Copilot expansion prevents category defensibility, and the founder team's execution reputation makes a talent acquisition the cleanest exit for both sides.

The bull case that would sting: ERP implementations genuinely do fail at 70% (per Trope's own citation), the $14.45B integration-services market is real, and if Trope wedges into two or three mid-tier partners before Microsoft's partner-Copilot ships, the proprietary training data from SOWs and cutover plans becomes a vertical-model moat CoPlane can't replicate. But the Unstated Pattern I trust here is that operator-friend deals from my Facebook IPG / Kortina graph beat cold generalist enterprise seeds every time, and nothing in the dossier suggests Trope is coming through that channel. The condition that would flip my verdict: Trope signs two named ERP implementation partners (Syntax-tier or above) as paying customers before the Series A raise AND restructures the pricing to align with partner economics — outcome-based or margin-share, not seat-SaaS. Absent that, this is a pass at any price the 2026 YC seed market will produce.
