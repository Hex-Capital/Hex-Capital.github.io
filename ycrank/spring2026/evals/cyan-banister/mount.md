# Mount -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Armilla AI already holds Lloyd's coverholder status serving Fortune 1000 companies with $25M in capacity. Testudo writes policies through Apollo, Atrium, and QBE syndicates with $9.25M per-insured limits. Corgi has raised $108M and hit $40M ARR since its 2025 regulatory approval. Mount walks into this landscape with two founders, zero insurance industry experience, no documented carrier license, no Lloyd's relationship, and no actuarial capability on the team or in their job postings. The company describes itself as an "insurer" — that's not a product description, it's a regulatory classification that requires state-by-state licensing in the US and typically years of groundwork. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin brought a decade of fraud detection from PayPal — his entire biography made him the inevitable person to build transparent lending. Mount's founders have no documented encounter with insurance, underwriting, or the liability systems they're proposing to replace.

Fabian Amherd built CNNs at MESH robotics and started a web development company at 16. John Bachmann founded Horizonn at 18, publishing five Swiss magazines and generating 400K in annual revenue. Both show early hustle — I respect anyone who starts building before the world tells them they're allowed to. But when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — I get two technically capable young founders from ETH Zürich's orbit who identified a market gap at the intersection of AI and insurance. That's pattern recognition, not biographical forge. Neither founder has documented personal exposure to AI agent failure, to insurance system dysfunction, to the liability gaps that swallow companies deploying autonomous systems. The gap between "I saw an opportunity" and "this problem found me and I couldn't walk away" is the gap between career optimization and conviction that survives a near-death company moment. Mount's founding story, as documented, sits on the wrong side of that line.

Mount's integrated red-team-then-insure model is the strongest element in their pitch — evaluate the AI agent's vulnerabilities, generate proprietary risk data, then underwrite based on what you actually observed rather than what an application form discloses. That data flywheel, if Mount achieves sufficient evaluation volume, could create genuine underwriting accuracy that competitors relying on Lloyd's capacity and application-stage disclosure cannot match. The ADR certification — positioned as "the SOC 2 for AI agents" — is a clever wedge: enterprises engage for risk assessment and convert to insurance customers. I can see the infrastructure thesis beneath the surface. But here's what separates this from my Crusoe Energy conviction: Crusoe's founders had already built portable data centers and deployed them to North Dakota gas fields before I wrote a check. Mount's data moat is entirely aspirational — no evaluations, no policies, no claims data, no evidence of a single AI agent assessed. The concept is architecturally sound; the execution evidence is absent.

S&P Global reported in February 2026 that traditional insurers are actively retreating from AI risk coverage. Over 90% of businesses want AI coverage and can't get it. There IS an invisible system here — deployers of AI agents may not realize their existing policies exclude AI-specific failures until a claim arrives and gets denied. That's information asymmetry, and Mount's certification layer makes it visible. But this is institutional opacity between enterprises and their carriers, not the kind of extractive structure that exploits vulnerable people who lack power or information. When Uber attacked the taxi medallion cartel, it liberated riders and drivers from a system designed to extract from them. When Ownwell automates property tax appeals, it fights for homeowners against an opaque bureaucracy. Mount fights for well-funded enterprises deploying AI agents against the fine print in their insurance contracts. That's a real problem — it's just not the kind that activates my deepest conviction.

The bull case an advocate would make: Mount's timing is impeccable. The EU AI Act's full applicability for high-risk AI deployer obligations arrives August 2026 — months away. Traditional insurers are fleeing. The AI agents market is projected to reach $183B by 2033 at 49.6% CAGR. No one else combines red-teaming with underwriting in a single platform. Armilla and Testudo rely on Lloyd's capacity and external risk assessment — Mount captures risk data at the point of evaluation, creating a structural advantage that compounds. YC backing with Pete Koomen provides network and credibility. The founders started companies at 16 and 18 — they're builders, not analysts. And perhaps the insurance expertise gap gets filled by their Founding AI Engineer hire (listed at $170K-$210K with up to 1.40% equity) or through YC's network. Give them the benefit of pre-seed stage. But here's why the bull case doesn't convert me: this space is not contrarian. Harper raised $47M. Corgi raised $108M. Armilla has Lloyd's backing. Testudo has LocalGlobe and prominent angels. AI insurance is consensus-attractive — VCs are competing for these deals, not shifting uncomfortably in their chairs. When I wrote my Uber check, 300+ AngelList investors had passed. Mount is entering a space where institutional capital is already flowing freely. The condition that would flip me: if Fabian or John had a documented personal encounter with AI agent failure — a system they built that caused real harm, an insurance claim they were denied, a liability event that changed their trajectory — and if Mount had completed even a handful of red-team evaluations with real enterprises generating proprietary risk data, I would reassess. The biographical connection and the first data would change everything.

Mount occupies a real market with real regulatory tailwinds and a genuinely clever product architecture. But my framework requires biographical inevitability in the founders, contrarian positioning against investor consensus, and an invisible system that exploits the powerless — and Mount delivers none of these at conviction-grade levels. The founders are competent market-gap identifiers in a consensus-attractive space, building an aspirational data moat with no insurance expertise on a team proposing to become an insurer. I wish them well, but this is not a Long Journey bet.
