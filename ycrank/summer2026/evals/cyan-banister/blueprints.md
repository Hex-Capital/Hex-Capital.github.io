# Blueprints -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ryan Morrissey and Bence Redmond are two University of Limerick students who left a software engineering programme to build a Polymarket strategy builder, and that sentence is the whole problem in miniature. Blueprints's high water mark on my ladder is *Physical-World Verifiability* -- I can sign up for the free tier, drag blocks, deploy "Prince," and watch the PnL tick -- and its low water mark is *Magically Weird*, because I count at least five funded or shipping competitors in the dossier and an open-source SDK published by the very platform Blueprints is parasitic on. That pairing -- tactile, observable, and exactly what everyone else in the YC S26 batch and the prediction-market Discord channels is also building -- tells me Blueprints is a competent demo in a consensus lane, not a misfit in an ignored one.

Flock Safety is the rung I keep wanting to climb to on verifiability. The mechanism that made Flock load-bearing was that I could read its Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons cafe and the artifact was *in the world*, accumulating evidence about itself whether or not the founder was pitching me. Blueprints clears the lower bar of that ladder -- a normal human can install the free tier (2,000 credits, 10 blueprints) inside an hour and feel the product -- but the artifact is a hosted SaaS dashboard, not a camera bolted to a pole, so the evidence it accumulates is a backtest curve and a $500K cumulative paper-and-live volume, not a deployed object I can photograph on someone else's lawn. Tactile, yes. Tangible in the Flock sense, no.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling I wrote my pass example about is exactly the rung Blueprints sits on for *Magically Weird*, and the failure mechanism there is that when six tier-one funds are already circling, the round prices the outcome as if it were certain. Substitute "six tier-one funds" for "Valory writing $13.8M into Polystrat, plus TurbineFi, Rainmaker, Simmer (whose 'self-improving agent harness' framing is the dossier's own words for the closest positional match to Blueprints), Bankr, and Polymarket's first-party github.com/Polymarket/agents library," and the structural picture is identical. I am supposed to find them before it becomes consensus, and the Finance Magnates headline -- "Prediction Markets Are Turning Into a Bot Playground" -- means consensus has already been printed in trade press. Blueprints did not get there first; they got there alongside the playground.

Founder Surfaced From Lived Experience is where I look for the Palmer Luckey moment, and the mechanism that has to compound over the next eighteen months is the founder's *prior* contact with the problem turning into a thousand small product decisions a generalist could never reproduce. Morrissey did a Stripe internship; Redmond did an AWS internship; neither has a documented trading desk, market-making seat, Polymarket-loss origin story, or even a public X account where I could watch them think about market microstructure on a Tuesday night. The dossier is silent on whether either founder ever lost money to a Polymarket arbitrage bot -- which would have been the autobiographical hook -- so I am scoring against my absent-evidence default, not below it, but the absence is itself the signal.

Capital-intensive frontier or unsexy-regulated would have to look like Vigil Labs or Mindbloom for Blueprints to flip a tier on this axis. The counterfactual is concrete: if Blueprints were registered as a CFTC-supervised introducing broker, or had built proprietary co-located hardware in a Polymarket-adjacent jurisdiction with a moat that compounds with each enforcement action, I would re-rate. As-is, the dossier says "co-located execution infrastructure" without disclosing whose rack, and the only regulatory exposure is the platform-inherited CFTC ambiguity that every competitor also absorbs. Software wrapper with borrowed regulatory risk is not the lane.

Single CEO with tiebreaker authority is the dimension where Blueprints quietly does fine -- Morrissey is named CEO on LinkedIn, Redmond is CTO, the titles are unambiguous on Day 1 -- and HQ Trivia is the single anchor I want to compare against. HQ collapsed in part because Yusupov and Kroll never resolved who held the tiebreaker; Blueprints does not appear to have made that mistake, but the dossier discloses no equity split and no operating agreement, so I am giving the default plus a small bump for clean titles, not a full mark. This is not the dimension that kills the deal; it is the dimension that saves the score from being lower.

My 36-month forecast: Blueprints either acqui-hires into Polymarket itself for a single-digit-million talent price by Q4 2028, or shuts down quietly as Polystrat's autonomous self-custody agent (already 4,200 trades in its first month, already outperforming 37% of human Polymarket participants) and Polymarket's own builders.polymarket.com program absorb the no-code-tooling oxygen. The mechanism is straightforward: when the host platform publishes an open-source agents SDK and runs a developer program, third-party hosted no-code wrappers become a feature, not a company.

The bull case that stings is HQ Trivia, and I have to honor it because I overrode my own co-founder rule there for exactly one reason -- tactile virality so explosive I could feel the pull in my own thumb before any metric existed. Could Blueprints be HQ on this axis? The dossier says 250 beta users and $500K cumulative volume over a public beta period -- that is not viral pull, that is a polite trickle, and Polystrat's first-month numbers eat it for breakfast. The Known Exception does not apply. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Blueprints crossing $50M monthly user-deployed volume with documented retention above 40% by Q2 2027, evidencing that "Prince" actually translates plain-English theses into live PnL the bots cannot strip-mine. Until then, this is a pass with warmth -- two Irish kids who shipped a real thing, in the wrong lane for my book.
