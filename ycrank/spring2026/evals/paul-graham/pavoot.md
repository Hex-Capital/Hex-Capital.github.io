# Pavoot -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Pavoot is two ETH Zürich computer scientists ? Gohar Tamrazyan and Ana Yoon Faria de Lima ? selling AI to CMOs they have never been, in a market where Vendelux already raised $16.4M and crossed $2.1M of revenue doing an adjacent version. The dimensional signature here is clear: a sharp technical pair iterating fast (they pivoted mid-batch from a media-hub photo distributor at START Summit into a pipeline-conversion agent) inside a consensus category that no one in their right mind would call a schlep no one else can see. That's a learning-rate-high, repels-low company, and that pairing tells you almost everything about how Pavoot will end up.

The dimension where Pavoot rates lowest is **Idea That Repels Before It Attracts**. The high rung is Stripe ? Patrick Collison willing to wade into PCI compliance and bank integrations that every other developer fled from. The middle rung is Replit ? a developer tool whose value was visible to anyone on Hacker News but which still required real conviction to build. The low rung is the pass-example case: two ex-consultants pitching B2B workflow automation, something everyone immediately agrees is a good idea. Pavoot is much closer to the low rung than the middle. "AI that turns events into pipeline" is the kind of sentence you'd find in a TechCrunch trend piece on agents in 2026; Vendelux is doing the predictive piece, Grip raised $14.5M doing the in-event piece, Cvent and Bizzabo own the buyer relationship, Salesforce Einstein and HubSpot Breeze can ship overlapping features natively. No one hears Pavoot's pitch and says "that will never work" ? they say "yeah, that makes sense, who else is doing it?" That is the tell.

The dimension where Pavoot rates highest is **Founder Learning Rate**, and the relative ordering matters. The high rung is Sam Altman at Loopt ? a 19-year-old whose slope was so steep I eventually championed him as YC president. The middle rung is the Replit team shipping iteratively in public on Hacker News. The low rung is a team that has been working on the same idea for a year and the product still looks like the original deck. Pavoot is somewhere between low-middle and middle: the dossier shows them pivoting from automatic logo detection and real-time photo distribution at START Summit into full-lifecycle GTM workflow within a single YC batch. That is real iteration, not theatrical iteration. But pivoting once is not Sam Altman's slope ? it's table stakes ? and the pivot is also evidence they hadn't deeply understood the customer they're now chasing.

**Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint** is the Airbnb-cereal-box dimension. The high rung is Brian and Joe selling $40 cereal boxes to fund the company; the low rung is the founders who treat every obstacle as a reason to raise more money. Pavoot has shipped into one real event with a real reference customer (Leo Braun at START Global) ? that is a non-trivial artifact for a pre-seed company ? but I don't see any cereal-box story in the dossier, no anecdote that makes me sit forward. I'm applying my default with a small positive adjustment for the live deployment. **Organic Problem Discovery** is the Drew-Houston-USB-drive dimension; the low rung is McKinsey alums identifying a TAM. Pavoot was founded after "a course at the ETH AI Center" ? that is, by the dossier's own account, an idea that came out of an academic AI course, not out of either founder's lived frustration with marketing ROI. Neither founder has been a demand-gen leader sweating an event budget. They are building for a customer segment they don't belong to. **Earnest Builder Identity** ? the Watsi dimension ? has no clear signal either way; I'm at default.

My 36-month forecast: Pavoot reaches somewhere between $300K and $1.2M ARR with a handful of mid-market customers, gets boxed in by Vendelux on the pre-event side and Salesforce/HubSpot on the post-event side, and either acqui-hires into Cvent or Vendelux for a low-eight-figures landing or pivots into a narrower developer-API-for-event-data play that better matches the founders' ETH technical strengths. The mechanism is straightforward ? two technically excellent founders with no GTM experience, in a category where the buyer is a CMO with a procurement process, against incumbents who already have the logos. Learning rate keeps them alive; the consensus-market positioning caps the upside.

The strongest objection is ClassDojo. ClassDojo violated my "hackers building for hackers" pattern entirely ? K-12 teachers, school district procurement, the unsexiest possible buyer ? and became a unicorn because the founders' execution was so good that schlep-blindness over the ugly sales motion created a real moat. Could Pavoot be ClassDojo? The bull case is that enterprise event marketing has exactly the bureaucratic, non-developer character that repels YC-style founders, and Pavoot's ETH technical depth lets them outbuild Vendelux. I don't buy it. ClassDojo's founders were obsessed with the classroom problem in a way that comes through in every interview; Pavoot's founders came from an AI course. ClassDojo had no Vendelux ? no $16.4M-funded direct competitor with two-year head start and existing logos. And ClassDojo's execution moat was earned over years of unglamorous teacher-by-teacher distribution; Pavoot has one reference customer. I'd flip my verdict if Pavoot showed me five paying logos with retention data and one of the founders had taken a months-long internship as a marketing ops lead at a company spending real money on events. Without that, this is a pass.
