# Totalis -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The most striking thing about Totalis isn't the founders or the idea -- it's the competitive structure they're walking into. Kalshi is a $22B company with $1.5B in annualized revenue that already built the exact parlay mechanism Totalis wants to generalize. Polymarket acquired a derivatives platform. Robinhood licensed Kalshi's RFQ system for its own "Custom Combos." When I wrote about schlep blindness, the insight was that founders overlook hard problems because their unconscious won't let them see ideas that involve painful schleps. But the schlep here isn't invisible -- every major player in the space is actively building parlay functionality inside their own walls. The founders aren't seeing what others can't see. They're seeing what everyone sees and betting they can do it better from a position of dramatically less leverage. That's a different situation entirely, and usually a worse one.

The thesis -- a neutral derivative layer across prediction market exchanges -- has a Plaid-like elegance to it conceptually. Exchanges are structurally conflicted: Kalshi can't credibly serve Polymarket traders, and vice versa. A neutral cross-platform parlay engine solves a real coordination problem. The "why now" is legitimate -- prediction market volume grew roughly 17x in a single year, parlays already account for over 20% of Kalshi's sports volume, and the CFTC is actively formalizing the regulatory framework. The market is genuinely exploding. But here's what gives me pause: the best startup ideas look initially like bad ideas. This one looks like a good idea. When an idea is obviously good and the market is obviously hot, you have to ask why the well-funded incumbents won't simply do it themselves. Kalshi has already introduced Builder Codes for third-party developers. They could open their parlay API tomorrow. Your entire company evaporates with one API change from a $22B counterparty.

On the founders: Eric Liu and Pravesh Mansharamani are clearly smart, clearly energetic, and clearly in the right domain. Pravesh built EigenBets -- an actual prediction market platform -- before Totalis. Together they built Goddid.Money (a Polymarket credit scoring tool) and won $7,600 at ETHGlobal. Pravesh has 11+ hackathon wins totaling over $55K and a string of relevant DeFi co-op terms at EigenLabs, SquidRouter, and Nethermind. These are impressive credentials for college students. The co-founder relationship looks solid: same university, same blockchain club, proven ability to ship together under hackathon pressure. But what I'm not seeing is the signal that separates determined builders from talented students. When the Airbnb founders ran out of money, they sold cereal boxes for $40 each. When Stripe's Patrick Collison wanted users, he'd grab your laptop and install the code on the spot. What have the Totalis founders done that's unreasonable? The dossier shows a private beta with a waitlist and zero public traction data. No user counts, no volume, no revenue signal. The team size is listed as zero on their YC page. I look for founders who are "relentlessly resourceful" -- not merely relentless, but resourceful, trying new things when the obvious path is blocked. Hackathon prowess demonstrates speed. It doesn't demonstrate the willingness to do things that don't scale, to go hunt down users one by one, to survive when everything is breaking.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it honestly. If prediction markets follow the trajectory of sports betting legalization -- and the volume growth suggests they might -- then cross-platform derivatives infrastructure becomes essential plumbing. The exchanges will eventually need neutral infrastructure the same way traditional finance needs clearinghouses and prime brokers. First-movers in financial infrastructure can build network effects around market maker liquidity and pricing data that compound over time. These founders are genuinely deep in the domain, they've built prediction market software before, and they're young enough that underestimation itself becomes an asset. If Totalis can bootstrap even modest market maker liquidity and demonstrate cross-platform parlay execution that works, the learning compounds -- and at the far end of that curve, incremental effort is a bargain because so few people are attempting it. The question is whether the window stays open long enough for a pre-seed team to establish itself before Kalshi, Polymarket, and Robinhood close it.

My honest read is that the window is probably too narrow. This isn't the kind of problem where a small team's intensity can overwhelm a larger competitor's resources, because the core challenge isn't technical -- it's political and commercial. You need exchanges to grant you API access and not compete with you. You need market makers to provide liquidity on your platform instead of (or in addition to) the exchanges'. You need regulatory clarity that a derivative layer doesn't require its own DCM registration. Each of these dependencies is controlled by someone else, and none of them have structural reasons to help you succeed. Good people can fix bad ideas, but even great people struggle when the external dependencies are this concentrated and this adversarial.

I keep coming back to the "playing house" pattern I've warned about. Not because these founders are insincere -- they clearly aren't. But because the gap between "we built a cool hackathon project in the prediction market space" and "we're building cross-exchange financial derivatives infrastructure" is enormous, and I don't see evidence they've begun to bridge it. Where are the traders they've talked to? Where are the market makers they've convinced to quote on their platform? Where's the scrappy, unreasonable thing they did to get their first user? Maybe it exists and just isn't in the dossier. But at pre-seed, the founders are the product, and the evidence I have suggests talented builders who haven't yet proven they're the cockroaches this particular fight requires.
