# Alchemy -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's a company that triggers every single one of my AI application-layer alarm bells simultaneously. "AI-powered image analysis for life sciences" -- read that tagline out loud and tell me which part of it is contrarian. AI? Hottest category in venture. Life sciences? Every institutional LP is demanding biotech exposure. Image analysis? Computer vision is the most mature subfield of ML. This is a consensus sandwich. When I look at the YC tags -- "AI-powered Drug Discovery, Machine Learning, SaaS, Biotech, Automation" -- I see five separate categories that every other investor at demo day is already excited about. And when every investor is excited, the upside is already in the price. There's no asymmetry here. PathAI has raised $255 million. Flywheel has raised $125 million. Leica's parent company Danaher acquired DRVISION in 2021 to bundle AI analysis with their microscopes. The market has spoken loudly that AI + microscopy = valuable. Which means if Alchemy is right, the returns are moderate. If they're wrong, it's zero.

Now let me run the kill test, because this is where it gets brutal. "Researchers upload images and describe their analysis needs in plain language; AI agents then assemble a tailored pipeline." You know who can build exactly this? Leica. Zeiss. Nikon. Every microscope manufacturer that already has customer relationships, distribution, and hardware lock-in with the exact labs Alchemy is targeting. When I say "every time you get pitched the AI version of [incumbent's product], that's perfect for the incumbent" -- this is what I mean. A microscope manufacturer bolting on a natural-language analysis pipeline to their existing platform is a product update, not a startup opportunity. And on the other side, CellProfiler, QuPath, and napari are free, grant-funded, deeply entrenched in academic workflows, and increasingly incorporating ML themselves. Alchemy is squeezed between incumbents who can bundle and open-source tools that are free. That's not a market position, that's a vise.

The bull case -- and I want to be honest about it -- is that there's a genuine structural gap. Hardware vendors can't easily unbundle their software from their microscope sales channel. Open-source projects can't raise capital to build polished agentic UX. The researcher who's spending 20 hours on manual annotation is real, and they don't have a great commercial option between "learn to code CellProfiler pipelines" and "buy a $200K Visiopharm enterprise license." If Alchemy nails the self-serve, bottom-up lab adoption motion and builds a library of validated processing nodes that become embedded in published papers and SOPs, there's a workflow lock-in story. It's the kind of thing where you'd say "what if they're right that individual research labs are an underserved segment?" And that's a fair question. But even in the best case, this is a better tool in an existing category. It's not creating a new behavior. Nobody's going to say "I Alchemy'd my images." It makes existing work faster -- it doesn't make previously impossible work possible.

On the founders: Baek has biomedical engineering training and medical device experience at Boston Scientific, which is adjacent but not specific to bioimage analysis software. McBride has strong CS credentials from UIUC with an early patent, which is impressive. But neither has documented experience in the specific domain they're targeting -- computational biology tools, research microscopy workflows, bioimage informatics. And the dossier shows "no shared employer or university overlap" between them. I care about how co-founders found each other. My best returns come from people I've watched through multiple ventures together -- Kortina and Magdon-Ismail had years of shared history before Venmo. Synthetic co-founder pairings from networking or incubator matching correlate with synthetic commitment. This isn't a dealbreaker, but combined with the domain experience gap, it's a concern.

The primitive test seals it for me. This is not a new category. It's not a new financial instrument, a new protocol layer, a new behavioral pattern. It's a faster version of something researchers already do with existing tools. "Better CellProfiler with AI" is an application, not a primitive. When I backed Venmo, the primitive was peer-to-peer mobile payments -- a behavior that literally didn't exist. When I backed Solana, the primitive was a new consensus mechanism. Alchemy is optimizing an existing workflow with current-generation models that are getting cheaper and more capable every quarter, which means the window for any single tool to capture durable value is shrinking, not expanding. The models improve, the moat melts. Two-person team keeping burn low is fine, but capital discipline doesn't matter when the fundamental thesis doesn't work. Pass.
