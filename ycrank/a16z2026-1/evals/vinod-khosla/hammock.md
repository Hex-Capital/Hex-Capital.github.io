# Hammock -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with what jumps off the page: Jesse Rose built Berry Street to $50M ARR in 2.5 years, partnered with 1,250+ insurance plans, and raised $50M through Series B. Then he left to build Hammock. That's a strong founder signal — the kind of trajectory that shows someone who can learn fast and execute in the brutal intersection of healthcare and payments. Will Dennis ran a $1B ARR consumer subscription at WeightWatchers and shipped product at WhatsApp to 2 billion users. On founder pedigree alone, this team is in the top decile of anything coming out of a16z Speedrun. I take that seriously.

But pedigree is not consequence. My first question is always: if Hammock fully succeeds, does the world visibly change? The answer here is no. Hammock helps 40 million HSA holders — predominantly affluent Americans already buying Equinox memberships, Eight Sleep mattresses, Oura rings, and AG1 supplements — capture a tax deduction they were already entitled to. The website claims $4,000/year in eligible expenses found per user. At a 30% marginal tax rate, that's roughly $1,200 in annual savings. That's a nice perk for people who can already afford $300/month gym memberships and $2,000 sleep technology. Nobody's life fundamentally transforms. This is the redistribution of existing spend I explicitly filter against — competing for share of an existing tax benefit, not creating new demand or collapsing a cost structure. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success means feeding billions without animal agriculture. Hammock's success means wealthier Americans save $100/month on taxes. The magnitude difference is not close.

The technical approach reinforces my concern. Hammock's core is an AI agent that scans purchase history for HSA-eligible transactions and routes users to a telehealth network for LMN generation. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The AI purchase-scanning capability uses transaction categorization that is technically accessible to any fintech with card data." This is existing LLM APIs applied to transaction categorization and form generation — a wrapper, not a wedge. There is no proprietary science, no novel architecture, no step-function improvement in cost or capability. Truemed raised $34M from a16z to build the merchant-side version of essentially the same value chain. Flex raised $18M as "Stripe for HSA/FSA." The fact that Hammock approaches from the consumer side rather than the merchant side is a go-to-market differentiation, not a technology discontinuity. I want to make 50 times my money or lose it all. A company whose primary innovation is bundling an HSA card with automated paperwork is designed to generate 3-5x returns in a competitive fintech category, which in my framework is a waste of attention.

The strongest bull case: Jesse Rose's Berry Street experience gives him institutional knowledge of provider networks, insurance eligibility, and LMN workflows that would take a naive founder years to acquire. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act created genuine regulatory tailwinds — gym memberships became HSA-eligible in January 2026, and 3-4 million newly eligible Americans entered the market. If Hammock captures the custodial HSA relationship, switching costs lock in users and the platform can expand into full healthcare finance: investments on balances, Rx discounts, expense consolidation across medical and wellness. That custodial relationship is real — Fidelity and HealthEquity built multi-billion-dollar businesses on HSA custody. But even this bull case describes a successful fintech company. A better Fidelity HSA for millennials who do CrossFit. Important to someone, but not to me.

I checked my Known Exceptions. The DoorDash override doesn't apply — DoorDash's consequence magnitude was transforming food access for hundreds of millions, which justified overriding weak technical novelty. Hammock's consequence is tax optimization for a subset of 40 million HSA holders. The Oscar Health parallel is superficial — Oscar was reinventing health insurance delivery from scratch, accepting regulatory risk because the affordability thesis for millions of uninsured Americans was powerful enough. Hammock isn't making healthcare accessible to anyone new. It's making Equinox tax-deductible. The regulatory risk here — IRS scrutiny of rubber-stamped LMNs, state-by-state telehealth compliance — is real and not offset by a transformative affordability endgame. Rose and Dennis are strong operators building a competent business in a genuine market, but the consequence of success doesn't justify my kind of bet. I'd rather lose my money on something where winning changes everything.
