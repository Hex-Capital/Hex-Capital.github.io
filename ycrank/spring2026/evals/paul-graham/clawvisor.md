# Clawvisor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for $70M in 2023, then became a YC Visiting Group Partner, and now he is back through the YC door as the sole founder of **Clawvisor** -- which means the dimensional signature here is the most domain-fluent founder I have read in this batch coupled with the configuration I most consistently avoid. **Clawvisor** is a solo bet by a Trust & Safety lifer attacking a problem adjacent to his last company, which is the "Dropbox-grade organic idea, Loopt-grade team configuration" pairing, and that is a real tension, not a rounding error.

The highest rung on the organic-idea ladder is Drew Houston's USB stick at **Dropbox** (2007) -- the founder hit the problem in his own workflow before anyone gave him permission to solve it. **Clawvisor** is not quite that, but it is close: Levine ran Trust & Safety engineering at Airbnb from 2012 to 2017, then spent five years building Berbix doing identity verification for the same buyer persona, and he is now building an authorization gateway for AI agents touching Gmail, Slack, and Stripe. The dossier evidence that pins **Clawvisor** below the Houston rung is that the agent-rogue-credential problem is one Levine *would* hit working with agents, not one the dossier shows he hit and then could not unhit -- there is no founder anecdote of an agent leaking his own Slack token. Still, the distance from biography to product is shorter for **Clawvisor** than for any cold-start founder in this cohort.

The lowest rung on the team-configuration ladder is the MBA pair from my own Pass Example -- the failure mechanism there is not the credentials, it is that there is nobody in the room who will stay up all night to finish the code. **Clawvisor** does not have that exact failure mode -- Levine himself is the engineer, his GitHub is live, the Go codebase ships -- but it does have the related one I called out in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010): a solo founder has no one to argue with at 2am, and Composio has 200+ paying customers and 25+ engineers while **Clawvisor** has one. Berbix had a cofounder. **Clawvisor** does not. That is not a neutral fact; that is the dossier omission I weight most.

On the schlep dimension, the mechanism that compounds is adapter sprawl: **Clawvisor** ships 14+ pre-built integrations to Gmail, Slack, Notion, Linear, GitHub, and Stripe, and each one is a maintenance grind as those upstream APIs mutate. Over 18 months, that adapter library is either a moat (because Composio is busy chasing 500 integrations broadly and nobody else is doing per-request purpose verification deeply) or a treadmill (because Google ships native agent permissions and the gateway middleware compresses). The **Coinbase** (2012) comparison is imperfect -- Levine is not staring down the SEC -- but the per-adapter security review work is the kind of schlep mainstream YC AI-wrapper founders are actively routing around, and that is the **Clawvisor** moat candidate worth naming.

The counterfactual for the hacker-genealogy score: **Clawvisor** would clear the high rung if Levine's GitHub showed a recognized open-source project pre-Berbix or a competitive-programming trail, the way Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize preceded **Stripe** (2010). What the dossier actually shows is an ex-Google engineer who shipped Berbix and is shipping a Go gateway with 202 stars and 27 forks -- solid hacker, not famous hacker. The stars-to-fork ratio (roughly 7.5x) is healthy for an OSS-distributed gateway at this stage, but it is not the Hacker News flip-the-room moment that **Dropbox** (2007) had.

The single-anchor parallel for animal resourcefulness is Brian Armstrong rebuilding the Android Bitcoin wallet after I had already rejected **Coinbase** (2012). **Clawvisor** has no such post-rejection stunt in the dossier -- what it has is a $70M exit, a YC partnership tour, and 202 GitHub stars on a working open-source artifact. Those are credentials and shipped code, which is better than the MBA pass case but is not the cereal-box improvisation that flipped **Airbnb** (2009). The honest read is that Levine has not needed to be resourceful in the **Clawvisor** chapter yet because the doors that were locked for Chesky and Armstrong opened for him on the strength of Berbix. That is genuinely useful, and it is also genuinely not the same signal.

Forecast, 36 months: **Clawvisor** reaches $1-3M ARR by mid-2028 and gets acquired by Okta, Cloudflare, or 1Password in the $40-90M range as the agent-auth feature inside a broader identity stack -- a near carbon-copy of Berbix to Socure, driven by the same mechanism (credentialed founder, real product, narrow wedge, capital-rich incumbents who would rather buy than build). The signature -- deep organic idea + solo configuration -- predicts feature-exit, not a defining-company outcome, because the depth of domain context lets **Clawvisor** ship a real wedge but the solo team cannot outrun Composio's $29M and 200 customers into platform scale.

The bear case I have to take seriously runs through my own Known Exceptions: I funded Sam Altman at **Loopt** (2005) when the team and tech were both ahead of the market, on force-of-personality grounds, and Levine as a YC Visiting Group Partner is exactly the alumni-vouch signal my Unstated Patterns flag as near-deterministic -- prior YC alumni vouching for prior YC alumni is how Kan got the **Twitch** (2007) re-fund. The bull would say **Clawvisor** is Berbix-Levine with a Loopt-style inside track and a Coinbase-shaped schlep moat, and at pre-seed that is enough. The dossier evidence that dismantles it: Loopt did not return capital, and the lesson I keep re-extracting from **Airbnb** (2009) is that animals work in pairs or trios -- Chesky-Gebbia-Blecharczyk, not Chesky alone. The condition that would flip my verdict to Invest is **Clawvisor** adding a technical cofounder of Levine's caliber in the next two batches; absent that, this is a Neutral that I would expect to read about in TechCrunch as an acquisition headline, not as a Stripe-shaped one.
