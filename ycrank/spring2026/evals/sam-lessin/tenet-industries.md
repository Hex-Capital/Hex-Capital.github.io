# Tenet Industries -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Helsing has raised €1.37 billion. Stark Defence has Peter Thiel, Sequoia, In-Q-Tel, and the NATO Innovation Fund behind ~$100M. White Stork has Eric Schmidt. Germany just handed out €300M-per-vendor drone brigade contracts to these exact players. Defense tech drones in Europe in 2026 is not a contrarian bet — it's the single hottest consensus category in European venture capital. Tenet Industries is a four-person pre-seed team in Stockholm trying to compete in a space where the smartest, most connected defense investors on the planet have already placed enormous bets and the pricing reflects it. When I look at this deal, my first question isn't "are expendable drones important?" — obviously they are, Ukraine proved that. My question is: is the market wrong about the *price* of this opportunity? And the answer is clearly no. The upside is already in the price. Every sophisticated LP and GP in Europe is chasing defense tech returns right now. That's the opposite of where I want to be.

The bull case an advocate would make goes like this: Helsing and Stark are building AI-first autonomy platforms at premium price points. Tenet Industries is doing something different — engineering drones around the factory, not the reverse. Emil Falk actually built and deployed automated manufacturing machines at Ragn-Sells facilities through WindowGlass Recycling, which won Venture Cup Startup of the Year in 2023. The "design-for-automation" platform thesis means Tenet Industries isn't competing on per-unit drone capability but on production velocity and cost across multiple airframe categories. Ukraine consumes thousands of drones monthly per MIT Technology Review — that's a volume problem, not a sophistication problem, and maybe nobody is optimizing the factory layer the way these guys want to. Sweden's $440M unmanned combat procurement budget with 2026-2028 delivery timelines is a captive home market. It's a credible argument. Falk's manufacturing deployment track record is the most interesting signal in the dossier. If I'd watched him through two or three ventures the way I watched Raj Dittmar before writing the Solana check, I'd pay more attention.

But I haven't. The co-founder relationship here hits one of my red flags directly — no shared prior employer or educational institution was identified across Hugo Frisk, Fabian Andersson, and Emil Falk. My best returns come from founders with years of shared history under pressure, not teams assembled for a YC application. And the sanity-insanity calibration is off in the wrong direction. Building cheap drones for NATO is the most *logical*, consensus-validated idea in European defense. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's fucking crazy" — they think "obviously, where do I sign?" When the idea is that sensible, you need extraordinary founder signal to compensate, and I just don't have it. Hugo Frisk is a self-taught electronics designer with no public track record. Andersson has essentially no retrievable professional history. Falk's manufacturing credentials are real but in glass recycling, not weapons systems.

Tenet Industries does score well on structural durability — I'll give it that. Physical drone manufacturing is the kind of business where software amplifies but cannot replace the core operation. This is the Metropolis pattern: AI-powered parking management that I seeded early because no LLM can replicate physical parking infrastructure. Nobody's going to prompt ChatGPT into producing kamikaze quadcopters. The factory floor, the supply chain, the physical assembly and testing — that's a genuine moat against AI commoditization. But Metropolis was in a category other investors found *boring*. Parking! Nobody wanted to touch it, which meant the price was wrong. Tenet Industries is in a category every investor finds *thrilling*, which means the price already reflects everything good about it.

The regulatory dependency red flag is also flashing hard. Tenet Industries literally cannot generate revenue without government defense procurement contracts. Export controls under the Wassenaar Arrangement, Sweden's ISP military equipment licensing, NATO qualification processes — this is a business whose unit economics are entirely dependent on favorable regulatory outcomes and multi-year government purchasing decisions. I've said publicly I don't think cities will let scooter companies be profitable, and that same structural skepticism applies here: defense ministries can change vendors, delay procurement, or impose requirements that destroy a pre-seed startup's economics overnight. The €300M German drone contracts went to Helsing, Stark, and Rheinmetall — all established. Capital discipline is also a concern: competing in defense hardware against companies with a thousand times your funding is not a "hire when it breaks" business. Tenet Industries will need to raise aggressively and repeatedly to build production capacity, which is exactly the factory-farm fundraising model I think is dying.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Tenet Industries secured a paid evaluation contract from the Swedish Armed Forces or any NATO defense ministry before their next raise, proving that the "design-for-automation" cost advantage is real enough that a procurement officer chose them over Helsing and Stark on unit economics. That would be actual evidence of mispricing — the market says the well-funded AI players win, but the buyer chose the cheap-factory approach instead. Without that, Tenet Industries is a reasonable team with a reasonable idea in a consensus-hot category, priced accordingly. The decisive dimensions are Consensus Mispricing — defense drones in 2026 Europe is as consensus as it gets — and Sanity-Insanity Calibration, where the idea is too logical and the founder signal too thin to compensate.
