# Palus Finance -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most structurally interesting thing about Palus is the innovator's dilemma they've correctly identified: Mercury, Brex, and Rho can't easily offer a bond-portfolio treasury product because it conflicts with their same-day-liquidity banking UX and cannibalizes their MMF fee-sharing revenue. That's a real structural observation, and I give the founders credit for seeing it clearly. But identifying a genuine innovator's dilemma and building a venture-scale business on it are two entirely different things. The dilemma creates a temporary gap in the market — it doesn't create a durable moat for whoever fills that gap. Mercury has $650M in annualized revenue and 200K+ business customers. If Palus proves the bond-portfolio treasury model works, Mercury builds a "Premium Treasury" tier in a quarter and absorbs the insight. The dilemma buys you time, not defensibility.

The sane-person-insane-idea filter fails on the idea side. "Better yields on idle startup cash" is one of the most consensus fintech pitches in existence — Mercury, Brex, Arc, Rho, and Meow are all already serving this exact customer need. The tactical differentiation (agency MBS instead of MMFs, 1-2 day settlement in exchange for ~1% yield premium) is clever financial engineering, but it's an execution variant on a thoroughly validated market, not a non-consensus thesis. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks it's crazy. They think "oh, another treasury product." Sam Lushtak's experience building investment research platforms for $3T+ AUM asset managers gives him relevant exposure to institutional fixed-income — he's seen how the big players optimize treasury. That's useful context. But "consultant who built tools for asset managers" is a different caliber of domain credibility than, say, someone who ran a treasury desk or built settlement infrastructure. The sane-person credential is moderate; the insane-idea component is essentially absent.

My framework asks: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? The answer is clearly weaker. Palus's entire product is a software layer — Plaid integration, Alpaca custody, portfolio management logic connecting to Regan Capital's MBSF ETF. There's no physical infrastructure, no operational complexity that software can't replicate, no protocol-layer lock-in. The theoretical future data advantage on startup cash-flow patterns might eventually enable superior duration-matching, but at pre-seed with a handful of YC batch customers, that's a story, not an asset. Agency MBS are a well-understood, decades-old asset class. The strategy is literally being executed through someone else's ETF. This is the opposite of my Craftwork thesis — where a painting company's operational moat in managing crews, scheduling, and quality control gets amplified by software but can't be replaced by it. Palus IS the software layer, and that's the category I've been arguing is dissolving.

The narrative ceiling concerns me deeply. At 0.25% annual fee on AUM, the unit economics require enormous scale to produce venture returns. A billion dollars in AUM — which would be extraordinary for a startup-focused treasury product — generates $2.5M in annual revenue. To reach $100M revenue, you'd need $40B AUM, which means expanding far beyond startup treasury into SMB and corporate treasury, where you're competing against Goldman Sachs and JP Morgan's institutional sales teams. The infinity story isn't there. "We help startups earn slightly better yields" doesn't expand into an economic category — it remains a niche optimization. Compare this to Venmo, where the initial "send money to your friends" primitive expanded into a payment infrastructure layer that reshaped how an entire generation moves money. Palus is repackaging an existing institutional strategy for a different customer segment. That's a distribution innovation, not a new primitive.

The strongest bull case: declining Fed rates are compressing MMF yields, making the agency MBS spread proportionally more attractive right now. The YC distribution channel gives them warm-start deal flow that a random fintech couldn't access. If they accumulate $100M+ AUM quickly, the switching friction from invested bond positions creates moderate retention. And if they build proprietary analytics on startup burn patterns, they could eventually offer cash-flow intelligence that transcends simple yield optimization — becoming the "treasury brain" for venture-backed companies. For this to work, they'd need to rapidly expand beyond yield into cash forecasting, runway optimization, and financial planning, turning treasury management into a broader financial operating system for startups. But that's a different company than the one described in this dossier, and that adjacent company already has well-funded competitors too.

This is a competent team solving a real but consensus problem with a replicable strategy and no structural moat I can get excited about. The subscription-style fee-on-AUM model, the software-only value chain, the application-layer positioning on top of existing financial infrastructure — these are exactly the characteristics I've spent the last two years arguing produce diminishing returns in a world where AI makes software cheaper by the month. Pass.
