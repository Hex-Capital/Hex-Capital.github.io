# Pax Historia -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking fact in this dossier is that a fan-made clone already exists on GitHub, built by a single developer using Google's Gemini API. That fact tells you almost everything you need to know about the nature of the "technology" here. When your core product can be architecturally reproduced by one person with an API key and a weekend, you do not have proprietary technology. You have a wrapper. The history of computing is littered with wrappers that achieved early traction and then got absorbed, outflanked, or commoditized by the platforms they depended on. Pax Historia processes 100 billion tokens per week through third-party models it does not own, cannot modify at the weights level, and whose pricing it does not control. This is not a technology company building from zero to one. This is a distribution play built on rented infrastructure.

What is the secret here? The implicit thesis appears to be: "LLMs can replace hand-crafted game design, creating infinitely emergent gameplay that makes scripted content obsolete." This is not a secret -- it is perhaps the single most consensus belief in Silicon Valley right now. Every gaming studio, every AI lab, every venture-backed startup in the generative AI space is exploring this exact thesis. When I invested in Facebook, the insight that real-identity networking would create a natural monopoly was something most smart people actively rejected. When we built Palantir, the idea that PayPal's fraud algorithms could transform intelligence analysis was dismissed by the entire intelligence community. A secret must be a truth that informed people disagree with. "AI will transform gaming" is not a secret -- it is a TikTok trend. The specific application to grand strategy narrows the thesis, but narrowness and contrarianism are not the same thing. Any product manager at Paradox Interactive has had this conversation in the last eighteen months.

The traction is undeniably impressive: 35,000 daily active users in four months, built organically by three people, with a community generating thousands of presets and millions of rounds. I do not dismiss this. But traction without structural defensibility is a trap. Groupon reached 50 million users faster than almost any company in history and had no moat. The question is not whether people enjoy this product -- clearly they do -- but whether that enjoyment translates into a monopoly position that compounds over decades. The UGC marketplace with 4,000+ presets is the strongest moat signal here, and I take it seriously: user-generated content creates a flywheel where each new scenario attracts players who create more scenarios. This is the Roblox analogy the founders likely make. But it breaks down on inspection. Roblox built its own engine, its own scripting language, its own economy, its own identity system. A Roblox game cannot be trivially ported to another platform because the creative tooling is proprietary. A Pax Historia preset is a text configuration layered over a third-party LLM -- it can be reproduced, forked, or migrated. The content library creates mild switching costs, not structural lock-in.

The founders demonstrate real execution ability -- three people building to 35K DAU organically is not nothing. But I see no evidence of the kind of definite conviction I require. Ryan Zhang's YC bio reads "I like history." Eli Bullock-Papa's background is Virginia Tech engineering and Capital One. These are not founders who have articulated a specific vision of what the world looks like in ten years if they succeed, or who have refused easier paths in pursuit of that vision. The VGTimes review documents fundamental gameplay issues -- AI ignoring player orders, lack of mechanical depth, pacing problems from inference latency -- and describes the optimal play strategy as running ChatGPT in a separate window alongside the game. When your product's best use case involves simultaneously using a competitor's product, you have not achieved 10x superiority. You have achieved an interesting demo.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If inference costs continue their precipitous decline -- and the trend line suggests they will -- the unit economics improve automatically without any company action. If the UGC library compounds to 40,000 or 400,000 presets, cold-starting a competitor becomes meaningfully harder. If the community identity deepens through the wiki, Discord, and subreddit, you get the kind of cultural lock-in that kept World of Warcraft dominant for a decade. And if Paradox and Firaxis are structurally unable to pivot to AI-first gameplay because it would cannibalize their $60-per-title DLC model, Pax Historia could own the entire AI grand strategy category by default. This is a real scenario. The problem is that it requires multiple contingencies to compound simultaneously, and the most critical variable -- whether LLM-generated gameplay can actually match the depth and satisfaction of hand-crafted game design -- remains unresolved. The VGTimes review suggests it cannot yet, and "not yet" in gaming means players leave before "eventually" arrives.

I am passing. The company has execution talent and genuine product-market resonance, but it lacks every element I prioritize: there is no contrarian secret, no proprietary technology, no structural path to monopoly that does not depend on variables outside the founders' control, and no founder signal suggesting the kind of civilizational ambition or intellectual depth that distinguishes the companies I back. This is a well-executed application of consensus technology to a specific genre of entertainment. It may become a good business. But "a good business" fails the power-law test -- even in the optimistic scenario, this does not return a fund.
