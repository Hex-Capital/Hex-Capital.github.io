# Confluence Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most interesting fact about Confluence Labs is not their 97.9% score on ARC-AGI-2 -- it is what happened immediately after. They open-sourced the solver under an MIT license. Consider the logic: a two-person team achieves state-of-the-art on the most discussed reasoning benchmark in AI, outperforming systems backed by orders of magnitude more capital, and then gives the implementation away for free. Either the founders believe the real value lies somewhere far beyond the benchmark -- in which case they haven't yet demonstrated that value -- or they are following the consensus playbook of building in public, which is fine for developer tools but structurally incompatible with monopoly. When Palantir built its intelligence platform on PayPal's fraud algorithms, the last thing we did was open-source the core technology. Defensibility requires keeping secrets, not publishing them.

The question I always ask first: what is the secret here? The implicit thesis is that learning efficiency will matter more than scale in data-sparse domains -- that drug discovery, materials science, and physics research need AI systems that extract signal from dozens of examples, not millions. But this is not a secret. It is a widely shared observation. Every AI-for-science company -- Isomorphic Labs, Recursion, Insilico Medicine -- understands that experimental data is expensive and scarce. The consensus already agrees that sample efficiency matters in these domains. A genuine secret would be a specific insight about *how* to achieve that efficiency in a way that creates structural advantage -- a particular architecture, a particular domain transfer that no one else sees. What Confluence offers instead is a general approach: LLMs plus program synthesis plus consensus voting. This is a technique, not a secret. And techniques, once demonstrated, get replicated. Imbue reached 95.1% on the same benchmark within days. The ARC-AGI-2 leaderboard moved from 54% to near-ceiling in three months. What looks like a breakthrough in February is table stakes by summer.

The bull case deserves articulation, because the raw talent signal is not negligible. Niranjan Baskaran was selected for the Research Science Institute at MIT -- 30 students worldwide, genuinely elite -- and reportedly built a prior startup to $5M ARR. Brent Burdick is a self-taught engineer who built substantive AI tooling at Morph Labs. Paul Graham's personal investment is selective. Two founders with no institutional backing outperforming Imbue's 10,000-H100 cluster on a reasoning benchmark is a legitimate demonstration of intellectual horsepower. If -- and this is the critical conditional -- the program synthesis approach transfers cleanly from abstract grid puzzles to molecular design or materials optimization, and if that transfer creates proprietary domain-specific advantages that compound through customer data, then this could become a genuine 0-to-1 company in scientific AI. The Palantir analogy would be: start with a general capability (fraud detection / sample-efficient reasoning), apply it to a specific high-value domain (intelligence / drug discovery), and build switching costs through deep integration. For this to work, Confluence would need to pick one domain, dominate it entirely, and accumulate proprietary data that makes their models structurally superior to anything a general-purpose lab could build.

But the evidence suggests they are doing the opposite. Their stated plan targets hardware engineering, biology, materials science, and "related fields" simultaneously -- the classic mistake of going after a giant market on day one rather than monopolizing a specific niche. There is no beachhead strategy visible. No named customer, no LOI, no specific domain where they have ten times more insight than anyone else. The ARC-AGI-2 benchmark tests abstract pattern recognition on colored grids, which is structurally distant from the molecular, crystallographic, and physical systems they claim to target. The translation gap between "solve novel grid puzzles" and "design a drug candidate from sparse experimental data" is not a detail -- it is the entire unsolved problem. And the competitive landscape is a Girardian warning sign: multiple well-funded players ($232M for Imbue, $600M for Isomorphic Labs, public-company resources at Recursion) pursuing overlapping theses in AI-for-science. When everyone agrees the market is important and the approach is sound, there is no secret left to exploit.

The founders are young and technically capable, but I see no evidence of the definite conviction that separates great founders from good ones. What does the world look like in ten years if Confluence Labs succeeds? What specific future are they building toward, and what would they refuse to compromise to get there? The dossier reveals a benchmark, an open-source repository, and a general ambition to help scientists -- but not a singular vision that could anchor a company for decades. The approach reads more like lean-startup exploration ("developing partnerships with researchers") than definite optimism. Compare this with Zuckerberg's refusal of a billion-dollar offer at twenty-two, or Musk's continued rocket development after three consecutive failures. Conviction is revealed under pressure, and I see no evidence yet of pressure applied or resisted.

I pass. The technical talent is real, but talent without a defensible secret is just skill applied to a competitive market. The ARC-AGI-2 result is already eroding. The core technology is open-sourced. The target market is broad and contested. And the implicit thesis -- that sample efficiency matters in data-sparse domains -- is the definition of consensus in AI-for-science circles. A genuine secret would be a specific, non-obvious insight about which scientific domain this approach transforms first and why no one else can replicate it there. Until that secret emerges, this is a company without a monopoly path, and companies without monopoly paths compete, and competition destroys value.
