# Async -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Async is a services-shop chasing three regulated verticals with a two-person team that has pivoted its own thesis three times inside a single YC batch — nonprofit fundraising agents, then Skope outcome-based billing, then Skope for law firms, now Async for law, healthcare, and real estate. The signature is a company whose highest-scoring dimension is the mere adjacency to regulated verticals and whose lowest is definite planning — a company where the founder cannot name what he believes for more than a quarter at a time. That is a very specific archetype, and it is not one that has ever returned a fund.

The **Stripe** (2010) analogy is the one Async invites and cannot cash. Patrick Collison's contrarian secret was named at the architecture level: legacy processors (Braintree, Authorize.net, PayPal-the-merchant-product) could not ship a developer-first API because their organizations were structured around bank partnerships and enterprise sales, not seven-line integrations. Async's dossier does not name an equivalent failure mode. Harvey has $1.22B raised and a $11B post-money, Spellbook has $63M and already ships a Word add-in, Legora has $5.55B at valuation — Async's differentiation is described as a "partner & audit → scope & deploy" engagement across three verticals, which is not a secret about why Harvey structurally cannot serve Clifton Law PLLC. It is the observation that Harvey is expensive. That is a positioning note, not a thesis.

The pass example from Zero to One — "AI-powered platform for [vertical X]" with no incumbent architectural blind spot — maps to Async almost line-for-line, and the failure mechanism is what Girard would call mimetic descent: the founders started at nonprofit fundraising, drifted toward whatever the batch and the market rewarded, and landed at "AI agents for service businesses" because that is where the crowd is looking. Async's own website still uses @useskope.com emails and its legal entity remains Skope Technologies Inc., which is the artifact of a team that has not yet held a single thesis long enough to reincorporate around it. Three pivots in twelve months is not "iterating to product-market fit" — it is indefinite optimism as an operating system.

On monopoly trajectory, Async's mechanism analysis over 18 months breaks down at the moat step: SOC 2 Type I plus Zero Data Retention plus Word/Outlook/SharePoint integrations describe table stakes for any 2026 vertical-AI entrant, not compounding switching costs. Spellbook already ships the Word add-in. Harvey already ships the workflow integrations. Async's proposed moat — "company-level memory" and integration depth — is the moat every AI wrapper claims in month one and none defends in month eighteen once the foundation model vendor collapses the abstraction. There is no pricing-power hypothesis five years out because there is no structural reason Async captures more of the workflow than the next YC batch's identical entrant.

For founder lineage, the counterfactual is what would flip Async into the **Asana** (2010) exception: Dustin Moskovitz got the override on missing structural moat because he had shipped Facebook to scale. Ben Smith is a University of Michigan dropout with a prior role at Merit and a retail background selling shoes and trading cards; Connor Park is a U-Michigan CS graduate with 18 GitHub followers and 72 total stars. Neither has shipped a product to scale, neither carries PayPal-Mafia adjacency, neither carries a technical credential (Olympiad, PhD, senior IC role at a scaled company) that would justify a lineage override. Nothing here is disqualifying at pre-seed — it is simply not signal, and Async needed lineage to be a signal because the secret dimension is empty.

The single-anchor parallel on atoms-heavy moat is **Palantir** (2003) — regulated customers, integration-depth switching costs, trust as the moat. Async gestures at the same territory by listing healthcare and legal, but Karp and Cohen spent fifteen years earning intelligence-community trust before Palantir compounded; Async has one named customer, Clifton Law PLLC, a solo/boutique firm, and no HIPAA posture beyond SOC 2 Type I despite claiming healthcare as a vertical. Regulated-adjacent is not regulated-anchored. The dossier flags that "healthcare adds HIPAA scope beyond current SOC 2 Type I posture," which is precisely the certification depth that would take Async's two-person team eighteen months to build and would give a real moat if they committed to it. They have not.

My 36-month forecast: Async pivots a fourth time — narrows to legal-only under either the Skope or Async name — reaches $500K–$1.5M ARR as a services-heavy boutique, and either winds down or is acqui-hired by a mid-market legal-AI player (Spellbook is the most likely acquirer given Word-add-in overlap) at a talent-adjusted price under $15M. The mechanism producing this is the serial pivot itself: founders who cannot hold a thesis for twelve months do not suddenly hold one for thirty-six, and the compounding advantage in vertical AI belongs to whoever narrows first and stays narrow.

The strongest bear-inverted case is that Async's services-first wedge into SMB service firms is exactly the gap Harvey structurally ignores at $11B post-money — that Async could run the early Palantir Forward-Deployed playbook bottom-up and compound proprietary workflow data no incumbent will collect. This stings because it is precisely the "competition is for losers" move the incumbents have priced themselves out of. It fails on the dossier: Palantir had Karp, Cohen, Lonsdale, and a fifteen-year definite plan for a single customer archetype; Async has three pivots, one boutique customer testimonial, and a two-person team spread across three regulated verticals. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: three named multi-seat law firm deployments at above $50K ACV within six months, with the healthcare and real-estate verticals publicly dropped from the site. Absent that narrowing commitment, Async is the archetype Zero to One was written against.
