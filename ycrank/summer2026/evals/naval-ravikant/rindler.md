# Rindler -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Rindler is a picks-and-shovels middleware bet where the market has all the room and the architecture has none of the compounding — Michael Serrano and Arthur De Los Santos are shipping an MCP server for websites-without-APIs into a category (agent infrastructure) that is genuinely platform-shaped, but the product itself is single-sided middleware where every customer's schema library is economically independent of the next customer's, and that pairing is the whole signature. Rindler will live or die on how fast the two founders can convert MIT CSAIL specific knowledge into a wedge before Browserbase's $40M and Composio's $79.6M eat the surface.

The HIGH rung on my leverage ladder is **Twitter** (2007) — media-as-protocol, zero marginal cost, anyone posts without permission. Rindler shares the code-native surface: the MCP standard is Anthropic/OpenAI/Google-endorsed, agent-agnostic, and distributes through the same permissionless developer-plumbing channel that let **Stack Overflow** (2008) and **Replit** (April 2023) compound without asking anyone. Where Rindler diverges from the Twitter rung is that the *consumers* of the leverage are agents, not humans posting media, so the viral loop is missing — an agent calling `extract_content` doesn't recruit the next agent. Rindler has code leverage on the build side but no media leverage on the distribution side, which is why it sits closer to **Notion** (2013) than Twitter — real permissionless architecture, but the distribution flywheel has to be manually cranked one developer at a time.

**Perplexity** (2022) is the exception I already made once on the network-effects axis — I waived criterion #2 because Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley ML retrieval PhD cleared criterion #3 emphatically and the permissionless API-distribution cleared #1 emphatically. Rindler triggers the same failure mode — single-sided, each customer isolated — but does not clear the same override gates. Rindler's founders are freshly-graduated MIT MEng and MEng-candidate, not a specific-knowledge extremist like Aravind, and Rindler's site-schema library is not a foundation-model moat. The failure pattern I'm actually looking at is the **Pinterest** miss inverted: on Pinterest I passed *despite* the architecture passing; on Rindler the architecture is thinner and the network mechanics are absent, so the "I don't get it" tiebreaker does not need to fire — the math itself is tighter.

On specific knowledge, MIT CSAIL MEng focused on "infrastructure of the future web" plus CSAIL Advanced Undergraduate Research plus a Telexistence robotics internship is real domain immersion, but the dossier gives me *no shipped artifact* — no GitHub repos found for either Michael Serrano or Arthur De Los Santos, no open-source contributions, no pre-YC prototype in the wild. That absence is what separates Rindler from Amjad Masad's decade-long Repl.it obsession before **Replit** (April 2023) took the $1.16B round. MIT CSAIL is credential; a public repo of an MCP-native browser agent with organic stars would be evidence. Rindler has the former, not the latter.

Market and pivot-room is where Rindler scores its highest — mechanism: over 18 months the "MCP middleware for API-less sites" wedge compounds into three adjacent pivot vectors visible from today's dossier — agent-native RPA for enterprise portals, MCP gateway infrastructure (adjacent to MintMCP/TrueFoundry), and vertical agent-ops for regulated verticals (healthcare portals, insurance carriers, government sites). YC S26's "Software for Agents" thesis (Forbes, February 2026) is the same category-timing signal that made me accumulate into **Perplexity** (2022) and **Tako** (October 2024) pre-narrative. Rindler has the pivot vectors a niche SaaS play would not.

Founders read clean — MIT classmates, co-founded together after graduating 2025, no litigation history, no unprompted honesty-signaling. Counterfactual for the trio score to flip a tier upward: I would need to see either Michael Serrano or Arthur De Los Santos writing publicly at depth on browser-automation or MCP internals — the **Airchat** (April 2024) template where the founder's own media presence *is* part of the specific knowledge on display. Silence at the default is where they sit; neither red flag nor top-quartile.

Forecast, 36 months: Rindler is acqui-hired by **Browserbase** or **Composio** for $15–40M in an infrastructure roll-up before hitting $2M ARR — the site-maintenance treadmill the YC page explicitly promises ("robust maintenance when sites change") crushes two-person coverage economics before the MCP-middleware category consolidates around whichever browser-infrastructure player owns the auth-and-anti-bot layer. This forecast is derived from the signature: high market/pivot-room (survives, doesn't die) but low network compounding (no independent breakout gravity) means the exit is category consolidation, not category creation.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that MCP standardization in 2024–2025 is Rindler's Twitter-2007 moment on a new protocol — being the agent-agnostic middleware layer for the largest slice of the web (sites without APIs) at the moment agents become the dominant client is the exact pre-consensus category-timing bet I made on **MetaStable** (2014) and **Perplexity** (2022). Composio's 1,000+ toolkits are all *API-having* services, which structurally leaves Rindler's wedge open. The dismantle: Browserbase already ships Stagehand under MIT license, and the CFAA/ToS exposure around automating logins-plus-2FA-plus-bot-detection is exactly the "labor and capital require permission" architecture I avoid — Rindler's wedge is actually *permissioned* leverage disguised as MCP-native code. Anon raised $8M specifically because that legal surface is hard. The condition that flips me to invest: Rindler ships an open-source MCP-server repo with 3K+ GitHub stars and 5+ named design partners inside 6 months of the batch — evidence that the code-leverage architecture is real rather than SDK-fronted enterprise sales in disguise.
