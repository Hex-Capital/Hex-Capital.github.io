# August -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Eight hundred thousand dollars in ARR in three months, deployed at a $170B+ bank, with a three-person team out of Tel Aviv. That's a real number. I'm not going to pretend it's not. Most pre-seed companies are still arguing about their logo when August is booking enterprise banking contracts. Bar Ittah and Tom Tankilevitch are clearly operators who know how to sell — you don't close a top-100 bank in three months by being confused about your product. The Xperiti exit to Ipsos, Tom's engineering leadership at Port when it hit $800M valuation, the Iguazio acquisition by McKinsey — these are people who've been inside the machine and know what enterprise AI deployment actually requires. The execution signal is strong.

Here's my problem: every bank CEO on planet earth woke up in 2025 and said "I need AI in my operations." This is not a contrarian bet. This is the most consensus idea in fintech right now. The Accenture survey August's own positioning leans on says 91% of banking executives consider AI a strategic priority. Ninety-one percent! When 91% of your buyer universe is already convinced they need what you're selling, you are not in a mispriced category — you are in a bidding war for market share. a16z Speedrun is not some underground contrarian signal; it's the factory conveyor belt operating at peak efficiency. nCino is public doing $500M+ in revenue. Heron Finance just raised $16M from Insight Partners. Numerated got scooped up by Moody's. FundMore is growing at 1,504% three-year revenue growth. Everybody sees this opportunity. When everybody sees it, the risk isn't that August is wrong about the market — it's that the price already reflects the upside.

The primitive test is where August falls apart for me. "The platform where banking work runs" is a great tagline, but what August actually does is automate underwriting, servicing, and risk workflows using domain-specific AI. That's not a new primitive — that's a better version of what nCino has been doing on Salesforce for a decade, minus the Salesforce dependency. August isn't creating a new behavior that didn't exist before. Nobody's going to say "August me" the way they say "Venmo me." Compare this to Metropolis, which I seeded when AI-powered parking sounded insane. Metropolis works because even though the AI is the intelligence layer, the moat is physical parking infrastructure — you cannot replicate garages and lots with a language model. August has no physical moat, no protocol-level lock-in, no new financial instrument. The "proprietary context layer" is bank-specific operational data accumulated through deployment — which is real switching cost, I'll grant that — but it's the same kind of accumulated workflow data that FIS, Fiserv, and Temenos already have at orders of magnitude greater scale from decades of servicing these exact banks.

The AI kill test is brutal here. FIS does $14 billion in annual revenue. Fiserv does $19 billion. These companies are already inside every bank August is trying to sell to. They have the regulatory relationships, the compliance certifications, the data. When I say "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI, this is perfect for Adobe" — well, every time you get pitched banking AI, this is perfect for FIS. The 23% adoption stat from Accenture — only 23% of banks have moved AI beyond pilot programs — buys August time, but it doesn't build a moat. It just means the incumbents are moving slowly, which is what incumbents always do until they don't. And Bar and Tom don't have the kind of insane ambition that breaks my framework open. AI for bank operations is exactly what two sane, experienced Unit 8200 engineers would build. Sane people, sane idea. That's a good company. It's just not my kind of bet.

The bull case I have to address: what if August's "autonomous bank" positioning is genuinely different from workflow automation? What if the proprietary context layer creates compounding intelligence that makes August ten times more valuable to a bank every year it's deployed, such that switching becomes architecturally impossible? What if the regulatory complexity of banking — the reason only 23% have deployed AI in production — creates such a deep moat around domain expertise that generalists and incumbents can't close the gap? And what if that $800K ARR in three months is the first data point on a curve that hits $10M+ within a year, proving that banks will pay aggressively for this? If all of that is true, August could become the infrastructure layer for modern banking operations. But I'd need to see evidence that the product creates genuinely new banking behaviors — not just faster versions of existing ones — and I'd need to see the second and third bank deployments proving this isn't a single-customer story. Right now, one $170B+ bank and a "growing pipeline" doesn't clear that bar.

Capital discipline is the one dimension where August genuinely shines by my standards. Three people, $800K ARR, three months. That ratio is exceptional. If Bar Ittah told me "this is the last money we'll ever raise," I'd believe the math works for at least another 12-18 months. That deserves real credit and is the strongest signal in the entire dossier — founders who generate revenue before they generate narrative are founders who understand that capital is a tool, not validation.

The dimensions that lock my verdict are Consensus Mispricing and Primitive Creation. August is a well-executed AI application company in the single hottest enterprise AI vertical of 2026, priced and distributed through consensus channels, building a better version of existing banking tools rather than creating a new category. Bar Ittah's Xperiti exit and Tom Tankilevitch's Port and Iguazio track record make them credible operators, but credible operators in a consensus market are someone else's bet, not mine. The capital efficiency is genuinely impressive, but efficiency doesn't overcome the structural problem that AI banking software is exactly the kind of thing incumbents will build or buy. Pass.
