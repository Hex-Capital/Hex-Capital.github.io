# Instance -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Lucy Cai and Claire Mao met in middle school, both went to MIT, and pivoted Instance from "AI data analyst for databases" in January 2026 to "physics-aware quality layer for synthetic video" by the Summer 2026 YC batch listing — and that pivot, six months wide, is the signature. Instance's highest dimension is a thin one: Lucy Cai's MIT Lincoln Laboratory work on ML-driven 2D-to-3D point cloud generation is genuinely adjacent to the world-model evaluation problem. Its lowest dimension is the definite-plan axis, because the company was something else two quarters ago. What I am looking at is a credentialed two-person team renting a thesis from the most recent arxiv wave, not a company built around a secret the founders carried into the room.

**Stripe** (2010) is the right rung to test Instance against on the contrarian-secret axis, because Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent failure mode — PayPal's developer experience — that the consensus had declared a closed category. Instance's claimed secret is that visual-only evaluators miss physics violations in synthetic video. The problem is that this is not a secret. T2VPhysBench (May 2025), WorldModelBench (NeurIPS 2025), VideoPhy-2 (Mar 2025), and LikePhys (Oct 2025) have already published this thesis openly on arxiv, four times, before Instance pivoted into it. The named incumbent failure mode that would make Instance load-bearing — that Encord and Meta-owned Scale AI *structurally cannot* ship a physics-violation module — is absent from the dossier. Instance is downstream of the secret, not upstream of it; the founders read the same papers everyone else read.

Cleantech 1.0 is the low rung on monopoly trajectory, and it failed for a reason Instance is replicating in miniature: the moat hypothesis terminated at "better technology" in a market where every incumbent had the workforce and capital to replicate the breakthrough within 18 months. Encord raised $60M Series C in 2025 and already markets to Toyota Woven and Skydio — physical-AI customers Instance would need to win. Surge AI is raising at >$15B with a profitable expert-annotator network. Scale AI sits inside Meta. Instance's pricing power hypothesis, were it stated, would have to survive any of those three deciding the physics-violation module is a feature rather than a category. The dossier offers no switching cost, no certification depth, no integration moat — only domain narrowness, which is a feature pitch, not a monopoly pitch.

On definite plan: a pivot from text-to-SQL to synthetic-video physics QA inside six months is the operational definition of indefinite optimism. The Jan 2026 LinkedIn posts describing Instance as an "AI data analyst for databases" and the Summer 2026 YC page describing a physics-aware video quality layer are the same company under two theses, and the Zero to One framework reads that as iteration-mode capital allocation, not a multi-year commitment. **SpaceX** had a fifteen-year reusability roadmap before the first Falcon 1 launched. Instance has a landing page.

Counterfactual on founder lineage: for Instance to clear the Asana exception — where Moskovitz's Facebook-shipping pedigree overrode missing structural moat — Lucy Cai or Claire Mao would need a shipped-to-scale prior outcome. SpaceX and AWS internships and an MIT Lincoln Lab spotlight are credentialed-outsider hints, not operator pedigree. The Asana override required someone who had built Facebook; the Quora override required Adam D'Angelo, who had been Facebook's CTO. Instance has neither. The lineage score lifts modestly above the first-time-founder floor because of the MIT Lincoln Lab vision-pipeline work and the SpaceX exposure, not because either founder has shipped anything to a customer at scale.

The atoms-heavy axis is the one where I'd most want to be wrong on Instance. The customer set — NVIDIA Cosmos teams, Genie/Veo class labs, robotics foundation-model startups — is atoms-adjacent, and the Founders Fund manifesto frames robotics-data infrastructure as exactly the sector where 140-character VC neglect has created openings. But Instance itself is pure software sitting one layer away from the atoms, the regulated customer is absent, and the structural barrier is a labeled-dataset moat that has not been built. Modern Meadow was a check because the atoms were inside the company; Instance is a check on the atoms being inside the customer.

Forecast, 36 months: Instance is either acqui-hired by Encord or Surge AI for under $15M by end of 2028, or pivots a second time into adjacent robotics-eval tooling and quietly winds down. The mechanism is straightforward — the academic benchmark commoditization removes the secret, the labeler incumbents remove the monopoly trajectory, and the pivot history removes the definite-plan signal. Power-law math does not work on a company that needs three things to break right when one of them is already openly published on arxiv.

The bull case worth steelmanning is the Quora exception: D'Angelo violated monopoly purism and definite-plan purism, and the override was a bet on knowledge-graph network effects compounding at scale through proprietary data flywheel. If Instance accumulates the canonical labeled-violations dataset before Encord or Scale ships a module — and if world-model labs anchor on Instance's benchmark the way ML anchored on ImageNet — the proprietary-benchmark-as-standard play could compound. The dossier evidence that defeats this: there are no disclosed customers, no labeling volume, no benchmark publication, and four academic groups have a head start with open data. The condition that would flip my verdict is a signed contract with NVIDIA Cosmos, the Genie team, or a Tier-1 robotics foundation-model lab — a named counterparty paying for Instance's benchmark as the gating evaluator — disclosed within the next twelve months. Absent that, this is the consensus AI-tooling shape Zero to One was written against. Pass.
