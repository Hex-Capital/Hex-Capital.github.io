# Origin -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment for AI-designed regulatory DNA is real — and the layer Origin occupies in the gene therapy stack is exactly the kind of narrow, unglamorous infrastructure that tends to become essential plumbing for an entire industry. ENCODE V4 unlocked the training data. Transformer architectures crossed the capability threshold for multi-task DNA generation and prediction in 2024-2025. FDA approved a cluster of gene therapies in 2023-2025, driving demand for safer expression control. AlphaGenome and DNA-Diffusion validated feasibility and created a benchmark. This convergence means the research inflection is genuine — designing regulatory elements computationally is now possible in a way it wasn't two years ago. And "AI-designed regulatory DNA elements" is a market that most investors would dismiss as incomprehensibly niche, which is usually where I lean in. This has the shape of the "boring but critical infrastructure" pattern I've found productive — PagerDuty for gene expression control, in a sense.

But here's where Origin runs into my single-miracle framework, and it's where my conviction breaks down. The company needs at least three sequential breakthroughs to reach meaningful commercialization: first, their AI-generated sequences must actually translate from computational benchmarks to in vivo function (beating AlphaGenome by 6.7% on binding prediction is a very different claim than "our designed promoters drive cell-type-specific expression safely in living tissue"); second, they need to build wet-lab validation infrastructure at scale — they describe assembling "the largest proprietary dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences across diverse cell-states," which is an enormous operational challenge for a 4-person team; third, they need pharma companies to adopt AI-designed regulatory elements in their therapeutic pipelines, which requires navigating procurement cycles, regulatory validation, and trust-building with organizations that move slowly by design. Each of these has meaningfully sub-100% probability, and they compound. When I co-founded Color Health, the core miracle was driving genetic testing costs down through lab automation — everything else was execution against a proven biological paradigm. Origin's core miracle — that computational predictions of regulatory DNA function translate reliably to in vivo gene expression — is still an open scientific question, not an engineering problem.

On product-to-distribution, I can see the structural path: if Origin's AI-designed regulatory elements become validated and trusted, the company becomes the design layer that sits upstream of every gene therapy program. That's a distribution channel for expression profiling, safety prediction, and cell-type targeting services. The free API for research institutions is a reasonable top-of-funnel strategy — build adoption in academic labs, generate validation data, then convert to commercial licensing with pharma. But I don't see evidence that the founders have articulated this multi-product trajectory. Right now, Axis is a single model solving one problem. The platform potential exists in the market structure but not yet in the company's demonstrated strategic thinking.

The bull case is worth articulating clearly, because it's compelling if the science works. Cell and gene therapy is a $13B+ market growing at 18-24% CAGR toward $100B+. Every gene therapy needs regulatory elements — they're not optional components but fundamental to safety and efficacy. If Origin establishes that AI-designed regulatory elements are demonstrably superior to rational design (higher specificity, fewer off-target effects, faster iteration), they occupy a chokepoint that every therapeutic developer must pass through. The proprietary wet-lab-validated dataset creates a compounding data flywheel that neither Google DeepMind (which doesn't run gene therapy wet labs) nor academic tools (which lack commercial infrastructure) can easily replicate. The advisory board — Kellis at MIT/Broad, Paulk at UCSF/Siren, Bashir at UIUC/CZ Biohub — is genuinely strong and could open doors to pharma partnerships. And the founders have shipped: Axis was built, benchmarked against DeepMind's system, and launched with a public API, which is real execution velocity for a pre-seed biotech company. If the in silico-to-in vivo translation gap closes faster than expected — if the scaling laws for regulatory DNA models follow a trajectory similar to what we saw from GPT-3 to GPT-4 — Origin could be building ahead of the capability curve in a way that captures enormous value.

I'm not writing this check. The multi-miracle structure is the primary reason — compounding low-probability events across in silico-to-in vivo translation, wet-lab scaling, and pharma adoption. The commercial adoption curve for AI-designed regulatory elements feels 3-5 years from inflection, not 12-18 months. And the model architecture is built on publicly available ENCODE data with standard transformers, which means the technical moat rests almost entirely on the proprietary dataset — a dataset whose current scale hasn't been disclosed. The founders are technically sharp (Axis benchmarking against AlphaGenome is impressive work), but they're recent CS graduates without deep biology operating experience, in a domain where the gap between computational prediction and therapeutic reality has historically been measured in years and hundreds of millions of dollars. This is a "right thesis, possibly too early" situation — and I've seen enough Webvan-before-Instacart patterns to know that being right about the direction but wrong about the timing is still being wrong.
