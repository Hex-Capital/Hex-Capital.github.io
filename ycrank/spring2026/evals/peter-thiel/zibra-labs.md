# Zibra Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Zibra Labs is two ex-Anyscale Ray Core engineers ? Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer ? building a multi-cloud spot scheduler for quant backtesting, and the dimensional signature tells you everything: the highest rung is technical conviction (50,000-node clusters, 6.4M parallel tasks, <50ms dispatch), the lowest rung is physical-world moat (pure software riding on AWS spot, atop a Ray framework whose 42.4k-star repo their former employer commercializes). That pairing names the company precisely. Zibra Labs is a competent vertical wrapper around an open-source primitive its founders helped author, aimed at a niche where the incumbents are themselves cloud commodities. Competition is for losers, and Zibra Labs has chosen a category where the most credible competitor is the company that trained both founders.

The Monopoly Secret ladder runs from Palantir at the top ? government data integration that the consensus called unsellable ? through Facebook in the middle, with Wish at the bottom for entering a no-moat commerce category against Amazon. Zibra Labs sits much nearer the Wish rung. The pitched insight ? "AI now produces hundreds of signals per day instead of a handful per quarter, so backtest compute is the new bottleneck" ? is a workflow observation, not a contrarian truth. Anyscale sees it. Coiled sees it; AWS already published a Coiled-on-Batch reference architecture for exactly this workload. Bridgewater and Two Sigma, the firms Zibra Labs's own site name-checks, see it from inside their own infra teams. When the consensus is in the room nodding, there is no secret.

The Physical-World Moat ladder is the lowest placement and the most damning given my revealed preferences. General Matter sits at the top with $900M in DOE HALEU contracts and atoms no software team can replicate; Palantir sits in the middle as software so embedded in physical operations that displacement is migration; pure SaaS wrappers sit at the bottom. Zibra Labs is a scheduling layer above commodity spot instances. The dossier discloses no patents, no proprietary dataset, no hardware, no regulated customer integration. The "moat" cited is "25+ combined years building distributed systems," which is a r?sum?, not a barrier. We wanted flying cars; Zibra Labs is a faster way to dispatch Python tasks to AWS.

Definite Founder Conviction sits between the SpaceX rung (a specific reusable-rocket plan against aerospace consensus) and the lean-startup low rung. Zibra Labs's founders have made hard, specific technical commitments ? the 6.4M-parallel-task figure is not the language of iterators ? and Zac Policzer's prior Venice tech-lead role at LinkedIn is real distributed-systems pedigree. But neither founder has any disclosed quant-domain history. The plan is "we know how to build schedulers, quants need schedulers," which is technical conviction without market conviction. Power Law Magnitude sits below the Lyft rung that justified backing a duopoly #2 because the category was civilizational. Backtesting software is a $0.4B TAM growing 9.18% to $0.52B by 2033. Even generously expanding to quant cloud spend, this is not a category where the winner returns a fund ? it is a category where the winner becomes a $200M acquihire for Anyscale or Databricks.

Distribution & Sales Architecture is the dimension where Zibra Labs has a defensible, if thin, claim. The high rung is PayPal embedding in eBay listings without being asked; the middle is Anduril's network-mediated DoD entry through Palmer Luckey; the low is "build it and they will come." Zibra Labs sits in the lower-middle: Anyscale's existing relationships with Bridgewater and Two Sigma are real network assets, but those are Anyscale's customers, not Zibra Labs's, and the dossier shows zero signed logos, zero LOIs, no Product Hunt, no Discord, no Twitter presence ? the YC page lists zero open jobs and the brand currently loses search to a Danish entity and a VFX startup. Founder-led enterprise sales into hedge funds without a quant-native founder is a long road.

My 36-month forecast: Zibra Labs reaches $1?4M ARR with three to eight quant-fund and prop-shop logos, then is acquired by Anyscale, Databricks, or a hyperscaler for talent plus a vertical book at a sub-$100M outcome ? or pivots to "AI training/inference HPC" as quant TAM proves too narrow, joining the dozen Coiled-shaped companies. The mechanism is simple: a competent vertical wrapper on commoditizing infrastructure, run by engineers from the upstream commodity vendor, in a category whose ceiling is set by the customers' willingness to outsource a function many of them already build in-house.

The strongest objection comes from Lyft and from Coiled itself. Coiled raised $26M ? a Bessemer Series A ? doing nominally the same thing without vertical focus, which suggests there is venture-scale appetite for cloud-Dask plumbing; Zibra Labs's vertical specialization could plausibly compound into a real wedge if quant workflows have proprietary requirements (tick-data formats, latency SLAs, audit trails) that generic Coiled cannot serve. And the Lyft exception establishes that I will back a #2 in a category if the category itself is creating enormous total value. The dismantling: Coiled's $26M raise is not validation of an outcome, it is a marker on the same treadmill, and Lyft's category was hundreds of billions in consumer value ? backtesting compute is not. The condition that flips my verdict: a signed, named anchor contract with a tier-one quant fund ? Citadel, Jane Street, Two Sigma, DE Shaw ? at material ARR, demonstrating that Zibra Labs has crossed from "ex-Anyscale engineers with a thesis" into the Palantir-pattern of operational embedding inside an institutional buyer. Absent that, this is a pass.
