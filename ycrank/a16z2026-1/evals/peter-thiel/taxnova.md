# Taxnova -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Taxnova enters a market where SPRX has raised $46 million, Boast.ai $30 million, and Neo.Tax $13 million — all pursuing the identical thesis that software can automate R&D tax credit preparation. The existence of a "competitive landscape" table with four funded competitors is, by my framework, not context for evaluation but the evaluation itself. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is shared by three companies with a combined $89 million in funding, there is no secret. "AI can automate tax documentation" is not a contrarian insight — it is the consensus of every fintech investor who has written a check in this category since 2020. The advisor-agnostic positioning and multi-jurisdiction strategy are go-to-market choices, not hidden truths about the world. A strategic variant within a competitive market is still competition, and competition is still for losers.

The multi-jurisdiction angle deserves a more careful examination, because complexity across regulatory regimes can sometimes function as a quasi-structural barrier. Taxnova has live UK HMRC compliance and lists US support as "coming soon." The UK's inquiry rate increase from 0.5% to 20% creates genuine urgency for audit-defensible claims — that is a real market catalyst. But SPRX, sitting on $46 million after a February 2026 Series B, can add UK jurisdiction support as a feature within a single product cycle. Regulatory complexity compounds linearly — each new country requires bespoke tax logic — but it does not compound exponentially the way network effects or data moats do. Palantir's government embedding created switching costs that approached structural infinity because the software became operationally inseparable from intelligence workflows. Taxnova's integrations take thirty minutes to set up, which means they take thirty minutes to tear down. That is not a moat; it is a convenience.

George Nichkov's background at Oliver Wyman and Gett, combined with Maria Malykh's program management at Criteo coordinating nine R&D teams, provides competent enterprise product experience — but not the profile of founders who have made irreversible commitments to an audacious vision. No prior exit from either founder. No shared prehistory at a common employer or institution — a specific absence the dossier confirms. The broader team's EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, and IRS domain expertise is the strongest human-capital signal here, but domain expertise in tax advisory is precisely the kind of knowledge that large competitors can acquire by hiring the same people. Revolut's Head of Tax as an advisor is a credible signal of regulatory seriousness, not of founder conviction that bends the world.

The strongest bull case for Taxnova is its distribution architecture — and I take distribution more seriously than most. The completed pilot with a Top 5 R&D advisory firm suggests a channel strategy that could scale without proportional sales costs: advisory firms bring the clients, Taxnova provides the infrastructure. This echoes the logic of becoming picks-and-shovels for an existing industry rather than displacing it. Named customers — incident.io, Paddle — generating $100K ARR at pre-seed is a genuine traction signal, and the $200K+ ACV enterprise pilot indicates pricing power. If Taxnova could become the invisible software layer that every advisory firm depends on globally, embedded so deeply that firms cannot process claims without it, that would be a monopoly pathway. But the advisory firms themselves are structurally incentivized to build or acquire this capability — the Big Four's innovation labs exist precisely to internalize tools like Taxnova before they become dependencies. And advisory firms switching between software vendors is a quarterly budgeting decision, not a multi-year migration.

The power-law arithmetic closes the case. The R&D tax credit software market is projected at $3.28 billion by 2034. Even monopolizing this entire market — an impossibility given the competitive fragmentation — would not produce a fund-returning outcome from an angel check. Taxnova references a "$10B R&D tax market," but leading with TAM inflation is the precise signal I have learned to discount. The Wish investment taught me what happens when you back a company in a market large enough to seem exciting but structurally resistant to monopoly: Wish IPO'd at $14 billion and lost over 90% because no-moat competition in a commodity market destroys value regardless of revenue scale. Taxnova operates in a smaller market with more funded competitors and no physical-world technology barrier — LLM-based narrative generation is, as the dossier itself acknowledges, becoming commoditized. The defensible layer is supposed to be jurisdiction-specific tax logic and integration depth, but neither creates the kind of multi-year replication barrier that justifies a power-law bet.

I pass. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret — where the presence of four funded competitors pursuing the same thesis constitutes dispositive evidence that no secret exists — and Power Law Return Magnitude, where a $3.28 billion total addressable software market with linear rather than exponential scaling dynamics caps the outcome well below fund-returner territory. Taxnova's distribution through advisory firms is its most thoughtful strategic element, but superior distribution cannot create a monopoly when the product layer is a commodity and the market is already crowded. The company may build a profitable business. Profitable businesses in competitive markets are not what I invest in.
