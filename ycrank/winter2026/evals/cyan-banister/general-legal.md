# General Legal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about General Legal: Y Combinator literally published a Request for Startups asking someone to build this exact company — "start your own law firm, staff it with AI agents, and compete with existing law firms." And three impeccably credentialed Harvard Law graduates who built the $650 million Casetext exit raised their hands and said "we'll do it." That's a strong team executing on an institutional thesis. But it's the opposite of the signal I've learned to trust. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. General Legal is popular and straightforward to assess. Legal AI attracted $6 billion in funding last year. Harvey's at $8 billion. Spellbook raised at $350 million. Lawhive just took $60 million specifically to enter the U.S. market. When I look at this landscape, I see a category where consensus has already formed — and consensus-based investing is not what I do.

The founders are undeniably accomplished. Ryan Walker was CTO of Casetext, Javed Qadrud-Din directed machine learning there, and J.P. Mohler built AI tools that actual judicial systems adopted. They all share the rare combination of practicing attorney credentials and real AI engineering chops. They went through the Thomson Reuters acquisition together and chose to leave and build again — that tells me something important about their working relationship and shared conviction. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is elite institutional trajectories. Harvard Law, Cooley, Fenwick & West, PhD in mathematics. These are insiders at the top of two prestigious fields who spotted a professional optimization opportunity. That's a perfectly valid reason to start a company, and many investors will find it compelling. It's just not what activates my conviction. I'm listening for the founder who was told no fifty times, who came from somewhere unexpected, who is solving a problem that kept them up at night because they lived it — not because they billed hours around it.

The economic access dimension concerns me too. General Legal serves growth-stage startups — companies that have already raised significant venture capital — with slightly cheaper contract review. Moving from $1,000-an-hour BigLaw to $500-per-contract is a real cost savings, but it's cost optimization for the already-funded, not access creation for people who were previously excluded. When I backed Uber, the story wasn't just cheaper rides — it was income for drivers who needed flexible work. When I backed Affirm, it was transparent credit for people trapped by hidden fees. I keep asking myself: who gets access to something they didn't have before? And the answer here is that well-funded startups get their contracts reviewed faster and cheaper. That's a fine business. It's not the kind of impact that makes me write a check from my own money.

I want to be fair about the bull case, because there's a real one. The "law firm, not a tool" positioning is structurally clever — it creates channel conflict for Harvey and Spellbook, who can't compete without alienating their existing lawyer customers. BigLaw can't match the pricing without cannibalizing their hourly billing model. The regulatory requirement for licensed attorneys is a genuine moat that pure-software competitors can't bypass. And Robin AI's financial collapse after raising $70 million suggests the pure-software contract review model may be harder than it looks, which validates the hybrid approach. If General Legal can maintain quality while scaling — and the 20-plus companies in private preview suggest early product-market pull — they could become the default commercial contracts provider for the entire YC ecosystem and expand from there. The team has literally done this before at Casetext. That's not nothing. For an investor who weights execution pedigree and market timing over the biographical and anti-consensus signals I prioritize, this is probably a strong bet.

But I keep coming back to the structural mismatch with how I invest. This company requires hiring more licensed attorneys to scale — every contract needs a human lawyer in the loop. That creates margin pressure and linear scaling constraints that make this more like a high-efficiency professional services firm than a technology company with compounding returns. The $500 price point is thin if you're paying attorney salaries and carrying malpractice liability. And Lawhive is coming into the U.S. with $60 million, seven-times revenue growth, and explicit plans to expand beyond consumer law. The founders' former employer Thomson Reuters owns the Casetext AI they helped build and has the resources, data, and institutional relationships to offer competing services. These aren't fatal risks, but they're the kind of competitive dynamics that favor the well-capitalized incumbent — and this team is showing up to that fight at pre-seed scale. For me, the combination of a consensus category, insider founders, limited economic access creation, and services-business scaling constraints puts this outside my strike zone. I'd tell these founders they'll have no trouble raising from investors who weight execution pedigree and market timing — this just isn't my kind of bet.
