# Minicor -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Minicor's 25,000 patients per day running through a single customer's workflows at pre-seed is the number that stopped me. Most YC pre-seed companies are still debating their landing page copy. Minicor has production-grade volume flowing through managed Windows VMs with SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certification already in hand. That compliance infrastructure took real time and capital to build, and it maps directly to a pattern I've seen repeatedly -- Gusto in payroll, Checkr in background checks -- where regulatory overhead that deters casual entrants becomes the startup's moat. Nobody glamorous is going to build managed HIPAA-compliant Windows desktop VM orchestration. That's exactly the kind of boring, operationally critical infrastructure where the absence of competition IS the competitive advantage.

Minicor's market timing rests on two converging structural shifts. First, Anthropic launched Claude computer use in October 2024, crossing the capability threshold that makes AI-driven UI interaction production-viable for the first time -- the same kind of step-function capability jump that unlocked Harvey's legal AI market when GPT-4 landed. Second, legacy system vendors like Epic and Cerner are actively restricting third-party API access, which means UI automation isn't a choice -- it's the only integration path. The nonobvious element isn't "AI will disrupt RPA" (that's consensus; UiPath's stock tells the story). The nonobvious element is that AI companies selling into healthcare, automotive, and financial services all hit the same wall: their customers run critical workflows on legacy Windows desktop apps with no writable APIs. Minicor is picks-and-shovels for that specific collision. Most of the well-funded competitors -- Skyvern ($2.7M seed), Induced AI ($2.3M), Browserbase ($40M Series B) -- are all building for browser automation. Minicor targets native Windows desktop and Citrix environments, a less crowded but structurally necessary niche. That said, the broader AI-automates-legacy-systems narrative is increasingly well-understood, which caps the nonobviousness score.

Minicor's "one API call triggers a full desktop workflow" architecture positions it as infrastructure that other businesses integrate into, not an end-user application. AI companies building for healthcare don't want to manage Windows VMs, tune computer-use agents for Epic's UI quirks, or maintain HIPAA compliance for desktop interaction logs -- they want to call an API and get structured data back. That's the Stripe model: abstract away gnarly complexity behind a clean API surface. Minicor's six customer logos and supported system list (Athena, Epic, Cerner, CDK Global, SAP, HighJump) suggest early progress toward becoming the default integration layer across multiple verticals. The switching costs compound -- once workflows are built, validated against compliance requirements, and integrated into production systems processing 25,000 patients daily, ripping out Minicor requires re-creating and re-validating every workflow. But here's my concern: Minicor is building this infrastructure layer on top of foundation model computer-use capabilities from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google -- a contested layer where the platform providers themselves are shipping competing capabilities. When foundation model accuracy moves from 85% to 96% (Minicor's current claimed range), the raw agent capability commoditizes, and Minicor's differentiation depends entirely on the managed orchestration, self-healing, and compliance layers remaining thick enough to justify the abstraction.

Minicor's single-miracle profile is favorable with an asterisk. The technology miracle -- foundation models achieving production-viable accuracy on desktop tasks -- is happening exogenously. Minicor doesn't need to build the core AI. Healthcare and financial services are proven buyers of workflow automation tools, so distribution doesn't require a miracle either. The one miracle Minicor needs is building sufficient depth in its managed orchestration and compliance layers before the foundation model providers vertically integrate into managed desktop automation. That's a race, not a bet on physics -- and races are more winnable than breakthroughs. But the timeline pressure is real. If Anthropic's next major release ships managed computer-use with built-in compliance tooling, Minicor's window narrows significantly.

Faizaan Chishtie (CEO) brings prior YC experience from Phoenix (W21) and grew Homeboard to mid-five-figure ARR before pivoting. Saheed Akinbile (CTO) spent three years as a Senior Software Engineer at Ford on Android Auto -- directly relevant to the legacy desktop systems Minicor targets -- and previously worked at Morgan Stanley, another target vertical. The clear CEO/CTO structure avoids the equal-co-founder deadlock risk I flag regularly. Their pivot from Laminar (low-code integrations) to Minicor originated from a direct customer encounter with the legacy desktop problem -- they discovered the market through operational pain, which I value. But neither founder has operated at a company during its hypergrowth scaling phase. Faizaan's largest prior outcome was mid-five-figure ARR. At the scaling challenges ahead -- managing VM fleet infrastructure, navigating enterprise healthcare procurement, building a sales organization for regulated verticals -- I'd want to see evidence they can hire and fire at pace. Mark Bailey's addition as a revenue executive with SaaS scaling experience partially addresses the go-to-market gap.

Minicor's strongest bull case: it maps to two of my highest-conviction unstated patterns simultaneously. It's "boring but critical" infrastructure (no prestige-seeking competition, deep workflow integration, regulatory barriers) AND it sits in a compliance-heavy regulated market (HIPAA, SOC 2 Type II) where the certification itself deters new entrants. The pivot speed from Laminar to production-grade Minicor with real customer volume demonstrates exactly the velocity I look for -- the only advantage an early-stage company has over an incumbent is speed, and Minicor moved fast. The bear case centers on foundation model commoditization: Minicor claims 93-96% click accuracy versus 80-85% for raw LLM computer-use, but that 8-16 percentage point gap is closing every quarter. If that gap closes to 2-3 points, Minicor's value proposition reduces to managed VMs + compliance -- still defensible, but a smaller business with lower margins than an AI-differentiated platform. Healthcare concentration (six of eight supported systems are healthcare) adds customer-concentration risk. Vendor countermeasures -- Epic blocking UI automation or opening APIs -- could eliminate the core use case in Minicor's primary vertical. The infrastructure positioning and compliance moat are durable enough to overcome these risks at the margin, but the commoditization timeline is the variable I'd monitor most closely. The verifiable evidence that would flip me to Pass: if Anthropic or OpenAI ships a managed desktop automation service with built-in healthcare compliance by Q3 2026, Minicor's window closes faster than they can build defensibility.

Minicor earns an Invest recommendation driven primarily by infrastructure-layer positioning (API-first architecture selling to AI companies, not end-users) and the "boring but critical" regulated infrastructure pattern that consistently outperforms in my portfolio. The compliance moat is real, the production traction is unusual for pre-seed, and the single-miracle profile is favorable. The commoditization risk and founder scaling gaps prevent a higher score -- I'd write a seed check but watch the foundation model accuracy curve quarterly.
