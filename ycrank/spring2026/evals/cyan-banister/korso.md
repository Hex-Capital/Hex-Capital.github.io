# Korso -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is that it's popular. Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins are already backing Uptool in the same exact category. Paperless Parts has $30 million and a head start. There are at least five funded startups building AI-powered quoting for manufacturers right now. When I look at my best investments -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe -- the defining feature was that *nobody else wanted in*. The deal flow was me, sometimes alone, writing a check into something that sounded ridiculous to everyone around me. AI quoting automation for manufacturers doesn't sound ridiculous to anyone in 2026. It sounds like exactly what every enterprise AI pitch deck is showing. That's consensus, and consensus-based investing is a very different game than the one I play.

If I could sit down with these founders -- and I always want to before I make up my mind -- my first question would be: tell me your story. Not the business, not the TAM slide, just how you ended up here. And what the dossier gives me is three smart UPenn engineering students from Irvine, California, who built things in Penn Electric Racing and did ML research and interned at GM and Rockwell Automation. Alex Liu dropped out, which catches my ear because I dropped out too -- I know what it means to bet on yourself when the conventional path is sitting right there. But I don't know *why* he dropped out. I don't know what hardship any of these founders have overcome. I don't know which of them has spent years inside a machine shop watching their family struggle to price quotes accurately at 2 AM. The biographical thread connecting founder to problem -- the thing I'm always listening for, the thing that told me Travis Kalanick would fight through anything for Uber -- I can't find it here. These are talented young engineers solving a problem they've identified intellectually, not one that was forged into them through lived experience.

Here's the bull case, and I want to be honest about it because I've been wrong before: the market timing is genuinely compelling. LLMs just crossed the threshold where they can reliably extract structured data from messy manufacturing documents -- drawings, RFQ emails, WhatsApp messages. Only 35% of procurement teams use AI for this today, which means the adoption window is early. The WhatsApp ingestion detail actually shows some real-world awareness of how smaller manufacturers communicate -- that's not something you'd think of from a Stanford dorm room. Martin Pan's GM experience gives him at least a window into manufacturing operations. And the end-to-end bundling of RFQ ingestion, quoting, PO tracking, and a conversational AI layer is more ambitious than what most competitors are building. If these founders have the persistence to survive enterprise sales cycles and the taste to build something manufacturers actually love, the data moat from accumulated pricing history could become very real over time. There's a version of this story where Korso becomes the operating system for mid-market manufacturing -- and that would be a massive outcome.

But that version requires things I can't see yet. It requires founders who will persist through the brutal slog of selling to manufacturers who don't want to change their workflows, who will outrun competitors with ten times their funding, and who have the deep domain intuition to build something that feels inevitable rather than incremental. When I think about the companies I've backed that navigated complexity in hard spaces -- Contraline fighting through FDA pathways, Anduril wrestling with defense procurement -- those founders had decades of domain obsession and scars to prove it. A 2025 internship at GM and some ML research, however strong, isn't the same signal. And critically, this company doesn't do the thing that really pulls me in: it doesn't create new economic access for people who lack it. It makes existing manufacturers marginally more efficient at something they already do. Compare that to how I see Uber -- as a poverty-alleviation tool that created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Korso is a solid B2B efficiency play, but it doesn't create new opportunity where none existed before.

On the team dynamics: Daichi and Martin share Penn Electric Racing and their hometown, which is a genuine shared-history signal. But Alex Liu's connection to the other two founders is opaque, and with three first-time founders, I'd want to know exactly how they fight and how they resolve disagreements. After HQ Trivia, I never skip that question. Nothing here raises active red flags, but nothing resolves my concern either.

I pass on Korso. These are capable young builders in a real market, but the idea is consensus, the founders lack the biographical urgency I look for, and the competitive field is already well-funded. The investment I'd make in manufacturing AI would look different -- it would come from a founder who grew up sweeping floors in their family's fabrication shop and who sees something about that world that the UPenn engineering curriculum can't teach. That person hasn't walked into my office yet on this one.
