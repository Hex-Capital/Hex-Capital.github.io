# Gikl, Inc -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The idea sounds good. That's my problem. "The best ideas look initially like bad ideas." Facebook sounded like a site for college students to waste time. Airbnb sounded like sleeping in strangers' beds. "Accelerating scientific discovery with AI" sounds like something a dean would put on a grant proposal. When someone pitches me "coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers — we're doing the same for science," my instinct is that this is a clean narrative built on a flawed analogy. Code has instant feedback loops: it compiles or it doesn't, tests pass or they fail. Science doesn't work that way. Verifying a scientific contribution from a non-expert is fundamentally harder than verifying whether a function returns the right output. The analogy breaks at exactly the point that matters most, and nobody seems to have noticed — or at least the positioning doesn't address it.

The founders are formidable, and I don't use that word lightly. Vajda ran Media Generation at Meta for over a decade — Emu, Movie Gen, the serious stuff. Yamamoto was Senior Staff on Core Llama at Meta's superintelligence lab, holds a physics PhD from Rice, published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, and co-authored with a Fields Medalist. They overlapped at Meta for roughly nine years. "The relationship between the founders has to be strong. They must genuinely like one another, and work well together. Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock." Nine years of concurrent employment at the same company is real evidence that this sock won't come apart. And Yamamoto is one of the rare founders who has genuine domain expertise — he's done real science, not just built tools for scientists. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." That checks out for at least one of them.

But formidable credentials and relentless resourcefulness are different things entirely. When the Airbnb founders ran out of money, they sold cereal boxes for forty dollars apiece. When the Collisons wanted early users, they'd say "give me your laptop" and install Stripe on the spot. What I'm looking for at this stage — and what I see no evidence of here — is that cockroach quality, the founders who just won't die. Vajda had a prior startup (Caarbon, outcome unknown), which is at least a data point. But the dossier shows zero traction: no users, no contributions, no waitlist, no Discord, no community of any kind. For a platform whose entire thesis depends on community density, the absence of even a tiny, passionate nucleus of contributors is notable. "Startups take off because the founders make them take off. You can't wait for users to come to you. You have to go out and get them." Where are the users they went out and got?

Here's the bull case, and I want to be honest about it because it's real. These founders have spent a decade inside the frontier of AI capability. They've seen what these models can do before the research papers land. If AI capability continues to compound — and "learning compounds" — the distance between expert and non-expert in science could collapse in ways most people can't yet imagine. The positioning is actually more contrarian than it first appears: everyone else in the AI-for-science space is either building retrieval tools for existing researchers (Elicit, Consensus) or trying to replace humans entirely with autonomous agents (FutureHouse, Sakana). ScienceSwarm is the only funded company I see betting that the right answer is humans *plus* AI, with the community as the product. If the platform reaches critical mass, the accumulated contribution data and community reputation become a moat that neither Google nor OpenAI would naturally build — big companies optimize for replacing human labor, not cultivating communities. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." Maybe this is that.

But the bull case requires too many simultaneous breakthroughs: that AI augmentation can make non-expert scientific contributions genuinely valuable, that these research-background founders can build community (a completely different skill than building models), and that they can reach critical mass before Google's AI Co-Scientist — already deployed at seventeen DOE national labs — makes the AI layer a commodity. "Most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended," and I'd want to see evidence that these founders are flexible enough to pivot hard when the citizen-science thesis hits reality. Right now, the platform lists six scientific domains with open challenges and a beautiful interface, but I'm worried about "playing house" — going through the motions of starting a startup without the messy, uncomfortable work of finding those first ten users who desperately need this. The schlep here isn't the AI. It's the community.

I keep returning to what I know about power-law returns: "You have to ignore the elephant in front of you, the likelihood they'll succeed, and focus instead on whether they'll succeed really big." If ScienceSwarm works, it's a platform with compounding network effects in arguably the most important domain in the world. That's a big "if." But the founders haven't shown me the determination signal that would make me bet through the uncertainty. Exceptional résumés are the weakest form of founder signal at this stage. What I need — and what's entirely absent — is any evidence of organic pull, any sign that real people are making real contributions, any indication that the founders have gone out and dragged their first users onto this platform by hand. Without that, I'm investing in a thesis, not a company.
