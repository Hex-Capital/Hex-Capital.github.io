# Nebula Security -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Eten Zou's team shipped a Chrome V8 sandbox escape (CVE-2026-6307), a V8 RCE (CVE-2026-5865), and a Linux kernel exploit that paid a $10,500 bounty -- and did all of it before taking the YC $500K. That is the Nebula Security fact that matters, and it sits at one end of the barbell. At the other end sits the fact that Nebula Security's second co-founder is not even named on the YC page and Eten Zou has no findable GitHub, LinkedIn, or Twitter. That is the analytical signature: Nebula Security is a documented-artifact company with an undocumented team, and everything downstream flows from that tension.

Brian Armstrong's hand-built Android Bitcoin wallet after his written **Coinbase** (2012) rejection is the canonical animal-resourcefulness rung, and Nebula Security lands one step below it, not two. A V8 sandbox escape is not a hackathon weekend; it is months of fuzzer engineering and constraint solving for a bug class Google pays six figures to eliminate. Nebula Security shipped it, wrote it up publicly at nebusec.ai/research, and collected a $10,500 kernel bounty on top -- these are the tracks animals leave. Where Nebula Security diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) rung is verifiability: the dossier flags that the YC page's "first nginx RCE" claim maps to CVE-2026-42945 credited to a researcher named "depthfirst," not to Eten Zou, which means one of Nebula Security's headline artifacts may be marketing rather than provenance. Armstrong's wallet had his name on it; Nebula Security's nginx bug apparently does not.

The **Kiko** (2005) failure pattern -- team fine on paper, chemistry and identity too thin to matter -- is the ladder rung I keep circling on Nebula Security's co-founder configuration. **Kiko** (2005) ended up on eBay because Kan and Shear were early on timing and a strong two-person unit; Nebula Security's problem is the opposite face of the same coin, which is that Nebula Security lists Team Size 2 on YC but the public record contains one name. That is not a **Reddit** (2005) dorm-roommate configuration; it is not even a hackathon-pairing configuration; it is a configuration I cannot inspect at all. When the dossier is silent on the second founder for a company whose entire pitch is founder-led offensive research, I do not get to assume Airbnb-caliber chemistry.

On idea organicity, the mechanism to watch over 18 months is whether Eten Zou keeps eating his own dogfood or whether Nebula Security drifts into being a services shop with an AI cover story. The founders published a Longinus writeup on Chrome V8 -- that is a researcher scratching his own itch, which is the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick pattern in a different key. But VEGA as a product is being sold to YC portfolio companies as a scanner, not used by the founders on the founders' own workflow, and the "$0K+ in bug bounties earned" counter on nebusec.ai suggests the artifact matters more than the SaaS today. Organic, but organic to research, not to the product being sold.

Counterfactually, Nebula Security's schlep score would flip a tier only if the dossier surfaced a specific offline grind -- CVE coordination with Google's V8 team, responsible-disclosure hell with Linux kernel maintainers, the paperwork of "Audited by Nebula Security" trust-mark licensing to enterprises. Chrome V8 bug submission is unambiguously a schlep, and Nebula Security is already inside it; the question is whether that grind compounds into a moat or evaporates once XBOW's $237M war chest hires the same researchers. Right now Nebula Security is doing the work but has not yet named the schlep as the moat.

**Stripe** (2010)'s hacker genealogy read as Patrick Collison winning Ireland's Young Scientist prize for Lisp work at 16 -- a public artifact anyone could verify. Nebula Security's hacker genealogy reads as three CVEs with writeups on the company site and one nginx RCE attribution the dossier could not corroborate. The V8 sandbox escape alone is elite-tier hacker output; a person who can pop V8 does not need a GitHub star count to prove it. What worries me is not the absence of the star count -- it is that I cannot tell whether the CVE work is Eten Zou or the invisible co-founder, and that ambiguity is exactly what the "hacker genealogy" filter is supposed to resolve.

Thirty-six months out, my forecast for Nebula Security is a $30-80M acqui-hire by a security incumbent -- Snyk, Sonar, or a Big Four consulting arm buying the audit brand -- driven by the research team's CVE output being more valuable than the VEGA product's ARR trajectory. XBOW's $237M and Oege de Moor's GitHub-Copilot lineage crushes Nebula Security on distribution and capital in the AI-scanner category, so the exit is on the research side, not the SaaS side. That is the **Kiko** (2005)-adjacent outcome: real talent, wrong shape of company for the category structure.

The strongest case against my forecast is the **Coinbase** (2012) precedent: a founder who ships a working exploit against a Google-hardened target before the seed check is exactly the animal profile that scales past better-funded competitors, because compliance-and-research grind compounds in ways venture capital cannot shortcut. If Eten Zou personally landed CVE-2026-6307 and the second co-founder is a comparable operator, XBOW's $237M does not matter -- animals eat war chests for breakfast, and I have watched it happen. What dismantles the bull case here is the specific dossier evidence that the nginx attribution does not tie to Eten Zou in public records, combined with a co-founder the YC page does not name. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: if Eten Zou is named as the reporter on the CVE-2026-6307 NVD entry and the second co-founder surfaces with a comparable CVE credit before Demo Day, I write the check at the top of the Invest range without a second meeting.
