# Gikl, Inc -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive structure of this market, and it's not favorable. Google has already deployed AI Co-Scientist across all 17 DOE National Labs and at Stanford and Imperial College London. OpenAI is building a fully automated researcher targeting AI research intern capability by September 2026. Sakana AI has $379M at a $2.65B valuation doing automated end-to-end research. FutureHouse raised $70M to build autonomous AI scientists. This isn't a market where ScienceSwarm is arriving early to an inflection -- this is a market where the most capitalized companies on Earth are already executing. The structural moment is real: AI models crossed the capability threshold for scientific reasoning in 2024-2025. But the structural moment benefits autonomous AI-for-science platforms far more than it benefits a crowdsourced citizen science platform. When Google gives away AI Co-Scientist for free to every national lab, the question isn't "does the technology work" -- it's "why does the citizen science model win despite the technology working well enough to automate the humans out entirely?"

The citizen science angle is contrarian, which I normally find interesting. But contrarian only creates value when the consensus is wrong for structural reasons. Here, the consensus -- that AI will augment and eventually replace professional researchers rather than enable amateur ones -- has strong structural logic behind it. Scientific discovery has fundamentally different feedback loops than software development. When coding assistants turn non-experts into developers, the verification is instant: the code compiles or it doesn't. In science, verification can take months or years and requires domain expertise that AI hasn't yet commoditized. The analogy ScienceSwarm draws -- "coding assistants turned millions into developers, we'll do the same for science" -- is memorable marketing but structurally weak. Foldit and the Polymath Project are the actual precedents for crowdsourced science, and both produced narrow, celebrated results without becoming scalable platforms. That was a decade ago, and the model hasn't scaled.

The payment structure problem is what concerns me most. This maps directly to why I've never invested in EdTech: who pays? Citizen scientists won't pay meaningful subscription fees for the privilege of solving unsolved math problems. Research institutions have access to Google and OpenAI tools. Pharma companies will use FutureHouse or build internally. The person who benefits from scientific progress (society broadly) is not the person who writes the check. No pricing page, no monetization signal, no revenue -- and I suspect that's not just because it's pre-seed, but because the founders haven't solved the payment structure question. When the inferred monetization path is "probably freemium SaaS or enterprise licensing," that tells me the business model is an unsolved problem layered on top of the community cold-start problem layered on top of the unproven citizen-science-with-AI thesis. That's three miracles, and I fund companies that need one.

The strongest bull case: these founders are genuinely world-class in AI. Vajda directed Meta's media generation efforts -- Emu, Movie Gen -- and Yamamoto was Senior Staff in the Core Llama group at Meta Superintelligence Labs. Yamamoto also has a physics PhD with publications in PNAS and Physical Review Letters and co-authored with a Fields Medalist, which gives real scientific credibility. If anyone can build the AI substrate that makes citizen science verification work, it's probably a team with this combination of frontier AI engineering and actual scientific research experience. If the "citizen scientist + AI" model turns out to be structurally better than autonomous AI scientists for certain categories of problems -- problems requiring creative leaps that AI can't yet make independently -- then ScienceSwarm could capture a genuinely novel position that Google and OpenAI aren't even competing for. That's a real scenario. But it requires the model to be validated first, and I see no evidence of that validation yet -- no traction, no user counts, no community metrics, no evidence that a single scientific contribution has been made through the platform.

On product-to-distribution: community platforms can become distribution channels -- Stack Overflow proved that with Teams. But the path from "open problems listed on a website" to "distribution channel for additional products" requires first solving the cold start, which requires solving the incentive design, which requires solving the payment structure. I don't see evidence the founders have thought about distribution beyond organic community growth. The platform features -- "submit a problem," "rally a team" -- suggest viral mechanics, but viral mechanics require existing engagement to amplify, and I see no engagement signals. The underlying AI capabilities (literature synthesis, hypothesis generation) are commodity via foundation model APIs, so the defensibility has to come from community density and accumulated contribution data -- neither of which exists today.

The technology cycle timing is subtly wrong for this specific approach. AI capability is advancing so rapidly that the window for "AI-augmented humans doing science" may be narrower than the founders assume. If autonomous AI researchers reach research-intern capability by late 2026 (OpenAI's stated target) and continue improving, the citizen science model could be leapfrogged before it reaches critical mass. Harvey worked because legal workflows are stable and repetitive -- you build ahead of the capability curve knowing the use case won't change. Science is different: the entire premise of what humans contribute is being redefined by every model generation. Building a platform that requires human contributions when the value of those contributions may be declining is a timing risk most investors wouldn't flag, but it's the kind of adoption curve dynamic I watch closely.
