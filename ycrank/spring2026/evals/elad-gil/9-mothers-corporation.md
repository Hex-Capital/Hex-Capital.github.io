# 9 Mothers -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, because it maps directly to a framework I use constantly: market-ending moves. Anduril just won a $20B Army framework contract for counter-UAS in March 2026. They have a $60B valuation, $2B in 2025 revenue, and the Lattice OS platform that integrates detection, tracking, and neutralization into a single software layer. When an incumbent captures the primary procurement vehicle for a category, every subsequent entrant has to explain why they're not competing against a distribution position that compounds. DroneShield already proved there's room for multiple winners at different price points -- A$216.5M in FY2025 revenue, 276% year-over-year growth -- but they operate primarily in detection, not kinetic kill. 9 Mothers is attempting both detection and neutralization in a portable form factor at the tactical edge. The question isn't whether the market is real. It's whether this team, at this stage, can carve out a niche that Anduril's platform and the primes' procurement relationships can't absorb.

The structural moment here is unmistakable, but it's consensus. Ukraine demonstrated that cheap FPV drones changed the cost calculus of modern warfare. Every defense-focused investor has been tracking the C-UAS space since 2022. The DoD is allocating $49.9B in defense tech investments. The counter-UAS market is projected at 26.5% CAGR through 2034. This is not the hidden market I look for -- it's not "too boring" or "too niche" or "too personally unfamiliar" to other investors. When Fortune Business Insights is publishing CAGR projections and GlobeNewsWire is covering procurement urgency, you're in a consensus market. The non-obvious angle might exist in the specific niche of man-portable, low-cost, acoustically-driven autonomous C-UAS -- systems too small and cheap for primes to prioritize, too edge-focused for Anduril's integrated platform approach. That's a plausible gap. But it's a hypothesis about where inside a hot market the opportunity lives, not the structural inflection itself.

The acoustic ML approach is the most technically interesting element. Job postings for Senior Applied Audio ML Engineer, DSP Engineer (Acoustic), and Senior Systems Engineer (Audio) suggest EDDA uses passive acoustic sensing as a primary or complementary detection modality. In contested electromagnetic environments -- which is exactly what a modern battlefield is -- passive detection has real advantages over radar or RF approaches that emit detectable signatures. If the acoustic-AI pipeline works at sufficient range and accuracy against Group 1 drones, that's a genuine technical differentiator. But this brings me to the founder-market fit problem. Smith spent a decade building a SaaS QA testing platform. Khomenko's ML experience is in QA automation and computer vision screenshot segmentation. Pyzh's background is cybersecurity consulting. None of them have built hardware, none have defense procurement experience, none have acoustic engineering backgrounds. They're hiring for these skills aggressively -- 14 job postings, $150K-$250K salaries -- but the founding team's domain gap for an autonomous weapon system is significant. When I invested in Anduril, Palmer Luckey had already built and shipped hardware at scale with Oculus, and the team included senior DoD procurement veterans. The distance between a QA SaaS and an ITAR-regulated autonomous weapon is one of the widest domain pivots I've seen at pre-seed.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Smith is a second-time YC founder with a decade of operational experience scaling a company that raised $42.3M. Smith and Khomenko worked together for 8+ years at Rainforest QA -- that's an unusually battle-tested co-founder relationship, which eliminates one of the three things that kill pre-PMF companies. Khomenko's Ukrainian background gives him a personal connection to the drone warfare threat that's driving this market. And the precedent is real: the tactical edge needs systems that are cheaper, smaller, and more portable than what Anduril and Epirus are building. If EDDA can deliver detection-plus-neutralization in a man-portable form factor at a fraction of Sentry Tower's cost, there's a genuine wedge. Defense procurement often has room for multiple vendors at different tiers -- the Army doesn't buy everything from one company. The question is whether this team can navigate ITAR compliance, DoD Directive 3000.09 requirements, and government contract vehicles without defense industry insiders on the founding team. That's what pushes this from "interesting structural position" to "requires multiple miracles" in my framework: build a working autonomous weapon system (hardware + AI miracle), AND navigate defense procurement without domain experience (distribution miracle). Two miracles compound low-probability events.

On the product-to-distribution axis, defense hardware is structurally limited compared to software platforms. A deployed C-UAS sensor network could theoretically become a distribution channel for ISR capabilities, electronic warfare tools, or battlefield communications -- but there's no evidence the founders think about EDDA as a platform rather than a point product. Anduril solved this with Lattice OS as a software layer that distributes additional capabilities through deployed hardware. Without a similar platform thesis, 9 Mothers risks being a single-product hardware company in a market where the category leader has already built the distribution moat. The technology cycle positioning is right -- edge AI inference, low-power ML accelerators, and the Ukraine-driven urgency create genuine "why now" -- but being at the right time is a shared advantage when every C-UAS startup is riding the same wave.
