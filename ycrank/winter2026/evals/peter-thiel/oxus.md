# Oxus -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Oxus is the competitive landscape surrounding it, which tells me everything I need to know about whether a secret exists here. AuditBoard has $200M+ in ARR, was acquired for over $3 billion, and is actively shipping AI features. Workiva is a public company launching "Workiva AI." MindBridge has raised $140 million for AI-powered audit analytics. Drata has raised $328 million. Every major incumbent and well-funded challenger in the audit software space has converged on the same thesis: AI will automate substantive audit work. When I ask my signature question -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- Oxus has no answer that the market hasn't already given. "AI can automate SOX compliance" is not a secret. It is the consensus, stated simultaneously by dozens of companies with vastly more resources. The Girardian dynamics here are unmistakable: everyone is imitating everyone else into a market where no one will earn monopoly returns.

The channel conflict deepens the problem. The Big Four accounting firms -- Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG -- currently earn $200 to $800 per hour performing the exact work Oxus proposes to automate. These firms also happen to be the primary relationship holders with every Chief Audit Executive and CFO in Oxus's target market. Oxus needs to sell through the very entities whose revenue it would destroy. This is not a theoretical concern; it is a structural go-to-market trap. Palantir solved an analogous problem in intelligence by going directly to CIA through In-Q-Tel, bypassing the defense contractors entirely. But Palantir had a co-founder (me) with direct government relationships and a seven-year runway to burn through before reaching profitability. A three-person pre-seed team attempting to navigate enterprise sales cycles with CFOs at public companies while simultaneously fighting the Big Four's distribution lock does not have that luxury.

The founders are credentialed in the way that raises my concern rather than lowering it. Federal Reserve CBDC research, D.E. Shaw, JPMorgan, BCG consulting -- these are the résumés of people who follow prestigious paths, not people who hold non-consensus beliefs against social pressure. I have said before that I value founders who exhibit a mild resistance to conventional social signals, founders whose conviction is rooted in something deeper than market analysis. The children's economics books published through Scholastic demonstrate industriousness and an unusual extracurricular achievement, but they do not demonstrate the kind of definite vision I invest behind. When Zuckerberg sat across from me, he had a specific theory about how real identity would create an unbreakable network. When Musk pitched reusable rockets, he could derive from first principles why the physics worked and the economics were inevitable. I see no analogous conviction here -- no theory about why SOX audit automation specifically resists commoditization, no contrarian view about the structure of the audit industry that most experts would reject.

The strongest bull case for Oxus is straightforward: SOX compliance is mandatory, painful, and expensive. Companies cannot opt out. The $300K-to-$1M+ annual outsourcing spend creates real pricing headroom, and a purpose-built AI tool that genuinely produces review-ready workpapers could save 70-90% of that cost. If Oxus can build deep domain expertise into its models faster than AuditBoard can bolt AI features onto its workflow platform, there is a window of opportunity. Audit data accumulated on the platform could create switching costs as teams build processes around Oxus's outputs. And the regulatory angle cuts interestingly: if the PCAOB or SEC eventually issues guidance endorsing specific AI-assisted audit methodologies, early movers who shape those standards could create a compliance moat. This is a real business opportunity. But a real business opportunity in a competitive market with a consensus thesis is precisely the kind of investment I avoid. The question is not whether Oxus can build a $20 million ARR company; the question is whether it can build a monopoly. I see no mechanism for that.

The technology itself -- an LLM-based document analysis and generation pipeline -- is commoditizing in real time. "Upload, analyze, generate, review" is the architecture of every vertical AI application in 2026. There is no proprietary technology visible here: no patents, no open-source repos demonstrating novel techniques, no evidence of a technical approach that could not be replicated by any competent team with access to the same foundation models and audit domain expertise. The 10x improvement, if it exists, is on cost -- not on capability. Cost advantages driven by cheaper compute rather than proprietary technology erode as the underlying models become ubiquitous. When I backed Stripe, the Collisons had built a developer API that was an order of magnitude simpler to integrate than anything available -- a genuine technological discontinuity. Oxus is applying generally available AI capabilities to a specific vertical. That is 1-to-n work dressed in 0-to-1 language.

This is a pass. Oxus occupies a competitive market with a consensus thesis, led by credentialed founders who have not yet articulated a contrarian view of the world, using technology that is rapidly commoditizing. The absence of a secret is not a gap that better execution can fill -- it is a structural condition that predicts competitive dynamics will destroy margins. Every other investor in the market sees the same opportunity, which means no one sees a secret, which means there is none.
