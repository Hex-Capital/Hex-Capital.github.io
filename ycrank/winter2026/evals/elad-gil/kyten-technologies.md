# Kyten Technologies -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural demand signal here is unusually concrete for a pre-seed company. The U.S. procured 50,000 UAS in 2025, plans to acquire 200,000 more in 2027, and the Army has stated intentions to build one million drones over two to three years. Meanwhile, more than 90% of the U.S. drone battery supply chain runs through Chinese-made cells, and NDAA compliance is forcing reshoring. The Pentagon is developing a dedicated battery procurement strategy for 2026. This isn't speculative market-sizing from a pitch deck — these are documented procurement mandates creating a supply gap that must be filled by someone domestic. When I invested in Anduril, the structural observation was that Google's Project Maven cancellation left a vacuum in AI-for-defense. Here, the vacuum is different but equally structural: surge demand colliding with supply chain dependency on a geopolitical adversary. That's the kind of inflection point I track.

The founders are the right people for this specific gap. Six thousand battery packs deployed to low earth orbit at SpaceX is not a credential you can fabricate or approximate. Cooper McBride progressed from Tesla intern to leading 30-person engineering teams at SpaceX and Shield AI — that trajectory across multiple aerospace companies demonstrates both technical depth and organizational scaling. Lucas Maddox spent seven years at SpaceX/Starlink managing test and manufacturing challenges. Together they have the process knowledge to build what they're claiming. The founder-market fit is as tight as I've seen for a hardware company at this stage.

My concern is whether this is software-driven or software-aware. Kyten claims "software-defined automated manufacturing" to compress timelines from 12+ months to 3 weeks. If that speed advantage is real and the software is genuinely the competitive engine — if it enables rapid customization across pack geometries and chemistries in a way incumbents structurally cannot replicate — then this is a defensible, software-driven manufacturing company. But if the software is tooling layered on top of conventional battery pack assembly, this is a smart toaster. The dossier doesn't provide enough evidence to resolve this question. The 170x speed improvement is an extraordinary claim. Design and prototyping might compress to weeks, but aerospace qualification cycles are dictated by customer and regulatory requirements that don't bend to manufacturing speed.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where this company struggles most in my framework. Battery pack manufacturing is fundamentally a per-unit, per-program business. I don't see the clear path where Kyten's initial product becomes a distribution channel for additional products the way Stripe's payment API became a channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. There's a version of this where accumulated qualification data across hundreds of programs creates a design library that makes Kyten the default starting point for any new aerospace battery pack — a "picks and shovels" platform for the defense drone buildout. But that's speculative. As currently structured, each customer engagement is bespoke work, not a platform relationship that compounds.

The bull case I want to engage with seriously: if the DoD really needs a million drones in two to three years, and the legacy supply chain moves at 12-month cycles with high minimums, someone will capture the rapid-turnaround, high-mix, small-batch segment of this market. EaglePicher is optimized for large prime contractor relationships. Amprius and Lyten are cell manufacturers, not pack integrators. Packet Digital makes cells, not assembled packs with integrated BMS. Kyten sits in a specific layer — pack-level integration and manufacturing — where there may genuinely be no credible domestic competitor positioned for speed. If the founders can deliver the 3-week turnaround at production quality, they could become the default supplier for every defense drone program ramping on aggressive timelines. The switching costs in aerospace are real — once a battery pack is qualified into a platform, requalification is expensive and time-consuming. That creates stickiness.

But applying my single-miracle framework, this plan requires more than one thing to go right. They need to (1) raise sufficient capital to build manufacturing capacity against competitors with $50M-$425M+ in funding, (2) secure NDAA-compliant domestic cell supply in a constrained market, (3) actually deliver the claimed 3-week turnaround at aerospace qualification standards, and (4) win contracts against established primes and well-funded startups. That's not one miracle — it's a sequence of them. The capital intensity alone is a fundamental constraint. Battery pack manufacturing requires facilities, equipment, and inventory. A two-person team with pre-seed funding is competing against Packet Digital with a $50M DoD award. The structural demand is real, but capturing it requires resources this team doesn't yet have and a fundraising path that depends on demonstrating capabilities they haven't yet proven outside of SpaceX.

I like the timing, I like the founders' domain credibility, and I respect the regulatory moat that NDAA compliance creates. But this is a capital-intensive hardware manufacturing business requiring multiple sequential breakthroughs, with no Kyten-specific traction and limited platform potential. The Anduril parallel is instructive but imperfect — Anduril was fundamentally software-driven with AI as its core competitive engine. Kyten's "software-defined manufacturing" claim remains unsubstantiated. I'd want to see the first production packs shipped to a real customer, evidence that the 3-week timeline holds at aerospace qualification standards, and a clearer articulation of why software is the competitive engine rather than just operational tooling. The structural moment is genuine. The question is whether this team, at this capitalization level, can capture it before better-funded competitors do.
