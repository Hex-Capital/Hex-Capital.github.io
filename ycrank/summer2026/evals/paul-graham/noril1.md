# Nori -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Antonio Sitong Li is a solo-listed founder shipping a $1,288 wheeled bimanual robot into a category where 1X NEO is $20K and Unitree G1 is $16K, and that pairing — a real hardware schlep taken on by essentially one name on the YC page — is Nori's whole signature. The schlep is present. The animal is present in outline. The cofounder is missing. This is what a Kiko-shaped risk looks like when the founder has actually shipped an artifact first: not disqualifying, but not the profile that made me lean forward on Coinbase either.

Coinbase is the anchor on the schlep dimension, and the mechanism there was that Brian Armstrong wanted the regulatory grind everyone else in the batch was flinching from. Nori sits close to that rung for a non-obvious reason: consumer hardware at $1,288 with promised autonomy on dishes, laundry, and coffee is a manufacturing-and-BOM schlep that every software-only YC applicant this batch will route around. Manufactured in San Francisco, teleoperation-recorded training data, a skill-sharing marketplace concept — Nori is choosing atoms in a batch that mostly chose LLM wrappers. Where Nori diverges from the Coinbase rung is that Armstrong's schlep was compliance, a moat that compounds legally; Nori's schlep is hardware cost, which Unitree's $5,900 R1 shows Chinese OEMs are already grinding at greater scale.

The lowest rung is cofounder configuration, and the failure mechanism I keep re-extracting is the solo-founder pattern I wrote about in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) — the person who has no one to argue with at 2am when the first supplier defaults. Nori's YC page lists team size 4 but names only Antonio Sitong Li as founder; no hardware lead, no ML lead, no ops lead is publicly identifiable. Consumer robotics at $1,288 with a summer 2026 ship date is the exact context where solo-founder execution risk stops being philosophical and becomes a support-ticket and returns problem. The dossier does not name a technical cofounder, which under my usual read means there isn't one.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism I care about is: did the founder ship before the check arrived? Antonio Sitong Li shipped NORI L1 at a claimed sub-$1,000 price before L2, ran Founders Inc to 1.5M impressions and 2K users, and got @ycombinator to officially promote Nori L2 with "Small but capable, Nori L2 is coming next week." That is a real pre-funding artifact trail — not Airbnb cereal boxes, not the Dropbox HN video, but not nothing. What compounds over 18 months if this holds: each shipped SKU teaches Antonio how to ship the next one. What breaks it: hardware returns and warranty on 1,000 units of consumer robotics with a solo founder.

On organic-idea fit, the counterfactual is straightforward: for Nori to flip a tier here, Antonio would need to have documented a personal moment — his own dishes piling up in a Columbia dorm, a specific chore he built a robot to escape — the way Drew Houston forgot his USB stick. Instead the dossier gives Columbia Graphics Lab research on "VR-demonstrated task goals for robot teams," which is category-adjacent research fellowship work, not personal pain. Nori is closer to "researcher who identified an opening" than "user who built the thing for themselves," and that costs it a meaningful chunk of the 25 points.

On hacker genealogy, the single-anchor parallel is Drew Houston at Dropbox — a Columbia/MIT-caliber technical undergrad with lab credentials, published research, and a shipped prior product. Antonio's Columbia CS + Architecture BA and Columbia Data Science Institute fellowship under Eugene Wu sit in that neighborhood on paper. What's missing versus Houston is the visible hacker trail — no GitHub was found in the dossier's search, which for a robotics founder in 2026 is a real absence. Hackers leave repos; robotics hackers leave URDF files and teleoperation demos on YouTube. Nori has the credentials but not the trail.

My 36-month forecast on Nori: ships under 2,000 units, hits a consumer-hardware reliability and returns wall by month 18, and either goes dead or pivots into selling teleoperation-collected household-task training data to Figure or 1X — the humanoid incumbents whose $39B and $20K price tags need exactly the ground-truth dataset Nori's leader-follower rig produces. The mechanism: solo founder + consumer hardware + promised multi-task autonomy at 1/12th the BOM of competitors resolves the same way Kiko resolved — the artifact ships, the market signal is real, but the configuration cannot sustain scale, and the salvage value is the data flywheel not the SKU.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the Coinbase-shaped one: Antonio has already shipped L1 before L2, Unitree's R1 at $5,900 proves the sub-$16K wheeled band is a live market, and if the consumer robotics category ever cracks at a $1,288 price point Antonio will be the incumbent brand with a running skill-sharing marketplace. That case stings because Armstrong also looked underpowered on paper before he mailed me the Android wallet. What defeats it in Nori's dossier: Armstrong shipped a working prototype to overturn a rejection; Nori's promised autonomy across dishes, laundry, coffee, and multi-robot bed-making at $1,288 has no independent third-party review, and the arXiv "Nori Bot" academic reference at sub-$1,000 tells me the form factor is BOM-replicable, not defensible. One condition would flip my verdict: a named technical cofounder with published robotics-ML work joining before Demo Day, plus a video of Nori L2 autonomously completing one demoed task end-to-end without teleoperation. Absent that, this is a pass I would revisit if Antonio comes back for a second YC.
