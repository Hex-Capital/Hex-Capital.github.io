# Hammock -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Five companies have raised over $60 million combined to attack HSA/FSA wellness spending in the last eighteen months. Truemed took $34M from a16z in December 2025. Flex raised $18M. Sika Health, Burst — the list keeps going. Hammock enters this space backed by a16z Speedrun, which also backs Truemed. When I look at a deal and see that kind of capital concentration from overlapping investors in a regulatory-tailwind market, I know exactly what I'm looking at: consensus. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act created a gold rush, and gold rushes attract prospectors, not pioneers. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "help people use their HSA for wellness." They nod and reach for their checkbook. That's the opposite of the signal I follow.

Jesse Rose built Berry Street to $50M ARR navigating insurance eligibility for telenutrition — that's real. He knows provider networks, LMN workflows, HIPAA compliance, and the gritty operational complexity of healthcare payments. Will Dennis ran a $1B consumer subscription at WeightWatchers and built product at WhatsApp for two billion users. These are serious operators with serious credentials. But when I ask my opening question — tell me the story, not the business — what I hear is professional pattern-matching. Rose spotted that his Berry Street playbook (insurance navigation for covered services) could extend horizontally to HSA wellness. Dennis saw that his consumer health subscription expertise mapped to a new product category. That's career logic, not biographical inevitability. Rose leaving Berry Street at $50M ARR right after it closed $50M in funding is an unexplained transition that deserves a conversation, not a red flag necessarily, but it's the kind of story gap that matters to me. I want to know what happened — whether that departure was forging or friction.

The HSA system genuinely exploits information asymmetry — Americans forfeit $4.5 billion annually because the complexity defeats them. That resonates with my pattern. But Hammock isn't making the system visible the way Affirm made lending terms transparent or the way Carta made cap tables legible. Hammock automates compliance with an opaque system: it generates LMN paperwork so you can spend money you already own. Compare that to Carta, which didn't just help you navigate cap table complexity — it *became* the cap table infrastructure and made the entire structure transparent. Carta replaced the opaque system with a legible one. Hammock works within the opaque system's rules, which means its value depends on those rules persisting. The OBBB Act already made gym memberships HSA-eligible without LMNs up to $500 per person. If Congress expands that exemption to massage, supplements, recovery tech — which the regulatory direction suggests — Hammock's core LMN automation becomes less necessary. You don't want to build a company whose moat erodes as the system it navigates becomes more transparent on its own.

The strongest bull case is Rose's founder-market fit. Berry Street is essentially the vertical proof that this horizontal play works — navigate insurance eligibility, connect patients with covered providers, automate the paperwork layer. If Rose can replicate that playbook across the full HSA wellness category and achieve custodial status for the HSA card, Hammock gets Carta-style switching costs: users won't transfer balances, re-establish investments, and rebuild spending history. The consumer-side approach (own the card, work at any merchant) is structurally superior to Truemed's merchant-integration model if it works, because it doesn't require convincing each wellness brand to integrate. That's a real insight. But it requires becoming or partnering with an HSA custodian — a regulated banking function — and the dossier shows no evidence that this infrastructure is built or even in progress. Hammock is in waitlist phase. The $4,000-in-eligible-expenses claim on the website may be modeled, not measured. Everything about the moat is theoretical.

Hammock's impact profile serves people who already have HSAs, already spend on Equinox and Eight Sleep and Oura rings, and already have employer-sponsored health coverage. The testimonial on the website references saving $3,200 a year on an Equinox membership — this is affluent wellness optimization, not expanding access to people locked out of a system. I believe every investment is impact investing, but the impact here flows to the already-comfortable, not to people navigating systems without power or information.

The dimension that most decisively shapes my view is contrarian conviction. Hammock operates in the most consensus-attractive corner of fintech right now — HSA wellness, post-regulatory-tailwind, with over $60M in competitor funding and a16z on both sides of the market. Rose and Dennis are competent, experienced operators who will likely build a functional product. But I invest my own money in founders who enter spaces that make other investors recoil, not spaces where other investors are already crowding in. Nothing about Hammock requires courage to articulate at a dinner party. The biographical forge is professional, not personal — strong domain expertise without the survival-born conviction that tells me a founder will endure when the consensus capital moves to the next tailwind.
