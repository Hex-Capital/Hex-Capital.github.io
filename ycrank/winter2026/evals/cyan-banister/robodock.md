# RoboDock -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The detail that stopped me in Zinny Weli's background wasn't Stanford or Zipline or Amazon -- it was Rwanda. She partnered with Ampersand to build the first EV battery swap station in East Africa for motorcycle taxi drivers. That's the kind of biographical thread I pull on. Someone who goes to Rwanda to solve charging infrastructure for people who actually need affordable transportation has a different relationship to this problem than someone who just sees a TAM slide about autonomous vehicles. It tells me something about what drives her. But I have to be honest: that's a single data point in an otherwise impressive-but-conventional Stanford robotics trajectory. I don't have the survival story here. I don't know what hardship forged these founders' commitment. Both Zinny and Celine have elite credentials and relevant experience at exactly the right companies -- but when I'm writing my own check, I need to feel that the founder would keep building this even if everything went wrong, and what I'm seeing is strong professional preparation, not the kind of biographical necessity that makes someone unkillable.

The founder-market fit, I should say, is genuinely excellent. Zinny literally designed autonomous drone charging infrastructure at Zipline and built the charging system for Amazon's home robot. Celine integrated actuator and sensor systems for autonomous semi-trucks at Plus and has deployed in genuinely harsh field conditions -- rural Indonesia, heritage sites in Peru, wildlife reserves in China. These two have built, with their own hands, the exact systems RoboDock needs to work. That kind of embodied technical knowledge matters in hardware robotics. And they've known each other seven years, which is a better foundation than most co-founder relationships I see at this stage.

Here's where I get stuck, though: this doesn't feel like an unpopular bet. When I look at the competitive landscape -- Rocsys with $42.7 million raised and a commercial product already launched, InductEV acquired for over $100 million, EVAR with Samsung backing, even Hyundai and Tesla building their own solutions -- I see a category where the consensus has already formed. The insight that "autonomous vehicles need automated depots" isn't hard to arrive at. It's the logical next infrastructure layer, and a lot of smart, well-capitalized teams have already arrived at it. Compare this to when I wrote my first check into SpaceX, or when we backed Crusoe when they wanted to mine Bitcoin on gas flares -- those were ideas that made people uncomfortable. Nobody was confused about whether the opportunity existed; they thought the founders were crazy for pursuing it. RoboDock's opportunity isn't confusing or controversial. It's obvious. And obvious opportunities at the pre-seed stage, when you're competing against teams with $40M+ head starts, make me nervous.

The bull case -- and I want to be fair to it -- is that RoboDock's combined charging-plus-inspection platform is genuinely broader than what Rocsys offers, and the fleet-agnostic positioning fills a real gap as mixed-brand AV depots emerge. If Waymo, Zoox, and whoever else are sharing depot infrastructure (which seems likely as the industry scales), they'll want a vendor that works across vehicle types, not OEM-specific solutions. The timing window is real: AV fleets are hitting a scale where manual depot operations become the bottleneck, and the next 2-3 years will determine who owns that infrastructure layer. If RoboDock can land one or two marquee depot deployments and build that closed-loop data advantage they describe, the switching costs in physical robotics are real. This could be the company that makes depot operations visible and manageable the way Carta made cap tables transparent -- taking an invisible back-office function and turning it into a platform. For that to work, though, they'd need to move fast against Rocsys's head start, and they'd need the largest AV operators to buy rather than build.

What gives me pause beyond the competitive dynamics is that RoboDock's current customer base appears to be... nobody, yet. The website metrics -- $1.2M recovered per depot, 30% energy reduction, 99% fleet uptime -- read as modeled projections, not customer-verified numbers. No named customers, no LOIs, no revenue signals. At pre-seed, I don't penalize absence of traction -- but in a space with funded competitors already deploying commercial products, the absence of even a pilot agreement is notable. I'd want to know: has Waymo or any fleet operator expressed concrete interest, or is this still a solution seeking its first deployment?

I respect the founders' preparation and the Rwanda thread gives me a flicker of the mission-driven energy I look for. But the honest assessment is that this is a competent team entering a space that's already competitive, without the anti-consensus quality that defines my best investments. The best opportunities I've backed looked bad to most investors at the time. This one looks good to most investors -- and that's exactly what makes me cautious.
