# Rex -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Rex is the canonical 2026 YC pass: an "AI agent for enterprise vertical" (Order-to-Cash) where the only thesis-positive signal is a mildly non-standard pricing instrument -- "outcome and SLA-based pricing" (rex.inc) -- bolted onto an otherwise consensus enterprise-AI cap table. Merlin Kafka and Lewis Blackwood spent four years building Sequence, an a16z-backed B2B fintech that just closed a $20M Series A in December 2025 for "AI agents to automate revenue operations" (Axios) -- which is to say, my Rex thesis has to survive the fact that the founders' own prior company is now a funded direct adjacent competitor. The high/low pairing on Rex is "structure slightly clever, category dead-on consensus," and that's a profile I almost never write a check into.

The Pass Example I keep in my head is almost verbatim Rex: "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], three months out of YC, tier-1 VC already in." Rex's Vercel Ventures investment and Guillermo Rauch personally picking Rex #1 out of 2,500 Vercel AI Accelerator applicants (Vercel blog, 2026) is precisely the signal that makes me MORE skeptical, not less -- Rauch is the most consensus-builder distribution channel in AI right now, and when the most consensus channel pre-selects you, the round price has already inflated past where I can generate alpha. Compare to Sublime Security, which I underwrote into enterprise security before the next AI-SOC consolidation wave actually arrived; the difference is Sublime was buying a category sleeve that generalist AI funds weren't pattern-matching yet, whereas Rex is buying the most pattern-matched sleeve of S26. The Vercel anointment is the markup signal, not the entry signal.

The Pass Example founder also had "no public writing I can find on why their take on agents is non-obvious," and Rex repeats that pattern exactly: Merlin Kafka has no Twitter handle the dossier could find, Lewis Blackwood's GitHub stars couldn't be retrieved, neither founder has published a single opinionated essay on why O2C agents specifically fail where rules-based incumbents like HighRadius ($484M raised, $3.1B valuation) succeed. Rex's "$60B outsourced function" framing on the YC page is the sanitized strategic-consulting deck language I de-rate by default -- it's the kind of TAM citation that any of fifty S26 finance-AI teams could have written. Domain depth from four years at Sequence is real, but it's the founders responding to opportunity, not pursuing a personal mission visible from outside the room.

On capital structure, Rex actually earns partial credit -- this is the dimension where I'd defend the entry to a skeptical LP. The 18-month mechanism: "outcome and SLA-based pricing -- pay for outcomes only" (rex.inc) genuinely cannibalizes the per-seat ACV that HighRadius, BlackLine, and Versapay ($42M revenue, 2024) all depend on, which means Rex can compound margin while incumbents are structurally blocked from matching the pricing without nuking their existing book. The compounding question is whether Rex collects enough cross-customer exception patterns from its self-reported ">$500M in receivables from F100" deployments before Qurrent (already cited at "$1.5B in annual payments, 2.7M AI agents deployed," TipRanks 2025) eats the same workflow data moat. Outcome-pricing is venture-shaped here in a way that a standard SaaS seat tier wouldn't be.

The counterfactual on founder distribution: Rex would have to land an actual named F100 logo with a public case study (not the WorkOS enterprise-readiness post, which is about auth plumbing, not customer reference) before the founder-brand dimension flipped from "absent" to "present" in any form -- and even then, the brand on the cap table would be the customer's, not Merlin Kafka's. Rex's homepage dashboard showing "342 customers / 1,909 invoices / $18.4M receivables managed" appears illustrative rather than aggregate, and the YC-cited ">$500M from F100 companies" lists no logos. There's no newsletter, no podcast, no community footprint, no inbound channel -- Rex is a pure enterprise sales motion priced like a labor replacement, which is a business model, not a moat.

On category sleeve, the single-anchor parallel is the Joe Lonsdale podcast line: "AI is just more internet" -- Rex sits in the exact sub-sleeve I've re-weighted away from for two years, AI-agents-on-top-of-LLMs for an enterprise back-office function. Compare directly to Lightning Labs at seed, where I was underwriting a piece of infrastructure that consensus VCs had abandoned because the post-ICO crash made crypto unfundable; Rex is the precise inverse, a category where every generalist YC partner in 2026 has three competing deals in their pipeline. The Sequence conflict makes this worse: my founders' prior company, in which a16z has a direct interest, is now selling "AI agents for revenue operations" into an overlapping buyer profile. That's not a market sleeve I have differentiated underwriting on -- that's a sleeve where I'm guaranteed to be paying consensus prices to take adjacent-conflict execution risk.

Rex 36-month forecast: $8-20M ARR by mid-2029, acquired by HighRadius or BlackLine for $60-120M as the incumbents need an AI-native agent layer to defend per-seat ACV against Qurrent and a half-dozen S26/W27 clones. That's a fine outcome for a Series A-led cap table at a $30-40M post; it's a structurally mediocre outcome for me as a price-sensitive seed entering at whatever Vercel Ventures + YC + the Rauch halo have already marked the round to. The forecast follows directly from the signature: clever-pricing-in-consensus-category exits via incumbent acquisition, not via the regatta tacking into a category-defining independent business.

The bull case that stings: Sublime Security looked like an enterprise security AI seed in a crowded category too, and I wrote it; Merlin Kafka and Lewis Blackwood have demonstrably built and sold this exact buyer profile at Sequence; Vercel #1-of-2500 plus a live F100 deployment managing $500M in receivables is the kind of pre-seed traction I should weight heavily per the calibration instructions; outcome-based pricing IS a non-standard instrument and arguably qualifies as "venture moments" framing where regulatory/operational complexity (50-state ERP heterogeneity, SOC 2 pending, GDPR EU residency) constitutes the moat. Fair. But Sublime entered a sleeve before consensus -- Rex enters mid-consensus with Guillermo Rauch on the megaphone, and the Sequence-as-direct-adjacent-competitor situation is a co-founder-dynamic risk dressed up as an alumni-network advantage. The verdict flips to Invest if Rex closes a named, publicly-referenced Fortune 100 logo at >$1M ACV on the outcome-pricing model AND the SOC 2 Type II lands before the next round prices -- absent both, this is a structurally consensus deal at a structurally consensus price, and I pass.
