# PLAN0 AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

PLAN0 AI is the dimensional signature I see most often in Spring 2026 YC batches and pass on most often: a sane, finance-trained founder (Arash Barati, ex-Drake Real Estate Partners economist, ex-Explora Ventures) executing a consensus-hot AI workflow play that Autodesk, Procore, Trimble, or CoStar can swallow as a feature inside eighteen months. High on founder rationality, low on structural durability. That pairing has a name in my book — "perfect for Adobe."

Consensus mispricing is where PLAN0 AI hurts most, and this is my entry point. Compare the ladder. High rung: Solana at four cents in 2018 when every smart crypto person told me Ethereum had won — risk fully baked in. Low rung: the half-dozen AI copilot wrappers I pass on every demo day because Adobe is sitting in the front row taking notes. PLAN0 AI sits on the low rung with one hand on the mid rail. The dossier pins it there: Provision already raised $7M USD for the same "AI co-pilots for pre-construction estimates" wedge, and Togal, Beam, and Kreo were benchmarked head-to-head in Robotics & Automation News in February 2026. That's six named competitors in a category Beck Technology is publishing tailwind reports about. The risk is not in the price; the hype is. I want to buy SOL at four cents, not buy the ninth AI estimating tool at YC-batch markup.

Structural durability is the second ladder where PLAN0 AI lands badly, and this is where I apply the AI kill test ruthlessly. High rung: Metropolis — AI-powered parking, but the parking infrastructure is physical and cannot be replicated by an LLM. Low rung: any pure SaaS that wraps a vision model over PDFs an incumbent already owns. PLAN0 AI is the low rung wearing a Bloomberg costume. Autodesk owns the plan files. CoStar owns the cost data. Procore owns the project workflow. Trimble owns the BIM and the estimating seats. Every one of those incumbents can ship "30-minute AI takeoff with localized cost forecast" as a checkbox feature, and the dossier itself flags this — "incumbent substitution from data owners." When the moat thesis is "we'll build the proprietary localized cost database from scratch" against four parties who already have it, that is not a moat, that is a roadmap for an acqui-hire.

Primitive creation: Venmo became a verb. Patreon created a behavior. PLAN0 AI compresses a two-week takeoff into thirty minutes versus Beam AI's two-to-three-day human-reviewed turnaround. That's speed, not category. Calling it "the CoStar or Bloomberg of construction" is aspiration borrowed from companies that took decades to compound real-world data flywheels — and CoStar already exists. PLAN0 AI sits below the Patreon mid rung, well clear of the "better CRM" floor only because the cost-forecast layer is at least an attempt at a data product rather than a workflow tool.

Sanity-insanity calibration is PLAN0 AI's strongest dimension and it is still only mid-tier. High rung is Raj on Solana — engineer through three companies, calm, pitching something every smart person called dead. Low rung is the incubator-matched co-founder pair with no shared history. Arash Barati maps cleanly to the buyer persona — Drake REP did real estate PE quantitative modeling, Explora Ventures put him inside the family-office investor universe PLAN0 AI claims to sell to. That's real founder-market fit and the "quant finance inspired neural networks" framing is at least authentic to his background, not LinkedIn cosplay. But the idea is not insane — it is the median Spring 2026 AI proptech pitch. And Shervin Barati, the CTO, has no verifiable public footprint at all per the dossier's WebSearch — likely a brother given the surname, which is fine for commitment, less fine for technical diligence I cannot do from the outside. Sane person, reasonable idea. That ratio doesn't excite me.

Capital discipline I cannot judge from the dossier — no burn data, no pricing page, "Hiring: False" on the YC page suggests at least reactive headcount. Default applies. Gusto is the high rung; Allbirds is the low rung; PLAN0 AI is unobserved. Where it gets dangerous is downstream — Provision's $7M chest will force PLAN0 AI to either raise a large A to compete on enterprise sales or get squeezed. The factory-farm dynamic is exactly the trap I warn about.

My 36-month forecast: PLAN0 AI reaches $0.5–2M ARR with a handful of mid-market GCs, fails to raise a competitive Series A against Provision and incumbent feature releases from Procore or Autodesk, and gets acqui-hired by Trimble, Procore, or a Tier-2 estimating incumbent for sub-$30M. Mechanism: the AI kill test fires on schedule — Autodesk Construction Cloud or Procore ships a "PLAN0-equivalent" feature bundled into existing seats, the differentiated 30-minute turnaround compresses to parity, and the cost-forecast claim ("trade-level accuracy 24 months out") fails to validate against actual non-stationary commodity markets. Arash's family-office network gets him in early doors but not durable enterprise contracts.

The strongest bull case stings, and I want to be honest about it. Slack violated my consensus-mispricing rule and returned billions because the primitive was real. The bull argument here: Arash's Drake REP and Explora rolodex unlocks anchor customers fast, the localized cost-data flywheel compounds because every new estimate refines the database faster than CoStar's manual ingestion ever did, and "the Bloomberg of construction" actually is a primitive nobody has built because the underlying cost data has historically been trapped in spreadsheets. CoStar took thirty years; AI lets PLAN0 AI leapfrog. That mechanism is not crazy. It is, however, dismantled by one fact in the dossier: Provision already has $7M for the identical thesis, and Autodesk, CoStar, and Trimble each control proprietary datasets PLAN0 AI would need to replicate. Slack won because no incumbent had Slack's data — every incumbent has PLAN0 AI's data. The flip condition: if PLAN0 AI signs a Tier-1 GC (Turner, AECOM, Bechtel, Skanska) on a multi-project license at six-figure ACV before Series A, I revisit. Absent that, pass.
