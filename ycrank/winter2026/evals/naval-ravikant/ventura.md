# Ventura -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founder pairing here is the most interesting signal, and it's genuinely strong. Swen Koller built and exited bravo — a B2B SaaS product that digitized ordering for food wholesalers still stuck on phone, email, and fax. He grew it to 1,000+ active restaurant users moving CHF 20M+ in goods through the platform. Now he's running the same playbook in industrial distribution with better AI tools and a bigger market. That's not classroom knowledge. That's scar tissue from selling software to an industry that doesn't want software, from integrating with systems that weren't designed to be integrated with, from understanding why a wholesaler's operations manager resists change and what breaks their trust. Jack Collins complements this with genuine ML engineering depth — nine years of production NLP systems, creator of magentic (2.4K GitHub stars, a real open-source LLM framework developers actually use), and founding engineer at Develop Health which hit $7M ARR in 14 months with five people. This is a team where each founder's specific knowledge would take a competitor years to replicate. The question isn't whether they can build the product. It's whether the product creates the kind of leverage that produces asymmetric returns.

That's where my enthusiasm cools. Ventura is efficiency automation, not leverage creation. It lets sales reps stop doing data entry — a real pain point, a real time savings, but not a new category of human capability. Compare this to what Notion did: one person with the right tool suddenly produces output that previously required a team. Or what Replit does: someone who would never have become a software engineer can now build software. Ventura makes existing distributors faster at existing workflows. Each AI agent deployment serves one customer's quoting pipeline. There are no network effects — distributor A's data doesn't make the product better for distributor B. The leverage is code leverage (LLMs parsing documents at scale), which is real but generic. Any company deploying LLMs gets that leverage. The structural question is: does the product's value compound, or is it a fixed efficiency gain that gets priced into a SaaS subscription?

The competitive landscape tells you this isn't contrarian. Distro (YC, same market), Endeavor AI ($7M seed from Craft Ventures), Go Autonomous ($13.4M, enterprise customers like Grundfos), Parspec ($31.5M, 288 customers) — four funded competitors in adjacent or overlapping territory. "AI agents for back-office automation" is the most consensus category in enterprise software right now. Nobody smart is dismissing this idea; they're funding four versions of it. When I invested in Uber at a $4M valuation, ride-sharing was illegal. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. Where's the equivalent disagreement here? The Deloitte report estimates 75-100 basis points of EBIT improvement from AI quoting — that's a consulting firm validating the thesis, which means the thesis is already priced in. You get paid for being right first, not for being right along with Deloitte.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. If Koller's playbook from food wholesale transfers cleanly — and it might, because wholesale distribution has structural similarities across verticals — this team could acquire customers faster than better-funded competitors who lack that operational intuition. Nine ERP integrations for a two-person team is either vaporware or evidence of extraordinary engineering velocity, and Collins's track record suggests the latter. The "live within days" deployment claim, if true, is a meaningful wedge against ERP vendors whose implementations take months. Industrial distribution is a $7.86 trillion market where the incumbents (SAP, Oracle, Epicor) have no incentive to build narrow vertical AI agents, creating a durable gap in the market structure. If Ventura accumulates enough per-customer data — pricing patterns, catalog matching accuracy, customer-specific workflows — those switching costs could compound into a real moat. The best version of this company looks like the picks-and-shovels layer for AI-driven industrial distribution, and the market is large enough that even a small penetration rate produces a meaningful outcome.

But I keep returning to the leverage test. This is a vertical SaaS company with an AI layer — closer to "Salesforce but for distributors" than to a platform that creates a new form of human capability. The technology uses generally available LLM capabilities; the differentiation lives in ERP connector coverage and workflow knowledge, both of which are replicable with capital and time. A well-funded competitor with distribution expertise could build this in 12-18 months. The founding team's specific knowledge is the best thing about this deal, and it's real — but the company they're building with that knowledge produces linear, not exponential, returns. I respect the founders. I think they'll build a real business. But the asymmetric upside I need — where nine investments go to zero and the tenth goes 1,000x — requires either a new category of leverage or a contrarian timing advantage that compounds. I don't see either here.
