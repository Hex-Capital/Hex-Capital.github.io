# General Legal -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The founder-specific-knowledge signal here is the strongest I've seen in the legal AI category. Three people who practiced at Fenwick and Cooley -- the exact firms that draft the exact contracts General Legal now reviews -- then pivoted into AI engineering, built CoCounsel at Casetext, and watched Thomson Reuters write a $650M check for their work. Ryan Walker has a PhD in mathematics and served as CTO. Javed Qadrud-Din went from Harvard Law to Director of ML with published computer science research. J.P. Mohler built the first AI tool adopted by a federal judicial system. This isn't domain expertise -- it's the kind of compound knowledge that forms when someone spends a decade oscillating between two fields that rarely intersect. They know where contract review breaks down because they've done it by hand at elite firms, and they know where AI breaks down because they've built production legal AI that real attorneys use. That combination can't be hired for.

The problem is what they're doing with that knowledge. General Legal is a law firm. Not a platform. Not infrastructure. A firm. Every contract requires a licensed, U.S.-barred attorney. The "10x efficiency" from their AI workflows is real and impressive, but it's labor productivity improvement, not leverage in the sense that matters for asymmetric returns. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question answered made the knowledge graph more valuable with zero marginal human cost, or to Replit, where one engineer's deployment serves millions of coders simultaneously. At General Legal, serving ten times more clients requires -- at minimum -- one-tenth as many attorneys per contract, but still attorneys. The unit economics can be excellent, the margins can be strong, and this can be a very good business. But "very good business" and "1000x outcome" are different categories.

The timing is what concerns me most. YC's 2025 Request for Startups literally said: "Start your own law firm, staff it with AI agents, and compete with existing law firms." When the accelerator publishes your thesis as a prompt, you're not contrarian -- you're consensus. Lawhive just raised $60M with $35M ARR and is expanding to the U.S. Harvey sits at $8B. Spellbook raised $50M from Khosla. Legal AI drew $6B in funding in 2025 with 70% going to AI tools. Robin AI's failure is instructive -- the pure-software approach collapsed -- but that just means the market has already learned the lesson General Legal is implementing. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was illegal and investors thought it was insane. When I backed Bitcoin, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. The best returns come from being right when the crowd is gloomy. Here the crowd is euphoric.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If AI capabilities continue improving at the current rate, the attorney-to-contract ratio could shift from 10x to 100x -- attorneys become supervisors spot-checking AI output rather than producers drafting and reviewing. At that point, General Legal starts to look like a platform with a regulatory moat that no pure-software competitor can breach. The accumulated contract data, client risk profiles, and workflow refinements compound with each engagement. And the Casetext team has already demonstrated they can build AI that improves with scale. If the leverage ratio shifts from 10x to 100x, the economics transform from "efficient law firm" to "legal infrastructure with an attorney-verification layer." That's a genuinely different business, and this is the team most likely to build it.

But I invest based on the architecture that exists, not the architecture that might emerge. Today, this is a services firm with AI augmentation and exceptional founders. The regulatory moat cuts both ways -- it prevents pure-software competitors from entering, but it also prevents General Legal from operating without the very constraint that limits its leverage. The $500 flat fee is compelling for customers and creates switching costs, but the firm bears malpractice risk on every engagement, and complex contracts that blow past the flat-fee economics are inevitable. The founders are playing a long-term game -- leaving Thomson Reuters post-acquisition to restart together shows genuine conviction, not exit-seeking behavior. The integrity and long-term signals are clean. But the structural leverage question and the consensus timing make this a neutral proposition for my framework. These are among the best founders I'd see in legal tech. I'm just not sure legal tech, structured as a firm, produces the kind of asymmetric outcome I optimize for.
