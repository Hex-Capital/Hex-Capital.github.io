# Concorda -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Legal AI is the most consensus space in venture right now. Legaltech funding hit $4.3B in 2025, up 54% from the prior year. AI adoption among legal professionals jumped from 19% to 79%. Harvey sits at an $11B valuation with $190M in ARR. EvenUp crossed $2B. Crimson — a YC-backed startup in London — is building literally the same product with the same positioning: "AI associate for litigation lawyers." When I look at Concorda, I see two credentialed founders entering a space where every sophisticated investor is already eagerly competing for deals. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "AI for lawyers." They reach for their checkbook. That's a signal I pay attention to, and it's the wrong one.

Samuel Oh went to Harvard for CS, UChicago for law, practiced at Avantech, and says he "spent years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way." Ke Ma went to Cornell, Northwestern for her JD, did a stint at McKinsey, then prosecuted cases as an Assistant State's Attorney in Cook County. These are accomplished, intelligent people. But when I ask my question — tell me the story, not the business — what I hear is professional frustration, not biographical inevitability. They worked in litigation, found it inefficient, and decided to build software. That's a market observation made by people well-positioned to make it, which is different from being forged by a problem you cannot walk away from. Neither founder's story suggests they'd be doing this without funding or without the current AI wave making it possible. The absence of a crucible doesn't mean they'll fail, but it means my strongest conviction signal — that this person was *made* for this problem — isn't firing.

What bothers me most about Concorda is the direction of value flow. The legal system is genuinely one of the most opaque, extractive structures in American life — people pay $500 an hour for work they can't evaluate, in a system they can't navigate, with outcomes they can't predict. That's an invisible system I would love to see disrupted. But Concorda doesn't attack that opacity on behalf of the people being extracted from. It makes lawyers faster at producing motions, discovery requests, and claim charts. The beneficiary is the law firm's margin, not the client's access to justice. Compare this to Affirm, which took the hidden structure of credit card fees and made it transparent *to the consumer*. Or Carta, which gave *founders* visibility into their own cap tables. Concorda gives power to the powerful side of an already asymmetric relationship. The hidden system stays hidden — it just runs more efficiently.

The $204K in annualized revenue within three months is the strongest signal in this dossier, and I want to be honest about that. At pre-seed, real revenue from real clients is meaningful. The dual services-plus-SaaS model is clever as a revenue wedge — deliver legal services, learn the workflow, convert to platform. But that same dual model creates a liability exposure that concerns me: Concorda is practicing law, not just selling software. Professional malpractice risk, state bar regulation, and the operational burden of running both a legal practice and a product company with two people — that's a lot of surface area. And the defensibility question nags. LLM-based document generation is increasingly commoditized. Harvey could ship litigation-specific modules tomorrow with their existing law firm relationships at 8 of the top 10 US firms. The firm-specific voice models and case data could create switching costs over time, but that's an inference, not evidence, and it assumes Concorda wins enough clients to build that data before incumbents close the gap.

The strongest bull case for Concorda is that vertical depth beats horizontal breadth — Harvey and CoCounsel spread across all legal functions while Concorda goes deep on litigation execution. If Oh and Ma can build the end-to-end litigation operating system with enough domain specificity that switching becomes painful, they win their niche even if Harvey dominates the broader market. The early revenue validates that litigation teams will pay for this. And the legal profession's conservatism means firms that adopt early and train their workflows around Concorda might stick. But this is a thesis about wedge dynamics in a market where a $11B-valued competitor with $190M ARR is already inside your target customer's building. I've been burned by products without structural moats — HQ Trivia had the right product at the right time and still died because attention-based businesses without infrastructure underneath are fragile. Concorda's moat today is domain knowledge, which is real but replicable.

Concorda's decisive gap for me is the combination of consensus market and absent biographical forge. Oh and Ma are qualified to build this — but qualification isn't conviction, and the legal AI space isn't a hard space that tests whether founders have the survival instinct to endure when the market turns against them. The revenue traction earns respect but doesn't change the structural picture. This is a competent team in a crowded, well-funded space, building a product that serves gatekeepers rather than disrupting them. I wish them well, and if Oh or Ma ever calls me from the other side of a near-death moment — cap table in ruins, Harvey eating their lunch, still building — I'd take that call with genuine interest. The forge creates the founder. Right now, the forge hasn't happened yet.
