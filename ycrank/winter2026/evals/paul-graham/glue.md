# Glue -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Glue is what's missing. The company describes itself as open source — their stated distribution strategy is "open-source penetration" to build trust and adoption. But no public repository exists. No GitHub org, no repo under either founder's account, nothing. For a company whose entire go-to-market depends on developers discovering and adopting their open-source tool, the absence of the actual open-source project is not a minor gap. It's like saying your distribution strategy is word of mouth when nobody has used the product yet. Meanwhile, the CTO's GitHub bio still reads "Founder @ Willow (YC W26)" rather than Glue, which suggests the current product identity may be days or weeks old. A pivot isn't inherently bad — Justin.tv became Twitch — but combined with zero visible artifact of the product existing, it raises the question of how far along this actually is.

How did these founders find this problem? This is where I start, and the answer concerns me. The dossier describes the problem as: AI coding agents are reshaping frontend development, but there's no design tool where agents are the primary user. That's a perfectly reasonable market observation. It's also the kind of observation you'd arrive at by spending a weekend reading about the "vibe coding" trend, mapping the competitive landscape, and identifying the gap. Perbhat built RAG agents at Microsoft Copilot Studio, which gives him adjacency to agent architectures. Tejas was an engineer at Meta and Microsoft. Both are capable. But there's no moment of organic discovery here — no "I was using Claude Code to build a UI and kept running into this specific wall." The problem was derived from analyzing what's missing in the AI dev tools ecosystem, not from a personal encounter with friction. That's the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" — sitting down and trying to think of startup ideas, rather than noticing a problem that won't leave you alone.

The schlep test is equally telling, and it points the wrong direction. Building a visual design canvas that syncs with code and accepts agent inputs is technically interesting work — exactly the kind of project that would attract a smart engineer for fun. Compare this to Stripe, where the schlep was dealing with banks, fraud prevention, PCI compliance, and money transmission licenses. Nobody wanted to touch that. Here, the technical challenge of rendering live UI previews on an infinite canvas is the kind of problem hackathon teams build proofs-of-concept for on weekends. It's appealing, not repulsive. When the core work is fun rather than tedious, you don't get the structural competition barrier that schlep blindness creates. And indeed, the competitive landscape confirms this: Lovable has raised $553M, Bolt $105M, Cursor $2.3B, Vercel $563M. These companies and their well-funded teams could build an agent-facing design canvas as a feature. Glue is entering the most crowded corridor in software right now with a two-person team and YC's standard check.

The strongest bull case for Glue would go something like this: the "persistent visual canvas for iterative agent collaboration" is a genuinely distinct product category that none of the existing players occupy. Figma is for designers talking to designers. Cursor is for developers in code. v0 generates components from prompts but doesn't persist. Lovable generates whole apps. If the EPD workflow truly collapses into a single "builder" role — and there's a reasonable argument it will — then being the canonical visual surface where that builder collaborates with their agent could be enormous. The open-source approach could generate Cursor-like bottoms-up adoption. And the founders, despite lacking a dramatic organic discovery story, do have the right mix of skills: AI agent architecture experience plus frontend engineering at scale. If they ship something genuinely good and the developer community latches onto it, the timing could be perfect. But "if" is doing a lot of work in that paragraph, and the market is hot enough that the signal-to-noise ratio for a new entrant is terrible.

Both founders can clearly write code — Meta and Microsoft engineering roles, a national mobile app development competition win, self-taught ML work — so the technical founder criterion is met in principle. But thirteen and twelve public repos with two and six followers respectively is a thin GitHub presence for founders whose strategy depends on open-source community adoption. The most successful developer tool founders I've funded tend to already have some gravity in the developer community before they launch. The founders' shared tenure at Microsoft provides a real co-founder relationship, which matters. But the overall picture is of competent big-company engineers who identified a gap in a hot market, not builders who stumbled into an unavoidable problem and couldn't stop working on it.

I'm passing. Not because the founders aren't smart or the market opportunity isn't real — it might be — but because this has the signature of an idea generated by the startup idea machine. Hot category, logical gap in the competitive landscape, no organic backstory, no evidence of user pull, possible recent pivot, and competitors with billions in funding. The best startups in crowded markets win through obsessive founder-problem fit and years of accumulated insight. Nothing in this dossier signals that kind of depth.
