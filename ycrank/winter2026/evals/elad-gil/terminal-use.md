# Terminal Use -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Terminal Use is not the product or the team -- it's the competitive landscape. E2B has raised $32M, Daytona $24M, Runloop $7M, Modal Labs $111M, and OpenAI itself is bundling hosted runtimes through Frontier. That's $174M+ in funded competition for what amounts to "agent deployment infrastructure." When I evaluate a market, the first question is whether it's non-obvious. This one isn't. Every VC in San Francisco can see that AI agents need hosting infrastructure. The TAM slides all show the same numbers -- $7.6 billion growing to $183 billion at 50% CAGR. When five funded companies and a platform provider are already pursuing essentially the same thesis, I'm not looking at a non-obvious market at an inflection point. I'm looking at a consensus-hot category where capital is flowing freely and the structural advantage will go to whoever controls the framework, not whoever builds the best hosting layer.

The "Vercel for background agents" framing is crisp, but the analogy actually reveals the core problem. Vercel succeeded because Guillermo Rauch created Next.js -- he controlled the framework that drove demand for his deployment platform. Terminal Use doesn't control any framework. They're building orchestration for Claude Agent SDK and Codex SDK, both of which are owned by companies with strong incentives to vertically integrate hosting. OpenAI has already done this with Frontier. If Anthropic bundles agent hosting with the Claude Agent SDK -- which is exactly the kind of "market-ending move" I track -- Terminal Use's value proposition as a third-party layer collapses. The differentiation claims (persistent filesystem storage, task-pinned versioning, manifest-based sync) are engineering features, not structural moats. E2B started ephemeral and is expanding to persistent. Daytona already offers stateful sandboxes. Within a year, every competitor in this space will have feature parity on filesystems.

The strongest signal for Terminal Use is the founding team's Palantir background. All three co-founders came from Palantir, and Vivek Raja specifically led deployment of "one of the largest agent use cases across US hospitals." That's unusually direct founder-market fit -- most agent infrastructure founders have built developer tools or cloud infrastructure, not actually deployed production agents at enterprise scale. The co-founders went to university together and worked together at Palantir, which reduces the co-founder conflict risk I weight heavily at pre-seed. These are genuine positives. But even strong operator backgrounds need a structural market advantage to win, and I've seen great teams repeatedly get outcompeted in consensus-hot categories where the moat is capital and distribution, not engineering talent.

The bull case would require believing that agent infrastructure fragments along the lines of "long-running background agents with filesystems" versus "ephemeral sandboxes" -- that these are genuinely distinct categories, not features. If the production agent workload pattern really does require fundamentally different infrastructure primitives (persistent state, version pinning, lifecycle management) that sandbox providers and platform providers won't prioritize, then Terminal Use could own that niche the way Vercel owned the frontend DX niche even though AWS existed. The Woz product -- deploying agents through Slack with MCP integration -- is a clever distribution wedge that creates workflow switching costs. If agents become embedded in daily team operations through Slack, ripping that out becomes painful. But I see too many "ifs" stacking up here: if frameworks don't bundle hosting, if filesystem agents are a distinct category, if Slack remains the right surface, if the feature differentiation persists against well-funded competitors. That's approaching multi-miracle territory.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has potential in principle -- agent hosting could expand into monitoring, testing, compliance, cost management -- but Terminal Use's current positioning doesn't demonstrate the kind of distribution thinking I look for. The CLI-first approach is standard for developer tools. The Slack integration is interesting but creates platform dependency, not distribution leverage. There's no evidence the founders are thinking about how their initial product becomes a channel for additional products, which at this stage I'd expect to see at least articulated, even if not yet built.

I'm passing on this one. The market timing is genuinely right -- production agent workloads are just starting to materialize -- but "right timing" is necessary, not sufficient. The structural moment is real, but it's visible to everyone, which means the market will be won through capital, distribution advantages, and framework control. Terminal Use has none of these today, and the path to acquiring them runs through competitors with 10-30x more funding. The Palantir pedigree is the right background for this problem, but I need a structural reason to believe this team wins the market, not just a capability reason to believe they can build the product.
