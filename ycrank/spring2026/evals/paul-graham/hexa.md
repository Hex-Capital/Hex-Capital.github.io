# Hexa -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hexa's signature is the inverse of what I want: highest on schlep — ERP plumbing into SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, Epicor — and lowest on the hacker-genealogy tells that would tell me Ishaan Makkar and Auri Nayak are the right people to grind through that schlep. $9K MRR from three customers in six weeks is a real artifact, not a deck, and I respect it. But this is exactly the shape — real market, plausible wedge, opaque founders — that I score in the middle and then watch lose to better-resourced teams over 36 months.

The Coinbase (2012) analogy is what makes Hexa interesting on schlep. Brian Armstrong's moat wasn't the code; it was that he wanted to do KYC, BSA, and state-by-state money-transmitter licensing while every other YC applicant in his batch was building consumer apps. Hexa's equivalent grind is ERP catalog reconciliation across SAP and Epicor for industrial distributors who still run on email threads and spreadsheets — "54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets," per Ishaan Makkar's own LinkedIn framing. That is real schlep. Where Hexa diverges from the Coinbase rung is that Armstrong personally absorbed the compliance work as the founder's job; Hexa's dossier shows no evidence that Ishaan or Auri have ever sat inside a distributor's quoting workflow, which is the difference between a schlep you've chosen and a schlep you've described.

The pass example I keep in my head — the two-MBA team with the polished AI-wrapper deck and a $50B TAM slide — fails on organic founder-pain, and Hexa is closer to that failure mechanism than I would like. Drew Houston lost his USB stick at MIT and built Dropbox (2007) for himself; the Collisons had been hacking commerce since Auctomatic (2007) before Stripe (2010). Hexa's origin story, as best the dossier reveals it, is that Ishaan Makkar read that distributors use spreadsheets and decided to build agents for them. No prior operating role at a distributor. No GitHub trail. No Twitter. The thesis is correct but it's a *spotted* thesis, not a *suffered* one, and that is the exact failure mode I flagged in "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012).

On animal resourcefulness, $9K MRR across three customers in six weeks is the dossier's strongest fact and it does work — three signed industrial distributors did not appear by accident, and somebody on the Hexa team is running a manual sales schlep I respect. But the cereal-box pattern from Airbnb (2009) is not just *revenue* — it is *revenue via a stunt I would not have thought of*. Hexa's revenue is conventional enterprise SaaS at ~$3K/customer/month booked through a Calendry demo flow. That's competent. It is not animal. The 18-month compounding question is whether Hexa's third, fourth, and fifth customers come from the same manual channel or from a repeatable one — at $9K MRR with no documented playbook, the dossier cannot tell me.

On hacker genealogy, Hexa would need a public technical artifact — a GitHub with a non-trivial parser for industrial PDFs, a blog post on multi-ERP schema normalization, an Auri Nayak DeepMind hackathon project that shipped — to flip from the bottom rung to the middle. The dossier surfaces Aurideep Nayak at a Cactus x Google DeepMind hackathon, which is the closest thing to a hacker tell in either founder's record, but it's a single attendance reference with no output named. Patrick Collison won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work *before* Stripe (2010); the absence of that kind of artifact in Hexa is the absence of a thing hackers leave behind.

On co-founder configuration, Hexa parallels the Stripe (2010) two-founder shape — a CEO with the thesis and a technical partner — but without the Collison-brothers history that made Stripe's pair load-bearing. Team size of 3 in the dossier versus 2 named founders implies an early hire, which is fine, but no public data documents that Ishaan Makkar and Auri Nayak worked together before Hexa. I score this near the absent-evidence default and move on; the YC interview surfaces co-founder chemistry better than any dossier.

My 36-month forecast for Hexa: $1–3M ARR, no Series A, acquihired by a mid-tier ERP integration vendor or one of the spend-management incumbents (Coupa, Zip) for under $30M, or quietly dead. The mechanism is that Didero raised a $30M Series A on Feb 12, 2026 targeting the identical ICP — "manufacturers and distributors who need to source raw materials" — with roughly 37x Hexa's capital base, and Traza is shipping the same quoting-agent wedge with $2.1M of pre-seed runway. Hexa's "full-stack OS" framing is the kind of breadth claim that signals the team has not yet been forced to choose a wedge, and 18 months of competing against a $30M war chest is when that choice gets made for you.

The steel-man here is that Didero's $30M is the curse, not the moat. I wrote in 2024 that "there is surprisingly little connection between how much a startup spends and how fast it grows," and Coupa, Zip, and SAP Ariba all burn enterprise sales calories that a three-person Hexa team could route around. $9K MRR in six weeks from three real distributors is genuine product-pull, and the Airbnb (2009) known exception is exactly the precedent — I rejected the idea, the founders overrode me with a stunt, the check was right. The dossier evidence that wins the argument back is the silence: no cereal-box equivalent for Hexa, no demo video on Hacker News, no founder anecdote of pre-funding hustle beyond standard enterprise sales. I would flip to Invest if Ishaan Makkar shipped one verifiable artifact in the next 60 days — an open-source ERP-to-LLM connector with real GitHub stars, or a signed LOI with a named Fortune-1000 distributor — that would convert this from a competent SaaS team into a hacker bet. Until then, pass.
