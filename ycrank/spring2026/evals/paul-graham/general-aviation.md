# General Aviation -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Ben Frank is a pilot, an aircraft mechanic, and an MIT aero engineer who has decided the right thing to do is replace WWII-era radar and VHF with satellite links — that pairing of organic founder-pain with a textbook regulatory schlep is what makes General Aviation interesting, and a solo founder with Team Size: 0 and Hiring: False is what makes me hesitate before writing the check. The signature here is unusual: high on schlep, high on organic-idea, conspicuously low on cofounder configuration. Domain-expert solo operators climbing a regulatory mountain are the profile, and that profile has produced both great companies and great corpses.

Coinbase (2012) is the high rung on the schlep ladder — Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind became the moat itself, the thing nobody else in his batch wanted to touch. The low rung is the polished AI-wrapper team from my pass example: pure software, no offline grind, no regulator to please. General Aviation sits closer to Coinbase than to anything else in the batch, and the dossier pins it there explicitly — DO-178/DO-254 safety assurance, RTCA standards, FAA TSO certification, ANSP procurement against Peraton and Verizon's $2.4B FENS contract. Multi-year cert timelines are exactly the schlep most YC applicants unconsciously route around. General Aviation is running toward it. That is the bull case in one paragraph.

The low rung is cofounder configuration, and General Aviation is the textbook bottom of the ladder. The Collison brothers at Stripe (2010) are the high rung — two siblings who had already shipped Auctomatic together at ages 17 and 19. The Airbnb (2009) trio is the middle. The low rung is the solo founder I explicitly warned against in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010). General Aviation lists exactly one name on its YC page: Ben Frank. Team Size is 0. Hiring is False. Hard-tech ATC requires RF, embedded, avionics certification, and government-sales headcount — none of which one human, however credentialed, fields alone. This is the dimension that drags General Aviation's total down hardest.

The organic-idea ladder runs from Drew Houston losing his USB stick at MIT before Dropbox (2007) at the top, to the Collisons hacking commerce at Stripe (2010) in the middle, to the AI-wrapper team with no founder-problem fit at the bottom. General Aviation lands near Houston: Ben Frank has flown inside this ATC system as a licensed pilot, repaired the aircraft inside it as a mechanic, and spoken with ~200 helicopter operators while CCO at Rotor Technologies. That's not a market-opportunity scan; that's living in the future the essay describes.

On animal resourcefulness, Armstrong's hand-built Android wallet after his Coinbase rejection is the high rung, Houston's pre-seed demo video is the middle, and the MBA pass example is the bottom. General Aviation sits in the lower middle. Frank has co-founded Rotabull, exited Seer Aerospace to Aviation Week, and run commercial for Rotor's R550X program — that's serial-operator evidence, not a one-night stunt. But the dossier names no General-Aviation-specific artifact: no working demo of a satellite ATC link, no signed FAA or NAV CANADA LOI, no equipped test aircraft. Animals leave tracks specific to the current hunt; this dossier shows the prior hunts. On hacker genealogy, Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize is the high rung, Houston's MIT-hacker demo is the middle, the MBA team with no GitHub is the bottom. General Aviation lands below the middle: MIT aero plus aircraft-mechanic hands-on is real hacker-physical lineage, but the dossier reports "No GitHub repos found" and no open-source footprint. Mechanics are hackers; they just don't show up on Hacker News.

My 36-month forecast: by May 2029, General Aviation has either pivoted into a drone/UTM software layer riding atop Starlink and Iridium with under $1M ARR and a finally-recruited technical cofounder, or it has been acquihired by Iridium/Aireon for under $30M after the FAA certification timeline outruns the seed runway. The mechanism is the signature above — schlep fit and organic founder-pain are real, but solo-founder recruiting drag plus DO-178 assurance timelines plus Iridium's $366.7M consolidation of Aireon's surveillance position squeeze General Aviation into being an application layer rather than an infrastructure replacement.

The strongest bull case borrows from Loopt (2005): I funded Sam Altman before mobile location was usable infrastructure on force-of-personality alone, violating my own timing-tailwind preference. Frank's pilot-plus-mechanic-plus-aero-engineer profile plus a prior exit could be the same kind of override — domain density so concentrated it bends the solo-founder rule the way Altman bent the technology-readiness rule. The May 2025 DOT announcement and the One Big Beautiful Bill funding are real political tailwinds; Verizon's FENS contract is visibly faltering. Why not lean in? Because the Loopt exception required Altman's animal test result, which I observed in the room, and the General Aviation dossier shows no equivalent stunt — no cereal boxes, no Android wallet, no demo on Hacker News, no signed ANSP LOI. The override needs an artifact, and General Aviation hasn't produced one yet. One flip condition: if Frank ships a working satellite-relayed two-way comms demo with even a regional ANSP (NAV CANADA's innovation arm, an FAA Tech Center trial) and adds a technical cofounder before Demo Day, I revisit at a higher valuation without hesitation.
