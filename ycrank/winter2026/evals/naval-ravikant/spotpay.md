# SpotPay -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything about the timing. KAST raised $10M for a stablecoin neobank with a Visa card. Dakota raised $12.5M. Slash has $60M at a $370M valuation. Stripe acquired Bridge for stablecoin payments infrastructure. YC itself started accepting stablecoin investments in February 2026. When the accelerator funding your company is simultaneously validating your infrastructure thesis as an institutional policy, you're not contrarian -- you're consensus. I've been burned by price sensitivity before (passing on my full Twitter allocation), but I've been hurt worse by the opposite mistake: deploying capital into categories where twenty smart teams are converging on the same insight simultaneously. Stablecoin-powered payments in 2026 is not Bitcoin in 2014. The asymmetric upside has been competed away at the category level.

The specific knowledge question is where I spend the most time, and here the signal is mixed in a way that bothers me. Thomas César-Herriau as Brex engineer #4 is a genuinely scarce credential -- he watched a fintech company scale from a handful of people to a $12B valuation, built transactional event systems, started the observability team, managed engineers across four offices. That's infrastructure knowledge you can't acquire from a textbook. But Brex is a B2B corporate card product for startups. Consumer cross-border payments in Caribbean and Latin American corridors involve different regulatory structures, different user psychology, different unit economics, different fraud vectors. His knowledge transfers meaningfully to building reliable payment systems, but the gap between "I know how to build fintech infrastructure" and "I understand the specific dynamics of remittance corridors between Trinidad and Brooklyn" is real. Zsika brings the lived experience of growing up across borders and feeling the pain of fragmented financial services. That's authentic. But his prior startup was Astros AI -- a monetization platform for mobile game studios through Techstars Seattle. Gaming monetization to cross-border stablecoin payments is a significant domain jump. The pattern I watch for is founders whose entire career has been converging toward this specific problem. What I see instead is a capable team applying transferable skills to a problem one of them experienced personally.

The leverage architecture is where this falls short of my threshold. SpotPay bundles existing capabilities -- transfers, card spending, yield, local payments -- on cheaper stablecoin rails. That's operational efficiency, not new leverage. Compare the structure to Uber: Uber didn't make taxis 6% cheaper. It turned every car owner into an entrepreneur and every smartphone into a dispatch system. That created a category of leverage that didn't previously exist. SpotPay makes remittances cheaper and more convenient by swapping the underlying settlement layer. The user gets a better deal, but they're doing the same things they did before -- sending money, spending money, saving money. No new category of human capability is unlocked. And critically, SpotPay depends on third-party infrastructure it doesn't own: Visa for card issuance, stablecoin issuers for the settlement layer, local payment rail integrators for each corridor. They're an application sitting on top of other people's leverage. My portfolio history reveals a strong preference for owning the infrastructure layer -- Alchemy over any individual DeFi app, CoinList over any individual token project, Stack Overflow over any individual developer tool. SpotPay is the app, not the rail.

The strongest bull case is corridor-specific network effects combined with Thomas's execution velocity. Five app updates in three months, a working Visa card, Spanish language support, ATM withdrawals -- that's a team that ships. If they capture the US-Caribbean corridor before well-funded competitors even notice it, senders pulling receivers could create a local network effect that's hard to dislodge. The cross-border payments TAM is $212 billion in revenue and growing. And most competitors are either B2B (Slash, Dakota) or geographically elsewhere (Chipper Cash in Africa). The consumer play in LatAm/Caribbean corridors is relatively uncrowded compared to the broader stablecoin neobank space. For this to be a great investment, SpotPay would need to dominate one or two specific corridors so thoroughly that network density makes them the default, then expand corridor by corridor before well-funded competitors arrive. It's plausible. But Chipper Cash needed $305 million to build 7 million users across 9 African countries. Corridor-by-corridor expansion is capital-intensive and regulation-heavy work for a two-person team with $450K.

I'd pass. The founders are competent and the CTO credential is strong, but the category has moved from contrarian to consensus, the product creates efficiency rather than new leverage, and the capital asymmetry against funded competitors is severe. The technology -- a Visa-enabled stablecoin wallet -- is explicitly reproducible by well-funded teams, which the dossier itself acknowledges. I want companies where the founder's specific knowledge produces an insight no one else has reached, building something that creates a form of leverage that didn't exist before, at a moment when the crowd thinks they're wrong. SpotPay has a founder who felt the pain, building something the crowd now agrees is right, using infrastructure someone else owns.
