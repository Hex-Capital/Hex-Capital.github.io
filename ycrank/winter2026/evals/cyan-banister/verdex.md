# Verdex -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me is a tension I can't resolve from the dossier alone: two Dartmouth graduates who finished school in 2025, with no documented background in agriculture or insurance, claim to be insuring 11% of all American farmland through one of the largest U.S. crop insurers. If that's real -- if two recent graduates walked into one of the most conservative buyer categories in enterprise software and landed a contract covering 100 million acres -- that's a story I desperately want to hear. How did they get in the door? Who did they know? What did they see that made an insurer trust two kids with no industry track record? That would be a remarkable founder story. But it's not in the dossier, and its absence is exactly what makes this hard for me to get behind.

When I evaluate founders, I start with the biographical thread that connects a person to their problem. I want to know what happened in their lives that made crop insurance verification the thing they'd pour their souls into. With Verdex, I see two smart engineers from Dartmouth -- Evan won the Society of Engineers Prize, Jad had a prior stealth startup -- but I don't see the biographical connection to farmers or insurers or the brokenness of manual inspections. That doesn't mean it doesn't exist. Maybe Jad grew up watching his family deal with crop loss in a way that scarred him. Maybe Evan spent summers working on farms. But what I can observe is two talented people who appear to have identified a market opportunity analytically rather than lived their way into it. And at this stage, when I'm betting on people more than product, that distinction matters enormously to me.

The category itself doesn't activate my weirdness detector. Satellite imagery for agriculture is increasingly well-understood and well-funded. Planet Labs is public at a $2.8 billion market cap. SatSure has raised nearly $28 million. RCIS -- one of the very carriers Verdex might be selling to -- is already piloting remote claims validation with satellite data and AI. This is not an idea people dismiss as insane; it's an idea large incumbents are actively pursuing. The insurance-specific workflow angle is a smart wedge, but it's an execution thesis, not a conviction thesis. Nobody is going to tell you that using satellite imagery to verify crop damage is a crazy idea. And in my experience, the companies that change the world are the ones that sound crazy before they sound obvious.

The strongest bull case here is real, and I want to name it honestly: if the single-customer traction is genuine and expandable, Verdex is accumulating a proprietary dataset across 100+ million acres that could compound into a meaningful moat. The USDA regulatory environment creates genuine switching costs and barriers to entry. The timing is right -- satellite revisit rates are up, imagery costs are down, and climate volatility is straining manual inspection capacity. The $5 billion global verification SAM is large and growing. If these founders can navigate the concentrated, regulated buyer landscape and expand from one carrier to several, they could build something defensible. But that's a lot of "ifs" for a team whose domain credibility I can't verify, operating with what appears to be total single-customer dependency. The Affirm comparison (making a financial system more transparent) doesn't quite land because the transparency here flows to insurers reducing their costs, not to farmers gaining economic access they previously lacked.

What I keep coming back to is this: I've learned that the best investments I've made were in founders whose personal histories made their companies feel inevitable. The problem and the person were inseparable. With Verdex, I see a real problem, a reasonable approach, and capable young founders -- but I don't feel that inseparability. And in a category that's increasingly crowded rather than dismissed, operating with a single customer in a market where the incumbents are already building similar capabilities, I need that founder conviction signal to override the competitive and concentration risks. Without it, this is a pass for me.
