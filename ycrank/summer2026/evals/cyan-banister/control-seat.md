# Control Seat -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Control Seat is an industrial-vertical AI play that sits in my favorite lane on category and my least favorite lane on governance: SCADA modernization is exactly the kind of unsexy, IEC-62443-shadowed, OT-installed-base swing I will fund when no one else wants to read the deck, but Jack Grodnick and Warren Shepard are two Dartmouth class-of-2026 co-founders with no named CEO and no tiebreaker disclosed. That pairing -- right lane, wrong cockpit -- is the analytical signature, and it is the signature that decides this one before the dollars do.

The SCADA category is where Control Seat earns its highest mark, and the load-bearing analogue is **Crusoe** (~2018): legacy industrial substrate, regulatory friction, incumbents who sell decades-old desktop stacks (AVEVA Wonderware, Siemens WinCC, Rockwell FactoryTalk), and a customer base most of my peers do not want to fly to. That is the lane where moats actually compound, because IEC 62443 and critical-infrastructure scrutiny (Globe Newswire, May 2026) are taxes on entry, not bugs. Where Control Seat diverges from Crusoe is in the founder's *of-the-place* quality -- Chase Lochmiller could walk a North Dakota well pad; Grodnick and Shepard did internships at SpaceX and Tesla, which is tangent, not residence. The lane is right; the social license to operate inside it is unbuilt.

**HQ Trivia** (2017) is the failure mechanism I cannot stop seeing in Control Seat's cap table: Yusupov and Kroll never resolved who broke ties, and when the product hit turbulence the partnership detonated. Control Seat's dossier names two co-founders, same graduating class, same school, no CEO designation, no operating agreement disclosed, no equity split visible -- the exact silence I treat as "they have not had the conversation yet." Industrial sales cycles are 18-month gauntlets where one founder has to own the roadmap call against an angry plant manager at 11pm; with Allen-Bradley, Siemens, and Beckhoff PLC integrations to ship simultaneously, the tiebreaker question is not academic, it is operational by month six.

On founder-lived-experience (mechanism analysis), the compounding question is whether SpaceX internships gave Grodnick and Shepard enough scar tissue that they will out-iterate Inductive Automation's 373-person, $41M-ARR, Fortune-100-deployed Ignition team on tag schemas and historian semantics over the next 18 months. I do not believe they will. SpaceX rebuilt its industrial control stack internally with engineers who slept on factory floors for years; an undergrad summer adjacent to that work is exposure, not ownership, and Control Seat's website language about "designs, builds, deploys, and runs" suggests they are absorbing integrator economics they have never personally bled through.

On magical-weirdness (counterfactual): for Control Seat's score on this axis to flip a tier, the dossier would need to show one or two credible AI-infrastructure VCs publicly passing on aesthetic or capital-intensity grounds the way they passed on Crusoe in 2018. Instead Control Seat is YC S26 with Jared Friedman as group partner, a $500K standard deal, and a category ("AI-native [legacy enterprise software]") that is the dominant 2026 batch meme. Unsexy verticals still get consensus rounds when the AI wrapper is loud enough; this round is priced as if the AI wrapper is the moat.

Physical-world verifiability is where Control Seat lands closest to my **Niantic** (~2015) anchor without reaching it: I could play Ingress on a Tuesday in 2012 and feel the pull; I cannot install Control Seat tonight without a Beckhoff PLC and a plant floor. The launch video on YouTube ("Real-time internal dashboards") is a dashboard screen-share, not an artifact I can touch in my own life within 48 hours. The product controls tactile machinery, which is more than a pure API gives me, but the evaluation surface is locked behind OT hardware I do not own.

My 36-month forecast for Control Seat: stalled between $400K and $1.5M ARR with two or three mid-market manufacturing pilots, unable to displace Ignition's bootstrapped ~$15K/server unlimited-tag pricing at the SMB end or AVEVA's installed base at the enterprise end, with one of the two co-founders quietly departed for a new project by month 24 because the tiebreaker conversation finally happened and someone lost. The mechanism is the signature itself: right lane, wrong cockpit -- the unsexy category will not save a co-founder pair who never named a CEO before they had to fire one.

The strongest case against my pass is the **DeepMind** (~2013) exception: Hassabis, Suleyman, and Legg arrived pedigreed and I wrote a check anyway because the polymath compounding was real, and one could argue Shepard's mechanistic-interpretability research plus Grodnick's Tesla manufacturing exposure is a baby version of that cross-domain stack pointed at OT. The objection stings because the lane is genuinely right and the AI-on-tag-data thesis is genuinely a 2024-LLM-threshold "why now." It does not win, though, because the DeepMind exception required Hassabis's chess-prodigy-plus-neuroscience-plus-game-design depth measured in decades, not three summer internships, and because the HQ Trivia governance failure is a near-term blow-up risk that compounds faster than any polymath thesis can pay off. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: a named Fortune-500 manufacturer signs a multi-plant deployment within 12 months and Control Seat publicly names one CEO with majority operating control. Absent both, I pass with one foot still in love -- this is the right neighborhood, just not the right house.
