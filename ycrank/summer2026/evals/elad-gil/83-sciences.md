# 83 Sciences -- Elad Gil Evaluation

83 Sciences is what happens when a JACS-published MIT researcher tries to wrap a workflow layer around a category — materials discovery — that keeps almost qualifying as re-opened without actually being re-opened. Eric Riesel's Crystalyze paper (JACS, Sep 2024, 67% accuracy on RRUFF experimental patterns) is a real shipped artifact, and Ian Naccarella plus Yankang Yang give the team the BCG/HBS operator ballast that *High Growth Handbook* (2018) is implicitly a profile of. The problem is that the highest-signal dimension here is founder-shipping-history, and the lowest is revenue velocity — which in the AI cycle is the pairing I've learned to read as "credentialed lab spinout, not yet a company."

Anduril (2022) is the closest ladder rung to what 83 Sciences claims on catalyst — Ukraine was a *specific* 24-month window where Pentagon software inadequacy was suddenly legible, and Trae Stephens and Palmer Luckey were the software-on-defense team uniquely positioned for it. 83 Sciences gestures at an analogous catalyst: DeepMind's GNoME (2023) and Riesel's own Crystalyze (JACS, 2024) crossed a generative-materials threshold that arguably makes proprietary experimental datasets the binding constraint. But the dossier's own TAM frame gives the game away — Precedence Research at $251.17M in 2026 growing 20.22% CAGR is a *steady-state* description of materials informatics, not a re-opening. Anduril had Ukraine and a Pentagon procurement thaw you could point at on a calendar; 83 Sciences has a research-community step-function that hasn't yet produced a named enterprise buyer changing behavior. That's the difference between a market that re-opened and a market that got a little more interesting.

83 Sciences discloses no revenue, no LOIs, no signed lab partners, no dataset scale, and zero open jobs on the YC page — which is the exact silhouette Lyft's Series C had on the axis I actually cared about at the time, information you'd expect if the curve existed. The failure mechanism I flag most often — "if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss Show #863, April 2026) — assumes a product is in-market and shippable to paying customers. 83 Sciences may be too early for that read to be fully damning, but the *category* of the pitch (in-person deployment into corporate R&D labs, chemicals/semis/pharma procurement) already tells you the sales cycle is multi-quarter and the traction curve, whenever it arrives, will not look like Harvey's $3B → $5B → $8B (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). Silence here isn't neutral; the shape of the future silence is bad.

Eric Riesel is the reason 83 Sciences clears my founder-shipping bar at all: Crystalyze is a real artifact with a public web interface (crystalyze.org), a JACS lead-authorship, and a measurable accuracy number — that is a shipped product, not a credential. Over 18 months this dimension compounds if Riesel keeps releasing model updates against the "Lab Brain" the way Aravind Srinivas shipped Perplexity iterations between our conversations (Tim Ferriss Show #863, April 2026); it decays if Naccarella and Yang — a Sila Nano/BCG strategist and a BCG principal who ran an internal AI rollout — end up owning product decisions while Riesel gets buried in academic partnership BD. The three-founder structure with no named CEO is the *Unusual Signs* (NFX) red flag: "every company needs a final decision maker," and the dossier doesn't tell me who that is at 83 Sciences.

For 83 Sciences to jump a tier on distribution position, the on-site data-capture wedge would have to convert into contractual exclusivity on negative results with two or three named partner labs within 12 months — the Color Genomics (2015) template, where the workflow layer on top of commodity sequencing became the durable position. That's the counterfactual: no exclusivity, no index. Absent that, CuspAI's reported $400M raise at $2.6B (SiliconANGLE, Jun 2026) buys enough BD headcount to replicate the "we'll come to your lab" motion, and Citrine ($81.3M raised, Tracxn 2025) already has the customer relationships. 83 Sciences' current moat is a promise of trust-based access it has not yet demonstrated it can convert.

Color Genomics (2015) is the single-anchor parallel I keep reaching for on the miracle question, and it's exactly where 83 Sciences fails the test. Color isolated its miracle to workflow-on-*commodity*-sequencing — the chemistry was solved, the cost curve was falling, and the work left was product and GTM. 83 Sciences requires at minimum three independent miracles: (1) labs sharing IP-sensitive unpublished data at scale, (2) generative models learning usefully from negative results when the current public benchmark from Riesel's own work is 67% accuracy on real experimental patterns, and (3) enterprise R&D procurement cycles in chemicals/pharma/semis compressing enough to hit escape velocity before the seed extension runs out. Compounding those three probabilities is the exact math NFX's *Unusual Signs of Billion-Dollar Companies* warns about, and it's why I've largely stayed out of this shape of deal.

My 36-month call on 83 Sciences: acqui-hired by a strategic — most likely a chemicals major (BASF, Dow) or CuspAI itself — at sub-$50M, or persisting as a 6-to-10-person research shop with 2-3 corporate R&D contracts and under $2M ARR. Not zero, not a fund-returner. The mechanism producing that outcome is the pairing from paragraph one: Riesel's shipping credibility gets the team through a seed extension and one or two lighthouse partnerships, but the absence of a re-opening catalyst and the three-miracle dependency chain keep 83 Sciences from ever hitting the growth curve that would attract a real Series A markup.

The strongest opposing case is the Color Genomics (2015) Known Exception, and it stings: 83 Sciences fits the *workflow-infrastructure-on-top-of-a-hard-thing* pattern I've quietly favored across Anduril, Applied Intuition, and Color itself, and Riesel-through-The-Engine gives them the exact founder-graph access I don't publicize but repeatedly fund into. The dossier even hands me the argument — on-site data capture is a structural moat that Uncountable and Citrine have not built. The dismantling: Color worked because I *co-founded and operated* it, not because I passively wrote a seed check, and the sequencing layer underneath Color was already commodity — Illumina had done the work. 83 Sciences is asking me to bet that generative models on unpublished negative-results data are as ready as sequencing was in 2015, and Riesel's own 67% Crystalyze accuracy says they aren't. The condition that would flip my verdict is narrow and testable: two signed multi-year data-exclusivity contracts with named industrial R&D partners (a top-5 chemicals firm or a top-3 battery cell manufacturer) inside 12 months, at which point 83 Sciences becomes the Color-template call and I'd want the follow-on.
