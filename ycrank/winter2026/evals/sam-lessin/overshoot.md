# Overshoot -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the structural problem with Overshoot: the founders are building the thing everyone already knows needs to exist. Real-time VLM inference for video? Of course someone needs to build that. Every inference company on the planet — Fireworks with $331 million, Together AI with $554 million — already has this on their roadmap. When the idea is that obvious, the question isn't whether it's a good product. It's whether a two-person startup can own the category before the incumbents with ten-figure war chests decide to care. This is a sane person with a sane idea, and in my framework, that's not where the returns live. The returns are already priced in by the market.

Let me give the founders their due, because they deserve it. Zakaria spent seven years at Uber building surge pricing — one of the canonical examples of low-latency, high-throughput systems engineering at scale — then moved to Meta AI writing GPU kernels for inference engines. Younes built a computer vision training and serving platform from scratch at Cosmonio, which Intel acquired. They're cousins, which means the co-founder relationship has the kind of deep pre-existing bond I look for — not speed-dating at a startup weekend. The technical credentials map directly to the product: one person understands inference optimization at the metal level, the other understands CV platform architecture. If you were casting the ideal team to build a video inference API, you'd cast these two. The "sane" component here is genuinely impressive. But that's half the equation.

The "insane" component is missing entirely, and that's where my framework breaks this deal. The core VLMs Overshoot serves — Qwen, InternVL — are open-source models available to anyone. The differentiation is a streaming, codec, and inference optimization layer. That's a technical lead, not a structural moat. Technical leads are measured in months, not years, especially when your competitors have hundreds of millions in capital. The company's own moat argument is "focus" — that video is different enough from text to justify specialization. I've heard this exact argument from every vertical SaaS company before the horizontal platform absorbed them. The sub-200ms latency claim is impressive engineering, but it's a number that gets matched, not a position that compounds. Run my AI kill test and the result is inverted from what I want to see: AI didn't create the moat here, AI IS the product. If you remove AI from this business, there is no business. That's the definitional opposite of my "cherry on top" framework.

The bull case — and I want to be honest about it because the founders are strong enough to warrant genuine engagement — goes something like this: video inference is genuinely, structurally different from text inference. Codecs, streaming protocols, frame-level processing, temporal analysis — these aren't features a text-optimized platform bolts on in a quarter. If Overshoot establishes itself as the default abstraction layer for real-time vision applications before incumbents prioritize the modality, it could become the Stripe of video AI — a new primitive that developers reach for by default. The Roboflow analogy is instructive: Roboflow focused on traditional CV and built a $63 million business. If VLMs are genuinely replacing traditional CV workflows (which Younes apparently witnessed firsthand at Intel), there's a market transition creating a window. Three hundred developers at pre-seed with a hackathon launch is not nothing. The narrative can expand from video inference to real-time spatial intelligence infrastructure, touching robotics, autonomous systems, physical security — genuinely large categories. If the "focus" bet is right, it's a massive outcome.

But I keep coming back to the structural position. This company is an application layer sitting on top of primitives it doesn't control. The VLMs are open-source. The streaming protocols are standardized. The GPU hardware is commoditized. Every layer in the stack below Overshoot is owned by someone else. Compare this to Aleo building zero-knowledge cryptographic primitives, or Helium building a decentralized wireless protocol — those companies created new layers that other businesses had to build on. Overshoot is building a convenience wrapper, elegantly engineered but structurally dependent. When Together AI or Fireworks decides that video is worth 10% of their engineering budget — which they will, because the market is growing at 30% CAGR — Overshoot's entire differentiation thesis gets tested against organizations with fifty times the capital and existing developer relationships. The lesson I internalized from Birchbox applies: narrative without structural moat produces companies that are too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound. Talented founders, real product, but the wrong structural position.
