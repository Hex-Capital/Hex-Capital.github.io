# FlowManual -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

FlowManual is two Harvard dropouts -- David Shijoon Bae (most recently National President of the California Association of Student Councils, Class of 2029 admit) and Michael Lin (CS & Statistics) -- pointing a frontier LLM at construction back-office documents. The dimensional signature is a near-bottom score on lived experience paired with a respectable single-CEO structure, which is the exact silhouette of a credentialed team that discovered the bid-versus-contract diff problem in a memo and then organized two people around it. The unsexy category is real -- construction back office is genuinely ugly -- but FlowManual is being built *toward* the category from Cambridge, not *out of* it from a job site.

Anduril is the load-bearing analogy on lived experience, and FlowManual breaks the analogy where it matters. Palmer Luckey had shipped Oculus to a billion-dollar Facebook exit before he ever picked up a Lattice contract; the defense work was a second act on top of a first product that already proved the founder could ship hardware in an adjacent domain. FlowManual's founders have neither a prior product nor an adjacent-domain trajectory into construction -- no GC apprenticeship, no estimating-software stint, no oilfield-childhood equivalent. The dossier shows zero named construction advisors, zero industry-side employment, and a single customer (Cedar Ridge Mechanical) that reads like a friend-of-friend mechanical contractor pilot. The persona-file pattern is unambiguous on this: when the dossier omits a founder backstory tying them to the problem, I read the absence as pitch-deck training, not as a missed detail in the research.

The consensus YC AI-agent pass is the failure mechanism to watch on the "magically weird" dimension: ex-pedigree founders dropping into a category where two and three credible VCs are already writing nine-figure checks, with the moat residing in the model rather than the founder. FlowManual sits inside that exact pattern. Trunk Tools just closed $40M from Insight in July 2025 on top of Redpoint's $20M Series A -- $70M of dry powder building the same AI document agent for GCs. Document Crunch is at $30.5M with Titanium. Both run on the same OpenAI and Anthropic substrate FlowManual will run on. This is the opposite of finding them before it becomes consensus -- the consensus has already raised $100M and is steamrolling the bid-vs-contract diff feature before FlowManual ships its second case study.

The single-CEO dimension is where FlowManual actually scores -- Bae is named CEO, Lin is named CTO, two co-founders rather than three, no co-CEO language anywhere on the YC page. Over 18 months this compounds as the *least* of FlowManual's worries; the structural inverse of HQ Trivia's Yusupov/Kroll feud is plainly visible in the role split. It will not save the company, but it removes one of the predictable blow-up vectors I underwrite against.

The capital-intensive lane is where FlowManual flirts with my thesis and then doesn't commit. Air-gapped, on-prem containerized deployment with AES-256-GCM and SHA-256 audit digests is a real unsexy-regulated wedge -- it is the kind of distribution Procore structurally cannot replicate without breaking its multi-tenant unit economics. For the score to flip a tier here, FlowManual would need a named defense-contractor or DOE-cleared GC pilot (Bechtel, Fluor, Kiewit on classified sites) where the air-gap is the entire reason for procurement, not a checkbox. Cedar Ridge Mechanical is not that customer. Without a security-constrained anchor design partner, on-prem becomes a services tax on a two-person team rather than a moat.

The Niantic parallel on physical verifiability is instructive and disappointing for FlowManual. I bet on Ingress because I had been playing it obsessively since 2012 -- the tactile artifact was in my hand. FlowManual offers a no-credit-card free trial, which is the right gesture, but the artifact is a back-office document review tool for mid-market general contractors. I can sign up; I cannot evaluate whether the color-coded margin labels are catching the deltas a real estimator would catch, because I am not a real estimator. The product is observable but not verifiable by anyone outside the buyer persona, and the buyer persona is exactly who FlowManual lacks on the founding team.

My 36-month forecast: FlowManual is either dead or acqui-hired for under $15M into Procore, Autodesk Construction Cloud, or Trunk Tools itself, with the most likely path being a quiet team acquisition by Trunk Tools in late 2027 once their Series B capital runs out the document-diff feature parity and the FlowManual founders need a soft landing. The mechanism is straightforward: $70M of competitor capital plus a founding team with no construction-side relationships equals customer acquisition cost that the seed round cannot finance, and the air-gapped wedge is too narrow without a defense-grade anchor.

The strongest bear-resistant case for FlowManual runs through the DeepMind exception -- pedigreed founders can override the lived-experience criterion if they exhibit polymath cross-domain compounding. Bae's CASC presidency does show unusual operating range for a 19-year-old, and Harvard dropouts have built category-defining companies before. The bull would argue that construction's back office is so under-automated that even a credentialed-outsider team with a working product can grab a foothold while Trunk Tools focuses on field workflows. The dossier dismantles this: Hassabis was a chess prodigy *and* neuroscientist *and* AlphaGo author -- the polymath claim was anchored to verified prior outputs across three domains. Bae and Lin have student leadership and an undergraduate transcript. That is not polymath compounding; that is two smart college students who left school nine months ago. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a signed pilot with a top-50 ENR general contractor (Turner, Skanska, Suffolk, Bechtel) where FlowManual displaces an incumbent rather than greenfields a workflow -- that single proof point would force me back to the table.
