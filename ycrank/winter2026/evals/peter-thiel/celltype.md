# CellType -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking thing about CellType is that its core technology was co-developed with Google DeepMind, published at ICML, and released under Apache 2.0 — and now the company is attempting to build a monopoly on top of research that its most dangerous potential competitor helped create and can freely use. This is not a peripheral concern. Isomorphic Labs — a Google DeepMind spinout with $600 million in funding — operates in the same broad space. Google's CEO personally endorsed Cell2Sentence, calling it "an exciting milestone for AI in science." When the most powerful company in AI publicly validates your approach, you do not have a secret. You have a consensus. The mimetic dynamics here are already in motion: every major AI lab and pharma company is pursuing computational drug discovery, and CellType has essentially handed its foundational insight to the field.

The question I always ask first — what important truth does this company see that others deny? — produces an ambiguous answer. The broad thesis, that AI can accelerate drug discovery, is held by Recursion, Insilico, Isomorphic Labs, Atomwise, Schrödinger, and a hundred others. That is not a secret; it is the most crowded consensus in biotech. The narrower claim — that the cell, not the molecule, is the correct unit of computational analysis, and that gene expression patterns can be processed as natural language — is more interesting. Most AI drug discovery operates at the structural level: protein folding, ligand binding, molecular dynamics. CellType's bet that transcriptomics-as-language captures something these approaches miss is genuinely non-consensus within the field. But the gap between "a novel research paradigm" and "a defensible monopoly" is vast, and the decision to open-source the methodology under Apache 2.0 actively closes that gap for competitors. An academic publishes; a monopolist protects. This choice reveals the founders' instincts, and those instincts are academic.

David van Dijk's credentials are formidable — 11,589 citations, NSF CAREER Award, associate professorship at Yale, and a Google DeepMind collaboration that most researchers would spend a career pursuing. He turned down Google to build CellType, which is a meaningful conviction signal; it reminds me that the best founders refuse the comfortable path. But I need to separate scientific excellence from commercial monopoly instinct. Alex Karp at Palantir had a philosophical doctorate and no commercial experience, but he understood from day one that Palantir needed to become indispensable to institutions that could not easily replace it. The switching costs were architectural, not intellectual. What is CellType's switching cost? A six-month pilot MOU with Senhwa Biosciences is a start, but pilot programs are not lock-in. If Recursion or Isomorphic Labs builds a comparable single-cell foundation model — and they have the resources and talent to attempt it — what prevents a pharma company from switching? The answer presumably lies in accumulated proprietary validation data and the agentic orchestration layer, but neither has been publicly demonstrated as differentiated. The company's moat is aspirational rather than structural.

The bull case is real and I want to engage with it honestly. If treating biology as language turns out to be the correct paradigm — the way treating search as a link-graph problem turned out to be correct for Google — then CellType has a genuine first-mover advantage in trained models, pipeline orchestration, and pharma relationship data that could compound into defensibility. The PageRank paper was also published, and Google still monopolized search. Van Dijk's decade of work in single-cell computational biology means no competitor can replicate his intuitions about which biological signals matter even if they replicate the architecture. The "cold tumor to hot tumor" result — validated in cell lines and intact human tumor microenvironments — is a concrete demonstration that computational predictions translate to wet-lab reality. And the claimed 100% inbound deal flow from Top 10 pharma suggests that the scientific credibility is generating real commercial pull. If the data flywheel from pharma partnerships creates a widening advantage — each engagement producing proprietary training data that improves predictions that attract more partnerships — this could become a Stripe-like wedge where early developer trust (here, pharma scientist trust) compounds into infrastructure status.

But I do not invest in bull cases that require the most optimistic reading of every uncertain variable. The landscape of AI drug discovery is a Girardian nightmare — dozens of well-funded companies imitating each other's ambitions, collectively burning billions of dollars while pharma incumbents hedge by signing non-exclusive pilots with multiple platforms simultaneously. Recursion alone has $461 million in funding and $74.7 million in 2025 revenue. CellType is a two-person team entering this arena with open-source IP, no revenue, and one pilot MOU. The technology is genuinely novel at the paradigm level, but I cannot identify the mechanism through which early-stage novelty converts into durable monopoly power when the core methodology is freely available. The power law demands that I invest only where success produces returns that justify the entire portfolio — and in a market with this competitive structure, even a successful CellType is more likely to become a mid-tier acquisition target than a category-defining monopoly.
