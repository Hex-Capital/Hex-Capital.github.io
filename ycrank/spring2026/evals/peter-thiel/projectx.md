# ProjectX -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Ninety-five companies. Forty-one funded. Twenty billion dollars raised collectively in cloud GPU platforms. When I see those numbers, I do not see a market — I see a war zone. And the first rule of my framework is that wars produce casualties, not monopolists. ProjectX enters this landscape claiming to build "Infinity, the cloud OS that runs all apps on any device," but what I want to know is: what does this company know that the other ninety-four do not? The dossier reveals an engineering distinction — per-app GPU streaming in browser tabs rather than full virtual machines — but engineering distinctions are not secrets. No serious cloud computing engineer would dispute that per-app streaming is more efficient than provisioning entire desktop environments. The question has always been execution, not insight. When Google acquires Cameyo for app virtualization and DigitalOcean swallows Paperspace for GPU workloads, the market is telegraphing that this capability is a feature for hyperscalers to absorb, not a foundation for an independent monopoly.

The founding team restructuring concerns me more than most investors would admit. Between the July 2024 press coverage and the Spring 2026 YC application, three of four original co-founders were replaced. Founding relationships are not interchangeable components — they are the load-bearing structure of a startup's capacity for definite action. I have written that founders should share a prehistory before starting a company together, because without that shared intensity, they are gambling on alignment that has never been tested under pressure. When the original co-founding team fractures before the company has raised meaningful capital, the signal is that the prehistory was insufficient. The current team — Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder — shows no visible shared employer or university overlap. Adhikary attended Lovely Professional University; Karmakar studied at Academy of Technology. Both are from West Bengal, but geographic proximity is not the same as forged-under-fire collaboration. The lean-startup pattern is also visible: multiple accelerators (Draper, IIM Bangalore, Stanford ASES, now YC), pilot programs, flexible billing — the vocabulary of discovery rather than conviction. Where is the irreversible commitment that would make no sense unless the vision is correct?

The strongest bull case requires believing that "cloud OS" is the correct abstraction layer — that just as Windows monopolized the PC by sitting between hardware and applications, Infinity could monopolize cloud computing by sitting between GPU infrastructure and end users. If browser-native GPU streaming reaches a quality threshold where local hardware becomes genuinely unnecessary for professional workloads — Blender, Unreal Engine, DaVinci Resolve — the addressable market becomes not just cloud desktops or GPU-as-a-service but all computing that currently requires expensive local hardware. The India education angle supports this: 75 percent of Indian students lack access to quality computing devices, and $2.4 million in signed LOIs from institutional buyers suggests real demand. Sub-3-second cold starts, if genuinely achieved, would represent a meaningful technical gap over competitors still offering persistent VM sessions. In this scenario, ProjectX's beachhead in Indian education — a market where hyperscalers have not focused their app-streaming efforts — could provide the monopoly-in-a-small-market starting position before expanding concentrically. The problem is that this bull case requires ProjectX to build and defend this position with $204,000 in total funding against competitors with three orders of magnitude more capital, while simultaneously hoping that AWS, Azure, and GCP decide not to offer per-app GPU streaming themselves. The capitalization mismatch alone is not disqualifying — but it requires either a genuine technological discontinuity or a distribution lock-in that I do not see evidence of here.

The power law calculation is unfavorable. The comparable exits in this space are instructive: Paperspace sold for $111 million, Cameyo for an undisclosed amount that Crunchbase suggests was modest, Frame sold twice for $165 million and then was divested. These are not fund-returning outcomes — they are acqui-hires and feature acquisitions by larger platforms. The market structure produces consolidation into hyperscalers, not independent monopolies. Shadow PC, the closest analogue to an independent cloud desktop company, raised over $100 million and remains a niche consumer product. Nothing in this competitive landscape suggests that the winner captures most of the value; instead, the value disperses across infrastructure providers who compete on price and availability. This is the Wish pattern applied to infrastructure: a company operating in a market where the moat cannot be built because the underlying commodity — GPU compute — is available to anyone willing to pay for it. The software abstraction layer above that commodity, however elegant, can be replicated by any well-resourced team with streaming expertise.

This is a competent team building an incremental improvement in a hypercompetitive market. The technical execution may be real — sub-3-second cold starts for GPU applications is non-trivial systems engineering. But superior engineering in a competitive market does not create a monopoly; it creates a slightly better product that must be sold slightly harder against slightly worse alternatives, forever. Competition is not a condition to be won through effort — it is a structural trap that no amount of execution can escape. I pass.
