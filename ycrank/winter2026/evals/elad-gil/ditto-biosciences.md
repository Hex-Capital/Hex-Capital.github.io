# Ditto Biosciences -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ditto is a pattern I've seen work before: a correct thesis that failed in an earlier incarnation because the approach was wrong, not the idea. Coronado Biosciences tried to use live pig whipworms as autoimmune therapeutics and failed Phase 2 trials for Crohn's disease. The underlying biology -- parasites have spent millions of years evolving proteins that suppress the human immune system -- was always sound. What Coronado got wrong was administering whole organisms instead of isolating the specific molecular machinery. That's structurally similar to how Webvan got the thesis right (people want groceries delivered) but the execution wrong (building warehouses before infrastructure existed). Ditto's approach of computationally screening parasite genomes and engineering isolated proteins is a fundamentally different attempt at a previously validated thesis. That boomerang pattern always gets my attention.

But when I apply my structural moment test -- what changed in the last 12-24 months that makes this possible now -- the answer is less crisp than I'd want. The dossier's "why now" is largely inferred: AI computational tools can now screen 1M+ proteins at scale, and AlphaFold-era protein structure prediction has made binding affinity estimation feasible before wet lab work. That's real, but it's also the "why now" for every AI-bio company launching in 2025-2026. When I invested in Harvey, I could point to the specific GPT-3 to GPT-4 capability jump and map exactly when model fidelity would cross the legal-grade threshold. Here, I can't identify a comparable single threshold crossing that unlocked parasite protein mining in a way that wasn't accessible 24 months earlier. The computational tools have been improving continuously rather than crossing a discrete capability boundary. And the market itself -- autoimmune disease therapeutics at $125B+ -- is well-known and well-funded by every large pharma company. This is not a market that looks "too boring" or "too niche" to other investors. What's novel is the source of therapeutics, not the market insight.

My biggest concern is the miracle count. Ditto needs the computational screening to identify viable candidates -- they've shown promising 1-2 nM binding affinity data, which is encouraging. But they separately need to solve immunogenicity for foreign-origin proteins, navigate an FDA pathway with zero precedent for this therapeutic class, and raise hundreds of millions for clinical development against competitors with $100M-$700M already deployed. That's at least three sequential miracles, and possibly four. When a plan requires compounding low-probability events, I pass regardless of how impressive the team is. The team acknowledges the immunogenicity problem and is building a tissue biobank to address it, which shows scientific awareness, but awareness of a problem and solving it are very different things.

The strongest bull case: if Ditto's discovery platform works, they're sitting on a massive unexplored chemical space -- more than 98% of parasite-derived proteins remain uncharacterized. That's not just a single drug company, it's potentially a discovery engine that generates dozens of therapeutic candidates across autoimmune indications, making it closer to a platform than a pipeline. The binding affinity numbers at pre-seed are genuinely unusual -- 1-2 nM puts them in the range of approved therapeutic antibodies, and they claim to have identified thousands of candidates. If even a fraction validate, the licensing and partnership value of that database could be enormous. And I've invested in regulated, capital-intensive companies before -- Anduril required defense procurement navigation, Color Health required CLIA compliance. Regulatory complexity can be a structural moat once you establish the pathway. A team with 50+ publications, a patent, and three years of working together at Arcadia Science is the right profile to establish that pathway. The co-founder relationship has been tested through real collaborative scientific work, which addresses one of the three things that kill pre-PMF companies.

But this doesn't pattern-match to where I deploy capital with high conviction. It's not software-driven infrastructure -- it's wet-lab drug development where the timeline to revenue is measured in decades, not quarters. The distribution trajectory follows standard pharma licensing rather than the product-to-distribution flywheel I look for. There's no mechanism by which an initial product creates a customer relationship that enables distributing additional products through the same channel -- each drug candidate goes through its own regulatory and commercial process. Compare this to how Stripe's payment API became the channel for Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. In pharma, each molecule is essentially a separate business. The founders are scientifically exceptional for this problem, but I'm looking at a capital structure where a pre-seed angel check gets diluted through Series A, B, C, and potentially D before any clinical validation occurs. The path from where they are today to where value crystallizes involves too many sequential gates that are outside the founders' control.

I respect the scientific insight and the team's domain depth. In a different portfolio construction -- one optimized for biotech venture with 10+ year horizons and follow-on reserve capital -- this would be more compelling. But for my framework, which prizes structural inflection timing, distribution moats, and single-miracle clarity, the fit isn't there.
