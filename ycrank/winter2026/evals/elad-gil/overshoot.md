# Overshoot -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The central question with Overshoot is whether specialization in video VLM inference constitutes a durable competitive position or a feature that well-funded general-purpose platforms will absorb within 18 months. Fireworks AI has $331M in funding, Together AI has $554M -- both already serve multimodal models and both have engineering organizations capable of adding video-specific codec and streaming optimizations as a product line, not a company-defining pivot. Overshoot's core moat argument is "focus" -- that image and video are fundamentally different modalities requiring specialized infrastructure. I hear this argument frequently from startups competing against better-capitalized horizontal platforms, and it's the weakest form of defensibility I encounter. Focus is an execution advantage, not a structural one. When the market gets large enough for incumbents to care, focus gets outspent.

The structural moment, however, is real. VLMs crossing the capability threshold to replace task-specific computer vision models is a genuine inflection point -- and the fact that Younes witnessed customers abandoning traditional CV workflows at Intel is exactly the kind of frontline signal I weight heavily. The transition from "train a custom model for every detection task" to "query a general VLM on live video" is as significant for computer vision as the GPT-3 to GPT-4 jump was for text-based AI applications. This is a legitimate "why now." But it's a consensus "why now." Every AI infrastructure investor understands that multimodal models are improving dramatically. The non-obvious version of this market would be deep vertical embedding -- physical security compliance, industrial quality inspection, autonomous edge inference -- where the video inference layer becomes structural infrastructure with real switching costs. A horizontal API competing on latency benchmarks doesn't generate the moat characteristics that produce asymmetric returns.

The founder profiles are the strongest signal in this dossier. Zakaria built surge pricing systems at Uber for seven years -- real-time, high-throughput distributed systems at massive scale -- then moved to Meta AI writing GPU kernels for inference engines. Younes was founding engineer at Cosmonio, building a CV training and serving platform from scratch before Intel acquired the company. This is not generic big-tech experience applied to a startup; this is the exact intersection of low-latency infrastructure and computer vision platform engineering that Overshoot's product demands. I've backed founders with this level of domain-specific technical depth before -- but in those cases the investment thesis also included a structural moat beyond execution speed. Harvey had workflow embedding in regulated law firms. Anduril had ITAR compliance and defense procurement barriers. Overshoot has sub-200ms response times, which is impressive engineering but a benchmark that erodes as competitors invest.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is where my conviction drops. Overshoot is currently a single API product -- connect a video feed, get a VLM response. The "3 lines of code" integration mirrors Stripe's developer-first approach, which is smart for initial adoption. But Stripe's enduring advantage wasn't API simplicity -- it was that the payment integration created an enterprise relationship through which they could distribute Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury. What additional products naturally flow through a video inference integration? Potentially model fine-tuning, video analytics, compliance tooling, or data pipeline management. But I see no evidence the founders have articulated this multi-product trajectory, and without switching costs that compound, a developer using Overshoot today can migrate to Together AI's video endpoint tomorrow with trivial effort. Three hundred developers using an API is a start, but without depth metrics it's impossible to distinguish genuine product pull from hackathon experimentation.

The bull case I'd need to believe: that the video modality is genuinely as different from text as the founders claim -- requiring specialized codec pipelines, streaming protocol optimization, and inference engineering that general platforms cannot bolt on as a feature -- and that this specialization creates the same kind of "boring infrastructure everyone needs" dynamic I saw with PagerDuty. If VLM-powered real-time video understanding becomes standard across physical security, robotics, gaming, and consumer applications, the inference layer serving those workloads becomes critical infrastructure for an enormous market. The founder-market fit would justify that bet. The VLM capability curve supports the timing. And 300+ developers suggest some early pull. What I'm missing is evidence of structural defensibility -- a distribution moat, a regulatory barrier, deep enterprise embedding, anything that converts a speed advantage into a position that compounds. Without that, this is a well-built product in a market where capital-rich incumbents will compete directly once the opportunity becomes large enough to matter to them.
