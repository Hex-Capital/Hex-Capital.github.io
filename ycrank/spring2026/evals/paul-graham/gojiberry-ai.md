# Gojiberry AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me before anything else: on March 9, 2026, one week after hitting #1 on Product Hunt and while participating in YC S26, Gojiberry AI was listed for sale on TrustMRR at $11.15 million. I've watched thousands of founders go through YC. The ones who build something important never have one foot out the door. You can't simultaneously be playing for a $11M exit and building a company designed to grow fast. Those require different decisions at every fork. The sale listing doesn't necessarily mean these founders are uncommitted — maybe it's a negotiating tactic, maybe they pulled it — but the instinct to list reveals something about how they see the company. It's a rational optimization move, and rationality at this stage is exactly what I don't want. I want founders who are irrationally committed to the problem.

The traction is real and I don't want to dismiss it. Going from zero revenue to $112K–$175K MRR with 1,000+ paying customers in roughly eight months, bootstrapped on $380/month in marketing spend, is serious execution. The pivot is the right shape too — they built a CRM auto-fill tool, got 2,000 waitlist signups and zero paying customers, recognized the signal, and rebuilt around intent-based lead generation. That pivot took maybe two months to start producing revenue. The growth from $24K MRR in November to $112K+ by March is steep. All three founders have prior exits: Dylan's Edusign reached $3.3M revenue and 600K users before selling, which is genuinely impressive for a self-taught developer. Pierre-Eliott and Roman built and sold CoCo AI together. These are people who know how to ship product and acquire customers. The $380/month total spend to generate this traction — cold emails, LinkedIn DMs, Reddit posts, YouTube — is the kind of scrappy distribution work I usually celebrate. Roman's 12-channel strategy is the "do things that don't scale" playbook executed well.

But here's what's missing from the origin story: nobody on this team was a frustrated small-team sales rep who couldn't afford 6sense. The founders came from Shopify e-commerce (CoCo AI was WhatsApp marketing), Web3 gaming (MechaChain), and edtech (Edusign). They arrived at sales automation the way consultants arrive at startup ideas — by surveying the landscape and identifying a gap. The dossier describes their problem statement in market terms: "Traditional tools either require manual signal research" and "Enterprise intent-data platforms start at $25K–$150K/yr, pricing out SMBs." That's a positioning statement, not a personal frustration. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his thumb drive. These founders built Gojiberry AI because the pricing spreadsheet showed a gap. The distinction matters because founders who lived inside the problem build differently — they have intuitions about what to build next that market researchers have to discover through A/B tests.

The competitive landscape tells me this idea sounds good to everyone, and that's the problem. Apollo ($150M ARR) just acquired Pocus to add signal-based selling. Clay reached $100M ARR at a $3.1B valuation. Lemlist is at $40M ARR. Instantly hit $20M ARR bootstrapped. HeyReach reached $10M ARR on $581K in funding. This isn't a space where other founders' unconscious filters are screening out the opportunity — it's one of the most popular categories in SaaS. When five or six well-funded competitors are all growing fast in your exact market, you're not in the "seems like a bad idea" sweet spot. You're in the "everyone already agrees this is a good idea" zone, which means the returns have been competed away before you start. Gojiberry's positioning as "opinionated and end-to-end for small teams" is a real wedge, but it's a positioning choice, not a moat. Any competitor could build an opinionated small-team mode in a quarter.

The strongest bull case is that these founders are distribution machines who've proven they can acquire customers cheaply, iterate fast, and build profitable software. If the data flywheel works — if 1,000+ customers generating outreach data genuinely improves lead scoring in ways competitors can't replicate — then the SMB wedge could compound into something larger. The 75% profit margin and <5% churn suggest real product-market fit, not manufactured traction. And the founders' prior exit experience means they won't make the naive mistakes that kill first-timers. If I squint, I can see a path where Gojiberry becomes the Stripe of sales intelligence for small teams — the thing that works so well out of the box that nobody bothers configuring Apollo. But that path requires the founders to be missionaries about this specific problem for the next decade, and the sale listing tells me they're mercenaries — skilled, effective mercenaries, but mercenaries nonetheless. The LinkedIn platform dependency adds structural fragility: building your core product on automated access to a platform that explicitly prohibits automation is building on sand, and I've seen that movie end badly.

These are competent, experienced operators building a profitable business in a crowded market they chose from the outside. That's a fine outcome for them and possibly for their customers. But it's not what I invest in. I invest in founders who are slightly unhinged about a problem they discovered by living inside it, building in a space that makes everyone else uncomfortable. Gojiberry AI makes me comfortable, and that's the tell.
