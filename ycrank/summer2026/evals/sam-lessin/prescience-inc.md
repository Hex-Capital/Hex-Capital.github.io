# Prescience, Inc. -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Rishab Jain has been a credentialed prodigy since age 13 — TIME 25, Regeneron $50K, two prior acquired companies — and his brother Aditya is finishing Harvard Medical School while running AI work at Broad; on the founder-conviction axis Prescience, Inc. is genuinely high, and on the owned-distribution axis Prescience, Inc. is a 2,761-follower void. That pairing is the signature: missionary clinicians attacking a capital-intensive insurance operation with no community on the cap table and no disclosed carrier partner. Everything that follows is a function of those two facts colliding inside a 2026 YC AI healthcare pitch.

On founder conviction, the load-bearing analog is **Ben Silbermann at Pinterest** — missionary temperament, builder who would not be talked out of the thing, the kind of person you write a check to because the resume itself is the moat against future doubt. Rishab Jain shares the temperament marker — "America's Top Young Scientist at 13" for a pancreatic-cancer detection model is not a resume line, it's evidence of a decade-long pattern of finishing things — and Aditya Jain's Doximity op-ed "Why I Didn't Apply to Residency" is exactly the public artifact I look for: a Harvard M.D. publishing a non-consensus belief about his own profession before raising a dollar. Where Prescience, Inc. diverges from the Silbermann analog is that Pinterest's missionary belief was about a product surface Ben could build alone in a weekend; Aditya and Rishab Jain's missionary belief is about operating a regulated insurance plan, which is not a thing two brothers finish on conviction. The conviction is real. It's pointed at a structure that conviction alone doesn't move.

The failure mode I keep underwriting against is the **canonical 2026 YC AI seed pitch from my Pass Example** — sharp team, $25K MRR, no public writing, no community, depends on paid acquisition and an enterprise sales motion the founders haven't run. That pattern shows up half-formed inside Prescience, Inc.: the homepage names "Series D, FAANG, MBB consulting, Goldman/JPM, Frontier Labs" as logo-tier targets without naming a single one, the demo is a cal.com/prescience/30min link, and the founder reach is Rishab Jain at 2,761 X followers and Aditya Jain with a handful of op-eds. That's not a community. That's a Calendly. Slow's Creator Fund thesis would not touch Prescience, Inc. because there is no owned distribution channel for the Jain brothers to compound against the cost of customer acquisition that operating an employer health plan demands.

On round-pricing — the dossier discloses no terms, no investors, no round size, and I punish that silence because Prescience, Inc. is wearing the most expensive costume in the 2026 YC closet: "AI Care Companion," "AlphaGo-like Time Machine," "Move 37 health trajectories." Over an 18-month window that branding compounds the wrong way for me — it attracts exactly the consensus AI-healthcare capital that will reprice this round to a number I cannot make money at, and the Jain brothers' credentials are the kind that close oversubscribed seeds at $25-30M post. The mechanism that turns conviction founders into bad entries is precisely this — the market reads the resume, not the structure, and the entry price stops being underwritable before the regatta's first tack.

**Ro** is the only honest parallel for Prescience, Inc. on capital structure — a high-burn DTC pharma operation that violated my capital-efficiency rule, justified by 50-state prescription-rail schleps that only venture money could clear. Prescience, Inc. wants to inherit that exception, but Ro had Zach Reitano with an actual physician network, Z Lerer firepower, and a single-product wedge (ED meds, then hair, then chronic care) that monetized inside year one. Prescience, Inc. is two brothers — one still in med school per the LinkedIn — building the predictive engine AND operating the plan AND running enterprise sales to "Frontier Labs" with no disclosed carrier partner, no licensure status, and a $600 PMPM forecast versus the $1,213 market benchmark that no third-party actuary has validated. Ro earned its capital intensity as moat; Prescience, Inc. has not earned it yet, and the difference is whether the next five tacks are underwritable today.

On sleeve fit — counterfactual — for Prescience, Inc. to clear my healthcare-DTC bar the way **PillPack** and **Hinge Health** did, the dossier would need either a named licensed carrier partnership (Aetna, Cigna, a regional Blue) or a signed mid-market employer LOI with disclosed pricing, and it has neither. Health-insurance-operation is sleeve-adjacent to my Ro/Truepill/Livongo cluster but it is not the same sleeve — Truepill was prescription rails sold to other operators, PillPack was D2C pharmacy, Livongo was condition-management SaaS. Prescience, Inc. is insurtech with an AI routing wrapper, which is closer to **Sana Benefits** ($111M raised, no AI emphasis) and **Sidecar Health** ($328M raised, $1.2B valuation) than to anything in my book — and being structurally similar to two well-funded incumbents who already raised the rounds is the opposite of the pre-consensus pricing I underwrite.

Thirty-six month forecast: Prescience, Inc. hits the state-insurance-licensing wall by month 12, pivots from operating-the-plan to selling the "Time Machine" predictive-routing engine as software into existing PEOs and ICHRA platforms (Justworks, Thatch, Sequoia One), lands $1-3M ARR on three to five mid-market logos, and raises a bridge instead of a priced Series A. The mechanism is the brothers' execution depth catching up to the scope: they can ship the model, they cannot ship a 50-state regulated insurance carrier from a two-person Boston team, and the rational tack is to sell the legible piece to the operators who already have the licenses.

Steel-man: the bull case is that the Jain brothers are exactly the missionary-clinician operators Ro proved could clear healthcare's regulatory schleps, Boston is the right city for it, Rishab Jain's two prior acquired companies say he finishes, and an AI engine fed by operated-plan claims data compounds into a Livongo-shaped data moat no ICHRA enabler can replicate. That stings, because Ro is my acknowledged exception and Prescience, Inc.'s founder profile is not weaker than early-Ro. What loses the case is the absence — no named carrier, no signed employer, no disclosed round, no public writing from Rishab Jain on why his take on AI care-routing is non-obvious — and the structural fact that Ro had Z Lerer and a single-SKU wedge that Prescience, Inc.'s "Diamond Plan / Time Machine / Care Companion / HSA funding" stack explicitly refuses. The condition that flips my pass to a check inside six months: a named licensed carrier partnership AND one signed employer LOI in the 100-250 headcount band with disclosed PMPM economics — that's the regatta tack I need to see resolved before Prescience, Inc. is venture-shaped instead of consensus-shaped.
