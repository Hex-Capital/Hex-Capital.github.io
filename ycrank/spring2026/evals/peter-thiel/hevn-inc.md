# HEVN, inc -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

HEVN, inc is a fintech wrapper attempting to occupy a known seam between Brex's US-EIN gating and Airwallex's $1.2B ARR cross-border footprint — its signature in my framework is highest on Power Law category size (the cross-border SMB banking market is provably real) and lowest on Monopoly Secret (the "USD account without EIN" pitch is a feature gap, not a hidden truth). Peter Volnov, Nikita Briuzgin, and Andrei Zhevlakov have shipped a 30M-user crypto wallet together, which is genuine execution provenance, but the company they have now built is structurally closer to Wish than to Palantir: a price-and-feature play in a category where Airwallex, Wise Business, Mercury, and Payoneer already operate with regulatory scale HEVN, inc has not yet earned.

The Monopoly Secret ladder runs from Palantir at the top — proprietary embedding in classified intelligence systems that nobody else could replicate — through Affirm at the middle, where Max Levchin's underwriting insight created a defensible POS-credit position, down to Wish at the bottom, a no-moat marketplace built on the consensus that cheap goods sell. HEVN, inc sits one rung above Wish and well below Affirm: the dossier's central claim that non-US firms want USD accounts without an EIN is not a secret — it is a UX gap Airwallex's "Brex-alternatives" blog explicitly markets against in 2026, and Mercury's December 2025 OCC charter application signals the same incumbents are tooling up for exactly this seam. There is no contrarian truth here; there is a list of features (0.5% cashback, SEPA/ACH/wire/stablecoin rails, "soon" FDIC-insured no-EIN accounts) that a competent product team at Airwallex could ship in two quarters. Competition is for losers, and HEVN, inc has entered a category with five funded competitors before writing its first sentence of marketing copy.

The Power Law ladder runs from SpaceX at the top — a category where the winner captures civilizational-scale value — through Lyft in the middle, where the #2 in a massive duopoly was still a fund-relevant outcome, down to Wish at the bottom, where the category itself was structured against monopoly. HEVN, inc sits between Lyft and Wish: the cross-border SMB banking category is real (Airwallex at $12B valuation, $1.2B ARR growing 85% YoY, per Sacra and the Airwallex blog), so even a #4 or #5 player in this market could plausibly reach hundreds of millions in revenue. But this is the Lyft override at best, and HEVN, inc has none of Lyft's category-creation timing — it is entering when Airwallex is preparing for IPO and Capital One is consolidating Brex at $5.15B. The realistic ceiling is a sub-$1B acquisition by a regional bank or Payoneer, not a fund-returner.

Definite Founder Conviction: SpaceX at the top — Elon's specific multi-decade plan for reusable rockets — Affirm at the middle, the AI-CRM pattern at the bottom where founders chase whichever category is hot. HEVN, inc sits closer to the bottom rung. The Volnov-Briuzgin-Zhevlakov trio came from HOT Labs and HERE Wallet — a TON-ecosystem crypto wallet — and have now pivoted into US-regulated fintech without prior banking-operations experience. That is not a definite plan; that is a category jump. The dossier notes HOT Labs is reportedly still operating in 2024-2025, which raises the bandwidth question Founders Fund flagged on indefinite teams: are these founders building HEVN, inc as a conviction bet, or as the next thing after the wallet thesis cooled?

Distribution: PayPal at the top — eBay sellers pulling the logo into auctions unprompted — Ramp in the middle with corporate-spend network effects across Founders Fund's portfolio, and the generic AI CRM at the bottom that "builds a great product and waits." HEVN, inc sits at the Ramp-to-bottom span with no evidence of pull: no Product Hunt launch, no press, no disclosed customer count, no active YC job postings as of May 2026, and the only plausible distribution edge — cross-marketing into the HOT Wallet's 30M users — is unstated and would be the wrong audience anyway, since retail crypto users are not non-US-incorporated SMBs running Google Ads. Physical-World Moat: General Matter at the top with a $900M DOE contract and uranium enrichment infrastructure, Palantir in the middle with deep operational embedding that approximates physical switching costs, and any pure-software wrapper at the bottom. HEVN, inc is the bottom rung — its product depends entirely on sponsor-bank contracts it does not control, and the FDIC-insured no-EIN account is marketed as "soon," meaning the most novel feature is also the least de-risked.

In 36 months I expect HEVN, inc to be either acquired by Airwallex, Payoneer, or a sponsor-bank parent for under $150M in stock, or operating at $3-12M ARR as a niche tool for ex-Soviet and MENA founders running USD ad spend, with no fund-returner trajectory visible. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature itself: a real category (Power Law present) entered without a secret (Monopoly Secret absent) produces a competent business, not a monopoly, and competent businesses in fintech get rolled up by the $1B+ ARR incumbent who already has the regulatory stack.

The strongest bull case for HEVN, inc invokes the Lyft exception: even the #4 player in cross-border SMB banking could be a $500M-$1B outcome if the category compounds at Airwallex's 85% YoY rate, and the Volnov team's HOT Wallet shipped at 30M-user scale, which is real distribution evidence that Founders Fund-style "execution provenance" investing rewards. Briuzgin being based in Dubai and the team's TON-ecosystem roots could open a genuinely under-served MENA/CIS founder market that Airwallex's Western GTM neglects. The bear case wins anyway because the analogue is wrong: Palmer Luckey's Oculus exit translated to Anduril because hardware execution is hardware execution, but shipping a TON wallet does not translate to running US bank-sponsor compliance — these are different operational categories, and Mercury's OCC charter application signals the regulatory ground is shifting toward incumbents, not new entrants. The single condition that would flip my verdict: a signed sponsor-bank contract delivering the FDIC-insured no-EIN USD account as a live product (not "soon") with one named non-US enterprise customer of recognizable scale — that would convert the feature claim into a genuine wedge and force me to reconsider whether HEVN, inc has actually built the structural seam Mercury and Brex left open.
