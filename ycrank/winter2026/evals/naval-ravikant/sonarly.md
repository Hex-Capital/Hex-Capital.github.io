# Sonarly -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The thing that jumps out immediately is the tech stack: LLM plus monitoring APIs plus MCP. Every component is rented. The language model isn't theirs. The monitoring data isn't theirs. The protocol isn't theirs. The production system map is stored as a markdown file. When I invested in Stack Overflow, it was accumulating a knowledge graph that got harder to replicate with every question and answer -- a genuine compounding asset. When I backed Alchemy, it was becoming fundamental infrastructure that developers built on top of. Sonarly is an integration layer sitting on top of other people's infrastructure, coordinating other people's data with other people's models. That's a coordination play, not an infrastructure play. And coordination layers without proprietary data or network effects are the definition of commoditizable.

The founders' specific knowledge is the bigger concern. Dimittri and Alexandre built a French edtech chatbot and got annoyed by 50 daily alerts on their 2-person team. That's a user's frustration, not a builder's insight. Every engineering team that's ever used Sentry and Datadog simultaneously knows alerts are noisy and investigation is manual. The question I always ask is: what do these founders know that can't be taught? What would take a competitor years to learn? The answer here seems to be nothing that a competent engineer with six months of monitoring experience wouldn't also know. Neither founder has documented time building observability systems, working at infrastructure companies, or developing novel approaches to distributed systems debugging. Their prior AI experience is a consumer chatbot for French university orientation. That's relevant for "can build with LLMs" but not for "deeply understands production infrastructure at scale." Contrast this with how the Datadog founders spent years operating infrastructure before building tools to monitor it -- that's non-transferable knowledge. Here, the knowledge transfer could happen over a weekend.

The structural insight about cross-platform aggregation deserves credit. Sentry won't integrate with Datadog's telemetry. Datadog won't pull in Sentry's errors. Each incumbent's AI remediation is locked inside its own ecosystem, and real production environments use three or four tools simultaneously. That's a genuine gap created by business model cannibalization dynamics -- incumbents benefit from keeping users inside their walls. But here's the problem: this isn't contrarian. It's obvious. Every developer who uses multiple monitoring tools sees this gap. The space has twenty-plus funded competitors working on AI-powered incident management. Sentry already reports 95% root cause accuracy. Datadog's Bits AI has been tested across 2,000+ customer environments. incident.io raised $96M. This is a hot, crowded category where pricing already reflects optimism -- exactly the kind of deal where asymmetric upside has been competed away.

The bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that a 2-person team from a French engineering school can out-execute Sentry's and Datadog's AI teams on fix quality, that the "living map" markdown file evolves into a genuine data moat with real switching costs, and that the cross-platform positioning remains structurally protected as MCP and similar standards make integrations easier for everyone -- including incumbents. If all three prove true, Sonarly could become the Switzerland of observability, the neutral aggregation layer that every multi-tool engineering team routes through. That's a real business. But a user on Hacker News already reported that only 30% of generated PRs are mergeable, with the rest being band-aid fixes. At that quality level, the product is a triage dashboard with aspirational code generation -- not yet the autonomous production engineer it claims to be. The compounding story requires fix quality to improve dramatically, and that improvement depends on LLM capabilities that Sonarly doesn't control.

The founders' multi-year working relationship and willingness to relocate to San Francisco from France signals genuine commitment -- no red flags on integrity or energy. But intelligence and energy without specific knowledge produces a well-executed commodity. I'd need to see either deep infrastructure expertise that the dossier doesn't reveal, or a proprietary technical approach to cross-tool correlation that goes beyond "connect APIs and prompt an LLM." Right now this reads as a smart team building something any other smart team could build in the same timeframe, in a market where the incumbents are already shipping competitive features as add-ons to their existing platforms. The leverage is borrowed, the knowledge is common, and the timing is consensus.
