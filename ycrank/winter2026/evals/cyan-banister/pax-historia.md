# Pax Historia -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Pax Historia: 35,000 people are using this thing every day, and nobody paid for a single ad to get them there. Four months, organic, browser-based, no mobile app yet. That's a real signal. I've seen enough consumer products to know that kind of organic pull doesn't happen by accident -- something about this game is grabbing people. The UGC flywheel with 4,000+ community-created presets, the Fandom wiki, the TikTok discovery -- it has the early fingerprints of a platform where the community is building the product for you, which is exactly what made Roblox so powerful. If I were evaluating purely on execution velocity, I'd be leaning forward in my chair.

But I can't invest the way I invest without hearing the founder's story, and here I'm essentially deaf. Eli Bullock-Papa went to Virginia Tech engineering, worked at Capital One, and -- intriguingly -- at Olive Garden. Ryan Zhang's entire YC biography is "I like history." That's three words. I don't know what drove these two people to build this specific thing. Did one of them grow up obsessed with alternate history because they were escaping a difficult reality? Did they meet in some unexpected way that fused their skills? I genuinely don't know, and that matters because my whole process starts with the question "tell me your story -- not the business." The execution here is undeniably strong, but execution without a biographical thread connecting founder to mission is something I've seen break down when the inevitable crisis hits. When Crusoe Energy nearly collapsed in 2020, it was the founders' deep, almost irrational commitment to their weird vision that kept them going while Jonathan helped them pivot. I need to know what keeps these founders going when the LLM costs spike or a well-funded competitor shows up.

And that leads me to my bigger concern: this doesn't feel like the kind of company I gravitate toward. AI-powered gaming is a hot category right now. Every VC I talk to is excited about what generative AI can do for games. When I backed SpaceX, private space was considered lunacy. When I wrote a check for Contraline, male contraception made investors physically squirm. Pax Historia would get enthusiastic nods at any demo day -- which is precisely the signal that makes me cautious. The most promising companies I've invested in were unpopular and difficult to assess. This one is popular and relatively easy to understand: you wrap an LLM in a map interface with geopolitical context and let people play alternate history. A fan already cloned it on GitHub with Gemini. The UGC library and community create real switching costs, but at 4,000 presets that moat is still shallow enough to wade across.

The strongest bull case here is genuinely compelling: if the UGC marketplace evolves into a real creator economy -- where preset makers earn revenue, where the content library becomes so rich that no competitor can replicate it from cold start, where mobile launch unlocks a massive new audience -- this could become the platform layer for AI-driven interactive entertainment. The structural inability of Paradox and Firaxis to pivot to this model (it would cannibalize their DLC revenue and require fundamentally different infrastructure) creates real space, similar to how taxi companies couldn't become Uber. And if inference costs continue their downward trajectory, the unit economics could work beautifully at scale. I respect that vision. But for me to write my own check, I need the founder's story to be inseparable from the company's mission, and I need the idea to be the kind of thing that makes other investors uncomfortable. This makes other investors excited, which is a different kind of signal.

I also notice what's absent from the picture: there's no economic access narrative here. My portfolio is shaped by companies that create income for people who need it, that make opaque systems transparent, that give underserved populations a foothold. Uber wasn't just logistics -- it was a poverty-alleviation tool for drivers. Affirm wasn't just fintech -- it was transparent credit for people being exploited by hidden fees. Pax Historia is entertainment. Good entertainment, clearly, given the traction. But it doesn't create economic opportunity for underserved populations or make an invisible system visible. The UGC marketplace could theoretically become a creator economy, but there's no evidence yet that creators earn anything from their presets. This is a dimension where the company simply doesn't activate my framework.

I'd tell these founders: keep building, because the traction is real and the execution is impressive. If I met them and heard a story that connected their lived experience to this problem in a way that made the hair on the back of my neck stand up, I might feel differently. But based on what I can see today, this is a well-executed consumer gaming product in a hot category with a thin moat and founders I can't yet read. That's a pass for me.
