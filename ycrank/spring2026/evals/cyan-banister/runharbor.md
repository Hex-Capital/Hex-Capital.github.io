# Harbor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps out at me immediately about Harbor: this isn't a weird bet. "AI for clinical trials" is the kind of category that makes every VC in a pitch meeting nod along -- of course legacy EDC systems from the 1990s should be automated, of course LLMs should extract data from source documents, of course the pricing should be simpler. Medidata knows it. Veeva knows it -- they've already announced AI Agents rolling out across R&D in 2026. Castor raised $65 million building toward the same insight. When the two largest incumbents in your space are both shipping AI features on your exact timeline, and a well-funded competitor already has 50,000 users, you're not operating in unpopular territory. You're operating in a crowded race where the question isn't whether the idea is right but whether a two-person team from YC can outrun organizations with thousands of existing customers and the ability to bundle AI features at marginal cost into existing contracts. The most promising companies I've backed were not popular and were difficult to assess. Harbor is popular and relatively easy to assess -- and that makes me cautious.

When I look at Albert Cai's path, I see genuine domain immersion but not the kind of biographical forging that makes me feel someone was built for this fight. He spent four years at Biolinq watching site coordinators grind through manual data entry during FDA-track studies, and he helped take a product from prototype through first-in-human trials to De Novo authorization. He chose this world over medical school. That's real. That's credible. But I'm not hearing a survival story -- I'm hearing a smart biomedical engineer who noticed an obvious inefficiency and decided to build a better tool. I've learned that the founders who persist through the kind of crisis that inevitably comes -- and in clinical trial software, that crisis will be the first time an AI extraction error shows up in an FDA audit -- are the ones whose connection to the problem runs deeper than professional observation. Nathan Leung is technically capable with strong early-stage startup experience from Jupiter, but his biography doesn't connect to healthcare or clinical trials at all. I'd want to sit across from Albert and ask him to tell me his story, not the business, before I could assess whether there's a deeper thread I'm missing from the public record.

What I do find genuinely interesting -- and what a generic analyst might overlook -- is the transparency play embedded in Harbor's pricing model. Flat monthly license, no per-subject, no per-site, no per-CRF charges. That's a direct attack on the opacity that Medidata and Veeva use to extract value from sponsors. I've seen this pattern work. Affirm did it to credit card companies by making interest rates transparent. Carta did it to the cap table management industry by replacing opaque spreadsheets. When you make an invisible system visible and strip out hidden pricing complexity, you create genuine loyalty among customers who felt exploited by the old regime. And the free academic tier takes this further -- under-resourced research institutions that can't afford Medidata now have access to modern clinical trial infrastructure. That's expanding economic access to a system that currently excludes smaller players. These are the kinds of structural choices that tell me the founders understand something about the market beyond the technology.

The regulatory navigation here is real and I respect it. Building 21 CFR Part 11 compliance, HIPAA, and GDPR into a two-person operation is genuinely hard work that most AI startups would wave away. That compliance infrastructure is a moat -- not an impenetrable one, but it takes serious engineering and documentation investment that any new entrant has to replicate. The customer testimonials are strong too: a 1,600-subject trial configured in one week versus legacy vendor timelines of months, source documents and CRFs built in two days instead of two months. Those aren't vanity metrics -- those are real operational improvements that clinical operations teams will fight to keep. Once a trial is running on Harbor's EDC, switching mid-study is practically impossible due to audit trail requirements. Every study they onboard is locked in.

For this to be a great investment through my lens, I'd need to believe that Albert Cai has the kind of relentless, almost irrational commitment that would carry him through the moment when Veeva offers his biggest prospect a free AI upgrade bundled into their existing contract -- because that moment is coming, probably within the next twelve months. I'd need to feel that this team has something the incumbents cannot replicate with engineering resources alone. The product clearly works and the traction is meaningful for pre-seed, but the competitive window is narrowing fast, and I don't see evidence of the kind of founder-forged-by-adversity conviction that makes me believe they'll outrun organizations with thousand-fold resource advantages. This is a competent team building a legible product in a space that's about to get very crowded. I wish it were weirder.
