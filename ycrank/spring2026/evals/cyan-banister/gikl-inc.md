# Gikl, Inc -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first: two of Meta's most senior AI researchers left after a combined twenty years to build... a community platform. Peter Vajda directed media generation — Emu, Movie Gen, the stuff that put Meta's generative AI on the map. Seiji Yamamoto was in the Core Llama group, Senior Staff, spanning pre-training through inference. These are people who know how to build AI infrastructure at planetary scale. And their product is a place where citizen scientists submit proof sketches to open problems across six domains. There's a real tension between the founders' capabilities and what they're building — it's as if two of the best rocket engineers in the world decided to build a community garden. The garden might be wonderful, but it doesn't obviously require their specific forge.

When I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — I get something incomplete. Yamamoto has a thread I can pull: he was a physicist, published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, co-authored with a Fields Medalist. He lived inside the academic system, felt how slow and bottlenecked the path from insight to publication actually is, then left for finance and tech. That's a real biographical connection to the problem. But he left physics over thirteen years ago. Is he returning to a wound that never healed, or did the AI capability wave make this look like a good market? The dossier gives me no evidence either way, and the absence of that evidence matters. Vajda's connection is thinner — he's an extraordinary AI builder, but "I can build AI products at scale" is a capability, not a crucible. Neither founder shows me the thing I look for most: the moment when the problem found them, when they couldn't walk away. The credentials are genuinely world-class. The biographical inevitability is not there.

The space itself concerns me deeply. "AI for scientific discovery" is one of the hottest consensus narratives in tech right now. Google deployed AI Co-Scientist at seventeen DOE National Labs. OpenAI is building a fully automated researcher. Sakana AI raised $379M at a $2.65B valuation. Elicit has $33M in funding and an estimated $18-22M ARR. FutureHouse raised $70M. When I look at my biggest wins — SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive, Contraline when VCs wouldn't touch male birth control — they all operated in spaces where the room got quiet. AI for science? The room is cheering. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "we're democratizing scientific discovery." The "citizen science" angle creates some differentiation from the autonomous-AI-scientist crowd, but differentiation isn't contrarianism. Being the only crowdsourced player in a crowded, well-funded space doesn't make you contrarian — it makes you the odd one out in a consensus category, which is a different and more precarious position.

I want to be honest about the bull case, because I can see it. If the coding assistant analogy holds — if AI really can compress twelve years of scientific training into a usable interface the way Copilot compressed years of programming education — then the first platform to build a dense community of AI-augmented citizen scientists owns something no one else has. The network effects would compound: every approach submitted, every verification, every counterexample becomes training data and institutional knowledge that makes the next contribution more valuable. Yamamoto's dual identity as physicist and LLM infrastructure builder is genuinely rare. And the "built for AI agents" framing hints at something potentially infrastructural beneath the community surface — a structured interface where AI agents submit and verify scientific work at scale, with humans providing the judgment layer. That could become a data asset no foundation model company can replicate, because it's not about the AI — it's about the accumulated structure of scientific reasoning across thousands of problems. If I squint, I can see the Crusoe pattern: a surface that looks like one thing (community platform) hiding infrastructure underneath (a proprietary scientific reasoning dataset). For this to be a Long Journey bet, I'd need to see evidence that the founders understand they're building the dataset, not the platform — and that they have a community-building capability that their Meta careers don't obviously demonstrate.

What I don't see is the invisible system being disrupted. Science is gatekept, yes — by credentials, by institutional access, by the glacial peer review process. But ScienceSwarm isn't making those gates transparent the way Affirm made credit card terms transparent or Ownwell made property tax assessments visible. It's building a path around the gates, which is access expansion, not system exposure. When I backed Carta, the thesis was that cap table management was deliberately opaque — the system extracted value from founders who couldn't see what they were signing. I can't identify the equivalent hidden tax in scientific research that ScienceSwarm exposes. The publishing cartels (Elsevier, Springer) are extractive, but this platform doesn't attack them. The credential system is exclusionary, but it's not exactly opaque — everyone knows you need a PhD to do science. Routing around a visible barrier is different from exposing an invisible one.

Defensibility at this stage is theoretical and threatened. The potential network effects are real but completely unvalidated — zero traction signals in the dossier, no user counts, no contributions, no community. Google is giving away AI Co-Scientist to the most prestigious research institutions on earth. For free. The technical components of literature synthesis and hypothesis generation are available through foundation model APIs. The moat has to come from community density and accumulated contribution data, which means solving the cold-start problem in a category where the incumbents are both richer and already deployed. That's not impossible, but it requires a community-building genius that I don't see evidence of in either founder's background.

I respect these founders. They're technically extraordinary. The impact potential — genuinely expanding who gets to participate in scientific discovery — is real and meaningful. But I don't feel the pull. The forge isn't there, the space isn't uncomfortable enough, and the invisible system test comes back negative. This is career-optimized founders in a consensus-hot space building a community product without demonstrated community-building capability against incumbents with orders of magnitude more resources. I wish them well, but this isn't my bet.
