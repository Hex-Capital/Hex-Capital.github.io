# Foaster -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Foaster is an AI consulting company helping enterprises operationalize AI. Let me just sit with that for a second. In April 2026, "AI transformation consulting" is maybe the single most consensus category in enterprise software. Enterprise AI budgets jumped from 2-3% to 8-12% of IT spend in three years. Celonis has $1.77B in funding. Wonderful AI raised $284M across three rounds. McKinsey already partnered with Wonderful for AI agent deployment. Deloitte, BCG, PwC — every Big Four firm is building AI practices at scale. When I look at Foaster, my first question isn't "is this a good company?" — it's "is there any mispricing left?" And the answer is no. Every sophisticated investor on the planet understands that enterprises need help deploying AI. The risk isn't in the price. The upside is.

Raphaël Dabadie and Alexandre Combes are the best part of Foaster, and I want to be honest about that because the founders deserve credit even when the category doesn't work for me. Alexandre built Répondia and handled 60k+ AI restaurant calls — that's real deployment at scale, not a demo. Their Werewolf LLM benchmark got 300k+ views on X, shared by OpenAI's president, with engagement from DeepMind and Mistral researchers. xAI's co-founder reached out to get Grok 4 added to their rankings. These are 23-year-old ENSAE Paris classmates who've co-authored research and co-built products — a real co-founder relationship, not an incubator match. But here's my problem with Foaster: the founders are sane, and the idea is also sane. "Automate consulting interviews with AI agents" is exactly what every smart person in a room would nod along to. There's no moment where I think "what you're doing is fucking crazy." Reasonable people with a reasonable idea is just... boring from my seat. The sane-insane ratio is inverted.

Foaster's core product — AI agents conducting 45-minute interviews to map enterprise workflows — is a method improvement on something McKinsey has done for decades. The case study shows 280 people interviewed in 5 days at a 350-employee firm, 1,200+ processes mapped, 9-day total delivery versus months for traditional consultants. That's impressive execution. But "faster consulting diagnostic" is not a new primitive. Foaster isn't creating a behavior that didn't exist before. Nobody's going to say "we got Foastered" the way people say "Venmo me." When I backed Venmo, Andrew and Iqram were creating a peer-to-peer payments behavior that literally did not exist — it became a verb. Foaster is a better version of an existing category, competing on speed and cost against incumbents. The upside is capped by the existing market's willingness to switch from McKinsey, and McKinsey has relationships Foaster will spend years and millions trying to build.

LIPTON Teas and Infusions as a client reference at pre-seed is a genuine signal — that's a multinational (Unilever spinoff), not a friend's startup. But let me run the AI kill test, which is where Foaster really breaks for me. The dossier says it plainly: "Any well-resourced team with consulting domain expertise could build a competing product." Foaster's core technology is LLM-based interviews running on foundation model APIs. Celonis, with $1.77B raised and ~$771M in 2023 revenue, could add AI interview capabilities to their process intelligence platform in a quarter. The Big Four have the client relationships, the domain expertise, and the AI practices already in motion. Foaster's accumulated operational maps create some switching costs per client, but that's a thin moat — there's no protocol-level lock-in, no physical infrastructure, no network effects. This is pure software built on someone else's foundation models. Every time I hear "Adobe for AI," I think: this is perfect for the incumbent. Foaster is "McKinsey for AI" — and that's perfect for McKinsey.

The bull case an advocate would make: consulting firms face structural cannibalization — automating the interview phase that drives billable hours directly conflicts with McKinsey's revenue model. Foaster can be the Celonis of unstructured organizational intelligence, capturing tacit knowledge that system-log mining can't touch. The founders have deployment experience (60k+ calls) and research credibility (12M+ views on benchmarks) that most pre-seed teams lack by an order of magnitude. The TAM is real — $11B in 2025, projected $91B by 2035 at 26.2% CAGR. And maybe the "surprisingly candid with AI interviewers" insight is a genuine behavioral discovery that creates a wedge. I checked my Known Exceptions — Slack violated consensus mispricing because it created a genuine new primitive (real-time workplace messaging replacing email). Could Foaster's AI interviews be a similar primitive? I don't buy it. AI interviews are a faster method for producing the same output consultants already deliver. Slack changed the behavior; Foaster accelerates the process. The exception doesn't apply. The one verifiable condition that would flip me: if Foaster showed me data that their AI interview outputs surface fundamentally different organizational insights than human consultants — not faster, not cheaper, but categorically different intelligence that creates a new decision-making primitive — I'd reconsider. That would move this from "better consulting" to "new primitive."

Foaster scores well on capital discipline almost by default — two-person team, enterprise consulting generates revenue from early engagements, and the LIPTON and case study references suggest paying customers exist even if revenue figures aren't public. But capital discipline at 6/10 can't rescue a company that scores poorly on the dimensions I weight most heavily. The decisive dimensions are consensus mispricing and structural durability: Foaster is in the hottest category in enterprise software (AI transformation), built on generally available foundation models, competing against Celonis ($1.77B), Wonderful AI ($284M), and every Big Four consulting firm on earth. The price already reflects the opportunity, and the moat doesn't survive the kill test. Pass.
