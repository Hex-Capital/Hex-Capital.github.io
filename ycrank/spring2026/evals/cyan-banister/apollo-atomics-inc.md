# Apollo Atomics, Inc. -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Apollo Atomics, Inc. is a paper reactor with a real dissertation behind it -- maximum frontier-hardware fit (a compact PWR aiming at 3¢/kWh), minimum tactile artifact (the 10MWe A-10 pilot is a 2028 target and the "40x smaller" steam generator only began experimental testing under the MIT collaboration announced April 16, 2026). That high/low pairing is the whole company in one sentence: thesis-perfect on the axis I most rarely get to fund, and structurally invisible on the axis I most reliably source from. Assil Halimi spent 2021-2025 writing the iPWR steam generator under Koroush Shirvan at MIT NSE, which is the rare case where the PhD *is* the product roadmap -- but a PhD is not Palmer Luckey shipping Oculus from a garage before he pivoted to defense.

SpaceX (2008) is the rung that Apollo Atomics, Inc. most wants to be measured against on capital intensity, and on that single axis it deserves the comparison -- nuclear is exactly the "too hard, too unsexy, too capital-intensive" lane where my checks have historically compounded, and the regulatory moat from an eventual NRC construction permit is the same kind of multi-year barrier that kept the rocket competitors away from Elon for a decade. Where Apollo Atomics, Inc. diverges from the SpaceX shape is the *founder-shipping* prior: Musk had already taken PayPal to exit and was self-funding the first three Falcon launches before any outside angel showed up, whereas Halimi's prior roles at Engie and EDF are research postings, not shipped-to-scale artifacts. Apollo Atomics, Inc. is asking me to underwrite frontier hardware on the strength of a dissertation and a research collaboration, not on a founder who has already moved physical atoms to a customer.

The Pass Example -- the consensus YC AI agent darling with six funds chasing -- is the rung whose failure mechanism keeps surfacing when I look at Apollo Atomics, Inc.'s competitive set. The mechanism is round-price-as-consensus-tax: when NuScale (only NRC-certified SMR), Oklo (public), X-energy (>$700M raised including the Amazon-led $500M), Aalo Atomics ($127M+ raised, July 2026 cold criticality at INL), and Kairos are all chasing the same hyperscaler dollar, the category is no longer "magically weird" -- it is the most-talked-about industrial bet of the 2026 batch cycle. Apollo Atomics, Inc.'s YC P26 stamp arrives into a market where the contrarian trade was made by whoever wrote into Aalo in 2024 at $27M, not by whoever writes here in 2026 behind Genesis Fund, Intuition, and NSF. I find them before it becomes consensus; nuclear-for-data-centers is consensus now.

Mechanism on Single Unambiguous CEO: Apollo Atomics, Inc. has a clean Halimi-CEO / Walker-COO delineation on paper, which is the structural inverse of the Yusupov-Kroll trap, and over 18 months the technical-CEO + ops-COO split usually compounds well *if* the COO is genuinely deferential on product. Drew Walker's background -- BA Political Science, George Washington, Blue Innovations Group, White House staff -- reads more "co-equal external face" than "Halimi's executor," and that asymmetry is exactly where the GameCrush (~2011) lesson lives for me; I would want to see the operating agreement before I read this as resolved rather than latent.

Counterfactual on Magically Weird: for Apollo Atomics, Inc.'s score on this axis to flip a tier upward, two of NuScale, Oklo, X-energy, or Aalo would need to have publicly passed on the compact-PWR architecture on aesthetic or licensing grounds and Halimi would need to articulate why their refusal was the misread -- the SpaceX-2008 "rockets are uninvestable" pattern. The dossier shows the opposite: every named competitor is pursuing a *different* coolant chemistry (sodium, molten salt, HTGR) precisely because the PWR lane is considered the incumbent's lane, and Westinghouse plus GE-Hitachi would cannibalize their large-PWR books to chase Apollo Atomics, Inc. down. That is a strategic moat argument, not a magically-weird argument, and I do not get to claim both.

Single-anchor parallel on Physical-World Verifiability: Niantic (~2015) is the anchor, and I funded it because I had been playing Ingress obsessively since 2012 -- the artifact existed in my hand before John Hanke pitched me. Apollo Atomics, Inc. is the opposite condition: nothing to install, nothing to photograph, nothing to ride, no well pad I can walk onto the way Chase Lochmiller could at Crusoe. The 10MWe pilot is a 2028 line item and the steam generator is in two-phase flow testing at MIT; I cannot do my own sourcing work on this company, which means I am underwriting a research program and a credential, not an observed object.

Forecast, 36 months: by mid-2029 Apollo Atomics, Inc. has filed an NRC construction permit application roughly on schedule, raised a $40-90M Series A led by a hyperscaler-adjacent strategic (Oracle/Stargate flow, Amazon-adjacent, or a Korean utility), has zero MWe operating and zero revenue, and is materially behind Aalo on demonstration -- alive but trailing, a credible technical company that will be priced this round at a number that already assumes Series B success. The mechanism is straightforward: Halimi's dissertation is real enough to keep raising on, and 2026-2029 hyperscaler nuclear demand keeps the round open, but the licensing clock and the 40x steam generator's unproven performance prevent a step-function event in the window.

The strongest bull case against my own forecast is the DeepMind exception: Hassabis arrived in 2013 with elite credentials and no shipped-to-scale prior, and I wrote the check anyway because the polymath compounding overrode my anti-pedigree default -- Halimi's 2021-2025 MIT NSE work on fuel performance, thermal hydraulics, and compact steam-generator design under Shirvan is exactly the cross-domain technical depth that DeepMind precedent says I should reward. The reason the exception does not save Apollo Atomics, Inc. here is that Hassabis was contrarian *on the market* in 2013 (deep RL was unfunded), whereas Halimi is conventional on the market in 2026 (every SMR investor is at this table) and contrarian only on coolant choice -- the polymath bonus does not apply when the category itself is consensus. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: if Apollo Atomics, Inc. publishes peer-reviewed two-phase flow validation of the 40x compact steam generator at PWR conditions before end-2027 *and* secures a signed term sheet with a named hyperscaler for a specific MWe offtake, I will follow into the Series A; absent both, I sit out.
