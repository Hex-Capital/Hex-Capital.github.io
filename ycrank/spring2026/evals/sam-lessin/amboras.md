# Amboras -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about "the Shopify of the AI era" — when four companies with $10M to $21M in funding are all racing to build the same thing, and the pitch deck leads with TAM size and AI capability, you're not looking at an insane idea. You're looking at the most consensus play in e-commerce circa Spring 2026. Genstore launched #1 on Product Hunt. Spangle AI is already at a $100M valuation with REVOLVE and Steve Madden as customers. Qeen.ai has Prosus writing eight-figure checks. When every VC in the room wants a piece of "AI-native commerce," the non-consensus premium has been fully arbitraged away. Amboras is a sane person walking into a crowded room and shouting the same thing everyone else is shouting.

Amin Mokadem has real credentials — CS from ETH Zurich and a prior e-commerce business at six-figure MRR. That's the "sane" half of my equation, and it's genuine. He's lived the pain of building and optimizing a store manually. But domain credibility paired with a consensus opportunity is precisely the combination my framework is designed to filter out. The best version of this bet was Genstore's seed round, maybe twelve months ago, before the category was legible to everyone. By March 2026, building an AI-powered store builder is like proposing a subscription box in 2013 — the window where this was a non-obvious insight has closed.

The structural moat problem here is not subtle — the dossier itself states it plainly: "The core capabilities rely on generally available foundation models and standard e-commerce infrastructure." Every capability Amboras describes — natural language store generation, automated A/B testing, conversion optimization — is a feature that Shopify can ship natively to 4.8 million merchants tomorrow morning. And Shopify is already doing it with Shopify Magic and Sidekick. My "cherry on top" test is brutal here: remove the AI layer and what's left? Nothing. There are no painting crews, no parking garages, no physical infrastructure that software amplifies but cannot replace. The software IS the product, and when software is the product in a category where foundation models are commoditizing at this rate, you're building on sand. Craftwork works as an investment because no LLM can hold a paintbrush. Amboras has no equivalent anchor in the physical world.

The strongest bull case is the agent-ready API angle — the idea that Amboras could become the protocol layer for machine-to-machine commerce, where AI purchasing agents discover and transact with stores programmatically. If that market materializes, the company that defines the API standard would be building a genuine primitive, something closer to what I look for in a Venmo or an Aleo. And Amin's technical background from ETH Zurich gives him the chops to build at that protocol layer. But this requires two things to be true simultaneously: first, that AI purchasing agents become a real market at meaningful scale (they haven't yet), and second, that a pre-seed startup with a 438-person waitlist, not Shopify with its installed base, gets to define that standard. The probability of both being true is low enough that it's not a foundation for a check — it's a lottery ticket stapled to the back of a SaaS pitch.

The waitlist numbers — 438 people, 169 of 200 cohort spots claimed — are fine for pre-seed but they're not the kind of organic demand signal that changes my analysis. No revenue, no named customers, no conversion data from the product itself. Three of four team members are completely invisible publicly. The second co-founder isn't even named anywhere I can find. When I'm writing a check from my own money, team opacity at this level isn't a neutral signal — it's a negative one, especially in a category where execution speed against well-capitalized competitors is the only variable that matters.

I don't invest in AI-first companies where AI is the core value proposition. I don't invest in hot categories where the pitch deck leads with market size. I don't invest in pure software businesses with no physical-world moat. Amboras hits all three of my avoidance patterns simultaneously. The founder is credible, but credibility in a consensus market doesn't produce venture returns — it produces a competent company in a knife fight with four other competent companies and an incumbent with infinite distribution. Pass.
