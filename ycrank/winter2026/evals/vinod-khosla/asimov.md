# Asimov -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at Asimov is a competitive map that should make the founders lose sleep. micro1 has $35M in fresh capital, $100M+ ARR, and is already shipping Ray-Ban glasses for crowdsourced physical data capture. General Intuition raised $134M at seed to train spatial reasoning from two billion gaming videos per year. Scale AI has $16.4 billion behind it and is extending into robotics. Sensei, from the very same YC ecosystem, is attacking the identical problem with different hardware. Asimov is the least capitalized serious player in a race where the prize goes to whoever builds the largest, most diverse dataset fastest. The dossier's own defensibility assessment says the quiet part out loud: "operational complexity rather than deep technical moats." When the barrier to your business is logistics rather than invention, capital advantage becomes the decisive weapon — and Asimov doesn't have it.

Now, the consequence-of-success question. If Asimov wins — if it becomes the dominant data marketplace for physical AI — what changes? Humanoid robots get better training data, which means they learn faster, which accelerates the entire physical AI revolution. That matters. The humanoid robot market is growing at 39% CAGR, and if the optimists are right, we're looking at a multi-hundred-billion-dollar industry within a decade. The data layer for that industry could be genuinely valuable — Scale AI built a $16.4 billion business being the data infrastructure for language and vision AI. But here's what I keep coming back to: Asimov doesn't make anything obsolete. It doesn't replace an incumbent system. It doesn't make the current way of doing things uneconomic. It collects video and annotates it. That's a services business dressed up as a platform. The consequence of success is a profitable data company, not a transformed industry. The robots themselves transform the industry. Asimov sells them supplies.

The timing, I'll give them credit for. The physical AI data bottleneck became acute in 2024-2025 as humanoid robot companies raised billions and immediately hit the wall — where do you get thousands of hours of diverse human motion data when your lab can only teleoperate one robot at a time? Asimov's insight that you can capture organic human motion via lightweight headband cameras worn during normal activities is clever and timely. They already have 5,000+ contributors across three continents, which represents real operational execution for a pre-seed company. The wave is real and accelerating. But — and this is critical — this is a consensus wave. Everyone sees it coming. When the "tipping point" is described in market reports and every robotics startup is screaming about data scarcity, you're not skating to where the puck will be. You're skating to where everyone already knows the puck is headed. My best investments were in areas where the experts actively mocked the thesis. Nobody is mocking the thesis that robots need training data.

The strongest bull case would be this: data network effects are real, and once Asimov's dataset crosses a quality and diversity threshold, it becomes the default training corpus that every robotics team must license — the ImageNet of physical AI. The 5,000-contributor network is a genuine head start. The annotation pipeline producing 3D body pose, depth maps, and semantic labels from raw egocentric video is technically non-trivial. If Asimov executes flawlessly on contributor management, annotation quality, and enterprise sales, it could build a flywheel that compounds faster than competitors can replicate. The founders have relevant pieces — Anshul's Scale AI experience means he's seen how the dominant data marketplace operates from the inside, and Lyem's defense tech robotics background combined with a prior YC stint at Blume suggests the learning rate is there. For this bull case to work, execution speed would need to outrun micro1's capital advantage, the annotation pipeline would need to be meaningfully better than competitors', and the contributor network would need to become self-reinforcing before well-funded alternatives absorb it. It's possible. But it requires winning an operational footrace against richer competitors in an approach that the dossier itself describes as replicable.

The founding team is young Berkeley undergrads — I have no problem with that; Peter Beck didn't have an aerospace degree. But the pattern I look for is outsiders attempting what experts declare impossible. These founders are insiders applying a known model (data marketplaces) to a new domain (robotics). Anshul literally worked at Scale AI. That's not an outsider reinventing an industry; that's a smart person extending his employer's playbook into an adjacent vertical. The approach is smart but not contrarian, operational but not inventive, well-timed but not early.

This is a capable team riding a genuine wave, and if I were making a pure financial optimization — looking for a reasonable probability of a decent outcome — I'd be more interested. But that's exactly the mode I avoid. I'm not looking for the company most likely to build a modest data business. I'm looking for the company whose success would make the world operate differently. Asimov, at its best, is picks and shovels for someone else's revolution. The consequence of its success is a valuable services company, not a disrupted industry. Some of my best investments looked crazy; this one looks sensible. That's the problem.
