# Confluence Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the sequence: build an impressive AI benchmark result, then go looking for a business. Confluence Labs achieved 97.9% on ARC-AGI-2, beating teams with hundreds of millions in funding. That's genuinely remarkable for a two-person team. But when I ask my first question — "how did they find this problem?" — I can't find the answer. There's no story about a founder who spent years in a drug discovery lab watching experiments fail because of data scarcity. No materials scientist who got fed up with running thousand-dollar experiments that yielded one data point. The problem they describe is real and important, but it reads like it was derived from the capability rather than the other way around. They solved abstract grid puzzles brilliantly, then looked around and said "where can we apply this?" That's the arrow pointing the wrong direction. Drew Houston didn't build a file sync algorithm and then go looking for people who lose USB drives. He kept losing his USB drive.

The schlep in AI-for-science is genuinely enormous — and it's the part these founders haven't touched yet. Getting a pharmaceutical company to trust your model with a drug candidate involves years of validation, regulatory navigation, and the kind of tedious integration work that makes your eyes glaze over. Materials science workflows have their own version of this pain. The benchmark is the fun part. It's the equivalent of building a beautiful payments API but never having walked into a bank. Stripe worked because the Collisons were willing to wade into the ugly banking and regulatory morass that every other programmer instinctively avoided. Confluence Labs has demonstrated they can solve elegant puzzles. The question is whether they'll endure the ugly, unstructured work of turning that into something a scientist actually relies on — and nothing in the dossier suggests they've started that journey or have the domain background to navigate it.

The technical hacker signal, however, is strong. Both founders clearly build things themselves. Brent is self-taught, dropped out of college, has 49 public repos and built a real AI pair programming extension. Niranjan got into the Research Science Institute at MIT as a high schooler and reportedly ran a startup to $5M ARR before this (though that's a LinkedIn snippet, not confirmed). They achieved the top score on a hard reasoning benchmark as two people against Imbue's ten-thousand-GPU operation. That's the kind of disproportionate output that makes me pay attention. These are hackers in the real sense — people who make things. If they'd stumbled into the right problem organically, this team profile would be exciting.

The strongest bull case: maybe I'm wrong about the direction of the arrow. Maybe the technical capability they've demonstrated in few-shot reasoning genuinely is the hard part, and the domain application is the easier translation step. Maybe Niranjan's prior startup experience (if the $5M ARR figure is real) means he knows how to find customers and build a business, not just win benchmarks. The ARC-AGI-2 result could function like a calling card that opens doors with research-intensive companies — the way a YC Demo Day performance opens doors with investors. If the underlying capability transfers from abstract grids to molecular design, and if these founders prove adaptable enough to learn the domain, this could be important. The "why now" is real: LLM-driven program synthesis just crossed a capability threshold, and the cost of physical experiments keeps rising. Someone will build the sample-efficient AI platform for science. The question is whether it'll be these two.

But the absence of demand evidence concerns me more than the absence of revenue. At pre-seed I don't expect revenue. I do expect some signal that the people you claim to serve actually want what you're building. No LOIs from labs. No waitlist from researchers. No anecdote about a materials scientist who saw the ARC-AGI-2 result and said "can you do this for my domain?" Ninety-four GitHub stars on an open-source solver is modest — and they open-sourced their primary differentiator under MIT license, which means anyone can build on top of it. The defensibility has to come from domain-specific work they haven't started yet, using domain expertise they don't appear to have yet. That's two miracles, and I generally avoid companies that need more than one.

These are talented builders working on a real problem, but the problem found them through a benchmark leaderboard rather than through lived experience. The gap between "we can solve abstract reasoning tasks" and "we can help a drug company design better experiments" is wider than it looks — and it's exactly the kind of schlep that the dossier gives me no evidence they've begun to confront. I'd want to see them spend six months embedded with actual scientists before I'd get excited.
