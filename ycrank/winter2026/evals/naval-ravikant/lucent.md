# Lucent -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The pivot tells the story. Lucent raised $1.3M in October 2025 for "behavioural datasets for browser agents" — an infrastructure play with genuine picks-and-shovels logic for the AI agent wave. By the time the YC W26 batch started, the product had become "AI that watches session replays to detect bugs." That's a move from infrastructure to application layer, from data moat to processing layer, from a novel category to a feature that FullStory, Sentry, and PostHog could each ship in a quarter. Investors funded one thesis and received another. That pivot direction — away from compounding infrastructure, toward a thin AI layer on top of incumbents' data — is the opposite of the trajectory I look for. When I backed Alchemy, blockchain infrastructure was so nascent that no incumbent occupied the developer API layer. Session replay is a mature, well-capitalized category where four companies with a combined $650M+ in funding already possess the sessions, the customers, and the engineering teams to add automated analysis as a product update.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Alisa Wu is clearly a fast executor — building and exiting an edtech tutoring app in nine months at age 22 is real energy. But what non-transferable knowledge does she bring to AI-powered session replay analysis? Her background is founding engineer at an ad creative platform, a tutoring app, and an Atlassian internship. She forked rrweb on GitHub, which shows engagement with the recording technology, but forking an open-source library is something any engineer does in a weekend. The deeper challenge here — reliably identifying bugs and UX friction from visual and DOM replay data across diverse applications — requires either deep ML/CV expertise or deep domain knowledge of how software fails in production. I see evidence of neither. The mention that she used "ChatGPT as her Chief Technology Officer" during fundraising is a tell: it signals AI-augmented development speed, not the kind of deep technical understanding that compounds into a moat. The founding engineer's identity and background are undisclosed, which makes it impossible to assess whether the team has the technical depth this problem demands.

The leverage architecture here is standard SaaS — code processing data at scale, which gives baseline software leverage. But it's a processing layer, not a platform. There are no network effects: one customer's bug patterns don't obviously improve detection for another customer in a different domain (though a shared taxonomy of failure modes could theoretically emerge over time). Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question-and-answer pair made the knowledge graph more valuable for every subsequent user, creating a compounding data moat that justified a $1.8B acquisition. Lucent processes sessions; it doesn't create a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. The incumbents already have the leverage — the session data, the customer relationships, the integration points. Lucent is asking to be a middleware layer between those incumbents and their own data.

The strongest bull case: session replay is a category where recording vastly outpaces reviewing. There are genuinely billions of sessions sitting unwatched in FullStory and PostHog databases. If multimodal AI just crossed the accuracy threshold for useful automated analysis — and if Lucent moves fast enough to own the "intelligence layer" before incumbents ship it themselves — there's a window where they become the default automated QA layer across the ecosystem. Wu's execution speed is a real asset in a window-of-opportunity play. And at 22, the compounding horizon is enormous — if she develops genuine specific knowledge through building this product, the next company she starts could be extraordinary. But for this company, in this market, with this competitive structure, I don't see how the window stays open long enough. Sentry at $3B valuation and $100M revenue has every incentive to add AI session analysis tomorrow. PostHog's open-source community could build it as a plugin. The concept — apply LLMs or vision models to session recordings — is an application of broadly available AI capabilities, not a proprietary breakthrough. When anyone can build it, you need distribution or data advantages to win. Lucent has neither yet.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. "Apply AI to automate X" is the most crowded thesis in technology right now. Nobody smart dismisses the idea that AI could analyze session replays — they just expect the incumbents to do it. That's the opposite of the bets that generate asymmetric returns. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I invested in Bitcoin, it was dismissed as nerd money. The disagreement was about whether the thing could work at all. With Lucent, the disagreement is about who will capture the value — and the odds favor the companies that already own the sessions, the customers, and the distribution.
