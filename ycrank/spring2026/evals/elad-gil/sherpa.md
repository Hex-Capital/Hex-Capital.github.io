# Sherpa -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Optimizely does $400M in ARR. VWO was acquired for $200M. Webflow bought Intellimize in April 2024 specifically to add ML-powered optimization to its platform. Mutiny has raised $72.6M. When I look at Sherpa's market, I don't see a graveyard that AI is about to unlock -- I see a thriving, well-capitalized category where every incumbent already has AI on its roadmap. The question I always ask first -- "what structural change is making this possible NOW that wasn't possible two years ago?" -- has a real answer: LLMs can now generate credible marketing copy and page variants autonomously. But that answer is available to everyone. Optimizely's product team knows it. VWO's engineering org knows it. Every growth marketer on LinkedIn knows it. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, the structural shift in digital asset infrastructure was invisible to mainstream investors. When I wrote Harvey's seed check, legal AI had been a graveyard for a decade and nobody believed the GPT capability curve would crack it open. Sherpa is operating in a market where the structural catalyst is real but consensus has already priced it in. That's the fundamental problem.

Sherpa's positioning fails my infrastructure-layer test decisively. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies built on -- compounding switching costs with every integration. Sherpa is an end-user application sitting on top of generally available foundation models and standard web technologies. The dossier notes 4,338,896 website visits of "learning data," but that's a rounding error in terms of building a proprietary data advantage -- any competitor with a few mid-market customers accumulates that volume in weeks. Sherpa doesn't provide an API that other tools depend on; it doesn't create a platform layer that compounds. It's a tool you install, and if a better tool appears tomorrow or Optimizely ships equivalent functionality as a feature, Sherpa's switching costs are minimal. The "single line of code" integration that Sherpa advertises as a strength is also a weakness: what's easy to install is easy to uninstall.

Sherpa's single-miracle test reveals at least two independent breakthroughs required. First, LLM-generated page variants must consistently outperform human-designed alternatives across diverse industries and use cases -- not just for Lindy, which is Sherpa's former employer and primary case study. Second, marketing teams must trust an autonomous AI agent to modify live production websites without human review, a significant behavioral change in an industry built around approval workflows. The technology miracle is partially exogenous (foundation models are improving on their own curve), which helps, but the trust-and-adoption miracle is Sherpa's alone to solve, and the generalizability miracle -- proving the system works beyond the founders' former employer -- remains unproven. Three named customers (Lindy, Gauge, Yeti Co), with detailed case study data only from Lindy where both founders previously worked, doesn't resolve this.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Sherpa's 40 experiments launched in 30 days for Lindy, yielding +30% total conversion lift, is a genuinely compelling product result if it generalizes. The CRO market is projected to reach $2.86B by 2032 at 11.4% CAGR. Incumbent consolidation -- VWO and AB Tasty merging under Everstone, Intellimize absorbed into Webflow -- creates a window where legacy platforms are distracted by integration. Sherpa's full-loop automation (analyze, generate, test, deploy) genuinely eliminates the multi-team coordination bottleneck that keeps most companies from running experiments at velocity. If Sherpa captures the mid-market segment that can't afford Optimizely's enterprise pricing and doesn't have dedicated CRO teams, it could build a real business before incumbents catch up. I've seen cases where a genuinely 10x faster workflow in a "boring but critical" category creates enough customer love to overcome commoditization risk -- Gusto did this with payroll. But payroll has regulatory complexity that creates structural moats. CRO does not. The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Sherpa showed me five unaffiliated customers with 90-day retention data demonstrating consistent conversion lift across different verticals, plus evidence that incumbents' AI features are shipping slowly due to business model conflicts (cannibalization of professional services revenue), I'd reconsider. Right now that evidence doesn't exist.

Ethan Kinnan graduated from the University of Washington in 2024 with a BS in Finance and served as Head of Partnerships at Lindy. Norbu Sonam holds a BS in CS from RPI and was a software engineer at Lindy with internships at Microsoft and IBM. Both are early-career founders without scaling experience, and the team has exactly one engineer building an AI-heavy product. I don't require founder pedigree -- I've seen operators from all backgrounds succeed when market structure is favorable. But Sherpa's market structure isn't favorable enough to compensate for founder inexperience. When I backed Parker Conrad at Rippling, he'd already scaled and lost Zenefits -- he'd lived the scaling challenges. Sherpa's founders bring relevant product intuition from Lindy but haven't demonstrated the decisiveness and team-building capability that the 1-to-100 transition demands.

Sherpa is building an application on a contested layer in an obvious market with well-funded incumbents. The two dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Nonobvious Market Timing and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning. On timing, Sherpa's "why now" is real but consensus -- every CRO platform and their investors see AI-powered variant generation coming. On infrastructure positioning, Sherpa is a tool, not a platform, with switching costs limited by its own ease of integration. These two criteria account for 55 of my 100 points, and Sherpa scores below default on both because the evidence actively cuts against the thesis rather than simply being absent. Pass.
