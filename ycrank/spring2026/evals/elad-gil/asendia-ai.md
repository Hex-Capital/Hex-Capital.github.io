# Asendia AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this company pivoted. The Product Hunt launches, the 53.5K website visits, the 15K minutes of AI interview prep -- all of that belongs to a B2C interview coaching product called "Sarah, your AI Job Coach." The current B2B staffing agency platform is a different business with zero publicly verifiable traction. Pivots can be a positive signal -- Slack pivoted from a gaming company, Twitter pivoted from a podcast platform -- but those pivots revealed a market insight the founders had stumbled into through building. Here, the pivot reads more like a search for a business model. The B2C product got attention but presumably didn't monetize, so the team reframed the technology for a B2B buyer. That's pragmatic, but it means I'm evaluating a company at essentially day zero in its actual market, with the additional concern that the founders are entering staffing agency workflows without direct operational experience in that world.

The structural moment question is where this falls apart for me. AI-powered recruiting is not a non-obvious market. It's one of the most consensus AI application categories in venture right now -- hireEZ has raised $76M, Paradox raised over $250M before being acquired, Humanly and Fetcher have tens of millions each. The TAM figures in the dossier actually underscore the problem: a $656M market growing at 7.2% CAGR is not an inflecting market. That growth rate is barely above GDP. Compare this to the structural inflection I saw in crypto infrastructure when I backed Coinbase, or the GPT-3-to-GPT-4 capability jump that made Harvey possible. Those were markets where the adoption curve was bending upward in a way most investors hadn't mapped. AI recruiting has been funded, analyzed, and competed over for years. The Paradox acquisition by Workday is presented as a "why now," but I actually read it as the opposite signal -- it tells me incumbents are absorbing the AI layer into their platforms, not that standalone AI recruiting tools will thrive independently. When Workday pays $250M+ for a conversational AI recruiting platform, Bullhorn (the dominant staffing ATS) is watching and building.

The staffing agency vertical focus is the most defensible strategic choice here -- it's narrower than "recruiting" and the workflow needs are specific enough that a horizontal tool won't automatically satisfy them. But even granting that, I need to see evidence that the founders understand staffing agency operations from the inside, and I don't. Rihab's background is cloud architecture at Google and Microsoft with some marketing at Siemens. Badis spent six years at Infineon as a product application engineer. Neither has run a staffing desk, managed a book of requisitions, or navigated the margin dynamics of temporary vs. permanent placements. The compliance claims -- SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA -- from a three-person team also raise questions. Those certifications typically cost $50K-$200K and require significant organizational process; at pre-seed, that investment would be unusual and worth verifying.

The strongest bull case: staffing agencies are operationally inefficient, margin-compressed, and underserved by incumbent ATS platforms that were built for workflow management, not AI-powered candidate qualification. If Asendia can build deep enough integrations with Bullhorn and other staffing-specific ATS systems, the switching costs could create a durable distribution wedge. The 50+ ATS integrations claimed on the website, if real, represent meaningful engineering work. And the multi-agent architecture -- sourcing, outreach, video screening, phone screening bundled together -- is more complete than any single competitor's offering. If the team executes quickly through YC and lands three to five mid-market staffing agencies with measurable fill-rate improvements, the compounding data from each screening interaction could create proprietary scoring models that general-purpose tools can't match. That's a plausible path. But it requires the founders to rapidly develop staffing domain expertise they don't currently have, sell into agencies with long procurement cycles, maintain four distinct AI agent products with a team of three, and do all of this while well-funded competitors attack the same market from adjacent positions. That's not one miracle -- that's four.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is structurally limited in a way that caps my excitement. Staffing agencies are end consumers of recruiting tools, not distribution channels for additional products. Stripe worked because developers who integrated Stripe's API then distributed Stripe to every business they built for. Staffing agencies don't distribute software -- they distribute people. The multi-agent bundle creates upsell opportunity within the recruiting workflow, but it doesn't create a distribution channel that compounds into adjacent markets the way a platform play would. This is a product company, not a platform company, and the ceiling reflects that.

I pass. The market is consensus, the competitive landscape is crowded and consolidating toward incumbents, the founders lack domain depth in their target vertical, and the recent pivot means there's no validated B2B traction to evaluate. The underlying technology -- LLM-powered sourcing, screening, and interviewing -- is increasingly commoditized through foundation model APIs, which means the moat depends on execution speed and agency-specific workflow design. That's a thin moat for a team that's new to the staffing industry.
