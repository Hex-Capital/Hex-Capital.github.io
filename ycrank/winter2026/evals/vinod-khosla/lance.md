# Lance -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's the tension that defines Lance: the most interesting piece of their technology — Computer Use Agents that can visually navigate any legacy software interface — is potentially a category-creating capability. An agent that can operate arbitrary software through vision alone, at production quality, could restructure every enterprise integration in existence. That's a trillion-dollar disruption. But Lance isn't pursuing that. They've taken this potentially revolutionary technology and pointed it at answering hotel phone calls and dispatching towels. They've narrowed the scope, chosen a defined vertical, sold to known customers with a known pain point. In doing so, they've done exactly what I warn founders against — reduced the probability of failure to the point where the consequence of success is a nice SaaS business, not a transformation anyone will remember.

If Lance succeeds completely — runs the voice operations for every hotel in America — what changes? Hotels answer more calls. Staff get freed up from repetitive tasks. Some incremental revenue gets recovered from missed restaurant bookings. That's a real business, probably worth $1-3 billion at scale. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. The hospitality industry still operates the same way. Compare that to what Impossible Foods set out to do — reclaim half the planet's arable land — or what Commonwealth Fusion Systems is attempting — eliminate fossil fuel power generation. Those are consequences worth the risk of total loss. "30% reduction in front desk call volume" is not a consequence I'd invest my own money to pursue at pre-seed odds, no matter how well-executed.

The competitive landscape confirms the problem. When Canary Technologies has raised $180M at a $600M valuation and already offers AI voice capabilities to 20,000+ hotels, when Duve has raised $85M, when Asksuite is already doing an estimated $17.5M in annual revenue — you're not attacking a space where the experts say it can't be done. You're entering a space where well-capitalized incumbents are already building. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing in public telecom was impossible — that's the signal I look for. Nobody is telling Lance that AI voice for hotels is impossible. They're telling them it's obvious, and then racing to build it themselves. The voice AI layer is commoditizing in real time — OpenAI, ElevenLabs, Vapi are all driving the cost of voice agents toward zero. Lance's value narrows to the operational execution layer, which is genuinely harder to replicate but also harder to sell in a demo.

The strongest bull case deserves honest engagement. The team is well-constructed for this specific problem — a CEO who built voice and AI products at Google and Salesforce and was a founding engineer at a nine-figure exit, a COO who grew up inside Marriott operations, and a CTO with agent infrastructure expertise and Stanford speech research. That's deliberate gene pool engineering, and I respect it. Fifty-plus hotels across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at pre-seed is exceptional enterprise traction. If the Computer Use Agent approach truly eliminates integration friction with legacy PMS systems, they could deploy 10x faster than API-dependent competitors and build an operational data moat before the incumbents catch up. The path to a significant outcome exists if they expand from voice handling to becoming the full operational intelligence layer for hotel groups — effectively becoming the nervous system that coordinates everything on-property. For that bull case to play out, they'd need to demonstrate that Computer Use Agents create a durable speed advantage over API-based competitors, that hotel groups consolidate their technology stack around Lance rather than their PMS vendor, and that the operational execution layer compounds in value faster than voice AI commoditizes.

But even the bull case produces a successful vertical SaaS company, not a world that operates differently. The founders are experienced insiders applying known technology to a well-understood problem — the opposite of the outsider profile I've spent my career backing. There's no contrarian thesis here that the market rejects, no "this is crazy but if it works" moment. This is competent execution in a competitive market. That's exactly the kind of bet I leave for someone else.
