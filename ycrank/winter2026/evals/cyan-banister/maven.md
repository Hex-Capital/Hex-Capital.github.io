# Maven -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Maven: it's the most legible company I've looked at in a while. Payments for voice agents. Two Berkeley CS grads. Amazon on the resume. API infrastructure. Developer tools. Every word on that list is something consensus investors already understand and already fund. And that's exactly the problem from where I sit. The companies I've done well with — SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline — they were hard to explain. People squinted at them. Payment middleware for AI voice calls doesn't make anyone squint. It makes them nod. When everyone nods, the price is already wrong, and you're competing on execution in a race where much larger, better-capitalized players can sprint past you the moment the market proves out.

I wanted to hear the founders' stories. I wanted to know what brought Wasi and Brandon to this specific problem — not the market analysis, not the API architecture, but the human thread. What I found is two technically capable engineers from Berkeley and Amazon who identified a gap in a fast-growing market. That's smart product thinking. But I couldn't find the biographical connection that tells me these are the people who will grind through the inevitable crisis when VAPI or Stripe decides to build this themselves. When Crusoe's Bitcoin thesis collapsed, my partner Jonathan moved to North Dakota because those founders had a kind of irrational commitment to turning waste into computation that transcended any single business model. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that no one else cared about — his whole career was a bet on this problem. With Maven, I see competence and good timing, but I don't see the biographical inevitability that tells me these founders will find the pivot when the obvious path closes.

The structural position concerns me. Maven sits as a thin layer between voice platforms on one side and payment gateways on the other. Both sides have strong incentives to absorb this functionality. Bland AI has $65 million, VAPI has $20 million, and Stripe already builds extensive checkout customization. The PCI compliance burden is real — it's the most legitimate moat signal here — but it's a known, well-documented regulatory path, not the kind of labyrinthine regulatory thicket that genuinely deters competitors for years. Compare that to Contraline navigating FDA approval for male contraception, or Anduril learning defense procurement from scratch. PCI DSS is hard, but it's not the kind of hard that builds a lasting moat against well-funded incumbents. Defensibility is one of the first things I look for, and I don't see it here.

Let me be fair about the bull case, because the timing argument is genuinely strong. Voice AI platforms raised over $145 million in the past eighteen months. That capital is creating a massive installed base of deployed voice agents, and those agents genuinely cannot collect payments today. If Maven executes fast enough, they could become the default middleware embedded in every voice platform's SDK — the way Twilio became default for communications APIs. PCI compliance creates enough friction that platforms won't build it casually. And if Maven captures that default position across VAPI, Retell, LiveKit, and Twilio simultaneously, the switching costs compound. For this to work, they'd need to become so deeply integrated that ripping them out is more painful than the transaction fees they charge. That's a real path. But it requires a kind of relentless, almost unreasonable founder conviction that I haven't seen evidence of here — and it requires winning a race against much larger players before the window closes.

What I don't see at all is the economic access dimension that runs through my best investments. Uber created income for drivers. Postmates for delivery workers. Affirm gave people transparent access to credit. Maven helps businesses collect payments more efficiently through AI voice agents. It's useful infrastructure, but it doesn't create meaningful opportunity for people who lack it. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or level a playing field. It optimizes an existing payment flow for a new channel. That's a fine business to build, but it's not the kind of bet that gets me out of bed.
