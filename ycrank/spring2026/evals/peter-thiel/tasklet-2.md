# Tasklet -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

$5M ARR with 1,200% growth since January 2026 is the number that demands attention, and the number that reveals the trap. Tasklet has built something people want — that much is undeniable. But every time I see explosive growth in a category where Lindy has raised $50M, Relevance AI has raised $37M, Zapier commands $310M in annual revenue, and OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft are each building their own agent capabilities directly into platforms with hundreds of millions of existing users, I recognize the pattern. This is not a monopoly forming. This is a market forming — a market in which many companies will grow quickly, compete fiercely, and destroy each other's margins. Andrew Lee's own admission that "it is super expensive to run" compounds the structural concern: Tasklet is growing fast in a category where the cost of serving each customer is high, the underlying technology is commoditized across foundation model providers, and a dozen well-funded competitors share the identical thesis that "workflows should disappear and agents should reason." The dossier itself identifies the core risk with unusual clarity: "the core technology (LLM-based agent with tool integrations) is reproducible." When the dossier's own risk section articulates the absence of a moat, the secret test has already failed.

Andrew Lee's question — "what if the workflow goes away? And you just let the agent reason about what to do" — is posed as a contrarian insight, but in April 2026, this is the consensus view of the entire venture ecosystem. Every AI agent company, every foundation model provider, every enterprise software incumbent is asking the same question. Tasklet's positioning against Zapier and Make assumes that drag-and-drop workflow construction will be disrupted by natural-language agent execution. This is correct. But being correct about a consensus trend is not a secret — it is a thesis that IDC, Grand View Research, and every GP at every fund in San Francisco agrees with. The canonical screening question is "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Tasklet's answer — that AI agents will replace explicit workflow construction — would produce nodding agreement in every investor meeting on Sand Hill Road. The cleantech lesson applies with uncomfortable precision: entrepreneurs swept into a hot category because the category is hot, armed with the same foundation models and the same thesis as everyone else, competing on execution quality rather than on a proprietary insight about the world.

Andrew Lee and Jonny Dimond have worked together for over a decade — Firebase, Google, Shortwave, now Tasklet. That co-founder prehistory is exactly the signal I look for; founding relationships forged under genuine pressure at Firebase and sustained through a Google acquisition are the kind that survive the stress of company-building. Lee built Firebase through YC S11 to a Google acquisition and a platform now used by 15% of developers — a genuinely impressive infrastructure achievement. The $20M Series A at $175M from USV, Lightspeed, Jeff Dean, and Patrick and John Collison reflects serious investors validating the team. But Tasklet emerged from a pivot away from Shortwave after four years, not from a bold, specific conviction about how the world should work. The sales@shortwave.com email still listed as the contact, the Shortwave blog as the announcement channel, the dossier's note that "Tasklet and Shortwave remain distinct products" — these signal a team still straddling two identities with eight people. The pivot itself reveals iterative discovery: Tasklet was not Lee's definite vision for the future, it was something he found while building something else. That is the lean-startup methodology dressed in impressive credentials.

The bull case deserves construction before I dismantle it. A Firebase-pedigree founder with a decade-long co-founder relationship, $5M ARR growing at 1,200%, backed by the Collison brothers and Jeff Dean — this profile would be compelling if Tasklet occupied a monopoly position. The Zapier business model cannibalization argument has real merit: Zapier's $310M revenue depends on per-workflow pricing that an agent-first model structurally undermines, creating genuine switching friction for the incumbent. Tasklet's context engineering from years of Shortwave development and its integration breadth via Pipedream, MCP servers, and HTTP APIs create real execution advantages. If this market consolidates the way ride-sharing did — into two or three survivors capturing most of the value — then the Lyft exception from my framework would apply: category magnitude so extreme that even a non-monopolist produces fund-relevant returns. But AI agent platforms have no structural reason to consolidate like ride-sharing did. There are no geographic network effects, no physical-world constraints limiting supply. This market structure more closely resembles SaaS productivity tools, where dozens of companies each reach $100M+ ARR without any achieving dominance. Zapier itself proves the point — $310M ARR and still no monopoly in workflow automation after fifteen years.

Tasklet occupies zero physical-world territory. No hardware, no government contracts, no regulatory barriers, no manufacturing constraints. The Ubuntu VMs on Google Cloud, the Pipedream integration layer, the LLM-based reasoning — all of this can be replicated by any team with access to the same foundation models and $50M in funding. When Google integrates Gemini agent capabilities directly into Workspace, when Microsoft extends Copilot into autonomous task execution across M365, when OpenAI's Operator matures — Tasklet faces platform providers who own the distribution, own the models, and own the existing enterprise relationships. Palantir survived this kind of competitive pressure because it embedded proprietary software into intelligence operations until switching costs became structurally infinite. Tasklet has no equivalent embedding mechanism — its integrations run through Pipedream's infrastructure, creating a platform dependency that compounds rather than resolves the vulnerability.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Tasklet demonstrated a specific, non-replicable technical capability — not better prompt engineering or more integrations, but a genuine 10x advantage in agent reliability or context management that competitors cannot match even with equivalent resources — and if that capability translated into enterprise lock-in visible in retention cohorts and expansion revenue. The Firebase team has the engineering talent to build something genuinely proprietary; the question is whether they have identified the architectural insight that creates structural dominance rather than incremental execution advantage. The dimension that locks this verdict is Monopoly Secret: Tasklet's thesis is the consensus view of the AI industry, multiple well-funded competitors share it, and the dossier identifies the core technology as reproducible. Strong execution in a competitive market without a proprietary insight is precisely the condition under which my framework predicts value destruction — not because Tasklet will fail, but because its success will be competed away.
