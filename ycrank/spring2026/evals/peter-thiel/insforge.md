# InsForge -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Supabase has $500 million in funding, 80,000 GitHub stars, and has already announced MCP support for AI agent interaction. InsForge has $2 million, 7,700 stars, and a "semantic layer" that wraps the same Postgres, S3, and OAuth primitives Supabase already exposes. The canonical question -- what important truth do very few people agree with you on? -- receives an answer here that everyone already agrees with: AI agents need programmatic interfaces to backend infrastructure. This is not a secret. It is a feature request. Every developer tool company on earth is adding agent-native capabilities in 2026. When the insight powering your company appears in MarketsandMarkets reports projecting 48.1% CAGR, you are not building on a hidden truth -- you are riding a trend. Entrepreneurs swept up by the AI agent wave rather than by hard analysis about what specifically they know that Supabase does not -- this is the cleantech pattern recurring in developer infrastructure.

InsForge's self-reported MCPMark benchmarks -- 1.6x faster task completion, 1.7x higher accuracy versus Supabase -- deserve scrutiny precisely because they are self-reported. No independent validation exists. But even granting the benchmarks at face value, they describe an incremental improvement to an interface layer, not a technological discontinuity. Supabase could narrow that gap with a dedicated engineering sprint funded by its war chest. InsForge's Apache 2.0 license means Supabase could literally study the implementation. The "semantic layer" is a software abstraction over commodity components -- there is no physical barrier, no proprietary dataset, no government contract creating structural lock-in. Compare this to Palantir, where embedding proprietary intelligence software into CIA and Army operations created switching costs that compound annually and approach the structurally impossible to reverse. InsForge's switching costs are those of any BaaS platform -- real but generic, not monopoly-creating.

Hang Huang's persistence -- six YC applications before acceptance -- demonstrates determination that Paul Graham would prize. But persistence in applying to an accelerator is not the same as definite conviction about what the world looks like in ten years. InsForge's roadmap reads as standard product-led growth: free tier at $0, Pro at $25/month, enterprise at custom pricing. The revenue signal of $300/month at the time of their fifth rejection, with no current figure disclosed, suggests the conversion funnel from open-source adoption to payment remains unsolved. Tony Chang's infrastructure experience at Databricks and Meta is directly relevant to building backend systems, and his ten hackathon wins signal rapid prototyping ability. But hackathon culture -- build fast, demo well, move on -- is the embodiment of indefinite iteration, not the definite optimism of a founder who has bet irreversibly on a specific vision of the future. InsForge's founders share an Amazon internship overlap, not the kind of shared high-intensity prehistory -- a previous startup, a research breakthrough, a combat deployment -- that forges the alignment required for a monopoly-building decade.

The strongest bull case for InsForge is the distribution evidence: 4,000+ databases hosted, #1 Product of the Day on Product Hunt, #1 on GitHub Trending during launch week, 1.5 million X impressions from their building-in-public strategy. This is genuine organic pull -- developers are adopting InsForge without marketing spend, embedding it into their workflows unprompted. The pattern resembles early PayPal, when eBay sellers added payment logos to their auctions before PayPal asked them to. If InsForge becomes the default backend that every AI coding agent reaches for -- the way Stripe became the default payments API -- the outcome is enormous. The AI coding assistant market projected at $127 billion by 2032 provides the TAM for a power-law return. One could argue InsForge is the Supabase-to-Firebase disruption replaying: just as Supabase won developers from Firebase by being open-source and Postgres-native, InsForge could win from Supabase by being agent-native at the architectural level rather than as a retrofit.

I find this bull case unpersuasive because it confuses early adoption with monopoly structure. InsForge's 7,700 GitHub stars represent one-tenth of Supabase's community. The distribution is impressive for a pre-seed company but does not yet constitute a network effect or a structural moat -- it constitutes awareness. Supabase's convergence toward MCP support, documented in the dossier, means InsForge is racing an incumbent with 250x its capital toward the same destination. This is competition, and competition is the one thing my framework cannot forgive. InsForge is not making a previously impossible thing possible; it is making an existing thing marginally more convenient for a new category of user. That is a feature, not a company. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that InsForge's semantic layer creates compounding, self-reinforcing advantages -- perhaps agent-generated schema data or usage patterns that improve the platform in ways no competitor can replicate without InsForge's specific installed base. A proprietary data flywheel would transform this from an interface layer into a genuine monopoly mechanism. Without it, InsForge is a well-executed software wrapper that a $5 billion incumbent will absorb or replicate.

The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Physical-World Technology Moat. InsForge scores low on both because its core insight is consensus -- not contrarian -- and its technology is a software abstraction over commodity primitives with no physical, regulatory, or data-driven barrier to replication. The distribution signal is the single bright spot, but distribution without monopoly structure produces growth without defensibility. Wish grew rapidly too.
