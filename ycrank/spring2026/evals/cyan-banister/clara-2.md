# Clara -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Clara: this might be the single most consensus AI application I could imagine funding in 2026. Every venture newsletter, every LP deck, every GP dinner conversation for the past two years has circled around "AI is going to transform healthcare." K Health has raised $441 million. Hippocratic AI is valued at $3.5 billion. Forward Health has burned through $546 million. When I look at a space where the biggest players have collectively raised over a billion dollars and every investor in the Valley is nodding along saying "yes, AI healthcare, obviously" -- that's exactly where my alarm bells go off. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Clara is popular and, frankly, pretty easy to assess. That doesn't make it a bad company. It makes it a company that doesn't need my kind of money.

Now, if I were sitting across from George Favvas, my first question wouldn't be about the product or the market -- it would be: tell me how you got here. And what I know from the dossier is that he's a serial entrepreneur from Montreal with multiple exits, a Stanford Healthcare Leadership certificate, and a track record of building Circle Medical to $100M in revenue with profitability. That's genuinely impressive. Caitlin Swift has frontline clinical experience in cardiology and primary care. Zeeshan Ahmed designed products at Instagram and Careem at massive scale. By any conventional measure, this is an A-team. But what I'm listening for isn't credentials -- it's the wound. The thing that makes this founder the only person on earth who could solve this problem, the biographical thread that means they'll keep going when the company is in crisis and everyone else would walk away. I don't hear that here. I hear experienced operators who see a market opportunity and have the playbook to execute on it. That's valuable. But it's a different kind of signal than what I invest on.

The bull case is genuinely strong, and I want to be honest about it. Favvas essentially ran a $100 million proof-of-concept for the human-first version of this company, and now he's rebuilding it as AI-first with two co-founders who were inside that same machine. The clinical operations knowledge -- provider licensing across fifty states, insurance billing, EHR workflows, regulatory compliance -- is extraordinarily hard to replicate. Most AI startups entering healthcare will crash against the wall of clinical operations complexity. This team has already climbed that wall once and knows every handhold. The data moat is real too: aggregating patient records from 150,000+ facilities and building longitudinal health profiles creates switching costs that deepen over time. If you believe AI-first primary care is inevitable -- and I do think something like it is coming -- these are among the best-positioned founders to build it. A more conventional investor would write this check without hesitation.

There is something that catches my eye, though, and it's the pattern I keep coming back to in my own portfolio -- companies that make invisible systems visible. Your medical records are scattered across dozens of providers, locked in incompatible systems, essentially invisible to you as a patient. Clara is trying to pull all of that into a single coherent picture that an AI can reason over. That resonates with why I backed Carta, why I backed Flock Safety, why Affirm made sense to me. The transparency angle is real. And if Clara's AI-first model genuinely makes primary care accessible to people who currently can't get a doctor's appointment -- the uninsured, people in rural areas, the working poor who can't take three hours off for a 15-minute visit -- that would be the kind of economic access story that gets my attention. But the dossier doesn't show me that mission. There's no pricing, no evidence of targeting underserved populations, no signal that this is anything other than a better mousetrap for people who already have healthcare. The potential is there. The evidence isn't.

Two things genuinely concern me beyond the consensus positioning. First, Favvas appears to still be listed as CEO of Circle Medical, which is a WELL Health subsidiary doing over $100 million in revenue. I need to know he's all-in on Clara. When I invest, I'm investing in a person's obsessive focus, and divided attention at the pre-seed stage is the kind of thing that kills companies slowly. Second, the regulatory tightrope -- marketing yourself as "an AI primary care doctor" while your legal page says "not medical advice" -- is exactly the kind of tension that attracts regulatory scrutiny from state medical boards and the FTC. The team can probably navigate it given their background, but it's a real exposure.

I want to be clear: I think Clara will raise money easily. Experienced repeat founder, hot category, YC backing with Garry Tan as partner, proven team chemistry from Circle Medical. This is a deal that conventional investors will compete for. But that's precisely why it's not a deal for me. I take extraordinary risk on founders and ideas that make other people uncomfortable. Clara makes people comfortable. It checks boxes. And in my experience, the investments that check all the boxes at the seed stage are the ones that deliver solid returns but not the defining outcomes I'm looking for. I'd pass and wish George well -- and I'd probably ask him to call me if the company hits a crisis that scares away his other investors, because that's when things get interesting.
