# Lance -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I check with any company is what structural change makes this market possible now when it wasn't before. For Lance, the answer has three parts: voice AI crossed a production-quality threshold in 2024-2025, Computer Use Agent technology emerged as a viable paradigm in late 2024, and hotel staffing shortages remain acute post-pandemic. All three are real. But here's my concern — none of these are non-obvious. Canary Technologies has raised $180M and serves 20,000+ hotels with AI voice already in their suite. Duve raised $85M. HiJiffy, Asksuite, Akia — the market has attracted serious capital and multiple funded competitors. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed boring and nobody else was funding it. When I backed Harvey, legal AI seemed like a science project. Hotel AI voice automation does not trigger the "too boring" or "too niche" pattern that's driven my best returns. This is a consensus opportunity that multiple well-resourced teams have already identified. The TAM slides citing $58B in "AI for Hospitality & Tourism" are exactly the kind of static market sizing I warn against — Lance's actual serviceable market is US hotel voice automation, which is a fraction of that number.

Where Lance gets interesting is the Computer Use Agent approach — vision-based agents that navigate existing hotel PMS software by interacting with the screen rather than requiring API integrations. This is genuinely clever because hotel technology is notoriously fragmented. Oracle Hospitality, Amadeus, and dozens of smaller PMS providers each have legacy interfaces that were never designed for third-party integration. If Lance can reliably navigate these interfaces through screen vision rather than waiting for API partnerships, that's a distribution accelerant that could compress deployment timelines from months to days. It mirrors a pattern I've seen work: when integration friction is the primary barrier to adoption in enterprise software, removing that friction is the miracle that matters. But there's a fragility problem — PMS UI updates or redesigns could break the automation without warning, creating an ongoing maintenance burden that scales with the number of PMS systems supported. And critically, Computer Use Agent technology is a general-purpose capability being provided by the foundation model labs. Anyone building enterprise automation can deploy this approach. It's not Lance's proprietary technology — it's an application of technology that Anthropic and OpenAI are making broadly available.

The team composition deserves attention. Caleb Chan was a founding engineer at a company with a 9-figure exit and built voice and AI agent technology at Google and Salesforce. Gavin Brennen grew up around Marriott operations — that's the kind of domain knowledge you can't hire for. Gatik Trivedi has speech and language research at Stanford, prior work on LLM evaluation at a YC company, and a prior startup exit. This is a team that combines AI product experience with genuine hospitality domain familiarity, and 50+ hotels deployed across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at pre-seed is meaningful execution velocity. That said, those 50+ hotels likely come through 2-3 hotel group relationships rather than diversified sales. Loss of a single management company relationship could represent a large portion of the customer base.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Lance's combination of voice-first AI plus operational execution (the Lance Pad staff coordination hub plus task dispatch) creates a wedge that pure chatbot competitors can't replicate and that broad-platform players like Canary won't prioritize deeply enough. If Lance Pad becomes the central coordination layer for hotel staff — replacing walkie-talkies, routing tasks, managing fulfillment — that starts to look like a distribution channel for additional products: revenue management, guest messaging, upselling, housekeeping optimization. That's the product-to-distribution trajectory I look for. The Gusto analogy would be: Gusto started with "just payroll" and became the HR platform for small businesses. Could Lance start with "just phone calls" and become the operations platform for hotel properties? The path exists, but I don't see evidence the founders are articulating this as a distribution strategy rather than just building features.

The single-miracle test yields a mixed result. The one miracle Lance needs is reliable end-to-end task execution through Computer Use Agents across diverse hotel PMS systems. If that works at production quality, voice AI is commoditized infrastructure they can plug in, and distribution follows through hotel groups. But the voice AI commoditization risk is real — when OpenAI, ElevenLabs, and Vapi make voice agents trivially deployable, Lance's most visible differentiator compresses to the operational execution layer, which is harder to demonstrate in sales cycles. And Canary Technologies, with 20,000+ hotel relationships and $180M in capital, could build or acquire the same operational execution capability. The competitive dynamics here are not favorable for a pre-seed company.

I respect the execution — 50+ branded hotel deployments at this stage is not nothing, and the founder-market fit is genuine. But the market structure doesn't match my framework. This is a well-identified opportunity with well-funded incumbents, and the core technology layer (voice AI) is commoditizing rapidly. The Computer Use Agent approach is interesting but fragile and non-proprietary. I'd want to see either a dramatically more defensible technical moat or evidence that the operational execution layer creates compounding advantages that incumbents structurally can't replicate. Right now, I see a good team executing well in a competitive market — which often produces a decent outcome but not the asymmetric return I'm underwriting.
