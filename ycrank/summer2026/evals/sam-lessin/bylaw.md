# Bylaw -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Bylaw is, almost line-for-line, the deck I wrote about in my pass example -- a Summer 2026 YC company selling an SDK that gates AI agent writes, competing into Galileo's $68M war chest and the Agent Control OSS release from March 2026, fronted by three first-year-Waterloo CS undergrads whose entire public surface area is two LinkedIns and no Twitter, no GitHub, no Substack, no Discord. The dimensional signature is brutal: the highest-scoring axis for Bylaw is a modest founder-market-fit story stitched out of Optiver, Meta and BitGo internships, and the lowest is the round itself, which sits in the most consensus-priced corner of the 2026 YC batch. That is not a venture moment. That is the regatta starting gun firing while Bylaw is still rigging the boat in a marina that has fifty identical boats already at sea.

Drop.io is the HIGH rung on the founder-conviction ladder because I bootstrapped a file-sharing primitive for two years against free incumbents on a missionary belief about ephemeral sharing -- the mechanism is pre-pitch, multi-year, publicly-legible obsession with a non-obvious belief the founder cannot be talked out of. Bylaw's three co-founders met in their first year of CS at Waterloo and built an evidence gate after seeing unverified writes at Optiver and BitGo internships -- which is a credible *origin story*, not a missionary thesis, and the gap matters. Mazin Al-Ani, Farhan Ur Rehman and Gurshabd Singh Varaich have produced zero public writing on agent reliability that I can find -- no opinionated essay on why NeMo's tool-permission frame is wrong, no thread on why Galileo's post-hoc evals miss the runtime cut, nothing. Writing is thinking, and an evidence-gate company whose founders have not published a paragraph on evidence is a company that responded to the YC agent-infra opportunity rather than pursued a personal mission into it.

The canonical pass-example deck -- "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $30M post-money seed, four tier-1 VCs already in" -- is the LOW rung on the round-pricing ladder, and the failure mechanism is that consensus AI-infra pricing at seed gives me zero alpha against the $68M Galileo just raised or the ~$300M Check Point paid for Lakera in November 2025. Bylaw fits the template with embarrassing fidelity: YC S26, AI agent guardrails, integrations name-dropped against LangSmith/Langfuse/OpenAI/Braintrust, no pricing page, no disclosed round size, no named lead -- which in a 2026 AI-infra batch means standard YC SAFE economics priced for a sector where Galileo released an Apache-2.0 substitute four months ago with AWS, CrewAI and Glean as launch partners. The dossier shows no tier-1 fund visibly passing, no category dismissal that I could be contrarian against, no price discount for being early in a sleeve others quit. Bylaw is the price I literally cannot make money at.

Capital structure compounds badly here over the next 18 months because Bylaw's product -- a runtime SDK gating sensitive agent actions across "five places at once" -- is a textbook land-and-expand developer-SaaS shape that will need a Series A within 18 months to fund GTM headcount against Galileo's Fortune-50 distribution (Comcast, Twilio), then a Series B to fund enterprise compliance certifications for the August 2026 EU AI Act high-risk window. That is four-to-five tacks in the regatta, each priced against a richer incumbent, with no revenue cushion disclosed and no unusual financing instrument -- not a royalty on policy-library accumulation, not a holdco wrapping the deterministic-policy IP separately from the SDK. Bylaw is a generic SaaS cap table getting force-fit onto a thesis whose moat narrative ("accumulated policy library and labeled evidence-conflict telemetry") is itself a 24-months-from-now hypothesis.

The brand-and-community score for Bylaw would only flip a tier if at least one of Mazin, Farhan or Gurshabd were running a technical newsletter or a GitHub project with measurable traction in the agent-reliability community before the round -- the way Bookshelved required John Fish's pre-existing YouTube audience to be underwritable. Instead the dossier shows no founder Twitter accounts found, no notable GitHub repos, no Product Hunt launch, no press, and a brand name ("Bylaw") that SEO-collides with municipal-bylaw tooling. There is no owned distribution channel on the Bylaw cap table -- which means customer acquisition for an SDK product runs through paid GTM or developer-relations hires the company has not yet funded, against a Galileo blog that already ranks for "AI agent guardrails."

Sublime Security is the single anchor I keep wanting to use for Bylaw on the category-sleeve axis -- enterprise security at seed, before the next consolidation wave, in a category SOC-SaaS investors had stopped underwriting at seed. The parallel breaks at the most important point: Sublime was *pre*-consensus in a sleeve other VCs had structurally abandoned, while Bylaw is *intra*-consensus in the most-funded sub-sector of 2026, with five named competitors in the dossier (Guardrails AI, Lakera, Prompt Security, Galileo, NeMo) and the launch partners of one of them already published. AI is just more internet; agent guardrails are a feature roadmap inside Galileo and Langfuse, not a sleeve I am convicted on at seed pricing.

My 36-month forecast for Bylaw: acquihired into Galileo, Langfuse, or Braintrust for sub-$15M total consideration by mid-2028, after Galileo's Agent Control OSS eats the deterministic-policy framing and Bylaw's SDK fails to clear $500K ARR against incumbent free-tier offerings -- a forecast that follows directly from the signature, where modest founder-market-fit cannot overcome consensus pricing in a category being commoditized in real time by a $68M competitor that shipped an open-source substitute four months before Bylaw's batch even started.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the EU AI Act high-risk-system clause taking effect August 2 2026, which could force enterprise compliance procurement into a focused runtime-evidence wedge that general-purpose observability tools won't satisfy at audit -- exactly the regulatory-schlep moat that justified Ro as a Known Exception on capital intensity. The bear-case dismantle is that the *same regulation* is what makes Galileo, Lakera/Check Point ($300M acquirer with enterprise procurement already wired), and Prompt Security ($23M raised, enterprise GenAI gateway shape) the rational buyers for compliance officers, and Bylaw's dossier shows no signed pilot, no design partner, no benchmark on the latency budget for reconstructing an evidence manifest before each write -- the technical claim is unvalidated and the GTM is unowned. I flip to invest if Bylaw publishes a signed paid pilot with a named Fortune-500 financial-services or healthcare buyer citing EU AI Act Article 9/15 compliance as the procurement driver, at terms that imply a sub-batch-median post-money and at least one tier-1 fund visibly passing -- absent that, Bylaw is the pass example, not the exception to it.
