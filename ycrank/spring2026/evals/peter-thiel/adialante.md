# Adialante -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Efraín Torres and Parker Jenkins are PhD MRI-physicists out of the University of Minnesota's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research — the lab that pioneered fMRI — selling per-scan prostate imaging from a trailer with two issued U.S. patents on a "FREE" gradientless architecture. That is the signature: Adialante is overweight on atoms-heavy regulated-sector moat and underweight on monopoly trajectory, which means I am looking at a credentialed-outsider hardware bet where the technical secret is real but the competitive geometry — Promaxo at $99.1M raised, Hyperfine at $90M Series D, Prenuvo at $275M — has already drawn well-capitalized peers onto adjacent rungs of Adialante's ladder.

Anduril (2017) is the load-bearing comparison on the atoms-heavy axis, and Adialante earns the comparison more than most YC pre-seeds I see. The shared mechanism is structural: a regulated customer (FDA-cleared diagnostic device sold into urology clinic workflow) plus hardware capex (mobile trailers Adialante owns and operates per-scan) creates a barrier that pure-software entrants cannot replicate within nine months of a model release. Where Adialante diverges from Anduril is the integration-depth moat — Palmer Luckey's defense thesis terminates in DoD trust relationships that compound over a decade, whereas Adialante's clinic-by-clinic per-scan model with 6 signed urology clinics and $30k of deposits from a top-10 urology counterparty is a distribution beachhead, not yet an integration moat. The atoms-heavy capex is genuine; the regulated lock-in must still be built.

The lowest rung on Adialante's monopoly-trajectory ladder is the failure mode I keep seeing in healthcare hardware: the pure-software SaaS pattern where the moat hypothesis terminates at "we have better tech," and any competitor closes the gap inside a funding cycle. Does that pattern appear in Adialante? Partially. The FREE physics is patented and PhD-gated, which is harder to clone than a model weight — but Hyperfine has already shipped FDA-cleared 64mT portable MRI to market, Promaxo has $99.1M deployed into in-office prostate MRI, and Prenuvo has $275M to expand into any segment it wants. The named incumbent failure mode for Siemens/GE/Philips — channel-conflict cannibalization of their multi-million-dollar capital sale — is real, but it does not obviously bar Hyperfine from extending its low-field portable architecture from brain to prostate. Adialante's monopoly trajectory is a race against well-capitalized adjacencies, not a category vacuum like Palantir (2003) walked into.

On the contrarian-secret dimension, the mechanism analysis over the next 18 months runs like this: Adialante's secret is "the urology clinic that orders the biopsy should own the MRI workflow, and the only way to deliver that is per-scan mobile, not capital equipment." That secret compounds if the clinic per-scan model becomes the default ordering pattern — because $12.75M in LOIs converts to revenue, FDA clearance lands, and the per-scan data corpus trains an AI quality advantage Hyperfine cannot replicate without trailer fleet operations. It fails if AUA guideline updates push reimbursement toward hospital-based imaging, or if Hyperfine simply licenses the per-scan model from a trailer operator. The secret is specific enough to score above the absent-evidence default — Adialante names Promaxo, Hyperfine, Prenuvo, and the prime incumbents explicitly — but it is not the kind of secret the market actively rejects. It is a secret the market has not yet gotten to.

On definite plan, the counterfactual is what would tip Adialante from indefinite to definite: a named 3-year roadmap for FDA de novo timing, trailer fleet count by year, and per-scan price floor — none of which the dossier surfaces. What I have is "first system operational by demo day" and a per-scan business model, which is closer to a milestone than a multi-year SpaceX-style roadmap. The score would flip a tier if Torres committed publicly to a specific FDA pathway date and a fleet-economics curve that the founder will not pivot from. The $1.18M NSF SBIR Phase II is partial validation of a definite technical roadmap, but it is grant scaffolding, not founder-committed monopoly architecture.

On founder lineage, Adialante most resembles Stripe (2010) on the credentialed-outsider axis without the operator track record — Torres is a UMN Student Entrepreneur of the Year and a first-generation Mexican-American PhD whose intellectual identity sits outside the median YC peer, which is exactly the unusual-pedigree signal I weight. The CMRR provenance is the real signal here; MRI physics is one of the few fields where PhD credentialing is the moat, not the resume line. What is missing is the shipped-to-scale operator pedigree of an Affirm-style Levchin re-up. Torres and Jenkins have shipped patents and a grant; they have not shipped a product to a billion-dollar exit. For pre-seed deep-tech this is acceptable but not load-bearing.

In 36 months I expect Adialante is at $3–10M ARR from regional urology clinic rollout with FDA clearance secured, and is acquired by Hyperfine or GE HealthCare for $80–180M rather than escaping to a category-defining outcome — the mechanism being that Promaxo and Hyperfine close the per-scan/mobile gap with deployed capital before Adialante's trailer fleet reaches density, and the rational exit is integration into a larger imaging platform that already has the FDA, payer, and channel relationships. This is a feature company, not a fund-returner; the atoms-heavy/regulated moat is enough to produce a real exit, not enough to compound into a Palantir-shaped outcome.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the Anduril analogy taken seriously: if FREE gradientless MRI delivers diagnostic-grade prostate imaging at trailer-scale cost, then Promaxo's single-sided in-office architecture and Hyperfine's brain-focused low-field stack are both structurally wrong for Adialante's market, and the per-scan data corpus from clinic operations becomes a compounding AI advantage no capital-equipment vendor can match without cannibalizing their installed base. That case stings because the CMRR pedigree is genuine and the channel-conflict thesis against Siemens/GE/Philips is the kind of structural incumbent blind spot I funded Anduril on. What dismantles it is the absence of integration-depth lock-in: clinics signing LOIs are not DoD program-of-record relationships, and $12.75M of non-binding LOI against $30k of deposit is closer to interest than to switching cost. The verdict flips to Invest if Adialante reports a binding multi-year exclusive with a top-10 urology network and an FDA de novo submission accepted before demo day — anything short of that, this is a Neutral hold and a watch position, not a check from my own balance sheet.
