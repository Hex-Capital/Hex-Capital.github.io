# ProjectX -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Two of ProjectX's three original co-founders — Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das, listed on Crunchbase as co-founders in 2022 — are no longer on the YC page, replaced by Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder. Founding relationships are the load-bearing structure of a startup. I have said that founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together, because without it they are just rolling dice. ProjectX has done something worse than rolling dice: it rolled them, didn't like the result, and rerolled. Rounak Adhikary and Bishal Karmakar share geographic proximity in Kalyani, West Bengal, but no confirmed shared employer, university, or prior high-intensity experience. Sourya Majumder's connection to the other two is unverifiable from the dossier. A founding team that has already been reconstituted once at pre-seed — before encountering any of the real stresses of scaling — is a structural warning, not a biographical footnote.

ProjectX's claimed insight is that every application should run in its own isolated virtual computer with dedicated GPU, accessible through a single browser URL, supporting Windows and Linux simultaneously. I ask the canonical question: what important truth do very few people agree with ProjectX on? The answer appears to be that per-application compute isolation is superior to per-desktop virtualization. But this is not a secret — it is an architectural preference that any competent systems engineer would recognize as plausible. Neverinstall ($1.21M raised), Vagon ($542K raised), and Workspot ($84.65M raised) are all building adjacent browser-based cloud compute products. Microsoft Azure Virtual Desktop and Amazon WorkSpaces dominate the incumbent landscape. ProjectX enters a market where multiple funded startups and two of the largest companies in human history are already competing. The existence of a competitive landscape slide with five named competitors is, by my framework, the clearest possible red flag. Competition is not a challenge to overcome through superior execution — it is evidence that the monopoly condition has not been met. ProjectX's per-app GPU isolation may be technically differentiated from Neverinstall's single-desktop streaming, but differentiation within a competitive market is the definition of incremental improvement, not zero-to-one creation.

Rounak Adhikary, born July 2002, has been building ProjectX since age 20, founding a web agency at 19 to fund the initial development — a biographical detail that demonstrates persistence. The bull case writes itself: a young founder with four years of obsessive commitment to a single problem, systems-level engineering talent in Rust, Go, and Kubernetes from Bishal Karmakar, Google Cloud's $204K investment, wins at Princeton's Tiger Launch and IIT Bombay's Eureka among 17,000 startups, and YC acceptance. The AI agent angle — human and agent workflows side by side — arrives at precisely the moment when every enterprise is trying to deploy autonomous agents into production environments. If AI agents become the primary consumers of compute, the traditional single-cursor desktop becomes obsolete, and ProjectX's per-app isolation architecture becomes the natural substrate. One could argue this is analogous to Palantir's earliest days: a technical architecture designed for a future that the market hasn't yet recognized. I take this argument seriously and reject it. Palantir's secret was not an architectural preference about how to organize software — it was an insight about the intelligence community's structural inability to synthesize information across classified silos, combined with a distribution strategy that embedded Palantir so deeply into CIA and Army operations that switching costs became effectively infinite. ProjectX has no equivalent institutional lock-in, no government customer, and no distribution insight of any kind. The "AI agents need new computing paradigms" thesis is the consensus view of every infrastructure startup pitching in 2025-2026 — it appeared in a dozen YC applications this batch alone, I would wager. Entering a hot category because the category is hot, rather than because you possess a specific secret about it, guarantees collision with every other trend-follower.

ProjectX's power-law potential is constrained by category economics. The DaaS market is estimated at $4.5-9.82 billion in 2025 with 11-19% CAGR — a modest, fragmented market dominated by platform incumbents who bundle desktop services with their cloud infrastructure at near-zero marginal cost. Microsoft does not sell Azure Virtual Desktop as a standalone product; it sells it as a reason to stay on Azure. Amazon does the same with WorkSpaces. ProjectX would need to build a standalone business competing against products that incumbents give away as platform attachments. Even in the optimistic scenario where ProjectX captures meaningful share of the power-user DaaS segment, the realistic outcome is a $200-500 million business — structurally irrelevant to power-law portfolio construction. The broader desktop virtualization market ($33.39 billion projected) does not help, because ProjectX is not competing for all of it — only the narrow slice of users who need per-app GPU isolation, which the dossier itself acknowledges is "a fraction of the total DaaS market."

ProjectX's distribution architecture is, as far as the dossier reveals, nonexistent. No revenue signal. No named customers. No LOIs. No user counts. The website returned a minimal loading page at time of research — nearly two years after the June 2024 public beta announcement in Business Standard. Zero job listings on the YC page. 1,542 LinkedIn followers. I have said that if you have invented something new but you have not invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business, regardless of how good the product is. ProjectX has not demonstrated that it has invented an effective way to sell anything — or, based on the website's state, that it has a product available to sell. The $204K from Google Cloud and approximately $72K in government grants and hyperscaler credits are infrastructure subsidies, not market validation. When those credits expire, ProjectX faces full hyperscaler compute costs for per-app dedicated GPU allocation — an architecture that is, by definition, more expensive per user than the shared-desktop model it seeks to replace.

ProjectX is a pure software abstraction layer running on other companies' GPU infrastructure, which places it at the absolute floor of my physical-world technology criterion. There is no hardware, no materials science, no regulatory barrier, no manufacturing process — nothing that a well-capitalized competitor could not replicate with eighteen months of engineering effort. The Wish parallel is instructive: a company entering a market dominated by incumbents (Amazon, Alibaba) without a genuine monopoly-creating secret, competing on execution and user experience rather than structural defensibility, in a category with no winner-take-all dynamics. The outcome of that investment taught me what happens when the monopoly criterion is absent, regardless of how large the addressable market appears. I would flip to Invest if ProjectX demonstrated one verifiable condition: a signed, non-cancelable contract with a government agency or Fortune 500 enterprise that creates operational embedding and switching costs — the Palantir pattern applied to cloud computing infrastructure. Absent that, ProjectX is a technically competent team building a feature that Microsoft could ship as a Windows 365 update.

The two dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Monopoly Secret and Distribution. ProjectX scores near the floor on both: no contrarian insight that the market disputes, no structural path to winner-take-all dominance, no evidence of organic pull or solved customer acquisition. A determined young founder pursuing a competitive software market against Microsoft and Amazon is still, in my framework, pursuing competition — and competition remains what it has always been.
