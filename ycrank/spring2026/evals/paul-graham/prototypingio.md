# Prototyping.io -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Prototyping.io isn't the founders or the technology — it's that everyone already agrees this is a good idea. AI for manufacturing is the kind of concept that makes people nod approvingly. Xometry is a public company doing $687M in revenue. Protolabs does $533M. Fictiv just sold for $350M. RMFG raised $4.5M and has shipped 100K+ parts. Jiga raised $19.1M and counts NASA as a customer. Toolpath raised $10M. Korso is in YC right now too. When I see that landscape, I don't think "big market, room for everyone." I think: the returns have been competed away before this company wrote its first line of code. The best startup ideas generate confusion or mild disgust from smart people. This one generates cap tables.

On the founders: Revanth has genuine domain credentials — IIT silver medalist, aerospace research at UT Austin, manufacturing work at Schlumberger. That's a real mechanical engineering background, not someone who read about DFM on Wikipedia last month. Prerit brings the AI/systems side — Microsoft AI, distributed systems publications at ACM and IEEE, founding engineer experience. The pairing covers the two essential halves of this problem. But cover isn't the same as compel. What I look for in that first conversation is the animal quality — founders who are slightly too intense, who have stories of bending reality when they were blocked. I don't see any of that here. No prior startup shipped. No creative survival stories. No obsessive side project that became the company. The dossier tells me they "recently joined forces," which means these two are essentially strangers who decided to start a company together. That's one of the patterns that worries me most. Co-founder relationships forged in the crucible of shared experience — college roommates, former colleagues who shipped something together — have a resilience that arranged marriages between complementary skill sets often lack.

The problem discovery concerns me too. The company's own framing is that hardware teams face "slow, fragmented processes." That's true, but it's a description you'd write after reading industry reports, not after personally spending three frustrated weeks trying to get a part machined for your own project. Revanth worked at Schlumberger, which is oil services — adjacent to manufacturing but not the custom parts procurement pain that Prototyping.io claims to solve. If Drew Houston had described Dropbox's origin as "file synchronization is a large and growing market," I would have been much less interested than when he said he kept forgetting his USB drive on the bus. The lived-experience origin story isn't just marketing — it's diagnostic. Founders who discovered their problem organically understand the texture of user frustration in a way that market-research founders simply cannot replicate.

Here's the bull case I have to take seriously: manufacturing DFM analysis is genuinely a schlep. Most AI startups go after clean digital workflows — text, images, code. Interpreting 3D CAD geometry, understanding tolerance stackups across six manufacturing processes, and mapping that to a supplier network with real physical constraints? That's hard, unglamorous work that repels the median YC applicant. If Prototyping.io can build a DFM analysis engine that catches issues engineers currently don't discover until parts come back wrong — and if that tool becomes the wedge that pulls ordering through the platform — that's a real product with a real moat. The Stripe parallel would be: nobody wanted to deal with PCI compliance, so the Collisons built the abstraction layer and captured the transaction flow. Maybe nobody wants to deal with DFM analysis, and Revanth and Prerit build the abstraction layer. In that world, the manufacturing domain expertise plus AI systems background is exactly the right founding team.

But the Stripe comparison breaks down in a critical way. When I invested in Stripe, I already knew Patrick Collison — I'd watched him build and sell Auctomatic as a teenager. I had years of evidence about his learning rate, his intensity, his taste. Here I have two résumés and a waitlist page. No working prototype that I can see. No obsessive early users banging on a crappy v1. No revenue, no LOIs, no evidence that any engineer has used this tool and found it indispensable. The product status is "Request Early Access," which tells me they're still building — or worse, that they built a landing page before building the product. At YC I'd want to sit across from these two for ten minutes and probe whether they have the determination and flexibility to outrun five better-funded competitors. From this dossier alone, I can't find that signal. The credentials are solid. The idea is real. But solid credentials in a consensus-good space with no distinctive execution evidence is exactly the profile that blends into the crowd.
