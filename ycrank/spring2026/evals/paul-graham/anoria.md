# ANORIA -- Paul Graham Evaluation

ANORIA is a polished consumer-hardware play built on a consensus thesis, and that pairing is what tells me to pass. The high signal is Michael Belhassen's five years at Apple shipping the iPhone 17 Pro enclosure — that is real, hands-on builder credibility. The low signal is the idea itself: an emotion-reading wearable with the tagline "EQ is the new IQ," launching three months after Luna Band debuted "emotional stress signatures" at CES 2026. ANORIA's strongest dimension is its founder's manufacturing chops; its weakest is that the idea sits exactly where my "Black Swan Farming" framework warns me away — in the part of the Venn diagram where everyone, including the trade press, immediately nods.

The dimension where ANORIA places lowest is Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, and the ladder runs from Stripe (Patrick Collison wading into PCI compliance and bank integrations that repelled every developer who looked at them) at the top, to Coinbase (a crypto exchange in 2012, when serious investors thought the entire category was a joke) in the middle, to the McKinsey-consultant B2B-workflow pitch I describe in my pass example at the bottom. ANORIA sits next to that bottom rung. "EQ is the new IQ" is not a thesis that knowledgeable people recoil from; it is a thesis they applaud at conferences. Whoop ($405M raised), Oura (>$2B valuation), Apple Watch, Friend, and Luna Band already validate the category — the dossier itself quotes industry coverage saying "differentiation now lives entirely in what you do with that data." That is not a sweet-spot bad idea. That is a contested software-execution scrum on top of incumbent hardware, and the consensus enthusiasm has already competed the returns away.

ANORIA's highest dimension is Founder Resourcefulness, and even there the placement is mid-low. The HIGH rung is Airbnb, where Brian and Joe sold $40 cereal boxes to fund the company when no investor would touch them. The MID rung is Stripe, where Patrick Collison had already shipped Auctomatic and sold it for $5M before he ever pitched me. The LOW rung is the McKinsey pair from my pass example, who confused 50 polite customer interviews for evidence. Belhassen has shipped real product — an iPhone enclosure is not a school project — but shipping inside Apple is not the same animal as shipping under constraint. The dossier contains zero stories of him hacking around obstacles, building revenue before fundraising, or doing something unscalable to find his first user. ANORIA sits a notch above the consultants because Apple iPhone Product Design is genuine builder craft; it sits well below Stripe because there is no narrative of personal scrappiness, only an institutional credit line.

On Organic Problem Discovery, the ladder is Dropbox (Drew Houston building it because he kept forgetting his USB drive) at the top, Octopart (physics grad students frustrated by parts search) in the middle, and the brainstormed B2B-workflow pitch at the bottom. ANORIA's origin story, as captured in the dossier, is the framing that "performance is 90% mental" and that "no consumer feedback loop exists for emotions." That is not a frustration. That is a slide. Belhassen is a hardware engineer with no visible domain experience in emotion science, signal processing, ML, or clinical psychology — the exact disciplines the product claim depends on. He did not live in the future of his own emotional regulation and build what was missing; he chose a category. ANORIA sits between the middle and bottom rung, leaning bottom.

Founder Learning Rate places ANORIA between Patrick Collison's Auctomatic-to-Stripe arc (MID rung) and the stagnant founder profile at the bottom — Belhassen is 26, Imperial First Class, five years at Apple, which gives him slope, but the dossier shows zero iteration evidence because the company is pre-launch with 12 LinkedIn followers. I apply the absent-evidence default. Earnest Builder Identity is the dimension I'd most want to test in person, but the public signal — "A HUMAN INTERACTION COMPANY," "EQ is the new IQ," May 5 launch teaser — reads as positioning, not obsession. Watsi at the top of that ladder embodies pure mission. Replit in the middle is hackers building for hackers. ANORIA is closer to neither than to the polished-pitch bottom; the founder X handle doubling as the company channel could go either way, but the language is borrowed framework, not lived urgency.

Solo-founder is the structural problem I keep returning to. ANORIA lists team size of five but names only Belhassen, and the missing names are precisely the people who would build the emotion-inference layer — the ML engineer, the signal-processing PhD, the clinical advisor. My "18 Mistakes" essay opens with solo founders for a reason: in a domain this technically novel, you need a co-founder who can talk you out of stupid algorithmic decisions. Belhassen can build the casing. The casing is not the company.

ANORIA in 36 months: acquired by Whoop or Oura as an acqui-hire for under $40M, with the wearable itself shipping one cycle and never reaching $5M ARR. The mechanism is incumbent fast-follow — Apple's HealthKit team adds an emotion-state output layer in iOS 22, Luna Band ships its second generation, and ANORIA's hardware-design advantage compresses into a feature checkbox the platforms own.

The bull case is ClassDojo — the Known Exception where I funded a non-developer-tools, non-hacker-audience consumer product because the founders' execution quality was exceptional and the schlep blindness was extreme. Could ANORIA be ClassDojo? No. ClassDojo's schlep was selling to school districts and managing parent relationships; that work genuinely repelled other founders. ANORIA's "schlep," if we are being generous, is FDA wellness-vs-medical-device positioning, but the dossier shows no posture on that, and consumer-wellness wearables is not a bureaucratic-procurement nightmare — it is the most-pitched category in YC consumer hardware. The exception does not apply. The condition that would flip my verdict: a named technical co-founder with peer-reviewed work in physiological signal processing or affective computing, plus a working prototype that emotion-labels free-living users (not lab subjects) above ~70% accuracy. Until then, ANORIA is a beautifully-machined casing in search of an algorithm, and I'm not the angel for that bet.
