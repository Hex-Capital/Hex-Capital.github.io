# Coalition Systems -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Andrew Badger is the most compelling thing about Coalition Systems. A former DIA case officer who graduated from the CIA's Farm, deployed to Afghanistan in 2014 supporting military intelligence operations, then advised McKinsey and Deutsche Bank on geopolitical risk — and he co-authored a book on Chinese espionage with the former Acting Director of the DIA. That is a person who has lived inside the coordination failures this company claims to solve. He has sat in rooms where allied forces couldn't share intelligence because the systems didn't talk to each other. He has watched people use Signal on personal phones for operationally sensitive communications because the formal C2 stack from General Dynamics was too rigid. Badger's biographical connection to this problem is visceral and firsthand. But Badger isn't the CEO. He's "Head of Strategic Partnerships." The CEO is Vijay Pathak, a 23-year-old Oxford DPhil student from Luxembourg whose prior company was an AI-driven political strategy advisory. When I ask my opening question — tell me the story, not the business — Pathak's answer is academic brilliance and a NATO Youth Award. That's a credential stack, not a forge. The person whose biography makes Coalition Systems inevitable is not the one steering it, and that structural mismatch sits uncomfortably with how I evaluate founding teams.

The "Slack for Defence" positioning concerns me for a different reason than most people would flag. It's not contrarian — it's deliberately legible. When I backed Anduril, Palmer Luckey was building autonomous weapons platforms and Silicon Valley treated defense technology like a moral contaminant. That took courage. Coalition Systems is branding itself with the friendliest possible consumer analogy. Defense tech has moved from politically toxic to consensus-attractive since my Anduril check — Palantir is public with $2.87B in U.S. government revenue growing 45% year-over-year, Second Front raised $152M at a $750M valuation, and a consortium of defense tech companies is jointly bidding on Pentagon contracts. The space no longer makes sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs. The allied coordination angle — cross-border classified operations spanning Five Eyes and NATO — is harder and less crowded than single-nation DoD software, and that's where Coalition Systems' genuine differentiation lives. But the "Slack for Defence" frame buries that differentiation under an accessible pitch-deck label.

Mattermost with 20,000-plus DoD users and $33.1 million in annual revenue is the elephant that Coalition Systems hasn't convinced me they can navigate. Mattermost is open-source, already accredited for DoD deployment including the USAF Air Mobility Command, and funded with $73.6 million. Coalition Systems at four people and roughly $500K from a16z Speedrun is attempting to differentiate on coalition-specific coordination features rather than intra-organizational messaging — a valid wedge strategy, but one that requires building cross-border accreditation infrastructure (FedRAMP, IL-5/IL-6, potentially ITAR) that typically takes years and demands cleared personnel working from SCIFs. The compliance barriers that theoretically create a moat are the same barriers that could crush a four-person pre-seed team before they ship a product. H.R. McMaster's advisory endorsement and Badger's intelligence community network provide real door-opening capacity, but doors opening is not the same as contracts closing in defense procurement.

The invisible system here is real but doesn't match my highest-conviction pattern. Military units coordinating through WhatsApp and Google Sheets because legacy C2 systems were designed for hardware-centric procurement cycles rather than actual warfighter needs — that's institutional dysfunction creating operational risk. The defense prime contractors (Lockheed, General Dynamics, L3Harris) build solutions optimized for programs of record, not for the sergeant in the field who needs to share a targeting update across coalition lines in thirty seconds. But this is different from the pattern I feel in my bones — Affirm exposing credit card opacity, Ownwell attacking the property tax system, Uber breaking the medallion cartel. Those companies shifted power from gatekeepers to ordinary people navigating systems designed to exploit them. Coalition Systems is building a better tool for professionals who already have institutional power. The impact is real — better coordination saves lives, and allied interoperability strengthens democratic security — but it doesn't generate the visceral recognition I carry from my own experience with opaque systems that prey on the vulnerable.

The strongest bull case runs through Badger's network and the post-Ukraine NATO interoperability mandate. If Coalition Systems can land a single coalition operations pilot through McMaster and Badger's connections — even a small one — the cross-organizational network effects of a coordination platform are genuinely powerful. Every additional allied organization that joins makes the platform harder to displace and more valuable to every existing user. The NATO urgency is real, the U.S. DoD has shown institutional willingness to adopt commercial software for core military functions (the Palantir Maven program of record in March 2026), and the allied coordination layer is a genuinely underserved niche. If Pathak grows into the CEO role and Badger's domain knowledge compensates for the CEO's limited operational experience, the advisory relationship with a former National Security Advisor could compress the typical defense GTM timeline. The question is whether a 23-year-old from Luxembourg can earn the trust of the people Badger's network gives him access to — generals and intelligence directors who evaluate vendors through a fundamentally different lens than YC demo day judges.

Coalition Systems sits in a space I believe in deeply but presents a founder story that doesn't activate my conviction. The decisive dimensions are the biographical forge — where Badger's extraordinary credentials are undermined by his non-CEO positioning and Pathak's academic-not-forged profile — and the contrarian conviction score, where defense tech's migration from toxic to consensus reduces the courage premium I assign. I'd watch this team. If Pathak demonstrates the kind of resilience that comes from building through rejection — if they land a classified pilot and survive the procurement valley — the story changes. Right now, the credentials outpace the forge.
