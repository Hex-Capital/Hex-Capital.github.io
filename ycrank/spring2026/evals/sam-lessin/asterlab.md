# Aster -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Autoscience raised $14M one month before Aster's YC batch with near-identical positioning — "the world's first automated AI research lab" — backed by General Catalyst and Toyota Ventures. Sakana AI sits at $479M in total funding and a $2.65B valuation. Google shipped its AI co-scientist on Gemini 2.0 in February 2025. FutureHouse and Edison Scientific have $320M combined. This is the opposite of a mispriced category. Every sophisticated investor on the planet has decided that "AI that does AI research" is a good bet, and they're pouring hundreds of millions into it right now. When I look at Aster, I don't see an opportunity the market has missed — I see an opportunity the market has already priced to perfection through its competitors. My entire framework starts with "is the market wrong?" and here the market is enthusiastically, aggressively right. There's no asymmetry left.

Emmett Bicker is technically impressive — I won't pretend otherwise. An 18-year-old who co-authored in Nature Communications, worked post-training at Magic on long-context coding LLMs, single-authored an arXiv paper claiming SOTA on 4 of 5 benchmarks, and holds a GPT-2 speedrun record at 95.2 seconds. That's real. The bull case writes itself: a prodigiously talented solo founder who can outpace $479M Sakana and Google's infinite resources through sheer velocity and taste. But here's the problem with Aster's "sane person, insane idea" test — the person might be sane, but the idea isn't insane. Nobody at a demo day hears "AI that automates AI research" and says "that's fucking crazy, it'll never work." They say "obviously, when can I invest?" That's the signal that the contrarian premium is gone. The insanity I look for is the kind that makes smart people uncomfortable — the Solana bet when everyone said Ethereum won, the creator ISA when people thought I was a lunatic. Aster makes people nod approvingly. That's a pricing problem for me.

Aster's primitive test is where it really falls apart. Sakana's AI Scientist is open-source on GitHub. Google's co-scientist runs on Gemini. The core technique — using LLMs to iteratively generate hypotheses, write code, run experiments, refine — is published, reproducible, and well-understood. Aster's 20x speed claim over existing frameworks comes from a single self-reported arXiv preprint with no independent replication, no institutional affiliation, and an h-index of 1 with 6 total citations. Speed is an execution advantage, not a new primitive. Aster isn't creating a behavior that didn't exist before — it's doing existing behavior faster. That's "better Ethereum," not "different architectural bet on how blockchains should work." Venmo created peer-to-peer payments as a category. Patreon created direct creator monetization. Aster creates... a faster version of what Sakana already open-sourced? I don't invest in incremental improvements to consensus categories. I invest in primitives that compound.

Aster fails the AI kill test more directly than almost any company I've seen, because Aster IS an AI application. Every major lab — Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta — has both the capability and the incentive to build autonomous research tooling internally. The dossier's inferred structural barrier — that labs won't productize this externally because it would cannibalize their research advantage — actually cuts against Aster, not for it. Labs will keep the best version for themselves and Aster gets the leftovers: academic researchers and smaller ML teams who can't afford to build their own. That's a real market, but it's not a venture-scale market with structural durability. No physical moats, no protocol-level lock-in, no network effects. The "data flywheel" the dossier infers doesn't exist today and faces the problem that every successful experiment trajectory Aster collects is also being collected by every well-funded competitor simultaneously.

The strongest case for Aster would invoke my Slack exception — sometimes a company in a consensus-hot category still deserves a check because it's creating a genuine new primitive. But Slack was creating real-time workplace messaging as a behavioral category that replaced email. Aster is entering a category where Sakana already published in Nature, Google already shipped a product, and Autoscience already raised $14M. There's no primitive to invoke the exception. The one condition that would flip me: if Aster demonstrated that its "novel discoveries" — the SecantPolar optimizer, the PulseDelta architecture — were adopted by major labs as production infrastructure, proving that Aster's output becomes an indispensable input to the broader ML ecosystem. That would make Aster a protocol layer, not an application. But today, with zero revenue signal, no pricing, one founder with 377 LinkedIn followers, and benchmarks that haven't been independently verified, that's speculative. Capital discipline gets a pass at pre-seed — solo founder, lean operation — but the compute-intensive workload means Aster will need to raise aggressively to compete with Autoscience's $14M and Sakana's $479M, which contradicts the "last money you'll ever raise" mindset I look for. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Consensus Mispricing (category is priced to perfection through competitors) and Structural Durability (pure AI software with no moat against the labs that invented the underlying capability). Pass.
