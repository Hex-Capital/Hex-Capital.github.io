# Polymath -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that immediately jumps out: Polymath is a two-person pre-seed team entering a market where Mechanize already has Nat Friedman, Daniel Gross, Patrick Collison, and *Jeff Dean* on its cap table and is working with Anthropic today. Prime Intellect has $70M in the bank. Scale AI -- which already owns the customer relationships -- is building Agent-RLVR in-house. Another YC company, Idler, has already closed a multimillion-dollar contract with a leading foundation lab. And Polymath has... a benchmark blog post from February. I don't care how good your generative models are -- when the field is this crowded, this well-funded, and this consensus, you're not finding a sane-person-insane-idea pairing. You're finding a perfectly reasonable team pursuing the hottest idea in AI infrastructure. That's not my bet.

Let me run the AI kill test, and it's brutal here: this company doesn't just fail the "cherry on top" test -- it inverts it completely. Would this business exist if AI didn't exist? Obviously not. The entire business IS AI selling to AI. I'm on record saying the winners of the AI race will be the companies with consumer distribution -- Apple, Google, Meta -- not startups. But the buyers here aren't even consumers; they're the frontier labs themselves. You're building picks-and-shovels for the gold rush, which sounds smart until you realize there are maybe five or six buyers on the planet who matter, and every one of them is also evaluating whether to build this capability internally. Anthropic's reported $1B+ RL environment budget sounds like a massive TAM until you realize that's one company's budget, and they could decide to spend it on their own internal tooling, or hand it to Mechanize, or to Scale AI, which already has the relationship. Customer concentration this extreme means you're not building a company -- you're bidding on a contract.

The "new primitive" question is where a generous analyst might find something. If Polymath's world generation models become the standard way training environments get created -- the way SWE-Bench became a reference benchmark -- there's a path to protocol-layer status. The Horizon-SWE benchmark is genuinely sophisticated: a 20,000+ commit monorepo with integrated services across Slack, Linear, Notion, Prometheus, Grafana, Sentry. That's real engineering. And the stated vision -- generating realistic long-horizon environments from a text description alone -- is ambitious enough to be interesting. But a benchmark is a marketing artifact, not a moat. SWE-Bench already exists. And the gap between "we built one impressive benchmark" and "we can automatically generate diverse environments at scale" is enormous and unvalidated. Dylan Ma's experience at Hume AI post-training models gives relevant domain signal, and Naren's systems background at Amazon and Plaid is solid infrastructure credibility. But "solid" isn't "sane person, insane idea." These are competent early-career engineers, not people who shipped critical training infrastructure at a frontier lab and are now doing something that makes the rest of the industry think they're crazy.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Surge AI's $1.2B revenue from labor-based data annotation creates a genuine innovator's dilemma. If Polymath's generative approach truly automates what Scale and Surge do with armies of contractors, the incumbents are structurally unable to cannibalize their own business model fast enough. That's a real structural insight. And the timing is impeccable -- frontier labs need this yesterday. If the team can land even one contract with a major lab, the revenue ramp could be vertical. But here's why this doesn't save the investment for me: the innovator's dilemma argument applies equally to every competitor in this space. Mechanize has the same structural advantage over Scale AI, plus it has Jeff Dean's credibility and Anthropic already as a customer. The automation insight isn't proprietary to Polymath.

I keep coming back to Birchbox -- narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst venture outcome. Polymath has narrative ("world generation models" sounds enormous) but no identifiable moat. No network effects, no physical operations, no protocol position, no switching costs. The software IS the business, and in 18 months, the next generation of models will make building RL environments dramatically easier for everyone, including the labs themselves. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than an AI infrastructure company competing against Jeff Dean's portfolio company for five customers.
