# Stage -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Stage: developer tools, SaaS, AI-adjacent, two Stanford CS grads from a quant trading firm. CodeRabbit just raised $88 million. Greptile raised $29 million. Graphite sold to Cursor for north of $290 million. Every venture firm in San Francisco is fighting to write checks in this exact category right now. When I apply my test — would this idea make a room of sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs? — the answer is the opposite. They'd be reaching for their checkbooks before the founders finished their sentence. That's not a signal I chase. That's consensus, and consensus is where my returns go to be average. Nobody needs courage to say "we're making code review better" at a dinner party. Nobody passed on this thesis 300 times. The market is crowded, well-funded, and actively hunted by every institutional investor I know.

When I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is: two talented engineers at Five Rings noticed that code review was painful, especially as AI-generated code increased volume. Dean built a coding agent internally and saw the review bottleneck from the inside. Charles worked across several engineering roles and felt the same friction. That's a legitimate professional observation. It's also exactly the pattern I've learned to recognize as career optimization rather than biographical inevitability. "I was at a prestigious firm and spotted an opportunity" is how a lot of good companies start, but it's not how my best investments start. My best investments start with someone who couldn't walk away from the problem if they tried — someone whose entire life funneled them toward this specific broken thing in the world. I don't see that here. I see smart people who noticed a gap. That's different from being forged by it.

The thing that really gives me pause is the invisible system question. When I look beneath the surface of a company, I'm looking for the hidden structure — the opaque, extractive thing that takes value from people who don't understand how it works. Taxi medallion cartels. Credit card fee opacity. Property tax systems designed to be incomprehensible. GitHub's pull request diff UI is… not that. It's a free, transparent tool used by tens of millions of developers. It's imperfect, sure. It could be better organized. But there's no information asymmetry being exploited, no gatekeeping, no extraction. Making diffs easier to read is an ergonomic improvement to a system that's already legible. That's a product improvement, not a structural revelation. And the companies that have changed my life as an investor — Uber, Affirm, Carta, Ownwell — all had that structural revelation at their core.

The strongest case for Stage is timing and the ten-year question. If AI writes the majority of code within five years, and I believe it might, then the human review layer becomes the last quality gate before production. Whoever owns the UX of that gate owns a chokepoint in the entire software development pipeline. Graphite's exit to Cursor cleared the most direct independent competitor from the field. The "comprehension crisis" is real — engineers are merging code they don't fully understand because AI generated it, and that creates genuine risk. If Stage's "chapters" become the default mental model for how teams review code, there's a workflow lock-in that could compound. The founders have the right technical depth — Dean's AI specialization and coding agent experience are directly relevant. If this were a hard space, if there were an invisible system underneath, if the founders had been broken and rebuilt by this problem, I could see the path. But the bull case requires me to believe that a UX innovation without proprietary data, network effects, or regulatory moats can survive when GitHub itself could ship a similar feature, when Haystack from the batch before is solving the identical problem with a different visual metaphor, and when Anthropic just launched its own multi-agent code review product. That's a lot of faith in a presentation layer.

Defensibility is where my concern becomes structural rather than philosophical. The dossier says it plainly: "No defensibility signals found in public sources." The "structured chapters" concept is a UX pattern, not a technical moat. Haystack is one batch ahead pursuing the same problem. Any well-resourced team — and there are many in this space flush with capital — could implement a similar organizational layer in a quarter. My worst investments — HQ Trivia, GameCrush — were products that captured attention without building infrastructure underneath. I need to see what's being built beneath the surface that compounds with every customer, and I can't see it here. Not yet. Maybe it emerges, but I don't invest on maybe when the space is this competitive and the surface innovation is this replicable.

I wish Charles and Dean well. They're clearly talented, and the problem they're solving is real. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. It's a consensus play in a consensus market, built by credentialed founders who observed a gap rather than being forged by one, attacking a transparent system with a thin moat. My money goes to the magically weird, and there's nothing weird about making developer tools better in 2026.
