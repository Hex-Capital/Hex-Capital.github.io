# Nine Fives -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Noah Levy spent roughly a decade as an RF engineer at SpaceX, designing satellite hardware. Andrew Kurtz spent nine years in aerospace at SpaceX and Aurora Flight Sciences, working on test automation for Falcon 9 ground support equipment. Nine Fives is not an idea they brainstormed — it's the instrument they wished existed every time they had to install a proprietary GPIB driver, wrestle with vendor-specific control software, and wait twenty minutes to run a test that should take seconds. This is about as pure an example of "live in the future, then build what's missing" as I encounter. When an RF engineer with a decade at SpaceX builds RF test equipment, the question of whether they understand the problem is already answered. Nine Fives' origin story doesn't need embellishment because it's the same story Drew Houston told about his USB drive — a personal frustration so specific and visceral that the product design is obvious once you've lived through the pain.

RF test equipment manufacturing is the kind of domain that makes your unconscious flinch. Nine Fives is competing with Keysight ($5.4B revenue), Mini-Circuits (estimated $111M–$400M), and Vaunix — companies with decades of catalog depth, global distribution, and established relationships with every defense contractor and telecom lab on the planet. Most founders look at this competitive landscape and their brain redirects them toward something cleaner, something software-only, something without BOMs and calibration fixtures and frequency-band limitations. That flinch is the opportunity. Nine Fives' insight — ethernet-enabled, zero-install, REST API-first instruments that work in 10 seconds — parallels what Stripe did to payments. Stripe succeeded because Patrick and John Collison were willing to wade through PCI compliance and bank integrations while every other developer-founder recoiled. Nine Fives is betting that the same dynamic applies to RF test infrastructure: Keysight can't unbundle their instruments from their proprietary software ecosystem without cannibalizing the high-margin driver and license revenue that funds their R&D. Mini-Circuits ships 60,000+ catalog products and structurally cannot invest in developer-grade software UX for any single one. The business model conflict that protects Nine Fives is real, even if adding a REST API to an existing attenuator is technically straightforward for any of these incumbents.

Nine Fives has two shipping products — the POE-ATTEN-6G and POE-SWITCH-6G — with published datasheets and reference manuals. At pre-seed, this matters more than it might seem. Building RF hardware that operates reliably to 6 GHz with sub-dB accuracy is genuinely hard engineering. The fact that Kurtz and Levy have gotten from zero to shipping instruments with full documentation, dual power (USB-C and PoE), and three control interfaces (browser UI, REST API, SCPI) tells me they can build. But it doesn't tell me how they built it — and that's the gap. I have no stories of creative workarounds, no anecdotes about hacking together their first prototype on a weekend, no evidence of the scrappy, slightly-too-intense behavior that I use to distinguish founders who will find a way through from founders who are merely competent. SpaceX alumni are, on average, impressive people. But "impressive on average" is not the same as "relentlessly resourceful in the specific," and Nine Fives' dossier gives me no stories to make that distinction.

The bear case writes itself and it's honest: Nine Fives is a hardware company with a narrow two-product line at 6 GHz max in a market moving toward mmWave bands at 24–40 GHz. Vaunix already sells 60+ instruments to 40 GHz. Hardware businesses are capital-intensive, slow to iterate, and difficult to scale at pre-seed funding levels. The moat is thin — Mini-Circuits or Adaura Technologies could add REST API endpoints through firmware updates without new hardware development. And "better developer experience for a niche instrument category" might be a feature, not a company. The strongest version of this objection is that Nine Fives could become a solid small business selling a few hundred attenuators a year to RF engineers who appreciate nice APIs, and never achieve the superlinear returns that make a startup investment worthwhile. If doubling the product catalog merely doubles revenue, that's a consultancy trajectory.

But I keep coming back to the organic problem discovery and the schlep. Nine Fives isn't building for a customer segment they've studied from the outside — Levy and Kurtz ARE the customer. The "why now" compounds this: Open RAN deployments, 5G testbeds funded by European and Japanese governments, and the broader shift toward DevOps-style CI/CD in hardware test are all creating demand for instruments with native API access that legacy GPIB-dependent equipment cannot serve. Nine Fives' dual SCPI/REST interface is a genuinely clever bridging strategy — it lets engineers integrate Nine Fives instruments into existing NI TestStand and Keysight workflows while also supporting headless Linux automation. That coexistence approach is how you get adopted instrument-by-instrument into test benches dominated by incumbents, which is the right initial wedge. On the earnest builder dimension, leaving well-compensated SpaceX and Aurora Flight Sciences roles to manufacture RF test equipment is not a status-driven move — nobody starts a programmable attenuator company to get on the cover of TechCrunch.

The condition that would move Nine Fives from cautious invest to strong invest: evidence of obsessive early users. If even five or ten RF test engineers are integrating Nine Fives instruments into their automated test benches and refusing to go back to GPIB-based alternatives, that would confirm the "small number of people want a large amount" signal I look for. Right now, Nine Fives has shipped products but I can't see whether anyone is using them with the intensity that predicts a real company. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation are organic problem discovery — which is genuinely excellent, among the strongest I see at pre-seed — and the absence of resourcefulness stories, which leaves the most important dimension in my framework scored on inference rather than evidence. Nine Fives gets a check from me, but a cautious one, weighted heavily on the schlep blindness and founder-market fit rather than on demonstrated scrappiness I can point to.
