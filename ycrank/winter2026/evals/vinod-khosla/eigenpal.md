# EigenPal -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about EigenPal is that its competitors validate its approach rather than dismiss it. Rossum has raised $114.5 million doing AI-native template-free document extraction. Hyperscience has raised $300 million. Google, AWS, and Azure all offer document AI APIs bundled into enterprise cloud contracts. When incumbents and well-capitalized competitors are all pursuing the same thesis -- that LLMs will replace template-based OCR -- you are not attacking a problem the consensus considers impossible. You are entering a market where the consensus already agrees with you and has a multi-year head start. That is one of the clearest red flags in my framework. My best investments -- Juniper against Cisco, Impossible Foods against the meat industry, OpenAI when AI was "laughable" -- were all in spaces where established players actively mocked the approach. Nobody is mocking AI document processing. Everyone is doing it.

Let me be direct about the consequence of success. If EigenPal wins -- captures significant market share in intelligent document processing -- the result is that enterprises process invoices, KYC forms, and loan documents somewhat more efficiently. That is a real business. It is not a transformation of how an industry operates. The IDP market is projected at $12 billion by 2030, which sounds large, but capturing even 5% of that means a $600 million revenue company at best. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. No fundamental behavior changes. The consequence of success is a successful enterprise software company in a competitive market -- which is exactly the kind of outcome where the probability of failure has been reduced to the point where success is inconsequential by my standards.

The technology commoditization problem compounds this. The core capability -- extracting structured data from messy documents using large language models -- is increasingly available through general-purpose AI APIs. You can hand Claude or GPT-4 a scanned invoice and get structured output today. EigenPal's differentiation sits in the workflow layer: evaluation-first testing, guardrails, observability dashboards, on-premises deployment. These are product features, not fundamental technology barriers. Features get replicated. When I invested in Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu had built a fundamentally different router architecture that Cisco couldn't replicate without abandoning its own design. I don't see an analogous structural advantage here -- I see a well-executed product layer on top of commoditizing infrastructure.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that enterprise document processing is a wedge into a much larger workflow automation market, that EigenPal's evaluation-first approach creates genuine switching costs that compound over time, and that the founders' combination of Elastic search engineering and McKinsey enterprise sales instincts lets them outmaneuver competitors with 50-100x more capital. The European bank customers and enterprise logos (McKinsey, Accenture, Elastic, MIT) suggest real sales traction at pre-seed, which is unusual and creditable. If the on-premises deployment capability is genuinely production-ready, that addresses a real gap in the market that cloud-native competitors like Rossum don't fill. And the IDP market's 33% CAGR means the tide is rising. A team that executes well in this market could build a $200-500 million outcome. For many investors, that is exciting. For me, that is the definition of inconsequential consequence.

The founders are competent -- Blaszyk's Elastic background gives him genuine document search and AI engineering depth, and the open-source docx-js-editor project (443 GitHub stars, 130-point Hacker News post) demonstrates an ability to build developer community awareness. Novak's McKinsey and Accenture consulting exposure maps directly to enterprise sales. But this is an industry insider team executing the industry playbook -- an enterprise search engineer and an enterprise consultant building enterprise document processing software. There is no outsider perspective here, no contrarian thesis that experts reject, no evidence of first-principles rethinking of how document processing should work. They are building a better version of what exists. That is precisely the kind of company I pass on -- smart people who should be working on harder problems.
