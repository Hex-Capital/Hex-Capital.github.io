# Thirdbrain Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Half the pitches I see right now are some variant of "we make fine-tuning easier." And every single time, I ask the same question: who does this NOT compete with? Thirdbrain Labs is building post-training infrastructure in a category where Scale AI sits at $29B, Together AI just raised $305M at $3.3B, Snorkel AI closed a $100M Series D at $1.3B, and Labelbox cleared $100M ARR. AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI offer managed fine-tuning as a checkbox feature. This isn't a category where the market is wrong — this is a category where the market is so enthusiastically right that the upside is priced into every participant ten times over. There is no mispricing here. There is consensus on top of consensus.

The "expert decision capture" framing is the one card Thirdbrain Labs is playing, and I'll grant it's a slightly different angle than "upload your dataset and click train." But let's be honest about what this is: it's RLHF with a better UX for non-ML teams. That's a feature improvement on existing workflows, not a new primitive. When I backed Venmo, Andrew Kortina wasn't building "better PayPal" — he created the behavior of texting money to your friends. That behavior didn't exist. Fine-tuning models? That behavior has existed since 2018. Making it easier for domain experts to do it is a nice product thesis, but nobody is going to say "we Thirdbrained our model" the way people say "Venmo me." The category already exists, the workflows already exist, and the incremental improvement of capturing expert feedback as a training signal is exactly the kind of thing Scale AI's Scale Labs — which launched in March 2026 — or Labelbox's expanding RLHF platform will absorb as a product sprint.

The Solana comparison illuminates why this doesn't work for me. When Raj pitched Solana, he was building a new Layer 1 consensus mechanism — that's a protocol-level primitive by definition. The market had priced it at zero because everyone "knew" Ethereum had won. Thirdbrain Labs is the structural inverse: application layer (not protocol), in a category every VC is bidding up (not dismissed), and offering an incremental improvement (not a new architectural bet). I invest in protocols, not applications, precisely because protocols compound while applications get commoditized. And in AI, the commoditization cycle is brutally fast — open-source tools like Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, and LLaMA-Factory are free and improving weekly. If your differentiation is a UX wrapper around fine-tuning compute that's becoming a utility, you're a feature for an incumbent, not a company.

Margaret Zhang has hustle — co-founding Coalition Systems, a defense AI startup she took to Kyiv, plus GTM experience at Spice AI — and David Huang's top-1% finish in Two Sigma's Halite AI Challenge signals real engineering chops. They're competent people. But competent people building something sensible is exactly what I'm not looking for. The sane-person-insane-idea test requires that the idea make smart people uncomfortable. Nobody is uncomfortable with "easier fine-tuning for enterprises." Every LP deck in Silicon Valley has a slide about it. The co-founder relationship also shows no documented shared history — Zhang from NYU Stern, Huang from UC Berkeley, no overlapping employers or ventures. My best returns come from founders I've watched across multiple cycles, or at minimum founders who've been in the trenches together before. The Coalition Systems ambiguity — whether Zhang has fully departed, pivoted, or is running parallel ventures — adds a layer of commitment uncertainty that needs answering.

The strongest bull case would require believing that "expert decision capture" is genuinely a different paradigm from standard RLHF/DPO — that Thirdbrain Labs has built something where domain experts, not ML engineers, can continuously steer production models in a way that creates compounding data moats per customer. If the Meta-Scale AI disruption (OpenAI and Google reportedly cutting Scale ties in mid-2025) genuinely fractures the enterprise post-training market, a lighter-weight tool could find oxygen. But this requires out-executing four billion-dollar incumbents simultaneously while also fending off free open-source alternatives, with two people and pre-seed capital. That's not a contrarian bet where the risk is in the price — that's a bet requiring three simultaneous miracles in the most competitive AI subcategory on the planet.

Thirdbrain Labs fails on the two dimensions that matter most to me: consensus mispricing and structural durability. The category is as consensus-hot as anything in venture right now — the exact opposite of Solana at four cents or Eaze in cannabis when no one would touch it. And the AI kill test is fatal: every incumbent with a cloud platform or data infrastructure business can replicate post-training UX improvements without breaking a sweat. Pass.
