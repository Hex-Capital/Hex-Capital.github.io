# Standout -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Dex raised $3.1M from a16z's Speedrun program in April 2025 building a candidate-side AI talent agent — almost exactly what Standout is building. When Andreessen Horowitz has already funded your thesis, you've lost the return premium I look for. The whole point of "Black Swan Farming" is that the best investments look wrong to smart people. Standout's pitch — AI agent that represents candidates instead of employers — sounds right to everyone. It sounded right enough to a16z that they already wrote a check for someone else doing it. Nobody hears "we help talented people find better jobs" and recoils. Nobody says "that will never work." That immediate consensus is precisely the tell that makes me nervous. Standout is operating in the overlap of two zones I try to avoid: an idea that sounds obviously good, and a space where well-capitalized competition arrived first.

Alexis Aftalion is the reason I don't dismiss Standout outright. He co-founded Zealy, scaled it to 1.5M MAU and $3M ARR in 12 months, and raised $3.5M from Redalpine. That's not a slide deck accomplishment — that's a real product with real users generating real revenue on a compressed timeline. Standout's co-founder Witold de La Chapelle brings a CMU CS degree, engineering stints at Dropbox, Samsara, and Chime, and — critically — a prior job board that reached 100K MAU. Both founders have shipped before. Both have scaled something. In my framework, prior shipping experience is one of the strongest invest signals, and Standout has it from both founders. The pattern I followed with Patrick Collison — backing him at Auctomatic, then again at Stripe — depended on watching someone demonstrate builder quality across multiple attempts. Alexis has that first data point from Zealy. But Stripe succeeded because the Collisons were tackling payment infrastructure that made other developers physically recoil from the compliance work. Standout's recruiting focus doesn't trigger that same schlep-blindness advantage. Recruiting is a space that attracts founders, not one that repels them.

Witold's background provides Standout's strongest claim to organic problem discovery. He spent years as an engineer at startups navigating exactly the broken recruiting experience Standout aims to fix — ghosting, irrelevant outreach, no one actually representing the candidate. He then built a 100K MAU job board, which means he was already obsessed with this domain before Standout existed. That sequence — lived the pain, then built toward a solution — maps to how Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Alexis's path is less organic: he came from Alven (a VC firm) and Rocket Internet before building Zealy, a web3 community platform with no connection to recruiting. His route to Standout looks more like opportunity identification than personal frustration. When one co-founder discovered the problem by living it and the other arrived via market analysis, I weight the authentic discovery but discount the blended signal.

Standout's "Hollywood agent" metaphor reveals a structural concern about superlinear returns. Talent agencies — the real ones in Hollywood — are fundamentally service businesses. An agent's income scales with the number of clients they personally manage. Standout's 1,457 indexed startups and 3,000+ open roles suggest they're trying to automate the matching layer, and their 35% beta-user-to-first-interview rate within 4 days is a legitimate early signal that the product works. But the question I keep asking is: does Standout's value compound? Does how well they've done determine how well they'll do next? LinkedIn's value compounds because every new profile makes the network more valuable. Standout's value might compound if candidate relationships deepen over time — if the AI agent learns enough about each person that switching costs become real. But at pre-seed, with no revenue, listed as "Free" on Product Hunt, and no public pricing, the compounding dynamic is purely hypothetical. The monetization path — presumably employer-paid placement fees — requires convincing companies to pay a candidate's agent, which is an unfamiliar buying motion in recruiting.

The bull case an advocate would make: Standout inverts the structural incentives of a trillion-dollar market. Every recruiting incumbent — LinkedIn, Indeed, Greenhouse — monetizes employers, creating a channel conflict that prevents them from genuinely representing candidates. Alexis proved at Zealy he can build community-driven products that reach millions. Witold's domain expertise and engineering background make him both the builder and the customer. The WhatsApp delivery channel is a clever do-things-that-don't-scale move that meets candidates where they already communicate. And maybe the idea only *seems* consensus-positive on the surface — the actual contrarian bet is that candidates, not employers, should be the primary customer of recruiting infrastructure, which most VCs still reject as a viable business model since "candidates don't pay." That structural inversion could be the non-obvious insight hiding behind an obvious-sounding pitch. I take this seriously. But when I look at what would actually make Standout a defining company rather than a competent one, I need to see evidence of the compounding dynamic — the superlinear return — and I don't. Standout can't point to a moat that deepens with scale, and Dex is running the same experiment with a 12-month head start and a16z's distribution network behind it. The condition that would flip me: if Standout showed me a cohort of candidates who used the product, got placed, and then returned for their next career move — repeat usage proving the AI agent relationship compounds over time. That would demonstrate the superlinear dynamic the current dossier lacks.

Standout has credible founders building a product that works in a market everyone already agrees is broken. That combination produces competent companies, not exceptional ones. Alexis Aftalion's Zealy track record (1.5M MAU, $3M ARR in 12 months) keeps Standout above the pass threshold — he's demonstrated he can build and scale. But the absence of any contrarian edge in the idea (7/20), combined with mixed organic discovery signals and no evidence of a compounding moat, places Standout squarely in neutral territory. The two dimensions most decisive: Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where Standout scores lowest because the thesis is already consensus-validated by a16z's investment in Dex; and Founder Resourcefulness, where Alexis's prior scaling success provides genuine upside signal but no specific stories of creative problem-solving under constraint push the score beyond moderate.
