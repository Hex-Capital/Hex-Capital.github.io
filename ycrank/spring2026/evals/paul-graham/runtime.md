# Runtime -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is how this idea was born. Gus Trigos spent years as a quant at BlackRock, then founded Mentum -- a fintech API that pivoted to procurement AI and got acquired by a logistics company in October 2025. Five months later he's in YC again, building governance tooling for AI coding agents. Nothing in his background connects him organically to this problem. He wasn't an engineering leader struggling to let his PMs use Claude Code. He wasn't running a platform team watching developers ship dangerous agent-generated code. He was a finance guy who ran a supply chain company. The most natural reading is that he finished one thing, looked around at what was hot, and picked the AI coding agent wave. That's precisely the failure mode I wrote about in "How to Get Startup Ideas" -- sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than noticing something broken in your own life.

The second thing I notice is that the problem itself doesn't have much schlep. Compare it to what Stripe tackled: years of banking relationships, regulatory compliance across dozens of countries, fraud detection, PCI standards. Or what Airbnb had to solve: trust between strangers, insurance liability, local housing regulations, photography logistics. "Coding agent governance" means sandboxed VMs, access controls, deployment gates, and activity dashboards. That's solid engineering work, but it's the kind of thing a competent infrastructure team at E2B or Daytona could build in a quarter. The dossier itself says the governance layer has "low technical barriers." When there's no schlep, there's no moat from avoidance. The reason nobody has built this yet isn't that it's too painful -- it's that the coding agent category is barely six months old. Being early to a non-schlep problem is a much weaker position than being willing to do what others unconsciously avoid.

The competitive dynamics make this harder. E2B has $32M and 15 million sandboxes a month. Daytona has $30.7M and momentum. Both already provide the infrastructure Runtime builds on. Adding governance features -- access controls, audit logs, deployment gates -- is a natural product extension for them, not a separate company. Meanwhile, Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google have every incentive to build team-level controls directly into their coding agents. The argument that agent vendors won't build governance because it "reduces usage" is clever but fragile. Enterprise buyers will demand governance, and vendors will build it to close enterprise deals. GitHub Copilot already has organization-level policy controls. Runtime is trying to own a layer that the players above and below it can both absorb.

To be fair, the bull case deserves examination. Trigos is a second-time YC founder who navigated a difficult pivot at Mentum, raised from Google's Gradient Ventures, and achieved an exit -- that's a genuinely uncommon pre-seed profile. The timing observation has structural logic: as autonomous coding agents proliferate, someone will build the governance layer, and a neutral third party has positioning advantages over agent vendors. If enterprise adoption of coding agents accelerates faster than vendors can build native governance, there's a real window. The multi-agent approach (Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI) could create vendor-neutral positioning that enterprise buyers prefer. And the $99/seat/month Teams tier at scale would be an excellent business. If Trigos can get into five mid-market companies through YC network referrals and build genuine switching costs through policy configuration, this could work. But "could work" requires the governance layer to be substantially harder to build than it appears, and I don't see evidence of that.

The traction tells the story plainly. 64 GitHub stars. Seven thousand PyPI downloads total, dropping to single digits per day recently. A Show HN with 4 points and 1 comment. The company says it's "live with fast-growing YC scaleups" but names none. At pre-seed I don't penalize thin traction -- but I do notice when there's almost no organic pull despite the product being launched and publicly available. When something people genuinely want exists, they find it and tell others about it. The signal here is the absence of signal.

Trigos has founder quality -- the Mentum exit and repeat YC admission demonstrate that. But founder quality has to meet the right problem, and this problem doesn't match this founder. A great coding agent governance company would probably be founded by someone who ran platform engineering at a company that deployed agents at scale and couldn't sleep at night because of what those agents were doing. That person would know exactly which guardrails matter, which ones are theater, and how enterprise security buyers actually think. Trigos is building from the outside looking in, in a space where the adjacent infrastructure players have 60x his capital and shorter paths to the same product.
