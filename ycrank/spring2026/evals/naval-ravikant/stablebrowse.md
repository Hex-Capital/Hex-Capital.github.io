# StableBrowse -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The architectural insight here is sharp: every cloud browser company is renting Chromium's rendering pipeline and then throwing it away. Browserbase, Kernel, Airtop — they all spin up full browsers, pay for pixels nobody looks at, then screenshot those pixels so a vision model can squint at them. StableBrowse says: strip the renderer, keep the network stack and JS engine, output semantic structure directly. That's not incremental — it's a different layer of the stack. If the performance claims hold (85MB per instance, 80ms cold start, 95% token reduction), the unit economics gap would be structural, not marginal. This is the kind of architectural divergence that makes incumbents choose between cannibalizing their own infrastructure or watching someone else eat their future. I respect the clarity of the insight.

But here's where my framework gets uncomfortable. Building a non-rendering browser engine that correctly handles modern web applications is one of the most specific-knowledge-intensive engineering challenges in all of software. Browser engines are cathedrals — Chromium has tens of millions of lines of code, built by thousands of engineers over decades, with intricate dependencies between the rendering pipeline, the DOM, JavaScript execution, and network handling. Ripping out the renderer while keeping everything else working correctly requires someone who has lived inside that architecture. I'm looking for the equivalent of what Aravind Srinivas was to AI search — someone who spent years at the engine level, whose intuition about where the rendering pipeline's tendrils reach into the JS engine is bone-deep. What I see instead: a recent Purdue graduate with AI/ML coursework, a possible Amazon engineer, and two founders whose backgrounds couldn't be confirmed at all. No public browser engine contributions. No Chromium commits. No WebKit patches. No prior open-source infrastructure projects. The specific knowledge this product demands and the specific knowledge this team has demonstrated are mismatched in a way that concerns me deeply.

The principal-agent test reveals something that initially looks favorable but isn't. With Perplexity, Google's agents were trapped — they couldn't cannibalize ad revenue. Here, Browserbase's $67.5M and 1,000 customers don't create the same trap. Adding a rendering-free engine mode would be a product extension for Browserbase, not a cannibalization. They already own the developer relationships. If StableBrowse proves the architectural approach works, Browserbase has every incentive and substantial resources to build their own version, and their existing customer base gives them distribution StableBrowse doesn't have. The principal-agent dynamic doesn't protect this company the way it needs to be protected.

Let me steelman the bull case, because the idea deserves it. Four people who can ship a working semantic browser engine — even an imperfect one — before anyone else would own the infrastructure primitive for the agentic web. Every agent framework, every workflow automator, every research tool would route through their API. The $180M flowing into cloud browser services validates demand, and StableBrowse's approach makes every dollar of that investment look like waste. YC accepted them; Tom Blomfield would have tested technical capability in ways this dossier can't capture. If even one founder has spent years hacking on browser internals — maybe through work that doesn't show up on public GitHub — the specific knowledge gap closes, and this becomes a genuinely compelling bet. The timing is right: LLM agents hitting production workloads are creating exactly the cost pressure that makes a rendering-free engine necessary. If these four can build what they claim, the leverage is extraordinary — pure code, zero marginal cost, API-based, and the performance delta is so large it creates immediate switching incentives.

But I can't invest on hypotheticals. Specific knowledge is my highest-conviction filter because it's the only durable moat at the earliest stage. Everything else — leverage architecture, market timing, team efficiency — depends on whether the founders can actually build the thing. A custom browser engine built by engineers without demonstrated browser engine expertise is like an AI search company founded by people who've never trained a model. The architectural vision is correct. The question is whether these founders are the ones who can execute it. With no traction signals, no public prototype, no GitHub presence, and no confirmed domain expertise, I'm being asked to bet entirely on hidden competence in the most technically demanding sub-field of systems engineering. That's not a risk profile I can underwrite with my own money at this stage.

I'd want to meet these founders. If one of them teaches me something about browser architecture I didn't know in the first fifteen minutes — if their fluency with rendering pipeline internals reveals years of obsessive engagement I can't see in public data — this evaluation changes entirely. But on paper, this is a strong idea searching for the specific knowledge to make it real.
