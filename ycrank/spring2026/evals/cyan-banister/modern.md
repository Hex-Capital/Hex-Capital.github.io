# Modern -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

ServiceNow paid $2.85 billion for Moveworks. Automation Anywhere paid roughly $1.5 billion for Aisera. Both acquisitions closed in 2025, absorbing the two largest independent AI-ITSM companies into incumbent ecosystems. Modern is entering the vacuum — and the bull case writes itself: the acquirers now face a cannibalization problem, because ServiceNow's revenue model depends on ticket volume, and autonomous resolution eliminates tickets. That structural conflict could slow the incumbents and give Modern a window. But when I sit with this, I realize the window itself is the tell. Every VC in San Francisco can draw this diagram on a whiteboard. Ravenna just raised $15 million from Khosla and Madrona in April 2025. Workativ is building the same feature set. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs — it's a space they're racing to fund. Modern is operating in one of the most consensus applications of AI in enterprise software, and consensus is where my portfolio goes to die.

Seb Poole has two prior exits — a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in February 2023 — plus a Hong Kong e-commerce venture that raised roughly 20 million AUD from investors including Alibaba. That's real operational muscle for a pre-seed founder, and I respect it. Aleks Tomovski brings DevOps, SRE, and platform engineering experience across Melbourne, Vilnius, Denver, Riga, and Skopje, which maps to Modern's integration-heavy technical requirements. But when I ask my opening question — tell me their story, not the business — I don't hear the biographical inevitability that drives my strongest bets. Seb's prior companies were in device resale and mental health platforms. Nothing in the dossier connects either founder to a personal, lived encounter with the pain of IT service desks drowning in tickets. This reads like two capable operators who identified a market gap after Moveworks and Aisera got acquired, not two people whose life experiences made this problem inescapable. The Melbourne connection between them is inferred, not documented, and I see no evidence of the kind of forge — survival under constraint, mission that predates the company — that I look for before writing a check from my own money.

Modern's pitch is that IT, HR, and Finance teams scale headcount linearly with ticket volume, and AI agents can break that curve. The website claims 62% reduction in ticket handling and 74% faster response time. But I keep testing for the invisible system — the opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it — and Modern doesn't pass. IT managers buying Modern already know exactly what their problem is. They have dashboards showing ticket backlog, resolution time, cost per ticket. There's no information asymmetry, no hidden tax, no gatekeeping structure being dismantled. Modern makes an understood process faster. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was that credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing from consumers who couldn't see it. When I backed Ownwell, the thesis was that property tax assessments were opaque and people were overpaying without knowing. Modern's target buyers are enterprise procurement teams with full visibility into their own pain. That's optimization, not revelation, and optimization doesn't activate my conviction the way structural transparency does.

Modern claims SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, and GDPR certifications and "most teams are live in under a day" — impressive operational maturity for a two-person team if accurate. The integration surface across twelve-plus enterprise systems (ServiceNow, Zendesk, Workday, Salesforce, Slack, Datadog, and others) creates switching friction that compounds with deployment depth. But the dossier is direct about the core risk: "The core technology (LLM-powered ticket resolution with workflow orchestration) is buildable by well-funded competitors." Modern's workflow data accumulation could become a moat over time, but at pre-seed with no confirmed revenue or user counts — the pricing page still says "Coming soon" — that moat is theoretical. The "Modern Managed" offering, where Modern operates the customer's service desk directly, introduces a services component that scales with headcount, not code. At two people, this is either a clever wedge or a margin trap, and the tension between services revenue and venture-scale returns is one I've seen sink companies that tried to be both.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Seb Poole is a repeat founder entering a $15 billion market at a structural inflection point, with the two largest independents absorbed into incumbents facing business-model conflicts. Modern's overlay-any-ITSM positioning and sub-day deployment could compress enterprise sales cycles enough to let a small team win disproportionate share before the acquisition-integration fog clears. The RTFKT exception in my framework acknowledges that narrow timing windows can substitute for structural moats — and the post-acquisition vacuum is genuinely narrow. But RTFKT was culturally bizarre in a way that defied categorization; Modern is an AI-agent-for-IT-tickets company entering a field with five named competitors and billions in recent M&A. The timing window is real, but it's visible to everyone, which means it's priced into the competitive landscape already. What would flip me: if Seb Poole told me a story about a specific experience — running IT operations at scale, watching a system break under ticket load, a personal encounter with the waste and dysfunction — that made Modern feel like the company his biography was building toward all along. Or if Modern showed me $50K in contracted ARR with three named enterprise accounts, proving the "live in under a day" claim is converting into revenue. Either signal would change my math.

Modern lands in Pass territory because the two dimensions most decisive for my framework both score below threshold. The biographical forge is competent but disconnected — repeat exits without mission inevitability. And the contrarian conviction dimension is actively negative — this is a consensus space with billions in recent investment and multiple well-funded competitors chasing the same vacuum. I wish Seb and Aleks well, and the repeat-founder profile means I wouldn't be surprised if Modern builds a solid business. But a solid business in a consensus market where the core technology is buildable by anyone with API access to a foundation model — that's not a Long Journey bet.
