# Synthetic Sciences -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the market temperature. "AI for scientific research" in early 2026 is one of the most consensus categories in technology. Edison Scientific raised $70M in December 2025. Elicit has 400,000 monthly researchers and $31M deployed. Google announced its own AI Co-Scientist. Sakana AI sits at a $2.65B valuation. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When I bought Bitcoin, serious people called it a toy. Here, every serious person already agrees this is a good idea. That's the pricing problem -- when the crowd has already turned optimistic, your upside gets compressed toward the mean. A two-person team with $1.5M competing in a category where one competitor alone has 47x their capital isn't contrarian. It's courageous, but courage without asymmetry is just variance.

The specific knowledge question is genuinely interesting. Aayam Bansal has 15+ publications at ICML, NeurIPS, ACL by age 18, with research stints at CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL. That's not career optimization -- that's obsessive curiosity manifesting as output at a rate that suggests the research workflow is something he's internalized at a cellular level. He didn't study the research loop; he lived inside it during the years when most people are studying for AP exams. There's a prior exit of an AI healthcare company, details unknown. Gangwani brings USACO Platinum and IOAI credentials -- strong technical signal, though less directly mapped to the research automation problem. Both founders are building something they've personally needed, which is the right starting point. But here's where my framework pushes back: the insight that "the ML research loop is fragmented and could be automated end-to-end" is something every ML PhD student discovers in their first year. Bansal's knowledge of the research workflow is real, but it's the kind of specific knowledge that a thousand other talented researchers also possess. The question is whether living inside the loop at 18 produces a qualitatively different product instinct than having lived inside it at 28. I'm not convinced it does.

The leverage architecture has a clear promise -- one researcher delegating to swarms of AI agents multiplies output the way Notion multiplied individual productivity. But the leverage here is derivative, not primitive. Synthetic Sciences orchestrates existing capabilities: frontier LLMs from OpenAI or Anthropic, cloud GPUs from Lambda or Together, existing research tools and pipelines. Unlike Replit, which created code leverage for people who couldn't previously code, this creates efficiency leverage for people who already know how to do research. That's valuable, but it's a productivity multiplier, not a new category of human capability. The underlying LLM capabilities improve across all providers simultaneously, which means the orchestration layer must continuously re-earn its differentiation. The "Flywheel" mode -- generating RL training data from product usage to improve agent capabilities -- is the one architectural idea that could create genuine compounding. If research process traces become a proprietary dataset that makes their agents measurably better than agents built on the same underlying models, that's a real moat. But it's a forward-looking claim with zero evidence of execution.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: these founders are research-native in a way that older competitors may not be. Bansal started publishing at an age when habits form permanently -- his intuition about what researchers actually want might be sharper than a team of experienced engineers building for researchers they've interviewed but never been. The pivot from InkVell (document formatting) to full-stack research automation suggests they discovered a larger problem through direct user contact, not through market research. The pricing is live, which means there's a deployable product. And at 18, the compounding horizon is extraordinarily long -- if they're building for decades, the current funding disadvantage is a temporary condition, not a structural one. The RL flywheel concept, if it materializes, is exactly the kind of technology that gets harder to replicate over time. I've seen young founders with deep technical ability outrun better-funded teams when they have genuine product instinct. The question is whether the product instinct is here.

What holds me back is the combination of consensus timing and competitive structure. This isn't a new category of leverage -- it's a better execution of a category that well-funded teams are already building. Edison Scientific has Jeff Dean advising and $70M to spend. Elicit has years of user data and 400K researchers. The unverifiable BixBench claim -- 92% on a "Verified" subset when published results show 17% on the general benchmark -- introduces noise rather than signal. The recent pivot from InkVell suggests the vision is still crystallizing, which at pre-seed is normal, but combined with the scope of what they're attempting (GPU orchestration, biology workflows, RL training infrastructure, four distinct product modes), two people trying to cover that surface area feels like ambition outrunning bandwidth. I want founders who know something others don't, building leverage that didn't exist before, at a time when people think they're wrong. Here I see founders who know something many others also know, building efficiency that partially existed before, at a time when everyone agrees they're right.
