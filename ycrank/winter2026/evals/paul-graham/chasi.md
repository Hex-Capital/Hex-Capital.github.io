# Chasi -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The equipment dealer industry is a textbook case of schlep blindness. Everyone building AI agents right now is chasing the same handful of verticals -- marketing, sales, customer support for SaaS companies. Nobody wants to build for forklift dealers. And that's exactly why I find Chasi interesting. Equipment dealers run a trillion-dollar distribution layer where fleet utilization sits below 60%, staff are drowning in phone calls, and the technology stack is a patchwork of legacy systems from CDK, Texada, InTempo, and a dozen others. The problem is obvious. The schlep -- integrating across that fragmented landscape, selling to conservative buyers who've used the same software for fifteen years, building AI that can correctly quote a Cat 320 excavator rental -- is exactly the kind of ugliness that makes founders unconsciously look elsewhere.

The origin story here isn't pure organic in the way Dropbox was. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive. These founders didn't run a dealership. But the pathway is genuine. Akash started his career building tractors at TAFE, moved to industrial automation at Titan Engineering, then spent years as the Industrials GTM lead at Axion Ray, where he was selling AI to companies like Cummins -- a major equipment OEM whose dealer network is precisely the kind of business Chasi now serves. Sarman deployed AI at Cummins, Harley Davidson, and Denso as part of Axion Ray's founding team. They didn't sit in a room brainstorming "what vertical needs AI agents." They spent years inside the industrial ecosystem, watching dealer networks operate with manual processes, and eventually couldn't ignore the opportunity. That's adjacent organic discovery -- not the strongest form, but genuine enough that the founders can probably describe specific dealers, specific failures, specific moments where they saw revenue leak through the cracks.

The co-founder dynamic is worth noting. They worked together at Axion Ray through its growth from pre-seed to a $64M Series B. That's the kind of pressure-tested relationship I want to see -- not two people who met at a co-founder matching event three months ago. Sarman has the technical credentials: Computer Engineering from Waterloo, founding engineer at Tesla building supply chain systems, and he's built real things (a Bluetooth mesh communication project, for instance). No public GitHub repos make it harder to evaluate his craft directly, but the Tesla and Axion Ray founding-engineer roles suggest someone who builds rather than manages. Akash brings the domain knowledge and sales instinct from years of actually selling AI to industrial customers. The CEO/CTO split maps cleanly onto their respective strengths.

What worries me is the competitive picture. Quipli has already launched Quinn -- essentially the same concept, an AI revenue engine for equipment rentals -- and they have "millions of transactions" worth of proprietary training data plus an existing customer base. Chasi's counter is that it integrates with any existing dealer stack rather than requiring migration to Quipli's platform. That's a real architectural advantage. Dealers who already use CDK or Texada aren't going to rip out their DMS to get an AI agent. But it also means Chasi has to build and maintain integrations across a dozen different legacy systems, each with its own quirks. That integration complexity is both the moat and the risk -- it's expensive to build and hard for competitors to replicate, but it's also expensive for Chasi to maintain as a two-person team. The question is whether the integration-first approach wins enough dealers quickly enough to build a data advantage before Quipli expands beyond its walled garden.

The bull case is strong enough that I don't dismiss it. If Chasi can establish itself as the platform-agnostic AI layer for equipment dealers -- the way Stripe became the payment layer for any developer regardless of their stack -- the market is enormous and the switching costs compound with every interaction the AI agents handle. Dealers who let an AI handle their sales inquiries, quotes, and follow-ups aren't going to rip it out once it's working. The founders' Axion Ray network gives them warm intros to exactly the right buyers. And the "live across equipment businesses in the US and Europe" claim, if real, suggests they're already executing. But "live across" without numbers could mean two customers or twenty, and at pre-seed the difference matters enormously.

What holds me back from writing a check is the thinness of the evidence that people actually want this. Being "live" is not the same as having users who would be devastated if Chasi disappeared. I want to hear a dealer say "Chasi is handling 40% of our inbound leads and our close rate went up 15%." I want to see a growth curve. The founders have the right background, the schlep is real, and the market is big enough -- but the signal that people are pulling the product toward them is still too faint for me to act on conviction alone.
