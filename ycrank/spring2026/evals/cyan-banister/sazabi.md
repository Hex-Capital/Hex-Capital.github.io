# Sazabi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Sherwood Callaway ran AI infrastructure for "Alice" at 11x, which the Zypsy memo calls "one of the largest agentic systems in production," and then walked out to build the observability layer for the exact debugging pain he just lived. That single biographical fact is the most interesting thing about Sazabi, and it is also the only thing that keeps me in the room -- because the rest of the dossier is a clean operator chasing a category that became consensus two weeks before he came out of stealth. Sazabi's signature in my book is "lived-experience founder, consensus-category timing" -- the rarer half of the pair is the founder, the punishing half is that Dash0 closed $110M at a $1B valuation on March 23, 2026, sixteen days before Sazabi's SiliconANGLE exit on April 8.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus before he shipped Anduril, which is the template -- a founder who built the adjacent thing at scale, then turned and pointed the same muscle at the problem he could not stop seeing. Sazabi's Callaway is on the same ladder, just lower. He shipped Opkit through YC W23, sold it to 11x, then ran the agentic infra for Alice -- which means when he says "AI behavior as a new class of telemetry," he is not reading it off a McKinsey slide, he is describing what was paging him at 2am. Where Sazabi diverges from the Luckey rung is consequence asymmetry: Luckey's prior product reshaped a consumer category, Callaway's prior product was a $1M-seed YC vertical AI play absorbed into a single acquirer. The lived-experience signal is real and I weight it accordingly, but Sazabi is not the founder who built the comparable thing at *scale* -- he built the comparable thing at *acquisition*.

The pass example I keep on file is the consensus YC AI darling -- two ex-FAANG PMs, oversubscribed seed, six top funds chasing -- and the failure mechanism is that the round prices the outcome before the product exists. Sazabi is not quite that, but the gravitational field is the same. Dash0 at a $1B valuation in March, Datadog and New Relic shipping AI features per Gartner, and Sazabi's own waitlist gated by a backer list that reads like a Vercel/Graphite/Daytona/Browserbase/LangChain/Mastra/Replit influencer roll-call -- this is the opposite of finding them before it becomes consensus. The "magically weird" Crusoe move was burning flared gas in North Dakota when three credible VCs had passed on aesthetic grounds. Sazabi's pitch is "logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events, traces are correlated events," which is an elegant architectural claim and also exactly the kind of slide six other AI-obs startups are putting in their deck this batch.

Sazabi's governance is the cleanest line in the dossier -- Callaway is sole founder per the Zypsy memo and SiliconANGLE, which means the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll failure mode is structurally absent on day one. Ed Carrel and Henry Ventura appear in the chat demo but the dossier names no co-founder with title parity, and a 10-person team under a single named CEO is exactly the configuration I want when the company has to pivot under pressure -- which, given Dash0's funding, it almost certainly will.

The capital-intensity dimension is where Sazabi simply is not my lane. SOC 2, SOC 1, ISO 27017, ISO 27001, GDPR are good hygiene but not regulatory moat -- they are table stakes any well-funded competitor clears in a quarter. There is no hardware, no defense angle, no fertility or rural-industrial or transgressive-category compounding. Sazabi is a B2B SaaS log pipeline with an AI agent on top, which is the cleanest software-only category in the dossier. Mindbloom and Vigil Labs sit in my portfolio because the regulators are the moat; Sazabi has none of that.

You cannot touch Sazabi. The platform is scheduled for delivery by end of 2026 per SiliconANGLE, the landing page returns "You're in" to a waitlist, and no normal human can install, deploy, or photograph the product in their own life right now. Flock Safety was sourced from a Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons -- I could see the artifact in the wild. Niantic was sourced from playing Ingress since 2012 -- I felt the pull in my hands. Sazabi is a chat demo on a marketing site and a Mastra podcast appearance, which is the same epistemic posture as the pass-example developer-tools abstraction: I have to take Callaway's word for it.

My 36-month forecast for Sazabi: acquired by Datadog, New Relic, or Vercel in the $40-120M range as an AI-agent-observability feature acqui-hire, after a pivot that narrows from "observability for fast-moving teams" to "observability for production AI agents" -- the Alice-shaped niche where Callaway's lived experience is actually load-bearing and where Dash0's broader enterprise positioning does not reach. The mechanism is Dash0's $155M war chest forcing Sazabi out of the general-purpose lane within 18 months, at which point the only defensible surface left is the one Callaway lived inside at 11x.

The strongest bear-rebuttal -- the one that stings -- is the DeepMind exception. Hassabis was a pedigreed PhD I would normally pattern-match as replaceable, and I wrote the check anyway because cross-domain compounding made him uncopyable in the age of the polymath. Could Callaway be that for AI-agent observability specifically? He has the YC founder loop, the operator loop at Brex and Crunchbase, the AI-infra loop at 11x, and the a16z Infra Scout loop layered together -- that is a polymath stack, and if Sazabi repositions toward agent-native telemetry early, the lived-experience moat might compound faster than Dash0 can copy. What dismantles the rebuttal is the timing: Sazabi is not yet pitching the agent-native niche, it is pitching general-purpose observability for early/growth-stage teams, which is the dimension where Dash0 already has $1B of validation and ten times the headcount. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is Sazabi publicly repositioning the wedge to AI-agent observability and landing a named design partner -- a Replit, a Cursor, a Mastra -- on a signed log-volume contract before end of 2026, at which point the Alice biography becomes the moat and I would re-evaluate at the Series A.
