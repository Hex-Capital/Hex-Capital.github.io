# Regbase -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

"Agentic AI research tools with human verification" — that phrase from Regbase's own website is the whole analysis compressed into seven words. The human verification layer is the tell. In my leverage framework, code scales without permission; labor scales with headcount. Regbase is blending both, which means the leverage ceiling is set by the slower component. Every new jurisdiction Regbase covers — and they're targeting thousands of subnational sources, content behind QR codes, non-English filings — requires custom ingestion and human validation. The dossier flags "ongoing engineering and operational overhead that scales with coverage breadth, potentially straining a 3-person team." That's not a strain on a 3-person team; it's a structural signal that Regbase must become a 30-person team, then a 100-person team, to deliver on its promise. Thomson Reuters and LexisNexis are not accidental conglomerates. They're large because regulatory content is operationally expensive to maintain at scale. Regbase is betting AI reduces that cost by 10x. Maybe. But 10x cheaper than Thomson Reuters' content operation is still a content operation, not a software company that works while you sleep.

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy both worked the ESG practice at Paul, Weiss, where they ran into a research project tracking laws across 50 countries that no existing tool could solve. That's real specific knowledge — they understand how BigLaw regulatory research gets consumed, what compliance officers actually need, where the workflow breaks. But I have to be precise about what kind of specific knowledge this is. Aresta-Katz's expertise is in the consumption pattern of regulatory research, not in a technical or intellectual breakthrough that competitors need years to replicate. If I hired three senior associates from Cleary Gottlieb's ESG practice, they'd bring functionally equivalent domain knowledge within months. Compare Regbase to Perplexity: Aravind Srinivas's years at Google Brain and OpenAI meant that his specific knowledge WAS the product's engine — no hire could substitute for it. Regbase's founders identified the gap correctly, but their knowledge is about the problem, not about a proprietary solution architecture. CTO Ethan Shea, Staff Engineer from Vercel's agentic AI projects with stints at Citadel and Microsoft, is a strong technical builder — but his specific knowledge is in AI infrastructure, not regulatory intelligence. He's a capable hired hand for this particular problem, not a co-founder whose entire knowledge stack converges on regulatory parsing.

Regbase's founding story passes my principal-agent test more convincingly than the specific knowledge test. Aresta-Katz and Godoy didn't read a market report about regtech's $2.8B TAM. They hit a wall at Paul, Weiss — a "100 hour project" that no tool could solve — and built the company to fix it. That's authentic principal behavior. You can't fake the frustration of spending weeks manually tracking regulatory changes across 50 countries when you know software should handle it. Regbase also claims Fortune 500 customers, global consultancies, and leading law firms — but names zero. At pre-seed, unverifiable traction claims don't help and slightly hurt. The $400M in expiring school safety grants found for one client and the 80 greenhouse gas disclosure requirements mapped across 45 countries for another are more useful signals — they show Regbase solving specific, high-value problems, not just demo-ing a dashboard.

The bull case is worth constructing honestly. Regbase sits at a genuine structural gap: CUBE raised $22M and serves 1,000+ clients but is locked into financial services. AscentAI raised $27M and got acquired by Edgewater Capital, also financial-services-only. Thomson Reuters covers 750+ regulatory bodies but optimizes for major national regulators, not the subnational long tail. Regbase targets the coverage blind spot — state and local laws, non-English filings, emerging ESG and PFAS regulations — that incumbents' business models are structurally misaligned to serve. The LLM capability curve just crossed the threshold where multilingual regulatory parsing became feasible at scale. Aresta-Katz and Godoy have the law firm network to sell into exactly the right buyers. If Regbase can accumulate subnational source coverage faster than incumbents extend downmarket, every new source is a compounding data advantage. This is a real wedge. The question is whether the wedge requires a content operation or a software company to exploit.

Regbase cannot be a billion-dollar business with five people. That's not a moral judgment — Varda Space Industries can't either, and I backed Varda because microgravity manufacturing creates leverage through physics that cannot be replicated on Earth. But Varda creates a genuinely new form of leverage. Regbase's leverage is a faster, AI-assisted version of what Thomson Reuters already does with a much larger team. The compound interest test is also mixed: each new regulatory source Regbase ingests does make the platform more valuable, but each source also requires maintenance as government websites change formats and URLs. The flywheel has friction. Regbase democratizes access to regulatory intelligence — but the primary buyers are still Fortune 500 companies and elite law firms, not a new population gaining access to something previously gatekept. This is efficiency for existing professionals, not structural access expansion like AngelList or CoinList unlocking participation for people who were previously excluded.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: evidence that Regbase's AI pipeline handles new source onboarding and format changes autonomously — without human verification — at 95%+ accuracy across non-English jurisdictions. That would transform the leverage architecture from a labor-code hybrid to genuine permissionless leverage. If Shea's agentic AI infrastructure can reduce the human verification layer to exception handling rather than standard workflow, Regbase becomes a different company. Until then, I see authentic founders with real domain pain building a content-intensive business in a space where the incumbents are large for structural reasons. Regbase scores highest on principal authenticity — the Paul, Weiss origin story is genuine and the founding team built from personal frustration, not market analysis. It scores lowest on permissionless leverage — the human verification requirement and source maintenance burden create scaling constraints that contradict the lean-team thesis. A competent company solving a real problem, but not my kind of bet.
