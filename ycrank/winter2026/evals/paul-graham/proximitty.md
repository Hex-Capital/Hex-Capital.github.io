# Proximitty -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Proximitty is what their CTO was building before this company: ACI.dev, a generic open-source tool-calling platform for AI agents with 4,700 GitHub stars. That's impressive engineering. But it's also revealing. When a founder builds a general-purpose AI agent framework and then applies it to loan servicing, the causal arrow runs from technology to problem, not from problem to technology. Drew Houston didn't build a generic file synchronization framework first -- he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Collisons didn't build a generic API toolkit -- they were trying to accept payments on their own projects. The strongest startup ideas start with a specific pain that demands relief. Proximitty's origin looks more like a capable engineer's toolkit meeting a consultant's market map.

That said, the schlep here is real. Loan servicing and collections is genuinely ugly work -- FDCPA, CFPB, TCPA, state-by-state regulations, the operational mechanics of contacting delinquent borrowers, negotiating hardship plans, skip tracing. This is the kind of regulatory and operational mess that makes most AI startups choose to build yet another coding assistant instead. The CEO Ho has direct exposure to this mess from McKinsey credit risk advisory work and from scaling Taptap Send, a remittance fintech. He's seen the inside of these institutions. But "seen the inside" as a consultant is different from "lived inside" as an operator who couldn't sleep because the manual workflows were breaking. McKinsey trains people to identify markets, not to feel problems. The schlep willingness gets partial credit; the organic discovery does not.

The competitive landscape concerns me more than it would in most cases. Prodigal Technologies is YC-backed with $14.2M raised, already doing AI-powered loan servicing analytics. Skit.ai has $22.4M in revenue doing voice AI for collections. LoanPro raised $100M. TrueAccord raised $78M. This is not a space that's been hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness -- it's a space where well-funded companies are already fighting. When I see five funded competitors with combined capital exceeding $250M, the question becomes: what do these two founders know that all those companies don't? The answer appears to be the "no-code, no-API" browser automation approach. But browser automation via computer vision is rapidly commoditizing -- Anthropic shipped computer use, OpenAI has Operator. What feels like a proprietary moat today could be a feature of every foundation model next year.

The bull case is worth articulating. If Proximitty can actually deploy inside banks without API integration -- just pointing AI agents at existing browser-based workflows -- that sidesteps the 6-to-18-month enterprise integration cycle that kills most fintech startups selling to regulated institutions. That's genuinely clever. Banks hate integrations. A product that overlays existing systems without IT involvement could spread faster than anything requiring a procurement review. Ho's McKinsey network gives him credibility in the room. Zhang's engineering is clearly strong -- five years building security infrastructure for 300,000 Bloomberg terminals, plus a well-starred open-source project. The claim about "publicly traded fintechs and banks globally" as customers, if true, would significantly change this evaluation. But it's unverified marketing copy with no named customers, no revenue disclosed, and no independent confirmation. At pre-seed I don't demand much traction, but I do demand honesty about where you actually are.

The co-founder relationship also gives me pause. No shared employer, no shared university, no visible prior connection. Ho attended LSE and worked at McKinsey and Taptap Send. Zhang was at Bloomberg and ACI.dev. They both listed "Stealth Startup" on LinkedIn, suggesting they came together for this venture. I've seen enough YC batches to know what happens when co-founders meet specifically to start a company rather than because they've been collaborating for years -- nine out of nine fell apart in one batch I tracked. That's not destiny, but it's a structural risk that compounds under the pressure of selling to slow-moving banks while your browser automation keeps breaking against UI updates.

Zhang is the strongest individual signal here. He's a genuine hacker-builder -- the ACI.dev project with 4,700 stars and 459 forks under Apache 2.0 isn't a weekend project, it's sustained open-source leadership. His Bloomberg infrastructure work at scale is exactly the kind of engineering that matters when you're building something that has to be reliable inside financial institutions. If I were scoring the CTO alone, I'd be more enthusiastic. But a strong CTO applying a generic capability to a consultant-identified market, against well-funded incumbents, using technology that's commoditizing -- that combination doesn't clear my bar for writing a personal check.
