# Galactic Resource Utilization Space, Inc. (GRU Space) -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I look at with any company is the miracle count. GRU Space needs lunar ISRU to work at scale on the actual Moon (never demonstrated by anyone), third-party launch vehicles to deliver payloads on schedule (CLPS has had significant failures), billions in funding they don't have, functional life-support for commercial guests 240,000 miles from Earth, and a legal framework for lunar commercial operations that doesn't exist under the Outer Space Treaty. That's five sequential low-probability events. If you have more than one miracle, you have compounding small odds -- and five miracles isn't a startup plan, it's a wish list. Every one of those has to work in sequence. I pass on companies that require two miracles. Five is disqualifying on its own.

The claimed structural moment is Trump and Isaacman announcing Moon base plans by 2030, plus NASA's CLPS program enabling commercial lunar payloads. But political announcements about lunar ambitions are cyclical noise -- Bush announced Constellation in 2004, Obama killed it in 2010, Trump 1.0 launched Artemis in 2017, and now we're on the third reset. Political will is not a structural inflection. CLPS is real infrastructure, but Astrobotic's Peregrine lander failed in January 2024, and the program has experienced repeated delays. SpaceX Starship, which GRU would ultimately need for larger payloads, is still in development. When I invested in Harvey, I could track the GPT scaling curve and calculate that model fidelity for legal work was 12-18 months away -- the technology was on a measurable trajectory. There is no equivalent scaling law for lunar construction technology. ISRU on the lunar surface hasn't been demonstrated once, let alone enough times to extrapolate a capability curve. This isn't a "non-obvious market at an inflection point" -- it's a market that doesn't exist yet and depends on infrastructure advances that aren't on any predictable timeline.

This is also fundamentally an atoms business, not a software-driven one. A hotel on the Moon is construction, logistics, life support, and hospitality operations in the most extreme environment conceivable. Software is a tool here, not the competitive advantage -- remove the software and you still have the same core business challenge of building a pressurized structure on the lunar surface. When I write about "software-aware" versus "software-driven," this is what I mean. Now, I made an exception for Anduril -- capital-intensive defense hardware -- but that exception was justified by specific conditions: a structural vacancy after Google's Maven cancellation, Palmer Luckey's track record, autonomous systems running on a clear AI capability curve, and immediate DOD budget demand. GRU Space has none of those override signals. There's no structural vacancy being created by an incumbent's exit, no proven technology curve to ride, and no immediate paying customer with an existing budget line.

The strongest bull case: Kevin Cannon is genuinely world-class in lunar regolith science -- one of the handful of people on Earth who deeply understand the material you'd need to build with on the Moon. If you believe SpaceX Starship will eventually crater launch costs and sustained political will drives real lunar infrastructure investment, then the first entity to demonstrate ISRU construction on the lunar surface would have an extraordinary first-mover advantage with near-zero replication risk for years. The reservation PR strategy is clever -- 15+ press hits at launch, the Trump family announcement, deposits creating a demand signal that could attract government contract interest. And Skyler Chan building a NASA-funded 3D printer that actually flew to space at Berkeley is a real credential for a 22-year-old. The boomerang pattern is worth noting: maybe lunar commercial habitation is the correct thesis at the wrong time, like Webvan before Instacart. But the Webvan-to-Instacart gap closed because smartphones, logistics networks, and gig labor were on predictable adoption curves. Lunar infrastructure isn't on any predictable curve -- the timeline for this boomerang could be 20 years, not 10, and a pre-seed startup can't survive that wait.

The product-to-distribution story is speculative but structurally possible -- if you're the first to build habitat infrastructure on the Moon, you become the distribution channel for all lunar construction (roads, landing pads, warehouses, government bases). The founders articulate this multi-product vision. But the initial product doesn't ship until 2032 at the earliest, and the distribution trajectory depends entirely on solving the five miracles first. It's the right framework applied to a timeline that exceeds any reasonable startup lifecycle. At the capital intensity required -- likely billions to reach the 2032 hotel -- this would need to be funded more like a defense prime contractor than a venture-backed startup, and the return profile would need to match.

I respect the ambition and Cannon's unique expertise. But I invest where I can see the adoption curve inflecting and map when capability thresholds will be crossed. This market has no measurable adoption curve yet. It's a decade-plus bet on external infrastructure development by third parties, requiring sequential breakthroughs no single entity controls. That's not my kind of investment at any stage.
