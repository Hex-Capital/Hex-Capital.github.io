# Auto -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Dave Evans and Sam Hare are probably the two best people on the planet to build this specific product. PlayCanvas acquired by Snap in 2017. Seene acquired by Snap in 2016. A PhD in computer vision. Director of Engineering at Snap running Mini-Programs, Games, AR Lenses. Seven years of overlapping tenure building the camera platform that 750 million people use. That's the strongest signal in this dossier — and it's the wrong signal for my framework. When I have to choose between domain expertise and better thinking, I pick better thinking every time. Evans and Hare aren't outsiders who don't understand what can't be done — they understand exactly what can be done because they've spent fifteen years doing it. That profile builds excellent products. It rarely builds companies that change the world.

The consequence question kills Auto for me before I reach anything else. If Auto fully succeeds — becomes the dominant camera AI app, hundreds of millions of subscribers, user-generated Frames creating a thriving ecosystem — what changes? People have a better camera app. They save $20 a month on redundant subscriptions. One App Store reviewer said it "made three existing app subscriptions unnecessary." That's the ceiling of the value proposition as described: subscription consolidation. Google Lens already processes 12 billion visual searches per month. Auto is competing for share of existing behavior, not creating new demand. Compare that to what I funded at Commonwealth Fusion Systems — limitless clean energy — or Impossible Foods — protein without animal agriculture. Those are categories where success means the world operates differently. Auto at maximum success is a very profitable consumer business. I don't invest to build profitable consumer businesses. I invest to change the cost structure of civilization.

The technical approach compounds this concern. The dossier says it plainly: "The underlying multimodal AI models (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) are available via API to any developer." Auto's Frame abstraction — turning a photo into a persistent mini-app — is a product concept, not a technical breakthrough. It's clever UX. But clever UX on commodity infrastructure is exactly the kind of bet where the moat is execution speed, not technology. "Building a 'photo → structured action' pipeline is achievable by well-funded competitors." When I invested in Rocket Lab, Peter Beck built novel electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a non-linear approach to launch cost reduction that couldn't be replicated by downloading an API. Auto's Frame is a design pattern. Apple could ship it as a feature in iOS 19 and wipe out Auto's differentiation in a single WWDC keynote. Platform dependency on the company most likely to build your exact feature into the OS is not technology risk — it's existential market risk, which is the kind of risk I avoid.

The strongest bull case: the Frame ecosystem becomes a platform. User-generated Frames create network effects — a library of community-built visual mini-apps that creates switching costs and organic distribution. If that flywheel spins up, Auto becomes the App Store for visual AI, and the outcome is genuinely large. Evans and Hare's Snap experience building exactly this kind of developer/creator ecosystem (SnapKit, Snap Lenses, Mini-Programs) is directly relevant. And the timing is real — multimodal models crossed the accuracy threshold in late 2023, Apple validated the camera-as-AI-interface with Visual Intelligence in December 2024, and consumer behavior is clearly shifting toward visual queries. For this to work, Auto needs the Frame ecosystem to reach critical mass before Google adds persistent task-workflows to Lens and before Apple expands Visual Intelligence into a platform. That's a race against two companies with a combined market cap north of $5 trillion. Winning that race requires a founder insight that creates unfair advantage, not just a head start measured in months.

I checked my exception framework. DoorDash violated my technical novelty requirement, but the override was planetary-scale consequence magnitude — transforming food access for hundreds of millions. Auto's consequence magnitude doesn't reach that threshold. OpenAI violated my outsider-founder preference, but the override was civilizational consequence — artificial general intelligence potentially transforming everything. Auto isn't in that category either. No exception applies. Auto is a well-executed consumer app in a competitive market built by domain experts on commodity infrastructure. The consequence of success is a $10/month subscription business, not a changed world. The two dimensions that lock this as a pass: consequence magnitude stays bounded by the nature of what a camera utility app can achieve, and the absence of non-linear technical approach means Auto's moat is execution tempo against trillion-dollar incumbents who own the platform it runs on.
