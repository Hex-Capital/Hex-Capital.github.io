# AICE Power -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been deploying clip-on current sensors with AI-powered load disaggregation since 2011, accumulating $41.8 million in funding along the way. Facilio has raised $45.9 million. Infogrid, $148 million. Enertiv, $18.4 million. AICE Power enters a market where at least five funded competitors have already staked claims on variations of the same thesis: attach sensors to electrical panels, apply machine learning, show building operators where they waste energy. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" about AICE Power, the answer appears to be that small commercial buildings -- supermarkets, fast food chains, retail stores -- are underserved by existing monitoring solutions. But no one disputes this. It is the consensus observation of every building analytics company and every energy consultant in Europe. The dossier itself frames the problem as self-evident: "these sites have no monitoring at all, and inefficiencies go undetected until they appear on the bill." A problem statement that requires no persuasion is not a secret. It is a market segment that others have consciously deprioritized, which is a different thing entirely -- and the reasons they deprioritized it (low per-site revenue, high deployment costs relative to savings, fragmented decision-making) may be correct.

AICE Power's "5% the cost of traditional BMS" claim and 30-minute installation time suggest a cost discontinuity that could unlock the small-venue segment. An advocate would argue this resembles the Palantir pattern: build an affordable, deployable system for institutions that were previously dark, embed it operationally, then expand concentrically as the data moat compounds. The E.Leclerc and Accor logos on AICE Power's website indicate that major multi-site operators have already engaged, and a single procurement decision at a chain like E.Leclerc could scale across hundreds of locations. Rémi Bouteiller's hardware credentials -- multi-chip PCB design, BLE firmware, Shenzhen manufacturing management at Amira Health -- are genuinely rare at pre-seed and directly applicable to sensor-to-cloud product development. Grégoire Chomette's MIT aeronautics degree and NASA research demonstrate computational rigor. These are competent founders with relevant execution capability. But competence in a competitive market is precisely the configuration my framework identifies as structurally doomed. The question is not whether AICE Power can build and deploy the product -- it is whether the product creates a monopoly. And clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware. NILM disaggregation has open-source implementations. The dossier states this explicitly: "the technical approach is replicable; defensibility depends on execution speed in multi-site deployment and data accumulation rather than proprietary technology." Execution speed is not a moat. It is the absence of one.

AICE Power's timing argument -- EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive requiring monitoring, rising European electricity prices post-2022 -- is the cleantech pattern I described in *Zero to One* Chapter 13 recurring in miniature. Companies whose "why now" depends on regulatory mandates and macroeconomic conditions rather than a technological discontinuity are structurally fragile. The EPBD creates demand for building energy monitoring generically, not for AICE Power specifically. Every competitor in this space benefits equally from the same regulatory tailwind. When I backed General Matter, the "why now" was not that the government wanted nuclear fuel -- that has been true for decades -- but that General Matter possessed a specific HALEU enrichment capability that no commercial supplier in the Western world could replicate. AICE Power possesses no analogous capability. Their sensors are commodity. Their algorithms are reproducible. Their regulatory tailwind blows for everyone.

The power-law calculation confirms the structural problem. The Building Energy Management System market is projected at $26.1 billion by 2035, with AICE Power targeting a sub-segment of the 41% commercial share -- perhaps $3-4 billion in addressable revenue at maturity. Even full monopolization of this sub-segment would not produce a fund-returner outcome, and monopolization is implausible given five funded competitors already operating. AICE Power at 10+ sites tracking 5+ MW is not on a trajectory toward winner-take-all dynamics. The market structure is fragmented by geography, building type, and customer preference -- the antithesis of power-law concentration. The realistic best case for AICE Power is a modest acquisition by Honeywell, Siemens, or Johnson Controls seeking to extend their BMS platforms downmarket, which is precisely the exit profile I avoid.

The strongest element in AICE Power's favor is the distribution architecture implied by the E.Leclerc and Accor relationships -- landing a multi-site operator and expanding across their portfolio. This is a genuine distribution insight: selling to the chain operator rather than individual building managers converts a fragmented market into a concentrated one. If AICE Power could sign exclusive monitoring contracts with the top 20 European retail and hospitality chains before competitors recognize the opportunity, the multi-site data advantage could compound into something defensible. But "could" is doing significant work in that sentence, and the dossier shows no evidence of exclusivity, no revenue figures, and no indication that AICE Power has articulated this land-and-expand thesis as a definite plan rather than an emergent pattern. The one verifiable condition that would shift my assessment: evidence that AICE Power has signed exclusive, multi-year monitoring contracts with three or more major chains, creating a structural lock on the multi-site small-commercial segment that competitors cannot replicate through superior technology alone. Without that, AICE Power is competing on execution in a crowded market with commodity technology and a regulatory-dependent tailwind -- the precise configuration that destroys value for everyone involved.
