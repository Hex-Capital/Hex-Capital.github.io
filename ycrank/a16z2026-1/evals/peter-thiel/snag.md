# snag -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Snag is a pivot. The app launched in November 2025 under the same corporate entity — OneRoof App, Co. — that raised $4.6M from General Catalyst for a neighbor community platform active in 395+ NYC buildings. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas have now co-founded three consecutive ventures together: Knock Knock City (luggage storage, acqui-hired by LuggageHero), OneRoof (neighbor community app), and now snag (sublet marketplace). I am supposed to value this shared prehistory — and I do value co-founder alignment forged under pressure. But three pivots across three product categories in housing-adjacent consumer apps is not the signature of definite optimism. It is the signature of founders searching for product-market fit through iteration, which is precisely the lean-startup methodology I reject. A definite founder does not discover the future through serial experimentation across luggage storage, neighbor communities, and sublet listings. A definite founder knows what to build and makes irreversible commitments that only make sense if the vision is correct. The OneRoof-to-snag transition reveals the opposite pattern: flexible reallocation of capital and team toward whatever the market seems to reward.

The canonical question — what important truth do very few people agree with you on? — yields nothing here. Snag's implicit thesis is that a gap exists between Airbnb (nightly tourist stays) and Zillow (12-month leases) for 1-3 month sublets, and that GenZ renters are underserved by fragmented Facebook groups and Craigslist posts. This is not a secret. It is a complaint shared by every college student who has ever searched for summer housing. Five named competitors — Blueground at $352M raised, June Homes at $50M, Kopa through YC, LeaseBreak with 100K+ users in NYC, and the legacy Sublet.com — are already in this market. The existence of a competitive landscape with multiple funded players pursuing the same thesis is, in my framework, the clearest possible red flag. The dossier's own commoditization assessment confirms it: "The AI listing-conversion technology is replicable by any team with ML capability." Scraping Facebook groups and Instagram posts with an LLM to generate structured listings is a feature that any engineering team could ship in weeks. Calling this "AI-native" does not transform a commodity capability into a monopoly.

The Wish comparison is instructive. Wish was a marketplace without structural defensibility competing on convenience and price in a crowded category — no secret, no monopoly structure, no technological discontinuity. It reached $14 billion at IPO and then destroyed over 90% of that value when the absence of moat became undeniable. Snag's network effects are real but geographically bounded: 30,000 users and 3,000 listings in NYC must be rebuilt from scratch in every subsequent city. This is the marketplace treadmill, not a monopoly flywheel. Facebook also expanded campus by campus — but Facebook possessed a genuine structural secret about real-identity networks creating inescapable density within closed communities. Snag possesses a listing aggregator. The expansion strategy is superficially similar; the underlying monopoly architecture is categorically different.

The strongest bull case: NYC's Local Law 18, effective September 2023, severely restricted Airbnb-style stays under 30 days, creating a regulatory gap that structurally channels demand toward 1-3 month sublets outside the law's scope. If similar regulations proliferate across major cities, snag rides a structural tailwind. The $6.5M in booking requests in 30 days with 40% month-over-month growth, if even directionally accurate, suggests genuine demand density. And the seven university partnerships — NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, Pace — represent a repeatable distribution playbook. But I must note: a business model that depends on favorable regulatory positioning is exactly the fragility I identify in my cleantech analysis. Regulation that creates a market can also destroy it — a single city council amendment extending Local Law 18 to cover 1-3 month sublets could eviscerate snag's NYC supply. Companies that create value despite or regardless of regulation are monopolies; companies that exist because of regulatory arbitrage are structurally fragile.

Snag's distribution is the one dimension where real evidence exists. The university partnership channel — seven schools, organic Facebook group recommendations in NYU housing groups, TikTok and Instagram presence targeting GenZ — demonstrates a campus-by-campus acquisition engine with organic pull characteristics. The 4.7/5 iOS rating across 184 reviews indicates genuine user satisfaction, not manufactured growth. Selin Sonmez's consumer product experience and Forbes 30 Under 30 recognition in Consumer Technology are legitimate credentials for executing a consumer marketplace. But superior distribution without a monopoly secret produces, at best, a modestly profitable marketplace — not a power-law outcome. The realistic upside for a geographic, city-by-city sublet marketplace, even with excellent execution, is a mid-hundreds-of-millions outcome. Blueground, with $352M in funding and an operator model with higher margins than peer-to-peer, sits at $750M valuation. Snag's peer-to-peer model takes lower rake on lower-priced inventory. This is not the category shape that produces fund-returner outcomes.

The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Return Magnitude. Snag has no contrarian insight — the sublet gap is consensus, the AI layer is commodity, and five competitors confirm the market is competitive by definition. The category's geographic fragmentation and the structural ceiling visible in Blueground's $750M valuation after $352M in funding make power-law returns implausible. Competent founders, real demand, working distribution — but no secret, no monopoly, no path to the kind of outcome that justifies writing a check.
