# shortkit -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the fundamental problem with ShortKit: Michael Seleman spent six years building YouTube Shorts infrastructure at Google, and now he's productizing that expertise as a developer SDK. That's a sane person with a sane idea. Every VC in the room nods along — short-form video is the dominant format, most teams can't build it well, an API-first approach sounds smart. Nobody thinks this is crazy. And when nobody thinks your idea is crazy, the non-consensus premium that generates outsized returns simply doesn't exist. Seleman's credential is exceptional — maybe a dozen people on Earth have built short-form video infrastructure at YouTube's scale — but exceptional domain expertise deployed against a consensus opportunity produces Mux-like outcomes at best, not the structural returns I'm underwriting at seed.

The deeper issue is that ShortKit is a pure software business in the exact category I believe AI is systematically dismantling. The moat IS the software — the SDK, the ML buffering, the codec optimization, the integration layer. Strip away the code and there's nothing. No painting crews, no parking hardware, no physical infrastructure that competitors can't replicate from a laptop. When I say "software is a business tool, not a business model," this is precisely what I mean. Every component ShortKit assembles — HLS transcoding, CDN delivery, adaptive bitrate laddering — is individually commoditized. The claimed differentiation is the integration of these components, tuned for short-form feed patterns. But integration layers are exactly what gets cheaper as AI improves. A strong engineering team using AI coding tools can now assemble a competent short-form video stack in weeks, not months. The "build" side of the build-or-buy equation gets lighter every quarter. ShortKit's value proposition degrades as its own enabling technology (AI-driven development) becomes more capable. That's the opposite of what I want in a portfolio company.

The competitive landscape makes this even more structurally precarious. BytePlus is literally TikTok's technology stack commercialized as an enterprise product. ByteDance invented the short-form feed pattern that ShortKit is building infrastructure around. If any incumbent has both the technology depth and the economic motivation to dominate this segment, it's the company that created TikTok. Meanwhile, Mux at $46 million in revenue and 4,900 customers could add short-form-specific SDK features as a product extension — they already have the developer trust, documentation, and billing relationships. ShortKit is sandwiched between a geopolitically complicated giant that owns the underlying technology and a well-funded incumbent that owns the developer relationships. That's a structurally ugly position.

The strongest bull case is the Stripe analogy: Stripe was "just" a payments API that became foundational infrastructure because the alternative was so painful that every developer adopted it, creating a standard that compounded. Could ShortKit become the Stripe of short-form video? For that to work, three things need to be true simultaneously: (1) the pain of building short-form feeds needs to remain genuinely severe even as AI reduces development costs, (2) BytePlus needs to stumble or remain unacceptable to Western customers due to geopolitical risk, and (3) Mux and Cloudflare need to decide the short-form niche isn't worth specializing in. If all three hold, ShortKit's deep domain expertise and purpose-built approach could establish it as the default primitive, with SDK integration creating meaningful switching costs. Seleman's YouTube Shorts pedigree gives him a real head start in building something technically excellent. I take that seriously. But Stripe succeeded because payments were regulated, complex, and deeply integrated into financial infrastructure — creating structural lock-in beyond software. Video delivery doesn't have those characteristics.

The narrative ceiling concerns me as much as the moat question. "Embeddable short-form video SDK" is a feature category, not an infinity story. The expansion paths all lead into direct competition with larger players — broaden to general video and you're fighting Mux; go deeper into recommendation and you're fighting BytePlus's core competency. Helium's narrative grew from "IoT hotspots" to "decentralized wireless infrastructure" over seven years because each layer it built created surface area for the next narrative expansion. ShortKit's narrative narrows as the product matures rather than expands. It solves a specific technical problem for a specific segment — that can be a good business, but it's not the kind of business where I see theoretical unlimited upside combined with structural defensibility. For my money, I need companies where AI makes the moat deeper, not shallower. ShortKit lives in the wrong quadrant of that analysis.
