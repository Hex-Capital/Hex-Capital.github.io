# Runtime -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Gus Trigos sold his last company (Mentum, YC S21) to Nuvocargo in 2025, then shipped four full-stack products there, then watched the non-engineers on his team try and fail to do the same — and Runtime is the thing he built so they could. That sentence is Runtime's analytical signature: the organic-idea dimension is near the top of my ladder, and the hacker-genealogy dimension is near the bottom. The pairing tells me what kind of company Runtime is — an operator-founded enterprise control plane built by someone who hit the exact wall at his last shop, not a hacker-tribe Lisp-prize bet of the kind I usually pattern-match on.

Drew Houston forgot his USB stick at MIT and built **Dropbox** (2007) for himself; the Collisons had been hacking commerce flows since **Auctomatic** (2007) before they built **Stripe** (2010). That is the HIGH rung on the organic-idea ladder — founders solving a problem they personally hit in their last workflow. Runtime sits a half-rung below that, because Trigos's Nuvocargo origin story is not quite "I forgot my USB stick" personal but it is the next best thing: he watched his own teammates get blocked deploying coding agents inside a company he had just been acquired into, and built Runtime to unblock them. Carlos Volante's CV at Modern Treasury scaling payments to $350B at 99.995% automation is the governance-and-audit-log scar tissue you'd ideally pick for the control-plane buyer. Runtime's pain origin is not invented for a deck; it is reconstructable from public tweets and from a founder's last job.

Patrick Collison won Ireland's 41st Young Scientist for Lisp before **Stripe** (2010); Aaron Swartz co-authored RSS before **Reddit** (2005). That HIGH-rung pattern — hackers leaving tribal markers years before the company — is conspicuously thin in the Runtime dossier. Trigos has a finance degree and a stats master's from Hult, was a BlackRock quant, and is self-taught in C++; Volante's lineage is payments-infrastructure at Finix and Hulu. Neither has a confirmed public GitHub handle in the dossier. The `runtm-ai/runtm` repo exists at 119 stars, which is a real artifact but not a years-of-pre-startup hacker trail. My default when this dimension is silent is to lean down, because hackers leave GitHub trails and the absence of one is itself a signal — I take the penalty here, just smaller than my full default because the OSS repo and Volante's payments-infra rigor are concrete.

Runtime's animal-resourcefulness reads as a compounding mechanism, not a single stunt. Trigos is a returning YC founder whose last company exited; "40+ countries with zero ad spend" on a Spring 2026 launch is the kind of inbound that compounds the way **Stripe** (2010)'s developer channel did — not because Runtime is the next Stripe but because the underlying mechanism (technical buyer self-discovers tool, brings it into the company) is the same. Over 18 months that compounding either continues (the wedge defends itself) or collapses (Anthropic ships a native enterprise team plane and the inbound dries up). No cereal-box stunt; a returning-founder profile instead.

Runtime would need named Fortune-500 logos with annualized contract value disclosed publicly for its schlep score to flip a tier. Today the schlep is real but software-coded: an adapter treadmill across Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, Copilot, Gemini CLI, Devin, and OpenCode; compliance work for buyers whose security teams are "blocking Claude Code"; AGPL-on-the-API-layer governance for self-hosting in customer VPCs. That is more schlep than the median YC AI-tools applicant in Runtime's batch, but it is not **Coinbase** (2012)'s bitcoin-compliance grind. The dimension stays mid; counterfactually, a signed BofA-or-equivalent contract with named compliance scope would push Runtime up.

Runtime's co-founder configuration is **Stripe** (2010)-shaped on the org chart — CEO with prior-exit operator profile, CTO with deep regulated-payments-infrastructure experience — but the Collisons are brothers who had been building together since they were teenagers, and the dossier surfaces no shared history between Trigos and Volante. YC team size is listed as 3 but only two founders are named, which is the kind of ambiguity the in-person interview resolves and I cannot from here. I stay at the neutral default.

Runtime in 36 months reaches $4-10M ARR selling the team-control-plane wedge to YC-adjacent scaleups and a thin slice of mid-market, then gets acquired by GitHub, Anthropic, or Cursor for a sub-$300M tag when one of those vendors decides the enterprise team plane belongs inside their own product. The mechanism producing this is exactly the high/low signature: organic founder pain gets Runtime to product-market-fit fast on the workflow buyer; the absence of hacker-tribe distribution and the platform-dependency on seven agent vendors means Runtime does not get to be the standalone Stripe-of-coding-agents — it gets to be the feature one of them buys.

The strongest bear case against an invest is platform-risk middleware: Runtime depends on seven third-party agents whose vendors (Anthropic, OpenAI, Cursor) all have enterprise SKUs and the channel-conflict incentive to ship native team-management; E2B at $21M with "88% of Fortune 100 signed up" and Daytona at $24M with a $1-2M ARR ramp in six weeks are both capitalized to move up-stack into Runtime's workflow plane; the AGPL-on-API license deters exactly the self-hosting-in-VPC enterprise buyers Runtime markets to; "Runtime" is a generic name. This stings. The dossier evidence that wins is the buyer specificity: E2B and Daytona sell sandbox APIs to agent builders, and infra companies historically lose workflow-buyer bake-offs to workflow-native incumbents; Trigos's origin story is workflow-buyer-shaped, not infra-shaped. The verifiable condition that flips me to pass is Anthropic or OpenAI shipping a team-control-plane SKU with audit logs, spend caps, and multi-CLI governance before Runtime closes a named six-figure enterprise contract — that is the substitution event that ends the wedge.
