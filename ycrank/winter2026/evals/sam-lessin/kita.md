# Kita -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my core problem with Kita: this is a sane person with a sane idea. Carmel Limcaoco is from Manila, went to Stanford, shipped features at Apple as an intern -- credible. Rhea Malhotra won the Firestone Medal, Stanford's highest honor for a CS thesis, and published at top robotics venues -- genuinely impressive technical chops. But "use AI to process lending documents faster in emerging markets" is not an insane idea. It's an obvious one. When I hear the pitch, I don't think "that's crazy" -- I think "of course someone is doing that." Ocrolus already built a $250M+ revenue business doing exactly this for US markets. The geographic expansion to Southeast Asia is novel in execution but not in concept. In my framework, sane-person-plus-sane-idea is the configuration that produces mediocre venture returns, because every sophisticated investor can underwrite the same thesis independently.

The deeper structural issue is that Kita fails my "would this be a good business if AI didn't exist" test in the most direct way possible. Without vision-language models, there is literally no product. The VLM capability IS the business. This is precisely the category I've been arguing against publicly -- AI-first companies where AI constitutes the entire value proposition rather than sitting as a cherry on top of an independently defensible operation. Compare this to Craftwork: remove the scheduling software and you still have painting crews, trucks, brand, and trained workers. Remove the VLMs from Kita and you have nothing. And here's what keeps me up at night about AI-first document processing: OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are improving these models every quarter. A well-prompted Claude or GPT-4V call can already extract structured data from messy PDFs with startling accuracy. The distance between "general-purpose VLM" and "specialized document extraction" is shrinking, not growing.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the localization angle is real and the founder-market fit is unusually tight. Filipino e-wallet statements, Indonesian payslips, Mexican utility bills -- these document formats don't exist in any standard training corpus. Carmel grew up in Manila and understands the lending ecosystem, the regulatory landscape, the specific financial instruments in ways no Silicon Valley team could replicate quickly. If Kita can accumulate enough repayment outcome data across Southeast Asian lenders, the feedback loop between document signals and loan performance could create a compounding data advantage that's genuinely hard to replicate. The six-figure paid pilot secured the same week they built the prototype suggests real pull, not manufactured demand. If I squint, I can see a path where Kita becomes the credit infrastructure layer for the unbanked -- a new primitive for emerging market underwriting. That's a big story.

But I can't get past the structural position. Kita is an application sitting on infrastructure controlled by others -- the VLM providers. It's not creating a new financial primitive or protocol that other companies build on. It's processing documents through an API. Every country requires bespoke regulatory navigation, bespoke document format training, bespoke lender relationships. That's linear scaling, not compounding. The per-country fragmentation means each new market is almost a separate business, which caps the "infinity story" and makes this look more like a solid regional services business than a protocol-layer investment with winner-take-most dynamics. The data flywheel is entirely prospective at this stage -- it requires years of loan performance data to become meaningful, and a two-person team spreading across the Philippines, Indonesia, and Mexico simultaneously is stretching thin.

The Birchbox lesson haunts me here: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces painful outcomes. Kita has a compelling narrative about financial inclusion and the unbanked. The founders are clearly capable. But the moat -- hyperlocalization plus a prospective data flywheel -- feels thin against the backdrop of rapidly commoditizing VLM technology and potential competition from regional fintechs who already have lender relationships and local data. I'd want to see the data flywheel actually compounding, the repayment-outcome feedback loop demonstrably improving accuracy beyond what a general-purpose model could achieve with prompt engineering, before writing a check. At this stage, I'm watching, not investing.
