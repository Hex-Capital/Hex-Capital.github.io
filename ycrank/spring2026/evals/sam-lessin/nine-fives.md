# Nine Fives -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Two SpaceX engineers manufacturing ethernet-enabled RF attenuators and switches -- actual physical instruments you bolt to a test bench -- in a YC batch where half the companies are pitching me LLM wrappers. Nine Fives is atoms in a world of bits, and that alone makes me pause. Andrew Kurtz (MIT, 9 years aerospace, Falcon 9 test automation) and Noah Levy (Cornell, senior RF engineer on SpaceX satellite hardware) are building the kind of thing no AI model will ever replace: a programmable attenuator that sits on a rack, attenuates an RF signal, and needs to be calibrated to sub-dB accuracy at 6 GHz. You cannot download that. You cannot prompt-engineer it. The physical moat here is real, and it maps to the same pattern I saw with Metropolis -- software amplifies the business, but the physical infrastructure is the thing. Metropolis controls parking garages; Nine Fives manufactures precision RF instruments. Neither can be commoditized by a foundation model.

Keysight pulls $5.4B in annual revenue selling full test system stacks where the margin lives in proprietary drivers and platform-specific software licenses. National Instruments got acquired by Emerson for $8.2B on the same model. Nine Fives' entire pitch -- "Simple, Networked, Driverless" -- is a direct unbundling attack on that margin structure. REST API, browser UI, zero-install, PoE-powered, operational in 10 seconds. This is the classic innovator's dilemma setup: the incumbents *can't* respond without cannibalizing the software ecosystem that funds their R&D. Mini-Circuits could theoretically add REST APIs to their 60,000+ catalog products, but as a components company shipping that volume, investing in per-product software UX is structurally deprioritized. Nine Fives is exploiting a business model conflict, not just a technology gap. That's the right kind of attack vector.

But here's where I get stuck: Nine Fives is not creating a new primitive. Nobody is going to use Nine Fives as a reference point for a new category of behavior. They're not inventing peer-to-peer RF testing or creating "test equipment as a protocol." They're building better, more network-friendly versions of instruments that already exist -- programmable attenuators and SPDT switches have been sold by Vaunix, Mini-Circuits, and Adaura for years. The REST API is a feature improvement, not a behavioral revolution. When I backed Venmo, the primitive was "send money to a friend from your phone" -- something that literally didn't exist as a consumer behavior. Nine Fives' primitive is "control your attenuator from a browser instead of installing GPIB drivers." That's valuable to RF test engineers. It's not a new verb. The dossier confirms Nine Fives differentiates against Mini-Circuits' RCDAT series and Vaunix Lab Bricks primarily on interface and connectivity -- that's execution in an existing category, not category creation.

Kurtz and Levy are clearly sane. SpaceX aerospace engineering, overlapping tenures, direct experience with the pain of RF test workflows -- the founder-market fit is excellent. But the idea itself is also sane. Building networked test instruments for a $4B market is a reasonable, defensible business proposition. It's not the kind of thing that makes smart people say "that's impossible." The sanity-insanity ratio is inverted from what I want -- I want a very reasonable person pitching something fucking crazy. Nine Fives is two very reasonable people pitching something very reasonable. That's fine for a lifestyle business or a $50-100M outcome, but it doesn't have the asymmetric upside profile I look for. Two guys competing against Keysight's $5.4B revenue machine is objectively audacious, but audacious-underdog is different from audacious-visionary.

The bull case is genuinely compelling: the 5G testing equipment sub-segment is growing at 9.5% CAGR to $4.13B by 2032, Open RAN is driving demand for software-defined test setups, and the DevOps-ification of hardware test creates structural demand for API-first instruments. Nine Fives has two shipping products at pre-seed with a two-person team -- that's real capital discipline. The AI kill test is passed convincingly; no model will replace a physical attenuator. If Nine Fives expands their product line from 2 instruments to 20, they become the de facto "modern networked test bench" vendor, and switching costs compound with every instrument integrated into automated test scripts. The Known Exception check: Metropolis was a physical-world infrastructure play I seeded early that's now at ~$5B. But Metropolis had compounding network effects -- more garages, more data, better pricing algorithms, more garages. Nine Fives sells individual instruments; each sale is relatively independent. There's no flywheel. And the 6 GHz frequency ceiling is a real constraint when the market is moving toward mmWave at 24-40 GHz -- Keysight operates to 50+ GHz, Vaunix to 40 GHz. Nine Fives is limited to sub-6GHz applications while 5G's most interesting testing demands are above that range.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Nine Fives showed me $200K+ in annual revenue from defense or telecom contracts with 50%+ gross margins and a credible product roadmap to 26.5+ GHz -- proving the business model works, the margins are real, and the frequency limitation is being addressed. At that point, the mispricing thesis sharpens dramatically because VCs would still be avoiding "niche hardware company" while the business is printing money. Without that evidence, Nine Fives is a solid industrial hardware company with excellent founder-market fit but no asymmetric return profile. The structural durability is the strongest dimension here -- 13/15 -- because physical RF instruments are genuinely AI-proof. But the absence of primitive creation (7/25) and the wrong sanity-insanity ratio (11/20) keep this in Neutral territory. Good business, wrong bet structure for my money.
