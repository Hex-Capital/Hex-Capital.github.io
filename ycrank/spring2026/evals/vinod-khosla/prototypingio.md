# Prototyping.io -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about this company is the competitive landscape it's walking into. Xometry does $687M in revenue. Protolabs does $533M. Fictiv just got acquired for $350M. RMFG has shipped 100,000+ parts. Jiga has $19M in funding and NASA as a customer. And then there's a wave of AI-native manufacturing startups — Toolpath, Korso, Factorem — all funded in the last eighteen months, all building some version of AI-powered manufacturing intelligence. Prototyping.io enters this market with two people, no traction, no revenue, and a gated waitlist. When I see a crowded space where every player is converging on the same enabling technology — LLMs and vision models applied to CAD geometry — I don't see a market ripe for disruption. I see a consensus bet. And consensus bets are not how I invest.

Let me start where I always start: what happens if this fully works? If Prototyping.io succeeds completely, hardware engineers get faster DFM feedback and smoother ordering workflows. Manufacturing quotes arrive in minutes instead of days. Design iterations shrink. That's genuinely useful — but it's a productivity improvement for a professional niche, not a transformation of how the world works. The actual cost of machining a part — the material, the machine time, the skilled labor — doesn't change because someone built a better front-end for quoting. Compare this to what excites me: Commonwealth Fusion Systems making energy limitless, Impossible Foods making protein without animals, OpenAI making intelligence abundant. Those are consequences that reshape civilization. A faster DFM tool reshapes the workflow of maybe 200,000 mechanical engineers. The magnitude gap is enormous.

The technical approach compounds my concern. "AI-native DFM analysis" in 2026 means applying commercially available vision and language models to interpret 3D geometry. This is useful engineering work, not a technology discontinuity. There's no novel architecture described, no proprietary dataset, no scientific breakthrough. The dossier explicitly notes the "why now" is that LLM capabilities crossed a threshold — which means every competent team in the space has access to the same enabling technology simultaneously. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had built fundamentally new rocket engines with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers. That was a step-function. Wrapping existing AI models around CAD file parsing is not a step-function. It's the kind of incremental capability addition that Xometry could ship as a feature update next quarter — and given that they already process millions of parts through AI quoting, they have vastly more training data.

The founders are competent. Revanth's mechanical engineering background — IIT silver medalist, UT Austin aerospace research, Schlumberger manufacturing — gives him genuine domain knowledge. Prerit brings strong software and AI systems experience from Microsoft and distributed systems research. The pairing covers both sides of the problem. But here's my concern: this is exactly the profile of domain experts building a logical extension of their experience, not outsiders reimagining an industry from first principles. Revanth knows manufacturing because he's lived it. Prerit knows AI systems because he's built them. They combined their expertise into a product that is... exactly what you'd expect from combining manufacturing expertise with AI systems expertise. Where is the insight that nobody else has? Where is the audacity that makes experts uncomfortable? When I backed the 19-year-old MIT dropout at Mach Industries to compete with Lockheed Martin, the naivety was the feature. Nothing about this team's positioning suggests they see something that Xometry's AI team, or RMFG's founders, or Jiga's team does not.

The strongest bull case would require Prototyping.io to become not a better marketplace but the intelligence layer for all of manufacturing — where their DFM models get so good that no hardware team ships a design without running it through their platform first, and this capability compounds into a data moat that makes every subsequent analysis better. If that happened, and if it reduced total product development cycles by 10x rather than 30%, you'd start to see hardware companies iterating at software speed, which would genuinely accelerate innovation. That's a real consequence. But nothing in the evidence suggests a path from "gated waitlist with two founders" to "indispensable intelligence layer" that doesn't run directly through five better-funded competitors with more data, more traction, and more resources. The data flywheel advantage requires volume to work, and they have none. The name confusion with Proto.io and Prototypr.io is a small but telling detail — organic discoverability matters for a platform that needs to attract engineers, and starting with a confused brand signal is an unforced error.

This is the kind of company I regularly pass on at YC Demo Day. Competent founders, real market, logical product — and a consequence of success that amounts to a moderately profitable marketplace business. I don't invest to build better Xometries. I invest to build things where, if they work, the world visibly changes. Nothing here clears that bar.
