# Control Seat -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Control Seat is two Dartmouth '26 graduates -- Jack Grodnick and Warren Shepard -- shipping an AI-native SCADA builder into AVEVA Wonderware, Siemens WinCC, Rockwell FactoryTalk, and Inductive Automation's Ignition. The dimensional signature is unambiguous: Control Seat ranks highest on raw founder intelligence (Shepard is salutatorian at ~3.99 GPA, both did SpaceX) and lowest on permissionless leverage architecture -- which is to say, two of the smartest 22-year-olds in this batch have aimed themselves at the most permissioned distribution surface in software. Industrial control is sold through plant managers, OT security teams, IEC 62443 auditors, and systems integrators. Code and media are not the leverage here; labor and capital are. That single inversion is the whole evaluation.

The leverage check is what I run first, and Control Seat fails it the way **Stack Overflow** (2008) passed it -- in the mirror. Stack Overflow was developers writing for developers, distributed by Google SEO and a permissionless link graph that compounded every time a junior engineer pasted an error message into a search bar. The mechanism was code-and-media native; the marginal cost of the (N+1) answer was zero, and the (N+1) answer made the prior N more valuable. Control Seat's own website says it "designs, builds, deploys, and runs" with a "competitive price guarantee" to beat any integrator quote (controlseat.com, 2026) -- that is the textbook capital-and-labor leverage description from nav.al/product-media (~2018), with the founders themselves as the labor. There is no GitHub repo with organic stars, no public technical writing pulling community contributions, no developer-pull motion in the Control Seat dossier. The launch artifact is a YouTube video. The architecture compounds nothing without gatekeeper permission, and the gatekeeper is a plant safety officer.

The **Pinterest** miss (Knowledge Project #18) is the lowest-rung pattern I keep returning to -- not because it failed, but because it taught me where my framework breaks: I passed despite the leverage architecture working, on personal-taste bias. The inverse failure mode is funding architecture that doesn't work because the founders are impressive. Control Seat triggers that exact failure mode. The two-Dartmouth-grads, ex-SpaceX/Tesla/Microsoft credential stack is precisely the "world-class team via four logos with no shipped artifacts named" pattern my own Red Flags section calls out -- Grodnick's GitHub (@jackgrodnick) has no notable star counts, Shepard's GitHub has no public repos, neither has an X account, and Control Seat itself was incorporated January 2026 with zero public deployments disclosed. Pedigree-as-thesis is the absence of specific knowledge under different vocabulary, and the dossier offers no shipped artifact in industrial control that predates the YC accept.

Specific knowledge -- the **Notion** (2013) Ivan-Zhao-rebuilding-personal-productivity-tools-since-adolescence filter -- is where Control Seat earns partial credit and no more. SpaceX runs genuine custom industrial telemetry, and an intern there has seen something most YC SCADA pretenders haven't. But the asymmetric math wants Aravind Srinivas-at-Perplexity depth: a Berkeley ML PhD in retrieval, not a summer touching the Falcon ground systems console. Compared against my **Replit** (April 2023) anchor where Amjad Masad had been building the product as obsession since 2011, Control Seat's founders have months of exposure, not years of immersion in PLC programming for Allen-Bradley, Siemens, and Beckhoff -- which is what the website claims they ship.

Network compounding is the **Uber** (2010) test -- does adding supply make demand more valuable, or vice versa. Control Seat is per-plant SCADA: each customer is economically independent of the next, no tag schema in plant A makes plant B's dashboard more useful, no historian from refinery X improves anomaly detection at refinery Y unless Control Seat builds cross-tenant ML, which the dossier does not claim. Mechanism analysis over 18 months: even if Control Seat lands ten plants, the 11th does not get cheaper or better because of the prior ten. That is the single-sided SaaS profile my criterion #2 anchors call out, and the **Perplexity** known-exception override does not apply here because criteria #1 and #3 do not also clear at top quartile -- they clear at bottom and middle, respectively.

The TAM is $12.89B (MarketsandMarkets, 2026) growing to $20.05B by 2030 -- not small, but the pivot vectors are constrained. Counterfactual for criterion #4 to flip a tier: Control Seat would need a credible adjacency into MES (Tulip's lane), developer tooling for industrial engineers, or telemetry-as-API for hardware startups -- none of which the dossier names. The "first idea never works" axiom (Venture Hacks, 2007-10) requires room to maneuver, and SCADA-with-AI is one product idea, not a platform shape. Inductive Automation's bootstrapped ~$41M ARR with 69% Fortune-100 penetration (getlatka.com, 2025) is the ceiling that says you can build a real business here without venture math, which is the opposite of what angel-stage asymmetric-payoff geometry rewards.

My 36-month forecast: Control Seat either reaches $1-3M in services-blended revenue and is acqui-hired by Rockwell, AVEVA, or Tulip for the team, or it pivots inside the YC year toward AI-native developer tooling for hardware/robotics startups -- the SpaceX-adjacent surface where Grodnick and Shepard have actual lived exposure -- and the SCADA framing becomes a forgotten origin pitch. It does not become a venture-scale outcome in industrial control. The mechanism producing this forecast is the same one that made **Anchorage Digital** the known exception it was: regulated-infrastructure plays demand category-timing conviction the angel has accumulated for years (MetaStable, 2014, onward), and I have accumulated none in SCADA.

The bull case that should sting: the **Anchorage Digital** override is exactly the precedent that permission-as-moat can flip a leverage failure into a leverage advantage, and the IEC 62443 / critical-infrastructure regulatory wall around SCADA (Globe Newswire, May 2026) is a structurally similar moat to OCC charter custody. If Control Seat's two SpaceX-formed founders execute the compliance gauntlet and AI-on-tag-data becomes a real wedge against Wonderware's decades-old desktop stack, the regulatory burden becomes the durable competitive shield. Dismantling: Anchorage worked because Naval had been advising and writing checks in crypto since 2014, and the founders brought institutional-custody specific knowledge from prior roles -- Control Seat brings neither category-timing conviction from me nor multi-year industrial-control specific knowledge from the founders. The verifiable flip condition: if Control Seat publishes a signed contract with a named Fortune-500 plant operator, or open-sources a PLC SDK that gets >500 GitHub stars from practicing controls engineers within twelve months, I revisit -- because either would convert one of the failing dimensions into top-quartile evidence.
