# Cascade -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Cascade's numbers stopped me: $183k in contracted ARR from zero in three months, 15 signed firms including the kind that build JFK terminals and nuclear reactors, and the first three clients more than doubling their spend. In construction software — an industry notorious for buying cycles measured in quarters, not weeks — that velocity is genuinely unusual. When someone tells me they got 15 construction firms to sign anything in 90 days, my first instinct is to check whether the product is delivering real value or whether the sales rep is just very good. Cascade seems to have both. Their Head of Growth was ranked #2 at Procore and drove $3M in ARR there — hiring that person, at this stage, was itself a resourceful move. The founders knew they needed domain sales credibility they didn't have, and they went and got the best person they could find at the dominant construction platform. That kind of self-awareness about your own gaps is underrated.

The problem is that when I look at Hannia Zia and Joana Ferreira, I see two very capable engineers from Google and Amazon who chose construction as a market rather than discovering it as a frustration. Hannia scaled Google Pay India from zero to 100 million users — that's genuinely hard, and it tells me she can execute at scale. Joana built ML systems at Google Search and Amazon, then led AI at UnlikelyAI — real technical depth. They worked together at UnlikelyAI for years, which means the co-founder relationship is tested, and that matters enormously. But the origin story doesn't start with "we kept hitting this problem ourselves." It starts with Joana growing up in a family of carpenters, which is the kind of biographical detail that shows up in pitch decks because investors like founder-market fit narratives. I don't doubt it's true — I doubt it's sufficient. The founders who built Dropbox forgot their USB drive every day. The founders who built Stripe spent years fighting payment APIs. Cascade's founders identified an underserved vertical through observation, not through the specific kind of lived frustration that produces deep product intuition about what construction firms actually need at 2 AM when a proposal deadline is approaching.

The Stripe comparison is instructive, because Cascade is attempting a similar structural play — combining fragmented, compliance-heavy infrastructure into a single vertical platform. Stripe unified payment processing, PCI compliance, and bank integrations. Cascade is unifying RFP discovery, proposal generation, Davis-Bacon compliance, and ERP sync for AEC firms. The schlep is real: government portal scraping across thousands of state and local sites, construction-specific compliance like Davis-Bacon certified payroll, integration with niche ERPs like Deltek Vantagepoint and Ajera. Most founders would look at that list and build a horizontal proposal tool instead. Cascade's willingness to go deep into AEC-specific plumbing creates genuine switching costs that AutogenAI, even with $65.3 million in funding, would find painful to replicate — because AutogenAI's broader customer base doesn't justify the specialized engineering investment. But the Stripe parallel also exposes the gap: Patrick Collison was a developer building for developers, and that identity match is why Stripe's product decisions were so consistently right. Hannia and Joana are tech people building for construction project managers, and that distance between builder and buyer is a real risk when the easy wins are exhausted and the product needs to make the subtle decisions that only deep domain empathy produces.

The expansion revenue signal is worth dwelling on. "First three companies have more than doubled their spend" is close to the obsessive-usage pattern I look for — a small number of users wanting the product a large amount, even in its early form. Eleven of fifteen clients signing in the last month suggests accelerating word-of-mouth or a sales motion that's finding repeatability. If Cascade can sustain anything close to this trajectory, the 5-7% weekly growth compass I use would point firmly toward a real company. But the question I can't answer from a dossier is whether this is product pull or sales push. A #2 Procore sales rep can generate impressive early numbers through relationship selling; the test is whether the product retains and expands without that specific person's Rolodex driving every deal.

For Cascade to become the kind of company I'd feel confident betting on with my own money, two things would need to be true: first, that the AI proposal quality is good enough that firms measurably win more bids (the "$120M+ in extra opportunities surfaced monthly" claim needs conversion data behind it); and second, that Hannia and Joana can develop the domain depth they currently lack through proximity to their most demanding clients. Hannia's pandemic tech response in Pakistan — building a COVID WhatsApp bot that reached 500,000 users and growing a Health Ministry page to 1.8 million followers — suggests she can operate effectively in messy, institutional environments far from Silicon Valley. That's not nothing. And the long co-founder relationship plus the smart early hire suggest a team that compensates for gaps rather than ignoring them. The traction is the strongest argument; the organic discovery gap is the strongest concern. Those two dimensions pulled in opposite directions, and the traction wasn't quite enough to overcome the absence of the founder-problem intimacy that defines my best investments.
