# 9 Mothers -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 9 Mothers is that counter-drone is one of the hottest categories in defense tech right now. Allen Control Systems already raised $30M from Craft Ventures and won an Army xTechOverwatch competition. Anduril is raising at $60 billion. DroneShield just posted $216 million in revenue. Defense-tech venture funding nearly doubled to $49 billion in 2025. When I look at this landscape, I don't see the unpopular, difficult-to-assess opportunity that gets my attention -- I see a category where consensus has already formed. The most promising companies tend to be the ones nobody wants to fund. That's not the situation here. The situation here is that everyone wants to fund counter-drone, and 9 Mothers is entering with less capital, fewer government relationships, and a narrower capability set than the competitor doing essentially the same thing one town over in Austin.

What I want to hear -- and can't from a dossier -- is the personal story. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA through YC S12, raised $41 million, scaled to $25 million in revenue and 141 employees. That's real entrepreneurial grit and I respect it deeply. But QA testing software is a long way from autonomous weapon systems. The biographical thread I'd pull hardest on is Roman Khomenko's. He's from Kharkiv -- a city that's been under relentless drone attack since 2022. He served as Ukrainian police. If Roman is building this because he watched his homeland get torn apart by the exact weapons EDDA is designed to defeat, that's the kind of biographical necessity that makes me lean forward. That's the founder who won't quit when the government contract takes eighteen months longer than expected. But I'm inferring that connection -- it's not stated anywhere, and I never invest based on my own narrative about someone else's motivation. I need to hear it from them.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If you told me "a Ukrainian AI engineer who watched cheap drones devastate his country teamed up with a repeat YC founder and a penetration testing expert to build the cheapest possible kinetic counter-drone system that a single soldier can carry" -- that story has elements I love. The democratization angle reminds me of why I backed Uber: it wasn't about logistics optimization, it was about giving economic access to drivers and affordable rides to passengers. EDDA at its best is about giving the individual soldier the same protection that currently only comes from million-dollar platform-mounted systems. Making the threat visible and defeatable at the ground level. And the team's 8-year working relationship between Smith and Khomenko is exactly the kind of battle-tested co-founder dynamic I look for after HQ Trivia taught me what unexamined partnerships cost. That's a genuine strength.

But I keep coming back to the competitive reality and the domain gap. ACS has the Bullfrog, covers Groups 1 through 3 (versus EDDA's Group 1 only), has SOCOM maritime contracts, and just tripled its Austin operations. 9 Mothers has no public evidence of government contracts, SBIR awards, testing partnerships, or even a working prototype demonstration. Building autonomous weapon systems that meet defense-grade standards requires manufacturing expertise, systems integration experience, and procurement relationships that this team hasn't demonstrated. My Anduril investment worked partly because Palmer Luckey had already built and sold Oculus -- he understood hardware at scale. I don't see that hardware manufacturing depth here. The founders are software and security people, and while Khomenko's computer vision skills are directly relevant to the AI core, the kinetic turret itself is a mechanical and ballistic engineering challenge that needs domain expertise I can't find on this team.

I invested in Anduril when defense tech was genuinely contrarian -- when most of Silicon Valley thought working with the Pentagon was morally unacceptable. That was a non-consensus bet. Counter-drone in 2026 is not that bet. It's the bet everyone is making. For me to write a check into a consensus category, the founder's story and the company's specific edge would need to be extraordinary enough to override my instinct to look elsewhere. With ACS meaningfully ahead in procurement and the team's defense domain gap unaddressed, I'd need to meet these founders, hear the real story behind why Russell Smith went from QA software to weapon systems and whether Roman's Kharkiv roots are truly driving this mission, before I could get to conviction. On paper, this is a competent team entering a crowded race late.
