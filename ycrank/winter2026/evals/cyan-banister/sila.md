# Sila -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Sila: this is one of the most popular ideas in tech right now. "AI-native messaging to replace Slack" — Glue raised $20 million for that exact pitch in October 2025, with David Sacks as co-founder. Dust.tt has $7.3 million in ARR building adjacent tooling. Slack itself is rolling out Agentforce. Microsoft Teams has Copilot. When I look at a deal and see four or five well-funded teams converging on the same insight simultaneously, that tells me consensus has already formed. And my entire investing life has been built on the opposite signal — finding the ideas that make other investors uncomfortable, the ones that sound insane at first pass. Mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota sounded insane. A private company launching rockets to orbit sounded insane. "Let's build a better Slack with AI agents" does not sound insane to anyone in Silicon Valley right now. It sounds like Tuesday.

The founder question is where I always start, and it's where I hit a wall with Sila. Mith Patel and Carl Huang are clearly capable builders — they shipped an AI chatbot across 16 Cornell classes and co-founded an AI course marketplace. That's real execution for students still in school. But when I ask my fundamental question — tell me your story, not the business — I don't find the biographical thread that connects these specific founders to the problem of reinventing team communication. They went from AI education tools to AI messaging. Why? What personal pain drove that leap? Was there a moment where Slack failed them so profoundly that they couldn't sleep until they fixed it? The dossier doesn't show that, and their LinkedIn profiles still list saiera as their primary venture. I need to feel that the founder and the problem are inseparable — that's what I saw with Crusoe's founders and wasted gas, with Contraline's founder and years of reproductive biology research. Here I see two talented recent graduates who appear to be chasing the current wave, which is exactly the pattern I've trained myself to avoid.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The architectural argument is genuinely interesting — Slack and Teams face real cannibalization risk if they rebuild their messaging layers from scratch, and that structural gap is where startups can win. Every generation does get a new communication platform. These founders can clearly build and ship quickly. If the "AI Teammate" concept — persistent agents with defined roles operating alongside humans — proves to be the right abstraction, and if Sila can build enough workflow lock-in before Glue and the incumbents converge, the winner-take-most dynamics of messaging platforms could produce an enormous outcome. The MCP standardization and LLM capability improvements create a genuine "why now." For this to work, though, Sila would need to be dramatically faster and more product-intuitive than Glue (which has a $20M head start and Sacks's distribution network) and would need the incumbents' retrofit approach to fail visibly enough to drive switching. That's a lot of things that need to go right simultaneously.

What I don't see here — and this matters to me — is any pathway to economic access or real-world impact for people who currently lack it. Sila is building productivity software for knowledge workers who already have jobs, already use Slack, and already have access to AI tools. When I backed Uber, I saw a tool that would create income streams for hundreds of thousands of drivers. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit for people getting crushed by hidden fees. Sila doesn't have that dimension. It's an optimization tool for people who are already in the system, not a bridge for people who are outside it. That's not disqualifying on its own, but combined with the consensus positioning and the absence of founder biographical depth, it means this company doesn't activate any of the signals I've learned to trust.

The one genuinely positive signal is the co-founder relationship. Mith and Carl have worked together across multiple projects — saiera, the Cornell chatbot, and now Sila. After what I went through watching HQ Trivia collapse entirely because of founder conflicts, I always look for evidence that co-founders have been through hard things together and come out the other side. A shared history at Cornell across two prior ventures is meaningful. But one positive dimension isn't enough to overcome the fundamental mismatch with how I invest. This is a popular idea, in a crowded market, built by founders who are technically skilled but haven't shown me the personal story that makes them the inevitable team to solve this particular problem with my money.
