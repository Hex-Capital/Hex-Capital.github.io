# Travo -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask is always the same: what is the secret? What does this company know about the world that most intelligent people would actively dispute? With Travo, I struggle to identify one. The thesis -- that AI can collect real estate data more cheaply than incumbents, and that niche asset classes like RV parks are underserved by CoStar's $30,000-per-year platform -- is not a secret. It is an observation. Every PropTech founder in San Francisco can see that CoStar charges extractive prices and ignores small asset categories. Every AI-native startup in 2026 believes that LLM-driven data collection will disrupt legacy data businesses. When your thesis is something every smart person in the room already agrees with, you do not have a secret -- you have a consensus view, which means you are about to compete, and competition destroys value.

The competitive landscape confirms this diagnosis. CoStar, Cherre, CompStak, Reonomy, Built AI -- multiple well-funded players are already building variations on "AI meets commercial real estate data." Travo's niche positioning in RV parks creates temporary differentiation, but it does not create a monopoly. The underlying technology -- scraping, aggregating, and analyzing unstructured property data with AI -- is table-stakes capability in 2026. Any of these competitors, or a new entrant with $5 million in seed funding and a real estate veteran on the team, could assemble a comparable dataset for RV parks within a year. The technology here is 1-to-n -- applying existing AI capabilities to a new vertical. That is not what I invest in. I invest in technology that makes the old approach structurally obsolete, not marginally more efficient.

The founding team deserves credit for one thing: the beachhead strategy is textbook. Starting with RV parks -- a narrow segment with concentrated buyers, underserved by the dominant incumbent, with a logical expansion path to adjacent niche asset classes -- is the right structural move. If I were advising this company, I would tell them this is their best instinct. The problem is that the team has zero documented experience in commercial real estate, property data, or even adjacent domains like finance or construction. Four Stanford CS undergraduates identified a market gap from the outside. This is the opposite of how my best investments have worked. At Palantir, we built from the inside -- the algorithms came from PayPal's fraud detection, the customer relationships came from years of intelligence community engagement, the domain knowledge was bone-deep. When I backed Zuckerberg, he was not applying technology to an observed social networking market -- he was a social creature who understood identity and community at an intuitive level that no MBA could replicate. Travo's founders are applying technical skills to a domain they appear to have studied, not lived in. In an industry where data quality is everything and sophisticated PE buyers will interrogate every comparable sale, the absence of domain expertise is not a gap -- it is a structural vulnerability.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that AI-driven data collection has crossed a quality threshold where it matches or exceeds CoStar's manual processes, that the RV park and outdoor hospitality niche is large enough and growing fast enough to sustain venture-scale returns, and that Travo can accumulate enough data depth to create meaningful switching costs before CoStar or a better-funded competitor replicates the approach. The innovator's dilemma argument has some force -- CoStar generating $3.2 billion in annual revenue has little incentive to invest in a market segment worth perhaps $10 million annually. If Travo executes well and expands across a portfolio of neglected micro-verticals, they could build something real. But "if they execute well" is indefinite optimism -- it is a hope, not a plan. I do not see a founder with a definite vision of what the world looks like in 2036 if Travo succeeds. I see a competent team that identified a gap and is iterating into it. That is the lean startup philosophy dressed in AI clothing, and it is not what generates power-law returns.

This is a pass. The company enters a market where the dominant incumbent is a genuine monopolist, surrounded by multiple well-funded startups pursuing overlapping theses, armed with technology that is not proprietary and lacking the domain knowledge that would make their data product trustworthy to institutional buyers. The niche strategy is sound but insufficient to compensate for the absence of a genuine secret. Most companies fail because they cannot escape competition. Travo has not yet demonstrated a path to escape.
