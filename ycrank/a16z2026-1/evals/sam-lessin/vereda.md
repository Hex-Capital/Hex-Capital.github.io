# Vereda -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Orbia is owned 68.8% by Bayer, 17.2% by Bravium, and 14% by Yara — the very manufacturers whose distribution networks Vereda is trying to disintermediate. That's the single most interesting dynamic in this dossier. These incumbents are structurally trapped: they cannot build a direct-to-farmer channel without torching R$167 billion in distributor relationships that feed their own revenue. This is the kind of channel conflict that creates real room for a startup to maneuver. It reminds me of the Metropolis thesis — we seeded a company applying AI to parking infrastructure, a physical-world operation that incumbents were too entangled in legacy relationships to modernize. Metropolis is now worth ~$5B. Vereda has a structurally similar setup: AI amplifying a physical supply chain where incumbents are frozen by their own business models. The question is whether the parallel holds or breaks.

Where it breaks is on mispricing. Brazil agrifoodtech investment surged 32% quarter-over-quarter in Q1 2025 with an 85% year-over-year increase. Agrofy has raised $66M. Grão Direto has pulled in $26M. This is not a category investors are avoiding — it's a category they're warming to. Now, the specific approach — a WhatsApp-native procurement agent that aggregates demand from small farmers and goes directly to manufacturers, bypassing the entire distribution layer — that's more contrarian than the broader category. Most US and European VCs look at "WhatsApp chatbot for Brazilian farmers" and their eyes glaze over. Too operational. Too geo-specific. Too weird. So there's some mispricing from approach and geography, but it's not Solana-at-four-cents mispricing. It's more like a 20% discount on a fairly priced category, not a 99% discount on a mispriced one.

The primitive test is where Vereda falls short for me. Group purchasing organizations have existed for decades. Agricultural marketplaces exist. WhatsApp commerce exists across dozens of categories in Brazil. Vereda is combining these three known elements in a novel way — which is competent product thinking, not primitive creation. Nobody is going to say "I'll Vereda you" the way they say "Venmo me." The embedded finance optionality — layering credit and crop insurance on top of proprietary transaction data — could evolve into something more primitive-like, but that's two or three turns of the crank away from where Vereda is today. The "$8M in potential revenue" against "$155M in GMV" implies a ~5% take rate, which is a marketplace commission structure, not a financial infrastructure margin. If they can move from marketplace commissions to becoming the credit and payment rails for Brazilian smallholder agriculture, that's a different company entirely. But right now, that's a hope, not a product.

João Souza's background — farming family, $1B+ in agribusiness transactions at BTG Pactual, founding team at a Series B B2B marketplace — is genuinely strong domain signal. Pedro Galindo coming from Patria Investments, the most active PE investor in LatAm agriculture, and teaching generative AI at USC, gives the pair unusual coverage across agriculture, finance, and AI. The founder-market fit is real. But I haven't watched these founders across multiple ventures the way I'd watched Raj Dittmar through three companies before writing the Solana check. They met at USC — shared educational and cultural context in Brazilian ag, which is better than a founder-matching service, but it's not a decade-long relationship forged through adversity. The idea itself — bypassing a $31 billion distribution layer with a WhatsApp bot — has some of the "sane person, mildly insane idea" quality I look for, but the insanity dial isn't cranked high enough. Disintermediation is a business school case study, not a crazy bet. Investing in a human being's future earnings for 30 years — that's crazy. This is aggressive but legible.

Vereda's structural durability is its strongest dimension relative to my framework. Agricultural inputs are physical products that must move from manufacturer to farm — no LLM replaces that logistics chain. The 300,000+ hectares served and 50+ cities covered represent real-world operational footprint, not user sign-ups. The proprietary demand data from WhatsApp interactions — what farmers buy, when, at what volumes, with what payment preferences — is genuinely valuable intelligence that manufacturers don't currently have in aggregated form. The WhatsApp-native approach, while creating Meta platform dependency, leverages 99% smartphone penetration in rural Brazil and eliminates app-download friction entirely. This is the "physical-world operations that software amplifies but cannot replace" pattern that I've consistently bet on. A team of six in Rio Verde, Brazil is inherently lean — Brazilian cost structure plus WhatsApp-native distribution means low burn relative to the operational surface they're covering. That's capital discipline by design, not by virtue.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Vereda captures enough farmer demand data and transaction volume in the next 18 months to become indispensable to manufacturers as a direct channel, then layers embedded credit on top of that data to transform from a 5% marketplace into a financial infrastructure play with 15-20% net margins. The "$155M GMV in 45 days" claim — even if it represents purchasing intent captured during a seasonal buying window rather than closed transactions — suggests the demand signal is real. If manufacturers actually sign on and Vereda closes that loop, the R$167B input distribution market has a genuine disruption vector. The risk that flips me from neutral to pass: if no major manufacturer has signed a direct supply agreement by the end of the next crop cycle, the entire model is a demand aggregation exercise with no supply to match it to. The dossier shows zero confirmed supplier agreements. That's the gap between thesis and business.

Vereda lands in my neutral zone — competent founders with genuine domain depth in a real market, but lacking the extreme mispricing and primitive creation that make me reach for my checkbook. The channel conflict protecting them from Orbia is structurally interesting, and the physical-world moat passes my AI kill test. But the absence of a new primitive (this is disintermediation, not category creation) and the moderate rather than extreme consensus mispricing keep this from clearing the bar. If João and Pedro come back in two years having closed direct manufacturer agreements and built a credit product on their transaction data, the conversation changes entirely.
