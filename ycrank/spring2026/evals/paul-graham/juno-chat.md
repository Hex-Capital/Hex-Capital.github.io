# Juno -- Paul Graham Evaluation

$600K in annual revenue, six months after launch, built by two people. That number is the thing I keep coming back to with Juno. At pre-seed, most founders are still arguing about their landing page copy. Isaac Tolley and Marshall Gould shipped an iOS app in October 2025 and hit a run rate that implies meaningful paid conversion from 50,000+ users by the time they applied to YC. The 4.7-star rating across 1,900+ reviews on the App Store isn't a vanity metric either — it means Juno's users, who are people managing fibromyalgia, POTS, ME/CFS, and EDS, aren't just downloading the app out of curiosity. They're staying. They're paying $9.99 a month or $99.99 a year. That pattern — a small number of people wanting something a large amount — is the signal I've learned to trust more than any TAM slide.

Isaac ranked 2nd in his Biochemistry cohort at UCL and did neuroscience research on neurological disease. Marshall published in The Oxford Scientist on the ethics of genetic screening — the kind of writing that reveals whether someone can actually think, which I care about more than most investors realize. But the credential that matters most is one that doesn't appear on any resume: both founders grew up with chronic illness. Juno exists because Isaac and Marshall lived through the 7.6-year diagnostic delays, the 15-minute appointments where you can't convey a complex medical history, the loneliness of managing symptoms between visits with no structured support. This is the purest version of "live in the future, then build what's missing" — except they didn't have to live in the future. They lived in a present that most healthy founders can't even see. When I backed Bindwell — two teenagers using AI for pesticide discovery — part of what convinced me was that they'd found a problem domain so unglamorous that no polished Stanford MBA would touch it. Juno has the same quality. Building an AI companion for people with conditions that many doctors still dismiss as psychosomatic is a schlep that repels almost everyone, and that repulsion is the moat.

Bearable has 900K+ members doing manual symptom tracking. Visible raised $1M for wearable-based pacing focused on ME/CFS. Lasa Health got $250K for clinician-facing pelvic pain diagnosis. Juno's competitive landscape tells you something: every existing player is either self-funded, barely funded, or narrowly scoped. No one with real resources has entered this space because chronic illness management looks like a terrible business — heterogeneous conditions, regulatory landmines, no clear enterprise buyer, patients who are often economically constrained. That's exactly the pattern I described in "Schlep Blindness." Your unconscious won't even let you see the opportunity because the work required to capture it looks so unpleasant. Juno's founders don't have that filter. They built the app because they needed it, and 50,000 other people apparently needed it too. The ClassDojo parallel is instructive — I invested in a K-12 classroom management tool that VCs dismissed as too unsexy and bureaucratically entangled, and it became a unicorn. When schlep blindness creates a vacuum and the founders' execution quality fills it, sector preferences become irrelevant.

Juno's iteration speed gives me a partial read on learning rate. Version 3.0 introduced medication tracking and community features; they're now at 3.1.2 as of April 2026. In six months they went from conversational AI to a platform with structured logging, community support groups, and provider-facing report generation. The "proprietary engagement layer" built from a year of Oxford and UCL research — a system that determines what questions to ask chronic illness patients and when — is the kind of non-trivial clinical artifact that a competitor can't replicate by spinning up a GPT wrapper over a weekend. Whether that research year constitutes genuine R&D or sophisticated prompt engineering, I can't tell from the dossier. But the 4.7-star rating and $600K ARR suggest Juno's users experience it as meaningfully different from asking ChatGPT about their symptoms.

The bear case is real, and it centers on a question I always ask: can the founders build the product themselves? No GitHub repos were found for either Isaac or Marshall. Isaac's background is biochemistry and neuroscience research. Marshall's is scientific writing and ethics. The app is published under "SharedGenes" with Marshall listed as developer, but the dossier surfaces no evidence of software engineering training for either founder. This is one of my stated red flags — "a founding team that relies entirely on outsourced or hired engineering lacks the iteration speed and product taste I consider essential." And yet: they shipped a product. It works. Fifty thousand people use it. At some point the artifact has to outweigh the credential gap, the way the Airbnb founders' cereal boxes outweighed their design-school backgrounds as evidence they could run a company. I'm also concerned about LLM dependency — Juno runs on GPT, and daily voice conversations with 50K+ users generate substantial API costs that could crush margins if OpenAI raises prices. The iOS-only distribution limits their addressable market, particularly since chronic illness patients in lower-income demographics disproportionately use Android. And the name collision with four other "Juno" companies is the kind of unglamorous problem that eats founder attention.

The condition that would flip me to Pass: if I discovered the engagement layer is thin prompt engineering rather than genuine clinical research, or if the $600K ARR is inflated by weekly subscribers churning at 80%+ monthly. Either would undermine the core thesis that Juno has built something meaningfully differentiated. But the organic problem discovery here is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed — two founders who literally grew up with the conditions their product serves, who spent a year doing clinical research before writing a line of product code, and who then acquired 50,000 users in six months without visible marketing spend. The earnestness is unmistakable. The schlep is real. The question is whether the technical foundation underneath can support what they're building. I'd want to sit across from Isaac and Marshall for ten minutes and hear them describe how they built the engagement layer — not what it does, but how they built it. That story would tell me whether I'm looking at the next ClassDojo or a well-intentioned app that hits a ceiling.
