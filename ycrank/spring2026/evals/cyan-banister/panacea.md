# Panacea -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Panacea is the shape of company my filter usually wants -- regulated, unsexy, FDA-adjacent -- packaged in a wrapper my filter usually rejects, which is a Calendly link to Haris Javed-Akhtar's free consultation call instead of a product I can install or photograph. The pairing tells you what kind of bet this is before any score: a category-correct, artifact-absent services shop entering a lane where Weave Bio just took $20M Series A from USVP in October 2025 with a Parexel partnership already inked. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game here is whether two ex-Anterior operators can outrun a funded incumbent in a category that is no longer ignored.

Vigil Labs (August 2025) is the right anchor for the lane Panacea is trying to enter -- regulated finance software where the moat compounds because most investors flinch at the compliance surface, and the founder is *of* the regulatory world rather than visiting it. Panacea passes the category test: FDA submissions at $26K user fees, $100K testing, $250K-$5M remediation when filings fail is exactly the "too hard or too unsexy" lane I fund. Where Panacea diverges from the Vigil rung is the founder-substrate question. Haris is MBBS Imperial College and ex-Partnerships at Anterior; Yaman is ex-Bloomberg, ex-AI Platform at Anterior. That is healthcare AI adjacency, not FDA submission lived experience -- the bench of "ex-FDA consultants with decades of experience" the website sells is, per the dossier, not named publicly. The buyer of a 510(k) engagement is risk-averse by definition and will ask who the reviewer was.

HQ Trivia (2017) is the failure mechanism I trace when co-founder governance is unresolved -- Yusupov and Kroll could not name a tiebreaker, and the product imploded with the cap table. That specific pattern does not appear here: Haris is the named CEO, Yaman is the named CTO, two-person team, no third co-founder, no shared-title fudge in the dossier. Panacea earns full credit on the structural test that has cost me real money elsewhere, even if no operating agreement is disclosed publicly. This is the cleanest dimension on Panacea's scorecard and the least diagnostic of outcome.

Mechanism analysis on the magically-weird axis: in October 2025 Weave Bio raised $20M Series A from USVP and announced the Parexel partnership; Innolitics is offering guaranteed 3-month AI/ML SaMD timelines; Complizen runs freemium AI FDA tooling; RQM+ has $301M in the bank. Panacea is entering a YC Spring 2026 batch as the third or fourth visible "AI-native FDA" entrant in eighteen months. The thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus, and this is the moment after consensus has already arrived -- the Parexel-Weave press release in October 2025 is the equivalent of a tier-one fund leading a Series A in your category before you've shipped. Compounding over 18 months, the AI efficiency gains Panacea is selling get arbitraged into table-stakes pricing across the lane.

Counterfactual on physical verifiability: to flip from a 4/13 into Postmates territory, Panacea would need to be a thing I can install, run, or watch deliver in front of me within 48 hours. Today the entire surface is a marketing site, a LinkedIn page at linkedin.com/company/panacea-bio/, and cal.com/haris-panacea/call. No Product Hunt, no public demo, no named pilot customer, no Twitter, no GitHub repo from either founder. The "12x faster client work" claim is internal and uncountersigned. If a single named biotech with a cleared 510(k) submission appeared on the homepage, the score moves materially -- absent that, the artifact is founder testimony.

Single-anchor parallel to HandUp (~2014) on the founder-surfaced axis: I overrode my own commercial-defensibility heuristic for HandUp because the founder's biography mirrored a lived problem I knew personally. Haris's MBBS is real medical training and Anterior gave both founders production exposure to healthcare AI workflows, but neither founder has been a 510(k) submitter, an FDA reviewer, or a regulatory affairs lead at a medtech that has cleared product. The lived contact is one ring out from the problem -- doctor-adjacent and AI-platform-adjacent, not regulatory-affairs autobiographical. That is enough to take a first call; it is not enough to override a crowded lane.

Three-year forecast for Panacea: alive, somewhere between $2M and $6M ARR as a boutique AI-augmented regulatory services shop serving early-stage medtech 510(k) filings, out-positioned in large-pharma by Weave Bio + Parexel, and most likely acqui-hired into a mid-tier CRO -- ProPharma or an RQM+ tuck-in -- rather than scaling into a venture outcome. The mechanism is straightforward: services revenue compounds linearly with consultant headcount, the AI tooling gets commoditized as Complizen and Innolitics ship parity features, and the lack of a tactile artifact or named ex-FDA bench caps enterprise sales velocity.

The strongest bull case I owe Panacea is that Weave Bio's Parexel partnership is actually a *gift* -- it concedes the large-pharma top of the market to an enterprise channel and leaves the fast-moving Spring 2026 medtech and seed-stage biotech segment wide open for a founder-led shop with fixed milestone pricing, and Haris's MBBS gives him social license to sit across from a medical device CEO that a generic regulatory-tech founder lacks. The Mindbloom precedent says regulated categories with founder authenticity can compound past skeptics. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway: the founder origin is analytical -- a doctor and an AI engineer who *identified* the FDA opportunity from Anterior, not a former FDA reviewer or a medtech founder who *lived* a failed submission. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a named ex-FDA reviewer on the team page with a cleared-submission count attached to their name, plus one disclosed medtech client with a 510(k) actually filed by Panacea -- with that, this becomes a "watch, then write" company instead of a respectful neutral.
