# Sparta -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I look for in any founder is the story that made this problem theirs — not a market they analyzed, but a wound that won't heal until they fix it. When I read Sparta's dossier, I find three UC Berkeley students with solid credentials, and I find Arya Kanna runs a Substack newsletter analyzing startup feasibility. That sentence tells me everything. Arya dropped out of Berkeley to build Sparta, which takes real nerve, but the founding origin reads like a well-executed market scan: data transfers are slow, AI infrastructure is booming, reinforcement learning can optimize things. That's how consultants find opportunities, not how founders get forged. Lalith Posam's resume — Pinterest Observability, Google YouTube ML, Fireworks AI, a PAKDD 2024 publication on time-series differential equations with Christos Faloutsos — demonstrates genuine technical depth. He's touched the machinery. But touching the machinery at an internship is not the same as being kept awake at night by the problem of wasted GPU cycles. Nobody on this team has a biographical reason they must be the ones to crack adaptive data transfer. If they pivoted to a different infrastructure optimization problem tomorrow, nothing in their stories would resist that turn.

Sparta operates in AI infrastructure — and right now, that's the warmest room in the building. Eighty-four billion dollars flowed into AI infrastructure mega-rounds in 2025. Every venture firm in San Francisco would take a meeting with a team building "Intelligent Data Delivery for High-Throughput AI." When I ask whether this idea makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs, the answer is the opposite: it makes them nod and reach for their checkbooks. That's not my kind of signal. My best investments — SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive, Contraline when male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable — came from spaces that generated rejection, not enthusiasm. Sparta's space generates consensus excitement, and consensus excitement at pre-seed is how you end up competing against every well-funded team in the a16z and YC ecosystems for the same customers.

I spent years building IronPort's email security technology before Cisco bought us for $830 million. That experience taught me what real infrastructure defensibility looks like: every customer made our spam filtering smarter, every enterprise deployment increased switching costs because ripping out your email security layer is terrifying. Sparta's drop-in CLI architecture — prefix your existing command with `sparta` and you're done — is brilliant for adoption but devastating for defensibility. What's one line to add is one line to remove. When AWS decides to ship adaptive transfer optimization as a native feature of DataSync, Sparta's customers can switch back before lunch. The dossier explicitly flags this: "the drop-in architecture minimizes switching costs both in and out." The potential data flywheel — Sparta's RL engine improving as it sees more network topologies — is real in theory but unproven at this stage, and Sedai already raised $20 million doing RL-based cloud optimization with a broader scope. The 45–70% throughput improvement claim has no public benchmarks or third-party validation backing it.

The strongest case for Sparta is timing and technical legitimacy at an infrastructure inflection. GPU clusters are scaling to thousands of nodes, data movement genuinely is a bottleneck, and static transfer settings genuinely do fail under fluctuating cross-cloud conditions. Lalith's publication record and internship trajectory demonstrate he can build real ML systems, not wrappers. If Sparta's RL engine delivers what it claims and accumulates a data moat that generalizes across heterogeneous network topologies faster than any competitor can train one, the company could become essential plumbing for AI infrastructure — the way Cloudflare became essential plumbing for the web. IBM Aspera is a sleeping incumbent buried inside a conglomerate that hasn't prioritized it in a decade. Signiant's $42.5 million in 2024 revenue proves enterprises pay for this category. The market exists, the timing is right, and the technical approach is defensible in principle. For this bull case to materialize, though, I'd need to see Lalith's RL models demonstrably outperform fixed heuristics across diverse production environments — and I'd need to see a distribution strategy that creates lock-in beyond CLI convenience.

What keeps me on the sideline is the absence of every signal I rely on most heavily. There is no invisible system being made visible here — IBM Aspera is expensive and proprietary, but infrastructure engineers choosing between Aspera, Signiant, and MASV are making informed vendor decisions, not being exploited by information asymmetry the way taxi medallion cartels exploited riders or credit card companies exploit borrowers. There is no biographical crucible connecting these founders to this problem. There is no contrarian discomfort — this is consensus AI infrastructure. The two dimensions that drive my strongest convictions — the founder's forge and the hidden system disrupted — both score below baseline. Sparta is a competent team with a technically sound approach in a well-timed market. For many investors, that's enough. For me, it's missing the elements that separate the bets I remember from the bets I forget.
