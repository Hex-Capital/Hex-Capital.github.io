# Dispatch -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Seven funded startups are building reentry vehicles. Varda has flown five missions and raised $329 million. Inversion has $125 million and a DoD contract. And Dispatch — two engineers, YC-stage capital, a heat shield tested in the Mojave — claims their approach is 100x cheaper. This is either the most interesting company in the batch or the most delusional. I've seen both movies. The question I always ask in a crowded deep-tech race is not "who's ahead?" but "does someone have a fundamentally different cost structure?" When Peter Beck started Rocket Lab, SpaceX had already reached orbit. The consensus was that the launch market was consolidating around one dominant player. But Beck had a genuinely novel engine architecture — electric turbopumps, 3D-printed chambers — that created a different cost curve for a different market segment. That's what I need to see from Dispatch: not a cheaper version of what Varda built, but a step-function cost discontinuity that opens a market Varda's economics can't serve.

The consequence question here is real but conditional. If in-space manufacturing becomes a major industry — semiconductors, pharmaceutical crystals, exotic fiber optics that literally cannot be produced in gravity — then the reentry logistics provider is critical infrastructure. The company that makes it cheap and frequent to bring finished goods back from orbit is the railroad of a new industrial revolution. That's a world-changing outcome. But here's my hesitation: the consequence depends entirely on whether in-space manufacturing demand materializes at scale. Rocket Lab's consequence was clear because thousands of small satellite operators already existed and needed affordable launch. The demand for reentry services depends on a market that is still mostly theoretical — Varda has proven it's technically possible, but we don't yet know if the addressable set of materials is worth billions or hundreds of millions. This is a bet on a bet, and I'm comfortable with that structure, but I need the inner bet (ISM demand) to be plausible at transformative scale. The TAM estimates range from $5 billion to $23 billion by 2032-2035 — a 5x spread that tells you nobody actually knows.

The 100x cheaper heat shield is the single fact that would make or break my interest. If it's real and it scales, Dispatch becomes the low-cost utility that every in-space manufacturer uses — the "picks and shovels" play at a price point that creates a market rather than competing for one. Heat shield fabrication "in an apartment" has the scrappy, first-principles feel of somebody who found a genuine insight. They've done a full-scale ground test, which is more than most pre-seed space companies can say. But ground tests with rocket exhaust are not orbital reentry. The thermal, aerodynamic, and structural loads of actual Mach 20+ atmospheric entry are a different beast — Inversion's first orbital attempt failed for reasons their ground testing didn't catch. The 100x claim is unverified by anyone outside the company. I've learned the hard way — KiOR had technically impressive lab results that never translated to production economics. An unverified 100x claim from a two-person pre-seed company requires extraordinary evidence that I don't yet have.

The founders are competent aerospace engineers from Astranis — Case in the CTO office overseeing spacecraft architecture, Mello leading avionics. They cover the core disciplines for reentry: thermal protection and guidance/navigation/control. This is domain expertise, not outsider naivety. I've said repeatedly that I prefer better thinking over domain knowledge, but I recognize the exception pattern: in orbital mechanics, physics doesn't care about your first-principles reasoning if you haven't done the math. The OpenAI exception applies here to some degree — deep technical competence matters when the physics is unforgiving. What I don't see is evidence of learning velocity or intellectual surprise. No public writing, no evidence of pivots, no signal about what they discovered that changed their thinking during YC. The hardware test is a positive data point — building and testing a full-scale heat shield at this funding level shows velocity — but I can't evaluate their rate of learning from a single demo.

Let me articulate the bull case honestly: if the 100x cost reduction holds at production scale, and if in-space manufacturing demand follows the trajectory of small satellite demand (explosive growth once access costs drop), Dispatch could become the defining infrastructure layer for a new industrial category. The "picks and shovels" model — serving all ISM companies rather than competing as a vertically integrated manufacturer — has the right structural shape for a platform monopoly. Varda's vertical integration is their strength today but could be their constraint tomorrow, the same way that integrated satellite manufacturers couldn't compete with the launch-as-a-service model. And the refurbishability angle, if demonstrated, compounds the cost advantage with each mission. In that scenario, my $500K option becomes worth billions. That's the shape of bet I'm drawn to.

But I can't get there today. The combination of an unverified core claim, a crowded competitive field with massively better-funded rivals, an enormous capital gap to first flight ($50-100M based on competitor spending), and no evidence of the outsider thinking or learning velocity that distinguishes my best bets — it adds up to a company where I'd want to see more before committing. This isn't a pass on the vision. Manufacturing in space is a problem worth working on. But right now, Dispatch is one of eight companies chasing the same opportunity, with less capital than any serious competitor and a headline claim I can't validate. I'll watch for the orbital test. If they actually reenter a vehicle for 100x less than Varda's cost, I'll be the first call they get.
