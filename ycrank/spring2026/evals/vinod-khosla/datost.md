# Datost -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most analytically telling fact about Datost is that nobody would call this idea crazy. An AI data analyst in Slack -- it's sensible, it's obvious, it's the kind of thing a dozen teams are building right now. Scoop Analytics raised $3.5M a year and a half ago on essentially the same thesis. Julius AI has two million users doing conversational analytics on the web. Zenlytic, Chata.ai, Narrator -- the landscape is populated and funded. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's CTO publicly declared they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Pat Brown, the food industry thought plant-based meat was a joke. The best signal I know is experts dismissing you. The worst signal is experts nodding along. Nobody is dismissing the idea that LLMs can help with analytics queries. This is consensus dressed up as innovation.

Let me start where I always start: if Datost succeeds completely -- dominates the Slack-native analytics category -- what changes? Companies get query results faster without leaving their chat window. Data teams handle fewer ad-hoc requests. The BI market shifts slightly toward conversational interfaces. That's a useful business. It's not a transformation of anything. Animal agriculture still stands. Energy grids still burn fossil fuels. Healthcare still kills people with diagnostic errors. Datost, at maximum success, makes an existing process 30% more convenient. I've spent forty years telling founders: you're too talented to spend your career on a problem this small. The consequence of success here is a mid-sized SaaS company in a crowded market -- not the obsolescence of an incumbent system.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If agentic AI becomes reliable enough that a Slack bot replaces not just ad-hoc queries but entire data teams -- if it evolves from "answer my question" to "autonomously identify what I should be asking" -- then the form factor advantage of living inside the collaboration layer could be profound. Square started as a simple card reader and restructured small business commerce. Could Datost's Slack-native position similarly restructure organizational data access? The difference is structural: Square created a new economic capability -- merchants who couldn't accept cards suddenly could. Datost accelerates an existing capability. The data team already answers these questions; Datost makes the answer arrive faster. Speed improvements don't create the kind of step-function disruption that builds category-defining companies. And the commoditization risk is severe -- the dossier itself notes that "any team with LLM API access and Slack API knowledge could build a basic version." When your technical moat is described that way in a research memo, you're in trouble.

The founders are young engineers -- Maceo is 21, Jason has relevant experience on Confluent's Data Governance team -- and they've worked together at Traba, which is a real signal of co-founder trust. But I see no evidence of the contrarian courage that marks the founders I back. They're not attacking something others declared impossible. They're not outsiders bringing a foreign discipline to an entrenched industry. They're software engineers building a software tool in a category that already has well-funded competitors. The rate of learning is impossible to assess from the dossier, which is fair at pre-seed. But the choice of problem itself tells me something about ambition calibration: when you pick a problem that five funded competitors are already working on, you're optimizing for probability of building something rather than consequence of building something transformational. That's the trade-off I reject.

The timing dimension offers the only genuine positive signal: LLM capabilities for multi-step reasoning and code execution are on a steep improvement curve, and the "agentic AI" paradigm is real and accelerating. But this is consensus timing. Every VC deck in 2026 has an "agentic AI" slide. Being positioned on an obvious curve isn't the same as skating to where the puck will be -- it's skating to where everyone already sees the puck going. My best timing bets were in cleantech in 2004, food tech in 2010, AI in 2019 -- years before the wave was consensus. Datost is arriving as the wave crests, not before.
