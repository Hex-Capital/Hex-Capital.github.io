# Revion -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Revion is that it's entering a space where the consensus has already formed. Toma raised $17 million from a16z. STELLA has $34 million and a strategic partnership with Reynolds and Reynolds. Mia Labs just closed a $20 million Series A. Tekion, with $665 million raised and a $4 billion valuation, has announced its own agentic AI for automotive service. When I see a category this crowded with well-capitalized players and marquee investors, I know the weirdness has been competed away. The most promising companies are not popular -- and AI voice tools for auto dealerships are extremely popular right now. Revion's technician in-bay angle is genuinely differentiated from the phone-answering competitors, but a differentiated product feature inside a consensus category is not the same thing as a non-consensus company. A feature can be replicated by any of these better-funded players with a single product sprint.

I don't know Zaki or Hanbo's personal stories, and that's the biggest gap for me. What I can see is their credentials -- LSE first-class honours, Morgan Stanley, Citi, Multiverse on the CEO side; Imperial College on scholarship, Goldman Sachs derivatives, IBM Research on the CTO side. These are impressive, polished establishment profiles. But I always want to know: how did you get here? Not the business -- how did YOU arrive at wanting to build voice tools for automotive technicians? Nothing in the available information connects either founder biographically to the automotive service world. No family dealership, no time wrenching on cars, no personal encounter with the frustration of handwriting repair orders in a noisy shop bay. When I backed the Crusoe team, the story of how they independently arrived at mining Bitcoin on stranded gas was inseparable from who they were. When I invested in Contraline, the founder had spent years in the academic research that nobody else cared about. Here, I see smart people who identified a market opportunity -- and that's fine, but it doesn't give me the conviction signal I need to write a check from my own money.

The bull case is real and I want to take it seriously. The technician in-bay workflow is a genuinely underserved use case. Everybody else is building AI to answer customer phone calls; Revion is building AI for the person standing under a car with greasy hands who needs to dictate a repair order. That's a meaningful insight, and the traction signals -- 30,000+ billable hours recovered annually at Ewing Automotive Group, partnerships with $500M+ and $700M+ revenue dealership groups, a testimonial from a former Ford UK Chairman -- suggest real product-market pull. The 9 named DMS/CRM integrations create switching costs once embedded. If Revion can own the technician workflow layer and expand from there, they could build a wedge that's hard for the phone-call competitors to replicate because the user, the context, and the data model are fundamentally different. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that the in-bay technician voice interface becomes a new system of record that the DMS incumbents can't easily absorb -- and that this 4-person team can win that race before Tekion ships its own version.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. A team of four trying to maintain two distinct product lines -- technician workflow tools AND dealer communication AI agents -- in a market where their nearest competitors have 5x to 150x their funding is a resource allocation bet I find hard to get comfortable with. The dealer communication product competes head-to-head with Toma, STELLA, Mia, and Flai. The technician product is where the differentiation lives, but splitting focus means neither gets the full attention of an already-tiny team. And the DMS integration dependency cuts both ways -- CDK Global manages $540 billion in auto commerce through its systems and could restrict API access or build competing features at any time. This isn't the kind of regulatory or structural complexity that creates a moat. It's execution complexity that creates fragility.

I don't feel the jolt here. This is a competent team building a smart product in a hot market, and that description is precisely what I try to avoid. The company doesn't create meaningful economic access for underserved people -- it helps dealership service departments bill more efficiently, which is valuable but not the kind of impact that gets me out of bed. The technician time savings are real, but a 50% reduction in BDC staffing is job elimination, not job creation. Nothing about this is stigmatized, weird, or dismissed by other investors -- quite the opposite. I'd need to hear Zaki and Hanbo's personal stories to see if there's something beneath the surface that the dossier can't capture, but based on what I can evaluate today, this doesn't match how I invest.
