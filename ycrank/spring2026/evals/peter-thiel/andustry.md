# Andustry -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Xometry trades at a $2.5 billion market cap on $687 million in revenue. Fictiv sold to MISUMI for $350 million. Laserhub raised a double-digit-million Series B. Leverage AI, Zip, and Fairmarkit all pursue AI-driven procurement. Andustry enters this landscape — a two-person pre-seed team building an AI sourcing marketplace for industrial parts — and the first question my framework demands is not whether the product works but whether the market structure permits monopoly. The dossier answers this question before I finish asking it: the competitive landscape slide practically writes itself. When I can name five funded competitors and a public incumbent attacking the same value chain, there is no secret. "AI can automate industrial procurement" is not a contrarian truth that makes knowledgeable people uncomfortable — it is the consensus view of every B2B SaaS investor who read about GPT-4 function calling in 2024. Andustry's differentiator — being a sourcing marketplace rather than a manufacturer-of-record like Xometry — is a positioning choice, not a secret. Positioning choices are imitable; secrets are not.

Johann Stürken's background deserves serious consideration before I dismiss Andustry entirely. A mechanical engineering degree from RWTH Aachen, stints at McKinsey and Airbus, and a family background in trading goods and supplier sourcing — this is genuine domain knowledge, not tourist-level interest. Het Dave provides the technical engineering capability from IIT Jodhpur. The bull case writes itself: Stürken understands the procurement pain viscerally, the channel conflict with Xometry (whose vertically integrated model makes pure sourcing cannibalistic to their core revenue) creates a structural opening, and €1M+ in beta transaction volume from a two-person team demonstrates real execution. The innovator's dilemma argument is legitimate — Xometry cannot easily offer a pure marketplace without undermining its manufacturing margin. If Andustry achieves liquidity in European SME procurement before Xometry expands meaningfully into that geography, the network effects could compound. This is the best version of the story, and it is not crazy. But it is not enough.

Andustry's 500k+ verified companies across 100+ countries is either the most remarkable bootstrapping achievement at YC this batch or a metric that obscures reality. Xometry acquired Thomasnet for $300 million specifically to access a comparable supplier database. The question is not how many companies are listed but how many actively respond to RFQs — the difference between a directory and a marketplace. The dossier flags this directly: "supplier engagement depth unverified." A two-sided marketplace where one side is passive is not a marketplace at all; it is a database with a search interface. Andustry's claimed network could be aggregated from public directories, trade databases, and web scraping — none of which constitutes proprietary data that strengthens with use. Compare this to what I look for: Palantir's data integration creates switching costs that compound annually because the software becomes operationally embedded in intelligence workflows. Andustry's switching costs are, by the dossier's own assessment, "minimal" at pre-seed stage, and the structural path to making them non-minimal is unclear given that industrial procurement relationships are inherently multi-vendor and price-sensitive.

Andustry's power-law ceiling concerns me as much as its monopoly deficit. The global industrial machinery market may be $743 billion, but industrial procurement marketplaces are not winner-take-all categories. They fragment by geography, product category, and customer segment. Xometry's $687 million revenue after years of operation and a $300 million acquisition represents perhaps 0.1% market penetration — this is a market that resists concentration structurally. The realistic best case for Andustry, even with perfect execution, looks more like a Fictiv ($350 million acquisition) or a Laserhub (mid-stage European niche player) than a SpaceX or Palantir. A $500 million exit would be a success for the founders but structurally irrelevant to fund returns. The power law demands that I ask whether this single company could return the entire fund — and in a fragmented, competitive, low-margin industrial category, the honest answer is no.

Andustry's distribution model — "Book a call" on the website, sales-led GTM targeting European SME procurement teams — is the standard playbook for B2B tools at this stage, neither inspired nor broken. The €1M+ beta transaction volume suggests some organic pull, which is a real signal. But I see no evidence of the kind of distribution insight that creates monopoly on its own — no viral embedding mechanism, no platform lock-in, no organic adoption curve where buyers pull the product into workflows unprompted. The pay-on-delivery, zero-upfront-fee model eliminates friction but also eliminates commitment; buyers can use Andustry alongside every other sourcing channel without cost, which means Andustry must win every transaction on price and speed rather than on structural lock-in. This is a pure software layer — no hardware, no physical-world technology barrier, no regulatory moat — built on foundation models that every competitor accesses from the same providers. The dossier itself acknowledges the core AI-matching technology is "technically reproducible." Andustry is the paradigmatic example of what I warned about: wrapping someone else's model in a domain-specific interface and calling it a moat.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: if Andustry demonstrated that its AI negotiation engine consistently achieves 15-20% cost savings over direct supplier relationships for a defined SME cohort, with supplier retention rates above 80% quarter-over-quarter, that would constitute evidence of a genuine data flywheel — the AI improving with each transaction in ways competitors cannot replicate without equivalent transaction volume. That would transform Andustry from a sourcing tool into a pricing oracle, which is a different and potentially monopolizable business. Without that evidence, Andustry remains what the Wish investment taught me to avoid: a marketplace in a massive market with no structural path to dominance, where the founders' determination and the category's size create the illusion of opportunity while competition destroys the economics.
