# Drafted -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Nick Donahue is running at the same wall that already killed his last company, and that is the most interesting thing about Drafted. He ran Atmos (YC S20) for five years, raised ~$20M from Khosla and Sam Altman, designed $200M of custom homes, built 50 of them, then watched the Fed jack rates and the whole thing collapse into a shutdown in March 2025. Most founders would route around residential design forever after that. Donahue came back to the same problem with a different wedge -- a specialized model trained on permitted plans, running at 0.2¢ per generation against 13¢ for general-purpose AI -- and he did it inside twelve months. That's the **Kiko** (2005) → **Twitch** (2007) shape: prior YC failure as a compounding signal, not a disqualifier. The hitch is that Drafted is listed with one named founder on the YC page, and that breaks a pattern I've held since "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010).

Brian Armstrong's hand-built Android wallet, post-rejection, is the canonical animal moment in my head, and Drafted lives between that rung and the **Airbnb** (2009) cereal-box rung on resourcefulness. Donahue did not need to ship a stunt prototype to overturn a rejection -- the relevant artifact is the entire arc of Atmos: $7M revenue, 40 employees, 50 homes built, then the courage to stand in the rubble and say the software-services hybrid was the wrong architecture and a pure-model approach is the right one. The dossier evidence that pins Drafted closer to the Armstrong rung than the Chesky rung is concrete: 120k users in a month, 325k+ floor plans generated, and a 65x inference-cost advantage shipped before the Spring 2026 batch even started. The reason I don't push it all the way to the **Airbnb** (2009) rung is that I don't see the equivalent of the cereal boxes -- a stunt that required no money and revealed a kind of force the resume cannot. What I see instead is a man who has been hitting the same nail with different hammers for six years.

The MBA-deck pass case I keep in my head -- "AI wrapper, $50B TAM slide, we'll hire engineers post-funding" -- fails the organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012), and Drafted is the antipode of that failure mode. Donahue grew up in a homebuilding family. He then spent five years inside the operational ugliness of custom residential design and discovered, in his own words via TechCrunch, that Atmos was "a glamorized architecture firm" because the software "never quite replaced the humans." Drafted exists because the founder personally lived the exact failure mode the product now attacks. That is the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick origin, transposed into a sector where almost nobody in any YC batch has the lived experience to even pose the problem correctly. Drafted scores near the top of this dimension and it is not close.

Schlep blindness is where I get more cautious about Drafted. **Coinbase** (2012) was beautiful because the moat was the compliance grind nobody else wanted; **Stripe** (2010) was beautiful because the moat was the bank-integration grind nobody else wanted. Drafted's schlep is real but narrower -- assembling and training on a corpus of *permitted* house plans, and getting structured CAD output to a fidelity that a structural engineer will actually accept. That is unpleasant in the way data work is unpleasant, and competitors like Maket.ai (in market since 2019, $2.47M raised) have apparently not done it as well. But the front door says "Free AI House Plans with PDF & CAD Downloads," which is a consumer self-serve posture, not a regulatory grind. The schlep is in the basement, not at the front door, and that is a weaker version of the Coinbase pattern.

Hacker genealogy for Drafted compounds badly over the next 18 months if I read the dossier strictly. Donahue went to NC State, has no GitHub footprint surfaced, and no competitive-programming or open-source lineage of the kind that made me overweight Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010). Somebody at Drafted trained a multimodal model with a 65x cost-per-generation advantage over general-purpose models -- that is non-trivial ML work and it implies a real technical core somewhere on a nine-person team -- but the dossier does not name that person, and hackers leave tracks. I dock this dimension and would un-dock it the moment a named ML cofounder surfaces. Patrick Collison being on the cap table is, in my own private accounting, partial compensation: he does not write checks into teams without technical taste.

Solo founder is the dimension where Drafted scores worst, against the **Reddit** (2005) Huffman/Ohanian dorm-roommate pattern and the Collison-brothers structure of **Stripe** (2010). The mitigant in the dossier is that four of the early Drafted employees followed Donahue over from Atmos, which means the *team* has multi-year shared scar tissue even if the cap table says one founder. That is closer to a de facto founding group than to a true solo bet. It does not erase the red flag, but it bends it.

In 36 months Drafted is at $5–15M ARR on a freemium-to-paid-plan-export model, has been bought by Autodesk in the $40–100M range to plug the consumer-DIY hole in their architect-licensed seat business, OR has been forced into a B2B pivot selling the inference engine to production homebuilders when consumer demand again collapses against mortgage rates -- the exact macro mechanism that ate Atmos. The dimensional signature -- highest on organic founder pain, lowest on cofounder configuration -- produces a founder who can ship the right product but may not have the second brain in the room to refuse the bad acquisition offer when it comes.

The bull case that stings: Patrick Collison wrote a personal check into Drafted. So did Ben Silbermann, Jack Altman, Alex Blania. That is not a generic angel list -- those are operators who have built model-and-data businesses and they are looking at the same dossier I am and saying yes. The **Stripe** (2010) Known Exception applies cleanly here: I bent my "complete the YC process" rule for the Collisons because the prior-startup outcome was load-bearing evidence, and the same logic argues Drafted should get credit for Atmos as a $200M-designed-homes proof rather than a $20M-burned cautionary tale. The dossier evidence that survives the bull case is the macro-rate exposure: Drafted's end customer is the same homeowner who could not close at Atmos, and no model improvement fixes a 7% mortgage. The condition that flips my verdict is a named technical cofounder or first ML hire with a shipped open-source artifact in generative modeling -- if that surfaces, Drafted moves from a thin Invest into something I would lead.
