# Sellara -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Sellara's most striking feature is one that would impress Paul Graham but leaves my framework cold: two founders who spent years on Citi's institutional trading desks, with Charles-André Jolly personally covering 180 institutional clients across Macro Credit Derivatives and IG Credit, and Spencer Secord building workflow automations integrated into core risk systems across six years on Cross Asset and Fixed Income desks. These are founders who have *lived* the problem. They know the exact workflows, the exact buyers, and the exact pain. Graham would score this founder-market fit highly. But my framework asks a prior question: does the *idea* create a structural path to monopoly? A brilliant, determined founder pursuing a competitive market is still a loser — all failed companies are the same in that they failed to escape competition.

Where is Sellara's secret? "Financial institutions rely on manual processes that AI can automate" is not a truth that few people agree with — it is the thesis of every bank innovation lab, every consulting firm's digital transformation practice, and every BFSI-focused AI startup founded since GPT-4 shipped. Beam AI already generates approximately $4.5 million in revenue doing adjacent work, was selected for Accenture's 2026 FinTech Innovation Lab, and employs 57 people. Blueflame AI raised a $5 million Series A for AI in investment workflows. Derivative Path and SigTech operate in overlapping territory. FIS, Bloomberg, and Broadridge will inevitably build AI workflow capabilities into their existing platform monopolies. When I see a competitive landscape with five named competitors and three incumbents, I am looking at a market where there is no secret — just a consensus thesis being pursued simultaneously by multiple funded teams. This is the cleantech pattern recurring in fintech clothing: entrepreneurs swept up by the "agentic AI" trend rather than by a specific, non-obvious insight about institutional trading operations.

Sellara claims its AI "outperforms leading LLMs by 60% and runs 30x faster" — but this claim has no published benchmark, no third-party validation, and no articulated mechanism. The distinction between proprietary model training on financial data (potentially defensible) and prompt engineering on commodity models (trivially replicable) is the entire difference between a technology moat and a consulting engagement. The dossier cannot determine which Sellara has built, and the company does not make the distinction publicly. General-purpose agent frameworks like LangChain and CrewAI lower the barrier to building workflow agents every quarter. Without a verifiable technical discontinuity — something that makes the old approach structurally obsolete rather than incrementally slower — Sellara is a software layer without atoms leverage, competing on execution speed in a market where execution speed is the only variable anyone competes on.

The Palantir comparison is instructive precisely because it reveals what Sellara lacks. Palantir's secret was not "intelligence agencies need better software" — that was obvious. The secret was that compartmentalized access controls and ontological data integration could be built into a single platform that agencies would embed so deeply into their operational workflows that switching costs would approach infinity. The government-as-customer pathway created monopoly through structural dependency, not product superiority. Sellara sells to private financial institutions — buyers who maintain vendor diversification as explicit risk management policy, who run competitive RFPs quarterly, and who will never allow a four-person startup to become a single point of failure in their trading operations. The switching costs, even if Sellara's agents integrate into Bloomberg and Outlook workflows, are modest compared to government embedding. A bank can rip out a workflow automation tool in a quarter; the CIA cannot rip out Palantir in a decade.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Jolly's 180-client Rolodex converts into rapid enterprise sales despite 6-12 month compliance cycles in regulated finance; that Sellara's claimed performance advantage is real and durable against both improving foundation models and competitors like Beam AI; and that integration-based switching costs in trading operations compound faster than competitors can replicate the integrations. The SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed stage are genuinely unusual and signal that the founders understand their buyers' procurement requirements — this is the kind of distribution sophistication I look for. If Sellara could land three top-20 banks in its first eighteen months, the integration depth plus compliance barrier might create enough lock-in to carve out a defensible position. But a defensible position in a fragmented market is a "nice $200 million business," not a monopoly. The power law demands that investments either produce extreme outcomes or do not matter, and Sellara's market structure — multiple competitors, vendor-diversifying buyers, incumbent platforms with distribution advantages — caps the realistic upside well below fund-returner territory.

I pass. The decisive dimensions are Monopoly Secret and Power Law Magnitude. Sellara enters a consensus market ("AI automates financial workflows") with at least five identifiable competitors and three platform incumbents, violating the foundational principle that every great company is built around a secret hidden from the outside. Jolly and Secord's distribution credentials are real — the strongest element of this company by a considerable margin — but superior distribution without a monopoly-creating insight produces a well-sold commodity, not a monopoly. The absence of any atoms-level technology moat or government-customer pathway eliminates the two mechanisms through which I have seen competitive markets produce monopoly outcomes despite initial fragmentation.
