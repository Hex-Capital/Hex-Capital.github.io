# Traverse -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Traverse is the gap between the problem they describe and the company they're building. The problem -- that most economically valuable human work involves ambiguity, subjective judgment, and taste -- is genuinely massive. If you could teach AI to reason about medicine, law, and business strategy the way RL taught models to reason about code, you'd be reshaping trillion-dollar systems. But Traverse isn't building that AI. They're selling training environments to the handful of companies that might. They've identified a civilization-scale problem and chosen to be a supplier to someone else's attempt at solving it. That's a fundamentally different bet, and it changes the entire consequence-of-success calculation.

If Traverse succeeds maximally -- captures the non-deterministic RL environments market, becomes the default vendor for every frontier lab -- the world gets marginally better AI models, and Traverse becomes a well-run B2B data company with a concentrated customer base. That's not inconsequential, but it's not transformational either. The value creation happens downstream, in the labs that use the environments to train models that actually practice medicine or construct legal arguments. Traverse captures supplier economics, not paradigm-shift economics. I've seen this pattern repeatedly in infrastructure plays: the company that sells the components rarely captures the value of what gets built with them. Square didn't sell payment processing tools to banks -- it replaced the entire POS infrastructure. Juniper didn't sell router components to Cisco -- it made Cisco's architecture obsolete. Traverse is selling RL data to AI labs. The ambition is calibrated to the buyer's budget, not to the scale of the underlying problem.

The competitive landscape sharpens this concern. Zachary Yu worked at Mercor, which is now a $10 billion company that explicitly lists healthcare and law among its RL environment targets. Surge AI is doing $1.2 billion in revenue and building dedicated RL environment capabilities. Applied Compute raised $100 million with an ex-OpenAI founding team. Traverse has two founders, YC's standard check, and a claimed expertise moat in combining domain knowledge with ML understanding. But here's the problem with expertise moats: they erode the moment a well-funded competitor hires the same experts. When Mercor decides to seriously pursue non-deterministic domains -- and at $10 billion they have every incentive and resource to do so -- they can assemble larger domain expert teams in months. The structural barrier Traverse claims -- that crowd-sourced annotation doesn't work for subjective verification -- is interesting, but it's an observation about the problem, not a proprietary solution to it.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The timing is real: RL is the primary remaining scaling axis for frontier models, lab spending on environments is growing 3-5x annually, and coding-domain environments are maturing while non-deterministic domains remain greenfield. If the verification problem for subjective domains is genuinely as hard as Traverse claims -- if formalizing taste requires a fundamentally different technical approach that can't be replicated by throwing bodies and money at it -- then being first with a working methodology in medicine or law could create a durable position. Idler, a comparable YC company focused on coding, already closed multimillion-dollar lab contracts. The buyers are spending aggressively and need specialized providers. Zachary Yu's insider knowledge from Mercor could translate into understanding exactly where the incumbents' approaches break down. If these founders have genuinely cracked something about non-deterministic verification during their time at YC -- a methodology, a technical insight, a verification architecture -- that's potentially valuable. But there's no evidence of it in the dossier. No proprietary datasets, no patents, no technical artifacts, no customer signals. The core claim is "this combination of expertise is rare," which is a hiring thesis, not a technology thesis.

The pivot from Clice AI -- a communication tool for mortgage loan officers -- to RL environments during the YC batch tells me these founders learn fast and are willing to abandon bad ideas. That's a positive signal for learning rate. But the direction of the pivot concerns me: they moved from an obscure vertical SaaS play to the single hottest category in AI infrastructure. That's not contrarian courage -- that's pattern-matching to market consensus. The founders who excite me are skating to where the puck will be in three years, not where every AI infrastructure investor is looking right now. Neither founder brings deep domain expertise in medicine, law, or any non-deterministic field, which is supposed to be the company's core differentiator. Two CS engineers from Waterloo building RL environments for frontier labs is a reasonable startup, but it's not the kind of outsider-attacks-impossible-problem bet that defines my portfolio.
