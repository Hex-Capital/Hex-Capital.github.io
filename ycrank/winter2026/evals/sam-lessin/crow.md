# Crow -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Crow is building exactly what I've spent the last two years publicly arguing against investing in. An embeddable AI agent that connects to product APIs and lets users type natural language instead of clicking through menus -- this is the purest expression of "AI IS the product" I've seen in a YC batch. My test is simple: would this business exist if LLMs didn't? The answer is unambiguously no. There is no cherry on top here because there is no sundae underneath. The AI capability isn't accelerating an otherwise-good business; it constitutes the entire value proposition. When I say "software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore," I'm describing what happens to companies like Crow as the underlying AI infrastructure commoditizes.

The founder-idea pairing fails my primary filter in a specific way. Aryan and Jai are clearly sharp -- Berkeley EECS and CS, internships at Qualcomm, TikTok, Amazon, Tesla, experience at a prior YC company. They're "sane" in the generic sense. But the idea isn't "insane" -- it's the opposite. Making software controllable via chat is the most consensus idea in enterprise tech right now. Every YC batch has multiple companies in this exact space. The tags on their own YC page read like a parody of consensus: "Artificial Intelligence, Generative AI, Chatbot, Enterprise, AI Assistant." When Brian Armstrong pitched Coinbase, people thought he was crazy. Nobody thinks adding a chat interface to SaaS is crazy in 2026. Competent people pursuing an obvious idea is precisely the pairing that produces the worst risk-adjusted returns -- you're paying a premium for narrative that every competing team shares.

The competitive landscape here is the most instructive signal in the dossier, and it tells a cautionary story. Command AI raised $23.8 million, built a 39-person team, reached $3.3 million in revenue, and exited to Amplitude for roughly $45 million. That's a venture outcome that barely returns invested capital -- the Birchbox pattern of a company too alive to kill quickly but too fragile to compound. WalkMe sold to SAP for $1.5 billion but had raised $307 million and needed an acquirer's distribution to survive. Intercom built Fin AI internally to $100 million ARR. The signal is clear: incumbents with existing customer relationships and distribution are absorbing this capability, not buying startups to get it. And CopilotKit has 10,000+ GitHub stars as an open-source MIT-licensed alternative. Crow is entering a space where the outcome ceiling for independents appears to be "modest acquisition by a platform company," and even that ceiling required Command AI years and millions of dollars to reach.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I owe it genuine consideration. If Crow's managed proxy layer -- handling auth, rate limiting, schema validation, observability, guardrails -- becomes the standard infrastructure every SaaS company routes through, it could function like an early Twilio or Stripe: abstracting away a complex integration problem that every product team faces but none wants to build from scratch. The "deploy in under a week" claim, if real, represents meaningful time-to-value. And the MCP server integration could position Crow at an emerging protocol layer for AI-to-API communication. In this scenario, Crow captures a middleware position between LLM providers and product APIs, with switching costs accumulating as customers define workflows and guardrails. That would be a genuinely interesting primitive. But here's why I don't buy it: Stripe works because payment infrastructure involves banking relationships, regulatory compliance, and fraud detection that create structural barriers requiring years to replicate. Crow's middleware -- connecting an LLM to an OpenAPI spec with some guardrails -- doesn't have equivalent structural complexity. OpenAI and Anthropic already offer function-calling that does the hard part. The managed-service wrapper is useful but not defensible.

The founders met on the first day of college at Berkeley, which at least satisfies my preference for pre-existing co-founder relationships over accelerator speed-dating. And Aryan's time at Shasta Health (YC S23) gives him some exposure to building AI products within the YC ecosystem. But neither founder has built infrastructure at the scale required to convince me they'll outrun Intercom's Fin or Amplitude's Command AI integration. The "sane" credential here is early-career competence, not the kind of deep infrastructure experience -- like Anatoly Yakovenko's Qualcomm systems engineering background before Solana -- that makes you believe someone can execute against an architecturally ambitious vision.

This is a pass. The product sits at the intersection of three categories I explicitly avoid: AI-first companies where AI constitutes the moat, SaaS tools vulnerable to commoditization by cheaper software, and consensus opportunities where every VC and every founding team sees the same thing. The competitive dynamics tell me the best-case independent outcome is a modest acquisition, and the base case is getting squeezed between open-source alternatives and incumbents adding the capability as a feature. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech layer searching for structural defensibility.
