# Galactic Resource Utilization Space, Inc. (GRU Space) -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

A hotel on the Moon. When I first read that, something flickered — that same feeling I get when an idea is so far outside the Overton window that most investors won't even engage with it seriously. And that flicker matters, because the ideas I've made the most money on were the ones that sounded absurd in the first pitch meeting. SpaceX was my very first angel check, and people told me I was throwing money into a rocket-shaped furnace. So I'm not someone who dismisses "impossible" space companies reflexively. But the flicker isn't enough. I need to understand who's behind it and why *they* are the person standing in front of me with this particular dream.

Here's where I get stuck: I can't find Skyler Chan's biographical thread to the Moon. He's 22, graduated early from Berkeley EECS, became an Air Force pilot at 16, built a NASA 3D printer that actually flew to space, worked at Tesla. That's an extraordinary resume for someone his age — genuinely impressive. But when I ask my foundational question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I hear is a prodigy's highlight reel, not a survival story that explains why lunar habitation is the problem this founder *must* solve. The founders I back tend to carry something heavy — a personal experience of being excluded, of watching a system fail someone they love, of being told they were nothing and deciding to build something anyway. Maybe Skyler has that story and it just isn't in the public record. But right now, the connection between founder biography and company mission reads as ambition-driven rather than necessity-driven. Ambition is required but it's not the signal I weight most heavily at the earliest stages.

What genuinely impressed me is that this 22-year-old recruited Dr. Kevin Cannon away from a tenured academic position and a competing lunar ISRU startup. Cannon is one of maybe a dozen people on Earth who deeply understands lunar regolith composition and how to work with it. That's not a hire you make by sending a cold LinkedIn message — that requires real conviction and the ability to sell a vision to someone who knows exactly how hard the problem is. If Cannon left his professorship and his CTO role at Ethos Space to join GRU as employee number one, that tells me something about Chan's persuasive power that the resume alone doesn't capture. It's the most meaningful signal in this entire dossier.

The anti-consensus dimension here is off the charts, and I want to honor that. Nobody is funding commercial lunar surface habitation. The closest analog, ICON, has half a billion dollars and is focused on infrastructure like landing pads, not hotels. Axiom and Vast are building LEO stations. GRU occupies a niche that literally no funded competitor is pursuing. The timing catalyst is real — Trump and Isaacman pushing for a permanent Moon base by 2030, CLPS enabling commercial payload delivery. This is the kind of structural inflection point where I've historically made my best bets. But here's the tension: the *hotel* framing activates my "who does this create economic access for?" filter, and the answer is billionaires paying $10 million per stay. This isn't Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm making credit transparent. The infrastructure play underneath — being the construction company for all lunar surface development — is far more interesting to me, and it maps to my Anduril and Crusoe Energy pattern of backing infrastructure at technological inflection points. But that's the long-term vision, not the current company.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if the 2029 demo mission works — if they actually convert lunar regolith into construction material on the Moon's surface — they would possess a dataset no commercial entity on Earth has. That's a moat you can't replicate in a lab. The government contract potential for lunar base construction dwarfs the tourism revenue. And sometimes the founder who looks too young and too audacious is exactly the person who changes an industry because they haven't yet learned what's "impossible." I felt something similar with early SpaceX — the audacity was the point. But the difference that keeps me from writing a check is the capital gap and the founder's track record. When I backed Elon, he'd already built and sold PayPal. He was betting his personal fortune. The biographical stake was enormous. Skyler Chan has talent and hustle, but he hasn't yet proven he can survive the kind of multi-year crisis that lunar hardware development will inevitably create. The fact that Dr. Cannon is an employee rather than a co-founder also concerns me — for a decade-long journey through hardware hell, I want to see equity-aligned partnership, not an employment relationship. This isn't a co-founder conflict risk, it's the opposite problem: there's no one sharing the existential weight.

I'd want to meet Skyler and hear his real story — the parts that aren't on his LinkedIn. Maybe there's a biographical depth that would change my conviction. But based on what I can see today, the weirdness is real, the domain expertise hire is impressive, and the timing window is genuine. What's missing is the founder survival narrative that anchors my highest-conviction bets, any economic access dimension, and a realistic path from YC-level funding to the billions required for lunar construction. I respect the ambition enormously, and I wouldn't be surprised if this founder does something extraordinary — just not sure this is the vehicle, at this moment, with my money.
