# Asendia AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The pivot tells the story. Asendia started as a B2C interview coach for job seekers -- "Sarah, your AI Job Coach" -- launched it on Product Hunt twice, collected upvotes, and then reoriented entirely toward B2B staffing agency automation for YC. This is a team searching for a market, not a team that has been living inside a problem for years and finally sees the technology to solve it. When I evaluate founders, the first question is always: what do they know that can't be taught? The answer here is that Badis Zormati knows how to build AI interview tooling -- he shipped a working product -- and Rihab Lajmi knows enterprise cloud architecture from stints at Google and Microsoft. What neither of them knows is how a staffing agency actually operates. Neither has recruited at scale. Neither has run a desk, managed a candidate pipeline for a client, or experienced the specific friction of filling a light industrial role versus a software engineering role. A smart generalist with six months of immersion in staffing operations could identify every problem Asendia claims to solve. That's the specific knowledge test failing.

The leverage architecture concerns me more than the founder gap. Staffing agencies are relationship-driven businesses with highly customized workflows. The 50 ATS integrations Asendia advertises sound like a distribution hook until you realize each integration is maintenance overhead, not a network effect. Every agency needs its own configuration, its own compliance posture, its own candidate evaluation criteria. This is SaaS that scales linearly with customer onboarding effort -- not a platform where one engineer's work compounds across millions of users. Compare this to what I look for: Replit creates code leverage for anyone who touches a browser. Stack Overflow's knowledge graph got more valuable with every question asked. Asendia's AI agents get marginally better with more interview data, but the dossier itself concedes the moat "depends on execution speed, agency-specific workflow design, and ATS integration depth rather than proprietary AI." Execution speed is a temporary advantage. Workflow design is linear labor. Integration depth is a switching cost, which is real but modest.

The timing is the opposite of contrarian. AI agents for recruiting is perhaps the most consensus startup category in 2026. Paradox raised $250M+ before Workday absorbed it. hireEZ has $76M in funding and $42M in revenue. Humanly acquired three companies in 2025 alone. Every enterprise ATS vendor is building or buying AI screening capabilities. When I invested in Uber in 2010, ride-sharing was illegal. When I bought Bitcoin in 2014, serious people called it a Ponzi scheme. "AI agents for HR" is a PowerPoint slide that every YC applicant and every corporate strategy team has already seen. You don't get paid for being right about something everyone already agrees on -- you get paid for being right first, and this market has already been discovered.

The strongest bull case: Paradox's absorption into Workday creates a genuine gap for an independent, modern AI recruitment platform serving mid-market staffing agencies that Workday won't prioritize. The CTO has proven he can ship AI interview products. The multi-agent architecture -- sourcing plus outreach plus video plus phone screening -- is more comprehensive than single-function competitors. And staffing agencies have directly measurable ROI: faster fill rates equal more revenue. If Asendia moves fast, owns the mid-market staffing vertical, and builds genuine switching costs through deep ATS integration, they could become a solid $50-100M revenue business. But that's the ceiling of the bull case -- a good vertical SaaS company. It's not a new form of leverage. It's not a platform that creates a category of capability that didn't exist before. It's a better tool for an existing workflow in an existing industry, competing against well-funded incumbents with the same underlying AI capabilities available via the same foundation model APIs.

The compliance claims deserve a note. SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA certification for a three-person pre-seed team is either genuinely impressive or genuinely misleading -- either way it's unusual. If real, it shows operational maturity beyond the norm. If aspirational, it's exactly the kind of overclaiming that triggers my integrity filter. I'd want to verify before writing a check, but I won't score it as a red flag without evidence.

I'm passing. The founders are building a competent product in a real market, and I respect the hustle of pivoting to where the revenue is. But this is exactly the kind of company my framework is designed to filter out: founders with adjacent rather than deep domain knowledge, building an application layer on commodity AI in a consensus category, without a leverage mechanism that creates nonlinear returns. The technology doesn't compound in any distinctive way. The market positioning isn't contrarian. The specific knowledge bar hasn't been met. Nine out of ten investments going to zero is fine if the tenth goes 1,000x -- but this company's upside case is a successful vertical SaaS exit, not the kind of asymmetric outcome that justifies angel risk.
