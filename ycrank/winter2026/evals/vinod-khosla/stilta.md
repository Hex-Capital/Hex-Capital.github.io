# Stilta -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at Stilta is not the company — it's the crowd around it. Five competitors have raised over $100 million collectively in the last twelve months in AI patent tools. Solve Intelligence already has eight-figure ARR and 400+ customers. Patlytics has Quinn Emanuel and Google as clients. &AI — another YC company — raised $6.5 million from First Round doing essentially the same thing with the same "agentic" framing. When I backed Juniper to go after Cisco, Cisco's own CTO said they'd never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, the food industry thought plant-based heme was a joke. The signal I look for is experts dismissing the idea. Here, the experts are racing to fund it. When five well-capitalized companies simultaneously validate your approach, you're not building something disruptive — you're entering a feature war with a $500K budget against teams holding $20-55 million. That's not contrarian courage; that's showing up late to a consensus trade.

The consequence question kills this for me before I get to anything else. If Stilta succeeds completely — becomes the dominant agentic AI platform for patent practitioners — what changes? Patent attorneys review documents faster. They reduce manual work by 50%, according to Stilta's own framing. Law firms capture more margin per partner. That's a fine business outcome, but it's not the kind of result that justifies the effort I look for. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. The patent system continues operating on the same principles. Compare this to the problems I fund: eliminating animal agriculture, achieving nuclear fusion, building artificial general intelligence. A $1.4 billion market growing to $5.7 billion is real, but it's a professional workflow niche, not a trillion-dollar system being made obsolete. The company has framed its own ambition at the level of practitioner efficiency, not industry transformation.

The team composition reinforces my concern. Four McKinsey/QuantumBlack engineers — smart, capable, experienced in building exactly this kind of enterprise AI system. And that's the problem. This is the archetype of what I call industry insiders executing the industry's playbook: consultants who built AI systems for clients at McKinsey, now productizing the same approach. There's no outsider perspective here, no biochemist wandering into the food industry, no New Zealand engineer deciding to build rockets. There's also no patent attorney, no IP domain expert, no one from the legal world who understands the buyer's pain at a visceral level. The team is homogeneous in background and training. I don't see evidence of contrarian thinking — I see evidence of pattern recognition from consulting engagements, turned into a SaaS product. That's competent execution, not invention.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If agentic AI genuinely automates end-to-end patent search and analysis with full auditability, the second-order consequence could be significant: democratizing IP protection for small companies and individual inventors who currently can't afford $50,000 patent searches. That's a consequence worth caring about. The McKinsey network gives warm introductions into conservative enterprise buyers that pure technologists struggle to reach. Having paying customers — "leading firms in both Europe and the US" — at this stage is a real signal. And the underlying technology trajectory is undeniable: agentic AI capabilities are improving on an exponential curve, and Stilta is right that their approach wasn't possible twelve months ago. If they execute faster than competitors and build sticky enterprise relationships, switching costs in legal software are substantial. The timing window is real.

But the timing window is open for everyone simultaneously, and that's the fatal flaw. Stilta isn't skating to where the puck will be — they're skating to where five other teams are already standing with bigger sticks. The agentic architecture isn't proprietary. The patent databases are controlled by incumbents who could build competing AI layers. The differentiation reduces to execution speed and sales relationships, which means this is a market share fight, not a technology disruption. I've seen this pattern repeatedly: a crowded field of well-funded competitors building similar products for a defined market, competing on features and sales execution. The outcome, even in success, is a mid-scale B2B SaaS company — perhaps a $200-500 million outcome if everything goes right. That's the kind of bet where the probability of failure has been managed down, but the consequence of success has been managed down with it. These founders are too smart for a problem this crowded.
