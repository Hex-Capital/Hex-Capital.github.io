# Crosslayer Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The founding team here invented MPIC -- a security protocol that is now mandated by the CA/Browser Forum for every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate on earth. That's not an exaggeration. Henry Birge-Lee coded the first implementation in 2016, and by 2025, the standard they created secures over 700 million websites through Let's Encrypt, Google Trust Services, and Cloudflare. I have funded a lot of companies where the founders had domain expertise. I have almost never seen a case where the founders literally wrote the global standard governing their product's problem space. The organic origin here is undeniable -- Birge-Lee has been working on cross-layer Internet attacks for nearly a decade, Cimaszewski has published analyses of real-world attacks like the $2 million KLAYswap theft, and Mittal's research shaped Tor and Let's Encrypt. Nobody sat down and brainstormed "what's a good cybersecurity startup idea." The idea grew out of years of living inside these protocol layers and seeing attack patterns that almost no other team on earth could see.

The schlep here is real but unusual. With Stripe, the schlep was regulatory -- dealing with banks, compliance, fraud. Here the schlep is intellectual. Monitoring across BGP, DNS, TLS, and JavaScript simultaneously requires a kind of cross-layer systems knowledge that barely exists outside a handful of academic groups. Most security companies unconsciously avoid building this because they'd have to hire four different teams of specialists and somehow get them to build a unified correlation engine. That's why the existing landscape is fragmented -- Kentik does BGP, crt.sh does certificate transparency, separate tools check JavaScript integrity -- but nobody stitches it all together. The same dynamic I saw with payments before Stripe: everybody knew the problem existed, but the work required to solve it properly kept everyone away. The difference is that Stripe's schlep was organizational and regulatory, while this one is epistemic. You can't even build this product unless you understand how attacks propagate across protocol boundaries, and that knowledge lives in a very small number of heads worldwide.

What concerns me is the gap between research excellence and commercial reality. This team has 15+ publications at USENIX and ACM CCS. Mittal holds the ACM Hopper Award. They won the 2025 IRTF Applied Networking Research Prize. These are extraordinary academic credentials. But there is zero evidence that anyone has pulled the commercial product toward them. No revenue signal, no user counts, no waitlist, no organic adoption. Their company GitHub has one repo with zero stars. The Open MPIC Project -- the open-source predecessor -- has 16 stars on its top repo, which is modest even for niche infrastructure tooling. The transition from "we published groundbreaking papers" to "enterprises are paying us monthly" is a chasm I've watched many academic founders fail to cross. They know how to navigate peer review and standards bodies. They have not yet demonstrated they know how to navigate a sales cycle with a CISO who has twenty other vendors competing for budget.

The bull case is that the MPIC mandate itself creates a market education tailwind. Before March 2025, most enterprises didn't think about BGP-layer attacks on their certificate infrastructure. Now there's a global regulatory mandate that forces awareness. If the team that wrote the standard also builds the monitoring product, that's a powerful positioning story -- "the people who defined the threat are the ones detecting it." Healthcare, crypto, and fintech organizations face catastrophic liability from impersonation attacks, which means willingness to pay should be high once deals materialize. And the technical moat is genuine: a competitor would need years to develop equivalent cross-layer expertise. If this team can learn to sell -- and YC is specifically designed to teach that -- they have a real shot at owning a category that doesn't exist yet but should.

But I keep coming back to the academic-to-commercial question. All three founders are from the same Princeton research group. Mittal is a tenured professor -- how committed is he to the startup versus his academic career? Having a "Chief Scientist" who's also running a university lab is a configuration I've seen work (rarely) and fail (often). Birge-Lee appears to be the genuine builder -- he coded the original MPIC implementation, he has 38 GitHub repos, and the company is writing Go libraries. That's the right signal. But the overall posture of the company still reads more like a research spinout than a startup in attack mode. Zero job postings. Calendly-based sales. No social media presence. These aren't damning at pre-seed, but they suggest the company hasn't yet made the psychological shift from "we have important research" to "we are going to acquire customers with the intensity of the Airbnb founders photographing apartments in NYC."

This is a team I'd want to watch. The founder-problem fit is in the top quartile of anything I've evaluated at this stage. The schlep is real and structurally deters competition. But "make something people want" is the test, and right now the evidence that people want this specific commercial product -- as opposed to the open standard they already gave away for free -- is absent. I'd need to see the first three or four enterprise customers genuinely pulling the product before my conviction would move from interested to committed.
