# Perceptron ML -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Perceptron ML is the **Pass Example** with a Stanford-NVIDIA resume bolted on -- two technical brothers running "an AI agent for [enterprise vertical]" into a category where EvenUp ($385M at $2B), Eve ($164M at $1B), Darrow ($60M), and FirmPilot ($27.25M) have already raised $636M+ to fight each other. Michael Marcotte's NVIDIA agent-orchestration depth is real and the temperament is non-manic, which lifts the founder dimension off the floor; everything else -- the round shape, the category sleeve, the absence of any owned distribution beyond a 478-follower LinkedIn page -- sits exactly where my framework says I do not generate alpha.

Solana at $0.05/coin is the high rung on price discipline because most funds had quit crypto post-ICO crash; that's what a non-consensus YC seed looks like when you actually get to underwrite illegible exposure. Perceptron ML is the inverse: legal AI in S26 is precisely the sleeve where consensus capital has stampeded -- $385M into EvenUp, $164M into Eve, $103M Series B for Eve specifically in plaintiff-side workflow -- and Perceptron ML's pitch ("event-driven engine" for plaintiff/contingency firms) is the exact seam those incumbents will extend into the moment a 2-person team validates demand. There is no evidence in the dossier of any tier-1 visibly passing, no mention of strategic ambiguity, no sub-batch pricing tell. This is the SAFE cap I literally cannot make money on, and the regatta tacks ahead require Perceptron ML to out-sell firms with 50x the war chest.

FirmPilot is the low-rung shadow on the founder-as-brand dimension -- well-funded legal AI vendor with $27.25M raised, zero founder personality on the cap table, growth bought through enterprise sales motion. The mechanism that kills these companies is that the founder owns no audience, no newsletter, no opinionated essay on why plaintiff firms misallocate marketing dollars, so customer acquisition collapses into a paid-and-outbound burn that needs the next round to feed it. Perceptron ML's dossier puts Michael Marcotte at 478 LinkedIn followers, no Twitter, no GitHub, no public writing on legal-tech or agent design; Peyton Marcotte is a Brown mechanical engineering student with PMARC (impressive, but adjacent to spaceflight, not law firms). The John Fish / Bookshelved test -- did this person have distribution two years before the round? -- is a categorical no.

Mechanism check on Capital Structure: a 2-person team naming **four verticals** (law, construction, investors, trading desks) in the YC blurb compounds badly over 18 months. Each vertical demands its own signal-source integrations, its own "configurable response" templates, its own human-approval workflow tuning -- and Perceptron ML has zero disclosed customers in any of them. The cap table will need three more SAFEs before a Series A buyer can story-broker the asset, because the proof requires picking ONE vertical and grinding to default-alive, which the YC description explicitly refuses to do. EvenUp got to $2B by being maniacal about personal injury demand letters; Perceptron ML is hedging across four sleeves with two people.

Counterfactual on Founder Conviction: for this to flip from a 7/20 to a 12/20, Perceptron ML would need Michael Marcotte to have written publicly -- a Substack, a long tweet thread, a Lawfare-style essay -- on why plaintiff-bar lead generation is structurally broken and why the FirmPilot/Darrow split is the wrong cut of the problem. NVIDIA hardware-debugging agents are genuine technical credibility, and PMARC with NASA/Brown/1517 support shows Peyton Marcotte can ship hardware against constraints, but neither artifact is evidence of a missionary belief about plaintiff law that Perceptron ML will not be talked out of. The "what if they're right?" inversion doesn't have anything to grip on, because no claim has been published to invert against.

Single-anchor parallel on Category Sleeve: this is the Pass Example almost verbatim. "AI is just more internet" (Lonsdale podcast, 2024) re-weights me away from agent shells on top of LLMs, and Perceptron ML's "ingests real-world signals, triggers a configurable response" is the canonical 2026 wrapper. Crypto with on-chain economic activity is the sleeve where I'm convicted; vertical AI agents into a legal market where $549M of late-stage capital is already deployed is the sleeve where I am structurally short. The MyCase stat -- AI adoption in law firms going 19% to 79% in one year -- is not bullish for Perceptron ML; it means the buyer is now sophisticated and the incumbents have first-mover lock-in on procurement.

36-month forecast: Perceptron ML reaches sub-$2M ARR on the legal vertical, fails to close a priced Series A in 2027, and gets acqui-hired by either FirmPilot or Eve for the Marcotte brothers' agent-orchestration engineering rather than the customer book -- with the residual cap table returning ~1x to seed SAFEs. The mechanism is simple: a 2-person team across 4 verticals against $549M of incumbent capital cannot win a procurement cycle in plaintiff law within 24 months, the burn forces a down-round or a strategic sale, and the technical depth (NVIDIA agents, formal verification) is what survives the wind-down.

The strongest bear case against my pass -- which is really the bull case for Perceptron ML -- is that brothers solve the co-founder authority problem (no manic-bias setup), Michael Marcotte's NVIDIA production-agent experience genuinely is rare in the legal AI cohort where most founders are ex-lawyers stapling GPT-4 to a doc workflow, and the detect-signal-then-launch-campaign loop is a real seam that neither Darrow (detection only) nor FirmPilot (campaign only) currently owns end-to-end. Robinhood is the Known Exception precedent here: Slow entered before the consensus pricing arrived, and the operator-friend channel overrode price discipline. The bear case stings because Perceptron ML could be the technical operator who actually executes the seam. The dossier evidence that wins for my pass: Perceptron ML has no disclosed legal-domain advisor, no published thesis on plaintiff-bar economics, no owned distribution, and explicitly names four verticals at a stage where focus is the only moat a 2-person team has -- the missionary-temperament test is failed not by the founders' character but by the absence of any artifact proving it. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Michael Marcotte publishes a 3,000-word essay on why FirmPilot and Darrow have the wrong half of the loop, and Perceptron ML kills three of the four verticals to commit to plaintiff law only. At that point we'll talk.
