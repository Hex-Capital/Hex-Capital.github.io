# Cascade -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

I regularly see companies building AI copilots for specific enterprise verticals, and Cascade is a textbook case. "AI Platform that finds & wins more projects for architecture & construction firms." The pitch structure is one I've encountered hundreds of times: large industry, underserved by technology, automate the painful workflow. Cascade reduces proposal creation from four weeks to days and scans government RFP portals so business development teams don't have to. Fine. Useful. But my first question is always the same — if Cascade fully succeeds, becomes the dominant platform for every AEC firm on Earth, does the world visibly change? Architecture firms write proposals faster. Construction companies find more RFPs. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. Buildings don't get cheaper for the people who live in them. The consequence of success is a profitable vertical SaaS company, which is a perfectly respectable outcome for most investors. I don't invest for respectable outcomes. I want to either lose my money or make 50x.

The traction deserves acknowledgment because it's genuinely strong — $0 to $183k cARR in three months, 15 companies signed with 11 in the last month, first three clients doubling their spend. Clients that built JFK, LaGuardia, Four Seasons hotels, nuclear reactors. That velocity at pre-seed is unusual, and it comes from a specific source: the Head of Growth, ranked #2 sales rep at Procore, who drove $3M in ARR in 2025. This is execution-driven traction from a sales network, not product-pull traction from a technology breakthrough. When I backed DoorDash — a known exception in my portfolio where I overrode the technical novelty requirement — the override signal was that transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people constituted enormous consequence magnitude. Cascade's consequence profile doesn't remotely approach that threshold. The DoorDash exception doesn't apply.

The technical approach confirms my concern. Cascade applies existing large language models to scan government websites, generate proposal drafts, and analyze construction specifications. The integrations with Deltek Vantagepoint, Ajera, and Costpoint are solid product work, but they're plumbing, not science. There's no novel model architecture, no proprietary training data that creates a step-function improvement, no 10x capability gain that couldn't be replicated by AutogenAI with its $65.3M in funding or by Procore deciding to build native RFP tools. The moat is domain-specific integrations and accumulated client data — switching costs, not technology discontinuity. I prefer technology risk to market risk, and Cascade has almost no technology risk. That sounds like a compliment. It isn't. It means the company is competing on execution and sales velocity in a market where well-funded horizontal players like AutogenAI and vertical competitors like Joist AI ($4M raised) and Workorb AI ($2.6M raised) are converging on the same opportunity.

Hannia Zia's trajectory is the strongest signal in the dossier. Yale CS, Google Pay India scaling from zero to 100 million users, Google Pay Singapore launch, Forbes 30 Under 30 Asia, product lead at UnlikelyAI. Joana Ferreira brings ML engineering depth from Google Search and Amazon, plus a personal connection to construction through her family of carpenters. They worked together at UnlikelyAI for years before starting Cascade, which means the co-founder relationship is tested. The $183k cARR in three months demonstrates execution speed. But execution speed in a well-understood B2B SaaS playbook is different from the learning rate I look for — the kind of first-principles reasoning that makes a 19-year-old dropout at Mach Industries dangerous to Lockheed Martin. Zia is clearly a talented operator. The question is whether she's building a company where the plan matters more than the people, and in vertical SaaS, the plan often does.

The strongest bull case: construction is a $13 trillion global industry, and Cascade's vision of becoming "the data layer for all construction projects" could expand beyond RFPs into pre-bid intelligence, bid leveling, joint-venture matching, and eventually reshape how the built environment gets planned and funded. If the data flywheel compounds — every connected ERP, every completed proposal, every project match improving the AI's scoring — Cascade could create a proprietary dataset that new entrants can't replicate. The upcoming features (permit filings, developer land purchases, government meeting minutes) suggest the founders see the broader opportunity. For this to become a company I'd want in my portfolio, Cascade would need to demonstrate that its data layer creates genuinely new demand — not just redistributing existing proposal spend but enabling firms to pursue projects they never would have found, changing win rates so dramatically that the economics of construction procurement shift structurally. I see no evidence of that discontinuity yet.

Cascade is a well-executed vertical SaaS company with impressive early traction and capable founders solving a real pain point. The two dimensions that determine my verdict are consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. On consequence magnitude, making AEC business development 3-4x faster is useful but affects thousands of BD professionals, not millions of lives. On technical approach, applying existing LLMs to a vertical workflow with domain-specific integrations is a wrapper, not a wedge. $183k cARR in three months from the #2 Procore sales rep is a sales achievement, not a technology achievement. This is designed to be a solid 5-10x return, and I don't spend my time on solid 5-10x returns.
