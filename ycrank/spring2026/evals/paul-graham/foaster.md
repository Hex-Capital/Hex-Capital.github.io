# Foaster -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Foaster is what's right there in the tagline: "AI-native consulting firm." I've spent twenty years telling founders that a startup is a company designed to grow fast, and that growth means something specific — it means the output of the company scales superlinearly with the input. A consulting firm, no matter how many AI agents you attach to it, is defined by engagements. A human consultant scopes the work, reviews the output, delivers the recommendations. Double the clients, you probably need to double the consultants. That's a services business. It might be a very good services business — a profitable one, even — but it's not what I mean when I say startup. The dossier hints at a future where the operational map becomes a persistent platform asset, a piece of software that companies subscribe to. That version of Foaster might be a startup. But right now what I see is consulting with better tools, and the distance between those two things is not a feature request. It's a fundamental change in what the company is.

The founders themselves are more interesting than their company. Dabadie created the most-followed Nadal fan account on Instagram at age eleven — 245,000 followers — and grew adjacent tennis communities to over 400,000 users. Then at eighteen he was recruited as an agent for Nadal's Academy. That's not a normal trajectory for an eleven-year-old. It's the kind of slightly-too-intense-about-everything pattern I notice. Combes built Repondia, where AI agents apparently handled 60,000+ customer calls for French restaurant groups. If that number is real, he's done a version of Foaster's core mechanism already — deploying conversational AI agents to talk to actual humans at scale. Their Werewolf benchmark on LLM social intelligence got 300,000+ views and was shared by OpenAI's president, which is a genuine academic accomplishment for two 23-year-olds at ENSAE. These are clearly sharp, energetic people. The question is whether they're pointed at the right problem.

And I don't think they are, because this idea doesn't pass my most basic filter: it sounds good to everyone. "AI makes consulting faster and cheaper" is the kind of thing you'd find in a pitch competition slide deck at any business school in the world right now. The competitive landscape confirms it — Distyl AI has raised $202 million, Aily Labs $101 million, PromptQL over $136 million, Skan.ai $57.6 million. When I look at a market where half a billion dollars in venture capital is already deployed against variations of the same thesis, my immediate reaction is that whatever return premium existed has been competed away. The best startup ideas are the ones where knowledgeable people's first reaction is skepticism. Nobody is skeptical about AI consulting. Everybody thinks it's a great idea. That's the tell.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? Combes's Repondia experience would need to be as real and transferable as it sounds — genuine operational knowledge of deploying conversational AI at scale, with lessons about failure modes, edge cases, and human trust dynamics that no amount of funding can replicate at a well-capitalized competitor. The operational map they build per client would need to become genuinely sticky — a living document that makes Foaster the system of record for how the company actually works, not just a one-time deliverable. And the innovator's dilemma would need to hold: McKinsey and BCG would need to be structurally unable to cannibalize their own billing model, creating a window that Foaster exploits before the well-funded AI-native competitors converge. All of that is possible. But I notice the dossier contains zero evidence of any of it happening yet — no revenue, no named customers, no signed LOIs, no case studies with outcomes. And I don't see how these founders organically discovered the consulting problem. They're statistics students. When did they personally suffer through a slow, expensive McKinsey engagement? The connection appears to be "we know how to build AI agents that talk to people" plus "consulting is a big market that involves talking to people." That's technology looking for a problem, not a problem demanding a solution. Drew Houston didn't build Dropbox because cloud storage was a large addressable market. He built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive.

I'd want to follow these founders. They have real energy and the kind of early intensity signals — the age-eleven community building, the 60,000-call AI system, the academic work that caught OpenAI's attention — that suggest they might eventually find the right problem. But this particular idea, in this competitive landscape, with a services-business model and no organic origin story, is not the bet I'd make with my own money.
