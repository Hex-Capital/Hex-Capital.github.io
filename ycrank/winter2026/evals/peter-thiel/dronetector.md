# DroneTector -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The drone threat is not a secret. When Ukraine is absorbing 1,600 drone attacks per month, when Gatwick cost stakeholders fifty million pounds, when Houthi drones doubled global shipping costs -- every defense ministry, every airport authority, every intelligence analyst on earth knows that small drones are a problem. This is the opposite of a secret; it is a consensus so total that it has spawned an entire industry of funded competitors racing to address it. DroneShield is public with AU$221 million in cash. Echodyne has raised $195 million with Bill Gates leading. Dedrone was acquired by Axon. Robin Radar has $52 million in revenue and 200+ units deployed in Ukraine. MatrixSpace raised $58 million with L3Harris as a strategic investor. When I look at this landscape, I see exactly the Girardian dynamic I avoid: companies imitating each other toward mutual margin destruction in a market everyone has already identified. Five well-funded players pursuing the same thesis is not a market with a secret -- it is a market where the secret has already been found and commoditized.

The question, then, is whether DroneTector's specific technical approach -- 94 GHz millimetre-wave radar exploiting micro-Doppler signatures from propeller rotation -- constitutes a genuine secret within the broader consensus. This is where it gets more interesting. Most counter-drone radar operates at lower frequencies (X-band, Ku-band) designed for larger targets. RF-based systems like Dedrone's cannot detect autonomous drones that emit no radio signals. DroneTector's thesis is that the physics of millimetre-wave frequencies at 94 GHz provides fundamentally higher resolution for nano-drone-sized targets, and that micro-Doppler classification -- reading the unique signature of spinning propellers -- can distinguish a $50 drone from a bird in a way that lower-frequency systems structurally cannot. This is a real technical claim, grounded in Matthew Moore's peer-reviewed PhD research, and it addresses a genuine gap. But it is a secret about engineering methodology within a consensus market, not a secret about the world that creates a path to monopoly. Echodyne could extend into millimetre-wave. Robin Radar has deep small-target expertise. The underlying physics is well-understood. The question is execution speed and production capability, which is a competition question -- and competition is where I lose interest.

The Palantir comparison is tempting but ultimately misleading. When we started Palantir, the intelligence community was not crowded with venture-backed startups building data-fusion platforms. The secret was that commercial anti-fraud technology could be repurposed for counterterrorism -- and almost no one believed it. The market was essentially empty of competitors pursuing that specific thesis. DroneTector's market is the inverse: acutely aware, heavily funded, and converging from multiple directions. NATO DIANA support is genuinely valuable -- it mirrors In-Q-Tel's early role with Palantir in providing institutional legitimacy and potential procurement access. But NATO DIANA is an accelerator that supports cohorts of companies, not a singular endorsement of monopoly potential.

Where I find genuine signal is in the founding team's technical depth and the hard-technology nature of the product. Moore is one of a small number of people globally who has built and experimentally validated 94 GHz radar for drone micro-Doppler classification. Doherty brings a decade of optical and laser physics from Oxford's Clarendon Laboratory plus the experience of founding a deep-tech venture (Quantum Fabrix, which raised £2 million from Paul Graham). Three PhDs in physics, atomic physics, and computer science building custom radar hardware -- this is the kind of team profile I respect. They are building in atoms, not bits. The concern with Doherty is the QFX timeline: he was listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix until October 2025, and QFX appointed a new CEO that same month. The transition to DroneTector raises questions about the depth of commitment and whether residual obligations exist. A founder who has already left one venture is not necessarily disqualified -- but the recency demands scrutiny.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: first, that 94 GHz millimetre-wave radar is not merely better but categorically superior for nano-drone detection -- a genuine order-of-magnitude improvement that makes lower-frequency approaches obsolete for this class of target. Second, that the micro-Doppler signature database DroneTector builds from deployments becomes a compounding data moat that no competitor can replicate without equivalent field experience. Third, that defense procurement relationships through NATO DIANA and DASA create switching costs and security clearance barriers that lock out later entrants. If all three prove true, DroneTector could become the standard detection layer for the most challenging segment of the counter-drone market -- the nano-drone problem that existing systems genuinely cannot solve. That is a real business. But it requires the competitive landscape to not converge on the same technical approach, which is a bet against the diffusion of well-understood physics. I would need to see evidence that the engineering barriers to production-grade millimetre-wave radar are higher than the dossier suggests -- that DroneTector's lead is measured in years, not months.

This is a competent team building real hardware for a real and growing market, with institutional validation from serious defense organizations. But the market structure works against monopoly. The secret is technical, not structural -- it does not create the kind of network effects or category-defining dynamics that produce power-law returns. I would watch this company with interest, particularly for signs that the 94 GHz approach proves categorically superior in field deployments, but I would not write a check today.
