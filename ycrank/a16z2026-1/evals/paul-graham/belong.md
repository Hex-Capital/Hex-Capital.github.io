# Belong -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Belong's tagline is "Building the Financial Infrastructure of Fandom." That phrase alone triggers every alarm in my pattern-matching system. When a startup's one-liner could appear unchanged in a Goldman Sachs research note — and Goldman Sachs did in fact publish a $4.2B "superfan" market estimate in 2024 — you're looking at an idea that sounds good to everyone. And the best startup ideas don't sound good to everyone. They sound crazy, or boring, or both. "Let strangers sleep in your apartment" sounded insane. "A link aggregation site" sounded trivial. "A debit card for music fans" sounds like something a strategy consultancy would put on slide 47 of a future-of-entertainment deck. Nobody hears this idea and says "that will never work." They say "oh, interesting" — which is the polite version of "I see why you think there's a market there." That's the tell.

Nick Holmstén founded Tunigo, got it acquired by Spotify, then ran Spotify's global music operation for seven years. Ash Pournouri managed Avicii, co-created the Brilliant Minds conference with Daniel Ek, and has 40+ portfolio companies. These are genuinely accomplished people with extraordinary music industry networks — Troy Carter, the former Lady Gaga manager, invested in the SAFE round. But accomplishment and the "animal" quality are different things. When I backed Patrick Collison, he was a teenager who'd already shipped Auctomatic. When I backed the Airbnb founders, they were selling cereal boxes to fund their company. The signal I look for is not "this person ran a division at a large company" but "this person hacked together something under desperate constraint and it worked." The Belong dossier is full of executive credentials and entirely empty of scrappy founder stories. Holmstén and Pournouri discovered this opportunity the way executives discover opportunities — by analyzing the industry they know well and identifying a structural gap. That's the opposite of living in the future and building what's missing. They lived in the music industry's executive suite and built what their experience suggested.

The deeper problem is that neither founder appears to be a builder in the technical sense. No GitHub profiles. No technical co-founder named in the dossier. Eleven employees at pre-seed with $2.7M in seed funding — that's a team size that suggests they hired engineers to build the product rather than building it themselves. My rule is simple: three requirements — tech expertise, idea, cofounders. Belong has the idea and the cofounders, but where's the tech expertise at the founding level? They're using Banking-as-a-Service infrastructure through Middlesex Federal Savings, which commoditizes the fintech plumbing. The proprietary elements — FanScore, KYF, Dropblock, all trademarked — are engagement features, not technical moats. Anyone with a BaaS partner and a Mastercard BIN can issue a branded card. The question is whether the fan identity layer creates enough lock-in to matter, and right now that layer exists as marketing copy, not as a product with users.

The structural comparison that keeps surfacing is Stripe — Belong even borrows the "financial infrastructure" framing. But the analogy breaks down at the foundation. The Collison brothers were developers who experienced the specific, visceral pain of integrating online payments. Every developer had this pain. Stripe's first users were people exactly like its founders. Belong's founders are music industry executives building a product for fans — a customer segment they observe from above rather than inhabit from within. Holmstén understands playlist curation and content strategy; Pournouri understands artist brand monetization. Neither of them is the 22-year-old who spends $300 a month following an artist on tour and wants recognition for it. Building for a customer segment you don't belong to and haven't deeply studied is one of my clearest red flags, and Belong's founder-market fit, impressive as it looks on paper, runs in the wrong direction — they have supply-side expertise (artist relationships) but demand-side distance (they are not fans, they are the industry).

The strongest bull case for Belong is the ClassDojo exception — a company outside my typical "hackers building for hackers" pattern that succeeded because the founders' domain expertise and execution quality in a schlep-heavy space overrode my sector preference. And Belong does have real compliance execution: SOC 2, Mastercard BIN operational, Apple Wallet integration, all by March 2026. Banking compliance is a genuine schlep. But ClassDojo's schlep was selling to school districts — a brutally unglamorous customer acquisition process that repelled every other founder. Belong's schlep is BaaS integration, which dozens of neobanks have navigated successfully. The compliance barrier creates maybe a six-month lead, not a structural moat. Meanwhile, Fanatics ONE already has 10 million FanCash members in sports. If Fanatics or Spotify decides to build fan rewards with financial rails, Belong's artist relationship advantage — which hasn't yet produced a single announced partnership despite the founders' networks — would be competing against platforms with orders of magnitude more users. The cold-start problem is severe: fans need exclusive artist content to justify adopting a new debit card, artists need fans on the platform to justify exclusivity commitments, and neither side has been seeded at scale.

Holmstén and Pournouri genuinely care about the music industry — Holmstén was in a band, wrote songs, built Tunigo out of passion for music discovery before the Spotify acquisition. Pournouri's eight-year commitment to building Avicii's career reflects real devotion to the artist-fan relationship. I don't question their earnestness about the problem space. But earnestness about an industry is different from the obsessive, builder-identity earnestness I look for — the kind where someone builds the first version in their apartment before thinking about a company. Belong launched with trademarked feature names, a polished website, and a 9-person team. That's the execution pattern of experienced operators raising capital to build a vision, not of obsessive builders who couldn't stop themselves from building something.

My pass on Belong comes down to two dimensions. First, organic problem discovery: this idea was identified through industry expertise and market analysis, not personal frustration — Holmstén and Pournouri saw the fan monetization gap from the executive level, which is the "identified an opportunity" origin story I've learned to be skeptical of. Second, the idea fails my Black Swan test entirely: it sits squarely in the "seems like a good idea and is a good idea" quadrant, which means the returns have already been competed away by the time investors like Goldman Sachs are publishing TAM estimates for "superfans." The founders' credentials are real, their networks are valuable, and their compliance execution is solid. A different investor — one who weights industry expertise and executive networks more heavily than I do — might reasonably invest. But my framework says the best bets come from builders who stumble into problems that repel everyone else, not from executives who identify opportunities that attract everyone.
