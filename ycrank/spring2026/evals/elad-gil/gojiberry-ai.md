# Gojiberry AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I need to address is the TrustMRR listing. This company was listed for sale at $11.1M on March 9, 2026 -- one week after their Product Hunt launch and while participating in YC S26. I've backed hundreds of companies, and I've never seen a founder simultaneously raising venture capital and listing their business on a marketplace. At best, this signals optionality-seeking; at worst, it signals the founders believe this is closer to a lifestyle business exit than a venture-scale trajectory. The listing price of 6x revenue on a business doing ~$175K MRR implies the founders themselves are pricing the outcome at roughly $2M ARR * 6x = ~$11M. That's not a venture outcome. When I invest, I need founders building toward 10x-1000x potential, not founders shopping for a buyer at single-digit millions.

On market structure timing: this fails my first test comprehensively. "AI for sales outreach" is perhaps the single most crowded AI application category in 2026. The structural shift -- LLMs crossing the threshold for generating personalized messages -- happened in 2023-2024. Everyone saw it. Apollo ($150M ARR), Clay ($100M ARR at $3.1B valuation), Instantly ($20M ARR bootstrapped), Lemlist ($40M ARR) -- these aren't exploratory bets, they're scaled businesses that already own this space. Apollo's acquisition of Pocus in March 2026 specifically validates signal-based selling as a feature, not a company. When an incumbent acquires the exact capability your startup provides, the nonobvious window has not just closed -- it was never open. Compare this to when I backed Coinbase: there was no $150M ARR crypto exchange already operating. The category barely existed. Here, Gojiberry is entering a market with five-plus well-funded competitors that have already proven the model works. That's not a nonobvious market -- it's a crowded one.

The infrastructure-layer test is similarly unfavorable. Gojiberry is a pure application sitting on LinkedIn's platform. There's no API layer, no developer ecosystem, no platform that other businesses build on. The entire product depends on LinkedIn continuing to permit automated messaging and signal scraping -- a dependency the dossier itself flags as a core risk. Stripe became the payments infrastructure that thousands of companies integrated into their core stack. Gojiberry is a Chrome extension that automates LinkedIn DMs. The switching costs are minimal: historical campaign data and trained ICP models have some retention value (the reported <5% churn is good), but there's no compounding network effect. When Apollo bundles equivalent signal detection into its 275M-contact platform at no incremental cost, Gojiberry's standalone value proposition compresses.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. Three founders with three prior exits, bootstrapping to $175K MRR with $380/month in marketing spend, 44% MoM growth, 75% profit margins, <5% churn -- that's exceptional capital-efficient execution. The comparison that comes to mind isn't one of my portfolio companies; it's HeyReach, which bootstrapped to $10M ARR in adjacent LinkedIn automation. The founders clearly know how to build, sell, and distribute SMB SaaS. If they can sustain this growth curve and reach $3-5M ARR profitably, that's a legitimate business. But "legitimate business" and "venture investment" are different categories. For this to be a venture outcome, you'd need to believe Gojiberry can either (a) move upmarket aggressively before incumbents lock down signal-based SMB selling, or (b) become the category-defining platform for small sales teams the way Gusto became the category-defining payroll tool for SMBs. The problem is that payroll has regulatory moats -- you can't just clone Gusto's compliance infrastructure. LinkedIn outreach automation has no such structural defense. The fact that five bootstrapped competitors have each independently reached $10M+ ARR in adjacent categories tells you everything about barriers to entry.

On the founder profile: Pierre-Eliott and Roman co-founded and sold CoCo AI together, and Dylan sold Edusign with $3.3M in revenue and 600K+ users. These are real exits, but they're small-scale. None of these founders operated inside a company during a 10-to-1000 scaling phase. What they've demonstrated is scrappy bootstrap execution -- which is valuable but different from the organizational scaling capability I look for when a company needs to compete against Apollo's 1,000+ person operation. The 12-channel distribution strategy Roman runs personally is impressive hustle, but it's also a ceiling: that approach doesn't scale to $50M ARR without fundamental organizational transformation.

I'm passing. The market is obvious and converging fast, the positioning is application-layer with platform dependency, the founders -- while operationally capable -- appear to be optimizing for a sub-venture exit, and the competitive dynamics are punishing. Strong execution in a structurally unfavorable market produces a good small business, not a venture-scale outcome.
