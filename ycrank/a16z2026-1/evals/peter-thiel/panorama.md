# Panorama -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Jaclyn Lunger has an MIT PhD in machine learning for materials science and was the first employee at Lila Sciences, which reached a $1.3 billion valuation with Nvidia backing. She built high-throughput virtual screening pipelines and generated the largest existing dataset of adsorption energies on perovskite surfaces. This is someone who has manipulated atoms at scale -- and she chose to build a Zapier competitor. The mismatch between the founder's deepest technical capability and the company's actual product is the most striking feature of Panorama's dossier, and it tells me everything about where this company sits in my framework. When your CTO's most impressive credential is irrelevant to what you are building, the company has not yet found its secret.

The canonical question -- what important truth do very few people agree with you on? -- produces silence when applied to Panorama. The claimed insight is that knowledge workers do not know which of their workflows could be automated, and an AI watching their Slack messages and Notion documents can discover these patterns proactively. Who disagrees with this? No one. Microsoft is building Copilot Studio on exactly this premise. Google launched AgentSpace with native access to the same workplace data Panorama must reach through APIs. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks for $2.85 billion precisely because observational workflow automation is the consensus direction for enterprise productivity. Panorama's framing as a "TikTok-like recommendation engine for workflows" is marketing language applied to an insight that appeared in every enterprise AI pitch deck of 2025. The cleantech pattern recurs: entrepreneurs swept up by social trends, building what everyone else is building, calling it contrarian because they added a recommendation layer.

The competitive landscape confirms the absence of monopoly structure. Zapier generates $310 million in annual revenue. n8n has $40 million in ARR and $254 million in funding. Workato has $150 million in ARR and $421 million in funding. These companies coexist because workflow automation is structurally fragmented -- different teams use different tools, different workflows resist standardization, and no single platform captures winner-take-all dynamics. Panorama enters this market with a Product Hunt launch (363 upvotes, #2 Product of the Day) and no public revenue, competing not only against these funded players but against Microsoft, Google, and Salesforce, each of which owns the workplace data natively that Panorama must access through integrations. The existence of a competitive landscape slide listing five similar companies is itself my clearest red flag -- it means the market is competitive by definition. Panorama's comparison to Wish is structurally precise: a large TAM with no monopoly path, multiple well-funded competitors pursuing overlapping theses, and a product differentiation that amounts to a feature rather than a new category.

The strongest bull case requires believing two things simultaneously: first, that Panorama's recommendation quality will be so dramatically superior -- ten times better, not incrementally better -- that enterprises choose a startup's API integration over their existing platform's native capability; and second, that the team-specific data accumulated over months of observation creates switching costs durable enough to resist commoditization. Jingwei Hao's infrastructure experience at Cash App, Lyft, Twitter, and Google gives him genuine insight into cross-team coordination failures, and Lunger's ML expertise could theoretically produce recommendation engines that generalist platform teams cannot match. But this argument requires Panorama to out-execute Microsoft and Google on their own data, in their own ecosystems, with their own customers. The cold-start dependency -- one to two weeks of data ingestion before any value materializes -- compounds the distribution problem. Every week of delayed value is a week where the trial user discovers that Copilot already does something similar without the integration overhead.

Jingwei and Lunger show no evidence of a definite, multi-year plan for how Panorama becomes the last mover in workflow automation. The product description reads as iterative discovery: observe, recommend, learn, expand. This is the lean-startup playbook applied to AI -- precisely the indefinite thinking my framework rejects. A founder with a genuine monopoly vision can describe exactly what the world looks like when they have won; Panorama's positioning suggests a team still searching for product-market fit within a crowded category. The SOC2 Type I certification and CASA Tier 3 rating demonstrate enterprise seriousness, but these are table stakes, not conviction.

Panorama is a pure software layer with no proprietary technology, no hardware moat, no regulatory barrier, and no physical-world component. The core concept -- observing work patterns and suggesting automations using LLMs -- is replicable by any team with access to the same foundation models. The GitHub organization has zero public repositories. No patents exist. The dossier identifies no structural barrier that would take a well-funded competitor more than months to overcome. In a world where I would rather back someone building flying cars than another iteration on 140 characters, Panorama represents the precise category of incremental software abstraction that my portfolio has increasingly moved away from.
