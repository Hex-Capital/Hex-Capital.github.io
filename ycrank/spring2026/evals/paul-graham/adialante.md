# Adialante -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about Adialante is the schlep. Building a novel MRI scanner from first principles using physics nobody else has commercialized, then deploying it in mobile trucks to urology clinics, then getting FDA clearance — every phrase in that sentence is designed to make a rational founder choose a different problem. This is the kind of idea where your unconscious literally won't let you see it. And that's usually where I get interested, because the disgust reaction from other founders is itself a moat. Nobody's going to copy this on a whim.

The technical foundation is real. Torres and Jenkins didn't read about low-cost MRI in a TechCrunch article and decide to build one. They invented the FREE technology during their PhD work at one of the world's top MRI research centers — UMN's CMRR. The work is published in peer-reviewed journals going back to 2021. They have an ISMRM Rabi Award winner on the team, which in MRI physics is the equivalent of having a Fields medalist doing your math. These people understand the domain at the level where the domain was created. The NSF validated the science with a Phase II SBIR. This isn't a pitch deck describing something they'd like to build — the core physics exists.

But here's what makes me hesitate: I can see the technology discovery was organic, but the company feels like a technology looking for a business rather than frustrated users who built what was missing. The classic pattern I trust — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Octopart founders unable to search for electronic parts — has the user discovering the problem through their own pain, then building the solution. Torres and Jenkins discovered a novel way to do MRI without gradient coils, then asked "where is MRI access worst?" and landed on prostate cancer screening. That's a perfectly reasonable way to start a company, but it's a different animal than building from lived frustration. Torres's background from the south side of Chicago and his stated mission to bring imaging to underserved communities adds genuine personal motivation, and I take that seriously — but it's a motivation that was layered onto a technical breakthrough, not a problem that demanded the breakthrough be invented.

The competitive picture worries me in a specific way. The broad category of "affordable MRI" no longer sounds crazy — Hyperfine is public with $13.6M in revenue, Promaxo has 69 systems installed and $80M+ raised, Chipiron just raised $17M for quantum-based MRI. Five years ago, pitching low-cost MRI would have gotten you laughed out of most investor meetings. Today it gets you compared to four well-funded competitors. The FREE physics is genuinely differentiated, and the per-scan mobile model is the most interesting strategic choice here — incumbents like Siemens literally cannot offer per-scan mobile service without cannibalizing their $1-3M hardware sales. But Adialante is pre-FDA while Promaxo is already selling prostate MRI systems. In a hardware race with regulatory gates, being behind is expensive.

What I can't evaluate from this dossier — and it's the thing that matters most to me — is whether these founders have the running back quality. I see academic excellence, genuine technical depth, entrepreneurship awards, successful grant applications. What I don't see are stories of creative workarounds when the system failed them, of shipping something ugly to real users over a weekend, of doing the manual unscalable thing to get their first clinic partner. The resourcefulness I look for isn't "won an NSF grant," which is resourcefulness within the established system. It's "convinced a urologist to let us scan patients from a van in the parking lot before we had clearance for anything." Maybe they've done things like that and it just isn't in the public record. But truly resourceful founders tend to generate stories that leak out, because the stories are too good to stay private.

The bull case is real: if FREE works at clinical quality and they get FDA clearance, the per-scan mobile model could do to MRI what Uber did to car ownership — transform a massive capital purchase into an on-demand service. The business model conflict with incumbents is a genuine structural advantage. The team has the technical depth to actually build the thing, which is more than I can say for 95% of medical device startups. And the prostate cancer screening use case is being pushed by clinical guidelines, so timing is favorable. If Torres turns out to have the commercial instincts to match his technical ability — and YC has helped plenty of academic founders find that gear — this could be an important company. But I'm investing my own money, and what I need to see is evidence that these founders can operate outside the lab with the same fluency they have inside it. Right now, that evidence is absent. I'd want to meet them.
