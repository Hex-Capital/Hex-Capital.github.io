# FullSeam -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is clean. At TaxProper, as the company scaled to process over a billion dollars annually in property tax payments, the founders watched their own accounting team drown in invoicing, reconciliations, and exception handling. They didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's hot in AI." They lived through the operational pain of a growing fintech company and discovered that the back-office work didn't scale with the business. This is organic problem discovery in the truest sense -- the kind that emerges from running a real company at real scale, not from reading industry reports about TAM.

And the schlep is genuine. Logging into nine different platforms, matching invoices to purchase orders, applying company-specific GL coding rules, chasing vendors for missing information, reconciling bank statements -- this is exactly the kind of tedious, detail-heavy work that makes smart founders look away. The accuracy bar for financial data is unforgiving. Get a GL code wrong and you create audit problems. The integration surface area across QuickBooks, NetSuite, Stripe, Xero, and five other platforms is enormous. Each one is its own API with its own quirks. This is real schlep. But here's where my enthusiasm gets complicated: the schlep that once kept people away is now attracting well-funded competition. Auditoria.ai has raised $60.5 million doing essentially the same thing -- multi-function AI automation across AP and AR. Stampli has $148 million. Vic.ai has $52 million. When the schlep becomes a recognized opportunity with hundreds of millions of venture dollars chasing it, the protective moat that schlep blindness creates starts to erode. The problem was hiding in plain sight five years ago. It isn't hiding anymore.

The repeat founder pattern is the strongest signal here. I've always been willing to re-fund founders who've been through the cycle. These three built TaxProper together at UIUC, took it through YC in S19, raised from Khosla, scaled it to 115,000+ properties worth $35 billion, and sold it to Opendoor. They didn't just survive -- they won. Now they're back, attacking a problem they discovered through that experience. The co-founder relationship spans years of shared struggle, which is the opposite of my biggest team red flag. When I see founders who've worked together through a full startup lifecycle -- build, scale, exit -- and voluntarily choose to do it again together, that tells me the relationship is durable. Dowling's Rhodes Scholarship and policy background demonstrate intellectual horsepower. Segal's actuarial background adds quantitative depth. These are serious, earnest people.

But I have a structural concern I can't shake: where is the technical founder? Dowling studied history and social policy. Coppa was Head of Product. Segal was an actuarial statistician. No public GitHub repos for any of them. For a company building AI agents that autonomously process financial documents and chain multi-step workflows across nine platforms, the absence of a visible builder on the founding team is conspicuous. At TaxProper they presumably hired engineers. Here, with a team of three and zero job postings, someone is writing the code -- but who? The product clearly exists; they have two named customers using it. So they've built something. But I can't see the hacker. Nearly every great company I've funded -- Stripe, Dropbox, Reddit, Replit -- had at least one founder who could build the core product with their own hands. The founding team here looks more like operators than makers. This is the gap that keeps me from moving to Invest.

The bull case is real, though. If one of these founders has taught themselves to code since TaxProper -- or if there's an unlisted technical co-founder or early engineer I'm not seeing -- this company looks quite different. Repeat founders with a genuine exit, attacking a problem they organically discovered, with deep domain expertise in financial operations, a working product with nine integrations, and two real customers already using it. The competitors have more capital but also more organizational bloat -- Stampli has $148 million in funding and only $11.2 million in revenue, which suggests their capital efficiency is poor. A lean team of three with $500K could potentially outrun a 200-person company with $60 million in burn if the product is good enough. The TaxProper precedent shows these founders know how to build and sell fintech SaaS to institutional customers. If the AI accuracy crosses the trust threshold and word-of-mouth starts spreading through the SMB finance community, this could compound quickly.

What holds me back is the convergence of two concerns: the competitive landscape makes this feel like a consensus idea despite the organic origin, and the founding team's technical gap creates execution risk for an AI-native product. The problem is real. The founders are credible. The schlep is genuine. But this is a race with well-capitalized competitors, and I want to see a hacker in the driver's seat for that kind of race.
