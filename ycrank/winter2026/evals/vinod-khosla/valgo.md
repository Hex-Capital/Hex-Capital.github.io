# Valgo -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

These founders wrote the textbook. Literally — "Algorithms for Validation," MIT Press, co-authored by Moss and Katz, taught as a Stanford course they run. Robert Moss spent seven years on the team that built the FAA-certified ACAS Xa collision avoidance system, now a worldwide standard. Sydney Katz's PhD on safe ML-based perception maps directly to the technical core. Harrison Delecki's thesis on sampling and estimation for safety validation is the methodological foundation of what they're building. In forty years of investing, I've rarely seen a team with this level of domain authority over the exact problem they're commercializing. And that's precisely what gives me pause. When the founders are the world's leading experts executing the obvious application of their own academic work, nobody thinks it's crazy. Nobody is dismissing this as impossible. The experts aren't mocking it — the experts *are* the founders. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. When Cisco's CTO told me Juniper would never work, that was information. When incumbents laugh at you, that's data about how large the opportunity is.

Let me start where I always start: if Valgo succeeds completely, what changes? Insurers can price coverage for autonomous systems. The insurance bottleneck lifts. Autonomous trucks and robots deploy faster because they can get the policies they need. That's genuinely important — you can't operate commercially without insurance, and insurers can't write policies without loss estimates. But the consequence of Valgo's success is that a risk quantification layer works correctly. The insurance industry still operates the same way, with the same carriers, the same actuarial frameworks, the same regulatory structure. Valgo provides better inputs to the existing system. It doesn't replace the system. Compare that to what I'm drawn to — Square didn't optimize the existing POS infrastructure, it made it obsolete. Impossible Foods didn't help the meat industry price products better, it attacked the entire $1.7 trillion system. Valgo is valuable plumbing between two existing industries. Important, but the consequence of success is a well-functioning bridge, not a transformed landscape.

There's a deeper structural issue I keep circling back to. The core value proposition — converting simulation data into loss estimates because historical claims data doesn't exist — addresses a transitional gap. Today, autonomous vehicles have near-zero claims history. But every mile driven, every incident recorded, every near-miss logged is claims data accumulating. In ten years, the actuarial deficit that makes Valgo essential will have substantially narrowed. Traditional methods will begin to work. Unless Valgo evolves into something far more ambitious than a simulation-to-loss-estimate pipeline, its fundamental utility has a built-in decay curve. The best infrastructure companies create permanent dependencies. The risk here is that Valgo builds a bridge that the market eventually walks past.

Now, the bull case — because I want to be honest about what would make me wrong. If "physical AI" truly becomes the defining technology deployment of the next decade — not just autonomous vehicles but surgical robots, warehouse automation, delivery drones, agricultural systems, all of which need insurance and none of which have claims history — then Valgo isn't solving a narrow AV problem. It's building the risk quantification infrastructure for the entire physical AI economy. In that world, Valgo accumulates proprietary simulation-to-loss datasets across hundreds of autonomy verticals, creating data network effects that no competitor can replicate. The "Algorithms for Validation" textbook becomes the equivalent of Black-Scholes for physical AI risk, and Valgo becomes the canonical pricing engine that every insurer on the planet uses. If the physical AI market truly reaches the scale projected and every deployment requires insurance pricing that only simulation-based methods can provide, the consequence magnitude is dramatically larger than the AV-specific framing suggests. I can see a world where this is a $10 billion infrastructure company. But that requires the "physical AI" framing to be real and broad, not a pitch deck wrapper around an autonomous vehicle insurance play.

The timing trajectory is favorable. Waymo is scaling. Aurora is pushing autonomous trucking toward commercial deployment. Goldman Sachs is projecting 50% insurance cost reductions from autonomy. The exponential technology curve in AI and robotics is clearly accelerating, and the gap between "these systems work" and "these systems are insured" is becoming acute. Valgo is positioned ahead of a real inflection — building before the demand is fully formed but after the trajectory is clear. That's the Gretzky principle, and on timing alone, they score well.

What concerns me beyond the consequence magnitude is Koop Technologies. Koop has $7M in funding, a Lloyd's of London partnership, and has been operating in this space since 2021. They're vertically integrated — data ingestion through policy issuance. Valgo positions as infrastructure to insurers, but Koop already controls the end-to-end workflow for the most advanced autonomy companies. A horizontally positioned risk model provider competing against a vertically integrated incumbent that already writes policies is a strategic challenge. The team composition — three Stanford PhDs from the same lab plus an insurance executive — is deliberately constructed and addresses the dual-sided market risk intelligently. But I'd want to see evidence that autonomy companies are willing to share simulation data with an independent intermediary when Koop offers a one-stop solution.

I respect this team enormously. They may be the best three people on Earth for this specific technical problem. But my job isn't to invest in the best team for a problem — it's to invest in problems whose consequence of success justifies every risk. Valgo is building critical infrastructure for a massive transition, and I don't dismiss that. I just can't get to the conviction that this is a problem whose solution transforms the physical world rather than lubricates a transaction between two existing industries. I'm a pass at this stage, but I'd watch the physical AI thesis closely. If Valgo demonstrates that their risk models generalize across five or ten categories of autonomous systems — not just vehicles — the consequence magnitude shifts materially, and I'd re-engage.
