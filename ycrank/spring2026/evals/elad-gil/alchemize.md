# Alchemize -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Alchemize is a workflow-replacement bet on US customs brokerage where Samuel Fu and Robert Hou are wagering that LLM reliability plus tariff-policy chaos has cracked open a $5.48B market (Mordor Intelligence, 2026) that legacy brokers built on "1990s software or cobbled-together Excel" (techbuzz.ai, 2025) can't defend. The signature here is a real-but-weak catalyst paired with the absence of an index distribution position -- exactly the shape where Flexport's installed base eats the AI-native upstart for lunch.

The catalyst dimension is where Alchemize sits highest, and it's still only mid-ladder. **Anduril** (2022) is the high rung -- Ukraine + Pentagon software inadequacy was a discrete 24-month re-opening with a named procurement bottleneck. The low rung is the **Lyft** Series C profile, where the market was growing but no specific catalyst had changed the math. Alchemize lands between these: the sub-15% Customs Broker License Exam pass rate plus aging workforce (broker median age >43, Mordor Intelligence 2026) plus tariff volatility plus LLMs hitting HTS-classification reliability *is* a real catalyst, evidenced by Flexport launching its Customs AI Suite in October 2025 and Amari AI raising $4.5M in 2025. But the underlying US customs market CAGR is 2.88% -- the catalyst opened a door, not a re-opening on the order of payments-post-PayPal or defense-post-Ukraine.

Index position is where Alchemize sits lowest, and this is the dimension that breaks the bet. **Stripe** (2010) is the high rung -- an index on e-commerce, where Stripe gets paid as every new entrant in the category touches the rail. **Decagon** (2023) is the mid rung -- workflow in the path of every post-LLM customer-support team. The low rung is a thin vertical app fighting named incumbents on features. Alchemize is at the low rung: it is a *brokerage*, not a layer that other brokers integrate against. Every entry Alchemize files is a zero-sum win against Flexport, KlearNow.AI ($69M raised, Crunchbase), Expeditors, and Livingston. Amari AI is the smart positioning here -- it sells AI to licensed brokers rather than competing with them. Alchemize chose the harder side of that fork.

Revenue velocity is invisible, and in the AI cycle silence is itself a negative signal -- the **Harvey** (2023) "$3B → $5B → $8B in months" template (TechCrunch, Nov 2025) is what "just working" looks like in dollars, and the dossier returns "No public data found" on revenue, pricing page, or customer logos. Pre-seed defaults apply, but the Hustle Commons framework of "stuff that is just working" can't be invoked without numbers.

Founder execution rhythm is the **Perplexity** (2022) ladder -- Aravind pinging me on LinkedIn and showing up a week later with the thing built. The low rung is the credential-without-artifacts founder. Alchemize lands at the low rung: Samuel Fu's surfaced headline is "Building AI-enabled customs brokerage" with no shipped products or GitHub repos in the public record, and Robert Hou's profile wasn't even retrievable (LinkedIn status 999). Neither founder has documented customs, freight, or trade-compliance history. The "single miracle" test makes it worse -- Alchemize requires LLM-accuracy-on-HTS-classification *plus* acquiring or hiring a licensed customs broker (binary regulatory gate) *plus* unseating incumbents that already control the Power-of-Attorney relationship. That's at least two miracles where **Color Genomics** (2015) was structured to need one.

My 36-month forecast: Alchemize lands at $1-4M ARR and gets acqui-hired by a mid-tier broker or by KlearNow.AI for sub-$50M, or shuts down -- a non-venture outcome driven by Flexport's AI Customs Suite (0.2% claimed auditor error rate, October 2025) absorbing the mid-market importers who would otherwise be Alchemize's wedge before Alchemize can build a license-and-relationship moat. The mechanism is straightforward: in a 2.88% CAGR market where the index position is already held by incumbents now shipping AI, an AI-native broker without documented customs-domain founders is a margin-compression bet, not a category bet.

The strongest bull case -- the one that stings -- is that customs brokerage at >$3T in US imports is exactly the kind of "boring infrastructure layer on a hard regulatory thing" that fits my Unstated Pattern (workflow-infrastructure-on-top-of-hard-thing, like **Anduril** and **Color**), and that Flexport's AI suite is bolted onto a freight-forwarder P&L it doesn't want to cannibalize, leaving an actual wedge for an AI-native pure-play. The legacy-broker unit-economics-cannibalization argument the dossier flags is real. But I dismantle it on founder-market fit: every Unstated-Pattern exception I made -- **Anduril** (Stephens at Palantir), **Color** (I operated it), **Mistral** (Llama at Meta) -- was gated by founders who had *shipped in the adjacent hard thing*. Alchemize's Phase 3 surfaced zero customs-domain artifacts for either founder, which is the disqualifier, not the pedigree. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: signed POAs with 10+ named mid-market importers within 6 months showing >20% MoM entry-volume growth, *and* a named licensed customs broker on the cap table. Without those, this is the **Lyft** Series C shape -- a worse version of the obvious bet.
