# Mantis -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that immediately stands out about Mantis is not the product or the market. It's the Louiza Labs situation. Georgia Witchel was CTO and co-founder of Louiza Labs, which entered YC in Summer 2025 doing physics-based simulation of human biology for medical device testing. That company raised $5M+. Now she's back at YC one batch later, Winter 2026, as CEO of a separate company working in an adjacent domain. I've funded founders who failed and came back -- Emmett Shear and Justin Kan sold Kiko on eBay for $258K and I put money into their next thing without hesitation. But Louiza Labs didn't fail. It raised real money. When a co-founder leaves a funded, operating company to start something nearby, the questions multiply. What happened with the co-founder relationship? Where does the IP boundary sit? Is this a deeper insight she gained from building the first thing, or is it a founder who couldn't stay the course? The dossier doesn't answer any of these, and the answers matter enormously.

The idea origin is where I get stuck. How did Georgia discover that clinical trial data infrastructure was the thing to build? The technical proximity is clear -- she's been inside biomedical simulation, she has the right degrees (Harvey Mudd CS, UW Bioengineering), she built related technology at Louiza Labs. But proximity to a domain is not the same as organic discovery of a specific problem. The pitch leads with market statistics: 80% of trials delayed, $15M lost per trial to data quality issues. Those are the kind of numbers you find when you research a market, not when you stub your toe on a problem. Contrast this with how the best biotech infrastructure companies start -- someone who spent years running actual clinical trials, drowning in Excel files and broken EDC exports, who finally snaps and builds the tool they needed. I don't see that story here. What I see is a talented engineer whose technical skills in physics simulation could apply to clinical data, and who identified clinical data as a large market. That's technology seeking a problem, not a problem that demanded a solution.

The product positioning reinforces this concern. The dossier surfaces at least four different descriptions: "digital twin company," "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," "domain-aware data platform," and "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform." When founders describe their company four different ways, they haven't yet figured out what it is. I don't expect pre-seed companies to have perfect messaging. But I do expect them to be able to say what they do in one sentence without jargon. The spread here suggests the product concept is still searching for its center of gravity, which in turn suggests the problem understanding may still be shallow.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If building Louiza Labs taught Georgia that the real bottleneck in biomedical simulation wasn't the simulation layer but the underlying data infrastructure -- that the data feeding into physics models was so fragmented and dirty that no simulation could produce trustworthy results -- then Mantis is the classic "second company" insight. You build the first thing, discover the actual problem is one layer down, and go build that instead. This would be a strong organic discovery, earned through building rather than researching. And the schlep in clinical data is enormous: fragmented systems (EDC, CTMS, labs, omics), regulatory sensitivity, pharma enterprise sales cycles, and the technical challenge of encoding biological semantics into a data layer. That's the kind of painful complexity that deters everyone, which means whoever solves it builds a real moat. If Georgia is that person -- someone who stared into the data layer from the simulation side and saw what was broken -- this could be a significant company. But I'm constructing that narrative from possibility, not from evidence in front of me.

Georgia herself has real strengths as a technical founder. Harvey Mudd CS is serious. She was CTO and built things at Louiza Labs. She's been a founding engineer, an algorithm engineer. Eight world records in ice climbing tells you something about determination that no resume line can convey -- you don't break world records in a sport that involves climbing frozen waterfalls unless you have an unusual tolerance for discomfort and risk. But she's a solo founder building something that requires expertise spanning physics simulation, large language models, clinical informatics, and enterprise pharma sales. I've been consistent on this: co-founders are for a startup what location is for real estate. The two other team members aren't listed as co-founders. For a company that needs to be credible to pharma enterprises while simultaneously pushing the frontier on simulation and ML, a solo founder with a team of three is structurally thin. And the competitive landscape includes Unlearn.AI with $130M+ and EMA qualification, plus Simulants sitting on Medidata's 30,000-trial dataset. This is a fight where you need co-founders who complement your blind spots.

The traction picture is essentially blank -- no revenue signals, no user counts, seven Twitter followers. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of metrics. But I do look for any sign of organic pull: a pharma company that heard about this and reached out, a clinical researcher who tried the prototype and started using it daily, even a single LOI. Case studies are mentioned on the website, which could indicate some customer engagement, but nothing is confirmed publicly. The absence of pull combined with the positioning ambiguity makes me think the product is still pre-product-market-fit in the most literal sense -- the fit between this specific product and a specific customer's actual workflow hasn't been demonstrated yet.

I respect what Georgia has built in her career, and the clinical data schlep is real enough that whoever solves it will build something valuable. But right now I see a talented engineer whose technology skills are looking for their highest-value application, not a founder who discovered a specific problem through lived pain and can't rest until it's solved. The Louiza Labs split raises unanswered questions. The solo founder structure concerns me for a domain this demanding. And the positioning hasn't converged. I'd want to understand the Louiza Labs story -- specifically, what she learned building it that made clinical data infrastructure feel urgent -- before I could get to conviction.
