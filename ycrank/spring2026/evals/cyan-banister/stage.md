# Stage -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice immediately: this is a popular idea in a popular market built by founders from popular institutions. Developer tools for AI-assisted coding is one of the hottest categories in venture right now — CodeRabbit just raised $88 million, Greptile pulled in $29 million, Graphite was acquired for north of $290 million. Every VC in town wants exposure to this space. And that's exactly my problem. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Stage is neither. Any investor can understand "better code review UX" in thirty seconds. When I can assess a company that quickly, when the thesis requires no leap of imagination, when nobody in the room is uncomfortable — that tells me the weirdness has already been arbitraged away. The price of being right here is low, but so is the ceiling on being early.

I want to know how Charles and Dean got here — not the business, the story. And what the dossier gives me is Stanford Computer Science, Five Rings quantitative trading, Apple internships, Citadel, varsity athletics. These are accomplished, clearly talented people. But I'm listening for the specific hardship that forged their commitment to *this* problem, and I don't hear it. They experienced code review friction at a quant trading firm. That's a professional observation — a good one, probably — but it's not the kind of biographical thread I trust at the earliest stage. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else dismissed. When I felt Travis Kalanick's gravitas at Uber, it came from a place of genuine obsession that had nothing to do with credentials. Charles and Dean are smart engineers who noticed something annoying at work. That's how most developer tools get started, and most developer tools don't become defining companies.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The timing argument is real — AI code generation tools like Cursor and Claude Code are producing volumes of unfamiliar code that human reviewers have to evaluate, and the old GitHub diff interface was never designed for that world. If Stage genuinely nails the UX for comprehending AI-generated changes, they could catch a structural wave. Dean's background building an in-house coding agent at Five Rings gives him direct insight into what AI-generated code looks like from the reviewer's seat. And Graphite's acquisition by Cursor in December 2025 removed the leading independent competitor, opening a window. The "chapters" metaphor is clever — it maps to how engineers already think about their own changes, just not how tools present them. If I squint, I can see a scenario where this becomes the default review surface for a new era of AI-augmented development. But I'd need to feel something beyond "that's a reasonable product bet," and I don't.

What I uniquely look for — and what's absent here — is whether a company creates economic access or makes an invisible system visible for people who currently lack power. Uber created income for drivers. Affirm made credit terms transparent. Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities that had none. Stage makes code review more efficient for software engineers, who are already among the best-compensated professionals on earth. That's a valid business, but it's not the kind of investment that keeps me up at night with that jolt of energy I felt with Niantic. There's no underserved population gaining access to something they were locked out of. There's no opaque system being made transparent. It's an optimization tool for people who are already doing fine.

The co-founder dynamic is actually the strongest signal here. Both founders overlapped at Five Rings, both came through Stanford CS, and both were competitive varsity athletes — the kind of people who understand sustained performance under pressure. I learned from HQ Trivia that even a perfect product at the perfect time can't survive founder conflict, so I always probe this dimension. The shared professional history at Five Rings suggests they've already navigated working together in a high-performance environment, and that counts for something. But solid co-founder alignment in a consensus market with no moat and no biographical grit isn't enough for me to write a check from my own money.

The competitive reality is sobering. Haystack, one batch ahead at YC, is attacking the exact same problem with a different visual metaphor. The "structured chapters" concept is a UX innovation, not a deep technical moat — any well-resourced player could implement something similar. And Anthropic just launched a multi-agent code review tool for enterprise customers, signaling that the biggest AI labs view code review as their territory. Stage would need to move exceptionally fast to establish workflow lock-in before the window closes, and nothing in the dossier suggests they have distribution advantages or early traction that would let them do that.
