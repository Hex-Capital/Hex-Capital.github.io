# RefortifAI -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The history of digital rights management is a history of defeat. Every generation of software-only DRM — from DeCSS cracking DVD encryption in 1999, to Denuvo's game protection being broken within days of each major release, to Widevine L3 being stripped by hobbyists — tells the same story: software protecting digital assets against a motivated adversary with physical access to the hardware is not a defensible position, it is a continuously retreating one. RefortifAI is proposing to build DRM for the most valuable digital assets ever created — AI model weights worth billions in training compute — using the same structural approach that has failed in every adjacent domain. The question is not whether model weights need protection. They obviously do. The question is whether software-only obfuscation constitutes a secret about the world, or whether it constitutes a refusal to learn from the world.

The monopoly test fails at the structural level. What important truth does RefortifAI know that the market disputes? The implicit claim is that software-based weight obfuscation can provide meaningful protection without requiring hardware trusted execution environments. The security establishment would reject this — not because they deny the need for model protection, but because decades of DRM history demonstrate that software-only approaches are breakable given sufficient adversarial motivation. If the security establishment is wrong and software obfuscation works, the company has a business. But it still does not have a monopoly. DRM is an arms race, not a moat. Each new protection scheme provokes new extraction techniques, requiring continuous investment to maintain parity. There are no network effects. Switching costs exist but are moderate — a model provider can redistribute weights with a different protection scheme or switch to TEE-based approaches as hardware matures. The structural pattern of DRM companies is not independent dominance but absorption: Widevine was acquired by Google, Denuvo by Irdeto, Macrovision became Rovi and then TiVo. DRM vendors become features of larger platforms, not platforms themselves. The Protect AI acquisition by Palo Alto Networks at $500M-plus confirms exactly this trajectory for AI security: competent companies in the space get absorbed, they do not become the next Palantir.

The power-law calculation follows directly from this structural diagnosis. If the ceiling for an AI security company is a $500 million acquisition — the Protect AI benchmark — and the model weight DRM sub-segment is narrower than the full AI security category, then the realistic best case for RefortifAI is an acquisition in the low hundreds of millions. That is a fine outcome for founders but structurally irrelevant to a fund built around the premise that the single best investment must return the entire fund. A $200 million exit on a pre-seed check is not zero-to-one — it is one-to-three. The addressable market statistics cited in the dossier — $463 billion confidential computing by 2034 — are the kind of galaxy-brained TAM numbers that include hardware TEEs, cloud confidential computing, and a dozen categories that have nothing to do with software DRM for model weights. Leading with TAM rather than with a specific insight about the world is precisely the thinking pattern I warn against.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that software-only weight obfuscation is technically durable enough to resist state-level adversaries, that the market for on-premise model deployment grows large enough to sustain an independent platform company rather than a feature, and that RefortifAI establishes the standard before Palo Alto Networks, NVIDIA, or the inference engine maintainers themselves build protection natively. If all three hold, the "anti-cheat for AI" framing is apt — Riot Games' Vanguard anti-cheat runs at the kernel level and has proven more durable than prior approaches, and perhaps inference-engine-level integration creates a similarly defensible position. The team's composition — RTL design at Tenstorrent and Texas Instruments, security research under Somesh Jha at Wisconsin, AI engineering at KLA's semiconductor inspection group — is legitimately well-suited to the specific technical challenge of hardened GPU runtime engineering. The drop-in replacement for vLLM and SGLang is a real distribution insight: meet developers where they already are. These are not negligible signals. But they describe a competent team in an arms-race business, not a monopoly in formation.

There is no government pathway visible here, no physical-world barrier, no network effect, and no evidence of the kind of definite conviction that would make me lean forward despite structural concerns. The founders come from engineering roles at established companies — competent but without the prior high-stakes venture or the audacious multi-year plan that characterizes my highest-conviction bets. The dossier reveals zero traction signals — no revenue, no LOIs, no named customers, no waitlist. At pre-seed, I do not penalize the absence of scale, but I do look for organic pull: someone requesting this product before it was built. That signal is absent. Competition is for losers, and an arms race against every reverse engineer and well-funded incumbent security division is the purest form of competition there is.
