# The Token Company -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact about The Token Company is not in its own dossier -- it is that Y Combinator accepted a second company in the same batch, Compresr, doing essentially the same thing. Two companies in one cohort pursuing API-based LLM prompt compression. Microsoft Research has an open-source implementation with 4,400 GitHub stars. Apple has published research on the same technique. A NAACL 2025 survey paper catalogs the breadth of existing approaches. This is not a secret. This is a crowded room where everyone arrived at the same insight simultaneously -- the surest sign that the insight is consensus, not contrarian. When I ask my standard question -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- the answer here appears to be "LLM prompts are bloated and can be compressed." Every AI researcher on the planet agrees with this. The mimetic convergence is total.

The company's one genuinely interesting claim -- that compression *improves* LLM output quality rather than merely preserving it -- deserves scrutiny because it is the only potential escape hatch from the commodity trap. If compressed prompts consistently produce better answers across diverse workloads, that would mean natural language instructions actively degrade LLM performance. That would be a real insight. The benchmarks cited are specific: +2.7 percentage points on financial QA, +4.0 on reading comprehension, a 268K-vote blind arena favoring compressed outputs. These numbers are real data, not hand-waving. But a genuine secret must also create a path to monopoly, and here the chain breaks. If compression reliably improves outputs, this truth will be published, replicated, and eventually absorbed by the LLM providers themselves -- who have every incentive to deliver better answers to their paying customers. A secret that anyone can verify and exploit within eighteen months is a temporary advantage, not a monopoly.

The structural economics are deeply unfavorable. LLM inference costs are declining roughly 10x per year. Building a cost-optimization middleware layer on top of a rapidly deflating commodity is the equivalent of founding a fuel-efficiency consultancy during a transition to free energy. The company attempts to pivot the value proposition toward quality improvement, which is strategically correct but reveals that the original cost-savings thesis is already eroding before the company has scaled. Consumption-based pricing at $0.05 per million tokens creates vanishing revenue as underlying token costs approach zero -- the pricing model is structurally coupled to the very deflation that threatens the business. This is a 1-to-n optimization play dressed in 0-to-1 language.

Otso Veisterä is technically impressive for an 18-year-old. Programming since age 7, national physics champion, building proprietary ML models in PyTorch and CUDA, coding a Chrome-to-Firefox extension converter in Rust at 69 GitHub stars. These are legitimate signals of raw intellectual horsepower. I have backed teenage founders before -- Vitalik Buterin was 19 when he created the concept for Ethereum -- but what distinguished Buterin was not his age or his programming ability. It was his vision of a fundamentally new computing paradigm that most experts said was impossible or unnecessary. Veisterä conceived this product at a hackathon and iterated from "Otsofy" to "The Token Company" -- the pattern of discovery and rebranding signals a builder searching for product-market fit, not a visionary executing a definite plan for the future. The second team member's background in VC analytics rather than deep ML research does not compensate for the absence of a clearly articulated, conviction-driven vision of where this goes.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that compression-as-quality-improvement is a durable, non-replicable advantage; that LLM providers will never internalize this capability despite clear incentives to do so; and that The Token Company can dominate this niche against Microsoft's open-source alternative, a same-batch YC competitor with four EPFL PhD researchers, and whatever Apple or Google ship next quarter. If all three are true, you get a real middleware business that sits in the inference pipeline of every LLM application. The Pax Historia case study -- 193 billion tokens per month, measurable purchase lift -- demonstrates that the product works for at least one high-volume customer. But "works" and "monopoly" are different categories. Drop-in middleware can be dropped out with equal ease. There are no switching costs, no network effects, and no structural reason why this company, rather than any of its competitors, becomes the last mover. The dossier itself identifies "LLM provider economic misalignment with self-compression" as a moat, but misalignment is a temporary market structure, not a permanent one. When the incentive to internalize compression exceeds the revenue lost from shorter prompts -- and improving output quality clearly crosses that threshold -- the middleware layer gets absorbed into the platform.

This is a competent team solving a real but consensus problem in a market structure that systematically destroys value. The absence of a genuine secret, the presence of multiple funded competitors pursuing identical approaches, and the deflation of the underlying commodity make this a pass. Competition in prompt compression is already underway, and as I have said in other contexts, all failed companies share the same fate: they failed to escape the gravitational pull of competition.
