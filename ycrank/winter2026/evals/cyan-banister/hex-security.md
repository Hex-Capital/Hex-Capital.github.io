# Hex Security -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me immediately: this is a crowded room. XBOW has $117 million, Novee has $51.5 million with ex-IDF offensive cyber founders, Aikido just hit a billion-dollar valuation with $13 million in ARR. There are two other YC companies — Casco and MindFort — building essentially the same product right now, in the same building. Open-source tools are proliferating. When I look at a space and see that much capital and that many smart teams converging on the same insight simultaneously, that tells me the consensus has already formed. AI-automated pentesting is the hot category of 2025-2026. And the investments that have shaped my career — SpaceX when private space was considered delusional, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded certifiable — they all shared one quality: nobody else wanted in. That's not what I see here.

The founder story is where I always start, and it's where I'm struggling with Hex Security. I want to hear how someone got here — not the resume, but the thread that connects their life to this specific problem in a way they couldn't walk away from. Ahmad Khan has a decade of real application security work at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM, and I respect that deeply. He's lived inside the pain of manual pentesting and knows exactly how broken it is. That's meaningful domain expertise. But the dossier doesn't give me the survival story — the moment that forged an unbreakable commitment. Huzaifa Ahmad built Talently to reverse-engineer applicant tracking systems and sent 200,000 job applications, which shows hustle and a hacker's instinct. Prama Yudhistira built AI coding agents at Codegen. These are capable engineers who spotted an opportunity. But I'm listening for something deeper — the biographical thread that tells me this founder will keep going through year three when XBOW has raised another $200 million and enterprise buyers are defaulting to the incumbent with the bigger sales team.

The three founders have no shared prior employer, no shared university, and no public evidence of a pre-existing relationship. After HQ Trivia — which was the right product at the right time and still collapsed because two co-founders couldn't resolve their conflicts — I probe this dimension hard. Three people from different backgrounds converging on a startup is not inherently a problem, but when I can't see how they found each other, how they've navigated disagreement, or what holds them together under pressure, that's a gap in my ability to assess the team's durability. I'd want to sit with them and ask: tell me about a time you three seriously disagreed, and how did you get through it?

Now, let me be honest about the bull case, because it's real. If you believe the market for automated pentesting is big enough to support multiple winners — and at $4.4 billion by 2031 it might be — then Hex Security's scrappy GTM of finding critical vulnerabilities across dozens of YC companies is exactly the kind of proof-of-work I admire. SQL injection exposing billions of records, a proof-of-concept worm that could infect entire networks — that's not a demo, that's a product that works. If Ahmad Khan's decade of hands-on security experience translates into a reinforcement learning training loop that compounds over time, and if the YC network gives them enough deployment surface to build a data moat before the well-funded competitors lock up the enterprise, this could be a very good company. There's also something genuinely valuable about making enterprise-grade security testing accessible to startups and mid-market companies that currently can't afford a six-figure manual pentest. That democratization angle resonates with me.

But I keep coming back to the same question I ask myself with every deal: is this unpopular, difficult to assess, and carrying real but surmountable technology risk? AI pentesting is popular — overwhelmingly so. It's not difficult to assess — every security-minded investor understands the thesis immediately. And the technology risk has been substantially de-risked by the frontier models that all these competitors are building on. The competitive dynamics concern me more than anything. Hex Security is entering a market where XBOW already has enterprise bank customers and a top HackerOne ranking, where Novee's founders have elite military offensive cyber backgrounds, and where Aikido is growing 5x year-over-year with a broader platform. The brand confusion with Hex Technologies (hex.tech, $150M+ raised) adds a practical headache on top of a strategic one. I need to feel that the founders have an unfair advantage that capital alone can't replicate, and I don't see it clearly enough here to write a check from my own money.
