# Squid -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Squid is something most investors would walk right past: a two-person pre-seed startup already running production infrastructure at National Grid. Not a pilot. Not a signed LOI. National Grid DSO launched a public-facing FlexPortal in February 2026 described as "powered by Squid." When a major national grid operator puts your software in front of the world, that's not a design partnership -- that's a customer who has already decided they need what you built. I've seen companies raise Series B rounds with less impressive customer evidence than this.

The organic discovery here is textbook. Both founders worked inside the broken system they're now fixing. George Kolokotronis was Head of Technology at Octopus Energy. Conor Jones worked at National Grid and BCG. They didn't read about grid planning inefficiencies in a TechCrunch article and decide it sounded like a good market. They sat in the rooms where planning decisions were being made across emails, spreadsheets, and conflicting model versions. They lived with the mess. Then they tried once with VoltQuant -- a grid intelligence platform that served these same customers -- and learned that the real leverage wasn't in intelligence for project developers but in the planning workflow itself. This pattern of founders who tried once and came back with a more fundamental solution reminds me of what happened with Justin.tv. Emmett Shear and Justin Kan needed to fail at lifecasting before they could see that game streaming was the real product. VoltQuant was Squid's Kiko.

The schlep in grid planning software is the kind that makes smart founders look the other way. You need SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications before a utility will even take your call. Sales cycles run 6-18 months. The data lives in proprietary GIS formats and legacy systems that haven't been modernized in decades. Your buyers are regulated monopolies that move at institutional speed. Every software engineer who's ever looked at utility infrastructure knows the tools are ancient -- but the regulatory compliance overhead, the procurement bureaucracy, and the institutional inertia create an invisible barrier that filters out almost everyone. Squid already has both certifications as a two-person team. That alone tells you something about these founders' willingness to do the ugly work.

The competitive landscape actually strengthens the case if you look at it through the right lens. Envelio got acquired by E.ON in 2021, which means every utility that competes with E.ON now has a reason to avoid Envelio's platform -- you don't want your planning infrastructure owned by a competitor. GE Vernova bundles grid planning inside massive enterprise suites alongside hardware, meaning a utility that wants better planning software has to buy the whole stack. Squid is offering the unbundled, browser-native alternative that an incumbent literally cannot build without cannibalizing their existing high-margin contracts. This is the structural advantage I look for: a lightweight tool that fits into the gap between what incumbents can offer and what customers actually need, protected by the fact that the incumbents' own business models prevent them from competing.

The bull case is strong. If Squid becomes the default planning platform for distribution network operators the way Stripe became the default payment layer for developers, the switching costs compound with every planning decision that gets versioned and tracked in their system. National Grid is proof that the product works. The question is whether the founders can sell beyond their personal network. Can they get to DNOs in Germany, France, the US -- utilities where they don't have a former employer relationship? The 6-18 month sales cycle means this won't show the 5-7% weekly growth I typically want to see. But in enterprise infrastructure, the right metric isn't weekly growth -- it's whether each new customer becomes deeply embedded. Grid planning software, once adopted as the single source of truth, creates the kind of data lock-in that makes churn nearly impossible.

The gaps are real but bounded. No revenue is visible -- the pricing page says "Coming Soon" at $0, which means either they're still figuring out monetization or they're using a freemium wedge to get into more utilities before charging. Customer concentration in two organizations from the founders' prior employer network is a risk, but at pre-seed, having *any* production deployment at a major utility is unusual enough to outweigh the concentration concern. The growth velocity constraint from enterprise sales cycles is structural -- this company will never grow like a self-serve SaaS product. But the flip side is that each customer relationship, once won, is extraordinarily durable. I'd want to understand their pipeline beyond National Grid and Octopus before writing the check, but the foundation is solid enough that I'd take the meeting.
