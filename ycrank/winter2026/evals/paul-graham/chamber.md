# Chamber -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Chamber is that it's operating in exactly the kind of category I warned about. GPU infrastructure optimization is not a hidden problem. Every CTO at every AI company knows their GPU utilization is terrible. Every cloud provider knows it. NVIDIA knows it so well they spent $700 million buying Run:ai. When the problem is this visible and this much money is already flowing toward it, you're not exploiting schlep blindness -- you're entering a known battlefield. Stripe worked because thousands of programmers unconsciously avoided the banking and regulatory pain for over a decade. Nobody is unconsciously avoiding GPU optimization. They're consciously throwing billions at it.

That said, the founding team's relationship to this problem is genuine, and that matters. These four founders didn't read about GPU waste in a TechCrunch article and decide to start a company. They built infrastructure optimization at Amazon together and delivered what they claim is hundreds of millions in cost savings. Jason Ong specifically worked on GPU scheduling tooling. Shaocheng Wang spent nine years building observability at AWS. When a team has been inside the machinery of this exact problem at one of the few companies operating at sufficient scale to understand it deeply, the idea isn't manufactured -- it grew from their own work. The organic origin is real even if the problem itself isn't hidden. This is more like what happened with many enterprise infrastructure companies: founders who built internal tools at big companies and realized the external market needed the same thing. That's a legitimate pattern, even if it's not my favorite pattern.

The structural opening created by NVIDIA's Run:ai acquisition is the strongest thing Chamber has going for it beyond the team's domain expertise. NVIDIA is open-sourcing Run:ai, which simultaneously commoditizes the commercial incumbent and ties its future development to NVIDIA's hardware ecosystem. Enterprises running mixed GPU environments -- hybrid cloud, multi-vendor -- have a real need for a vendor-neutral orchestration layer that NVIDIA is structurally disincentivized to build well. This is the kind of market event that creates genuine windows. But windows close. Every infrastructure startup in the YC W26 batch and beyond can see this same opening. The question is whether Chamber can move fast enough to own it before the next well-funded team shows up.

What concerns me most is the absence of any evidence that someone actually wants this product. No revenue, no user count, no waitlist numbers, no case studies, no testimonials. 82 Product Hunt upvotes with 4 reviews is the kind of signal I have to squint to see. For a product that deploys with a single Helm command onto existing Kubernetes clusters, adoption friction should be low enough that even at pre-seed you'd expect some signal of organic pull -- a handful of platform engineers installing the monitoring dashboard, a few companies testing the free tier. If that traction exists, it's not public, and the absence of even anecdotal evidence is a gap. At pre-seed I don't require revenue, but I want to see someone pulling the product toward them.

The team profile also gives me pause in a specific way. Charles Ding, the CEO, is a second-time founder with a prior exit and an MBA from Booth. That's a fine background for many companies, but for developer infrastructure, I'd rather see a founder whose identity is primarily "programmer" rather than "engineering leader with an MBA." Andreas Bloomquist's background -- economics degree, web development bootcamp, product development -- is similarly more operational than technical. The deep technical capability lives in Ong and Wang, but the absence of any public GitHub presence for a developer tools company is unusual. When I backed the Collison brothers, or Amjad at Replit, or Steve and Alexis at Reddit, these were people whose hacker identity was visible from space. Chamber's team reads more like experienced engineering managers who decided to start a company than hackers who couldn't stop building.

The bull case is straightforward and real: four people who literally built this capability at Amazon, attacking a validated $700M market where the incumbent just got absorbed into a hardware vendor, with a product that deploys in one command and starts with a free tier. If Chamber can accumulate enough production deployments to train genuinely superior AI models for fault detection and demand forecasting -- the data flywheel -- the switching costs compound and the moat deepens. That's a legitimate path. And the "why now" is cleaner than most: Run:ai's acquisition created the opening, GPU scarcity makes optimization urgent, and the agentic AI capabilities that enable autonomous orchestration are only now becoming reliable enough. If I'm wrong about this being a consensus category, and it turns out that the combination of Amazon-grade domain expertise plus vendor neutrality plus agentic AI is rarer than the competitive landscape suggests, Chamber could be significantly undervalued.

But I keep coming back to the same concern. When I look at my best investments, the founders were working on problems that other people's unconscious minds wouldn't let them see. Chamber is working on a problem that every AI infrastructure team on the planet is staring directly at. The execution may be strong, the team may be qualified, and the timing may be right. But this is a company entering a crowded, well-lit room, not one that found a door nobody else noticed.
