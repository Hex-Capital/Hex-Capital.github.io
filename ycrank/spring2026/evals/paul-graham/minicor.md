# Minicor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about Minicor is the sheer unpleasantness of what they're building. Automating legacy Windows desktop applications — EHRs, dental practice management systems, automotive dealer platforms — through the UI because there are no APIs. Then wrapping that in SOC 2 Type II certification and HIPAA compliance, deploying on Citrix environments, and making it reliable enough for healthcare workflows handling 25,000 patients a day. This is the kind of problem that makes most founders physically recoil. I wrote about this in "Schlep Blindness" — your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. Minicor is neck-deep in schlep territory, which is exactly why I find it interesting. The competitive moat in this kind of business isn't technical brilliance; it's the willingness to do work that repels everyone else. SOC 2 Type II at pre-seed with a five-person team is not a credential you stumble into. That represents months of deliberate, unglamorous effort that most pre-seed founders would never prioritize.

What makes the founder story work is that both of them lived inside the problem before they decided to solve it. Saheed spent three years as a senior engineer at Ford Motor Company and before that worked at Morgan Stanley — two organizations where the systems of record are exactly the kind of entrenched Windows desktop applications Minicor automates. Faizaan worked at Phoenix, a YC W21 company, connecting systems together. These aren't founders who read a market report about RPA and decided the TAM was attractive. They sat inside legacy enterprise environments, experienced the integration pain firsthand, and recognized that the arrival of computer-use AI models created a new way to attack it. That's the "live in the future, then build what's missing" pattern I look for — except here the "future" was AI models that can see and interact with screens, and the "missing" was the managed infrastructure to make that reliable in production.

The co-founder relationship also checks important boxes. They met in 2019 in a UX class at Ottawa, built a non-profit called Software For Love together during the pandemic, and started Minicor in 2023. That's six-plus years of working together before this company. I've seen so many startups die from co-founder conflicts — it's one of the top killers. These two have been through the hardest test of a working relationship, which is building something together when nobody's paying you. The non-profit background also tells me something about their motivation. They built software for organizations that needed it, for free, before anyone was watching. That's the kind of earnestness I look for but rarely find articulated in a dossier.

The architecture decision is worth noting because it reveals engineering taste. Minicor stores automations as deterministic code and only invokes the AI agent for recovery and edge cases. This is exactly the right design for healthcare and financial services — you want the happy path to be predictable and auditable, with AI as the self-healing layer when something unexpected happens. Most teams in this space would lead with "we're an AI agent company" and make everything probabilistic. Minicor made the harder, less marketable choice that's actually correct for their customers. That kind of judgment is hard to teach.

The commoditization risk is the obvious objection. Anthropic released Computer Use in public beta, and any team with API access can build desktop automation. But this misunderstands where the actual difficulty lives. The model is a component; the product is managed Windows VMs at scale, compliance certifications, observability with full video recordings, Citrix support, and accumulated knowledge of how specific legacy UIs behave across versions. UiPath has already shipped an "AI Healing Agent" feature, which validates the direction but also reveals the disadvantage of bolting AI onto a legacy RPA architecture versus building API-first from scratch. The real question is whether Minicor's positioning — selling to AI companies that are themselves selling into legacy industries — creates enough distribution velocity before incumbents close the gap. That's a genuine race, and I don't know who wins. But I'd rather bet on the small team that chose this problem from lived experience than on UiPath's AI features committee.

Where I have less conviction is in the founder stories I can't find. I want to hear about specific moments when things broke and how they hacked around it. The dossier gives me their resumes and their production metrics, but not the narratives that reveal determination under pressure. Saheed built a lottery system and payment processor in Nigeria — that sounds like exactly the kind of scrappy, resourceful work I'd want to hear more about, but the details aren't here. The production deployment at "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" is impressive for a company founded in 2024, and "thousands of workflows a week" suggests real usage, not just a demo. But I'm scoring partly on inference rather than direct observation of the animal quality I typically look for in a 10-minute interview.

This reminds me, in structure if not in scale, of the Stripe bet. Not because Minicor will be Stripe — that's absurd to predict — but because the pattern rhymes: founders who worked inside the systems they're now building infrastructure for, tackling a compliance-heavy integration problem that most developers consider beneath them, with an API-first product that makes a painful thing simple for other developers. The Collisons made payments infrastructure boring in the best possible way. Minicor is trying to make legacy system integration boring in the same way. I'd write a check here. It's not my highest-conviction bet, because I'm working with a dossier instead of a 10-minute conversation, and the commoditization timeline is a real unknown. But the schlep factor, the genuine founder-market fit, the earnest builder background, and the already-deployed production infrastructure at pre-seed — that's enough signal.
