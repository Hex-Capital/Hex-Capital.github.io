# Overdrive Health -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Overdrive Health isn't the AI or the billing -- it's how popular this category is right now. AKASA has raised $205 million. Candid Health just closed a $99.5 million Series C. CodaMetrix has $95 million. And within YC alone, there are at least two other companies -- Amby Health and CareSwift -- going after the exact same ambulance billing niche. When I see that kind of capital concentration and competitive density in a space, my entire body tenses up. The most promising companies are the ones nobody wants to fund because they look ridiculous. AI medical billing looks like a very good idea to a very large number of very well-capitalized investors, and that's precisely the signal that tells me the price of being right here has already been bid up.

If I could sit down with Michael and Daniel, the first thing I'd want to hear is their story -- not the EliseAI playbook, not the TAM slide, but how they personally arrived at ambulance billing. And what I see in the dossier is... a gap where that story should be. These are two elite-track engineers -- Harvard, Duke, Jane Street, Citadel, EliseAI -- who built AI agents at a healthcare unicorn and then pattern-matched to the next vertical. That's intelligent. It's logical. It's exactly the kind of career optimization that produces competent companies but rarely produces the ones that change everything. When I backed SpaceX, it wasn't because Elon had a resume that qualified him for rocket science -- it was because his obsession with Mars was so deep and personal that failure wasn't an option he could psychologically accept. I don't see that kind of biographical inevitability connecting these founders to ambulance billing. They could just as easily be automating dental billing or property management invoicing.

The acquire-and-automate model is the genuinely interesting move here, and I want to give it its due. Buying a traditional ambulance billing agency with ~20 providers and $50 million in annual claims, then deploying AI to nearly double revenue per employee -- that's operationally clever. It reminds me a little of what attracted me to Crusoe Energy: acquiring physical infrastructure and then layering technology on top to create economics that pure software companies can't replicate. If Overdrive can repeat this acquisition playbook across multiple agencies, they could roll up a fragmented market with cost structures that traditional billers simply can't match. The bull case is a Constellation Software-style acquisition engine powered by AI economics, building an unassailable position in EMS billing before expanding to other specialties. That's a real business. And the regulatory complexity of operating as a billing services provider -- bearing direct HIPAA and CMS responsibility -- does create a moat that scares away the pure SaaS players.

But I keep coming back to a detail that would keep me up at night: the founder role confusion. The YC page lists Michael Schroeder as the sole founder and CEO. LinkedIn and other sources show Daniel Inge as Co-Founder and CEO, with Michael as Co-Founder and CTO. That's not a typo -- that's a fundamental disagreement about who runs the company and who gets credit for founding it, playing out in public. After HQ Trivia, where I watched a perfectly timed product get destroyed by co-founder conflict, I've learned to treat this kind of ambiguity as a canary in the coal mine. Maybe it's an innocent administrative oversight. But the fact that YC's own page appears to erase one co-founder while he's listed as CEO elsewhere? That's exactly the kind of unresolved dynamic I probe before writing a check.

The economic access angle is real but indirect. Efficient ambulance billing helps small EMS providers survive, which matters enormously for rural communities that depend on local ambulance services. There's a legitimate "making invisible systems visible" story here -- the billing process between providers and payers is opaque, and AI automation could bring transparency. But the primary economic beneficiary is the billing operation itself through higher margins, not underserved communities directly. Compare that to Uber, where the driver on the other side of the app was earning income that didn't exist before, or Affirm, where the consumer was getting transparent credit for the first time. Here, the value creation flows to the business layer, not to the people at the edges of the system.

I respect what these founders have built -- operational traction, relevant experience, a smart go-to-market strategy. In a different market environment, with a biographical thread connecting them to this problem and less competitive heat in the category, I'd lean in harder. But my portfolio was built on backing the weird, the dismissed, and the deeply personal before consensus formed. Overdrive Health is competent execution in a consensus category, and that's simply not where I invest.
