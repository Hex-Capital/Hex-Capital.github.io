# Docura Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did this founder find this problem? Akhil Sachdev is a UC Berkeley CS grad with internships at Rivian, Salesforce, and Zuora. His GitHub has OCR and document-processing repos. There is zero documented connection to healthcare, workers' compensation, or medico-legal practice. No family member who's a QME. No prior job at a medico-legal firm. No essay about watching a physician drown in paperwork. What I see instead is someone with document-processing technical skills who surveyed markets where those skills could apply and landed on medico-legal reports. That's the pattern I described in "How to Get Startup Ideas" as the dangerous one -- starting from your capabilities and searching for a problem, rather than starting from a problem you're already living with. I can't prove this origin story is manufactured, but the absence of any organic backstory is itself informative. When founders stumble into a problem through lived experience, they can't help telling you about the moment it happened. When they don't volunteer that story, it's usually because there isn't one.

Now, here's what makes this interesting despite that concern: the schlep is enormous, and it's real. Medico-legal reports in workers' comp require compliance with AMA Guides 5th and 6th Edition, state-specific regulations, HIPAA, and the exacting standards of documents used in legal proceedings. This is exactly the kind of problem most founders unconsciously filter out. Look at the competitive landscape -- Wisedocs raised $20M and has $14.4M in revenue, but they sell to insurance carriers. DigitalOwl got acquired by Datavant, but they also sell to insurers. CLARA Analytics, Gradient AI, same story. Every well-funded player in this space chose the easier customer: the insurance company writing big checks. Nobody built for the evaluating physician, because the physician market is fragmented (roughly 1,900 active QMEs in California), the compliance requirements are brutal, and the sales cycle requires trust-building with doctors who are personally liable for report accuracy. That's schlep blindness operating exactly as I described it -- the problem is obvious, the market participants are right there, but everyone unconsciously avoids the hard sell.

But willingness to wade into a schlep is not the same as having the domain knowledge to survive it. This is where the solo founder concern becomes structural rather than stylistic. I required Drew Houston to find a co-founder, and Dropbox was a consumer product where the domain was "files." Sachdev is building for physicians generating legally binding medical reports, in a regulated industry, with no documented healthcare expertise and no co-founder who brings that expertise. When I see a team of one with no hiring plans on the YC page, building a product where an AI hallucination in an impairment rating could expose a physician to legal liability, I worry about the mismatch between ambition and team composition. The bull case would require Sachdev to find a co-founder with deep medico-legal domain knowledge -- someone who has actually written these reports -- and to do it soon. That's the Drew Houston path: the organic problem-founder fit was so strong that I made co-founder recruitment a condition rather than a rejection. Here, the organic fit is weaker, which makes the co-founder gap harder to excuse.

The traction signals are thin but not empty. "Providers using the platform have doubled their case volume" is interesting if true -- a physician processing twice as many cases is earning twice as much money, and that's the kind of economic dependency that's hard to fake. One named testimonial from Irina Williams, MD, QME, is better than zero. The product exists: a web app at app.docurahealth.com, a Chrome extension, reports generated in under five minutes. Sachdev built all of this himself, which is the kind of technical founder behavior I value. His OCR projects on GitHub show he's been working on document extraction problems before this company existed. He is unambiguously a builder, not a manager.

What would have to be true for this to work? Sachdev would need to be one of those rare founders who compensates for missing domain experience through sheer intensity of learning -- spending months shadowing QMEs, understanding every nuance of AMA Guides impairment ratings, becoming the domain expert through relentless immersion rather than prior experience. DoorDash's Tony Xu personally delivered food to learn the logistics, and he hadn't worked in restaurants. If Sachdev has been doing the equivalent -- sitting in physicians' offices, watching them draft reports page by page, understanding the compliance requirements at a granular level -- then the missing organic backstory matters less. But I don't see evidence of that intensity in the dossier. And he'd need a co-founder. Every hard thing about this company -- the regulatory compliance, the physician trust-building, the sales cycle -- gets harder as a team of one.

The problem is real. The schlep is genuine. The founder can build. But the missing organic connection to the problem, combined with solo execution in a domain requiring deep specialized knowledge, keeps this in neutral territory for me. If Sachdev came back with a physician co-founder and could tell me the specific story of how he discovered this problem -- not from market research, but from watching someone struggle with it -- I'd look again more seriously.
