# Adialante -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The thing that grabbed me immediately about Adialante isn't the MRI physics -- it's the business model conflict they create for incumbents. Siemens, GE, and Philips sell MRI systems at $1-3 million a pop plus recurring service contracts. That's their entire economic engine. A per-scan mobile model where clinics pay nothing upfront and never own the hardware is structurally impossible for those incumbents to copy without detonating their own revenue base. I've seen this dynamic before. When I first backed Venmo, banks could have built peer-to-peer mobile payments trivially, but doing so would have cannibalized their wire transfer fees and checking account economics. Business model conflict isn't a feature advantage -- it's a structural moat that gets deeper over time because the incumbent's rational response is always to protect existing revenue, even as the disruptor eats the margin underneath them. Adialante is setting up that same dynamic in a $7 billion market.

Now, the sane-person-insane-idea calibration. Efrain Torres invented the core FREE technology at the University of Minnesota's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research -- one of the top MRI labs on the planet. His team includes an ISMRM Rabi Award winner, which in MRI physics is roughly equivalent to a Fields Medal finalist in math. Five PhDs spanning MRI physics, image reconstruction, and computational imaging. The "sane" credentials here are genuinely deep and specifically relevant to this exact problem -- these aren't generalist engineers who decided MRI sounded interesting. They are the people who built the physics. The "insane" part is equally real: they're proposing to eliminate gradient coils, which is like proposing to build a car without a transmission. Gradient coils have been fundamental to MRI for four decades. When Torres says "we remove a third of the hardware," that's not an incremental cost reduction -- it's a rearchitecture of how magnetic resonance imaging works at the physics level. Most medtech VCs I know would hear "novel MRI physics from a PhD student" and reflexively pass. That self-selection is exactly what I look for.

Where my enthusiasm gets checked is on the "sane" dimension beyond science. Torres and Jenkins are scientifically brilliant, but I see zero evidence of commercial, operational, or healthcare business experience on this team. They're simultaneously attempting four hard things: finishing the hardware, navigating FDA clearance, building mobile fleet operations, and selling to risk-averse urologists. Running "imaging clinics on wheels" across multiple jurisdictions -- staffing, scheduling, maintenance, compliance -- is a fundamentally different skill from building an MRI scanner in a lab. This is the gap between being a sane researcher and being a sane operator. Promaxo already has FDA clearance and 69 installed prostate MRI systems. By the time Adialante clears regulatory, Promaxo may own the early-adopter urology clinics. That timing disadvantage is real and can't be hand-waved away.

The AI-resistance profile here is exceptional, though, and this is what keeps me engaged. The moat is layered: patent-pending physics that took years of PhD-level research to develop, physical hardware that can't be replicated with code, mobile fleet operations that require boots on the ground, and eventually FDA clearance that creates a multi-year regulatory barrier. AI actually makes this business better -- AI-based image reconstruction can improve image quality at lower field strengths -- without enabling any competitor to replicate the underlying physics with software. This is the "cherry on top" archetype: a business that would be compelling without AI, but where AI non-obviously improves operations. Compare that to an "AI-powered diagnostic" startup where the next foundation model release makes you obsolete overnight. Adialante is closer to Craftwork in my portfolio -- a physical-world operation where technology amplifies but cannot substitute for the real-world complexity -- except with harder IP and a regulatory moat on top.

The bull case deserves real articulation. If FREE works as described -- one-tenth the size, one-tenth the cost, one-tenth the power, completely silent -- this doesn't just compete with existing MRI. It expands the market. Every urology clinic, every rural hospital, every mobile health unit in every developing country becomes a potential MRI site. The narrative path runs from prostate screening to all cancer imaging to point-of-care diagnostics globally. That's an infinity story with coherent expansion logic at each stage. The per-scan model with zero capex for clinics removes the adoption barrier entirely. And the business model conflict with incumbents means the natural acquirers (Siemens, GE) would pay enormous premiums to either control or eliminate this threat to their hardware sales business. The floor isn't zero either -- patent-pending physics, NSF validation ($1.19M SBIR Phase II), and the team's scientific reputation have real asset value even in a downside scenario.

Ultimately, this is a long-duration infrastructure bet that resembles our Helium investment profile: genuinely novel technology, physical-world moat, years of patience required before traction becomes visible, and a team that's technically exceptional but commercially unproven. Helium took seven years from seed to unicorn. Medical devices move even slower. The question is whether Torres and the team can make the academic-to-commercial transition and whether the mobile operations layer will compound into defensibility or collapse into operational chaos. I lean toward writing a check because the structural setup -- physics IP plus business model conflict plus AI resistance plus regulatory moat -- is the exact combination my framework is built to identify. But I'd want to see them add a healthcare operations executive before I'd feel fully comfortable with the execution path.
