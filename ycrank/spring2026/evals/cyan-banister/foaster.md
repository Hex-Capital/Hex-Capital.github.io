# Foaster -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Celonis has raised $1.77 billion. McKinsey has partnered with Wonderful AI. Deloitte, BCG, and PwC are building AI practices with client relationships that span decades and continents. Foaster is two twenty-three-year-olds from ENSAE Paris with a case study from a 350-employee professional services firm and a LinkedIn mention of LIPTON Teas and Infusions. I don't say this to dismiss them — I've written first checks into companies where the odds looked worse. I said yes to SpaceX when rockets were exploding. But those bets had something that made the asymmetry work: the space itself was abandoned. Nobody else wanted to be there. Enterprise AI consulting in 2026 is the opposite — every well-resourced player on earth is sprinting toward this market. The tags on Foaster's YC page say it all: "Artificial Intelligence, B2B, Enterprise Software." If I described this at a dinner party, nobody would shift in their chair. They'd nod and ask for the deck.

Raphaël Dabadie built 400,000-member tennis communities at age eleven and joined Rafael Nadal's team as an agent at eighteen. That's genuine hustle — the kind of resourcefulness I recognize from my own years of figuring things out with nothing. Alexandre Combes built Répondia, handling 60,000+ restaurant calls with AI agents, which is real execution at scale. Both co-created LLM benchmarks that got 12 million combined views and caught the attention of OpenAI's president and xAI's co-founder. These are clearly talented people. But when I ask my question — tell me your story, not your business — what I hear is: "We're exceptional AI researchers who spotted a massive market." Foaster's predecessor entity Répondia handled restaurant calls in France; the pivot to enterprise AI transformation consulting is a market-opportunity pivot, not a mission-inevitability pivot. I don't feel the biographical forge — the thing that would make Raphaël and Alexandre incapable of working on anything else. The problem didn't find them; they found the problem.

Foaster's core claim is that AI agents conducting 45-minute interviews surface candid operational knowledge that human consultants and system-log tools like Celonis cannot capture. That's a real insight — people probably are more honest with a machine than with a McKinsey associate who might recommend cutting their department. The case study numbers are concrete: 280 people interviewed in five days, 1,200+ processes mapped, nine-day total delivery versus months for traditional consulting. But here's what I keep turning over: where is the invisible system? Management consulting is expensive and opaque, yes, but the enterprise buyer already knows they're paying a premium for it. There's no hidden tax being extracted from someone who doesn't understand the system — the CFO who signs the McKinsey engagement letter knows exactly what she's buying. Compare this to Affirm, where consumers genuinely did not understand how credit card interest was compounding against them, or Ownwell, where homeowners didn't know their property tax assessments were systematically inflated. Foaster makes consulting faster and cheaper for enterprises that already have purchasing power. That's optimization, not revelation.

The bull case deserves honest construction: Foaster's founders have demonstrated AI agent deployment at genuine scale through Répondia's 60,000+ calls, their research benchmarks earned engagement from OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Mistral AI, and NVIDIA researchers, and they've already landed a recognizable enterprise client in LIPTON Teas and Infusions. The AI consulting market is projected to grow from $11 billion to $91 billion by 2035 at 26% CAGR, and LLM quality crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 that makes sustained 45-minute unstructured AI interviews newly viable. The interview-based approach captures tacit organizational knowledge — opinions, perceived bottlenecks, handoff frustrations — that Celonis's structured log mining fundamentally cannot access. If Foaster accumulates enough proprietary operational maps across enough enterprises, the compounding data asset could create real switching costs. And at twenty-three, these founders have decades of runway to grow into the enterprise sales motion. I checked my known exceptions: the HQ Trivia cultural-timing override, the RTFKT narrow-window override, the Superplastic creative-weirdness override — none apply here to rescue a pass. Foaster isn't occupying a narrow cultural moment or defying genre categorization. It's entering a well-lit, well-funded market through the front door.

Foaster's defensibility concerns me more than the founders' age or team size. The core technology — LLM-based interviews and operational mapping — runs on generally available foundation models. The dossier explicitly flags that "any well-resourced team with consulting domain expertise could build a competing product." Celonis, with $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in 2023 revenue, sits adjacent and could add interview-based capture to its process intelligence platform. McKinsey has already made its move by partnering with Wonderful AI for enterprise AI agent deployment. Foaster's narrow advantage is speed of execution and accumulated interview know-how, but that's a temporal lead, not a structural moat. My biggest losses — HQ Trivia, GameCrush, IRL — all shared a common thread: no infrastructure-grade defensibility beneath an initially compelling surface. Foaster's operational maps per client do create some switching costs, and that's the seed of a moat, but at pre-seed with two people and foundation-model dependency, the structural barriers aren't yet visible.

The condition that would flip me: if Raphaël or Alexandre had spent years inside a broken enterprise transformation process — not as consultants, but as the employee being consulted, watching millions wasted on recommendations that never got implemented because the consultants never understood the actual workflows — and Foaster emerged from that lived frustration rather than from spotting a market gap after building restaurant AI agents, I would score the biographical forge dramatically higher and the contrarian conviction differently. The product might be the same, but the founder-problem bond would change everything about my conviction through the inevitable valley ahead. Right now, Foaster is a well-executed market-opportunity play by talented young builders in the most consensus AI category of 2026, and that combination — consensus space, absent biographical forge, no invisible system, competitive encirclement from billion-dollar incumbents — puts it outside the territory where I write checks from my own money.
