# ProjectX -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Rounak Adhikary is twenty-three, holds a Bachelor's in Interaction Design from Lovely Professional University, and is pitching "the first OS where humans and agents work with no limits" with $704K raised across two rounds and a company Twitter at 14 followers since October 2022. ProjectX's signature, before I read another slide, is the inverse of what makes me write a check: the highest-loading dimension here is the capital-intensity of per-app GPU virtualization, which is real, and the lowest is whether any of the three co-founders surfaced this problem from lived contact with operating-system internals — they did not. ProjectX is an analytically-discovered company in a consensus 2026 lane, dressed in infra clothing.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage before Anduril ever existed; that is the high rung on the founder-lived-experience ladder, and the load-bearing mechanism is that the prior shipped artifact is what gave him the social license to walk onto a defense base and be taken seriously. ProjectX's founders have not shipped a comparable product to scale in an adjacent domain — Rounak built Woldix, a tech-consulting shop that self-funded the early stages, and Bishal Karmakar lists "systems, streaming, cloud infra" as a specialization with no public GitHub repos to verify it. A consulting LLC and a LinkedIn descriptor are not the Keyhole-to-Niantic trajectory. The origin story that would move me is "I spent four years writing a hypervisor at a place that ran out of GPUs for agents and watched the kernel choke" — the dossier offers no such moment.

HQ Trivia's Yusupov and Kroll did not name a tiebreaker, the partnership collapsed in feud, and that is the low rung I carry into every co-founder governance read. ProjectX's founding roster in the 2022 incorporation was Adhikary, Ronit Banerjee, and Suvrakamal Das; the YC P26 roster is Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder. That is a complete swap of two of three co-founders before the batch, with no public explanation. CEO / CTO / COO titles are listed but I see no operating agreement, no founder-vesting protocol, no documented tiebreaker. The pattern that ended GameCrush — unresolved authority colliding with capital — is already half-present here, and the resolution mechanism is undocumented.

The "magically weird" filter is mechanism-based: a weird company is one where two or three credible investors have *visibly* passed on aesthetic or capital-intensity grounds and the founder can articulate why they were wrong. ProjectX is not weird. Tracxn lists Canonical, OpenStack, and Neverinstall as top competitors; Paperspace took $35M and exited to DigitalOcean for $111M cash in July 2023; Kasm, Shadow, Amazon WorkSpaces, Azure Virtual Desktop, and Google's cloud workstations are all in the lane. "Agent-native OS" is the consensus narrative beat of the 2025-26 YC batches, not an idea that gets laughed out of a partners' meeting. Over eighteen months the dimension compounds against ProjectX, because every hyperscaler RFP for agent sandboxing will be answered by an incumbent with cheaper GPU pools.

Counterfactually, the capital-intensity dimension would flip a tier if ProjectX published a benchmark showing per-app GPU cold-start in single-digit seconds at a density-per-host that undercut AWS WorkSpaces GPU bundles by 40%+, because the per-app isolation architecture would then be a defensible technical wedge rather than a positioning claim. The dossier offers no benchmarks, no density numbers, no pricing page — the WebFetch on projectx.cloud returned a loading screen. Without that data, ProjectX scores at the default for an infra-adjacent software company that is not actually frontier hardware: real technical work, but the moat is unproven and the unit economics of "each app in its own independent computer with its own GPU" are the precise place a hyperscaler crushes a startup.

Flock Safety I sourced from a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café — the founder had shipped something so present in physical space that the artifact introduced itself. ProjectX is browser-delivered, which in principle clears the bar for an artifact a normal human can touch, but the dossier has no Product Hunt launch, no Chrome extension, no Discord community, no Tweet of someone using it, and the marketing site itself failed to load during research. Fourteen Twitter followers across three and a half years is not an absence of marketing budget — it is an absence of someone caring enough to put the product in front of a person.

My 36-month forecast: ProjectX quiet-acquihires into a DaaS or GPU-cloud incumbent (Kasm, Neverinstall, or a DigitalOcean-class buyer) for under $10M in stock, or winds down, after failing to demonstrate per-app GPU unit economics that beat AWS WorkSpaces' GPU bundle pricing — the mechanism is hyperscaler substitution colliding with a founding team whose technical depth in hypervisor and GPU virtualization is asserted on LinkedIn but absent from public artifacts.

The bull case that would sting: Bishal is genuinely an outsider systems engineer from Academy of Technology (not Stanford), Rounak self-funded the company at nineteen via Woldix which is exactly the persistence-and-outsider-trajectory I funded in Thumbtack and HandUp, and the agent-OS thesis is a real expanding market — someone wins the sandboxed-compute-for-agents category, and HandUp is the precedent for me writing into a mission founder without venture-scale defensibility. I dismantle that with the dossier itself: HandUp's override was *biographical mirroring* of my own homelessness origin, and ProjectX has no such mirror — a twenty-three-year-old design grad pivoting from consulting into the consensus lane is not the HandUp pattern, it is the consensus-YC-AI-darling pattern I described in my Pass Example. The flip condition is concrete: a named tier-1 enterprise customer (an AI lab, a Fortune 500 IT org) signing for the per-app GPU isolation use case with a dollar figure, plus a public benchmark from Karmakar showing the multi-VM session orchestration at competitive cost — give me those two artifacts and I re-read.
