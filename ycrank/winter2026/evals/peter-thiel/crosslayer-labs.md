# Crosslayer Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The founding team invented a global Internet standard. Not contributed to, not influenced -- invented. Multi-Perspective Issuance Corroboration is now mandated by the CA/Browser Forum for every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate on Earth, deployed at Let's Encrypt, Google Trust Services, and Cloudflare, securing eight million certificates daily. An ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award recipient co-founded the company. These are credentials that money cannot buy and competitors cannot fabricate. And yet credentials are not secrets, and credentials are not monopolies. The question I must answer is whether this extraordinary technical authority translates into a business that escapes the gravitational pull of one of the most mimetically competitive markets in technology.

What is the secret here? The implicit claim is that the Internet's security architecture has fundamental cross-layer vulnerabilities -- attacks that propagate from BGP to DNS to TLS to JavaScript -- that single-layer monitoring tools systematically miss, and that the industry's current approach of siloed monitoring creates a structural blind spot. This is a genuine technical insight, and the team has the publications to prove it. But I would not call it contrarian. No competent security researcher would argue that cross-layer attacks are impossible. The disagreement, such as it is, is about prioritization -- whether these attack vectors are important enough relative to ransomware, phishing, and endpoint compromise to justify a dedicated monitoring product. That is a market-sizing question, not a secret about the world. The KLAYswap incident -- $2 million stolen via compromised JavaScript in a cryptocurrency theft -- illustrates real damage, but $2 million is not the kind of number that keeps CISOs at JPMorgan awake at night. The insight is real; I am not convinced it is large.

The competitive landscape concerns me more than the technology. Cybersecurity is the canonical Girardian nightmare in enterprise software -- hundreds of startups and a dozen massive incumbents all imitating each other, all competing for the same CISO budgets, all claiming differentiation while converging on the same buyer. Censys has raised $75 million. IONIX has raised $50 million. SecurityScorecard has raised north of $290 million. CrowdStrike commands 15.8% mindshare in attack surface management. The dossier correctly notes that these incumbents focus on breadth rather than cross-layer depth, and that building the correlation engine requires specialized expertise. But specialized expertise is a temporary moat, not a structural one. CrowdStrike acquired Reposify. Palo Alto acquired Cortex Xpanse. The M&A pattern in EASM is clear: large platforms absorb point solutions. A company built around a narrow attack vector -- however technically sophisticated -- faces the structural question of whether it becomes a feature or a franchise. Most cybersecurity startups become features.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. Palantir, which I co-founded, followed a similar arc: academic insight (PayPal's fraud algorithms), narrow initial customer base (CIA and intelligence agencies), deep technical moat that incumbents could not replicate quickly, and years of patient building before the business scaled. If Crosslayer Labs can establish itself as the definitive authority on cross-layer Internet security -- the team no one else can replicate -- and expand from cryptocurrency and fintech (where these attacks cause measurable financial loss) into healthcare and critical infrastructure, there is a path to a defensible, growing business. The MPIC mandate creates a market education tailwind that the founders themselves engineered, which is clever -- shape the regulatory landscape, then build the company that benefits from heightened awareness. The relationships with every major Certificate Authority (Google, Apple, Amazon, Let's Encrypt, Cloudflare) forged through the standardization process could accelerate enterprise distribution in ways that a cold-calling sales team cannot match. If cross-layer attacks escalate -- and the increasing sophistication of state-sponsored cyber operations suggests they might -- this team is uniquely positioned to own the category.

But I keep returning to the fundamental structural question: does this company have a path to monopoly? The technology is differentiated but not 10x discontinuous -- it correlates signals from existing monitoring layers rather than creating a capability that did not previously exist. There is no obvious network effect; one customer's data does not make the product meaningfully better for the next customer, at least not at the scale visible today. Economies of scale in SaaS monitoring are limited. The founders' authority and publications create a knowledge moat, but knowledge moats erode as PhD students graduate and incumbents hire. I see a company that could become an excellent $50-100 million ARR business in five to seven years -- which is a good outcome for most investors, but not the power-law return I require. The absence of any revenue signal, any named customer, any LOI, or any indication of commercial traction beyond YC and small angel investors compounds my uncertainty about the transition from academic authority to commercial monopoly. Prateek Mittal's decision to leave a tenured professorship signals genuine conviction, and I respect that -- but the vision as articulated reads as "monitor Internet infrastructure security better" rather than a definite plan for how this company dominates a category for decades.

The founders are technically world-class in a domain that matters. But world-class technical founders in a hypercompetitive market, building a point solution around a narrow attack vector, without visible commercial traction or a clear monopoly mechanism, is not a configuration I invest in. The market they are entering punishes excellence and rewards consolidation. I pass -- not because the team lacks capability, but because cybersecurity lacks the structural conditions for the kind of monopoly that produces outlier returns.
