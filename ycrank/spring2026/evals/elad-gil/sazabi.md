# Sazabi -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Sazabi's core architectural wager -- that logs alone, processed by LLMs, can replace the entire three-pillars paradigm of logs, metrics, and traces -- is the most analytically interesting feature of this company. Callaway states it plainly: "Fundamentally, logs are just events, metrics are aggregated events and traces are basically correlated events" (SiliconANGLE, Apr 2026). If correct, Sazabi collapses the instrumentation burden from three signal types to one, which would be a genuine paradigm shift in how engineering teams approach production monitoring. But the OpenTelemetry standard, backed by the CNCF ecosystem and adopted by Dash0, SigNoz, and increasingly by Datadog's own tooling, codifies the opposite assumption. Sazabi isn't just competing with incumbents -- Sazabi is competing with the emerging industry standard. That's a high-variance position. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto was dismissed; nobody was building the counter-infrastructure. Here, the counter-infrastructure (OTel-native observability) has $155M in funding at Dash0 alone and 600+ customers already.

"AI-native observability" is not a nonobvious market. Dash0 raised $110M at a $1B valuation in March 2026 with AI agents for observability. Datadog ships AI-powered features to 47K+ customers. Cribl sits at $3.5B valuation routing observability data. Every growth-stage investor I know is looking at this category right now. The structural shift -- LLM capabilities crossing a threshold enabling reliable log analysis -- is real, but it's recognized. What's potentially nonobvious about Sazabi is the combination of two narrower claims: first, that logs-only ingestion eliminates sufficient complexity to justify rejecting the three-pillars consensus; second, that AI coding agents (Claude Code, Cursor, Codex) create a new class of production code requiring purpose-built observability that Datadog's architecture can't easily accommodate. The Zypsy blog specifically notes Sazabi "captures agent decisions, output deviations, and anomalies." That second angle -- observability designed for AI-agent workflows -- has more nonobviousness to it because incumbents' architectures were built for human-written code with human-configured monitors. But I've seen enough "we're building X for the AI era" pitches to know that timing specificity matters: the AI-agent code monitoring market may be real, but Sazabi's full platform launch isn't until end of 2026, and fast-moving incumbents can ship features in quarters, not years.

Sazabi does sit at the infrastructure layer, which is where I prefer to invest during platform shifts. Observability tooling embeds in engineering workflows -- once a team configures their logging pipeline, dashboards, and alerts against Sazabi's backend, ripping it out is painful. The "Perfect Memory" feature, which accumulates customer-specific incident history and traffic patterns, creates data-driven switching costs that compound over time. SOC 2 Type II, SOC 1, ISO 27017, and ISO 27001 certifications at what appears to be a pre-seed stage is unusual and signals either enterprise ambition or sophisticated security positioning -- either way, it creates a barrier newer entrants won't match quickly. The integration points with coding agents position Sazabi as infrastructure for the agentic development workflow, not just another dashboard. But -- and this is critical -- the dossier's own assessment notes the approach is "architecturally reproducible." Sazabi's moat depends on execution speed and data accumulation rather than structural barriers. Stripe's moat was the network effect of thousands of companies building on its API; Sazabi's logs-only approach doesn't have an equivalent structural lock-in mechanism beyond accumulated context.

Sherwood Callaway is the strongest signal in this dossier. Employee ~70 at Brex, where he started the Infrastructure, Observability, and Bill Pay teams during Brex's hypergrowth from fintech startup to multi-billion-dollar company -- that's the operator-scaler profile I look for. He experienced observability pain firsthand at exactly the kind of fast-moving engineering organization Sazabi targets. Two YC batches (Opkit S21, Sazabi S26), a successful acquisition by 11x in September 2024, and subsequent experience rebuilding an AI agent system at 11x gives Callaway a rare combination of observability infrastructure expertise and production AI-agent experience. The angel backing from leaders at Vercel, Graphite, LangChain, Replit, and Browserbase isn't decorative -- these are distribution channels into exactly the developer communities where Sazabi needs early adoption. The single-founder listing on YC's page despite Justin Ko's deep co-founding history at Opkit raises an organizational clarity question, but the working relationship (roommates, co-founders, three companies together) suggests stability.

Sazabi's miracle count concerns me. The primary miracle is technological: can logs-only ingestion via LLM processing genuinely replace metrics and traces at production scale with sufficient accuracy? This miracle is partially exogenous -- LLM capabilities are improving on their own curve, similar to how Harvey benefited from GPT capability improvements happening independently. But Sazabi needs a second thing to go right: distribution against Datadog ($2.7B ARR, 47K customers) and Dash0 ($1B valuation, 600+ customers, shipping now). Sazabi is pre-product with full launch scheduled for end of 2026. By then, Dash0 will have 12+ months of additional market traction. The target segment -- "early-stage and growth-stage technology companies" per SiliconANGLE -- has clear payers (engineering budgets are real), but these are small contracts with high churn as startups fail or get acquired. Expanding upmarket to enterprise, where observability spend concentrates, requires a sales motion Sazabi hasn't built.

The bull case an advocate would make: Callaway's Brex observability experience gives him the exact operational insight to identify what's broken in the current paradigm. The logs-only thesis, if correct, is a 10x simplification that creates a genuine cost and complexity advantage over three-pillars incumbents. AI-agent observability is a nascent sub-category where no incumbent has a head start. The strategic angel network creates organic distribution into the developer community. And incumbents face a classic innovator's dilemma: Datadog's revenue model is built on per-signal pricing (metrics, traces, and logs billed separately), so collapsing to logs-only would cannibalize their own revenue streams. That's a real structural argument. But I keep returning to the same problem: the broader category is crowded with well-capitalized competitors who are shipping now, and the architectural bet against OpenTelemetry may create adoption friction that no amount of AI sophistication can overcome. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Sazabi's logs-only approach demonstrably reconstructs metrics and traces with accuracy comparable to native instrumentation in a production environment at a named customer. That's the single miracle -- show me it works.

Sazabi's total picture is a strong founder with genuine domain expertise building infrastructure in a real market, dragged down by a market timing score that reflects how consensus "AI-native observability" has become and a competitive landscape where well-funded companies are already shipping. The decisive dimensions are Nonobvious Market Timing (the category is too recognized to score high on my most-weighted criterion) and Single-Miracle Viability (the distribution challenge against Dash0 and Datadog compounds the technology risk). Callaway's operator profile is the strongest element and nearly compensates, but market structure matters more than founder quality in my framework -- I've seen great founders get crushed by competitive markets where timing advantage had already been captured.
