# Heavi -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari scaled WayCool Foods to $250M+ ARR and roughly $750M in valuation, then left to get ASE T8 certified as a diesel mechanic. That sequence tells me everything about principal versus agent. An agent optimizes their career arc — Dasari would have stayed at WayCool or started something in agricultural technology where his reputation was already built. A principal follows the problem they can't stop thinking about. The ASE T8 certification is the signal: you don't study for the national standard in heavy-duty truck service excellence as a fundraising strategy. You do it because you need to understand the work at the level of the people doing it. Dasari's father ran Ashok Leyland, one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers. This isn't a founder who discovered the heavy-duty vehicle market through a McKinsey slide deck — this is someone whose specific knowledge in commercial vehicles is literally inherited, then deliberately deepened through hands-on certification and operationalized through marketplace-building experience at WayCool.

The specific knowledge stack here is layered in a way I rarely see at pre-seed. Dasari carries three non-replicable knowledge layers: commercial vehicle industry intuition absorbed over a lifetime through his family, supply chain marketplace architecture from scaling WayCool to $250M+ ARR across 28 funding rounds, and now direct domain fluency as a certified diesel mechanic. Michael Holkesvik adds a fourth layer — technical marketplace construction, having built NCX's satellite imagery and ML-powered landowner marketplace to 15,000 members as CTO. The structural parallel to my own trajectory is instructive: I took specific knowledge from Epinions about what founders needed and built AngelList to democratize startup investing. Dasari is taking specific knowledge from WayCool about supply chain marketplace dynamics and applying it to a domain where his family gave him non-replicable access. The WayCool-to-Heavi transition isn't a pivot — it's the same founder applying the same structural pattern (fragmented supply chain, information asymmetry, middleman-dependent procurement) to a vertical where his knowledge advantage is deeper.

The leverage audit reveals a hybrid architecture. Heavi's AI procurement agent — accepting a VIN, searching OE systems and aftermarket catalogs, contacting vendors autonomously — is genuine code leverage with zero marginal cost per search. Every additional shop using the system doesn't require Heavi to hire another person. That's the right structure. But the marketplace component introduces physical-world friction: parts have to be sourced from real vendors, priced in real-time, and delivered to real shops. This isn't Notion scaling to millions with pure software. The "marketplace" tag in Heavi's description suggests an eventual take-rate on transactions, which means revenue compounds with volume without proportional headcount — good — but also means Heavi depends on vendor integrations and data access that aren't yet secured. FleetPride or TruckPro could restrict access tomorrow. The leverage is real but permissioned at the data layer, which caps my score below where pure software would land.

The compound interest question is where Heavi gets genuinely interesting. Every parts search generates proprietary data on pricing, availability, fitment accuracy, and vendor reliability across a fragmented ecosystem with thousands of OEM part numbers and cross-references. This data flywheel doesn't exist anywhere else — existing catalog tools are static, and shop management platforms like Fullbay track workflow, not procurement intelligence. If Heavi accumulates enough procurement data across enough shops, the switching cost becomes the intelligence itself: your AI knows which vendors reliably deliver, which aftermarket parts actually fit, which cross-references are accurate. The 65.5% understaffing rate and 67,000-technician projected shortage by 2026 means the knowledge that retiring mechanics carry in their heads is actively evaporating from the industry. Heavi is trying to capture it before it disappears. That's a time-sensitive data moat — rare at pre-seed.

The bear case centers on Fullbay. They have 5,000+ shops on their platform, $19.4M in 2024 revenue, and just acquired Pitstop's AI capabilities in March 2026. Distribution plus AI plus existing shop relationships is a formidable combination. If Fullbay adds AI-powered parts procurement as a feature inside their ERP, Heavi's standalone product faces the classic platform-absorbs-feature risk. The counterargument: Fullbay's revenue model is per-shop SaaS subscriptions for workflow management. Rearchitecting from workflow tool to autonomous procurement agent requires a fundamentally different technical approach and potentially a different business model (marketplace take-rate versus SaaS subscription). Incumbents rarely make that transition cleanly — their existing revenue creates the principal-agent problem I look for. Fullbay's 5,000 shops are agents of their current product roadmap. The co-founder relationship also gives me mild pause: no shared prior employer or university between Dasari and Holkesvik, likely connected through the SF ecosystem. It's not a "co-founder matching event" red flag, but the trust layer is unproven. NCX's trajectory — $74.4M raised, then core program discontinued after Verra declined certification, followed by 40% layoffs — suggests Holkesvik has experienced a venture that looked promising but hit structural market risk. That experience could be valuable pattern recognition or could indicate insufficient market-risk diligence. At $100K contracted revenue from 5 design partners reporting 30% time savings and 40% margin improvement, the early validation partially mitigates these concerns.

Heavi fits my access democratization pattern cleanly: institutional knowledge locked in retiring experts' heads, being made available to every understaffed shop through AI. The $100K in contracted revenue from a 2-person team demonstrates the team-to-value leverage I look for — real paying customers without a sales team. The decisive dimensions are specific knowledge productization, where Dasari's multi-generational commercial vehicle background combined with proven supply chain marketplace execution creates a founder whose disappearance would eliminate Heavi's core insight, and the leverage architecture, where the AI agent is genuine code leverage but the physical marketplace layer introduces friction that pure software companies avoid. I'd write a check. The specific knowledge density justifies the bet even with the leverage ceiling.
