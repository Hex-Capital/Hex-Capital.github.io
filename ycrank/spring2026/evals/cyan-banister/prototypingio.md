# Prototyping.io -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Prototyping.io is that it doesn't make me uncomfortable, and that's actually the problem. When I look at the AI manufacturing landscape in early 2026, I count at least five funded startups attacking variations of the same insight -- RMFG with $4.5M and 100K parts shipped, Jiga with $19.1M and NASA as a customer, Toolpath with $10M, Korso in YC alongside them -- plus two public companies in Xometry and Protolabs that are already layering AI onto their platforms. This is a category where consensus has already formed. The venture world has collectively decided that "AI-native manufacturing" is a real opportunity, and when everyone agrees something is a real opportunity, the returns have usually already been priced in. The companies that have changed my portfolio -- SpaceX, Crusoe Energy, Contraline -- all had the quality of making other investors squirm. Nobody squirms at "we're going to do AI-powered DFM analysis."

I want to hear the founders' stories, and here I find myself looking at credentials instead of biography. Revanth has an IIT silver medal, an aerospace MS from UT Austin, and time at Schlumberger. Prerit built AI systems at Microsoft and published at top distributed systems venues. These are genuinely strong resumes for this particular problem -- if I were evaluating them purely on domain fit, the mechanical engineering depth paired with AI systems expertise covers exactly the right ground. But credentials tell me someone is capable; they don't tell me someone is inevitable. What I'm always listening for is the moment a founder reveals the personal thread that makes them unable to NOT build this thing -- the way Travis couldn't stop thinking about taxi medallions, or the way Contraline's founder had spent years in a research lab no one else cared about. Prerit's LinkedIn says he "recently joined forces with Revanth," which tells me this partnership is new and the origin is opportunistic rather than forged through shared adversity. That doesn't make them bad founders, but it means I can't evaluate the thing I weight most heavily.

The bull case here is genuinely worth articulating, because the structural insight is real even if it doesn't light up my framework. Xometry makes money when engineers order parts -- their incentive is to process orders quickly, not to tell engineers their designs have manufacturability problems before the order is placed. That business model tension is a legitimate opening for a DFM-first platform to build loyalty with engineering teams by saving them iteration cycles and failed prototypes. If Prototyping.io can build the best DFM analysis engine and create a tight feedback loop between design optimization and ordering, they could own the top of the manufacturing funnel while incumbents fight over the bottom. The reshoring tailwind is real. And the $1.5B online CNC market growing at 9.2% CAGR provides a genuine expanding wedge. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that AI-native DFM analysis becomes a moat rather than a feature -- that the data flywheel from processing CAD files creates compounding advantages that Xometry can't replicate by bolting on similar analysis to their existing engine. That's possible, but it requires winning the race against both incumbents and the cohort of funded peers, which is a different kind of bet than the ones I tend to make.

On economic access, I can see a version of this that democratizes custom manufacturing for small hardware teams who today can't afford dedicated manufacturing engineers -- that resonates with how I think about companies like Thumbtack connecting skilled workers with customers. But the primary users are engineers at companies, not populations locked out of economic participation. The transparency angle -- making DFM constraints visible earlier in the design cycle -- is real but incremental. This isn't Carta revealing hidden cap table information or Affirm making predatory credit card fees transparent. It's making an existing professional workflow faster, which is valuable but doesn't create the kind of systemic access change that gets me emotionally invested.

Two founders who recently found each other, building in a space that five other funded teams are also attacking, with a pre-launch product behind an early access gate and no visible traction -- I keep looking for the signal that would override these concerns, the way SpaceX's sheer ambition overrode my usual preference for capital efficiency, and I can't find it. The founders are qualified. The market is real. The timing rationale is sound. But nothing about this company is difficult to assess or unpopular, and those are the qualities I've learned to trust as predictive of outlier returns. I'm passing, but I'd genuinely like to hear their personal stories -- there may be a biographical thread that the public record doesn't capture. If Revanth spent years watching engineers at Schlumberger waste weeks on avoidable DFM failures and built this out of that specific frustration, that would change my read considerably.
