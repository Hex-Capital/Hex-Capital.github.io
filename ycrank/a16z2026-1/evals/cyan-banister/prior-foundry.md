# Prior Foundry -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The policy consulting industry is one of the most quietly extractive systems on earth. Governments — meaning taxpayers — pay McKinsey and BCG hundreds of millions of dollars a year for reports that take months to produce, are often recycled across clients, and are deliberately opaque enough to justify the next engagement. It's a system designed to keep the buyer dependent. Prior Foundry is building something that could make that entire structure visible and dispensable: on-demand simulations that show how populations will actually respond to policy decisions, delivered in hours instead of quarters. When I think about what drew me to Affirm — Max Levchin attacking the hidden structure of credit card interest that extracted from people who didn't understand it — I see the same structural thesis here. The consulting cartel depends on governments not being able to do this analysis themselves. Prior Foundry hands them the tool. That's a genuine "invisible system made visible" play, and it's the strongest dimension of this company for my framework.

The founders are credentialed to the teeth — Shirin Abrishami Kashani is a Knight-Hennessy Scholar at Stanford with direct OECD policy experience, Keshav Sivakumar deployed government technology at Palantir and co-founded Lumitics, and Jonne Kamphorst literally built the Stanford research on LLM-based behavioral simulation that underpins the product. But when I ask my opening question — tell me the story, not the business — what I hear is academic progression, not biographical inevitability. Shirin's choice to study refugee and forced migration at Oxford hints at something personal, maybe something forged by displacement in her own family history, but the dossier doesn't confirm it. Without that confirmation, what I see is three exceptionally smart people who converged at Stanford around an intellectually exciting research breakthrough and decided to commercialize it. That's legitimate, but it's different from a founder who would be working on this problem in a garage with zero funding. And Jonne starting as Assistant Professor at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026 while serving as Chief Scientific Officer of a San Francisco company? That's academic career hedging, not burning the boats. When I wrote my SpaceX check, Elon wasn't keeping a faculty appointment in case the rockets didn't work.

Prior Foundry's early traction signals are genuinely arresting for a pre-seed company: $60K ARR within two months of operation, a partnership with the OECD spanning 38+ member governments, an Allegheny County pilot, and more than $1M in distributor pipeline. The OECD relationship is the kind of institutional distribution channel that most govtech companies spend years trying to build — and it could become an infrastructure moat if Prior Foundry embeds deeply enough across those 38 member states. But here's where the ten-year question gets complicated. The foundational generative agent architecture is published and open-sourced on GitHub. The survey datasets (GSS, WVS, ESS) are public. The 85% accuracy benchmark was measured on replicating survey responses, not on predicting real-world policy outcomes — and that gap between "can our synthetic agents answer surveys like real people" and "can we predict what happens when you change immigration policy" is not an engineering problem, it's an epistemological one that may not have a clean answer.

There's real contrarian discomfort in this space that a generic analyst might miss. "We simulate how different racial, income, and ideological groups respond to government policy changes" — say that sentence at a dinner party of sophisticated VCs and watch people shift in their chairs. The ethical sensitivity is genuine: if a government uses these simulations to craft more manipulative messaging around a harmful policy, Prior Foundry has a serious reputational and moral problem. That discomfort is signal to me — Flock Safety made civil liberties advocates nervous too, and it became a massive company — but the discomfort needs to sit alongside the open-source vulnerability. A tool that's both ethically sensitive AND technically replicable from public code and data is in a harder position than one that's ethically sensitive with a hardware moat.

The bull case for Prior Foundry comes down to institutional lock-in at a unique inflection point. If Shirin's OECD relationships translate into embedded contracts across multiple national governments, and if each deployment generates calibrated simulation data that improves the model beyond what a competitor starting from public research could match, then Prior Foundry becomes the Palantir of policy simulation — sticky, compounding, and structurally advantaged by being first. The OECD distribution channel is the kind of thing you can't replicate by raising more money. The "why now" is also real: the Stanford paper proving LLM behavioral simulation at 85% accuracy dropped in November 2024, LLM inference costs are falling, and governments worldwide are under pressure to make evidence-based policy in an era of declining public trust. If Prior Foundry captures this moment, the ten-year destination could be extraordinary — every government decision run through simulation before implementation.

My verdict sits in the Neutral tier, pulled upward by the invisible system thesis and the OECD distribution signal, pulled downward by the absence of biographical forge and the open-source defensibility concern. The dimension that most holds me back is Founder's Biographical Forge — I don't see the survival instinct, the biographical inevitability, the founder who would be doing this in a basement without funding. What I see are three accomplished academics who spotted a commercialization opportunity from their research. Jonne's Sciences Po appointment crystallizes this: he's keeping his academic career alive alongside the company, which tells me this isn't yet the all-consuming mission that produces the founders I back with my highest conviction. The invisible system dimension and the OECD traction keep me from passing outright — there's something real here, and if Shirin's story is deeper than the dossier reveals, I'd want to hear it directly.
