# Mochatrade -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Utkarsh Sinha built Martian Wallet from an Aptos hackathon project at Columbia into a product with 2M+ installs, raised $3M from Race Capital and Jump Capital, and sold it to Lightspeed-backed Pontem in May 2025. That's a real shipping story. He and Parth Maheshwari built the thing together, from the same IIT Kharagpur and Columbia CS pipeline, and they've already survived the full cycle of building, scaling, and exiting a self-custodial crypto product. When I backed Patrick Collison at Auctomatic before Stripe, what I was really buying was the demonstrated ability to ship -- not the specific company. Mochatrade's founding pair has that shipping pedigree. Chetan Manda adds product design credibility from Sprinklr, a public company, though his connection to the other two founders is unaccounted for in the dossier. The team composition is solid but not extraordinary -- I don't have the stories I'd need to see the animal quality, the specific moments where Mochatrade's founders hacked through a wall in some creative way nobody expected. The Martian Wallet arc reads as competent execution in a favorable market (the Aptos ecosystem was flush with incentives), not as the kind of struggle that reveals character under pressure.

Mochatrade's origin story -- "we were traders from India who wanted access to global markets with leverage" -- sits in a gray zone for organic discovery. It's personal experience, which is better than a McKinsey framework, but it's also a frustration shared by millions of India's 200M+ demat account holders. Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive -- that's a specific, almost embarrassingly mundane frustration. "I wanted leveraged access to US stocks from India" is a real problem, but it's the kind of problem that 50 people in every IIT batch have discussed over chai. The specificity I want -- the moment where the founders realized nobody was building this exact thing and felt compelled to build it themselves -- doesn't come through in Mochatrade's dossier. The stated motivation is genuine but generic within their demographic.

Mochatrade's market is what concerns me most. On-chain RWA perps volume surged 162% month-over-month in January 2026, Hyperliquid's HIP-3 framework already lets anyone deploy stock perps, and Trade[XYZ] has crossed $100B in cumulative volume since October 2025. Ostium has $24M in funding and $25B in cumulative volume. dYdX raised $87M. The CFTC is signaling openness to perpetual contracts onshore. Every one of these facts points in the same direction: this is a hot market where the idea sounds obviously good to everyone. That's precisely the anti-signal I've spent two decades warning against. My entire Black Swan Farming framework is built on the premise that when everyone agrees an idea is good, the returns have been competed away. Mochatrade's thesis -- "US stock perps for global traders, starting with India" -- could appear verbatim in a crypto trend report. Nobody's first reaction to this is "that will never work." Their first reaction is "oh, like Ostium but India-focused." The Stripe analogy breaks down here: when the Collisons started Stripe, no incumbents could structurally offer what they built. Mochatrade's competitors can and do offer stock perps -- Hyperliquid's infrastructure layer makes it trivially deployable.

Mochatrade's regulatory positioning is where the schlep analysis gets complicated. The India-specific regulatory maze -- SEBI's crackdown on retail derivatives (91% loss rate per their own data), RBI restrictions on crypto, 30% crypto gains tax, 1% TDS on transactions -- is genuinely the kind of ugly, complex work that repels polished founders. Building local-currency on-ramps under these constraints is a real schlep. But there's a difference between a schlep that creates a moat and a schlep that creates an existential threat. Stripe's regulatory work with PCI compliance was a moat because compliance was a prerequisite customers had to meet -- Stripe just made it easier. Mochatrade's regulatory exposure is the opposite: SEBI could decide that offering 20x leveraged US stock perps to Indian retail traders via crypto rails is exactly the kind of product they should ban, especially given their own data on retail derivative losses. The regulatory schlep here might not be a wall you can climb over. It might be a wall that falls on you.

The bull case for Mochatrade is real and specific: Utkarsh has already scaled a self-custodial crypto product to 2M+ Indian users (Martian Wallet's install base was heavily in emerging markets), giving the team a distribution playbook competitors lack. The timing convergence -- CFTC exploring perps rulemaking, NSE launching international US stock access via GIFT City, 200M+ demat accounts in India -- creates a window. No well-funded competitor is building the India-specific consumer layer with local-currency on-ramps. If Mochatrade can capture even a sliver of the 62% retail-driven NSE F&O volume and redirect it to US stock perps, the numbers are enormous. And the self-custodial architecture smartly reduces counterparty risk, which matters to a post-FTX user base. If I saw evidence that Mochatrade had early users trading obsessively -- even a small group using a crappy v1 -- or that they'd built and deployed the local-currency on-ramp and had real INR-to-stablecoin conversion volume, I'd move toward writing a check. That's the verifiable condition: show me a working product with real users completing trades, and the founder pedigree becomes a much stronger signal.

Mochatrade has competent founders with a relevant prior exit, building in a space they personally understand, on a product that maps to genuine demand. But the market consensus, the capitalized competition, and the existential regulatory exposure push this from a bet I'd make with my own money to one I'd watch. The dimension that hurts Mochatrade most is "Idea That Repels Before It Attracts" -- this idea attracts everyone, which is exactly why the returns have likely been competed away already. The dimension that helps most is founder resourcefulness -- the Martian Wallet exit is real evidence of shipping ability, even if I lack the struggle narratives I'd need to score it in the top quartile. Mochatrade needs to demonstrate that its India-specific distribution is a genuine moat, not just a geographic filter that any well-funded competitor can replicate once the market is proven.
