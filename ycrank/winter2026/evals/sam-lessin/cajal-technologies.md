# Cajal -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cajal is that their core competitor has already raised $295 million. Harmonic AI is pursuing "mathematical superintelligence" with formal verification in Lean 4, has a $1.45 billion valuation, and just achieved gold-medal-level performance on International Math Olympiad problems. Cajal is two people with YC's standard check. Now, resource asymmetry doesn't automatically kill a startup -- I seeded Solana when Ethereum had orders of magnitude more capital and developer mindshare. But the difference is that Anatoly Yakovenko had spent years at Qualcomm building the specific systems architecture that made Solana's throughput thesis credible. When I look at Luke and Pedro, I see a computational neuroscience MPhil student and a CS grad from Spanish universities -- smart people, clearly -- but neither has a documented history in formal verification, theorem proving, or Lean programming. The dossier says the team's backgrounds "align with the interdisciplinary challenge" but couldn't find prior work in the actual core discipline. That's not sane-person-insane-idea. That's capable-people-consensus-idea, and the capability gap in the specific domain they're attacking is concerning.

Which brings me to the consensus problem. AI for mathematical reasoning is one of the hottest areas in AI research right now. DeepMind, OpenAI, and well-funded startups are all pouring resources into this. When Harmonic raises $295M for formal verification in Lean and it gets covered across every tech publication, the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged away. This is the opposite of what I look for. I backed Venmo when mobile peer-to-peer payments seemed unnecessary to everyone. I seeded Helium when decentralized wireless networks sounded like a fever dream. The best signal is when smart people tell you the idea is crazy -- and nobody is saying AI for formal verification is crazy in 2026. They're saying it's the future. That's exactly when I get skeptical, because it means the returns are being competed away in real time.

The structural moat question is where this really falls apart for me. Cajal is an AI system built on top of Lean, which is open source. Their core product is Tau, a multi-agent system that autonomously discovers proofs. Strip away the AI and ask: what's the business? There isn't one. The AI isn't the cherry on top -- it's the entire sundae. LeanDojo from Caltech, NVIDIA, and MIT is open-sourcing comparable tooling under MIT license, which means the infrastructure layer beneath Cajal is actively being commoditized by academic groups. I learned the hard lesson with Birchbox that narrative without structural moat produces the worst venture outcome. Here, the moat is purely technical execution in a domain where the founders don't have documented deep expertise, against competitors with 150x the funding. The verified training datasets product line is the one piece that interests me -- curated, verified mathematical corpora could compound into something defensible -- but that's a data licensing business, not the "autonomous scientific discovery" narrative they're selling.

The strongest bull case is this: if formal verification becomes mandatory infrastructure for AI safety, quantum computing certification, and financial system validation, then the company that builds the best applied verification tools owns a critical chokepoint. Cajal's focus on applied domains -- quantum computing and quantitative finance rather than math olympiad problems -- could be a faster path to revenue than Harmonic's grander vision. There's a world where Harmonic builds the research prestige play and Cajal builds the practical commercial tool, the way Palantir commercialized intelligence community technology while DARPA-funded labs stayed academic. If the verified corpora compound and become the gold standard for training mathematical reasoning models, that starts to look like a data primitive other companies depend on. But for that to work, I'd need to see founders with deep formal methods credibility and some evidence -- any evidence -- that customers in quantum or finance are pulling this product. Neither signal exists in the dossier.

The co-founder dynamic raises my specific pattern-match concern: they met at Entrepreneurs First's Bridge program, which is literally a startup matchmaking residency. My portfolio tells me that founding teams forged through deep pre-existing relationships -- Andrew Kortina and I worked together for years before Fin, the Venmo founders knew each other from college -- survive the crucible of building something genuinely hard. EF Bridge is designed to pair strangers with complementary skills, which is fine for many startups but worries me when the technical challenge requires the kind of shared intuition that only comes from working through problems together over years. Formal verification at this level isn't a "move fast and learn" domain -- it's closer to building cryptographic infrastructure, where one wrong assumption in your proof system invalidates everything.

I'm passing. The idea is consensus, not contrarian. The founders are smart but lack documented expertise in the specific technical domain. The product is AI-first with no business underneath if the AI layer gets commoditized. And they're building an application on an open-source protocol against a competitor with $295 million. Everything about my framework says this is the wrong bet for my money.
