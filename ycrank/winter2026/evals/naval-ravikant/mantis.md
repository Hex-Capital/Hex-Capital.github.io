# Mantis -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice isn't the technology or the market — it's the founder trajectory. Georgia Witchel was CTO and co-founder of Louiza Labs, a YC S25 company that simulates human biology for medical device testing, raised $5M+, and then six months later she's back at YC with a closely related company under a different name. I invest in long-term games with long-term people. When a founder leaves a funded company in a domain she ostensibly has deep conviction about, to start something adjacent, the question isn't whether she's smart — she clearly is — but whether she's found the problem she'll spend a decade on, or whether she's still searching. The positioning ambiguity reinforces this: the company describes itself variously as a "digital twin company," "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data," a "domain-aware data platform," and a "unified biomedical device testing and regulatory platform." That's four different companies. At the earliest stage, I need to see a founder who has locked onto a specific insight with almost irrational conviction. Here I see someone whose specific knowledge is genuine but whose application of it is still in flux.

The specific knowledge itself is real and worth examining. Harvey Mudd CS, computational math at Johns Hopkins, biomedical engineering at UW, and then actually building physics-based biological simulations at Louiza Labs — that's a rare intersection. You can't acquire that in six months of research. The combination of physics simulation with machine learning for biological systems is the kind of knowledge that lives at the boundary between disciplines, where the most interesting problems tend to cluster. This is why I've been making deep-science bets — Extropic, Atomic Semi — the people who understand both the physics and the computation are vanishingly rare. Georgia appears to be one of them. The question is whether that knowledge gets properly channeled into one company that compounds for a decade, or dissipated across multiple attempts.

The leverage architecture has potential but a fundamental tension. "Databricks for Biomedical and Clinical Data" is an infrastructure positioning — my favorite kind of bet. If you build the canonical data layer that clinical trials run on, every new trial and every new customer makes the platform more valuable. That's compounding. But the distribution mechanism is enterprise sales to pharmaceutical companies, which is among the most unleveraged go-to-market motions that exist. Long sales cycles, regulatory procurement requirements, heavy customization, entrenched vendor relationships. Medidata has 30,000+ trials and 9 million patients of data. Unlearn.AI has $130M+ and EMA qualification. A three-person team with no confirmed customers going against that installed base isn't leverage — it's attrition warfare. The technology creates leverage; the business model consumes it.

The strongest bull case: if the convergence of LLM capabilities with regulatory acceptance of simulation-based clinical data creates a genuine architectural inflection — meaning the old statistical approaches become obsolete and physics-enhanced datasets become the new standard — then the first company to build that infrastructure layer could own the category. Georgia's cross-disciplinary knowledge would be the moat. Pharma companies losing $15M per trial to data quality issues have real budget for a solution. And the physics simulation angle is genuinely differentiated from Unlearn.AI's statistical digital twins and Syntegra's purely generative approach. If she can narrow the positioning, land two or three pharma customers who depend on the platform, and prove that physics-enhanced data actually reduces trial delays, the compounding dynamics of an infrastructure play could take over. That's a real path to something valuable.

But I keep returning to the structural concerns. Solo technical founder in a domain requiring enterprise sales, regulatory navigation, and scientific credibility simultaneously. The Louiza Labs departure is unexplained publicly — amicable spin-out or something messier? Potential IP entanglement with a funded company in an overlapping domain. No confirmed traction — no revenue, no named customers, no LOIs in the public record. The clinical data management market is real but the company's position within it is undefined. I've seen founders with extraordinary specific knowledge fail because they couldn't narrow their aperture to a single problem long enough for compounding to work. The knowledge is there. The leverage concept is there. What I don't see yet is the lock-in — the evidence that this founder has found the one problem she'll pursue with irrational focus for the next decade.
