# Pulsent -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Toplyne raised $17.5M from Tiger Global and Sequoia to do almost exactly what Pulsent is pitching — identify PLG conversion signals in B2B SaaS and act on them — and then shut down and returned capital to investors. That's the fact that should anchor every conversation about Pulsent. The category has a corpse, and the corpse had $17.5M and two of the best cap tables in venture behind it. Pulsent's twist is "but now we have AI agents that execute autonomously instead of flagging signals for human reps." OK. Maybe that's a real unlock. But the burden of proof is on Pulsent to explain why the buyer persona that wouldn't pay Toplyne enough to survive will now pay Pulsent for an autonomous version of the same value proposition. The dossier doesn't contain that explanation.

Intercom's Fin AI agent is approaching $100M ARR. Pendo surpassed $200M ARR and is layering on AI features including predictive engagement scores that highlight expansion-ready accounts. Pulsent is building an integration layer across PostHog, Mixpanel, Amplitude, Snowflake, BigQuery, Stripe, Salesforce, and Attio — every one of those platforms has an engineering team 100x larger than Pulsent's two-person shop. The dossier itself flags it: "The core components — LLM-powered message generation, event stream ingestion, session replay analysis — are accessible via commercial APIs and open-source tools." This is the AI kill test in its purest form. Every time Pulsent gets pitched to a prospect, Intercom is already in that prospect's stack, and Intercom's product team is asking "should we just add this?" The answer is obviously yes. Pulsent isn't a protocol. It's not infrastructure. It's an application-layer integration sitting on top of other people's data pipes and other people's delivery channels. That's a feature, not a company.

"AI agents for B2B sales engagement" is the consensus hottest category in the Spring 2026 YC batch — I'd bet there are five companies in this batch alone pitching some variant of it. Pulsent carries every tag that makes me reach for the pass button: Artificial Intelligence, SaaS, B2B, Sales Enablement. There is zero mispricing here. Every VC at demo day understands why this should exist. Nobody is uncomfortable with the category. Nobody thinks the founders are crazy for building it. The market has priced in the upside already. Compare this to when I wrote the Solana check at four cents — every smart crypto investor thought I was wrong, Ethereum had won, alternative L1s were dead money. That mispricing is what created the 2,000x. Pulsent has the opposite dynamic: the price reflects the optimism, which means the only direction for surprise is down.

Nicolò Magnante brings Bain and BCG consulting experience, and Arseniy Shishaev spent three years at Datadog building data pipeline tooling that handles "hundreds of millions of data assets using complex recursive queries" — that's directly relevant to Pulsent's event-processing architecture. They co-founded Bluco together, worked together 3+ years, and Bluco won a €25K AXA grant and landed clients including Porsche and Trenord railways. That's a real co-founder relationship, not a manufactured one. Arseniy's Datadog pedigree is exactly the kind of technical foundation you'd want for this product. But here's the problem with my sane/insane framework: these are sane people pitching a sane idea. "AI agents that watch user behavior and send personalized outreach" is what every B2B SaaS founder already believes should exist. There's nothing about Pulsent's thesis that makes a smart person say "that's fucking crazy." It's rational, it's obvious, and that's precisely why it doesn't excite me. Sane person, sane idea — that's the configuration I find least interesting.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Pulsent already has paying customers at pre-seed. Arseniy's Datadog background gives Pulsent a genuine technical edge in event pipeline architecture that most AI wrapper companies lack. The structural gap between analytics platforms (PostHog can observe but won't send emails — channel conflict) and engagement platforms (Intercom can send messages but lacks deep product analytics) is real and may persist because neither side is incentivized to fully cross that boundary. The "visual AI" timing argument — that multimodal models capable of interpreting session recordings only became viable in December 2025 — could represent a genuine capability unlock that Toplyne didn't have. And the 3+ year co-founder relationship with a prior company together is exactly the pattern I look for. If Pulsent can demonstrate that autonomous execution (vs. Toplyne's human-in-the-loop flagging) fundamentally changes buyer willingness to pay, and if the data unification layer creates real switching costs per customer, this could carve out a durable niche before Intercom or Pendo close the gap. But that's a lot of "ifs" in a category with a dead body and two incumbents converging from both sides with hundreds of millions in ARR.

Pulsent would flip me from Pass to Invest on one verifiable condition: demonstrate $50K+ MRR with >120% net revenue retention from customers who previously used both an analytics tool and an engagement tool and ripped them out in favor of Pulsent's unified agent. That would prove the integration layer creates enough value to resist incumbent encroachment and that the buyer persona actually pays — the thing Toplyne couldn't prove with $17.5M. Without that, Pulsent is a well-executed AI wrapper in a consensus-hot category where the risk isn't in the price — it's hidden behind the enthusiasm. The two dimensions that killed this for me are Consensus Mispricing (every VC wants to fund AI agents for sales, zero asymmetry) and Structural Durability (pure SaaS integration layer with no moat AI can't dissolve). Pulsent's founders are capable, but capable founders in a bad structure still lose.
