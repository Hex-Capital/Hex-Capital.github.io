# Andustry -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

500k+ verified companies across 100+ countries, claimed by a two-person pre-seed team — that number is either extraordinary operational velocity or a database scrape rebranded as a network. Andustry's core claim is that AI agents can autonomously discover suppliers, negotiate quotes, and normalize comparison data for SMEs buying injection-molded parts and CNC-machined components. I start where I always start: if Andustry fully succeeds — becomes the dominant global platform for industrial parts sourcing — what happens to the world? Purchasing managers at mid-size manufacturers get quotes in days instead of weeks. Procurement departments shrink. Some brokers lose their margins. That is operationally useful. It is not consequential. Nobody's life materially changes. Hundreds of millions of people do not wake up in a different world because industrial sourcing got 3x faster. Andustry is competing for redistribution of existing procurement spend — the $743B industrial machinery market the dossier cites — not creating new demand that didn't exist before. When I funded Impossible Foods, the consequence was feeding billions without animal agriculture. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, the consequence was limitless clean energy. Andustry's consequence, at maximum ambition, is a more efficient transaction layer. That's a different category of outcome entirely.

Johann Stürken brings RWTH Aachen mechanical engineering, McKinsey consulting, Airbus experience, and a family background in trading goods — that's genuine industrial domain knowledge paired with Het Dave's CS foundation from IIT Jodhpur. The bull case an advocate would make goes like this: Andustry democratizes global manufacturing access for the millions of SMEs that can't afford procurement departments, the way DoorDash democratized restaurant access for consumers who couldn't get delivery. The €1M+ transaction volume at pre-seed with just two people shows real execution. The pay-on-delivery model eliminates the friction of upfront commissions that Thomasnet and traditional brokers charge. And if Andustry builds a verified supplier network with proprietary transaction data, the matching gets better with scale — classic two-sided network effect. The advocate points to DoorDash as the Known Exception: Khosla funded an execution-heavy marketplace without novel technology because the consequence magnitude was planetary and the immigrant founder (Tony Xu) demonstrated extraordinary learning velocity. Here's where the parallel breaks: DoorDash touched hundreds of millions of consumers. Andustry touches SME purchasing managers. The breadth of human impact is categorically different, and that's why the DoorDash exception doesn't rescue Andustry from its fundamental consequence problem.

Andustry's technical approach — LLM-based agents for supplier discovery and quote normalization — is application-layer work on top of existing models. The dossier states plainly: "The core AI-matching technology uses LLM-based agents applied to procurement, which is technically reproducible." Multiple AI procurement startups already exist: Leverage AI, Zip, Fairmarkit. Xometry, with $687M in FY2025 revenue and its $300M Thomasnet acquisition, already has 500k+ sellers and AI quoting infrastructure. Andustry argues that Xometry's vertically integrated model creates channel conflict that prevents it from offering pure sourcing — a reasonable structural argument, but a defensive moat built on an incumbent's organizational constraints is fragile. The moment Xometry decides the sourcing market is worth cannibalizing its own margins, Andustry's differentiation evaporates. There is no proprietary science here, no step-function technology breakthrough, no 10x cost reduction in the underlying product. This is a wrapper with a clever business model, not a wedge driven by technical non-linearity.

Johann Stürken's domain credentials are strong — the mechanical engineering degree, the consulting pedigree, the family trading background — but Andustry's founding profile is exactly the kind I find less compelling: domain experts building a better version of what they know rather than outsiders reimagining the system from first principles. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught New Zealander building rocket engines in his garage. When I backed the Mach Industries founder, it was a 19-year-old MIT dropout taking on Lockheed Martin. Those founders didn't know what couldn't be done. Johann Stürken knows exactly what industrial procurement looks like — that's useful for execution but suggests incremental improvement rather than radical reimagination. I see no evidence in the dossier of pivots, surprises, or rapid learning during the YC batch — just a well-executed plan by competent people. The €1M+ transaction volume is real execution signal, and I respect it, but execution velocity in a well-understood market is not the same as learning velocity in an uncertain one.

Andustry's path to radical affordability is modest at best. The platform eliminates broker commissions and listing fees — saving buyers perhaps 2-5% on transaction costs — but the industrial parts themselves still cost what suppliers charge. Andustry is not inventing a new manufacturing process, not discovering cheaper materials, not using AI to design parts that use 90% less material. It is making the procurement layer more efficient. Compare that to what excites me: Curai Health delivering AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost, or fusion energy too cheap to meter. Andustry makes sourcing somewhat cheaper. That is fine for a venture fund optimizing for 3-5x returns. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money, and the consequence profile here caps the upside.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Andustry demonstrated that its AI agents could autonomously redesign parts specifications to achieve 10x manufacturing cost reduction — not just finding cheaper suppliers, but fundamentally re-engineering what gets built and how — that would be a non-linear technical approach with genuine consequence magnitude. A platform that uses AI to collapse the cost of custom manufacturing, not just the cost of finding a manufacturer, would be a different company entirely. As it stands, Andustry is a competent B2B marketplace with solid founder-market fit, real early traction, and a large addressable market — precisely the kind of company that generates the moderate returns I find uninteresting. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are consequence magnitude (the world doesn't change if Andustry succeeds) and non-linear technical approach (LLM wrappers applied to procurement are reproducible and incremental). Both point clearly to Pass.
