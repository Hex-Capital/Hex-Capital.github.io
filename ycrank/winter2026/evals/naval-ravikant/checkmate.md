# Checkmate -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what's missing from the founder profiles: neither Neil Garde nor James Sha has ever worked at a hedge fund, a PE firm, an expert network, or anywhere in the institutional research workflow they're trying to automate. Garde spent eight years as a Google PM across AI, AR/VR, and games, then built Monzo's US operation. Sha built NLP at Google, ranked content at Meta, and co-founded a fitness tech company that scaled to 300K users before acquisition. These are strong builders -- Berkeley-trained, Google-seasoned, with a real exit between them. But the knowledge they carry is about building AI products, not about how a senior analyst at a $10B long/short fund actually conducts a channel check, manages MNPI exposure, or reads the micro-signals in an expert's hesitation. When I backed the founders who built Stack Overflow, they weren't just talented engineers -- they had lived inside the developer knowledge problem for years. The gap here is between competence and obsession. Smart generalists identified a workflow that looked automatable and applied their considerable technical skills to it. That's how you build a feature, not necessarily how you build a company that survives a $4B incumbent entering your exact market.

The leverage architecture is real but incremental. An analyst who can dispatch AI agents to ten expert calls simultaneously instead of sitting through them one by one gains genuine throughput -- that's a 10x on time. But this is automation leverage, not a new category of capability. Compare it to what Uber created: before Uber, there was no mechanism for a car owner to monetize idle time through a smartphone. That was a qualitatively new form of leverage. Checkmate takes an existing interaction -- the expert network call -- and replaces the human interviewer with an AI one. The output is the same artifact (a transcript and memo), produced more efficiently. Efficiency gains are valuable, but they compress to commodity pricing faster than new leverage categories do. The network-agnostic integration model -- forwarding calendar invites from any expert network -- is clever distribution design, but it's a product feature, not a structural leverage mechanism.

The timing is consensus, not contrarian. AlphaSense launched its AI Agent Interviewer in 2025, built on its $930M Tegus acquisition. InsightAgent already offers both autonomous and CoPilot modes. Brightwave raised $21M for AI-powered financial research. Nobody smart is arguing that AI won't transform expert research -- the debate is over who captures the value. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, the consensus was that cryptocurrency was irrelevant. When I invested in Uber, the consensus was that ride-sharing was illegal. Here, the consensus is fully aligned with Checkmate's thesis. The question isn't whether AI will automate expert calls -- it's whether a pre-seed startup can outrun a $500M-ARR incumbent with 6,500 enterprise customers and a 200,000-transcript library, while also fending off funded competitors like InsightAgent. That's an execution race, and execution races favor the player with distribution, capital, and existing customer relationships.

The strongest bull case: Checkmate's network-agnostic positioning could be structurally superior to AlphaSense's walled garden. If institutional buyers don't want to be locked into one expert ecosystem -- and compliance teams value the ability to run AI interviews across GLG, AlphaSights, Guidepoint, and direct relationships simultaneously -- then the Switzerland approach wins on flexibility. Sha's NLP background at Google is directly applicable to building adaptive conversational agents, and Garde's experience navigating regulated fintech at Monzo maps to the compliance challenges of selling to hedge funds. The SOC II certification and MNPI guardrails, once embedded in a fund's compliance workflow, create real switching costs. If expert acceptance normalizes quickly and Checkmate captures the compliance-trusted workflow layer across all networks, the transcript corpus compounds into a defensible data asset. This is the path to a platform play -- the interview infrastructure layer beneath all expert networks, the way Stripe became the payment layer beneath all commerce. For this to work, they'd need to build trust faster than AlphaSense can open its walled garden, and they'd need the expert networks themselves not to restrict AI agents from joining their calls. Both are possible but neither is assured.

The technology compounding is weak at this stage. The core capability -- LLM-powered voice agents conducting structured multi-turn conversations -- is increasingly accessible to any well-funded engineering team. The dossier itself flags this: "the barrier to entry is moderate." The potential data moat from accumulated transcripts is real in theory but depends entirely on winning the adoption race, and AlphaSense starts with a 200,000-transcript head start. The compliance infrastructure has more defensibility than the AI itself -- SOC II, MNPI guardrails, and expert consent workflows are slow to build and costly to switch -- but compliance is a necessary condition for the sale, not a technology moat. The multiple brand identities (Checkmate Research, Expert Insights, Labyrinth Technologies) suggest the company is still finding its positioning, which is normal at pre-seed but adds friction in a market where institutional trust is the primary purchase criterion.

This is a competent team in a real market building something that works. But the specific knowledge lives in AI product development, not in institutional research. The leverage is automation, not creation. The timing is consensus, not contrarian. And the technology is accessible, not compounding. I'd want to see founders who've spent years inside the expert network ecosystem and can articulate non-obvious insights about why the current workflow fails in ways that no outsider would recognize. Without that, this is a feature race against a gorilla.
