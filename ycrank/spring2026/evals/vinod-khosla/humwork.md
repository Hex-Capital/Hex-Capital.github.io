# Humwork -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Tendem, backed by $72M from Bezos Expeditions via Nebius, already offers MCP-based human expert routing for AI agents with 10,000+ experts — ten times Humwork's 1,000+. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where a well-funded incumbent has already built the same product using the same protocol, I don't ask "can they compete?" I ask "is the consequence of winning so enormous that competition is irrelevant?" With OpenAI, every other AI lab existed, but the consequence of AGI dwarfed the competitive landscape. With Humwork, the consequence of full success is that AI agents get unstuck faster. Developers save twenty minutes. That's a useful service. It's not a changed world. Nobody's healthcare becomes free. No basic human need is transformed. Humwork at maximum scale is a well-run marketplace generating solid revenue in a market that, by the dossier's own cited projection, reaches $16.4B by 2030. That's a fine business for most investors. It's not my kind of bet.

Humwork's core thesis contains a structural contradiction I can't ignore. I've said publicly that 80% of jobs will be done by AI by 2030 and that AI will deliver free healthcare, free education, free legal services. Humwork monetizes the gap between what AI agents promise and what they deliver today — the 20% of cases where agents stall and need human rescue. But AI capability is improving on a steep curve. Every dollar Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google invest in model quality shrinks Humwork's addressable problem. The 87% resolution rate Humwork claims is impressive operationally, but it measures how well humans fix what AI can't — and the set of things AI can't do is compressing rapidly. Humwork is a bridge business on a bridge that's getting shorter. I've lost enough money on businesses with structural ceilings — I don't need to add another.

Yash Goenka is the most interesting thing about Humwork. Repeat YC founder across consecutive batches (S25 with Qualify.bot, P26 with Humwork), serial AI builder since 2021, patent holder for graphene supercapacitor manufacturing, UC Berkeley rocketry team reaching Mach 1.2. The breadth signals energy and velocity. But the pattern also concerns me: Instawrite.ai, Jarvys.ai, Iris BCI, Phonecall.bot, Qualify.bot, and now Humwork — six distinct products across five years, each in a different domain. When I talk about learning rate, I mean a founder going deep on one hard problem and adapting faster than anyone expected. Yash shows the opposite pattern — horizontal exploration across many problems rather than vertical depth on one. The pivot from Qualify.bot to Humwork in nine months, both under Orange AI Inc., suggests he found no durable product-market fit with loan qualification agents and moved on. That's not learning velocity in my framework. That's search velocity. Rohan Datta brings genuine engineering depth — five years of data science at Microgrid Labs and an AI voice platform processing 1M+ call minutes — but his civil engineering background and the team's overlapping AI/ML skill sets don't create the capability diversity I look for in early gene pools.

The MCP integration layer that defines Humwork's distribution is, by the dossier's own assessment, "technically replicable — any company with an expert network could build an MCP server." Tendem has already done so. The 30-second matching SLA and the <2-minute average first reply are operational achievements, not technological ones. There is no proprietary science here, no novel architecture, no step-function improvement in cost or capability. Humwork is a wrapper around existing MCP infrastructure connected to a curated freelancer network. When I backed Rocket Lab, the Rutherford engine's electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers represented a genuinely non-linear approach to launch cost. When I backed Impossible Foods, synthesizing heme protein was a science breakthrough. Humwork's core technology is a routing algorithm matching agents to humans. That's engineering, not invention.

The strongest bull case for Humwork is this: the 130% net retention in software engineering suggests genuine product-market fit, the human-in-the-loop AI market is growing at 24.9% CAGR, and Yash's rapid iteration proves he'll find the wedge that Tendem's broader positioning misses. An advocate would point to DoorDash — a marketplace with no technology breakthrough that I funded because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access was planetary and Tony Xu's learning velocity was exceptional. But the DoorDash exception required planetary-scale consequence magnitude to override the absence of technical novelty. Humwork's consequence magnitude doesn't approach that threshold. Faster AI agent debugging for developers is not feeding billions or democratizing transportation. The exception doesn't apply. One condition would flip me: if Humwork demonstrated that its expert resolution data created a proprietary training signal that made AI agents permanently better — turning human interventions into model improvements at scale — that would be a non-linear technical approach with genuine consequence magnitude. The data flywheel would need to be real, demonstrated, and defensible against the AI labs building those models themselves.

Humwork adds cost to AI workflows rather than removing it. My deepest investment pattern — the one I don't always state but that drives my highest-conviction bets from Impossible Foods to Commonwealth Fusion Systems to Curai Health — is making the expensive radically affordable. Humwork charges agent operators for human expert time. It inserts a new cost layer between AI agents and their users. If AI is supposed to make expertise free, Humwork's business model depends on AI failing to do so. That's a bet against the most powerful technology trend of our generation. I'd rather fund the companies making the experts unnecessary than the company monetizing their necessity.
