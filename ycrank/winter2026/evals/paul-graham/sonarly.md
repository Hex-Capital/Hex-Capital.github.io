# Sonarly -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that immediately concerns me about Sonarly is the competitive landscape. Sentry already reports ~95% root cause accuracy and ~54% fix rate on their own Autofix feature. Datadog shipped Bits AI Dev Agent tested across 2,000+ customer environments. incident.io raised $62M in a Series B. When I see a pre-seed startup entering a category where two public companies and three well-funded startups are all racing to ship AI-powered incident remediation, I think about what I observed in 2025 — the most impressive companies I'd seen were not working on AI. That's not because AI is bad. It's because when everyone agrees a category is hot, the upside has already been priced. The structural argument here — that incumbents can't build cross-platform aggregation because it would cannibalize their lock-in — is clever. But clever structural arguments are not the same as schlep blindness. Stripe didn't win because of a clever structural argument about payment processors. It won because the Collisons were willing to walk into banks and deal with compliance for years while everyone else looked away.

The organic origin is real, and I want to give credit for that. Dimittri and Alexandre were running MEORIA with two people, getting 50 alerts a day, and they got fed up. That's genuine. They didn't sit down and brainstorm "what's hot in AI" — they lived with a broken workflow and decided to fix it. But the schlep here is thin. Connecting to Sentry and Datadog via OAuth, deduplicating alerts, and piping context to Claude Code — this is integration work, not the kind of painful, unsexy complexity that structurally deters competitors. When I think about schlep blindness, I think about problems where your unconscious literally prevents you from seeing the opportunity because the work is so ugly. Payment processing regulations. Trust between strangers. Last-mile food logistics. Wiring together monitoring APIs is hard engineering work, but it's the kind of hard that a good team at Sentry or Datadog can ship in a quarter.

The founders have been working together since CY Tech and co-founded MEORIA before Sonarly, so they pass the co-founder test — their relationship predates the company. Moving from France to San Francisco to do YC shows real commitment. But I'm missing signals that make me lean forward. No public GitHub repos from either founder. No prior experience building infrastructure or developer tools. Self-taught in AI through tutorials. The gap between building an edtech chatbot and building production infrastructure tooling that engineers at serious companies will trust with their codebase — that gap is enormous. A Hacker News commenter reported only 30% of generated PRs were mergeable. For a product whose entire value proposition is automated code fixes, that number needs to be dramatically higher. This isn't a nice-to-have UX issue; it's the core product working or not working.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that cross-platform aggregation creates enough value that teams prefer a third-party layer over their existing monitoring tool's built-in AI features; that a 2-person team can maintain reliable integrations across Sentry, Datadog, Grafana, and whatever comes next while also improving fix quality from 30% to 80%+; and that the "living map" of production systems creates meaningful switching costs before incumbents copy the cross-platform approach or one monitoring platform becomes dominant enough to make aggregation irrelevant. If all three are true, Sonarly occupies a genuinely valuable position — the monitoring-agnostic remediation layer that every engineering team plugs in alongside whatever observability stack they've already chosen. The structural opening created by incumbent lock-in dynamics is real. And the problem is real — alert fatigue wastes enormous amounts of engineering time. But needing three things to all go right is close to my "more than one miracle" red flag.

The one user case study — 180 alerts reduced to 5 actionable — is a compelling data point for the deduplication and triage value, even if the automated fix quality needs work. If they focused on the triage layer as the wedge and treated automated PRs as the long-term play, there might be a path. But the product is free, there's no pricing, no revenue signal, and modest initial traction (30 HN upvotes is fine but not a signal of organic pull). At this stage I'd want to see engineers adopting Sonarly and refusing to give it up — the kind of bottom-up love that developer tools need to survive. I don't see that yet.
