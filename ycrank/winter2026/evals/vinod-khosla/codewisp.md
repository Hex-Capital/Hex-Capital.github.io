# CodeWisp -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: Elvin Fu is the most qualified person in the world to build this exact product, and that's my biggest concern. Top-20 on Scratch out of 135 million users, 20 million students taught game development, two game engines built from scratch, 84,000 YouTube subscribers in game dev education. He knows this domain cold. But I've said it a hundred times -- experience is a set of biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing. Fu isn't an outsider reimagining game creation from first principles. He's the ultimate insider building the next logical step in his career. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he had no aerospace pedigree, which is precisely why he could rethink launch economics from scratch. When I backed Pat Brown, he was a biochemist who didn't know what the food industry considered impossible. Fu knows exactly what's possible and what's not in game development education, and he's built something that fits neatly within those boundaries.

Let me apply my first test: if CodeWisp succeeds completely -- dominates its category, becomes the default platform for AI-generated games -- what changes? More people make simple web browser games. That's fine. It's a real business opportunity. But no trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. Animal agriculture doesn't get disrupted. The power grid doesn't transform. Even within gaming, the $290 billion industry continues to be dominated by AAA studios, mobile publishers, and platforms like Roblox and Steam. CodeWisp at full success is a popular consumer creation tool -- closer to Canva for games than to anything that restructures an industry. The consequence of success is a good business, not a transformation of how the world works. I've passed on companies with stronger teams because the ambition was calibrated too small for the talent involved.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. Roblox started as a simple platform for kids to create games and became a $25 billion company with powerful network effects. If CodeWisp rides the AI-native wave and becomes the platform where a generation creates, publishes, and discovers games through natural language -- and if the 1,513 published projects are the seed of a flywheel that compounds into millions -- the platform network effects could be enormous. Fu's distribution advantage (20 million students reached) is a real asset that competitors can't easily replicate. And the timing is genuinely good: "vibe coding" is early, LLM code generation is on an exponential improvement curve, and browser-based game distribution has matured. If I squint hard enough, I can see a world where AI-generated games become a new entertainment category the way user-generated content on YouTube disrupted traditional media. But that vision requires CodeWisp to become something much more ambitious than what the current product suggests -- and the dossier shows no evidence that the founder is thinking at that scale.

The technology layer concerns me most. The core capability -- LLM-powered code generation for games -- is a wrapper on third-party APIs. Rosebud AI already has $9.2 million in funding and $2.7 million in 2024 revenue doing essentially the same thing. Replit has $222 million in funding and a game builder. There's no proprietary model, no novel technical approach that makes the existing way of creating games economically unviable. The "differentiation" is the educational courses and community -- valuable for distribution, but not a technology moat. When I invested in Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu had a fundamentally different architecture for routing that made Cisco's approach obsolete at scale. I don't see the equivalent here. The community of 1,513 games with a top game at 1,355 views is a start, but it's thin traction against competitors who are further along with more capital.

The timing trajectory is the one dimension that works in CodeWisp's favor. LLM capability for code generation is genuinely on an exponential curve, and the cultural moment around AI-generated games is early enough that category leadership is still available. But being well-timed in a commoditizing technology layer isn't sufficient -- you need either proprietary technology or overwhelming network effects, and at 1,513 projects with modest view counts, the network effects haven't ignited. The single-founder listing with two unidentified team members adds risk without adding information. I don't know if the team is engineered to address the critical risks -- ML capability, growth engineering, game design depth -- because I can't see who's on it.

Fu is too smart to spend his career on a problem this bounded. He has genuine distribution, real technical chops, and deep domain knowledge. But he's pointed all of that at making a slightly more accessible version of tools that already exist, in a space where better-funded competitors are already operating. The money either expires or you build something that changes the world -- and I don't see world-changing ambition in the current framing.
