# Confluence Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Confluence Labs: two people -- one a self-taught dropout who started coding in 2022, the other a research prodigy who left an Ivy League school -- built a system that scored 97.9% on one of the hardest reasoning benchmarks in AI, humiliating models that cost billions to train. And then they open-sourced it. That's a bold move, and it tells me something about these founders' confidence in what they know versus what they've shown. Brent Burdick's trajectory in particular catches my eye -- teaching yourself to code and within four years achieving state-of-the-art results on a benchmark designed to test general intelligence is genuinely remarkable. I taught myself to program in public libraries, and I know what that kind of self-directed obsession feels like from the inside. But here's where my investing instinct diverges from my admiration: when I sit with this company and ask myself the question I always ask -- tell me your story, not the business -- I can't find the thread that connects these founders' lives to the specific pain of scientists struggling with data-sparse experiments. The problem seems to have found them through a benchmark competition, not through a lived experience that won't let them sleep at night.

The category itself is where I hesitate most. AI is the most crowded, most consensus-driven space in technology right now. Every fund, every accelerator, every corporate R&D lab is pouring money into AI reasoning, AI for science, AI for drug discovery. When I backed SpaceX, private space was considered delusional. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. When we funded Crusoe, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was actual lunacy. Those companies were unpopular and difficult to assess -- the two qualities I've learned are the strongest predictive signals. Confluence Labs is popular and easy to assess. They have a benchmark score that makes them legible to every AI-literate investor in the Valley. Paul Graham backed them. They're in YC. Imbue closed within three points of their result within days. This is a leaderboard race in the hottest category in tech, and leaderboard races favor the best-capitalized teams. Imbue has $232M and 10,000 H100s. Isomorphic Labs has $600M and Google's infrastructure. The fact that two brilliant people achieved a remarkable result with minimal resources is inspiring, but it doesn't constitute a moat -- especially when they've MIT-licensed the demonstration.

Let me give the bull case its due, because it deserves serious consideration. If the insight that underpins their ARC-AGI-2 result -- that you can get LLMs to solve novel problems from minimal examples through structured program synthesis -- genuinely transfers from abstract grid puzzles to real-world scientific domains, then Confluence Labs is sitting on something structurally different from every big AI lab. OpenAI, DeepMind, and Anthropic are architecturally committed to data scale. Their entire business model, their compute infrastructure, their hiring -- it's all oriented toward training on ever-larger datasets. A company that can extract maximum signal from dozens of data points rather than billions occupies a completely different niche, and it's a niche that matters enormously in fields where each experiment costs millions of dollars. That's a genuinely contrarian technical thesis hiding inside a popular category. If these founders can prove the transfer from benchmarks to biology or materials science -- even one compelling demonstration with a real research partner -- the story changes dramatically.

But I'm not investing in "if" stories with my own money at this stage, not without the biographical conviction I need. I don't see the founder who spent years in a lab watching experiments fail and thought "there has to be a better way." I don't see the personal stake that makes this the only problem worth solving. What I see are two extremely talented young engineers who won a hard competition and are now searching for the commercial application. That's a fine starting point for many investors, but it's not how my best investments have worked. My best investments start with a person who is inseparable from the problem -- Travis and the taxi medallions, Crusoe's team and the waste gas they couldn't stop thinking about. I also notice the Atlassian Confluence naming collision, which signals something about market awareness that nags at me. And the co-founder relationship is a blank -- no shared history, no visible stress-testing, no information about how they found each other or how they fight. After HQ Trivia, I don't write checks into unknown co-founder dynamics without at least a conversation about how they handle disagreement.

This is a pass for me, but it's a respectful one. These founders are building real capability, and someone with a different thesis -- someone who leads with technical benchmarks and TAM analysis rather than biographical grit and anti-consensus conviction -- should take a hard look. If they come back with a demonstrated application in a specific scientific domain and a story about why that domain is personal to them, I'd want to have coffee.
