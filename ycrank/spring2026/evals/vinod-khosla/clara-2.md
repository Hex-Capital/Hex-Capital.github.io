# Clara -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Clara: every co-founder came from the same company. George Favvas, Caitlin Swift, Zeeshan Ahmed -- all Circle Medical alumni. Favvas built that business to $100M in revenue with profitability. That's a remarkable achievement. And now he's assembling the same crew to build version 2.0 with AI at the center instead of the clinician. This is the pattern I know well, and it makes me cautious. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he'd never worked in aerospace. When I backed Pat Brown at Impossible Foods, he'd never set foot in a food company. The outsiders didn't know what the industry considered impossible, so they attempted it anyway. Favvas knows exactly what healthcare delivery considers impossible -- and I suspect that knowledge has constrained his ambition to what's achievable rather than what's transformational.

Let me be precise about the consequence of success. If Clara works as described -- AI handles initial clinical reasoning, human providers review and approve -- you've dramatically improved clinician throughput. Maybe 5x, maybe 10x. In a country facing an 86,000-physician shortage by 2036, that matters enormously. Tens of millions of people could access primary care who currently can't. That's a genuine, large-scale human impact. But here's where I part from the enthusiasts: the architecture preserves the fundamental structure of the system. State licensing still constrains scaling. Insurance billing still dictates economics. Board-certified clinicians still sit in the loop as the regulatory and liability backstop. Clara makes the existing healthcare delivery machine run faster. It doesn't replace the machine. Compare this to what an 80%-automated AI physician would mean -- no human reviewer for routine encounters, costs dropping by an order of magnitude, primary care available to every person on the planet through a phone. That's the version with civilization-scale consequences. But this team, because they've operated inside healthcare's regulatory constraints for a decade, has designed something that fits within those constraints rather than challenging them.

The bull case is real, and I want to give it its due. Favvas is one of the rare founders in this YC batch who has actually scaled a healthcare company past $100M. He knows every operational landmine -- provider licensing across 50 states, insurance billing codes, clinical liability structures, EHR integrations. The timing is arguably perfect: Carbon Health's Chapter 11 bankruptcy just demonstrated that clinic-heavy primary care models are structurally broken, LLMs crossed clinical reasoning thresholds in 2024-2025, and the physician shortage is accelerating on a predictable curve. The data integration with 150,000+ healthcare facilities creates genuine switching costs as patient histories accumulate. And there's something I respect about a founder who builds a $100M human-first telehealth company and then says "my own model is obsolete -- AI should be primary." That's a form of contrarian courage, even if it comes from an insider. If healthcare regulation evolves faster than expected to accommodate AI-first care, and if Clara executes its operational playbook while competitors stumble on the clinical operations they've never built, this could become the dominant primary care platform in the U.S. within a decade. That outcome justifies a significant bet.

But I keep returning to the technology question. The dossier states plainly that "the core AI reasoning layer is not proprietary -- differentiation depends on the clinical operations wrapper." This is an operational moat, not a technological one. K Health has $441M in funding and 413 employees building AI triage. Hippocratic AI sits at a $3.5B valuation with 115 million clinical interactions already logged. OpenAI and Google could integrate medical records into their foundation models. Clara's edge is that Favvas knows how to run a telehealth operation -- but operational expertise is replicable. When I invested in Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu had a fundamentally different router architecture that made Cisco's approach structurally obsolete. When I invested in Square, the technology made traditional POS infrastructure unnecessary. Clara's technology doesn't make anything obsolete; it makes the existing clinician workflow more efficient. That's the difference between disruption and optimization.

The team composition reveals the same pattern. A CEO-operator, a nurse practitioner, and a product designer -- all from the same company, all steeped in the same operational worldview. There's no AI researcher, no machine learning scientist, no one who might push the technology boundary past "LLM wrapper with clinical operations." For a company whose entire thesis is "AI primary care doctor," the absence of deep AI talent in the founding team tells me where the actual differentiation lives: not in the intelligence layer, but in the regulatory and operational scaffolding around it. That's a defensible business, perhaps. But it's not the kind of technology bet that keeps me up at night with excitement.

I'd write a check if Favvas told me the human-in-the-loop was a temporary regulatory concession on the path to fully autonomous AI medicine -- that Clara's real ambition was to prove AI clinical judgment was superior to human judgment and then lobby to change the regulatory framework. That would be a bet on where medicine needs to go, not where it is. Instead, what I see is an experienced operator building a better, AI-enhanced version of the company he already built. The probability of a successful business is genuinely higher than most YC companies I evaluate. And that's exactly what concerns me.
