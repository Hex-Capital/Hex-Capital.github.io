# Dispatch -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Dispatch: this market has already been discovered. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, private space was considered genuinely insane -- there was no Varda with $329 million and five successful reentry missions, no Inversion with $125 million in government backing, no seven other funded startups building the same category of vehicle. The whole reason SpaceX was a generational investment was because nobody else believed it was possible. Dispatch is entering a space where the technical feasibility has already been demonstrated, the TAM projections are being published by market research firms, and YC itself funded a near-identical company -- Reditus Space -- just one batch earlier. The consensus has already formed. The weirdness has been arbitraged away. And that's the core tension for me, because the most promising companies are supposed to be unpopular and difficult to assess, and this one is neither.

Now, I want to be fair to the thing that IS interesting here: two engineers building a full-scale reentry heat shield in an apartment for what they claim is 100x cheaper than competitors. That has the scrappy, first-principles energy I'm drawn to. It reminds me of the early days when founders were doing things in garages that incumbents spent millions on. If that 100x cost claim holds up at production scale -- and that's a massive "if" since it's unverified and ground-testing with rocket exhaust is fundamentally different from Mach 20+ atmospheric reentry -- then Dispatch could be the cost disruptor in a market where everyone else is burning through enormous capital. The picks-and-shovels positioning as a logistics provider rather than owning the manufacturing payload is also smart, because it means they don't have to solve two hard problems simultaneously. That model creates the potential to serve multiple payload operators, which is the kind of infrastructure play I've seen work before.

But when I try to do what I always do first -- ask these founders to tell me their story, not the business -- I come up empty. Payton Case has a B.S. from Purdue, worked at Aerospace Corporation on Lunar Gateway, then the Office of the CTO at Astranis. Andrew Mello has a Northeastern engineering degree, did stints at Apple, Zoox, and Astranis. These are excellent aerospace credentials. But credentials aren't what I'm listening for. I want to know what happened in their lives that makes returning payloads from orbit the thing they cannot NOT do. I want the biographical thread that tells me when they're facing a funding gap of tens of millions of dollars -- because the gap between $500K in YC funding and the $40-125 million their competitors have spent before even demonstrating reentry is staggering -- they won't quit. I don't see that thread. I see two talented engineers who identified a market opportunity through their professional experience, and that's a fundamentally different thing than a founder whose identity is fused to the mission.

The bull case I genuinely considered: what if Dispatch is to Varda what SpaceX was to Boeing? A scrappy, radically cost-efficient challenger that does it for pennies while incumbents spend dollars? If the heat shield technology really does represent a 100x cost breakthrough, and if that advantage compounds across the full vehicle architecture, Dispatch could reach orbital demonstration at a fraction of competitor spend and become the default return logistics provider for an entire ecosystem of space manufacturers. The picks-and-shovels model means they'd benefit from every company that wants to make things in microgravity without needing to win the manufacturing race themselves. That's a real vision, and it's the kind of infrastructure-at-an-inflection-point bet I've made before with Crusoe. But Crusoe had something Dispatch doesn't: a founding story that was magically weird in a way nobody else saw, and a willingness from our team to go all-in during the crisis. Here, I'd need to see the founders demonstrate that same level of obsessive, identity-level commitment, and I'd need the cost claims validated beyond a single ground test before I could get comfortable with the capital intensity ahead.

The economic access dimension is essentially absent here. In-space manufacturing of semiconductors and pharma serves existing high-value industries. There's no angle where Dispatch creates meaningful opportunity for underserved populations or makes an opaque system transparent for people who currently lack access. That matters to me -- it's not a dealbreaker alone, but combined with the consensus market dynamics and the missing biographical narrative, it means I don't feel the jolt. I'm not daydreaming about what this becomes. I respect the technical ambition, I appreciate the scrappy heat shield story, and I think the co-founders' shared Astranis experience gives them a solid working foundation. But this isn't my kind of bet.
