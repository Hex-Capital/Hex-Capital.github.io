# Thirdbrain Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Thirdbrain Labs is entering what may be the most consensus market in AI infrastructure right now. Post-training customization -- fine-tuning, RLHF, preference learning -- is the topic of every AI infrastructure blog post, every cloud provider roadmap, and every incumbent's expansion strategy. Scale AI launched Scale Labs for post-training evaluation in March 2026. Together AI expanded fine-tuning to 100B+ parameter models in September 2025. AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI offer managed fine-tuning natively. When I invested in Perplexity in 2022, the key was that AI-powered search was considered a dead category by most investors -- the structural capability shift was happening but consensus hadn't caught up. With Thirdbrain Labs, I see the opposite dynamic: the structural shift (open-source base models creating demand for post-training) is real, but every investor and incumbent in the ecosystem has already recognized it. There is no nonobvious window here. The "why now" is consensus, which means the timing advantage that early entrants in nonobvious markets enjoy -- the breathing room to build before well-capitalized players arrive -- has already closed.

The infrastructure-layer positioning deserves more nuance. Thirdbrain Labs frames itself as "end-to-end post-training infrastructure" that captures "expert decisions" as training signals, which at least aims at the right layer -- a platform other businesses integrate into production workflows, not a point solution. The comparison to Stripe is instructive but ultimately unfavorable: Stripe won because legacy payment processors couldn't build modern APIs, giving Stripe years of structural advantage. Thirdbrain Labs' competitors -- Scale AI at $29B valuation with ~$2B in 2025 revenue, Together AI at $3.3B, Labelbox crossing $100M ARR -- are modern, API-first, and actively expanding into exactly this category. And free tools like Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, and LLaMA-Factory lower the bar for any team with modest ML capability. The dossier states the differentiation between Thirdbrain Labs' "expert decision capture" and standard RLHF/DPO workflows "is not publicly documented in enough detail to assess defensibility." That's a significant gap -- if the core differentiation can't be articulated clearly enough for a dossier researcher to identify it, I worry it can't be articulated clearly enough for a buyer either.

Margaret Zhang's background raises a specific question I'd need answered before going further. She co-founded Coalition Systems, a defense AI startup backed by a16z Speedrun, building "secure AI coordination software for allied defense" -- she traveled to Kyiv to meet with Ukraine Ministry of Defence operators. That's a meaningful venture with real stakes. The dossier flags that the relationship between Coalition Systems and Thirdbrain Labs is unclear: pivot, parallel venture, or full departure. For a two-person team where execution speed is the only advantage against Scale AI and Together AI, I need confidence that this is the singular focus. David Huang brings solid ML credentials -- UC Berkeley Regents' Scholar, top 1% in Two Sigma's Halite AI challenge, Stanford NLP coursework -- but neither founder has documented experience scaling a company through hypergrowth or a prior exit. Zhang's GTM experience at Spice AI and VC fellowship at INVC give her network access, but network access isn't the same as operator experience at a company growing from 50 to 500 employees.

The strongest bull case for Thirdbrain Labs: the Predibase acquisition by Rubrik for $100-500M validates that fine-tuning infrastructure companies are acquirable at meaningful multiples even with modest scale. The Meta-Scale AI deal disrupting Scale's customer relationships (with OpenAI and Google reportedly cutting ties) creates genuine market turbulence that a nimble two-person team could exploit. And if "expert decision capture" is genuinely architecturally distinct from standard RLHF annotation workflows -- if it creates a flywheel where each customer's accumulated expert feedback makes their models progressively harder to replicate elsewhere -- that could build real switching costs over time. For this bull case to work, Thirdbrain Labs would need to demonstrate that their UX creates a fundamentally different workflow loop than what Scale Labs, Labelbox, or a competent ML engineer with Axolotl can achieve. I don't see that evidence yet.

The single-miracle test is unfavorable. Thirdbrain Labs needs the product differentiation to be real AND distribution to work against incumbents with orders-of-magnitude more capital and existing customer relationships. That's two miracles, not one. The technology miracle (foundation models improving) is happening exogenously, which is good, but it's happening for every competitor simultaneously. Scale AI's $29B war chest, Labelbox's 80%+ Fortune 500 penetration, and Together AI's 450K+ developer user base mean distribution alone requires a breakthrough. My framework for Thirdbrain Labs lands on Pass -- driven primarily by the absence of nonobvious market timing (this is the most obvious AI infrastructure category) and the competitive landscape requiring multiple simultaneous wins against well-capitalized incumbents.
