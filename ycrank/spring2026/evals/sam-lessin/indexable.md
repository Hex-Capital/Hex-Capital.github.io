# Indexable -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the problem with Indexable from my seat: six funded startups, three major cloud providers, and an open-source project called Zeroboot all competing in AI agent sandbox infrastructure. E2B has 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up. Daytona crossed a million in ARR within two months of relaunch. Cloudflare shipped Dynamic Workers in March. This is not a contrarian market. This is the most consensus infrastructure category in tech right now. When I hear "AI agent sandbox" in 2026, I hear what I heard when people said "social commerce platform" in 2011 — an idea so obviously good that every investor and every incumbent is already moving on it, which means the returns are priced in before the first check clears.

Andrew Gazelka is clearly on the "sane" side of the ledger — and that part is genuinely compelling. Building Hyperion, a Minecraft game engine in Rust handling 10,000+ concurrent players with a thousand GitHub stars, is a direct technical analog for managing massive concurrent stateful environments. That's not a side project; that's a proof of competency that maps almost perfectly onto the VM forking and state management challenges he's now tackling. His xAI tenure and prior exit add credibility. The decision to build a custom VMM from the kernel up rather than wrapping Firecracker like everyone else — that's a bold technical bet. Most engineers would say "Firecracker is good enough, ship faster." Gazelka is saying "good enough isn't good enough, I'll build the hypervisor." Within the category, his technical approach is contrarian even if the category itself is not. But my framework requires both halves of the equation. A credible systems engineer building the technically superior version of something every VC already wants to fund is not the same signal as Brian Armstrong proposing a regulated crypto exchange when the whole Valley thought Bitcoin was a joke.

The structural moat question is where this investment breaks down for me specifically. The moat here is software — custom VMM, custom kernel patches, content-addressable storage engine. Deep software, hard software, genuinely impressive software. But software. My whole thesis for the last two years has been that software moats are dissolving as AI makes development cheaper. Now, kernel-level hypervisor code is toward the far end of the AI-commoditization curve — this isn't a React dashboard anyone can vibe-code in an afternoon. But the trajectory is clear. Daytona already claims sub-90ms with off-the-shelf Docker isolation. The gap between 26ms and 90ms is real today but narrows as Firecracker improves, as AI-assisted systems programming matures, and as the competitors with 10-20x more capital invest in closing it. Contrast this with Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews showing up at your house. No amount of AI closes that gap. Indexable's moat is a speed advantage in a category where every player is optimizing for speed — that's a treadmill, not a fortress.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: if stateful sandbox forking becomes the foundational primitive for how all AI agents interact with the world — the way containers became the primitive for cloud deployment — then Indexable is a protocol-layer investment with the kind of compounding dynamics I look for. Fork-snapshot-as-a-primitive that every agent platform integrates with. If the 26ms full-environment fork including databases becomes the API standard, switching costs compound and Indexable becomes infrastructure, not a feature. That's the Solana analog — deep systems engineer building a new compute primitive that the ecosystem standardizes around. And the three-person team building from the kernel up is exactly the kind of operationally insane ambition that I've seen produce outsized returns when it works. Gazelka's Hyperion background suggests he might be one of the few people on earth who can actually pull this off technically.

But I keep coming back to the distribution problem. The custom VMM only wins if it becomes the standard, and standards in infrastructure are set by adoption, not by technical superiority. E2B is open-source with massive enterprise penetration. Modal has $50M ARR and is in talks at a $2.5 billion valuation. Cloudflare has every developer on earth already using their CDN. Gazelka is building the technically superior product with YC-level funding against competitors who have the distribution to set the standard regardless of technical merit. In the protocol-layer investments I've made that worked — Solana, Helium, Lightning Labs — the founders had a structural distribution advantage or were so early that no incumbents existed. Indexable is entering a market where the incumbents arrived first and raised tens of millions before Indexable's website even resolved. The site returned a 403 at research time. There's no public repo, no Product Hunt launch, no press, ~86 Twitter followers. The "early access DM" approach might work for a genuinely novel category — it doesn't work when your competitors have open-source SDKs and Fortune 100 logos.

This is ultimately a sane person with a sane idea executed at an unusually deep technical level. That's a profile that produces solid companies, sometimes good acquisitions, but rarely the kind of structural breakout my framework targets. The idea doesn't provoke confusion or skepticism — it provokes nodding. And when every investor in the room is nodding, I'm looking for the exit.
