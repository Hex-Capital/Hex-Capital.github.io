# Nori -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Nori is what happens when a shipping founder — Antonio Sitong Li, who took NORI L1 out the door before L2 — bets the whole architecture on a *multi-miracle* consumer promise: $1,288 hardware that also does dishes, laundry, coffee, and cooperative bed-making. The dimensional signature is a founder-execution-rhythm signal wrapped around a single-miracle failure — which is exactly the profile I've spent 15 years learning to walk away from, no matter how much I like the shipping cadence.

Antonio Sitong Li shows the one thing I actually screen for at pre-seed: he shipped NORI L1 (sub-$1,000, x.com/AntonioSitongLi/status/2045149378432688308), then iterated to NORI L2 at $1,288 with a Grande SKU at $1,322, and pushed a launch teaser that pulled 15.7K views on day one — that is a Perplexity-shaped rhythm, where **Aravind Srinivas** (2022) kept showing up a week later with the thing we'd discussed built. The load-bearing analogy is *iterative artifact production against a moving target*, and Nori diverges from Perplexity on one specific: Aravind was iterating *software* where the ship cost was zero and the feedback loop was hours; Antonio is iterating *hardware manufactured in San Francisco* where L1 → L2 is a real BOM redesign and any L3 iteration burns quarters, not weekends. The rhythm is real. The medium punishes it.

Fusion and novel battery chemistry are the failure mode I quote most often on Tim Ferriss #863 — "capitalization issues and science risk and market risk" compounded — and Nori's product page walks straight into a version of it. To deliver on the norirobotics.com promise, Antonio needs the $1,288 BOM to close, the leader-follower teleoperation-to-autonomy transfer to generalize to dishes and laundry in unstructured homes, the skill-sharing marketplace to hit critical mass before Unitree ships a wheeled variant, and a 4-person team in NYC to manufacture in SF on a summer 2026 shipping timeline announced in June 2026. That's four miracles stacked, each independent, and per the NFX single-miracle test I compound the odds and pass — the L1 → L2 shipping cadence doesn't buy down any of them.

Mechanism analysis on the catalyst: the Unitree R1 dropping to $5,900 in July 2025 and 1X NEO landing at $20K/$499 per month in October 2025 *is* a real re-opening in humanoids, and the VLA foundation-model wave cited in State of Robotics 2026 lowers training-data cost for household tasks — but the catalyst compounds *toward* the bipedal-humanoid players who have the capital to eat the BOM, not toward a $1,288 wheeled cart. Over 18 months this catalyst gets *worse* for Nori, not better: every incremental dollar Figure ($39B post-money Sep 2025) and 1X raise buys them the manufacturing volume to slide down to Nori's price point, and Unitree already prints at Shenzhen scale. Nori is running toward a market where the catalyst benefits its competitors' unit economics more than its own.

Counterfactual on revenue velocity: 726 X followers, one 15.7K-view teaser, YC's official promo tweet, and pre-orders open with zero unit count disclosed — the score flips a tier only if Nori discloses a named pre-order figure north of 1,000 units at $1,288 (~$1.3M booked) with a shipping ETA that holds. In the AI cycle I've said the quiet part loud: "if you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss #863) — and hardware is *harder*, not easier, to grow silently on. The absence of a unit count 30 days after YC's official promotion is the signal, not the noise.

Single-anchor parallel on distribution: **Stripe** (2010) was an index on e-commerce — every merchant who came online eventually touched the payment rails, so Stripe won as the *category* grew. Nori is the opposite structural position. If wheeled bimanual wins over bipedal, Unitree captures it with 5x the manufacturing muscle; if bipedal wins, Nori is stranded on the wrong form factor; if the humanoid market compounds at MarketsandMarkets' 28.1% CAGR to $50B by 2035, Nori doesn't sit in the workflow path — it sits in a *substitute* path competing on price against companies with 100x its capital. That's not an index. That's a #2-in-a-winner-take-all pattern, and I passed on **Lyft** Series C for the same shape.

Forecast, 36 months: Nori ships some units of L2 into a hobbyist/research audience (the arXiv "Nori Bot" community exists and is real), fails to deliver the demoed household-autonomy capability at $1,288, and either pivots to a teleoperation dev-kit SKU sold to robotics labs at ~$2,500 (a real business, sub-scale) or shuts down when Unitree releases a $1,500 wheeled bimanual by late 2028. Not zero — Antonio ships — but not the "one of that dozen or two really important companies 10 years from now" bar I hold angel checks to.

The strongest bull case is the **Color Genomics** (2015) precedent I have to take seriously: I violated my "stay out of hard tech" rule when the company was *workflow infrastructure on top of commodity hardware* rather than a chemistry bet, and Nori's skill-sharing marketplace could be exactly that — a data flywheel on top of commodity wheeled manipulators where every robot teaches every other robot, the Raspberry-Pi-for-robotics play that eats the global research budget. It stings because the arXiv "Nori Bot" paper (2605.16537) proves latent demand for sub-$1K mobile manipulators, and Antonio's Columbia Graphics/UI Lab work on VR-demonstrated task goals is exactly the teleop-to-autonomy substrate a marketplace would ride. What kills the bull case in the dossier: Nori is *marketed to consumers* at $1,288 with promised dishes and laundry, not to researchers at $2,500 with an open skill SDK — the positioning commits to the multi-miracle path, not the workflow-layer path. Flip condition: within 12 months, Nori discloses ≥1,000 shipped units *and* an independent third-party video of autonomous dishwashing at the demoed price, or repositions publicly as a developer platform with ≥500 paying research labs. Absent either, I pass.
