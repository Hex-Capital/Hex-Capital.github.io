# Squid -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking fact about Squid is that National Grid DSO -- one of the largest distribution network operators in Britain -- has already launched a public-facing product powered by a two-person pre-seed startup. That is genuinely unusual. Most pre-seed companies are begging for design partners; Squid is already embedded in critical infrastructure. But then you read the next line: both founders previously worked at National Grid and Octopus Energy, and their prior company VoltQuant served these exact same customers. The traction is real, but it is relationship-derived, not market-derived. The question is whether this represents an insider advantage that compounds into monopoly, or a warm introduction that flatters the early metrics and obscures the absence of a structural moat.

What is the secret here? The implicit thesis is that grid planning is trapped in spreadsheets and legacy desktop tools, and a browser-native, versioned, collaborative workspace can replace them. This is not a secret. This is a consensus observation shared by every energy software investor, every utility regulator pushing RIIO-ED2 modernization targets, and at least five funded competitors I can identify in the dossier. Envelio built a digital twin SaaS for grid planning and was acquired by E.ON. Camus Energy raised $25 million and grew ARR 500%. Plexigrid secured $11.6 million. IQGeo is publicly traded. GE Vernova bundles planning tools into enterprise suites worth billions. When five companies and an incumbent are all attacking the same problem with overlapping solutions, the landscape is a Girardian nightmare of mimetic convergence. Everyone can see that utilities need better planning software. The insight is shared, which means the margins will be shared, which means no one achieves monopoly returns. The "AI-powered" modifier does not rescue the thesis -- adding AI to enterprise workflows is the single most consensus investment thesis in technology today. You cannot build a contrarian position on the most popular idea in the market.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If Squid becomes the system of record for grid planning decisions -- the versioned model that every engineer references, every assumption is tracked against, every capacity decision is documented within -- then switching costs could become formidable. Utilities that adopt Squid's model as their single source of truth would find migration extraordinarily painful, the same way Palantir's intelligence integrations create deepening lock-in with every year of deployment. The Stripe comparison is instructive: Stripe won by making payments integration 10x simpler for developers, a constituency that had no voice in enterprise procurement. If Squid is making grid planning 10x more accessible for the planning engineers who actually do the work -- bypassing the CIO's procurement process with a browser-native tool that requires no IT installation -- that could be a genuine wedge. The founders' domain depth is unquestionable. They have lived inside the organizations they are selling to, built a prior company in the same space, and carried their customer relationships forward. That is not nothing.

But the Stripe analogy fractures on the dimension that matters most. Stripe's secret was genuinely contrarian -- that payments was a developer problem, not a finance problem, at a time when no one in payments thought about developers at all. Squid's thesis is not contrarian. Every grid operator already knows their planning tools are inadequate. The Ofgem regulatory framework is explicitly mandating modernization. The timing is driven by policy and subsidy, not by a technological discontinuity that Squid alone has perceived. When timing is driven by regulation, every competitor benefits equally from the tailwind. The technology itself -- browser-native delivery, version control for network models, embedded AI for stress testing -- is competent engineering, not a 10x discontinuity. These are architectural choices that any well-funded team could replicate in eighteen months. Envelio, backed by E.ON's balance sheet and distribution network across European utilities, is the most dangerous competitive analogue: an overlapping product already embedded in major utilities, with an incumbent parent that can cross-sell and bundle in ways a two-person startup cannot counter.

The VoltQuant backstory also warrants scrutiny. The founders built a previous company in this exact domain, serving these exact customers, raised roughly a million pounds across angels, SAFEs, and government grants -- and then started over. That trajectory could indicate deeper insight refined through failure, or it could indicate that the first product never achieved escape velocity and the founders are cycling through adjacent positioning in the same market without finding a genuine product-market discontinuity. The absence of any explanation for VoltQuant's conclusion makes this ambiguous, and ambiguity at the foundation level is not where I want to deploy capital. The CEO's BCG background adds a mild negative signal -- I have consistently found that the most important companies are built by engineers and domain obsessives, not by consultants who rotate through industries. The CTO's credentials are stronger -- Cambridge engineering, Head of Technology at Octopus, genuine AI/ML experience at AWS -- but one strong technical co-founder does not resolve the strategic question of whether the company has identified something that others have missed.

I would need to see evidence of a genuine secret -- a thesis about grid planning that most utility executives and energy software investors would actively argue against -- before committing capital. What I see instead is a well-executed product in a crowded category driven by consensus demand and regulatory tailwinds. The founders have meaningful advantages in domain knowledge and customer relationships, and the UK DNO beachhead is sensibly chosen. But domain expertise and warm introductions are not monopoly-creating mechanisms. They are advantages that erode as the market matures and larger players consolidate. This is a company that may build a good business. It is not a company built on a secret.
