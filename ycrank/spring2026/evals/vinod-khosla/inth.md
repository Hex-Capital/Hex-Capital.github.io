# Inth -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Guillermo Rauch calling Inth "@shadcn for Consent Management" is precisely the endorsement that would excite most seed investors — and precisely the signal that tells me this isn't my bet. Rauch's comparison reveals the ceiling: shadcn is an excellent component library that makes developers' lives easier. It is not a company that changes how billions of people live. Inth has built what appears to be genuinely superior engineering — 0.6KB bundle and 30ms LCP versus 50-100KB and 445-709ms for OneTrust and Usercentrics — and that execution quality is real. But when I ask my first question — "if Inth fully succeeds, does the world visibly change?" — the answer is that cookie consent banners load faster and developers spend fewer hours on compliance plumbing. That is a business. It is not a consequence I would write an apology letter to my LPs for pursuing.

Christopher Burns has built an impressive open-source distribution engine: 1.8M npm downloads, 388.3K monthly, 1,678 GitHub stars, customers including Vercel, Expo, and Zed. For a pre-seed company with three people, that adoption curve is legitimately strong. Burns is a second-time founder — Everfund raised $2.15M, processed payments across 33+ countries — and his decade of TypeScript experience plus RedwoodJS contributions demonstrate framework-level credibility. The 411 releases from v1.0 (May 2025) to v2.0 (April 2026) show iteration velocity. These are competent-founder signals. What I don't see is evidence of the learning rate I look for: what surprised Burns during YC, what assumption Inth abandoned, what the team knows now that they didn't six months ago. The resume is solid; the trajectory of insight is invisible.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Inth could follow the Stripe playbook — start with a narrow developer-first integration (payments for Stripe, consent for Inth), build trust through open-source adoption, then expand into the broader privacy infrastructure stack (DSARs, data mapping, automated compliance). The regulatory tailwind is genuinely expanding: GDPR, CCPA, DMA, India's DPDP Act, Canada's CPPA. Every new jurisdiction is a forcing function for adoption. Inth's framework-native SDKs for React, Next.js, and Svelte create real switching costs once embedded, and the BigCommerce Catalyst integration shows platform-level distribution emerging. The TAM projections range from $732M to $11.59B by 2034. If Inth becomes the default consent layer for every modern web application, that's a meaningful business with compounding network effects. I checked my Known Exceptions — DoorDash violated my technical novelty requirement because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access for hundreds of millions justified the override. Does Inth's consequence magnitude justify a similar override? No. DoorDash changed what people eat and how restaurants survive. Inth changes how fast a consent banner renders. These are categorically different magnitudes of consequence.

Inth's technical approach — headless architecture, server-side rendering with geolocation-based jurisdiction detection, zero external script overhead — is well-engineered but not non-linear. The 5-8x performance improvement (89ms versus 445-709ms) is achieved through architectural decisions any strong engineering team could make: eliminate the external script, render server-side, ship less JavaScript. This is the difference between building a new rocket engine and putting existing engines on a lighter airframe. Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers was a step-function technology change. Inth's headless consent architecture is smart engineering within known paradigms. The self-created CookieBench benchmark reinforces this concern — when you have to build your own measurement tool to demonstrate your advantage, the advantage may be real but it's living within an established performance optimization framework, not breaking out of one.

The $732M-$1.82B CMP market is, by my standards, a redistribution fight. Inth is competing for existing compliance budgets against OneTrust ($500M revenue, $1.13B raised), Usercentrics ($117M revenue), and a long tail of incumbents. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence wasn't capturing share of the existing meat market — it was creating an entirely new category of protein production. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, it wasn't about taking share from solar and wind — it was about energy too cheap to meter. Inth's success means developers use a better tool for a regulatory requirement they already satisfy with worse tools. The existing spend gets redistributed; the world doesn't get a new capability. The path to radical affordability exists in a narrow sense — open-source Apache 2.0 versus OneTrust's $10K+ ACV is a real cost collapse — but making consent management cheaper is not making healthcare free or energy limitless. It's making a business tool affordable.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Inth demonstrated that its consent infrastructure unlocks a genuinely new capability — say, enabling real-time, granular data-use negotiations between users and applications that create new categories of privacy-respecting services impossible under current CMP architectures. That would be a consequence worth betting on. As it stands, Inth is a well-executed developer tool in a compliance category, built by a capable second-time founder with strong ecosystem relationships. The execution is admirable. The ambition is insufficient. I don't mind failing, but if I succeed it needs to be worth succeeding for — and a faster cookie banner, no matter how elegantly engineered, doesn't clear that bar.
