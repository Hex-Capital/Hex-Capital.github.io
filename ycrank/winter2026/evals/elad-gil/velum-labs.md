# Velum Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the product direction instability. Velum has presented three distinct product framings before shipping to a single customer: a homomorphic-encryption firewall for content-level access control, an "operating system for data quality," and an "ontology engine for enterprise AI." Their GitHub organization is still populated with forks of Microsoft SEAL and GPU-accelerated homomorphic encryption libraries from the prior direction. The flagship "Firewall" repo has one star. When I evaluate founders, I ask whether they can name their single critical obstacle in one sentence and show their entire company is organized around solving it. Three product framings in rapid succession at pre-launch isn't iteration -- it's a team that hasn't found their problem yet. Compare this to how Harvey approached legal AI: the founders identified legal workflow as their target before GPT-4 existed and built relentlessly against that single thesis. Velum is rotating framings, which means they're also rotating miracles. That pattern makes conviction difficult.

The market itself is my larger concern. Data observability is not a non-obvious market -- it's a well-understood, heavily funded category. Monte Carlo has raised $236 million at a $1.6 billion valuation. Atlan has raised $206 million. Bigeye, Great Expectations, and Soda have collectively raised another $165 million. When I see five well-funded competitors and $500+ million in category funding, this isn't "too boring" or "too niche" -- this is consensus. The data quality market growing at 11-15% CAGR is steady but doesn't represent a structural inflection. It's been growing at roughly this rate for years. Nothing here resembles the gap I look for between how a market appears today and where its adoption curve is heading. The ontology sub-angle is somewhat differentiated architecturally, but "LLM-powered data quality" is exactly the kind of AI-augmented enterprise feature that every incumbent in the category is pursuing. Bigeye just launched a "bigAI" suite. Monte Carlo is integrating AI into anomaly resolution. The "why now" of foundation model capabilities applies to every company in this space, not uniquely to Velum.

The incumbent platform risk compounds this. Snowflake's Horizon governance suite and Databricks' Unity Catalog already include data quality monitoring. If ontology-based contract derivation proves valuable -- and that's still unvalidated -- these platforms have the ultimate distribution advantage: they ARE the data layer. A two-person startup selling a standalone ontology tool into hospitals, banks, and government agencies is fighting against companies that can bundle equivalent functionality as a native feature. When I think about my product-to-distribution framework, the platform companies already own the distribution channel that Velum would need to build. Stripe succeeded partly because it created a developer relationship that didn't exist -- there was no incumbent distribution channel for payment API integration. Velum is trying to insert a new layer into an architecture where the incumbent platforms already have the customer relationship and the data access.

The strongest bull case would require believing that automatic ontology derivation from live data traffic is architecturally distinct enough that incumbents cannot retrofit it -- that this is a paradigm shift from anomaly detection and rule-based validation, not an incremental improvement. If that's true, and if LLM capabilities for NER and relationship extraction continue improving (which they will), then Velum could be building ahead of a capability curve where the product gets meaningfully better every year as foundation models improve. Alen Rubilar-Muñoz's technical profile supports this possibility: CalHacks overall winner, principal ML engineer at a data platform company, research in topological neural networks, and a pinned "ontology" GitHub repo that directly addresses the core technical challenge. The SOC 2 Type II certification and targeting of regulated verticals (hospitals, banks, government) could create the kind of compliance moat I've seen work in other investments. But for this bull case to hold, the team would need to stop pivoting and demonstrate that their ontology approach actually works at enterprise scale -- and they'd need to do it before Databricks or Snowflake ships a native version. No accuracy benchmarks, case studies, or even design partner results exist to validate the core technical claim.

I pass. The market is consensus, not non-obvious. The product direction instability suggests the founders haven't found their single miracle. The distribution challenge is structural -- standalone data quality tools face platform bundling risk from companies that own the data layer. The founders show technical talent but no evidence of execution velocity toward a specific product thesis. The "ontology engine" framing might be directionally interesting, but I'd need to see the team commit to one product vision, ship it, and demonstrate that automatic ontology derivation produces data contracts that enterprises actually trust -- and I'd need to see a clear distribution strategy that doesn't depend on outrunning Snowflake and Databricks.
