# Modern -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

ServiceNow paid $2.85B for Moveworks, then inherited a structural paradox: its entire business model charges per ticket, per agent, per seat — and Moveworks exists to eliminate tickets. Automation Anywhere acquired Aisera for ~$1.5B in November 2025. Both acquisitions cleared the field of independent AI-ITSM players, and Modern is walking into that vacuum with a two-person team. The timing argument is legitimate. But timing is capital-available leverage — it's permissioned, not permissionless. The question I always ask first is whether the founders carry knowledge so specific that if they vanished, the company's core insight would evaporate with them.

Seb Poole has two exits — a refurbished-device trading platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in February 2023 after raising $1.5M from E15VC. He raised 20M AUD from Alibaba-linked investors for his Hong Kong e-commerce venture before that. This is genuine entrepreneurial execution. But the knowledge stack is in e-commerce logistics and B2B mental health compliance — not in IT service desk operations, not in workflow orchestration, not in the guts of ServiceNow or Zendesk. Aleks Tomovski brings DevOps and SRE across multiple geographies, which is adjacent to Modern's problem space but not the same as having spent years inside ITSM automation watching where tickets die and why. Modern's founders identified this market through opportunity analysis — the post-acquisition vacuum is visible to anyone paying attention — not through years of obsessive engagement that produced insight no market researcher could replicate. Compare this to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, who had spent years at Google Brain building the exact retrieval systems his company would productize. Modern's founders are competent operators entering a domain, not domain obsessives who couldn't stop building.

Modern's leverage architecture is split. The SaaS platform — AI agents resolving tickets with plain-language workflow definitions, 12+ enterprise integrations, zero marginal cost per resolution — is genuine code leverage. The website claims 62% reduction in ticket handling and 74% faster response times, which signal real automation value. But Modern also offers "Modern Managed," where the company operates the customer's entire IT service desk. That's labor leverage by definition — it scales linearly with customer count and requires headcount. A two-person team cannot manage multiple enterprise service desks without hiring. "Managing other people is incredibly messy." Modern is simultaneously building a leveraged product and an unleveraged services business, and at this stage I can't tell which one the founders are more committed to. Notion never offered to manage your workspace for you — the product was the leverage, full stop.

Modern's principal authenticity signal is the dimension that most concerns me. Seb Poole's trajectory — e-commerce in Hong Kong, refurbished devices, mental health SaaS, now ITSM automation — is the pattern of a serial entrepreneur who finds fundable markets, not someone whose identity is fused with a specific problem. Each company was in a completely different domain. That's execution skill, not principal commitment. The strongest bull case: Poole's Frankie Health required healthcare-grade compliance (similar to Modern's SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, GDPR posture), and building B2B products that pass enterprise security review is itself a form of specific knowledge. That's fair. But the specific knowledge of navigating compliance processes is necessary to win enterprise deals — it's not the core insight that makes Modern's product different from Ravenna's $15M-funded alternative or Workativ's similar feature set. If you asked me whether Modern would exist if the Moveworks and Aisera acquisitions hadn't happened, I don't think the answer is yes.

Modern's team-to-value ratio is genuinely impressive at the surface level — two people shipping a product with enterprise security certifications and integrations across ServiceNow, Zendesk, Workday, BambooHR, SAP, Salesforce, Slack, and more. That's the kind of output-per-person number that makes me pay attention. But the managed-services offering undercuts this signal. Modern is not architecting for the future I believe in — billion-dollar companies with five people. It's hedging between AI leverage and human services, which is a common pre-seed pattern but not the one that produces outlier returns. Ravenna raised $15M from Khosla and Madrona with a cleaner positioning (Slack-native, mid-market, pure software). Modern's broader scope across IT, HR, and Finance could be an advantage or could be unfocused execution across too many verticals for a two-person team.

Modern democratizes access to enterprise-grade IT automation for mid-market companies that couldn't afford Moveworks or Aisera's pricing, and the "live in under a day" deployment versus ServiceNow's months-long implementations does expand access meaningfully. The structural opening created by both independents being acquired is real — ServiceNow's agents are now constrained by their parent company's ticket-based revenue model, unable to fully cannibalize their own pricing. That principal-agent conflict at the incumbent level is exactly the kind of structural advantage I fund. But Modern needs founders whose specific knowledge can exploit that opening faster than Ravenna, Workativ, and the inevitable next wave of YC companies entering the same vacuum. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Modern has already accumulated proprietary workflow resolution data from paying customers — actual enterprise deployments generating compound learning that Ravenna's $15M can't buy. The website shows customer outcome metrics but no confirmed revenue or user counts. Without that compounding data flywheel in motion, Modern is a competent team with good timing in a market that will attract dozens of competitors within twelve months.
