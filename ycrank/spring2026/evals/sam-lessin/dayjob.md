# Dayjob -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Dayjob is George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks doing three years of depot ride-alongs at Gaea since 2022 and converting that schlep into $359K of ARR in seven months — high on operator-earned conviction, almost zero on anything Slow's framework treats as leverage outside the four walls of the company. That high/low pairing tells me what Dayjob actually is: a competent vertical-SaaS execution team that earned the right to attack UK skip-hire fleets, wearing an "AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" jacket that puts the round on exactly the YC consensus rail I de-rate.

On founder conviction the high rung is Ben Silbermann at **Pinterest** — missionary temperament for a long arc that other funds couldn't read until the markup, sourced through operator-network rather than process. The shared mechanism is "spent years inside the problem before the company existed," and Dayjob's George Postlethwaite cleared a version of that bar: five years at Deliveroo launching UK grocery, then co-building the Gaea ERP for e-waste recyclers since 2022, then a January 2025 pivot when the schedulers-not-the-recyclers turned out to be the real customer. Where Dayjob diverges from the Pinterest rung is the artifact layer — Silbermann's obsession was legible in product surface and public posture; Postlethwaite's @georgepos6 has an unretrievable follower count and I cannot find a single opinionated essay on waste-fleet scheduling from either co-founder. Three years of depot work earns me a meeting; the absence of any pre-pitch public take on why AMCS's native routing is wrong is what keeps Dayjob below the rung.

On founder-as-brand the low rung is the canonical YC team that depends entirely on paid acquisition and hired sales — and Dayjob sits squarely on it. The failure mechanism at that rung is that every dollar of ARR has to be re-bought at the next vertical, every customer is a cold pipeline conversation, and the Series A buyer can't underwrite a community asset because there isn't one. Dayjob's distribution at $496K ARR is Allstone, Coastal Recycling, and one unnamed UK fleet, sourced founder-led through prior Gaea relationships — that's a Rolodex, not a channel. There is no newsletter, no Discord, no opinionated industry podcast, no entrepreneurial-creator profile that the Slow Creator Fund framework would even attempt to score. The pattern is identically the one I flag in the Pass Example.

On round pricing — mechanism over 18 months — Dayjob's positioning as "AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" is the exact 2026 YC template I treat as deselection-by-default, and Tom Blomfield's batch tends to price vertical-AI agent companies at $25–40M post regardless of UK-fleet ARR concentration. The Gaea-to-Dayjob pivot story plus $496K ARR will get bid above the batch median by funds running the "vertical AI is the new SaaS" playbook, and I cannot generate alpha from that SAFE cap. The mitigating factor is real but small: waste/skip-hire is genuinely unsexy enough that a few tier-1 generalists will skim past, which is what pulls Dayjob a notch off the floor.

On capital structure — counterfactual — Dayjob would need to be roughly $1.2M+ ARR with a path to default-alive inside 24 months on the next $2M, or alternatively structured as a UK-fleet rollup with revenue-share economics in the AMCS-substitute layer, for the score to flip a tier. Neither is in the dossier. What is in the dossier is a five-person London team, $496K ARR concentrated in three customers, a US expansion underway against Routeware's 1,000-hauler footprint, and an overlay product that AMCS ($225M+ raised, $273M revenue) can ship as a native module the quarter after Dayjob looks threatening — that is a four-to-five-round venture trajectory on a generic SaaS cap table, not the Teamshares-shaped instrument I get excited about.

On category sleeve — single-anchor parallel to **Ro** — both Dayjob and Ro attack an unsexy operational vertical that software-trained VCs dismiss, and both rely on workflow-depth-as-moat rather than algorithmic novelty. The disanalogy is the venture-moment test: Ro needed venture capital to clear 50-state prescription-rail licensing, and that regulatory schlep was itself the moat. Dayjob's underlying optimisation is well-trodden territory (OptimoRoute, Route4Me, Samsara, RouteSmart all ship it), depot integration into UK ERPs is not a Ro-grade regulatory schlep, and the "industrial logistics" framing in the dossier is contradicted by every cited customer being waste/skip-hire — meaning Dayjob is in a sleeve I don't have, attacking a market where the venture-moment thesis doesn't carry.

Forecast at 36 months: Dayjob reaches $3–6M ARR across UK and early US waste fleets, gets acquired by AMCS or Routeware for $20–40M as a routing-agent bolt-on inside their existing ERP suites, with Slow-sized seed economics that don't return a fund — the mechanism is the highest-dimension (founder execution) producing real revenue, while the lowest-dimension (no owned channel, no community asset) caps the terminal value at whatever the natural strategic buyer will pay.

The strongest bull case against that forecast: Postlethwaite's Deliveroo-grocery-launch background plus three years of Gaea depot exposure is exactly the "missionary temperament for non-linear time" filter, $359K in seven months is genuine velocity, and waste/skip-hire is precisely the kind of "boring, illegible, dismissed-by-SaaS-VCs" sleeve I claim to want. That bull case stings because it's mostly true on the founder dimension. What dismantles it is the surface area of the overlay product — an agent that sits on top of AMCS or Routeware ERPs is the most-copyable feature shape in enterprise software, and the moment Dayjob's depot relationships look threatening at scale, AMCS ships a native AI-routing module bundled into existing licenses and Dayjob's switching-cost story collapses. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Dayjob signs three or more US enterprise fleets at >$200K ACV inside 12 months without AMCS or Routeware shipping a generally-available native AI-routing release in the same window — that would mean the workflow-integration depth is real moat rather than feature surface, and at that point I'd revisit at whatever the next round prices.
