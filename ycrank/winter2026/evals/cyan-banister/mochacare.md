# MochaCare -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that caught my attention first isn't the product or the market -- it's the 3:30am handoff. Nick goes to bed at 3:30am, Pranav wakes up at 3:30am, and together they cover 24/7 operations for their clients. That's not hustle theater. In a managed services business for home care agencies, the coverage IS the product. When a caregiver calls in sick at 2am and a 78-year-old is expecting someone at 7am, there's no "we'll get back to you during business hours." I've seen a lot of founders talk about doing whatever it takes, but structuring your sleep schedule around your customers' emergencies -- that tells me something real about commitment.

When I ask founders to tell me their story, I'm listening for the thread that ties them to this specific problem in a way that can't be manufactured. With Pranav, I find it: eight years of companion caregiving for neurodiverse people. That's not a weekend volunteer gig you put on a pitch deck. That's a decade of your life spent inside the care system, understanding what it feels like when coordination breaks down, when shifts get missed, when vulnerable people are left waiting. He was raised by grandparents, and so was Nick. They both know what it means when the people who raised you need care and the system fails them. Nick's connection comes through his grandmother's first-day caregiver no-show -- a real moment, though a single incident rather than a sustained immersion. The biographical signal is genuine but asymmetric between the two founders. Pranav's is the deeper well. Both have strong technical credentials -- Stanford AI/HCI, Spotify, Amazon, GoDaddy AI agents -- and those are relevant for building the tooling. But I'm always more moved by the biographical thread than the resume, and here the thread is real without being extraordinary.

Where I get stuck is the weirdness factor -- or rather, the absence of it. Home care operations software is a known category with well-funded incumbents. AlayaCare has raised over $274 million. WellSky was backed by Battery Ventures. AxisCare just took growth equity from Frontier. The managed services wrinkle -- bundling human operators with AI tools -- is a genuine differentiation, but it's not the kind of thing that makes other investors laugh at you or call you crazy. Nobody's going to say "that's the dumbest idea I've ever heard" about outsourced home care scheduling. Compare this to Crusoe Energy, where the pitch was literally "we want to mine Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota." That sounded insane. MochaCare sounds sensible. And in my experience, the most promising companies are the ones that sound wrong before they sound right. This one sounds reasonable from the start, which means the pricing probably reflects that -- it's a competitive space where the value of the insight has already been partially arbitraged.

That said, let me sit with the bull case, because there's a version of this that genuinely excites me. If MochaCare is a Trojan horse -- using managed services to embed deeply in agency operations, accumulating proprietary data about caregiver matching, scheduling patterns, and referral flows, and then gradually automating the human work as AI capabilities improve -- that's a real flywheel. The switching costs of a managed service are structurally higher than pure SaaS. You don't just cancel a subscription; you lose your operations team. And Careswitch getting acquired by Paradigm in September 2025 removed the closest AI-first competitor from the independent market, opening a lane. The timing with LLM capabilities reaching commercial viability for scheduling and interview automation is legitimate. If these founders can execute the transition from labor-intensive managed services to AI-heavy operations while maintaining the stickiness, the margin profile could flip dramatically. And the economic access angle is real -- caregivers are often low-wage workers who need fast onboarding and reliable shifts to earn income. Making the invisible machinery of home care coordination work better serves both the vulnerable people receiving care and the economically underserved people providing it. That resonates with what I care about.

But I keep coming back to the margin question and the moat question. A human-in-the-loop managed service business has a fundamentally different cost structure than SaaS. BPO companies with healthcare experience could replicate this model. The AI components -- scheduling automation, applicant tracking, interview tools -- are individually available from multiple vendors. The integrations with WellSky, AxisCare, and AlayaCare are useful but also create dependency on platforms that could restrict access or build competing services at any time. The defensibility signals are nascent, which is expected at pre-seed, but I need to see something -- a proprietary data advantage, a regulatory moat, a network effect -- that would make this hard to replicate in two years. Right now, I don't see it clearly enough to feel that jolt of conviction.

This is a good team building a reasonable business in a growing market with real personal connection to the problem. I respect the grind and Pranav's deep caregiving experience. But for my money, I'm looking for the bets that feel uncomfortable -- the ones where I'm the only one in the room who thinks it could work. MochaCare doesn't give me that signal. It gives me a signal of competence and compassion, which matters, but isn't enough to pull me off the sideline at this stage.
