# Meridian -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what Meridian is. "The AI operating system for consulting firms." I regularly see this exact pitch structure at YC Demo Day — AI for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for lawyers — and my response is always the same: if this company succeeds completely, what happens? Consulting firms find old PowerPoint decks faster. Their junior analysts spend 30% less time searching SharePoint. Managing partners retain institutional knowledge when associates leave. That is a useful product. It is not a consequence worth caring about. The $23.2B knowledge management TAM cited in the dossier is a number on a slide, not an argument for why the world changes. Meridian's full success scenario is a moderately profitable vertical SaaS company, and in my framework, that is a bet designed to return 3-5x — a waste of my time and attention.

The claimed ~$700K in contracted revenue four months in deserves examination because it is the strongest signal in the dossier. If verified, that is genuinely impressive pre-seed traction and suggests Meridian has found real pain in consulting workflows. But three problems. First, this is entirely self-reported on the a16z Speedrun portal with zero independent verification — no named customers, no press coverage, no third-party validation. Second, revenue velocity does not upgrade consequence magnitude. DoorDash had explosive early traction and I backed it, but the consequence of DoorDash succeeding was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people. The consequence of Meridian succeeding at scale is that McKinsey's proposal generation pipeline runs more smoothly. Third, the competitive moat underlying this traction is thin. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The core technology stack (RAG over documents, LLM-based generation, template-based output) is reproducible with off-the-shelf components." Glean has $200M in ARR and $610M in funding. Adding consulting-specific templates to Glean is a product sprint, not a strategic impossibility.

Kashyap Nathan's resume has interesting lines — Palantir, OpenAI, DARPA — but the LinkedIn data suggests these were internships or early-career rotations, not deployment leadership roles. Chris Farrington's headline still reads "Incoming SWE Intern — Deloitte." Shanay Champaneri is a current Cornell undergraduate. I have no problem with young founders — I backed a 19-year-old at Mach Industries building weapons systems to compete with Lockheed Martin. But what made that bet was the improbability of the ambition. A teenager building cruise missiles is audacious in a way that demands attention. Three students building a knowledge management tool for consulting firms is competent but conventional. There is no evidence in this dossier of rapid learning, surprising pivots, or first-principles insights that would distinguish these founders from dozens of other smart young engineers applying LLMs to enterprise workflows. The YC departure is a data point I would want to understand — it could signal conviction that they had outgrown the batch, or it could signal dysfunction — but without explanation it is noise, not signal.

The differentiation claim rests on accumulated behavioral data: "behavioral signal on how consulting work actually gets done. You can't download it. You have to earn it one engagement at a time." I have heard this argument before from every vertical SaaS company that plugs into a customer's workflows. The theory is sound — switching costs compound over time. But this is a moat built from operational stickiness, not from a technology discontinuity. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where the moat was synthetic heme protein — a genuine science breakthrough in food chemistry that took years of R&D to replicate. Meridian's behavioral data layer is a feature that any well-funded competitor can build by deploying their own product at consulting firms. Kenley, a YC S24 company, is building essentially the same thing. Glean could add it in a quarter. The "data moat" framing is a consulting pitch for defensibility, not actual defensibility.

The strongest bull case for Meridian is this: consulting is a $300B+ industry with genuinely broken knowledge management, the founders have relevant enterprise deployment experience from Palantir, and $700K in four months suggests they have found a nerve. If they can accumulate enough behavioral data before Glean or Microsoft Copilot moves downstream, the switching costs could create a durable vertical position. For this to work, three things must be true simultaneously: the traction is real and accelerating, Glean chooses not to build consulting-specific features, and the behavioral data layer creates genuine lock-in rather than just organizational inertia. That is a plausible path to a $500M company. It is not a plausible path to changing how millions of people live.

Meridian fails my primary filter. The consequence of full success is efficiency gains for a professional services vertical — not radical affordability of a basic human need, not a new category of demand, not a world that looks visibly different. The technology is a competent application of existing LLM capabilities, not a step-function breakthrough. The ambition is to build a better tool, not to replace the need for the tool. I pass.
