# Mochatrade -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Utkarsh Sinha built Martian Wallet to 2M+ installs and got it acquired by Lightspeed-backed Pontem in May 2025. That's a real data point — the guy knows how to ship self-custodial crypto products and get them into the hands of emerging-market users. But here's what I keep coming back to: Mochatrade isn't building a protocol. It's building a consumer app on top of infrastructure that Hyperliquid already made commoditized through HIP-3. Trade[XYZ] has $100B+ cumulative volume deploying stock perps on that same infrastructure since October 2025. I generally don't invest in dapps — I invest in protocols. Protocols compound. Applications compete on distribution and die when the next distribution hack comes along. Mochatrade is a dapp, full stop.

Ostium raised $24M from General Catalyst and Jump Crypto and has processed $25B in cumulative volume on broad RWA perps. dYdX raised $87M. Hyperliquid is printing $55M a month in platform revenue. The on-chain perps category is not contrarian — it's well-capitalized and accelerating. Mochatrade's angle is "India first" with local-currency on-ramps, and most US VCs would avoid that because SEBI is hostile, India slaps a 30% tax on crypto gains plus 1% TDS, and the RBI doesn't love any of this. That regulatory discomfort does create some mispricing. But here's the thing I've learned the hard way with Eaze: there's a difference between "the market is wrong about this category" and "the market is correctly pricing real regulatory risk." When I said the cities weren't going to let the scooter companies be profitable, that wasn't pessimism — it was structural analysis. Mochatrade's entire business model requires SEBI, RBI, the SEC, and the CFTC to all simultaneously not kill it. That's not mispricing I want to bet on. That's a landmine that the price correctly reflects.

Mochatrade is offering perpetual futures on 50+ US stocks with 20x leverage to Indian retail traders on crypto rails. Perps already exist. Stock perps already exist — Ostium, Kwenta, Trade[XYZ] all offer them. The "primitive test" here fails clearly. Mochatrade is bringing an existing financial instrument to a new geography. That's Robinhood's playbook, not Venmo's. Venmo created a verb — a behavior that didn't exist before. Mochatrade is offering leverage to a market that already has massive F&O participation (62% of NSE volume is retail, per CFA Institute 2025 data). These traders already know what derivatives are. Mochatrade is a better on-ramp, not a new category. Better on-ramps get competed away by whoever has more capital for local partnerships.

Sinha, Maheshwari, and Manda are a credible team — IIT Kharagpur and Columbia for the technical co-founders, IIT Guwahati and Sprinklr product leadership for the CPO. Sinha and Maheshwari built Martian Wallet together at Columbia, which means the core technical relationship is battle-tested across a build, scale, and acquisition. Manda's connection to them is less clear from the dossier, but Director of Product at a publicly traded SaaS company is a legitimate credential. The sanity part checks out — these are calm, credentialed builders with a prior exit. But the idea isn't insane. Offering stock perps to Indian traders via crypto rails is aggressive and regulatory-risky, sure, but it's not the kind of thing where I hear it and think "that's fucking crazy but what if they're right?" It's more like regulatory arbitrage dressed up as product innovation. The ratio is off — sane people with a clever but fundamentally incremental idea.

SEBI reported 91% of Indian retail F&O traders lose money. Mochatrade wants to offer those same traders 20x leverage on US stocks via self-custodial crypto. The bull case an advocate would make: India has 200M+ demat accounts, the CFTC is actively exploring bringing perps onshore (CoinDesk, March 2026), on-chain RWA perps surged 162% month-over-month in January 2026, and Sinha is one of the few founders who has actually scaled a self-custodial crypto product to millions of users in exactly this demographic. If India-specific distribution and on-ramp integration are the real moat — not the technology — then Mochatrade's team has a defensible head start that Ostium and Hyperliquid won't bother replicating until it's too late. That's a real argument. I don't buy it, because distribution moats in crypto are paper-thin — we've watched them evaporate overnight with vampire attacks and incentive programs — and because the regulatory overhang means Mochatrade could build the perfect India on-ramp and then watch SEBI shut it down. One verifiable condition that would flip me: if Mochatrade secured an explicit regulatory safe harbor in GIFT City or IFSCA jurisdiction for offering stock perps to Indian residents, that would transform this from a regulatory-dependent bet into a genuinely licensed operation with a structural moat. Without that, the risk isn't mispriced — it's correctly priced.

Mochatrade fails my framework on the two dimensions I weight most heavily. On consensus mispricing, the broader perps category is well-funded and accelerating, and the India-specific regulatory risk is real, not just narrative discomfort — so the market isn't wrong to be cautious. On primitive creation, stock perps for a new geography is distribution, not category creation. The founder credentials are the strongest signal here — a prior crypto exit with 2M+ users is genuinely rare at pre-seed — but strong founders on an incremental thesis in a regulatory minefield don't clear my bar. I'd watch Sinha across this venture the way I watched Raj across three companies before backing Solana. If Mochatrade finds a regulatory path that gives it structural permission to operate, and if the team pivots from application layer toward something more infrastructural, there's a future check to write. Not this one.
