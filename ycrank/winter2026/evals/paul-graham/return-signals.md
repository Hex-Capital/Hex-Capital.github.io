# Return Signals -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I look for is how the founders found the problem. Not whether the problem is real — e-commerce returns absolutely destroy margins, everyone in retail knows this — but whether these specific founders stumbled into it through their own experience or went looking for it. Ilya spent his career building conversational AI in healthcare: SmartTriage at Kaiser Permanente, medical chatbots at Curai Health, a symptom checker at MDandMe. Alejandro did business development at proptech companies. Neither has any visible connection to e-commerce, retail, or apparel. What I see is a pattern I've written about: a talented technical person with a specific capability (conversational AI) who went looking for a commercially viable application. The capability is real. But the origin is backwards. The best founders don't start with "I know how to build X, what should I apply it to?" They start with "this thing is broken and it's driving me crazy." When the tool precedes the problem, you get something competent but fragile — because the founder doesn't have the deep domain intuition that carries you through the years of ugly work between launch and product-market fit.

The structural gap Return Signals occupies is genuinely interesting. Attentive and Klaviyo send marketing messages. Loop Returns manages the logistics after a customer files a return. Nobody is proactively reaching out via conversational SMS between delivery and return initiation. That's a real gap, and the business model conflict is legitimate — Attentive makes money when brands send more marketing messages, which is orthogonal to preventing returns. Loop makes money managing returns, so preventing them cannibalizes their core business. This is the kind of observation that makes smart investors lean in. But I've seen this movie before: a startup identifies a gap between two large incumbents and positions itself in the space between them. The problem is that gaps between products are features, not companies. Attentive doesn't have a structural reason to avoid post-delivery outreach — they could add it as a premium tier tomorrow. The business model conflict argument assumes incumbents can't evolve their pricing models, and they can.

The bull case rests on Ilya's technical execution. He built one of the largest patient-facing ML applications at Kaiser Permanente. Healthcare conversational AI is harder than e-commerce conversational AI — the stakes are higher, the regulatory requirements stricter, the domain knowledge deeper. If he made it work there, the e-commerce application should be technically achievable. The claimed metrics are compelling if verified: 70% of return intents converted to exchanges, 61-80% response rates on SMS outreach, sub-0.1% opt-out. Those numbers would represent extraordinary ROI for brands. The problem is that no customer names are disclosed, no revenue is shared, and the metrics come entirely from the company's own website and YC page. At pre-seed I don't expect a customer list, but I do want at least one brand willing to put their name on a testimonial. The absence is notable.

I also can't find any evidence of how the co-founders met. Ilya's career was in healthcare AI in California. Alejandro's was in proptech business development. No shared employer, no shared university, no visible prior relationship. I've seen what happens when co-founders meet specifically to start a company — in one YC batch, nine teams that added co-founders they didn't know all fell apart within a year. I can't confirm that's the case here, but when two people from completely unrelated industries team up with no visible prior connection, I notice.

Where Ilya genuinely shines is as a technical builder. He has a PhD in physics, published ML research, and has built production AI systems at scale. He's clearly the person writing the code and building the product. That's the configuration I prefer — a technical founder who builds, paired with a business-oriented co-founder. And being a second-time founder (with claims of one acquisition and one company reaching $40M ARR) suggests he's been through the crucible before. But strong technical ability in one domain doesn't automatically transfer to domain authority in another. Knowing how to build great conversational AI doesn't mean you understand why a customer returns a pair of shoes versus exchanges them, or how a merchandising team processes sizing feedback, or what makes a brand trust an AI to represent their voice via SMS to their customers.

This is a competent team attacking a real problem with relevant (but transplanted) technical skills, positioned in a genuine market gap that may or may not be defensible. The idea wasn't discovered organically, the co-founder relationship is opaque, no customers are willing to be named, and the competitive moat against well-funded incumbents is thin. If everything the company claims about its metrics is true and they can sign three to five recognizable brands in the next six months, this could work. But I invest based on what I can see, not what I have to hope.
