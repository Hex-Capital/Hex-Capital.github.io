# Regbase -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Natalie Aresta-Katz spent her Paul, Weiss years building the manual regulatory-tracking workflow that Regbase now automates -- which is the single most interesting line in the dossier and also the only one that lights up my framework. Regbase's signature is a high-founder-market-fit / low-physical-verifiability pairing: a founder who genuinely lived the problem, building a SaaS abstraction in a regtech lane where Legora just printed a $5.6B valuation (TechCrunch, April 2026) and FiscalNote is sitting public at $84.8M ARR. That is "competent operator automating her own ex-employer," not "magically weird."

Anduril-Luckey (2017) is the high rung on lived-experience -- Palmer had shipped Oculus to scale before he ever touched a defense contract, and the founder biography preceded the company by years. Regbase's Aresta-Katz does not clear that bar; she ran the ESG regulatory research workflow at Paul, Weiss but did not ship a prior product to scale. What she does have is the autobiographical first-contact pattern: she IS the customer she is now selling to, which is closer to the **Crusoe**-Lochmiller "of the place, not visiting it" template than to a McKinsey-deck founder. Ethan Shea building agentic infrastructure at Vercel pairs cleanly to that -- the customer-problem owner plus the systems engineer is the right shape, even if neither has the prior-product-at-scale flag I want.

HQ Trivia is the failure rung on CEO clarity, and the failure mechanism was Yusupov and Kroll never naming the tiebreaker -- the feud killed a company whose product I could literally feel pulling me to my phone. That pattern does not show up in Regbase: Aresta-Katz is named CEO, Shea is CTO, Godoy is CBO/Head of Operations, and the equity/authority lines look delineated rather than 50/50. The dossier is silent on a formal operating agreement or vesting protocol, so I am defaulting near the upper-mid of the band rather than top of it -- silence is not absence here, it is just unverified.

**Magically weird** is where Regbase loses me on mechanism. Over 18 months the regtech category compounds against Regbase, not for it: Legora at $5.6B (TechCrunch, April 2026) has the capital to bolt multilingual sub-national ingestion onto a horizontal legal-AI surface in two quarters, FiscalNote already launched PolicyNote AI per its FY2025 10-Q, and Corlytics sits inside Verdane (April 2024) with cross-sell into financial-services compliance. Regbase's claim to catch what "Harvey, Legora, ChatGPT, and local counsel miss" has no third-party benchmark in the dossier, which means the wedge is asserted, not measured. I am being asked to fund the seventh entrant into a lane where the consensus has already priced the prize.

**Capital-intensive / unsexy-regulated** is where the counterfactual matters. Regtech is regulated-adjacent -- compliance, ESG, multilingual statutory text -- but Regbase is software, not hardware, not energy, not fertility. To flip this dimension a tier I would need Regbase to be ingesting something architecturally moat-y that incumbents cannot rebuild: a proprietary network of in-country human reviewers in twenty non-English jurisdictions, or a regulator-sanctioned data partnership in a market like the EU AI Act registry. The dossier shows neither -- the moat description is "proprietary multilingual crawling," which is a feature, not a regulatory artifact. **Vigil Labs** (August 2025) lives deeper in regulated-finance plumbing than Regbase does.

**Physical-world verifiability** -- the single-anchor parallel here is **Flock Safety** (~2018), which I sourced because I read the founder's Wi-Fi name at a Four Seasons caf? and could touch the camera the company shipped. Regbase has no pricing page, no public demo, no app I can install, no artifact I can photograph in the wild; it is enterprise SaaS sold through a Paul, Weiss network into Fortune 500 legal departments. A normal human cannot use Regbase in 48 hours, and I cannot stumble into evidence of it the way I stumbled into Flock. This is the dimension that pins Regbase low even with founder-market fit doing real work.

My 36-month forecast: Regbase reaches $1?3M ARR riding the Paul, Weiss and Sodali / FrameworkESG referral lattice into a dozen mid-market law-firm and consultancy seats, then gets quietly acquired by Thomson Reuters, Wolters Kluwer, or FiscalNote for a low-nine-figure outcome that is fine for the founders and forgettable for a Long Journey fund. The mechanism is the signature: real founder-problem fit (the workflow exists, the pain is real, the network closes early customers) without the magically-weird wedge or the tactile artifact that turns a regtech tool into a category-defining company.

The strongest bull case invokes the **HandUp** (~2014) exception -- I wrote that check because the founder's biography mirrored my own, overriding the commercial-defensibility filter. Aresta-Katz mirrors a customer trajectory, not a personal-mission trajectory, so HandUp does not cleanly apply. The **DeepMind** (~2013) polymath exception also fails: Aresta-Katz is a domain specialist, not a cross-disciplinary compounder. The bull case worth respecting is operational -- a founder who lived the workflow and an ex-Vercel staff engineer building agentic infrastructure can ship faster than a public company with 22% YoY ARR decline (FiscalNote 10-Q, FY2025). I would flip to Invest if Regbase produces one verifiable artifact within 12 months: a named Fortune 500 general counsel attesting on the record that Regbase caught a sub-national or non-English regulatory development that Harvey, Legora, or FiscalNote demonstrably missed, with a measurable dollar consequence avoided. Until then, this is a pass with warmth, not a pass with prejudice.
