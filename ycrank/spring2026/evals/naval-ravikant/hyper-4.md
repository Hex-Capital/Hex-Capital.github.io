# Hyper -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Hyper is a two-person San Francisco company shipping a "silent learning agent" that ingests Notion, Cursor, Claude Code, email, and LinkedIn DMs and re-injects that context into someone else's chat turn -- a knowledge-layer middleware whose biggest market is unambiguously platform-shaped, and whose distribution lives at the pleasure of Anthropic and Cursor. The signature is a wide-market product riding gatekept rails, which is the exact inversion of the architecture I keep funding.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung on leverage because anyone could post and the marginal cost of replication was zero -- no Apple, no AWS-but-a-toll, no third-party API key required for distribution to compound. Hyper's distribution mechanism, per the YC description, is "inject knowledge into third-party AI tools" -- Cursor and Claude Code. That is the inverse of permissionless. Anthropic ships native cross-tool memory and Hyper's channel narrows; Cursor restricts third-party context injection and Hyper's channel narrows again. The dossier even names this as a Key Risk and lists no mitigation. Code is being written by Shalin Shah and Kanyes Thaker, so the *production* is permissionless. The *distribution* is rented. That is labor-and-capital leverage wearing a code-leverage costume, and the leverage check is the first thing I run.

Perplexity (2022) is the LOW rung on network value because each user's query does not directly make the next user's query better -- the only reason I tolerate it in the portfolio is the Known Exception where criteria #1, #3, and category-timing all clear emphatically. Does that pattern appear in Hyper? Criterion #1 (leverage) does not clear -- the Cursor/Claude Code dependency is the opposite of what cleared for Perplexity through its API and product distribution. Criterion #3 (specific knowledge) is real but not extreme. So the exception precedent does not load. Hyper's knowledge graph is per-customer; the (N+1) Hyper customer does not make the Nth customer's silent agent smarter, because data security at the Notion/email/LinkedIn-DM boundary forbids cross-tenant compounding. Single-sided B2B SaaS sold by the seat, with no asymmetric-user-value compounding underwriting the 10,000x math.

Kanyes Thaker was Founding Data Engineer at Snorkel AI -- this is the specific-knowledge gate doing actual work, because Snorkel is the canonical company for production data labeling and weak supervision feeding enterprise RAG, which is precisely the cleaning-and-retrieval problem Hyper claims to solve. Shalin Shah ran Autonomy at Matic Robots, less directly relevant, but the search snippet citing a 3M-user accessibility app in high school is a builder-shipping signal. They were roommates and Matic colleagues, both Berkeley 2021. Specific-knowledge mechanism over 18 months: the Snorkel-trained intuition about how cleaning pipelines fail at scale is the one defensible asset Hyper has against Glean's six-year head start. If Kanyes ships a meaningfully different ingestion architecture publicly -- a paper, an open-source connector framework -- this dimension flips a tier. If the team stays heads-down and silent through 2026, it does not.

Big-market test for Hyper is where I would have to flip the score down a tier for it to drop to pass: the market would have to be a single named vertical with no adjacent pivot path. It isn't. The Hyper wedge is "team knowledge layer for AI-first companies," which can pivot into agent memory infrastructure, into developer context APIs, into a Cursor-replacement workspace, into per-employee assistants. Glean is at $200M ARR per the December 2025 Fortune piece, Dust raised $40M from Sequoia -- that is market-validation evidence, not market-size disqualification. The Naval-axiom "niche markets don't work because the first idea never works" is satisfied here; Hyper has pivot-room. The problem is not the market.

Founder trio for Hyper reads clean. Berkeley EECS and CS+Math, ex-Matic, ex-Snorkel, ex-Wit.ai -- shipped artifacts named in the dossier (Kanyes's Ferret repo, Shalin's accessibility app). No contested exits, no partner litigation, no self-promotion-about-ethics tells, no 50/50-with-no-CEO red flag visible because the dossier does not specify equity but co-roommate co-workers reads as a high-trust pair. Compare to Ivan Zhao at Notion (2013) on this single axis: Zhao had the adolescent-onward obsession that made Notion survive multiple rebuilds; Shalin and Kanyes have credible builder-DNA but no comparable lived-problem-ownership of the knowledge-fragmentation problem from the user side. The integrity leg passes; the intelligence leg passes; the energy leg is unverifiable at pre-seed. Default-band, not standout.

Forecast over 36 months: Hyper gets acqui-hired by Anthropic or Cursor for the ingestion-connector team at sub-$50M before crossing $5M ARR, after one of those platforms ships native cross-tool memory and absorbs the wedge. The mechanism is the gatekeeper-dependency I flagged in the leverage paragraph compounding against a 2-person team racing Glean's $600M war chest and Dust's Sequoia balance sheet -- the connectors and the Snorkel-flavored cleaning pipeline are valuable to the platform that eats the layer, but not valuable enough standalone to outrun the platform.

Bear of my own bear: the bull case is that Anthropic does NOT ship native cross-tool memory because doing so puts Anthropic in the data-residency and SOC 2 business it has spent two years avoiding, and Hyper becomes the third-party trust layer that holds the customer's Notion-and-email keys so Claude doesn't have to -- the Anchorage Digital pattern where regulation-and-trust becomes the permission-as-moat. This stings because it is exactly the override I used for Anchorage. The dismantling: Anchorage had a Treasury OCC charter (January 2021) that is genuinely non-replicable; Hyper has no equivalent regulatory or trust asset disclosed, no SOC 2 mentioned on heyhyper.ai, no enterprise design partner named. The Anchorage exception requires permission-as-moat to actually exist, not to be hypothetically achievable. Verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Hyper closes two named Fortune-500 enterprise design partners with signed data-processing agreements before December 2026, demonstrating the trust-layer thesis is real rather than aspirational. Until then, pass and watch.
