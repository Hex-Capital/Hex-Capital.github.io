# GUILD -- Paul Graham Evaluation

GUILD is a credentialed two-person pair pitching a category-defining label ("Neoprime") with no shipped artifact behind it -- Kaya Celebi and Erim Gurlemis share Duke CS/Stats undergrad, Columbia MSc CS, and overlapping stints at Hartree Partners and Morgan Stanley, but the dossier surfaces nothing they built or hustled before applying to YC. That high/low pairing -- strong cofounder configuration over zero pre-funding animal evidence -- is the inverse of the pattern I underwrite. It is the "clever idea, soft founders" silhouette I named in my own pass example, dressed in a defense costume.

Brian Armstrong rescued his rejected **Coinbase** (2012) application by building an Android Bitcoin wallet -- that is the HIGH rung of animal resourcefulness, a founder who manufactured a fact in the world to overturn a "no." GUILD's dossier offers nothing analogous: no signed supplier MOU, no CAGE code application underway, no $10K of personal capital deployed to fund a pilot run, no cold-call campaign to DoD program offices documented. The website is "a single-page 'the AI-Native Neoprime' tagline" as of June 2026. Kaya Celebi and Erim Gurlemis may be animals in private, but animals leave tracks, and the dossier records none -- no demo video on Hacker News like Drew Houston's pre-seed **Dropbox** (2007), no improvised revenue stunt like Brian Chesky's cereal boxes. Absence here is evidence against.

The MBA-team AI-wrapper I describe in my pass example fails because nobody on it has personally suffered the problem. GUILD's founders worked at a commodities trading firm and an investment bank; neither Kaya Celebi nor Erim Gurlemis has documented time at a defense prime, at DIU, in uniform, at a CMMC auditor, or even as a subcontractor scrambling for an SBIR. The Collisons hacked commerce flows at **Auctomatic** (2007) for years before **Stripe** (2010) -- by the time they pitched, the product was their autobiography. GUILD reads the opposite way: two ML engineers spotted that defense-tech VC hit $38B in 2025 and that Neoprimes are growing "5-20x faster than legacy primes," then designed a company around the category. That is the "spotted a market opportunity" tell from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012), not the organic-pain pattern.

GUILD does score real points on the schlep dimension -- which is the one place this dossier surprises me upward. CAGE codes, CMMC, ITAR, security clearances, past-performance qualification, supplier-quality engineering: this is exactly the unglamorous regulatory grind that defines a **Coinbase** (2012) moat once you survive it, and "no software-only YC applicant in its batch wanted to run" the work. GUILD has chosen a category whose floor is unpleasant enough to discourage imitators. The compounding question over 18 months is whether Kaya Celebi and Erim Gurlemis will personally do the schlep -- file the paperwork, sit in the SCIF, badge into the program office -- or whether they will try to abstract it behind "AI" and "supplier partners." The dossier's silence on a single named supplier relationship or compliance milestone leans me toward the latter.

Counterfactual on hacker genealogy: for GUILD to flip from a 7 to an 11 on this axis, the dossier would need a kcelebi repo with material star count, a documented OSS contribution, a competitive-programming placement, or evidence Erim Gurlemis shipped non-trivial infrastructure at Morgan Stanley beyond an internal ML model. What exists is two Columbia MSc CS degrees and a GitHub handle whose "top repo star count [is] not retrievable." Credentials are not hacker lineage -- Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize (2005) was hacker lineage. GUILD's pair clears the technical-by-credential bar but no further.

Stripe-the-pair parallel: the Collisons at **Stripe** (2010) were two brothers with a decade of shared building behind them by the time of YC, and that depth of relationship is what I underwrite when I say I prefer pairs to solos. GUILD's Kaya Celebi and Erim Gurlemis share Duke, Columbia, Hartree, and Morgan Stanley -- a genuinely long co-trajectory, the longest plausible relationship I can derive from a pre-seed dossier without an interview. This is the one dimension where GUILD reads cleanly to me. It is also, unfortunately, the cheapest dimension on my rubric.

Thirty-six month forecast: GUILD does not win a meaningful prime defense contract, fails to clear CMMC Level 2 or post a named DoD program-office award, and pivots within 24 months to selling AI-procurement software to existing primes -- competing directly with Procurement Sciences -- at sub-$500K ARR, or dies. The mechanism is the capital-intensity mismatch the dossier itself names: a two-person team promising to "source, fund, produce, and deliver" against Anduril at $30.5B, Hadrian at $476.5M raised, Shield AI at $5B, and Mach at $185M will discover that the "fund and produce" verbs require working capital and headcount the standard YC $500K does not buy, and the orchestration-layer pivot is the soft landing.

The bull case stings on one specific point: my **Airbnb** (2009) known exception exists precisely because I was wrong to reject ideas that looked structurally implausible, and the Cipher Brief Neoprime tailwind is the kind of policy shift that can carry weak founders further than they deserve. Two ML engineers with finance-ops backgrounds might be exactly the unsexy operator profile that a DoD program office trusts more than another Palmer Luckey clone -- the schlep is real and the founders are at least credentialed to grind it. But Airbnb passed the animal test via cereal boxes before I wrote the check. GUILD has shown no equivalent stunt, and "Neoprime" is a label, not Obama O's. The condition that would flip my verdict is concrete: a signed supplier integration with a named Tier-2 defense manufacturer plus a documented prime or subprime contract award before Demo Day. Until then, pass.
