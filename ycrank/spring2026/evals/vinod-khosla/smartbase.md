# Smartbase -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Smartbase's own ROI calculator tells me everything I need to know about consequence magnitude: 500 POs per month at 15 minutes each, saving ~$33,750 per year per facility. That is the company's best-case pitch for what success looks like — a manufacturer saves one clerk's salary. Scale that to every small manufacturer in America and you have a nice vertical SaaS business generating moderate returns for its investors. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No new capability is created. The world with Smartbase in it looks almost identical to the world without it, except some back-office workers do different tasks. I regularly pass on exactly this profile — AI applied to a specific enterprise workflow, producing incremental efficiency gains that compound into a 3-5x return. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money, and Smartbase is architected for neither extreme.

Conexiom, with $170M raised and ~$75M in estimated annual revenue serving 16 of the top 20 industrial distributors, has been solving this exact problem for two decades. Smartbase's positioning is to be a cheaper Conexiom for SMBs — a legitimate market gap, but not a technology discontinuity. The dossier is explicit: "The core technology — OCR/AI extraction from documents and ERP API integration — is technically reproducible" and "increasingly commoditized via LLM APIs." Smartbase is applying existing LLM capabilities to a known workflow. There is no novel science, no proprietary architecture, no step-function improvement in cost or capability. This is a wrapper, not a wedge. When I funded Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had built genuinely novel technology — electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers that created a non-linear cost reduction in small satellite launch. Smartbase has nine ERP integrations maintained by two people. That is operational grit, which I respect, but it is not the kind of technical leverage that produces asymmetric returns.

The bull case an advocate would make: Sam Goldman and Taira Fujioka are outsiders who went and slept on factory floors, immersed themselves in a physical-world industry they had no prior experience in, pivoted decisively from Anytrace within a year, and got accepted back into YC — a non-trivial signal of learning velocity. Taira's background as the youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference is directly relevant AI/ML infrastructure expertise. The "why now" is genuine — LLMs crossed a capability threshold in 2024-2025 making document extraction viable for SMBs without expensive per-customer template configuration. Five-figure annual contracts with first customers in a Spring 2026 batch shows rapid commercial traction. An advocate would invoke my DoorDash exception: execution-heavy, no technical novelty, but consequence magnitude large enough to override. Here is where the argument collapses. DoorDash's consequence magnitude was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people — planetary scale. Smartbase's consequence at full success is that manufacturers process purchase orders faster. The exception requires consequence magnitude that changes how millions live; Smartbase does not meet that threshold at any reasonable scale.

Goldman and Fujioka do show some of the learning machine signal I value — the factory floor immersion, the willingness to kill Anytrace and restart, the intellectual flexibility to move from web3 (Sphinx) to AI support (Anytrace) to manufacturing. But three distinct problem domains across three ventures in rapid succession — smart contracts, then AI support engineering, then manufacturing PO automation — also maps to a red flag: the founder presents a different pitch every six months. I need to see evidence that Smartbase represents genuine conviction born from primary research, not the next iteration in a search for product-market fit. The YC re-acceptance is a positive signal, and Harj Taggar's involvement suggests the pivot story was compelling, but I have insufficient evidence to distinguish rapid learning from rapid thrashing. Score: above default but not high.

Smartbase targets redistribution of existing clerical labor spend — the $26B TAM they cite is money already being spent on data entry workers — rather than creation of new demand or capability. This is one of my explicit avoid signals. My highest-conviction investments create new categories: OpenAI created artificial general intelligence capabilities that did not exist. Impossible Foods created protein without animal agriculture. Commonwealth Fusion Systems is creating limitless energy. Smartbase makes an existing back-office process marginally cheaper. The affordability angle exists — bringing Conexiom-level automation to SMBs who cannot afford enterprise pricing — but radical affordability means making an expensive necessity 10x cheaper or free. Saving $33K per year on a single workflow at a manufacturing facility is cost optimization, not cost revolution.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Smartbase demonstrated that its AI extraction accuracy on heterogeneous PO formats (handwritten, faxed, multilingual) exceeded 99% without per-customer training — a genuine technical breakthrough in document understanding that Conexiom and generic LLM APIs cannot match — and the founders articulated a roadmap from PO entry to full autonomous procurement that would eliminate the procurement function entirely, not just automate one step. That would shift consequence magnitude and technical approach scores meaningfully. As it stands, Smartbase is a competent vertical SaaS play in a real but unexciting market. The consequence magnitude is the decisive dimension: if this fully works, the world looks approximately the same, and that is not a bet I make with my own money.
