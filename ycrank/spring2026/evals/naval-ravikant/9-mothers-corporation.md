# 9 Mothers -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see is a leverage mismatch. Defense hardware is the structural inverse of everything I optimize for. Each EDDA unit has a marginal manufacturing cost. Scaling revenue means building more physical systems, hiring field engineers, navigating ITAR compliance, and surviving 12-36 month procurement cycles. Fourteen active job postings spanning robotics, acoustic DSP, electrical engineering, and systems engineering at $150-250K — before shipping a single unit — tells me this company's growth function is headcount-linear. Midjourney hit $500M revenue with 163 people because pixels have zero marginal cost. Weapon systems are not pixels.

The specific knowledge question cuts to the core of my hesitation. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA — a no-code testing platform. His specific knowledge is in CI/CD pipelines, Ruby infrastructure, and DevOps. Roman Khomenko spent eight years doing QA automation, computer vision on screenshots, and production ML pipelines at that same company. These are genuinely skilled software builders. But building an autonomous counter-drone weapon system requires integration across acoustic sensing, real-time target tracking, kinetic neutralization, hardened electronics, and military-grade reliability in contested electromagnetic environments. The AI detection module is maybe 30% of the full system. The founders' specific knowledge covers that 30%. The other 70% — hardware, weapons integration, defense procurement, field deployment — must be hired in. When the knowledge has to be hired, you've lost the specific knowledge advantage. You're an agent managing other agents.

Now, the principal authenticity signal is more interesting than the specific knowledge score suggests. Khomenko graduated from Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics. Kharkiv has been under sustained drone and missile attack since 2022. This is not a founder who identified counter-drone systems through market research or trend analysis. The problem found him through lived experience of the most personally brutal kind. And the co-founder relationship — Smith and Khomenko working together for eight years at Rainforest QA — is exactly the kind of deep trust I look for. These are not strangers from a co-founder matching event. They are battle-tested partners who chose each other again for something harder.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. The timing is real — $49.9B in defense tech procurement urgency, the Army handing Anduril a $20B framework contract, and battlefield evidence from Ukraine that cheap autonomous drones have fundamentally changed tactical warfare. Defense primes are structurally incapable of building cheap, portable systems — their business models depend on expensive platforms with long integration cycles and fat margins. There is a real gap at the tactical edge for a sub-$50K autonomous C-UAS system that a squad can mount on a vehicle. If EDDA's acoustic-AI approach works — passive detection that doesn't emit a radar signature, harder to jam — it could be genuinely differentiated. And the regulatory barriers that I normally view as fragile moats actually compound in defense: once you're an approved vendor with ITAR certification and deployed systems generating proprietary acoustic drone signatures, switching costs are real. This is closer to Varda's physics moat than to a regulatory capture play.

But here's where my framework is honest: even accepting the bull case, this company needs capital leverage and labor leverage to win. It needs government relationships, procurement specialists, manufacturing capacity, and field support teams. Anduril has a $60B valuation and $2B in revenue. DroneShield is doing A$216M in revenue across 70+ countries. Epirus has $550M in funding. 9 Mothers is a pre-seed startup competing against organizations with established Pentagon relationships and deployed systems. The acoustic-AI approach may offer differentiation, but as the dossier notes, the technique is replicable with sufficient R&D investment — and these competitors have orders of magnitude more R&D capital. The founders cannot overcome the capital and relationship asymmetry through code leverage alone, because this is not fundamentally a code-leverage business.

I respect the Ukrainian connection. I respect the co-founder relationship. And I acknowledge the timing may never be better for a company like this. But my framework exists for a reason — I systematically avoid businesses where scaling revenue requires proportional headcount, where the moat depends on government relationships, and where the founders' specific knowledge doesn't cover the full stack of what the company must build. The strongest version of 9 Mothers is still a company that needs 200 employees and $100M in capital before it reaches meaningful revenue. That is the opposite of a bet I make.
