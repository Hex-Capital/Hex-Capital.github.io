# Orthogonal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Orthogonal is that Stripe has already arrived. Not "might arrive someday" -- Stripe has shipped the x402 protocol, partnered with OpenAI on an Agentic Commerce Protocol, and processed roughly 50 million transactions. When I look at a company, one of the first things I ask is whether there's a moat, because defensibility is one of the number one things I look for. And here, the core product -- API aggregation, unified billing, key management -- is technically replicable, built on open-source standards (MCP), and sits directly in the crosshairs of an incumbent that already has millions of developers and merchants on its platform. That's not a company fighting in a space others avoid. That's a company fighting for territory that the most formidable payments company on Earth has already claimed.

I don't know these founders' stories. And that's not because I didn't look -- there's simply nothing in the public record that tells me why Christian Pickett and Bera Sogut are the people who *must* solve this problem. I see strong résumés: Coinbase, Vercel, Google, Amazon Robotics, two-time ICPC World Finalist. Those are genuinely impressive credentials, especially Bera's competitive programming background. But credentials are not conviction. When I backed the Crusoe team, or when I felt that pull toward Contraline, there was a biographical thread that made the founder inseparable from the mission. Here, I see two talented engineers who identified a real developer pain point from their professional experience at payments and API companies. That's founder-market fit in the conventional sense, but it's not the kind of personal stake that tells me they'll keep going when Stripe launches a competing feature and every VC in the room tells them to pivot.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If you believe that the explosion of AI agents will create a massive new API economy -- and the market projections suggest exactly that -- then whoever becomes the "app store" or unified access layer for agent-to-API interactions captures an extraordinary position. Stripe's x402 approach is blockchain-native and adds friction that many developers don't want. There's a genuine gap for a curated, fiat-friendly, developer-obsessed aggregation layer. Christian's specific experience building billing at Vercel and payments at Coinbase is precisely the background you'd want for this product. And leveraging the YC network to bootstrap the supply side is smart -- it's a warm start to a cold-start problem. If these founders execute with speed and taste, they could own the developer experience layer while Stripe focuses on the protocol layer. That's the version of this story where it works.

But I keep coming back to the consensus problem. Agentic payments is one of the hottest categories in tech right now. Stripe, Mastercard, Coinbase, multiple YC companies from this very batch -- everyone sees this opportunity. The "Crypto / Web3" tag on YC, the competitive landscape crowded with Nevermined, Nightmarket, and Prava, the breathless TAM projections -- these are the hallmarks of a category where the weirdness has already been arbitraged away. The most promising companies I've backed were ones that other investors considered too strange, too early, or too niche. Orthogonal is none of those things. It's a well-understood category play by well-credentialed founders, which is exactly the kind of bet that consensus-based investors love and that I've learned to be cautious about.

The co-founder dynamic has a positive signal -- they met at McGill and have been close friends since, which is better than strangers who met at a co-founder matching event. But I don't know how they fight, how they've navigated disagreement, or what they've survived together beyond school. And with a two-person team in a category this competitive, the pressure will come fast. The 16 API integrations and YC partnerships show early hustle, but no revenue, no user counts, and no waitlist data means I'm evaluating potential without any demand signal beyond LinkedIn followers and single-digit GitHub stars.

I respect what these founders are building, and I think the problem is real. But this doesn't activate any of the signals I've trained myself to trust. There's no biographical thread that makes this deeply personal. There's no weirdness that others can't see. There's no economic access story for underserved populations. And the competitive landscape is the opposite of empty. This is a pass for me -- not because the company is bad, but because it's not my kind of bet.
