# General Instinct -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

General Instinct is the analytical signature I dread most in a pre-seed AI dossier: a credentialed-researcher pair — Bill Jiao out of Siemens' multimodal foundation model team, Guanming Wang out of DeepMind with a Cambridge MPhil — building a software abstraction layer ("any frontier model, any edge device, within any latency budget") into a category where Qualcomm just paid for Edge Impulse in March 2025 and Modular sits on $380M at a $1.6B valuation. The high end of General Instinct is the founders' AI research depth. The low end is that I cannot touch this product, cannot find it in the wild, and cannot find a single drone, robot, or factory floor where either founder spent a year living the pain they now propose to solve.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is my HIGH rung on founder-from-lived-experience because he shipped Oculus from his parents' garage and *then* went to the defense problem with social license to walk onto a base. General Instinct's founders are research-shaped against that template — DeepMind and Siemens are real frontier-model rooms, and the polymath exception I made for Hassabis at DeepMind (~2013) sits in my head as I read Wang's profile. But General Instinct's problem domain is robotics, drones, and manufacturing — and neither Jiao nor Wang has shipped a robot, flown a drone in production, or stood on a factory floor watching a Jetson box brown out. The "111 ms cold start on Jetson Orin NX" line is a researcher's benchmark, not a roboticist's scar. The biography that put Palmer in front of defense isn't here; what's here is two ML-vision researchers who chose physical AI because that's where the frontier-model compression problem is interesting.

The Pass Example I keep in my back pocket starts with "two ex-FAANG PMs, both Stanford CS, deck opens with a $200B TAM slide" — the LOW rung on magical-weirdness, the consensus AI darling that six funds chase. General Instinct doesn't trigger the deck-pattern, but it triggers the *category* pattern exactly. Edge-AI runtime is the opposite of magically weird in Spring 2026: Qualcomm acquired Edge Impulse for the same workflow eleven months ago, Modular is the cross-hardware compute layer the entire industry already knows about, NimbleEdge raised $3.32M doing on-device ML for mobile, and NVIDIA, Apple, and Qualcomm all ship first-party compilers that monetize their own silicon. General Instinct is not ignored or laughed at — it is competing for the same dollar as three better-capitalized incumbents and one chipmaker-owned acquisition target. That is the inverse of "find them before it becomes consensus."

On capital-intensity, General Instinct flirts with my frontier-hardware sleeve the way Crusoe (~2018) did — robotics, drones, and manufacturing are the verticals where the moat *should* compound — but the actual product General Instinct is selling is a software runtime. Eighteen months of compounding on a software-only compression toolchain against open-source baselines (llama.cpp, ONNX Runtime, TensorRT-LLM) is the wrong slope; the moat in physical AI compounds at the *device* layer, where the chipmaker controls the SDK, or at the *deployment* layer, where the operator controls the well pad. General Instinct sits between those two layers, which is the squeeze position, not the moat position.

On governance, General Instinct lists two co-founders and no named CEO. I have read the HQ Trivia (2017) post-mortem too many times to treat that as a footnote — Yusupov and Kroll were two talented people without a tiebreaker and the whole thing came apart in feud. The dossier gives me no shared prior employer, no shared university, no documented vesting protocol — "international students deep into ML vision research who dreamed of building startups since high school" is a friendship origin, not an operating agreement. The counterfactual: if the YC page named one of them CEO with majority operating control, General Instinct moves up half a tier on this axis without changing anything else. It does not.

On physical-world verifiability, the single anchor parallel is Flock Safety (~2018) — sourced because I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café. General Instinct is the structural opposite. I cannot install Instinct Edge in my house, cannot photograph it, cannot hand it to a friend. The one cited artifact is a multimodal classifier hitting 111 ms cold start on a Jetson Orin NX, which is a screenshot in a deck, not a tactile artifact in the world. The Hustle Fund line I keep returning to — "I am gifted with the ability to make these connections" — fails on General Instinct because there is nothing in my physical environment that would have surfaced this company to me.

My 36-month forecast for General Instinct: acqui-hired into NVIDIA, Qualcomm, or Modular for under $30M as a talent-and-IP tuck-in, or pivoted off the horizontal runtime into a vertical robotics perception stack (drone autonomy, warehouse pick-and-place) once the channel conflict with TensorRT and Qualcomm AI Engine becomes operationally visible inside the first two paid pilots. The mechanism is straightforward: a cross-device compression runtime is structurally hostile to chipmakers who monetize their own compilers, and the two founders' research pedigree is exactly the profile those acquirers buy small.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the DeepMind (~2013) exception. Wang is ex-DeepMind, Jiao has filed two patents on tardigrade proteins as a high-school junior and worked on Siemens' first multimodal foundation model — that is a polymath pairing on paper, and "the age of the polymath" is the thesis I have stated publicly. If frontier robotics foundation models from Physical Intelligence, Skild, or Figure define the next decade, an independent cross-device inference runtime could be the Stripe of that layer, and General Instinct's founders are the right shape to build it. The reason I do not let that exception save General Instinct is that Hassabis's polymathy was paired with a domain-defining bet — AGI — that no incumbent was making, whereas General Instinct's bet is a compiler abstraction in a category where three incumbents and one chipmaker are already making the same bet better-funded. The condition that flips my verdict is a named, paid deployment with a frontier robotics counterparty — Figure, Physical Intelligence, Skydio, Anduril — in the next twelve months. Absent that, this is a polite pass.
