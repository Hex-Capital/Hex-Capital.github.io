## The Idea

- **Problem:** ~85% of disease drivers lack a druggable protein, and most disease originates in DNA rather than the protein it codes for, leaving currently "undruggable" oncology targets like MYC, TP53, and APC inaccessible to small molecules (YC Launch page, 2026).
- **Approach:** A DNA-sensing protein scans the genome for a hardcoded target sequence and undergoes a sharp structural change on recognition, which the team hijacked into a kill-switch that destroys the cell only when the diseased sequence is present (YC Launch page, 2026).
- **Differentiation:** Versus CRISPR therapeutics (Beam, Intellia, CRISPR Therapeutics) which edit the genome ex vivo or in vivo, FinalDose claims a single platform molecule reprogrammed by guide sequence to trigger targeted cell elimination rather than gene correction (YC Launch page, 2026).
- **Business Model:** [Inferred]: Therapeutic asset / platform development model — revenue likely from pharma partnerships, milestone payments, or out-licensing of programmed candidates; no pricing page exists (finaldose.ai, May 2026).
- **TAM/SAM:** No public TAM/SAM data found for this specific segment; company cites unlocking ">80% of cancers that existing drugs cannot reach" (YC Launch page, 2026).
- **GTM / Distribution:** [Inferred]: Preclinical pipeline development followed by IND filings and pharma co-development deals, given the founders' academic/CRISPR-screening backgrounds and the platform framing on the YC launch page (YC Launch page, 2026).

## Defensibility

- **Moat today:** Technical complexity — proprietary repurposing of a DNA-sensing protein's structural change as a kill switch, built on the founders' Oxford CRISPR-screening research pipelines (YC Launch page, 2026); no patents publicly cited.
- **Future moat:** [Inferred]: A reusable platform where "same molecule + new guide = new drug" could create cumulative regulatory/preclinical data advantages across multiple indications; unproven because no candidate has entered clinical trials per public sources.
- **Market structure:** [Inferred]: Regulatory complexity and biological validation costs deter rapid entry by incumbents whose pipelines are committed to existing modalities (mAbs, ADCs, base/prime editing); not a structural barrier yet because the FinalDose mechanism itself is publishable science.
- **Commoditization risk:** Academic labs working on DNA-sensing proteins (e.g., Cas-family structural sensors) and well-funded gene-editing companies (Beam, Intellia, Scribe) could pursue similar programmable cell-killing constructs (CRISPR Medicine News, 2024).

## Key Risks

- **Technical feasibility:** Programmable cell-killing via DNA-sensing structural change is unproven at the in vivo therapeutic performance level — no clinical or in vivo efficacy data is publicly disclosed (finaldose.ai; YC Launch page, 2026); standard preclinical-to-IND timelines (3–5+ years) apply with no public mitigation cited.
- **Specificity / off-target risk:** A genome-scanning kill-switch must distinguish a diseased sequence from billions of bases without triggering apoptosis in healthy cells; no specificity or off-target benchmarks are public (YC Launch page, 2026).
- **Delivery:** Therapy depends on delivering a protein/RNA effector to tumor cells across diverse cancer types; the company has not publicly disclosed a delivery modality (LNP, AAV, etc.) (finaldose.ai, May 2026).
- **Capital intensity vs. funding disclosed:** Oncology drug development typically requires $100M+ to reach clinic; only YC-stage funding is publicly visible and no Series A is reported in Crunchbase results (Crunchbase search, May 2026).
- **Competitive crowding:** Well-capitalized public companies (Beam, CRISPR Therapeutics, Intellia) and venture-funded peers (Scribe, KSQ) target overlapping "undruggable" oncology space (Labiotech, 2025; Motley Fool, 2026).
