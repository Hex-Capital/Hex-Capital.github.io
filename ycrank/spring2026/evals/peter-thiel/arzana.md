# Arzana -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Conexiom has raised $170 million and generates $28.7 million in annual revenue automating sales order processing for manufacturers. Paperless Parts has 500+ customers in manufacturing quoting. Esker serves 3,000+ enterprises and was acquired by Bridgepoint and General Atlantic. DigiFabster has launched its own AI Quote Agent. Arzana enters a market where four funded competitors are already building variations of the same product — AI that reads manufacturing documents and writes structured data into ERP systems. The existence of a competitive landscape slide with four named rivals is, in my framework, not context. It is the verdict. All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition. When I look at Arzana, I must ask what secret it possesses that Conexiom, with $170 million and years of integration experience, does not. The dossier does not reveal one. "AI can automate manufacturing front-office work" is not a secret — it is the operating thesis of every company in the table above. The underlying technology — LLMs parsing emails and PDFs, writing to ERP APIs — is accessible to any team with competent engineers and API documentation. Arzana's four modules (Quoting, Estimating, Order Entry, Dashboard) describe features, not a monopoly mechanism.

William Alexander, 21, grew up working at a manufacturing sourcing company in Okoboji, Iowa. Marshall Kools, 24, worked manufacturing jobs in Wisconsin and wrestled Division I at Stanford. These are not the biographies of founders playing tourist in an unfamiliar industry — they have authentic roots in the communities they serve, and Arzana maintains offices in both Okoboji and Appleton alongside San Francisco. Marshall earns roughly $10,000 in annual salary. Eight named customers, including Zeiss, at the pre-seed stage is evidence of genuine pull, not manufactured traction. The Milltown Paper CEO testimonial — reducing quote generation from 15 minutes to one click — describes real operational value. An advocate would construct the bull case around this distribution signal: Arzana's forward-deployed engineers embedding onsite at Midwest factories build the kind of relational trust that Silicon Valley SaaS companies cannot replicate from a distance, and the per-customer AI models trained on historical job costs create compounding switching costs. This is the Palantir playbook in miniature — embed, integrate, become irreplaceable.

Palantir is the structural parallel Arzana's forward-deployed model invites, and the comparison is precisely what condemns the investment. Palantir's secret was not that it deployed engineers alongside analysts — that was a tactic. The secret was that the intelligence community needed human-computer symbiosis for ontological data integration, at a time when every other defense contractor was selling fully automated black-box analytics. The CIA, through In-Q-Tel, became Palantir's founding customer not because Palantir had good engineers on-site but because Palantir held a contrarian insight about how intelligence analysis actually works. Arzana has no equivalent insight. Its product applies consensus AI capabilities to a problem that multiple funded competitors have already identified. The forward-deployed model without an underlying secret is expensive professional services with a SaaS billing wrapper — it creates per-customer lock-in but not market-level monopoly. I co-founded Palantir; I know the difference between embedding that compounds into structural dominance and embedding that merely raises customer switching costs. Arzana is the latter.

Arzana's TAM citation — $17.46 billion manufacturing operations management software growing to $76.71 billion by 2033 — is exactly the kind of large-market framing that signals competitive rather than monopoly thinking. The relevant question is not whether the market is large but whether Arzana can monopolize a specific segment of it, and the competitive landscape proves it cannot. Conexiom processes $100 billion in annual B2B transactions. Esker has quarterly revenue exceeding €43 million. These are not sleeping incumbents awaiting disruption — they are active competitors building the same AI capabilities Arzana offers. The commoditization risk noted in the dossier is accurate: Conexiom has already launched an "AI Platform," and Paperless Parts has released AI-powered quoting automation. Arzana's ITAR compliance mention hints at a potential government-adjacent beachhead — the one path that could create structural defensibility in my framework — but this is mentioned as a feature bullet, not as a strategic thesis. A company that treats defense compliance as a checkbox rather than a monopoly wedge has not understood the opportunity.

The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: Arzana would need to demonstrate that its manufacturing-specific AI models produce measurably superior accuracy — the claimed 99.9% — on a class of documents that horizontal competitors structurally cannot handle, AND that this accuracy gap widens with each customer deployment through proprietary training data that creates a genuine data network effect across its customer base. If Arzana's estimating models, trained on one mold maker's historical job costs, could improve quoting accuracy for a different mold maker in a different state — a cross-customer data flywheel rather than per-customer lock-in — that would constitute a secret about manufacturing data that Conexiom's document-processing approach does not possess. The dossier describes per-customer model training, which is table stakes, not a network effect. Absent evidence of cross-customer compounding, Arzana is building eight separate integrations, not a monopoly.

William and Marshall deserve credit for choosing an unsexy market where their personal histories give them authentic access — most Stanford graduates do not return to Iowa and Wisconsin to sell to factory operators. Arzana's distribution at pre-seed, with eight named customers and a New York Times feature, exceeds what most YC companies achieve. But distribution excellence in a competitive market produces a viable business, not a monopoly. "Superior sales and distribution by itself can create a monopoly, even with no product differentiation" — I wrote that, and I stand by it, but the precondition is that you are selling into a market without four funded competitors already occupying the channel. Arzana's distribution is strong; its structural position is weak. I pass.
