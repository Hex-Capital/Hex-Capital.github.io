# Netter -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Netter is a schlep-bet without an organic-pain anchor — two Mines Paris/ENS Ulm students, Samuel Ladroue and Amin Hamzaoui, betting their twenties on the connector-and-ontology grind that Palantir Foundry monetizes at the top of the market and that Databricks won't touch at the bottom. The signature pairing I see across the dossier is high on schlep, low on organic idea: Netter has run toward 120+ connector integrations and mid-market ERP/TMS/IoT plumbing, which is real work nobody enjoys, but neither founder appears to have personally suffered the mid-market-operator-without-a-data-engineer problem. That asymmetry is what I have to think about.

Coinbase (2012) is the right HIGH rung for the schlep dimension — Brian Armstrong picked up bitcoin compliance because everyone else in the batch was routing around it, and that grind became the moat. Netter's 120+ native connectors and per-customer ontology mapping across SaaS/ERP/TMS/IoT is the same shape of unpleasant work: every connector breaks when a vendor ships an API change, and ontology reconciliation across a Carrefour deployment is months of manual modeling no Databricks rep wants on their plate. Where Netter diverges from the Coinbase rung is that the schlep is not yet a *regulatory* moat — connector libraries can be cloned, and the dossier flags that Snowflake, Databricks, and Microsoft Fabric are all shipping LLM "chat with your data" features into the same wedge. The work is unpleasant; whether it's defensibly unpleasant is the open question.

The AI-wrapper pass example I wrote — two-MBA team, glossy deck, no shipped artifact — fails on organic idea because the founders never lived inside the problem. Netter's profile is not that pattern, but it isn't Drew Houston's USB stick at Dropbox (2007) either. Samuel Ladroue's mathematics-and-CS training at Mines Paris and Amin Hamzaoui's ENS Ulm coursework do not put either founder inside a nursing home, a Tesco store, or a logistics dispatcher's day-to-day. The dossier offers no anecdote of either founder watching an operations manager fight a SaaS export at 11pm. Mid-market operational pain is real, but it is not Samuel and Amin's pain, and "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) treats that gap as load-bearing.

Animal resourcefulness in Netter's dossier hinges almost entirely on whether the Carrefour FR, Tesco UK, and Auchan ES logos are paid production deployments or marketing. If two unfunded students from Mines Paris actually got procurement contracts with three of Europe's largest grocers, that is an animal moment on the order of the cereal boxes — those are nine-figure-revenue accounts whose procurement cycles eat better-funded startups alive. The dossier explicitly says "logo-on-site does not confirm paid production deployments" and the $12M funding figure is uncorroborated against Crunchbase or any announcement, which is exactly the kind of inconsistency I want resolved in the interview before I write. Over 18 months this dimension compounds or it collapses: either Samuel surfaces a signed Carrefour contract value and Netter becomes a European retail data platform, or the logos remain decorative and the animal test fails retroactively.

Hacker genealogy at Netter would flip a tier if either Samuel or Amin had a visible GitHub trail — an open-source connector library, an OCaml contribution out of ENS Ulm, anything that put a shipped technical artifact on the public record the way Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize did before Stripe (2010). ENS Ulm is genuinely elite technical pedigree; it has produced the OCaml and Coq communities. But the dossier returns "No public repos found" for both founders, and credentials-without-artifact is what the Hackers and Painters (May 2003) filter is designed to discount. The counterfactual is narrow and testable: one named open-source project from Amin would move this dimension a full tier.

Stripe (2010) is the single-anchor parallel for Netter's cofounder configuration — two technically-trained co-conspirators from a shared educational context, which is what the Collison brothers had via family and what Samuel and Amin have via Mines Paris. That parallel is structurally clean but evidentially thin: the dossier confirms the shared school but offers no description of prior projects, hackathons, or shipped code together, which is the texture that made the Collison pairing load-bearing rather than incidental. Two French elite-school students who haven't built together for years is not the Reddit (2005) dorm-roommate pattern; it's a reasonable default that the interview would have to confirm.

My 36-month forecast for Netter is acquisition by a mid-market integration platform — Workato, Tray.io, or Boomi — at sub-$75M for the connector library and the two founders, or alternatively a pivot to European retail specifically (Carrefour/Tesco/Auchan as the wedge) reaching $3–6M ARR without breakout. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: the schlep is real enough to produce a sellable asset but the lack of organic founder-problem fit means Netter won't out-iterate Snowflake's chat layer or Databricks' Mosaic AI on the horizontal pitch, and the founders will rationally take a strategic exit when the wedge narrows.

The bull case that stings is this: if the Carrefour, Tesco, and Auchan logos are real paid deployments, then Samuel and Amin have already done something almost no pre-seed YC founder has ever done, and the animal test is passed on a scale that dwarfs Airbnb's cereal boxes. European grocery procurement is brutal; landing three of them as students would be a black-swan signal the way Houston's Hacker News demo was for Dropbox (2007), and the missing organic-pain story would matter less because the market voted. The dossier evidence that wins against that case is the explicit unverified status of the logos plus the uncorroborated $12M figure — two anomalies in the same dossier shift my prior toward "marketing framing" rather than "stealth excellence." The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a case study URL or press release naming a contract value at any one of Carrefour FR, Tesco UK, or Auchan ES. Without it, Netter is a neutral pre-seed bet with a real schlep and an unverified animal moment, and I don't write the check today.
