# snag -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The $6.5M in booking requests over 30 days with 40% month-over-month growth is the number that jumps off the page -- and it's also the number that requires the most scrutiny. Booking *requests* are not completed transactions, and completed transactions are not revenue. Snag has no public pricing page, the app is free to download, and monetization is entirely inferred. I've seen this pattern before in consumer marketplaces: impressive GMV growth that masks the fundamental question of whether the company can extract a take rate from an activity that currently happens for free on Facebook groups. When I invested in Wish, I made the mistake of reading growth velocity as a substitute for margin architecture and infrastructure positioning. That stock collapsed 99% post-IPO. Snag shares the structural DNA: consumer marketplace, no B2B layer, no API, no pricing power demonstrated, and growth concentrated in a single geography.

Start with market structure timing, which is where my framework always begins: what structural change makes snag possible now that wasn't possible two years ago? The dossier identifies three catalysts -- NYC's Local Law 18 restricting sub-30-day Airbnb rentals, GenZ workforce flexibility trends, and LLM maturity enabling social post conversion. I find each of these underwhelming as structural shifts. Local Law 18 has been in effect since September 2023 -- that's a 2.5-year-old regulatory environment, not a fresh window opening. GenZ housing flexibility is a demographic gradient, not a step function. And converting Facebook group posts into structured listings is a basic NLP task that was feasible with pre-LLM technology; it doesn't represent a capability threshold being crossed the way GPT-4 unlocked legal document analysis for Harvey. The sublet market itself is obvious -- every college student in America has dealt with this problem for decades. LeaseBreak already has 100,000+ registered users in the same NYC sublet niche. Blueground has raised $352M in adjacent furnished rentals. This is not a market where consensus has missed the window. It's a market where multiple well-funded players already compete for the same renter.

Snag is a pure consumer application. Other businesses do not build on snag. There's no API, no developer integration layer, no B2B revenue stream, no platform that third parties embed into their workflows. The "AI model" that converts social media posts into structured listings is a feature inside the application, not infrastructure that compounds through usage. Compare this to Stripe, which became the payments API that thousands of companies built their checkout flows on, creating switching costs that compound with every integration. Snag's AI scraping capability is replicable by any competent engineering team -- the dossier itself acknowledges this under commoditization risk. The defensibility story rests entirely on marketplace liquidity density in NYC, which is real but geographically fragile: 30,000 users and 3,000 listings locked to a single city, and each new market requires rebuilding that density from scratch. That's a capital-intensive, city-by-city slog, not a compounding infrastructure moat.

The single-miracle test surfaces two distinct challenges that need to resolve simultaneously. First, snag needs a distribution miracle: achieving marketplace liquidity in each new city beyond NYC, where the university density, sublet culture, and Local Law 18 dynamics may not replicate. Second, snag needs a monetization miracle: proving it can charge a take rate on transactions that renters and subletters currently complete for free on social media. These are independent challenges -- solving distribution in a new city doesn't solve the monetization question, and proving monetization in NYC doesn't guarantee liquidity elsewhere. Two miracles, compounding odds. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas bring real strengths to this fight -- they've co-founded three consecutive ventures together, Selin has a prior exit through Knock Knock City's acquisition by LuggageHero, and the Forbes 30 Under 30 recognition plus General Catalyst backing for OneRoof signal genuine consumer product capability. But the OneRoof pivot also tells a story: $4.6M raised for a neighbor community app that didn't find product-market fit, leading to a rebrand as snag in late 2025. Persistence is a founder virtue, but a third venture pivoting from a second venture that pivoted from a first venture raises the question of whether the market is genuinely opening or whether these founders are searching for product-market fit in a space that may not structurally support a venture-scale outcome.

The strongest bull case: snag is capturing an existing behavior (finding sublets through fragmented social channels) in a new medium (a curated, verified marketplace app), which maps to my framework about capturing existing behavior rather than creating new behavior. The 7 university partnerships with NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, and Pace create a campus-by-campus distribution channel with real switching costs. And the 4.7/5 iOS rating with 184 ratings suggests genuine user satisfaction, not just download volume. If snag can prove monetization and demonstrate that NYC's liquidity flywheel replicates in Boston, LA, or Chicago, the TAM opens substantially -- the global short-term rental market is projected at $344B by 2034. But "if" is doing enormous work in that sentence. The path from 30,000 users in one city with zero proven revenue to a venture-scale outcome requires the kind of multi-miracle execution that my framework is designed to filter against.

I'm passing on snag. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are market timing and infrastructure positioning. The sublet market is not undergoing a nonobvious structural shift -- it's an obvious, well-understood category with existing competitors and no technology threshold being crossed that changes the game. And snag is a consumer application with no infrastructure layer, no B2B revenue, and no platform characteristics that create compounding switching costs. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas are capable consumer founders with a battle-tested partnership, and the NYC traction is genuinely impressive for a pre-seed company. But impressive early traction in a consumer marketplace without demonstrated monetization, without infrastructure positioning, and without a clear structural catalyst is the pattern that produced Wish, not Stripe.
