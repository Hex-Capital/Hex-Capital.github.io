# Salus -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Salus: this is one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. Everyone -- and I mean everyone -- agrees that AI agents need guardrails. Fiddler has $100 million in funding and already shipped a Realtime Guardrails API. Guardrails AI has nearly 6,000 GitHub stars and an open-source framework anyone can use today. Alphabet acquired Galileo. When I look at a space and see that level of validation and capital already deployed, my instinct is to walk the other way. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. AI guardrails in early 2026 is neither -- it's a category slide in every enterprise AI pitch deck. That doesn't mean there won't be a winner, but it means the weirdness has already been arbitraged out of the opportunity, and the price of being right here is competing against teams with multi-year head starts and deep enterprise relationships.

When I try to understand Kevin and Vedant's stories -- not the business, just how they got here -- what I find are two clearly brilliant Stanford CS students who were roommates. Vedant's research portfolio across five Stanford labs is genuinely impressive, and Kevin's Symbolic Systems background is the kind of interdisciplinary thinking I respect. But I'm listening for something different. I'm listening for the hardship that forged this specific commitment, the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem. I don't hear it. I hear talented students who identified a real technical gap in a hot market and built a clean solution for it. That's admirable engineering, but it's not the signal that has historically predicted my best investments. Travis had years of operating companies and a gravitas born from getting punched in the face repeatedly. The Crusoe founders had independently arrived at an insight from working directly in energy waste. What I see with Salus is smart people executing well in a category that's already attracting significant capital and attention.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. The timing genuinely is right -- agents are transitioning from text generators to action executors, and the failure mode shifts from "bad output" to "bad action with real-world consequences." That's a meaningful inflection. The 58% blocked-action recovery rate is a real, measurable result, not just hand-waving. The cross-platform positioning -- sitting at the orchestration layer across OpenAI, Anthropic, LangChain, and CrewAI -- is strategically smart because no single LLM provider has incentive to build guardrails that work across competitors. If Salus can accumulate enough data from processing millions of agent actions across diverse deployments, they could develop a genuine data moat that's hard to replicate. The self-repair feedback loop is a technically elegant mechanism. If I squint, I can see a version of this that becomes essential infrastructure -- the way Stripe became the default payment layer not because payments were a weird idea, but because they executed the developer experience better than anyone. For that to happen, though, these founders would need to outrun well-funded incumbents in enterprise sales, which is a very different challenge than building an elegant SDK.

What I don't see -- and what I uniquely look for -- is any dimension of economic access or making invisible systems visible for people who need it. Salus serves engineering teams at companies deploying AI agents. It's infrastructure for infrastructure. My portfolio has a consistent thread of companies that create real opportunity for people who previously had none: Uber for drivers, Postmates for delivery workers, Affirm for people trapped by hidden credit card fees, Flock Safety for communities without public safety data. Salus operates entirely within the developer ecosystem. That doesn't make it a bad business, but it doesn't activate the part of my investment framework that has historically driven my highest-conviction bets.

On co-founder dynamics, Stanford roommates is actually a positive signal in my experience -- there's a pre-existing relationship with shared context. But I'd want to probe much deeper. How do Kevin and Vedant argue? Who makes the final call when they disagree? The roommate relationship tells me they can coexist; it doesn't tell me they can survive the kind of crisis that tests every startup. Without that information, I note it as neutral rather than a red flag.

This is a pass for me. Not because Salus is a bad company -- the technical approach is sound, the market is real, and the founders are clearly capable. But it fails my primary filters: it's consensus, not contrarian; the founders don't have the biographical grit narrative I weight most heavily; it doesn't create economic access for underserved populations; and the competitive landscape is already crowded with well-capitalized players. I invest my own money in companies that most people think are crazy, built by founders whose life stories explain why they won't quit. Salus feels like a company that would get funded by plenty of other investors, and that's precisely why it's not my kind of bet.
