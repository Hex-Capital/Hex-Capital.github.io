# Arden -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Arden is what happens when two technically capable nineteen-year-olds — Aryaman Khanna out of Berkeley CS and Databricks, David Lomelin out of MIT mech interp — pick SOX testing off a market-opportunity menu rather than out of personal frustration. The dimensional signature is unmistakable: Arden's highest dimension is founder learning rate (Lomelin already shipped Blueprint to 2,500 businesses, which is more than most YC applicants have done), and its lowest is organic problem discovery (neither founder has a single line of audit, Big Four, or internal-controls work in their history, and the dossier's most concrete origin anecdote is them visiting Financial District audit-firm lobbies). That pairing describes a particular kind of company — smart kids who can build, but who chose the problem instead of being chosen by it.

On organic problem discovery, the ladder runs from Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive at the top, through Replit's Masad building the IDE he wished existed in the middle, down to my Pass Example — the two ex-consultants who "identified an opportunity in B2B workflow automation" by interviewing fifty potential customers. Arden sits a hair above that bottom rung, and only because the lobby visits are at least slightly more resourceful than a McKinsey deck. Khanna and Lomelin did not encounter SOX 404(b) testing in their own work; they read the 2025 KPMG SOX Survey, noticed Midship raised $4.15M doing the same thing, and went to find auditors to talk to. That is the wrong direction. Live in the future, then build what's missing — Arden is doing the inverse, which is finding a problem and reverse-engineering whether they live near it.

On founder learning rate, the ladder runs from Sam Altman's nineteen-year-old trajectory at Loopt through to Replit's compounding velocity, with the bottom rung reserved for founders who've been on the same idea a year with no iteration. Arden sits closer to the middle than the bottom, and the pin is Lomelin's Blueprint — a UGC marketing platform that actually reached 2,500 paying-or-free businesses. That's a real shipping artifact from a teenager, the kind of thing that made me pay attention to Patrick Collison at Auctomatic. It is not nothing. But Bindwell's two teenagers had the same property and an additional one — they were doing chemistry no one else could do — and Arden's technical core is "LLM agents plus connectors," which the dossier itself flags as replicable by any well-resourced compliance team.

On founder resourcefulness, Arden lives between my middle rung (the Collisons shipping faster than seemed possible) and my low rung (the consultants waiting for fundraising to validate them). The "applied three weeks past the YC deadline" snippet is mildly Airbnb-shaped if true, but unverified, and there's no cereal-box-equivalent here — no revenue, no named public-company logo, no story of the founders personally performing a SOX walkthrough by hand to learn the schlep. On the schlep test, Arden is genuinely in Stripe-adjacent territory: PCAOB AS 2201, COSO, read-only enterprise integrations across Workday/Okta/NetSuite/ServiceNow — this is the unglamorous regulated complexity I love. But Midship's $4.15M seed from Costanoa with ex-Deloitte audit founders, plus Oxus inside YC itself, means the consensus has already formed. Schleps are only moats when other founders won't touch them; three teams touched this one in the same six-month window. On earnest builder identity, neither founder shows the Watsi-style obsession with the problem itself — SOX testing reads as chosen, not loved, and there's no evidence Khanna or Lomelin built a v0 because they couldn't stop thinking about audit workpapers.

My 36-month forecast: Arden reaches somewhere between $500K and $2M ARR, gets out-flanked on enterprise logos by Midship's audit-domain founding team, and either acqui-hires into AuditBoard or Workiva for a modest number, or pivots to a broader "compliance agent" product targeting SOC 2 and ISO. The mechanism is the signature — fast-learning builders who didn't live in the problem will out-ship a v1, then lose the trust battle when external auditors and audit committees want to talk to someone who's been through a Big Four engagement, which neither Khanna nor Lomelin has.

The bull case stings, and I want to name it honestly. Bindwell was two teenagers in an unsexy regulated domain with no prior pesticide experience, and I personally invested. The Reddit lesson is that the idea is mutable — if Khanna and Lomelin have the running-back quality, SOX is just where they happen to be standing, and they'll find the opening. SOX testing is a real schlep; the connector breadth Arden claims (15+ systems) is real work; Lomelin's Blueprint shows he can ship. If they had cereal-box-level scrappiness and even one named public-company customer to point to, this would be an Invest. But the dossier doesn't show that — it shows MIT and Cal logos under "partnerships," SOC 2 Type I "in progress," no ARR, and a more credentialed direct competitor with disclosed fintech logos. The verdict flips if I see one signal: a named Fortune 500 SOX program lead willing to say on the record that Arden's workpapers passed external auditor review at year-end. Until then, I pass — but I'd take the meeting on their next company.
