# Prana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me immediately: AI healthcare captured 54% of all digital health funding in 2025. K Health has $441 million. Lotus Health just raised $41 million with a *free* model. Amazon launched a competing product in January with built-in distribution to hundreds of millions of Prime members. And Prana is entering this arena as a four-person pre-seed company with a $39-per-visit model. I've spent my career looking for companies that are unpopular, difficult to assess, and have technology risk that isn't insurmountable. An AI doctor app in 2026 is the opposite of unpopular — it's the single most crowded category in digital health. Every fund in the Valley has an AI healthcare bet. That's consensus investing territory, and consensus-based investing is fundamentally different from what I do. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, private space was a punchline. When I backed Contraline, male contraception made VCs physically uncomfortable. Prana doesn't make anyone uncomfortable — it makes everyone nod along, and that's exactly the signal I've learned to distrust.

Before I look at anything else, I always want to know the founder's story — not the business, the person. Why does Meer Patel need to build this? He has impressive credentials: Johns Hopkins biomedical engineering, deferred admission to Brown Medical School, co-developed a deep learning model for brain pressure monitoring. That deferral tells me he's committed to the startup path over the safe medical career. But I can't find the biographical thread that connects him to the problem of primary care access. Did he watch a family member's health deteriorate because nobody caught the warning signs between annual physicals? Did he grow up without insurance, terrified of what a doctor visit would cost? The dossier doesn't tell me, and that absence matters in my framework. The best founders I've backed were solving problems that were carved into their biography before they ever wrote a line of code. Vishvam Rawal is a quant applying trading infrastructure to health signals — technically elegant, but there's no personal stake in healthcare that I can identify. Sanjit Menon brings clinical training and prior startup experience with EminenceAI, which scaled to 400+ customers — that's real entrepreneurial evidence, but exam prep to primary care AI isn't a biographical inevitability, it's a career pivot.

The dimension where Prana most resonates with how I think about investing is economic access. A $39 doctor visit with no subscription, no insurance requirement, available in all 50 states — that is genuinely meaningful for the tens of millions of Americans who avoid primary care because they can't afford it or don't have coverage. Free AI consultations as the entry point lower the barrier further. When I invested in Uber, I saw it as a poverty-alleviation tool before I saw it as a logistics play — drivers earning income, riders getting affordable transportation. Affirm made credit transparent for people who were getting destroyed by hidden fees. Prana's pricing model has that same structural quality of creating access where exclusion existed. If they can execute the self-insured employer channel, they could reach populations that existing primary care has abandoned. That's a real signal, and it's the one thing that kept me from dismissing this outright.

But I have to be honest about the bull case and why it still doesn't get me there. If Prana's continuous monitoring model genuinely catches clinical drift — the slow creep of blood pressure, glucose, and metabolic risk that slips through annual physicals — and if the data flywheel creates real switching costs over time, and if they can navigate multi-state physician licensing and the evolving FDA landscape for AI health tools, then you could imagine them building the primary care relationship layer that the big players are too structurally conflicted to build. Amazon charges $199 a year (or $99 with Prime). K Health is locked into health system partnerships. Lotus is betting on a sponsorship model that may not survive a downturn. Prana's simplicity — $39, no strings — could be a wedge that survives where complexity fails. And Forward Health's collapse after burning $657 million proves that this market wants a software-first approach, which is exactly what Prana is building. Those are genuine arguments. But they require Prana to out-execute companies with 100 to 1,000 times their capital in a space where the technology stack — LLM triage, wearable integration, video visits — is built on widely available components. I don't see a defensible moat here, and defensibility is one of the first things I look for in early-stage companies.

The unverified traction claims concern me in a way that goes beyond the numbers. The website reports 4.9 out of 5 stars from 3,412 reviews, but there's no app store listing, no Trustpilot page, and the YC page describes the company as "live in beta with early users." That inconsistency isn't just a data problem — it's a credibility signal. When I sit across from a founder, I'm looking for raw authenticity, not curated narrative. A polished website with unverifiable review counts triggers the same instinct that makes me wary of over-rehearsed pitch decks. I want to hear the founder say "we have 47 users and three of them love us" — that tells me more than a 4.9-star rating I can't trace to its source. Three co-founders from three different institutions with no visible prior working relationship adds another dimension of uncertainty. I learned from HQ Trivia that even the right product at the right time fails when founder dynamics haven't been stress-tested. I'm not flagging a red flag here — I simply don't have enough information to evaluate this dimension, and at pre-seed, that ambiguity is the norm.

This is a team with genuine domain competence building a product with genuine economic access potential in a space that is genuinely hard to navigate. Under different competitive conditions — if this were 2019 and they were the first people crazy enough to suggest an AI could triage primary care — I might feel that jolt of energy I look for. But in 2026, this idea is as consensus as it gets, and the founders' stories, at least from what's available, don't carry the biographical weight that makes me willing to bet against the odds. I'm passing, but I'd genuinely like to hear Meer Patel's personal story if our paths cross — there might be something there that the public record doesn't capture.
