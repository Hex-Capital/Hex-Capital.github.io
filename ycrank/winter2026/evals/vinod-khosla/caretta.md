# Caretta -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is the question I keep coming back to with Caretta: if this works perfectly -- if every sales rep in the world has a real-time AI whispering answers during calls -- what changes? Reps close deals faster. Organizations lose fewer deals to unanswered technical questions. Knowledge transfers more efficiently. These are genuine productivity gains. And they are exactly the kind of incremental improvement I tell founders they are too smart to spend their careers pursuing. The consequence of maximum success here is a well-run B2B SaaS company competing in the conversation intelligence space alongside Gong, Salesken, Balto, and Cresta. Nobody's industry gets replaced. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The world doesn't operate differently. The ambition is calibrated to the size of a product feature, not the size of a problem that demands solving.

The competitive dynamic makes this worse, not better. When Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router, that was my invest signal -- the incumbent's dismissal proved the innovation was threatening enough to deny. When I look at Caretta's positioning against Gong, I see the opposite pattern. Gong just raised $250M in Series F at a $7.25B valuation. Their engineering team is not going to look at real-time call assistance and declare it impossible. They are going to look at it and say "we should ship that by Q3." The dossier itself states this plainly: "Real-time speech processing and LLM-powered knowledge retrieval are capabilities accessible to well-funded competitors." The underlying stack -- speech-to-text via NVIDIA's Parakeet model, RAG over internal documents, LLM-powered answer generation -- is commodity infrastructure assembled by a four-person team. There is no technology here that creates structural obsolescence of the incumbent approach. Real-time versus post-call is a feature axis, not a disruption axis.

The bull case would require believing that real-time call assistance is the wedge into something genuinely transformative -- an AI that doesn't assist the sales rep but replaces the sales rep entirely. If Caretta's data flywheel compounds aggressively enough, every call making the system smarter, you could imagine an eventual autonomous sales agent that handles entire customer conversations without human involvement. That would be a genuine transformation of the global sales industry. The timing supports this vision: LLM latency crossed the real-time threshold in 2024-2025, creating a narrow window where a startup could accumulate customer-specific data before incumbents build equivalent features. If the founders articulated this larger ambition -- not "we help reps answer questions" but "we are building the system that makes human sales reps unnecessary" -- I would evaluate the company very differently. But nothing in the dossier suggests this vision. The product is described as surfacing answers for humans, not replacing them. The company has aimed for what everyone agrees would be useful rather than what experts dismiss as impossible.

The founding team is three TU Delft computer science graduates who previously built a Discord bot scaled to 30,000 users. They pivoted from VoysAI to Caretta, which shows iterative learning -- a positive signal. But their composition raises a specific concern for this venture: they are building a sales enablement tool with no one on the team who has sold enterprise software. The critical risk for Caretta is not technology (the stack is well-understood) but distribution -- convincing revenue leaders at B2B organizations to adopt a new tool during live customer calls, which requires navigating procurement, security reviews, and organizational change management. A team of three engineers without sales domain expertise attacking this problem is precisely the kind of mismatch that gene pool engineering is designed to prevent. The team is homogeneous rather than risk-mapped.

I have funded companies where the probability of failure was 90% and the consequence of success was civilization-scale. Caretta has a much higher probability of building a working product -- the technology exists, the market is validated, the problem is real. But they have reduced their failure risk to the point where the consequence of success is, in my view, inconsequential to the scale I invest for. A modestly better sales call tool in a market with four well-funded competitors is not a problem too important not to work on.
