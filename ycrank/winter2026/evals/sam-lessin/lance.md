# Lance -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about AI voice agents for hotels: every VC on Sand Hill Road can look at the stat "65% of hotels report staffing shortages" and draw the obvious line to "let's automate the front desk with AI." That's not an insane idea. That's a sane idea. And sane people with sane ideas produce sane returns — which is another way of saying mediocre ones. Caleb Chan, Gavin Brennen, and Gatik Trivedi are clearly credible. A founding engineer at a 9-figure exit, voice and AI product work at Google and Salesforce, Stanford speech research, a prior YC company, and someone who grew up inside Marriott operations — that's a genuinely strong team. But credibility without contrarianism just means you're well-positioned to fight a war of attrition in a consensus market. Canary Technologies has $180 million and 20,000 hotels. Duve has $85 million. When I back someone, I want the rest of the investor community to think I've lost my mind. Nobody thinks "AI for hotel phones" is crazy. They think it's inevitable — and inevitable means the returns are already being arbitraged away.

The deeper problem is structural. Lance fails my "cherry on top" test in the most direct way possible: remove the AI and there is no business. AI isn't enhancing an already-great operation the way it does for Craftwork, where the painting crews and scheduling logistics exist independently of any software layer. AI *is* Lance. The voice interaction is the product. The "Computer Use Agent" technology — vision-based agents navigating legacy PMS screens — is genuinely clever, but it's a technical workaround for a problem that disappears the moment Oracle Hospitality or Amadeus ships native AI voice handling in their own PMS suites. And those companies have every incentive to do exactly that. Meanwhile, the voice layer itself is being commoditized so fast that OpenAI, ElevenLabs, and Vapi are making it table-stakes infrastructure. Lance is caught between incumbents above (Canary, Oracle) and commodity providers below (voice AI APIs), with a middle layer of "screen-scraping legacy software" as the moat. That's not a moat — it's a temporary integration hack.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement: 50+ hotels across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at pre-seed is legitimately impressive deployment velocity. If Lance can embed deeply enough into daily hotel operations — replacing call handling, coordinating staff dispatch via Lance Pad, becoming the operational nervous system — the switching costs could compound into real lock-in before incumbents move. Gavin Brennen's Marriott background isn't just nice domain knowledge; it's distribution access into an industry where relationships matter more than demos. And the operational execution layer (dispatching towels, booking restaurants, coordinating fulfillment) is genuinely harder to replicate than the voice interaction itself. If I squint, I can see a world where Lance becomes the "operational middleware" for independent hotel groups that can't afford Canary's full suite — a wedge strategy that uses voice as the entry point but builds defensibility through operational depth. The question is whether that operational depth compounds fast enough to matter before the incumbents absorb the category.

I don't think it does. The hotel technology market is structured around large enterprise relationships with slow procurement cycles. Canary can flip on AI voice as an incremental module for its existing 20,000+ hotel customers at near-zero marginal acquisition cost. Lance has to sell property by property or hotel group by hotel group against that incumbent gravity. The Computer Use Agent approach — navigating PMS interfaces visually — introduces a fragility problem that API integrations don't have: every time a PMS vendor updates their UI, Lance's automation can break without warning. That's not a moat; it's a maintenance burden dressed up as differentiation. And the broader dynamic here reminds me of every "vertical AI agent" play I've seen in the last two years: real problem, credible team, working product, and zero structural reason why the category won't be absorbed by either the platform providers (OpenAI, Google) or the vertical incumbents (Canary, Oracle Hospitality) within 18 months.

This is a competently executed company in a consensus category with commodity technology risk and well-funded incumbents. The founders deserve credit for their traction and their team composition. But I invest my own money in non-consensus bets with structural moats that get stronger as software gets cheaper. Lance gets weaker as software gets cheaper — because cheaper AI means every competitor and incumbent can replicate its core value proposition faster. Pass.
