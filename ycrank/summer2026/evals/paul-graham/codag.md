# Codag -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Michael Zhou is a one-word company: an animal. The dossier line that stops me is "bootstrapped Codag until his bank account hit zero, pivoted 5 times, did door-to-door sales" -- door-to-door for a developer log tool, which is the wrong channel by every textbook and therefore the right tell. That single sentence is doing more work in my head than the 42 GitHub stars or the MCP integrations with Claude Code and Codex. But Codag is also team size 1, which is the configuration my own "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) explicitly tells me to avoid, and the pairing of those two facts -- HIGH animal, LOW cofounder -- is the analytical signature of this dossier. This is a lone-hacker bet, not a team bet.

**Airbnb** (2009) is the ladder rung Codag is reaching for on resourcefulness. The mechanism that mattered with Chesky and Gebbia wasn't the cereal margins, it was the willingness to ship revenue via a stunt no investor would have suggested -- the same shape as Michael Zhou turning down seven-figure new-grad offers and then knocking on doors for a Rust log compressor. Where Codag diverges from the **Airbnb** (2009) rung is that the cereal stunt produced actual paying customers ($40 x 1,000+ boxes) before YC, while Codag's dossier shows no revenue figure, only a free tier and $19/$199 price points with no named buyer attached. The animal is real; the receipts from being an animal are not yet on the table. I score this near the top of the dimension anyway, because door-to-door for B2B infra is the exact unscalable improvisation "Do Things that Don't Scale" (July 2013) was written about.

The solo-founder pattern is where I lose money most consistently. **Kiko** (2005) is the rung I keep returning to -- two founders, not one, and even with two it failed and required a re-fund as **Twitch** (2007); the failure mode was not headcount but the absence of someone to argue with at 2am. Codag's YC page lists team size 1 with 0 open jobs, and the pivot-5-times history reads to me like exactly the symptom a missing cofounder produces: nobody in the room with enough standing to say "stop, this one." The dossier offers no named cofounder pipeline, no Z Fellows partner who followed him in, no Shopify or Okta colleague waiting to join. That's not stage-default silence; that's structural.

Drew Houston at **Dropbox** (2007) is the organic-pain anchor I test every dev-tool founder against -- he forgot his USB stick at MIT and built the thing for himself. Codag's founder-market story is one inferential step weaker: Michael Zhou worked at Shopify and Okta, both of which generate enormous log volumes, so the dossier's [Inferred] tag on founder-market fit is doing real work. The five prior pivots from a VSCode workflow visualizer (`michaelzixizhou/codag`, 346 stars) to the current `codag-drain` log compressor (42 stars) tells me the current idea was reached by pattern-matching on "agents need infrastructure," not by Michael Zhou personally suffering inside an agent's context window. Organic ideas don't usually require five tries.

On schlep, the mechanism analysis matters more than the rung-bracketing: Codag's moat compounds only if the template-grouping accuracy improves with private customer log corpora over 18 months, because the underlying Drain3 algorithm is public IBM research and the Codag implementation is itself open-source under `codag-megalith/codag-drain`. Pure-software schleps without a regulatory or physical-world component (which is what **Coinbase** (2012) had with compliance) tend to get bundled -- Datadog and ClickHouse already ship log clustering, and a Datadog product manager who reads the codag.ai homepage can fund this feature internally inside a quarter. The schlep exists but it's the wrong kind: tedious enough to be real, not unpleasant enough to be a moat.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual that would flip Codag from a mid-rung score to a top-rung score is straightforward: if the `codag-drain` repo were at 2,000 stars instead of 42, or if Michael Zhou had a competitive-programming or published-systems-research footprint the way Patrick Collison had his Young Scientist Lisp prize (2005) before **Stripe** (2010), I'd be writing a different paragraph. As-is, Rust at 99.3%, a shipped CLI, an HTTP reference server, and a LogHub-2.0 benchmark with blind-judge evaluation is the work of someone who ships -- just not yet the work of someone whose name appears in other people's READMEs.

My 36-month forecast: Codag does not reach $5M ARR and is acquihired by Datadog, Grafana, or Anthropic for the MCP integration and Michael Zhou personally, at a price between $8M and $25M, sometime in 2028. The mechanism producing this is the signature: animal-grade founder hustle keeps Codag alive and shippable through the first 18 months of pure-software-schlep grind, but solo execution plus an open-source core plus an incumbent-friendly feature set means the exit shape is talent-and-tech, not category-defining. This is the **Kiko** (2005) outcome shape, not the **Stripe** (2010) shape -- and like Kan and Shear, Michael Zhou is exactly the founder I'd re-fund on his next attempt.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **Loopt** (2005) precedent: I funded Sam Altman before mobile location was usable infrastructure, accepting weak idea-stage fit because the founder's animal-test result was decisive. Michael Zhou's bank-to-zero / door-to-door / five-pivots story is structurally the same override signal, and "within two years, agents will be the primary consumer of logs" is the same kind of tailwind bet Altman was making on smartphones. The dismantling: Altman had cofounders Nick Sivo and Alok Deshpande, both technical, both long-relationship -- Codag has zero, and the **Loopt** (2005) exception was an idea-quality override, not a team-configuration override. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Michael Zhou recruits a technical cofounder he has shipped with before (Shopify or Okta colleague, named on the YC page) within six months, and the `codag-drain` repo crosses 500 stars on its own merits. Until then, lean pass, but stay close -- he's the kind of founder whose second company I want first call on.
