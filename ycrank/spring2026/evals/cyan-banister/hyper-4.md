# Hyper -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Hyper is two Berkeley '21 engineers from Matic Robots building "shared memory for humans and AI" against Glean's $200M ARR ? that pairing tells me Hyper has just enough technical adjacency to look credible and almost nothing else my framework rewards. The highest dimension here is the founders' Matic perception-and-autonomy background, which is genuinely sensor-fusion-shaped work. The lowest is contrarian conviction: Hyper is operating in the most consensus-attractive corner of B2B AI in 2026, the corner where Glean just raised at $7.2B and Dust is at $7.3M ARR with 66 people. That high/low pairing makes Hyper a technically-literate consensus bet ? exactly the shape my Pass Example was written about.

On Founder's Biographical Forge, my ladder runs from Substrate's James Proud at the top ? "forged in fire and came out unbroken," a founder whose biography made the semiconductor fight inevitable ? down through Serve Robotics in the middle, where the Postmates operating context made delivery robotics a natural extension, to my Pass Example at the bottom: "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Hyper sits closer to that bottom rung than the middle. Shalin Shah was Autonomy Lead at Matic and Kanyes Thaker was Perception/Motion Planning at Matic and ex-Snorkel ML ? real engineers, but the dossier offers zero evidence that knowledge fragmentation across Notion and Cursor is a problem either of them lived inside. There is no story of being broken by lost context, no biographical reason these two specifically must build the company brain. The March 2026 Product Hunt iOS voice launch followed by a May 2026 cross-tool team-memory pivot reads more like searching for product-market fit than a forge ? when I ask "tell me your story, not the business," the dossier doesn't answer.

On Contrarian Conviction in Hard Spaces, Anduril sits at the top of my ladder ? defense AI when Silicon Valley wouldn't touch it ? with Superplastic in the middle as my Known Exception for genre-defying creative weirdness, and the Pass Example at the bottom: consensus AI in a consensus market. Hyper is at the bottom rung, not between rungs. The dossier itself names Glean ($610M raised), Dust ($21.5M raised), Notion AI, Mem.ai, and Guru as direct competitors, and notes that "AI-driven knowledge management" is a $7.66B?$11.24B market growing 46.7%. There is no room in my chair to shift uncomfortably; this is the room every sophisticated VC is comfortable in. The Superplastic exception doesn't apply ? Hyper is not genre-bending, it's genre-crowded.

Invisible System Made Visible is where my conviction lives, and Hyper barely registers. Uber attacking the medallion cartel and Affirm attacking credit card opacity sit at the top of this ladder; Carta sits in the middle (cap table friction is administrative more than predatory); the bottom rung is any tool serving an already-transparent market. Hyper's claim ? "AI assistants start cold across Notion, Cursor, Claude Code" ? is workflow annoyance for AI-first teams, not an extractive system exploiting ordinary people who don't understand it. There is no hidden tax here. My homeless-teenager radar doesn't ping. On Infrastructure-Grade Defensibility, Flock Safety's compounding camera network anchors the top, Carta's switching costs sit in the middle, and HQ Trivia/IRL with no structural moat sit at the bottom; Hyper sits between Carta and HQ Trivia, leaning toward HQ Trivia, because the dossier explicitly says "No public data found" on moat signals and Glean and Notion AI are already shipping the same wedge with vastly more distribution. On Cascading Impact, Uber expanding transportation access tops the ladder, Postmates' gig flexibility sits in the middle, mainstream productivity SaaS at the bottom; Hyper serves "AI-first teams" ? already-privileged knowledge workers with Cursor and Claude Code seats ? putting it near the bottom rung.

Hyper in 36 months: acqui-hired by Notion or Glean for sub-$50M for the two engineers' ML chops, or quietly dead after a second pivot back toward the iOS voice product. The mechanism is straightforward ? Glean's ARR doubled from $100M to $200M in nine months, and Notion is shipping the external connectors that obviate Hyper's ambient-layer thesis; a two-person team with no proprietary data and no exclusive integrations does not survive that compression unless it builds something Glean can't or won't, and the dossier shows no evidence of that something.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-pass ? meaning the bull case I have to dismantle ? is that Hyper's pivot from iOS voice to cross-tool company brain is exactly the kind of mid-batch iteration that produced Crusoe Energy's Bitcoin-to-AI-infrastructure transition, and that the Matic sensor-fusion background is a real technical edge for fusing signal across heterogeneous tool surfaces. That stings, because I do credit pivot survival. But Crusoe was attacking flared gas in North Dakota oil fields ? a literal invisible extractive system that nobody in Silicon Valley could see ? and the founders had been inside that system. Hyper's "pivot" is between two consensus product shapes inside the same crowded productivity market, and the founders are reading the market rather than reporting from inside a problem. The condition that flips my verdict: a signed enterprise design partner ? a named Fortune 500 logo with a paid contract above $50K ARR ? where Hyper displaces an existing Glean or Notion deployment. That would be evidence that Hyper has found a real wedge that incumbents can't close. Until then, Hyper is the Pass Example written almost word-for-word.
