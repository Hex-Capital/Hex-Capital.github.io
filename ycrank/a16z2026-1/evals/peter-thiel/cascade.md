# Cascade -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Cascade's Head of Growth — ranked #2 sales rep at Procore, responsible for $3M in ARR during 2025 — is the single most interesting fact in this dossier, and it reveals both Cascade's greatest strength and its structural ceiling. Procore is the dominant construction management platform; having its second-best salesperson now selling Cascade into the same customer base is an extraordinary distribution asset for a three-month-old company. $183k in contracted ARR from zero, 15 signed firms including builders of JFK Airport and nuclear reactors, first three clients doubling their spend — this is what solved distribution looks like. But distribution alone produces a profitable vertical SaaS business, not a monopoly. The canonical question remains: what important truth has Cascade discovered that very few people agree with?

The honest answer is that Cascade does not possess a secret. "AEC firms waste weeks finding RFPs across fragmented portals and writing proposals manually" is not a contrarian insight — it is the consensus of every construction technology investor, every Procore customer success manager, and every AEC CTO who has attended an industry conference in the last five years. The thesis that vertical AI tools will outperform horizontal RFP platforms in specialized industries is not wrong, but it is widely held. AutogenAI has raised $65.3M pursuing AI proposals. Joist AI raised $4M specifically for AEC content. Workorb AI raised $2.6M for AEC proposal automation. Inventive.ai raised $4M for general RFP workflows. When I count four funded competitors pursuing materially the same thesis — vertical AI for construction proposals — I am looking at a competitive landscape slide, which is itself the clearest signal that no secret exists. "All failed companies are the same: they failed to escape competition." Cascade's differentiation — Davis-Bacon compliance, Deltek and Ajera ERP integrations, combined discovery plus proposals — amounts to feature advantages, not structural monopoly. Features can be replicated by any competitor with six months and an adequate engineering team.

The comparison that haunts me here is Wish. Not because Cascade is poorly run — the traction suggests the opposite — but because Wish demonstrated what happens when execution quality operates without monopoly structure. Wish grew rapidly in a competitive e-commerce market, IPO'd at $14 billion, then collapsed over 90% because it never had structural defensibility. Cascade operates in a smaller arena, but the dynamic is identical: multiple funded players, no proprietary technology, no network effects where each user makes the platform more valuable for other users, and a "data flywheel" that is really just accumulated customer data behind a login wall. The aspiration to become "the data layer for all construction projects" invokes the Palantir pattern — embed so deeply that switching becomes structurally impossible — but Palantir achieved this by integrating into classified intelligence operations where displacement requires physical-world migration of entire analytical workflows. Cascade integrates with ERPs. These are categorically different switching costs.

Hannia Zia and Joana Ferreira bring credible backgrounds — Zia scaled Google Pay from zero to 100 million users in India, and Ferreira built ML systems for Google Search and Amazon. Their years working together at UnlikelyAI satisfy the shared-prehistory criterion that I consider essential for founding teams. The roadmap — Pre-Bid Intelligence from government meeting minutes, Private Market Signals from permit filings, Bid Leveling, Team Up for joint-venture matching — shows specificity beyond lean-startup iteration. But this is a product roadmap, not a theory of monopoly. A definite founder articulates not just what they will build next quarter but why, when they succeed, no competitor will be able to follow. Cascade's expansion plan extends the platform's surface area without explaining what structural barrier prevents AutogenAI from building identical features with their $65.3M in capital.

The bull case requires believing that vertical depth in AEC creates winner-take-all dynamics — that the first platform to integrate discovery, proposals, compliance, and ERP sync for construction firms will accumulate enough proprietary workflow data to make its AI recommendations unreplicable. The $183k cARR in three months with expansion revenue from early clients is the strongest evidence for this thesis: real AEC firms are paying and paying more, which means the product solves a genuine problem. If Cascade can reach 200-300 mid-to-large AEC firms before competitors build comparable vertical integrations, the accumulated data and embedded workflows could create meaningful switching costs. The Head of Growth's Procore network is a time-limited advantage — but time-limited advantages that produce rapid penetration can become permanent advantages through data accumulation. This is the Ramp pattern: win on distribution speed, then defend on data depth. The question is whether AEC project pursuit generates the kind of compounding data advantage that corporate spend management does, and I am not convinced it does — each firm's proposal history helps that firm, not the platform's other customers.

The power-law calculation eliminates Cascade from my framework regardless. Construction software is an $11.78 billion market growing at 9.7% — respectable but structurally incapable of producing a $10 billion standalone outcome for a pre-bid workflow tool. Cascade occupies a subsegment of a subsegment: AI-assisted project discovery and proposal writing within AEC software. The realistic upside is a $300-500 million acquisition by Procore, Autodesk, or Trimble — a good outcome for founders and a16z, but structurally irrelevant to a portfolio that needs single investments returning billions. This is a pure software layer with no physical-world technology, no hardware component, no regulatory barrier to replication, and no government embedding beyond scraping publicly accessible portals. Cascade is a well-executed vertical SaaS company entering a competitive market without a secret. The distribution is genuinely excellent. Everything else is consensus.
