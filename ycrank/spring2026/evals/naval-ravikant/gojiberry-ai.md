# Gojiberry AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the TrustMRR sale listing at $11.15M, posted March 9, 2026 -- one week after a Product Hunt #1 launch and concurrent with YC S26 participation. A founder who is simultaneously raising venture capital and listing the company for acquisition is sending two signals at once, and they contradict each other. I invest behind people building for decades, not people keeping optionality on a flip. The pattern across all three founders reinforces this: CoCo AI built and sold in roughly two years, Edusign built and sold, MechaChain acquired. Three exits, all small, all fast. That's not compounding -- that's serial arbitrage. Smart, profitable serial arbitrage, but the game they're playing is not the game I invest in. When you invest in a company designed to sell early, you miss out on compound interest. Compound interest is where all the returns are.

The specific knowledge test fails cleanly. Pierre-Eliott came from YouTube and e-commerce. Roman came from Web3 gaming. Dylan built attendance tracking software. None of them lived inside B2B sales intelligence before mid-2025. They started with a CRM auto-fill product, got 2,000 waitlist signups and zero paying customers, then pivoted to intent-based lead gen. That pivot shows excellent market reflexes -- but it's the opposite of deep domain conviction. The insight that small sales teams need affordable intent data is available to anyone who reads a single industry report. What does this team know about the signal-to-conversion mapping in B2B sales that a smart generalist couldn't learn in three months? The dossier offers no evidence of non-transferable knowledge. Their real skill -- bootstrapping SaaS products to $500K+ ARR with minimal spend -- is genuinely impressive and genuinely teachable. An AI agent will be able to run the exact 12-channel distribution playbook Roman documented in that Superframeworks case study.

The leverage architecture is standard SaaS: per-seat pricing at $99 and $249 a month, revenue growing linearly with customer acquisition. There is no network effect, no protocol, no marketplace dynamic where usage by one customer improves the product for others. The claimed "data flywheel" is an inference in the dossier, not a demonstrated reality -- each customer's campaign data is functionally siloed. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question-and-answer pair compounds the knowledge graph for all users, or to Notion, where every template created is leverage for the next user. Gojiberry is a tool, not a platform. It helps individual sales reps do outreach more efficiently, but it doesn't create a new category of leverage that didn't exist before. LinkedIn automation with better signal detection is an incremental improvement on what Apollo, Lemlist, and Instantly already offer, not a structural shift in who can do what.

The bull case deserves a paragraph because the execution metrics are genuinely remarkable. $175K MRR with $380/month total marketing spend and 75% profit margins is one of the most capital-efficient growth stories I've seen at this stage. 44% month-over-month growth with <5% churn and $127 CAC with 1.3-month payback -- those are nearly perfect unit economics. If these numbers hold, the business reaches $10M+ ARR within a year with minimal dilution. The three founders have proven they can build and sell software companies repeatedly. For this to be a great investment in my framework, two things would need to be true: first, that the data flywheel is real and that 1,000+ customers' outreach data creates a compounding signal-to-conversion model that new entrants can't replicate -- turning this from a tool into an intelligence layer. Second, that the sale listing was a calculated anchor for acquisition interest they intend to decline, not a genuine exit signal. If both are true, you have a capital-efficient, high-margin SaaS with a nascent data moat in a growing market. I find the first unlikely given the technical depth of the team, and the second unlikely given the pattern of three prior quick exits.

The timing is pure consensus. Apollo acquired Pocus in March 2026. Clay hit $100M ARR at a $3.1B valuation. Instantly, HeyReach, and Expandi have each crossed $10M ARR bootstrapped. Every investor and every sales leader understands that signal-based outreach is replacing volume-based cold email. Nobody smart is dismissing this category -- they're all funding it. The only angle that approaches contrarian is serving SMBs at $99/month while incumbents charge $25K+, but that's a go-to-market strategy, not a secret. The enterprise players aren't serving SMBs because it's structurally unprofitable for their sales motions, not because they haven't thought of it. That creates a real niche, but niches in consensus categories produce moderate outcomes, not asymmetric ones. I need to be non-consensus and right. Here, I'd be consensus and adequate.

The CTO's background is the final signal worth noting. Dylan Teixeira has a Master's in Digital Marketing and Innovation, is self-taught as a developer, and his GitHub shows 21 repositories with no significant community traction. He built Edusign to 600K users, which demonstrates real building ability. But in my framework, this is the technology business, and I want a technologist whose depth in the core technical problem creates compounding defensibility. LinkedIn signal detection and personalized message generation are well-understood engineering problems -- there's no novel science here, no proprietary model, no technical moat that would take a funded competitor more than a few months to replicate. When Apollo can buy Pocus and integrate signal-based selling into a platform that already has 275M contacts, the application layer gets compressed. You want to be the platform, not the app sitting on someone else's platform.
