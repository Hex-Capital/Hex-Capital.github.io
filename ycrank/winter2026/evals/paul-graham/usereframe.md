# Reframe -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Bryan Zin designed iPhones at Apple. Not the software -- the physical product. Which means he spent years navigating the exact labyrinth Reframe is trying to automate: coordinating overseas vendors, tracking waterfall build schedules, chasing shipping timelines across time zones. That's the single most interesting thing about this company. The question is whether his proximity to the problem was close enough.

There's a distinction I care a lot about between being *in* the problem and being *next to* the problem. Drew Houston didn't observe someone forgetting their USB drive -- he forgot his own USB drive, repeatedly, until it drove him crazy enough to build Dropbox. The Collison brothers didn't watch developers struggle with payments -- they were the developers struggling with payments. Bryan was a product design engineer. He sat adjacent to procurement and supply chain coordination. He saw the emails, the spreadsheets, the delays. But was he the one sending those emails at 2am to a factory in Shenzhen? Was he the one manually reconciling vendor quotes against build matrices? Product design engineers interact with the supply chain machinery, but they don't operate it. That's a meaningful gap. The idea feels informed by genuine experience -- this isn't something conceived by reading a McKinsey report on procurement inefficiency -- but it's one degree removed from the visceral, daily pain that produces the strongest founder-problem bonds.

The schlep here, though, is real and substantial. International hardware procurement coordination is exactly the kind of problem smart founders unconsciously avoid. It involves dealing with factories in different time zones, navigating cultural communication norms, understanding sequential dependencies in build schedules where a two-day delay on one component cascades through the entire production timeline, and negotiating pricing with suppliers who have decades of experience extracting margin. Nobody wakes up excited to build this. That's actually a good sign -- the ugliness of the problem is a natural barrier that keeps the competitive field thinner than it would otherwise be. Procure AI raised $13M but they're horizontal, covering all procurement categories. Building something that truly understands hardware build matrices and waterfall scheduling is specific enough that broad platforms won't prioritize it.

On the technical side, Eric Wiener is clearly a builder. Fifty-nine repos on GitHub, published PyTorch work on point cloud denoising, ML engineering experience at Zoox and DoorDash Labs. He's not a resume engineer -- he's someone who writes and ships code. For an AI agent product, having someone with deep ML engineering experience building the core system matters. The pairing of Eric's ML depth with Bryan's hardware domain knowledge is structurally sound. This is the right kind of team composition for this problem. What I can't assess is whether they've actually built anything yet. The website is a headline. No prototype, no demo, no evidence of a working system. At pre-seed through YC, a bare website isn't unusual, but it means I'm evaluating potential rather than execution.

The bull case for Reframe goes like this: hardware manufacturing coordination is a genuine schlep that incumbent procurement platforms ignore because it's too narrow for their horizontal ambitions. LLMs in 2025 finally reached the capability threshold to handle multi-turn vendor negotiations over email -- a task that was impossible even two years ago. Bryan's Apple supply chain experience gives him pattern recognition for what good coordination looks like, and Eric can build the agent infrastructure. If they start with consumer electronics companies managing overseas production -- a narrow wedge where Bryan has direct network access -- they could accumulate proprietary data on supplier pricing and reliability that compounds into a real moat. The best version of this story looks like vertical SaaS that gets smarter with every build cycle it manages, eventually becoming indispensable to its customers' operations. That's a compelling outcome.

But I keep coming back to a concern. The framing -- "AI agents to automate X" -- has become the most common startup pitch pattern of 2025. When I see a company where one founder has domain experience and the other has ML skills, and they combine those into "AI agents for [domain founder's previous industry]," I want to make sure the idea was discovered rather than assembled. There's a version of this story where Bryan genuinely couldn't stop thinking about how broken hardware procurement was after leaving Apple, and the AI agent capability breakthrough gave him the tool to finally fix it. And there's another version where two talented people decided to start a company, looked at what each of them brought to the table, and reverse-engineered an idea at the intersection. I don't have enough signal to distinguish between these two stories, and that's what holds me back from stronger conviction. No users, no revenue, no LOIs, no visible prototype -- just the idea and the team credentials. The credentials are good. But credentials aren't the same as the kind of obsessive, can't-not-work-on-this energy that produces the best companies.
