# Lamina Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Lamina Labs' dossier itself identifies the core problem: the approach of prompt-to-educational-animation is "achievable by combining open-source Manim with commercial LLMs." That sentence, buried in the commoditization risk section, is the entire evaluation. When the dossier documenting a company concedes that the technical approach can be replicated with open-source tools and commercial APIs, I'm looking at a wrapper business. TMA.live is doing it. Knowlify raised $3M and already has Google and Amazon teams as customers. Leap MCP is doing it. The 11-second generation speed that @aaronsiim praised on X is engineering polish, not a technology discontinuity. Rocket Lab didn't build a faster way to book launches on existing rockets — it invented electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers. Lamina Labs is building a faster way to call Manim.

Kartikesh Mishra's MIT CSAIL research under Daniela Rus on drone natural language command-following and his 3DAttnVAE-GAN project demonstrate genuine technical capability. Sudip Rokaya leaving MIT to build Lamina Labs shows conviction. These are smart, capable founders — exactly the kind of people I'd want to see tackling a problem worth tackling. But Lamina Labs has them building an AI copilot for a specific professional vertical: educational content creators. I see this pitch dozens of times a batch. AI for accountants. AI for recruiters. AI for animators. The pitch is always identical: large TAM, underserved by technology, we automate the workflow. Lamina Labs' TAM slide says $5.88B in AI education growing to $32.27B — but their actual serviceable market, generative AI in EdTech, is $530M. Even capturing that entire market produces a moderately successful SaaS company, not a world-changing outcome. I don't invest to make 3-5x.

The bull case deserves construction: 3Blue1Brown has 6M+ YouTube subscribers proving explosive demand for visual mathematical education. Both Lamina Labs founders are immigrants — Mishra and Rokaya both came to MIT from South Asia — fitting my revealed preference for outsider founders. If Lamina Labs genuinely democratizes 3Blue1Brown-quality visual explanation, every teacher in every developing country could instantly produce world-class animated lessons. Education access could meaningfully improve for billions. That's a consequence worth funding. The DoorDash exception applies here — Tony Xu transformed food access without novel technology, and the consequence magnitude overrode my technical novelty requirement. Could Lamina Labs be the DoorDash of education content? The answer is no, because the analogy breaks at the causal chain. DoorDash's consequence was direct: order food, food arrives. Lamina Labs' consequence requires three links: teacher uses tool → creates better video → students learn more effectively. And the bottleneck in global education has never been animation quality. It's access to schools, teacher training, curriculum design, internet connectivity. Making whiteboard videos faster is a nice productivity gain. It doesn't make education free or universally accessible the way Curai Health aims to make primary care free or Commonwealth Fusion aims to make energy limitless.

Knowlify's $3M seed round and enterprise customers (Lamina Labs has neither public revenue nor named customers) represent a more immediate competitive threat than the theoretical commoditization risk. Lamina Labs is entering a space where a better-funded YC S25 company already has traction, and the product has already pivoted once (Pictor to Simi), suggesting ongoing product-market fit exploration rather than locked-in conviction. The "deterministic, mathematically precise" claim is Lamina Labs' primary differentiation against accuracy-challenged competitors like TMA.live, but no public evidence validates this claim — no benchmark, no published comparison, no user testimony beyond a single X post about speed. At pre-seed I don't penalize absent traction metrics, but I do need to see a technology wedge that couldn't be replicated in a weekend hackathon, and Lamina Labs hasn't shown one.

The founders' technical depth — Mishra's computer vision research, Rokaya's prior startup experience with Harmonic — scores reasonably on learning capacity, but I see no evidence of the rapid adaptation I prioritize. The product rename and tagline evolution could indicate iteration, but equally could indicate uncertainty. No evidence of what surprised them, what they learned during YC, or how their thinking evolved. For a team with MIT CSAIL pedigree, they're domain experts applying domain knowledge — the opposite of my preferred outsider pattern. The ambition level is the decisive gap: "visual infrastructure for EdTech AI applications" is a competent product category, not an improbable moonshot. Nobody hears that description and says "that can't be done." Compare that to Hermeus (Mach 5 passenger aircraft) or Mach Industries (a teenager competing with Lockheed Martin). Lamina Labs is attempting something achievable and incremental in a competitive space. The consequence of success is a useful tool, not a different world. I'd rather lose my money on something where success rewrites the rules.
