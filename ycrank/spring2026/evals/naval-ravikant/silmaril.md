# Silmaril -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Five AI security companies acquired for $1.4B+ combined in eighteen months — Protect AI for $500M by Palo Alto Networks, Robust Intelligence for $400M by Cisco, Prompt Security for $180M by SentinelOne, Lakera by Check Point, CalypsoAI for $180M by F5. Every single one of those founding teams is now an agent inside a corporate structure. Their innovation velocity just dropped by an order of magnitude. Silmaril walks onto a field that five competitors just vacated — two founders, no board to report to, no integration roadmap consuming their engineering bandwidth. The principal-agent dynamic here mirrors what made Perplexity's timing against Google so compelling: it's not that the incumbents lack the capability, it's that their internal structure prevents them from deploying it with founder-speed. Palo Alto's newly acquired Protect AI team is now navigating procurement processes and platform integration requirements instead of shipping adaptive classifiers. Silmaril's two principals can iterate faster than five agent-teams combined.

Aum Upadhyay's claim of building AWS's security and privacy framework — the system that "prevented over $1.8B in damages" — maps directly to Silmaril's core product. This isn't adjacent expertise. Building cloud-scale security infrastructure at the world's largest cloud provider is specific knowledge that would take a competitor years at AWS to accumulate. Eduardo Velasco's low-latency ML models generating $400M in annual revenue at Amazon explain the 20ms latency overhead claim — production ML at that speed requires intuitions about model architecture, quantization, and inference optimization that come only from shipping fast models at scale. Together, these two cover both halves of Silmaril's value proposition: Aum knows what attacks look like at cloud scale, Eduardo knows how to classify them in twenty milliseconds. The knowledge is real and directly productized. Where it falls short of the highest tier: this is operator knowledge earned inside a large organization, not the kind of obsessive independent exploration that Aravind Srinivas brought to search. If both founders disappeared, another pair of senior Amazon engineers with similar tenures could plausibly reconstruct the core approach. The specific knowledge creates a head start, not an unbridgeable gap.

Silmaril's leverage architecture is where the investment case gets structurally interesting. The autonomous red-teaming agents that continuously probe customer applications generate proprietary attack data — each deployment creates training signal that improves the classifier for all customers. This is a data flywheel that compounds, not a static model that degrades. "Simulations and nested RL environments" for synthetic attack generation means Silmaril trains its own classifier on data no competitor can access — they're manufacturing their own training set, not scraping the internet. The 5-line SDK integration into LangGraph is permissionless distribution: any developer can adopt without a procurement cycle. Code leverage plus data compounding is the combination I look for. The constraint: this is still enterprise B2B, which means the distribution eventually requires sales teams and the leverage gets diluted by human-intensive GTM motions. Silmaril's approach — blocking novel attack patterns "in under an hour" through self-healing — creates switching costs that accumulate over time, but the 5-line integration cuts both ways: lightweight to adopt, lightweight to remove.

The principal authenticity signal is where Silmaril leaves me wanting more. Both founders left Amazon — good jobs, good compensation — to build this. Their shared Amazon tenure suggests a real working relationship, not a startup-weekend matchmaking. The "$2.2B in combined impact" framing and the customer testimonial claiming "8 breaches prevented, $28M in damages blocked" in two months show they're shipping real product. But I see no evidence that either founder was working on AI security problems independently before Silmaril — no prior open-source AI security tools, no blog posts dissecting prompt injection taxonomies, no community presence in the AI safety space. GreenDragon at 10 GitHub stars was built as part of Silmaril, not before it. The company reads as two competent operators identifying a market opportunity and applying their Amazon expertise to it. That's a good company. It's not the same as a founder who was already building this because they couldn't stop thinking about it.

Two technical founders shipping a product with documented customer impact — that's the team-to-value leverage ratio I want to see. Silmaril's two-person team has built autonomous red-teaming agents, an RL-based training pipeline, a multihead classifier, and an SDK integration layer. Eduardo's ML background means they're not hiring ML engineers; Aum's security background means they're not hiring security consultants. The AI-native development approach — using RL environments to generate training data rather than manually labeling attack examples — is exactly the kind of force multiplication that lets two people do the work of twenty. Cursor, Midjourney, Bolt.new — the pattern is consistent. Silmaril fits it.

The bear case is real and I won't dismiss it. LLM providers are investing heavily in native safety — if Anthropic or OpenAI ships effective built-in prompt injection defense, Silmaril's value proposition narrows. The 99% detection rate claim lacks independent verification, and security professionals in the YC LinkedIn comments flagged "validation methodology and deterministic reproducibility" concerns. The five acquirers now have AI security bundled into platform suites with established enterprise sales channels — CISOs prefer fewer vendors. Silmaril's SAM may be low single-digit billions in a market where incumbents have already paid $1.4B to own the category. But here's why the thesis holds: enterprise deployments are multi-model and multi-framework. A vendor-neutral defense layer retains value in heterogeneous environments where no single LLM provider controls the stack. The shift from single-turn chatbots to multi-agent workflows with MCP, tool calling, and confused-deputy attack patterns creates an attack surface that model-level safety cannot address — the vulnerabilities live at the application layer, not the model layer. Silmaril's execution-state monitoring approach ("does this execution lead to a harmful outcome?" rather than "does this input look malicious?") is architecturally suited to this shift in ways that acquired-and-integrated pattern-matching guardrails are not.

Silmaril earns an invest from me on the strength of two dimensions: specific knowledge directly productized into the company's core engine, and a two-person team with leverage architecture that compounds through autonomous data generation. The market timing — post-acquisition vacuum plus the explosion of agentic AI creating novel attack surfaces — is a genuine window. What would flip me to pass: if the next two quarters show LLM providers shipping native multi-agent security controls that match Silmaril's detection rates, or if the 99% detection claim fails independent benchmarking. What keeps me in: two principals with deep Amazon expertise moving at founder speed against five agent-teams buried inside corporate integration roadmaps. You get paid for being right first. The window is open and Silmaril is running through it.
