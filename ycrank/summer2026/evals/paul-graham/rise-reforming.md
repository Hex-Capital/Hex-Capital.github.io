# Rise Reforming -- Paul Graham Evaluation

George Rose and his two UChicago Molecular Engineering co-founders are running toward a real schlep — containerized reforming units at wastewater plants, catalyst chemistry, 1,800+ hours of syngas runs — but Rise Reforming is not a company anyone in this trio started because they personally couldn't stand something in their own life. That is the signature. High on schlep, low on organic origin. It is the profile of competent hard-tech students who picked a large industrial pain to point their credentials at, not the profile of Drew Houston forgetting his USB stick.

Coinbase (2012) is the ladder rung I keep circling on the schlep dimension, because Brian Armstrong's willingness to eat compliance grind became the moat — nobody else in that YC batch wanted to talk to state money-transmitter regulators. Rise Reforming is doing chemical-industry work of a similar unpleasantness class: exclusive biogas supply agreements with wastewater and agricultural sites, a pilot at a Chicagoland wastewater plant, catalyst coking risk on biogas-derived syngas, a $5M conditional DME offtake that has to survive real engineering. The load-bearing part of the Coinbase analogy is that the grind itself keeps softer founders away. Where Rise Reforming diverges is that Emvolon has already raised $10.5M and is raising up to $30M Series A into the same modular biogas-to-methanol schlep, plus DOE grants near $8.3M and a Montauk Renewables JV — the schlep is not routing competitors around it the way Coinbase's compliance moat did. The unpleasant work is real; the exclusion effect is weaker than I need.

Dropbox (2007) is the organic-idea rung Rise Reforming most conspicuously fails to reach. Houston had personally lost his USB stick at MIT; the product was a note-to-self before it was a company. Rise Reforming's founders are UChicago undergrads whose YC pitch reads "US chemical industry is centralized and fossil-dependent, exposed by shocks like Winter Storm Uri" — that is a market-thesis sentence, not a scar. The tell is sharper: earlier UChicago and 776 Foundation coverage describes Rise as "mixed post-industrial plastic waste into carbon-negative DME," and the S26 pitch is biogas-to-DME/methanol. Founders who pivoted their feedstock class between undergraduate coverage and YC are following opportunity, not pain. That is not disqualifying — Airbnb (2009) also wasn't personal pain — but it removes the strongest form of conviction I typically lean on.

The animal-resourcefulness signal at Rise Reforming compounds moderately over 18 months but does not accelerate. George Rose won $50K at DOE EnergyTech UP 2024, raised a prior $400K from UChicago sources, and picked up a 776 Foundation Climate Fellowship — that is more pre-funding motion than most pre-seed hardware teams show, and it is the same institutional-prize path Sam Altman rode at Loopt (2005). But prize circuits are not cereal boxes. What compounds Airbnb-style is a stunt no other founder would have thought of; Rise Reforming's stack is legible achievement in a well-lit prize funnel. Eighteen months out, this animal-signature funds the next round but does not produce the "we sold cereal at $40 a box" story that flips a skeptical partner meeting.

Hacker genealogy at Rise Reforming would flip a tier only if one of the three co-founders had shipped a non-trivial open-source combustion simulator, published in the Gagliardi Group's computational catalysis line as first author, or built something outside the credential path that a stranger on Hacker News would recognize. Lucas Zubillaga Maharg was in the Gagliardi Group and interned at Argonne, Jona van Oord was in the Patel Lab — these are strong lab credentials, but no GitHub trail, no X presence, no artifact that reads as hacker-signature rather than research-assistant-signature. Chemistry hackers exist; the dossier does not surface one here.

Reddit (2005) is the single portfolio parallel for the co-founder configuration. Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates who had spent years in each other's company before the check; Rise Reforming has three UChicago Molecular Engineering undergrads who co-founded as students and grew through the same lab ecosystem. That is a real long-relationship signal on the co-founder axis — not a hackathon pair. The team of 4 with three technical co-founders sharing the same academic origin is close to the pattern I want, distinct from the founder-shopping pattern I avoid.

Rise Reforming in 36 months is alive but subscale — $2-6M ARR from two to four deployed containerized units, still raising against Emvolon's Series A/B lead, most likely path is a strategic investment or minority acquisition from Topsoe or Thyssenkrupp rather than an independent breakout, because modular hardware in a capital-intensive segment doesn't compound at software rates and the pipeline ARR gap ($14M in PitchBook vs. $92M in the YC description) will reconcile downward when MOUs meet binding-contract friction. The forecast is consistent with the signature: legitimate schlep, credentialed team, no founder-pain flywheel — that shape produces a real company that gets outraced, not a zero and not a Stripe.

The strongest objection is the Airbnb (2009) exception itself. I mentally rejected Airbnb before the cereal boxes changed the conversation, and Rise Reforming has its own version — the $50K DOE prize, the 776 Fellowship, seven supply agreements, a conditional $5M DME offtake — that a partner more sympathetic to hard tech would read as the animal signature I am undercounting because it wears a lab coat instead of a Y Combinator hoodie. The bull case is: this is what schlep-blindness in climate looks like, and I am the one blinded. The reason I don't take the exception: Airbnb's cereal-box moment was a stunt no professional investor would have designed; Rise Reforming's motion is entirely inside a well-lit prize-and-fellowship funnel that hundreds of hard-tech undergrads walk through each year. The verdict flips if Rise Reforming converts at least three of the seven biogas supply MOUs into binding revenue-generating contracts with the containerized unit actually operating at the Chicagoland wastewater pilot within 12 months — that would be the equivalent artifact, and I would re-underwrite.
