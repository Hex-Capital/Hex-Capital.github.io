# 21st -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the uncomfortable truth about 21st.dev: this company is building software tools for people who are building software, in a world where I have been publicly arguing for two years that software moats are dissolving faster than anyone wants to admit. "Infrastructure and UI building blocks for the agentic internet" is not an insane idea — it might be the single most consensus pitch in Y Combinator's Winter 2026 batch. When I hear "agentic internet infrastructure," my immediate question isn't "can this team build it?" but "how many other teams are building the exact same thing right now, and which incumbent with real distribution is going to absorb this functionality in six months?" Vercel already launched AI Elements — open-source React components for AI interfaces, built on shadcn/ui, with an AI SDK doing 20M+ monthly downloads. That's not a hypothetical competitive threat. That's an existential one.

The founders clear the "sane" bar. Serafim has two prior acquisitions and built Via Protocol to $1.5B in gross transaction volume — that's real. Sergey shipped at Deel. They've launched 9 products in 10 months, which demonstrates execution velocity that's genuinely impressive. But here's where my framework diverges from conventional analysis: the "insane" half of the equation is completely absent. Developer tooling for AI agents is the hottest category in tech. Every VC wants exposure to this narrative. When every investor thinks something is a great idea, the non-consensus premium has been arbitraged away and you're buying narrative at market price. The 9-products-in-10-months pattern also concerns me — it reads less like focused conviction and more like searching for traction across a sprawling surface. Compare that to Anatoly Yakovenko spending years focused exclusively on Solana's proof-of-history mechanism during crypto winter, when everyone thought he was nuts. That's conviction in a single structural insight. Four distinct product surfaces with three people is not conviction — it's optionality anxiety.

The AI kill test is where this falls apart for me. My "cherry on top" framework asks: would this business be compelling if AI didn't exist? Strip away AI and you have a React component registry competing with npm, which has millions of packages. Strip away AI and there's no Magic MCP, no Agents SDK, no 1Code. The business doesn't just get worse without AI — it evaporates. That's the exact opposite of what I look for. Craftwork is a great painting company that happens to use software. 21st.dev is a software company that happens to exist because of AI. When the next model generation makes UI generation trivially better inside every IDE natively, the component registry's value proposition narrows. When Cursor and Windsurf build their own agent orchestration layers — which they will, because that's core to their product — 1Code's value proposition narrows. The defensibility analysis in the dossier acknowledges this directly: "no structural barrier identified at this stage prevents a well-resourced incumbent from building a comparable full-stack offering."

The strongest bull case I can construct: the 200K MAU and 1.4M developer community represent a genuine network effect that could compound into a standard. If 21st.dev becomes to agent UIs what shadcn/ui became to general React development — the default component vocabulary — that's a real primitive position. The MCP ecosystem is genuinely nascent, and early positioning in new protocol standards has produced massive returns historically (this is the Helium pattern — showing up years before the market validates). If the team can consolidate the fragmented agent app stack into a single coherent platform before incumbents do, the switching costs could become real. For this bull case to work, though, you need to believe that (a) MCP becomes a durable standard rather than being supplanted by whatever Google or Microsoft propose, (b) Vercel doesn't just integrate all of this into its existing platform, and (c) a 3-person team can maintain four product surfaces against well-capitalized competitors. I don't believe any of those three things with enough conviction to write a check.

The protocol position analysis seals the pass for me. 21st.dev is building ON someone else's protocol (MCP belongs to Anthropic), not creating one. They orchestrate other companies' coding agents (Claude Code, Codex), not their own. They use shadcn/ui as a foundation layer, not their own component primitive. At every layer of the stack, they're an application sitting on top of infrastructure controlled by others. My biggest returns — Solana, Aleo, Helium, Lightning Labs — came from betting on teams that were creating the primitive itself, not building tools on top of someone else's primitive. When you're the protocol, you capture compounding network effects. When you're an application on someone else's protocol, you're one API change away from irrelevance.
