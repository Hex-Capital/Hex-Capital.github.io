# qomplement -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what concerns me before anything else: the competitors in this space — Nanonets pulling $100M in revenue, Reducto raising $108M, Rossum at $44.9M in revenue — are *already* processing the same documents. The distinction between "extracting data from a PDF" and "filling data into a PDF" is a product feature, not a company. Any of these funded incumbents could ship a filling module in a sprint. When I look at a company, I want to see a structural reason the first mover wins — a reason that compounds, that gets harder to replicate with each customer. I don't see that here. The workflow builder and database connectors create some switching friction, but that's stickiness, not a moat. I've been burned by this before — products that feel indispensable to early users but have no structural barrier to a well-funded competitor absorbing the functionality into their existing platform.

When I ask my first question — tell me the founder's story, not the business — the dossier gives me a CS degree from Tec de Monterrey, a prior startup in nearshoring, and a stint at Contalink, a YC S21 accounting fintech. That's professional exposure to document workflows, and I respect it. But professional exposure is not biographical inevitability. I don't hear a story where this problem *found* these founders — I hear a story where smart engineers noticed a gap in the market and built a product to fill it. That's career optimization, not a forge. The CTO appears to have no prior ventures or public technical footprint. Two classmates from the same university starting their first meaningful venture together — I've seen this pattern many times, and it doesn't give me the signal I need. I want founders who would be working on this problem even if no one would fund them.

The space itself is the opposite of contrarian. Document AI is one of the most actively funded categories in enterprise software right now. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "AI for documents" at a dinner party — they nod and ask about your Series A. The local-first deployment angle is technically interesting, and the Latin American enterprise focus creates some geographic differentiation, but these are positioning choices within a consensus market, not a contrarian thesis that requires courage to articulate. When I backed Flock Safety, people questioned the ethics of surveillance. When I backed Contraline, VCs physically recoiled. The feeling I get from document filling is... comfortable. And comfortable has never been where my returns come from.

I want to give the bull case its due, because there's something here. The "filling" side of document workflows genuinely is underserved — everyone chased extraction because it's the more technically glamorous problem, and millions of operations workers are still manually copying data into PDF forms and spreadsheet templates. The transport client automating 10,000+ invoices monthly and saving $15,000 a month is a real signal that this solves a real pain point. Kerim's quote about making AI accessible for Latin American companies without system replacements resonates with the part of me that believes every investment should expand access. If the team can build deep enough workflow integrations that customers wire their operations around qomplement's triggers and connectors, and if they can move fast enough that extraction vendors don't simply absorb this as a feature — then you have a capital-efficient wedge into a massive market with a geographic head start. That's the path to something meaningful.

But I keep coming back to what's missing from my framework. There's no invisible system being disrupted here. Manual document filling is tedious and expensive, but it's not opaque or extractive — nobody is being exploited by the fact that forms need to be filled out. Compare that to how Affirm exposed the hidden cost structure of credit cards, or how Ownwell revealed that property tax assessments are systematically inflated. Those companies revealed something that was deliberately hidden from ordinary people. Document filling is a workflow problem, not a power-asymmetry problem. And when I run the ten-year imagination exercise — where does this technology end up in a decade? — I don't see infrastructure that transforms what's possible. I see a feature that gets absorbed into the platforms enterprises already use. Microsoft's Power Platform already offers some form-filling capability. This isn't a Long Journey bet.
