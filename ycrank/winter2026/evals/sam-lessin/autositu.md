# AutoSitu -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about AutoSitu is the competitive density surrounding it -- PermitFlow with $91M raised, CivCheck acquired by Clariti, CodeComply.Ai, Archistar with $6.7M in revenue, Blitz AI deployed across five states. Seattle's mayor signed an executive order directing AI review of development applications. Austin launched a pilot. This is not an insane idea. This is a hot market with capital flooding in and government mandates accelerating adoption. When the mayor of a major city is signing executive orders telling departments to use your category of product, the non-consensus premium has been fully arbitraged away. You're buying narrative at market price.

The founders are credibly sane -- Xuanshu Lin brings genuine domain depth from Harvard GSD and hands-on urban planning work, and George Zhai has production AI chops from GM and McLaren with 7 patents. That's a well-composed team for this specific problem. The domain-technical pairing is exactly what you'd want. But "sane person, sane idea" is just a competent team in a consensus market. The magic of the framework is in the COMBINATION -- Brian Armstrong was sane, but building a regulated crypto exchange in 2012 was genuinely insane. Building AI for municipal plan review in 2026, when every city planning director has already read the LinkedIn thought pieces about it, is not insane. It's obvious.

Now let me run the AI kill test, and here's where it gets uncomfortable for AutoSitu: AI IS the product. Strip away the LLM-based document analysis and there is nothing left. No physical infrastructure, no painting crews, no parking hardware, no wireless protocol. The dossier itself admits "the core technology (LLM-based document analysis against regulatory text) is reproducible." The claimed moat is jurisdiction-specific regulatory data accumulated across 1,500+ plan reviews. That's real but fragile -- every competitor is accumulating the same kind of data in overlapping jurisdictions, and the next generation of models will be better at cold-reading regulatory text without needing that training data. The workflow integration switching costs in government are genuine, but they protect whoever gets there first in each municipality, which could just as easily be Blitz AI or Archistar or an Accela acquisition. This is the Birchbox lesson applied to GovTech: the data never compounds into genuine lock-in when the underlying capability is reproducible.

The strongest bull case I can construct: municipal procurement is ugly, slow, and relationship-dependent in exactly the way that creates operational moats software-focused VCs undervalue. Once you're embedded in San Jose's cross-department workflow, you're sticky for years. Government contracts renew. The 11+ named city engagements at pre-seed with a two-person team is legitimately impressive distribution velocity -- most GovTech startups struggle to get one pilot in their first year. If AutoSitu can lock up enough Tier 1 cities before the market consolidates, the switching costs become their moat regardless of technical reproducibility. That's the Teamshares playbook -- operationally unglamorous, institutionally embedded, accumulating advantage through execution rather than technology. If I squint, I can see it. But Teamshares' moat is owning actual businesses with real cash flows. AutoSitu's moat is a software integration into a government workflow -- and I've been very clear about what I think happens to software moats.

AutoSitu doesn't create a new primitive that other businesses build on. It's an application layer -- a tool that processes documents against regulatory databases. There's no protocol position, no compounding network effect where the product becomes infrastructure for an ecosystem. Other companies don't get better when AutoSitu succeeds. The narrative frame of "regulatory infrastructure for the built world" gestures at something bigger, but the actual product is a compliance checking tool, and the expansion path (more jurisdictions, more document types) is linear, not exponential. I don't see the infinity story here -- I see a solid vertical SaaS business in a category where the word "SaaS" should make everyone nervous.
