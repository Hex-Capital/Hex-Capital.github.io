# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The counter-drone market in 2026 is a textbook case of mimetic convergence. Five funded competitors. A publicly traded company doing $216 million in annual revenue with 276% year-over-year growth. A $127 million acquisition by Axon. Over a billion dollars in counter-drone contracts closing in a single month. Every defense minister, every airport authority, every venture capitalist on Earth has watched the same footage from Ukraine and drawn the same conclusion: drones are a threat, detection is needed, money will be spent. When I see unanimous agreement among intelligent people that a market is large and urgent, my first instinct is not excitement -- it is suspicion. Consensus means there is no secret, and a market without a secret is a market where no one achieves monopoly.

So what is Milliray's secret? The implied thesis is that existing detection systems operate at fundamentally wrong frequencies, and that 94 GHz millimeter-wave radar is the only approach capable of reliably detecting nano-scale drones that slip past conventional systems. This is a genuine technical claim with supporting physics -- higher frequencies yield finer resolution on small radar cross-sections, and the CEO has published peer-reviewed work demonstrating micro-Doppler signatures at exactly this frequency band. But a technical trade-off within well-understood physics is not a secret in the sense that matters for investment. No serious radar engineer would argue against the resolution advantages of millimeter-wave frequencies; they would argue about the trade-offs -- shorter range, greater atmospheric attenuation, higher component costs. The debate is about engineering optimization, not about a truth the world actively rejects. Robin Radar extended its detection range from 5 km to 12 km with a software upgrade. Blighter already detects nano drones at 3.6 km. The question is not whether 94 GHz radar is better on one specific dimension, but whether that dimension is enough to create a monopoly. I do not see the mechanism by which it does. DroneShield, Robin Radar, Dedrone, Blighter, and Fortem are all converging on the same problem from different technical angles, and several have meaningful operational deployments. This is a Red Ocean dressed in defense camouflage.

The strongest case for Milliray follows the early Palantir pattern: three PhD-holding founders with deep, domain-specific technical expertise, institutional backing from NATO DIANA and the UK Ministry of Defence's DASA, and a beachhead strategy targeting European sovereign defense customers who increasingly want alternatives to American systems. The CEO, Matthew Moore, did not stumble into this market -- his doctoral research at St Andrews was literally the construction and testing of 94 GHz Doppler radar systems for drone detection. That level of founder-technology alignment is uncommon and deserves recognition. The COO's automated reasoning background maps cleanly onto the classification problem of distinguishing drones from birds using micro-Doppler signatures. If there were no competitors -- if this team had identified a detection gap that nobody else was pursuing -- I would be significantly more interested.

But the bull case requires several things to be true simultaneously. First, that nano-drones become the dominant threat vector, making mmWave resolution the decisive capability rather than a nice-to-have supplement. Second, that incumbents with orders of magnitude more capital and existing customer relationships cannot add millimeter-wave capabilities through acquisition or internal R&D within the 18-24 months it would take Milliray to establish production contracts. Third, that a three-person academic spin-out can navigate defense procurement cycles that routinely stretch beyond two years, competing against companies with dedicated BD teams and existing vendor qualifications. And fourth -- this one concerns me most -- that the CTO, Thomas Doherty, is fully committed. His LinkedIn lists him as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, an ion trap quantum computing venture. Running two deep-tech companies as one of three founders is not a sign of the singular, definite conviction I look for. When Musk was building SpaceX, he was not simultaneously running an unrelated quantum computing company.

The small-market entry strategy is actually the strongest element here from my framework's perspective. Starting with NATO DIANA and DASA as institutional beachheads, targeting the specific gap of nano-drone detection for European sovereign defense customers -- that is a narrow enough niche to potentially dominate before expanding. The European desire for non-American defense technology is a structural tailwind that creates a genuine opening for a UK-based provider. If Milliray can secure even one meaningful deployment contract through these channels, the switching costs inherent in defense procurement would create real stickiness. But "if" is doing enormous work in that sentence. The company describes itself as "deployment-ready" with no disclosed customers or revenue.

The fundamental problem remains: Milliray is bringing a technically superior sensor to a market that is already crowded, well-funded, and operating on a consensus thesis about the importance of drone defense. The technology is real, the founders are technically credentialed, and the beachhead strategy is sound -- but none of these constitute a secret about how the world works that most smart people disagree with. This is an engineering company competing in a hot market, not a monopoly built on a contrarian truth. I have invested in defense technology -- Palantir, Anduril, General Matter -- but in each case, the founders saw something about how the defense establishment should work that the establishment itself rejected. Milliray sees what everyone sees, and proposes to build a better version of what everyone is already building.
