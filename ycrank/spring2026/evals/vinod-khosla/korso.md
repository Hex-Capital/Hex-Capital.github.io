# Korso -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is a company building AI-powered quoting automation for manufacturers -- and my own fund already wrote a $6 million seed check to Uptool, which does effectively the same thing. Paperless Parts has $30 million in Series B funding. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all building variations. When five-plus funded startups converge on the same workflow automation opportunity using the same broadly available LLM technology, you are not looking at a contrarian bet. You are looking at consensus. And consensus, in my experience, produces a crowded market where everyone fights for incremental share and nobody builds something transformational. The experts and incumbents aren't dismissing this approach -- they're racing to build it. That's exactly the wrong signal.

Let me ask the question I always ask first: if Korso succeeds completely -- captures the entire quote-to-order automation market for manufacturers -- what happens? Manufacturers process RFQs faster. Quotes go out in minutes instead of hours. Purchase orders get tracked automatically. And then what? The manufacturing industry continues to operate exactly as it does today, except the back-office workflow is more efficient. Nobody's food gets cheaper. No carbon leaves the atmosphere. No industry becomes structurally obsolete. The consequence of success is a solid B2B SaaS business. I have nothing against solid SaaS businesses -- they just don't justify my attention as an investor. I tell founders regularly: you are too smart to spend your careers making quoting 30% faster for machine shops. Go find a problem where success means the world operates differently.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Manufacturing is a massive sector, and the "intelligence layer" framing suggests Korso's ambition extends beyond quoting into a platform for all manufacturing operations. If they could genuinely become the operating system for how manufacturers run -- not just how they quote -- that's a different magnitude of consequence. The 35% AI adoption rate in procurement suggests the window is early, and the LLM capability threshold that makes reliable document extraction possible is genuinely recent. There is a version of this company that starts with quoting, accumulates proprietary pricing and supplier data across thousands of manufacturers, and leverages that data moat into production planning, supply chain optimization, and demand forecasting. That version would be interesting. But nothing in the dossier indicates the founders are thinking at that scale. The website describes "quote-to-order workflow" -- a feature, not a platform transformation. And even the platform version faces the fundamental problem that the underlying technology is commodity: every competitor has access to the same frontier LLMs and RAG architectures.

On the founders -- Daichi, Alex, and Martin are current or recent UPenn undergraduates. Martin's GM automations internship and Daichi's ML research provide relevant technical grounding. Alex Liu's dropout story and claim of reading "more research papers after dropping out than while in school" is the most interesting founder signal -- it suggests intellectual intensity and self-direction. But I evaluate founders on contrarian courage and learning rate, not credentials. Where is the evidence that these founders are attempting something the establishment considers impossible? They're building a SaaS tool that five other companies are also building. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, every aerospace insider said a New Zealander without a PhD couldn't build an orbital launch vehicle. Nobody is saying AI can't automate manufacturing quotes -- everyone agrees it can, which is why the space is crowded. The team also lacks anyone with enterprise sales experience, which matters when your GTM is "Contact Sales" to manufacturing operations teams with multi-stakeholder purchasing committees. That's a gene pool gap.

The technology here is application-layer integration of broadly available LLM capabilities, not a fundamental breakthrough. RAG over business documents, multi-channel message ingestion, structured data extraction from unstructured inputs -- these are engineering challenges, not research frontiers. The dossier itself flags that "the underlying LLM/RAG technology is broadly available" and that differentiation depends on "depth of manufacturing-domain training, integration quality, and speed of customer acquisition rather than proprietary model architecture." When your own competitive analysis says differentiation isn't in the technology, you've told me everything I need to know about the disruption potential. This doesn't make existing approaches obsolete -- it makes them incrementally faster.

I pass. The consequence of success is a workflow efficiency tool in a crowded market built on commodity technology. Some problems are too important not to work on. This one is too incremental to justify the team's talent.
