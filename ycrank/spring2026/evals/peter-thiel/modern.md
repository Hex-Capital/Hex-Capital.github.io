# Modern -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Seb Poole shipped Frankie Health into Unmind and Aleks Tomovski ran Canva's internal developer platform -- Modern is the operator-pedigree-carries-it profile applied to a wedge where the secret is missing. The dimensional signature is unambiguous: highest on founder lineage, lowest on contrarian secret. That pairing names the company: Modern is the **Asana** (2010) archetype -- competent shipped-to-scale operators attacking consensus workflow territory -- except Poole's Frankie exit into Unmind is not Moskovitz exiting **Facebook** (2004), and "AI-native ServiceNow" in Spring 2026 is not workflow software in 2010 when the category was open. The override that worked for Moskovitz does not arithmetically clear here.

Stripe (2010) is the load-bearing analogy for the contrarian-secret dimension and it is exactly where Modern fails. Patrick Collison's secret was that PayPal had "won" payments while shipping a developer experience legacy processors structurally could not fix -- the incumbent failure mode was named, specific, and architectural. Modern's pitch is "ServiceNow was built to manage tickets rather than eliminate them," which is the identical sentence Moveworks used before ServiceNow acquired it for $2.1B, the identical sentence Serval raised $127M on at a $1B mark from Sequoia and Redpoint, and the identical sentence Atomicwork raised $40.3M on from Khosla. When five funded companies and the incumbent's own M&A team all name the same failure mode, it is not a secret -- it is the 2026 consensus. Modern has restated what the market already agrees with.

ServiceNow at ~40% share and 8,600 customers is shipping native AI agents while Salesforce enters ITSM with AI per the April 2026 Register piece -- the monopoly trajectory dimension compounds against Modern over an 18-month window rather than for it. The mechanism is straightforward: ITSM moats are integration depth and switching cost, both of which accrue to whoever already holds the workflow surface, and ServiceNow's response to Moveworks was acquisition, not capitulation. Modern's stated future moat -- "workflow data accumulated from automated resolutions" -- is the same data-flywheel claim every competitor in this wedge makes, and a flywheel six companies claim simultaneously is not a flywheel. Pricing power at maturity collapses to per-seat parity with Atlassian JSM and Freshservice.

Modern's definite-plan dimension would flip a tier only if the dossier surfaced a named architectural commitment Poole and Tomovski refuse to pivot from -- a specific protocol layer, a specific vertical depth (regulated healthcare IT, defense contractor IT, FedRAMP), a specific 36-month milestone. The dossier surfaces none. It surfaces 80%+ automation rates and percentage testimonials from unnamed "Global IT team" and "Series C healthtech" -- iteration-mode artifacts, not definite-optimism architecture. The absence is the signal: founders with definite plans lead with them, and the homepage leads with channel coverage (Slack, Teams, Email, Voice, WhatsApp, Telegram), which is feature surface area, not architectural commitment.

Affirm (2014) is the single anchor on founder lineage. Max Levchin shipped PayPal to a $1.5B exit and re-upped on a regulated consumer credit thesis where his prior infrastructure work was directly load-bearing. Poole's Frankie-into-Unmind exit is real and his enterprise relationships at Disney, Uber, and MLB are real, and Tomovski's Canva/Wix platform engineering is the rare ex-Canva profile that actually shipped infrastructure rather than design surface. This is a credible two-person team -- mid-rung, not high-rung, because the prior exit was modest and neither founder carries the PayPal Mafia or Facebook-CTO override that historically let me write checks against missing structural moats.

The atoms-heavy dimension is the cleanest fail. SOC 2 Type 2 and ISO 27001 are procurement table stakes, not regulatory moats in the Palantir (2003) or Anduril (2017) sense -- no defense customer, no FedRAMP High, no integration depth that takes five years to displace. Modern is pure software in a commoditizing AI-agent wedge, which Founders Fund's 2011 manifesto called the canonical indefinite-optimism failure mode for exactly this reason.

My 36-month forecast: Modern reaches $3-8M ARR through Poole-led mid-market enterprise sales by mid-2028, then gets acquired by ServiceNow, Atlassian, or Freshworks for $80-180M as the AI-ITSM category consolidates around the incumbent platforms -- a 3-5x outcome for seed investors, a zero for power-law math, the exact shape of a competent-operator-on-consensus-wedge exit that the Asana override cannot reproduce without Moskovitz-tier pedigree.

The bull case stings if you believe the wedge is large enough that consolidation produces multiple billion-dollar outcomes -- Serval already cleared $1B on paper, Moveworks cleared $2.1B in M&A, and Poole's enterprise relationships could land three Fortune 500 logos at $500K ACV inside 18 months. The Asana exception exists precisely because operator pedigree sometimes overrides missing structural moat in workflow software. The bear-case dismantle is that Asana required Moskovitz at Facebook scale and a category that was open in 2010; Modern enters in 2026 with six funded competitors and the incumbent buying the closest analog. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: Modern lands a named Fortune 100 or federal-tier logo on a contract structure with five-year integration depth -- defense, intelligence, regulated healthcare with HIPAA-deep workflow lock-in -- before Demo Day. Absent that, this is the indefinite-optimism check Zero to One was written against. Pass.
