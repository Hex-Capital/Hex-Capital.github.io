# Thirdbrain Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumped out at me about Thirdbrain Labs isn't the product — it's Margaret Zhang's path from co-founding Coalition Systems, a defense AI startup where she traveled to Kyiv to meet with Ukraine's Ministry of Defence, to building post-training infrastructure for domain experts. That pivot contains a potential story I want to hear. Someone who went into a war zone to understand how AI coordination works in the most consequential context imaginable might have a visceral understanding of why specialized models matter. But the dossier doesn't connect those dots. The relationship between Coalition Systems and Thirdbrain Labs is opaque — pivot, parallel venture, or clean break? When I ask "tell me your story," I need to understand why Margaret walked away from defense AI, and whether David Huang's decade of ML coursework and his top-1% finish in Two Sigma's Halite challenge were pulled toward this problem or pushed toward it by market timing. What I see in the dossier is two capable people with relevant credentials — Zhang's GTM experience at Spice AI, Huang's technical depth — assembling for a market opportunity rather than being forged into inevitability.

Thirdbrain Labs is building post-training infrastructure in a space where Scale AI sits at $29 billion, Together AI at $3.3 billion, Snorkel AI at $1.3 billion, and Labelbox north of a billion in valuation. AWS Bedrock and Google Vertex AI offer managed fine-tuning natively. This is the opposite of a space that makes VCs shift in their chairs — it's the space VCs are tripping over each other to fund. When I invested in Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I backed Crusoe, people thought flare-gas Bitcoin mining was a joke. Thirdbrain Labs occupies consensus territory in the hottest sector in technology. The "expert decision capture" framing is a product differentiator, not a contrarian thesis. No investor needs courage to say "AI infrastructure" at a dinner party in 2026.

My portfolio actually includes Together AI, so I've watched this space from the inside. Together AI succeeded because it built genuine compute infrastructure for open-source models — a structural layer that becomes harder to displace with scale. Thirdbrain Labs is positioning as a workflow layer on top of that kind of infrastructure, and Lee Jacobs would ask the hard question: what stops Together AI, or any of these billion-dollar incumbents, from adding an expert-feedback workflow in a quarter? The dossier explicitly flags that open-source tools like Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, and LLaMA-Factory are free and improving rapidly. With no public customers, no data flywheel, and no proprietary datasets documented, the moat here is a UX hypothesis — not infrastructure that compounds. Predibase's acquisition by Rubrik for somewhere between $100 million and $500 million proves there's exit value in this space, but Predibase had LoRA-based fine-tuning depth and a differentiated technical approach. Thirdbrain Labs hasn't yet demonstrated what their "expert decisions as training signal" means in practice versus standard RLHF or DPO workflows.

The invisible-system test is where Thirdbrain Labs falls quiet for me. Fine-tuning is complex, but it's not extractive. ML engineers know what the process costs and what it involves. There's no hidden tax being levied on people who don't understand the system — there's a technical barrier being lowered for teams with domain expertise but limited ML capacity. That's valuable engineering work, but it's not the structural power shift I look for. Compare this to Affirm attacking credit card opacity or Ownwell automating property tax appeals — those companies exposed systems that were actively extracting value through information asymmetry against ordinary people. Thirdbrain Labs is making a professional tool more accessible to other professionals.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The timing is genuinely interesting: open-source base models from Meta, Mistral, and DeepSeek created a massive new population of teams that want customization but lack ML engineers. If Thirdbrain Labs nails the workflow that lets a robotics team or a biotech researcher steer a production model through their own expert decisions — without writing training code — that's a real product with real switching costs once models are embedded in production. Zhang's GTM instincts from Spice AI and her investor network from INVC and Temasek could accelerate enterprise distribution. The Meta-Scale AI disruption in mid-2025 opened competitive space. For this to work, two things must be true: the "expert decision capture" mechanism must be genuinely differentiated from commoditized RLHF tooling, and the team must build embedded production workflows before incumbents close the window. Neither is evidenced yet.

Thirdbrain Labs lands as a pass for me. The two dimensions that locked this were Founder's Biographical Forge — where I couldn't find the life experience that made post-training infrastructure inevitable for Margaret Zhang or David Huang rather than a well-reasoned market bet — and Contrarian Conviction, where the competitive landscape of multi-billion-dollar incumbents and free open-source alternatives makes this one of the most consensus-crowded spaces I've seen in a pre-seed dossier. Capable founders, real market, but not a Long Journey bet.
