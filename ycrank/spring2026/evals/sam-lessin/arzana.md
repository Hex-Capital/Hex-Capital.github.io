# Arzana -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Marshall Kools pulled down ten thousand dollars in personal salary last year while embedding engineers in Wisconsin factories. That's a real signal. A 24-year-old D1 wrestler from Neenah eating ramen to configure ERP integrations at paper converting shops — that's not a mercenary fundraiser, that's a missionary. And William Alexander, 21, grew up working for a manufacturing sourcing company in Okoboji, Iowa before studying Econ and CS at Stanford. These two have authentic manufacturing DNA in a way that 99% of YC founders pitching "AI for industry" absolutely do not. The Midwest office footprint — Okoboji and Appleton alongside San Francisco — is the opposite of a vanity presence. So when I look at Arzana's 8 named customers including Zeiss at pre-seed with a 5-person team, I believe that's real. The forward-deployed model of sending engineers onsite before configuration is the kind of physical-world grunt work that creates genuine switching costs and that most software founders find beneath them. There's something to like here.

But here's where my framework forces an uncomfortable conclusion about Arzana. The product is AI agents that read emails and PDFs and write structured data into ERPs. That is the textbook case I keep warning people about. Epicor already has your customer's data. SAP already has your customer's data. Oracle/NetSuite already has your customer's data. When I say "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI, it's perfect for Adobe" — Arzana is pitching me Epicor for AI. And Epicor will build this. They're probably building it right now. Conexiom already has $170M in funding and $28.7M in 2024 revenue doing essentially the same thing and has launched its own AI Platform. Paperless Parts has $51M raised and 500+ customers in manufacturing quoting. Esker has 3,000+ enterprise customers and got acquired by Bridgepoint and General Atlantic. Arzana is entering a category where well-funded players already exist and where the underlying LLM capability — extracting structured data from unstructured documents — is rapidly commoditizing. The dossier itself flags this: "the underlying technology (LLMs reading emails/PDFs and writing to ERPs) is accessible to well-funded competitors."

Arzana fails my primitive test cleanly. Quoting, order entry, estimating, customer updates — every one of these workflows exists today. Arzana makes them faster. That's execution in an existing market, not category creation. Nobody will use "Arzana" as a verb. Nobody will describe a new behavior by Arzana's name. Compare this to Venmo, which created the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments — something that didn't exist and became a reference point for an entire category. Arzana is "better Conexiom for smaller manufacturers." That's a perfectly reasonable business. It's just not the kind of bet that produces asymmetric returns. The TAM is real — $17.46B in manufacturing operations management software growing at 19.1% CAGR per Grand View Research — but Arzana's path to capturing meaningful share runs through direct competition with incumbents who have more customers, more data, and more capital.

Now, the bull case deserves real examination. The specific segment Arzana targets — sub-enterprise manufacturers, job shops, mold makers, paper converters in the Midwest — is genuinely underserved by Conexiom and Esker, which price for large enterprises. The forward-deployed model creates a Metropolis-like physical-world moat: you have to send engineers to Iowa to win these customers, and most software companies won't. Arzana's per-customer AI models trained on historical job costs create real switching costs — once your estimating accuracy depends on Arzana's trained models, ripping them out is painful. The ITAR compliance requirement is a legitimate barrier to entry. And the founders' capital discipline — $10K salary, Midwest offices, 5-person team doing real deployment work — is exactly what I preach. If Arzana could reach profitability serving 50-100 small manufacturers without raising another dime, this becomes a real business regardless of what Epicor does.

But the Metropolis parallel breaks on inspection. Metropolis owns and operates parking infrastructure — physical assets that no software company can replicate from a laptop. Arzana's forward-deployed engineers go home after deployment. The ongoing product is software running against someone else's ERP. The switching costs are real but not structural in the way that owning a parking garage is structural. When SAP ships native AI quoting in their next major release — and they will — Arzana's small manufacturer customers will face a choice between a startup's bolt-on integration and their ERP vendor's native feature. That's not a fight Arzana wins long-term without building something much deeper than what the dossier describes. The sanity-insanity ratio also doesn't fire for me: William and Marshall are impressive young operators, but the idea itself — automate manufacturing paperwork with AI — is sensible, obvious, and already being pursued by multiple well-funded companies. I want to back sane people doing insane things. Arzana is sane people doing sane things well.

The condition that flips me to Invest: Arzana demonstrates that the per-customer trained models achieve accuracy levels (on quoting, estimating) that generic AI cannot replicate even with the same ERP data — i.e., the forward-deployed training process creates a proprietary data asset that compounds with usage and that ERP vendors can't shortcut by shipping a feature. Show me 20+ customers with >12 months of retention and pricing power that proves the data moat is real, not theoretical. Until then, Arzana's strongest dimension is capital discipline — Marshall's $10K salary and the Midwest footprint are the genuine article — but capital discipline alone doesn't overcome an AI application-layer positioning that my framework explicitly flags as a losing structural bet. The AI kill test and the absence of primitive creation are the two dimensions that lock this as a Pass.
