# Indexable -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Andrew Gazelka built a Minecraft engine that processes 1,000 players in 455 microseconds across 12 cores, targeting 170,000 simultaneous players in a single shared world. That's not a side project -- that's years of obsessive work at the exact boundary where state management, memory systems, and concurrent mutation become unsolvable for normal engineers. The specific knowledge required to handle 170,000 entities mutating shared state in real time is the same specific knowledge required to fork a running virtual machine -- files, processes, memory, databases -- in 26 milliseconds. You cannot train someone to do this. You cannot read a textbook and acquire it. This is knowledge that emerged from genuine curiosity pushed to extremes, which is exactly the filter I apply before anything else. The Hyperion project is Gazelka's Blogger -- the way Evan Williams spent years understanding public communication before Twitter, Gazelka spent years understanding massive concurrent state before building infrastructure that manages the state of AI agents. Add xAI experience for production AI context, a custom VMM built on KVM, a content-addressable storage engine, a modified Linux 6.19 kernel, and a custom Nix fork written in Rust -- this is someone operating at a layer of the stack most founders don't know exists.

The leverage architecture maps cleanly to my framework. Indexable is infrastructure, not application. One team builds the sandbox primitive; thousands of agent developers consume it through SDKs in five languages. This is the Alchemy pattern -- when Alchemy built the blockchain developer platform, they weren't building a DeFi app, they were building the layer underneath every DeFi app. Indexable sits in the same structural position for AI agents. Every company building autonomous agents that write code, query databases, or manage processes needs isolated compute with state preservation. The consumption-based model means revenue scales with the proliferation of agents across the ecosystem, not with Indexable's headcount. That's leverage -- disproportionate output from fixed engineering input.

The contrarian question is where this gets interesting. Sandbox infrastructure for AI agents is emphatically not contrarian -- E2B has $43.8M in funding, 88% of the Fortune 100, and 500 million sandbox sessions. The consensus view is that this market exists and E2B has won it. But the architectural bet underneath is genuinely non-consensus: Gazelka is betting that ephemeral execution (what E2B's Firecracker architecture provides) is the wrong primitive for the next generation of agents. He's betting that agents need to fork execution mid-stream, explore parallel paths, backtrack, and diff against prior states -- capabilities that are structurally impossible on Firecracker without a fundamental rebuild. This is the kind of bet where most smart infrastructure engineers would say "E2B is good enough" and move on. The question is whether the shift from single-turn agent calls to multi-step autonomous workflows creates demand for a qualitatively different compute primitive. If it does, E2B's $43.8M becomes a liability -- they'd have to abandon their core architecture to compete. That structural asymmetry is where the upside lives.

The bear case is real and I don't dismiss it. E2B's dominance is not cosmetic -- 88% Fortune 100 adoption and seven-figure monthly revenue represent genuine ecosystem lock-in. Daytona has $31M and offers fork/snapshot capabilities today, with a 12-month head start on enterprise relationships. Modal Labs at $1.1B+ valuation could add forking as a feature within their broader serverless platform. The performance gap -- 26ms versus 90ms versus 200ms -- might not matter enoughirtual for developers to absorb migration costs. And Gazelka is a solo technical founder with no visible commercial experience, going up against well-capitalized competitors with established sales organizations. The counterargument is that E2B's architecture can't reach 26ms forking without a ground-up rebuild, and the performance gap isn't just speed -- it's the difference between forking being a latency-sensitive operation you avoid and a cheap primitive you use constantly. If agents evolve toward tree-search-style exploration (fork, evaluate, prune, merge), the company that makes forking nearly free wins a different market than the one E2B occupies.

The solo founder gap is the one dimension that gives me pause. Not because I require co-founders -- some of the best companies are built by singular technical minds. But infrastructure products targeting enterprise buyers need someone who understands distribution, pricing, and the enterprise procurement cycle. Gazelka's public profile is purely technical: systems engineering, Rust, game engines, open-source contributions. The long-term orientation signals are positive -- Hyperion is a multi-year passion project, BountyBot was built and acquired, and the depth of the technical stack (custom VMM, custom kernel, custom Nix fork) suggests someone building for decades, not for a quick flip. But the commercial execution risk at this stage is non-trivial.

This is a picks-and-shovels bet on compute scarcity in the AI agent era -- the same structural thesis that pulled me toward Extropic and Atomic Semi. The founder has specific knowledge that exists outside any standard pipeline. The technology compounds -- each optimization to the VMM, each improvement to the content-addressable storage engine, each kernel modification makes the system harder to replicate. The leverage architecture is clean infrastructure. What's missing is traction evidence and commercial co-founding talent. I'd want to meet Gazelka, understand who the other two team members are, and see early usage data. But the technical foundation and the founder's specific knowledge clear my bar.
