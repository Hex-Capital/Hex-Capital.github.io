# Standout -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Dex raised $3.1M from a16z Speedrun in April 2025 building a candidate-side AI talent agent with voice calls. Standout launched on Product Hunt in February 2026 building a candidate-side AI talent agent on WhatsApp. The positioning is nearly identical — represent the job seeker, not the employer — and Standout arrived later with less capital and a funded mirror image already in market. When I evaluate whether I want to be the first check into something, I'm looking for a space where no one else is willing to go. Recruiting technology attracted $80M into Juicebox alone, $23M into Perfect, and a16z already bet on the exact same structural thesis through Dex. This is not a space that makes investors uncomfortable. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we're fixing recruiting with AI." They nod, because they've heard it thirty times this quarter.

Alexis Aftalion co-founded Zealy and scaled it to 1.5M MAU and $3M ARR in twelve months — that's real execution, and I respect what it takes to build community at that velocity. Witold de La Chapelle studied computer science at Carnegie Mellon, engineered at Dropbox, Samsara, and Chime, and built a job board that reached 100K MAU. These are competent, proven builders. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I'd hear is "I built a web3 community platform and my co-founder worked at tech companies and built a job board." That's a resume. It's not a forge. I don't see evidence that either founder was personally broken by the recruiting system, that they navigated something exploitative that left them unable to walk away from this problem. Witold was the target user — a startup engineer who presumably experienced recruiting friction — but experiencing friction is not the same as being forged by it. The dossier surfaces no shared history between the co-founders, no biographical inevitability connecting them to candidate representation. Standout reads like two capable people who identified a market gap, not two people whose lives demanded they fill it.

Standout's structural insight — that recruiters serve employers, not candidates, creating misaligned incentives — is real. Ghosting, irrelevant outreach, no negotiation support: that's a genuine information asymmetry, and the "Hollywood agent for talent" framing captures something true about how the power dynamic should be restructured. When I think about Affirm, Max Levchin attacked credit card opacity that affected hundreds of millions of people who didn't understand the terms extracting value from them. Standout is attacking recruiting opacity for tech professionals at Sequoia-, a16z-, and YC-backed startups — the most in-demand, highest-information workers in the economy. These candidates already have leverage. They already get recruited aggressively. The hidden system in recruiting exists — but it exists most painfully for warehouse temp workers navigating staffing agencies, for immigrants who don't understand employment contracts, for people without networks or credentials. Standout chose the population that least needs the system made visible. The invisible system test partially passes in concept but fails in application.

Standout's 35% beta metric — users reaching a first interview within four days — signals product quality, and 1,457 indexed startups with 3,000+ open roles shows the team can build quickly. The Product Hunt launch hit #6 of the day with 167 upvotes. These are respectable pre-seed signals. But the core technology is an LLM-based conversational agent doing job matching over WhatsApp. The dossier explicitly flags commoditization risk: "replicable." Dex could add WhatsApp delivery tomorrow. Juicebox, at $850M valuation with 5,000 customers, could pivot to candidate-side features. Standout's product is listed as "Free" with no public revenue signal and no visible pricing — the monetization path requires convincing employers to pay placement fees to a candidate-side agent, which is an unfamiliar buying motion in an industry structured around employer subscriptions. WhatsApp platform dependency adds fragility rather than moat. When I think about my failures — HQ Trivia, GameCrush — they were products I loved that had no structural reason to win against well-funded competition. Standout's current defensibility profile triggers that same pattern recognition.

The strongest bull case: Alexis proved he can build community virally, Witold has domain experience, the candidate-agent model inverts an entire industry's incentive structure, and if Standout captures startup talent network effects early, the data from deep conversational intake becomes a proprietary asset that LinkedIn can't replicate because LinkedIn serves employers. That's a real argument. But it requires Standout to outrace Dex (which has a16z's distribution network and a year's head start), establish a monetization model that doesn't exist yet in this market, and build data moats from a free product with no evidence of retention or repeat usage. I checked my known exceptions — HQ Trivia's cultural moment override, RTFKT's narrow timing window, Superplastic's creative weirdness — and none apply. Standout is not culturally electric, not operating in a narrow window, and not genre-defyingly weird. It's a well-executed recruiting chatbot in a crowded, consensus-attractive space. What would flip me: if Alexis or Witold told me a story about being personally exploited by the recruiting system in a way that fundamentally shaped their understanding of the problem — and if they showed me employer contracts or LOIs proving the monetization model works — I'd reconsider. The biographical forge and the business model are the two things I'd need to see before writing a check.

Standout's founders are capable builders in a real market, and the structural insight about candidate representation has intellectual merit. But my framework demands biographical inevitability, contrarian spaces, invisible systems affecting the powerless, and infrastructure-grade moats. Standout scores below my threshold on every dimension that matters most to me — the founders lack a personal crucible connecting them to this problem, the space is consensus with a funded direct competitor already in market, the "invisible system" serves the least exploited population in the labor market, and the technology is replicable with no structural moat at this stage. The two dimensions that locked this verdict are Founder's Biographical Forge (no evidence of personal stakes beyond career relevance) and Infrastructure-Grade Defensibility (LLM agent with no proprietary depth against a16z-backed Dex).
