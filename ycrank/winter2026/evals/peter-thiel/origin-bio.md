# Origin -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Origin is that their flagship achievement is outperforming Google DeepMind's AlphaGenome by 6.7% on regulatory element binding prediction. This is not a secret -- it is a benchmark. When a four-person startup defines its value proposition relative to a side project from one of the most well-resourced AI research organizations on the planet, it has already conceded the terms of competition. DeepMind can iterate on AlphaGenome with a hundred researchers, a billion dollars in compute, and access to every genomics dataset on Earth. A 6.7% marginal improvement on a shared benchmark is the precise opposite of the 10x technological discontinuity that creates a monopoly. It is the kind of incremental advantage that dissolves within eighteen months.

What is the secret here? The implicit thesis is that AI-designed synthetic regulatory DNA elements are the critical safety bottleneck in cell and gene therapy, and that a unified generation-prediction model unlocks this bottleneck in a way that creates a new category. But this is not a truth that most intelligent people reject -- it is a truth that most intelligent people in computational biology already accept. DeepMind built AlphaGenome. Broad Institute researchers published DNA-Diffusion in *Nature Genetics*. Asimov has raised $209 million for adjacent genetic design work. Dyno has $100 million and partnerships worth $4 billion with Novartis, Roche, and Sarepta. The entire "AI for biology" space is experiencing the most intense mimetic convergence of any investment category since cleantech in 2007. Every sophisticated venture investor wants to fund this thesis. When everyone agrees, there is no secret, and where there is no secret, there is no monopoly.

The claimed moat is a proprietary wet-lab-validated dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences. In principle, this is the right instinct -- a compounding data flywheel could create defensibility that the model architecture alone cannot. But this is an aspiration, not a reality. The company has four people. They have not disclosed dataset scale, lab infrastructure, validation throughput, or partnerships with any institution that could provide high-throughput experimental capacity. Building "the largest proprietary dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences across diverse cell-states" requires industrial-scale wet-lab operations that are capitally intensive, operationally complex, and slow. Meanwhile, Asimov, Dyno, and any well-funded pharma company with CRISPR screening capacity could generate comparable datasets as a byproduct of their existing operations. The moat is contingent on winning a race against better-funded competitors in a domain -- experimental biology at scale -- where the founders have no demonstrated operational advantage.

The strongest bull case for Origin requires believing three things simultaneously: that the regulatory DNA layer remains a distinct, standalone business rather than being absorbed as a feature into broader platforms; that Origin's wet-lab dataset compounds faster than competitors can replicate it; and that the unified generation-prediction architecture creates enough of a head start to survive DeepMind's inevitable iteration. If all three hold, Origin could become the standard regulatory element design platform for the $100B+ gene therapy market -- a narrow but defensible monopoly analogous to what Stripe built for developer payments. The founders show genuine intellectual precocity: Malhar Bhide published in *Nature Scientific Reports* as a high school student, and the advisory board -- Kellis at MIT/Broad, Paulk at UCSF, Bashir at UIUC -- is unusually strong for a pre-seed company. The niche itself is well-chosen: regulatory elements sit at a specific layer of the gene therapy stack that none of the adjacent competitors directly address today. If the founders develop the definite conviction to execute a Palantir-style long game -- years of patient dataset building before monetization -- the opportunity exists.

But I do not see that definite conviction yet. The company offers a free API to researchers with no pricing page and no disclosed revenue. This is the iterate-and-discover approach: put a tool out, see who uses it, figure out the business later. It is precisely the lean startup methodology I consider "code for unplanned." The founders are talented ML engineers, but their backgrounds are in computer science and computer vision, not in gene therapy development. They did not arrive at this problem through the kind of deep domain immersion that produces genuine contrarian insight -- the way our team at Palantir arrived at intelligence analysis through years of building fraud detection at PayPal. They arrived at it through the mimetic pull of "AI for biology" as an investment category. I see competent engineers applying transformer architectures to a promising biological problem, not founders who have identified a truth about the world that the consensus rejects. The regulatory DNA design space may well become important, but Origin has not demonstrated that it possesses a structural advantage that would make it the last mover in this space rather than an early entrant that gets outspent and outscaled.
