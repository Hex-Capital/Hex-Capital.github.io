# Hlabs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Hlabs: 40 robotics companies placed orders for actuator control boards within four months of launch, from a solo founder selling physical hardware out of Austin. That's genuine pull. Nobody orders circuit boards out of politeness. The demand signal is real — the question is whether supplying modular components to robot builders is a business whose consequence of success justifies the effort, or whether Paul Hetherington is building the world's best toolbox when he should be building the house.

Let me be direct about the consequence magnitude problem. "Making an industrial supply chain for building robots in the USA" is important infrastructure work. If it succeeds completely, robotics startups prototype faster, the US reduces dependence on Chinese component suppliers, and defense programs have ITAR-compliant actuator boards. That matters. But it doesn't fundamentally change how any industry operates. Nobody's life is transformed by a better FOC controller. The company that uses that controller to build a humanoid that replaces warehouse labor — that's where the consequence lives. Hlabs has positioned itself as a picks-and-shovels play, which is the classic move for reducing probability of failure. There's obviously demand; dozens of robotics companies need these parts. But by choosing the safe layer of the stack — components, not systems — the upside ceiling compresses. I've seen this pattern many times: smart founders who derisk their way into a solid but inconsequential business.

The bull case I have to genuinely contend with: platform shifts create enormous value in the component layer. Qualcomm didn't build phones; it built the chips inside them and is worth $200 billion. Arm didn't build mobile devices; it designed the architecture and is worth $150 billion. If humanoid robots are the next computing platform — and the trajectory suggests they might be, with Tesla, Figure, 1X, and Unitree all converging on commercial humanoids simultaneously — then whoever standardizes the component stack could capture outsized value. Hlabs' open-source software creating developer lock-in mirrors the playbook that built Arm's ecosystem dominance. The US manufacturing angle isn't just positioning; ITAR requirements for defense robotics create a structural moat that Chinese competitors literally cannot cross. If the humanoid wave produces 200 robotics startups over the next three years rather than consolidating around 5 vertically integrated players, Hlabs is sitting on a chokepoint. That's the scenario where this becomes a very large outcome.

But I don't think the technology itself creates structural disruption. An FOC actuator control board, a Jetson-powered main board, camera modules, wireless communication — these are existing technologies integrated into a convenient developer-friendly package. It's closer to Arduino than to Juniper Networks. When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu had built a fundamentally different routing architecture that made Cisco's approach technically obsolete. Hlabs isn't making existing actuator technology obsolete; it's making it easier to buy and assemble in the US. That's a distribution and integration play, not a technology disruption. The open-source software adds value, but open-source reference designs for robotic actuators already exist on GitHub. The moat here is thin — it's developer ecosystem and supply chain geography, not physics or proprietary IP.

The timing is the strongest dimension. The humanoid robotics wave is clearly at an inflection point — more robotics startups are being founded now than in any comparable period, compute costs for edge AI have dropped to enabling levels, and US trade policy is actively pushing domestic sourcing. Paul is building into an accelerating trend before it's consensus among component suppliers. He's also a second-time YC founder whose career arc — from embedded systems research to ML infrastructure (Mystic AI) to hardware robotics — traces the technology stack from cloud to edge to physical world. That shows rate-of-change awareness. But Mystic AI raised $1.31 million over six years from thirteen investors, which doesn't suggest explosive execution or learning velocity. And now he's a solo founder on a hardware company with a multi-product roadmap spanning four new products in Q1 2026. Hardware demands gene pool engineering more than almost any other category — you need manufacturing, supply chain, QC, firmware, developer relations, and sales all executing simultaneously. A team of one managing all of that while also running through YC is a structural risk that concerns me deeply.

I'm landing at a pass, with genuine respect for the demand signal and timing. The 40+ orders tell me this is a real business serving a real need. But I invest for consequence magnitude that justifies the attempt, and a component supplier — even a well-positioned one — caps its own upside by design. If Paul told me he was using these components to build the robot itself, attacking a specific physical-world problem that the components enable, I'd lean forward. The tools are interesting; what you build with them is what changes the world.
