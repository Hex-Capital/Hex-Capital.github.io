# Heavi -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari built WayCool Foods to $250M+ ARR in India's agricultural supply chain, exited, then went and got ASE T8 certified as a diesel mechanic. That is an unusual move. Most founders who scale a company to three-quarters of a billion in valuation go start another company in an adjacent market or take a board seat somewhere comfortable. Dasari went and turned wrenches. That tells me something about his relationship to the problem — he wanted to understand truck repair from the shop floor, not from a spreadsheet. Paired with Michael Holkesvik, who built NCX's marketplace to 15,000 members as CTO before that company hit the wall when Verra declined certification, you have two founders who have both scaled and failed. $100,000 in contracted revenue from five design partners before raising real money, with those partners reporting 40% margin improvement — that is meaningful signal for a two-person pre-seed team. These are serious operators.

The question I cannot get past is: what happens to the world if Heavi fully succeeds? Truck repair shops find parts 75% faster. Fleet downtime drops. Margins improve for shop owners. The 67,000 technician shortfall becomes less painful because less experienced mechanics can locate parts without calling three vendors. All of that is genuinely valuable to the industry. But the consequence is bounded — it stays inside a $25 billion US parts dealer market and an $18 billion repair services market. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, success meant limitless clean energy for civilization. When I backed Impossible Foods, success meant feeding billions without animal agriculture. When Heavi succeeds, repair shops are more profitable. I cannot manufacture consequence magnitude that is not there. Heavi's "AI workforce" framing gestures at a bigger vision, but the current product is an intelligent parts procurement agent — it searches OE systems, aftermarket catalogs, and ecommerce sites, then contacts vendors. That is an agentic workflow over existing data sources, not a step-function technology breakthrough. It is the pattern I explicitly pass on: apply current LLM capabilities to automate 30-40% of a profession's workflow in a specific vertical. The domain complexity of heavy-duty parts fitment databases creates a real barrier to entry, but domain complexity is not the same as a proprietary technical insight. FleetPride or TruckPro could build a similar agent with sufficient investment, and Fullbay's acquisition of Pitstop in March 2026 signals the incumbents are already moving toward AI integration across their 5,000+ shop install base.

The bull case deserves honest examination. If Heavi captures procurement and expands into AI-powered diagnostics, repair guidance, and full shop management — becoming the operating system for every independent heavy-duty repair shop — the consequence magnitude increases meaningfully. Dasari's family connection to Ashok Leyland (his father was Managing Director of one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers) provides a network into commercial vehicle OEMs that no competitor has. The technician shortage is structural and worsening: 28,500 annual openings against fewer than 11,000 training completions creates a deficit that compounds every year. In that world, Heavi becomes critical infrastructure for keeping freight moving. I can see a $5-10 billion company here. But I checked this against my DoorDash exception — where I overrode the technical novelty requirement because the consequence of transforming food access for hundreds of millions was enormous. DoorDash touched every consumer in America. Heavi, even at maximum success, touches tens of thousands of repair shops and their fleet customers. The consumer-scale consequence that triggered my DoorDash override is absent.

The 40% margin improvement Heavi's design partners report is telling in a way that cuts against my framework. Margin improvement means shops capture more profit per repair — value accrues to shop owners, not to fleet operators or end consumers as lower prices. There is no evidence Heavi envisions making truck repair radically cheaper for the people who pay for it. My deepest pattern — making the expensive free — requires that technology gains flow to affordability, not to incumbent margin expansion. Heavi is optimizing an existing supply chain's economics, not collapsing a cost structure. Dasari knows how to build this kind of supply chain marketplace — WayCool was precisely that — and he will likely build another successful one. But "successful marketplace in a vertical industry" is a 3-5x return profile, and I have said publicly that 3-5x returns are a waste of time and attention when you can make 50x or lose everything.

The founders are the strongest element here, and the weakest element is the ceiling on what success looks like. I am passing not because Heavi will fail — these founders will probably build a good company — but because even wild success does not change the world enough to justify a spot in a portfolio constructed around civilizational consequence. The dimension that locked my decision is Consequence Magnitude: a $25-43 billion US market for truck parts and repair, optimized by an AI procurement agent, does not produce the kind of outcome explosion I require. The second decisive factor is Non-Linear Technical Approach: searching across existing databases and contacting vendors through LLM agents is capable engineering, not a technology discontinuity.
