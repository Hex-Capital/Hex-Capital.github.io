# Sellara -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about Sellara that jumped out immediately: Charles-André Jolly covered 180 institutional clients at Citi across Macro Credit Derivatives, IG Credit, and Short Term Credit Sales. Spencer Secord spent six years on Cross Asset and Fixed Income desks at the same bank. These two didn't read a McKinsey report about "the future of AI in capital markets" — they lived inside the broken workflows they're now trying to automate. That's real. And Ahmad Roumie's ETH Zurich robotics MSc plus a YC W23 CTO stint gives them a technical co-founder who's shipped ML in production. On paper, the team composition is strong. But strong teams in bad categories is how you lose money slowly instead of quickly.

The mispricing test kills Sellara for me before I get anywhere else. AI agents for financial services workflows is not contrarian — it's the single most consensus application of AI in 2026. The dossier itself notes 44% of finance teams are projected to use agentic AI this year, a 600%+ increase. Beam AI already has $4.5M in revenue and 57 employees targeting overlapping BFSI buyers in the same city. Blueflame raised a $5M Series A for adjacent institutional AI. Accenture and Deloitte are building custom AI solutions for banks. Every smart LP and every smart GP I talk to says "AI for financial operations" when you ask them where AI creates value. When every sophisticated investor agrees something is a good category, the upside is already in the price. There's no asymmetry left. I'm looking for the bet where people think I'm wrong — and nobody thinks "AI will automate bank back offices" is wrong.

Sellara fails the primitive test cleanly. Booking, allocations, reconciliations, compliance, reporting — these are existing workflows performed by existing people using existing systems. Sellara makes them faster. That's execution improvement in an established category, not primitive creation. Venmo created a behavior — "Venmo me" became a verb because peer-to-peer mobile payments didn't exist as a consumer action before. Nobody's going to say "Sellara me." This is "better RPA with LLMs," which is useful, valuable, potentially a good business — but it's not creating a new behavioral or financial primitive. It's competing on execution against Bloomberg's AI features team, FIS's product roadmap, and Broadridge's modernization initiative. Every one of those incumbents is building this exact capability right now.

The AI kill test is where I'd spend the most time if I were on the fence, and I'm not on the fence. Sellara claims its AI "outperforms leading LLMs by 60% and runs 30x faster" — no published benchmark, no third-party validation. Even if true today, model capabilities improve every quarter. Bloomberg already sits inside every institutional trading operation Sellara wants to sell into. Bloomberg has the terminal, the data feeds, the compliance relationships, the existing contracts. "Deploy in weeks, not quarters" is a nice pitch against FIS's implementation cycles, but Bloomberg adding an AI workflow layer to their existing terminal is definitionally faster than a four-person startup navigating six-to-twelve-month vendor onboarding at a regulated financial institution. This is exactly the pattern I keep describing — every time you get pitched "Bloomberg for AI," you think, this is perfect for Bloomberg. The SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications are impressive for pre-seed and show the founders are serious, but certifications are table stakes for selling to banks, not moats.

The strongest bull case: Jolly's 180-client Rolodex is genuinely valuable distribution. If Sellara can convert even five percent of those relationships into six-figure enterprise contracts, they'd have meaningful revenue fast. The two Citi co-founders building workflow automation they personally needed — that's the kind of founder-market fit that produces real products, not toy demos. And the compliance certifications at pre-seed signal capital discipline and enterprise seriousness. Metropolis is my portfolio anchor for "AI amplifying physical-world operations," and Sellara shares the DNA of applying AI to unsexy operational infrastructure. But Metropolis has parking garages — physical assets that no LLM can replicate. Sellara has software agents sitting on top of commodity models, and every incumbent with distribution is looking at their demo thinking "we should add that feature." The structural asymmetry that made Metropolis a seed-to-$5B story doesn't exist here.

I'm passing. The decisive dimensions are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability. AI workflow automation for institutional finance is the most consensus AI application in the market right now — the pricing reflects the opportunity, and Beam AI's $4.5M existing revenue proves the category is already being captured by startups with more resources. And Bloomberg, FIS, and Broadridge will build this as a feature before Sellara can build it as a company. The founders are clearly competent, the domain expertise is real, and the compliance certifications show discipline — but competent founders in a consensus-hot category with no structural moat against incumbents is exactly how I described the AI application layer trap on Lonsdale's podcast. If this were a protocol-level bet on financial infrastructure — a new settlement primitive, a new clearing mechanism — I'd look harder. As an application-layer AI tool competing against incumbents with distribution, it's a feature, not a company.
