# Seeing Systems -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice here is the co-founder relationship. Alexander and Matthew Le Maitre are brothers from Guernsey. I've said cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate, and I've warned that nine companies in one batch added unknown co-founders and all nine fell apart. Brothers who grew up together are at the opposite end of that spectrum. They're not going to have a co-founder divorce when things get hard. And things will get hard — they're building military hardware in a war-influenced market with $500K and two people.

How did they find this problem? This is the question I always ask first, and the answer here is convincing. Alexander was already building Explosive Ordnance Disposal training hardware for military users before Seeing Systems existed. He wasn't sitting in a coffee shop reading about Ukraine and thinking "defense drones are hot." He was inside the military world, designing hardware for soldiers, learning what they needed and how procurement worked. Matthew was at Cambridge's Mobile Robotics Lab working on autonomous systems and context-aware drones — his dissertation was on simulating autonomous vehicle traffic. The problem grew organically from what both brothers were already doing with their hands and their heads. And the schlep here is staggering. ITAR compliance, UK export controls, EU dual-use regulations, military certification processes, hardware manufacturing, shipping prototypes to an active war zone, dealing with electronic warfare countermeasures that shift weekly. This is the kind of problem that causes most smart founders to unconsciously look away. Nobody wakes up and thinks "I want to navigate multi-nation arms export controls while simultaneously iterating on hardware that gets obsoleted in weeks." That's why the incumbents are slow and the barrier to genuine competition is higher than it appears from the outside.

The traction for a two-person pre-seed company is striking. UK Royal Marine Commandos. Multiple other UK military units. Four NATO forces. Prototypes shipping to Ukraine. Named operations — Kull with Estonian forces, Osprey with UK elite personnel. They have a Forward Deployed Engineer model, embedding themselves directly with military units for real-time feedback. That's literally doing things that don't scale, applied to defense. They're not building in a lab and hoping procurement catches up; they're physically going to the users, watching how the drones perform, and iterating on the spot. This is what I look for — the willingness to do the unscalable, hands-dirty work of being in the field. It reminds me of the Airbnb founders flying to New York to photograph listings, except these founders are flying to military exercises to watch their drones perform under conditions that approximate combat.

The technical coverage is unusually complete for a two-person team. Matthew brings the software and AI side — Cambridge computer science, Jane Street engineering discipline, autonomous systems research. Alexander brings the hardware — self-taught in embedded electronics, firmware, PCB design, with prior military hardware experience. Together they cover the full stack: the physical drone platforms, the embedded systems, and the swarm AI software. They build everything themselves. There's no slide saying "we'll hire engineers after we raise." The product exists. It's in the hands of soldiers.

The obvious objection is competition and commoditization. Anduril is at $30.5 billion. Shield AI at $5.6 billion. Stark raised $100 million and has a €2.4 billion German contract. Ukraine produces four million drones a year. Why would a two-person team with $500K survive this? But look more carefully. Anduril builds large autonomous platforms across all domains — different segment entirely. Shield AI builds AI pilots for larger aircraft. Stark, the closest competitor in expendable combat drones, failed all four strikes in UK military trials in October 2025 — while Seeing Systems is already embedded with the same UK military. The modularity thesis is the key differentiator: rather than building fixed-design drones that get obsoleted when EW countermeasures shift (which happens weekly in Ukraine), they build a platform where you swap the module that's been countered. The fibre-optic guided variant specifically addresses RF jamming, which is the dominant threat vector right now. Being small and fast in a domain where the obsolescence cycle is measured in weeks might actually be the right structural advantage. Big defense primes are optimized for multi-year platform programs. A two-person team iterating in the field iterates faster than a division at BAE Systems.

This is a hardware company in defense, which would normally be outside my comfort zone. I'm a software person. But I made my largest personal check in Boom Supersonic because the ambition was large enough and the importance was clear enough to override my software priors. Seeing Systems isn't at that scale of ambition — they're not reinventing supersonic flight — but they're attacking a problem that matters for NATO security, they discovered it organically through prior military work, and the schlep is so significant that it provides structural protection against the wave of defense-tech tourists chasing the trend. The gap is the economics: no revenue signal, defense procurement is slow, and $500K doesn't last long when you're manufacturing hardware. They'll need to raise again soon, and whether they can convert military engagement into actual contracts before the runway disappears is the central risk. But the organic origin, the enormous schlep, the brother co-founder bond, the full-stack technical capability, and the remarkably early military traction all point in the same direction.
