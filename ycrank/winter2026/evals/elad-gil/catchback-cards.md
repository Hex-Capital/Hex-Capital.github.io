# CatchBack Cards -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the tag line: Crypto / Web3, NFT. In 2026. Consumer NFT sentiment cratered in 2022 and never recovered. Attaching blockchain branding to a consumer product targeting mainstream card collectors isn't a technology bet -- it's a marketing headwind. The dossier itself flags this: "Consumer sentiment toward NFTs has significantly declined since 2022, and the crypto association could narrow the addressable audience." When I look at Coinbase, I backed crypto infrastructure during the blockchain emergence -- there was a structural adoption curve inflecting upward. Here, the consumer crypto wave has passed its mainstream moment. Cryptographic randomness verification is a genuinely useful feature, but you don't need blockchain branding to deliver it. The Nexus Layer 1 partnership and on-chain positioning feel like they serve the technology narrative more than the customer need, and that's a pattern I associate with companies building for the wrong audience.

The structural moment question is where this falls apart for me. What changed in the last 12-24 months that makes a mystery pack marketplace for trading cards possible now? The trading card boom started in 2020-2021 during COVID. Pokémon TCG hit record revenue in fiscal 2024, but that's a continuation of a multi-year trend, not an inflection point. The market is large -- $13-46 billion depending on how you scope it -- but it's consensus-large. Whatnot is valued at $11.5 billion. Fanatics is at $31 billion and already runs an "Under Wraps" mystery box program with official league licensing. This is the opposite of a non-obvious market. When I invested in PagerDuty, every other investor thought operations alerting was boring and niche. Here, every investor already knows trading cards are big -- the question is whether a two-person startup can carve out defensible territory against billion-dollar incumbents who can add the same feature set in a quarter.

The multi-miracle problem is severe. CatchBack needs to simultaneously: (1) achieve marketplace liquidity starting from 700 users, (2) fund buyback guarantees from their own balance sheet in a physical-goods inventory business, (3) navigate IP enforcement risk from Pokémon Company, MLB, NBA, and other licensors who actively police distribution of their products, and (4) outmaneuver Whatnot and Fanatics who have distribution at scale. That's at least three independent miracles, and I've been explicit about this framework -- compounding low-probability events means you're very likely to fail. The buyback guarantee in particular concerns me: this creates a capital-intensive balance sheet business with physical inventory and shipping, not a software-driven platform. Remove the cryptographic verification and you have a card dealer with an app. That's software-aware, not software-driven -- the margin structure is physical goods, not software.

The strongest bull case: the "pack rip" experience is genuinely addictive and behaviorally distinct from what Whatnot (live-stream, synchronous) and eBay (search-based) offer. $13K MRR with 700 users is real monetization at pre-seed, which means roughly $18-19 per user per month -- collectors are spending real money. If CatchBack could own the asynchronous, gamified pack-rip format and the Steph Curry collaboration drives a viral moment, there's a path where this becomes the default mystery pack experience for a generation of mobile-first collectors. The cryptographic verification, stripped of blockchain branding and positioned simply as "provably fair packs," could become a trust standard that creates switching costs. And Joseph Zhang's combination of AWS engineering, McKinsey strategy, and genuine card-collecting passion is the kind of founder-market fit where the person understands both the technology and the customer because they ARE the customer. For this to work, though, CatchBack would need to win the pack-rip format before Fanatics or Whatnot claim it -- and I see no structural reason why those incumbents couldn't replicate the experience with their existing distribution and licensing advantages.

Joseph Zhang shipped a working iOS app and hit $13K MRR within weeks of launch -- that's real execution velocity. His background at AWS and Nexus gives him the engineering credibility to build the product, and quitting his job to sell Pokémon cards shows the kind of founder intensity I value. But Sophia Kim's background being completely undocumented is a real gap in a two-person company. When I evaluate early-stage teams, I need to understand what both founders bring -- and here I can only assess half the team. The product-to-distribution trajectory has some interesting elements: marketplace, buyback, card-backed loans, peer lending. But expanding into lending adds regulatory complexity (lending is regulated at the state level) without the structural advantage I had at Color Health where the regulatory barrier itself became the moat. Here, regulation is a cost center, not a competitive advantage.

I'm passing. The market is consensus, the timing thesis relies on a technology cycle (consumer crypto) that's contracting rather than expanding, the business has physical-goods margin structure with inventory and shipping costs, and the path to success requires overcoming multiple independent obstacles against entrenched competitors with orders-of-magnitude more distribution. The strongest signal -- $13K MRR with fast execution -- isn't enough to override the structural concerns.
