# Beacon Health -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Beacon Health: the most promising companies I've backed tend to be unpopular, difficult to assess, and actively dismissed by other investors. "AI agents for primary care" is none of those things. In 2026, healthcare AI is arguably the most consensus category in venture capital. Aledade has a billion dollars in revenue. Pearl Health raised $75 million from Andreessen. Innovaccer is valued at $3.45 billion. Stellar Health has raised $165 million. The fact that every major firm is pouring capital into VBC enablement and healthcare AI tells me the weirdness has been arbitraged away. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, people thought Elon was out of his mind. When I backed Contraline, VCs wouldn't even take the meeting because male contraception sounded too strange. Beacon Health doesn't have that quality — it sounds sensible, fundable, and exactly like what every health tech pitch deck looks like right now. That's a problem for how I invest.

Mark Pothen's biographical thread is genuine, and I want to be honest about that. He grew up in his mother's primary care practice — that's not manufactured founder-market fit, that's a kid who watched his mom drown in paperwork and administrative burden while trying to care for patients. Then he went and embedded himself in a practice for six months, living inside the operations he wants to automate. I respect that. It reminds me of how I found Uber — not through a pitch deck but through years of watching the taxi medallion system up close, through my driver Roger, through lived frustration with a broken system. Mark has that lived frustration. But I'm looking for the biographical grit that says "this person has been through something that forged them" — the kind of resilience you can't fake. What I see here is a capable early-career founder with a relevant background, not someone whose survival story and their company's mission are inseparable. He went to engineering school, did product management, started a company. It's a solid path, but it doesn't give me the signal I got from the Crusoe founders or from Travis Kalanick's intensity.

The bull case is real and I don't want to dismiss it. Six customer logos and a 40,000-patient IPA deployment at pre-seed — that's not nothing. That's a product that works and that people are willing to put into their workflows, which in healthcare is exceptionally hard. The "autonomous agent inside the EHR" positioning is genuinely differentiated from the dashboards-and-analytics approach everyone else takes. If Beacon can execute on the promise of acting like a human employee inside the EHR — closing coding gaps, executing outreach, ordering labs — rather than just surfacing recommendations a burned-out clinician has to act on, that's a meaningful product leap. And there's a version of this where the economic access story resonates with me: independent primary care practices are dying, squeezed between fee-for-service revenue pressure and the administrative burden of VBC contracts. A tool that helps those practices capture the revenue they're already entitled to could be the difference between a community keeping its doctor and losing them. That's making an invisible system visible — showing practices the money they're leaving on the table — which maps to my Carta and Affirm pattern. For this company to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that the autonomous execution layer is genuinely defensible, that EHR vendors won't shut them out, and that the founders can outrun billions of dollars in funded competition through sheer depth of integration and workflow knowledge.

But I don't believe those things with enough conviction to write a check. The commoditization risk is severe — the core technology of LLM agents executing structured workflows inside EHRs is reproducible by any well-funded team with healthcare domain knowledge. Fourteen other startups are already building healthcare AI agents. Epic and Oracle are building their own. The EHR integration that creates switching costs today could become a vulnerability tomorrow if those platforms restrict third-party access. And the co-founder dynamic is opaque — Mark is from NJIT in New Jersey, Obinna is from UT Austin in Texas, no shared employer or university overlap, no visible story about how they found each other or how they handle disagreement. After HQ Trivia taught me that co-founder conflict destroys even perfectly timed products, I need to understand that relationship before I invest. I can't evaluate what I can't see.

This is a competent team building a real product in a real market, and I think they'll find investors who are excited about it. But it doesn't set off the alarm bells of recognition that my best investments have triggered. I'm not feeling the jolt. When something is popular and well-understood and every smart investor in the room nods along — that's usually the moment I start looking for the exit, not the entry.
