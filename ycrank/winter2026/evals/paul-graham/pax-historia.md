# Pax Historia -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Thirty-five thousand daily active users in four months, organically, with a team of three. That number stopped me. At YC we've funded over five thousand companies and I can count on two hands the pre-seed teams that hit those numbers before Demo Day. The community has self-organized into fan wikis, a subreddit, TikTok tutorials, and four thousand user-created scenario presets. When users start building infrastructure around your product without being asked — encyclopedias, guides, derivative content — that's not marketing. That's love. This is the strongest user pull signal in this batch that I've seen described.

But here's what nags at me. When I ask the question I always ask first — how did the founders find this problem? — the answer is thin. One co-founder's YC bio is "I like history." That's endearing. It is not the same as Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive on the Chinatown bus, or Brian Chesky not making rent. Liking history is a hobby. The idea — use LLMs to generate emergent geopolitical narratives in a strategy game — is exactly the kind of thing a smart team would generate in a brainstorming session in 2024 when LLMs suddenly became capable enough. It sits in the intersection of two hot trends: AI and gaming. That doesn't make it wrong, but it does make me worry about the origin. The best startup ideas are discovered, not invented. A fan-made clone already exists on GitHub, which tells you this idea occurred to multiple people simultaneously. That's usually a sign you're building in a space where the problem is obvious and the schlep is low — the opposite of what I look for.

The schlep test is where I'd normally get excited or walk away, and here the answer is complicated. Running 100 billion tokens per week is operationally nontrivial. Making AI responses consistent enough to sustain deep strategy gameplay — where players expect cause and effect to be coherent across hundreds of turns — is genuinely hard. The VGTimes review documents real problems: AI ignoring orders, pacing issues from latency, a "systemic paradox" where optimal play involves running ChatGPT alongside the game. These aren't bugs to be fixed; they're fundamental tensions in the architecture. But this is technical schlep, not the kind of regulatory-logistical-human schlep that creates lasting barriers. Stripe had to deal with banks and compliance regimes that took years to navigate. Here, the schlep is "make LLMs behave consistently in a game context," which is hard but also something that improves automatically as models improve. The moat isn't in the schlep; it's in the community.

That community-as-moat argument is the strongest bull case. The UGC marketplace — where players create, publish, and discover scenario presets — starts to look like Roblox for grand strategy. Each new preset makes the platform more valuable; each new player is a potential creator. If this flywheel compounds, you get the kind of defensibility that no LLM wrapper alone provides. Four thousand presets with fifty-plus plays each means the content library already has enough depth to retain new users. The question is whether the flywheel's spin rate can outrun the commoditization of the underlying AI mechanic. If a well-resourced competitor — say Paradox with an AI studio, or Latitude pivoting from AI Dungeon — launches a competing product, can Pax Historia's community library hold users? At 35K DAU the lead is real but not insurmountable.

The business model worries me more than anything else here. A dollar buys fifteen to twenty minutes of gameplay. That's the economics of an arcade in 1985, not a modern free-to-play game. Inference costs are the marginal cost of every player action, which means the company gets less default alive as it grows, not more — the opposite of software economics. They're processing 100 billion tokens a week and their revenue model depends on costs continuing to fall. That's a bet on the entire AI infrastructure industry, not on their own execution. The job postings advertising $150K-$240K salaries suggest they're staffing for growth rather than aiming for ramen profitable. I'd want to see a much clearer path to unit economics before I felt comfortable that this company controls its own destiny.

The founders built something real with three people and clearly have technical ability — you don't serve 35K DAU with a browser game processing that token volume without genuine engineering skill. Eli's Virginia Tech engineering background and Devpost portfolio suggest a builder, not a manager. But I don't see evidence of the specific kind of relentless resourcefulness that separates great founders from good ones. No story of overcoming a crisis, no creative survival hack, no evidence of having been told no and finding another way. They may have those stories — the dossier just doesn't surface them. What I see is a competent technical team that caught a wave at the right moment. Catching a wave is good. But I invest in founders who can keep surfing when the wave changes shape.

The honest summary: this company has the best user traction signal I could ask for at this stage, sitting inside an idea that makes me nervous because it feels born from timing rather than pain. If the community flywheel becomes the core product and the AI layer becomes commodity infrastructure underneath it, this could be significant. If the AI layer *is* the product and it gets commoditized, the 35K DAU evaporates. I'd watch this one closely through the batch and look for evidence that the founders are building toward community lock-in rather than AI novelty.
