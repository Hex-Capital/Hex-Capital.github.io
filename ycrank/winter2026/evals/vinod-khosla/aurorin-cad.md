# Aurorin CAD -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Jared Friedman made a statement that stopped me: building a CAD application from scratch — something that took SolidWorks decades and 10 million lines of code — is now "plausible" for a small team because AI coding crossed a threshold. If that claim is true, it's not just a statement about one startup. It's a statement about the entire software industry's competitive moat structure collapsing. The most interesting thing about Aurorin isn't the CAD product — it's the meta-thesis that AI-assisted development has compressed the engineering effort required to challenge deeply entrenched, technically complex incumbents. That thesis, if correct, opens a category of venture investment that didn't previously exist: full-stack rebuilds of complex enterprise software by tiny teams.

Let me apply my standard first question: if Aurorin succeeds, what's the consequence? They displace SolidWorks, NX, Creo, CATIA — the geometric kernels built in the 1980s that still constrain how every physical product on earth gets designed. The direct market is $12.5 billion and growing. But the second-order effect is what interests me more: CAD is the bottleneck for all hardware innovation. When AutoCAD replaced drafting boards, it didn't just save drafting time — it changed what was designable. If an AI-native kernel makes mechanical engineering 10x faster, you accelerate the development cycle for every robot, every rocket, every medical device, every manufactured object. That's a lever on the physical world's innovation velocity. It's not fusion energy, but it's a foundational tool for the kind of physical-world transformation my portfolio is built around — Rocket Lab needs faster CAD, Commonwealth Fusion needs faster CAD, every hardware company does. The consequence is meaningful, though bounded compared to the problems I find hardest to walk away from.

The structural setup reminds me of Juniper in 1996. Cisco dominated networking with legacy architecture, and their CTO told me they'd never build a TCP/IP router. The incumbents had every structural disincentive to rebuild: VAR channel dependencies, perpetual-license revenue, massive installed bases generating maintenance fees. The opening was an architectural paradigm shift — TCP/IP replacing proprietary protocols — that the incumbents couldn't adopt without cannibalizing themselves. Aurorin faces an analogous setup: Dassault, Siemens, and PTC cannot rebuild their kernels from scratch without destroying their existing businesses, and PTC proved this by paying $470 million to acquire Onshape rather than building internally. The paradigm shift here is AI-native design plus GPU acceleration, and the only way to fully exploit it is to control the kernel — which is exactly what Aurorin is doing and what Adam, Zoo, and every AI-layer startup cannot do. Building on Parasolid and then adding a chat interface is like strapping a jet engine to a horse carriage. The geometry engine has to be designed for AI from the ground up.

The founder profile is interesting in the right ways. Michael Baron has no college degree, interned at SpaceX three times on propulsion and GNC, and optimized GPU drivers at Apple. He's an outsider to the CAD software industry — he's never built CAD tools, he's used them. That's the distinction I value: he knows the pain without knowing what the industry considers impossible, which frees him to attempt a ground-up rebuild. The SpaceX background isn't just a credential — it means he's designed complex hardware in legacy CAD environments and experienced the four-hour assembly opens firsthand. The Apple GPU work provides specific technical foundation for building a GPU-accelerated kernel. What concerns me is that he's alone. Not "one person, not even a company" in the Impossible Foods sense — where the problem's magnitude made one person sufficient to justify the bet — but one person attempting what is arguably the most technically complex category of software engineering that exists. Building a production-grade parametric B-Rep kernel with constraint solvers, file format interoperability, sheet metal, surfacing, and the hundred other features mechanical engineers require — this isn't a chat app. No co-founder, no advisors identified, not hiring. That's my single largest concern.

The timing thesis is specific and credible in a way most "AI is hot" arguments are not. Friedman isn't saying "AI is a trend you should ride." He's saying a specific capability threshold — AI coding tools — has made a specific engineering task feasible that was previously infeasible for a small team. That's the kind of "why now" I look for: a technology curve crossing a threshold that opens a category. GPU compute costs have fallen, GPU programming models have matured, and LLMs can serve as genuine design copilots for parametric operations. The window is open now. But I also note that the AI-for-CAD space is no longer empty — Adam has $4.1 million and 10 million social media impressions, Zoo has $5.12 million. The full-stack kernel approach is still contrarian, but the broader category is attracting capital and attention. The question is whether Aurorin can build kernel completeness fast enough to matter before the plugin approaches gain too much user mindshare.

If I'm wrong to hesitate, here's what would have to be true: AI-assisted development really has compressed CAD kernel development from a multi-hundred-person, multi-decade effort to something a small team can achieve in 2-3 years. The kernel reaches sufficient feature completeness to handle real production engineering, not just demo parts. And the AI-native architecture creates such a profound usability gap — not 30% better, but 10x better — that engineers switch despite the massive friction of CAD migration. If all three hold, this is a generational company in a $12+ billion market with structural advantages that compound over time. I'd take a small option-value bet at the YC seed level to watch whether Baron can recruit a founding team that addresses the kernel completeness and enterprise go-to-market risks. The technical thesis is sound, the timing is right, and the architectural strategy is correct. But the gap between a compelling demo and a production CAD system is measured in years of grinding kernel engineering, and right now it's one person against that mountain.
