# Voxel Energy -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Voxel Energy: the market they're in is the single most consensus investment thesis in technology right now. "Data centers need more power" is on the cover of every trade publication, on every VC's slide deck, and already has billions of dollars flowing toward it. Exowatt has $140M from a16z and Sam Altman. Crusoe just raised over $2B at a $10B valuation. The Washington Post is writing features about off-grid data center power. When the Washington Post is writing about your category, you are not pursuing an insane idea. You are pursuing the most obviously sane idea in the market. And that's where my framework starts to break down for this company -- because the founders are genuinely credible, which means we have a sane team with a sane idea, which in my book is the worst possible configuration for a seed investment. The returns are already being priced in by every capital allocator in the ecosystem.

Now, let me steelman the team because they deserve it. Max Pfeiffer didn't study batteries in a classroom -- he built an actual electric vehicle company, Maxwell Vehicles, that's been delivering since 2019. Forbes 30 Under 30, power electronics from Tesla and UW's hyperloop program. This is a person who understands DC power systems at the component level, and the DC-native architecture claim -- 4% conversion loss versus 30% in traditional AC systems -- is the kind of insight that comes from having your hands on physical hardware, not from reading whitepapers. Evan Schmidt brings a decade of data center construction management. Casey Spencer has three hardware companies behind him plus Tesla Autopilot project management. This is a team that could actually build what they're describing, which puts them in the top quartile of credibility for this problem. If I were scoring founder quality alone, I'd be enthusiastic.

What I find most interesting about Voxel is the second-life EV battery bet. That supply chain is about to go from roughly 25-30 GWh today to 330-350 GWh by 2030 -- a 65% CAGR. Being early in aggregating degraded battery packs, understanding their heterogeneous chemistries, and building the management systems to make them reliable at data center SLAs is genuinely hard operational work that software cannot replicate. This has structural parallels to Teamshares aggregating small businesses -- an unglamorous, operationally complex task that most competitors won't have the patience to execute. The claim of "thousands of acres under contract" and actively amassing battery inventory at pre-seed suggests real physical assets as a downside cushion, which I weight heavily. The Sift partnership for real-time battery degradation monitoring tells me they know the reliability question is the technical risk that matters.

Where the thesis falls apart for me is on the "new primitive" question. Voxel is not creating a layer that other businesses build on. They're selling powered data center capacity -- a service, not a protocol. Every megawatt they deploy requires proportional capital expenditure. There are no network effects, no compounding dynamics, no winner-take-most trajectory. This is infrastructure-as-a-service, and while infrastructure businesses can be excellent, they don't produce the exponential return curves I need at seed. Craftwork works for me because painting is a local market with fragmented competition and low capital intensity. Data center power is a global market where your competitors have literal billions in the bank. The capital-intensity mismatch between a 3-person pre-seed team and the scale required to matter in this market is severe -- and unlike a protocol play where code scales infinitely, every unit of capacity here costs real money to deploy.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if the DC-native architecture genuinely delivers 4% conversion losses at scale, and if they can lock up second-life battery supply before the market matures, and if the well-funded competitors' different approaches (thermal storage, natural gas) prove inferior to battery-solar for the specific use case of AI training workloads -- then Voxel could emerge as the preferred off-grid solution despite being outspent 1000-to-1. The fact that Exowatt, Crusoe, and Enchanted Rock are each pursuing fundamentally different technical approaches means Voxel isn't competing head-to-head on identical technology. They're making a distinct bet on which energy storage medium wins. And the "cherry on top" AI angle is real -- battery management software that predicts degradation and optimizes dispatch across heterogeneous packs would make this business significantly more efficient without constituting the moat itself. If I squint, there's a version of this that looks like early Helium: physical infrastructure with a technology edge, dismissed by mainstream investors as too operationally complex, that compounds over years. But Helium was dismissed; Voxel's category is being aggressively bid up. That difference in market sentiment is the difference between non-consensus returns and consensus returns.

I respect the team and I respect the physical moat, but I'm passing. My check goes to companies where the idea itself is the filter -- where most investors hear the pitch and say "that's crazy." When most investors hear "off-grid power for AI data centers," they say "where do I sign?" That's the opposite of what I'm looking for.
