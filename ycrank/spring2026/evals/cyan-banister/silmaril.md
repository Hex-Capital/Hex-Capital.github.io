# Silmaril -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Aum Upadhyay and Eduardo Velasco hacked OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Microsoft fifteen times in two weeks before they ever shipped Silmaril, which means the founder dimension is the one place Silmaril punches well above its stage -- and yet the category they walked into is exactly the consensus AI-security lane where Lakera already sold to Check Point and Protect AI Guardian already sold to Palo Alto Networks in 2025. That high/low pairing is the signature: lived-experience whitehats inside an aesthetically obvious, already-being-rolled-up category. Silmaril is the inverse of a Crusoe -- founder-of-the-place, but the place itself is the most-photographed corner of the AI-tools batch.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril is the load-bearing analogy for what Silmaril gets right on origin: a founder who shipped something concrete in the adjacent domain before pitching the company, not a PM who read about the problem in a memo. Eduardo chaining a prompt injection to root inside ChatGPT, and Aum's stint building the AWS security/privacy framework cited internally at $1.8B in damages prevented, are autobiographical artifacts -- they touched the bug before they sold the firewall. Where Silmaril diverges from the Anduril rung is that Luckey had a *product* (Oculus) sold to Facebook for $2B as evidence of shipping capacity; the Silmaril pair have exploit screenshots and a ModernBERT classifier, not a prior company shipped to scale. That gap is why they land in the "lived-experience" half of the founder ladder but not at the top of it.

The Pass Example founder -- two ex-FAANG PMs, Stanford CS, $20M seed oversubscribed, six top funds chasing -- is the mechanism Silmaril most resembles on the non-consensus dimension, even though the founders themselves are not that archetype. Garry Tan personally posting the launch on X on April 14, 2026, Microsoft/OpenAI/Anthropic/Google/Perplexity/Dropbox arranged as a logo strip on the homepage, and a TAM citation of $7.99B at 32.5% CAGR are the exact deck-furniture I treat as a deselection signal. The "find them before it becomes consensus" filter wants two sophisticated investors to have visibly passed on aesthetic grounds -- Silmaril's dossier shows the opposite: YC's partner-channel megaphone is pointed straight at it, and four well-capitalized incumbents (HiddenLayer $56M, Promptfoo $23.6M, Lakera $30M pre-acquisition, Protect AI inside PANW) are already crowding the runtime-defense seat.

Single-CEO governance is the one structural risk Silmaril sidesteps without drama: Aum is CEO, Eduardo is CTO, titles are delineated on LinkedIn, and there is no hint of the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll co-equal feud or the GameCrush activist-investor mess. Eighteen months out, the test is whether Aum can keep Eduardo on the technical side as the company tries to land enterprise contracts that demand a single throat to choke -- absent a published founder-vesting or tiebreaker doc, I score this above the absent-evidence default but not at the ceiling, because the conversation is implied, not documented.

Capital-intensity is where Silmaril fails the Vigil-Labs / Crusoe filter cleanly: a P90 20ms classifier wrapped around inference calls is software-only, no hardware moat, no regulatory clause that protects the incumbent for two years, and the SOC/ISO 27001/CSA badges are table stakes for selling into any enterprise SaaS, not a compounding moat. If Silmaril were a fertility clinic, a defense drone, or a regulated lending stack, the score would flip a tier; as a guardrail API positioned next to "over a dozen guardrail providers" per TrueFoundry's 2026 review and open-source NeMo Guardrails, the moat is the team, not the category.

The physical-verifiability axis closest to Silmaril is Postmates -- where I could *watch* the delivery happen in front of me at The Lobby. The dossier gives me five lines of code into LangGraph and a 95.60% accuracy claim across 131 attack techniques, self-published with no third-party benchmark. I cannot ride this, I cannot photograph it, and the case-study damage figures ($68M, $20M, $28M cumulative) are the company's own arithmetic. A normal human cannot install Silmaril in 48 hours and feel the protection -- only a developer integrating it into an agent stack can, and even then the verification is statistical, not tactile.

My 36-month forecast for Silmaril: acquired between $40M and $90M by a security incumbent that already owns the enterprise-AppSec channel -- Cloudflare, Datadog, Wiz, or CrowdStrike -- as a feature bundle and acqui-hire of the two whitehats, not a standalone billion-dollar runtime-defense category leader. The mechanism producing that outcome is the same one that ate Lakera and Protect AI Guardian in 2025: prompt-injection defense is real, but it does not survive as a standalone procurement line item once the enterprise security platforms ship a default. Silmaril's founder caliber gets it a soft landing, not a Stargate.

The bull case that stings: Lakera was *also* a small classifier-firewall team and Check Point paid for it anyway, which means the acqui-bundle outcome I just forecast could itself be a 5-10x return on a pre-seed check -- and the Aum/Eduardo whitehat origin is exactly the kind of demo-able provenance that drives enterprise sales when the buyer is a CISO who watched them pop ChatGPT. The retort is that Lakera raised $30M and had eighteen months of design-partner work before Check Point cut the check; Silmaril is competing for the same enterprise pilots as HiddenLayer's $56M war chest at preseed, and the "Microsoft/OpenAI/Anthropic/Google" homepage logos are described in the underlying LinkedIn post as whitehat *targets*, not paying customers -- the dossier explicitly flags commercial-relationship ambiguity. I would flip to invest if Silmaril produces one signed, named, paid Fortune-500 contract (not a logo strip, not a vulnerability disclosure credit) within six months of the YC demo day. Until then, Silmaril is a magically-talented pair in a magically-obvious category, and I am never playing the game I seem to be playing -- I want the *next* lane, not this one.
