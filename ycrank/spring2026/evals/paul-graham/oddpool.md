# Oddpool -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Oddpool is a schlep-spotter built by contractors to the problem rather than natives of it -- Avi Arora and Ritesh Malpani correctly identified that quant desks spend "weeks normalizing data" across Kalshi and Polymarket, and they have the Microsoft-ML and Bloomberg-buy-side chops to build the plumbing, but the dossier nowhere shows either founder personally lost money on a prediction-market trade because their tick data was missing. The highest dimension here is the schlep itself; the lowest is the organic-pain origin. That pairing is the whole tell.

The schlep ladder is where Oddpool gets most of its weight. **Stripe** (2010) was the canonical schlep: the Collisons ran toward payments-rails compliance work that every other YC applicant in their batch was unconsciously routing around, and that grind became the moat. Oddpool's grind is the same shape one rung down -- 700,000+ indexed markets with historical orderbooks back to March 2026 at 1-5 minute granularity is exactly the kind of dataset that cannot be backfilled by a later entrant, and the fact that Polymarket *bought* Dome on February 19, 2026 rather than building neutral aggregation themselves is the market telling you the work is unpleasant in a structurally productive way. Where Oddpool diverges from the Stripe rung is that the Collisons had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007); Oddpool's founders arrived at prediction-market data because they spotted CFTC application filings climbing, not because they were already inside the trade.

The pass case I keep replaying is the AI-wrapper pitch from my own Pass Example -- the team that spotted a market opportunity rather than scratching a personal itch -- and the failure mechanism there is that when the market shifts, founders without personal pain in the domain lack the compass to know where to point next. Oddpool's dossier reads disturbingly close to that pattern on dimension two: Avi was training prompt-injection models at Microsoft securing 20B requests/year, Ritesh was making Bloomberg buy-side trading 40% faster, and nowhere does the dossier say either of them lost a backtest, ran a Kalshi arbitrage, or built `Awesome-Prediction-Market-Tools` on GitHub *because* they themselves needed it for their own trading. The repo is a curator's artifact, not a sufferer's. **Dropbox** (2007) is Drew Houston's USB stick. Oddpool is Avi reading CFTC filings.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism that matters over 18 months is whether founders compound shipped artifacts faster than well-funded competitors compound headcount. Oddpool has "several thousand dollars in MRR within months" from the prosumer tier and 762 arb opportunities surfaced for Pro users last week -- this is shipping, not deck-building, and it puts Oddpool above the floor. But there is no cereal-box-equivalent stunt in the dossier; no anecdote where Avi or Ritesh did something nobody else would do to get the first dollar. The MRR came from a $30/mo Stripe checkout, not from an animal moment.

On hacker genealogy, the counterfactual is what would have to be true to flip this dimension a tier: a published technical artifact predating Oddpool that demonstrated taste, not just throughput. Avi's prompt-injection model at Microsoft scaled to 20B requests/year is real production engineering, and Ritesh's distributed-systems MS plus Bloomberg trading-system work is real plumbing experience -- this is competent technical lineage. What's missing is the **Stripe** (2010)-Lisp-prize signal: a hacker-community artifact (a competitive-programming win, an open-source library others build on, a recognized handle) that I'd recognize independently of the resume. The `aarora4` GitHub repo is a links list, not a library.

The co-founder configuration on Oddpool is structurally **Reddit** (2005) -- Avi and Ritesh have been roommates since freshman year at Georgia Tech, which is the Huffman/Ohanian dorm pattern that historically predicts the kind of disagreements that don't break teams. The problem is that Ritesh's LinkedIn lists him as co-founder of Benchspan (YC P26), a separate active Spring 2026 YC company. This is not a side project; it is a peer-batch parallel commitment on a two-person team. I have funded re-treads (**Twitch** (2007) after **Kiko** (2005)) but I have not knowingly funded a co-founder running two YC startups in the same batch, because the YC bootcamp is a full-attention instrument.

My 36-month forecast: Oddpool gets acquired by Kalshi or Polymarket for $8-20M in the same shape and on the same clock as Dome -- which raised $5.2M and exited 4-5 months post-Demo Day for an estimated $10-20M per Gabriel Jarrosson. The mechanism is that venue-neutrality is structurally unstable; the moment either Kalshi or Polymarket decides cross-venue data leakage is hurting their order flow, the cheapest defense is to buy the aggregator. The 700K-market history dataset is the asset that gets bought, not the company that gets scaled.

The strongest opposing case is that Dome's exit is bullish evidence, not bearish: the slot exists, the buyers have shown they will pay, and Oddpool sits in the slot Dome vacated with a better dataset (tick-level orderbook depth versus FinFeedAPI's REST snapshots) and a real freemium funnel feeding the enterprise motion. Under "Black Swan Farming" (September 2012) the right move is to back the slot even at a $10-20M outcome because variance pays the portfolio. The dossier evidence that breaks the bull case is the founder split: Ritesh running Benchspan simultaneously means Oddpool is structurally a one-and-a-half-founder company, and one-and-a-half-founder companies do not become **Stripe** (2010) -- they become acquihires. One condition flips my verdict to Invest: Ritesh publicly winds down Benchspan and commits to Oddpool full-time before Demo Day. Until then, this is a Neutral that I'd watch, not write.
