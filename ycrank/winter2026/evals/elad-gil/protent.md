# Protent -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Protent is the competitive landscape, not the product. AI video surveillance is one of the most heavily funded segments in security tech right now: Verkada at $5.8 billion valuation with $1 billion in annualized bookings, Lumana AI at $64 million raised building vision-language models for the same "turn any camera into a proactive sensor" positioning, Spot AI at $93 million raised. When I see five well-funded companies within a two-degree radius of the same value proposition, that's not a non-obvious market -- it's consensus. Protent's bet is that law enforcement is a distinct enough vertical to warrant a specialized player, and that's a reasonable GTM wedge, but it's a wedge into a market where well-resourced incumbents can extend with a product decision rather than a company decision. Verkada adding a "law enforcement mode" is a feature sprint, not a strategic pivot. The market archetype test fails here -- this isn't PagerDuty where operations alerting seemed boring and nobody was funding it. This is a category with nearly a billion dollars in recent venture capital.

The structural moment question is more nuanced. Vision-language models crossing the capability threshold for real-time multi-feed analysis with behavioral understanding IS a legitimate inflection point -- two years ago you couldn't do sentiment-aware escalation detection across hundreds of simultaneous camera feeds at acceptable latency and cost. That's real. But the timing advantage accrues to every player in the space simultaneously. When I invested in Harvey, the GPT scaling curve was a timing insight that most legal-tech investors hadn't internalized. Here, every AI video company has already internalized the VLM capability jump -- Lumana literally built their entire product around it. The "why now" exists but isn't proprietary to Protent's thesis.

Where Protent genuinely surprises me is the deployment velocity. Three major metro police departments -- Atlanta, Chicago, St. Louis -- at pre-seed with a two-person team and $500K. Government procurement is supposed to take 12-24 months with RFPs, compliance reviews, and budget approvals. Getting deployed in three metros before your YC batch starts suggests either exceptional hustle, existing relationships from the Lockheed Martin work, or pilot programs that aren't yet formal contracts. Either way, it's a meaningful execution signal. I learned at Google that the best founders compress timelines that everyone else treats as fixed -- and government timelines are the ones most people consider immovable.

The bull case for Protent maps closely to the Anduril pattern in my portfolio: a GovTech company where regulatory complexity (CJIS compliance, evidence chain-of-custody requirements, law enforcement procurement certification) creates switching costs that deter software-focused competitors. Once you're integrated into a police department's dispatch workflow and your escalation models are trained on their specific deployment patterns, ripping you out is operationally painful. The data flywheel from law enforcement-specific scenarios -- what does pre-violence escalation actually look like across different urban environments -- could create genuine defensibility that a commercial-first player like Verkada can't replicate from their enterprise data. If that thesis holds, Protent becomes the embedded intelligence layer for American law enforcement, and the expansion path into federal agencies, DHS, and allied nations is massive. That's the version of this company that matters.

But the Anduril analogy breaks down on two fronts. First, Anduril entered a structural vacuum -- Google's Project Maven cancellation meant there was literally no credible startup building AI for defense. There's no equivalent vacuum in law enforcement video AI; it's a crowded adjacent market with well-funded competitors who could shift focus. Second, Anduril had Palmer Luckey, who brought celebrity, defense-establishment relationships, and proven ability to build hardware at scale. Protent's founders bring relevant technical backgrounds -- the Lockheed Martin video intelligence experience is genuinely applicable -- but they appear to be in their early twenties with no prior evidence of navigating the kind of enterprise government sales cycles that define success in GovTech. The product can be technically excellent, but selling multi-year contracts to municipal police departments requires a sales motion these founders haven't demonstrated they can build. More fundamentally, this company faces a multi-miracle problem: they need (1) technically superior real-time analysis, (2) successful government procurement at scale, and (3) navigation of surveillance ethics and regulatory risk that could eliminate their market in specific jurisdictions. Multiple cities have already banned AI surveillance technologies. That's three independent low-probability obstacles, and my framework says that's two too many.

The surveillance ethics dimension is the wildcard that makes me least comfortable. Protent's messaging focuses on escalation detection rather than facial recognition, which is smart positioning. But "AI system monitors citizens in real time and alerts police to potential threats based on behavioral signals" is one investigative journalism piece away from a political firestorm, regardless of the technical nuance. The YC LinkedIn post already generated substantial critical commentary referencing dystopian surveillance. Regulatory risk in this space isn't theoretical -- it's actively materializing. This isn't a moat-through-regulation pattern like Coinbase navigating financial compliance. This is a market where regulation could be antagonistic rather than protective.

I respect the execution velocity and the founder-market fit on the technical side. But the core market isn't non-obvious, the competitive dynamics are unfavorable at their scale, the multi-miracle problem is real, and the regulatory environment is actively hostile in ways that create existential risk rather than structural moats. This is a pass for me.
