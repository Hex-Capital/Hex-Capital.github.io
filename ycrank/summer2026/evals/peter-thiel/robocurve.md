# Robocurve -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Robocurve is a credentialed-outsider pair — Jay Chooi with a Rhodes Scholarship, an IOAA Gold Medal, and top-7% contributor status on the UK AISI Inspect Evals repo — building third-party infrastructure for a category where the buyers self-report and the substitutes are free. That is the signature. The highest dimension is founder lineage; the lowest is monopoly trajectory. Everything else about Robocurve is downstream of that pairing.

Palantir (2003) is the load-bearing analogy on the dimension where Robocurve almost activates my framework: the contrarian secret. Palantir's secret was that intelligence-grade analytics required a customer Silicon Valley refused to sell to, and the moat was integration depth no consensus VC would underwrite. Robocurve's stated secret — frontier robotics labs (Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, Figure) cannot credibly grade themselves, so an independent operator captures the "trusted score" position — is structurally the right shape of thesis. But it terminates at the wrong incumbent. Palantir's incumbent was the CIA's inability to correlate data across silos; Robocurve's incumbent is a set of pre-revenue labs racing to 2028 who have every incentive to keep evals in-house. That is a gap in the customer's willingness to be judged, not a blind spot in the incumbent's architecture. The secret is real but soft.

RoboEval and ManipulationNet are the failure mechanism I need to interrogate on monopoly trajectory. ManipulationNet is a five-university consortium — Rice, NIST, MIT, Berkeley, CMU — publishing reproducible robotics benchmarks for free, and RobotPerf is a similarly free open-source computing-benchmark consortium. This is the exact pattern that killed cleantech-adjacent measurement companies in the last cycle: when the reference standard is a public good, the commercial operator gets no pricing power at maturity. Robocurve's registration as a Public Benefit Corporation with a "Societal Preparedness" mission is the founders themselves telegraphing that they read the market the same way. The Anduril override — regulated customer creating switching costs — is not available here because DoD robotics evals would flow to NIST, not to a PBC startup.

On definite plan, the mechanism analysis is straightforward: Robocurve has shipped Inspect Robots v1, published worldevals on PyPI, and completed one pilot on a real robot in eighteen months of existence. That is a definite artifact, not indefinite iteration. But the 24-month trajectory compounds only if a paying frontier lab signs before the ManipulationNet consortium expands its charter, and the dossier discloses no such contract. The definite plan is present at the software-artifact layer and absent at the commercial-milestone layer, which is the layer that matters for pricing-power accumulation.

Counterfactually, on founder lineage: Robocurve's score flips a tier upward only if Chooi's Inspect Evals contribution converts into an actual government evals contract — the AISI-to-NIST wedge is the plausible bridge, and it is exactly the bridge his Rhodes-plus-MATS-plus-Inspect-Evals résumé was optimized for. Aris Zhu's IEEE RAL 2025 co-authorship on generative robot policies (GPC) supplies the hands-on robotics half. This is the Moskovitz-Asana profile — credentialed-outsider founders in a category where the structural moat is missing, and the operator-pedigree weight has to carry the check.

On atoms-heavy structural moat, the single-anchor parallel is Modern Meadow: hardware is present as a cost of operation, not as a barrier to entry. Robocurve operates physical robot setups, but the capex line is a gross-margin drag, not a Lockheed-Raytheon-style prime-contractor moat. Any well-capitalized academic group can stand up equivalent hardware — ManipulationNet already has.

My 36-month forecast on Robocurve: the company converts into a foundation-model-lab-funded evals consortium in the MLPerf mold, reaches $0-500K of grant or contract revenue, and either persists as a reference standard at sub-venture scale or gets acquihired into Scale AI or Physical Intelligence at modest terms. This is the shape the PBC structure was designed for and the shape the free-substitute market permits. The forecast is consistent with high-lineage-low-monopoly signature: talented founders, no pricing power.

The strongest objection is that AI evals became a real venture category — METR raised, Scale bought evals firms, Apollo Research took government money — and physical-robotics evals could be next as humanoid foundation models scale toward 2028. Chooi's Inspect Evals fluency is the exact AISI-adjacent credential that could turn Robocurve into the NIST-blessed reference for robotics safety certification, and regulated certification depth is a Thiel-shaped moat. I take the objection seriously, but the mechanism that killed the bull case is already in the dossier: the AI evals firms that captured value did so before the government stood up its own capacity, and UK AISI plus US AI Safety Institute have moved faster in robotics-adjacent domains than they did in LLMs. Robocurve is more likely to be a supplier to a government evals body than to become one. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest is a signed contract at $250K+ ARR with a named frontier lab (Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, or Figure) or a NIST/DoD independent-evaluation award — either would demonstrate the pricing-power hypothesis the current dossier lacks.
