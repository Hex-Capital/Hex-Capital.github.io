# Indexable -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's a company building a custom virtual machine monitor, a custom Linux kernel, a custom storage engine, and a Rust reimplementation of Nix — with three people — in a category where I count six funded startups and three cloud giants all shipping sandbox products in the same quarter. My firm already wrote a check to Morph Labs in this space. The interesting question isn't whether Andrew Gazelka can build this. The interesting question is whether it matters enough.

Let me start where I always start: consequence of success. If Indexable wins — if they become the default runtime layer for autonomous AI agents — what changes? AI agents fork environments 5x faster than they do today. Coding agents branch and backtrack more fluidly. Agent platform builders integrate a cleaner API. That's a valuable infrastructure business, and the agentic AI market growing at 44% CAGR to $199 billion means the revenue opportunity is real. But I'm not seeing an industry made obsolete. I'm not seeing a physical system replaced. I'm seeing better plumbing for a revolution someone else is building. When I backed Juniper, TCP/IP routing didn't just speed up Cisco's architecture — it made the entire legacy telecom switching paradigm economically irrelevant. Indexable's 26-millisecond fork doesn't make E2B's 150-millisecond fork economically irrelevant. It makes it slower. That's a feature advantage, not structural disruption. The consequence of success here is a strong infrastructure company — think hundreds of millions in revenue, maybe a few billion in value. Not nothing, but not the magnitude that makes me ignore a 90% failure rate.

The technology, though — I have to give Gazelka credit. Building a custom VMM on KVM rather than wrapping Firecracker or Docker is the kind of first-principles decision that gets my attention. It's the difference between optimizing someone else's architecture and building your own. Hyperion — his Minecraft engine handling 10,000 concurrent players with shared mutable state in Rust — is directly analogous to what he's doing with VM forking. He's an outsider to cloud infrastructure applying game engine concurrency thinking to the problem, and that lateral transfer is exactly how breakthroughs happen. The content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication and the RDMA transport layer suggest he's thinking about this at a depth his competitors aren't. E2B wraps Firecracker. Daytona wraps Docker. Gazelka builds from the metal. That architectural divergence is meaningful, and if the performance gap widens — if 26ms becomes 5ms while competitors plateau at 90ms — the compounding advantage could become structural.

But I keep coming back to the competitive landscape, and this is where my framework pushes back hardest. When I invested in cleantech in 2004, I was alone. When I wrote the OpenAI check in 2019, other VCs thought AI was a science fiction fantasy. The signal I look for is when the experts dismiss it and the outsiders haven't crowded in. AI agent sandboxes in 2026 are the opposite: Cloudflare launched Dynamic Workers in March, Vercel has its sandbox product, Fly.io shipped Sprites in January, E2B has 88% of the Fortune 100 signed up, Daytona crossed a million in ARR within two months of relaunch. This is consensus. And consensus timing, even with superior technology, changes the risk profile fundamentally. You're not skating to where the puck will be — you're skating to where every other player on the ice is already heading.

The bull case I'd need to believe: stateful environment forking isn't just a faster version of existing sandboxes but a fundamentally new computational primitive. If agents that can branch, explore ten parallel hypotheses, and merge the best outcome represent a qualitatively different capability — one that only full-state forking at 26ms enables — then Indexable isn't selling faster containers. It's selling a capability that doesn't exist yet. And the custom VMM becomes a moat because the architectural decisions compound: every optimization to the storage engine, the memory manager, the fork path is built on foundations competitors would need years to replicate. In this world, Gazelka's game engine background becomes the decisive advantage — he's been solving massive shared-state concurrency problems for years in a domain where the cloud infrastructure establishment never thought to look. If that's the reality, this could be worth multiples of what a faster E2B would be worth. But I don't have evidence that this capability gap translates to a qualitatively different product experience. The dossier shows no users, no revenue, no testimonials from agent builders saying "we can do things with Indexable we literally cannot do with anything else." Without that signal, I'm left projecting.

Gazelka's profile is strong for this problem — xAI tenure, deep Rust expertise, a prior exit, and demonstrated ability to build high-performance systems that handle extreme concurrency. But I see no contrarian courage in the direction. Building a custom VMM when everyone else wraps Firecracker is contrarian at the implementation level, not at the category level. The bravest version of this founder would be attacking a problem where there aren't six funded competitors and three cloud providers validating the market for him. The single-founder structure with two unnamed team members and a founding engineer job posting also gives me pause — this custom VMM, custom kernel, custom storage engine stack is a maintenance surface area that would stress a twenty-person team, let alone three.

I'm passing, but not because the technology is weak or the founder isn't capable. I'm passing because the consequence of success is a strong infrastructure business in a crowded category, not a transformation that justifies the risk of attempting something the establishment considers impossible. Gazelka has the raw engineering talent to pursue something far more ambitious than better sandboxes. The tools he's building — hypervisor-level control of computation, content-addressable storage, sub-millisecond state forking — could be pointed at problems where success means the world operates differently, not just where AI agents run 5x faster. I'd tell him what I tell a lot of talented engineers: you're too good at building to spend it on a problem this competitive and this bounded.
