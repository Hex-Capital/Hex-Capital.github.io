# Hex Security -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hex Security is the competitive landscape, because it tells me this is a consensus idea. XBOW has $117M. Novee has $51.5M and ex-IDF cyber unit founders. Aikido has $84.5M and is already at $13M ARR. That's a quarter billion dollars deployed against essentially the same thesis: AI agents that automate penetration testing. When I see a pre-seed company entering a category where multiple competitors have raised nine-figure war chests, my default question isn't "can they win?" — it's "why does everyone already agree this is the right idea?" The most dangerous thing a founder can do is work on something that sounds good to everyone. Ideas that have consensus support have already been priced accordingly. AI-automated pentesting in 2026 is like mobile apps in 2012 — the opportunity is real but the competition is so visible that being right about the market isn't sufficient. You need to be meaningfully different, and I don't yet see what makes Hex Security meaningfully different.

That said, one founder has the right origin story. Ahmad Khan spent roughly a decade doing manual pentesting and application security at Cruise, Salesforce, and IBM. He was the person running black-box pentests, writing custom CodeQL queries, doing source code audits. When someone who has done that work for ten years says "I'm going to automate it," that's organic problem discovery. He didn't read a market report about cybersecurity TAM — he lived inside the tedium and repetition of manual security assessments. That's exactly the kind of founder-problem relationship I look for. But here's the wrinkle: only one of the three founders has this background. Huzaifa comes from general software engineering and hackathons. Prama built AI coding agents at Codegen. Both are capable builders, but neither was living inside the security problem. The team feels assembled around an idea rather than three people who independently converged on the same pain. And the co-founders appear to have no shared history — different universities, different cities, no overlapping employers. I've seen too many teams of strangers fall apart within a year to ignore that signal.

The schlep here is genuinely underappreciated by most observers. Building autonomous offensive security agents — tools that chain exploits, write proof-of-concept worms, compromise networks — is legally and operationally terrifying. This isn't a scanner that flags CVEs. It's software that attacks production systems. The liability exposure alone would make most founders' lawyers have a stroke. Enterprise buyers will demand scope controls, safe-harbor agreements, insurance, and audit trails that are painful to build and maintain. That's a real schlep, and it's the kind of ugly, regulatory work that separates serious companies from demos. Their claim of finding critical SQL injection vulnerabilities and building a PoC worm across YC company infrastructure proves the technology works — which is a meaningful pre-seed milestone.

The bull case for Hex Security is straightforward: Ahmad Khan's deep domain expertise is rare, the product demonstrably works (finding real vulnerabilities in real companies), and the liability schlep of autonomous offensive tooling creates a natural barrier that will filter out less determined teams. If the technology is genuinely commoditizing — if frontier models make it possible for a lean three-person team to match the output of XBOW's $117M operation — then Hex Security's capital efficiency becomes an advantage rather than a handicap. The YC network provides instant distribution to hundreds of potential customers. And markets with lots of funded competitors sometimes produce a clear winner precisely because the problem is real enough to sustain a large company. Stripe had competitors when it launched too. The question is whether Hex Security's combination of domain expertise and RL-trained agents produces measurably better results than alternatives.

But I keep returning to the competitive density. This isn't a case of schlep blindness — the market is wide awake. XBOW already has enterprise bank customers and top HackerOne rankings. Novee has purpose-trained offensive AI models and elite military cyber founders. Aikido has $13M in actual ARR. Hex Security would need to be dramatically better at finding vulnerabilities — not incrementally better, but category-defyingly better — to overcome these head starts with $500K versus their competitors' combined quarter-billion. And the brand confusion with Hex Technologies (hex.tech, $150M+ raised) creates a gratuitous operational headache that a more careful founding team would have avoided. When you're competing for attention in a crowded category, naming yourself after an existing well-known company is the kind of unforced error that makes me wonder about judgment.

I see a team that can build, with one founder who genuinely understands the problem from the inside. But the idea isn't hiding in plain sight — it's standing under a spotlight with hundreds of millions of dollars already pointed at it. Ahmad Khan's decade of pentesting experience is the strongest signal here, and if this were a two-person company with Khan and one technical co-founder he'd worked with for years, building something quietly in a less crowded corner of offensive security, I'd be more interested. What I see instead is a consensus idea, a team of recent acquaintances, and a capital disadvantage that borders on structural. Sometimes the scrappy team wins. But the evidence that this particular scrappy team has the specific, unusual quality needed to win against these specific, well-funded competitors — that evidence isn't here yet.
