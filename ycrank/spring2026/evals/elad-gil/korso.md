# Korso -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is that it's entering a market where the non-obvious window has already closed. Paperless Parts has $30M at Series B with an established customer base. Uptool raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins -- in 2024. Gendra, Mercura AI, and Endeavor AI are all building variations of the same thing. When four tier-one venture firms have already funded a direct competitor for AI-powered manufacturing quoting, this is no longer a market that "appears too boring or too niche to most investors." It's a market that's been identified, sized, and capitalized by sophisticated investors. My best investments -- PagerDuty, Gusto, Coinbase -- were in markets where I was one of the only people who saw the structural moment. Here, the structural moment is consensus.

The underlying market does have the right characteristics. Manufacturing quoting is genuinely boring, genuinely painful, and genuinely universal -- every manufacturer who takes custom orders goes through the RFQ-to-quote-to-PO cycle. The "why now" is real: GPT-4-class models crossed the accuracy threshold for extracting structured data from unstructured manufacturing documents -- engineering drawings, freeform RFQ emails, WhatsApp messages -- sometime in 2024. Only 35% of procurement teams are using AI for RFQs, which means the adoption curve is early. If I were evaluating this market in isolation, it maps to patterns I like: boring infrastructure that every participant in a large industry needs, at a technology inflection. The problem is that the market's attractiveness is precisely why it's already crowded.

The product-to-distribution potential here is interesting but unproven. The end-to-end quote-to-order lifecycle -- RFQ ingestion, document extraction, quote generation, PO tracking -- could become a wedge into broader manufacturing operations. This is structurally similar to how Gusto went from payroll to HR to benefits to compliance. A manufacturer that relies on Korso for quoting could plausibly adopt Korso for production scheduling, invoicing, and supplier management. The multi-channel approach (email plus WhatsApp) hints at international manufacturing awareness, which is smart -- a huge volume of RFQ traffic in Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America flows through WhatsApp, and most US-focused competitors ignore this. But there's no evidence the founders have articulated this expansion vision, and "Contact Sales" as the sole distribution signal doesn't tell me they're thinking about distribution as a strategic asset rather than a checkbox.

The bull case would require believing that Korso's specific combination -- end-to-end workflow coverage, WhatsApp ingestion for international manufacturers, and a RAG-powered conversational assistant -- creates enough differentiation to win despite being later and smaller than Uptool and Paperless Parts. If Korso focused exclusively on non-US manufacturers communicating via WhatsApp, there might be a genuine wedge that the US-focused competitors miss. International manufacturing procurement is a distinct workflow with different communication norms, different document formats, and different compliance requirements. A team that truly understood this segment could build meaningful switching costs before the better-funded competitors noticed. But I don't see evidence that this is the deliberate strategy rather than a feature bullet, and executing an international manufacturing GTM from San Francisco with a three-person team of recent undergraduates is its own set of compounding challenges.

The founding team concerns me in this specific competitive context. Daichi and Martin have relevant engineering backgrounds -- Penn Electric Racing, ML research, a GM automations stint -- and their shared history at UPenn suggests a tested working relationship. But they're entering a market that demands enterprise sales credibility with manufacturing operations leaders, competing against teams that already have customers and capital. No prior startup experience, no shipped product visible in the dossier, no GitHub repos, no traction signals whatsoever. In a market with no competitors, I'd give a young technical team the benefit of the doubt. In a market where Uptool has $6M and Paperless Parts has $30M and established distribution, execution velocity isn't a nice-to-have -- it's existential. The plan here requires building an accurate product, winning enterprise deals against better-funded competitors, and surviving long manufacturing sales cycles on a pre-seed budget. That's not one miracle. That's three.

I'll note that Andrew Miklas -- PagerDuty co-founder -- is the YC partner, which is thematically appropriate given PagerDuty's own "boring infrastructure" trajectory. But partner assignment doesn't change the competitive math. This is a real market at a real technology inflection, and the founders have relevant technical DNA. What's missing is the non-obvious angle. The market looked non-obvious in 2023. By Spring 2026, it's been discovered.
