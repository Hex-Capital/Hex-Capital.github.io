# Terminal Use -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: five well-funded companies and OpenAI itself are all converging on the same thesis — that AI agents need deployment infrastructure. E2B has $32M and is used by 88% of the Fortune 100. Daytona has $24M and hit $1M ARR in under three months. Modal Labs has $111M and $50M ARR. And OpenAI Frontier is bundling hosted runtimes directly into their SDK. Terminal Use has zero GitHub stars, no public customers, and standard YC funding. When everyone agrees on the problem and the well-capitalized players are already executing, you're not investing in a contrarian insight — you're investing in an execution race where you've handed your team the smallest engine. I don't fund races where capital and distribution determine the winner.

But the more fundamental issue is consequence magnitude. "Vercel for background agents" is the company's own framing, and I take them at their word. If Terminal Use succeeds completely — captures the orchestration layer for agent deployment the way Vercel captured frontend deployment — the result is a multi-billion-dollar developer tools company. That's a good business. But nobody's life changes. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. No physical reality transforms. Developers have slightly less DevOps friction when deploying agents. The agents themselves might change the world, but Terminal Use is the plumbing underneath the plumbing. The consequence of total success here is a profitable infrastructure company in a crowded category — and I've spent forty years learning that the world has enough of those. When I backed Square, the consequence was replacing the entire payment infrastructure of small businesses globally. When I backed Juniper, the consequence was making Cisco's architecture obsolete. What's the Terminal Use equivalent? Making it 50% faster to deploy a coding agent? That's the kind of outcome where you've reduced failure probability to the point where success doesn't much matter.

The team is competent but represents exactly the pattern I'm wary of. Three engineers from Palantir — the same company, similar roles, building infrastructure tooling that maps directly to what they did before. Vivek led agent deployment at US hospitals, Stavros worked on coding infrastructure, Filip worked on the Ontology platform. This is industry insiders executing the industry's playbook with industry-standard thinking. The dossier itself acknowledges the core capabilities — containerized sandboxes, filesystem management, auto-scaling — are "well-understood patterns." Where is the technical leap that makes the existing approach uneconomic? Filesystem forking and manifest-based sync are solid engineering, not disruption. I look for the kind of technological discontinuity that makes incumbents structurally obsolete, and I don't find it here.

The strongest bull case, which I want to take seriously: if AI agents become the dominant computing paradigm — if every piece of software eventually becomes an agent — then the orchestration layer is as consequential as AWS was in 2006. The persistent filesystem focus could prove to be the right architectural bet when ephemeral sandboxes can't handle the long-running, stateful nature of production agents. Vivek's direct experience deploying agents across hospital systems at Palantir is genuinely rare expertise at this moment — most people building agent infrastructure haven't actually deployed agents in production at scale. And the Vercel analogy might undersell the outcome: Vercel was worth $2.5B in a world where frontend was one paradigm among many; if agents become everything, the "Vercel for agents" could be much larger. The timing is also real — the production gap between prototype agents and deployed agents is widening right now, and someone will fill it. If Terminal Use's specific bet on persistent filesystems and long-running agent orchestration proves architecturally correct while competitors optimize for ephemeral execution, they could own the category despite less capital. That's a legitimate thesis. But it requires Terminal Use to be right about the architecture *and* win the execution race *and* avoid being bundled by model providers — three simultaneous low-probability requirements, which is exactly the pattern I flag as a red flag.

The timing trajectory is the one dimension where I give real credit. The agent production gap is genuine, accelerating, and early. Gartner's prediction of 40% enterprise agent adoption by 2026 suggests demand is about to explode. But this is consensus timing — every competitor in the landscape is riding the same wave. Being early matters; being early alongside five better-funded competitors who arrived at the same moment doesn't.
