# Lamina Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What I want to know first, before anything about the product, is how a kid from Humla -- one of the most inaccessible districts in all of Nepal, a place where food has to be airlifted in during winter -- ends up at MIT studying computer science and mathematics, and then walks away from that to start a company. That journey from Humla to Cambridge is the kind of biographical signal that makes me sit up. I know what it's like to teach yourself everything from nothing, to come from a place where nobody expects you to end up in the rooms you end up in. Sudip Rokaya's path has that quality. And his co-founder Rohan Bhattarai -- olympiad medals in physics, math, and computing from Nepal, published astrophysics researcher at Caltech -- brings serious intellectual firepower. These are clearly talented, determined people. But here's where I get stuck: I can't find the thread that connects their specific hardship to this specific problem. When I backed the Crusoe team, the story of how they got there and what they were building were the same story. With Lamina Labs, I see two impressive young scientists building a useful tool, but I don't yet hear the biographical inevitability -- the "I lived this problem and I'm the only person who could solve it" signal that anchors my highest-conviction bets.

The product itself -- deterministic, mathematically precise animation from a text prompt in under eight seconds -- is genuinely clever engineering. The insight that billions are flowing into Hollywood-style AI video while almost nobody is building for educational explanation is a real observation. And the combination of Sudip's systems engineering chops (his ghostclaw project shows he thinks at the infrastructure level) with Rohan's deep physics and mathematical precision maps cleanly onto the technical challenge. If you're going to build animation that has to be correct to the equation, to the proof, to the physical model, you want exactly this pairing. I respect the technical vision. But "deterministic" is a differentiation, not a category. The broader space -- AI-generated video and educational content -- is one of the most heavily funded, most competitive categories in tech right now. Synthesia at $4 billion. Knowlify already through YC with $3 million raised and 200,000 videos generated. Vyond at $35 million ARR. This is emphatically not an unpopular idea. And the investments I've made that really worked -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe -- they all shared a quality of being actively dismissed by other investors. Nobody is dismissing AI video tools right now. They're throwing money at them.

Let me make the strongest case for this company, because I want to be fair to what I see. If Lamina Labs executes on the vision -- if any educator, anywhere in the world, can type a prompt and get a mathematically precise animation in eight seconds -- that is a genuine democratization of knowledge. Think about the physics teacher in rural Nepal, or the community college instructor in Mississippi, or the self-taught student watching Khan Academy who wants to create their own explanatory content. Today, making a quality STEM animation requires either coding in Manim (hours, plus Python expertise) or weeks in After Effects. Lamina could collapse that to seconds. That IS making an invisible system visible -- the system being "only people with expensive tools and specialized skills get to create high-quality STEM content." If I squint, I can see the Carta parallel: taking something that was opaque and locked behind expertise, and making it transparent and accessible. And two people from Nepal building the tool that equalizes educational content creation globally -- there's a poetic rightness to that. The bull case is that they become the infrastructure layer for all deterministic educational animation, that their rendering pipeline becomes the standard, and that "Pictor" becomes a verb the way "Google" did for search. The 8-second speed claim, if it holds, is a real technical moat.

But I keep coming back to the commoditization risk, and it's the kind of risk that bothers me most -- not technology risk, which I embrace, but replication risk. The text-to-animation pipeline depends on LLM capabilities that are improving rapidly for everyone. Manim is open-source with 35,800 GitHub stars. A well-funded team wrapping an improving LLM around Manim could approximate this value proposition in months. And when I think about defensibility -- which is one of the first things I look for at the early stage -- I don't see the structural moat. No regulatory barrier. No network effect. No data flywheel yet. The speed advantage is real but speed is a feature, not a fortress. Both founders are undergraduates, which doesn't concern me in isolation (I dropped out myself), but it means they haven't yet been tested by the kind of crisis that separates founders who persist from founders who return to school. I don't have any signal on how they handle conflict as co-founders, and after HQ Trivia, that's something I always want to understand before writing a check.

This is a company I'd want to have coffee with, not because of the product but because of the people. Sudip's journey from Humla to MIT to YC is genuinely extraordinary, and Rohan's physics credentials are the real thing. But my investment framework demands that the company itself be non-consensus, that it occupy a space others are afraid of or dismissive of, and that the founders' biographical arc point inexorably toward this particular problem. On all three counts, I'm not there yet. The idea is smart but not weird. The space is hot, not dismissed. And the founder-problem connection, while plausible, isn't the kind of inevitable biographical match that drives my highest conviction. I'm passing, but I'm watching.
