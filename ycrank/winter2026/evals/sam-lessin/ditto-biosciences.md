# Ditto Biosciences -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Mining tick saliva and parasitic worm genomes for immunosuppressive drugs is exactly the kind of idea that makes most investors visibly uncomfortable — and that discomfort is the signal. Coronado Biosciences tried a version of this a decade ago, feeding patients live pig whipworms, and cratered in Phase 2. That failure salted the earth for anyone thinking about parasites as medicine. So three PhDs with deep parasitology, evolutionary biology, and protein engineering pedigrees walking into a room and saying "we're going back to the parasite well, but we're isolating the specific proteins instead of using the whole worm" — that's a genuinely contrarian move. Adair Borges is a UCSF-trained virologist and Miller Fellow at Berkeley with 50+ publications and a patent. Dennis Sun has a Harvard-Berkeley pedigree with an NSF fellowship and biotech operational experience. Emily Weiss worked at Illumina and DuPont and publishes in Nature Microbiology. These are not people cosplaying as scientists. This is the sane-person-insane-idea combination at high fidelity — the kind of pairing where the founders' specific credibility makes the contrarian bet legible rather than reckless.

What makes Ditto structurally interesting through my lens is that it passes the AI test in a way that almost no YC company in this batch will. The moat isn't software. It's a proprietary database of 1M+ parasite-derived proteins, a tissue biobank mapping immune memory, and deep biological expertise at the intersection of parasitology, evolutionary genomics, and protein engineering — a talent pool roughly the size of a single university department. AI is genuinely "cherry on top" here: computational screening makes it feasible to analyze a million proteins against known drug targets, but the insight that parasites have already solved the immunosuppression problem through evolutionary pressure is a biological insight, not a computational one. Strip out the AI, and you still have a compelling drug discovery thesis grounded in evolutionary biology. Strip out the biological expertise, and the AI is worthless. That's the exact inversion of every "AI-powered" startup I refuse to fund, and it maps directly to what I see in companies like Craftwork — the complexity lives in the physical world (or in this case, the biological world), and software amplifies but cannot substitute for it.

The narrative has real elasticity. "We're systematically mining evolution's R&D lab" is an infinity story with historical proof points — aspirin came from willow bark, penicillin from mold, GLP-1 agonists from Gila monster venom, taxol from Pacific yew. If parasite-derived proteins become a recognized therapeutic modality the way monoclonal antibodies did, Ditto is building the foundational discovery platform for that entire drug class. The autoimmune market alone is $125 billion. And the claim that over 98% of viral and parasite proteins remain uncharacterized means the search space is almost entirely unexplored — this isn't a crowded field being rediscovered, it's a genuine frontier. The narrative can grow from autoimmune disease into broader immunology, into oncology, into a general platform for extracting evolved molecular solutions from nature's protein library.

Here's where my framework creates real tension with this deal: there is no downside cushion whatsoever. Zero revenue. Zero customers. Zero commercial validation. The 1-2 nM binding affinity data is scientifically meaningful but commercially it's a first step on a journey that costs hundreds of millions of dollars and takes a decade. Drug development has a 90% failure rate, and the capital requirements mean this company will need to raise enormous follow-on rounds from biotech-specialist investors who evaluate on completely different criteria than I do. Biotech is structurally the anti-Craftwork — instead of real revenue providing a floor while narrative provides the ceiling, it's all narrative and no floor until you hit clinical validation. My "infinity optionality plus downside cushion" framework demands both components, and this has only one. That said, I've held through seven years of Helium going from seed to unicorn, and I know that structural conviction sometimes requires tolerating long timelines with zero intermediate revenue signals. The question is whether the structural thesis is strong enough to justify the binary risk.

The primitive-or-application question is genuinely ambiguous. If Ditto succeeds, the protein database and tissue biobank could become infrastructure that pharma partners build on — closer to a new drug discovery primitive than a single pipeline company. The co-founders worked together for three years at Arcadia Science, co-authoring research, which gives me confidence in the relational depth of the founding team — not speed-dating co-founders but genuine intellectual collaborators. But "could become a platform" and "is a platform" are separated by years of clinical validation. Right now, this is a drug discovery company with platform aspirations, not a protocol others are already building on.

I lean toward writing a check here, but with clear-eyed recognition that this sits at the edge of my framework. The founder-idea calibration is among the strongest I'd encounter — deeply credentialed scientists pursuing an approach that most investors dismiss because of Coronado's failure, working with biological moats that AI strengthens rather than erodes. The narrative ceiling is genuinely unbounded. What it lacks is the revenue cushion I normally require and the certainty that this becomes infrastructure rather than a single drug bet. Biotech's capital intensity and binary outcomes make this a fundamentally different risk profile than the physical-operations-plus-tech businesses I gravitate toward. But the structural logic is sound, the team is real, and the contrarian positioning is authentic — not manufactured for fundraising but rooted in genuine scientific conviction about an unexplored biological frontier.
