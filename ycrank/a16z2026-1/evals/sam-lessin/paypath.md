# PayPath -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$2M ARR in under twelve months with three people and a $500K check from a16z Speedrun. I'll say it plainly: that's real execution. PayPath is generating roughly $4 in annual recurring revenue for every $1 raised, and managing $500M+ in debt assets on their platform. Dean Glas and Matthew Lippl are clearly capable operators — Glas has a prior exit to TikTok via GeoGif, Lippl has fifteen years building product, and they co-founded SellX together before this. The capital efficiency here is the kind of thing that makes me lean in. But capital efficiency alone isn't an investment thesis. It's table stakes for survival.

Here's my problem: PayPath calls itself "The AI Operating System Powering Modern Debt." Every word of that tagline is designed to attract the exact capital I think is mispriced in the wrong direction. AI. Operating System. The framing puts this squarely in the category where I believe incumbents eat startups for breakfast. Can HighRadius — $300M+ raised, enterprise distribution, existing relationships with every major financial institution — bolt on an AI co-pilot and automated communication layer? Of course they can. Can someone stitch together Twilio, OpenAI's API, and a compliance middleware and get 80% of what PayPath offers? The dossier itself acknowledges this: "a well-funded competitor could assemble a similar stack." When your own competitive analysis concedes the point, I take it seriously.

What saves PayPath from being a straight pass is the financial plumbing underneath the AI marketing. Managing $500M+ in debt assets, processing payments, handling CFPB and TCPA compliance across enrollment, communication, and settlement workflows — that's not an LLM wrapper. That's infrastructure. This maps to a pattern I've seen work: Gusto didn't win payroll because it had better UI or smarter automation. Gusto won because once you're running someone's payroll — processing the money, filing the taxes, managing the compliance — ripping you out is borderline impossible. PayPath is building the same kind of switching-cost moat in debt resolution. Replace four or five systems simultaneously? Nobody does that voluntarily. The multi-system replacement angle is the most defensible thing about this company, and it has nothing to do with AI.

The primitive test is where PayPath falls short for me. Debt collection software is a $6 billion market today growing to $14 billion by 2034. PayPath is competing for share of an existing market, not creating a new one. Aktos is building a system of record for collection agencies. Prodigal has $14M from YC, Accel, and Menlo for lending analytics. Tratta processes $30M+ a month. Nobody will use "PayPath" as a verb. Nobody will describe a new behavior by reference to what PayPath created. This is "better debt software" — and I've been explicit that "better X" in an existing category caps your upside at what the existing market will tolerate in switching costs. Compare this to Venmo, which created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavioral category that literally didn't exist. PayPath isn't creating a new primitive; it's executing well in a fragmented vertical.

The bull case I have to take seriously: debt resolution is a genuinely unsexy, underserved vertical where legacy vendors like FICO Debt Manager run on batch-processing architectures with eighteen-month implementation cycles. Most top-tier VCs aren't paying attention — Aktos's $4.4M from 8VC and Prodigal's $14M are modest checks for a $6B market. If PayPath's unified platform becomes the system of record for mid-market debt resolution firms, the financial infrastructure position compounds exactly like payroll infrastructure did. Dean Glas's prior exit to TikTok and the repeat co-founder relationship with Lippl suggest they can navigate pivots and execute under pressure. And $2M ARR with three people means they clearly don't need hand-holding from their investors. But what would need to be true for this to be a massive outcome is that the debt resolution vertical is big enough and sticky enough to support an independent platform company at scale — and that neither HighRadius from above nor Aktos/Prodigal from below can squeeze them out. The missing piece is domain expertise: neither Glas nor Lippl has a documented background in debt, lending, or collections. In a space governed by CFPB enforcement actions and state-level licensing requirements, learning compliance on the fly while managing half a billion in assets with three people is the kind of risk that doesn't show up until it detonates.

The sanity-insanity ratio here is inverted from what I look for. These are sane founders building a sane product in a sane market. That's fine. It's just not what gets me excited. Nobody told Glas and Lippl they were out of their minds for building debt resolution software. Nobody's consensus-wrong about whether this market exists. The risk is already priced into the category because the category is boring, not because the market has actively dismissed it. Boring-mispricing and wrong-mispricing are different things — and I make money on wrong-mispricing.

PayPath is a well-executed vertical SaaS company with genuine financial infrastructure characteristics and impressive capital efficiency. The dimensions that hold it back are Primitive Creation — this is incremental improvement in an existing category, not category creation — and Consensus Mispricing — debt-tech with an AI label is neither contrarian enough to create asymmetric upside nor consensus-hot enough to signal danger. The capital discipline score is the bright spot and the reason this isn't a straight pass.
