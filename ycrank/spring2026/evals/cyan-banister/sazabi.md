# Sazabi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that strikes me immediately about Sazabi: this is one of the most popular categories in tech right now. AI observability. Every fund I know is looking at this space. Arize just raised $70M. LangSmith hit a billion-dollar valuation. Langfuse got snapped up by ClickHouse. Datadog -- a fifty-billion-dollar company -- is layering AI features into its existing platform. Laminar is in this same YC batch. And Sherwood Callaway's angel backers read like a who's-who of the AI developer tools ecosystem: Anthropic, Vercel, Brex, LangChain, Replit. This is an insider's company built with insider support in the hottest corner of developer infrastructure. My entire approach to investing is built around finding the unpopular thing before it becomes consensus, and AI observability became consensus sometime in 2024. When everyone in the room wants to invest, I've learned the price is wrong and the weirdness has already been arbitraged away.

Let me talk about Sherwood. Professionally, his trajectory is impressive -- built observability infrastructure at Brex, founded Opkit and got it acquired by 11x, then led development on one of the largest agentic systems in production. That's real domain depth across both halves of this problem: traditional observability and AI agent behavior. The a16z scout role, the network of backers, the SOC 2 and ISO certifications for a ten-person team -- all of it signals someone who knows exactly how to play the enterprise developer tools game. But when I sit across from a founder, I'm not looking for the professional resume. I'm looking for the biographical thread -- the hardship, the personal stake, the thing that means this founder will still be grinding on this problem when everything goes wrong at 2 AM on a Tuesday and the board is asking hard questions. Dev Bootcamp in 2014 hints at a career transition story I'd want to hear more about, but the dossier gives me nothing to work with. I don't know why Sherwood specifically needs to solve this problem beyond the fact that he's professionally qualified to. And for me, professional qualification without personal mission is a weaker signal at the earliest stages.

The strongest bull case here is real, and I want to be honest about it. The "logs are all you need" thesis -- that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces from logs, giving you three pillars for the price of one -- is actually a somewhat contrarian bet within the observability orthodoxy. Datadog's entire business model is built around charging separately for metrics, traces, and APM. If Sazabi is right that logs are sufficient and everything else can be derived, that's a structural challenge to how the incumbents make money, and incumbents have a hard time cannibalizing their own pricing models. That's a genuine insight. And the timing argument is solid -- AI coding agents are writing real production code now, creating failure modes that traditional tools genuinely weren't designed for. If Sherwood can execute fast enough to establish the schema for how agent decisions get logged, that could become a de facto standard with real lock-in. This is the scenario where being the connected insider actually helps -- the backers from LangChain, Vercel, and Anthropic could drive adoption through their own ecosystems.

But even the bull case doesn't match how I invest. This company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations -- it makes engineering teams at well-funded AI startups more productive. There's a faint echo of my "invisible systems" pattern, surfacing opaque AI agent decisions the way Carta surfaced cap tables or Flock Safety surfaced neighborhood safety data. But the beneficiaries here are senior engineers at companies already deep in the AI ecosystem, not people being locked out of economic opportunity. The space isn't regulated or stigmatized -- nobody is uncomfortable funding developer tools. And in a market where Langfuse already has two thousand paying customers and twenty thousand GitHub stars, Sazabi is waitlist-only with no public traction. Execution speed is everything in a crowded market, and I'm not seeing evidence of it yet. This feels like exactly the kind of competent, well-networked, consensus bet that I'd expect to see funded easily without me -- which means my capital doesn't add the kind of value I'm looking for.
