# Checkmate -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is a company where the incumbents aren't dismissing the approach -- they're already building it. AlphaSense, at $4B+ valuation and $500M ARR, launched its own AI Agent Interviewer in 2025. InsightAgent exists with both autonomous and co-pilot modes. When I backed Juniper Networks, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, the meat industry laughed. The signal I look for is experts telling you it can't be done. Here, the experts are telling you they're doing it themselves. That's the opposite signal. Checkmate is entering a competitive race, not creating a new category.

The consequence question is where this falls apart for me. If Checkmate succeeds completely -- if every expert network call in the world is conducted by an AI agent -- what changes? Hedge fund analysts save time. They do more calls per week. Research throughput increases. The expert network market grows from $4 billion to maybe $17 billion by 2035. And that's the ceiling. Nobody's life changes fundamentally. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The food system doesn't transform. The energy grid doesn't shift. Institutional investors get slightly better primary research at lower cost. I have a visceral reaction to smart founders calibrating their ambition to a $4 billion niche market where success means being a good enterprise software company. The expert network market is real, growing, and... contained. Checkmate has de-risked the problem by choosing an existing workflow with known buyers and proven willingness to pay, and in doing so has capped the consequence of success at a level I find hard to get excited about.

The founders are clearly capable. Garde spent eight years as a PM at Google across AI, AR/VR, and gaming, then built Monzo US from zero to one -- that's real enterprise-building experience. Sha is a published researcher, Forbes 30 Under 30, built Onyx to 300K+ users and got it acquired. They have complementary skills: product leadership plus deep technical NLP expertise. The team is well-assembled for this specific problem. But that's precisely my concern -- they're industry insiders executing an obvious playbook. Two Google/Meta AI engineers building an AI tool for automating knowledge work is the consensus bet of 2025-2026. There's no contrarian thesis here, no outsider irreverence, no attempt at something the world considers impossible. They're applying proven technology to a proven market in a way that multiple well-funded competitors are simultaneously pursuing.

Let me steelman this. The bull case would require me to believe that network-agnostic positioning becomes a durable strategic advantage as AlphaSense locks itself into its Tegus walled garden and traditional expert networks (GLG, AlphaSights) can't build this without cannibalizing their brokering model. In that scenario, Checkmate occupies the interstitial layer between all expert networks and all institutional investors, and the proprietary transcript corpus compounds into a genuine data moat -- better follow-up questions, cross-call pattern recognition, industry-specific interview protocols. Over time, maybe they expand beyond expert calls to become the operating system for all institutional primary research. If that happened, you'd have a category-defining platform company. I can see the architecture. But even this best case produces a successful financial tools business, not a civilizational transformation. And the path to that best case runs directly through AlphaSense's distribution advantage (6,500+ enterprise customers) and the trust barriers of compliance-sensitive hedge funds choosing a pre-seed startup over a $4B incumbent for unsupervised expert interactions involving potential MNPI.

The timing trajectory is the one dimension that partially works. LLM voice quality crossed a usability threshold in 2024-2025, and the technology curve is clearly accelerating. But timing advantage requires being ahead of the market, not concurrent with it. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, nobody else was writing checks for AI capabilities. Checkmate is building simultaneously with AlphaSense's AI Interviewer launch and InsightAgent's product. The technology trend is real but it's already consensus -- and when the trend is consensus, the advantage shifts to the player with the most distribution, not the most agile startup. The commoditization risk in the dossier is real: the core capability of an LLM voice agent conducting structured interviews is increasingly accessible to anyone with API access and domain knowledge.

I pass. Not because the team isn't strong or the market isn't real -- both are credible. I pass because Checkmate has reduced the probability of failure to the point where the consequence of success is, to me, inconsequential. These founders are too talented to spend their careers making expert network calls 15% cheaper. The problem isn't important enough to justify the effort.
