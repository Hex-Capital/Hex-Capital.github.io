# Salus -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's my problem with Salus: nobody thinks this is a bad idea. When I hear a pitch and every engineer in the room nods along -- "yes, agents need guardrails, obviously" -- I get uncomfortable. My best investments were in companies that made smart people uncomfortable. Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing would never work in public telecom. The food industry laughed at plant-based heme. When the experts validate your approach rather than dismissing it, you're usually building something incremental enough to be non-threatening. Salus is building something sensible. Sensible is not what I invest in.

Let me apply the test I apply to everything first: if Salus succeeds completely -- captures the entire runtime agent validation market -- what changes? Engineering teams have fewer agent failures. Companies save money on mistaken refunds and bad API calls. The agents already doing the transformational work do it with fewer errors. This is valuable. It is not transformational. Salus is selling seatbelts for someone else's revolution. The AI agent transformation is civilization-scale -- but the guardrails layer is a cost-reduction play measured in reduced error rates and avoided support tickets. The consequence of success is a solid infrastructure business, not a fundamental change in how any industry operates. Compare this to what Square did: Square didn't make existing payment terminals 30% more reliable -- it replaced the entire POS infrastructure with a phone and a dongle. Salus makes existing agents 58% more self-correcting. That's an optimization, not a disruption.

The competitive picture reinforces this. Fiddler AI has $100M in funding and has already launched a Realtime Guardrails API. Guardrails AI is open-source with nearly 6,000 GitHub stars. Galileo got acquired by Alphabet. When well-funded incumbents and an open-source alternative already occupy your space, you're not creating a new category -- you're competing in an existing one. I look for companies where I'm inventing a category that hasn't been opened to venture capital before. AI agent guardrails in early 2026 is consensus, not contrarian. Every major LLM provider is building toward native validation in their SDKs. The insertion point between the agent and the tool call is exactly where OpenAI and Anthropic will eventually put their own safety layer, because it's the obvious place to put it.

The strongest bull case: agentic AI is genuinely on an exponential trajectory, and Salus is positioned at precisely the right moment -- the transition from text-generation to action-execution. Gartner projects 40% of enterprise apps will embed agents by end of 2026. If every deployed agent needs runtime validation, and Salus becomes the standard middleware layer across frameworks, the market expands enormously. The cross-platform positioning (working across OpenAI, Anthropic, LangChain, CrewAI) mirrors how Stripe succeeded by being framework-agnostic for payments. The 58% self-repair recovery rate is a genuine technical signal -- if that number improves to 80-90%, it becomes indispensable. And the evidence-cache mechanism could compound into a data advantage over time. For this to be a great investment, Salus would need to become the Stripe of agent safety: the default infrastructure layer that every agent deployment requires, with network effects from accumulated validation patterns making it irreplaceable. That's a real path. But Stripe replaced incumbent payment infrastructure -- Salus wraps it.

The founders are clearly capable -- Vedant Singh's research breadth across five Stanford labs covering LLM reasoning, NLP, and applied AI is directly relevant, and Kevin Pan's Symbolic Systems background maps well to understanding agent decision-making. But they're Stanford CS students building exactly what you'd expect Stanford CS students to build. There's no outsider advantage here, no "irreverence and foolish confidence" attacking a problem the industry says is impossible. They're insiders executing the insider playbook in a space that's already crowded with well-capitalized competitors. The team is two roommates with identical backgrounds -- no evidence of deliberate gene pool engineering to address the venture's key risks (go-to-market against a $100M competitor, enterprise sales cycles, framework API dependency).

I pass. Not because the founders aren't smart or the timing isn't right -- the timing is genuinely good. I pass because the consequence of success is a useful middleware company, not an industry transformation. These founders are too talented to spend their careers making agents 58% more self-correcting. I'd rather see them attempt something that makes people look at them funny.
