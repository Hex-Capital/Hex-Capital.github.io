# primitive -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me about Primitive isn't the founder or the technology -- it's that AgentMail, backed by Paul Graham and Dharmesh Shah with $6M from General Catalyst, already has 500+ B2B customers and saw its user count quadruple in a single month. That's not a market waiting to be discovered. That's a market where consensus has already formed. And my entire investing life has been built on the opposite -- finding the companies that are unpopular, difficult to assess, and make other investors uncomfortable. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, people thought private space was insane. When I backed Crusoe, mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded like a fever dream. Email infrastructure for AI agents? That sounds like a perfectly reasonable, well-understood extension of existing developer tools. Nobody is going to call you crazy for building this. And that's precisely the problem from where I sit.

I always want to hear the founder's story before I hear about the business. With Ethan Byrd, what I can see is a resume -- a strong one, genuinely. Google, Meta, AWS, Microsoft, then founding Actual AI and raising $3.2M with 32 active pilots. That's real execution evidence. But a resume isn't a story. I can't find the biographical thread that connects this specific person to this specific problem. Why email infrastructure for agents? What happened with Actual AI -- did the 32 pilots go somewhere? Is Primitive a pivot, a successor, a fresh start? His LinkedIn still says "Founder & CTO @ Actual AI," which leaves questions I'd want answered before writing a check. The best founders I've backed have an almost irrational commitment to their particular problem because they lived inside it. I don't see that signal here -- I see a talented engineer who noticed a market opportunity, which is a different kind of bet.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because it exists. The timing is genuinely right -- Gartner's projection of 40% of enterprise apps featuring AI agents by 2026 is not hype, it's the trajectory we're living through. Agents absolutely need communication primitives, and email is the universal protocol. Byrd's webhook-first architecture could be a genuinely better technical approach than AgentMail's mailbox model -- treating email as a developer primitive rather than giving agents "inboxes" may prove more natural for programmatic use cases. And Byrd has the technical depth to know this: he's seen the guts of email and cloud infrastructure at the companies that built it. If he can move fast enough to capture a distinct segment of the developer market that wants raw webhook delivery over managed mailboxes, there's a real business here. Having a funded competitor validates demand -- AgentMail's explosive growth proves the need is urgent and real.

But that bull case doesn't overcome my fundamental concern: this is a well-understood technical domain with no structural moat and a well-funded competitor that has a nine-month head start, major institutional backing, and network effects already forming. The webhook-routing mechanism is technically reproducible. Google's A2A protocol could commoditize the entire layer. And from my specific lens, nothing about this company creates economic access for underserved populations or makes invisible systems visible -- this is infrastructure serving developers at well-capitalized AI companies. It doesn't activate the part of me that saw Uber as a poverty-alleviation tool or Flock Safety as a way to give communities data they'd never had. Primitive is a competent bet in a rational market, and I've learned that those are someone else's investments, not mine.

The solo founder dynamic isn't a dealbreaker -- I've backed solo founders before and at least it avoids the co-founder conflict that destroyed HQ Trivia. But a team of one competing against AgentMail's three co-founders and $6M, with three unfilled roles including both founding engineering positions, means execution capacity is severely constrained at exactly the moment when speed matters most. In a market where the competitor is quadrupling usage month-over-month, being early in the hiring process is a real vulnerability.
