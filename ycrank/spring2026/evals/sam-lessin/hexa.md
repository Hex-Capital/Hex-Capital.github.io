# Hexa -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Hexa is the 2026 YC archetype I described in my own pass example almost word for word -- "AI agent for [enterprise vertical], early MRR, fresh out of YC" -- except the dossier helpfully names the vertical (manufacturers and distributors), names the MRR ($9K from 3 customers in 6 weeks), and names the $30M Series A competitor (Didero) that closed in February. The dimensional signature on Hexa is a founder with one credible LinkedIn thesis post about manufacturing spreadsheets sitting on top of the most consensus AI-agent-for-vertical template in the batch, against an incumbent wedge competitor with 37x the capital. That pairing tells me everything I need about the regatta ahead.

Solana at $0.05 is the ladder rung I use for "round is mispriced" -- pre-consensus, category abandoned by serious money, my entry price absorbs every downstream markup. The mechanism that made Solana load-bearing was that nobody else was bidding. Hexa is the inverse: Didero just took $30M from Chemistry, Headline, and M12 to do "agentic procurement for manufacturers and distributors" -- that is the same ICP Hexa names on hexaagents.com, and that round is the loudest possible signal that tier-1 capital has already decided this category is hot. The dossier discloses no price for Hexa's round, and silence on price in a category Didero just blessed at $30M is not bullish ambiguity -- it's the default-consensus prior I punish. There is no Solana-shaped discount available here; Hexa is being priced inside a category Didero already heated.

The pass-example failure mode is a standard SaaS cap table that needs five more rounds before default-alive -- and Hexa's structure is exactly that. Hexa is shipping a per-seat or per-customer subscription (~$3K/mo implied by the YC disclosure), with no pricing page, integrations across eight ERP/CRM stacks, and a Calendly demo flow as the GTM. There is nothing about Hexa's instrument that I could not buy via the next twenty YC SAFEs in the same batch -- no revenue share, no holdco, no royalty on the catalog-pricing data they claim will become the moat. Hexa needs to out-raise Didero to win this fight, which means Hexa needs four more tacks of dilution before the Series A starting line, and each tack is underwritten against a competitor with a four-quarter head start on burn.

Ishaan Makkar did publish the "54% of manufacturing companies still rely on spreadsheets" framing on LinkedIn, and that is one signal above the absolute floor -- the founder has at least articulated the thesis in his own words before pitching. Over 18 months, conviction either compounds into a category-defining narrative (the Sublime Security path, where the founder out-writes the category) or it stalls at one LinkedIn snippet and the company becomes indistinguishable from Traza and Didero in buyer memory. Hexa's dossier shows no Twitter presence for either Ishaan or Auri Nayak, no GitHub, no prior operating role in manufacturing or distribution that the searches surfaced -- the conviction surface area is thin enough that the next pitch from a domain-deep operator in this vertical will out-position Hexa overnight.

What would have to be true for Hexa's distribution score to flip a tier? Ishaan or Auri would need owned reach into the manufacturer/distributor buyer -- a niche newsletter for ops leaders at $50M-$500M industrial distributors, a podcast that the heads of operations at those shops actually listen to, a Discord of plant managers. None of that exists in the dossier. Hexa is selling enterprise software to a notoriously hard-to-reach SMB-industrial buyer through Calendly demo links, which is the most expensive distribution motion in B2B and the exact opposite of what the Slow Creator Fund thesis underwrites. The $9K MRR was almost certainly sourced through the YC network and personal outreach, neither of which compounds after batch ends.

John Fish / Bookshelved is my single-anchor parallel on category sleeve, because John sits squarely inside a sleeve I'm convicted on (entrepreneurial creator with owned distribution) and Hexa sits squarely inside one I've publicly de-rated -- vertical AI agents wrapping LLMs around an ERP, which is precisely the "AI is just more internet" pattern I called out on Lonsdale's podcast. There is nothing crypto-shaped, nothing DTC-margin-shaped, nothing creator-shaped, and nothing prescription-rail-regulated about Hexa. The category fit is the cleanest "no" on the entire scorecard.

My 36-month forecast on Hexa: ARR caps somewhere between $400K and $1.2M, Didero's Series A capital wins the heads-up sales cycles at the larger distributors, and Hexa exits via acqui-hire into one of the ERP integration targets it lists -- Epicor or a NetSuite SI partner -- at a number that returns the SAFE at par or 1.5x, with the founders rolling into a Director of AI role. The mechanism is straightforward: in a category where Didero has already raised $30M and Traza is in market with overlapping wedge, the regatta has too many tacks for a SAFE-funded three-person team to complete before running out of cash or differentiation.

The strongest bull case on Hexa is the Wizard-of-Oz read: $9K MRR in 6 weeks from three industrial distributors is real money from a buyer that almost never moves fast, the "54% on spreadsheets" thesis is directionally correct, David Lieb is a serious YC partner, and Didero's $30M is itself category validation that derisks the wedge. A reasonable investor could argue Hexa is the cheaper, broader bet on the same thesis Didero just got paid for. That case stings because I have been wrong about category-validating Series As before -- Robinhood is my own Known Exception for this exact mistake. But Robinhood applies to existing portfolio positions where my earliest-stage entry absorbs the consensus, not to a new entry on the back of someone else's $30M markup. The condition that would flip my verdict: Hexa closes its seed below $8M post with no tier-1 lead, AND Ishaan publishes a substantive long-form essay on why Didero's procurement-only frame is structurally wrong before the round closes. Absent both, this is a pass that doesn't need a second meeting.
