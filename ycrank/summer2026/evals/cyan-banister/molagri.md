# Molagri -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Molagri is what happens when two University of Toronto coronavirus biochemists -- Zaky Hassan and Min Jin, six years of lab-mate trust, Nature Communications and Nature Microbiology on the CV -- decide that the molecular-target tools that worked on SARS spike proteins will work on a pest insect's gut. The dimensional signature is loud: the unsexy-regulated category fit is the strongest thing here, and the founder's lived contact with the actual problem is the weakest. That pairing tells me what Molagri is -- it's a bench-science pivot into a contested ag category, dressed in the silhouette of a Crusoe-style "too hard, too unsexy" swing without the rural-industrial biography that made Crusoe load-bearing.

Anduril is my high rung on lived founder experience: Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from a garage before he ever touched a defense contract, so when he walked onto a military base he was *of* hardware, not visiting it. Molagri's founders are *of* viral structural biology. Hassan's PhD work is coronavirus antibody research; Jin's publications are on aminopeptidase N as a merbecovirus receptor. Neither has spent a season on a soybean field, neither has filed a PMRA submission, neither shows an entomologist or agronomist among the named four-person team. The dossier flags it directly -- "no agriculture-industry operator, regulatory-affairs, or commercial-crop-protection experience found on either profile." A pollinator is not a pathogen. The translation from "I can crystallize a viral protein" to "I can hit a pest-unique molecular target that survives field conditions and a five-year EPA path" is not free, and it is exactly the autobiographical gap I read as the absence of the Anduril-Luckey pattern.

HQ Trivia is the rung I revisit when I think about how a company quietly dies from the inside: the Yusupov/Kroll feud was unresolved governance metastasizing while the app was still going viral. Molagri does not have that problem -- Zaky Hassan is named CEO unambiguously on the YC page, Min Jin is the co-founder, six years of lab-mate runway means they have already fought about whose name goes first on a paper. The pattern HQ Trivia warned me about doesn't appear in Molagri's dossier; the pattern Molagri is missing is something different -- it's the operator who can walk into a Bayer or Corteva commercial meeting and not get eaten.

Mechanism analysis on the magically-weird dimension: biopesticides genuinely is the "too unsexy" lane -- incumbents Bayer, Syngenta, and Corteva cannibalize their own broad-spectrum SKUs if a pollinator-sparing chemistry actually works, which is the behavioral moat the dossier names. But weird only compounds when the category is also *uncrowded*. Molagri lists seven-plus funded competitors -- GreenLight Biosciences at $277M raised, Provivi at $248M, AgBiome at $230M before it sold its brands to Certis and its platform to Ginkgo, plus RNAissance Ag, Innatrix, Pebble Labs, AgriCell. That AgBiome footnote is the one that should make any seed investor in Molagri sweat: $230M of venture capital exited at fire-sale prices because the unit economics of biopesticide distribution did not bend. The category is unsexy; it is no longer non-consensus.

Counterfactual on capital-intensive frontier swing: Molagri would jump a tier here if a single specific thing were true -- a named pest-unique molecular target with a published in-vitro IC50 against a target pest species (corn rootworm, diamondback moth, varroa-safe), even pre-field. The Canadian PMRA pathway is real, the neonicotinoid restrictions in the EU are real, the 9.6%-18.5% CAGR to a $20.46B 2035 market is real. The frontier is there; what's missing is one sentence in the dossier that names the molecule and the pest. Without it I'm scoring the category, not the company.

Single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability: Flock Safety I invested in because I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons cafe -- the product was already in the world, photographable, installable. Molagri's website returned a 403 to the dossier's own WebFetch. There is no formulation I can spray on a tomato plant, no field-trial photograph, no extension-agent testimonial, no pre-print with efficacy numbers. The molecular target is asserted but unnamed. For a Cyan check the tactile artifact is the entry point; with Molagri I have a YC company-description paragraph and two Nature papers in an unrelated organism.

My 36-month forecast for Molagri: still pre-revenue, in EPA/PMRA submission limbo on a single lead candidate, raised a $4-8M bridge from an ag-specialty fund (Fall Line, Pontifax Agtech, or AgFunder) at a flat or modest-up valuation, with the most probable terminal outcome being a sub-$30M platform sale to Ginkgo Bioworks or Certis Biologicals on the AgBiome template. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: a strong unsexy-category fit with a weak founder-problem-contact score produces companies that get the regulatory door open but cannot generate the commercial pull to escape the AgBiome gravity well.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the DeepMind exception in my own file -- Hassabis arrived with pedigree credentials, not an outsider trajectory, and the polymath compounding across chess, neuroscience, and games was the override. Hassan and Jin co-publishing in *both* Nature Communications and Nature Microbiology is a non-trivial signal of cross-domain molecular fluency, and one could argue that designing pest-specific targets with built-in resistance modeling is exactly the kind of "age of the polymath" play I claim to want. The bear answer is that DeepMind's polymath was within a *coherent* domain stack -- intelligence, learning, games -- not a jump from viral spike proteins to agricultural entomology and field formulation chemistry and EPA toxicology and grower distribution. The dossier names zero ag-industry advisors. One verifiable condition would flip me: Molagri publishes or pre-prints a named pest-target with in-vitro efficacy data AND adds a named operator from the Bayer/Syngenta/Corteva commercial side or a former PMRA biopesticide reviewer to the team within 12 months. Until then, Molagri is the category I want with the founders I don't.
