# Saffron -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Saffron is the gap between the polish and the substance. They have a beautifully structured comparison table on their website showing how they stack up against HackerRank, CodeSignal, and Rounds.so across a dozen feature dimensions. What they don't appear to have is users. This is a pattern I've seen many times. "A characteristic mistake of young founders is to go through the motions of starting a startup. [...] We saw this so often we made up a name for it -- playing house." I'm not saying that's definitively what's happening here, but a company with zero public traction signals and a polished competitive matrix raises that question. The Airbnb founders, by contrast, were sleeping on air mattresses in strangers' apartments and selling cereal to keep the lights on. They had no competitive matrix. They had desperation and ingenuity. I want to see some version of that here, and I don't.

The underlying insight is real: engineering interviews that ban AI tools when engineers use AI all day are measuring the wrong thing. That's a genuine discontinuity, and "worrying that you're late is one of the signs of a good idea" -- the fact that Rounds.so, Aptora, and others are emerging in adjacent spaces confirms the timing. But here's my concern: this idea doesn't look bad initially. It looks obviously correct. The best ideas in my experience -- Facebook, Airbnb, even Stripe -- sounded questionable or even stupid when you first heard them. "Let candidates use AI the way they actually work and measure how they use it" is something any engineering manager would nod along to immediately. When an idea sounds obviously right to everyone, the question becomes: why does this specific team win? Because the incumbents will hear the same idea and nod too. HackerRank has $221M in revenue and $114M in funding. CodeSignal has $90M. They already offer configurable AI tools in their assessments. Adding interaction tracking and code attribution is an incremental product extension for them, not a rebuild-from-scratch moment. The schlep here isn't seeing the problem -- it's building enough of a lead before the incumbents respond.

The founders have impressive credentials: MIT, Stanford, Harvey Mudd, Jane Street backgrounds, NeurIPS and ICML publications. Two of the three went to the same high school in Las Vegas, which is a genuinely positive signal -- the relationship between founders has to be strong enough to survive what a startup does to it, and friendships forged before the company existed tend to be more durable. But credentials are not determination. "You can recognize genuinely smart people by their ability to say things like 'I don't know'" -- and I'd add that you can recognize genuinely determined people by their ability to show you what they've already done under constraint. Where's the evidence that these founders are relentlessly resourceful? Yao built edtech products reaching 50,000 users, which is the strongest signal in the batch -- that shows he can ship things people actually use. But the collective team hasn't demonstrated the cockroach quality I look for. No revenue, no LOIs, no "we personally onboarded our first five customers by sitting with their hiring managers for six hours each." The domain expertise question also nags at me: these founders have been through rigorous technical interviews (Jane Street's hiring bar is legendary), but experiencing hiring from the candidate side is different from understanding it from the employer side. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise" -- and the domain here is employer hiring workflows, not being an interviewee.

The bull case is genuinely compelling, and I want to be honest about it: if AI-augmented coding becomes the universal default within two years -- which seems likely -- then every company hiring software engineers will need assessment tools built for that world. The incumbents' entire product architecture and content investment is oriented around algorithmic puzzles, which is a paradigm on the way out. Saffron's $20/assessment pricing with zero interviewer hours is structurally superior unit economics to both the puzzle platforms and especially to Karat's human-interviewer model. The potential data moat -- capturing granular human-AI interaction patterns across thousands of assessments to train better scoring models -- is the kind of thing that compounds over time. If these founders prove to be faster learners and more aggressive executors than they currently appear, and if they get enough assessment volume before HackerRank wakes up, this could be a real business. The market is big enough and the paradigm shift is real enough that whoever wins this transition captures something meaningful.

But conviction requires more than a reasonable idea in a real market. It requires seeing something in the founders that tells me they'll find a way through when things get hard -- and things always get hard. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." I'm not saying these are bad people; they're clearly smart and technically capable. But at this stage, when I'm purely picking founders, I need a signal of the quality I once described as someone who "does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive." The dossier gives me credentials and a well-built website. It doesn't give me obsession. The Anthropic platform dependency adds a structural concern -- building your core experience on Claude Code specifically means a single API pricing change or a competitive move from Anthropic could gut your product. That's the kind of existential risk that requires founders with the running-back flexibility to go sideways or even backwards when the path forward disappears. I'd need to meet these founders before I could assess whether they have that quality, and right now, the public evidence doesn't give me enough to bet my own money.
