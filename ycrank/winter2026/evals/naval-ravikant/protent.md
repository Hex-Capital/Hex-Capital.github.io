# Protent -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Protent is a paradox that demands explanation: the founders appear to be in their early twenties, yet they claim deployments with police departments in three major metro areas -- Atlanta, Chicago, St. Louis. Government procurement is where startups go to die slowly. It takes seasoned enterprise sales leaders months to navigate RFPs, CJIS compliance, and municipal budget cycles. Either these founders have cracked something about government distribution that most people haven't -- or "deployed with" means something much less binding than a signed contract. The dossier shows no revenue signal whatsoever. That ambiguity is the central puzzle of this deal.

On specific knowledge: Srihan Balaji deployed RL-optimized video intelligence at Lockheed Martin and worked in AI/ML at Amazon. Abhisheik Sharma published NLP and sentiment analysis research. These are relevant credentials, not deep ones. Srihan graduated from Thomas Jefferson High School around 2021, which puts him at roughly 22-23 years old. His Lockheed and Amazon experience is likely one to two years -- possibly internships. That's exposure to the problem space, not the kind of accumulated, non-transferable understanding that would take a competitor years to reconstruct. Compare this to when I backed the Notion team -- Ivan Zhao had spent years in a near-monastic relationship with the problem of how humans organize thought. Or my Clearview investment, where the founders had built something technically unprecedented that no one else had attempted. Protent is applying established ML techniques -- object detection, sentiment analysis, reinforcement learning -- to video feeds. A competent team at Lumana, which has $64M and vision-language models already deployed on 50K cameras, could build escalation detection as a feature, not a company.

The leverage architecture concerns me most. Software has inherent leverage -- code scales without headcount. But Protent's go-to-market is the opposite of leveraged. Government procurement means long sales cycles, per-deployment customization, compliance certification overhead, integration with existing bodycam and dispatch infrastructure. Each new police department is a project, not a click. This isn't Replit, where one engineer's work serves millions of users. It's closer to a defense contractor model wrapped in SaaS pricing. The "SaaS" tag on their YC page doesn't change the underlying physics of selling into government. Per-camera pricing on a multi-year government contract is recurring revenue, sure -- but it's not the kind of leverage that produces nonlinear output from minimal input. You're renting access to police procurement, and rent requires continuous effort.

The strongest bull case for Protent: if these metro deployments are real and generating real data, they could build the most accurate escalation-detection models in the market through a data flywheel that no competitor with commercial-only deployments can match. Law enforcement video is categorically different from commercial security footage -- the escalation patterns, the behavioral signals, the operating conditions are unique. If Protent accumulates thousands of hours of real law enforcement scenario data, that becomes genuinely hard to replicate. The GovTech compliance moat (CJIS, FedRAMP) also compounds -- once you're certified and integrated, switching costs are real. And the timing might be right: Verkada validated the $1B+ market for AI video intelligence, but nobody has built the purpose-built law enforcement layer. That's a wedge. If the founders can execute on government sales despite their youth, the defensibility could be real. What would change my mind is evidence that these deployments are generating revenue and that the founders have discovered a repeatable motion for government procurement that doesn't require the typical 18-month cycle.

But the weight of the evidence points to Pass. The specific knowledge is thin -- resume credentials, not lived obsession. The technology is built on increasingly commoditized foundations. The competitive landscape features players with 100x to 1400x more capital. The contrarian angle -- that AI surveillance of citizens by police is a good thing to build -- generates controversy, but controversy and contrarian insight are different things. Everyone in the AI video space knows the government opportunity exists; the real question is whether the political and regulatory environment will allow it to scale. Multiple U.S. cities have already banned facial recognition. "Sentiment analysis" on live video of citizens is one bad headline away from becoming the next regulatory target. And critically: this is an application-layer company in a vertical I've learned to be skeptical of. My best returns come from infrastructure that enables an entire category -- not from the application sitting on top of commoditized infrastructure selling into the hardest possible customer segment.
