# Async -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Async is a two-person services consultancy — Ben Smith and Connor Park, both University of Michigan — dressed in AI-agent-for-vertical clothing, and its dimensional signature is a capital structure that could go non-standard if anyone underwrote it that way, paired with founders whose three pivots inside a single YC batch (nonprofit fundraising → Skope outcome-billing → Skope law firms → Async service businesses, per Launch HN August 2025 and withasync.com) actively falsify the missionary-temperament filter. That pairing — a maybe-interesting shape sitting on top of an unresolved thesis — is what I need to price.

The canonical Pass Example from my own playbook is the 2026 YC seed pitching "an AI agent for [enterprise vertical]" at consensus AI Series B pricing, and Async is that pitch with the vertical field left as a dropdown: law, healthcare, real estate. Harvey ($1.22B raised, $11B post-money per TAMradar March 2026), Legora ($700M raised, $5.55B March 2026), and Spellbook ($63M Series B, Khosla-led, October 2025) already ship the same Word add-in Async leads with. When Sequoia and GIC are writing $200M into Harvey the same quarter my SAFE would price, my seed dollar generates zero informational alpha on the category — I am buying commodity exposure to a curve the market has already resolved, at whatever the S25 batch median clears. No tier-1 has visibly passed here because the round has no signal at all — Async is not non-consensus, it is beneath the consensus radar, which is not the same trade.

Teamshares is the load-bearing analogy for the second dimension because Sam Altman-shaped SaaS cap tables cannot underwrite an employee-ownership rollup, and only a fund willing to structure differently can play. Async's "partner & audit → scope & deploy → prove & expand → maintain & compound" engagement language on withasync.com is genuinely services-shaped — retainer per firm, custom agent per workflow, integrations into SharePoint and Outlook and CRM — and if Ben Smith and Connor Park told me they were building a holdco of vertical AI practices with revenue-share economics into each deployed firm, I would lean in. But the dossier shows a two-person team targeting three regulated verticals (healthcare adds HIPAA scope beyond current SOC 2 Type I) on a generic YC SAFE, which is Teamshares' structural insight without the structural instrument — services drainage force-fit into a venture cap table.

Drop.io is the founder-conviction comparison that matters here because I bootstrapped a file-sharing primitive against free incumbents for three years before the Facebook exit in October 2010, and the mechanism was that I could not be talked out of the thesis. Async has pivoted the underlying thesis three times in twelve months — nonprofit fundraising agents, then outcome-based billing infrastructure, then legal-AI, now horizontal service businesses — and no public writing exists from either Ben Smith or Connor Park explaining why the market got service-firm AI wrong. Connor Park's GitHub shows 18 followers and 72 total stars across 11 repos; Ben Smith has no findable Twitter. That is not missionary temperament running a Wizard-of-Oz sprint; that is a two-person team optimizing toward whatever the S25 partner call blessed most recently, and the pivot velocity is the anti-signal.

John Fish's Bookshelved (January 2024) is the single-anchor parallel for founder-as-brand: I underwrote the check because Fish arrived with an owned YouTube distribution channel that made customer acquisition a rounding error. Async arrives with the opposite — one named customer (Adriena Clifton, Clifton Law PLLC, a solo/boutique firm) on the withasync.com testimonial slot, zero Product Hunt presence, zero Chrome Web Store listing, useskope.com email domain still colliding with the withasync.com brand. The counterfactual for Async to clear the Bookshelved bar would be Ben Smith running a legal-ops newsletter with 10K+ paid subscribers before the round, or Connor Park having shipped an open-source workflow-agent framework with real GitHub gravity — neither exists.

Solana at $0.05 in 2018 is the category-sleeve anchor because "crypto is fundamentally disruptive" and "AI is just more internet" was the framing I gave Joe Lonsdale on podcast #113 in 2024, and Async sits on the wrong side of that sentence. Over 18 months, Async's category compounds against it, not for it: Harvey pushes down-market on price to defend the $11B mark, Spellbook's $50M Series B funds a Word add-in that already overlaps Async's lead interface, and the AI-consultancy tier (Deloitte's AI practice, boutique GenAI shops) commoditizes the custom-agent-build motion from below. The sleeve has three well-funded incumbents pressing from above and undifferentiated services shops pressing from below, and Async's two-person team is in the squeeze zone with no distinguishing instrument.

My 36-month forecast for Async is a sub-$10M acqui-hire into a mid-market legal-tech or vertical-SaaS acquirer (think a Litera, an Ironclad, or a Clio-adjacent buyer — not Harvey or Legora, who are too far up-market to notice), or a fourth pivot into pure AI-consulting services with sub-$1M ARR by end of 2029. The mechanism is that Ben Smith and Connor Park are executing a rational YC-batch optimization loop rather than a missionary long-arc thesis, and the loop terminates when either an acquirer takes the team-and-tech or the seed runway forces a services-consulting resting state. Neither outcome returns a Slow-shaped fund.

The bull case that stings is Fin: Andrew Kortina and I explicitly chose "a completely manual process" (Latitud Podcast #27) and pivoted from consumer voice assistant to workplace analytics by July 2019 — the Async pivot arc looks superficially like the Fin arc, and a Sam Lessin who lived that arc has to take the pattern seriously. The dismantle is that Fin had one thesis (human-assistance-as-a-service) executed through multiple product surfaces, whereas Async has swapped the underlying customer three times (donors → software buyers → law firms → service SMBs) — that is not tacking through a regatta toward a fixed compass bearing, that is switching regattas. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Ben Smith publishes a public essay staking out a non-obvious claim about why vertical-agent deployment fails at Harvey's scale but wins at boutique-firm scale, and Async holds its current customer segment for four consecutive quarters without a fourth pivot. Absent both, this is a pass.
