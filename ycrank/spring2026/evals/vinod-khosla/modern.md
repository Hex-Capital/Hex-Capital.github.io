# Modern -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Moveworks sold to ServiceNow for $2.85 billion. Aisera sold to Automation Anywhere for roughly $1.5 billion. Two exits totaling over $4 billion in a single year — and the pitch for Modern is that the market now has a "vacuum." I see the opposite. ServiceNow just bought the technology it lacked, and its 8,000-person sales force will embed Moveworks' autonomous resolution into every enterprise renewal conversation by Q3. Automation Anywhere will do the same with Aisera across its RPA customer base. Modern isn't filling a vacuum — it's entering a market where the incumbents just armed themselves with exactly the weapons Modern is trying to build from scratch with two people. The competitive timing story is a bull case that sounds good for thirty seconds and then collapses when you ask who has distribution.

Modern's website claims 62% reduction in ticket handling and 74% faster response time — metrics that tell me everything about why this doesn't fit my framework. A 62% improvement is not a discontinuity. Nobody's life fundamentally changes because a password reset takes four minutes instead of ten. I have said publicly that I see AI copilots for enterprise verticals at every YC Demo Day — "AI for accountants, AI for recruiters" — and my response is always the same: the consequence of full success is a moderately profitable SaaS company. Modern automates IT tickets, HR onboarding, and expense approvals. If it works perfectly, CIOs save some headcount. The world does not look different. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means limitless clean energy, or Impossible Foods, where success means protein without slaughtering ten billion animals a year. Modern's success means faster password resets at mid-market companies. The consequence magnitude is not there.

Seb Poole is the one dimension where Modern shows real signal. Two prior exits — a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in February 2023 — plus roughly 20 million AUD raised from investors including Alibaba for an earlier e-commerce venture in Hong Kong. That is a founder who has built, sold, and rebuilt across three different industries: e-commerce, mental health SaaS, and now enterprise IT automation. The cross-domain trajectory suggests genuine first-principles thinking and learning velocity, not a domain expert running the same playbook. Aleks Tomovski's engineering background spanning DevOps and SRE across Melbourne, Vilnius, Denver, and Skopje adds legitimate infrastructure credibility. If Modern were attacking a problem with 100x consequence magnitude, this is a founding team I'd look at seriously. But a good athlete running a 40% efficiency play is still a 40% efficiency play.

Modern's "plain language workflow definition" and integration with twelve enterprise systems — ServiceNow, Zendesk, Workday, BambooHR, SAP, Salesforce, Slack, Datadog — sounds like a product, not a technology moat. The dossier itself acknowledges that "the core technology (LLM-powered ticket resolution with workflow orchestration) is buildable by well-funded competitors." Ravenna raised $15 million from Khosla Ventures and Madrona in April 2025 with a similar AI-agent-for-ITSM thesis, Slack-native and mid-market focused. Workativ offers "similar AI-agent feature set." When five companies are building the same thing simultaneously using the same foundational LLM capabilities, nobody has a technology wedge — they have an execution race. I prefer technology risk to market risk, and Modern has all market risk and zero discernible technology risk. There is no proprietary model, no novel architecture, no science breakthrough. This is integration engineering, not a non-linear approach.

The "Modern Managed" offering — where the company operates the customer's IT service desk — actively contradicts the radical affordability thesis. Managed services scale linearly with customer count and require headcount growth. This is not a path to making IT operations 10x cheaper; it is a path to becoming an outsourced IT staffing company with an AI layer. When I evaluate whether a startup could make an expensive service approach zero marginal cost, Modern's managed-services component moves in the opposite direction. The self-serve platform has some cost-reduction potential, but 62% ticket reduction is not the 10x collapse in cost that my framework requires.

Let me construct the strongest bull case: Seb Poole is a repeat founder with two exits and demonstrated cross-domain learning velocity. The ITSM market is $15 billion growing at 16% CAGR. ServiceNow's acquisition of Moveworks creates a genuine business model conflict — ServiceNow prices by ticket volume, so eliminating tickets cannibalizes its own revenue model, which should slow full deployment of autonomous resolution. Modern's "live in under a day" positioning compresses the enterprise sales cycle. The SOC 2 Type 2 and ISO 27001 certifications at this stage show operational maturity unusual for a two-person pre-seed. And the DoorDash exception exists — I funded an execution-heavy marketplace with no novel technology because the consequence magnitude and founder quality overrode the technical novelty requirement. Could Modern be a DoorDash exception? No. DoorDash was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of consumers — planetary-scale consequence magnitude. Modern is optimizing back-office workflows for IT administrators. The exception requires extreme consequence magnitude to activate, and Modern does not have it. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Modern demonstrated that its autonomous resolution capability could replace entire IT departments — not reduce ticket handling by 62%, but eliminate the IT service desk function entirely at a Fortune 500 company — the consequence magnitude would shift from incremental efficiency to structural labor transformation. Show me a customer that eliminated 90%+ of IT service desk headcount, and the conversation changes.

Modern has a competent repeat founder in Seb Poole attacking a real but incremental problem with a commoditized technical approach. The consequence magnitude scored lowest because enterprise IT ticket automation, even at full success, does not materially change how millions of people live — it changes how thousands of IT administrators allocate their hours. The non-linear technical approach scored low because LLM-powered workflow orchestration with integrations is the definition of a wrapper, not a wedge. The founder quality is the sole dimension that rises above median, and it is insufficient to override the thesis mismatch on every other dimension.
