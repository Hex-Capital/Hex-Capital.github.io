# Advocate -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Advocate's value proposition is "no new hires, no workflow changes, pure margin." That sentence tells me everything I need to know about the ambition ceiling. When the best version of your pitch is "nothing changes except we capture more billing revenue," you have described an optimization tool, not a company that changes the world. If Advocate succeeds completely — every skilled nursing facility and home health agency in America running voice-to-billing on every care coordination activity — the consequence is that facilities extract more money from CMS for work already being performed. No patient receives better care. No cost comes down. No new capability emerges. The consequence of full success is a redistribution of existing government healthcare spend, and I have a specific red flag for that: companies targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success means feeding billions of people protein at a fraction of the cost of animal agriculture. Advocate's success means Medicare pays more. That is not the kind of consequence that justifies my attention.

The technical approach compounds my concern. The dossier is explicit: "The core technology — speech-to-text plus medical code mapping — is built on widely available components." Speech recognition via Whisper or cloud ASR, LLM-based code matching against CPT databases — this is integration work, not invention. Abridge, DeepScribe, and Suki already capture clinical voice data and are extending into coding. CodaMetrix, with $109M raised, already automates medical coding. Adding care coordination billing codes to any of these existing pipelines is, as the dossier notes, "a natural product extension." When I evaluate non-linearity, I ask: is this a 10x improvement or a 2x improvement? Advocate is neither — it is a new application of existing 1x technology to a specific billing workflow. That is what I mean when I talk about wrappers versus wedges.

The founding team has sensible credentials. Justinas Banys built data systems at Garner Health, which reached $1.35B in valuation — he knows healthcare data infrastructure. Andrew Baran's personal connection to care coordination through his mother and his healthcare investing experience at Radian Capital provide authentic domain context. But I have no evidence of the learning velocity I look for. No public GitHub repos from the CTO of an AI company. No traction signals — not even a functioning website (advocatewellbeing.com redirects to chiefofhealth.com with minimal content). The Leilo co-founding claim in Baran's profile could not be independently verified against public sources that name different founders. When I evaluate founders, I look for trajectory — what changed, what surprised them, what they learned in the past three months. Nothing in this dossier shows me that trajectory.

The strongest bull case: care coordination billing is genuinely under-captured across thousands of fragmented facilities. CPT 99490 alone is ~$62/patient/month, and most facilities lack the staff to document and code these activities. Advocate could spread virally with a percentage-of-recovered-revenue model, generating high-margin recurring revenue with a painless adoption path. The CTO's Garner Health experience and the CEO's personal domain connection give them an authentic entry point. If they move fast and lock in facility workflows before Abridge or CodaMetrix extend downstream, they could build switching costs through audit trail accumulation. I do not dismiss this — it is a legitimate small-to-mid-scale business opportunity. But a legitimate business opportunity is not what I invest in. I want to either lose my money or make fifty times my money. Advocate is designed to produce three-to-five-times returns in a niche healthcare IT segment. That is fine for most investors. It is a waste of my time and attention.

What moves in the wrong direction on my framework is the affordability dimension. I have said repeatedly that AI should deliver free healthcare. Advocate does the opposite — it increases aggregate healthcare spending by helping facilities capture reimbursement they were previously leaving unclaimed. Every dollar Advocate recovers is a dollar CMS pays that it was not paying before, for care that was already being delivered. This is value extraction from the public payer, not value creation for patients. My portfolio thesis converges on making expensive necessities radically cheap. Advocate makes the healthcare system marginally more expensive. The two dimensions that lock this as a Pass are consequence magnitude — billing optimization for facilities does not change how anyone lives — and technical approach — voice-to-CPT-code mapping on commodity AI components with CodaMetrix, Sully.ai, and every major AI scribe company positioned to build the same thing.
