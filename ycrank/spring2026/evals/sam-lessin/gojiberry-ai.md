# Gojiberry AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The thing I can't stop staring at is the TrustMRR listing. On March 9, 2026 -- one week after hitting #1 on Product Hunt and while actively participating in YC S26 -- the company was listed for sale at $11.15 million, roughly a 6x revenue multiple. That's either the most sophisticated optionality play I've ever seen from a pre-seed team, or it's the clearest possible signal that the founders themselves view this as a flip, not a build. When I look at the full picture -- CoCo AI bootstrapped and sold, Edusign bootstrapped and sold, MechaChain sold to Kevuru Games -- I see a founding team that has mastered the art of building small profitable software businesses and exiting before the market catches up. That's a legitimate skill. It's just not the skill that produces venture-scale outcomes. These founders have "tizz" in abundance but they're applying it to a sprint, not a marathon. Helium took seven years from seed to unicorn. Solana required holding through an entire crypto winter. This team is simultaneously raising venture capital and pricing their exit at $11 million. That tells me everything about the ceiling they see.

Now, run the founder-idea calibration. Pierre-Eliott, Roman, and Dylan are clearly competent operators -- three exits between them, 100+ paying customers within 60 days of a pivot, aggressive distribution on $380/month. The "sane" part checks out. But "AI-powered LinkedIn intent signals for small sales teams" is not an insane idea. It might be the single most consensus B2B SaaS pitch in 2026. Apollo, Clay, Instantly, Lemlist, HeyReach, Amplemarket -- the competitor table in this dossier reads like a census of the category. When Apollo acquires Pocus in March 2026 specifically to add signal-based selling to its 275-million-contact database, you're not looking at a non-consensus frontier. You're looking at a category reaching terminal velocity. This is a sane person with a sane idea, which is my weakest configuration -- it means the opportunity is already priced, and you're buying execution risk in a commodity market where the incumbents have orders-of-magnitude more distribution.

The structural moat question is where my framework delivers the harshest verdict. Every component of Gojiberry's product -- LinkedIn signal detection, LLM-generated personalized messages, automated follow-up sequences, CRM integration -- is pure software. Not software amplifying physical operations, not software enabling a new protocol, but software doing software things on someone else's platform. This is exactly what I mean when I say the SaaS era is over. LinkedIn can build this natively tomorrow. Apollo just bought the capability last month. Any team with LinkedIn API access and an LLM can reproduce the feature set. The claimed "data flywheel" from 1,000 customers is not a moat when your largest competitor has a 275-million-contact database. The <5% churn and CRM integrations create switching friction, not structural defensibility. And the existential platform dependency -- both signal detection and outreach delivery running through LinkedIn's infrastructure -- means a single TOS enforcement change could disable the product overnight. This is the Birchbox pattern applied to B2B: a replicable model in a market where dozens of well-funded competitors can build the same thing faster. The lesson I took from Birchbox was that narrative without structural moat is the worst venture outcome -- too successful to die quickly, too fragile to compound. Gojiberry's moat erodes with every new model release and every incumbent feature launch.

The strongest bull case demands honest engagement. The unit economics are genuinely remarkable for a pre-seed company: $175K MRR, 55% month-over-month growth, 75% profit margins, $127 CAC with a 1.3-month payback period. If you believe execution velocity IS the moat -- that this team can simply out-iterate competitors in the SMB segment that enterprise players can't profitably serve at $99/month -- then the math works for a meaningful outcome. HeyReach bootstrapped to $10M ARR. Instantly bootstrapped to $20M ARR. There's a legitimate path where Gojiberry becomes a $20-50M ARR business serving the underserved SMB tail. The pricing floor argument has some structural logic: 6sense at $58K ACV genuinely cannot serve a $99/month customer without reinventing its entire go-to-market. But this is the bull case for a good business, not for an infinity story. The founders' own behavior -- listing for sale at 6x revenue -- suggests they agree. I'd rather fund a painting company with a tech layer than a tech company that IS the layer, and Gojiberry is all layer, no substrate.

There is no new primitive being created here. LinkedIn is the protocol. LinkedIn provides the signal layer, the identity graph, the messaging infrastructure. Gojiberry is an application that reads from and writes to someone else's platform -- the textbook definition of a DApp without its own chain. Compare this to Venmo, which created a new peer-to-peer payment primitive, or Aleo, which established zero-knowledge privacy as infrastructure. Gojiberry creates nothing that other companies build on. It's a feature set waiting to be absorbed, and Apollo's Pocus acquisition is the opening act of that absorption. The narrative ceiling is correspondingly constrained: "GTM Brain for small sales teams" doesn't elastically expand into a new economic category. It contracts as incumbents build downward and AI makes every competitor's version of this tool 90% as good within months.
