# Tepali -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the ghost of a different company. Chrisvin's personal website references founding "Lotus, an AI startup backed by Y Combinator (W26)" with a different email domain. A GitHub repo called "lotus-mcp" exists under his account. But the YC page says Tepali. Something changed during the batch -- a rename, a pivot, or both. This matters enormously for how I evaluate the idea, because the question I always ask first is: how did the founders find this problem? If you arrived at medspas mid-batch after starting somewhere else, your connection to medspa operations is measured in weeks, not years. That's not organic discovery. That's the startup idea machine running at batch speed.

Even setting aside the pivot signal, the origin story here reads like analogous reasoning, not lived experience. Vishnu built all-in-one software for coffee shops at Dripos. Chrisvin watched AI transform field services at Rilla. Together they concluded: medspas are a fragmented, high-growth service vertical that needs the same treatment. That's a perfectly logical thesis. It's also exactly the kind of idea I've warned about -- one that emerges from pattern-matching across markets rather than from personally encountering a broken workflow. Neither founder appears to have run a medspa, managed one, injected Botox, or reconciled a medspa's ad spend with patient LTV at 2am on a Sunday. The problem they're solving is real. But they found it by looking at market maps, not by living inside the pain. When I funded Stripe, the Collisons hadn't just noticed that payments were broken in the abstract -- they'd been personally frustrated writing payment integration code as developers. That gap between observing a market and inhabiting a problem is the gap I see here.

The competitive landscape is the other thing that concerns me. This is not a market hiding in plain sight because the schlep scares everyone away. Boulevard has $188M in funding and an $800M valuation. Zenoti is a unicorn. Vagaro is at $1B. These are not sleepy incumbents -- Boulevard explicitly raised $80M last year to invest in AI capabilities. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where three competitors have raised a combined $400M+, and the stated differentiation is "we're AI-native," I worry about the clock. The "built before AI" architectural advantage is real but temporary. Boulevard's engineers are not going to sit still while a two-person team tries to eat their market with an LLM layer. The structural argument that incumbents can't retrofit AI into legacy architectures is the same argument people made about every technology wave -- and it's sometimes true, but it's never as durable as founders hope.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Vishnu's Dripos experience is genuinely relevant -- he spent years building exactly this type of consolidated operational software for multi-location service businesses, working directly with support teams to understand fragmentation pain. That's not nothing. CMU CS plus hands-on vertical SaaS experience means he can build the product. And the medspa market is growing 20%+ annually, meaning a wave of new locations are making first-time software decisions with zero switching costs. If Tepali can position itself as the default choice for greenfield medspas -- the way Stripe positioned itself as the default for new developers -- the greenfield strategy could work without needing to rip customers away from Boulevard. The marketing-to-LTV attribution feature is also clever: it's the kind of specific, measurable value proposition that medspa owners can immediately understand because they're bleeding money on Meta ads with no idea what's working.

But for me to invest my own money, I need the founders to have an unfair advantage rooted in genuine domain knowledge, not just technical competence applied to a market they researched. Vishnu knows how to build multi-module operational software -- that's transferable skill, and it's valuable. But he doesn't know medspas. The specific domain knowledge that would make Tepali's AI actually smarter than what Boulevard ships in six months -- understanding injection protocols, treatment spacing, seasonal demand patterns, provider licensing requirements across states -- that knowledge has to come from somewhere, and right now it's not on the team. They're building a HIPAA-compliant medical records system as two people without healthcare experience. That's not schlep blindness being overcome; that's a compliance minefield being underestimated.

I'd want to revisit this if two things change: first, if the founders can show me medspa owners who found the product and refused to give it back -- real pull, not projected demand from TAM slides. And second, if they've brought in genuine medspa operational expertise, either through a domain-expert hire or through spending enough months embedded in actual medspa operations that they've developed the intuitions no market research can substitute for. Right now, this is a smart team applying transferable skills to a researched market. Sometimes that works. But it's not the pattern that produces my best investments.
