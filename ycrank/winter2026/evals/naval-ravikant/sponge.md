# Sponge -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is real and directly transferable to the problem. Three engineers from Stripe's crypto division -- one who led stablecoin financial accounts development, one who helped launch Stripe's first cryptocurrency product, one who built payment systems in the same unit -- are now building financial infrastructure for AI agents. This isn't consultants discovering a trend. These people spent years inside the machine that processes a meaningful fraction of internet commerce, specifically in the part of that machine that handles novel monetary rails. Building payment systems that handle real money at scale produces knowledge you can't acquire from documentation or weekend hackathons. When I backed CoinList, part of the thesis was that the people who understood token distribution mechanics at a plumbing level would build better infrastructure than those who understood tokens at a theoretical level. The Sponge team has that same quality -- they know where the pipes connect.

The architecture maps cleanly to my infrastructure preference. Wallet plus Gateway plus Catalog is a three-sided picks-and-shovels play: agents need wallets, businesses need to accept agent payments, and both sides need a discovery layer. This is structurally similar to what AngelList did for startup capital -- not the companies raising money, not the investors writing checks, but the rails connecting them. Each new service on the Catalog makes the Wallet more useful; each funded Wallet makes the Gateway more attractive to businesses. That flywheel, if it spins, creates leverage that scales with code, not headcount. The pay-per-use transaction model means revenue grows with volume, not with sales reps. This is the right shape for a leverage business.

But here's where my conviction breaks down: this is not a contrarian bet. It's consensus dressed in infrastructure clothing. Mastercard unveiled Agent Pay. Visa introduced Intelligent Commerce. PayPal, Stripe, and Coinbase all launched agent payment toolkits. Four funded competitors have raised over $40 million combined -- Skyfire with a16z and Coinbase Ventures backing, Payman with Visa money, Natural with nearly $10 million at seed. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, smart people thought I was foolish. When I invested in Uber at $4 million, ride-sharing was illegal. Agent payments in 2026 is the opposite dynamic -- everyone agrees this is a real market. You don't get asymmetric returns betting on something the entire payments industry is already building toward. The efficient market is working here: the opportunity is priced in.

The existential threat compounds the consensus problem. Stripe -- the founders' former employer, the company whose infrastructure knowledge they carry -- launched x402 agent payments support on Base in February 2026. Stripe has millions of existing merchant relationships. If Stripe decides to build a full Wallet-plus-Gateway product, the competitive moat here evaporates. The founders know Stripe's architecture intimately, which cuts both ways: they know the gaps in Stripe's approach, but Stripe knows how to build what they're building. Former-employer risk is one of the most underappreciated dynamics in startup investing. The specific knowledge that qualifies these founders is the same knowledge their former employer retains institutionally.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If the agent economy materializes at scale -- genuinely autonomous agents conducting millions of daily transactions -- then the two-sided marketplace that achieves liquidity first wins. Stripe and Visa are optimizing for their existing human-merchant flows; they may not build the full-stack agent-native product. The Catalog as a discovery layer is something incumbents won't prioritize because it doesn't serve their existing customers. And the crypto-native architecture (multi-chain, stablecoin-based) positions Sponge for agent-to-agent transactions that traditional payment rails can't handle efficiently. If I believed this team could establish marketplace network effects before the better-funded competitors, and if Stripe stays focused on primitives rather than building the full stack, this could be the CoinList of the agent economy -- the infrastructure layer that every agent transacts through. The early implementations with AgentMail, Steel, and an Amazon beta pilot suggest product velocity.

But I keep returning to the leverage ratio of the investment itself. My best returns came from being non-consensus and right. Here, I'd be consensus and hoping to be first. The founders have genuine specific knowledge but acquired it corporately rather than through the kind of obsessive independent exploration that produces truly non-replicable insight. The technology -- TypeScript SDK, MCP integration, multi-chain wallet -- is competent engineering, not physics-first innovation that compounds at the fundamental layer. This is a good team building in a real market with real competitors, which is the profile of a reasonable bet, not an asymmetric one. I want the 1,000x outcome from the portfolio, not the 3x.
