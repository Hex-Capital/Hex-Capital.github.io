# Mount -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Armilla AI is already a Lloyd's coverholder serving Fortune 1000 companies with $25M in backed capacity. Testudo has Lloyd's syndicate backing from Apollo, Atrium, and QBE with $9.25M per-insured limits. Corgi has raised $108M and crossed $40M ARR. Harper has $47M in funding and 5,000 customers. Mount has a website published April 20, 2026 and two job postings. When I look at this competitive landscape, the first question isn't whether Mount's integrated red-teaming-plus-underwriting model is architecturally clever — it is — but whether these two founders understand what building a full-stack insurance company actually requires. Neither Fabian Amherd nor John Bachmann has any documented insurance industry experience. Mount calls itself an "insurer" on its website, but there's no evidence of carrier licensing, MGA status, Lloyd's coverholder arrangements, or reinsurance partnerships. This isn't schlep blindness — the founders seeing an unglamorous problem others avoid. This might just be blindness.

Fabian Amherd started a web development company at 16, studied CS with an ML focus at ETH Zürich, and deployed custom CNNs for real-time object detection at MESH, a robotics spinoff. John Bachmann founded Horizonn at 18, built it into five magazines generating 400K in annual revenue. Both are young builders, which is a pattern I respond to — Patrick Collison was a teenager when I first backed Auctomatic, and the Bindwell founders were 18 and 19 when I invested in their AI pesticide company. Mount's founders have that early-start intensity. But what I need from young founders is stories — not credentials, stories. How did Fabian solve a problem at MESH that should have been impossible with his resources? How did John land his first advertiser at Horizonn when no one takes an 18-year-old magazine publisher seriously? The dossier gives me résumé entries where I need campfire tales. Mount's founders might have the animal quality, but I can't see it through a credential list. Truly resourceful founders generate notable anecdotes; their absence from every public surface — no Twitter, 284 LinkedIn connections, 3 GitHub repos with 11 total stars — doesn't prove the quality isn't there, but it doesn't help me either.

The bull case for Mount is real, and I want to lay it out honestly. Insurance for AI agents is a genuine schlep — state-by-state licensing, actuarial modeling for a risk category with no loss history, reinsurance negotiations, regulatory compliance across jurisdictions. The EU AI Act's full deployer obligations arrive August 2026, creating a regulatory forcing function. Traditional insurers are actively retreating from AI risk coverage according to S&P Global's February 2026 report. There's a supply vacuum in a market projected to reach $182.97B by 2033. Mount's architectural insight — combining red-teaming with underwriting so every evaluation generates proprietary risk data — is the kind of compound flywheel that could produce superlinear returns if they achieve evaluation volume. Stripe succeeded partly because payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled other developers. Mount could be the Stripe of AI agent risk. That's the pitch, and it's coherent.

Here's why it doesn't hold. When Patrick and John Collison built Stripe, they were developers who had personally experienced the agony of integrating payments into web applications. They lived in the future — building software products — and built what was missing — a payments API that didn't make you want to quit programming. Mount's origin story, as presented in every available source, starts with market analysis: AI agents create liability, insurers can't price the risk, therefore opportunity. Fabian's ML background at ETH and MESH is strong technical foundation, but it's foundation for building evaluation tools, not for understanding the insurance buyer's pain. John's media company experience demonstrates commercial intensity but has zero overlap with insurance distribution or enterprise risk management. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. Mount feels like it was assembled from the intersection of two trend lines — AI agents and insurtech — rather than discovered through lived frustration with an unsolved problem.

Mount's idea also fails my Black Swan test in a specific way: AI agent insurance is not an idea that repels before it attracts. Nobody hears "insurance for AI agents in the era of the EU AI Act" and says "that will never work." They say "that makes sense." Four well-funded competitors already validate the category. This is exactly the dynamic I describe as a negative signal — when the consensus is that an idea is good, the returns have already been competed away. Mount's differentiation is the integrated red-teaming layer, which is genuinely novel, but "insurance with better data" is an incremental positioning advantage, not the kind of contrarian bet that produces power-law returns. The ADR certification concept — "SOC 2 for AI agents" — is interesting as a potential standard-setting play, but Mount has no visible traction, no community adoption, and no regulatory endorsement that would give it authority to define industry benchmarks.

One verifiable condition would flip me from Pass to Invest: evidence that Mount has already secured a carrier partnership, MGA license, or surplus lines authorization — and has at least one pilot customer with a bound policy. That would demonstrate that the founders have actually navigated the insurance regulatory schlep rather than simply identifying it as an addressable market. The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery, where Mount's market-analysis origin and absent personal frustration narrative score well below the threshold I need, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where four funded competitors and regulatory tailwinds confirm this is consensus rather than contrarian. Mount's founders are young and ambitious, and the insurance schlep is real, but the combination of a consensus idea, no domain expertise, well-capitalized competitors already writing policies, and an absent origin story puts this outside my framework for deploying personal capital.
