# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Fern: this is one of the most well-trodden, consensus-validated markets in AI right now. Otter.ai just crossed $100M in annual recurring revenue. Fireflies.ai hit a billion-dollar valuation. Read.ai has raised $81 million. Every VC I know is excited about AI meeting assistants. And that's precisely the signal that makes me pull back. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Fern is popular and easy to assess -- it's a better mousetrap in a category where everyone already agrees the mice need catching. When I backed SpaceX, people thought private rocketry was lunacy. When I wrote checks for Uber, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities. I'm not feeling that tension here. Nobody is going to tell Amit and Robert they're crazy for building a smarter meeting assistant. They might tell them it's crowded. That's a very different kind of skepticism, and it doesn't create the opening I look for.

If I were sitting across from these founders, my first question would be what it always is: tell me your story, not the business. And from what I can see in this dossier, their story is a strong technical career arc -- Amit's PhD at Purdue, his work training multimodal LLMs on Meta's LLaMA team, Robert's four years building low-latency inference at Reality Labs and infrastructure at Citadel Securities. Those are impressive credentials. But I'm listening for something else entirely. I want to know what broke them open, what hardship or obsession made them unable to NOT build this thing. What I see is two talented engineers who built similar technology at Meta and realized they could commercialize the approach independently. That's a legitimate startup origin, but it's a career-progression story, not a survival story. The biographical thread connecting the founder to the problem -- the thing that tells me they'll keep going when everything falls apart -- I can't find it here.

The bull case deserves a genuine hearing, because the founder-market fit at the technical layer is unusually tight. These two literally built Meta's multimodal assistant for Ray-Ban smart glasses -- real-time audio, video, contextual awareness, low latency. That's not adjacent experience; that's the exact engineering challenge Fern is tackling. The "social awareness" concept -- an AI that understands conversational turn-taking and knows when to speak versus stay silent -- is a genuinely hard problem, and most incumbents built their stacks around batch transcription pipelines that would need to be rebuilt from scratch to compete. If Amit and Robert can establish this real-time participation capability before OpenAI and Google bake it natively into their platforms, and before Otter or Fireflies can retool their architectures, there's a legitimate window. For an investor who weights technical moats and timing windows more heavily than I do, this could be compelling.

But even granting that technical edge, I keep returning to what this company does in the world. When I think about my portfolio -- Uber creating income for drivers, Postmates for delivery workers, Affirm giving transparent credit access to people who'd been exploited by hidden fees, Flock Safety bringing public safety data to communities that couldn't see what was happening around them -- these companies made invisible systems visible for people who needed it. Fern makes sales teams more productive. That's fine, but it doesn't create economic access for anyone who lacks it. It optimizes the workflow of already well-resourced knowledge workers. I don't feel that pull toward something that changes who gets to participate in the economy.

There are also practical concerns that compound my hesitation. The name collision with another YC company called Fern is a real problem -- confusing your brand with a company from a recent YC batch creates unnecessary friction in a space where you're already fighting for attention against well-funded incumbents. The privacy dynamics of Shadow Mode -- an invisible AI listening to everything in a meeting -- will face serious resistance from enterprise procurement teams and create regulatory headaches across jurisdictions. And the commoditization risk is real: every major foundation model provider is racing to improve real-time voice capabilities, and the "social awareness" layer that differentiates Fern depends on fine-tuning and proprietary data they haven't yet accumulated. On the positive side, the co-founders have a documented prior working relationship from Meta, which gives me some comfort that they won't implode the way the HQ Trivia founders did -- but I'd still want to hear how they argue before writing a check.

This is a technically strong team building an incrementally better product in a consensus market. For a different investor, the tight technical founder-market fit and the architectural gap between Fern and incumbents might be enough. For me, I need the founder's biography and the company's mission to be inseparable, I need the idea to make most people uncomfortable, and I need to see who gains access to something they didn't have before. Fern doesn't hit any of those marks. I'm passing.
