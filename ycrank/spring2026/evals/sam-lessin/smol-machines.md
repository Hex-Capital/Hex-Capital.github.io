# smol machines -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

BinBin He is a solo aerospace-engineer turned hypervisor hacker shipping a multi-platform microVM runtime alone, and smol machines is the kind of company where the highest-rated dimension on my card (founder missionary signal, demonstrated by the v0.8.0 release cadence and 3.4k GitHub stars in six weeks) is in direct tension with the lowest (it sits squarely in the AI-adjacent OSS infrastructure sleeve I have spent two years explicitly re-weighting away from). That pairing -- missionary builder, commodity sleeve -- is the analytical signature, and it's the signature that decides this for me before I even look at the round.

Drop.io is the only honest analog I have on the conviction axis: a solo-ish operator bootstrapping a primitive against free incumbents, shipping code first and arguing about it later. The mechanism that made Drop.io load-bearing for me was that the founder kept tacking through "this should already exist for free" objections by simply shipping the next version, and BinBin He is visibly doing the same thing -- libkrun + Hypervisor.framework + KVM is a real systems-engineering surface area for one person to hold in their head, and the 499-point Show HN with 152 comments is the kind of unprompted developer reaction you cannot fake. Where smol machines diverges from the Drop.io analog: BinBin's LinkedIn says "Stealth Startup," there is no public long-form writing on why the `.smolmachine` format wins, and the founder's own HN admission that "I haven't used Windows in a decade" is the kind of un-rehearsed line I like temperamentally but which tells me he is engineering-first without a commercial counterweight.

The canonical pass example on my desk is the YC AI-agent seed at $30M post with four tier-1s already in, and the failure mechanism there is that the category itself is the consensus -- you cannot price-discount your way out of being in the same sleeve as Daytona's $24M Series A (FirstMark, Feb 2026) and E2B's $21M Series A (Insight). smol machines is the systems-layer version of that exact trade: hosted sandbox competitors are already capitalized at a combined ~$45M, and smol machines is the local-first Apache-2.0 alternative without a pricing page. The pattern that worries me is not that smol machines is bad -- it's that "developer infrastructure for AI agent isolation" is now the consensus problem statement, and my whole edge is buying before the consensus problem statement exists.

On capital structure (mechanism analysis over 18 months): Apache-2.0 OSS with no disclosed monetization compounds the wrong direction for a solo founder against well-funded hosted incumbents. The Docker / HashiCorp / MongoDB OSS-to-commercial path that smol machines is implicitly walking takes 5-8 years and a commercial co-founder; in 18 months smol machines either ships a hosted tier (and starts looking like E2B at a worse cap table) or doesn't, and the cumulative-cash burn to get to default-alive on enterprise support contracts is exactly the "needs five more rounds" failure mode the Slow letter inverts.

On founder-as-brand (counterfactual): for smol machines to flip a tier on this axis, BinBin He would need to convert the 3.4k-star repo into a named-creator distribution surface -- a weekly screencast on hypervisor internals, a Substack on the `.smolmachine` format spec, a recognizable Twitter footprint beyond @binsquares with unretrievable follower count. The project has community; the founder does not. That gap is the difference between "Apache-2.0 project absorbed by an incumbent" and "BinBin He is the named author of the portable-VM standard," and the dossier shows zero motion toward the latter.

On round pricing (single-anchor parallel to Sublime Security): I bought Sublime before the enterprise-email-security consolidation wave because the pricing reflected a category most security investors had abandoned to incumbents. smol machines is the inverse -- the category is being actively recapitalized in real time (Daytona's $24M closed in February 2026, three months before smol machines's YC batch starts) -- which means whatever YC-batch pricing smol machines is being shown is reflecting a sleeve that's already heated up around it, and I get no asset-class mispricing.

36-month forecast: smol machines gets acqui-hired by Docker, HashiCorp, or a hyperscaler (most likely AWS, given Firecracker adjacency) for under $25M between mid-2027 and 2028, with BinBin He joining as a principal engineer; the OSS project persists, no Series A happens, no hosted commercial tier ships at scale. The mechanism is the missionary/commodity-sleeve signature -- a builder this strong doesn't fail to ship, but the artifact ships into a sleeve where the only liquidity event for solo-founder OSS infra is the incumbent absorb.

The strongest case against me is the Sublime Security override applied differently: if smol machines is actually the Mitchell-Hashimoto-circa-2012 moment for portable VMs, then BinBin's `.smolmachine` format becomes the de facto artifact standard the way Vagrant boxes did, and the 3.4k stars in six weeks is the leading indicator I'm dismissing because I'm pattern-matching on the AI-sleeve framing rather than the systems-primitive framing -- HashiCorp eventually IPO'd at billions on exactly this arc. That objection stings, and I want to honor it: the dossier evidence that breaks it is that Mitchell had Armon Dadgar as a commercial co-founder by year two and Vagrant had a multi-year head start before competing hosted offerings emerged, while smol machines is solo with E2B and Daytona already monetizing the workload. The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict: BinBin He recruits a commercial co-founder and signs one named enterprise design partner at >$50K ACV within the next two YC batches -- absent that, the acqui-hire forecast holds.
