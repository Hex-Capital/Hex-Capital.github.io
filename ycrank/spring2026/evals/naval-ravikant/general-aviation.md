# General Aviation -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

General Aviation is a founder-strong, leverage-wrong company: Ben Frank's MIT aero '14 plus pilot-plus-mechanic plus Rotabull-plus-Seer-plus-Rotor stack is genuinely rare specific knowledge, and he is pointing it at the single most permission-gated buyer in transportation -- the FAA -- to displace a $2.4B Verizon FENS contract and a Peraton prime-integrator complex. The dimensional signature is high-specific-knowledge / low-permissionless-leverage, which in my ledger is the Anchorage Digital silhouette without the MetaStable (2014) category-timing conviction underneath it.

Permissionless leverage is where General Aviation breaks against me hardest. The HIGH rung on this ladder is **Twitter** (2007) -- media-as-protocol, zero marginal cost, anyone posts, no gatekeeper -- and the LOW rung is **Anchorage Digital**, where I had to override criterion #1 because regulated crypto custody requires an OCC Treasury charter and was the structural opposite of permissionless distribution. General Aviation sits BELOW Anchorage on this ladder, because Anchorage's permission-as-moat was at least underwritten by a decade of pre-consensus crypto positioning since **MetaStable** (2014), whereas General Aviation's path to revenue runs through FAA TSO approval, RTCA DO-178/DO-254 certification, and sovereign ANSP procurement cycles that the dossier itself notes took NextGen "20+ years and remains incomplete." That is App Store curation with a federal seal -- capital-and-labor leverage that requires permission from the most permissioned buyer in American infrastructure. Starlink already runs 17 FAA trial sites and Verizon already holds $2.4B of FENS; if General Aviation wins, it wins by lobbying and certification headcount, not by code compounding without gatekeepers.

Specific knowledge is where General Aviation actually lights up my framework. The HIGH rung is **Stack Overflow** (2008), where Atwood and Spolsky were practicing developers writing for developers; the LOW rung is the ex-FAANG-PM-identified-a-TAM pattern I keep flagging in pitch decks. Ben Frank sits between Stack Overflow and **Notion** (2013) on this ladder -- closer to Notion -- because he is a licensed pilot AND aircraft mechanic AND MIT aerospace engineer AND has shipped three aviation companies including Rotabull and Seer Aerospace (which exited to Aviation Week) and was Chief Commercial Officer at Rotor Technologies where he spoke with ~200 helicopter operators (MIT News, Feb 9, 2024). This is exactly the "lived problem ownership" my anchors describe, and it is the one dimension where General Aviation would not embarrass itself sitting next to Ivan Zhao's adolescent productivity-tool obsession. The specific-knowledge gate, on its own, clears.

Market-and-pivot-room places General Aviation between **Uber** (2010) at the MID rung and the niche-vertical-SaaS LOW rung I refuse to fund. The $10.44B-to-$21.39B ATC market with 8.3% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights) is real money, but the pivot vectors inside it -- ANSP surveillance, ANSP comms, operator subscription, drone UTM -- all route through the same regulator. Compare Uber's pivot from black-car-only to UberX, which happened in commercial markets without an FAA-equivalent gating each iteration. General Aviation has TAM but not pivot-room; the "first idea never works" axiom from Venture Hacks 2007-10 will collide with a 20-year certification clock.

Network-and-compounding-value places General Aviation below **Perplexity** (~2022), which I waived as a Known Exception precisely because criteria #1 and #3 cleared at top quartile -- General Aviation does neither at top quartile, so the Perplexity override is unavailable. The aircraft-equipage-meets-ANSP-adoption two-sided dynamic is real in theory, but the dossier shows zero traction, zero signed LOI, and Hiring: False at team size 0 -- closer to single-sided B2G infrastructure than to the **Uber** marketplace compounding I anchor on. Founder trio: Frank reads clean -- Seer exited to Aviation Week, no partner litigation, no honesty-volunteering of the kind I flag in Knowledge Project #18 -- so the 10-year-partner test passes, with a caveat that solo-founder-at-team-size-0 in a hard-tech avionics company means he is the entire hiring funnel for the DO-178/DO-254 talent the dossier specifically calls out as missing.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation either gets acqui-hired by Iridium, Peraton, or a defense prime for $15-40M as a regulatory-relations-plus-IP tuck-in, or it pivots away from displacing ATC into a software/analytics layer for Part 135 operators reaching $200K-$1M ARR -- with dead as the third tail given the certification clock and solo-founder recruiting risk. The mechanism producing this forecast is the high/low signature itself: Ben Frank's specific knowledge is liquid in an acquisition market that values aviation-domain operators, but his leverage architecture cannot compound on its own without sovereign procurement that does not arrive on a seed runway.

The bull case I have to defeat is the Anchorage exception: pre-consensus category timing (the May 2025 DOT announcement, the One Big Beautiful Bill funding, Starlink trials) could let permission-as-moat flip from a bug to a structural advantage, and Ben Frank's specific knowledge is at a level that justifies waiving criterion #1 the way I waived it for Hoan Ton-That on integrity. It stings because the asymmetric math at angel size genuinely tolerates this -- one X downside, and if FAA awards a real contract the upside is real. The reason it does not win: Anchorage had me as advisor and was downstream of MetaStable (2014), meaning I had been dollar-cost-averaging into crypto for seven years before the check; I have no equivalent aviation-infrastructure sleeve, and "I have not been quietly accumulating in this category for three years" is the exact condition the Known Exception requires. The verifiable condition that flips me to Invest: a signed FAA OTA or NAV CANADA pilot contract within 12 months of the YC batch, OR demonstrated Part 135 operator revenue at $25K+ MRR -- either of which would prove General Aviation can ship inside its seed runway rather than wait on a 20-year certification curve. Until then, pass.
