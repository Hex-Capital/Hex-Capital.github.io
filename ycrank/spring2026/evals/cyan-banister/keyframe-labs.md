# Keyframe Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Parth Radia spent five years at Ericsson building photorealistic digital humans — he led a 30-person team of scientists and engineers, graduated the company's first intrapreneurship product, and shipped commercial voice and video synthesis before leaving to start Keyframe Labs. That's an unusual depth of domain expertise at pre-seed; most founders pitching me generative video have a few months of fine-tuning a diffusion model and a demo reel. Parth built a human scanning rig in Los Angeles and brought Ericsson's first generative AI product to market (Ericsson blog, July 2023). His co-founder Kaahan Radia — likely a sibling given the shared surname — brings five years of perception engineering at Zipline and publications at CVPR and JAMA. The technical bench is real. But when I apply my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is a professional journey, not a biographical forge. The founding narrative reads as "I built this inside a large company and realized I could do it better on my own." That's expertise. Expertise is valuable. But it's not the same as a life that made you incapable of ignoring this problem. I don't see evidence that Keyframe Labs' mission is biographically inevitable for either founder — and for me, that absence matters.

Tavus has raised $64.2 million with Sequoia and Scale backing, has a three-plus year head start, and already ships a Conversational Video Interface with sub-one-second latency (BusinessWire, November 2025). Synthesia sits at a $4 billion valuation with $146 million ARR. HeyGen cleared $100 million ARR by October 2025. Keyframe Labs is entering one of the most actively funded, consensus-attractive spaces in AI right now. When I apply the test — would this idea make a room of sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs? — the answer is no. VCs are writing checks in AI video avatars as fast as they can find companies. Nobody is uncomfortable here. Nobody has been told "no" 300 times. The space is hot, the category is legible, and the competitors are well-capitalized. That's the opposite of contrarian conviction. Keyframe Labs' $0.06-per-minute pricing and emphasis on no-code deployment speed are competitive differentiators, not structural disruptions — they're playing the same game at a different price point.

Keyframe Labs is building a real-time generative video API that wraps around existing LLM and voice-agent stacks, with a model called "persona-1.5-live" delivering roughly 500 milliseconds of latency. The product serves enterprise training, recruiting, telehealth, and consumer AI — nine testimonial categories on the website, though no named customers or quantified outcomes are disclosed. When I look beneath the surface for the invisible system — the opaque, extractive structure being made transparent — I don't find one. Keyframe Labs is making AI agents more engaging by giving them photoreal faces. That's a product improvement. It's not exposing a hidden tax, cracking open an information asymmetry, or shifting power from gatekeepers to users. The current avatar market may overcharge enterprises (Tavus doesn't publicly list per-minute pricing), but B2B pricing opacity in a well-served tech market is a far cry from the structural exploitation that drives my highest-conviction bets — the kind Affirm exposed in credit card lending or Uber exposed in the taxi medallion system.

The bull case for Keyframe Labs is genuinely worth articulating. Voice agents are exploding — Vapi, Bland, Retell have created a massive installed base of deployed agents that lack a visual component, and somebody is going to own the real-time video layer that sits on top. Parth Radia may be the single most qualified person in the world to build that layer, given his five years of exact-domain production experience at Ericsson. The $0.06-per-minute price point could function like a wedge into the developer long tail that Tavus's enterprise positioning misses. And the ten-year question has a compelling answer: if every AI agent eventually needs a face, real-time generative video becomes infrastructure — a "Twilio of video avatars." The LiveKit-based architecture (evidenced by the forked livekit/agents repo on GitHub) means Keyframe Labs can scale on proven WebRTC infrastructure while focusing R&D on model quality. I take this case seriously. But "the most qualified person in the room" building "a cheaper, faster version of what well-funded competitors already ship" is a venture bet on execution superiority with a two-person team against Tavus's $64.2 million war chest. My Substrate investment works as a structural parallel — James Proud was "forged in fire" and is challenging semiconductor monopolies during a CHIPS Act inflection. But Substrate is attacking a concentrated monopoly (ASML) at a regulatory tipping point. Keyframe Labs is entering a fragmented, well-funded market with no regulatory moat and no monopoly to crack. The parallel breaks down where it matters most.

The condition that would flip me: if I sat across from Parth Radia and heard why this problem is biographically inevitable for him — not the Ericsson career path, but something deeper about what visual human presence means to him personally — and if Keyframe Labs showed me early customer retention data demonstrating that their product is becoming embedded infrastructure (developers can't rip it out, usage compounds), I would re-evaluate from the ground up. The domain expertise is real and the timing window for the voice-agent visual layer is narrow. But today, Keyframe Labs reads as a competent team building a competitive product in a consensus market without the biographical inevitability, structural disruption, or contrarian positioning that makes me write a check from my own money. The two dimensions that locked this verdict: contrarian conviction, where active evidence of a well-funded consensus market pushed the score well below my default, and invisible system, where Keyframe Labs offers a better product but disrupts no hidden structure.
