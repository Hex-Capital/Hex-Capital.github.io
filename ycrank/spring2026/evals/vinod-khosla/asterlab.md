# Aster -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Emmett Bicker dropped out of college three weeks in, at eighteen, moved to San Francisco, and built a system that achieved state-of-the-art on 4 of 5 research benchmarks while solo-authoring the paper that reported the results (arXiv 2602.07040, Feb 2026). He has been doing ML since fifteen. He published in Nature Communications on neuroscience. He set the NanoGPT speedrun world record at 95.2 seconds. This is the Mach Industries founder archetype — a teenager who doesn't know what can't be done, building something audacious against incumbents with a thousand times more resources. When I backed Mach Industries, people told me a 19-year-old MIT dropout couldn't compete with Lockheed Martin. The informed observers were wrong because they were measuring experience when they should have been measuring learning rate. Bicker's trajectory from high school robotics captain to Magic post-training researcher to Nature Communications co-author to solo-founding Aster — all before turning twenty — is one of the steepest learning curves I've seen at YC Demo Day. The rate of capability accumulation across domains (neuroscience, robotics, ML training, research automation) is the signal I weight most heavily when the founder hasn't been operating long enough to have a track record of pivots.

Aster's consequence magnitude deserves serious attention. The company isn't automating a single profession's workflow — it's automating the process that produces AI itself. If Aster fully succeeds, the ideation-experimentation loop in ML research collapses from months to hours. Aster's system already produced novel artifacts — the SecantPolar optimizer and PulseDelta language modeling architecture (asterlab.ai) — that didn't exist before the system ran. That's not a copilot. That's a machine generating genuine research output. The recursive implication is what makes this consequential: AI that accelerates AI development accelerates everything downstream — healthcare AI, energy optimization, drug discovery, robotics. It's meta-level leverage on the entire technology stack. I wouldn't put this at OpenAI's consequence magnitude — OpenAI was building the general capability layer itself, while Aster is building an accelerant for that layer — but it's substantially above "better productivity tool for ML engineers."

Sakana AI has $479 million and an open-source AI Scientist. Autoscience raised $14 million in March 2026 with literally the same tagline — "the world's first automated AI research lab" (BusinessWire). Google's AI co-scientist runs on Gemini infrastructure with functionally unlimited compute. FutureHouse has $250 million in philanthropic funding plus a $70 million spinout. Aster is a solo founder with YC's standard check. The competitive landscape is the hardest part of this evaluation, and I won't pretend it isn't a problem. The core technique — using LLMs to iteratively generate and test code — is published, understood, and in Sakana's case, open-source on GitHub. Aster's 20x speed claim (arXiv 2602.07040) is the primary technical differentiator, and it comes from a self-reported, single-author preprint without independent replication. The dossier is explicit: "the underlying approach is not proprietary at the conceptual level." This is where Aster diverges from the Mach Industries parallel. Mach builds physical weapons systems where manufacturing know-how and government contracting relationships compound into durable barriers. Aster builds software where a well-funded team at Sakana or Google can replicate the approach in quarters, not years.

The bull case an advocate would make: Bicker has already demonstrated the ability to out-research teams with orders of magnitude more resources — matching the best human solution on ZAPBench with 1/190th of the compute. The competitive landscape validates the category, and category validation at this stage is a feature, not a threat. Google's AI co-scientist is internal infrastructure, not an external product — and the dossier notes the structural barrier: "productizing autonomous research as an external tool could cannibalize their internal research advantage." Sakana focuses on paper generation, not program optimization. Autoscience targets Fortune 500 enterprise managed services while Aster offers self-serve access to individual researchers — different GTM, different customer. The speed advantage, if it holds, is the kind of technical edge that compounds: faster iteration means more experiment data means better experiment design. And the affordability angle is powerful — today, frontier ML research requires teams of PhD-level researchers earning $300K-$1M each. Aster could make that capability available to anyone with an API key, which is precisely the "making the expensive free" pattern that animates my highest-conviction bets from Impossible Foods to Curai Health.

I find that bull case partially persuasive but not fully convincing. The Known Exception that would apply here is DoorDash — when consequence magnitude is high enough and the founder is a genuine learning machine, I override the technical novelty requirement. Aster's consequence magnitude is high, and Bicker's learning velocity is exceptional. But the DoorDash exception worked because food delivery logistics, while not technically novel, had massive execution barriers — building a three-sided marketplace across hundreds of cities. Aster's execution barrier is less clear. A software platform for automated ML experiments doesn't have the same network effects or geographic density advantages. The commoditization risk documented in the dossier — Sakana's open-source release, Google's internal capabilities, Autoscience's near-identical positioning — is real, not theoretical. What keeps me in the Invest range rather than pushing me to Pass is Bicker himself. The kid matched the best human solution on ZAPBench at 1/190th compute. He set a world record on the NanoGPT speedrun. He published in Nature Communications while still in high school. The learning rate is extraordinary, and at this stage, I am betting on the athlete more than the current product configuration. The condition that would flip me to Pass: if independent replication of the 20x speed claim fails, or if Autoscience or Sakana demonstrate equivalent benchmark performance within six months, the competitive moat dissolves and I'm backing a solo founder in a commodity market.

Aster scores highest on the founder dimension and the affordability dimension — Bicker's trajectory is genuinely remarkable, and the vision of democratizing AI research capability maps directly to my "making the expensive free" instinct. Aster scores lowest on non-linear technical approach — the speed advantage is real but unverified independently, and the conceptual approach is well-understood and reproducible. The investment case rests on the founder's learning rate being durable enough to stay ahead of better-capitalized competitors in a rapidly commoditizing category. At the pre-seed stage, that's often exactly the right bet — I'd rather have the best athlete with a thin moat than a thick moat with a mediocre athlete. I'll take this option at the YC price.
