# Auxos -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Auxos is a pre-seed AI-market-research wrapper led by Ashton Daniel, an ex-BCG consultant who watched Fortune 500 clients pay for expensive panels and decided that was the problem -- which means the highest dimension here is a thin slice of founder-adjacent technical depth (Kerry Lu's Amazon Ads simulation infra modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic) and the lowest is "magically weird," because Auxos enters a category where Aaru already carries a $1B Series A headline (TechCrunch, Dec 2025) and Evidenza is bootstrapped to 100+ enterprise customers including Salesforce and GE. That high/low pairing tells me exactly what Auxos is: a credentialed, athletically-disciplined three-person team running directly at a consensus AI-research category that two well-funded incumbents already own.

On lived experience, my ladder runs Anduril-Luckey (shipped Oculus before he ever pitched defense) at the top, DeepMind-Hassabis in the middle as the polymath override, and the consensus YC AI-agent darling at the bottom where two ex-FAANG PMs "discovered the problem in a memo." Auxos sits closer to the bottom rung than the middle. Ashton Daniel did not run the surveys -- he watched BCG clients buy them; that is witnessing, not living. Kerry Lu's ad-simulation infrastructure at Amazon is the one piece of biography that touches the actual problem surface, and Jerry Wu's Meta Reels ranking work is adjacent ML plumbing, not customer-research pain. None of the three founders has ever been the marketer who got fired for shipping the wrong message, which is the autobiographical hook I needed to feel.

On magically weird, the ladder is Crusoe (burning North Dakota gas flares for Bitcoin, ~2018) at the top, HQ Trivia in the middle (weird format, but became consensus fast), and the YC AI-agent darling at the bottom. Auxos lands squarely on the bottom rung. "Simulated audiences for customer research" is the precise sentence HBR validated in November 2025 (hbr.org) and that Aaru, Synthetic Users, Evidenza, Viewpoints.ai, Brox.ai, and Synthelos are all already chasing -- six named competitors before Auxos has a customer logo on the site. The thesis is to find them *before* it becomes consensus, and Auxos is showing up after the $1B headline. There is no aesthetic, regulatory, or capital-intensity reason a credible VC would have to pass on aesthetic grounds, which means there is no priced-in misread for me to exploit.

On single unambiguous CEO, the ladder is Anduril-Luckey at the top, Niantic-Hanke in the middle, and HQ Trivia / GameCrush at the bottom where unresolved authority detonated the company. Auxos has Daniel listed as CEO, which is something, but it is a three-way split among Columbia, Duke, and UPenn fencers who met on the Team USA circuit -- no disclosed operating agreement, no documented tiebreaker, no investor signal that the founder-vesting conversation has happened. Three friends who fence together is a beautiful origin story until pricing diverges from positioning and somebody has to be wrong; I have seen this movie.

On capital-intensive frontier swing, the ladder is SpaceX (2008) at the top, Vigil Labs (August 2025) in the middle as regulated finance, and the AI-SaaS wrapper at the bottom. Auxos is the bottom rung -- a pure LLM-orchestration product where PyMC Labs has already published the recipe and Weavely lists a free ChatGPT version. On physical verifiability, the ladder is Flock Safety (the camera I could photograph in a parking lot) at the top, Postmates (the deli sandwich that arrived at my door) in the middle, and B2B developer-tools abstractions at the bottom. Auxos is a B2B web dashboard that generates synthetic survey results -- I cannot install it, ride it, scan it, or hand it to a friend over dinner. The product is founder testimony plus a chart.

My 36-month forecast: Auxos plateaus under $2M ARR and is acqui-hired into a martech incumbent -- Qualtrics, SurveyMonkey/Momentive, or a Salesforce-adjacent research suite -- with the three founders landing engineering or research-lead seats, while Aaru and Evidenza take the enterprise and mid-market layers respectively. The mechanism is straightforward: in a consensus category with a $1B-valued leader and a profitable bootstrapped incumbent already at 100+ logos, a pre-seed team without a lived-problem wedge cannot win the wedge fight; they can, however, ship clean infrastructure (Lu's simulation chops are real) that a larger martech buyer would rather acquire than rebuild.

The bull case stings in exactly one place, and I want to name it honestly: Kerry Lu built ad-simulation infrastructure modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic at Amazon and Jerry Wu shipped ranking systems generating $100M+ at Meta -- that is not generalist polymath bench, that is two engineers who have actually run population-scale simulation in production, which is the *exact* technical primitive Auxos needs. The DeepMind exception in my book was credentialed founders whose cross-domain compounding overrode the bias against pedigree, and you could argue Lu-plus-Wu is a smaller version of that. I am dismantling it on this: DeepMind had Hassabis as a chess prodigy AND neuroscientist AND game designer compounding into one founder; Auxos has three credentialed engineers in a consensus category without a single one of them having lived the marketer's pain. The exception applies when polymath compounding *creates* the category; here the category already exists and the founders are running at it from the supply side. I would flip my verdict if Auxos shipped a named Fortune 500 LOI in the next two quarters where the buyer cites a specific decision Auxos correctly predicted that traditional research got wrong -- a tactile outcome I could verify, not a logo.
