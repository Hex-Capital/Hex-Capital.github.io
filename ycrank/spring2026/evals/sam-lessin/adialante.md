# Adialante -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Efraín Torres published a paper in *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine* in 2022 that eliminates gradient coils from MRI — a component so fundamental to the technology that removing it is roughly analogous to building an airplane without wings and making it fly anyway. That's the FREE technique. Patent-pending, peer-reviewed, originating from a PhD under Michael Garwood at the University of Minnesota, one of the top MRI research programs on the planet. Adialante isn't building a better MRI — they're building a different machine based on different physics. When I backed Solana, the bet wasn't "faster Ethereum." It was a fundamentally different architectural approach to consensus. Adialante's FREE technique occupies the same structural position: a new primitive at the physics layer, not an application-layer improvement on the existing stack. That distinction matters because primitives compound and applications get commoditized.

Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model — a truck shows up at a urology clinic, patients get scanned, the clinic pays per scan with zero capital expenditure — is where the Metropolis parallel clicks hardest. Metropolis was AI-powered parking seeded early: physical infrastructure that software amplifies but no algorithm can replace. Now it's a $5B company. Adialante has the same structural DNA. You cannot replicate a custom gradient-free MRI scanner inside a mobile clinic with a software update. Siemens, GE, and Philips sell multi-million-dollar machines to hospitals — their entire business model, sales force, and channel economics are built around capital equipment sales. Pivoting to a per-scan mobile fleet would cannibalize their installed base. That's not a competitive response they'll make willingly. And AI actually helps Adialante more than it hurts them — better reconstruction algorithms make their novel encoding scheme more clinically viable, while AI does nothing to solve the physical access problem that 90% of the world's population faces.

Torres and Jenkins met on the first day of undergrad at Marquette, went to graduate school together at UMN, and built Adialante from Torres's PhD research. That co-founder relationship — forged over a decade of shared academic and personal experience — is the kind I trust. It's not two strangers matched by an accelerator. Torres has Boston Scientific and Imaging Biometrics on his resume before the PhD, so he's touched real medical device operations, not just published papers. The team roster tells me something: four PhDs in MR physics, computational physics, and systems engineering, plus dedicated software engineers. Adialante has assembled the kind of bench strength that takes years and specific domain networks to recruit. The idea — reinvent MRI physics from scratch and drive trucks to clinics — is the kind of thing that makes most investors reflexively say "too hard, too slow, too expensive." I like that reaction in others. It means the price is wrong.

Here's what concerns me, and I'll be direct about it: Adialante has raised roughly $3M total including a $1.18M NSF SBIR Phase II grant. Hyperfine took over $200M and 5+ years to get FDA clearance for a much simpler portable brain-only MRI. Adialante's FREE technique has no predicate device — they're likely looking at a De Novo pathway, which is slower and less predictable than a 510(k). Manufacturing MRI hardware, fitting out mobile clinics, hiring clinical staff, running validation studies — this is a business that will burn tens of millions before the first dollar of clinical revenue. Their $3.5M seed target from 2024 is a bridge, not a foundation. Adialante fundamentally cannot survive as the last money they ever raise. That violates one of my core heuristics. The Allbirds exception taught me that physical product innovation alone doesn't overcome capital-intensive scaling risks — Allbirds peaked at $4B then collapsed 97% to delisting. The difference: Adialante has a patent-pending physics moat, an FDA regulatory barrier that protects once crossed, and an operational complexity that compounds with scale. Allbirds had wool.

The bull case an advocate would make is compelling: Adialante operates in a category most VCs avoid (hardware medtech), has invented genuinely new physics published in a top peer-reviewed journal, and delivers through a physical-world model that AI cannot touch. Competitors have raised $44-100M each pursuing adjacent approaches — Promaxo at $72-81M for in-office prostate MRI, Openwater at $100M for optoelectronics — but none combine novel physics with mobile-as-a-service delivery. The unmet need is staggering: 90% of humanity lacks MRI access. If Adialante's FREE technique achieves diagnostic equivalence and FDA clearance, they've created a category — "MRI-as-a-service" — that gets referenced the way Venmo created "Venmo me." That's the primitive test in action. The regulatory gate (FDA clearance) is simultaneously Adialante's biggest risk and their deepest moat: once through, no competitor can follow for years. The condition that would flip me from invest to pass is specific and verifiable: if the FREE technique fails to demonstrate diagnostic equivalence to conventional MRI in a controlled clinical comparison for prostate imaging, the entire thesis collapses. No clinical validation, no deal.

Adialante scores highest on structural durability — patent-pending physics, physical fleet operations, regulatory barriers, and incumbent channel conflict create a moat stack that no LLM wrapper will ever replicate. The consensus mispricing is real but moderate — hardware medtech is unfashionable, not taboo, and the adjacent category has attracted significant capital ($72-100M to competitors). The capital discipline score is the weakest dimension and the honest acknowledgment that this business structurally requires the kind of multi-round financing I philosophically oppose. But I've made this exception before for physical-world moats that compound — Metropolis required patience and capital before the $5B valuation materialized. Adialante's FREE technique is either a genuine new imaging primitive or it isn't, and the peer-reviewed physics says it is.
