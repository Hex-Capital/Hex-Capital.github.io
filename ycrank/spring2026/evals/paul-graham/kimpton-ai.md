# Kimpton AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Level III Capital ran for four years with $10M in assets under management, and during that time Jack Zumwalt, Mauricio Ortiz, and Adrian Del Bosque built the internal tooling they needed to do their jobs as portfolio managers. Then they productized it. That origin story is almost a textbook illustration of "live in the future, then build what's missing" — they didn't study the buy-side market from the outside, they were the buy-side market. When I backed Drew Houston, the signal was that he built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Kimpton AI has a version of that same signal: the founders spent four years drowning in fragmented workflows, stitching together data vendors and manual analysis, and eventually their frustration crystallized into a product. The $1–3M annual cost they cite for building comparable tooling in-house isn't a number they pulled from a market report — it's a number they lived. Of the five dimensions I evaluate, Kimpton AI scores highest here, and it's not close.

Zumwalt came out of Goldman Sachs, studied CS at Oxford, and co-founded Level III Capital at roughly age 22. Ortiz engineered critical infrastructure at Goldman's Marcus division before co-founding the fund. Del Bosque brings SpaceX and Vistra Corp before joining as principal engineer. The co-founder relationship has real depth — Zumwalt and Ortiz met at a Goldman intern event approximately seven years before founding Level III, and all three have worked together since 2021. That four-year operating history is better evidence of co-founder durability than most pre-seed companies can offer. What I don't see in Kimpton AI's dossier is the "animal" quality — the specific stories of creative workarounds that reveal determination under pressure. Running a small hedge fund and building internal tools is competent and purposeful, but it follows a rational career arc. There's no cereal box moment. No "we built the first version in a weekend and had users by Monday." The absence of those stories is mildly negative, because truly relentlessly resourceful founders generate notable anecdotes whether they're trying to or not.

Kimpton AI's deepest problem from my framework is that "AI for portfolio managers" repels nobody. AlphaSense has $500M+ in ARR and $1.63B raised. Hebbia raised $161M at a $700M valuation. Microsoft acquired Fintool in January 2026 to embed agentic financial AI directly into Office. Bloomberg and FactSet have both the data and the distribution to add agentic layers whenever they choose. Precedence Research projects the global AI-in-asset-management market at $38.94B by 2034. Every institutional investor, every data vendor, every platform company agrees this is a good market. And that agreement is precisely the signal I distrust most. My entire Black Swan farming framework rests on the premise that the best investments initially look wrong to smart people. Kimpton AI's pitch could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece, and no one at any LP meeting would flinch. The returns in consensus-good markets get competed away by well-capitalized incumbents doing the obvious thing.

The bull case is real, and I should state it honestly before I dismantle it. Kimpton AI has arguably the strongest founder-market fit in this entire category — four years of firsthand PM workflow experience versus AlphaSense's broad enterprise search approach or Hebbia's document analysis orientation. Kimpton AI produces structured Trade Proposals with targets, stops, and conviction levels stored against personalized `mandate.md` and `strategy.md` files — that's a workflow depth that broad-market competitors haven't matched. The testimonials from PMs at a $40B+ sustainable equity fund suggest real enterprise engagement. Microsoft's Fintool acquisition validates the category at the same time it removes a direct competitor. And the co-founders' Goldman engineering backgrounds combined with hedge fund operating experience is a rare combination — most fintech AI founders have one or the other, not both. The SOC 2 Type II certification in review signals enterprise seriousness that most pre-seed companies lack. If Kimpton AI can lock in early buy-side customers through workflow depth before the larger platforms catch up, the mandate/strategy personalization creates real switching costs.

But I keep returning to the competitive encirclement. When I invested in Stripe, payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled everyone — the Collisons were practically the only developers crazy enough to wade into PCI compliance and bank integrations in 2010. Kimpton AI's domain is schlep-heavy too — FactSet licensing, SEC regulatory exposure on trade proposals, SOC 2 compliance — but the schlep hasn't repelled other well-funded teams. It's attracted $1.8B+ in venture funding to direct competitors. The Stripe analogy breaks down at exactly the point it matters most: Stripe had no well-capitalized competitors executing on the same vision. Kimpton AI has several, plus incumbents with the data assets already in hand. The FactSet data dependency compounds this — Kimpton AI's core product functionality relies on a single vendor that could restrict access, raise costs, or build competing features. Product iteration from October 2025 through March 2026 — portfolio integration, chart workspace, context references — shows steady development cadence but not the explosive growth signal that would indicate Kimpton AI is pulling away from alternatives.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that a meaningful number of PMs have switched from Bloomberg Terminal or AlphaSense to Kimpton AI as their primary research workflow — not as a supplement, but as a replacement. That would demonstrate the product depth the category competitors lack and the kind of obsessive early-user love that signals "a small number of people want a large amount." Right now Kimpton AI has testimonials but no disclosed user counts or revenue, and 571 Twitter followers after six months suggests the product hasn't yet generated the organic pull that distinguishes a breakout from a competent entry. The decisive dimensions for my evaluation are Organic Problem Discovery, where Kimpton AI genuinely excels — these founders built what they needed, from lived experience, in the way I most trust — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where Kimpton AI fails my framework clearly. The first dimension earns them a strong score; the second costs them the investment.
