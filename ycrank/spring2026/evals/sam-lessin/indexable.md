# Indexable -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Indexable is the competitive landscape, and it tells me everything I need to know about the idea's consensus level. E2B has $43.8 million in funding and 88% of the Fortune 100 as customers. Daytona just raised $24 million and already offers fork-and-snapshot. Modal is in talks at a $2.5 billion valuation. Blaxel came through YC a year ago. Google open-sourced its own Agent Sandbox at KubeCon. "Sandbox infrastructure for AI agents" is not an insane idea -- it's one of the most consensus categories in all of early-stage investing right now. When I see a market this hot with this many well-funded players, I know the non-consensus premium has been completely arbitraged away. You're buying narrative at market price.

Andrew Gazelka is clearly a sane person. The Hyperion project -- building a Minecraft engine that handles 170,000 concurrent players in a single world, processing 1,000 players in 455 microseconds on 12 cores -- is exactly the kind of insane systems engineering work that maps directly onto building a custom VMM with sub-30ms forking. He was at xAI, he writes Rust, he's hacked on the Linux kernel. The founder-domain fit is tight. If you told me "this person is building a custom virtual machine monitor," I'd say yes, this is exactly who should be building it. But a sane person with a sane idea is my least investable combination. The returns get priced in because every other VC can see what I can see. Brian Armstrong was a sane person with what most people thought was an insane idea -- building a regulated crypto exchange when Bitcoin was considered a joke. Gazelka is a sane person building exactly what everyone already agrees needs to exist.

Here's my deeper structural problem with Indexable: it fails the "cherry on top" test in the most direct way possible. If AI agents don't proliferate, this company has zero market. Not a reduced market -- zero. The entire business is a derivative bet on agentic AI adoption. I want to invest in businesses that would be good even if AI didn't exist but become great because AI non-obviously accelerates them. Craftwork paints houses with or without AI; the scheduling software is a cherry on top of a real operational business. Indexable isn't a business with an AI cherry -- it's the cherry without the sundae. The custom VMM is genuinely difficult systems engineering that an LLM can't replicate, which gives it real technical moat relative to competitors. But the moat protects a castle built on someone else's land. If the agentic AI market shifts architecturally -- if agents run inside LLM providers' infrastructure, or if the compute model changes -- the castle disappears regardless of how well-built the walls are.

The bull case deserves real engagement because the technical work is genuinely impressive. Version-control semantics for compute environments -- fork, branch, diff, commit -- could be a new primitive. If agentic workflows become the dominant computational paradigm, the ability to branch execution mid-stream and diff against prior states could become as foundational as git is to code. Gazelka's 26ms forking is an order of magnitude faster than Fly.io Sprites and meaningfully faster than Daytona's container-based approach. E2B would have to rebuild their entire Firecracker-based architecture to match it -- that's a genuine structural advantage, not just a feature delta. If I believed this was creating a new computational primitive rather than entering a hot existing category, the calculus changes entirely. That's the Airtable exception in my portfolio -- something that looks like a tool on the surface but functions as infrastructure that other workflows build on. The question is whether developers actually need version-control semantics for compute at the primitive level, or whether "faster forking" is a feature within an existing category. I don't have conviction it's the former.

What tips me toward pass is the market structure. E2B's incumbency at 500 million sandbox sessions and $12 million-plus ARR, combined with Daytona's direct overlap and $31 million war chest, means Indexable is fighting for position in an already-structured market, not defining a new one. Solana entered when the Layer 1 blockchain space was still open for new protocols to establish themselves. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments when the category didn't exist. Indexable is entering a market where the category leader has already established itself with 88% of the Fortune 100. Being architecturally superior doesn't guarantee market capture when the incumbents have distribution and the switching costs are real -- developers who've already integrated E2B's SDK aren't going to re-architect their agent orchestration for faster forking unless the performance difference is existential, not incremental. Twenty-six milliseconds versus two hundred milliseconds matters in some workloads, but most teams building agents today are optimizing for "works" before they optimize for "works in 26ms."

The solo technical founder without visible commercial experience in a market that requires enterprise sales adds another layer of concern. Infrastructure companies live or die on go-to-market execution against established competitors. The technical chops are obvious, but I see no signal of the "rizz" side -- the ability to sell, to build partnerships, to navigate enterprise procurement. YC helps, but it's not a substitute for a commercially-minded co-founder when you're going head-to-head against E2B's sales team.
