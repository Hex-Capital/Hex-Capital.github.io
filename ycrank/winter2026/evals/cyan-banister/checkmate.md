# Checkmate -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Checkmate: who is this for, and who does it help? The product automates expert network calls for hedge funds, PE firms, and MBB consulting shops -- institutions that already sit at the very top of the financial food chain. When I invested in Uber, I saw a company that would put income in the pockets of people like my driver Roger. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit replacing the hidden fees that hurt people who could least afford them. Checkmate does the opposite -- it takes the $500-$2,000 per call that experts earn for sharing their hard-won industry knowledge and routes that value toward making already-wealthy institutions more efficient. I'm not against enterprise software, but when I imagine this company at full scale, the people on the losing end are the human experts being replaced. That's a meaningful economic direction, and it's the wrong one for how I think about impact.

The founders have strong credentials -- Neil Garde did eight years as a PM at Google across AI and AR/VR, then built Monzo's US operation from zero. James Sha has published robotics research with Pieter Abbeel, built Onyx to 300K+ users and an acquisition, and made Forbes 30 Under 30. These are accomplished people. But when I ask my foundational question -- tell me your story, not the business, and help me understand why THIS problem -- the dossier gives me nothing. Neither founder appears to have worked at a hedge fund, a PE firm, or an expert network. There's no biographical thread connecting them to the pain of sitting through expert calls or navigating MNPI compliance. What I see is two talented Google engineers who identified a lucrative vertical for AI voice agents. That's pattern recognition of a market opportunity, not the bone-deep conviction that comes from living inside a problem. The founders I back at their best are people whose life story makes the company feel inevitable -- where the person and the problem are inseparable. Here, the person and the problem are connected by market analysis.

And then there's the weirdness question, or rather the absence of it. AI voice agents automating enterprise workflows is not unpopular or difficult to assess -- it's one of the most consensus AI applications of 2025-2026. AlphaSense, a $4B company with $500M in ARR, already launched its own AI Interviewer built on its Tegus acquisition. InsightAgent is doing the same thing with both autonomous and copilot modes. The a16z Speedrun acceptance confirms what I'd expect: this is the kind of company that institutional investors immediately understand and find reasonable. That's precisely the signal that makes me step back. The most promising companies are ones that other investors struggle to even categorize. Checkmate slots neatly into "AI for financial services" -- a box that every fund in town is trying to fill right now. The network-agnostic positioning versus AlphaSense's walled garden is a smart product decision, but it's a feature strategy, not the kind of foundational insight that creates a generational company.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: someone is going to win the autonomous expert interview market, the TAM is growing to $17B by 2035, and Checkmate's team has the exact right technical DNA -- NLP at Google, AI research at Berkeley, a prior acquisition. The compliance infrastructure they're building early (SOC II, MNPI guardrails) creates real switching costs once embedded in a fund's workflow. And the network-agnostic approach could become the standard integration layer sitting between every expert network and every institutional buyer. If AlphaSense stays locked in its proprietary garden and InsightAgent stumbles, Checkmate could become the default. I take that case seriously. The compliance complexity in financial services is genuine, and the founders are building for it. But even in the bull case, this is a race against well-funded incumbents in a market that everyone sees -- not the kind of invisible, dismissed opportunity where I've made my best investments.

I'll say plainly: Checkmate might be a perfectly good investment for someone whose framework rewards technical execution against a clear market opportunity. But my framework rewards something different -- outsider founders with biographical stakes, ideas that look ridiculous to consensus, and companies that expand economic access downward. On every dimension I care most about, this one is quiet.
