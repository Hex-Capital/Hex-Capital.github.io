# Zatanna -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact in this dossier is not about the product or the market — it is that search engine caches show this company was a "lawtech company focused on jury trials," then "Adaptive Healthcare Admin Agents," and is now "Request Based Scraping Infrastructure." Three completely unrelated products across three unrelated domains. This is not pivoting in the sense that PayPal pivoted from cryptography to payments along a coherent thread of financial innovation. This is the lean startup methodology in its purest and most damaging form — try something, discard it, try something else, repeat until you stumble into traction. It is the antithesis of definite optimism. When I ask "what is the founder's concrete plan for the future?", the honest answer is that they have had at least three incompatible plans in rapid succession. A founder who pivots from jury trials to healthcare agents to web scraping does not have a vision of how the world should work — they have a process for discovering what customers might pay for. That process is precisely what I mean when I say lean is code for unplanned.

What is the secret here? The implicit thesis is that protocol-level HTTP request replication is fundamentally superior to browser-based scraping — faster by an order of magnitude, 200x more memory-efficient, and harder to detect. But this is not a secret. It is a well-known trade-off that every sophisticated data broker and in-house scraping team already understands. The dossier itself states plainly that "protocol-level HTTP scraping is a known technique used by sophisticated in-house teams and some boutique scraping services." The reason Bright Data, Apify, and Firecrawl use headless browsers is not ignorance — it is a deliberate architectural choice that trades performance for generality. Zatanna is packaging a known technique as infrastructure, which is a product decision, not a contrarian insight about the world. No intelligent person would disagree with the claim that direct HTTP requests are faster than spinning up Chrome. The question is whether the trade-off — bespoke reverse-engineering per target site, linear maintenance burden, integration fragility when sites update — makes the approach commercially superior at scale. That is an empirical question, not a secret.

The competitive landscape confirms my concern. This is a crowded market with funded players at every tier: Firecrawl with $16.2M raised and YC backing, Bright Data at $300M ARR, Apify at $13.3M revenue, ScraperAPI acquired and generating ~$4.8M annually. When I see four or five companies all serving the same customers — developers and businesses that need web data at scale — I see mimetic competition in action. Everyone is imitating everyone else. The total addressable market is roughly $1 billion, which means even complete market domination would not produce power-law returns. A $1B market with five well-funded competitors is the structural opposite of monopoly. It is exactly the kind of Red Ocean where margins trend toward zero as companies match each other's features, pricing, and customer acquisition tactics.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that AI agents will expand the scraping TAM by 5-10x in the next few years, that protocol-level speed advantages will compound as request volumes scale exponentially, and that Zatanna can build a sufficiently large integration library before incumbents pivot or acquire. Alex Blackwell's anti-bot bypass experience at Pikkit is genuine domain expertise — it is the one piece of the puzzle that suggests authentic founder-market fit on the technical side. If the sub-500ms claim holds across a broad integration library, the cost-per-request difference at AI-agent scale could be significant. There is a version of this story where Zatanna becomes the performance layer underneath AI data pipelines, and the browser-based incumbents face a classic innovator's dilemma — cannibalizing their premium browser rendering to compete on protocol-level speed. But this scenario requires the TAM to grow dramatically, the team to stop pivoting and commit with conviction, and the integration library to scale faster than it breaks. None of these are structurally assured, and two depend on market forces outside the company's control.

The fundamental problem is architectural: each new target requires bespoke reverse-engineering, and each integration is fragile against site updates. This creates a maintenance burden that scales linearly with coverage — the opposite of the compounding advantages that define monopoly. Browser-based scrapers, for all their inefficiency, benefit from generality: they work on any site without custom integration. Zatanna's approach works brilliantly on a handful of high-value targets but becomes an engineering treadmill at scale. There is no network effect, no economy of scale in the integration library that compounds faster than it decays, and no structural moat that prevents a well-resourced competitor from replicating any individual integration within weeks.

I would pass. The company lacks a genuine secret, operates in a competitive market with insufficient TAM for power-law returns, and the pivot history signals indefinite rather than definite thinking. The CTO's domain expertise is the one bright spot, but expertise alone does not create monopoly.
