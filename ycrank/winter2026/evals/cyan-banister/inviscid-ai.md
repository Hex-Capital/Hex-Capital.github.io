# Inviscid AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Inviscid AI isn't the technology or the founders — it's the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about timing. PassiveLogic has raised $125 million for physics-based building digital twins. Trane Technologies paid over $103 million to acquire BrainBox AI in January 2025. EkkoSense is deployed at 300+ data center sites. 75F just raised a $45 million Series B with Carrier and Breakthrough Energy Ventures as investors. Cadence, an $80 billion public company, already offers CFD-based digital twins for data centers. This is not an unpopular category that's difficult to assess. This is a category where consensus has fully formed, the big checks have been written, and the strategic acquirers are already active. When I look for investments, I'm looking for the pocket of dreamers working on something everybody else thinks is ridiculous. AI-powered building optimization is the opposite of that — it's one of the most validated enterprise categories in climate tech right now. The weirdness has been completely arbitraged away.

When I sit down with a founder, I want to hear their story before anything else. How did you get here? What happened in your life that makes you the person who has to solve this specific problem? With Inviscid AI, I'm essentially reading a blank page. Kabir Jain describes himself as someone who "likes physics and AI." Ziming Qiu says "I make simulations :)." Those are interests, not callings. Ziming is a fifth-year PhD student at NYU Tandon with published work in computer vision and medical image analysis — camera pose estimation, not fluid dynamics. The leap from image analysis to physics-informed neural networks for building airflow is a real one, and I'd need to understand it. But more fundamentally, I don't see the biographical thread that connects either founder to buildings, to energy waste, to HVAC systems, to the lived experience of managing a facility where airflow kills people or burns money. The strongest founders I've backed — from Travis at Uber to the Crusoe team to the Contraline founder — had stories where the problem was inseparable from who they were. I can't find that thread here.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The data center cooling market is projected to reach $56 billion by 2030, growing at 16.4% annually, and AI compute density is creating thermal management problems that existing solutions genuinely struggle with. If physics-informed neural networks can truly deliver 1,000x speed improvement at 95%+ accuracy over traditional CFD solvers, that's a transformative capability. The Trane/BrainBox acquisition proves the exit path is real. And there's a genuine technical gap: traditional CFD vendors optimize for design-time simulation, not real-time operations, while data-driven platforms like BrainBox optimize against historical patterns without physics-based understanding. A team that nails the intersection — real-time, physics-informed, operationally deployed — could be exactly the acquisition target that Cadence or an HVAC OEM needs. For this to work, though, these founders would need to be the best PINN-to-production team in the world, and I see no evidence of that yet. The underlying methodology is published academic research, the case studies on the website span three unrelated verticals (HVAC, coastal infrastructure, storm surge forecasting), and nothing suggests a production deployment with a real customer. Three verticals with two people tells me they're searching for fit, not executing from conviction.

I also struggle to find an economic access story here. The companies I'm most drawn to — Uber creating income for drivers, Affirm providing transparent credit, Thumbtack connecting tradespeople with customers — make invisible systems visible and create opportunity for people who lacked it. Inviscid AI is an enterprise optimization tool for facilities managers and data center operators. It saves commercial building owners on their energy bills. That's a fine business, but it doesn't create the kind of positive-sum economic impact that gets me out of bed. When I think about my portfolio's common thread — transparency where opacity existed, access where exclusion existed — this doesn't fit. It's incremental efficiency for sophisticated commercial users, not a fundamentally new kind of access.

This is a pass for me. I see a technically capable PhD student and a co-founder with undocumented background, building in a space where the consensus has already formed and the big money has already moved. The technology approach (PINNs for CFD) is academically published rather than proprietary, the case studies suggest they're spreading thin across verticals rather than drilling into one with missionary focus, and neither founder's story tells me why they're the ones who have to solve this. Sometimes a company is exactly what it appears to be on paper — a reasonable technical team pursuing a reasonable market opportunity — and for a lot of investors, that's enough. But I'm not looking for reasonable. I'm looking for the founders and ideas that make most people uncomfortable, and this one doesn't.
