# Miso Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Miso Labs is an Aoden Teo hack — an 8-billion-parameter TTS model with 32×2048 residual vector quantization shipped by a two-person team — wrapped around a problem neither founder is documented to have personally suffered. The signature here is hacker artifact without organic pain: Teo's combinatorics publications and the MisoLabsAI/MisoTTS repo do the talking, while the dossier offers no anecdote where Cassidy Dalva or Aoden Teo were themselves the developer waiting 700ms for an ElevenLabs callback. That asymmetry is what I have to price.

Drew Houston at **Dropbox** (2007) is the right HIGH rung for the resourcefulness ladder — he posted a demo video to Hacker News before the check, and the artifact was the pitch. Miso Labs has the artifact half of that pattern: an 8B open-weight model on Hugging Face, an Aoden Teo launch tweet at 312K views, MarkTechPost and Kingy AI coverage on June 4 2026. That is more than most Spring 2026 pre-seed dossiers carry, and it is real animal work — you do not train an 8B model with RVQ as two people without staying up nights. What Miso Labs lacks that Houston had is the *rejection-and-return* texture, the Brian Armstrong-at-**Coinbase** (2012) pattern of building the prototype *because* the institutional door closed. The dossier shows shipping, not scrapping. That keeps Miso Labs above the MBA-deck floor but well below the cereal-box ceiling.

The **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick origin is the failure mode I keep checking against on organic-idea fit, and Miso Labs inverts it. Houston built the thing he himself kept needing; the Collisons at **Stripe** (2010) had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007). Miso Labs' founders are a Stanford economics major who was Managing Editor at The Stanford Daily and a Stanford mathematics competitor with combinatorics papers. Neither biography contains a moment where they were building a voice agent and got burned by 700ms latency. The product is a beautiful answer to a problem someone else has — which is exactly the configuration "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) warned about. I do not see Dalva or Teo as the customer of Miso One.

On schlep: the work Miso Labs is doing is *hard* — training 8B-parameter foundation models is not easy — but hard and schlep are different things. **Coinbase** (2012) was a schlep because the work was unpleasant compliance grind nobody wanted; voice foundation models in 2026 are the consensus AI play with ElevenLabs at an $11B valuation and Cartesia having raised $100M in October 2025. The category is the opposite of schlep-blind; it is schlep-crowded. There is no regulatory exposure named in the Miso Labs dossier, no manual onboarding, no offline ops. Eighteen months of compounding here looks like Cartesia's Sonic Turbo (sub-40ms TTFA, already shipped) and ElevenLabs v3 squeezing the open-weight position from both ends while mlx-community forks the weights downstream.

Hacker genealogy is where Miso Labs scores highest, and the right single-anchor parallel is Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010). Aoden Teo's ResearchGate combinatorics work plus a World Cube Association competitor ID plus a shipped 8B model with a non-trivial RVQ architecture is the genuine article — that is a hacker, not a credentialed CS-degree holder. The drag is Cassidy Dalva: a Stanford Daily News Managing Editor and King Center research fellow is impressive on a different axis but does not show up in the GitHub trail. Half the team has hacker lineage. That is better than zero, materially worse than the Collison brothers.

For cofounder configuration, the counterfactual test: what would have to be true for Miso Labs to flip a tier here? The dossier would need to show Dalva and Teo had built something together for two-plus years pre-Miso — a Stanford project, a prior startup, a paper. Instead we get "both Stanford alumni" and "no public data found on prior shared employer." That is the YC-interview-will-tell-us-more zone. Pair, not solo, complementary on paper (technical/operational), no documented chemistry. Neutral default.

My 36-month forecast for Miso Labs: acqui-hired by Cartesia or ElevenLabs at sub-$75M for Aoden Teo's model team within 24-30 months, with the hosted API never crossing $2M ARR. The mechanism: open weights remove the model-weight moat (mlx-community already shipped MisoTTS-8bit), Cartesia's sub-40ms TTFA already beats Miso Labs' 110ms headline, and a 2-person pre-seed team cannot outspend ElevenLabs' $500M Series D R&D budget on the parameter-count axis. Without an organic founder-pain loop pulling them toward an unobvious wedge, Miso Labs gets out-iterated in the consensus category.

The bull case that stings: Aoden Teo is the animal, and "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) says one animal is sometimes enough. Shipping an 8B model with novel RVQ codebook scaling as a 2-person team in months is not nothing — that is the Houston demo-video pattern at higher technical difficulty. The **Loopt** (2005) exception exists precisely for cases where founder force overrides idea-level skepticism; if Teo is that level of animal, Miso Labs deserves the check regardless of the schlep verdict. What dismantles the bull: Sam Altman at **Loopt** (2005) was the *whole* team's animal energy at YC interviews; Miso Labs' dossier gives me Teo's artifact but no Dalva counterpart on resourcefulness, and the organic-pain hole stays unfilled. The verdict flips to Invest if Miso Labs publishes 30-day download counts on Hugging Face north of 50K combined with a named enterprise design partner pulling Miso One into a shipped voice agent — that would prove pull I am not currently seeing.
