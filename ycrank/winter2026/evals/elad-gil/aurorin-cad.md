# Aurorin CAD -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural thesis here is specific and genuinely interesting: AI coding tools have crossed a capability threshold where building complex software — the kind that previously required hundreds of engineers and millions of lines of code — is now feasible for a solo founder. Jared Friedman made this point explicitly: "This would have been impossible before AI coding got good, because Solidworks is 10M lines of code. But it is now plausible." That's a supply-side inflection point. The CAD kernel hasn't been rebuilt from scratch in decades because the engineering cost was prohibitive. If that cost has truly collapsed by an order of magnitude, the window is open for someone to build a modern kernel optimized for current hardware and AI integration. The timing parallel to my Harvey thesis is instructive — Harvey built ahead of the model fidelity curve, positioning to capture market share when GPT-4 shipped. Aurorin is building ahead of the AI-coding productivity curve, betting that what's "plausible" today becomes "clearly superior" within 12-18 months as these tools continue to improve.

But here's where my framework creates friction: the CAD market is not non-obvious. It's a $12.5 billion market that every infrastructure-focused investor knows about. Onshape raised significant capital, got acquired for $470M by PTC — a respectable outcome, not a generational one. Shapr3D, Adam, Zoo — multiple startups are attacking CAD from different angles right now. This isn't PagerDuty, where operations alerting seemed boring and niche. CAD disruption is a well-known opportunity that has resisted multiple attempts over decades. The "why now" is novel — the supply-side cost compression from AI coding — but it's an enabling inflection that benefits every entrant equally, not a structural moat for Aurorin specifically. Adam has already raised $4.1M and generated 10M+ social media impressions. Zoo has open-source traction. The custom kernel is a differentiator, but the AI coding tools that make Aurorin's approach feasible also make it feasible for a well-funded team to attempt the same thing six months from now.

My single-miracle test creates the sharpest objection. Aurorin's path to becoming a production engineering tool requires: (1) building a geometrically precise parametric kernel that meets engineering tolerances, (2) achieving feature completeness across sheet metal, surfacing, assemblies, drawings, and file format interoperability, (3) integrating AI that's genuinely useful beyond demo-quality interactions, and (4) overcoming enterprise switching costs in organizations where CAD replacement involves retraining, PLM integration, and regulatory compliance. That's three to four sequential breakthroughs, each with independent probability of failure. Compare this to Harvey, where the single miracle — model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy — was being delivered by OpenAI, freeing the Harvey team to focus on workflow and distribution. Aurorin's miracles are mostly internal. AI coding tools help with development speed, but they don't solve the domain knowledge problem of what a production CAD kernel needs to support, or the enterprise sales problem of displacing SolidWorks at Boeing.

The bull case I have to genuinely engage with: Michael Baron's background is unusually well-matched. Three SpaceX internships working on Raptor combustion simulation and Dragon GNC means he's lived inside complex 3D geometry workflows and experienced the pain of legacy CAD firsthand. Apple GPU driver optimization is precisely the skillset needed for a GPU-accelerated kernel. The fact that he's shipped a downloadable product for both Mac and Windows as a solo founder — regardless of feature completeness — is real execution velocity. And the product-to-distribution trajectory in CAD is structurally attractive: the Stripe analogy applies. A CAD kernel is an integration point that could distribute simulation, manufacturing, PLM, and collaboration tools, just as Stripe's payment API distributed Atlas, Billing, and Treasury. If Aurorin achieves kernel viability, the expansion surface is enormous. The incumbent cannibalization dynamic is also real — Dassault and Siemens face the same structural problem that led PTC to acquire Onshape for $470M rather than rebuild internally.

Where I land: the timing thesis is real and specific, the founder-market fit is strong for a pre-seed stage, and the product-to-distribution potential in CAD is structurally compelling. But the multi-miracle problem is disqualifying under my framework. This isn't a company that needs one breakthrough and can treat everything else as a checkbox — it needs kernel completeness, AI quality, and enterprise adoption to all work sequentially. That's the pattern I explicitly avoid. I'd want to see evidence that the kernel has reached production-grade precision in at least one meaningful workflow, or that enterprise engineers are already using it for real work, before the multi-miracle concern recedes. Today, with no visible traction, no user counts, and a free product with no monetization, I'm watching but not writing a check.
