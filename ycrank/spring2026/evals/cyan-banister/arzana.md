# Arzana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

William Alexander grew up working for a manufacturing sourcing company in Okoboji, Iowa, and that single sentence in the Arzana dossier is the only reason I am still reading past slide one. The signature on Arzana is a lived-origin founder shipping an invisible product into a category three of his own YC P26 batchmates -- Korso, Smartbase, Tiny -- are simultaneously building. The thing the founder *is* points up; the thing the founder is *building* points down. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but I have to be honest about which game this actually is.

Chase Lochmiller at Crusoe grew up adjacent to oil-and-gas operators and could walk a North Dakota well pad without flinching -- the social license was the moat before the technology was. Arzana's William Alexander has the structural twin of that biography: an Iowa childhood inside a manufacturing sourcing firm, which is the only kind of resume that lets you cold-call a shop floor in Milltown Paper or Iowa Mold without sounding like a Stanford kid reading from a script. Where Arzana diverges from the Lochmiller rung is the NYT line that "neither founder had a tech background prior to Arzana" -- Lochmiller was a quant who could *build* the energy arbitrage, where Alexander has the social license but is shipping a multi-ERP integration platform across Epicor, JobBoss, NetSuite, SAP, Salesforce, and HubSpot without a technical co-founder of record. The lived origin is real. The execution stack to honor it is not yet on the cap table.

HQ Trivia's collapse was not a product failure -- the trivia app was so addictive I could feel the viral pull before any metric existed -- it was Yusupov and Kroll proving they could not co-pilot under stress. Arzana is the inverse pattern on tactility: William named CEO, Marshall named COO, titles delineated, no 50/50 ambiguity in the dossier, but I cannot install the product, ride it, scan it, or photograph it in my own life within 48 hours. Arzana's "product" is a Zeiss back-office workflow I will never see; the logo wall (Tecton, Koike, Tier1, Givi, Kuebler) is the only artifact a normal human can verify, and a logo is not a thing you touch. Where HQ Trivia overrode governance with tactility, Arzana has the opposite combination -- governance looks fine, tactility is absent.

Arzana's competitive density compounds the wrong way over 18 months: Korso is doing RFQ/quote agents, Smartbase is doing PO-to-ERP order entry, Tiny is doing full ERP for Excel factories, and all three are sitting in the same P26 Demo Day room as William and Marshall. When four YC companies converge on "AI for manufacturing back-office" inside one batch, that is not category timing -- that is the inverse of "find them before it becomes consensus," and Tom Blomfield's portfolio will be cross-introducing them to the same Bessemer and Greylock partners on the same Tuesday. Eighteen months out the buyer cannot tell Arzana from Korso from Smartbase on a procurement call, and price discovery happens at the customer, not the cap table.

Arzana would need to flip one fact to break from neutral into invest territory: a hire or co-founder addition who shipped enterprise integration software at scale before joining -- the Anduril-Luckey shape, where someone in the building has actually built the thing the company claims to build. The "99.9% accuracy" claim across heterogeneous PDFs and emails is the kind of number a non-technical founding team writes onto a website because they have not yet hit the long tail of edge cases; Lochmiller would have left it off. If Arzana lands a CTO out of Epicor's integrations team in the next two quarters, the score moves materially.

SpaceX in 2008 was the template for me writing into capital-intensive frontier risk most VCs called uninvestable. Arzana fails this axis cleanly -- it is a software overlay on incumbent ERPs, no hardware, no regulatory moat, no physical infrastructure compounding with time, just an LLM parsing PDFs into Epicor. The vertical is unsexy enough to get partial credit (Iowa manufacturers are not the consensus AI buyer), but unsexy-software is not the same shape as Crusoe-flares or Anduril-drones. The moat the dossier proposes -- "proprietary training data from historical job costs and parts-matching" -- is exactly the kind of moat the polymath thesis says collapses, because the model is the moat and the model is replaceable in a quarter.

My 36-month forecast on Arzana: $2-6M ARR with a Midwest mid-market manufacturer footprint, then an acqui-hire by Epicor, QAD, or a NetSuite partner channel in the low-to-mid eight figures once the incumbents' embedded agentic features close the overlay gap. The mechanism is the high/low pairing -- William's Iowa origin is enough to win the first 30 logos because he can talk to a Givi or Kuebler ops director without flinching, but the absence of a tactile product means no compounding distribution, and the absence of a technical founding stack means the integration surface area gets eaten by the incumbents Arzana is currently overlaying.

The strongest case against my pass is this: NYT featured Arzana in "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype" on February 8, 2026, before Demo Day, and Zeiss is on the logo wall -- that is real distribution and a real Fortune 500 reference at pre-seed, neither of which the consensus YC AI-agent pass-example had. Bessemer would buy this story. The bear case wins on a single Unstated Pattern: my heavy-hardware sleeve (SpaceX, Anduril, Crusoe) hides under a consumer-angel reputation precisely because I do not write the software-only enterprise check, and Arzana's "no tech background" founders building a 10-ERP integration platform is the structural inverse of Lochmiller-of-the-oilfield -- Alexander is of the *customer*, not of the *technology*. The verifiable condition that flips me to invest: William hires a named, shipped-at-scale ERP-integrations technical co-founder within two quarters of Demo Day, and the Korso/Smartbase/Tiny trio meaningfully diverges in approach rather than converging.
