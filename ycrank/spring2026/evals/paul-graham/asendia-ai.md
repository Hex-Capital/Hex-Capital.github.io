# Asendia AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The pivot tells the story. Asendia started as a B2C interview prep tool — "Sarah, your AI Job Coach" — launched it on Product Hunt twice, got respectable upvotes, and then showed up at YC as a B2B agentic recruitment platform for staffing agencies. The B2C product is gone. All the traction numbers in the dossier — the 286 upvotes, the 15K minutes of interview prep, the website traffic spike — belong to a product that no longer exists. What we're actually evaluating is a company with zero public evidence of traction on its current product, building for a customer they've never been.

That's what concerns me most: neither founder has worked at a staffing agency, run a staffing agency, or — as far as I can tell — been personally frustrated by the specific workflows they're automating. Rihab was a cloud architect at Google and Microsoft. Badis was an application engineer at Infineon. The CTO's prior B2C product makes sense as organic discovery — he was probably prepping for FAANG interviews himself, got annoyed, and built something. That's the right origin story pattern. But the leap from "I built an interview coach for myself" to "I'm building an agentic platform for staffing agencies" has a gap in the middle where personal experience should be. When I ask myself whether these founders lived in the future and built what was missing, the honest answer is that they lived in corporate cloud engineering and built what market research suggested. The CEO's HR Technologies France speaking engagement shows she's learning the domain, but learning the domain from the outside is a different thing than having felt the pain from the inside.

The competitive landscape is the other problem. "AI agents for recruiting" is not an idea that repels anyone. It's the kind of idea that appears in every AI startup trend report published in the last eighteen months. Paradox raised $300M before Workday acquired them. hireEZ has $42M in revenue. Humanly just acquired three companies to expand its platform. When everyone immediately agrees an idea is good, the return premium has been competed away. The Asendia team's pitch — that they bundle sourcing, outreach, video, and phone screening into one platform — sounds like a feature roadmap, not an insight. And they're attempting this breadth with three people. Four distinct AI agents plus fifty ATS integrations is an extraordinary surface area for a team that hasn't hired its first engineer yet. The best early products I've funded did one thing so well that users would tolerate everything else being broken. This is the opposite pattern — a platform-shaped silhouette with no evidence of depth anywhere.

The strongest argument for this company: the CTO actually built a product before. FAANG AI shipped, got users, generated real interview minutes. That's not nothing — most people who say they want to start a startup never get to the point of having users at all. The pivot from B2C to B2B could be a sign of genuine learning, not aimlessness. They realized individual job seekers don't pay, staffing agencies do, and they redirected. The technical infrastructure from the B2C product — the AI interviewing, the screening models — is presumably reusable. And the staffing industry genuinely does run on phone calls and spreadsheets in a way that should embarrass everyone involved. If the founders develop real relationships with a handful of agencies and iterate obsessively on what those agencies actually need, there's a path. The Paradox acquisition creates a real opening for independent players. But "there's a path" is different from "I see the specific founders who will walk it," and I don't have that conviction here.

The compliance claims bother me in a way that's hard to articulate precisely. SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance for a three-person pre-seed company founded in 2024 — these are expensive, time-consuming certifications. Either they've achieved something impressive that would itself be a story of resourcefulness worth telling, or the claims are aspirational and the website is overstating where they are. The dossier can't resolve this, but the ambiguity itself is a data point. Founders who communicate with precision about what they've actually accomplished versus what they intend to accomplish — that's one of the things I'm listening for. When the website claims "$6M higher revenue from placements" as a customer testimonial without any verifiable context, I hear marketing language where I want to hear honesty about early uncertainty.

I keep my money in my pocket on this one. The founders are competent engineers who can ship product, but competent engineers who can ship product are the starting line, not the finish line. What I need to see — and don't — is evidence that these specific founders understand the staffing agency world with the kind of depth that only comes from having lived inside it, and that they're attacking a problem ugly enough to repel the dozens of well-funded competitors already circling this space. Right now it looks like two capable people who picked a hot market and are trying to out-feature the incumbents. That's a strategy that works in corporate product management. It almost never works in startups.
