# Interfaze -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural economics here are running in the wrong direction. Interfaze's core value proposition is cost: route deterministic tasks -- OCR, scraping, speech-to-text, classification -- to specialized small models instead of expensive frontier LLMs, saving developers money. But frontier model pricing has been collapsing at roughly 10x per year. GPT-4-class output that cost $60/M tokens two years ago is approaching single digits. Interfaze charges $3.50/M output tokens today. Within 12-18 months, frontier models will match or undercut that price point while delivering broader capabilities. When I evaluate market timing, I'm looking for a structural cost curve or capability threshold that's opening a window. Here the cost curve is closing the window. That's a fundamental headwind I can't ignore. The "why now" in the dossier -- small models improved, AI agent frameworks emerged, frontier LLMs are expensive -- is not nonobvious. Every AI investor on the planet knows these facts. And the third claim, high frontier costs, is a depreciating asset, not a durable structural shift.

The infrastructure-layer question is where this gets interesting, and where I'd want to be wrong. Interfaze positions itself as a unified API consolidating OCR, scraping, STT, and classification behind OpenAI-compatible endpoints. That's a real developer convenience -- instead of integrating Firecrawl for scraping, Whisper for STT, and Tesseract for OCR separately, you get one endpoint. The IEEE CAI 2026 paper documenting the controller architecture that routes between specialized DNNs and a user-selected LLM suggests genuine technical thinking about how to structure this orchestration layer. But here's what I notice: the OpenAI API compatibility that makes adoption frictionless also makes departure frictionless. Compare this to Stripe, where the payment integration compounds switching costs over time as you layer on billing, fraud detection, and treasury. Interfaze's integration is a single API call that you can redirect to any compatible endpoint. That's distribution, not infrastructure lock-in. Real infrastructure embeds progressively deeper into customer workflows. A drop-in replacement API is the architectural opposite of that.

The competitive landscape compounds my concern. This isn't a boring, overlooked category where the absence of prestige-seeking competitors IS the competitive advantage -- that pattern worked for Gusto in payroll and Checkr in background checks because nobody glamorous wanted to build payroll software. AI developer infrastructure is the opposite: it's the most actively competed category in technology. Firecrawl has 50K GitHub stars and $14.5M raised. Apify does $13.3M in revenue. Google Cloud Vision and Document AI handle OCR and document processing at enterprise scale. And every frontier lab is adding multimodal capabilities -- vision, audio, structured output -- that directly overlap with Interfaze's task set. When I invest in infrastructure, I want to see a category where the startup faces structural barriers to competition, not one where it faces structural exposure to encroachment from players with orders-of-magnitude more resources.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if there's a fundamental architectural reason why specialized models will permanently outperform general-purpose transformers on deterministic tasks -- a genuine "different tool for a different job" dynamic -- then a well-designed orchestration layer becomes a durable category, not a transitional hack. The IEEE paper argues this case. And the history of computing does include examples of specialized processors (GPUs, TPUs) coexisting with general-purpose CPUs rather than being absorbed. If Interfaze's controller architecture proves to be the right abstraction for combining perception models with language models, and if the 10M API requests in beta represent real developer pull (not just free-tier experimentation), this could be the early infrastructure layer for a multi-model future. Khemlani's prior exit at Stayr ($1M to $3M ARR in two years) shows he can build products that generate revenue, and Khurdula's ML research credentials are genuine. But I'd need to see evidence that developers are paying -- not just calling the API -- and that the cost advantage persists as frontier pricing drops. The dossier shows pricing but no revenue figures after nearly two years of operation, which is the silence that concerns me most.

On the single-miracle test: the one miracle Interfaze needs is for their architectural thesis to hold -- that specialized model routing remains durably superior to frontier models for deterministic tasks. But this is a miracle they don't control. It depends entirely on what OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google do with their model architectures. If GPT-5 handles OCR, classification, and structured extraction deterministically at low cost, Interfaze's entire category evaporates. That's an exogenous dependency on the largest, best-funded companies in AI not solving the same problem. I've seen this pattern before -- startups building optimizations on top of rapidly improving platforms get absorbed by the platform layer itself. The companies that survive are the ones embedding in workflows so deeply that even when the underlying platform catches up on capability, ripping out the integration is more expensive than keeping it. Interfaze hasn't built that depth yet.
