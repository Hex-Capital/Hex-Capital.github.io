# Docura Health -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me immediately is that Docura Health calls itself an "AI-native medico-legal firm." The word "firm" caught my attention because it implies something operationally thicker than software -- a services business, maybe, with AI as the engine. But when I pull the thread, there's no firm here. There are no physicians on staff, no case management operations, no physical-world complexity. What exists is a web application that ingests medical records and outputs structured reports using an LLM. Strip the branding and you're looking at document summarization plus template-based generation -- the single most commoditized capability in the current AI landscape. This is a feature wearing a company's clothing.

The founder-idea pairing doesn't activate my filter in the right direction. Akhil Sachdev has a UC Berkeley CS degree and a string of internships at enterprise software companies -- Salesforce, Zuora, Rivian, Cisco. That's a perfectly competent engineering background, but nothing in his history lends specific credibility to navigating medico-legal compliance, physician sales cycles, or workers' compensation regulation. The "sane" part of my framework requires that the founder's background makes you believe they can uniquely execute on THIS particular idea. An engineer who built OCR projects on GitHub can obviously build a document processing tool -- but can he sell to QME physicians, ensure AMA Guides compliance holds up under legal scrutiny, and navigate HIPAA in a domain where errors have litigation consequences? There's no evidence either way, which is itself a signal. Meanwhile, the idea -- using AI to generate medical-legal reports faster -- is about as consensus as it gets. Sixty percent of workers' comp professionals already want automation. Deloitte says eighty percent of claims could be automated. When the entire industry is asking for exactly what you're building, you're not pursuing an insane idea. You're building what everyone expects. That's the opposite of what I look for.

Here's where my framework gets decisive: would this business be good if AI didn't exist? The answer is unambiguously no. Without LLM-based summarization and generation, there's nothing here -- no service, no operations, no physical infrastructure, no protocol. AI isn't the cherry on top; it's the entire sundae. That means I need to ask the follow-up question: can a startup defend this AI capability against incumbents with distribution? Wisedocs has $14.4 million in revenue, 116 employees, and has been trained on over 100 million documents. DigitalOwl got acquired by Datavant, giving it access to the largest health data infrastructure in the country. These companies serve the same ecosystem -- medical record review in insurance -- and extending downstream into physician-facing report generation is a product decision, not a technology breakthrough. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "Any AI startup or established medico-legal services firm could build a similar tool." When the memo flags commoditization risk this explicitly, I pay attention.

The strongest bull case I can construct goes like this: the "firm" framing is prophetic, not decorative. If Docura evolves into an actual medico-legal services business -- managing case assignments, handling scheduling, automating billing, ensuring compliance end-to-end -- then it becomes the kind of operationally complex, physical-world-adjacent business I actually love to fund. Think of it like Craftwork but for medical evaluations instead of house painting: the moat would be in the operational machinery, not the software layer. The ~1,900 active QMEs in California represent a concentrated initial market where deep workflow embedding could create real switching costs. And if you squint, the regulatory complexity of AMA Guides compliance and state-specific workers' comp rules could function as a barrier that generic AI tools won't trivially overcome. But this requires believing that a solo founder with internship-level experience will independently discover the services-business playbook, hire physicians or compliance specialists, and build operational infrastructure in a regulated industry -- all with $500K. That's a lot of weight to put on a vision I'm inferring rather than one the founder has articulated.

The narrative ceiling concerns me structurally. California QMEs are a starting point of roughly 1,900 physicians. Expand to all U.S. workers' comp evaluators, then to other insurance medical evaluations, and you're in the hundreds-of-millions range -- a real business, but not an infinity story. The global medico-legal software market is projected at just over a billion dollars by 2031. There's no obvious path to this becoming infrastructure that an ecosystem builds on. No network effects, no protocol dynamics, no compounding primitive. It's a vertical application on someone else's AI infrastructure, targeting a niche of a niche. I don't see the narrative elasticity that lets this story keep expanding with each milestone.

I'm passing. The structural logic points in the wrong direction on almost every dimension I care about. AI IS the product, not the amplifier. The idea is consensus, not contrarian. The founder lacks domain-specific credibility. The competitive moat is the technology itself, which is the most perishable kind. And the market, while real, has a visible ceiling. If this were a medico-legal services company that happened to use AI to be ten times more efficient -- actual physicians on staff, operational complexity that software alone can't replicate, a business that would exist and profit without AI -- I'd be interested. But that's not what's in front of me.
