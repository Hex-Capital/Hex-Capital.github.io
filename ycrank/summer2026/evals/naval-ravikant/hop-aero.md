# Hop Aero -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Hop Aero is two credentialed propulsion engineers -- Matija Milenovic (ex-porkchop, ex-Destinus) and Jacob Balaj (ex-SpaceX Falcon 9/Heavy I&T) -- building a sub-orbital rocket whose only customer is the U.S. Department of Defense and whose every test campaign requires FAA Part 450, ITAR, and AFRL SBIR approval. That is the signature: top-quartile specific knowledge wrapped around the maximally permissioned leverage architecture I have on file. The $1.25M USAF SBIR award is not a moat, it is the gatekeeper's leash, and the $3M pre-seed from Lunar Ventures and Outsized Ventures is buying a lottery ticket on a procurement officer's calendar.

Run the leverage check first, the way Twitter (2007) ran it. Twitter is the HIGH rung because anyone could post, the platform charged nothing, and the marginal cost of replication was zero -- code and media compounding through permissionless distribution. Hop Aero inverts every clause of that mechanism: payloads are physical, the platform is a launch pad, the marginal cost is millions per vehicle, and the distribution channel is USTRANSCOM CRADAs that already belong to SpaceX, Sierra Space, Rocket Lab, and Blue Origin per the March 2024 Breaking Defense reporting. The dossier names $102M of AFRL P2PD prime money sitting with SpaceX since January 2022; Hop Aero's distribution architecture is not just non-permissionless, it is competing for permission against the company that already has it. This is labor and capital leverage in its purest form -- exactly the structure nav.al/product-media flags as the architecture I do not fund.

The Pinterest pass haunts me on networks -- I missed a permissionless media product with compounding user-added value because "I didn't get it." That failure mode does not transfer to Hop Aero. Hop Aero has one customer (DoD), one payload-receiver (forward operating units), and zero mechanic by which the Nth user makes user N-1 more valuable. Adding a second DoD program office does not compound the first; it just adds a second procurement cycle. The asymmetric-payoff math from Knowledge Project #18 -- one X down, 10,000 X up -- collapses because the upside is bounded by the size of a single buyer's budget line, not by network density.

Specific knowledge is where Hop Aero earns its only real points. Milenovic ran a 20+ person supersonic UAV team at Destinus and shipped propulsion hardware to orbit on SpaceX Transporter-3 via porkchop; Balaj integrated and tested Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy boosters at SpaceX, with prior reps at Masten, Virgin Orbit, and SpinLaunch. Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity is the single-anchor parallel -- a PhD-deep founder whose non-trainable knowledge cleared the criterion #3 bar at the top quartile and let me waive criterion #2 entirely. The difference: Srinivas's specific knowledge compounded through an API and a free web product, distribution architecture that was permissionless by construction. Hop Aero's specific knowledge compounds through a launch license. Same depth, opposite leverage stack.

Big market with pivot room -- mechanism analysis over 18 months. Rocket cargo to contested island chains is one vertical with one buyer, and the dossier's own "Inferred" GTM is the SBIR-to-STRATFI-to-program-of-record ladder that every defense-space startup walks. Pivoting Hop Aero's hardware sideways into commercial point-to-point or hypersonic testbed work is theoretically available, but each pivot consumes a flight-test campaign that costs more than the entire pre-seed. The Venture Hacks axiom -- "the first idea never works, you need room to maneuver in a big market" -- assumes pivots are cheap. In rocket cargo a pivot is a re-qualification cycle measured in years and tens of millions. The market is large in tonnage but narrow in optionality.

The Buffett trio reads clean on the data available -- two operators with shipped hardware, no contested exits, no public self-promotion about ethics. I would not hold this dimension against Hop Aero. The default of 7/14 plus a small bump for verified shipping behavior at SpaceX and porkchop is the right place, but a clean integrity read does not save a check whose leverage architecture is upside-down from my thesis.

Forecast at 36 months: Hop Aero raises a $20-40M Series A in 2027 on the back of one SBIR Phase III or STRATFI conversion, conducts one or two suborbital test flights, and either gets acqui-hired by Stoke Space or Anduril for the propulsion team, or grinds at $5-15M of annual DoD contract revenue without escape velocity. Dead is unlikely; the founders are good enough to survive. 10,000x is impossible; the buyer set is too narrow and the incumbents are too funded.

Strongest opposing case: Anchorage Digital is the precedent where I broke criterion #1 because regulation-as-moat became a non-permissionless structural leverage in a category I was already accumulating into since MetaStable (2014). The bull case for Hop Aero asks me to apply the same exception -- treat ITAR and FAA Part 450 as the moat, treat USAF SBIR as the category-timing call, and ride founder specific knowledge into a defense-tech supercycle. The dossier dismantles this on its own terms. Anchorage was advisor-stage conviction inside a category I had been writing checks in for seven years; rocket cargo is a category where SpaceX already holds the $102M prime contract and the regulatory moat protects the incumbent, not the entrant. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Hop Aero announces a signed Space Force prototype contract above $25M with named scope before Series A, demonstrating that the gatekeeper has actually picked them over SpaceX, Inversion, or Stoke. Until then this is capital-and-labor leverage applying for permission, and that is the bet I do not write.
