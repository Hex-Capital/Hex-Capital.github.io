# StableBrowse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about StableBrowse is the gap between the ambition of what they're claiming and the visibility of who's claiming it. They say they've built a browser engine that strips the entire rendering pipeline — CSS layout, compositing, rasterization — while keeping JavaScript execution and network handling intact. That's not a wrapper around Chromium. That's not a cloud service managing browser instances. That's a claim about operating at the engine level, which is one of the most technically difficult things in software. Google has thousands of engineers working on Chromium's rendering stack, and these four founders are saying they've removed it surgically while maintaining web compatibility. I'd love to evaluate whether they can actually do this. But I can't find them.

Four co-founders, and the dossier surfaces almost nothing. No confirmed GitHub repos. No open-source work. No prior exits. No blog posts or technical writing. Somansh Shah graduated Purdue in 2025 with AI/ML coursework and won a startup expo for $6,600. One person who might be the same Deepit Shah worked at Amazon. Jay Mehta might have founded two prior companies — unconfirmed. Sarthak Awasthi is essentially a ghost. I can't even confirm how these four know each other. When I'm putting in my own money, the first thing I want is to hear founders tell me stories about problems they've hacked through. Not their pitch — their stories. I can't do that from a dossier, but I should at least be able to see artifacts: a side project that got traction, a technical blog post that shows deep understanding of browser internals, an open-source tool that other developers use. Something that proves these are the kind of people who build things compulsively. The complete absence of public artifacts from a team claiming to rebuild the browser from the engine level is not a neutral signal. Truly obsessive builders leave traces.

The idea itself has real merit, and I want to be honest about that. This sits squarely in the "hackers building for hackers" category that I'm drawn to. Every developer building AI agents right now is paying a tax for running full Chromium instances that render pixels no one looks at. Stripping the rendering stack and outputting semantic markdown instead of screenshots is the kind of insight that seems obvious in retrospect but required someone to actually do it. The performance claims — 85MB RAM per instance, 7x faster execution, 95% token reduction — if true, would create unit economics that cloud browser providers literally cannot match without rebuilding their core infrastructure. Browserbase has $67.5 million invested in managing fleets of standard Chromium. They can't easily cannibalize that to adopt a fundamentally different architecture. That structural divergence is real, and it's the kind of moat I like — not a patent or a network effect, but the fact that the right approach requires starting over, and incumbents can't start over.

The bull case is strong enough that I want to articulate it clearly. If these founders are the real thing — if there's an engine-level systems programmer hiding behind that thin public profile — this could be a Stripe-like bet. Stripe worked because the Collison brothers were willing to tackle the painful regulatory and integration schlep of payments that repelled other developers. StableBrowse works if these founders are willing to tackle the painful compatibility schlep of a non-rendering browser engine that repels other teams. The timing is right: agent frameworks are exploding, $180 million has already flowed into browser infrastructure for AI, and every one of those dollars went to companies running standard Chromium. A purpose-built semantic engine is an architectural step-change, not an incremental improvement. If I could sit with these founders for ten minutes and hear them explain how they handle Shadow DOM rendering assumptions, or how their JavaScript execution stays correct when the layout engine isn't feeding back computed styles, and they answered with the specificity of someone who's lived inside this problem — I might write a check. The idea rewards exactly the kind of technically obsessive founder I bet on.

But I don't have that ten minutes, and what I can observe gives me too little to work with. The "built-in evasion with human-like fingerprints" claim on their website bothers me slightly — it positions the product in an adversarial relationship with bot detection systems, and a non-standard browser engine is actually easier to fingerprint than real Chromium. More importantly, web compatibility for a rendering-free engine is an open research problem, not an engineering project with a known finish line. Websites make implicit assumptions about browser behavior that break in unpredictable ways when the rendering pipeline is absent. The dossier notes no public benchmarks on compatibility coverage, and for a claim this technically ambitious, the absence of proof is itself informative. Four recent graduates with no visible track record in browser engineering, tackling a problem that has humbled teams with ten times their resources — I need some evidence that they're the exception, and right now I have none.

The idea is in my strike zone. The architecture is genuinely differentiated. The timing is favorable. But I invest in founders, not architectures, and these founders are invisible to me. If someone I trusted told me these four had the animal quality — if I'd seen them build something at a YC dinner, or if they'd shipped even a small tool that developers loved — this evaluation would look very different. As it stands, I'm passing on what I can see while acknowledging that what I can't see might be exactly what matters.
