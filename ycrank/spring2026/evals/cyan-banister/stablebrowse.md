# StableBrowse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I know about StableBrowse's four co-founders: almost nothing. One graduated from Purdue last year with AI coursework and won a campus startup competition. One might have worked at Amazon. Two are essentially invisible — no confirmed profiles, no public repos, no prior companies, no trail of building in the open. I can't answer my first and most important question — "tell me your story" — because there is no story available to tell. And I don't mean they haven't had time to build traction; I mean I can't find evidence that any of these founders were *forged* by a personal encounter with the problem of browsers being too heavy for AI agents. This reads like four technically capable people who identified a market gap during the AI agent boom, not people whose lives made them incapable of ignoring this specific problem. The absence of biographical narrative isn't just a gap in my data — it's a gap in the signal I rely on most. I can't invest my own money in a team I can't feel.

The architectural idea is genuinely interesting, and I want to be honest about that. Stripping the rendering pipeline out of a browser engine and outputting semantic markdown instead of pixels — that's not an AI wrapper. That's infrastructure work at the engine level, and if these performance claims are real (85MB per instance, sub-80ms cold start, 95% token savings), the unit economics advantage over Browserbase and Kernel would be structural. When I run my ten-year question — where does this go? — I can see a version where every AI agent on the internet speaks through a semantic browser layer instead of screenshotting pixels and parsing them like a human would. That would be foundational infrastructure. The architectural divergence from competitors who manage fleets of standard Chromium instances is real — they'd have to cannibalize their own product to chase this. If someone with deep browser engine experience and a track record of shipping systems at this level of complexity brought me this idea, I'd lean forward.

But that's the bull case, and it depends on "if" doing a lot of heavy lifting. Building a browser engine that maintains compatibility with the chaos of the modern web — SPAs, Shadow DOM, WebSocket-driven UIs, the full zoo of JavaScript runtime behavior — while stripping the rendering stack is one of the hardest engineering challenges in infrastructure. This is the kind of work that teams of dozens at Google and Mozilla have spent years on. I need to see evidence that this four-person team has the depth to execute it, and I see none — no public benchmarks, no open-source repos, no technical artifacts, no prior experience building systems at this level that I can verify. The claimed metrics are on a website with no validation. And four co-founders with no discernible shared history and no clear CEO? That's a structure I've been burned by. HQ Trivia taught me what happens when there's no clear captain — and that was with two founders who'd already built Vine together. Four unknown co-founders is an amplification of that risk.

The space itself is the opposite of what pulls me in. AI agent infrastructure is the most consensus-attractive category in venture right now. Competitors have raised a combined $180 million-plus. Browserbase alone has $67.5M, a thousand customers, and revenue. This isn't a room where VCs shift uncomfortably in their chairs — they're elbowing each other to get in. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I wrote my check to Crusoe, Bitcoin mining with waste gas sounded like a scam. StableBrowse sounds like exactly what every investor at every dinner party in San Francisco is excited about in 2026. There's no courage required to articulate this thesis, and that's a problem for me, because my biggest returns have come from bets that required courage. Furthermore, there's no hidden system being disrupted here. The customers are developers and enterprises who understand exactly what they're paying for when they run cloud browsers. There's no information asymmetry, no opaque tax being extracted from people who don't understand the system. This is cost optimization for the technically sophisticated, not liberation for the structurally disadvantaged.

I wish these founders well. The timing is real, the architecture is differentiated, and the problem is genuine. But without a biographical thread I can pull, without contrarian conviction in a hard space, and without an invisible system being revealed, this isn't a Long Journey bet. It's a technically interesting infrastructure play in a crowded, well-funded, consensus category — and I need more than that to write a check from my own money.
