# Grade -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing I keep coming back to with Grade is the competitive landscape, and what it tells me about the kind of bet this is. Lumanu has raised $20.6 million. Trolley has raised $44.4 million. Creative Juice has $20 million plus a $50 million creator fund. These are not hypothetical future competitors — they're funded, established, and already in the creator payout space. When I look at my best investments — SpaceX, Crusoe Energy, Contraline — they succeeded precisely because nobody else was building in those spaces. The loneliness of the bet was the signal. Grade is entering a category where multiple well-capitalized teams already operate, and while the performance-based pay angle is a real differentiator, it's a product design advantage, not a structural moat. The core technology — connecting performance APIs to payment rails — is technically reproducible by any team with access to Stripe or Wise.

That said, when I look at Lotanna and James through my usual lens — tell me how you got here, not what you're building — there's something genuinely compelling. Lotanna was a sprinter for England who became a content creator and experienced firsthand the absurdity of getting paid flat fees when his content performed wildly differently across posts. That led him to XPO, then Creator Check, then Grade. He's been circling this problem across multiple companies for years. James sold his first startup at 22, built Pray Screen to $40K MRR in two months and sold it, helped 55,000 Canadians find COVID vaccines with FindMyVax. These are builders who ship things. They've worked together across Creator Check and four mobile AI apps, exiting multiple times. That co-founder history is exactly what I look for after watching HQ Trivia disintegrate — they've already survived the stress of building, scaling, and selling companies together. I don't have to wonder how they handle conflict because they've demonstrated it through shared exits.

Where this falls short for me is the weirdness test. The most promising companies are the ones that make other investors squirm, that look slightly crazy at the pitch, that require conviction because the data doesn't exist yet to prove them right. "Payroll for creators" doesn't make anyone squirm. It's sensible. It's logical. The creator economy is a $205 billion market growing at 23% CAGR — everyone knows this. I'm not the first check into something nobody else wants to fund. I'm looking at a better mousetrap in a category where multiple well-funded mousetraps already exist. That's a fundamentally different kind of bet than what I'm drawn to.

The bull case I want to engage with honestly: the specific insight that creator compensation is shifting from flat fees to performance-based pay is underappreciated, and if that shift accelerates, the existing players (Lumanu, Trolley, Tipalti) would have to rearchitect their entire payment logic around variable compensation rules. Grade would be the native infrastructure for that new paradigm. The $380K in payouts processed in 30 days with 120% month-over-month growth is real traction at pre-seed. And the "zero creator onboarding" design — paying creators just by email — is the kind of product insight that comes from having actually been a creator yourself. If performance-based pay becomes the dominant model and Grade captures that transition, this could be the Affirm of the creator economy — making compensation transparent where it was previously opaque. That resonates with my pattern of backing companies that make invisible systems visible.

But here's where I land: I invest my own money in companies where I feel that jolt, where the founder's story and the problem are so entangled that I can't imagine anyone else solving it, and where the idea is uncomfortable enough that I'm one of the few people willing to write the check. Grade has solid founders with proven execution in a real market solving a genuine problem — but it doesn't need me. Any number of investors would see this as a reasonable bet. And the competitive capital disadvantage against Lumanu and Trolley makes the execution path harder without a deeper structural moat. The name confusion alone — competing with a Nordic HR-tech company for the same LinkedIn listing — suggests a long, expensive brand-building road. I respect the founders' craftsmanship and domain conviction, but this is a consensus play in a recognized category, and consensus-based investing is not what I do.
