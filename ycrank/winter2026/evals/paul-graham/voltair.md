# Voltair -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Voltair is the schlep. Not a single schlep -- a stack of them. Building a drone that physically clamps onto a live power line and draws power from it means dealing with arc flash physics, utility safety certification, FAA beyond-visual-line-of-sight regulations, custom power electronics, field operations in rural terrain, and selling to the most risk-averse buyers on earth. Each one of those is a schlep that would make a normal founder change course. All six together? That's the kind of problem the human unconscious literally refuses to see. I wrote about this in "Schlep Blindness" -- thousands of programmers knew payments were broken before Stripe, but nobody wanted to deal with banks. Here, plenty of people in the utility industry know power line inspection is a disaster (5-11 year cycles to inspect a full grid, helicopters, wildfires that bankrupt entire companies), but nobody wants to build a robot that perches on a wire carrying thousands of volts. The schlep is so severe it functions as a moat before the company has even scaled.

What makes this especially interesting is how the founders found it. Hayden Gosch didn't read a TechCrunch article about drone inspection. He spent two years at Seattle City Light doing system protection engineering, then did R&D at Schweitzer Engineering Laboratories -- a company that makes the actual protection relays on power grids. He was inside the belly of the utility beast. The UW article says he "posed the original power line inspection problem." His co-founder Ronan Nopp, who he's been friends with since middle school, had independently designed controls for a manned eVTOL aircraft. When two people with that specific combination of domain knowledge collide, the idea that emerges isn't manufactured. It's discovered. The Venn diagram of "understands power systems deeply enough to know you can safely tap a distribution line" and "can build an autonomous fixed-wing drone" is vanishingly small. That intersection is where Voltair lives.

The technical execution so far is impressive for any team, but especially for founders who graduated in 2025. Five flying prototypes. A working charging mechanism validated on actual power lines. Roughly 2,000 utility poles inspected. A pilot with Big Bend Electric Cooperative -- a real utility in Washington State. This is not a slide deck. These are hacker-builders in the most literal sense: they designed the charging clamp, the power conversion system, the drone airframe, and the controls. Nopp turned down SpaceX to do this, which is the kind of signal I pay attention to -- not because SpaceX is impressive (though it is), but because choosing uncertainty over a prestigious sure thing tells you something about a founder's conviction. These two remind me of the kind of young technical founders we funded in YC's early batches, the ones who were already building before anyone told them they could.

The bull case is significant. If the self-charging mechanism works reliably at scale -- and they've already demonstrated it on live infrastructure -- Voltair could compress a 5-11 year inspection cycle into 60 days. The problem is acute and getting worse: PG&E went bankrupt from wildfire liability, utility grid hardening spending has tripled, and extreme weather isn't slowing down. This isn't a nice-to-have; it's a survival tool for utilities. Their "drone data provider" framing suggests a recurring revenue model rather than hardware sales, which is the right instinct. And the competitive landscape is actually favorable: Skydio and Percepto don't self-charge, Cyberhawk uses human operators, and the closest competitor (Nomadic Drones in Munich) uses inductive charging rather than direct contact, which is a fundamentally different and less efficient approach.

My concerns are real but not disqualifying. These are very young founders with no prior startup experience. The four-co-founder structure worries me slightly -- Nopp and Gosch have a middle school friendship anchoring the core relationship, but Gotskind (George Washington University, aerospace consulting) and Weissbluth (Rice, operations) came from outside that orbit, and there's no public record of how or when they joined. I've seen teams fall apart when co-founders were assembled rather than grown together. The utility sales cycle is punishing -- 12 to 24 months from pilot to contract -- and as a hardware company, they'll burn more capital per month than a software startup. They'll need to raise real money before utilities move at utility speed. There's no revenue signal yet, which at pre-seed is understandable but combined with the long sales cycle creates a "default dead" risk if future rounds are slow.

Still, the core bet here is one I recognize. An obvious problem that everyone in the industry knows about, hidden behind a schlep so severe that almost nobody attempts it, discovered organically by founders with precisely the right domain expertise, who have already built working hardware. When I invested in Boom, the logic was similar -- frighteningly ambitious hardware, enormous regulatory complexity, but the kind of importance that means if they pull it off, the return is extraordinary. Voltair isn't supersonic jets, but the structural pattern rhymes. The schlep protects them. The organic origin means their motivation is durable. And they're already flying.
