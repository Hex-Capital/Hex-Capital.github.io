# Andustry -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Johann Stürken's resume reads like a case study I'd use to explain why consulting backgrounds make me nervous. McKinsey, Airbus, Capgemini Invent -- this is the trajectory of someone who learned to identify market opportunities through frameworks and competitive analysis. My reflexive response to founders with this profile is skepticism, because the way to get startup ideas is not to sit in a strategy firm and analyze adjacencies. But Andustry's dossier contains one detail that complicates this read: Johann's "family background in trading goods and supplier sourcing" (YC company page). That's not a framework. That's growing up watching your family haggle with suppliers across borders, absorbing the pain of non-standardized quoting and weeks-long response times before you ever heard the word "TAM." The question I can't answer from this dossier is which Johann built Andustry -- the McKinsey analyst who identified a $743B market, or the kid who watched his family struggle with cross-border procurement.

Het Dave provides the technical half of Andustry's founding pair, bringing a CS degree from IIT Jodhpur and a software engineering background. But the evidence of technical intensity is thin. His GitHub shows a personal pages repo and no notable public projects. For a company whose entire value proposition rests on AI agents that autonomously discover suppliers, negotiate on behalf of buyers, and normalize quotes -- sophisticated agentic workflows -- I want to see the technical co-founder's fingerprints on open-source work, or at least evidence of the kind of obsessive building that leaves artifacts. Andustry's claimed 500k+ verified companies in its network and €1M+ in beta transaction volume suggest something real has been built, but I can't assess the technical depth from what's available. The two co-founders share no prior employer, no shared university, no visible history of working together before Andustry. How they found each other and why they trust each other under pressure is a blank spot.

Andustry's €1M+ transaction volume in beta with a two-person team is the strongest signal in this dossier. Getting real money to flow through a B2B procurement platform is genuinely hard -- it requires convincing buyers to trust your supplier vetting, convincing suppliers to respond to RFQs from an unknown platform, and managing the logistics of cross-border industrial parts delivery. If that number is real and not an artifact of a few large one-off transactions, it demonstrates a degree of resourcefulness. The Stripe parallel tempts me -- Patrick and John were willing to tackle the painful schlep of payment infrastructure that repelled other developers, and Andustry is tackling the equally unglamorous schlep of cross-border industrial sourcing. But the parallel breaks at a critical point: the Collison brothers were developers building payment APIs for developers. Andustry is building for SME procurement teams, a customer segment neither founder appears to belong to. Johann's family trading background gets him closer than most, but "my family did this" is not the same as "I personally felt this frustration every day and couldn't stand it anymore." Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. I don't see that level of personal, visceral problem ownership here.

Andustry's competitive landscape actively concerns me. Xometry is public with $687M in FY2025 revenue and acquired Thomasnet for $300M, giving it 500k+ sellers. Fictiv was acquired by MISUMI for $350M. Laserhub raised a double-digit-million Series B focused on European metal parts. Multiple AI procurement startups -- Leverage AI, Zip, Fairmarkit -- already exist. The dossier argues that Xometry faces channel conflict in adding a pure sourcing mode, which is a reasonable structural argument. But "AI to source and sell industrial products" does not make anyone say "that will never work." It makes them say "oh, that makes sense" -- which in my framework is the worst possible reaction. The best startup ideas sit at the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Andustry sits squarely in the "seems like a good idea and is probably a good idea" quadrant, which means the returns have been competed away before Andustry even starts. When I funded Coinbase, people thought cryptocurrency was a joke. Nobody thinks AI procurement is a joke. They think it's obvious. That's the problem.

The strongest bull case for Andustry runs like this: cross-border industrial procurement for SMEs is a massive schlep that venture-backed competitors avoid because it's messy, low-margin, and requires navigating tariffs across 100+ countries. Xometry and Fictiv chose to be the manufacturer-of-record precisely because that model is cleaner. Andustry is the only company willing to be a pure sourcing marketplace in this space, absorbing the regulatory complexity that repels others. Johann's family literally did this work, making the problem discovery organic. The €1M+ in beta transactions proves real buyer demand. And the pay-on-delivery model with no upfront fees removes adoption friction. This is a ClassDojo-style exception -- an unsexy domain where the schlep itself is the moat. I respect this argument. The schlep is real. But ClassDojo's founders demonstrated exceptional execution quality that overcame the sector preference, and I see no comparable evidence of execution intensity from Andustry's founders. What would flip me: if Johann and Het could tell me specific stories of creative workarounds -- how they onboarded their first 50 suppliers one by one, what manual processes they performed before automating them, how they handled a cross-border delivery that went sideways. The stories are the X-ray. Without them, I'm reading a resume and a website, not evaluating founders.

Andustry lands in neutral territory because the schlep is genuine but the founder signal is ambiguous. Johann Stürken's family trading background (organic discovery, positive) is offset by his McKinsey career path (framework-driven thinking, negative). Het Dave's technical contribution lacks visible artifacts. The €1M+ beta transaction volume (resourcefulness, positive) is offset by the absence of any specific stories of constraint-driven creativity. And the idea itself -- AI-driven procurement matching -- lives in a validated, well-funded category that generates consensus enthusiasm rather than the skepticism I look for. The two dimensions that most determined this score are Organic Problem Discovery (where Johann's family background elevates Andustry above a pure-consulting origin but doesn't reach the level of "I lived in the future and built what was missing") and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (where multiple well-funded competitors confirm this idea sounds good to everyone, which is exactly when I get nervous).
