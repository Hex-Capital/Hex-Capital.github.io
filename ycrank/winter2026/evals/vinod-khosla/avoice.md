# Avoice -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first: a team of smart people has looked at the architecture industry -- $370 billion in services, 110,000+ firms, crushing documentation burden -- and decided the highest-value thing to build is a tool that helps architects write specs faster and source materials more efficiently. They've explicitly chosen not to touch design. They've positioned themselves as "Harvey for Architecture," which sounds impressive until you realize Harvey works because BigLaw firms bill $1,500/hour and drown in millions of pages of text that LLMs were born to process. Architecture firms bill $150-300/hour, operate on thin margins, and their core deliverables are spatial and visual, not textual. The analogy flatters the positioning but obscures the economics.

The consequence of success here is what stops me cold. If Avoice captures the entire market -- every architecture firm in the world uses it -- you get a productivity SaaS business saving architects 13 hours a week on documentation. That's genuinely useful. Nobody's denying the pain is real. But the world doesn't operate differently. Buildings don't get cheaper to construct. The 39% of global carbon emissions from the built environment doesn't change. Housing doesn't become more affordable. The $13 trillion global construction industry continues largely as-is, except the paperwork preceding it gets done faster. I've spent forty years separating problems that are important enough to justify the effort from problems that produce a nice business. This is the latter. When someone tells me their ambition is to be a tool *for* the incumbents rather than a replacement *of* the incumbent system, they've told me the ceiling.

Chawin's entrepreneurial track record is genuinely impressive for pre-seed -- Yindii reaching 700K users and profitability, multiple ventures across geographies, an edtech exit at $1M+. He's an outsider to architecture, which I normally prize. But the pattern of his career reveals something: he builds solid, execution-oriented businesses in underserved verticals. Surplus food, edtech, coatings, now architectural documentation. Each one a real market with real pain and a sensible business. None of them an attempt at something the consensus would call crazy. He spent two years building architecture sales teams before founding Avoice -- that's diligent market research, not first-principles reimagination of how the built environment gets created. The CTO's background is largely opaque, which for a product requiring both multimodal AI sophistication and deep architectural domain knowledge is a gap I'd want addressed.

The bull case deserves honest engagement: architecture is genuinely underserved by software. Revit is universally hated. The firm-specific learning loop -- where Avoice's Studio Library absorbs each firm's standards, annotations, and material preferences -- could create real switching costs over time. The timing of multimodal AI crossing the threshold for drawing comprehension is a legitimate tailwind. And if Avoice evolves from documentation tool to true "operating system for architectural practice," the expansion surface into construction coordination, permitting, and project delivery could multiply the consequence. The $300M in active projects on the platform suggests real adoption is happening. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe the documentation layer is a wedge into a much larger transformation of how buildings actually get delivered -- that controlling the information flow between design and construction eventually lets you restructure the entire delivery chain. That's a path I could imagine, but it's not the path they're articulating.

What concerns me most is the competitive landscape already validates the approach. Swapp AI has $21.3M. Monograph has $20M. Cove.tool has $36.6M. Togal.AI has $22.65M. When nobody laughs at your idea, when well-funded competitors are already building adjacent products, when Autodesk sits above you with dominant distribution and a financial incentive to add AI features to Revit -- you're not skating to where the puck will be. You're skating to where everyone already is. My best investments -- Juniper, Impossible, OpenAI -- were in spaces where the experts actively dismissed the possibility. Nobody is dismissing "AI for architecture documentation." They're funding it. That's a signal I take seriously.

This is a competent team building a useful product in a real market. If I were optimizing for probability of success, this would be attractive -- real pain, reasonable approach, demonstrated execution ability. But I don't optimize for probability of success. I optimize for magnitude of consequence. And the magnitude here, even in the best case, is a well-run vertical SaaS business with solid retention in a fragmented market of budget-constrained small firms. These founders are too capable to spend their careers on a problem this bounded.
