# REV1 -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most striking fact about REV1 isn't the founders or the technology -- it's that DraftAid, a YC W24 company, is building the identical product with a two-year head start, claiming 210,000+ drawings generated and $150K in paid pilots. Meanwhile, SolidWorks shipped a beta Auto-Generate Drawing feature in its 2025/2026 release. Leo AI raised $9.7M for a broader mechanical engineering AI copilot. When four separate teams -- including the dominant incumbent -- converge on the same sub-task simultaneously, you're not looking at a contrarian bet. You're looking at consensus dressed up as innovation. I've learned the hard way that "anyone in this business who's chasing hot markets gets killed." The asymmetric upside I look for requires being right when most people think you're wrong. Here, most people think this is right, and they're already building it.

Alex Rivero's specific knowledge is genuine but not rare. He's designed hardware at Tesla and Apple, created manufacturing drawings by hand, felt the tedium of GD&T annotation across hundreds of parts. That's real pain. But it's also pain that every one of the 52 million CAD users worldwide has felt. The insight -- "these drawings should be automated" -- is available to anyone who's spent six months as a mechanical engineer. Compare this to Evan Williams spending a decade on Blogger before understanding what Twitter needed to be, or Ivan Zhao's years of obsessive thinking about the relationship between tools and thought. Rivero is a competent engineer who noticed a workflow inefficiency. That's a good product idea. It's not the kind of non-transferable knowledge that creates founder-market fit so deep that competitors can't close the gap.

The technical pairing works on paper. Louis Liu solo-built Tesla's global Supercharger and Robotaxi planning platform, has published ML research, and brings AWS-level infrastructure experience. A domain-expert mechanical engineer plus an ML-capable systems builder is exactly the team you'd design for this problem. That's the good news. The bad news is it's exactly the team DraftAid also assembled, and probably the team Leo AI has at 10x the funding. The technologist requirement is met -- Louis is clearly a builder, not a business guy with outsourced code. But having the right team composition isn't a differentiator when your competitor has the same composition and more runway.

The leverage question is where this falls apart for me. REV1 saves engineers 25% of their time on drawing creation. That's efficiency -- getting the same output faster. It's not leverage in the sense I care about. Notion didn't just save people time filing documents; it gave one person the organizational capacity of a team. Replit didn't speed up coding; it made coding possible for people who couldn't code before. REV1 makes a tedious task less tedious. The output is the same 2D drawing that would have been created anyway. There are no network effects -- one customer's drawings don't make the product better for another customer. There's no protocol or platform dynamic. It's a point solution for a sub-task, and point solutions get absorbed by platforms. SolidWorks adding this natively is not a threat on the horizon; it's a threat in beta today.

The bull case I'd need to believe: that GD&T accuracy is so safety-critical in aerospace and medical devices that a standalone tool with proven correctness will command enterprise pricing and switching costs that incumbents can't match. That PLM integration depth creates lock-in. That the multi-CAD angle -- serving companies using SolidWorks and Siemens NX and PTC Creo simultaneously -- is a structural wedge no incumbent will fill because they won't serve competitors' ecosystems. And that the proprietary training data from customer-specific annotation conventions compounds into a moat over time. If all of this is true, REV1 could become the picks-and-shovels play for hardware documentation the way Alchemy became infrastructure for blockchain developers. But none of it is evidenced yet, and the DraftAid head start means someone else is accumulating that data first.

I respect the founders for leaving Tesla and Apple to build this. The co-founder relationship forged on Robotaxi projects is real, not manufactured. But when I look at my own portfolio, the bets that generated 100x returns were companies that created entirely new categories of leverage -- Twitter compressed public thought into a new medium, Uber turned every car into a taxi, CoinList created permissionless token distribution. REV1 automates a well-understood sub-task in a market where the problem is already being solved by multiple funded competitors. The failure mode here isn't that the product doesn't work; it's that it works fine but never becomes more than a feature.
