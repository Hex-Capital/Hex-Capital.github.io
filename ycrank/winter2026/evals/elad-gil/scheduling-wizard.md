# Scheduling Wizard -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Scheduling Wizard is the business model choice, and it cuts both ways. They're not selling scheduling software -- they're outsourcing the entire scheduling process from hospital departments. This is counterintuitive. Every other company in the space (QGenda, Amion, Lightning Bolt, YouShift) sells a tool and lets the customer operate it. Scheduling Wizard takes in constraints and hands back completed schedules. For overworked residency program directors spending hundreds of hours annually on this, the value proposition is obvious. The question is whether this is a software-driven company using a service wrapper as a distribution strategy -- the way Stripe's developer relations team is a service layer on top of an API -- or whether it's a consulting operation with a scheduling tool. A smart toaster is just a toaster, and a smart scheduling consultancy is just a consultancy. The "Scheduling Programming Language" could resolve this question, but the dossier doesn't provide enough evidence to determine how much of the work is automated versus manually performed by three recent graduates.

On the market's structural moment, I'm not finding what I need. Physician scheduling has been broken for decades. The existing solutions are either cheap and legacy (Amion at $449/year) or enterprise-scale and expensive (QGenda, acquired for $2.5B). The dossier's "why now" leans on two inferred signals -- LLM advances making constraint-solving more accessible, and post-COVID burnout increasing demand. Neither is sourced. QGenda's $2.5B exit by Hearst validates market value but doesn't create a structural opening -- if anything, it signals consolidation at the enterprise tier, which compresses the space for new entrants. The medical scheduling market growing at 13% CAGR is steady, not inflecting. I'm looking for the equivalent of what I saw with Harvey -- a technology capability crossing a specific threshold that unlocks a market that was previously impossible. Constraint optimization in scheduling is a well-established field in operations research; this isn't a case where GPT-4 suddenly made something possible that wasn't before. The market fits the "too boring" archetype I like -- physician scheduling triggers the same dismissive reaction that payroll triggered before Gusto -- but boring alone doesn't do it. Gusto's structural moment was the shift to cloud-based HR and the explosion of small business formation. I need the inflection, not just the boredom.

The traction deserves genuine credit. Nineteen departments across fourteen hospitals -- including Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, HCA Healthcare, and UCSF -- is remarkable for a pre-seed company founded by three 2024 JHU graduates. That's not a single-institution proof of concept; it's multi-system adoption with reference-quality brand names. The founding team's origin within Johns Hopkins gave them a natural entry point, but expanding to Mass General and UCSF required selling beyond their home network. Oberly's background in applied mathematics from Cambridge, DoD war games modeling, and Minor League Baseball scheduling is precisely the domain expertise you'd want for constraint optimization. The product-to-distribution trajectory has some promise: the YC page mentions expansion into patient triaging with UCSF and broader "provider management systems," which suggests the team sees scheduling as a wedge into hospital operations infrastructure. Land-and-expand within hospital systems -- from one department to adjacent departments, from scheduling to other operational workflows -- is a viable distribution playbook. But the managed-service model creates friction here: adding products means adding operational complexity, not just shipping features.

The bull case would require believing three things. First, that the Scheduling Programming Language and AI workflows automate enough of the scheduling process that the managed service scales sublinearly with departments -- that going from 19 to 190 departments doesn't require 10x the team. Second, that the managed-service model is a trojan horse: once you're producing schedules for a department, you become embedded infrastructure that enables expanding into adjacent hospital operations (patient flow, staffing optimization, capacity planning) -- turning a single-product service into a platform. Third, that QGenda's absorption by Hearst creates a strategic vacuum at the department level, where mid-market academic programs feel underserved by the enterprise platform's complexity and overserved by Amion's simplicity. If all three are true, this is the Gusto pattern: boring market, deep embedding, multi-product expansion. But that's approaching the multiple-miracles territory I try to avoid. The scalability question alone is a real unknown, and the competitive threat from YouShift building a self-serve version with Bain Capital backing adds pressure from a model that scales more naturally.

The technology cycle positioning is the weakest dimension. I'd want to hear the founders articulate specifically what became possible in 2024-2025 that wasn't possible in 2020. If the answer is "LLMs help us build faster," that's a productivity tool, not a structural enabler. If the answer is "our Scheduling Programming Language encodes constraint logic that would have taken a 20-person engineering team to build five years ago, and modern AI capabilities let three people match that output," that's more compelling -- but it's not in the dossier. Without a clear technology inflection driving the timing, this looks like a team that found a real pain point through proximity to Johns Hopkins and built a useful service around it, which is respectable but isn't the structural-moment pattern that generates my highest conviction bets.
