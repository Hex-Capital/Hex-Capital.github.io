# Quanto -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Quanto has $800K in contracted ARR at pre-seed. That number stopped me. Most companies at this stage are showing me slide decks with projected revenue curves — Anderson Petergeorge is showing me signed contracts. He scaled his own bookkeeping practice to 40 clients in six months as a solo CPA before deciding to build the product, and that sequence matters: the product grew out of the practice, not the other way around. His parents are Sri Lankan refugees. He was the first in his family to go to college. There's a forge there — resourcefulness born from real constraint. But when I sit with it, Anderson's connection to accounting is professional, not existential. He chose to become a CPA at KPMG and CPP Investments; the CPA shortage didn't find him through the kind of biographical collision that I look for in my strongest bets. He experienced capacity constraints in his own firm and built a tool to fix them. That's genuine founder-market fit. It's not biographical inevitability.

Kajanth Nithiyananthan rounds out the team with the execution credibility Quanto needs — leading a 60-person org at SkipTheDishes, growing merchants at Shopify, and founding Atomic Build, a seven-figure product studio specifically building AI for accounting firms. He's been in this problem space from the technical side before Quanto existed. The co-founders don't share a prior employer or university, which means this partnership was likely forged through Toronto's startup ecosystem or Kajanth's accounting AI studio work. The pairing is complementary: Anderson brings the CPA network and lived operator pain, Kajanth brings product leadership at scale. I don't see a 50/50 split or unclear CEO dynamic — Anderson is clearly the business lead, Kajanth the technical lead. That governance clarity matters to me after HQ Trivia.

Here's where Quanto loses me as a Long Journey bet: AI for accounting firms is one of the most consensus-attractive spaces in venture right now. Basis has raised $138M at a $1.15B valuation and already serves 30% of the top 25 U.S. accounting firms. Digits has $95M in funding and $29.9M in 2024 revenue. Docyt has $27.2M. When I look at a space and see that much capital flowing in, with no investor discomfort, no regulatory stigma, no social squeamishness — I know this isn't where I add value. Nobody is shifting in their chair when you pitch "AI makes accounting firms more efficient." Compare this to when I wrote my Flock Safety check after noticing a founder's WiFi name at a YC event — surveillance cameras that made civil liberties advocates nervous. Or Contraline, where male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Quanto is solving a real problem, but it's solving it in a room where every other investor is already reaching for their checkbook.

The invisible system test is what separates Quanto from the companies in my portfolio that I feel deepest conviction about. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was making credit card opacity visible — showing consumers the actual cost of borrowing that issuers deliberately obscured. Carta made cap table information accessible to founders and employees who couldn't see their own equity. These companies redistributed information power from gatekeepers to the people being gatekept. Quanto's QuickBooks Intelligence Diagnostics — the 0-to-100 book health score, the detection of unreconciled transactions — does create transparency, but for the accounting firm, not for the small business owner trapped in an opaque professional services relationship. Quanto makes the intermediary more efficient. It doesn't disrupt the intermediary on behalf of the person who depends on it. That's a meaningful business. It's not a structural power shift.

The strongest bull case for Quanto would require me to see it differently: not as an AI wrapper for accounting workflows, but as emerging infrastructure for how accounting capacity gets allocated in an era of CPA shortage. Botkeeper's 2026 shutdown creates a specific customer acquisition window — displaced firms looking for exactly this tool. The bundling of front-office prospecting with back-office onboarding is genuinely differentiated from competitors who do one or the other. And if the data from firm onboarding workflows compounds into proprietary benchmarking and pricing intelligence, the switching costs deepen with every client. At $800K ARR, Anderson has already demonstrated that accounting firm owners will pay for this. But I keep coming back to the competitive dynamic: Basis at $1.15B is expanding downmarket, Digits at $29.9M revenue recently launched AI agents for accounting workflows, and Quanto's core integration sits on top of QuickBooks — meaning Intuit itself is the ultimate platform risk. The individual components are technically reproducible by any team with sufficient capital, and Quanto's competitors have 100x to 200x more of it.

The dimension that locked my verdict is Contrarian Conviction. My biggest wins — SpaceX, Anduril, Crusoe, Flock Safety — came from spaces where I was often the only investor willing to write a check, and the discomfort of other investors was itself a signal that something structurally important was happening beneath the surface. Quanto operates in the opposite environment: a well-funded, consensus-attractive market where the main risk isn't that you're too early or too weird, but that you're one of many well-executed entrants racing toward the same mid-market customer. Anderson's biographical resilience and genuine domain experience earn Quanto a score above the floor of my framework, and that $800K ARR commands respect. But I invest my own money where I feel the kind of conviction that comes from seeing something others refuse to see — and Quanto's thesis is visible to everyone.
