# Tenet Industries -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Helsing has raised €1.37B. Stark Defence has ~$100M and Peter Thiel on the cap table. White Stork has Eric Schmidt. Tenet Industries is four people in Stockholm with a YC badge and a thesis about automating drone factories. The timing argument for expendable strike munitions is as strong as any I've seen — Ukraine consumes thousands of drones monthly, Sweden allocated $440M for unmanned combat systems through 2028, Germany awarded ~€900M in drone brigade contracts — but strong timing in a space where your best-funded competitor has 2,700x your capital is a different proposition than strong timing where you're the first check in. When I wrote my check into Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley. Palmer Luckey was practically radioactive. That was contrarian. In 2026, after Helsing's €12B valuation and every European VC scrambling for defense exposure, building strike drones is consensus. Tenet Industries is entering a space that no longer makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs — it makes them reach for their checkbooks.

Emil Falk is the founder who catches my attention. He built WindowGlass Recycling, won Venture Cup's Startup of the Year in 2023, and deployed production machines at four Ragn-Sells facilities by autumn 2024. That's not a slide deck — that's hardware shipped into commercial environments, which is the hardest thing a young founder can demonstrate. Tenet Industries' thesis — design the drone around the factory, not the reverse — is precisely the kind of insight that comes from someone who has wrestled with automated manufacturing firsthand. Hugo Frisk is a self-taught electronics designer, which resonates with me personally; I built my career as a self-taught engineer. But when I ask my first question — "tell me their story, not the business" — I don't hear why these three specific people MUST build weapons. Falk's forge is in manufacturing automation, not in defense. No one on this team has a visible biographical connection to warfare, military service, or the defense procurement system they're trying to disrupt. The skills transfer is real; the biographical inevitability is absent. Tenet Industries has founders who CAN build this — I'm not convinced they're founders who can't NOT build this.

Tenet Industries' "design-for-automation" platform across multiple airframe types — FPV quadcopters, fixed-wing, Shahed-type, missile-class — reminds me structurally of what Crusoe Energy was doing when I first encountered them: building portable infrastructure that could be deployed flexibly across contexts. Crusoe looked like a Bitcoin mining company but was secretly an energy infrastructure company. Tenet Industries looks like a drone company but could secretly be a munitions manufacturing infrastructure company. If the platform genuinely enables rapid production of different airframe types from a single automated line, each government contract and each new form factor strengthens the manufacturing IP. That's a compounding moat. But Crusoe had something Tenet Industries doesn't yet have: a genuinely novel insight that no one else was pursuing (stranded gas flares as compute fuel). Automated drone manufacturing is being pursued by Rheinmetall (targeting 500-1,000 FV-014 units per month), by Helsing (delivering hundreds of HX-2 monthly to Ukraine), and by dozens of Ukrainian startups that are battle-tested in ways Tenet Industries is not. The manufacturing platform thesis is unproven, and no patents or proprietary processes are publicly documented.

The defense procurement system is, in fact, one of the most opaque and extractive structures I know of — legacy primes charging $50,000+ per Switchblade when a $500 kamikaze quadcopter can accomplish similar battlefield objectives. That markup is a hidden tax on national security, paid by taxpayers who have no visibility into why their governments are buying hundred-thousand-dollar munitions when the war in Ukraine proved that cheap, expendable drones are what attrition warfare actually demands. Tenet Industries could be making that invisible system visible by demonstrating that mass-produced strike drones at radically lower price points are not just viable but superior for modern combat. Sweden's defense minister calling drone warfare "central to modern combat" validates the structural shift. But Tenet Industries doesn't frame itself this way — the messaging is "drones as ammunition," which is a product description, not a system-disruption thesis. The invisible system is there, but the founders haven't articulated that they see it, which makes me wonder if they're building toward cost disruption of procurement or simply building another drone company in a hot market.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Emil Falk is a proven hardware-manufacturing founder entering the highest-conviction inflection point in European defense since the Cold War. Sweden's NATO membership (March 2024) gives Tenet Industries home-field advantage in a country actively spending $440M on exactly what they're building. The cost tier is structurally different from Helsing and Stark — those companies are building expensive, AI-heavy platforms, while Tenet Industries is targeting the high-volume, low-cost segment that Ukraine's consumption patterns demand at thousands of units monthly. This is infrastructure at an inflection, my highest-conviction pattern, and the manufacturing-automation thesis is the kind of unsexy, process-level moat that compounds invisibly while everyone else chases the AI-autonomy narrative. An advocate would point to my RTFKT exception — cultural inflection points can substitute for infrastructure moats when the timing window is genuinely narrow — and argue that Europe's defense rearmament window IS that narrow. That's a real argument. But RTFKT was a $8M seed in a space with no well-capitalized incumbents racing to the same prize. Tenet Industries faces Helsing at €1.37B, Stark at ~$100M with Sequoia and In-Q-Tel backing, and Rheinmetall pivoting its industrial might toward exactly this market segment. The timing window exists, but it's crowded with competitors who arrived earlier and better-funded.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: a signed LOI or evaluation contract with the Swedish Armed Forces or another NATO defense ministry. That would demonstrate that Tenet Industries can navigate procurement bureaucracy, that their manufacturing thesis is credible to military buyers, and that being a Swedish NATO company provides the home-field advantage the bull case claims. Without any traction signal — no revenue, no contracts, no customer announcements, no battlefield validation — I'm evaluating a thesis about a thesis. The manufacturing-automation platform could be transformative, but I need evidence that it exists beyond the YC company page description. Tenet Industries sits at the intersection of a real inflection and a real capitalization problem, and the founders — while skilled — haven't shown me the biographical forge that tells me they'll survive the valley of death that a company this undercapitalized in this competitive a landscape will inevitably face.
