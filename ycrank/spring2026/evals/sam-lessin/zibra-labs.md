# Zibra Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a credible substrate-deep infra play aimed at a thirty-account market with zero founder distribution — its highest dimension (capital structure that genuinely is venture-shaped, because each quant-fund contract is six-to-seven figures) collides with its lowest (Ibrahim Rabbani has five GitHub repos and no findable Twitter, Zac Policzer the same), and that pairing is the whole evaluation. This is the inverse of every deal I actually write checks into: the structure works and the people-as-asset doesn't.

On the round itself I want to put **Sublime Security** at the high rung — pre-consensus enterprise security before the consolidation wave, a category most security investors had abandoned to late-stage SOC SaaS. Zibra Labs sits below Sublime and above my canonical Pass Example because "HPC for quant backtesting" is a genuinely unsexy niche the YC consensus is not pattern-matching to in Spring 2026 (Tom Blomfield as group partner, no press, no oversubscription signal), but the dossier gives me no round size, no price, no list of tier-1 passes — I'm applying the absent-evidence default and slightly bumping it because the category itself is illegible enough that I doubt four tier-1s are bidding for a quant-HPC seed. The divergence from Sublime: Sublime had a buyer narrative ("the next email-security consolidation"); Zibra Labs has Anyscale ($281M raised, $1B valuation) sitting directly upstream as the obvious bear, which compresses the legibility-translation story I'd later have to broker to a Series A buyer.

The Pass Example failure mechanism — "AI agent for enterprise vertical, no public writing, no community footprint, founder responding to opportunity rather than pursuing a personal mission" — is exactly the pattern I now have to test Zibra Labs against on the founder-as-brand dimension. Rabbani and Policzer have substrate-level domain credibility (Ray Core tech-lead, LinkedIn Venice/Liquid/Espresso Senior Staff) but the dossier flags "Twitter: No public account found" twice and "GitHub: 5 public repositories, no notable starred repo." There is no newsletter, no Discord, no opinionated long-form post about why Ray's scheduler is wrong for backtest sweeps. Writing is thinking, and I cannot find the writing. The Ray ecosystem is a distribution proxy — Bridgewater, P72, Man Group, Two Sigma are Ray users, not Zibra customers, and the dossier is explicit about that distinction — which means cold founder-led outbound via cal.com is the actual GTM, not owned community.

Capital structure is where Zibra Labs actually scores well, and the mechanism analysis is straightforward: multi-cloud spot orchestration sold to quant funds at consumption pricing produces whale-customer economics where two or three named hedge-fund logos at $1-3M ARR each get you to default-alive without five rounds of dilution. That is a venture-shaped curve, not a SaaS treadmill. The eighteen-month compounding question is whether workload telemetry across 6.4M-task fan-outs becomes a scheduler-learning moat or just gets re-implemented by Anyscale's vertical team — the former is a venture moment, the latter is a feature.

Counterfactually, the founder-conviction score flips a tier the moment Rabbani publishes one opinionated essay titled something like "Why Ray Core's scheduler is wrong for quant backtest sweeps" — he is literally the person qualified to write it, and its absence at the YC announcement is the diagnostic. The asymmetric-conviction filter isn't asking for marketing; it's asking whether the founder has a non-obvious belief they cannot be talked out of. The dossier shows depth without published opinion, which is the exact missionary-vs-mercenary read I run on every founder.

On category sleeve, the single-anchor parallel is **Sublime Security** again — enterprise infra for a specialized buyer set, no crypto sleeve, no DTC sleeve, no creator sleeve. Zibra Labs lives in the "niche enterprise infra that doesn't fit my pre-convicted lanes but isn't the AI-agent-for-vertical commodity exposure I explicitly de-rate either." The "AI infrastructure" tag in the dossier is misleading — this is HPC-for-finance, not foundation-model anything — which protects it from my "AI is just more internet" deselection. Mid-low sleeve fit, not a red flag.

My 36-month forecast: Zibra Labs reaches $3-8M ARR with two to four mid-tier quant fund customers (not Two Sigma, not Bridgewater — those build in-house) and gets acqui-hired by Anyscale or absorbed into AWS's HPC org for a modest premium that returns the seed but doesn't return the fund. The producing mechanism is the signature pairing: the structure can carry whale economics, but without founder distribution or public conviction the team has to cold-close eight of roughly thirty addressable buyers against an upstream incumbent ($281M Anyscale war chest) who employs the CEO's former colleagues.

The strongest bull case stings: Rabbani didn't just use Ray, he was tech-lead on Ray Core, and that's a relationship asset — the warm intro graph into Bridgewater, P72, and Man Group runs through him personally, and one $5M ARR contract from a single named fund inside twelve months would invalidate my entire "no distribution" read because in this market distribution IS the operator-friend channel, not Twitter followers. That's the **Ben Silbermann at Pinterest** override pattern from my Known Exceptions — operator-network sourcing overriding the public-conviction filter. I dismantle it on the dossier's own caveat: Ray users are not Zibra customers, and Anyscale is the structurally better-positioned vendor of that exact relationship. The condition that flips my verdict: a signed contract or LOI with a named tier-1 quant fund at $1M+ ARR disclosed within six months of YC Demo Day. Until then, pass — but the kind of pass where I'd take the meeting if Rabbani emailed me directly.
