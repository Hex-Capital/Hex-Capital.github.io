# Piris Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me first: Ali Khalatpour built the first room-temperature terahertz semiconductor laser. He led the optical engine for NASA's GUSTO mission. Sixteen published research works, 572 citations. That's not a resume you build by accident — that's a life organized around photons. And Keyvan Moghadam comes from the other side of the equation, having built AI infrastructure at Meta and X from zero to one. On paper, this is one of the most technically credentialed founding teams I've seen at pre-seed. The combination of a world-class optical physicist and a scaled AI infrastructure engineer is almost surgically designed for the problem of photonic interconnects for inference. If I were evaluating purely on founder-market fit in the narrow technical sense, I'd be reaching for my checkbook.

But I can't get past what I don't know. My first question to any founder is always about their story — not the technology, not the market, but how they got here. What's the path that makes Ali the person who HAS to solve this problem, not just the person who CAN? A decade in photonics labs at MIT, Harvard, and Stanford tells me he's brilliant. It doesn't tell me what keeps him going at 2am when the prototype fails and the Series A isn't closing. I've learned that academic credentials predict capability but not resilience under startup duress. The dossier gives me their CVs but not their narratives, and without that biographical thread connecting founder to mission, my primary evaluation channel is blocked. It's like trying to read a room through frosted glass.

The competitive landscape here genuinely concerns me, and this is where the Crusoe Energy comparison breaks down in a way that matters. When we backed Crusoe, nobody else was doing what they were doing — mining Bitcoin on flared gas sounded absurd, and they had the space to themselves. Piris Labs is entering a category where Lightmatter has raised $850 million, Ayar Labs just closed $500 million at a $3.8 billion valuation, and Marvell paid $3.25 billion to acquire Celestial AI. NVIDIA is pouring $4 billion into photonic manufacturing capacity. The thesis that copper interconnects are hitting physical limits is no longer contrarian — it's the consensus view among the largest semiconductor companies on Earth. What's differentiated is Piris Labs' full-stack inference service approach versus the component-level plays everyone else is running, and that IS interesting. But "interesting differentiation within a consensus category" is fundamentally different from the non-obvious, unpopular bets that tend to work for me.

Let me steelman the bull case, because it deserves honest engagement. If Khalatpour can deliver on those performance claims — 5x lower latency, 10x lower power per bit, 2x lower cost per token — and if the full-stack vertical integration creates genuine switching costs once customers are onboarded, this could be a company that the Lightmatters and Ayar Labs of the world can't easily replicate. The component players don't have the inference software; the software players don't have the photonics. That compound moat, hardware co-designed with software, is the kind of defensibility I look for. And the timing is undeniably right — $400-450 billion in global data center capex projected for 2026, trillion-parameter models making the memory wall commercially acute. If this team can demonstrate a working system at even modest scale, the fundraising environment for photonic inference is extraordinarily favorable right now. YC gives them the network to raise serious capital fast. The path from $500K to a real Series A exists if the technology works.

But that path requires the technology to work at production scale, not just lab scale, and it requires navigating it with a two-person team against competitors who have hundreds of engineers and billions in capital. This is the kind of bet where I'd need to sit across from Ali and Keyvan, hear how they found each other, understand what drives them beyond intellectual fascination, and feel whether there's that jolt of energy I trust. Without that meeting, what I see is a technically impressive but fundamentally consensus-category infrastructure play that doesn't activate the patterns I've trained myself to follow. It doesn't create obvious economic access for underserved populations. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to people who need transparency. It's solving an important engineering problem for AI companies that already have enormous budgets. That's valuable work, but it's not the kind of bet where I have edge.

The co-founder dynamic is another open question. No shared institutional background, no visible history of working together before founding. After HQ Trivia, I probe how co-founders handle conflict before I write a check. Hardware startups face brutal stress — manufacturing delays, capital raises that take longer than expected, pivots when the first architecture doesn't scale. I'd want to understand how these two navigate disagreement, especially given the inherent tension between the hardware-first and software-first perspectives they each bring.
