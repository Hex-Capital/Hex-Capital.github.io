# Pocket -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Pocket is the traction. $27M annualized revenue and 30,000 devices shipped in five months -- at a company that just entered YC W26. Most pre-seed companies are showing me a prototype and a handful of beta users. Akshay Narisetti is showing me a working hardware business with real customers buying a physical product. Even if you discount the ARR figure as an optimistic annualization of their best month, the underlying numbers are hard to fake. Thirty thousand people paid real money for a physical device. That's not a TAM slide. That's demand.

How did they find this problem? This is where it gets interesting. Akshay didn't sit down one day and think "AI wearables are hot, let me build one." He built Omi first -- an open-source AI wearable that attracted 7,800 GitHub stars and 1,400 forks. The commercial product grew out of a community that already wanted this thing. That's the organic pattern. It's the same trajectory as Wozniak building computers for the Homebrew Computer Club before Apple existed. The product preceded the business plan. When I see a founder who built the open-source version for free because he was genuinely fascinated by the problem, and then thousands of people showed up wanting it -- that's not a manufactured startup idea. That's discovery.

The schlep here is real but underappreciated. Most software founders look at hardware and unconsciously filter it out. Building a physical device means dealing with manufacturing, supply chain, firmware, Bluetooth pairing failures, returns, and all the unglamorous work that makes consumer electronics hard. Then add HIPAA compliance for healthcare, multi-platform app development, and privacy regulation across jurisdictions. This is a compound schlep -- not one hard thing but six hard things layered on top of each other. The Trustpilot complaints about undelivered orders and sync issues aren't red flags to me in the way they'd be for a mature company. They're evidence of the schlep. You can't ship 30,000 hardware devices in five months without things going wrong. The question is whether the founders fix the problems, and the 4.8 rating on iOS (747 reviews) suggests most users are happy with the actual product.

My biggest concern is the competitive landscape. This is a hot category. Plaud is bootstrapped and printing money at a $250M ARR trajectory. Anker has entered. Meta bought Limitless, Amazon bought Bee. When I said in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies weren't working on AI, this is partly what I meant -- consensus categories attract crowding. But there's a counterargument that changes my calculus: the acquisitions actually cleared the independent field. Limitless and Bee are gone as standalone products. Plaud is China-based and bootstrapped. For a US-based, HIPAA-compliant, YC-backed alternative, the competitive dynamics have actually improved through consolidation. Pocket didn't enter a crowded market -- the market crowded around them and then two of the three competitors got absorbed. That's a structural advantage, not a structural risk.

Akshay is 23 years old, graduated from Georgia Tech in 2024, built a viral Arduino project that got 19.9 million views, turned down a Google offer, got selected for the Harvard/MIT Augmentation Lab (1 of 16 globally), and publicly declined acquisition offers for Pocket. This is a hacker who builds things because building things is what he does. His GitHub profile, his GSoC participation, his personal projects -- OCR scanners in Kotlin, Rubik's Cube solvers -- all point to someone who would be making things whether or not there was a company attached. The co-founder situation is the one structural worry: Gabriel Dymowski comes from blockchain and enterprise consulting, not hardware or consumer products, and they met through On Deck rather than through years of working together. Gabriel's fundraising experience (EUR 2.2M for DoxyChain) and enterprise instincts are valuable for the HIPAA and teams sales motion, but this is a recent partnership and the pairing hasn't been tested by adversity yet.

The bull case for Pocket is that they've already crossed the threshold from "interesting project" to "real business" at a speed that rivals the best hardware companies I've seen. If they can solve the quality and fulfillment problems at scale, maintain their growth trajectory even at a slower rate, and defend their position against Plaud through vertical differentiation (healthcare, enterprise, US data residency), this could be a very large company. The subscription economics layer recurring revenue on top of hardware sales, and the personal conversation archive creates genuine switching costs over time. The commoditization risk from Chinese manufacturers is real, but Plaud itself proves that the category leader doesn't need patents to win -- execution, brand trust, and software quality matter more than hardware IP.

I'd invest. The organic discovery through Omi, the compound hardware-software-regulatory schlep, the genuine hacker founder, and the extraordinary early traction all fit my framework. The competitive dynamics are the main risk, but the market structure has actually shifted in Pocket's favor through consolidation. This isn't a contrarian bet -- it's an obvious problem that most founders avoided because the schlep of building hardware scared them off. Akshay didn't avoid it because he was already there.
