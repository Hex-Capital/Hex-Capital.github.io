# AutoSitu -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about AutoSitu is that five funded competitors are already building AI plan review tools, PermitFlow has raised $91M, Blitz AI claims to be the "most widely adopted" platform in local government, and CivCheck already got acquired. When incumbents and capital markets have already validated a category, the contrarian signal I look for -- experts dismissing the idea, outsiders not yet crowding in -- is entirely absent. This is consensus investing territory. Every VC in the country has seen the "AI for permitting" pitch by now. That doesn't mean the market isn't real. It means the opportunity to build something category-defining, where the money either expires or you build a transformational company, has been substantially narrowed by competitive density.

Let me address consequence magnitude. If AutoSitu succeeds completely -- becomes the standard AI review layer across all 19,000 U.S. municipalities -- what changes? Permits get processed faster. That matters for housing supply, and the housing crisis is genuinely important. But the company has explicitly positioned itself as a "copilot" where "final discretionary decisions and subjective design reviews remain with qualified human professionals." They're optimizing an existing bureaucratic process, not replacing it. The consequence of full success is that the same system runs 50-70% more efficiently. Compare that to what I look for: technologies that make the incumbent approach structurally obsolete. A faster version of the same municipal review process is not the same as reinventing how buildings get approved. The housing crisis needs a 10x expansion in supply, which requires zoning reform, by-right permitting, and regulatory restructuring -- not marginally faster reviews within the existing framework.

The founding team is competent but presents the exact pattern I'm wary of. Xuanshu Lin is a domain expert -- Urban Planning at Michigan, Harvard GSD, planning competition awards. He knows what the municipal review process looks like from inside. George Zhai brings serious AI engineering credentials from GM and McLaren with seven patents. This is a strong execution team. But they're industry insiders building a better version of the industry's existing workflow. Lin knows exactly how plan review works today, which means he's optimizing the current system rather than questioning whether the system should exist. My highest-conviction bets have been on outsiders who didn't know what the industry considered impossible -- a biochemist attacking the meat industry, a New Zealander building rockets. AutoSitu feels like experienced people aiming for a better process, not naive outsiders attempting to reinvent one.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The 11 named city engagements as a two-person pre-seed team is genuinely impressive traction for govtech, where procurement cycles typically run 6-18 months. If those are real paid deployments rather than pilot conversations, that's exceptional founder-market fit and suggests Lin's domain network is a legitimate distribution advantage. The timing convergence -- LLMs capable of regulatory document reasoning, political pressure on housing, municipal staffing shortages -- is real and favorable. And if AutoSitu can accumulate jurisdiction-specific regulatory data across enough municipalities, the switching costs and data moat could compound into a durable position. The strongest version of this company is a horizontal infrastructure layer that every municipality depends on, generating predictable SaaS revenue with deep lock-in. That outcome would be a good business -- potentially a very good business. But it's a $500M-$2B outcome, not a world-reshaping one.

The technology itself -- LLM-based document analysis against regulatory text -- is explicitly described in the dossier as "reproducible." The differentiation is operational (jurisdiction-specific data, workflow integration) rather than technological. This is a classic execution race in a category that's already been identified by the market, not a technology breakthrough that creates a new category. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. Nobody in the permitting space is saying AI plan review will never work -- they're all building it simultaneously. The absence of expert dismissal tells me the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening to incumbents' mental models.

I respect what this team has accomplished in traction, and the problem of slow permitting is real. But I invest in consequences that justify the attempt, and I'm not seeing a path to the kind of outcome that makes me lose sleep over missing it. This is a well-executed company in a competitive market pursuing efficiency gains within an existing system. My portfolio is full of bets where the probability of failure was 90% but the consequence of success was civilization-scale. AutoSitu has probably a 40% chance of building a solid business, with a consequence of success that is, in my framework, insufficient to justify the risk-adjusted opportunity cost of the capital and attention.
