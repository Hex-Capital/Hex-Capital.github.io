## The Idea

**Problem:** Companies sponsoring U.S. work visas and the candidates applying face a process that is slow, expensive, and error-prone. Traditional immigration law firms charge $10,000–$35,000 per visa petition (YC Launch page) and take 6–8 weeks to prepare a single case. The U.S. processes 1M+ work visas annually (company description), and incumbents still rely on fax machines, email, and 1990s-era case management software. For O-1, EB-1A, and EB-2 NIW petitions — which require extensive narrative drafting and evidence compilation — the bottleneck is attorney labor hours.

**Approach:** LegalOS pairs 24 specialized AI agents with licensed immigration attorneys who have 40+ years of experience (YC Launch page). The AI agents draft petition narratives, compile evidence packages, and anticipate USCIS objections. Every case is reviewed and signed by a licensed attorney. The company studied 12,000 successful petitions to train its process (YC page). This enables delivery of USCIS-ready petition packages in as little as 48 hours versus the industry-standard 6–8 weeks. Supported visa types: O-1, H-1B, L-1A, L-1B, TN, EB-1A, EB-1C, and EB-2 NIW.

**Differentiation:** Unlike traditional immigration law firms (e.g., Fragomen), LegalOS automates the most labor-intensive parts of petition preparation — narrative drafting and evidence assembly — rather than simply digitizing intake forms. Unlike immigration SaaS platforms such as Envoy Global or Boundless, which provide case management tools to employers and existing law firms, LegalOS operates as the law firm itself, bundling software and legal services. Unlike Alma (tryalma.com), which also targets high-skill immigration with an AI-assisted platform, LegalOS emphasizes its 48-hour turnaround and family-practice legacy of 40+ years. The company rebranded from "Legal Bullet" to "LegalOS" in October 2025, reflecting an expanded scope as an "operating system" for business immigration (LegalOS blog post, Oct 17, 2025).

## Defensibility

LegalOS's current defensibility rests on its proprietary dataset of 12,000 analyzed successful petitions (YC page) and the 40+ years of immigration case data from the Asir family's practice. The 24 specialized AI agents built by the CTO represent technical know-how in a narrow domain. The company's 100% approval rate on filed petitions (YC page), if maintained at scale, could serve as a trust signal that compounds over time — immigration is a domain where failure has severe personal consequences for applicants, creating high switching costs once a firm establishes reliability.

**Market structure:** Traditional immigration law firms face a structural barrier to replicating this model: their revenue depends on billable attorney hours. An AI-first approach that delivers petitions in 48 hours instead of 6–8 weeks cannibalizes the billable-hour model. Large firms like Fragomen (5,659 employees, ~$948M revenue per RocketReach via search snippet) would need to restructure their entire workforce and pricing model. Immigration SaaS platforms like Envoy Global sell to law firms as customers, creating a channel conflict if they tried to become the law firm themselves.

**Commoditization risk:** The core technology — LLM-based narrative generation and document assembly — is accessible to any well-funded team. Other YC W26 companies in the legal vertical (General Legal, Arcline) could pivot to immigration (Artificial Lawyer, Feb 2026). Alma ($5.1M raised, TechCrunch Jul 2024) is a direct competitor already targeting the same high-skill immigration segment with AI. Lawhive ($100M total raised, Fortune Feb 2026) has built a general-purpose AI law firm model that could expand into immigration. The key differentiator is the domain-specific training data from the family practice, which is harder to replicate.

## Key Risks

**Regulatory classification risk:** The company website states "LegalOS Inc. is not a law firm and services do not constitute legal advice" (legalos.ai disclaimer), yet the YC page and marketing describe it as an "AI-native immigration law firm." This dual positioning creates potential unauthorized practice of law (UPL) risk in states with strict UPL enforcement. Mitigation: every case is reviewed and signed by licensed immigration attorneys (YC page), and the company appears to operate attorneys within its structure.

**Name collision with Legal OS (Berlin):** A separate, established company called "Legal OS" (legalos.io) operates in the legal tech space — a Berlin-based generative AI platform for in-house legal teams, backed by Google (LinkedIn, EU-Startups). This creates brand confusion risk in search results, industry coverage, and enterprise sales.

**Immigration policy sensitivity:** U.S. work visa volumes are subject to political and regulatory shifts. H-1B eligible registrations dropped 26.9% from FY 2025 to FY 2026 (USCIS data via search snippet). A sustained reduction in visa issuance would contract the addressable market. The company's reliance on U.S. employment-based immigration concentrates geographic risk.

**Scaling the attorney bottleneck:** While AI handles drafting and evidence compilation, every case requires licensed attorney review and signature (YC page). Scaling from "dozens" of cases to thousands requires proportionally scaling the attorney workforce, which may limit the margin advantage and throughput gains that the AI architecture promises.

**Competitive convergence:** Multiple well-funded players are entering the AI-assisted immigration space simultaneously. Alma ($5.1M raised) targets the same high-skill visa categories. Lawhive ($100M raised, $35M ARR) is expanding its AI law firm model to the U.S. Fragomen has acquisition capacity (acquired SimpleCitizen in 2020) and could build or buy AI capabilities.
