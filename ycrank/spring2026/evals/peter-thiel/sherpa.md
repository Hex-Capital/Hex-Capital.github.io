# Sherpa -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Optimizely generates $400M in annual recurring revenue selling manual A/B testing workflows to enterprises. VWO was acquired for $200M. AB Tasty does $38.9M. Intellimize was bought by Webflow. Every single one of these incumbents has an AI roadmap. And now Sherpa proposes to enter this market by wrapping generally available foundation models around the same conversion optimization loop these companies have owned for a decade. I ask my canonical question — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — and I hear nothing back but consensus. "AI can automate website testing" is not a secret. It is the stated product strategy of every CRO incumbent in the market. When I see a company whose entire thesis appeared in the last three Optimizely investor presentations, I know there is no secret, and without a secret there is no monopoly, and without a monopoly there is nothing worth funding.

Sherpa's competitive landscape reveals the structural problem with surgical clarity. Mutiny has raised $72.6M. Optimizely has $200M+ in total funding. VWO and AB Tasty merged under Everstone Capital. Intellimize was absorbed into Webflow's platform. This is not a market waiting for a new entrant — it is a market consolidating around well-capitalized incumbents who already own the customer relationship, the experimentation infrastructure, and the data. Sherpa's claimed differentiator — that it generates variants autonomously rather than requiring human-supplied alternatives — is a feature, not a company. The moment Optimizely ships an LLM-powered variant generator (which its AI roadmap explicitly contemplates), Sherpa's differentiation evaporates. This is the pattern I have warned about repeatedly: wrapping someone else's model is the purest form of competition, because you are competing not just with other startups but with every incumbent's next quarterly feature release.

Ethan Kinnan holds a BS in Finance from the University of Washington (graduated 2024) and previously served as Head of Partnerships at Lindy. Norbu Sonam holds a BS in Computer Science from RPI with internships at Microsoft and IBM. Both founders are young, early-career, and working on their first company. I do not see the kind of definite founder conviction — the bold, specific, irreversible multi-year plan — that characterizes my highest-conviction investments. Sherpa's approach reads as iterative by design: analyze visitor data, generate variants, test them, deploy winners, repeat. This is the lean methodology applied to CRO, and it is precisely the "iterate toward product-market fit" thinking I consider a red flag. Palmer Luckey had built and sold Oculus. The General Matter team came from SpaceX, Tesla, and national laboratories. Sherpa's founders came from a Series A AI agent startup where they were employees, not founders. The co-founder relationship — both worked at Lindy — satisfies the shared prehistory criterion at a minimal level, but the intensity and duration of that shared experience does not compare to the forging environments I look for.

The bull case deserves honest examination. Sherpa ran 40 experiments in 30 days for Lindy, producing +30% total conversion lift — a result that, if reliably repeatable across cold prospects, would create powerful organic distribution through word-of-mouth. The "one line of code" integration claim echoes Stripe's developer-first wedge. The 4.3M+ website visits of learning data could theoretically compound into a data flywheel that improves variant quality over time. And the CRO market's consolidation wave — Intellimize acquired April 2024, VWO/AB Tasty merged January 2025 — creates a window where incumbents are distracted by integration. I checked my Known Exceptions: does the Lyft exception apply, where category-level value creation is so massive that even the #2 player produces fund-relevant returns? No — the entire CRO software market is $1.2B, projected to reach $2.9B by 2032. Total domination of this market would not produce a power-law outcome. Does the Stemcentrx exception apply, where a genuine contrarian secret overrides domain uncertainty? No — there is no contrarian secret here. The bull case collapses on structural grounds.

Sherpa's traction concentration compounds the concern. The flagship case study — Lindy, +30% conversion lift — is the founders' former employer. Gauge and Yeti Co appear on the website without detailed results. Dependence on a warm relationship for your primary proof point is not distribution; it is a demo. Sherpa has 8K LinkedIn followers and a well-received announcement post (260 reactions, 106 comments), which suggests Ethan Kinnan has real network energy — but founder-led sales through a personal LinkedIn audience is not a solved distribution architecture, it is the starting condition of every pre-seed SaaS company. On the physical-world moat dimension, Sherpa is a pure software layer consuming third-party LLM APIs with no proprietary hardware, no regulatory barrier, and no physical-world manipulation. A well-funded team could replicate the core product in months. This sits at the exact opposite pole from General Matter, Anduril, or Substrate — the companies where I place my highest personal conviction.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Sherpa demonstrates repeatable 20%+ conversion lift across 50+ unaffiliated customers with zero founder-touch onboarding, AND articulates a specific expansion path into a market with power-law economics (pricing optimization, revenue intelligence, or autonomous commerce that grows the addressable category by an order of magnitude beyond the $2.9B CRO ceiling). Without both — repeatable autonomous results AND a path to a market large enough to matter — Sherpa remains a feature competing in a consolidating market against incumbents with 100x its resources. The monopoly secret is absent, and the power-law magnitude is structurally capped. Those two dimensions are decisive.
