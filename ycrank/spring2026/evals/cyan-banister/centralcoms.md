# CentralComs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Kyle Chang spent three years at Indoor Finders shipping facility-management AI to NVIDIA, NASA JPL, Cushman & Wakefield, and Samsung, and CentralComs is the cleanest dimensional signature I've read this batch: a founder with real adjacent-domain reps pointed straight at the most consensus AI category in YC Spring 2026. The high dimension is lived experience, the low dimension is magical weirdness, and that pairing tells me CentralComs is a competent operator's company chasing a lane where EliseAI just raised $250M at $2.2B and four YC peers — Avery, PARES, Assembly, Wayline — are running the same play. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game CentralComs seems to be playing is the one everyone is already playing.

Anduril-Luckey (2017) is the load-bearing analogue for CentralComs on founder origin: Palmer shipped Oculus VR from his parents' garage before pivoting to defense, so when he walked into the Pentagon he was *of* the hardware, not visiting it. CentralComs gets a fractional version of that — Kyle Chang shipped facility AI to Cushman & Wakefield, which is the largest commercial real-estate services firm on earth, and that is a real artifact, not a memo. Where CentralComs diverges from the Anduril rung is the residential gap: Cushman is corporate office occupancy, not apartment maintenance tickets running through AppFolio and Buildium, and the dossier offers no biographical moment — no rental-property childhood, no eviction story, no roof leak Kyle personally chased a vendor for. Kevin Wong's "Technical" role is the kind of LinkedIn line that fills space when nothing else fits. Founder fit is there in adjacency form, not in the autobiographical form that pulled my **HandUp** check.

The Pass Example in my own playbook is the consensus YC AI-agent darling — six top funds chasing, $200B TAM slide, four-logo team page — and the failure mechanism is that the moat is the model, not the founder, so a polymath generalist rebuilds it in a quarter. CentralComs is downstream of that pattern with a worse competitive frame: EliseAI is at ~$100M ARR with 10% of U.S. apartments penetrated (SiliconANGLE, August 2025), AppFolio Realm-X is the native AI inside the very PMS CentralComs integrates with and is publicly claiming 10.3 hours/week saved per user, and Buildium Lumina is the same story one platform over. CentralComs is the external agent layer between three vendors who each have product-roadmap and channel reasons to close that surface area — that is not "find them before it becomes consensus" (Yahoo Finance, March 2025), that is arriving the quarter after consensus formed.

The CEO question is the **HQ Trivia** (2017) ghost — Kyle and Kevin are both listed as co-founders out of UC Berkeley with no public tiebreaker, no documented operating agreement, no named CEO on the YC page. Mechanism over 18 months: the first hard pricing decision (per-unit vs. per-seat), the first integration tradeoff (depth on AppFolio vs. breadth across three), and the first hire above the cap table will each surface the unresolved authority question, and the dossier gives me no evidence they've had the conversation. I default below midpoint on CentralComs here for the same reason I sat outside the **Niantic** door — silence on governance reads as "they haven't run the fire drill."

Counterfactual for the capital-intensity dimension: what would have to be true for CentralComs to clear the **Vigil Labs** (August 2025) bar? A regulatory clause — Fair Housing Act compliance baked into the leasing agent, eviction-notice timing requirements per state, ADA-compliant maintenance triage — that incumbents can't replicate without a legal-engineering team. The dossier has none of that. CentralComs is a software wrapper around three vendor APIs whose ToS can change next quarter; "integration dependency" is the dossier's own phrase. That is the opposite of the **Crusoe** moat compounding in a domain other investors refuse to enter.

Single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability: **Flock Safety** was a license-plate reader I could photograph in a parking lot, and the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café is what put the company in front of me. CentralComs is an AI agent operating inside an AppFolio dashboard I will never log into — I am not a property manager, my friends are not property managers, and the artifact is invisible to my environmental sourcing method. That is not disqualifying on its own, but it removes the channel by which I actually source conviction.

Thirty-six-month forecast: CentralComs reaches $1–3M ARR servicing small-to-mid residential property managers, then gets acquihired into AppFolio or Buildium for the Realm-X/Lumina roadmap, or stalls into a lifestyle SMB SaaS as the native PMS agents absorb the cross-platform layer. The mechanism is the consensus-category compression — EliseAI takes the apartment top of the market, the PMS incumbents take their own installed bases, and the cross-PMS aggregator gets squeezed in the middle.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the **DeepMind**-shaped override: Kyle's NVIDIA + NASA JPL + Cushman & Wakefield client list at Indoor Finders is genuine polymath compounding across enterprise facility AI, and property managers actually do run multiple PMS stacks — a cross-vendor agent layer is a real structural insight the natives cannot ship without channel conflict. That argument stings because Cushman is the world's largest CRE services firm and "I shipped to Cushman" is not a deck line, it's an artifact. It loses to two facts in the dossier: Cushman & Wakefield is corporate occupier work, not residential apartment leasing and maintenance, so the customer transfer is a category jump not a domain extension; and CentralComs is in the same YC batch lane as Avery, PARES, Assembly, and Wayline, which is the consensus signal the thesis is built to deselect on. One condition flips me: if Kyle can show a signed pilot with a named multi-PMS property manager operating across all three of AppFolio, Buildium, and Yardi — not an LOI, a deployed pilot with usage data — the cross-vendor insight stops being a slide and starts being a tactile artifact, and I take a second meeting.
