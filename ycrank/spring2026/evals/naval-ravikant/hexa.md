# Hexa -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Hexa is a vertical AI-agent overlay on SAP, Oracle NetSuite, Microsoft Dynamics, and Epicor that fails my leverage check on opening read -- the dossier's demo-only Calendly link to "ishaan-hexaagents/30min" tells me everything about distribution architecture before I get to the founder section. The signature pairing here is a plausible-enough operations market (Hexa's strongest dimension) sitting on top of capital-and-labor leverage that requires permission from three of the largest ERP vendors on earth (Hexa's weakest). That pairing names what Hexa is: a permissioned-distribution wrapper in a category the gatekeepers themselves are now shipping into.

Leverage architecture is where Hexa breaks against my entry-point filter most cleanly. The HIGH rung on this ladder is **Twitter** (2007) -- media as protocol, zero marginal cost of replication, no gatekeeper. The MID rung is **Notion** (2013), code-leverage but with PLG distribution that still didn't need anyone's permission. The LOW rung is the enterprise B2B SaaS motion I have repeatedly avoided -- "labor and capital leverage require permission" (nav.al/product-media, ~2018). Hexa sits at the LOW rung and arguably below it: the product writes back into SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, and Epicor -- four platforms that can revoke API access, ship competing native agents (SAP Joule, Dynamics Copilot are named in the dossier via top10erp.org), or simply outcompete on the in-suite default. The Calendly-only contact path on hexaagents.com confirms a demo-led enterprise motion, not the open-source-pull pattern that put me into **Stack Overflow** (2008) or **Replit** (April 2023).

Founder specific-knowledge is the second axis where Hexa thins out, and it matters more than the others because at pre-seed it is often the only legible signal. The HIGH rung is **Perplexity** (2022), where Aravind Srinivas brought a Berkeley ML PhD in retrieval to the problem. The MID rung is Ivan Zhao at **Notion** (2013), rebuilding personal productivity tools since adolescence. The LOW rung is the "ex-FAANG PM who identified market opportunity in $X TAM" anti-pattern from my Red Flags. Ishaan Makkar's public footprint on Hexa is a LinkedIn headline reading "Stealth," no prior manufacturing or ERP employment retrievable, no GitHub, no X account, no shipped artifact named in the dossier. The unverified search snippet about "Mann Patira" and "FR8" does not change this -- I do not underwrite on unverified snippets. Hexa is closer to the LOW rung than the MID rung on this ladder, which in a 500-word factual dossier is the load-bearing miss.

Market and pivot-room is Hexa's strongest dimension and the reason this is not a Strong Pass. HIGH rung: **Twitter** (2007), consumer media, infinite adjacent surface. LOW rung: dental-SaaS-for-orthodontists, the niche-vertical anti-pattern. Hexa lands closer to the MID rung -- manufacturing operations is a real and large category, and the four named workflows (quoting, procurement, finance, customer service) give Hexa at least two pivot vectors inside the original mandate. But the same dossier names Distro, Turian, Go Autonomous, Arphie, Altura (€8M raised), and AutoRFP.ai as overlapping competitors; pivot-room is being consumed by entrants in real time.

Network effects per criterion #2 are essentially absent. HIGH rung: **Uber** (2010), the Nth driver makes the (N-1) rider more valuable. MID rung: **Stack Overflow** (2008), every answer compounds the next searcher's value. LOW rung: single-tenant B2B automation where customer A's usage does not increase customer B's value. Hexa is at the LOW rung -- agents running inside one manufacturer's ERP do not compound across customers without a cross-tenant data layer, which the dossier does not describe. The Perplexity exception (criterion #2 waived because #1 and #3 cleared top quartile) does not apply here because Hexa fails #1 and #3 first.

The founder trio reads as quiet, not bad. HIGH rung: Spolsky and Atwood at **Stack Overflow** (2008), public technical writing predating the company. LOW rung: the **Clearview AI** integrity contestation that I tolerated only because the specific-knowledge gate was extreme. Hexa is neither -- Ishaan Makkar is opaque, two co-founders are unnamed on the YC page, and I apply the absent-evidence default (~7/14). No red flag, no signal either. The 50/50-no-CEO anti-pattern is unresolvable from the dossier because team composition is undisclosed.

36-month forecast: Hexa is dead or pivoted out of the manufacturer/distributor ERP-overlay category by Spring 2029, with sub-$1M ARR at the inflection point, crushed in a vise between SAP Joule and Dynamics Copilot on the incumbent side and Distro/Turian/Go Autonomous specialization on the startup side. The mechanism is straightforward -- a 3-person team advertising native integration depth across eight ERPs cannot match either a single-stack specialist on depth or an in-suite incumbent on default-channel access, and the dossier shows zero customer wins, zero press, zero Product Hunt launch, and a "not hiring" status to interrupt that trajectory.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is the **Anchorage Digital** exception applied in spirit: vertical regulated-or-complex domains can flip "permissioned distribution" into "permission-as-moat" when the integration depth becomes the structural barrier. If Hexa achieves production-grade write-back across SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, and Epicor before incumbents close their APIs, the switching cost for a mid-market manufacturer who has rewired four workflows around Hexa is non-trivial -- and the 54% spreadsheet-reliance stat is real pain. I dismantle this with the dossier: Anchorage had a Treasury OCC charter granted January 2021 as the moat artifact and me as advisor inside the category since **MetaStable** (2014); Hexa has no charter, no exclusive partnership, no proprietary dataset, and the same eight integrations every named competitor is racing to ship. Permission-as-moat requires an actual asymmetric permission. Hexa has eight ordinary API keys. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: three named mid-market manufacturer logos at >$50K ACV with documented six-month retention -- the **Stack Overflow** developer-pull equivalent translated to a permissioned market. Until then, pass.
