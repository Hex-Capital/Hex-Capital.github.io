# Panorama -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Tell me the story. Not the business. That's where I always start, and with Panorama, it's where I get stuck. Jingwei Hao progressed from Senior Engineer to Staff Engineer to Tech Lead Manager at Lyft, built key infrastructure at Cash App, shipped code at Twitter and Google. Jaclyn Lunger earned a PhD at MIT in machine learning for materials science, then became the first employee at Lila Sciences, which went on to raise $350M at a $1.3B+ valuation with Nvidia backing. These are genuinely impressive credentials -- Lunger in particular has a rare signal, having been founding scientist at a unicorn. But when I ask myself what life experience made these two people *incapable of ignoring* the problem of workplace workflow automation, I hear silence. Hao's infrastructure background gives him proximity to coordination problems at scale, sure. Lunger's ML chops are real. But proximity is not inevitability. The founding narrative reads as "we were at top companies, we saw an opportunity in AI workflow automation" -- that's career optimization, not a biographical forge. And the co-founders share no visible institutional overlap, no shared history that suggests a relationship tested under pressure.

Panorama is building in one of the most consensus-attractive spaces in technology right now. Workflow automation is a $23.77 billion market that every major platform player is racing to own. Zapier sits at a $5B valuation on $310M in revenue. n8n just raised $254M. Workato has taken $421M. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks for $2.85 billion. Microsoft is building Copilot Studio. Google has AgentSpace. When I evaluate whether an idea would make a room of sophisticated VCs shift in their chairs, Panorama does the opposite -- it makes them nod along. "AI-powered workflow automation" is the kind of thing every investor understands immediately, which means every investor is already funding something adjacent to it. There's no courage required to articulate this thesis at a dinner party. Compare this to when I wrote my check into Anduril -- defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley. Or Contraline -- try pitching male birth control to a room of VCs and watch the body language. Panorama provokes no discomfort, and in my framework, that's not a neutral signal. It's a negative one.

The "TikTok-like recommendation engine for workflows" framing is clever, and the observational approach -- watching workplace data for 1-2 weeks before surfacing automations -- is a genuine product insight that differentiates Panorama from Zapier's user-configured model. But I keep running my invisible system test and coming up empty. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was attacking credit card opacity -- a genuinely hidden, extractive structure that charged people fees they didn't understand through terms they couldn't parse. When I backed Ownwell, they were exposing the property tax system. Panorama's problem -- knowledge workers spending time on repetitive tasks -- is real, but it's not opaque. People *know* they fill out the same Notion doc every Friday. They *know* they manually create Linear tickets from planning docs. There's no gatekeeper, no information asymmetry, no hidden tax being extracted. Panorama is solving an efficiency problem, not exposing a structural injustice. That's a fine business, but it doesn't activate the part of my framework that drives my highest-conviction bets.

The defensibility picture concerns me most. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The core concept of observing work patterns and suggesting automations is replicable." Microsoft, Google, and Slack/Salesforce have *native access* to the same workplace data Panorama must integrate with via APIs. If Satya Nadella decides Copilot should observe your team's patterns and suggest automations -- and he has every incentive to, given ServiceNow's $2.85B Moveworks acquisition validated the category -- Panorama's entire value proposition gets absorbed into a platform feature. The data moat is theoretical: longer usage generates more tailored recommendations, but Panorama has to survive long enough for that flywheel to matter, and the cold-start dependency of 1-2 weeks creates adoption friction that works against them in the critical early period. No patents, no public repos, no structural barrier beyond "product design philosophy." This is the pattern I've been burned by -- HQ Trivia had a brilliant product insight too, but without structural defensibility, the product insight alone wasn't enough.

The strongest bull case: Lunger's track record as founding scientist at a company that reached $1.3B is genuinely rare signal at pre-seed. The LLM timing is real -- observational workflow discovery was not technically feasible at commercial quality before 2023. And if Panorama can build recommendation quality that incumbents can't match because their models are trained on aggregate patterns across thousands of teams, that could become a real moat over time. The Product Hunt launch at #2 with 363 upvotes and 60 comments shows the product resonates with early adopters. For this to become a Long Journey bet, I would need to see evidence that the founders' biographical connection to this problem runs deeper than what's in the dossier, that they're building proprietary infrastructure beneath the product surface that compounds with each customer, and that enterprise teams are showing retention patterns suggesting genuine switching costs. Right now, I don't see those signals.

Panorama has capable founders in a real market, but it fails the tests that drive my highest-conviction investments. No biographical forge connecting these founders to workflow automation as a personal mission. No contrarian discomfort -- this is a consensus AI bet in a consensus market. No invisible system being disrupted. And no structural moat to prevent Microsoft or Google from absorbing the insight. This is a pass for me. The two dimensions that lock the verdict are the absent biographical forge -- Jingwei and Jaclyn are talented operators, but nothing in their stories suggests they *must* solve this problem -- and the consensus market positioning, which puts Panorama directly in the path of the best-funded incumbents in technology.
