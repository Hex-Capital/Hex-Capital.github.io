# Superset -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that worries me most about Superset is that Anthropic already shipped multi-agent orchestration for Claude Code. When the platform you're building on starts giving away your feature for free, you've got a very specific problem. It's not that you can't survive it — plenty of companies have — but it means you need to be building something much deeper than what is, right now, essentially a nice GUI around git worktrees and tmux. Cursor isn't going to build great support for Claude Code. Anthropic isn't going to build great support for Codex. That's the real argument for an independent orchestration layer. But "the incumbents won't cooperate with each other" is a weaker moat than "we know something the incumbents don't." And when I look at this company, I don't see a non-obvious insight. I see a well-executed product in a space where everyone can see the opportunity.

The founders are legit builders. Kiet Ho shipped Onlook to 25,000 GitHub stars — that's not trivial. You don't get that kind of open-source traction by accident; it requires taste in developer experience and real community-building instincts. All three are former YC CTOs, which means they've each been through the furnace at least once. And they shipped Superset fast — repo created in October, Product Hunt #1 by February. That's the kind of velocity I like to see. But I notice something: three CTOs from three different YC companies, none of which became breakout successes, who came together to build something in the hottest category of 2025. The dossier shows no shared history before this company — no common employer, no common university. That doesn't mean the team is weak, but it does suggest this is a team assembled around an opportunity rather than three people who've been obsessing over the same problem together. The best founding teams I've backed usually had a history before the company. The Collisons were brothers. The Reddit guys were college roommates. When co-founders find each other through a startup idea rather than finding a startup idea through each other, the foundation is thinner.

The origin story here doesn't give me what I look for. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, it was because he kept forgetting his USB drive on the train to New York. That's the kind of origin I trust — a specific moment of frustration that metastasizes into a company. With Superset, the origin reads more like: "CLI-based AI agents are proliferating, developers need to run multiple ones in parallel, the current workflow is messy." That's a correct observation. It might even be a good business thesis. But it's the kind of thing you arrive at by watching the market, not by living inside a problem until you can't stand it anymore. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas — and this feels like a startup idea someone thought of by noticing a trend.

Here's what would have to be true for this to be great: the AI coding agent market stays genuinely fragmented — three or four major agents, each best at different tasks — and developers settle into a workflow where they routinely run multiple agents simultaneously across different parts of a codebase. In that world, the orchestration layer is infrastructure, not a feature. Superset's agent-agnostic positioning becomes a structural advantage because no single agent vendor will build an equally good experience for competitors' products. And Kiet Ho's demonstrated ability to build developer communities (25k stars at Onlook, 8k at Superset) means they could own the community hub where multi-agent workflows evolve. That's a real bull case. The problem is it requires the market to cooperate in a very specific way — and the more likely outcome is that one or two agents dominate and their native tooling becomes good enough. 8,000 GitHub stars are encouraging, but Kiet's previous project also had great stars and didn't become a lasting business.

The idea doesn't pass my contrarian test. Nobody hears "an IDE for orchestrating multiple AI coding agents" and says "that's crazy, it'll never work." They say "oh, that's obviously needed." When everyone agrees an idea is good, the returns have already been priced in through competition. Cursor has $29 billion in valuation and the resources to add this as a feature. Claude Squad already provides a free open-source alternative. Conductor is in the same YC network doing the same thing. This is a space with high consensus appeal and low barriers — the opposite of where I find the best risk-adjusted returns.

I respect the execution speed and the team's shipping credentials. If these three founders came to me with an idea that made smart people uncomfortable — something with the schlep factor of payments infrastructure or the weirdness of renting air mattresses to strangers — I'd be more interested. They clearly know how to build and ship developer tools. But right now they're building a beautiful wrapper in a category where the platforms are moving fast, the competition is obvious, and the moat is UX polish rather than deep technical or domain insight. That's a race I wouldn't bet my own money on.
