# Cardboard -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cardboard is the competitive landscape. "Lots of players in the market doing this" was the reaction on Hacker News, and HN commenters are usually right about crowding. Descript has $101M in funding and ~$55M ARR. OpusClip has $50M and 10M users. Kapwing, Vizard, CapCut, and Adobe are all converging on the same basic insight: video editing is slow and AI can speed it up. When I see a category where every well-funded player is moving in the same direction, I get nervous. Not because the problem isn't real -- it obviously is -- but because obvious problems in hot categories don't give startups the structural advantage I look for. This is the opposite of schlep blindness. Everyone sees this problem. Everyone is working on it.

That said, there's something genuinely interesting underneath the crowding. Saksham and Ishan aren't just bolting AI features onto a simple UI. They're building a full non-linear editor from scratch on WebGPU and WebCodecs -- a custom hardware-accelerated renderer in the browser -- while simultaneously building an agentic AI layer on top of it. Most AI video startups take the easy path: build a thin wrapper around foundation model APIs, skip the hard editing infrastructure, ship something that looks impressive in demos but falls apart for real production work. Cardboard is doing the ugly engineering work. That's a form of schlep I respect, even if it's technical schlep rather than the regulatory or logistical schlep that creates deeper moats.

The organic discovery question is where I'm most uncertain. Ishan spent five years at HackerRank building performance-critical browser applications, and he personally produced Apple-style launch videos for their all-hands meetings. Saksham creates content with ~250k views per month. So both founders genuinely edit video and genuinely experience the pain. That's not manufactured -- they didn't read a TechCrunch article about the AI video market and decide to start a company. But "video editing takes too long" is also something every content creator on earth already knows. The insight isn't hidden. Compare this to Stripe, where thousands of programmers knew payments were broken but the schlep of dealing with banks kept everyone away. Here, the schlep is "build a really good editor," which is hard engineering but doesn't create the same kind of structural barrier. Adobe could throw fifty engineers at this tomorrow. The problem Cardboard solves is obvious, the solution requires execution intensity rather than unique domain insight that only these founders possess.

Where Cardboard genuinely shines is the team composition. Ishan is clearly a strong technical builder -- 105 GitHub repos, 791 followers, created a tool with claimed 5M downloads at age twelve, and five years of exactly the kind of browser performance engineering this product demands. Saksham has AI and ML depth, published at ACL, and built an AI startup before (Iterate AI, which shut down -- and he came back, which I take as a positive signal). They've known each other for fifteen years. That co-founder relationship is real. And thirteen product releases since November 2025 with a team of two is the kind of shipping velocity that tells me these founders are builders, not talkers. The 1,000+ early users and named customers like PostHog suggest the product works well enough for real teams to adopt it.

The bull case is this: if you believe AI will fundamentally restructure video editing -- not just add features to existing editors but create an entirely new workflow paradigm where you describe what you want and an agent builds it -- then the winner will be whoever builds the best native AI-first editor from the ground up. Incumbents can't do this without rewriting their entire stack and cannibalizing their existing per-seat pricing model. Among the startups, Cardboard is doing harder engineering work than most, with founders who have the right technical background and genuine domain exposure. If they execute on both the NLE and the agentic layer, they could end up with the only product that's simultaneously a professional-grade editor and a genuine AI collaborator. That's a real vision.

But I keep coming back to the category problem. When I wrote that the most impressive YC companies I've seen recently are not working on AI, this is exactly the pattern I was warning about. AI video editing is consensus. The idea could have been conceived by any technically capable team reading industry trends. The foundation models powering the AI layer are commercially available -- Cardboard's AI advantage isn't proprietary. And competing against Descript at $55M ARR with $2.1M in funding and two people requires not just good execution but sustained miracles in both engineering output and distribution. The technical craft here is genuine. The founders are strong builders who came to this through real experience. But the market structure works against them in ways that determination alone can't overcome.
