# Zibra Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a hacker-genealogy bet without an animal-resourcefulness story: Ibrahim Rabbani was tech-lead on Ray (~12M weekly downloads, PyTorch Foundation) and Zac Policzer was Senior Staff on LinkedIn's Venice/Liquid/Espresso, but the Zibra dossier names no cereal-box equivalent, no pre-funding revenue stunt, no first paying quant fund. Those are two of the strongest pure-infrastructure resumes I've read this batch, pointed at a market neither founder appears to have lived inside.

On hacker genealogy, the Collison brothers at **Stripe** (2010) are the load-bearing analogy -- Patrick won Ireland's Young Scientist (2005) for Lisp work, then shipped Auctomatic before they wrote a line of payments code, and that prior-artifact pedigree is what made me lean in before the idea proved itself. Zibra Labs has the equivalent signal in Rabbani's Ray Core leadership: Ray is the substrate Bridgewater, P72, Man Group, and Two Sigma already run on (zibralabs.ai, 2026), which is roughly the technical-taste equivalent of having shipped a programming language people respect. Where Zibra Labs diverges from the Stripe rung is that the Collisons had also shipped a commerce product (Auctomatic, ~$5M sale at ages 19/17) before Stripe -- Rabbani and Policzer have shipped distributed systems, but neither has shipped a quant artifact, and that asymmetry is what knocks Zibra Labs down from the top rung on this axis.

On animal resourcefulness, the failure mode I keep watching for is the polished-MBA AI-wrapper team with a glossy deck and no shipped revenue -- the pattern from my Pass Example. Zibra Labs is not that team; these are real hackers. But the specific tell of an animal -- the **Airbnb** (2009) cereal boxes, Brian Armstrong's post-rejection Android wallet at **Coinbase** (2012), Drew Houston's pre-seed Hacker News demo for **Dropbox** (2007) -- is conspicuously absent from the Zibra Labs dossier. No customer logo, no signed LOI with a named quant fund, no public pricing page on zibralabs.ai as of May 2026, no Product Hunt, no Twitter footprint for either founder, and "founders' Ray network already includes Bridgewater, P72, Man Group, Two Sigma" is a sleight of hand -- those are Ray users, not Zibra customers, and the dossier is explicit about that. Animals leave tracks; Zibra Labs's tracks are from the previous job.

Idea organicity is where the mechanism analysis matters most over 18 months. The organic-idea filter compounds because founders who hit their own problem keep finding the next adjacent problem before competitors notice -- Drew Houston's USB stick at **Dropbox** (2007) is the canonical case. Zibra Labs is inverted on this axis: Rabbani and Policzer built the substrate (Ray, Venice) and are now pointing it at quants, which means every customer call is a discovery call rather than a self-recognition exercise. Over 18 months this typically manifests as feature drift -- the product becomes "general HPC for AI workloads" because that's the founders' actual domain -- and the dossier's tags ("Artificial Intelligence, Fintech, Cloud Computing, Infrastructure") already hint at that gravity.

Schlep zone: counterfactually, what would have to be true for Zibra Labs to score in the **Coinbase** (2012) tier here? The dossier would need to name the specific unpleasant work the founders are personally absorbing -- the on-prem security audits, the SOC 2 grind, the per-fund compliance review, the manual onboarding into a quant fund's proprietary data formats. Zibra Labs gestures at the schlep (sub-50ms dispatch across 6.4M tasks on spot, multi-cloud orchestration into firms that build their own grids) but does not show the founders running toward the parts that scare other YC applicants. The schlep is plausibly there; it just isn't claimed.

Co-founder configuration parallels **Stripe** (2010) without quite reaching it. Two technical founders, overlapping employer history at LinkedIn, complementary infra coverage (Ray scheduling on one side, planet-scale derived-data systems on the other) -- this is a real pair, not a hackathon pairing, and the Collison comparison is the right reference point. Zibra Labs falls short of the Collison rung only because the dossier doesn't establish years of building together outside of LinkedIn's org chart.

My 36-month forecast for Zibra Labs: lands two to four mid-tier quant fund contracts (not the Two Sigma/Bridgewater names that build their own grids), reaches roughly $2-5M ARR, and gets acquired by Anyscale or a hyperscaler -- most likely Anyscale, since Rabbani's Ray Core lineage makes the integration trivial and Anyscale ($281M raised, $1B valuation per Intel Capital 2021) has the obvious vertical-wedge motive. The mechanism is the hacker-genealogy/animal-resourcefulness asymmetry: elite infra builders without an organic pull into trading tend to optimize the substrate beautifully and then sell it to the company that already owns the substrate.

The strongest case against this forecast is the Stripe analogy itself: the Collisons hadn't run a payment processor either, and I funded them anyway because the hacker quality was overwhelming and the schlep was real. One could argue Zibra Labs is the same shape -- Ray-grade hackers running toward a tedious enterprise-sales schlep into paranoid quant funds, and that's exactly the pattern "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) told me to lean into. The dossier evidence that defeats this bull case is Auctomatic: the Collisons had shipped commerce ($5M exit at 19/17) before Stripe, and Rabbani/Policzer have shipped infra but nothing trading-adjacent, which is the specific gap "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) warns about. The verifiable condition that flips Zibra Labs from neutral to invest: a signed paying contract with a named tier-1 quant fund (Two Sigma, P72, Bridgewater, or Citadel) within six months -- not a Ray reference, an actual Zibra Labs purchase order.
