# Alchemy -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Phillip Baek was about to join Boston Scientific as an EP Mapping Specialist. Liam McBride was building BoBo, a shared living app with Flutter and Firebase that raised $20K in non-dilutive funding from student developers at UIUC and UT Austin. Then they're making AI-powered microscopy image analysis for biotech researchers. I keep looking for the bridge — the moment where one of them was staring at confocal images at 2am and thought "this is insane, I should automate this" — and I can't find it. Neither founder has published research in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis. No Google Scholar results, no PubMed results. Baek's biomedical engineering degree from UT Austin gives him vocabulary in the domain, but vocabulary isn't the same as the kind of bone-deep frustration that produces great startup ideas. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, he didn't need to research the file-syncing market. The problem was already in his body. Alchemy's origin story reads like the opposite trajectory — a market identified, not a problem lived.

Alchemy's core pitch — researchers describe analysis needs in plain language, AI assembles a pipeline, 98% reduction in analysis time — sounds great. That's actually the problem. "Use AI to automate tedious lab work" is the kind of idea that gets enthusiastic nods from everyone at a dinner party. No researcher would say "no, I prefer spending 3 hours on H. pylori microscopy analysis." Compare this to when Brian Chesky told people strangers would sleep in your apartment — they recoiled. When Patrick Collison said he was going to rebuild payments infrastructure — people said "that sounds terrible." Alchemy's idea sits firmly in the zone where it sounds obviously good, which in my framework means the returns have already been competed away. PathAI has raised $490M. Proscia has $130M and 400% year-over-year case growth. Aivia was acquired by Leica and plugged into Danaher's distribution network. The incumbents are in clinical diagnostics, sure, but the research lab segment has CellProfiler, napari, Cellpose, and ImageJ — free, well-maintained by the Broad Institute and Chan Zuckerberg Initiative, widely cited in publications. Alchemy needs researchers to pay for what they're accustomed to getting free. That's not impossible, but it means the idea needs to be coupled with extraordinary execution or domain depth, and I'm not seeing strong evidence of either.

McBride's technical credentials are real — BS in ECE and MS in Computer Science from UIUC is a solid foundation for the ML engineering Alchemy requires. The BoBo app demonstrates he can ship a product: he recruited developers across two universities, built a working mobile application, and secured $20K in non-dilutive funding. That's not nothing. At pre-seed, prior shipping experience matters. But it's a long way from the stories I look for — the Airbnb founders selling Obama O's cereal boxes to cover rent, or the Stripe brothers personally installing their payment code on friends' laptops. Alchemy's dossier contains no anecdotes of creative workarounds, no evidence of doing things that don't scale. The single UC Santa Cruz PhD endorsement — reducing microscopy analysis from 3 hours to under 10 minutes — is a genuine signal of product value, but one testimonial on LinkedIn doesn't tell me whether the founders personally sat with that researcher, hand-tuned the pipeline, and iterated in real time. The how matters as much as the what.

The strongest bull case for Alchemy goes like this: the LLM moment genuinely changed what's possible in bioimage analysis, and the window opened in 2024-2025 for natural-language orchestration of CV pipelines. Enterprise incumbents are pointed at clinical diagnostics and large pharma — none of them are going down-market to serve the individual PI with a self-service tool at researcher-friendly prices. Open-source tools require scripting expertise that many wet-lab biologists simply don't have. Alchemy could be the ClassDojo of microscopy — tackling an unsexy, fragmented market (individual labs instead of individual classrooms) that venture capital ignores because the sales motion looks too bottom-up and the price points look too small. ClassDojo reached unicorn status by going where polished founders wouldn't. Could Alchemy do the same? The parallel breaks down on execution evidence. When I backed ClassDojo, the founders had already demonstrated exceptional execution quality — the product was in thousands of classrooms, teachers were using it obsessively in its crappiest form. Alchemy has 719 LinkedIn followers and one endorsement. The dossier notes the website returned only a title line at time of research. I'd need to see Alchemy with 20-30 labs using the product weekly, with researchers who would be angry if it disappeared, before this parallel holds weight. That's the verifiable condition that would flip me: evidence that a meaningful cluster of researchers have integrated Alchemy into their daily workflow and can't go back.

Alchemy's commoditization risk is the dimension a generic analyst would flag but underweight. I weight it heavily because of what it reveals about moat structure. The dossier states the core capability — "LLM-driven orchestration of CV models" — relies on "generally available foundation models and open-source segmentation tools (e.g., Cellpose)." The barrier to replication is described as moderate, and defensibility depends on execution speed and model library breadth. This is not schlep blindness protecting Alchemy — there's no regulatory complexity, no physical-world integration pain, no compliance burden that repels competitors. Stripe had PCI compliance and bank integrations. Bindwell had pesticide chemistry and agricultural regulation. Alchemy has prompt engineering and model orchestration, which is exactly what every AI lab and every large microscopy vendor will have access to within 18 months. Leica could add a natural-language interface to Aivia in a product cycle. The clock is ticking from the moment the idea is validated, and Alchemy doesn't yet have the installed base or proprietary model library to stay ahead.

The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — both score below their defaults because the evidence actively works against Alchemy rather than being merely absent. The founders jumped from a shared living app to microscopy AI without a visible personal connection to the problem, and the idea generates consensus enthusiasm rather than skepticism. Alchemy is two capable young builders in a real market, but capable young builders in a consensus market with thin moats and free alternatives is precisely the profile where I pass. The founders may prove me wrong — they got into YC, and Aaron Epstein clearly saw something. But from what this dossier shows me, Alchemy is a solution that sounds like a startup idea rather than something that grew out of a real problem, and I've learned to trust that distinction.
