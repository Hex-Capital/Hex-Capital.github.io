# 9 Mothers -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The founding team's domain leap is the first thing that jumps out, and it tells me something important about how to score this. Russell Smith built Rainforest QA -- a no-code testing platform -- and raised $41 million doing it. Roman Khomenko spent eight years shipping production ML pipelines for that same company. Bogdan Pyzh ran penetration testing engagements at Leviathan Security Group. These are credible operators with a deep working relationship, and I give real weight to the Smith-Khomenko pairing specifically: eight years of building together at Rainforest QA is exactly the relational depth that survives genuine adversity. But none of them have ever built a weapon, navigated a defense procurement cycle, manufactured defense-grade hardware, or managed ITAR compliance. The "sane" part of my framework checks out -- these are builders who've shipped at scale. The problem is the idea isn't "insane" in the way I mean it. Counter-drone is the single hottest category in defense tech right now. The U.S. Army's $500M+ C-UAS budget request, the Ukraine battlefield lessons, $49 billion in defense-tech venture funding in 2025 -- this is consensus. When I say "sane person, insane idea," I mean Brian Armstrong building regulated crypto infrastructure when everyone thought Bitcoin was a joke, or Teamshares proposing to buy hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership when it sounded logistically absurd. "Let's build a counter-drone turret" is the opposite: a sane idea in an overcrowded market where every VC wants exposure.

The competitive positioning makes this worse, not better. Allen Control Systems has $30 million from Craft Ventures, won a U.S. Army xTechOverwatch competition, secured a SOCOM maritime contract, tripled its Austin operations, and covers Groups 1 through 3 with M240, M2, and M134 integration. Anduril is raising $4 billion at a $60 billion valuation with a $642 million USMC contract. Epirus has over $550 million raised. 9 Mothers is pre-revenue with no disclosed government contracts, no SBIR awards, and no evidence of any procurement traction. The gap between where ACS sits today and where 9 Mothers sits is not a "we're earlier stage" gap -- it's a "we haven't entered the game yet" gap. In defense, that matters enormously because procurement relationships compound. The team that gets the first OTA contract gets the operational data, which improves the algorithms, which wins the next contract. First-mover advantage in government sales is structural, not just temporal.

Here's what I do like, and I want to be honest about the bull case because the physical-world angle resonates with my framework. EDDA is a hardware product with an AI layer on top. You cannot replicate a kinetic turret with an LLM wrapper. The manufacturing, the mechanical engineering, the testing, the field integration -- this is the kind of operational complexity I look for in a Craftwork or a Metropolis. If I apply my "cherry on top" test with some generosity, the AI targeting and tracking system makes a good physical product into a great one. The smaller, cheaper, soldier-portable positioning against ACS's heavier weapon systems could carve a genuine niche -- the military needs point defense solutions distributed down to the squad level, not just mounted on MRAPs. And the narrative can expand: from Group 1 counter-drone to broader autonomous point defense to a platform for AI-driven kinetic engagement across multiple threat categories. That's a real infinity story within defense. If 9 Mothers could secure even one meaningful government contract, the entire trajectory changes because the procurement flywheel begins spinning.

But I keep coming back to the protocol-versus-application distinction that drives my biggest returns. EDDA is a product, not a primitive. Each turret deployed doesn't create a layer that other systems integrate with. There's no ecosystem forming around it, no network effect, no compounding dynamic where success breeds more success beyond linear unit sales. Compare that to Helium building decentralized wireless infrastructure, or Solana creating a new blockchain protocol, or even Venmo establishing a peer-to-peer payment primitive. Those investments compound. A turret company scales linearly with sales effort and manufacturing capacity, constrained by defense procurement timelines. That caps the magnitude of the outcome in a way that makes me hesitant even if the product works.

The final issue that prevents me from getting to conviction: AI isn't the "cherry on top" here -- it's the entire cake. Strip out the autonomous detection, tracking, and engagement system and you have a manually operated turret, which already exists across every military in the world. The AI capability is what makes this company exist, which means the competitive moat depends on maintaining an AI performance edge against every other team -- including ACS with $30 million, defense primes with billions, and whatever the next YC batch produces -- working on the exact same computer vision problem. That's a feature race, not a structural moat. I've learned the hard way from Birchbox that narrative magnitude without structural defensibility produces disappointing outcomes. The counter-drone narrative is massive, but I don't see what structurally prevents this from being competed away once the technical approach is validated.

I respect the team's ambition and their co-founder relationship. If Smith had spent five years at Anduril or Raytheon before starting this, and if the market weren't already saturated with well-funded competitors running the same playbook, this would score differently. But credible operators in a consensus market with a funded competitor already winning contracts is not my bet.
