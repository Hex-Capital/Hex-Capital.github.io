# Cedar -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Greg Gunn and Beier Cai have been circling the same problem for over a decade, and that trajectory is the most interesting thing about Cedar. They built Hootsuite from 5 to 1,500 employees and watched referral hiring buckle under the weight of hypergrowth. Then they spent five years building Commit.dev, a professional network for startup engineers — essentially a talent graph with different plumbing. Now they're building Cedar, an AI-driven system that extracts private relationship data from employees to create a proprietary hiring graph. This is not a team that sat down and brainstormed "what's a big market." The Hootsuite-to-Commit.dev-to-Cedar arc is the kind of organic problem discovery I almost never get to see at this resolution: they lived inside the failure mode (referral programs that collapse at scale), built an adjacent company to explore the space (Commit.dev), and now they're attacking the core problem with a mechanism that didn't exist until LLMs made fifteen-minute conversational extraction feasible. Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive. Greg and Beier kept losing candidates to a broken referral process at Hootsuite. That's the same species of origin story.

The co-founder relationship deserves its own assessment. Ten years across three companies — Hootsuite, Commit.dev, Cedar — is about as battle-tested as co-founder pairs get. Beier wrote the first line of code at Hootsuite; Greg was the first business hire. They've already had every hard conversation about equity, roles, and what happens when things break. I list co-founder conflict as a top startup killer, and the strongest inoculation against it is having survived a prior company together. These two have survived two. The advisor bench — Laszlo Bock (former SVP People Ops at Google), Tiffany Fenster (former Head of Recruiting at Stripe), Brendan Browne (former VP Global Talent Acquisition at LinkedIn) — reads like a who's-who of exactly the people who understand why referral hiring at scale costs companies like Stripe $10M a year and 100 dedicated staff.

Here's where my enthusiasm hits friction: "Killing LinkedIn with Agents" is the kind of headline that makes everyone nod. Nobody pushes back on "referral hiring is better than job boards" — it's consensus. AI recruiting attracted $208M in the first eleven months of 2025. Perfect raised a $23M seed. Moonhub got acquired by Salesforce. Cedar is entering a space that, at the category level, doesn't repel anyone. The Black Swan test asks whether knowledgeable people's first reaction is skepticism or confusion. When I hear "AI for recruiting," nobody is confused. Nobody thinks it won't work. That means the returns at the category level have already been competed away. Where Cedar might pass the repulsion test is at the mechanism level: the idea of calling every employee at a company to systematically extract their private professional relationships has a quality that makes enterprise compliance teams flinch. The data sensitivity risk is real — GDPR, internal legal pushback, the general creepiness of mapping someone's personal network without their contacts' knowledge. That specific schlep could function as a moat, because most competitors (Perfect, hireEZ, Gem) stay safe by scraping public profiles. Cedar's approach requires navigating the messy, uncomfortable space of private data, and that discomfort repels the competitors who rely on clean, public datasets.

The claimed revenue — $2.5M to $3M contracted while still in stealth with four people — is either the single strongest signal in this dossier or its most dangerous distortion. If real, closing enterprise contracts of that magnitude without a public website, without a sales team, without press, with just four people, is itself the resourcefulness story I look for. That's doing things that don't scale in the extreme: Greg personally selling into enterprises using his Hootsuite network and advisor relationships. But the figure is self-reported on an accelerator application, the two numbers ($2.5M and $3M) don't match each other, and no customer names, case studies, or independent validation exist. I've seen founders round up aggressively on applications. The Commit.dev situation compounds my uncertainty — that company raised $6M and its current status is "unclear," which could mean anything from a quiet wind-down to an acqui-hire to an ongoing zombie. If Commit.dev failed or was abandoned, that's not disqualifying — I've written that founders commonly succeed only on their third or fourth try — but the opacity feels deliberate rather than incidental.

For Cedar to become a defining company, the proprietary relationship graph has to achieve sufficient density that each new customer makes the graph meaningfully more valuable for all customers — a true network effect, not just a data accumulation story. The question is whether the graph compounds across company boundaries or stays siloed within each enterprise. If Employee A at Company X mentions knowing Person B, and Person B's employer is also a Cedar customer, the graph becomes a two-sided matching engine with exponential value. If the graph stays within each enterprise, it's just a better internal referral database — valuable, but not a network-effect business. The distinction between those two outcomes determines whether Cedar is a $50M company or a $5B company. The mechanism exists for cross-company compounding, but achieving it requires navigating exactly the data-sensitivity concerns that enterprise legal teams will resist.

Cedar's founders have the deepest organic connection to this problem of anyone I've evaluated in the recruiting space. The decade-long co-founder relationship, the lived experience at Hootsuite, the five years at Commit.dev building talent infrastructure — this is the opposite of choosing an idea from a menu. The revenue signal, if roughly accurate, confirms that enterprises want this badly enough to pay before Cedar even has a public website. What holds me back from stronger conviction is that the category doesn't repel — AI recruiting is a crowded, consensus-approved space — and the specific mechanism's defensibility depends on achieving cross-company graph density that hasn't been demonstrated. The organic discovery score and co-founder quality push Cedar into Invest territory; the absence of contrarian positioning and the unverified revenue keep it at the threshold rather than deep within it.
