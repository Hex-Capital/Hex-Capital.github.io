# InsForge -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Let me think about how InsForge looks the moment the dossier opens: a six-person team in San Francisco, Spring 2026 YC, building "agent-native BaaS" against Supabase, Apache 2.0, 10.7k GitHub stars, and a self-reported benchmark claiming 1.6x speed and 1.7x accuracy over Supabase. This is, almost line-for-line, the Pass Example I keep in my own head — "AI [thing] for [vertical], some MRR, three months out of YC, tier-1s circling." InsForge's analytical signature is a developer-community distribution story (its highest dimension) bolted onto a category I actively re-weight away from (its lowest) — open-source goodwill underwriting a consensus AI-infra round. The 10.7k stars are real; the asset class is wrong.

InsForge's strongest axis is owned distribution: 10.7k GitHub stars, 919 forks, 1,036 Discord members, four Show HN launches (item IDs 44772898, 45291644, 45449787, 48181342), and 198 Product Hunt upvotes. The HIGH rung here for me is **John Fish / Bookshelved** — a founder who arrived at the round with an audience already inside the asset, which is the only kind of distribution I underwrite at seed because paid CAC erases the upside. InsForge diverges from Bookshelved in a way that matters: the audience here is *project-attached*, not *founder-attached*. The 10.7k stars belong to the repo, not to Hang Huang or Tony Chang — neither founder's X follower count was even retrievable in the dossier, neither has surfaced essays on agent infrastructure. If Supabase ships a credible MCP layer, the stars migrate; with Bookshelved, the audience moves with John. That's the difference between a community moat and a leaderboard placement.

The LOW rung on category-sleeve fit is my own canonical pass: the YC AI-infra deck where the company is the SAFE-priced commodity exposure to a consensus thesis. InsForge's positioning — "agent-native cloud infrastructure," MCP server for Claude Code and Cursor — sits in the dead center of that pattern. "AI is just more internet" (Lonsdale #113, 2024) is the relevant frame: when the interface gap is 18 months old (MCP shipped late 2024) and Supabase has already shipped its own MCP server per the sealos.io 2026 comparison, the interface advantage is a feature gap inside someone else's roadmap, not a sleeve. The crypto sleeve I'm convicted on — Aleo, Algorand, Lightning Labs — had pre-consensus pricing *and* category abandonment by tier-1s. InsForge has neither: agent-tooling is the most over-bid theme of the S26 batch.

On round dynamics (Criterion 1), the mechanism analysis is straightforward over an 18-month window: 10.7k stars plus an "$4M raised" snippet (mindworks.vc, unconfirmed) plus a Sam Altman-adjacent category equals a seed priced for a Series A. Even if the round closes at YC-standard $20M post on a $500K check, that's not the price at which I can make money against Supabase's $5B October-2025 valuation and $544M of dry powder. Consensus pricing on an interface-layer category that the incumbent can replicate in two quarters is the textbook mis-priced-against-me scenario, not mis-priced-for-me.

On capital structure (Criterion 2), the counterfactual is what tells the story: for InsForge to flip a tier here, the company would need to be either bootstrappable on usage revenue from its 2,000+ self-hosted databases or structured as something other than a standard SAFE-into-preferred SaaS cap table — a usage-based holdco, a revenue share against the open-core managed tier, anything that isn't "five rounds to compete with a $544M-funded incumbent." Nothing in the dossier suggests that posture. The pricing page exists but tiers aren't published; "5 enterprise teams" are launch partners, not revenue customers. InsForge will need to raise against Supabase's burn curve, and that's the failure mode the Slow letter calls out by name.

On founder conviction (Criterion 3), the single-anchor parallel is **Sublime Security**: a founder team that arrived with deep domain context (email security, prior detection-engineering work) and a non-obvious belief about why incumbents would fail in their specific lane. Tony Chang's Databricks networking-infra tenure is a real credential, and Hang Huang's pro-LoL-player background is the kind of weird temperament marker I actually like — competitive obsession is hard to fake. But the dossier surfaces no essays, no opinionated long-form writing on why MCP wins or why Supabase's dashboard-first architecture is structurally trapped. "Writing is thinking" (Buteau, ~2023) — and I cannot find the thinking. That's not disqualifying; it's why this dimension lands mid-low rather than high.

My 36-month forecast on InsForge: stalled between $1M and $5M ARR, with a talent-acquihire outcome to Vercel, Cloudflare, or Databricks at a price that returns roughly 1-2x to the seed — because Supabase's MCP layer compresses the interface differentiation by Q3 2027 and InsForge cannot out-raise a $5B-valuation incumbent on managed-hosting economics. The mechanism is straightforward: open-source stars convert poorly to managed-tier revenue when the dominant alternative ships the same interface for free inside an already-trusted product.

The bull case that should sting: MCP-native really is a clean-sheet interface advantage, the 1.7x accuracy and 30% token-reduction benchmarks (47.6% vs 28.6% on 127 backend tasks) are non-trivial if reproducible, and Tony Chang's Databricks networking experience is exactly the substrate for shipping infrastructure that doesn't fall over in production — the InsForge team could plausibly become the default backend the agent ecosystem picks because they shipped first and shipped opinionated. The Robinhood Known Exception almost applies: get in early enough and the consensus markup is upside, not deselection. It doesn't apply here because Slow would be entering *at* the consensus moment, not before it — Robinhood worked because the seed predated the category heat by years, and InsForge is the category heat. The single condition that would flip my verdict: an independent third-party reproduction of the InsForge-vs-Supabase benchmark on real-world agent workloads showing the 1.7x accuracy gap holds at >50% of tasks, *combined* with a round priced under $12M post — at which point the asset is mispriced in my direction, not the founders'.
