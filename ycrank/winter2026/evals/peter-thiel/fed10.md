# Fed10 -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells me everything I need to know before examining the founders. FiscalNote, a public company with $120 million in revenue, launched PolicyNote -- its AI-focused platform -- in January 2025. Quorum, with $61 million in revenue and half the Fortune 100 as customers, is integrating AI features. Plural Policy raised $14 million for AI-powered policy collaboration. USLege is pursuing AI state policy tracking with venture backing. And now Fed10 enters this same arena with the thesis that AI agents can replace policy consultants. When I count five companies and two well-capitalized incumbents all converging on the same insight at the same time, I am not looking at a secret. I am looking at a consensus. And consensus, by definition, cannot produce monopoly returns. The entire field is engaged in mimetic convergence -- each company imitating the others' thesis that LLMs applied to legislative text will displace traditional analysis. The more alike they become, the more they will destroy each other's margins. This is the structural dynamic that my Girardian framework identifies as value-destroying before the first dollar of revenue is earned.

What would a genuine secret look like in this space? It would be an insight about legislative intelligence that most policy professionals would actively argue against -- perhaps that automated analysis is *more reliable* than human consultants for high-stakes regulatory decisions, or that the real bottleneck in government affairs is not analysis but relationship-mediated access that software could restructure. But Fed10's positioning -- "completing in seconds what would take a human consultant days" -- is the most obvious application of large language models to any professional services category. This is the equivalent of saying "AI can summarize documents faster than humans." Every intelligent person agrees with this. It is not a secret; it is a truism. And truisms do not create monopolies.

The founders deserve more attention than the thesis. Armand Iorgulescu, Zayan Islam, and Winston Wei passed legislation together before they could legally vote. That is genuinely unusual. Most AI startup founders have never spoken to a legislator; these founders have drafted recommendations that became law and served as gubernatorial appointees. This is the kind of practitioner-turned-builder profile that creates authentic domain insight -- they know what policy professionals actually do because they *were* policy professionals, which is a stronger foundation than most AI-wrapper companies possess. But domain expertise is necessary, not sufficient. The question is whether their lived experience reveals a non-obvious truth about how this market works. The dossier shows no evidence of such a truth. Their positioning -- AI agents that replace consultants -- is precisely what every competitor is also saying. Domain expertise without a contrarian thesis produces a better product, not a monopoly.

The bull case requires one to believe that the incumbent platforms are structurally incapable of adding AI features to their existing products, and that Fed10's domain-informed approach will produce accuracy so superior that it becomes the de facto standard. FiscalNote's 97% stock decline and organizational distress lend some credibility to the first premise -- a company in crisis cannot innovate effectively. If Fed10 could achieve genuinely reliable automated policy analysis while FiscalNote and Quorum stumble with bolt-on AI features, the cost advantage (replacing $500/hour consultants with software) could unlock a long tail of organizations that were previously priced out of professional legislative monitoring. That market expansion -- making policy intelligence accessible to the thousands of businesses affected by regulation but unable to afford consultants -- is a real opportunity. But this argument applies equally to every competitor in the space. Nothing about Fed10's technology or positioning suggests it would capture this expansion monopolistically rather than sharing it with Plural Policy, USLege, and whichever incumbent manages to ship competent AI features first.

The technology itself is a commodity application. LLM-based document analysis applied to public legislative data -- the dossier itself notes that "the barrier to building a basic version is low." This is a 1-to-n improvement: legislative tracking and analysis already exist; Fed10 does it cheaper and faster with generally available AI tools. There is no proprietary technology, no novel algorithmic breakthrough, no data source that competitors cannot access. The underlying legislative data is entirely public. The analysis layer could develop some proprietary value over time through customer interactions and policy outcome training data, but this is speculative and unproven. When Quorum has 1,800 customers generating feedback loops and FiscalNote covers 12,000 local governments, a pre-seed company's data advantage is theoretical at best.

The go-to-market strategy compounds my concern. Fed10 appears to target "all 50 states and Congress" from inception, selling to "businesses, trade associations, and policy-impacted organizations" -- a maximally broad addressable market with no visible beachhead. The playbook I look for is the opposite: dominate a tiny, specific segment so completely that the product is indispensable to that group, then expand concentrically. Facebook started at Harvard. PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Fed10 starts with everyone. That is not monopoly strategy; that is competitive strategy, and it means they will encounter incumbents' sales organizations at every turn. Without a narrow wedge where they are 10x better for a specific user -- say, state-level cannabis policy for multi-state operators, or AI regulation tracking for technology companies -- they are fighting on terrain that Quorum and FiscalNote have occupied for years.
