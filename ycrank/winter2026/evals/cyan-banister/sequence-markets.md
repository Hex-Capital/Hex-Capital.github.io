# Sequence Markets -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Sequence Markets: this is one of the most consensus ideas I've seen in a while. Smart order routing is a concept that's been around in equities for decades -- it's how every major stock brokerage works. Importing it into crypto is a perfectly logical thing to do, and that's exactly why Talos, FalconX, CoinRoutes, and 1inch have already done it, collectively raising over $800 million. When I'm looking at a deal and I can immediately list four well-funded competitors doing the same thing, that's not my kind of bet. The most promising companies are the ones where other investors think you're crazy for writing the check. Nobody is going to think I'm crazy for backing "better trade execution in crypto." They're going to think it's sensible, and sensible at the pre-seed stage against entrenched competition is where I get nervous.

I couldn't find the founders' stories -- and I mean that literally. Peter Bai has a solid professional background: TMX Group, managing macro trading at a $12.8B pension fund, co-leading Western AI. Those are real credentials in market microstructure. But when I ask my first question -- "tell me your story, not the business" -- what I'm hearing back is a resume. Where's the hardship? Where's the moment that forged an unbreakable commitment to THIS problem? With Muhammad Awan, there's almost nothing -- his YC tagline is "I like building cool projects." I respect that building things is fun, but that phrase tells me nothing about why he's the person who needs to solve liquidity fragmentation in digital assets. This doesn't mean they lack depth -- public data at pre-seed is thin, and these could be extraordinary people whose stories just aren't documented yet. But from where I sit, I can't see the biographical thread that makes me believe they'll persist when things get genuinely dark, which in a space this competitive, they will.

The bull case here is real and I want to name it honestly. Peter Bai has the exact domain expertise you'd want for this problem -- market microstructure from TMX Group, institutional macro trading, and AI research. The gap between enterprise-scale platforms like Talos (which require massive commitments) and retail-friendly single-venue execution is genuine. If Sequence can wedge into that middle tier -- active traders and small institutions who can't access Talos but need better than Coinbase -- there's a real market. The non-custodial architecture is smart from a regulatory standpoint. And if they build a data flywheel from routing decisions, execution quality could compound in ways that create real switching costs. If I believed the founders had a deep, personal, almost spiritual commitment to this specific problem -- the kind of commitment that would keep them building through a crypto winter, through a competitor raising another $200 million, through exchange API changes that break their integrations overnight -- I'd look at this differently.

But I don't see evidence of that commitment, and the space itself doesn't light up any of my other filters. This company doesn't create economic access for people who lack it -- it optimizes execution for people who are already trading crypto across multiple exchanges. These are sophisticated, well-served users. Compare this to what Affirm did for transparent credit or what Uber created for drivers who had no income pathway -- there's no analogous unlock here for underserved populations. It also doesn't make an invisible system visible in the way Carta did for cap tables. Better price execution on crypto trades is an optimization, not a revelation. And while crypto regulation is genuinely complex, it's no longer the stigmatized, avoided category it once was -- the fact that Talos just raised at $1.5 billion tells you the institutional market has embraced this space. The regulatory complexity exists, but it's not creating the kind of moat that deters everyone else from entering.

On the co-founder dynamic, I simply don't have enough to evaluate. I can't tell how Peter and Muhammad complement each other, how they handle disagreement, or what shared experience bonds them. After HQ Trivia, this is something I probe deeply before writing a check, and the absence of any signal here keeps me cautious. Five-person team with two co-founders whose relationship is undocumented -- I'd need to sit across from them and feel the dynamic before getting comfortable.
