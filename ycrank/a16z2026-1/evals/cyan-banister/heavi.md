# Heavi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Sanjay Dasari grew up in the orbit of Ashok Leyland — his father Vinod Dasari ran one of India's largest commercial vehicle manufacturers. Then Sanjay went and got ASE T8 certified as a diesel mechanic himself. Then he built WayCool Foods into a $250M+ ARR agricultural supply chain marketplace and raised $200M in equity before exiting in late 2024. That biographical thread — executive suite to the shop floor to marketplace founder — is genuinely unusual and I respect the path. When I ask "tell me their story," Sanjay's has real connective tissue linking heavy vehicles, supply chain complexity, and marketplace execution into a coherent arc. But I keep pressing on the distinction between a founder who strategically assembled the perfect resume for this problem and one whose life made them incapable of working on anything else. The ASE T8 certification feels like preparation — deliberate, smart, credible preparation — but not the forge I look for. The diesel mechanic struggling to find a turbo actuator for a 2019 Freightliner Cascadia at 11pm because three vendors are closed and the fleet customer is losing $1,200 a day in downtime — that person was forged by the problem. Sanjay studied it. That's meaningful but different. Michael Holkesvik brings Yale CS credentials and CTO experience at NCX, where he scaled a landowner marketplace to 15,000 members, though NCX's core carbon credit program was discontinued after Verra declined certification — a regulatory failure mode worth noting. Together they've raised $275M across prior ventures, which proves fundraising capacity but not yet that Heavi's specific problem found them rather than the reverse.

The hidden system beneath Heavi's surface is real, and it's where my conviction gets warmer. Heavy-duty parts procurement today runs on institutional knowledge trapped in the heads of retiring technicians, fragmented across thousands of OEM part numbers, aftermarket cross-references, and vendor relationships that exist only in someone's phone contacts. With 65.5% of shops understaffed and a 67,000-technician shortfall projected, that knowledge is literally walking out the door. Shop owners who lack experienced techs are paying more, waiting longer, and often buying the wrong part — an information asymmetry that costs them money they don't know they're losing. This reminds me structurally of what Flexport attacked in freight forwarding: not a cartel deliberately extracting rent, but a system so fragmented and opaque that inefficiency itself becomes the hidden tax. Heavi's agent-based approach — accepting a VIN, searching OE systems and aftermarket catalogs, contacting vendors — is attempting to make that invisible procurement knowledge visible and accessible to any shop regardless of whether they have a 30-year veteran on staff. The $100K contracted revenue from five design partners reporting 30% time savings and 40% margin improvement suggests the opacity is real and the solution lands.

Where I cool off is the contrarian conviction test. "AI workforce for heavy vehicle mechanics" doesn't make anyone shift in their chair — it makes them nod approvingly. This is "AI applied to an unsexy vertical," which in 2026 is becoming one of the most consensus investment categories in the market. Nobody at a dinner party would be uncomfortable hearing this thesis; they'd call it smart and obvious. Compare that to when I wrote my Anduril check into defense tech that was politically toxic, or my Contraline check into male birth control that made VCs physically uncomfortable. Heavi operates in a market that's underserved, not stigmatized. The a16z Speedrun application itself signals how mainstream this framing has become. And the "AI wrapper" concern applies here: Heavi's parts data isn't proprietary, the catalogs and vendor networks exist independently, and Fullbay — with $19.4M in revenue, 5,000+ shops, and its March 2026 acquisition of Pitstop's AI capabilities — has both the distribution and the strategic intent to build similar procurement features. FleetPride and TruckPro, as large parts distributors, could build their own AI procurement tools with first-party inventory data Heavi would need to scrape or license.

The bull case I take seriously: Sanjay has already built a supply chain marketplace to massive scale in a fragmented, low-tech industry (Indian agriculture), and the muscle memory of navigating that kind of customer base — slow adopters, relationship-driven sales, physical-world complexity — transfers directly to heavy-duty repair shops. The $100K in contracted revenue from five partners at pre-seed, with quantified impact metrics, is a real signal that the product delivers value where it matters. If Heavi executes fast enough to accumulate proprietary fitment and pricing data across thousands of vehicle-vendor-part combinations before incumbents add AI, the data moat could compound in a Flexport-like way. The technician shortage creates genuine urgency — shops need this solution now, not in three years — and that urgency compresses the adoption cycle for a traditionally slow-moving customer base. If Sanjay's Ashok Leyland network opens OEM data partnerships that competitors can't access, the defensibility picture changes materially.

Heavi has competent, credentialed founders attacking a real market with early validation, but it doesn't activate my highest-conviction patterns. The biographical forge is strategic rather than inevitable. The space is underserved, not contrarian. The invisible system is real but driven by fragmentation rather than deliberate extraction. The moat is theoretical with a credible incumbent threat from Fullbay bearing down. What would flip me is evidence that Sanjay personally experienced the procurement pain as a working mechanic — not just studied it — and that Heavi has secured exclusive data partnerships with OEM systems that create structural access barriers. Without those signals, this is a competent pre-seed bet in a real market, but not a Long Journey bet.
