# Aseon Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Aseon Labs is the rare pre-seed where the catalyst is louder than the curve — Waymo crossed ~3,000 vehicles and >$350M annualized revenue across 10 cities by January 2026 (Time, Apr 2026 via search snippet), which is exactly the fleet-density threshold at which centralized depot dead-mile economics start to break, and Aseon Labs is selling distributed pods into that crack. The signature is straightforward: highest dimension is the market re-opening, lowest is revenue velocity, and the question is whether George Kalligeros and Dan Keene convert the Pushme operational muscle into AV-operator contracts before the oligopsony customer set vertically integrates around them.

**Anduril** (2022) sits at the high rung of the catalyst ladder — Ukraine in a 24-month window forced a specific software-on-defense re-opening that peers misread as a graveyard. **Lyft** Series C sits at the low rung — rideshare in steady state, no catalyst, just market sizing math. Aseon Labs places between Anduril and the mid rung (Brex 2017's fintech wave) and closer to Anduril: Waymo's ~$350M ARR across 10 cities (Time, Apr 2026), Zoox expanding to Austin/Miami, and Rocsys's April 2026 multi-bay launch are three independent signals that the AV-servicing market just crossed from pilot to commercial within the last 24 months. This is a *named* catalyst with dollar figures attached, not TAM theater.

**Harvey** (2023) sits at the high rung of the revenue-velocity ladder — $3B → $5B → $8B in months, the canonical "just working" curve (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). Pre-revenue AI promoters who couldn't disclose growth sit at the low rung — silence is itself a signal. Aseon Labs places between mid (Decagon 2023, real but not Harvey-shaped) and low: the dossier discloses "early pilot deployments allocated" with *unnamed* AV operators, projected customer outcomes (50% reset-cost reduction, $50K+/vehicle/year added revenue per Yahoo Finance, May 2026) but zero Aseon revenue, no named pilot counterparties, no signed LOIs with Waymo or Zoox. In a market this hot, I expect at least one named logo at launch — the absence is conspicuous, not neutral.

On founder shipping rhythm, **Perplexity** (2022) is the high rung — Aravind shipped a thing between meetings — and the pedigree-without-artifacts pattern is the low. Kalligeros and Keene place at mid-to-upper-mid: building Pushme from a Bath final-year project into 5,000 battery-swap stations across 40 cities with $300M+ in fleet hardware deployed and a TIER acquisition (Crunchbase) is industrial shipping at a scale most YC founders never see, and it is *exactly* the operational primitive — distributed energy/servicing infrastructure for vehicle fleets — that Aseon Pods replicate. This is the **Brex** (2017) archetype from *High Growth Handbook*: operationally-disciplined operators, not Perplexity-style improvisers.

On index position, **Stripe** (2010) is the high rung — an index on e-commerce, every entrant must touch the rails — and **Lyft** Series C is the low rung where you're fighting #1 for share of a network-effect market. Aseon Labs places at mid: the neutral-multi-OEM pod is *structurally* indexed on robotaxi growth (Waymo + Zoox + WeRide + Pony.ai all need servicing), but the customer set is a five-company oligopsony where Waymo already runs a 19-charger SF depot at >2 MW (Government Technology via snippet). That's not Stripe — that's a workflow layer where one of five buyers may eat you in-house. On single-miracle architecture, **Color Genomics** (2015) is the high rung — workflow on commodity sequencing — and Aseon Labs sits below the **Anduril** mid rung: components are off-the-shelf, but the company needs *integration reliability across charge+wash+inspect* (Rocsys does 99.9% on charging *alone*), *permitting across many metros*, and *Waymo/Zoox not vertically integrating*. That's two-and-a-half miracles, not one.

36-month forecast: Aseon Labs lands at $5–15M ARR with 2–3 non-Waymo AV operators (Zoox plus one autonomous delivery fleet is the likely shape), raises a Series A in the $20–40M range led by a strategic adjacent to EV charging (ChargePoint orbit) rather than a pure venture lead, and does not penetrate Waymo, which continues building owned depots. The mechanism: the catalyst is real and the Pushme operational moat is replicable for the long tail, but the largest customer in the category will keep this in-house, capping the index position at "infrastructure for everyone except the leader."

Strongest objection — the bull case: Waymo's bespoke depot model doesn't scale to 50 cities. Once Waymo expands beyond the current 10, the marginal cost of building 19-charger facilities per metro becomes prohibitive, and Kalligeros's Pushme rolodex (TIER deployed across 40 cities) is exactly the playbook Waymo *can't* replicate without distracting from the AV stack itself. The Anduril Known Exception applies in spirit: hardware-adjacent software/ops layer riding a re-opened category with founders who shipped at scale. The objection stings — Anduril's $4.6B post-money looked rich and I led it. What dismantles it here is the dossier gap: Anduril had visibly compounding contracts at the time I led; Aseon Labs has *zero* named pilot counterparties and no revenue disclosed at launch. **One verifiable condition flips this to Invest**: a named multi-pod contract with Zoox, Waymo, or a top-3 autonomous delivery operator at $1M+ ACV within 6 months. Until then this is a watch, not a check.
