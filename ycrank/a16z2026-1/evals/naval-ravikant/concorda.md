# Concorda -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Concorda's dual revenue model — "AI-native litigation services" plus B2B SaaS — is the first thing I interrogate, because it reveals a structural tension at the core of the company. Legal services is labor leverage. SaaS is code leverage. These are not complementary scaling mechanisms; they're fundamentally opposed. Every hour Samuel Oh and Ke Ma spend delivering litigation services is an hour not spent building product that works while they sleep. $204K annualized revenue in three months with two people is a genuinely strong pre-seed signal — but the critical question the dossier can't answer is which revenue line is generating it. If it's services revenue, Concorda is a small law firm using AI tools. If it's SaaS subscriptions, it's a software company with real early pull. The distinction determines whether this is code leverage or labor leverage wearing a software costume.

The specific knowledge question lands in an interesting middle ground. Oh has Harvard CS and UChicago Law, plus years of drafting motions as a practicing litigator. The lawyer-engineer is a genuinely rare archetype — most legal tech founders are either engineers guessing at legal workflows or lawyers hiring engineers to build what they describe. Oh appears to be both. His quote — "I spent years drafting motions knowing there had to be a better way" — is the kind of statement that signals a principal, not an agent. He was living inside the problem before deciding to productize it. But compare this to Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, whose specific knowledge was in the core technology itself — AI systems architecture from Google Brain and OpenAI. Oh's specific knowledge is in the domain (litigation), not in the AI systems that power the product. In the current AI landscape, domain knowledge is necessary but depreciating in value as foundation models absorb domain expertise. The durable moat comes from knowing how to build the AI systems, not from knowing the domain the AI operates in.

Ma's trajectory raises a different flag. Northwestern Law, McKinsey, Cook County ASA, then short stints at Sage, Captify, and Simon & Schuster before Concorda. No GitHub repos. No visible technical background. This is an operator's resume, not a builder's. In my framework, a team where only one person can ship code is structurally fragile — the product's entire velocity depends on Oh, while Ma handles operations, sales, and legal delivery. When you're also running a legal services practice alongside building software, one technical founder is a bottleneck that compounds. Contrast with Notion, where the entire founding team could build — that's how a small team creates a product used by millions without proportional headcount.

The leverage audit exposes Concorda's deepest vulnerability. The four product modules — Case Command, Motions & Briefs, Discovery, Claim Charts — are LLM-powered document generation with litigation-specific prompting and firm-voice calibration. This is increasingly accessible technology. Harvey AI at $190M ARR and $11B valuation already serves eight of the top ten US law firms and could add litigation-depth modules with a fraction of its engineering resources. Crimson, a YC-backed London startup with identical positioning ("AI associate for litigation lawyers"), is a direct competitor at the same stage. When I look at Concorda's product through my leverage framework, I see a company renting permissionless leverage from foundation model providers rather than owning it. The firm-specific voice models and case strategy persistence could build switching costs over time, but that's a hypothesis about future compound interest, not a structural moat today.

The strongest bull case: Oh's lawyer-engineer specific knowledge, combined with the litigation vertical's genuine underservice (Harvey is broad, EvenUp and Supio are personal-injury-only, Thomson Reuters is research-centric), creates a real wedge opportunity. The $204K revenue figure suggests actual customers are paying actual money within months of launch — that's organic pull, not manufactured traction. If Concorda can use the services wedge to land firms and then convert them to pure SaaS, the leverage architecture transforms. Small litigation firms competing against BigLaw's resources is a structural access problem that AI could solve — giving a three-partner firm the analytical output of a fifty-lawyer department. That democratization angle, while indirect (serving firms rather than individuals), is real. For this to work, Oh needs to be building the AI systems himself, not just orchestrating API calls, and the services revenue needs to be a temporary bridge, not a permanent crutch.

The compound interest test is where I remain uncertain about Concorda. Each case processed should theoretically improve firm-specific models and litigation pattern recognition — data flywheel mechanics that compound. But the commoditization pressure from foundation model improvements works against this: when GPT-5 or Claude's next generation absorbs more legal reasoning capability natively, the value of Concorda's accumulated case data may depreciate. Harvey's 8,000+ firm user base generates compound interest at 100x the rate. Concorda needs to find a compounding advantage that Harvey's breadth prevents it from matching — and the litigation-vertical-depth argument is plausible but unproven against a well-capitalized horizontal competitor.

Concorda sits in the zone where real specific knowledge meets compromised leverage architecture. Oh is a genuine principal with lived domain expertise, and the early revenue signal is meaningful. But the services/SaaS hybrid structurally limits the leverage I look for, the one-technical-founder constraint caps product velocity, and the commoditization dynamics of LLM-based document generation make me question the durability of any current product advantage. I'd want to see the services revenue decline as a percentage of total revenue over the next two quarters and evidence that Oh is building proprietary AI capabilities beyond foundation model orchestration. Without those signals, Concorda is a competent vertical play in a market where Harvey can deploy more capital and Crimson can deploy identical positioning.
