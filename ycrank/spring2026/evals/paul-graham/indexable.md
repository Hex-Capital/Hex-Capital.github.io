# Indexable -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about Andrew Gazelka isn't the company — it's Hyperion. Who builds a Minecraft game engine in Rust targeting 10,000 concurrent players in a single world? Someone who can't help themselves. That project has a thousand GitHub stars, and it's the kind of artifact that tells you more about a person than any resume line ever could. His GitHub bio says "do less, deeply" — and the body of work backs it up. Two hundred seventeen repositories, the Starstruck achievement three times, "#1 r/rust for 24 hours." This is someone who has passed right through professional and crossed into obsessive. I recognize the type. The xAI tenure and the BountyBot exit are confirming details, but Hyperion is the X-ray. That project is also an almost perfect technical precursor to what Indexable is building — managing massive concurrent mutable state is precisely the problem you solve when you fork an entire running VM including its database in 26 milliseconds.

The schlep here is real. Most founders building AI agent sandboxes reach for Firecracker or Docker and wrap it in an API. Gazelka is writing a custom virtual machine monitor on KVM, patching the Linux 6.19 kernel with non-upstream code, reimplementing Nix in Rust, and building an RDMA-capable transport layer. This is the infrastructure equivalent of the Collisons deciding to build Stripe from the banking layer up instead of wrapping PayPal. It's the kind of thing that makes experienced engineers wince and say "why would you do that?" — which is precisely the disgust reaction that creates competitive moats. E2B and Daytona are building at the application level on top of existing virtualization primitives; Indexable is building the primitives themselves. If the 26ms full-environment fork with database state holds up in production, that's not a feature advantage, it's a category difference.

But I have a real problem with the structure of this company, and it's one I've written about explicitly: Gazelka appears to be a solo founder. The YC page lists only him. The GitHub org shows only him. With a technical surface area this enormous — custom VMM, custom kernel, custom storage engine, custom Nix — the key-person risk isn't abstract, it's acute. I've seen enough startups to know that the hard part isn't building the first version; it's maintaining it while simultaneously finding customers, hiring, and not burning out. The custom kernel alone could consume weeks when something breaks. One person can't hold all of this and also do founder-led sales to AI agent platforms. I've said before that starting a startup is too hard for one person, and I especially mean it for companies where the technical depth means you can't easily hire your way out of a knowledge bottleneck.

The bull case is compelling enough that I want to articulate it clearly. If autonomous AI agents become the dominant mode of software development — and the trajectory from Claude Code to Codex to Devin suggests that's plausible — then every agent platform needs sandbox infrastructure that can handle stateful operations, not just code execution. The market is growing at 40%+ CAGR. E2B's Fortune 100 adoption proves the demand is real. And nobody else is building from the hypervisor level. Gazelka's technical skills are an almost perfect match for this problem — the Hyperion-to-VMM lineage is as natural a founder-market fit as I've seen. If he finds a co-founder with complementary go-to-market instincts, and if the 26ms forking benchmark translates to a product that agent platforms can't live without, this could be the Stripe of AI infrastructure. The approach is contrarian in execution even if the category is hot, and the technical moat is genuine — you can't replicate a custom VMM by throwing money at Docker containers.

What keeps me from the Invest tier is the accumulation of absent signals. I don't know if Gazelka discovered this problem organically at xAI or identified it through market analysis — and that distinction matters enormously in my framework. The website returns a 403. There are no public repos, no press mentions, no inclusion in any sandbox comparison article. "Early access DM" on Twitter with 86 followers is not evidence of desperate early users banging down the door. The competitive field is dense with well-funded players who already have customers. I'm not penalizing the pre-seed stage — I'm noting that at this stage, the most important signal would be a small number of users who can't live without this product, and that signal is completely absent from the record. The idea itself — sandboxes for AI agents — is consensus enough that it shows up in every cloud provider's product roadmap. The contrarianism lives entirely in the approach, which earns partial credit but doesn't give you the return premium of an idea that initially sounds wrong.

Gazelka has the technical intensity I look for. The schlep is real. But a solo founder building the entire virtualization stack from the kernel up, entering a crowded market with no visible traction, asking me to bet that the approach moat will hold against competitors with 10-20x more capital — that's a collection of risks that the builder quality alone doesn't overcome. Find a co-founder who complements the kernel wizardry with distribution instincts, get five agent platforms integrating the fork API into their core workflow, and this becomes a very different conversation.
