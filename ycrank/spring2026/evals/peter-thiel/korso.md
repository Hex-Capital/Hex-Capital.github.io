# Korso -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Five funded competitors building the same product with the same technology in the same market. That is not a competitive landscape slide — it is a diagnosis. Paperless Parts has $30 million and an established customer base. Uptool raised $6 million from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins — in a seed round, for a company founded in 2024, doing essentially the same thing Korso describes. Mercura AI, Gendra, Endeavor AI — the list continues. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is something that at least five other venture-backed teams independently arrived at, that answer is not a secret. It is a consensus thesis dressed in the language of AI novelty. "Manufacturers have inefficient quoting workflows that LLMs can automate" is an observation that appeared in a Khosla Ventures memo before it appeared in Korso's pitch. The existence of this crowded field is not incidental to my evaluation — it is the evaluation.

The underlying technology compounds the problem. The dossier states plainly that "the underlying LLM/RAG technology is broadly available." Korso's claimed differentiation is feature bundling — end-to-end coverage from RFQ ingestion through PO tracking, with WhatsApp and email as channels. But feature scope is not a moat. It is a roadmap, and roadmaps are trivially replicable. Paperless Parts can add WhatsApp ingestion in a quarter. Uptool can append PO tracking. When your differentiation can be described as "we do steps one through five while they only do steps two through four," you have not escaped competition — you have described a temporary feature lead that any funded competitor can close. The canonical question is whether the company possesses proprietary technology that is ten times better than existing alternatives, network effects that compound with usage, economies of scale that crush smaller entrants, or branding that commands loyalty. I see none of these. The data moat hypothesis — that accumulated pricing history creates switching costs — is real in theory but requires years of customer accumulation to materialize, years during which better-capitalized competitors are accumulating the same data faster.

The strongest bull case requires me to infer a strategy the founders have not articulated. Perhaps WhatsApp ingestion signals a geographic wedge — targeting manufacturers in Latin America or Southeast Asia where WhatsApp is the primary business channel, a segment the U.S.-focused competitors would ignore. That would constitute a genuine small-market monopoly play: dominate quoting automation for WhatsApp-native manufacturers in a specific region before expanding. If the founders possessed deep domain knowledge of, say, Brazilian metal fabrication supply chains, and had built relationships with specific manufacturing clusters, and could demonstrate that their RAG system was trained on Portuguese-language technical drawings — that would be a secret. It would be a specific insight about a specific market that the Khosla-backed competitor overlooking it from San Francisco could not easily replicate. But the dossier contains no evidence of this or any comparable wedge strategy. Three recent Penn undergraduates with a "Contact Sales" button and no visible traction do not yet constitute a definite plan to monopolize anything.

The founders bring relevant technical backgrounds — Martin Pan's GM automations experience and Daichi Hiraoka's ML research provide genuine manufacturing and AI grounding. The shared Penn Electric Racing history between Hiraoka and Pan offers some co-founder alignment, which matters. But these are internship-level credentials in a market where Paperless Parts has been selling to machine shops for years. This is a pure software play serving the physical world without touching it — no hardware, no regulatory barrier, no government customer creating structural lock-in. Compare this to Hadrian, which builds AI-powered manufacturing equipment that physically produces defense components, creating a moat no software wrapper can replicate. Korso sits on the opposite end of that spectrum: a SaaS layer on commodity LLMs, competing for attention in a market already saturated with funded alternatives. Competition is not an obstacle to be overcome through superior execution — it is a structural condition that destroys value for all participants.
