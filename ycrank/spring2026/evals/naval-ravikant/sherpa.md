# Sherpa -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Optimizely generates $400M in ARR selling manual experimentation tools. Sherpa's bet is that an AI agent can automate the entire CRO loop — analysis, variant generation, testing, deployment — and render that manual workflow obsolete. The architecture of the bet is clean. The problem is that Sherpa's core capability — calling an LLM to generate headline variants and page layouts — is available to every competitor through the same foundation model APIs. When I evaluate whether a company's leverage is owned or rented, Sherpa's answer is rented. The 4.3M+ visits of learning data create some proprietary signal, but the data flywheel is nascent, and at pre-seed volumes it's a rounding error compared to what Optimizely or VWO accumulate daily across their existing customer bases. The technology layer here is a wrapper around generally available models. Innovation without a moat flows to the customer as consumer surplus — and eventually to the foundation model provider.

Ethan Kinnan studied finance at the University of Washington and served as Head of Partnerships at Lindy. Norbu Sonam studied computer science at RPI with an AI concentration and worked as a software engineer at Lindy, with internships at Microsoft on Copilot image generation and at IBM. Both have relevant AI agent-building skills. Neither has spent years obsessively studying conversion rate optimization. They didn't arrive at Sherpa because they couldn't stop thinking about how A/B testing should work — they arrived because they worked at an AI agent company and identified CRO as an application domain. That's market research, not specific knowledge. Contrast with Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity, whose years at Google Brain and OpenAI meant his specific knowledge WAS the product. If Ethan and Norbu disappeared tomorrow, a competent AI engineer with CRO domain knowledge could rebuild Sherpa's core functionality in weeks. The specific knowledge here is generic — how to orchestrate AI agents — and it's precisely the kind of knowledge that society can train someone else to replicate.

Sherpa's leverage audit reveals a structural problem the founders may not have considered. When I backed Perplexity, the moat wasn't just Srinivas's expertise — it was Google's principal-agent problem. Google's search team has the knowledge to build AI search, but the ad revenue model makes them agents of their own P&L, structurally unable to cannibalize their core business. Sherpa faces the opposite dynamic: Optimizely's agents can add LLM-powered variant generation as a feature that *enhances* rather than cannibalizes their existing experimentation platform. There is no business model conflict preventing incumbents from absorbing Sherpa's core innovation. Webflow already acquired Intellimize for exactly this reason. VWO and AB Tasty merged under Everstone Capital in January 2025 with AI roadmaps. The incumbents own the customer relationship, the experimentation infrastructure, and the data. Sherpa is building on terrain where the incumbents can advance without retreating.

Sherpa's two-person team shipping 40 experiments in 30 days for Lindy, yielding +30% total conversion lift, is genuine output. Two founders serving three named customers — Lindy, Gauge, Yeti Co — with a single-line-of-code integration is the kind of team-to-value ratio that gets my attention. This is where Sherpa scores highest against my framework. Norbu's 29 public GitHub repos and engineering background at Microsoft suggest a builder, not a pitch artist. The product exists and produces measurable results. If I were evaluating Sherpa purely on team leverage density, two people generating verifiable conversion lift across multiple customers is the signal that companies like early Notion emitted — small teams creating disproportionate output.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Sherpa could be the company that owns the full autonomous CRO loop before incumbents wake up. The 30% lift number for Lindy across 40 experiments is not trivial — if repeatable across hundreds of customers, it represents millions in incremental revenue for Sherpa's clients. Market consolidation — Intellimize absorbed by Webflow, VWO/AB Tasty merging — creates a window where incumbents are distracted by integration. The data flywheel, if Sherpa reaches sufficient customer density, could compound into a proprietary advantage that pure technology replication cannot match. And the "why now" is real: LLM capabilities crossed a quality threshold in 2024-2025 that makes autonomous variant generation credible for the first time. Sherpa's democratization angle — giving marketing teams without engineering resources access to continuous experimentation — maps to my AngelList pattern of unlocking access to capabilities previously gatekept by technical specialists. But the bull case requires believing that orchestration speed and data accumulation outrun incumbent feature absorption — and Sherpa's single-customer concentration around Lindy, the founders' former employer, weakens the evidence that this is repeatable with cold prospects. One verifiable condition that would flip me: if Sherpa demonstrates the same +20% lift results across five or more customers acquired without a prior relationship, within the next two quarters, the data flywheel thesis becomes investable.

Sherpa scores strongest on team-to-value leverage and weakest on specific knowledge productization. A two-person team producing measurable conversion gains is real. But the founders' knowledge is general-purpose AI agent building, not the deep CRO-specific expertise that would make them irreplaceable. The leverage is rented from foundation models and vulnerable to incumbent absorption. The principal-agent dynamics favor the incumbents, not the startup. I pass — not because the founders are untalented, but because the architecture of the bet doesn't create the kind of compounding, proprietary advantage that produces power-law returns. The right structure would be a founder who spent five years as head of CRO at a major e-commerce company, built their own testing infrastructure out of frustration, and then automated it with AI. That founder's specific knowledge would be the product. Sherpa's founders have the AI skills but not the domain density.
