# Regbase -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Natalie Aresta-Katz and Isiah Godoy both worked on the ESG practice research team at Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison — one of the most prestigious law firms on the planet — where they manually tracked regulations across 50 countries for clients who needed answers yesterday. When I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is professional origin, not biographical inevitability. They saw a gap in the workflow they were performing daily, and they built Regbase to fill it. That's legitimate founder-market fit. They know the buyer, the workflow, the pain. But I've learned to distinguish between founders who identified a problem through their career and founders whose entire life trajectory made them incapable of walking past this problem. The Paul, Weiss story reads like "we were at a top firm and saw the opportunity" — exactly the pattern that makes me pause. Ethan Shea's technical credentials are strong — Microsoft, Citadel, staff engineer leading agentic AI at Vercel — but his connection to the regulatory domain appears to be Regbase itself, not a deeper thread. I don't see evidence that any of the three founders would be working on this without funding, which is always my litmus test.

Regbase operates in regulatory intelligence, a space where CUBE Global has raised $22M and acquired Thomson Reuters' Regulatory Intelligence division, where AscentAI raised $27M before being acquired by Edgewater Capital, and where Thomson Reuters itself covers 750+ regulatory bodies across 130+ countries. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift uncomfortably in their chairs. Nobody at a dinner party would raise an eyebrow if you said "I'm building AI for regulatory compliance." VCs understand this market, they're actively funding it, and the thesis requires zero courage to articulate. Regbase's angle — subnational, non-English, low-media-coverage sources — is a genuinely smart wedge, but it's a positioning choice within a consensus category, not a contrarian conviction in a hard space. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Regbase makes nobody uncomfortable, and that absence of discomfort is data for me.

Regbase's strongest signal, the one that gives me genuine pause before passing, is the hidden system underneath: regulatory information scattered across thousands of obscure local government websites, content locked behind QR codes, filings in dozens of languages that no single platform indexes comprehensively. That IS an opaque structure. The case study where Regbase identified $400M in expiring school safety grants for a protective equipment client proves the point — that money was sitting there, invisible, because the system that published it was fragmented beyond anyone's ability to monitor manually. When I think about Flexport mapping the complexity of global logistics so that smaller businesses could compete in international trade, I see a structural parallel: Regbase is mapping the complexity of global regulation so that compliance teams can see what was previously invisible. But here's where my framework distinguishes: the taxi medallion system was deliberately extractive — it concentrated power and extracted rents from riders. The regulatory fragmentation Regbase attacks isn't deliberately extractive; it's bureaucratically chaotic. The opacity hurts corporations and law firms who can afford the 100-hour manual project, not vulnerable people without power or information. The second-order cascade — missed environmental compliance leading to community harm, missed safety grants leaving schools unprotected — is real but indirect.

The bull case an advocate would make is compelling: Regbase's data moat from indexing thousands of fragmented sources with custom ingestion and human verification is operationally hard to replicate, even for a well-funded competitor. The timing convergence is strong — LLMs crossing the capability threshold for multilingual document parsing while ESG, AI governance, and climate disclosure regulations explode in volume globally. The unnamed Fortune 500 customers, if real, represent extraordinary pre-seed traction. And the team composition — two domain experts who literally did the manual work plus a technical co-founder with agentic AI experience from Vercel — is well-architected for the problem. I checked my known exceptions: could this be like RTFKT, where a narrow timing window at a cultural inflection overrides the absence of contrarian signal? The regulatory explosion IS a genuine inflection. But RTFKT had the "magically weird" quality that made people do a double-take; Regbase has the quality that makes people nod approvingly. Approval is not the same as conviction.

Regbase's defensibility sits at an interesting threshold. The platform indexes sources that Thomson Reuters and CUBE structurally underserve — those incumbents optimized for major national regulatory bodies and financial services content, which is a fundamentally different ingestion architecture than crawling obscure local government sites across languages and formats. Each new source Regbase adds requires custom pipelines and validation, creating compounding operational barriers. But CUBE's acquisition of Thomson Reuters RI shows incumbents are actively consolidating capabilities, and general-purpose LLMs are improving at exactly the parsing and translation tasks that form Regbase's technical core. A 3-person team racing against this commoditization trajectory needs to build coverage depth fast. The human verification layer builds trust-based switching costs with compliance-sensitive buyers — nobody wants to change their regulatory intelligence provider — but it also constrains how quickly Regbase can scale coverage.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if I could sit with Natalie or Isiah and hear a story about WHY regulatory opacity is personal — not professional, personal — something from their lives before Paul, Weiss that connects to why hidden systems that harm people who can't navigate them is the problem they were born to solve. Because the infrastructure-at-inflection pattern is real here, the data moat potential is genuine, and the timing is right. What's missing is the biographical forge that tells me these founders will survive the valley of death that every startup enters. Crusoe Energy nearly died in 2020 and the founders held because they were forged for it. I need to know Regbase's founders are forged, not just credentialed. Without that, this is a competent team building a solid product in a consensus market — and that's exactly the kind of bet I leave for other investors.
