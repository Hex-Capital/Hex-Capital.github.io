# Ontora -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: the dossier itself acknowledges that "barriers to replication are moderate" and the core technology is "broadly available foundation models." When your own research memo is telling you the moat is thin, listen to it. Ontora is building AI agents that interview employees and map organizational processes -- and the underlying stack is RAG plus LLMs plus enterprise integrations. You know who can build that? Celonis, which has $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in revenue and is already expanding into AI-powered process intelligence. Accenture, which has 700,000 employees and builds exactly this kind of thing as internal tooling. Hell, any mid-tier systems integrator with a competent AI team could spin up this product in a quarter. When I look at a company and the first thing I think is "this is a feature for someone who already has distribution," that's my AI kill test failing in real time.

Now, the consensus mispricing question: is the market wrong about AI agents for enterprise? No. The market is wildly enthusiastic about AI agents for enterprise. This is the hottest category in venture right now. Half of every YC batch is some variation of "we deploy AI agents that automate [professional service]." Consulting automation specifically is practically a meme -- it's the second thing anyone says after "AI will replace lawyers." There is no narrative mispricing here. If anything, the category is overpriced relative to the actual defensibility of any individual player. When every VC at demo day is nodding along because the TAM slide says "$1 trillion consulting market," the upside is already baked into every term sheet in the room.

The strongest bull case deserves honest engagement: McKinsey, BCG, and Bain structurally cannot build this product because it cannibalizes their billable-hour model. That's a real innovator's dilemma, and it's the kind of structural paralysis that occasionally creates genuine openings. If Ontora nails the product and the big three consulting firms spend five years in denial the way newspapers spent five years ignoring Craigslist, there's a real window. The Vertiv testimonial -- "what used to take months, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" -- suggests the product actually works. And Arnold's experience building CoAnalyst at Porsche, an agentic RAG system for SAP procurement data, is directly relevant domain work, not some random pivot into enterprise AI. If you squint, you can see a version of this where the consulting firms' inability to self-disrupt creates a genuine gap and Ontora fills it before Celonis or Worktrace AI get there. But that "if" is doing enormous work in that sentence, and the gap is being attacked from every direction simultaneously.

The primitive test kills this for me. Ontora isn't creating a new behavior -- it's automating an existing one. Management consulting interviews have existed for decades. Process mapping has existed for decades. Ontora does them faster and cheaper with AI. That's a 10x improvement on an existing workflow, not a new category. Compare this to Venmo, which created the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments -- something that literally didn't exist before. Or Teamshares, which created employee-owned small business rollups as an investable category. Ontora is "better consulting," and "better X" is exactly the kind of bet where execution matters more than insight, and where the player with more capital and distribution wins. Celonis adding a conversational interview layer to their existing process mining platform is a product sprint, not a strategic pivot.

On the founders: Arnold is clearly competent -- Porsche AI work, strong educational pedigree across CODE Berlin, Stanford, and ETH Zürich. But the idea isn't insane. It's one of the most obvious applications of current LLM technology. My framework requires a sane person pitching an insane idea, and "AI agents replace consultants" is about as consensus-sane as it gets. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's crazy" -- they think "obviously, tell me why you win." And the co-founder situation is opaque: no visible shared history between the founders, one co-founder (David Korn) is essentially a ghost in public records. I can't evaluate the team dynamic when a third of the team is invisible.

The capital discipline looks fine on the surface -- three people, lean, no open headcount -- but enterprise sales for organizational diagnostics requires a GTM machine that doesn't exist yet. You're selling to operations VPs and strategy teams at companies like Vertiv, which means procurement cycles, security reviews, and six-month evaluation processes. Building that sales motion on a 3-person team with YC money is possible but extremely constrained. The real question is whether they'd need to raise aggressively to build enterprise distribution before Celonis eats their lunch, which puts them right back on the factory-farm fundraising treadmill I think is dying.

Pass. This is a competent team building an obvious product in a consensus-hot category with moderate-to-low structural defensibility. The pricing is wrong -- not because the market undervalues it, but because the market is overvaluing the entire category of AI application-layer startups relative to their actual moat depth. I'd rather find the weird, uncomfortable, contrarian bet that nobody else wants to touch.
