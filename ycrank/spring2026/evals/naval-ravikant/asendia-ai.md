# Asendia AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Asendia AI started life as "FAANG," a B2C career coaching chatbot for job seekers, then pivoted to selling voice AI screening agents to staffing agencies. That pivot is the first thing I evaluate, because pivots reveal whether specific knowledge drove the company or market research did. Rihab Lajmi conducted 35+ recruiter interviews and went through "dozens of interviews herself" at Google, Microsoft, and Amazon. That's diligence. It's not specific knowledge. Specific knowledge in recruiting would look like someone who spent a decade inside a staffing agency watching the workflows break, who built internal tools that colleagues begged to use, who understood placement economics at the unit level before writing a line of product code. Rihab and Badis Zormati have strong cloud architecture and AI engineering backgrounds -- Google Cloud, Microsoft Cloud, Infineon -- but these are transferable technical skills. Society can train those. A competitor with the same resume lines and access to the same APIs could build an equivalent product. Asendia AI's core insight -- "staffing agencies need voice AI for screening" -- is visible to anyone who reads a Staffing Industry Analysts report.

Asendia AI's leverage architecture has a structural problem I see in roughly half the AI companies pitched to me: they are renting someone else's leverage. The dossier itself flags it -- "voice AI screening agents can be built on top of commodity LLMs and telephony APIs (Twilio, etc.)." Speech-to-text, LLM-based evaluation, text-to-speech -- every component is available off the shelf. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had built the systems that make AI search work from the inside. Asendia AI is calling APIs that OpenAI and ElevenLabs built. The 50+ ATS integrations are real work -- Greenhouse, Bullhorn, Lever, Ashby -- and create genuine switching costs. But integration depth is plumbing, not leverage. It doesn't compound. A well-funded competitor like Humanly ($52M raised, YC-backed, 9,000 interviews per day) or a revitalized Sense HQ ($90M raised, $500M valuation) can replicate every integration Asendia AI has built, with ten engineers working for three months. The code leverage exists in form but not in substance -- Asendia AI has zero-marginal-cost screening, but so does every other company with an OpenAI API key and a Twilio account.

The bull case is real and I want to state it clearly. Workday acquired Paradox for its enterprise HCM suite, which will likely deprioritize standalone staffing agency customers -- creating a structural channel conflict that leaves Asendia AI's exact buyer underserved. The $600B global staffing industry has under 1% AI penetration. Asendia AI already has ~10 paying clients across US and European markets in healthcare, IT, and blue-collar verticals, with SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA certifications -- genuine compliance work that takes months for a 3-person team. HireVue charges $35K+/year for enterprise-only async video; Asendia AI targets a different buyer at presumably lower price points with live voice screening. The timing argument -- voice AI quality crossing usability thresholds in 2024-2025 -- is valid. An advocate would say this is a vertical SaaS wedge into an enormous, sleepy market with a structural opening created by Workday's acquisition. I check my Known Exceptions: does the Varda pattern apply, where the technology creates a genuinely new form of leverage? No -- voice AI screening is not physically impossible for competitors. Does the Clearview pattern apply, where the capability is so unprecedented it overrides philosophical concerns? Also no -- the capability is commodity.

Three people building 50+ ATS integrations, compliance certifications, and serving ~10 paying clients across two continents is genuine output. Asendia AI scores well on team-to-value leverage -- Badis Zormati's 6 years of AI engineering at Infineon and Rihab Lajmi's cloud architecture experience translate into a team that ships without needing to hire an army. The founding engineer job posting ($150K-$180K, 0.50-1.00% equity) suggests they plan to stay lean. But I keep returning to the compound interest test: does Asendia AI's advantage compound over time? The data flywheel -- screening interactions linked to placement outcomes improving matching accuracy -- is theoretically present but the dossier notes "no public evidence this flywheel is producing measurable results yet." Without a compounding mechanism, Asendia AI is in a footrace with better-capitalized competitors, and footraces reward labor leverage, not the permissionless kind.

Rihab Lajmi left Google at 27 to build this. Both founders left Tunisia at 18 for Germany, studied and worked in Munich, and are building together as a couple. I don't penalize couple co-founders categorically -- the shared context can create trust that takes co-founders years to build otherwise. But the principal authenticity question remains: would Rihab and Badis be building AI recruiting tools if no one would fund them? The pivot from B2C career coaching to B2B staffing agency tooling suggests they followed the market to the money, which is rational but is agent behavior, not principal behavior. A principal would have been building for staffing agencies before the pivot -- because they couldn't stop. The Product Hunt traction (75 upvotes, #2 of the day) was for the prior B2C product. Asendia AI's current B2B identity is less than 18 months old.

Asendia AI democratizes access to AI screening for staffing agencies that can't afford HireVue's $35K+ price point -- but this is cost reduction, not structural access expansion. Staffing agencies already screen candidates. Asendia AI makes it faster and cheaper. Compare this to AngelList giving founders access to angel investors they literally could not reach before, or CoinList removing securities lawyers from token launches. Asendia AI removes labor hours, not gatekeepers. The access pattern is present but shallow.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Asendia AI's placement-outcome data flywheel is producing measurably better candidate-job matching than competitors -- something proprietary that compounds with usage and cannot be replicated by calling the same APIs. That would transform this from a commodity wrapper into a specific-knowledge engine. Without it, Asendia AI is innovation without a moat. The value flows to staffing agencies (lower screening costs) and eventually to the foundation model providers (who capture the AI margin). The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Specific Knowledge Productization -- where the B2C-to-B2B pivot and transferable technical backgrounds place this below the absent-evidence default -- and Permissionless Leverage Architecture, where commodity AI components prevent genuine compounding despite the correct structural form. Pass.
