# Sponge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three engineers who built Stripe's crypto payments infrastructure are now building payments infrastructure for AI agents. The Stripe parallel is obvious and tempting -- maybe too tempting. When the Collison brothers started Stripe, they were solving a problem every programmer had personally experienced: integrating payments was needlessly painful, and the pain had been hiding in plain sight for years because nobody wanted to deal with banks and compliance. The question I keep asking about Sponge is: did these founders discover the agent payments problem the way the Collisons discovered the developer payments problem, or did they look at a hot market category and apply their resume to it?

I don't see evidence of organic discovery here. The dossier describes their Stripe Crypto background and then connects it to the agent economy thesis, but there's no story of a specific moment where one of them tried to make an agent pay for something and hit a wall. No "I kept running into this problem in my own work" narrative. Instead, the construction looks like: "We're payments experts. AI agents are the next big thing. Agents will need payments. Let's build that." This is exactly the brainstorming-session origin I worry about -- smart people with relevant skills picking a trending category rather than stumbling into a genuine problem. The agent payments space already has Skyfire ($9.5M), Natural ($9.8M), Payman ($13.8M), and Nevermined ($7M). When four funded companies are already building in your category, it's not schlep blindness keeping people away. It's a gold rush.

The strongest bull case is that these founders have possibly the most relevant background imaginable. They didn't just work at Stripe -- they built Stripe's stablecoin financial accounts and crypto payment systems. That's the exact infrastructure this product requires. And the two-sided marketplace vision (Wallet + Gateway + Catalog) is more architecturally ambitious than competitors who focus on a single side. If agent commerce actually takes off, the team that builds the marketplace where agents discover and pay for services -- not just the payment rail -- could capture outsized value. The parallel would be Stripe expanding from a payment API into an economic infrastructure layer. For this to be a great investment, two things need to be true simultaneously: the agent economy needs to arrive on a timeline that matters (next 2-3 years, not 5-10), and Sponge needs to establish the marketplace network effect before Stripe itself builds x402 into a full-stack agent commerce product. That second condition is the hard one, because their former employer just launched agent payments support in February 2026.

On the technical side, I'm genuinely impressed. Three CS-trained engineers from top programs (Waterloo, UofT), all with serious industry experience (Stripe, Google, Facebook), and they've already built a working product with a TypeScript SDK, MCP integration for Claude, multi-chain support, and security controls. These are people who can build. The co-founder relationship also passes my test -- they worked together at Stripe Crypto, not three strangers who met at a hackathon. That matters.

But the market structure concerns me more than the team's capabilities can offset. Stripe launched x402 agent payments on Base the same month Sponge is in YC. Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, and Coinbase all announced agent payment toolkits. When every major payment incumbent is building in your category, you're not attacking a problem that's been hiding in plain sight -- you're competing for a problem everyone can see. The fact that this is consensus makes me nervous. The traction signals -- 19 YC launch upvotes, "multiple teams" using the platform, an Amazon beta pilot whose details are sparse -- don't yet demonstrate the kind of organic user pull that would override my category concerns. I'd want to see agents autonomously spending real money through Sponge at meaningful volume before I'd get excited.

This is a competent team in a real but crowded market, building something that might matter enormously or might get absorbed into Stripe's product suite within two years. The founders can build, and they know the domain. What's missing is the organic origin story that would tell me this company exists because these specific founders couldn't not build it, rather than because they identified a market opportunity and matched it to their skills. The difference matters because the next three years of building agent financial infrastructure will require the kind of irrational persistence that only comes from genuine obsession with the problem, not from rational career optimization.
