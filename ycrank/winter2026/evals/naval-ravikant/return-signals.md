# Return Signals -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first question I always ask: what does this founder know that can't be taught? Ilya Valmianski built SmartTriage at Kaiser Permanente -- described as one of the largest patient-facing ML applications in deployment -- and built medical conversational AI at Curai Health. That's real specific knowledge. Building conversational AI that works on real humans at scale, where the stakes are health outcomes, is genuinely hard. You can't learn that from a course. But here's the tension: his specific knowledge is about the *instrument* -- conversational AI -- not the *domain* -- e-commerce returns. He's a surgeon who learned his craft in one operating theater and is now performing in another. The technique transfers. The anatomical knowledge doesn't. Nobody on this founding team appears to have spent years inside the apparel supply chain understanding why customers return a size 8 dress versus exchange it for a 6. The co-founder Alejandro comes from proptech BD, which is tangential at best. I look for founders whose specific knowledge qualifies them uniquely for *this particular problem* -- Williams at Twitter had spent a decade on public communication, not just on building web apps. Here, the knowledge is about the method, not the problem.

The leverage test is where this falls short for me. Return Signals is a B2B SaaS tool that helps brands reduce returns via SMS conversations. Each new customer requires integration, onboarding, brand-specific tuning of the conversational model. Brand A's sizing data doesn't help Brand B -- different products, different customers, different return patterns. There's no network effect, no protocol, no platform dynamic where usage by one party makes the system more valuable for everyone else. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question-answer pair compounds the knowledge graph for all users, or Notion, where every template created by one user becomes leverage for millions. Return Signals is standard vertical SaaS with an AI layer. That's not zero leverage -- software has baseline leverage by definition -- but it's not creating a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. It's applying existing AI capabilities to a specific workflow. The distinction matters because leverage is what separates a business that scales linearly with headcount from one that scales exponentially with code.

The contrarian timing argument is weak. "AI for customer support" is one of the most crowded categories in 2026 -- it's consensus, not contrarian. The specific angle of *proactive* post-delivery engagement rather than reactive support is a clever positioning choice, but it's a feature differentiation, not a worldview that smart people actively dismiss. Nobody is arguing that reducing returns is a bad idea. The "business model conflict" argument -- that Attentive and Klaviyo optimize for message volume while Return Signals optimizes for margin preservation -- is interesting but fragile. Attentive at $500M ARR has the engineering resources to ship a post-delivery conversational feature in a quarter. Loop Returns at $176M raised already owns the returns workflow and could layer AI conversations on top. When your moat is "the incumbents haven't built this feature yet," you're in a race, not a position.

The bull case I want to take seriously: if the claimed metrics hold at scale -- 61-80% response rates, 70% conversion of return intents to exchanges, 5-10% absolute margin uplift -- the product essentially sells itself. A DTC apparel brand with $50M revenue and 30% return rates would save millions. Ilya's experience building conversational AI at Kaiser scale is directly relevant to the core technical challenge of making AI messages feel human enough to get responses. The Emotive acquisition removed the closest competitor. And the data feedback loop -- product-specific sizing feedback, return-reason patterns, customer sentiment -- could compound over time within each brand relationship. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) the domain-specific data becomes a genuine moat faster than incumbents can replicate it, (b) the narrow apparel vertical expands naturally to all high-return e-commerce categories, and (c) the founders develop deep e-commerce domain knowledge quickly enough to stay ahead of well-funded competitors who already have it. That's three things that have to go right simultaneously, and I prefer bets where one thing going right is sufficient.

The core technology -- LLM-driven SMS conversations -- is accessible to any competent team with API access to foundation models. The defensibility, if it exists, would come from brand-specific conversational models and product-level return data accumulated over time. But at pre-seed, this moat is entirely hypothetical. The underlying science isn't novel, the protocol isn't proprietary, and the integration layer is standard e-commerce infrastructure (Shopify, Gorgias). This is an execution play, not a technology compounding play. I look for investments where the technology gets fundamentally harder to replicate with each passing year. Here, a well-funded competitor could build a comparable product in months.

This is a competent technical founder applying real conversational AI expertise to a reasonable niche problem. But it's an application-layer play in a space where I'd want to see infrastructure. It's a domain transfer where I'd want to see domain obsession. It's a feature where I'd want to see a platform. The returns are likely capped by the dynamics of vertical SaaS selling cost-reduction to mid-market brands -- not the asymmetric, power-law outcomes I optimize my portfolio for.
