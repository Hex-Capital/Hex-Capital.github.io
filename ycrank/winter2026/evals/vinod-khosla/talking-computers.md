# Talking Computers -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most telling fact about this company is what the incumbents are doing. When I backed Juniper Networks, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, the meat industry considered plant-based heme a joke. When I wrote my largest check ever into OpenAI in 2019, most VCs thought AI was a research curiosity. In every case, the incumbents dismissed or ignored the space. Here, the opposite is true: Microsoft has merged AutoGen into a unified Agent Framework with enterprise GA targeted for this quarter. Google launched A2A protocol. OpenAI ships Swarm and Assistants API. Anthropic has MCP. Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent inquiries. When every major platform company is actively building in your category, you are not skating to where the puck will be — you are standing where every other player on the ice is already converging. That is the most dangerous competitive configuration for a two-person pre-seed startup.

Let me start where I always start: consequence of success. If Talking Computers succeeds at building "the workplace for AI agents," what changes? Enterprises coordinate their AI systems more efficiently. That is a real product solving a real problem, and the projected $52 billion agentic AI market by 2030 confirms demand exists. But making enterprise AI agents talk to each other more smoothly is an operational improvement, not an industry transformation. Nobody's food system changes. No trillion-dollar incumbent becomes structurally obsolete. The world operates roughly the same way, just with better-coordinated software agents. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means replacing fossil fuel power generation entirely, or Impossible Foods, where success means reclaiming half the planet's arable land. The gap in consequence magnitude is enormous. This company is building middleware — useful, potentially profitable middleware — but middleware whose success case is "enterprises deploy AI agents with less friction." I have spent my career arguing that smart people should not waste their ambition on problems this operationally bounded.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. The argument goes like this: agent-to-agent communication is an emerging protocol layer, analogous to HTTP for the web. Incumbents face structural lock-in incentives — Microsoft wants agents on Azure, Google on GCP, OpenAI within its ecosystem. None of them can credibly build a vendor-neutral communication standard because neutrality undermines their platform strategy. A Switzerland-of-agents play could capture extraordinary value if it establishes the interoperability standard. The founders have genuinely relevant technical backgrounds: Parsa's work in Rust systems programming, compilers, and SAT-based package resolution at prefix.dev, combined with Zayaan's inference optimization experience at Modular (a serious ML infrastructure company) and GPU programming expertise, covers the systems-level and ML-level competencies you would need. If multi-agent systems evolve the way microservices did — from monolithic orchestration to distributed, protocol-based communication — the neutral middleware layer could be immensely valuable. I take this seriously. But the "if" is doing a lot of work. And Relevance AI, with $24 million in funding from Bessemer, is already building a "no-code multi-agent workforce platform" that sounds structurally identical.

What concerns me beyond the competitive dynamics is the absence of any contrarian signal. The founders are technically capable Waterloo CS graduates building in the single hottest consensus category in enterprise software. There is no evidence of outsider thinking, no indication they are attempting something the experts consider impossible. They have chosen the safest possible wave to ride. The possible name change from Speedtrain to Talking Computers during the YC batch could indicate learning rate — pivots sometimes reveal founders updating their mental models rapidly — but it could equally indicate lack of conviction in the original thesis. Without more information, I cannot distinguish. Both founders are early-career with internship-level experience, which is not disqualifying (I have backed young founders many times), but early-career founders building in a consensus category without a demonstrated technical insight that incumbents cannot replicate is a fundamentally different bet than early-career founders attacking a problem everyone else has dismissed.

The team composition also reveals a gap. Two systems-level engineers building an enterprise B2B "workplace" product. No one on the team has enterprise sales experience, product design experience, or go-to-market expertise. For a developer tool, this might not matter. But "Facility, the Workplace for AI" is framed as an application-layer product for operators, not a code-level framework for developers. Selling workplace software to enterprises requires understanding procurement cycles, building integrations with existing toolchains, and navigating the organizational politics of AI deployment. The gene pool is technically complementary but functionally homogeneous — and the functional gap (enterprise GTM) is precisely where this company's execution risk is highest.

I pass. The consequence of success is a useful enterprise product in a crowded, consensus-validated market where the three largest technology companies on earth are already competing. The founders are technically competent but show no evidence of contrarian courage or outsider perspective. The technology faces immediate commoditization risk from open-source alternatives and platform-bundled offerings. The timing is real — multi-agent systems are genuinely accelerating — but arriving at an opportunity after Gartner has documented a 1,445% surge in interest is not prescient timing; it is consensus timing. I would tell these founders: you are too technically talented to spend your careers building middleware that Microsoft will bundle for free. Go find a problem that terrifies the establishment and matters to civilization.
