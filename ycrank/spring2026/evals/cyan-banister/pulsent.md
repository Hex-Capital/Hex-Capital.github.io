# Pulsent -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Toplyne raised $17.5M from Tiger Global and Sequoia to solve almost exactly what Pulsent is solving — identifying PLG conversion signals in B2B SaaS — and shut down, returning capital to investors after failing to find product-market fit. That precedent is the first thing I see when I look at Pulsent, and it shapes everything else. Toplyne didn't fail because the founders were weak or the funding was thin. It failed because the category itself has a structural problem: B2B SaaS buyers have fragmented tools, they know they have fragmented tools, and yet they resist paying for a unification layer because each individual vendor keeps shipping "good enough" features. Intercom's Fin AI agent is approaching $100M ARR. Pendo surpassed $200M ARR and is adding AI features. Pulsent is building in a space where incumbents with 100x the engineering headcount are moving fast toward the same capability set. That's not contrarian — that's consensus with extra steps.

Nicolò Magnante's path — Bain, BCG, HEC Paris — is the archetype of the founder who saw a market opportunity from inside a consulting deck. When I ask "tell me your story," I need to hear something that explains why this person couldn't NOT build this. Pulsent's founding story is a pivot: Nicolò and Arseniy Shishaev co-founded Bluco, an AI recruitment company, in 2024 at STATION F in Paris, won a €25K AXA grant, landed clients like Porsche — and then pivoted to Pulsent in early 2026. The YC description claims Nicolò "scaled two startups to millions in ARR," but that's unverified and generic. A pivot from AI recruitment to AI user engagement tells me the founders are searching for a market, not bound to a mission. Arseniy's three years at Datadog building data pipelines handling "hundreds of millions of data assets" gives him genuine technical chops for Pulsent's event-processing architecture — that's real founder-market fit on the engineering side. But technical competence isn't biographical forge. I've met hundreds of ex-Datadog engineers. The question is whether something in their lives made them unable to walk away from this specific problem, and I don't see that evidence anywhere in Pulsent's story.

The bull case for Pulsent is real, and I want to be honest about it. The "why now" argument — that advances in visual AI in December 2025 enabled agents to watch user sessions and understand behavior at human-level quality — is a genuine technological inflection. Pulsent's architecture of unifying data from PostHog, Mixpanel, Amplitude, Snowflake, BigQuery, and Attio, then deploying autonomous agents that execute across email, LinkedIn, and WhatsApp, fills a structural gap: analytics vendors risk channel conflict by becoming engagement senders, and engagement vendors lack deep product-analytics pipelines. Pulsent has paying customers and processes "hundreds of millions of events daily." Three named B2B SaaS co-founders — from Pletor, Leadbay (YC F25), and Basalt — give testimonials on pulsent.ai. The co-founders have worked together for three-plus years and already built one company together, which reduces the co-founder dynamics risk I care about deeply since HQ Trivia. If Pulsent's continuous learning loop genuinely improves message timing and content per account over time, that accumulated behavioral model could become a data moat. These are all legitimate pre-seed positives.

But here's where my framework breaks this apart. Pulsent operates in one of the most consensus-attractive categories in venture right now — AI agents for B2B sales engagement. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch this. Every fund with an AI thesis is looking at this space. When I invested in SpaceX, rockets were blowing up. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in my own backyard. When I backed Contraline, VCs wouldn't even take the meeting on male birth control. Pulsent's thesis requires zero courage to articulate at a dinner party. That absence of discomfort is, for me, a strong negative signal — not because consensus bets can't work, but because consensus bets rarely produce the asymmetric returns that make angel investing a viable asset class.

The invisible system test is where Pulsent falls furthest from my framework. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was attacking credit card opacity — a hidden structure that extracted billions from consumers who couldn't understand their own interest rates. Ownwell attacks property tax systems that overcharge homeowners who don't know how to appeal. Pulsent's "hidden system" is that B2B SaaS companies have fragmented analytics tools and can't engage users in real time. That's an integration problem, not a power-asymmetry problem. The companies Pulsent serves — well-funded B2B SaaS startups — are not being exploited by opaque structures. They have abundant data; they just haven't unified it efficiently. Pulsent makes already-privileged companies better at extracting revenue from their users. That's legitimate business value, but it's the inverse of the structural power shift I look for when I deploy my own capital.

Pulsent's defensibility sits in an uncomfortable zone. The data unification layer creates real switching costs once a customer maps their PostHog, Stripe, and Salesforce pipelines — I've seen this pattern work at Carta, where cap table switching costs became a genuine moat. But the dossier is clear that "the core components — LLM-powered message generation, event stream ingestion, session replay analysis — are accessible via commercial APIs and open-source tools." Pendo, Intercom, and Userpilot all have engineering teams over 100x Pulsent's size. The integration layer is reproducible. Toplyne proved that even with $17.5M and top-tier investors, this category's moats didn't compound fast enough. One verifiable condition would change my mind: if Pulsent demonstrated that their AI agents produce measurably superior conversion outcomes — not testimonials, but A/B-tested lift against Intercom's Fin on the same customer base — that would be evidence of a proprietary capability gap I can't see today. Without that, Pulsent is a well-executed product in a space where execution alone doesn't protect you.

The dimension that most decisively places Pulsent outside my portfolio is the absence of contrarian conviction — this is a consensus AI bet in a consensus market, and my entire career was built on being the first check into spaces everyone else rejected. The second decisive dimension is the missing biographical forge: Nicolò and Arseniy are competent and technically capable, but their path from Bain to BCG to a pivot from AI recruitment reads as smart people searching for the right market, not founders who were shaped by a problem they couldn't escape. I wish Pulsent well — the Datadog pedigree, the YC backing, the paying customers are all genuine assets — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
