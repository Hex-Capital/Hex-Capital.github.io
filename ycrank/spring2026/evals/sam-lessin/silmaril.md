# Silmaril -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Silmaril is two ex-Amazon/AWS security engineers who hacked OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Microsoft fifteen times in two weeks and turned the artifact into a ModernBERT classifier with a self-retraining loop -- a technically credible wedge in the exact category Check Point just paid for Lakera and Palo Alto just paid for Protect AI Guardian. That pairing is Silmaril's signature: the founder-conviction axis is the highest thing on the page (a public, dated hacking spree is the closest thing to a missionary artifact this dossier offers), and the founder-as-brand axis is the lowest (no GitHub, no essays, Twitter follower counts not even retrievable for either Aum Upadhyay or Eduardo Velasco). That's a hacker-pair selling a runtime commodity into a category whose incumbents were already acquired -- which is a very specific kind of company.

The price/non-consensus dimension is where Silmaril sits closest to my Pass Example, not my Solana anchor. Solana cleared this bar because the entire crypto category had been abandoned post-ICO crash and I was buying at $0.05 with no bidders -- Silmaril is the inverse on every variable: Garry Tan personally posted on X on April 14 2026, the YC launch account amplified the same day, and the category just had two M&A exits (Lakera/Check Point, Protect AI/PANW in 2025) that turn every generalist seed fund into a buyer for the "next Lakera." The dossier shows no round size, no price, no lead -- which under my absent-evidence default already costs them, because silence on round dynamics in a hot category is almost always consensus pricing dressed in modesty. This is the deck that "screams hot deal" without having to say it.

Teamshares is the rung I keep returning to on capital structure because it represented a financing shape no other fund could underwrite, and Silmaril fails that test in the most pedestrian way available: a SaaS/self-hosted SDK that plugs into LangGraph "in 5 lines of code" with no published pricing and a competitor set where HiddenLayer has $56M, Promptfoo has $23.6M, and Lakera had $30M before the Check Point bag. Silmaril needs at minimum four more rounds to match HiddenLayer's current war chest, and there is nothing in the financing instrument -- no revenue share, no holdco, no royalty on the attack-corpus data -- that lets me buy this any way other than a standard SAFE. The pattern that failure follows is the Pass Example template: a SaaS cap table that will need five rounds to reach default-alive in a category where the buyers are already chosen.

Founder conviction is the one dimension where Silmaril clears the absent-evidence default rather than slipping under it. Aum Upadhyay built the AWS security/privacy framework the company claims prevented $1.8B in damages; Eduardo Velasco chained a prompt injection to root in ChatGPT; the pair then ran an unsanctioned whitehat campaign against the four largest model labs before incorporating. That's not Antoine Buteau's "Writing is thinking" filter -- there are no essays, no public take on why classifier-based defenses beat constitutional methods -- but the hacking artifact is the closest equivalent: a dated, verifiable, slightly-reckless commitment to the category that predates the round. The mechanism over the next 18 months is whether they convert that artifact into public writing and a research-channel reputation; if they don't, the conviction signal decays into "two more ex-Amazon AI safety hires" within a year.

Founder-as-brand is where Silmaril looks like the dossier I'd skip without a meeting. Marina Mogilko is the high rung because she came with millions of subscribers, zero CAC, and a 30-year compounding earning curve I could structure against directly -- Silmaril comes with two LinkedIn pages I can't tell apart from any other YC P26 security pair, two Twitter handles whose follower counts can't even be retrieved, no GitHub presence, no Discord, no newsletter, no Product Hunt launch. The distribution thesis here is "developer SDK + YC amplification + Garry Tan retweet" -- which is exactly the GTM every other YC AI-security pair will run this batch. Nothing on the cap table is the founders' owned audience; the cap table is just equity in a runtime guardrail SaaS.

Sublime Security is the single-anchor I owe this category, because it's the one recent enterprise-security check on my list and the obvious pattern-match Silmaril wants me to make. The counterfactual that would flip Silmaril a full tier on category fit is this: if the dossier had said the team had spent three years building this at AWS internally and was leaving to commercialize, or if there were a clean data-network-effects story where customer attack corpora compound across tenants in a way HiddenLayer's architecture can't replicate, I'd treat this as Sublime-adjacent. What I actually see is a classifier with self-published 95.60% accuracy on 131 attack techniques and P90 20ms latency benchmarks that no third party has reproduced, in a category one TrueFoundry review calls "over a dozen guardrail providers." That's commodity exposure to "AI is just more internet" -- the exact sleeve I down-weight.

The 36-month forecast for Silmaril is acqui-hire by a mid-tier security vendor (Snyk, Wiz, CrowdStrike, or Cisco) at $20-60M on the strength of the founders' research credentials and the attack-corpus dataset, after failing to raise a competitive Series A because HiddenLayer and the Check Point/Lakera bundle have eaten the design-partner pipeline. The mechanism producing it: Silmaril's wedge (runtime classifier) is the most commoditized layer of the AI-security stack, the founders' best asset is portable IP and a research reputation rather than enterprise revenue, and the natural buyers have already proven they will pay for this exact shape at sub-$100M prices.

The strongest case against the acqui-hire forecast is the Sublime Security exception applied with force: Silmaril's hacking artifact is genuinely missionary, the M&A wave proves enterprise willingness-to-pay, and a self-healing classifier loop fed by multi-tenant attack traces could become the one true data-network-effects moat in a category everyone else is faking. If Silmaril ships an independently-benchmarked accuracy lead against Lakera-on-Check-Point and signs two Fortune 100 design partners in the next 12 months, this becomes a $200M+ standalone outcome and I will have been wrong to anchor on the Pass Example template. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a third-party benchmark (TrueFoundry, MITRE ATLAS, or a published academic eval) showing Silmaril's classifier beating both Lakera and Protect AI Guardian on a held-out attack corpus, paired with a named paying customer disclosed in writing rather than a logo wall the dossier flags as "described as targets of whitehat findings rather than paying customers." Until then, the price is consensus, the structure is generic, and the distribution is borrowed from Garry Tan's timeline.
