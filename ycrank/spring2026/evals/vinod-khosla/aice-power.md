# AICE Power -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Verdigris Technologies has been deploying clip-on CT sensors with AI disaggregation on commercial buildings since 2011, with $41.8M in funding behind it. AICE Power is entering that exact technical architecture — commodity clip-on current sensors, a 4G gateway, cloud-based load disaggregation — fourteen years later, targeting a smaller building segment. When I evaluate a company, I start with one question: if AICE Power fully succeeds, does the world materially change? The answer here is that supermarkets and fast food chains save 15-20% on their electricity bills. That's real money for those operators. It is not a changed world. Nobody's life is fundamentally different because their local E.Leclerc reduced energy waste. The consequence of AICE Power's maximum success is a profitable vertical SaaS company worth perhaps $500M-$1B, which is a fine outcome for most investors but not for the way I invest. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. A company whose ceiling is "moderately successful building analytics platform" doesn't generate the asymmetric payoff my framework requires.

AICE Power claims deployment at "5% the cost of traditional BMS" — Honeywell and Siemens systems that require weeks of installation and rewiring versus AICE Power's 30-minute clip-on install. That 20x cost reduction is the strongest feature of the pitch, and I take it seriously. But there is a critical distinction my framework forces me to make: AICE Power is making monitoring affordable, not making energy affordable. Commonwealth Fusion Systems is my portfolio anchor for radical affordability — fusion makes the underlying resource, energy itself, too cheap to meter. AICE Power makes the instrument that watches energy consumption cheaper. The $7.6B BEMS market growing to $26.1B by 2035 is a TAM slide, not a vision of a different world. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence wasn't "cheaper food analytics" — it was protein without animal agriculture, feeding billions differently. AICE Power's affordability thesis operates one level removed from what I care about.

Rémi Bouteiller's hardware credentials are genuinely impressive — founding engineer at Amira Health designing multi-chip PCBs, building BLE firmware with two-week battery life, managing manufacturing in Shenzhen. French Eurobot Champion 2020. Grégoire Chomette brings MIT Aero/Astro and NASA asteroid risk assessment, with seven peer-reviewed publications. These are strong technical profiles for the problem AICE Power is solving. But I evaluate rate of learning, not credential density. I have no evidence of how Chomette and Bouteiller have adapted, pivoted, or been surprised during AICE Power's existence. Their backgrounds tell me they can build this product — Bouteiller's sensor-to-cloud hardware stack experience is directly applicable — but I cannot assess whether they are learning machines or executing a predetermined plan. At pre-seed with 10+ sites live, the traction is respectable. E.Leclerc and Accor as named clients suggest real procurement conversations. That is meaningful for a two-person team in France and Switzerland.

The bull case deserves honest construction. AICE Power is positioning at the low end of a market where incumbents — Honeywell, Siemens, Johnson Controls — have business model conflict that prevents them from serving small-format commercial buildings profitably. The EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive adopted in 2024 creates a regulatory tailwind that could make monitoring mandatory across commercial buildings in Europe. If AICE Power captures the small-commercial segment across Europe with a land-and-expand motion through multi-site operators like E.Leclerc, they build a data moat from thousands of electrical load signatures that improves disaggregation accuracy beyond what competitors can match. The DoorDash exception in my portfolio — an execution play without novel technology, funded because consequence magnitude was planetary — is the obvious parallel an advocate would cite. But DoorDash transformed food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. AICE Power optimizes electricity bills for building operators. The consequence magnitudes are not in the same universe. And the regulatory tailwind is itself a red flag in my framework: after KiOR and Range Fuels burned hundreds of millions, I now insist that technologies beat existing alternatives on economics alone, without depending on policy mandates. AICE Power's "why now" leans heavily on EU regulation, which makes me less interested, not more.

The technical approach fails my non-linearity test decisively. The dossier states it plainly: "clip-on CT sensors are commodity hardware" and "software disaggregation via NILM is an active research area with open-source implementations." AICE Power's technical approach is replicable — the dossier itself concludes that "defensibility depends on execution speed in multi-site deployment and data accumulation rather than proprietary technology." This is the definition of an execution play without a technology wedge. Verdigris, Facilio ($45.9M raised, $6.4M ARR), Infogrid ($148M raised), and Enertiv ($18.4M raised) are all operating in adjacent spaces with more capital and longer track records. AICE Power's differentiation is market segment targeting, not a step-function technical insight. I don't invest in companies whose competitive advantage is "we serve smaller customers." That is a go-to-market choice, not a moat.

One verifiable condition would change my assessment: if AICE Power demonstrated that their monitoring platform could autonomously control building systems — not just detect waste but eliminate it in real-time, reducing energy consumption by 50-70% without human intervention — that moves from monitoring to optimization, from instrument to infrastructure, from 2x to potential 10x impact. But AICE Power is building "real-time alerts" and anomaly detection, which is information delivery, not autonomous action. The company is competent, the founders are technically qualified, the early traction with named enterprise clients is real, and the cost advantage over traditional BMS is meaningful. None of that changes the fundamental calculus: AICE Power is an incremental improvement to building energy management using commodity technology in a market with well-funded competitors. My money is for bets where the consequence of success reshapes how people live. AICE Power reshapes how facility managers read their dashboards.
