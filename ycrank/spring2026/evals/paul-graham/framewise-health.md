# Framewise Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Tane Kim dropped out of medical school to build Framewise Health. That gets my attention. Medical school is one of the most psychologically difficult things to quit — not because the work is easy but because the social scaffolding around it makes leaving feel like failure. When someone walks away from that path to start a company in the same domain, it usually means one of two things: they saw something broken up close that they couldn't unsee, or they were already looking for an exit and a startup provided the narrative. Tane's time at the Lundquist Institute at Harbor-UCLA and at Sarepta Therapeutics in translational biology puts real clinical exposure on the résumé. The Guatemala fieldwork — medical consultations, health education workshops — is literally the problem domain Framewise Health operates in. But I don't have the story. I don't know the moment. Did Tane watch a Spanish-speaking patient get discharged with a stack of English printouts and think "this is insane"? That specific frustration narrative is missing from everything public about Framewise Health, and its absence bothers me because founders who discover problems organically tend to tell that story compulsively. The CMS HRRP penalty number — $320 million across 2,273 hospitals — leads the pitch instead, which reads more like market sizing than lived pain.

David Cui brings Brown CS and software engineering experience from SOTATEK, which gives Framewise Health the technical co-founder the clinical side needs. But I have no shipping history to evaluate. No public GitHub, no prior products, no side projects visible in the dossier. The two-founder team from the same university is a pattern I've seen work — shared context reduces communication overhead — but the combination of a junior engineer and an early-career clinical researcher selling into hospital procurement is concerning. Framewise Health's go-to-market requires convincing hospital quality officers and CMOs to integrate with their EHR systems. That's not a six-week sales cycle. Every healthtech founder I've funded or talked to describes hospital sales as a twelve-to-eighteen-month grind through compliance, IT security, and committee approvals. A two-person pre-seed team tackling this without visible prior enterprise sales experience or named advisors in hospital administration needs extraordinary resourcefulness — and the dossier gives me nothing to evaluate on that dimension. No stories of creative workarounds, no revenue before YC, no evidence of doing the unscalable manual work of personally conducting discharge education to validate the concept.

Framewise Health's core problem — hospital readmission penalties — is real, quantifiable, and persistent. $1 million per year per hospital in CMS HRRP penalties is the kind of clear budget line that makes enterprise sales conversations tractable. The value-based pricing model anchored to penalty savings is smart. But here's what concerns me: "AI-personalized patient recovery videos to reduce hospital readmissions" is an idea that sounds good to everyone. Nobody's first reaction is skepticism or confusion. The competitive landscape confirms this — Synthesia at $4 billion valuation, Mytonomy acquired by Elsevier in March 2026, Health Recovery Solutions with $84.7 million raised, VidifyAI already advertising HIPAA-compliant healthcare video with SOC 2 Type II. When I look at the companies I've backed that returned the most, they were ideas people thought were stupid. Airbnb — strangers sleeping in your apartment. Coinbase — internet money for nerds. Stripe — yet another payment API when everyone thought PayPal had won. Framewise Health sits on the wrong side of that filter. The convergence of AI video quality, FHIR standardization, and HRRP penalties is visible to anyone who reads a healthcare trade publication. That accessibility is a problem, not a feature.

The bull case is genuine, and I want to state it clearly before explaining why it doesn't move me enough. Tane Kim's clinical background at Harbor-UCLA and Sarepta creates founder-market fit that generic AI video companies lack. The EHR integration via FHIR, combined with the clinician review workflow, builds switching costs that Synthesia would need significant healthcare-specific investment to replicate. The timing convergence is real — FHIR adoption mandated by CMS, AI video crossing the quality threshold, $320 million in annual penalties providing persistent buyer motivation. This resembles my ClassDojo investment in structure: an institutional buyer with painful procurement (school districts and hospitals share that DNA), a mission-driven founder who cares about the population they serve, and a domain-specific product that general-purpose platforms can't easily replicate. If Framewise Health showed me Airbnb-level resourcefulness — if David and Tane had personally filmed discharge videos for patients, hand-delivered them, tracked readmission rates, and shown me that their ten patients had zero readmissions compared to a ward average of fifteen percent — I'd be writing a check. That's the verifiable condition: evidence that the founders have done the manual, unscalable version of this product and measured its impact on actual patients in an actual hospital.

Framewise Health's regulatory dependency is the structural risk that keeps me from finding the upside case compelling enough on its own. The entire economic justification rests on CMS HRRP penalties, and the dossier itself notes that research in 2024 identified $284-297 million in potential penalty redistribution due to Medicare Advantage calculation issues. If CMS restructures the penalty formula — which is an active policy discussion — the budget line that justifies Framewise Health's pricing model could shift or shrink. This isn't a fatal problem, but it means the company's growth story scales with a government program's policy stability, not with the kind of compounding user demand that creates superlinear returns. Stripe grew because every new internet business needed payments and every new Stripe customer made the platform more valuable for the next. Framewise Health grows because CMS continues to penalize hospitals at a rate that exceeds the cost of the software. That's a linear value proposition dressed up in SaaS economics.

The dimension that most determines my score is founder resourcefulness — or rather its complete absence from the evidence. I weight this at thirty points because it's my single most predictive signal, and truly resourceful founders generate stories. The absence isn't neutral; it's mildly negative. Framewise Health's founders may have that quality — YC accepted them, and Aaron Epstein saw something in the interview — but I'm investing my own money based on what I can observe, not what a partner might have seen in a ten-minute session. Combined with an idea that passes the schlep test (hospital sales are genuinely painful) but fails the Black Swan test (nobody thinks this is a bad idea), Framewise Health lands as a competent team pursuing a real problem through an obvious approach. I'd pass today and revisit if the founders can show me a pilot hospital with measurable readmission reduction data — that's the artifact that would prove both the product and the founders' ability to push through the institutional barriers that make this domain hard.
