# Panorama -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Jaclyn Lunger has an MIT PhD in machine learning, built high-throughput virtual screening pipelines, and was the founding scientist at Lila Sciences — a company that went on to raise $350M at a $1.3B+ valuation with Nvidia backing. Jingwei Hao progressed from Senior Engineer to Tech Lead Manager at Lyft, owned key infrastructure at Cash App, and contributed to Envoy Mobile. These are genuinely strong resumes. And they're building... a better Zapier. That mismatch between founder caliber and problem magnitude is the most striking thing about Panorama. When I see a scientist who helped build a unicorn-valued materials discovery engine turn around and build a workflow automation tool, I don't think "brilliant founders attacking a big market" — I think "brilliant founders attacking a small problem." The consequence of Panorama fully succeeding is that knowledge workers save time auto-drafting their Friday Notion documents and converting planning docs into Linear tickets. Nobody's life fundamentally changes.

Panorama describes itself as a "TikTok-like recommendation engine" for workflows — observing Slack messages, Notion documents, and project management tools over 1–2 weeks to surface repetitive patterns and suggest automations. The framing is clever, but the underlying technology is LLM-based pattern recognition applied to workplace data. No patents filed. No public repositories on GitHub. No novel algorithm described in any source. Lunger's PhD was in ML for materials science — computational chemistry, not workflow intelligence — and while her ML skills transfer, the application doesn't require a breakthrough. Zapier does $310M in annual revenue with user-configured automation. n8n hit $40M ARR with an open-source approach. Panorama's differentiation is "we suggest the automations instead of making you build them." That's a UX improvement, not a technology discontinuity. Microsoft Copilot Studio, Google AgentSpace, and Slack's own AI features all have native access to the same workplace data Panorama must access through APIs — and adding observational workflow suggestions is an obvious product extension for any of them.

The competitive dynamics here are precisely what I avoid. Panorama is targeting redistribution of existing workflow automation spend, not creation of new demand. The $23.77 billion workflow automation market already has incumbents with massive distribution advantages. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks for $2.85B in 2025, signaling that enterprise platform owners are consolidating AI workflow capabilities. When I backed Impossible Foods, there was a genuine science breakthrough — synthesized heme protein — that incumbents couldn't replicate with a product feature update. Panorama has no equivalent moat. The concept of observing work patterns and suggesting automations is replicable by any team with LLM API access and workplace integrations. The dossier's own assessment confirms this: "Execution speed and recommendation quality are the near-term differentiators, not structural barriers."

The strongest bull case for Panorama would require believing that the observational approach creates a compounding data flywheel — that Panorama's recommendations get dramatically better with scale in a way competitors can't match, and that this advantage compounds faster than incumbents can respond. If Hao's experience coordinating infrastructure across 20+ engineering teams at Cash App gives him unique insight into cross-functional workflow patterns, and if Lunger's ML sophistication produces recommendation quality that's genuinely 10x better than what Microsoft or Google will ship, maybe this becomes the default operating system for knowledge work. But I don't see evidence of that compounding mechanism. The cold-start problem — requiring 1–2 weeks of data before generating value — actually works against the flywheel thesis by creating friction at the exact moment when user engagement matters most. And the name collision with Panorama Education (YC S13, 15M+ students) creates practical GTM headwinds that compound over time.

Panorama is the archetype of the company I pass on. Talented founders, real market, incremental improvement. A 40% efficiency gain in knowledge worker workflows doesn't change how anyone lives. Consequence magnitude locked this at Pass — if I can't answer "the world is visibly different" when I imagine full success, the option isn't worth pricing. The non-linear technical approach score confirms it: no proprietary science, no step-function improvement, just LLM APIs applied to a known problem. Lunger and Hao deserve a harder problem. I'd be more interested if they were trying to eliminate knowledge work entirely rather than making it 30% faster.
