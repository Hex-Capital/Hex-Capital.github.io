## The Idea

**Problem:** Millions of GenZ and young renters need short-term, flexible housing (1-3 month sublets) but are locked into 12-month leases designed for an older generation with stable income and roots (snagsublets.com/manifesto). Today they cobble together sublets via Facebook groups, Instagram DMs, and Craigslist — fragmented, low-trust channels with no verification, inconsistent listing quality, and high search friction (company description, a16z Speedrun portal). The platform lists partner universities including NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, and Pace (snagsublets.com), indicating a student-heavy user base.

**Approach:** Snag is an AI-native sublet marketplace. A proprietary AI model converts social media posts (from FB groups, IG, etc.) into structured, high-quality listings instantly and matches renters with suitable sublets within seconds (company description). The platform offers instant messaging, user verification/ID systems, and save/favorite functionality (snagsublets.com; App Store developer responses). Available on iOS and Android (App Store; Google Play).

**Differentiation:** Snag targets the gap between Airbnb (nightly stays, tourist-priced) and Zillow/traditional rental platforms (12-month leases). Unlike LeaseBreak (lease-break-specific, no AI), Kopa (furnished rentals with housemate matching), or Blueground (operator-managed furnished units at premium prices), Snag aggregates peer-to-peer sublets via AI scraping of social channels and focuses specifically on GenZ renters seeking 1-3 month flexibility (snagsublets.com/manifesto; company description).

**Business Model:** No public pricing page found. Listing and browsing appear free for users (App Store listing shows the app is free). Subletter earnings are displayed on the website as testimonials (e.g., Maku: +$55,781; Anastasia: +$41,400) — these are customer earnings, not company revenue (snagsublets.com). [Inferred]: Most likely monetization path is a transaction fee or service fee on completed bookings, consistent with marketplace models in the space (Airbnb, Kopa).

**TAM/SAM:** The global short-term rental market reached $124.52B in 2024 and is projected to reach $344.06B by 2034 at 10.70% CAGR (Precedence Research, 2024 via search snippet). The US apartment rental market is tracked by IBISWorld but a specific figure was not retrieved. No public TAM/SAM data found for the US peer-to-peer sublet segment specifically.

**GTM / Distribution:** University partnerships with 7 named NYC-area schools drive student adoption (snagsublets.com). Organic social presence on TikTok (@snagsublets) and Instagram (@snagsubletsnyc) targets GenZ (snagsublets.com). Organic Facebook group posts recommend the app to student housing groups (Facebook, apartments.nyu group). [Inferred]: Primary distribution is campus-by-campus viral expansion through university partnerships and social media, starting in NYC.

## Defensibility

- **Network effects:** Two-sided marketplace with 3,000 listings and 30,000 student users in NYC (company description; snagsublets.com). Liquidity in a single city creates switching costs — renters go where listings are, subletters go where renters are.
- **Data advantage:** Proprietary AI model trained on social media sublet posts to auto-generate structured listings (company description). Volume of ingested posts creates a data flywheel as more social channels are scraped.
- **University partnerships:** Relationships with 7 NYC institutions (snagsublets.com) create a distribution channel competitors must replicate school by school.

**Market structure:** Airbnb could enter but faces regulatory constraints around short-term rentals in NYC (Local Law 18 restricts stays under 30 days) and its business model is optimized for nightly tourist stays, not monthly peer-to-peer sublets (public regulatory record). Zillow and traditional platforms are built around 12-month leases and broker economics that don't suit 1-3 month sublets. [Inferred]: The structural barrier is business model incompatibility — incumbents' unit economics and regulatory positioning make monthly peer-to-peer sublets a poor fit for their platforms.

**Commoditization risk:** The AI listing-conversion technology is replicable by any team with ML capability. The core defensibility is network density in specific geographies (NYC first). Facebook and Craigslist already host this activity but lack curation, verification, and matching — they could add features but historically have not invested in vertical housing tools.

## Key Risks

**Name disambiguation / search confusion:** "Snag" is a common English word. PitchBook and Tracxn return profiles for other companies named "Snag" (e.g., a Wilmington, DE company that raised $5.68M in Series A in June 2024 from Ivy Ventures — PitchBook via search snippet). This creates investor diligence friction and may affect SEO/discoverability.

**Single-city concentration:** All 3,000 listings and 30,000 users are in NYC (company description; snagsublets.com). Marketplace liquidity must be rebuilt from scratch in each new city. NYC-specific factors (university density, sublet culture, Local Law 18) may not replicate in other markets.

**Pivot risk / prior entity baggage:** Snag is a rebrand/pivot of OneRoof, which raised $4.6M as a neighbor community app (Tracxn via search snippet). The iOS app was first released under the Snag brand in Nov 2025, meaning the current product is ~5 months old. Investor clarity on burn from the prior product, remaining runway, and cap table complexity from 3 prior rounds warrants diligence.

**GMV ≠ revenue and metric definition ambiguity:** The "$6.5M in requests" metric (company description) refers to booking request volume, not completed transactions or company revenue. Conversion rate from request to completed sublet, and whether/how the company monetizes, are unverified.

**Regulatory exposure:** NYC housing regulation is active and evolving. While 1-3 month sublets currently fall outside Local Law 18's short-term rental restrictions, future regulation targeting sublets or requiring landlord consent for marketplace-facilitated sublets could constrain supply.
