# Valence -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most striking thing about Valence is the structural tension at its core: three founders with genuinely rare low-latency trading infrastructure knowledge building a business that depends entirely on API access they don't control. Daniel built FPGA systems at Optiver and IMC -- firms where microseconds translate directly into P&L. Neo worked on latency-sensitive strategy research at DRW and Cubist, then went and built an event-contract exchange prototype on his own time. These aren't resume lines; this is the kind of knowledge you absorb through the fingertips by sitting next to market makers and watching order flow at the speed of hardware. You can't teach someone how an order book *feels* at sub-millisecond resolution. That knowledge is real. But having the specific knowledge to build excellent infrastructure is a different question from whether the infrastructure creates durable value in a market where the exchanges have every incentive to cut off your oxygen.

My portfolio is dominated by picks-and-shovels plays -- AngelList for funding, CoinList for token launches, Alchemy for blockchain development, Stack Overflow for developer knowledge. I'm structurally biased toward infrastructure over applications. Valence maps to this pattern: it doesn't bet on which prediction market exchange wins, it provides the execution layer across all of them. The parallel to CoinList is obvious -- a single access point for a new asset class. But CoinList had its own regulatory moat and projects *needed* CoinList for compliant token distribution. Valence's exchanges don't need Valence. Kalshi raised $1.7 billion at an $11 billion valuation. They're not going to happily let a three-person startup route their professional flow through a layer they don't control. The structural space the dossier identifies -- that exchanges won't build cross-venue aggregation because it cannibalizes their own liquidity -- is only true until the aggregator becomes large enough to matter. Then the incentives flip.

The leverage question is where this falls short of my bar. Valence makes existing professional traders more efficient across existing venues. That's valuable but incremental. Compare it to Uber, which turned every car into a taxi and every smartphone into a dispatch system -- a new form of leverage that created a new type of economic actor. Or Replit, which made programming accessible to people who would never have become traditional software engineers. Valence doesn't enable a new type of person to participate in prediction markets; it gives people already doing it a better dashboard. Smart order routing across fragmented venues is an optimization, not a category. The technology itself -- order routing, co-location, API aggregation -- is well-established in traditional equities and crypto. 1inch built DEX aggregation for decentralized exchanges. The pattern is proven but it's also replicable. Any well-funded fintech shop with two senior engineers from Jane Street could build this stack in six months.

The timing question cuts both ways, and neither direction is contrarian. Prediction markets are one of the hottest categories in fintech right now -- Kalshi, Polymarket, Robinhood, DraftKings, and FanDuel are all pouring capital in. Building infrastructure for a booming market is smart, but it's not non-consensus. The aggregation thesis -- that fragmentation across venues increases the value of a routing layer -- is correct as a trend, but it assumes fragmentation persists. In crypto, exchanges consolidated toward Binance and Coinbase; the aggregation layer mattered most during peak fragmentation and less afterward. If prediction markets follow the same pattern, Valence's window might be narrower than it appears. The founders haven't articulated a theory for why smart skeptics are wrong about aggregation-layer durability -- which is the absence of signal I weight heavily.

The bull case I take seriously: if the claimed traction is real -- $200M+ monthly volume, 1B+ contracts all-time, a quant firm trading $150M monthly through the platform -- that's extraordinary for a pre-seed three-person team and suggests they've built something professional traders actually need. If prediction markets hit $100B+ annual volume and fragment across ten or more venues globally, the Bloomberg Terminal of prediction markets could be a massive business. These founders are the right people to build it, and they're building it before anyone else with comparable infrastructure chops has entered the space. The question I can't resolve from the dossier is whether these metrics are verifiable -- they'd represent a meaningful share of total prediction market volume, which would make this a very different conversation.

I'd put this in the category of competent infrastructure play with strong founder-market fit but without the leverage architecture or contrarian positioning that makes me reach for my checkbook. The founders know what they're doing technically. They're building a real product for a real market. But they're building a thin execution layer in a consensus-hot category, dependent on exchange cooperation, using established technology patterns. The specific knowledge is in the infrastructure engineering, not in a non-obvious insight about where prediction markets are headed. I want to see the founder who knows something about prediction markets that Kalshi's team doesn't -- not just the founder who can build a faster order router.
