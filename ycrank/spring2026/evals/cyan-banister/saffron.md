# Saffron -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Saffron: this is possibly the most popular idea I could imagine in the current market. "AI-native technical assessments" -- every engineering leader I talk to is asking about this, every VC is looking for this, and every incumbent in the space is already bolting AI features onto their platforms. HackerRank and CodeSignal already have configurable AI tools in their assessments. When I look for companies, I'm looking for ideas that make other investors uncomfortable, that are hard to explain at a dinner party, that get dismissed as crazy. AI hiring tools in 2026 are the opposite of that. This is the kind of deal where every seed investor in San Francisco nods along enthusiastically, and that consensus energy is precisely what makes me pull back. The best investments I've made -- SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline -- were ones where people looked at me like I'd lost my mind.

Before I get to the product, I want to understand these founders. And here's where I hit a wall. Robert, Jerry, and Kazuma have extraordinary credentials -- MIT, Stanford, Harvey Mudd, Jane Street, NeurIPS publications. They are brilliant young people. But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- "Tell me your story, not the business" -- I can't find the biographical thread that ties these founders to hiring assessment as their life's problem. Robert founded a nonprofit for underprivileged children and Jerry built assistive technology for blind users, which tells me they have genuine empathy and care about impact. I respect that. But the connection to "engineers should be evaluated differently when using AI" feels intellectual, not visceral. They probably observed the problem at Jane Street, which is known for its rigorous technical hiring, and thought, "We can improve this." That's a reasonable product insight, but it's not the kind of bone-deep conviction I saw in the Contraline founder who'd spent years in academic research that everyone ignored, or the Crusoe founders who had independently arrived at an insight about gas flares that seemed ridiculous but was rooted in real physics. When a founder's biography and their company's mission are inseparable, that's the strongest signal I know. I don't see that fusion here.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. The timing is genuinely excellent -- AI-assisted coding has become the default working mode and assessments built around algorithmic puzzles on a whiteboard are measuring the wrong thing. The specific execution has teeth: a browser-based IDE loaded with the employer's actual codebase, full session replay of every AI prompt and interaction, per-line attribution of human versus AI code. Nobody else does all of that together. At $20 per assessment with zero interviewer hours, the unit economics crush Karat's human-interviewer model. The data moat is potentially powerful -- every assessment captures granular human-AI interaction patterns that could train scoring models nobody else can build. And the founding team's technical depth is clearly sufficient to build this. If I were a different kind of investor -- one who evaluates market timing, team credentials, and product differentiation in a systematic way -- I'd probably be writing a check. Yao has already shipped products to 50,000 users. Choji has published at the top ML venues. They have the skills to execute.

But I'm not that investor. What I uniquely look for is whether a company creates economic access for people who currently lack it, whether it makes an invisible system visible in a way that shifts power. Saffron serves employers -- well-funded tech companies that are already hiring engineers. It makes their process more efficient, which is valuable, but it doesn't fundamentally change who gets access to engineering careers or make an opaque power structure transparent. Compare that to what I saw in Affirm, where hidden credit card fees were replaced with honest interest rates, or Flock Safety, where neighborhood safety data that had been invisible to communities became accessible. There's a version of Saffron that could go there -- if the AI-attribution data showed that candidates from non-traditional backgrounds were actually more effective AI-augmented engineers than pedigreed candidates, that would be the kind of invisible-system-made-visible insight that gives me that jolt of energy. But that's not the thesis they're building toward, at least not based on what I can see.

The platform dependency on Anthropic also concerns me. I spent years in security at IronPort, and one of the things that taught me is that building your core product on a single vendor's infrastructure means your fate is in their hands. Claude Code is the integrated AI agent in every Saffron assessment. If Anthropic changes pricing, deprecates features, or decides the assessment layer is a natural extension of their own product, Saffron has no fallback. That's not technology risk in the way I find exciting -- it's dependency risk that compounds over time.

I'll note one positive signal on the team dynamics side: Robert and Kazuma went to Clark High School together in Las Vegas before going to MIT and Harvey Mudd respectively. Pre-existing relationships that predate the startup are meaningful to me after what I watched happen with HQ Trivia. A three-person founding team also avoids the 50/50 deadlock that destroyed that company. I don't know how these three handle conflict, but the structural setup doesn't raise flags.

This is a well-built product addressing a real market need, built by technically excellent founders. A lot of investors will fund this gladly. But it doesn't pattern-match to how I invest. It's popular, it's easy to assess, and it's the kind of opportunity where the consensus forms quickly. My best investments have been the ones where I was early and alone, not the ones where I joined a crowd. I'm passing -- not because the company is bad, but because it's not my kind of bet.
