# Auxos -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Auxos is a credentialed-engineer team building a technically credible wrapper on the hottest AI sub-category of the 2026 batch, with real founder-market fit and exactly zero owned distribution -- which is the YC archetype my entire framework is built to de-rate. Ashton Daniel ran panels at BCG, Kerry Lu built ad-simulation infra at Amazon Ads "modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic," Jerry Wu shipped Instagram ads infra credited with "$100M+ incremental revenue per year" -- and they are walking into a category where Aaru just printed a $1B headline mark in December 2025 and Simile raised a $100M Series A. The dimensional signature is "high founder-market fit, low everything-else-I-care-about," and that is not a Sam Lessin check.

The Solana seed at $0.05/coin is the rung I anchor on for round dynamics: the entire category had quit, the price reflected it, and I got paid for absorbing illegibility. Auxos sits at the opposite pole on the same axis. Aaru at $50M+ / $1B and Simile at $100M Series A are not "the consensus crowd quit" -- they are "the consensus crowd just decided this is the next vertical AI rollup." Auxos's pitch -- digital twins for customer research, anchored to recruited real people instead of pure synthetic -- is structurally the "Aaru but with real interviews" version of a category leader's pitch, which is the exact "Stripe of [Y]" pattern that fails the contrarian-conviction filter. No round terms are disclosed, but a Spring 2026 YC company in the hottest AI insights sub-vertical three months post-launch does not price below batch median by accident, and silence on round dynamics is itself the signal I punish.

The pass-example founder with no pre-funding public writing and no community footprint is the LOW rung on founder-as-brand, and Auxos walks straight into that pattern. Three Twitter handles are listed -- @TheAshtonDaniel, @kerry_lu_, @jwoobers -- and the dossier explicitly notes "count not retrievable" for all three; no GitHub repos found across any co-founder; no newsletter, no podcast, no opinionated essay on synthetic respondents, no Product Hunt presence, no Discord, no waitlist disclosed. The CEO's primary distribution artifact is a cal.com booking link. This is the canonical paid-acquisition B2B sales motion the $60M Slow Creator Fund was explicitly designed to ignore -- the founders are excellent engineers, not asset-class people the cap table is built around.

Capital structure compounds the problem over 18 months: the differentiator Auxos sells -- "one real person behind every digital twin" -- is the same cost structure Auxos cites as broken in legacy research ("weeks to recruit, moderate"). If per-twin recruitment doesn't amortize across enough queries, Auxos becomes a Mechanical Turk wrapper with worse unit economics than Aaru's fully synthetic approach; if it does amortize, the moat is a dataset the next vertical entrant will rebuild in nine months. Either path requires 4-5 more rounds to reach default-alive against $50M-and-$100M-funded competitors, which is the exact factory-model cap table I treat as deselection.

Counterfactually, the round dimension only flips a tier if I learn Auxos is closing at sub-$10M post-money while at least one tier-1 has visibly passed, OR if the founders have structured a non-standard instrument -- a per-customer audience-rights deal, a holdco wrapping multiple research verticals, a revenue-share against insights consumed -- that no Aaru-chasing fund can match. Nothing in the Auxos dossier hints at either. The single-anchor parallel for category fit is the canonical pass-example YC AI agent pitch from my own framework: Auxos is the "AI agent for [enterprise vertical]" template with "market research" filled in, and "AI is just more internet" applied to a $56B legacy insights TAM is the textbook commoditized-LLM-wrapper exposure I've spent two years saying I won't pay seed prices for.

My 36-month forecast for Auxos: acqui-hired into Qualtrics, Dynata, or UserTesting as a channel-defense move at $15-40M total transaction value, OR rolled into Aaru/Simile as a real-respondent fidelity layer; ARR settles in the $0.5-3M band, the Series A doesn't clear at a markup, and the team's pedigree extracts a soft landing rather than a venture outcome. The mechanism is straightforward -- consensus-priced entry into a category leader's wake at SAFE terms cannot generate the 50x I'd need against the dilution Auxos will eat clearing four rounds against Aaru and Simile.

The strongest bull case I owe Auxos: Kerry Lu's Amazon Ads simulation work and Jerry Wu's Instagram ads infra are the precise technical primitives the category lacks, the real-person grounding is a genuine validation differentiator versus Aaru's fully synthetic populations, and the Aaru $1B mark means even a $200M acquisition clears a 50x at YC entry pricing -- this is the Robinhood-style exception where the earliest-stage entry price absorbs subsequent consensus rounds. Real answer: Robinhood worked because Slow was early enough on initial entry that consensus arrived after the position was opened, and Auxos is not early on initial entry -- Aaru's $1B mark is six months *before* Auxos's YC launch, so I'd be buying *into* consensus, not absorbing consensus *after* entry. The verdict flips only if Auxos discloses a round below YC batch median with one tier-1 visibly passing, or if Ashton Daniel publishes an opinionated essay on why synthetic-only populations like Aaru fail in ways real-person grounding fixes -- a non-obvious belief he will not be talked out of. Neither exists in the dossier. Pass.
