# Mendral -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me before anything else: Sam and Andrea have been building together for fifteen years. Docker, Dagger, now Mendral. They went through the container revolution side by side, co-founded a company that raised over thirty million dollars, and then walked away from it together to start something new. After what happened with HQ Trivia — where the product was perfect and the timing was perfect and the co-founder relationship destroyed everything — I've become almost obsessive about this dimension. Fifteen years across three companies is not a professional relationship, it's a marriage. They've already survived the kinds of crises that break most founding teams. That signal is genuinely rare, and I want to name it clearly because it's the best thing about this company from where I sit.

But when I try to hear the founder story — not the resume, the *story* — I'm getting credentials instead of biography. Docker's first employee. VP of Engineering. Lead Architect at Docker. Google. Microsoft. EPITECH. These are impressive facts, and they tell me Sam and Andrea are exceptionally capable infrastructure engineers. What they don't tell me is what compelled them to leave Dagger, what personal wound or obsession drove them to build an autonomous CI agent specifically, or why this problem keeps them up at night beyond professional interest. Maybe there's a powerful story I'm not seeing in the public record — maybe there's a moment where Sam watched engineering teams drown in CI failures for the hundredth time and felt something break — but I can't evaluate what I can't hear. My whole process starts with the biographical thread connecting the founder to the problem, and here I'm finding domain expertise where I'm looking for personal stake. Those are related but not the same thing.

The category concerns me more than the company itself. AI DevOps agents are exactly the kind of consensus bet I try to avoid. Everyone with access to Claude or GPT-4 is building AI agents for developer workflows right now. GitHub owns both Actions and Copilot — they could extend into autonomous CI remediation with distribution advantages Mendral can never match. The most promising companies I've backed looked ridiculous when I invested. SpaceX was "that crazy rocket company." Crusoe Energy was "mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota." Mendral is "AI agent for CI/CD" — and I can picture every investor at every fund nodding along. That's not the signal I trust. When something is easy to understand and easy to fund, the price gets bid up and the weirdness premium disappears. The specific approach — autonomously opening fix PRs rather than just surfacing errors — has some edge to it, and the traction is real (sixteen thousand investigations closed per month, PostHog processing over half a million CI jobs weekly through the platform), but the differentiation is incremental rather than structural.

Let me make the bull case honestly, because the traction deserves respect. Five paying customers at pre-seed, including PostHog, processing billions of log lines — that's not just a demo, that's production infrastructure. The accumulating dataset of CI failure patterns could become a genuine moat if they move fast enough. The founders aren't just domain experts — they *built* the infrastructure that modern CI/CD runs on. Docker containers are the substrate of every CI pipeline these days. If anyone understands CI log structures and failure patterns at the deepest level, it's these two. And the Dagger-to-Mendral pivot might actually be the conviction signal I'm looking for — they saw LLMs cross a capability threshold and had the courage to leave a funded company to chase it. If the data flywheel compounds faster than GitHub or Copilot can replicate, and if the trust barrier of autonomous code changes creates a switching cost that protects early adopters, this could become the default layer between CI platforms and engineering teams. That's a real business.

But it's not my kind of bet. The company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations — it makes already well-compensated software engineers at well-funded startups more productive. That's legitimate value, but it's not the kind of impact that draws me in. Uber created income for drivers who had nothing. Affirm gave transparent credit to people excluded from the financial system. Mendral saves PostHog's engineers time debugging flaky tests. I'm not dismissing that, but it doesn't activate the part of my investing brain that imagines a company changing how resources flow to people who need them. The space isn't regulated or stigmatized in ways that create the kinds of moats I've seen in defense tech or psychedelic therapy or male contraception. It's technically complex, yes — the Firecracker microVM sandboxing and tiered LLM routing are genuinely sophisticated engineering — but technical complexity without regulatory or social barriers doesn't deter well-funded competitors the way I'd want.

The co-founder relationship nearly pulls me over the line, and the domain depth is as strong as I've seen at pre-seed. If I were an investor who weighted execution capability and market timing above anti-consensus weirdness and biographical grit, I'd be writing a check. But I've learned to trust my own framework, even when the conventional signals are strong. The best investments I've made were the ones where I felt something others couldn't see yet — and with Mendral, I think most investors see exactly what I see.
