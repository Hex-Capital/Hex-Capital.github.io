# Hedge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hedge is the inverse of the company I usually back: a strong schlep dimension paired with a thin hacker dimension, two growth operators named Luke who got themselves licensed as a California surplus lines broker (#6018729) before they got themselves a GitHub presence. That pairing is the analytical signature. When I read the Hedge dossier, the thing that registers as signal is that Luke Button and Luke Rosa actually went and obtained CA E&S authority and stood up appointments with 30+ carriers plus Lloyd's syndicates -- the kind of regulatory grind I called out in "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) -- and the thing that registers as anomaly is that for a company whose entire wedge is LLM agents parsing ACORD forms, neither founder has a discoverable open-source footprint.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the schlep-dimension anchor for Hedge, and the mechanism is the same one Brian Armstrong rode: do the licensing and compliance work other smart YC applicants are unconsciously routing around, and let the regulatory perimeter become the moat. Casey is doing ACORD tooling without a license; Harper picked workers' comp/GL because it is the easy end of the pool; Hedge went after 45+ specialty E&S classes through Taven Insurance Services LLC dba Hedge Specialty with NPN 22154671 attached. That is the **Coinbase** (2012) pattern transposed onto surplus lines. Where Hedge diverges from the Armstrong analogy is the artifact: Armstrong rescued his rejected application with a hand-built Android Bitcoin wallet, and the Hedge dossier offers a self-reported 30-minute median first response and three anonymous retail-principal testimonials rather than a built thing I can inspect on Hacker News.

The MBA-wrapper pass case from my own Pass Example is the failure mechanism to test against Hedge's hacker-genealogy dimension, and the test is whether the founders leave hacker tracks. Luke Button is described as Head of Growth at Antimetal, growth at Traba, and CEO of Fernstone -- all operator titles. Luke Rosa is Head of Scaled Systems at Traba, where the dossier says he "replaced humans with AI agents to manage $20MM+ in revenue" -- which is closer to a hacker artifact than anything Button has, but it lives inside a private company and produces no public commit history. Patrick Collison won the Young Scientist prize for Lisp work at 16; Aaron Swartz wrote the RSS spec at 14. The Hedge founders did neither, and "No public repos found" appears twice in the dossier. That is the cleanest negative signal in the file.

Fernstone is the dimension that worries me on its own axis -- the organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012). Luke Button is currently the listed CEO and co-founder of Fernstone (YC F25, launched October 2025), an AI-native retail insurance brokerage with 100+ business clients, and *also* the co-founder of Hedge in YC Spring 2026. Over an 18-month horizon, this compounds badly in one of two ways: either Button is running both, in which case neither gets founder-mode attention of the kind I demanded in "Founder Mode" (September 2024), or Fernstone is being wound down quietly and the Hedge thesis is "we learned retail was the wrong layer, wholesale is the right layer" -- which is a competitive-analysis origin, not a personal-pain origin. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick; Luke Button noticed retail brokerage margins were thinner than wholesale.

What would have to be true for Hedge's animal-resourcefulness score to flip a tier is a named pre-funding stunt -- the equivalent of Chesky's Cap'n McCain's boxes at **Airbnb** (2009). The Hedge dossier offers Rosa's $20M-revenue agent deployment at Traba, which is real, and Button's prior YC shipping of Fernstone, which is real. Both register. Neither is a cereal-box moment. A signed wholesale placement with a named retail agency, a published bind ratio against incumbent Amwins/RT Specialty/CRC, or a single retail principal willing to put their name on a testimonial instead of an anonymous quote would move Hedge from MID to HIGH on the dimension where I most need the lift.

Compared singly to **Stripe** (2010) on co-founder configuration, the two-Lukes-from-Traba structure passes the threshold I set in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) -- two founders with prior working relationship, complementary coverage (growth/distribution versus scaled-systems/agent infrastructure). It is not the Collison-brothers ceiling. But it is not a hackathon pairing either, and Hedge clears the bar I most often see broken at the application stage.

My 36-month forecast for Hedge is mundane: $4-8M in wholesale commission revenue, acquired by a mid-market wholesaler rollup (Worldwide Facilities-adjacent, or by Novella itself after its $21M Series A becomes a Series B) for $40-80M -- a respectable operator outcome, not a Black Swan. The mechanism producing that ceiling is the same one producing the dimensional signature: growth operators executing a real schlep can build a real business in E&S wholesale, but without the hacker dimension the product layer ends up looking like the other AI brokerages in the cohort, and the acquirer pool prices it on commission run-rate rather than on platform terminal value.

The bull case that should sting is the **Coinbase** (2012) exception itself. Armstrong was Head of Risk Management at **Airbnb** before Coinbase -- an operator title, not a hacker title -- and I funded him anyway because he had the schlep appetite and shipped the wallet. The Hedge founders have the schlep appetite documented in CA license #6018729 and the appointments with 30+ carriers. If I am being honest, the only thing separating Hedge from the Armstrong pattern is the absence of the equivalent shipped artifact and the presence of a better-funded direct competitor (Novella, $21M, Brewer Lane led, May 2026) that **Coinbase** did not face at its YC moment. That competitive density matters less than my essays sometimes admit -- Stripe shipped into a crowded payments field too -- but the Fernstone overlap is the disqualifying tell, because the Armstrong pattern required undivided founder attention on a single regulatory grind, and Hedge does not yet have that. The condition that flips my verdict is a clean public statement from Button that Fernstone is shut down or fully spun out and that Hedge is his only company; absent that, the soft pass holds.
