# FullSeam -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with FullSeam in one sentence: this is a sane person with a sane idea, and that's the worst combination for generating outsized returns. Thomas Dowling is a Rhodes Scholar who co-founded TaxProper, processed over a billion dollars in annual property tax payments, and got it acquired by Opendoor. All three founders worked together at UIUC and through the TaxProper journey. The "sane" credentials are legitimate — probably top-decile for a pre-seed team. But "AI agent that automates accounts payable and receivable" is not an insane idea. It is quite possibly the single most obvious application of large language models to business workflows that exists in 2026. The company literally self-tags as "Artificial Intelligence" and "SaaS" — my two publicly stated anti-patterns in one product. When I described Coinbase as a "sane person, insane idea" bet, the key was that in 2012 most of Silicon Valley thought Bitcoin was a joke. Nobody in 2026 thinks automating AP/AR with AI is a joke. Everyone thinks it's a great idea. That's the problem.

Run my AI kill test and the answer is immediate: remove AI and there is no FullSeam. The AI isn't cherry-on-top — it IS the sundae. This fails the test I apply to every company now. Would this be a good business if AI didn't exist? No, it would be another workflow tool fighting Stampli and Bill.com for integrations. Meanwhile, the incumbents who own the platforms FullSeam plugs into — Intuit with QuickBooks, Oracle with NetSuite, Xero — are actively shipping native AI automation features. I said on CNBC that the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution, and in this market, the distribution belongs to the accounting platforms themselves. FullSeam is middleware sitting between platforms that have every incentive and capability to absorb this functionality. The nine-integration breadth is real engineering work, but it's a software moat, and software moats are exactly what I've been arguing are dissolving.

The competitive landscape makes this worse. Auditoria.ai raised $60.5 million and already does the same multi-function AP/AR agentic automation. Stampli has $148 million and 500K customers. Vic.ai has $52 million. FullSeam has $500K. The capital asymmetry isn't just a funding disadvantage — it's a signal that this opportunity is fully consensus. When four well-funded companies are already attacking the same thesis, you're not betting on a contrarian insight. You're betting on execution speed against opponents with 100-300x your resources. I've seen this movie before with Birchbox — narrative magnitude in a replicable category produces the worst venture outcome.

Now, the bull case deserves genuine engagement because the founders earned it. If TaxProper's team can ship faster than Auditoria.ai and build genuine workflow-data lock-in before Intuit ships native agents, there's a credible path to a meaningful acquisition outcome. The combined AP+AR TAM is real at ~$9.5 billion. The founding team's experience processing $1B+ in financial transactions means they understand the edge cases and compliance requirements that trip up teams building financial software for the first time. And the deep co-founder relationships — all from UIUC, years of working together through an exit — are exactly what I look for in team durability. If Intuit or Sage decided to buy rather than build, FullSeam could be a solid $100-200M acquisition in three to four years. That's not nothing. But it's not an infinity story. The narrative ceiling is capped by platform dependency — FullSeam will always be an application on someone else's infrastructure, never a primitive that others build on.

The structural position is what kills this for me. FullSeam creates no new primitive. It establishes no protocol. It builds no physical-world operation that software amplifies but cannot replace. It's pure software automating other software. Compare this to Craftwork, where the moat is painting crews and scheduling logistics that no AI can replicate — FullSeam's moat is API integrations and GL coding rules, which any LLM can learn. The "workflow data moat" the dossier mentions is described as "nascent and unproven," which is the polite way of saying it doesn't exist yet. And in a category with this many well-funded competitors racing to accumulate the same data, first-mover advantage on workflow intelligence is measured in months, not years.

I respect these founders. A second-time YC team with a real exit, deep domain experience, and long co-founder relationships is exactly the profile I want to back — on a different idea. If Dowling, Coppa, and Segal walked in with something structurally weird, something most investors would dismiss, something that leveraged their fintech operations experience into a physical-world or protocol-layer play, I'd lean forward. But AI-powered SaaS for accounting automation is a feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent with distribution, and my money is too expensive to bet on a race where the starting line is this crowded.
