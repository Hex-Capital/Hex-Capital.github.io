# Haladir -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most revealing fact in this dossier is not the research paper or the team composition -- it is the trajectory of self-description. Haladir was first "an AI-powered mainframe modernization suite" for COBOL migration. Then it became focused on "AI-led software development" correctness. Now it is building "Operational Superintelligence" through reinforcement learning with solver-verified rewards for operations research. Three identities in what appears to be a matter of months. This is not pivoting in the way a company refines its approach while holding a singular vision -- this is a team searching for a thesis. The language of definite optimism ("superintelligence") has been grafted onto the practice of indefinite exploration. When I hear a founder describe their plan as iterative and pivot-ready, I know they lack conviction. When I see a company whose entire framing has shifted twice before it has a single customer, the conclusion is the same regardless of what they call themselves.

What is the secret here? The implicit claim is that operations research is the next frontier for RLVR -- that the same methodology proven for code verification and mathematical reasoning can be extended to constrained optimization through formalized reward signals. This is a perfectly reasonable observation. It is also the observation that any competent ML researcher would arrive at after reading the DeepSeek R1 paper. If verifiable rewards work where you can check answers computationally, and solvers can check optimization answers computationally, then solvers should generate rewards for RL training in optimization domains. The logic is straightforward. It is not a secret -- it is a syllogism. A genuine secret would be something that most experts in the field actively reject: perhaps that enterprise operations are *fundamentally more formalizable* than the industry believes, or that solver-verified RL agents will render the entire $10 billion operations research software industry obsolete within five years. But nothing in the dossier suggests the founders hold or articulate such a thesis. ConstraintBench documents a problem (LLMs fail at constrained optimization with 30.5% joint feasibility-and-optimality); the proposed solution (train them better using solver-verified rewards) is the consensus response to any demonstrated LLM weakness.

The monopoly question is where this unravels entirely. Each operations research domain -- crew scheduling, vehicle routing, supply chain optimization, production planning -- has its own constraint structures, its own solver requirements, its own data formats. Formalizing rewards for crew assignment does not automatically transfer to warehouse logistics. This creates a consulting-like dynamic where value scales linearly with domain-specific effort, not exponentially through network effects or platform economics. There is no structural mechanism I can identify by which early dominance in one OR vertical compounds into an unassailable position across verticals. Compare this to Palantir, where every government deployment deepened integration and raised switching costs, or to Stripe, where every developer using the API attracted more developers through ecosystem effects. Haladir's benchmark ownership is a thin reed -- benchmarks in ML are superseded every twelve to eighteen months, and ConstraintBench with two GitHub stars has not yet demonstrated the community adoption that would make it a standard others build against.

The strongest case for this company would require believing three things simultaneously: that formalized reward signals for operations research represent infrastructure so difficult to build that well-funded AI labs and adjacent formal-verification companies (Harmonic at $295M, Code Metal at $161.5M) will not build it themselves; that this team of undergraduate researchers can translate a benchmark paper into enterprise-grade infrastructure that logistics and supply chain companies will pay for; and that the accumulated formalization of constraints across verticals will create a compounding data and IP advantage that eventually constitutes a moat. If all three are true, this could be the picks-and-shovels play for AI-driven operations -- the company that makes the informal formal across the entire enterprise optimization stack. I have backed young founders before. Zuckerberg was nineteen. But Zuckerberg had a product that 100,000 people were already using and the conviction to reject a billion-dollar acquisition offer. Buterin had a whitepaper that reimagined the structure of programmable money. What Haladir has is a benchmark paper and a tagline borrowed from the frontier-AI discourse. The gap between the ambition of "Operational Superintelligence" and the reality of two GitHub stars and zero customers is not a gap I see being bridged by secret knowledge the rest of the world is missing -- it is a gap I see being filled by the same incremental formalization work that any competent team could undertake.

The technology is sound but not discontinuous. Using solvers to verify RL rewards is a novel intersection of known methods, not a new method. The ConstraintBench finding that frontier LLMs achieve only 65% constraint satisfaction is genuinely informative, but documenting a problem is not the same as possessing a 10x solution to it. No deployed system or demonstrated improvement has been shown. The RLVR methodology itself is well-documented in public research -- the barrier to replication is interdisciplinary expertise, not proprietary technology. This is important but not unassailable. A team at Databricks, which has already applied RLVR to the BIRD SQL benchmark, could extend similar techniques to constrained optimization with their existing infrastructure and vastly larger engineering team.

I pass. The thesis is logical but not contrarian. The trajectory suggests searching, not building. The monopoly path requires a compounding mechanism I cannot identify. And the founding team, while technically capable enough to publish a research paper, has not yet demonstrated the kind of conviction -- the refusal of an easier path, the specific and unwavering vision for what the world looks like if they succeed -- that distinguishes founders who build monopolies from founders who write papers.
