# Crow -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most revealing data point in this entire dossier isn't about Crow. It's about Command AI. A YC company from S20 built essentially the same product — in-app AI assistance for software products — raised $23.8M, reached $3.3M in revenue with 39 people, and was acquired by Amplitude for roughly $45M. That's not a bad outcome in absolute terms, but it's not the kind of outcome that makes angel investing work. Angel returns follow a power law, and I need the companies I back to have a plausible path to being enormous. When the most directly comparable company in your space topped out at $3.3M revenue before getting absorbed by an incumbent, that tells you something about the gravitational pull of this category — it tends to collapse into features of larger platforms rather than standing alone.

The first question I ask about any startup is how the founders found the problem. Did they stumble into it through their own experience, or did they sit down and generate it? The dossier gives me no organic origin story for Crow. Both founders "worked at AI startups building conversational interface infrastructure," and the timing of the company coincides precisely with LLM function-calling reaching production reliability in 2024. The idea reads as: "This technology now works, so let's make it easy to embed." That's technology looking for a problem, which is the inversion of how the best startups begin. Drew Houston didn't start Dropbox because cloud storage APIs had matured — he started it because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Collisons didn't start Stripe because payment APIs were possible — they started it because they were personally sick of integrating with PayPal. When I look at Crow, I see an idea that could have been conceived by any pair of smart engineers who read the OpenAI function-calling docs and noticed that CopilotKit had 10,000 GitHub stars. That's not organic discovery. That's pattern matching.

The schlep test is where this falls apart most clearly. Stripe worked because the schlep was enormous — banking regulations, PCI compliance, fraud detection, currency conversion, international tax law. The ugly regulatory and logistical complexity created a moat that took a decade to build. What's the schlep for Crow? Connecting an LLM to APIs via OpenAPI specs and building a managed proxy with auth and rate limiting. That's real engineering, but it's the kind of engineering any competent team at Intercom or Amplitude can replicate in a quarter. CopilotKit already does the core of it open-source. When the hard part of your problem is something that an open-source project with 10K stars has already addressed, you don't have schlep blindness protecting you — you have a commoditization timer running.

The strongest argument for Crow is timing and packaging. Maybe Command AI was too early — before LLM function-calling was reliable enough for production action execution. Maybe the managed-service wrapper, done well, becomes the category winner the way Heroku beat raw AWS for a generation of developers. And the founders have genuine strengths: both are Berkeley EECS/CS, the co-founder relationship is real (met first day of college, bonded over football, coded together), Aryan's military background suggests determination, and Jai's Regents' Scholarship signals raw intellectual ability. If the in-app AI copilot category is going to produce a standalone winner rather than being absorbed into incumbents, a fast-moving two-person team with YC backing and a self-serve developer GTM has a reasonable shot. The WalkMe-to-SAP acquisition at $1.5B and Whatfix at $138M revenue prove the broader "help users navigate complex software" market has real money in it. If conversational action execution is genuinely better than guided walkthroughs — and it might be — the category could be large.

But I keep coming back to structure. Intercom built Fin to $100M ARR internally. Amplitude acquired Command AI. SAP acquired WalkMe. The incumbents are not asleep — they're actively consolidating this space. And unlike payments, where Stripe's regulatory moat made it nearly impossible for incumbents to replicate the product, connecting LLMs to APIs is something every platform company can do. The moat here is execution speed, not structural complexity. Execution-speed moats work when you're racing against slow-moving incumbents, but Intercom shipped Fin to $100M ARR faster than most startups ship anything. I'd need to see evidence that Crow has discovered something non-obvious about the problem — some insight into action safety, workflow orchestration, or enterprise deployment that isn't apparent from the outside. The dossier shows no such insight. What I see is a well-built product in a category where the ceiling is capped by commoditization from below (open-source) and consolidation from above (incumbents acquiring).

I'm passing. The founders are competent and their co-founder relationship is solid, but the idea doesn't pass my organic test or my schlep test. This is a hot category with well-funded competitors and low structural barriers, which is precisely the configuration I avoid. The best startups attack problems that everyone knows about but nobody wants to touch. Everyone wants to touch AI copilots right now.
