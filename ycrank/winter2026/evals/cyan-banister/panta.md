# Panta -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Panta is that it's popular. Harper raised $47 million in February doing essentially the same thing. Fernstone and Acolite are in YC attacking adjacent problems. Newfront just got acquired for $1.3 billion. When I see a category this validated, this competed, with this much capital already deployed — that's usually my signal to look elsewhere. The most promising companies tend to share a few characteristics: they are not popular, and they are difficult to assess. Panta is neither. An AI-native insurance brokerage is now a recognized category with institutional backing. The weirdness has been priced out. If I'm the fifth or sixth investor to see this thesis, I've already lost the advantage that matters to me.

The founder story is where I struggle most. I don't know Vincent and Frank's personal stories — the dossier gives me Google ML engineer and Apple full-stack engineer who identified a large, manual market and applied their AI skills to it. That's a smart analytical bet, and I respect the technical credentials enormously. But when I ask "tell me your story — not the business," I'm listening for the biographical thread that makes this founder inseparable from this problem. Was Vincent's family construction business unable to get coverage? Did Frank's parents run a trucking company that got gouged on insurance? I have no evidence of any personal stake beyond professional capability. The one signal I find encouraging is that both founders went and got their own broker licenses rather than hiring domain experts — that shows a willingness to go deep, to get their hands dirty in the actual machinery of the industry. That's real. But it's not the same as having lived on the wrong side of this problem.

Where Panta genuinely speaks to me is the economic access dimension. This is the part a generic analyst might overlook. Hard-to-place businesses — construction crews, cannabis operators, hazmat truckers, small manufacturers — these are the people who currently wait weeks for coverage, get gouged on pricing, and sometimes can't get insured at all. The traditional brokerage system is one of those invisible power structures where opacity benefits the incumbents at the expense of the small operator. When I see that Panta undercut a traditional quote by 38% on a complex oil-and-gas placement, that resonates with the same pattern I saw in Affirm making credit transparent, or Carta making equity visible. These are real businesses run by real people who are being poorly served by an industry that scales with headcount instead of technology. If Panta can consistently deliver faster, cheaper placement for the businesses that traditional brokers deprioritize, that's genuine economic impact. The E&S market specifically — $135 billion, growing at 12.5% annually — is where the most complex, most underserved risks live. That's a meaningful niche.

I also appreciate the regulatory complexity here. Getting licensed in 50 states as a surplus lines brokerage is not trivial. Operating autonomous AI agents across real carrier portals, navigating E&O liability, managing binding authority — this is genuinely hard in the way that deters most early-stage competitors. My years at IronPort taught me that regulated complexity can be the best moat if you're willing to do the compliance work others won't. But here's my concern: Harper has the same licenses, more funding, and a twelve-month head start on carrier relationships. In insurance, carrier relationships compound — each placement teaches you which carriers accept which risks at what price. That data advantage grows with volume, and Harper has roughly 25x Panta's disclosed customer base. The E&S specialty focus buys Panta some differentiation, but it's unclear whether that niche is defensible once Harper decides to move upmarket into harder risks.

The bull case I take seriously: these are exceptional technical builders who chose the hardest version of a massive problem. The Mac Mini infrastructure running real agents across carrier portals is not an LLM wrapper — it's systems engineering of real complexity. The early traction (127 reviews at 4.9/5, hundreds of businesses in three months) suggests the product works. And the E&S market is structurally underserved in a way that could sustain a large independent business even with well-funded competitors. If Vincent and Frank have the kind of quiet, relentless drive that their career trajectories suggest — and if their co-founder relationship can survive the inevitable funding gap with Harper — this could be the Crusoe Energy of insurance: a company that started in an overlooked corner and became infrastructure. But I'd need to hear their story to believe it. Right now, I see smart engineers in a hot category, and that's not enough for me to write a check from my own money.

On co-founder dynamics, I have almost nothing to work with. No shared history, no public data on how they met, no signal on how they handle disagreement. After HQ Trivia taught me that even perfect timing and product can't survive founder conflict, this absence of information keeps me cautious. Two engineers from different companies with no visible prior relationship building together in a high-stress, competitive market — I'd want to probe this deeply before any commitment.
