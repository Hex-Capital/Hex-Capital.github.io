# Modaic -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Modaic's most striking feature isn't what's present — it's what's absent from a field that's already been claimed. Braintrust raised $80M at an $800M valuation in February 2026 and already counts Dropbox among its customers. Patronus AI has $20M and a team of ex-Meta ML engineers. Confident AI is building open-source community gravity around DeepEval. Into this field walk Farouk Adeleke and Tyrin-Ian Todd — two people, ~116 GitHub stars across their organization, and claimed design partnerships with Accenture, Dropbox, and Vercel that no independent source could confirm. The Dropbox overlap with Braintrust's existing customer list is particularly conspicuous. I've backed founders who were outgunned before — I wrote my Uber check when 300+ AngelList investors had passed — but those founders were entering spaces nobody else wanted. LLM optimization tooling is a space everybody wants. Every fund I know has this category on their wishlist. No one shifts in their chair when you pitch "we make AI decisions more reliable for enterprises."

When I ask my opening question — tell me their story, not the business — what comes back for Modaic is credential, not crucible. Farouk Adeleke dropped out of Boston University to build this, with prior stops at Wayfair, IBM, and Harvard. Tyrin-Ian Todd graduated from MIT in AI & Decision Making, trained foundation models at the MIT Media Lab, interned at Bloomberg and Axon. Both are technically sharp. But neither founder's biography reveals a personal encounter with the problem that predates the company. Farouk built Spydr ("GitHub for LLM context") as recently as June 2025 — Product Hunt #12 with a score of 140 — and then moved to Modaic. Tyrin co-founded Dyle, an AI customer success agent, before pivoting here. Two different products from each founder within roughly a year doesn't read as mission inevitability. It reads as talented people exploring the AI space looking for the thing that sticks. That exploration is legitimate, but when I compare it to the Crusoe Energy founders who witnessed gas flaring firsthand in North Dakota oil fields and couldn't unsee it, or to Flock Safety's founder whose obsession with neighborhood safety predated any business plan, the biographical signal here is thin.

The invisible system test is where Modaic falls furthest from my framework. LLM classification errors and brittle content moderation rules are real operational problems — but they're problems experienced by engineering teams inside well-resourced enterprises. Modaic's customers are Accenture, Dropbox, Vercel. These are not vulnerable people trapped inside opaque structures that extract value from them. The information asymmetry Modaic addresses is between a company and its own AI system — operational inefficiency, not structural exploitation. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity, it was revealing hidden costs that affected millions of consumers who didn't understand what they were paying. When Ownwell automated property tax appeals, it was disrupting a system that punished people who couldn't afford consultants. Modaic is building developer tooling for sophisticated enterprises. That can be a good business, but it doesn't carry the structural weight that drives my deepest conviction bets.

The strongest bull case for Modaic: DSPy represents a genuine paradigm shift in how LLM programs get built — moving from artisanal prompt engineering to declarative, optimizable programs — and Modaic is positioning itself as the infrastructure layer for that shift. Farouk's LinkedIn headline literally reads "dspy infra @ modaic.dev." If DSPy becomes the dominant paradigm, the per-customer feedback flywheel (routing uncertain decisions to humans, using that feedback to rewrite model instructions) could create real switching costs as customers accumulate calibrated instruction sets. The convergence of enterprise LLM adoption moving from pilots to production in 2026 creates genuine timing pressure. And the architectural argument against Braintrust — that retrofitting an active optimization loop onto an observability platform requires rearchitecting their core product — has structural merit. For this bull case to work, DSPy would need to become the standard, Modaic would need to build enough data gravity before Braintrust's $80M catches up, and those unverified enterprise partnerships would need to be real and deepening. That's a lot of contingencies stacked on a two-person team building on an MIT-licensed open-source framework they don't control.

Modaic lands as a Pass for me. The two dimensions that lock this are biographical forge and contrarian conviction — together they account for 55 of my 100 points, and Modaic scores below my defaults on both because the evidence actively points away from my criteria rather than merely being absent. Credentialed founders exploring a consensus-attractive AI tooling market, competing against entrenched players with hundred-million-dollar war chests, building on someone else's open-source foundation. I wish Farouk and Tyrin well — the technical architecture is thoughtful and the DSPy bet could prove prescient — but this is a market-gap identification investment, not a biographical-inevitability investment, and those aren't the bets that have defined my career.
