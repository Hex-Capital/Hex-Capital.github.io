# Burt -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

HappyRobot has raised $62M, reports eight-figure revenue, and has signed DHL, Werner, and Schneider. Two other YC W26 companies -- Lanesurf and Lighthouz AI -- are attacking the same freight broker customer base from different angles. Parade has $36M and is adding AI automation features. When I see a pre-seed company entering a market where the category leader is already at $500M valuation and scaling rapidly, the asymmetric upside has been priced away. This is not contrarian. This is consensus with a capital C. The market has already decided that AI agents for freight brokerage are valuable -- the question is who captures that value, and the answer is unlikely to be the team that arrived last with the least capital and the least domain knowledge.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Bobby Zhong and Kurt Sharma are clearly competent engineers -- Replo experience building coding agents, UC Berkeley EECS, founding engineer roles at YC companies. They know how to build AI agents. But what do they know about freight brokerage that a competitor can't learn? The answer, based on everything in this dossier, is nothing. Neither founder has worked in logistics, run a brokerage, managed carrier relationships, or processed a single POD document before starting this company. The YC page still references "fine tune and deploy specialized models" in its title, which tells me the logistics focus is recent -- they went looking for a vertical to apply their AI agent skills to, rather than being pulled into this problem by years of accumulated frustration and insight. That's the difference between teachable skill and specific knowledge. Building LLM agents is a skill any strong engineer can develop. Understanding why a particular broker's POD chasing workflow breaks down at the carrier communication layer -- that's knowledge born from lived experience.

The bull case would require believing that engineering velocity and AI-native architecture can substitute for domain depth in a relationship-driven industry. It's possible. If Burt embeds deeply with three or four mid-market brokerages, they could accumulate process-specific workflow data that makes their agents meaningfully better than generic solutions. HappyRobot's enterprise focus on DHL and Werner might leave the long tail of smaller brokerages underserved -- and the freight downturn creates genuine urgency to cut back-office costs. If the founders can learn the domain fast enough while iterating on the product, and if their "configurable AI teammate" approach proves more adaptable than HappyRobot's enterprise playbook, there's a path. But that path requires winning a learning race against teams that started with domain knowledge and a funding race against teams with 100x their capital. I've seen that movie before, and the ending is rarely good for the underfunded generalist.

The leverage architecture here is standard labor-replacement SaaS -- automating tasks that humans currently perform manually. That's a real business, but it's not creating a new form of leverage that didn't previously exist. Compare this to what Uber did: it didn't automate the dispatcher's job, it created an entirely new category where every car owner became a potential driver. Burt is making existing freight brokerages slightly more efficient. That's arithmetic improvement, not geometric leverage. The product integrates with existing TMS platforms rather than replacing them, which means it sits at the application layer, not the infrastructure layer. My portfolio leans heavily toward infrastructure and picks-and-shovels plays for a reason -- they compound. A task automation layer on top of someone else's workflow orchestration system doesn't compound in the same way.

Bobby's career arc -- Educado for seven months, Moonwish Studios, PipeIQ internship, Pirros for a year, Replo, now Burt -- reads as someone searching for the right problem rather than someone who found their problem years ago and can't stop working on it. That's not an integrity concern; it's a founder-problem fit concern. The best founders I've backed had been thinking about their specific problem long before they started a company. The pivot from a general model fine-tuning platform to logistics AI teammates in the span of a few months suggests the domain choice was strategic rather than obsessive. In a market this competitive, obsession is not optional.
