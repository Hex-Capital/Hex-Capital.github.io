# Remix -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Remix is that the market it's entering is the exact inverse of schlep-blind. AI content generation is the *first* thing every developer thinks of when they get access to a new language model. It's the "recipe site" of the LLM era -- the idea that sounds reasonable precisely because it requires no painful insight to arrive at. Thousands of teams have already built here. Jasper raised $131 million. Copy.ai got acquired. Canva, HubSpot, and Hootsuite are all bolting on AI content features. When I wrote about schlep blindness, I was describing how great opportunities hide behind ugly, tedious work that founders unconsciously avoid. AI content generation is the opposite: everyone consciously runs toward it. That means competition is structural and permanent, not temporary.

How did Sam Kaplan find this problem? The dossier answers clearly: he explored "multiple ideas including GPTTok and ChatSF" before landing on Remix. The parent company publishes a separate app called "Resonate Chat." The YC community noted "vague pivot language suggesting lack of focus." This is a founder who decided to start a company and then went searching for the right idea. Compare that to Drew Houston, who kept forgetting his USB drive and built Dropbox because he was sick of the problem. Or the Collisons, who were so frustrated with payment APIs that they just started fixing them. When the idea comes after the decision to start a startup rather than before it, the motivation structure is inverted. The startup exists to be a startup, not to solve a problem the founder can't stop thinking about.

The voice-first angle is the strongest element of the pitch, and I want to be honest about the bull case. If speaking into your phone and getting ten pieces of polished content back is genuinely 10x faster than typing prompts into Jasper, that's a real UX wedge. The filmmaking background (15 short films) gives Kaplan some consumer sensibility that pure engineers lack. And "the first consumer application bringing Claude Code to consumers" is at least a novel framing -- if the multi-agent architecture produces meaningfully better output than single-prompt tools, users will notice. The scenario where this works is one where voice-first input creates a habit loop so natural that creators default to it before they even consider alternatives. But for that to happen, the output quality has to be extraordinary, because the switching cost from existing tools is near zero and every incumbent can add a microphone button. This needs to work not because the market is big -- it's the *because* the market is big that it's dangerous. Consensus opportunities attract consensus competitors.

Sam Kaplan is technically capable -- Harvard CS, engineering manager at Brex during its hypergrowth phase, building with LLMs since GPT-3 beta. He shipped an iOS app. That's real. But the solo founder issue compounds the market risk. I've been direct about this for twenty years: cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. I made Drew Houston find one, and Houston had already built a working prototype of something people clearly wanted. Here, we have a solo founder in a maximally crowded market with a pivot history and an app that has one rating on the App Store. That single rating is actually worse than having no app at all, because it means the product is live and nobody is pulling it toward them. At Airbnb, when the founders flew to NYC, they found hosts who *needed* the rentals to pay their rent. Where are the users who need Remix?

The market dynamics are the final concern. Jasper's revenue fell from $120 million to $55 million after ChatGPT commoditized the basic text generation that was their core value proposition. It recovered to $88 million, but the trajectory tells a story about demand ceilings in AI content generation. When the foundational capability is available for free from ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, the wedge for a dedicated tool has to be extremely specific and extremely defensible. Voice-first input and parallel agents are differentiating features, not moats. Any well-resourced team can add both within a quarter.
