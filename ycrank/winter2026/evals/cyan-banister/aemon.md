# Aemon -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me first: two twenty-year-old twin brothers claim they beat Google DeepMind's AlphaEvolve on circle packing with under ten dollars of compute. That's a David-and-Goliath story, and I'm usually the investor who gets a jolt from those. When I wrote my first angel check into SpaceX, part of what I responded to was someone attempting something that seemed absurd relative to the incumbents. But the thing about SpaceX is that private space was deeply unpopular — people thought Elon was burning money on a fantasy. When I look at Aemon, I see two brilliant young researchers entering one of the most popular categories in all of venture capital right now. AI agents for R&D. Cognition raised $900 million. Sakana AI is valued at $2.6 billion. Periodic Labs took $300 million at seed. Lila Sciences raised $550 million. This isn't a pocket of dreamers nobody else has found — this is a category where consensus has already formed and enormous capital has already been deployed. That LinkedIn announcement got 248 likes and 73 comments. That's popularity, and popularity is not my signal.

If I were sitting across from Ray and Richard, my first question would be the one I always ask: tell me your story, not the business. And what the dossier gives me is impressive credentials — ICLR and EMNLP publications before turning twenty, international medals in math and robotics, dropped out of top CS programs to build this. That's prodigious talent. But it's not a survival story. There's no biographical thread I can trace from these founders' lived experience to the specific problem of R&D optimization. Richard wrote a LinkedIn post called "Why I started a business at 20" — that's a young person seizing a technical opportunity they're well-positioned to capture, which I respect, but it's fundamentally different from the founder who's been obsessed with a problem because they've lived inside it. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else dismissed. The problem was biographical, not opportunistic. With Aemon, I see sharp minds recognizing a gap between what DeepMind built internally and what's available commercially. That's a legitimate insight, but it doesn't give me the conviction I build from a founder whose identity is fused with their mission.

The one dimension where Aemon genuinely lights up for me is co-founder alignment. After what happened with HQ Trivia — a perfect product at the perfect time destroyed entirely by founder conflict — I probe this harder than anything except the founder's story. Twin brothers who grew up together, published research together, and both chose to drop out of school to build this company together? That's the kind of deep relational foundation I've come to prefer. I've said publicly that I like family-run businesses because conflict resolution has already been battle-tested over years. Twins have been negotiating with each other since they shared a womb. I don't know the unnamed third team member, which is a gap, but the core co-founder bond here is as strong as I've seen at pre-seed.

Let me give the bull case its due, because I want to be honest about what could make this a great investment. The structural argument is real: DeepMind built the most capable evolutionary code optimization system in the world but will almost certainly never sell it as a standalone product — it's peripheral to Alphabet's mission. That's the same structural gap Palantir exploited when it took intelligence-community tools and sold them to enterprise. If Aemon can be the commercial layer for a capability that only exists inside one of the world's largest companies, that's a genuine moat. Add to that: these founders demonstrated they can achieve comparable results to a team with essentially unlimited resources using less than ten dollars of compute. If that efficiency advantage holds across problem domains beyond circle packing, the unit economics could be extraordinary. And the timing — AlphaEvolve validated the category in May 2025, frontier LLMs crossed the capability threshold for reliable code generation at scale — creates a window. Everything about this screams "technically excellent team at a real inflection point."

But I keep coming back to what's missing from my framework. This company doesn't create economic access for underserved populations. It optimizes R&D processes for engineering teams at well-funded organizations — tech companies, research labs, financial firms. That's valuable work, but it's not making an invisible system visible or opening a door for people who were locked out. It's not operating in a stigmatized or regulated space that other investors won't touch — it's operating in the single hottest category in venture. And the founders, while technically exceptional, don't show me the kind of biographical grit I've built my entire investment practice around. I've learned to trust that the founders who persist through the inevitable crises — the ones who don't quit when the Bitcoin market collapses or the regulatory environment shifts — are the ones whose identity is bound up in the problem itself, not the ones who identified a clever market gap. I could be wrong. These twins might have that depth and I'm just not seeing it in the available information. But based on what I can evaluate, this is a pass for me — not because the company isn't good, but because it doesn't fit how I invest.
