# Tasklet -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Tasklet is a Harvey-style revenue curve bolted to a Lyft-style structural position -- $5M ARR with >1,200% growth since January 1 (USV blog, 2026) is "just working" in the **Harvey** (2023) sense, but Andrew Lee himself frames the category as a "race to be a horizontal platform" (Cognitive Revolution, 2026), which is the structural disclosure that puts Tasklet on the wrong side of my **Lyft** pass framework. The two signals don't cancel -- they define the trade. Tasklet is a velocity bet whose underlying position is contested, and the question is whether the runtime DNA from Firebase + Shortwave converts that velocity into an index position before OpenAI's agent layer and Zapier Agents subsume the wedge.

**Harvey** (2023) is the load-bearing analogy on revenue velocity, and Tasklet earns the comparison on the curve itself -- $5M ARR reached within roughly four months of the October 2025 beta launch, with the standalone product surpassing Shortwave's first four years of monthly revenue (Shortwave blog, 2026). That's not enterprise-pilot tourism. The divergence from **Harvey** is the buyer composition: **Harvey** (2023) compounded on named AmLaw-100 logos signing six-figure contracts where switching costs are bar-association-deep, while Tasklet's $35/Pro and $100/$250 tier mix (tasklet.ai/pricing, 2026) reads as power-user prosumer expansion -- the exact cohort that ChatGPT's agent shipping eats first. I would pay up for the curve in the **Harvey** template; I'd pay up *less* for the curve when the cohort is structurally exposed to the platform that catalyzed the market.

**Lyft** Series C failed my test because rideshare's network effects accrued to the #1, leaving #2 captured at a fraction of the economics (20VC interview) -- the failure mechanism was structural, not executional. Tasklet's dossier surfaces the same pattern from a different vector: Lindy ($53.6M raised), Gumloop ($20M), Relay.app ($8.2M), plus Zapier Agents, OpenAI, and Anthropic, all overlapping directly (Crunchbase; Lindy blog, 2026). The dossier even quotes the Lindy comparative blog noting Tasklet "is leaner and faster but covers fewer connectors" than Zapier -- meaning Tasklet trades integration breadth for agentic execution speed, which is exactly the kind of feature-level differentiation that **Lyft** also had against Uber and that didn't matter. Andrew Lee's "always bet on the models" thesis (Cognitive Revolution, 2026) makes the **Lyft** parallel sharper, not weaker -- the CEO is publicly arguing the same logic that killed Shortwave's email client applies to horizontal agent UIs.

Founder execution rhythm on Tasklet is where my **Perplexity** (2022) template lights up without rung-bracketing -- Andrew Lee co-founded Firebase (YC S11, Google acquisition 2014), Jonny Dimond was tech lead for Cloud Firestore at Google post-acquisition, and they've shipped together for roughly 12 years across Firebase, Google, Shortwave, and now Tasklet (USV blog, 2026). The Tasklet runtime is not a 2025 prototype; it was carved out of the multi-year agent stack already running inside Shortwave (Shortwave blog, 2026). 32 new integrations shipped in a single release including Microsoft apps (LinkedIn @taskletai, 2026) is the live shipping-rhythm artifact. This is a developer-platform team that has previously shipped infrastructure to Google scale -- pedigree is irrelevant, prior-shipped-artifacts are decisive, and Firebase is the artifact.

Mechanism analysis on the market catalyst: Andrew Lee names the catalyst precisely -- frontier model quality crossed a threshold where on-the-fly UI generation and computer-use replace dedicated SaaS interfaces (Cognitive Revolution, 2026), which is the MCP + Claude-computer-use + on-demand-UI step-function from the last 18 months. That's a real re-opening, on the same template as transformer-after-ChatGPT triggering my **Harvey** (2023) and **Decagon** (2023) entries. But the catalyst here is the *same* catalyst every agent startup names, which compresses the differentiation window -- 18 months from now, the catalyst's beneficiary is whichever player owns either the model (OpenAI, Anthropic) or the integration registry (Zapier), and Tasklet has to wedge itself between them on runtime quality. The catalyst is real; the share-of-catalyst-value is the question.

Counterfactual on single-miracle: for Tasklet to register as a single-miracle bet rather than a multi-miracle dependency, computer-use reliability would have to be effectively solved by the foundation-model providers (Tasklet inherits it for free) AND Tasklet's integration registry would have to grow faster than Zapier's 7,000+ connector lead by some compounding mechanism I cannot identify in the dossier. Today the dossier flags computer-use as "an industry-wide unsolved reliability problem" (The New Stack, 2026) and enterprise logging/security/cost controls as "on the roadmap" rather than GA (thecrunch.io, 2026) -- that's two independent miracles plus a horizontal-platform race, which is the **NFX** *Unusual Signs* multi-miracle pattern I almost always pass on. The flip condition is concrete: if computer-use becomes a commodity primitive shipped by Anthropic/OpenAI at >95% task reliability within 12 months, Tasklet's miracle collapses to "own the runtime/integration layer," which is single-miracle.

The 36-month forecast: Tasklet gets acquired by a model provider (OpenAI or Anthropic) or a workflow incumbent (HubSpot, Salesforce, or Zapier itself) in months 18-30 at $600M-$1.5B for the runtime + the Firebase-DNA team, OR plateaus at $30-60M ARR as ChatGPT's persistent agents and Zapier Agents absorb the prosumer wedge that drove the 1,200% curve. The mechanism producing this bifurcation is Andrew Lee's own thesis applied to his own product -- the horizontal-platform race rewards either the model owner or the distribution incumbent, not the independent runtime, unless the runtime gets bought for the team and the agent stack before that compression happens. The Firebase precedent makes the acquisition path the base case: this is the second time Andrew Lee has built developer-cloud infrastructure that a strategic should buy, and the Google/Firebase 2014 outcome is the literal template.

The strongest objection cuts the other direction -- the bull case is that Tasklet is actually executing the **Stripe** (2010) / **Coinbase** (2013) index template I most reliably underestimate. Stripe looked like a horizontal payments race against Braintree, Adyen, and PayPal in 2010, and I almost missed that "horizontal commodity race" disguises the index position right up until distribution compounds. If Tasklet's integration registry crosses a network-effect threshold where every new connector makes every existing customer more valuable, the Firebase team is building the *Stripe of agent runtimes* and $5M ARR in 4 months is the early signature, not the ceiling. The dismantling: Stripe owned a regulated rail (card networks) that took 8 years to compound -- Tasklet's "rail" is the MCP standard and HTTP APIs, both of which are open, non-regulated, and replicable by Zapier in a quarter. Andrew Lee's "always bet on the models" disclosure is the difference -- Patrick Collison never argued publicly that Visa would commoditize Stripe. **The flip condition for my verdict**: if Tasklet discloses net revenue retention >120% from cohorts older than 6 months by Q4 2026, the workflow stickiness is real, the **Stripe** index thesis activates, and I lean in at the next round; absent that, the **Lyft** structural position holds and I take the velocity-driven entry as an acquisition bet, not a generational one.
