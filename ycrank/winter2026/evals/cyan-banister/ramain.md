# RamAIn -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see immediately: this is one of the most crowded categories in all of tech right now. AI agents, computer use, browser automation -- every fund in Silicon Valley is writing checks into this space. Browser Use raised $17M from Felicis. Simular pulled in $27M with ex-DeepMind founders and a Microsoft partnership. Anthropic and OpenAI are building computer-use APIs as first-party features. When I look at a deal, the first thing I ask myself is whether it's unpopular and difficult to assess. RamAIn is the opposite of that. Everyone understands this category. Everyone wants to fund it. The weirdness has been completely arbitraged away, and what's left is an execution race against teams with ten to fifty times more capital and, in Simular's case, deep DeepMind pedigree and a platform partnership with Microsoft. That's not where I find my best investments.

The founders are clearly intelligent. Vansh Ramani's research credentials are real -- an ICLR 2025 publication, contributions to Meta's FAISS library through his Panorama algorithm, time at CMU's ML department. That's not nothing. Shourya Vir Jain bootstrapped a previous company to six-figure revenue, which tells me he can sell and build at the same time. But when I ask my first question -- tell me your story, not the business -- what I'm hearing is two accomplished IIT Delhi students who saw a hot technical category and are executing well within it. I'm not hearing a survival story. I'm not hearing about a specific pain that kept them up at night before they knew what a startup was. There's no biographical thread connecting these founders to the specific problem of enterprise workers trapped in legacy UI workflows. That thread is what separates the founders who persist through the inevitable crises from the ones who pivot when the category cools down.

The bull case here is worth articulating honestly. The pre-training approach -- teaching agents interface structures in advance rather than reasoning from screenshots at runtime -- could be a genuinely different architectural choice that compounds into a data moat. If RamAIn accumulates pre-trained models across hundreds of enterprise interfaces, that library becomes harder to replicate than a runtime-only approach. Vansh has the technical chops to build this. And the target verticals -- healthcare, insurance, pharmacy operations -- are genuinely hard spaces where compliance requirements and legacy systems create real switching costs. If they can land two or three enterprise customers in a regulated vertical and demonstrate the 10x speed claim with real data, they could carve out a defensible niche that the bigger players don't bother to contest because the per-vertical customization isn't worth their time at scale. That's a real path.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. The foundation model providers are building this capability natively. Anthropic's Computer Use API and Claude Cowork, OpenAI's CUA -- these aren't theoretical threats, they're shipping products from the companies that control the underlying models RamAIn depends on. The middleware layer between foundation models and enterprise users is exactly where platform providers squeeze out startups once the category matures. And with no revenue, no named customers, no LOIs, and pricing listed as "Free," there's no signal yet that anyone needs this specific implementation enough to pay for it. I've seen too many technically excellent teams build impressive demos in hot categories only to discover that the platform ate their lunch. The companies I back tend to be doing something the platforms would never do because it looks too weird, too niche, or too stigmatized. Enterprise GUI automation is none of those things.

On co-founder dynamics, both founders share an IIT Delhi background, which gives them a common foundation, but I have no visibility into how they handle disagreement or crisis. They're young, this appears to be their first serious venture together, and selling into enterprise healthcare and insurance with a two-person team is going to test that relationship in ways a university project never could. I learned from HQ Trivia that even the best product and timing can't survive founder misalignment -- and enterprise sales pressure on a tiny team is one of the fastest ways to surface cracks.
