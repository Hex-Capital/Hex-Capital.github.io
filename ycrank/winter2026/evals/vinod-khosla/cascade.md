# Cascade -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Cascade: the entire AI agent observability space has been validated, funded, and partially consolidated *before this company has a visible product*. Braintrust raised $80M at an $800M valuation. Raindrop took $15M from Lightspeed with an approach the dossier describes as "closely mirroring" Cascade's methodology — training custom models on production data. ClickHouse acquired Langfuse. Snyk acquired Invariant Labs. Anthropic acqui-hired HumanLoop. When every major infrastructure player and well-capitalized startup is building in your category, and incumbents like Datadog and New Relic are extending their platforms toward your problem — nobody is laughing at you. Nobody thinks what you're doing is impossible. And that is precisely the signal that concerns me most. My best investments — Juniper against Cisco, Impossible Foods against the entire meat industry, OpenAI when AI was laughable — were in companies that experts actively dismissed. Cascade is entering a space where the experts are already writing checks.

The consequence of success here is a well-run B2B SaaS infrastructure company. If Cascade wins — truly wins, becomes the dominant platform for AI agent reliability — the world gets better-monitored AI agents. That matters operationally. But it doesn't restructure an industry. It doesn't make an existing trillion-dollar system obsolete. It adds a layer of tooling to a system that's still being built. Compare this to the bets that define my portfolio: Impossible Foods succeeding meant reclaiming half the planet's arable land. Commonwealth Fusion succeeding means replacing fossil fuel power generation. OpenAI succeeding meant redefining what machines can do. Cascade succeeding means enterprise engineering teams have better dashboards and evaluators for their AI agent deployments. I'm not dismissing the business opportunity — monitoring infrastructure can build real companies. But when I ask my foundational question — "if this works, does it matter at a scale that justifies the attempt?" — the honest answer is: it matters at the scale of a SaaS business, not at the scale of a civilization-level problem.

The founders have the right technical pedigree — almost too right. Adam researched agentic safety at BAIR Lab. Haluk built production monitoring at Netflix and Amazon and studied failure mode taxonomies for AI agents at BAIR. This is a team of domain experts executing the domain's playbook. They know what correct AI agent monitoring looks like because they've spent their careers studying exactly that. In most venture frameworks, this is a strength. In mine, it's a caution. People who know the field deeply tend to build what the field expects rather than what the field hasn't imagined. I see no evidence of contrarian thinking here — no signal that these founders are attempting something the consensus considers impossible. They're building precisely what the consensus has decided is needed, which is why a dozen other companies and acquirers are building the same thing.

The strongest bull case for Cascade is timing and data defensibility. The agentic AI market is genuinely on an exponential trajectory — from $5 billion to potentially $200 billion by 2034. If AI agents become the primary interface for enterprise operations, the reliability layer becomes critical infrastructure, and custom evaluator models trained on each customer's production data could create real switching costs. A company that captures this data flywheel early could compound its advantage faster than late entrants. And the founders' combination of AI safety research and production systems engineering at Netflix and Amazon covers both sides of the technical problem well. If I believed this market would consolidate to one or two winners and that Cascade could outrun competitors with 50-100x more capital, I'd be more interested. But the M&A pattern in this space — four acquisitions in one year — suggests the more likely outcome is absorption into a larger platform before Cascade reaches escape velocity. That's seeking an acquisition outcome even if it's not the stated goal, because the market structure pushes toward it.

The fundamental mismatch with how I invest is this: Cascade is a company designed to reduce the probability of failure — literally, that's the product, making AI agents fail less. The founders have de-risked their own venture by choosing a consensus category with proven demand. Everything about this company is oriented toward reducing risk rather than maximizing the consequence of success. The technology is capable but not structurally disruptive to any incumbent system. The approach is sound but shared by multiple well-funded competitors. The team is qualified but not contrarian. I look for bets where a 90% chance of failure is acceptable because the 10% scenario transforms an industry. This is closer to a 50% chance of building a moderate business — which, in my framework, is exactly the kind of risk-reduction that makes the consequences of success inconsequential.
