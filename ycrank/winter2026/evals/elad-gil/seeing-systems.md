# Seeing Systems -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The military drone market in 2026 is the opposite of what I look for. When defense tech VC funding nearly doubles in a single year -- $27.2 billion to $49.1 billion -- and European defense venture grew 132% year-on-year, and Ukraine is producing four million drones annually, you are not in a non-obvious market. You are in a consensus market at peak attention. Every LP, every generalist fund, every government accelerator is writing checks into defense drones right now. That's the exact pattern I describe in my "End of Cycle" essay -- capital flooding into a sector because the thesis is obvious, not because any individual company has a structural edge. When I backed Anduril, the structural moment wasn't "defense matters" -- it was that Google's Project Maven cancellation created a specific vacuum with no credible startup competitor. In expendable strike drones, there is no vacuum. There is Stark with $100 million raised and a €2.4 billion German contract. There is Shield AI at $5.6 billion. There are hundreds of Ukrainian manufacturers who have been iterating drone designs under actual combat conditions for three years. Seeing Systems is entering a market where the structural moment is visible to everyone, which means the returns accrue to whoever has the most capital and the fastest manufacturing, not whoever has the best thesis.

That said, there is a more interesting sub-thesis buried inside this company that most investors would miss. The real structural shift isn't "drones matter" -- it's that electronic warfare countermeasures are evolving on a weeks-not-years cycle, which fundamentally breaks the traditional defense procurement model of buying fixed-design platforms. If EW countermeasures make any specific drone design obsolete within weeks of deployment, then modular architecture isn't a nice feature -- it's a structural necessity. Stark's approach of mass-manufacturing fixed-design kamikaze drones makes sense in a stable EW environment, but the Ukraine battlefield is proving that the EW environment changes faster than fixed designs can adapt. And Stark failed all four strikes in UK military trials in October 2025, which validates the modularity thesis in a concrete way. The fibre-optic guided variant of Banshee addresses jamming immunity specifically -- that's building against a capability gap that RF-dependent competitors face. This is a contrarian bet within a consensus market, which is more interesting than the surface-level framing suggests.

The execution velocity here is genuinely impressive for a two-person team with $500K. UK Royal Marine Commandos, multiple other UK military units, and four NATO forces as customers or partners at pre-seed -- that's not a pitch deck claim, that's operational access that typically takes defense startups years and millions of dollars to establish. Alexander's prior EOD hardware work for military users clearly opened doors that a cold-start team couldn't. The Forward Deployed Engineer model -- embedding engineers with each military unit -- is the Palantir playbook applied to hardware, and it creates the kind of tight feedback loop and relationship lock-in that makes defense procurement stickiness real. The fact that they're shipping prototypes to Ukraine and running named operations (Kull with Estonian military, Osprey with UK elite forces) means they're building product against real operational feedback, not theoretical requirements documents.

Where my framework pushes back hardest is on the miracle count. Building a successful defense drone company from here requires: (1) developing Aerie AI swarm coordination software that works in contested environments -- this is listed as "in development," meaning it doesn't exist yet; (2) scaling modular hardware manufacturing from prototypes to production volumes; (3) navigating ITAR and multi-country export controls across at least five NATO jurisdictions with a two-person team; (4) winning formal procurement contracts before runway runs out; and (5) competing for those contracts against companies with 100-1000x more capital. That's five miracles, and my rule is clear -- if you need more than one, you're compounding low-probability events. The bull case requires that modularity becomes the single miracle: NATO standardizes on the modular platform, and everything else -- software, manufacturing, procurement -- follows from institutional lock-in. But even framed that way, manufacturing scale-up alone is a separate hard problem that software companies never face, which is why I generally avoid hardware bets. The product-to-distribution trajectory is conceptually sound -- hardware creates the distribution channel for Aerie AI software, training, modules, and maintenance as recurring revenue -- but the software layer that could make this a truly software-driven company rather than software-aware hardware company does not yet exist.

The strongest bull case: if NATO decides it needs a standardized modular drone platform (analogous to how NATO standardizes ammunition calibers), and Seeing Systems' existing relationships with UK Royal Marines and four NATO forces position them as the de facto standard, then this company captures an enormous recurring revenue stream across allied militaries. The modularity thesis means every EW countermeasure evolution generates new module sales, turning adversary innovation into a revenue driver rather than an existential threat. The brothers' complementary skill set -- Cambridge robotics researcher and Jane Street engineer for software, self-taught embedded electronics and prior military hardware builder for hardware -- covers the full stack credibly. And if Stark continues to stumble in UK procurement, there's a genuine opening in the UK and broader NATO market. The question is whether a $500K company can survive long enough to reach that outcome against competitors with two to three orders of magnitude more capital. For my money, the execution evidence is strong enough to keep watching, but the consensus market, multi-miracle requirement, and brutal competitive landscape make this a pass at my threshold.
