# Interfaze -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Interfaze is the gap between credentials and traction. Yoeven Khemlani has a prior exit — Stayr grew to $3M ARR and was sold. Harsha Vardhan Khurdula has a peer-reviewed paper accepted at IEEE CAI 2026 describing the exact architecture Interfaze is built on. These are not empty credentials. A repeat founder paired with a genuine ML researcher is a combination I take seriously. When I backed Patrick Collison the second time around, the prior exit at Auctomatic was part of what told me to write the check. But Khemlani's prior company was in proptech in Singapore, and now he's building AI infrastructure for developers in San Francisco. That's a big domain jump. The question I'd want to answer in a 10-minute interview is: why this problem? What was the specific moment of frustration? The dossier gives me nothing on origin story, and that absence nags at me.

The architecture itself — routing deterministic tasks to specialized small models via a controller, rather than throwing everything at a frontier LLM — is intellectually honest. There's a real insight buried in there: most of what developers use GPT-4 for doesn't require GPT-4, and paying $60 per million output tokens for OCR or classification is wasteful. If you've ever processed a thousand invoices through an LLM and gotten inconsistent field extraction on 8% of them, you understand why deterministic outputs matter. The IEEE paper gives this substance beyond a pitch deck. But here's what my schlep-blindness filter tells me: this is not a schlep. Building elegant model routing architectures is exactly the kind of problem that attracts brilliant engineers. It's the opposite of what repelled everyone from payments infrastructure when the Collisons started Stripe. The proof is in the competitive landscape — Firecrawl has 50,000 GitHub stars and $14.5M in funding, Apify does $13.3M in revenue, and every frontier model provider is adding multimodal capabilities quarter by quarter. When your space has that many well-funded players, you're not in schlep territory. You're in consensus territory.

And "AI developer tools" in Spring 2026 is about as consensus as it gets. I keep coming back to the framework: if a good idea were obviously good, someone else would already have done it. Everyone agrees that AI infrastructure is important. That's the tell. The small-model routing thesis adds a layer of contrarianism — most of the industry is obsessed with scaling single models larger, and betting on task-specific small models is at least an arguable departure from consensus. But it's a technical disagreement, not the kind of deep contrarianism that made early Airbnb or Coinbase look crazy to smart people.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about it. If frontier model costs don't drop fast enough — if OpenAI and Anthropic keep pricing output tokens at premium levels for another 18-24 months — then there's a genuine window for an architecture that delivers 90% of the output quality at 10% of the cost for deterministic tasks. Khemlani has proven he can build a product and get it to revenue — $1M to $3M ARR at Stayr is not nothing. The IEEE publication gives the CTO genuine credibility with the developer audience they're targeting. And 10 million API requests in 9 months of beta, while modest, means real developers are using this in real workflows. The company that consolidates OCR, scraping, STT, and classification into a single API with consistent output formatting could become a meaningful piece of developer infrastructure. If Khemlani's operational instincts from Stayr combine with Khurdula's ML depth and they find their wedge before the bigger players close the gap, there's a path to something valuable.

But what holds me back is the tempo. JigsawStack was founded in July 2024. It's now March 2026 — twenty months — and the product is still in beta. The rebrand to Interfaze adds confusion rather than clarity; the email is still jigsawstack.com, the GitHub org still carries the old name. Twenty months to beta for what's fundamentally an API product doesn't show me the learning velocity I look for. When I think about my best investments, the founders were iterating weekly — shipping, measuring, adjusting. Here I see a thoughtful architecture, a published paper, and a product that hasn't launched. The co-founders also have no shared history before this company, which isn't disqualifying but means I can't evaluate how they work under stress. The team of five is split across San Francisco, Singapore, and India. At this stage, I want to see founders who are in the same room arguing about what to ship tomorrow, not coordinating across time zones.

I don't see enough here to write a check from my own money. The founders are clearly competent — prior exit, real research, working product. But competence in a consensus market isn't what generates the returns I'm looking for. I'd need to see the origin story, the specific frustration that led to this, and evidence that users are choosing Interfaze obsessively over the alternatives. Right now it reads more like a well-executed market entry than an organic discovery that couldn't not be built.
