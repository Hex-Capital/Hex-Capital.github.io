# August -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Eight hundred thousand dollars in annual recurring revenue in three months, from a single $170B+ bank. That number stopped me. At pre-seed, most founders are still arguing about their landing page copy, and Bar Ittah and Tom Tankilevitch have already convinced a massive commercial bank to pay real money for AI that runs underwriting, servicing, and risk workflows. I don't dismiss that — it's the kind of traction that suggests the product works and the pain is acute. But traction and thesis fit are different conversations, and I need to have both before I write a check.

So I ask my first question: tell me the founders' stories. Bar and Tom are both ex-Unit 8200 — Bar built fraud prevention infrastructure at Xperiti before it was acquired by Ipsos in 2023, then scaled AI growth at OnFire AI. Tom architected the AI backbone of a national intelligence unit, then led engineering at Port through its growth to an $800M valuation. These are serious builders with two exits between them. The forge is real — military intelligence and high-stakes enterprise AI create people who can execute under pressure. But when I look for the biographical inevitability that connects these founders to commercial banking specifically, I find AI engineers who chose a vertical, not people whose lives were shaped by the problem they're solving. Bar's Xperiti experience in fraud prevention is adjacent to financial services risk, and that's meaningful, but it's not the same as having lived inside the broken system. This reads more like talented operators who identified a massive, underserved market — which is a fine reason to build a company, but it's not the biographical crucible I look for when I'm writing my own check.

The space itself gives me pause. When 91% of banking executives call AI a strategic priority and fintech investment hits $53B in a single year, I'm not the first one at the table — I'm the last. nCino is already public at $500M+ revenue. Heron Finance raised a $16M Series A. Numerated got acquired by Moody's. This is a well-funded, consensus-attractive market where sophisticated investors are actively competing for deals. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you say "AI for banking operations." Compare that to when I wrote my check for Anduril — defense tech was politically radioactive in Silicon Valley. Or Contraline — try pitching male birth control to a room of VCs and watch them squirm. August operates in a space that every corporate strategy deck in America is already talking about. The regulatory complexity of banking creates real barriers to execution, but regulatory complexity is not contrarianism — it's just a hard problem that well-funded competitors are also solving.

Here's what I keep circling back to as I apply the invisible system test. When I backed Affirm, the thesis was clear: credit card companies were exploiting information asymmetry against consumers, hiding fees in fine print, profiting from confusion. Affirm made those terms transparent to the people being extracted from. August is doing something structurally different — it's making commercial banking operations more efficient for the banks themselves. The opacity in underwriting and risk workflows is internal to these $170B+ institutions, not a hidden tax on ordinary people who don't understand the system. August optimizes the powerful; it doesn't empower the powerless. Could faster, more automated underwriting eventually expand credit access to underserved borrowers? Maybe. But that's a speculative second-order effect, not the company's core value proposition. The platform where "banking work runs" is infrastructure for institutions, not liberation for individuals.

Where August genuinely excites me is on the defensibility dimension, and I want to be honest about that. The "proprietary context layer" that compounds with usage — where each deployment accumulates bank-specific operational knowledge — is exactly the kind of data flywheel that creates infrastructure-grade switching costs. Once a $170B+ bank's institutional knowledge, policies, and workflow logic are embedded in August's system, ripping it out becomes nearly unthinkable. And the timing is right: only 23% of banking executives have moved AI beyond pilots into production, meaning August is arriving at the moment when demand crystallizes but supply hasn't consolidated. nCino being built on Salesforce's architecture is a genuine structural constraint that an AI-native competitor can exploit. If August can expand beyond its single bank into multiple deployments, the context layer compounds into a moat that general-purpose AI providers can't replicate without doing the same painful integration work.

The bull case is a company that becomes the operating system for how commercial banks actually work — not the core ledger, not the customer-facing app, but the intelligence layer where credit decisions, risk assessments, and loan servicing actually happen. Bar and Tom have the technical depth to build it, and the $800K ARR proves the product solves real pain. If they can convert that growing pipeline into three or four more enterprise deployments, the single-customer concentration risk dissolves and the context layer becomes a genuine flywheel. But for my portfolio, the absence cuts are the biographical forge and the invisible system. I need founders whose life story made them incapable of ignoring this problem, and I need companies that shift power from gatekeepers to the people being gatekept. August shifts power from legacy processes to modern AI within the same institutions that already hold the power. That's valuable — it's just not my bet.
