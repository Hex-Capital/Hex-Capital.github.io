# Alchemy -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask of any company is: what is the secret? What important truth do the founders of Alchemy believe that most intelligent people actively reject? I have searched the dossier carefully, and I cannot find one. The thesis — that AI and natural-language interfaces can make microscopy image analysis faster and easier for researchers — is not a secret. It is a consensus. Every microscope manufacturer on the planet is integrating AI into their analysis pipelines. Leica acquired DRVISION in 2021 specifically to bundle AI analysis with their hardware. Zeiss and Nikon are doing the same. PathAI has raised $255 million on essentially the same thesis applied to pathology. Grand View Research projects the AI-in-microscopy market growing at nearly 15% CAGR through 2033. When market research firms, hardware incumbents, venture-backed competitors, and open-source communities are all converging on the same insight simultaneously, you are not looking at a secret — you are looking at a trend. And trends produce competition, not monopoly.

The competitive structure here is the kind of Girardian convergence I find most troubling. Consider the landscape: at the free end, CellProfiler, QuPath, napari, and ImageJ are entrenched in academic workflows with large contributor communities and zero switching cost. At the enterprise end, Danaher's Aivia, Visiopharm, and PathAI are backed by hundreds of millions in capital and deep customer relationships. In between, any team with access to open-source segmentation models like Cellpose or StarDist and a foundation model API can assemble something functionally similar. Alchemy sits in this middle ground claiming differentiation through natural-language pipeline assembly — but this capability is downstream of generally available vision-language models. It is a UX layer, not a technological discontinuity. The 240x speedup the company claims (20 hours to 5 minutes) is striking as a number, but it reflects the power of the underlying foundation models, not proprietary technology that Alchemy uniquely controls. A well-funded competitor — or an open-source project with a weekend hackathon — could replicate this interface within months.

The founders present an interesting pairing on paper: Baek brings biomedical engineering from UT Austin and medical device experience at Boston Scientific; McBride brings computer science from UIUC and received a patent at 19. But I notice several things that concern me. Neither founder has documented experience in bioimage analysis, computational biology software, or research microscopy — the precise domain they are targeting. Baek's resume includes a Master's in psychological counseling from Columbia and a marketing analytics internship at Tropicana, which suggests a searching quality rather than the relentless single-mindedness I look for in founders. Compare this to the canonical pattern: Zuckerberg was building social software obsessively since high school. Musk had spent years studying rocket engineering before starting SpaceX. The Collison brothers had been building payment systems since their teens. The founders of Alchemy appear to have identified a market opportunity rather than having lived inside a problem until they could see what no one else could see. There is no public evidence of a definite vision for how this company transforms the world — no published thinking, no visible intellectual framework, no statements of conviction under pressure.

To be fair to the bull case: the structural gap between hardware-bundled vendors who cannot easily unbundle their analysis tools and open-source projects that lack resources for productized agent workflows is real. If Alchemy could move fast enough to accumulate validated pipeline data across hundreds of research labs — creating a library of domain-specific, publication-tested analysis nodes — there is a version of the future where switching costs compound. Labs that embed Alchemy pipelines into their SOPs and published methods create a form of workflow lock-in. And the bottom-up distribution model, where individual researchers adopt the tool before institutional procurement gets involved, has produced monopolies before — this is how Stripe captured developers and expanded into enterprise. If both of these dynamics materialized, and if the data flywheel from accumulated image-model pairings created a compounding quality advantage, Alchemy could become the default analysis platform for research microscopy. But this requires the founders to have a specific plan for navigating the pricing pressure from free open-source alternatives, the bundling threat from hardware incumbents, and the commoditization risk from improving foundation models — and I see no evidence of such a plan.

The naming collision with Alchemy, the $345 million blockchain infrastructure company, is a small but telling detail. It signals either unawareness of the competitive landscape or insufficient concern with brand distinctiveness — and in a domain where SEO and discoverability matter for bottom-up adoption, this is not trivial. More fundamentally, it suggests the founders may not have thought carefully enough about positioning, which is adjacent to the question of whether they have thought carefully enough about monopoly. A company that cannot even monopolize its own name faces an uphill battle monopolizing a market.

This is a competent team building a useful product in a real market — and that is precisely why I would pass. Useful products in real markets with consensus theses and multiple funded competitors are the definition of what I avoid. The absence of a genuine secret, combined with a competitive landscape that includes both free open-source tools and well-capitalized incumbents, means that even if Alchemy executes well, the most likely outcome is a decent small business competing on features and price rather than a monopoly generating outsized returns. The power law demands that I invest only in companies where the upside, if the secret is correct, is extreme. Here, even the optimistic scenario produces a company fighting for share in a market where the price floor is zero.
