# Adialante -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Adialante is, in one phrase, a schlep-zone hardware company built by professional MRI physicists whose pain is researcher-pain, not patient-pain — Efraín Torres and Parker Jenkins came out of UMN's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research wanting cheaper magnets, not because either of them ever lost a relative to a missed prostate biopsy. That high-schlep / low-organic-origin pairing is the analytical signature: the work is exactly the kind of unpleasant grind I love to fund, but the founders backed into it from the supply side rather than from their own bodies, and that asymmetry shows up everywhere else in the dossier.

Coinbase (2012) is the right ladder rung for the schlep dimension, because Brian Armstrong's edge was an appetite for bitcoin compliance that other smart founders unconsciously routed around — the grind WAS the moat. Adialante sits remarkably close to that rung: a novel gradientless MRI architecture requiring FDA 510(k) or de novo clearance, mobile trailers with drivers and technicians across geographies, per-scan reimbursement code negotiation with CMS, and clinic-channel sales to urologists. Every layer of that stack is something a software-only YC applicant in S26 will flinch at. Where Adialante diverges from the Coinbase rung is that the schlep is also capital-intensive — Armstrong's compliance grind didn't require manufacturing a magnet — and capital-intensive schleps compound more slowly than regulatory-only ones. Still, $1.18M of NSF SBIR Phase II money and two issued U.S. patents on the FREE architecture are real evidence the founders are running TOWARD this, not away.

Dropbox (2007) is the wrong rung for Adialante on organic pain, because Drew Houston forgot his own USB stick and built the thing for himself. Torres and Jenkins did not have prostate cancer; they had a research thesis about portable MRI. That's a meaningfully different category of origin story. The dossier tells me UMN named Torres Student Entrepreneur of the Year 2023 and that both founders are co-inventors on the patents — which is wonderful evidence of technical seriousness — but it does not tell me either founder ever sat in a urology waiting room watching a man get told he needed an MRI he couldn't access. When the idea comes from the lab rather than the body, the founders are more likely to flinch when the clinical workflow turns out to be uglier than the physics, because the physics is the thing they actually loved.

On animal resourcefulness, Adialante's $30,000 in actual deposits from a named top-10 urology clinic before the first physical system was even operational is the closest thing in the dossier to a cereal-box moment — converting a non-shipped product into hard cash from a sophisticated buyer is exactly the kind of pre-funding stunt that compounds. Over 18 months this either snowballs (each deposit funds the next trailer, each trailer trains the AI on more scans, each scan tightens the next clinic sale) or it stalls the moment FDA clearance slips and the deposit-holders ask for their money back. The $12.75M LOI number is much weaker — LOIs are non-binding and I have watched many founders confuse them with revenue — so the $30k is the real signal and it is genuine but small.

For Adialante to flip from Neutral into Invest territory on hacker genealogy, I would need to see one of the founders with a public GitHub of open-source MRI reconstruction code, a Hacker News footprint, or a recognizable artifact outside the academic patent system. The dossier explicitly notes "No public GitHub account found" for both Torres and Jenkins. PhD-level MRI physics is a real and rare skill, but it is a different tribe from the Lisp/competitive-programming lineage that runs from Patrick Collison's Young Scientist prize through Aaron Swartz's RSS work — academic-physics hackers ship papers and patents, HN hackers ship demos. Adialante's team is the former, and the patent-plus-grant moat is real, but it does not light up the unstated pattern I lean on.

Reddit (2005) is my single-anchor parallel on co-founder configuration: Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates with years of shared context before the company existed. Torres and Jenkins pursued graduate studies in BME at the same UMN lab and worked together on MRI/CT research before founding Adialante in 2022/2023 — that is the same multi-year, technically-paired, complementary-roles profile I prefer over hackathon-pairings. Four named team members and a CFO suggests they are also avoiding the solo-founder anti-pattern without bloating into a manager-mode org. This dimension does not carry the company on its own, but it removes a category of risk I would otherwise underwrite around.

My 36-month forecast for Adialante: still alive but pre-revenue or sub-$1M ARR, with FDA clearance for the prostate indication either just received or six months out, 1–3 trailers operational in California, and a hardware-style Series A raised on the strength of the SBIR Phase II + patents rather than on traction — not dead, not a Hyperfine-style IPO, just slow. The mechanism producing that forecast is the high-schlep / low-organic-origin signature: regulatory and hardware schleps stretch timelines past what software founders plan for, and researcher-origin teams typically under-budget the clinical-workflow ugliness because the physics was the part they cared about.

The strongest objection to that forecast comes from my own Coinbase exception: Armstrong looked exactly like this — a regulated, slow, capex-heavy schlep that other YC investors discounted because it didn't fit the software-compounding template, and the grind itself became the moat. If Adialante's FREE physics genuinely produces diagnostic-grade prostate imaging at sub-million-dollar hardware cost, the cannibalization conflict with Siemens/GE/Philips means no incumbent can chase them down their per-scan trailer model without blowing up an installed-base service business, and that is precisely the kind of structural advantage I keep underwriting. The dossier evidence that wins, though, is the line that the first MRI system was only "scheduled operational by demo day" — Armstrong had a working Android wallet in his hand when he overturned my rejection; Adialante is selling deposits on hardware that has not yet imaged a patient. One verifiable condition would flip my verdict to Invest: a published image of a prostate scan from the operational system, at diagnostic quality reviewed by an independent radiologist, before FDA submission. That is the artifact-before-deck filter, applied to a hardware company.
