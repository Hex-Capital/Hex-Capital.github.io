# Cajal -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cajal is that it is walking into a Girardian minefield. Harmonic AI has raised $295 million at a $1.45 billion valuation to build "mathematical superintelligence" using formal verification in Lean 4. LeanDojo, an open-source project from Caltech, NVIDIA, and MIT, provides free infrastructure for LLM-based theorem proving. DeepMind's AlphaProof has demonstrated medal-level mathematical reasoning. And into this crowded theater of mimetic convergence steps a two-person team with YC funding and no documented prior experience in formal verification. The competitive landscape alone tells you something important: the thesis that "AI can automate formal verification" is not a secret. It is a consensus view held by some of the best-funded and most talented organizations on the planet. When every sophisticated actor converges on the same insight simultaneously, the insight has ceased to be contrarian and has become a race — and races are won by the best-resourced, not the most visionary.

What is the secret here? Cajal's stated problem — that LLMs are stochastic and cannot guarantee correctness — is an observation that anyone who has spent five minutes thinking about AI safety or software verification would agree with. The proposition that formal verification can fill this gap is shared by every player in the space. The only potentially differentiating claim is that applied scientific domains (quantum computing, finance) represent a better initial market than pure mathematical competition problems. But this is a go-to-market positioning choice, not a truth about the world that most intelligent people would reject. Harmonic could pivot into applied domains in a quarter. Any well-funded team with Lean expertise could target quantum computing verification if the market demanded it. A positioning choice that can be replicated by a competitor with 150 times your capital is not a moat — it is a hope.

The founders present a puzzle. Luke Johnston is a Master's student at Cambridge's Computational and Biological Learning Lab, studying meta-reinforcement learning in orienting behavior — genuinely serious academic work, but in computational neuroscience, not formal verification or type theory. Pedro Nobre has a computer science background from Spanish universities, won an HPE hackathon, and interned at a research department. They met through Entrepreneurs First's Bridge program. These are competent young people, and I do not dismiss them for their youth — Zuckerberg was 19, Buterin was 19. But what distinguished those founders was not youth itself; it was the combination of youth with an already-demonstrated, almost pathological commitment to a specific vision that most people considered absurd. I see no evidence that Johnston and Nobre have that kind of deep domain conviction in formal verification. Their technical backgrounds are adjacent — machine learning, general CS — but not in dependent type theory, proof automation, or the mathematics of verified systems. Building a multi-agent system that autonomously discovers and formalizes proofs in Lean is one of the hardest engineering challenges in AI. Doing it without demonstrated expertise in the core domain, while competing against teams that have precisely that expertise and $295 million in the bank, requires a founder advantage I cannot identify from the available evidence.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. If you believe that the applied dimension is structurally different from pure math — that formalizing quantum computing algorithms or financial model constraints requires domain knowledge that Harmonic's pure-math focus does not produce — then Cajal could own a niche before anyone else gets there. The data moat argument has some merit: every verified proof adds to a proprietary corpus, and verified training datasets for Lean 4, Coq, and Isabelle could become increasingly valuable as AI labs hunger for high-quality mathematical training data. If frontier AI labs are willing to pay enterprise prices for verified datasets, Cajal could generate revenue quickly from a small number of customers. And the Cambridge affiliation, while not a moat, provides access to the kind of mathematical talent that this company needs. If everything breaks right — the applied-domain thesis proves structurally defensible, the data corpus compounds faster than competitors can replicate it, and the founders acquire deep formal verification expertise on the job — this could work. But "if everything breaks right" is the language of indefinite optimism, not definite conviction.

The durability question is where this falls apart most clearly. Lean is open source. LeanDojo provides open infrastructure. The techniques for combining LLMs with proof assistants are being published in academic papers that anyone can read. When a company's core technology stack is built on open-source foundations, and the fundamental approaches are being developed simultaneously by multiple well-funded research groups, the question is not whether the technology works — it may well work — but whether this particular company can maintain a defensible position as the technology matures. Harmonic, with its $295 million war chest, can hire the best Lean developers in the world. DeepMind can deploy thousands of TPUs against the same problem. A two-person team's only structural advantage would be a secret — a non-obvious insight about where value concentrates in this space — and I cannot identify one. I pass.
