# Tenet Industries -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Helsing has raised €1.37 billion and delivers hundreds of strike drones per month to Ukraine. Stark Defence has ~$100M from Thiel, Sequoia, In-Q-Tel, and the NATO Innovation Fund, led by a founder who already built a drone unicorn in Quantum Systems. White Stork has Eric Schmidt writing checks. Tenet Industries has four people in Stockholm and a YC batch. That capitalization gap — north of 100x against the nearest well-funded competitor — is the defining fact of this evaluation. Normally I'd say "other investors' fear is a positive signal," but here the situation is reversed: other investors aren't afraid of this market, they're pouring billions into Tenet Industries' direct competitors. The competitive landscape isn't empty and waiting for a disruptor. It's already crowded with well-capitalized insurgents.

Tenet Industries' thesis — engineering the drone around the factory rather than the reverse, building an automated manufacturing platform across multiple form factors (FPV, fixed-wing, Shahed-type, missile-class) — is where my interest sharpens. This is not "we build a better drone." This is "we build a better factory." That distinction matters. Helsing's differentiation is AI autonomy software. Stark's is hardware design pedigree. Neither claims a unified production platform designed from first principles for automated multi-format manufacturing. If Tenet Industries' design-for-automation approach actually works, it becomes the low-cost producer — and in attrition warfare where Ukraine consumes thousands of drones monthly, the low-cost producer wins. Current loitering munitions like Switchblade cost tens of thousands per unit. Collapsing that to sub-thousand-dollar price points is a 10x+ cost reduction, which is exactly the discontinuity I look for. But at pre-seed with zero demonstrated production capability, zero patents, and zero battlefield validation, this is aspiration dressed as thesis. The non-linearity is hypothetical.

Emil Falk is the founder signal worth examining. He built WindowGlass Recycling from concept to commercial deployment — machines operating at four Ragn-Sells facilities by autumn 2024, Venture Cup "Startup of the Year" 2023, awards at the European Championship including Innovation of the Year. That's not a pitch deck achievement; that's a hardware product manufactured and deployed at industrial sites. The relevance to Tenet Industries' design-for-automation thesis is direct: Falk has actually scaled hardware manufacturing before. Hugo Frisk is self-taught in electronics design — no formal credentials, which in my framework is a feature, not a bug. Peter Beck built Rutherford engines in a garage in New Zealand without a formal aerospace degree, and we put $28 million into Rocket Lab on that signal. But Beck had demonstrated rocket engine innovation; Frisk's public record shows no comparable technical proof point. Fabian Andersson is essentially invisible in public data. The team has relevant skills but I see no evidence of the rapid adaptation or first-principles reasoning that would make me confident they're learning machines rather than competent executors.

The consequence magnitude, if Tenet Industries fully succeeds, is real but bounded. Sweden allocated SEK 4 billion (~$440M) for unmanned combat systems through 2028. Germany awarded ~€900M in drone brigade contracts. NATO nations committed to 3.5% GDP defense spending. A company that enables mass-produced strike drones at radical price points could shift how 500 million Europeans are defended — that's consequential. But it's not civilizational in the way fusion energy or artificial general intelligence would be. It doesn't make a basic human need free. When I backed Mach Industries — the closest portfolio parallel, a young outsider building weapons to compete with Lockheed Martin — the founder was a 19-year-old MIT dropout with a technical insight so sharp that the irreverence felt earned. Tenet Industries shares the structural pattern (outsider founders, defense disruption, manufacturing thesis) but not the intensity. Mach had already raised $100M and demonstrated capability when I was most engaged. Tenet Industries is at the option-pricing stage, and the option price is cheap, but the information to decide whether to exercise is almost entirely absent.

The bull case deserves construction: Tenet Industries' design-for-automation thesis is genuinely differentiated in a market where no competitor claims this approach. Emil Falk's proven hardware-manufacturing track record provides more founder evidence than most pre-seed defense startups can muster. Sweden's March 2024 NATO membership, combined with a $440M drone procurement program with 2026-2028 delivery timelines, creates a natural first customer on a timeline that matches Tenet Industries' development arc. The market timing — European defense spending at multi-decade highs, drone warfare proven decisive in Ukraine — is as favorable as any I've seen. And the affordability thesis (munitions at 1/10th the cost) maps directly onto the structural pattern behind my strongest bets. Under my Known Exceptions, DoorDash was funded despite lacking technical novelty because consequence magnitude was enormous and the founding team showed extreme learning velocity. Could Tenet Industries merit a similar override? Not yet. The consequence magnitude here is meaningful but not planetary-scale, and the founder learning velocity signal — while promising with Falk — isn't strong enough across the full team to trigger that exception.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: a demonstrated prototype of the automated manufacturing platform producing drones at verified sub-$1,000 unit cost with quality metrics that meet military-grade specifications. That single proof point would retire the technology risk on the non-linear manufacturing thesis and separate Tenet Industries from every competitor competing on drone design rather than factory design. Without it, I'm pricing an option on an unbuilt factory against competitors who already have production lines delivering to governments. The affordability dimension scores well — radical cost reduction is the explicit core of Tenet Industries' value proposition — but affordability in defense doesn't activate my deepest conviction the way affordability in healthcare or energy does. The two dimensions that lock this evaluation are the unproven non-linear technical approach and the brutal competitive capitalization gap. Both could change with evidence. Neither has changed yet.
