# smol machines -- Elad Gil Evaluation

BinBin He worked on Firecracker at AWS -- the VMM that powers Lambda and Fargate -- and is now building smol machines on top of libkrun, which itself incorporates code from Firecracker, rust-vmm, and Cloud Hypervisor. That founder-domain match is real. But it immediately surfaces my biggest concern: smol machines is building a CLI wrapper on an infrastructure layer it doesn't own. libkrun is maintained by Red Hat's containers organization. I've seen this pattern before -- when your foundational technology layer is controlled by an upstream maintainer whose priorities may diverge from yours, you're not building infrastructure, you're building an application on someone else's infrastructure. That's the exact position I counsel founders to avoid. If Red Hat makes breaking changes, deprioritizes macOS support, or a cloud provider forks libkrun into their own toolchain, smol machines has to either absorb the breakage or re-engineer its core. The `.smolmachine` artifact format is an attempt to create an owned infrastructure layer -- and it's the right instinct -- but 2,500 GitHub stars and 97 forks don't yet constitute the kind of ecosystem lock-in where that format becomes a standard anyone depends on.

The market structure timing question for smol machines is whether two converging shifts -- libkrun reaching production maturity with sub-200ms boot times on macOS Hypervisor.framework, and AI coding agents needing sandboxed execution environments -- create a nonobvious window. The libkrun maturity threshold is genuine: a library-based VMM that boots 225x faster than QEMU and runs natively on Apple Silicon was not available two years ago. That's a real technology capability jump. The AI sandboxing angle is also real -- Claude Code, Cursor, and Codex all need places to run untrusted generated code. But here's where I apply the timestamp check: E2B already raised $21M for cloud-hosted AI agent sandboxes and claims 88% Fortune 100 adoption. Fly.io has $115M and Firecracker-based Machines. Docker has $393M+ and massive ecosystem inertia. The "AI needs sandboxes" thesis is not nonobvious anymore -- it's becoming consensus. The specific angle smol machines occupies -- local, desktop-native, no-cloud-dependency VMs -- adds differentiation, but I need to ask: is "local execution" a structural advantage that compounds, or is it a niche preference that cloud-hosted solutions will erode as latency and privacy concerns are addressed server-side? I don't have a confident answer, which tells me the nonobviousness is moderate, not strong.

smol machines requires at least two miracles to work. The technology miracle -- sub-200ms boot with full hypervisor isolation -- is largely solved, which is good. But the distribution miracle is substantial: smol machines needs developers to adopt `.smolmachine` files as a packaging standard in a world where Docker images are the lingua franca. Docker's image format has a decade of ecosystem tooling, CI/CD integration, and registry infrastructure. Asking developers to learn a new artifact format and build pipeline is a high-friction conversion, even if the underlying isolation model is technically superior. Then there's the monetization miracle: smol machines is fully Apache-2.0 open-source with no pricing page, no commercial tier, and no stated revenue model. Docker itself -- the canonical open-source container company with 20x smol machines' adoption -- has spent a decade struggling to convert usage into revenue. That's not a reassuring precedent. The Docker comparison is the honest portfolio anchor here: massive developer adoption, category-defining technology, and a decade of monetization pain that nearly killed the company.

BinBin He's systems engineering credentials are strong -- 476 commits, Rust-heavy codebase (82.8%), prior AWS container/Firecracker work, and sustained open-source output (ERA at 303 stars, envmap at 125 stars). That's direct domain expertise, not adjacent. But the YC page lists team size as 1, and Fu Qiao's background is entirely undocumented in public sources -- no employment history, no education, no GitHub repos found. smol machines targets four platform combinations (macOS and Linux, each on x86_64 and aarch64) with 29 open issues and known limitations including no Windows support. Maintaining a cross-platform hypervisor-level tool with one or two engineers is an extraordinary engineering surface area. I've seen great individual engineers build remarkable prototypes that stall at the exact point where maintaining compatibility across platforms and responding to enterprise requirements demands a team of five to ten. The scaling question isn't organizational yet -- it's whether the engineering surface area is manageable at all before headcount grows significantly.

The strongest bull case for smol machines: AI agent frameworks are about to standardize on a local sandboxed execution format, and smol machines' `.smolmachine` files become that standard the way Docker images became the standard for cloud deployment. BinBin He's Firecracker pedigree gives him the credibility to win that standard. If three or four major AI coding tools adopt smol machines as their execution backend, the distribution miracle solves itself and the company becomes genuine developer infrastructure with compounding switching costs. The GitHub discussion referencing AI agent sandboxing (smolvm issue #3) suggests this demand is already organic, not manufactured. In this scenario, smol machines is early Stripe for AI agent execution -- the infrastructure layer everyone builds on. I take this bull case seriously. But the condition that would make me act on it is specific: I'd need to see at least one major AI agent framework (Claude Code, Codex, or equivalent) formally integrate smol machines as a supported execution backend, or signed LOIs with enterprise development platform teams. Without that, smol machines is a beautifully engineered tool searching for a distribution channel, and I've watched too many technically superior tools lose to worse technology with better distribution. Docker's shared-kernel model is "worse" isolation, but Docker's ecosystem is a moat that technical superiority alone doesn't breach.

smol machines scores well on founder-market fit and technology execution but falls short on the dimensions that most drive my conviction: the market timing is real but not deeply nonobvious, the infrastructure positioning is aspirational rather than proven, and the monetization architecture is entirely absent. My single verifiable condition to flip to Invest: evidence that a major AI development platform has adopted or is actively integrating smol machines as an execution backend, because that would resolve both the distribution miracle and validate the infrastructure-layer thesis simultaneously.
