# General Aviation -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

When I read "Fix ATC by connecting aircraft to the internet" on Ben Frank's YC page, the dimensional signature snaps into focus immediately: General Aviation is a capital-and-regulatory-heavy procurement bet led by the paradigmatic aviation domain expert -- highest on capital-risk-that-repels-VCs, lowest on founder-defies-expert-consensus. That pairing is the inverse shape of what I usually fund. My consequence-first map of the world if General Aviation wins is "the FAA's $32B modernization program is delivered by a startup instead of Thales/Collins/L3Harris" -- which is a meaningful industrial reshuffle, but it is not the **OpenAI** (2019) success-state where a civilizational variable moves.

**Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) is the HIGH rung on capital risk that repels consensus VCs -- I led the $115M Series A because the chokepoint was a physics miracle (HTS magnets at field strength) plus a deployment cost no Tier-1 generalist would touch, and the success-state collapsed the global thermal-electricity regime. General Aviation shares the chokepoint structure -- certified safety-of-life avionics, FCC/ITU spectrum, FAA SWIM integration, and a global ANSP interop layer are exactly the kind of regulatory walls that repel software VCs -- but it diverges from Commonwealth Fusion on the nature of the miracle. There is no physics here; the technical risk is certification and procurement, which is a different beast from "can we confine plasma." That distinction matters because regulatory miracles are awarded to consortia with lobbyists, not pre-seed teams of 0; the FAA Common Automation Platform shortlist (Collins, Leidos, Thales, Indra, Frequentis -- The Air Current 2025) is the precise document telling me how General Aviation gets sourced out.

The "20 years at Salesforce" founder profile I described in my Pass Example is the LOW rung on defying expert consensus -- the pure domain-expert continuity pattern my TED Talk warned against, where the UC Berkeley study of 28,000 predictions showed experts were "only slightly more accurate than chance." Ben Frank's dossier is the aviation version of that profile: MIT aerospace, Rotabull aviation parts SaaS, Seer Aerospace fleet analytics (acquired by Aviation Week 2023), Rotor Technologies autonomous helicopters, licensed pilot and aircraft mechanic. General Aviation is run by the most credentialed aviation-software operator a YC partner could draft. That is fine for executing inside the industry's orthodoxy; it is the opposite of the **Physical Intelligence** (2024) Levine/Finn shape where a research PI publicly contradicts the field. I see no public position from Frank that contradicts FAA or Aireon orthodoxy on what satellite ATC requires -- and absence of that "instigator" pattern is the soft negative my framework defaults to.

On consequence magnitude, the 18-month compounding mechanism cuts against General Aviation. The dossier names a $14.7B ATM TAM growing 9.7% (GMInsights 2025) and a $32B FAA program -- but Iridium's $366.7M acquisition of Aireon (May 2026) just compounded the consolidation of space-based aviation surveillance under a public-company balance sheet with existing FAA and ICAO relationships. Every month General Aviation does not have a signed FAA OTA or ANSP LOI, the whitespace shrinks toward Iridium-Aireon-plus-incumbents, not toward a clean-sheet entrant. The success-state asymptotes from "displace ground-based ATC globally" to "sell a connectivity middleware layer to Part 135 operators" -- a real business, not a civilizational one.

On founder learning rate, the counterfactual is what would flip General Aviation from 9/15 to 13/15. The Seer Aerospace exit to Aviation Week (2023) is a genuine prior outcome -- I do not dismiss it -- and the Rotabull → Seer → Rotor → General Aviation sequence shows a founder who keeps moving up the aviation stack from parts to analytics to autonomy to ATC. But there is no documented pivot inside General Aviation, no "we were wrong about X" public update, no **Slash**-style Kanye-pivot moment that justified my $100M Series C. If Frank publicly wrote "we tried selling to FAA primes and pivoted to ANSP-direct after Q2," the score moves a tier. Today the dossier is silent and the silence is what I price.

On capital-profile fit, the single-anchor parallel is **Cognition** (Series B April 2024, $175M) -- a deal where the roadmap was obviously capital-hungry from day one and the company had a stack-deep product before the round. General Aviation's roadmap is even more capital-hungry -- certified avionics, satellite constellation, spectrum, multi-year procurement cycles easily exceed $200M to first commercial milestone -- but team size 0 at pre-seed means General Aviation cannot absorb a Khosla-shaped check today. The capital intensity is in the right zip code; the absorption capacity is not.

My 36-month forecast: General Aviation does not win an FAA prime contract, raises a modest seed and Series A from aviation-specialist funds, and pivots into aircraft-data-connectivity middleware sold into Part 91/135 operators and the MRO/parts buyers Frank already knows from Rotabull -- ending 2029 alive at $500K-$2M ARR, an acquisition candidate for Garmin or Honeywell, not a category-collapser of Thales. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature itself: the procurement chokepoints repel consensus VCs (so the round will be small and specialist), the domain-expert founder will execute competently inside aviation orthodoxy (so the pivot will be intelligent and adjacent), and the consequence ceiling is industrial reshuffle not civilizational redirection (so the outcome is a tuck-in exit, not an IPO).

The strongest bull case against my pass invokes the **Glean** Known Exception: I funded Arvind Jain, an ex-Google search domain expert, because capital scale and consequence magnitude on Fortune-500 information asymmetry overrode the consensus-credential profile. The argument would be that Ben Frank is the aviation Arvind Jain, the $32B FAA program is the Fortune-500 information asymmetry analogue, and consequence-magnitude-plus-capital-fit overrides the defies-experts gap. The bear answer is that Glean attacked a multi-buyer Fortune-500 market with thousands of decision-makers; General Aviation attacks a single-buyer government regime (FAA) whose CAP shortlist (Collins, Leidos, Thales, Indra, Frequentis -- The Air Current 2025) is already a closed list, and the only operational space-based ADS-B asset (Aireon) just got absorbed by Iridium for $366.7M (May 2026). One verifiable condition flips my verdict: a signed FAA OTA, FAA SBIR Phase III, or named foreign ANSP LOI within 12 months -- without that, General Aviation is a competent aviation-middleware company and I am over-resourced for the deal.
