# Meridian -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Meridian's most striking data point is the claimed ~$700K in contracted revenue four months after launch, with 3x month-over-month growth and competitive wins against Databricks and Palantir in bake-offs. If verified, that would be an exceptional traction signal for any pre-seed company, let alone one run by recent university students. But every one of these numbers originates from the founders' own a16z Speedrun portal submission -- no independent press, no named customer testimonials, no third-party validation appears anywhere in the public record. When I look at the founder profiles more carefully, Kashyap Nathan's "FDE @ Palantir" likely maps to an internship (his LinkedIn confirms "spring internship"), Chris Farrington's LinkedIn headline reads "Incoming SWE Intern - Deloitte," and Shanay Champaneri is still enrolled at Cornell. The pattern of presenting intern-level roles with language that suggests senior positions, combined with entirely self-reported traction, means I have to heavily discount the revenue claim. I've backed young founders before -- age isn't disqualifying -- but credential inflation is a negative signal about judgment, and unverifiable traction at this stage is just a claim, not evidence.

The market structure timing question kills my enthusiasm. What structural shift makes consulting-specific AI knowledge management possible NOW and nonobvious to consensus? The answer is RAG architectures plus GPT-4-class models, which crossed a quality threshold for document retrieval and generation in 2023-2024. But this catalyst is maximally consensus -- it's literally the thesis behind Glean's $7.2B valuation, Microsoft Copilot's enterprise push, and dozens of YC-backed companies including Kenley (YC S24), which targets the identical buyer. When I backed Harvey for legal AI, the nonobvious element was that legal technology had been a graveyard for a decade and most investors were still scarred by prior failures -- the structural shift was real but the scars created a window where capital was underallocated. Consulting KM is the opposite: capital is overallocated to this exact thesis right now. Verticalization for consulting adds some specificity, but "build vertical AI for [professional services category]" is the most popular pitch in venture today. There's no structural shift here that consensus has missed.

Meridian builds at the application layer on top of contested infrastructure -- foundation models, embedding systems, vector databases -- all actively fought over by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Microsoft. This is precisely the pattern I flag: when the underlying platform layer hasn't been won, applications built on top carry platform risk. Contrast this with Stripe, which became the infrastructure layer itself, or even Gusto, which embedded so deeply into payroll compliance that the regulatory complexity IS the moat. Meridian's defensibility thesis rests on accumulated behavioral data ("behavioral signal on how consulting work actually gets done -- you can't download it, you have to earn it one engagement at a time"). That's a compelling aspiration, but it requires years of sustained adoption at scale before the data asset becomes meaningful. At pre-seed with unverifiable traction, the moat is theoretical. Meanwhile, Glean with $200M ARR and $610M+ in funding could add consulting-specific features in a single product cycle.

The single-miracle test is where Meridian scores best. The technology miracle is exogenous -- LLMs continue improving on their own curve. Consulting firms are proven buyers of productivity software with clear budget authority (managing partners, not IT committees). The core miracle Meridian needs is distribution: can they win enough firms before Glean or Microsoft adds consulting-vertical features? That's one miracle, and it's a tractable execution challenge rather than a breakthrough. This favorable structure is offset by the fact that the distribution window may be narrow -- Kenley has YC's network behind it, Glean has massive enterprise sales teams, and Interloom just raised $16.5M for adjacent tacit-knowledge capture.

The bull case for Meridian requires three things to be true simultaneously: the $700K contracted revenue is real and represents genuine pull from consulting managing partners; the data moat compounds faster than horizontal competitors can verticalize; and the founding team, despite their early-career backgrounds, can scale a company selling to sophisticated enterprise buyers who typically require gray-haired account executives. If those three conditions hold, this could look like an early-stage Veeva -- a vertical platform defending against Salesforce by being purpose-built for a specific professional services workflow. The Deloitte connection through Farrington provides at least first-hand exposure to consulting pain points, and managing partners referring the product "unprompted" (if true) suggests real product-market pull. But I've seen too many companies with impressive early claims that couldn't survive independent diligence. The YC batch departure -- accepted to F25 then left for undisclosed reasons -- adds another uncertainty that the dossier doesn't resolve.

My pass comes down to two dimensions. First, nonobvious market timing: "AI for enterprise knowledge management" is the most obvious category in venture right now, and consulting verticalization doesn't rescue it from consensus. I don't have an edge backing the 15th company pursuing this thesis. Second, the founding team: Kashyap Nathan, Chris Farrington, and Shanay Champaneri bring intern-level enterprise experience to a market where the buyer is a consulting managing partner expecting Fortune-500 vendor maturity. The High Growth Handbook lesson is that strong markets compensate for some execution gaps, but when the market itself is crowded and consensus, execution capacity becomes load-bearing -- and the team evidence doesn't support that weight.
