# noetic -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Noetic isn't the product or the market — it's that this is a compliance automation company built by three Yale undergraduates who are still completing their bachelor's degrees. And I need to be honest about what that tells me and what it doesn't. I've backed young founders. Age isn't the issue. The issue is that when I ask myself my first question — "tell me your story, how did you get here?" — what I hear back is: elite university, quantitative trading internships at D.E. Shaw and Citadel, a robotics research lab, and a logical observation that compliance paperwork is tedious. That's a fine origin for a startup, but it's not the kind of biographical thread I need to feel conviction. I'm looking for the founder who has been through something that makes them incapable of *not* solving this problem. I don't see that thread here. These are bright, credentialed kids who noticed a real pain point in their lab work, and that's valid — but it's a very different signal than a founder whose life story is inseparable from the mission.

The competitive picture concerns me in a specific way. Saphira AI came through YC just two batches earlier, positioning as "Vanta for Hardware," and already has $440K in revenue. They have founders from Twitter, Tesla, Amazon, and Hyperloop — people who lived inside hardware organizations that wrestle with these exact certification challenges daily. Noetic is entering the same YC network, targeting the same robotics and hardware customers, with the same distribution channels, but from behind. The differentiation — upstream regulatory identification and lab matching versus Saphira's traceability and report generation — is real but thin. In my experience, when two startups share a customer base and a distribution channel and the earlier one already has revenue traction, the second one needs something extraordinary to overcome that headstart. I don't see that extraordinary thing here.

I'll give the bull case its due, because the timing argument is genuinely interesting. UL 3300 becoming OSHA-mandatory on December 31, 2025, creates a compliance wave that every robotics company deploying near humans must now ride. The first retail robot just got certified in March 2026. This is the kind of regulatory inflection point that creates urgent, concentrated demand — and if Noetic can position itself as the tool that gets you through UL 3300 in weeks instead of months, there's a wedge. Hardware compliance is also one of those complex, regulated spaces I'm naturally drawn to — it's the kind of category where regulatory friction creates structural barriers that keep lazy competitors out. The founders' APOLLO Lab background means they've at least touched the problem domain, and the standards licensing and IP complexity would deter most AI-wrapper startups from entering seriously. If I squint, I can see a version of this story where Noetic builds a proprietary knowledge graph of regulatory requirements that compounds with every customer engagement, creating real defensibility over time. For that to work, though, you'd need founders who have navigated regulatory environments before, who have relationships with testing labs and standards bodies, who understand the political dynamics of compliance certification — and nothing in the dossier suggests this team has any of that yet.

What I keep coming back to is that this doesn't activate any of my core investment signals. It's not anti-consensus — "use AI to automate paperwork" is possibly the most consensus idea in Silicon Valley right now. It's not creating economic access for underserved populations — it's making compliance cheaper for well-funded hardware companies, which is useful but doesn't carry the positive-sum economic impact I look for in companies like Uber or Affirm or Thumbtack. The founders aren't misfits or outsiders who've been told no repeatedly — they're Yale students with elite internship pedigrees. There's no biographical grit, no "magically weird" quality, no jolt of intuitive energy. The one area where I see real signal is the co-founder dynamic: three classmates who shared a research lab and had a pre-existing personal relationship before founding. That's actually a decent foundation — similar to the family-business resilience pattern I've come to appreciate. But it's the only signal that maps to my framework, and it's not enough to build conviction around.

This is a competent team building a logical product in a real market. I don't doubt these founders are smart. But I'm looking for something different — I'm looking for the dreamer solving a problem that nobody else thinks is solvable, the misfit whose life experience makes them uniquely suited to push through walls that would stop anyone else. Noetic feels like a well-reasoned thesis project, not a founder's life mission. I'd pass and wish them well.
