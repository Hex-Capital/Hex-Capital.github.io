# Chasi -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

What strikes me first about Chasi isn't the founders or the product -- it's the market itself. Equipment dealers sitting on fleets utilized below 60%, billions in idle cranes and forklifts while phone calls go unanswered and quotes rot in email inboxes. That's exactly the kind of invisible waste I've learned to pay attention to. When I backed Carta, nobody was excited about cap table management -- but the opacity in how equity was tracked was creating real harm. When I backed Affirm, nobody thought transparent credit terms were sexy. There's a pattern here: unglamorous systems hiding enormous value destruction, and founders who show up to fix what nobody else wants to touch. Chasi is pointing at one of those systems. The question is whether these are the right founders to crack it open.

And that's where I get stuck. My first question is always "tell me your story" -- not the business, not the market, but how you ended up here as a human being. What I see in Akash and Sarman's backgrounds is impressive professional trajectory: Akash designed tractors at TAFE, built racing cars, ran GTM for industrial AI at Axion Ray. Sarman was a founding engineer at Tesla, deployed AI at Cummins and Harley Davidson, and helped build Axion Ray from pre-seed through its $64M Series B. That's serious domain credibility. But domain credibility isn't what I invest on first. I'm listening for the biographical thread -- the hardship that forged an unbreakable bond between this person and this problem. Why equipment dealers specifically? Was there a moment of personal reckoning, a family connection to this world, a failure that made this problem feel like destiny? The dossier gives me career moves but not that thread. These founders made a smart professional decision to apply their industrial AI expertise to an adjacent vertical. That's rational. It's not the same as being haunted by the problem.

The competitive dynamics are worth dwelling on because they reveal something about timing. Quipli already launched Quinn -- an AI revenue engine for equipment rental that's trained on millions of proprietary transactions. That's a real data advantage. But Quinn only works within Quipli's own customer base, which constrains its reach. Chasi's bet is that being stack-agnostic -- plugging into whatever dealer management system already exists -- gives it broader reach in a fragmented market where nobody wants to rip and replace their existing tools. That's a sound structural insight. Equipment dealers are famously resistant to changing vendors, so the integration-first approach is smart. The question is whether "we work with what you already have" is a defensible moat or just an easier initial sale that competitors eventually replicate. I think the founders' Axion Ray experience, where they navigated exactly this kind of multi-system integration challenge in industrial settings, is genuine edge here. But the underlying technology -- LLM-powered agents handling sales inquiries -- is broadly available. The moat has to come from depth of domain training and integration lock-in, not from the AI itself.

Let me state the bull case clearly, because it's real. If you squint at this the right way, Chasi is doing for equipment dealers what nobody else is willing to do: going deep into a trillion-dollar distribution industry that most AI startups consider beneath them. The founders have genuinely rare experience deploying AI in industrial settings. They're already live across businesses in the US and Europe at pre-seed, which suggests they can sell to this notoriously skeptical customer base. The economic access angle is indirect but meaningful -- when equipment is allocated more efficiently, the small contractor who needs a crane for a three-day job can actually get one instead of being ignored because the dealer's sales team is overwhelmed. And the co-founder relationship is one of the strongest signals here: Akash and Sarman worked together at Axion Ray through multiple funding stages, which means they've already survived the stresses of building a company together. I learned from HQ Trivia that even perfect timing and product can't overcome founder conflict -- so a co-founder team with battle-tested alignment is genuinely valuable.

But I keep coming back to my primary filter. This feels like a well-crafted enterprise AI play by experienced operators who identified a market gap. It doesn't feel like the kind of company where the founders would rather die than stop working on this problem. Nobody's going to call this idea insane -- they're going to call it sensible, maybe even boring. And "sensible" has never been my signal. The companies that have defined my portfolio -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe Energy -- all had a quality of seeming slightly unhinged to the mainstream. AI agents for equipment dealers is rational and overlooked, but it doesn't make anyone uncomfortable. It doesn't trigger that jolt of energy I felt with Niantic. It's a competent bet in a real market, and someone will make money here. I'm just not sure it needs to be my money.
