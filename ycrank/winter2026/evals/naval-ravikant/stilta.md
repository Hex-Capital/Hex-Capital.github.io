# Stilta -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything. Five direct competitors raised over $100 million combined in the last twelve months -- Solve Intelligence at $55M with eight-figure ARR and 400+ customers, Patlytics at $21M with Google Gradient backing, Ankar at $20M from Atomico, and &AI at $6.5M from First Round doing exactly what Stilta does, also out of YC. When that many smart investors are simultaneously funding the same thesis, the pricing already reflects the optimism. Stilta enters with $500K against competitors holding twenty to a hundred times more capital and established customer bases. This is the pattern I've warned about for years -- chasing a hot market where the asymmetric upside has already been arbitraged away by the crowd. The dossier itself notes "the concurrent explosion of funding into AI patent tools in 2025" as a "why now" signal. But that explosion is precisely the problem. When everyone agrees on the timing, you're not early -- you're on time, which in venture is the same as late.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Four McKinsey consultants building an AI tool for patent attorneys. McKinsey literally trains people to be generalists who can analyze any industry in six weeks -- that's the institutional purpose. "If they can train you to do it, eventually they will train a computer to do it." Where is the founder who spent years inside a patent office, who understands in their bones why examiner search reports miss relevant prior art in specific technology domains, who can articulate a non-obvious insight about patent prosecution that would surprise a thirty-year IP partner? I see none of that. I see a team that identified a vertical where AI could be applied and brought strong engineering credentials to it. Tobias Estreen led agentic AI teams at McKinsey and studied physics and ML at KTH -- that's genuine technical capability. Petrus Werner built scalable systems at QuantumBlack and AWS. These are good engineers. But their specific knowledge is in building enterprise AI systems, not in understanding the deep structure of patent work. That makes this a general capability applied to a specific vertical, which means any equally talented team can reach the same point with the same effort.

The leverage architecture is incremental rather than transformative. "Reduce manual work by up to ~50%" is an efficiency claim, not a leverage claim. A patent attorney using Stilta is still a patent attorney doing patent work, just faster. Compare this to what Replit did -- it didn't make existing programmers 50% faster, it turned non-programmers into programmers. That's creating a new form of leverage. Stilta doesn't enable a new type of person to do patent analysis. It doesn't create network effects where each user makes the platform more valuable for every other user. It's SaaS sold through enterprise sales to law firms -- a distribution model that scales linearly with the number of salespeople you hire. The "Cursor for patent practitioners" positioning is honest but revealing: Cursor works because code is universal and developers are the fastest-growing professional category on Earth. Patent practitioners are a fixed, small, conservative buyer pool with long procurement cycles.

The strongest bull case would be this: the team's knowledge of building auditable, reliable AI systems for conservative enterprise buyers IS the scarce, relevant knowledge -- not patent law expertise. The argument is that anyone can learn patent workflows, but building AI agents that law firms actually trust requires exactly the kind of enterprise AI discipline McKinsey/QuantumBlack instills. If trust and auditability are the real moat, then the team that builds the most reliable system wins regardless of domain depth. And they have paying customers already -- "leading firms in both Europe and the US" -- which suggests the trust layer is working. Oskar Block bootstrapped two prior companies to $1M+ ARR, demonstrating he can sell and close. If they can move faster than their better-funded competitors on the agentic architecture -- leveraging the fact that a four-person team makes decisions faster than a sixty-person company -- they could establish trust relationships that become switching costs. In legal, the first tool a firm trusts tends to stay. That's a real thesis. But it requires believing that execution speed beats capital advantage in enterprise legal sales, which history doesn't support.

I'd pass. Not because the team lacks intelligence or energy -- they clearly have both. But because there's no specific knowledge that couldn't be replicated by any of the five well-funded competitors already in the space, no contrarian positioning to generate asymmetric upside, and no leverage mechanism that creates nonlinear returns. This is a competent team entering a consensus market with the least capital. The technology -- agentic AI with audit trails -- is genuinely useful but not proprietary. The dossier itself acknowledges "the core agentic AI architecture is not proprietary to Stilta." When the technology isn't proprietary and the market is crowded, you're betting on pure execution. I prefer bets where the founder knows something nobody else knows, building something that creates leverage that didn't previously exist, at a time when everyone else thinks they're wrong.
