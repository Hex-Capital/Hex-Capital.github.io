# Crebit -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Crebit's traction numbers stopped me mid-scroll: $1M+ in FX volume and 45 university partners within three months of launch, built by two people. Two founders, both under 21, both dropped out of MIT and Stanford respectively to ship this. That's the kind of team-to-value ratio that usually makes me reach for my checkbook. But then I ran my specific knowledge filter, and the picture became more complicated.

Jensen Coonradt is clearly a prodigious builder -- MLH Top 50 out of 600,000 hackers, Lendora acquired by BankSocial with a Federal Reserve pilot scheduled, internships at Amazon and NASA. He builds fast and he builds real things. Simmi Sen has genuine entrepreneurial credibility through Tints, a streetwear brand that attracted Taco Bell seed funding and celebrity adoption. These are impressive young people. But cross-border payments is not a hackathon. The specific knowledge required to navigate money transmission licensing across seven jurisdictions, manage FX risk at scale, maintain KYC/AML compliance in Brazil, Nigeria, the Philippines, and Mexico simultaneously -- this knowledge lives in people who spent years at Wise, Stripe, or inside correspondent banking networks. Jensen's blockchain experience through Lendora is adjacent but not equivalent. The founders identified this opportunity after the GENIUS Act passed in July 2025 -- that's market research timing, not the obsessive domain immersion I look for. If Jensen disappeared, another strong technical builder could replicate the stablecoin-to-fiat bridge. The insight is not non-replicable.

The leverage architecture tells a split story. On one side, stablecoin settlement as a replacement for correspondent banking is genuinely permissionless leverage -- zero marginal cost per transaction once the rails are built, and Crebit can process payments while the team sleeps. The principal-agent problem at Flywire is structurally real: Flywire's $623M in revenue comes from FX spreads built on correspondent banking inefficiency. Adopting stablecoin settlement would cannibalize their own margin. This is the same dynamic that let Perplexity attack Google -- incumbents can't destroy their own profit engine. But here's where the analogy breaks: Aravind Srinivas was facing agents constrained by their own business model. Jensen Coonradt is facing Stripe, which launched stablecoin payouts across 100+ countries with no legacy revenue to protect and infinite engineering resources. Mastercard and Brex are building the same infrastructure. Crebit's stablecoin bridge is rented leverage built on someone else's rails -- the moment Stripe offers this as a feature, the cost advantage narrows to zero. The university portal embedding provides some switching cost, but 45 university integrations is a speed advantage, not a structural moat.

The co-founder relationship passes my principal-agent diagnostic cleanly. Jensen and Simmi met through a Bank of America scholarship program, then went through Jane Street together -- this is a relationship built through shared intellectual intensity, not a "find a co-founder" Meetup. Both walked away from elite institutions to build this. Dropping out of MIT and Stanford at 19 and 20 is a real cost with real signal. They're not hedge-fund analysts testing entrepreneurship on nights and weekends. But I need to separate commitment from domain fusion. These founders are committed principals, but they're principals who chose this problem recently, not principals whose identity is inseparable from it. Compare to CoinList, which I spun out of AngelList -- I had years of specific knowledge about what the token launch market needed because I had been living inside it. Jensen has been living inside hackathons and blockchain experiments, which is closer than most, but not the same as having spent years watching international students get gouged on tuition payments and understanding exactly why the correspondent banking system makes it inevitable.

The strongest bull case: Crebit is doing to Flywire what AngelList did to the old-boys-network of angel investing -- using technology to democratize access to something previously gatekept by expensive intermediaries. International students paying 2-10% markups on tens of thousands of dollars is a real and painful form of structural exclusion. The GENIUS Act created a regulatory window. Two extraordinary young builders executed at remarkable speed. If Jensen develops the regulatory and financial infrastructure expertise through doing -- if Crebit's compliance team grows but the core product stays code-leveraged -- the early university partnerships could compound into a genuine distribution moat before Stripe builds the vertical solution. The 45-university footprint in three months suggests distribution velocity that larger players can't match with their sales cycles.

The risk that would flip me to Pass: Stripe's stablecoin payout infrastructure makes Crebit's core technical advantage a commodity. The technology is replicable -- per-country rail integrations (PIX, UPI, M-Pesa) require compliance work, not specific knowledge. Multi-jurisdiction licensing for a two-person team in seven countries is structurally unsustainable without significant hiring, which degrades the team-to-value ratio that currently impresses me. And immigration policy sensitivity -- Flywire flagged $30M in potential revenue pressure from student flow changes -- means Crebit's entire addressable market is subject to political risk the founders cannot control.

Crebit lands in my neutral zone. The access democratization pattern is real and maps clearly to my CoinList thesis. The team-to-value execution is genuinely exceptional at 2 people. But the specific knowledge gap -- the dimension I weight most heavily at 30 points -- holds me back. Jensen is a remarkable builder who chose this problem, not a domain expert who couldn't stop building toward it. When Stripe can replicate the core technology as a feature, the only durable advantage is founder-specific knowledge, and I don't see enough of it to underwrite the bet from my own capital.
