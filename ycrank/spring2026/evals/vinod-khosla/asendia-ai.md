# Asendia AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Asendia AI's dossier reads like the company I describe when someone asks me what I *don't* fund. Voice AI agents that screen candidates for staffing agencies, saving recruiters five hours a day and improving fill rates by 70% — that's a 2x efficiency gain in a single profession. If Asendia AI fully succeeds, staffing agencies process candidates faster. Good for them. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. A candidate still gets a job through a staffing agency; they just talked to a bot instead of a person on the first call. The consequence of complete success is a profitable SaaS company in a $694M market growing at 8.3% CAGR. I don't invest my own money to generate moderate returns in moderately growing markets. I want to either lose it or make fifty times.

Asendia AI's core technology — speech-to-text, LLM-based candidate evaluation, text-to-speech, telephony integration — is built on commodity components the dossier itself identifies as "widely available." The differentiation is "domain-specific fine-tuning, ATS integration depth, and compliance infrastructure," which is integration work, not a technology discontinuity. Compare this to how Rocket Lab built novel electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a genuinely new way to solve a physics problem. Asendia AI is assembling existing APIs into a workflow product. Fifty ATS integrations and SOC 2 certifications are execution achievements, not technical moats. When I invest, I want a company whose approach produces a 10x step-function, not one whose advantage is that they connected Bullhorn to Twilio before someone else did.

Rihab Lajmi does show real learning signals. She pivoted Asendia AI from a B2C career coaching product ("FAANG" / "Buddy") to B2B staffing agency tooling after conducting 35+ recruiter interviews — she talked to the market, heard that the buyer was the agency not the candidate, and changed course. That's exactly the adaptation I look for. Both founders left Tunisia at eighteen for Germany, worked at Microsoft, Google, and Infineon, and are building in a second language in a third country. I notice immigrant founders who operate with that kind of drive — my portfolio over-indexes on them. Badis Zormati's six years of AI engineering at Infineon and EE from TU Munich provide legitimate technical depth. The pivot from B2C to B2B is evidence of intellectual honesty, not instability. These are capable founders. But capability applied to a bounded problem still produces a bounded outcome.

The bull case an advocate would make: Workday's $1.5B acquisition of Paradox in August 2025 validates the category and creates a structural opening — Workday will fold Olivia into its enterprise HCM suite, orphaning standalone staffing agency buyers who don't use Workday's core platform. Asendia AI is positioned in that gap with live voice AI (deeper than Paradox's text chatbot), 50+ ATS integrations, and ~10 paying clients across US and European markets in healthcare, IT, and blue-collar verticals. The timing is real — voice AI crossed a usability threshold in 2024-2025, and Asendia AI is early to staffing-agency-specific deployment. A DoorDash-style exception applies: execution-heavy, immigrant founders, real market need. But the DoorDash exception existed because the consequence magnitude was planetary — transforming food access for hundreds of millions. Asendia AI's consequence of success is that staffing agencies screen more efficiently. That is not the same scale of human impact. The exception doesn't trigger.

Asendia AI competes against Paradox ($253M+ raised), HireVue ($93M raised, $89M revenue in 2024), Humanly ($52M raised, YC-backed), Sense HQ ($90M raised, $500M valuation), and Fetcher ($41M raised). These organizations have 10-100x more capital, mature customer bases, and established integrations. Asendia AI's ~10 paying clients represent real pre-seed traction, but the competitive set is formidable and the technical moat is thin. When the underlying technology is commodity, the company with more capital and more customers wins the integration race. Asendia AI's path to dominance requires outrunning better-funded competitors in a feature war built on the same foundational APIs — that's an execution grind, not a technology bet. I prefer technology risk to market risk. Asendia AI is pure market risk.

One condition would flip me: if Asendia AI demonstrated a proprietary model or data flywheel that produced measurably superior candidate-to-placement matching — not 20% better, but a step-function improvement where their AI placements outperform human recruiter placements by 5-10x on retention or performance metrics, creating a genuinely new capability that doesn't exist today. That would shift the consequence from "faster screening" to "fundamentally better hiring," which changes the calculus. No evidence of that exists in the dossier. The decisive dimensions are consequence magnitude (bounded to workflow efficiency in a single industry) and non-linear technical approach (commodity technology assembled, not invented). Both point clearly to Pass.
