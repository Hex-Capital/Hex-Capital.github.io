# Arzana -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Arzana isn't the product. It's the schlep. I wrote years ago that "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps," and that's why Stripe-type problems sat unsolved for a decade. Manufacturing front-office automation is that kind of problem squared. You have to parse handwritten notes, integrate with eleven different ERP systems, sit in offices in Iowa watching someone manually enter RFQs from faxed PDFs. Every Stanford CS grad with a GPT wrapper walks right past this. William Alexander and Marshall Kools walked toward it -- and they have a field engineer stationed in Okoboji, Iowa, not because it's strategic, but because that's where the factories are. When the Airbnb founders went to New York and knocked on doors, I thought that was "a very good sign...that these guys were actually on the ground hunting down and understanding their users." Arzana is doing that, except replace Brooklyn apartments with manufacturing plants in the Midwest.

The founder determination signals here are unusually strong for a pre-seed company. Alexander taught himself Mandarin in Hong Kong, learned discus throwing from YouTube videos and hit a state ranking, and has taken exactly one coding class. Kools was a D1 wrestler at Stanford, a Wisconsin state champion -- and made approximately ten thousand dollars last year. These are not people motivated by money. "The startup founders who end up richest are not the ones driven by money. They're usually trying to improve the world." Whether or not they're trying to improve the world in some grand sense, they're clearly obsessed with this problem in the way I look for -- what I once called "someone who does what they do so well that they pass right through professional and cross over into obsessive." The fact that they previously co-founded Brilla together and then pivoted to manufacturing AI tells me two things: first, the co-founder relationship survived a failed startup, which is the real stress test. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock." Second, they pivoted. "Most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended." The founders who scare me are the ones who cling to their original idea. These two found a better problem and went after it.

Now, the objections. The CEO has taken one coding class. For a company building AI agents that integrate with enterprise ERP systems, that's not nothing -- it's a real gap. The entire technical stack rests on two founding engineers with limited public track records. I've said before that "good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people," and the open question is whether Alexander's extraordinary determination compensates for the thin technical bench, or whether this is the kind of company that breaks when the founding engineer gets sick for a week. Endeavor AI has fourteen times more disclosed funding and a two-year head start. And maintaining eleven-plus ERP integrations with four people is the kind of surface area that can quietly devour an engineering team.

But here's what I keep coming back to: they have Schneider National and Zeiss on their customer page. Those are real companies. A Tier One Technologies VP says Arzana cut their time-to-quote from five days to one and their win rate "skyrocketed." At pre-seed, with four people, from Iowa. That's not playing house. "A characteristic mistake of young founders is to go through the motions of starting a startup. We saw this so often we made up a name for it -- playing house." These founders are not playing house. They're in the field, they have published pricing ($2,500-$7,000/month), and they have named enterprise logos. The bull case is that this is the Stripe pattern for manufacturing -- a painful schlep that everyone avoids, attacked by determined founders who are embedded in the customer base, building integrations that create real switching costs once deployed. If they can hire two or three strong engineers in the next six months and keep landing mid-market manufacturers at that price point, the compounding effects of workflow-specific data and ERP integration depth could create a moat that Endeavor's broader approach can't replicate. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." Front-office quoting at manufacturing plants is deceptively small.

The market timing is right. LLMs crossed a capability threshold in the last two years for parsing the kind of unstructured documents -- handwritten notes, varied PDF formats, messy email threads -- that constitute real manufacturing workflows. Prior OCR and rule-based systems couldn't handle this format diversity. Incumbent ERP vendors face a business model conflict: building lightweight, fast-deploy AI layers would cannibalize the professional services revenue their implementation partners depend on. That's the kind of structural opening that gives a small, fast-moving startup room to establish itself before the elephants turn around. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors" -- what kills them is not making something people want, and the early evidence suggests Arzana is making something manufacturers genuinely want.

I'd invest, with eyes open about the technical team risk. The founders pass my animal test. They're earnest and determined in a way that reminds me of the best YC companies at this stage -- not because they have everything figured out, but because they clearly won't stop until they do. The non-technical CEO is a real concern, not a dealbreaker. What I need to believe is that Alexander and Kools can recruit engineers who are as obsessed with this problem as they are. If they can, the schlep itself becomes the moat -- because nobody else wants to do this work.
