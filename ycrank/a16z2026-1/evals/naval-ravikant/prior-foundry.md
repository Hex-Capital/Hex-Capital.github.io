# Prior Foundry -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The principal-agent problem at McKinsey is what makes Prior Foundry interesting. Policy consulting is pure labor leverage -- armies of analysts billing $500 an hour for months to produce behavioral intelligence that's outdated by the time it's delivered. McKinsey has the relationships, the credibility, and probably the talent to build AI-based policy simulation. But every simulation that replaces a six-month engagement cannibbalizes their own billable hours. They're agents of their P&L. Shirin Abrishami Kashani, Jonne Kamphorst, and Keshav Sivakumar, with no legacy revenue to protect, can build the tool that McKinsey's consultants never will. This is the same structural dynamic that makes Perplexity viable against Google -- the incumbent's business model becomes the insurgent's moat.

Specific knowledge density here is genuinely high, and it's distributed across the team in a way I find compelling. Jonne Kamphorst didn't read about LLM-based behavioral simulation and decide it was a market opportunity -- he was inside the Stanford lab that pioneered the methodology. The arxiv paper demonstrating 85% accuracy on survey response replication comes from his research ecosystem. That's not a resume credential; it's years of accumulated understanding about how to calibrate synthetic agents against real human behavior that would take a competitor significant time to replicate. Shirin brings the other half of the specific knowledge stack -- OECD policy analysis experience means she knows what decision-makers actually need, how they consume intelligence, what format makes them act. Keshav's Palantir deployment background completes the triad: he's done the hard work of getting software into government hands. Each founder's knowledge would take years to acquire independently, and the combination is harder to assemble than any single expertise.

The leverage audit reveals a structural tension. Prior Foundry's product is permissionless leverage -- AI simulation that runs in hours instead of months, zero marginal cost per additional scenario. Three people generating $60K ARR in two months with a >$1M pipeline through a distributor is the right ratio. But the distribution channel is deeply permissioned. Government procurement is gatekept by compliance officers, budget committees, and multi-year cycles. The OECD partnership spanning 38+ member governments is a remarkable institutional wedge for a pre-seed company -- it's the kind of distribution hack that most govtech startups spend years trying to establish. Still, every new government contract requires permission. This isn't Notion spreading virally through teams. The product has code leverage; the GTM has labor leverage. That ceiling matters.

One signal concerns me as a principal-agent diagnostic. Jonne Kamphorst started an Assistant Professor position at Sciences Po Paris in January 2026 while serving as Prior Foundry's Chief Scientific Officer. A principal's identity is fused with the company. An academic appointment in a different timezone is a hedge -- it preserves optionality in a way that true principals don't. Shirin's trajectory is cleaner: leaving a Stanford PhD path and OECD career to build this is principal behavior. Keshav's prior founding experience at Lumitics (raised $557K seed, built and shipped) demonstrates he's done this before. But the CSO responsible for the core scientific methodology splitting attention with a tenure-track position creates exactly the kind of agency problem I look for in the negative column.

Prior Foundry fits a pattern I've backed repeatedly -- democratizing access to resources locked behind institutional gatekeepers. AngelList gave everyone access to startup investing. CoinList removed securities lawyers from token launches. Prior Foundry gives policy teams simulation capability previously available only to organizations that could afford McKinsey. The Allegheny County pilot shows this working at the local government level, where budgets can't support traditional consulting. The commoditization risk from the open-source genagents repository is real -- the foundational code is on GitHub -- but the defensible layer isn't the algorithm. It's the calibration quality built from accumulated simulation results, the domain-specific workflow tooling, and the OECD relationship that no GitHub repo provides.

The validation gap between 85% survey replication accuracy and actual policy outcome prediction is the thesis-specific risk that would flip me. If Prior Foundry's simulations start producing confident predictions that diverge from real-world policy outcomes -- or worse, if a government acts on a simulation that turns out to be systematically wrong across a demographic group -- the entire product category takes reputational damage. The evidence that would increase my conviction: a published case where Prior Foundry's simulation predicted a non-obvious population response that was subsequently validated by real-world data. That would convert "interesting research tool" into "decision-critical infrastructure." Without that validation, I'm betting on the team's specific knowledge and the structural advantage against consulting incumbents, which is enough for me at pre-seed but not enough for a top-quartile score.

The dimensions that determine my tier: specific knowledge productization is Prior Foundry's strongest signal -- Jonne's research origins, Shirin's OECD experience, and Keshav's Palantir deployment background create a founder-knowledge stack that IS the product. The permissionless leverage architecture is where the thesis partially breaks -- AI-native product mechanics are strong, but government distribution is inherently permissioned. $60K ARR in two months and the OECD partnership earn conviction at pre-seed. I'd write a small check.
