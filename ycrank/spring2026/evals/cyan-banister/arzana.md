# Arzana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what caught my eye first: a 21-year-old kid from Okoboji, Iowa — population barely four thousand — who taught himself Mandarin in Hong Kong, learned discus throwing from YouTube well enough to rank at state, and has taken exactly one coding class in his life, is now selling AI automation to Fortune 500 manufacturers with a field engineer who's also from Okoboji. The New York Times ran a piece called "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype," and honestly, they don't. In a world where every other AI company is two ex-Google engineers in San Francisco building a wrapper on GPT, William Alexander is driving around Iowa and the Midwest installing software in actual manufacturing plants. That's unusual, and I notice unusual. But unusual and inevitable are different things, and when I ask myself whether this founder was forged for this specific problem, the answer gets complicated.

Tell me their story, not the business — that's always my first question. Alexander grew up around manufacturing in rural Iowa. He went to Stanford, studied econ and computer science, and his first startup was... Brilla, a gourmet meal delivery service on campus. Then he pivoted to manufacturing AI. Kools was a D1 wrestler, state champion, management science degree, co-founded the same meal delivery startup as COO. There's real grit in both of them — the self-teaching, the athletic discipline, Kools making $10,000 last year while building this. I see survival instincts and resourcefulness. But when I'm looking for the biographical forge — the thing that happened in someone's life that made them incapable of walking away from this problem — I don't find it. Growing up near manufacturing isn't the same as growing up inside the broken system. The pivot from college meal delivery to factory automation suggests the problem didn't find the founder; the founder went looking for a problem. That's not disqualifying, but it's different from the founders I write my highest-conviction checks for, the ones who would be doing this work without a dime of venture money because they can't not do it.

Now, the invisible system question: Is there something opaque and extractive being made transparent here? Manufacturing front-office workflows are genuinely messy — emails, PDFs, handwritten notes, spreadsheets that only one person in the office understands. A five-day quote cycle that Arzana cut to one day at Tier One Technologies is real pain being eliminated. But this is operational friction, not a predatory structure. Nobody is being exploited by slow RFQ processing the way people were being exploited by the taxi medallion cartel or credit card opacity. The manufacturers themselves are the ones suffering from their own disorganization. That's a business worth building, but it doesn't trigger the same conviction in me as something that shifts power from gatekeepers to the people being gatekept. It's efficiency, not liberation.

The bull case is worth engaging with honestly, because the traction here is striking for a pre-seed company. Twenty-plus plants, Schneider National and Zeiss logos on the website, published pricing at $2,500 to $7,000 a month, and a customer testimonial with specific metrics. If even half those logos are paying customers, that's real revenue with a four-person team — the kind of early signal that suggests genuine product-market pull. The ERP integration depth — eleven-plus integrations with custom builds in under a week — creates real switching costs once deployed, and the timing is genuine: LLMs just crossed the threshold for reliably parsing the messy, unstructured documents that define actual manufacturing workflows. Prior approaches couldn't handle the format diversity. And Alexander's Iowa network is a distribution advantage that most AI startups simply cannot replicate — you can't Zoom your way into a manufacturing plant in the same way you can sell SaaS to a tech company. If the thesis is that manufacturing AI requires being physically embedded in the manufacturing world, these founders are better positioned than almost anyone in Silicon Valley. That's the case for this becoming something meaningful.

What holds me back is the combination of things I can't get past. The CEO has taken one coding class, which means the entire technical foundation rests on two engineers with limited public track records — that's key-person risk concentrated in exactly the wrong place for an AI company. Endeavor AI raised seven million dollars from Craft Ventures to pursue the same market, and the core technology — LLM-based document parsing — doesn't have a proprietary architecture moat. The defensibility is in the integrations and the customer relationships, which are real but take time to compound, and time is something a better-funded competitor can use against you. This isn't a space that makes sophisticated investors shift uncomfortably in their chairs; it's a space that a well-capitalized team could enter deliberately. And the pivot from meal delivery leaves me without the biographical certainty I need to believe these founders will stay in this trench for the decade it might take.

I respect what they're building. The traction is real, the market pain is real, and the Iowa connection gives them a genuine edge in distribution that most AI companies would kill for. But when I run my framework — the forge, the invisible system, the ten-year infrastructure question — I don't find enough of what makes me write first checks. This is a competent team executing well in a growing market. For many investors, that's enough. For me, I need to feel that this founder would be building this even if the world told them to stop, and that the company is secretly building infrastructure beneath its surface that will look obvious in retrospect but looks weird today. Arzana looks sensible today. That's not the same thing.
