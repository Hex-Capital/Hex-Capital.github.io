# Cofia -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Cofia is that Bardeen already shipped their product. In May 2025, Bardeen launched its "Work Intelligence Platform" with $22-25M in funding and strategic backing from Dropbox and HubSpot, describing AI agents that "learn how work actually happens, then execute and improve it." That is, essentially word for word, Cofia's value proposition. When I invested in Juniper Networks to challenge Cisco, nobody else was attempting TCP/IP routing for public telecom -- Cisco's CTO told me they would never do it. When I backed Impossible Foods, nobody else was trying to replicate meat at the molecular level. The signal I look for is when experts dismiss the idea and outsiders haven't crowded in. Here, we have the opposite: a well-funded startup has already validated and launched the concept, incumbents like Zapier are adding AI capabilities to their $310M-revenue platform, and Microsoft Power Automate sits in the background with limitless distribution. Cofia is not arriving before the market -- they are arriving after a competitor with ten times their capital has already defined the category.

Let me step back to the question I always ask first: if Cofia succeeds -- if automations truly write themselves by observing how knowledge workers move data between apps -- what is the consequence of that success? The answer is that people copy-paste less between SaaS tools. Workflows get slightly more automated. The $24 billion workflow automation market shifts from user-initiated to AI-initiated. This is a real productivity improvement, but it is not a transformation of how the world operates. Nobody's health changes. No fossil fuel gets displaced. No trillion-dollar system becomes structurally obsolete. The consequence of success is a better Zapier -- a meaningful business, perhaps, but not one that justifies the kind of risk I optimize for. This is precisely the class of company I warn founders about: you have reduced your probability of failure by choosing a well-understood market, and in doing so, you have made the consequences of success inconsequential.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If proactive AI automation is not just a feature but the beginning of a platform shift -- if observing user behavior and generating automations generalizes into an AI layer that sits between humans and all software -- then the consequence of success expands dramatically. The founders have relevant backgrounds: Moses Wayne rose to Senior Staff Engineer at Duolingo, building monetization and subscription infrastructure, which maps to systems that observe and respond to user behavior at scale. Paola Martínez led retention at Brilliant.org, directly relevant to understanding user motivation and behavioral patterns. The macro technology curve is real -- LLM inference costs are dropping exponentially, making continuous observation economically feasible in ways that were impossible two years ago. If Cofia's observation engine develops capabilities that Bardeen and Zapier cannot replicate -- some unique technical approach to workflow understanding -- this could be a genuine platform play. But I see no evidence of that differentiation in the dossier. The website contains only analytics tracking code. No product is visible. The dossier explicitly states that "the core AI capability relies on broadly available LLM technology, making the approach reproducible by well-resourced competitors." For this bull case to hold, I would need to see something technically proprietary, and I see nothing.

The founders are competent professionals from strong institutions -- Stanford, Duke, Duolingo, Brilliant.org -- but they are industry insiders building an industry product. There is no outsider perspective here, no first-principles rethinking of what automation means. They are not biochemists who wandered into the food industry or New Zealand engineers who decided to build rockets. They are product managers and software engineers building a SaaS productivity tool. This is the expected playbook from people with their backgrounds, not the contrarian leap I fund. I also see no evidence of the rapid learning and evolution of thinking that I look for in YC founders -- no pivots, no abandoned assumptions, no sign that their understanding of the problem has changed dramatically during their batch.

I pass on this. The consequence of success does not justify the effort from my perspective. The technology is reproducible, the market is crowded with well-funded competitors, and the founding team -- while capable -- is executing a consensus playbook in a consensus market. Some problems are too important not to work on. Making Zapier slightly more automatic is not one of them.
