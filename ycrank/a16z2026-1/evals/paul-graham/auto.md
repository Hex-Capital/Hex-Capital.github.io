# Auto -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Dave Evans built a 3D engine at PlayCanvas that has 14,700 GitHub stars. Sam Hare has a PhD in computer vision and built the mobile 3D reconstruction technology that Snap acquired in 2016. Between them, they've built two camera-related companies, sold both to Snap, and then spent the better part of a decade running Snap's camera engineering — AR Lenses, Games, Mini-Programs, the whole interactive content stack. This is an unusual density of domain expertise for a pre-seed team. When I backed Patrick Collison at Stripe, part of the signal was that I'd already watched him build and sell Auctomatic — I knew the quality of the builder before I evaluated the idea. Auto's founders have that same pattern: repeat builders returning to the problem they know most deeply, now with a new technical unlock (multimodal vision models crossing the accuracy threshold in 2023-2024) that makes something possible that wasn't before. If you asked me to name twenty people in the world best positioned to build an AI-powered camera platform, Evans and Hare would be on the list.

The problem is that Auto's idea sounds good. It sounds good to everyone. "Your phone camera should be smarter and do useful things" — no one hears that and says "that will never work." An App Store reviewer calling it a replacement for three existing subscriptions is positive early signal, but the underlying concept is consensus-obvious. Google Lens already processes 12 billion visual searches per month. Apple built Visual Intelligence directly into the operating system. ChatGPT Vision exists. When the three most valuable technology companies on Earth are already attacking your problem from different angles, you're not in the sweet spot where the idea repels before it attracts — you're in the spot where the idea attracted so powerfully that trillion-dollar incumbents arrived before you did. The "Frame" abstraction — turning photos into persistent mini-apps — is genuinely clever UX, but it's a pattern, not a patent. Google could add persistent workflows to Lens in a quarterly release.

Where Auto partially recovers is in the schlep analysis. The Frame concept has platform DNA that none of the incumbents are incentivized to build. Google Lens monetizes through search ads — turning photos into standalone task-apps pulls users away from the search funnel. Apple Visual Intelligence is an OS feature designed to sell iPhone 16 hardware, not to become an extensible app platform. The user-generated Frame ecosystem, if it reaches critical mass, creates the kind of switching cost that pure search results never do. Evans spent years at Snap building exactly this type of extensibility layer — SnapKit, Mini-Programs, Games — so he understands platform dynamics from the inside. Hare led the Lens Experience team, meaning he knows precisely where Snap's camera products failed to become platforms and where the gap exists. The question is whether this insight, which comes from building at Snap rather than from being a frustrated camera user, translates into a product people want badly enough to pay $9.99 per month for alongside free alternatives from Google and Apple.

The organic discovery here is real but comes from the builder side rather than the user side. These founders didn't encounter Auto's problem while ordering food or forgetting a USB drive — they encountered it while spending a decade building camera products and watching the technology approach but never quite reach the point where a phone camera could generate genuine contextual intelligence. That's a valid form of "living in the future" — they lived in the camera-as-platform future longer than almost anyone — but it lacks the emotional urgency of personal frustration. I don't have an Airbnb cereal box story from Evans or Hare. I have two very capable builders who shipped v1.0 on March 17, 2026 and reached v1.2.6 by April 6 — three weeks of rapid iteration — which shows the building pace I'd expect from this caliber of team, but not the kind of desperate resourcefulness that tells me they'll find a way when the incumbents start squeezing.

The strongest bull case for Auto is that Snap itself was a camera company competing against Apple Photos, Google Photos, and Facebook, and it built a $15+ billion business by owning the camera UX layer better than anyone else. Evans and Hare helped build that layer. If multimodal AI is the new AR — a technology that transforms what a camera can do — then these founders have already run this play once from inside a winning company, and now they're running it independently with the benefit of that experience. The Frame ecosystem could become to AI cameras what Lenses became to Snapchat: the platform layer that makes the product defensible. If that's true, Auto's $9.99/month competes not with Google Lens (free, search-oriented) but with a bundle of single-purpose apps, and the competitive frame shifts entirely.

The risk that would flip me is the one that's already visible: Apple controls the platform Auto runs on, and Apple Visual Intelligence is expanding from iPhone 16 to iPhone 15 Pro in iOS 18.4. Every expansion of Apple's built-in visual AI narrows the wedge Auto can occupy. Evans and Hare haven't shipped an Android version, and with no public download counts, user metrics, or revenue data, I can't assess whether the Frame concept generates the obsessive small-group usage that signals product-market fit. Five App Store ratings at 5.0 stars is a warm anecdote, not evidence. Auto's founders are among the best I've seen for this specific problem, but the idea lacks the contrarian quality that creates return premiums, and the competitive landscape is the opposite of a schlep that repels — it's a honeypot that attracted every giant in tech. Exceptional founders in a consensus-obvious space with existential platform risk — that's a bet I'd watch closely but not write a check for today.
