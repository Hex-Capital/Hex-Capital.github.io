# One Robot -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells me almost everything I need to know about this company before I evaluate anything else. NVIDIA is distributing Cosmos world foundation models for free -- over three million downloads -- and actively integrating learned simulation into Isaac Sim. World Labs, founded by Fei-Fei Li, closed a billion-dollar round in February 2026 to build spatial AI and 3D world models. Saturn is building a physics-aware generative engine for autonomous systems. Open-source frameworks from UT Austin and NVIDIA itself provide free simulation and synthetic data generation. When I see this many well-resourced players converging on the same thesis -- that learned world models will replace or augment physics-engine simulation for robotics -- I see a market where the secret has already been spoken aloud. The core insight that physics engines fail at visual realism and deformable-object dynamics is understood by every serious robotics researcher. One Robot is not building on a truth that most intelligent people reject; it is executing a narrower variant of a thesis the entire industry has already embraced. This is the difference between a secret and a consensus insight dressed in specialized clothing.

The founders' domain experience is the strongest signal in this dossier. Sarabu and Shon worked together at Industrial Next, where they directly encountered the bottleneck they now address -- policy evaluation taking longer than policy training. Sarabu's trajectory through NASA JPL, Google, Georgia Tech, and a bootstrapped company he took to profitability demonstrates genuine technical depth and entrepreneurial capability. Shon spent five years at Tesla during the Model 3 production ramp and on the Dojo supercomputer team -- this is serious large-scale AI infrastructure experience. These are not "men in suits" marketing simulation software; they are engineers who have trained robots with their own hands since 2020. I take this seriously. But domain expertise and a genuine problem do not by themselves create a monopoly. The question is whether their task-specific approach creates structural defensibility that compounds over time, or whether NVIDIA subsumes this capability into its platform the way it has absorbed so many other vertical features. The history of NVIDIA's ecosystem strategy suggests the latter.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. If VLA models become the dominant paradigm for robot control -- which the trajectory from Physical Intelligence, NVIDIA Gr00t, and others makes plausible -- then every robotics team will need evaluation and training environments that physics engines structurally cannot provide for contact-rich tasks. Textiles, deformable objects, sub-millimeter assembly: these are precisely the domains where hand-crafted physics breaks down and learned simulation could deliver an order-of-magnitude improvement. One Robot's task-specific approach -- training world models on actual robot data for each customer's manipulation workflow -- could create a data flywheel where each engagement improves model quality in ways that a horizontal platform like Cosmos never matches. This is the Stripe analogy: a developer-first tool that becomes infrastructure because the integration depth makes switching prohibitively costly. If the VLA market expands rapidly and One Robot establishes itself as the standard toolchain for manipulation-specific simulation, the data moat could become real. What would have to be true for this to be a great investment is that task-specificity genuinely matters more than scale in world model quality for manipulation, that NVIDIA's horizontal approach cannot capture this value, and that the VLA market itself materializes at sufficient scale within the company's runway. Each of these conditions is plausible but uncertain.

My core concern is structural, not executional. One Robot is building a better tool for an existing workflow -- simulation for robotics policy development -- rather than creating an entirely new category of value. Going from physics-engine simulation to learned world models is a "1 to n" improvement, not a "0 to 1" creation. The company makes something that already existed (robotics simulation) work better for a specific class of tasks. This is valuable engineering, but it does not create the kind of category-defining monopoly that produces power-law returns. Stripe did not make payments slightly better; it made programmatic payments possible for developers for the first time. PayPal did not improve checks; it created email-based payment. One Robot improves simulation for contact-rich manipulation, which is meaningful but categorical in a different way. The market they serve -- teams actively training VLAs for industrial manipulation -- is also narrow and early-stage. This is not a fatal flaw at pre-seed, but combined with the convergence of well-funded competitors on the same thesis, it limits the ceiling on what this company can become as an independent entity.

The beachhead strategy is actually well-conceived. Focusing on contact-rich manipulation tasks -- textiles, box folding, precision assembly -- is exactly the niche where physics-engine simulation fails most dramatically and where learned world models add the most value. Starting with material handling and manufacturing, where the founders have direct relationships from Industrial Next, creates a plausible path to initial dominance in a small segment. The expansion path from manipulation to broader robotics simulation is logical. But even here, the endgame is not a monopoly -- it is a specialized tools company that competes with NVIDIA's platform on one dimension and with well-funded startups on others. A company that needs everything to go right -- VLA adoption, task-specificity mattering, NVIDIA not caring enough to compete -- is a company whose probability-adjusted return does not justify the power-law standard I apply to every investment.
