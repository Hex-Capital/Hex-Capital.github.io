# Sila -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sila is that David Sacks has already raised $20 million to build the same company. Sacks -- who was COO at PayPal, who built Yammer and sold it to Microsoft for $1.2 billion, who understands enterprise communication software as deeply as anyone in Silicon Valley -- launched Glue in October 2025 with the explicit positioning of "agentic team chat." The language on Sila's thesis page and the language in Glue's announcement are nearly interchangeable. When two companies describe their product in the same words, neither has a secret. And when one of those companies is led by someone with $20 million, a decade of enterprise distribution relationships, and the PayPal Mafia network behind him, the other company is not in a monopoly race -- it is in a competition. I know how that ends.

The deeper problem is that "messaging needs to be rebuilt from scratch for AI agents" is not a contrarian truth -- it is consensus dressed in architectural language. Slack is building Agentforce. Microsoft Teams has Copilot with agent capabilities deployed to hundreds of millions of users. Dust.tt has $7.3 million in ARR building AI agents that sit alongside existing communication tools. Glean has $200 million in ARR adding agentic capabilities to enterprise knowledge. Notion AI is adding agents to its workspace. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" about Sila, the answer appears to be something every major platform company and at least three well-funded startups already believe. The Girardian dynamics are unmistakable: multiple players converging on the same vision, imitating each other's positioning, preparing to burn capital to acquire the same customers. This is a Red Ocean forming in real time.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously, rests on the innovator's dilemma. Slack generates revenue per human seat; AI agents that replace human seats cannibalize that model. Microsoft Teams is bundled with Office 365 and structurally incentivized to upsell Copilot at $30/user/month rather than rearchitect the messaging layer. If the transition from "messaging with AI bolted on" to "AI-native collaboration where agents are first-class teammates" is as fundamental as the transition from email to Slack, then the incumbent architectures truly cannot adapt without destroying their own economics. The founders' concept of persistent "AI Teammates" with defined roles operating continuously is a more radical vision than Glue's MCP-connected AI within threads -- it implies a workspace where the ratio of AI agents to humans inverts over time. If that inversion happens, the company that designs for it from day one has a genuine architectural advantage. And the founders are young, technical, and have shipped AI products together at Cornell. History shows that messaging platforms can be disrupted -- Slack displaced HipChat despite Atlassian's resources, because Slack understood something HipChat didn't. But for this analogy to hold, Sila would need to understand something that Glue, Dust, Slack, Microsoft, and every other player in this space does not. I see no evidence of that understanding.

The founders themselves present a mixed signal. Two Cornell CS graduates, class of 2025, who co-founded saiera (an AI course marketplace) and built a generative AI chatbot deployed across 16 Cornell classes. They can build and ship -- that much is clear. But their LinkedIn profiles still list "Founder @ saiera" rather than Sila, and saiera.com remains live. The product is in waitlist mode with no disclosed users, revenue, or engagement metrics. I do not see the markers of definite optimism here -- no articulation of a specific, concrete vision for how workplace collaboration transforms over the next decade, no evidence of the kind of intellectual framework that would distinguish their thesis from every other "AI-native" pitch in the current YC batch. When Zuckerberg sat across from me, he could explain precisely why real-identity networking would monopolize social behavior. When Musk described reusable rockets, he could derive from first principles why the physics worked and the economics followed. What is Sila's equivalent insight? That AI agents should have roles? That is a product feature, not a secret about the world.

The market entry strategy compounds my concern. Sila is targeting the $36 billion team collaboration market with no visible beachhead. There is no equivalent of "Harvard first" -- no narrow, concentrated group of users they can completely own before expanding. The waitlist model suggests controlled rollout, but controlled is not the same as focused. The absence of a specific initial market, combined with the presence of well-funded competitors targeting the same broad category, means Sila is entering the largest possible market on day one and hoping to differentiate through product quality. That is a strategy that requires being 10x better than every alternative, and I see no evidence -- no shipped product, no proprietary technology, no unique data asset -- that supports a claim of order-of-magnitude superiority. The name itself, shared with at least four other companies across battery materials, fintech, real estate, and home services, is a small but telling symptom: even the brand identity begins in competition rather than monopoly.
