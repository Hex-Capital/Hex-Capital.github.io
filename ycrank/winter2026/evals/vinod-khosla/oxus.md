# Oxus -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct about what I see here: Oxus is building a better way to fill out audit paperwork. If they succeed completely — capture every SOX compliance automation dollar available — the consequence is that companies spend less on outsourced auditors. The audit still happens. The same regulatory framework persists. The same control testing occurs. The output is the same workpaper, just drafted by an AI instead of a $500/hour consultant. I have a visceral reaction to this kind of proposition. The founders are clearly smart — three MIT graduates with legitimate finance credentials — and they're spending their careers on making compliance documentation 60% faster. The world doesn't operate differently if Oxus wins. That's the fundamental problem.

The bull case is real and I want to engage with it honestly. AuditBoard was acquired for over $3 billion. The audit software market is growing at 15% CAGR. There's genuine pricing headroom against $300K-$1M+ in annual outsourcing spend. LLMs have genuinely crossed a capability threshold that makes document understanding and workpaper generation technically feasible for the first time — that's legitimate "why now" timing. And the insight that incumbents like AuditBoard and Workiva manage workflow but don't automate the substantive audit work is a real gap observation. If Oxus becomes the category-defining platform for AI-native audit execution, it could be a billion-dollar company. The question I ask is not "can this be a good business?" — it probably can. The question is whether the consequence of that success justifies the effort of exceptional people. A billion-dollar audit automation company doesn't change how the world works. It changes how audit teams allocate their Tuesday afternoons.

The competitive dynamics compound my concern. AuditBoard already touts "AI-driven insights." Workiva has launched "Workiva AI." These are incumbents with thousands of enterprise relationships and the budget to bolt on AI features at zero marginal cost to their existing contracts. The dossier itself acknowledges the commoditization risk: "Any AI-focused startup or incumbent could theoretically build similar document analysis and workpaper generation." When the technology moat is domain-specific prompt engineering and fine-tuning on audit workflows — not a fundamental scientific breakthrough — the incumbents can replicate it. And then there's the channel conflict that the dossier correctly identifies: the Big Four firms that perform outsourced audit work are both the natural sales channel and the entity whose revenue Oxus displaces. You're asking Deloitte to recommend a product that eliminates Deloitte's billings. That structural misalignment makes distribution brutally hard for a three-person team selling to Chief Audit Executives at public companies.

The founding team is competent but fits a pattern I find concerning. Janet Liu interned at the Federal Reserve and worked at D.E. Shaw. Melinda Liu worked at JPMorgan. Christine Watts consulted at BCG. These are precisely the industry-adjacent insiders who know the system well enough to optimize it — and that knowledge constrains them to optimizing rather than replacing. Nobody here is the outsider who doesn't know what's supposed to be impossible. Nobody is pursuing something that Cisco's CTO would mock. They've identified a genuine pain point in a market they understand, and they're building a sensible product to address it. That's good business execution. It is not the kind of bet I make with my own money. The team is also missing a deep audit practitioner — someone who has been a Chief Audit Executive or spent a decade inside Big Four SOX engagements. Finance backgrounds from trading and investment banking are adjacent, not identical, to internal audit domain expertise.

I pass on companies like this regularly. The market exists, the technology works, the team is credible — and the consequence of total success is a modest business in a competitive market where incumbents are already moving. These founders are too talented for a problem this bounded. SOX compliance is defined by statute; you cannot disrupt a regulatory mandate, you can only make compliance with it cheaper. That ceiling — the regulation itself — caps the ambition. I would tell this team: go find the problem in finance that regulators haven't figured out yet, not the one they codified twenty years ago.
