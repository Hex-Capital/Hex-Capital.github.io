# Thirdbrain Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Thirdbrain Labs is that it's building in a space where everyone already agrees there's a market. "Unlocking the next one billion specialized models" is exactly the kind of tagline that gets heads nodding at a demo day. Scale AI is valued at $29 billion. Together AI raised $305 million at a $3.3 billion valuation. Labelbox crossed $100 million in ARR. Snorkel AI just did a $1.3 billion Series D. When I see a two-person pre-seed company entering a space where the incumbents have collectively raised billions and are actively building the exact product described on Thirdbrain Labs' website, the question isn't whether the market is real — everyone knows the market is real. The question is whether these founders see something the $29 billion company doesn't. And from the dossier, I can't find what that something is. The "expert decisions" framing sounds like it could be genuinely novel or it could be standard RLHF preference learning with better marketing copy. Without technical artifacts — no public repos from either founder, no research papers, no open-source contributions, no technical blog posts from CTO David Huang — I have no way to evaluate which.

My framework starts with founders, not markets, so let me look at Margaret Zhang and David Huang directly. Zhang has the kind of resume that looks impressive on paper: NYU Stern, VC fellowship at INVC, investment associate at Temasek, Head of Partnerships at Spice AI, co-founder of Coalition Systems. Huang graduated from UC Berkeley on a Regents' Scholarship, finished in the global top 1% of Two Sigma's Halite AI challenge, and recently completed Stanford's NLP with Deep Learning course. These are credentials. What I don't have are stories. When I backed the Airbnb founders, it wasn't their resumes that convinced me — it was the cereal boxes, the $30,000 in credit card debt, the fact that they'd hand-delivered breakfast to their first hosts. I look for what I call the "animal" quality, and it shows up in anecdotes, not LinkedIn endorsements. The closest signal here is that Zhang traveled to Kyiv to meet Ukraine Ministry of Defence operators for Coalition Systems, which does suggest a willingness to go where the problem is. But one data point isn't a pattern.

The Coalition Systems question actually worries me more than it reassures me. Zhang co-founded a defense AI startup backed by a16z Speedrun, and now she's running Thirdbrain Labs, an ML fine-tuning platform — a completely different domain. The dossier notes that "the relationship between Coalition Systems and Thirdbrain Labs is unclear." Defense AI coordination and post-training model infrastructure don't share an obvious through-line. When Patrick Collison went from Auctomatic to Stripe, both were developer-facing products built by a hacker who wanted to make the web work better — you could see the thread. Here, the thread from Spice AI (web3 data) to Coalition Systems (defense AI) to Thirdbrain Labs (model fine-tuning) looks more like someone scanning for the next wave than someone obsessively pursuing a problem they can't stop thinking about. My framework for organic problem discovery asks: did these founders live in the future and build what was missing, or did they identify an opportunity from the outside? Zhang's background in VC and partnerships, Huang's recent NLP coursework in 2024 — both suggest the latter.

The strongest bull case for Thirdbrain Labs is a market-timing argument: open-source base models from Meta, Mistral, and DeepSeek have gotten good enough that post-training customization is now the bottleneck, and the Meta-Scale AI deal in mid-2025 disrupted the market leader's customer relationships, opening a window. If Thirdbrain Labs has genuinely cracked a workflow that lets domain experts — not ML engineers — continuously steer production models, that's a real product because every hospital, law firm, and manufacturing plant wants custom AI but can't hire enough PhDs. Zhang's GTM experience could be a real asset in enterprise sales, and Huang's competitive programming chops suggest he can build under pressure. For this to work, Thirdbrain Labs needs their "expert decisions" approach to be meaningfully different from what Labelbox, Scale AI, and every open-source RLHF toolkit already offers — and they need that differentiation to hold up as Hugging Face AutoTrain, Axolotl, and LLaMA-Factory keep improving. That's a lot of "needs to" for a company with no public traction, no named customers, and no visible technical artifacts.

When I compare Thirdbrain Labs to the Stripe pattern in my portfolio, the contrast is clarifying. Stripe worked because Patrick Collison was a hacker building payment APIs for hackers, tackling the PCI compliance schlep that repelled every other developer. The schlep was the moat — nobody else wanted to deal with bank integrations and regulatory paperwork. Thirdbrain Labs is building infrastructure for ML practitioners, but the underlying fine-tuning compute is commoditizing fast: AWS Bedrock, Google Vertex AI, and Together AI all offer managed fine-tuning APIs. The "schlep" here — if it exists — is in the UX of capturing domain expertise, not in the infrastructure itself. And UX advantages get copied. Predibase built a compelling LoRA-based fine-tuning product and got acquired by Rubrik for somewhere between $100 million and $500 million — a decent outcome, but not the kind of power-law return that justifies angel risk. That acquisition ceiling is the structural concern for Thirdbrain Labs.

The dimensions that most decisively place Thirdbrain Labs in Pass territory are Organic Problem Discovery and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts. There is no origin story in the dossier — no moment of personal frustration, no "I was trying to fine-tune a model for my robotics lab and everything was broken" narrative. The company's positioning is built around market language ("next one billion specialized models"), not a specific pain that Margaret Zhang or David Huang personally experienced. And the idea doesn't repel anyone — AI model customization infrastructure is the consensus bet of 2025-2026, validated by billions in incumbent capital. My entire Black Swan Farming framework says the best investments initially look wrong to most people. This one looks right to everyone. That's not where the returns are.
