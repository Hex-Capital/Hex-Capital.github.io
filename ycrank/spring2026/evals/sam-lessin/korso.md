# Korso -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Epicor has already launched Prism — an AI agent that automates RFQ workflows inside their own ERP — and that single fact tells you almost everything you need to know about Korso's structural position. When the incumbent with the installed base ships the feature natively, the startup building it as a standalone layer has a distribution problem that no amount of engineering elegance solves. Korso is pitching "ERP-agnostic AI agents for manufacturing operations" into a market where SAP, Oracle, and Epicor are all actively shipping AI agent roadmaps within their existing platforms. The dossier explicitly tags this: Epicor Prism launched in 2026 for exactly the RFQ workflow Korso targets. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about — every time you see a startup doing "AI for [workflow that an incumbent already owns]," the incumbent wakes up one morning and says "fuck yeah, we should add that." Korso is the feature, not the company.

"AI in manufacturing" projected at $34.18B growing to $155.04B by 2030 — that's a consensus number screaming that every LP deck in Silicon Valley has this chart in it. Korso sits in exactly the category where there is zero mispricing. Paperless Parts has raised $45-51M and is adding AI to their CPQ platform. Leverage AI has $14.9M and does PO/RFQ automation for manufacturers. Turian in Berlin is building cross-industry agentic AI for procurement with 17 employees. When four funded competitors already exist and the TAM slide looks this clean, the upside is in the price. Nobody at demo day is going to hear "AI agents for manufacturing" and think "that's crazy" — they're going to think "oh, another one." The pricing is consensus. The risk-reward asymmetry I look for — where the market has dismissed a category and you can buy in cheap — runs in the opposite direction here.

Martin Pan's time at General Motors gives Korso some domain credibility, and the Penn Electric Racing overlap between Pan and Daichi Hiraoka means the co-founders aren't strangers matched by an incubator. But the sanity-insanity ratio is inverted from what excites me. Automating manual RFQ processing and quote generation for manufacturers is an eminently sensible idea — nobody hears that pitch and thinks "what you're doing is fucking crazy." It's obvious. It's the kind of thing every product person leaving Google or Meta would put on their "potential startup ideas" list. The founders are early-career engineers without prior startup experience or enterprise sales backgrounds, pitching a sensible solution to a well-understood problem. Compare this to when I backed the creator ISA concept — investing $1.7M in Marina Mogilko for 5% of her earnings for 30 years. People thought I was a lunatic. That's the ratio I want: a sane investor doing something the market considers insane. Korso is sane people doing a sane thing. Boring.

The bull case deserves construction: ERP systems are genuinely fragmented and disgusting to integrate with. SAP's API is different from Epicor's is different from JobBOSS's, and each customer's instance is customized to hell. An ERP-agnostic layer that works across all of them could theoretically be hard for any single incumbent to build, because each incumbent only optimizes for their own ecosystem. Korso's "tribal knowledge" capture angle — converting institutional pricing knowledge into a data layer — creates real switching costs per account. And the long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing for multi-week manufacturing processes is architecturally non-trivial. If Korso executes perfectly, they become the connective tissue across a fragmented landscape, the way Plaid became the connective tissue across fragmented banking APIs. That's the steel-man. But Plaid was creating a new primitive — a universal API layer for financial data that enabled an entire ecosystem of fintech apps. Korso isn't enabling a new ecosystem. Korso is automating existing quoting workflows. The Plaid analogy breaks at the primitive test. And three first-time founders with no enterprise sales experience need to out-execute Leverage AI ($14.9M raised), Paperless Parts ($45-51M raised), and the ERP vendors themselves — simultaneously. None of my Known Exceptions rescue this: Slack got a pass on consensus pricing because it was creating a genuinely new behavioral primitive; Korso is not creating a new behavior, it's accelerating an existing one.

Korso's structural durability fails the AI kill test I apply systematically. The core capability — LLM-based document parsing, email triage, ERP API integration — uses generally available AI infrastructure. The dossier itself flags this: "The barrier is not the AI model but the ERP integration depth and manufacturing domain specificity." When your own dossier concedes that the AI isn't the moat, and your only moat is integration depth, you're in a race against every other funded team doing the same integrations. Korso has zero visible traction — no public customers, no revenue signals, no user metrics, no press coverage — while competitors have tens of millions in funding and head starts. The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Korso demonstrated that their workflow orchestration engine had become a genuine protocol layer that third-party manufacturing tools built on top of — if other companies started integrating with Korso the way apps integrate with Stripe — that would be a primitive, not a feature. But nothing in the dossier suggests that trajectory.

Consensus Mispricing and Primitive Creation are the two dimensions that lock this verdict. Korso operates in a consensus-hot AI category with multiple funded competitors and active incumbent response — the opposite of the mispricing I hunt for. And automating existing RFQ workflows is incremental improvement, not category creation — Korso will never become a verb. When the market is right about the category and the company isn't creating a new primitive, I don't have an edge, and there's no asymmetry to exploit.
