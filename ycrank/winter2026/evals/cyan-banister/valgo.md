# Valgo -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

These founders wrote the literal textbook. Not metaphorically -- "Algorithms for Validation," MIT Press, forthcoming. Robert Moss, Sydney Katz, and Harrison Delecki didn't just study safety validation for autonomous systems at Stanford; they defined it, teach the course on it, and in Moss's case, spent seven years at MIT Lincoln Lab building the FAA-certified ACAS Xa collision avoidance system that's now a worldwide standard for aircraft. I've seen a lot of deep founder-market fit over 130+ investments, but I've almost never seen a team where the founders' intellectual authority IS the product's moat. Nobody else on the planet could build this specific thing as credibly. That matters. It also creates a tension with how I evaluate founders.

Here's my honest tension: when I sit down with a founder, my first question is always "tell me your story -- not the business." I'm listening for the biographical thread that connects the person to the problem in a way that's inseparable from who they are. With Valgo, the connection is intellectual mastery, not lived hardship. These are Stanford PhDs from the same lab, the same advisor. They arrived at this problem through academic brilliance, not because the system failed them or because they saw something broken in their own lives. I notice that Robert Moss came from Wentworth Institute of Technology before Stanford -- that's not the typical feeder school for a Stanford CS PhD, and I'd want to hear that story. But the dossier doesn't give me the adversity narrative that usually anchors my conviction. This is a team I'd respect enormously, but I'd need to sit across from them and understand what's driving them beyond the elegance of the intellectual puzzle.

What I do find genuinely compelling is the structural position this company occupies. It reminds me of what I noticed with Carta years ago -- there was this invisible, opaque system (cap tables buried in spreadsheets, equity terms nobody understood) and someone built the transparency layer that made it legible. Valgo is doing something structurally similar: insurance pricing for autonomous systems is currently a black box where insurers either refuse coverage or guess wildly because they have zero claims history to work from. Valgo takes simulation data that autonomy companies are already generating and transforms it into something insurers can actually use. That's making an invisible system visible -- translating one world's language into another's. And the infrastructure-at-inflection-point pattern is one I've backed repeatedly, from SpaceX to Crusoe to Anduril. When a major technological shift creates a structural gap that neither side of a market can fill on their own, the intermediary who bridges that gap can become essential infrastructure.

The bull case here is strong enough that I want to be clear about it. If autonomous vehicle and robotics deployment continues accelerating -- and Waymo and autonomous trucking companies are crossing commercial thresholds right now -- then insurers face an urgent, unavoidable problem that their existing tools cannot solve. The team that literally wrote the textbook on safety validation, that built the FAA-certified collision avoidance system, and that teaches this subject at Stanford has a credibility advantage that no competitor can replicate. Koop Technologies has a four-year head start and a Lloyd's partnership, but they're an MGA writing policies directly. Valgo's positioning as the risk quantification infrastructure layer means they could potentially serve Koop AND every other insurer, rather than competing with them. If they nail the go-to-market and get even a handful of major autonomy companies pushing simulation data through their API, the data network effects could create real switching costs. This could be the canonical risk-pricing infrastructure for the entire autonomous systems era.

But I keep coming back to two things that hold me at neutral. First, the dual-sided market adoption problem is real and thorny -- they need autonomy companies to share proprietary simulation data (which is competitively sensitive) AND they need insurers to trust simulation-derived loss estimates (which is culturally foreign to traditional actuarial teams). Neither side has established norms for this workflow. That's not insurmountable technology risk; it's go-to-market complexity of the kind that kills technically brilliant companies. Second, and more fundamental to my framework: I don't feel the jolt. The thing I felt with Niantic, the energy I felt with Crusoe when they described mining Bitcoin on flare gas -- that visceral sense of a world that could exist. Valgo is intellectually impressive, logically sound, and well-timed. But it's a sophisticated B2B infrastructure play for insurers and autonomy companies, not the kind of boundary-breaking weirdness that activates my deepest conviction. The idea that simulation data can replace claims history for insurance pricing is clever, but it's the kind of clever that actuaries and engineers nod along to -- it's not the kind of "that's insane, tell me more" reaction I had when I first heard about SpaceX or Contraline.

I want to be honest: a different investor with a more analytical framework would probably score this higher than I do. The founder-market fit on the technical side is elite. The timing is right. The moat is real. But my process starts with people and stories, and what I have here is academic excellence without the adversity narrative that usually drives my first checks. These founders are building something important, and I wish them well -- I'd just want to have that conversation about how they got here before I know whether this is my kind of bet.
