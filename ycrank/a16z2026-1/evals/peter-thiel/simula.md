# Simula -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The dossier's own competitive landscape table answers the monopoly question before I finish reading it. Koah Labs has raised $26 million. ZeroClick has raised $55 million, led by Ryan Hudson — who built Honey into a $4 billion PayPal acquisition. ChatAds, Aryel (now OneTag), and at least a dozen unnamed teams are all building "the ad network for AI apps." When I can count four funded competitors on a single page of a company brief, there is no secret. There is a consensus opinion — that AI applications need native advertising infrastructure — and multiple well-capitalized teams racing to execute on it. Simula is not building a monopoly. Simula is entering a competition.

The structural problem runs deeper than headcount or capital disadvantage. OpenAI launched its own ad system in February 2026 and reached $100 million in annual recurring revenue within two months. Google has announced plans to bring ads to Gemini this year. The platforms that generate AI conversations are the natural owners of the advertising layer within those conversations — this is not a prediction, it is already happening. Simula's thesis requires that these platforms leave third-party AI app publishers unserved, that those publishers choose Simula's SDK over Koah's or ZeroClick's, and that interactive ad formats (brand ambassadors, sponsored mini-games) outperform simpler contextual placements in a market that historically rewards lower-friction ad units. Each assumption is individually contestable; their conjunction is structurally improbable. The Wish comparison is instructive: a company that entered a competitive market dominated by platforms with superior distribution and zero structural defensibility. Wish IPO'd at $14 billion and lost over 90% of its value — the most expensive lesson in my portfolio about what happens when the monopoly criterion is absent.

Yizhen Zhen's credentials — Dartmouth economics, Google SWE, Bain Capital PE, Harvard Business School — are impressive for someone who graduated college in 2024. But credentials are not conviction. Nothing in the dossier reveals a specific, bold multi-year plan for how Simula becomes the last mover in AI advertising. The self-reported claim of "integrating into AI apps covering 5M+ DAUs" carries the qualifier "at full rollout" — language that signals aspiration rather than accomplished fact. No independent verification was found for any traction metric. The co-founders — Zhen from Dartmouth/HBS/Google and Logan Nicholson from BYU/Talsey AI — share no identifiable prehistory: no common employer, no shared university, no prior venture together. Founders should share a prehistory before they start a company together. Otherwise they are rolling dice on alignment under the extreme pressure that a competitive market will inevitably impose.

The bull case would require believing that Simula's specific format innovation — brand ambassadors and sponsored games versus contextual display or reasoning-time insertion — creates such superior engagement that publishers lock in despite better-funded alternatives. If AI gaming and entertainment apps explode as a category, and if interactive formats prove categorically superior to simpler units, the first SDK to achieve publisher density could build an advertising network effect between demand-side advertisers and supply-side publishers. Unity Ads achieved something like this in mobile gaming. But Unity had years of head start, deep engine integration creating genuine switching costs, and no platform-level competitor building the ad system directly into the game engine. Simula has a 30-minute SDK integration — which means a 30-minute rip-and-replace when Koah offers better eCPMs or OpenAI extends its ad platform to third-party developers. The switching cost that Simula advertises as frictionless onboarding is simultaneously the absence of any moat.

Simula is a pure software layer — an SDK with no hardware component, no physical-world manipulation, no regulatory barrier, no proprietary data asset, and no technical discontinuity. The ad formats it offers (interactive characters, mini-games) are creative product choices, not technological breakthroughs. A well-resourced competitor could replicate them in weeks. ZeroClick's $55 million war chest and Hudson's proven distribution instincts present a more formidable version of the same thesis. Koah's $26 million and 35 million ad impressions with 7.5% CTR represent traction that Simula has not independently demonstrated. The category will produce revenue for someone, but it will not produce a monopoly — and a non-monopoly outcome in a competitive SDK market produces acqui-hire returns, not fund-returners. The power law is unforgiving: a company that could become one of four mid-scale ad networks serving AI publishers does not matter at the portfolio level, regardless of how the category grows.

The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Monopoly Secret and Physical-World Technology Moat. Simula possesses neither a contrarian insight that the market disputes nor a technical barrier that capital cannot replicate. The competitive landscape is not a future risk — it is a present reality, with $81 million already deployed by direct competitors and the platforms themselves moving to own the ad layer. Pass.
