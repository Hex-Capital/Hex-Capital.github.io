# Alchemy -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me first wasn't the idea or the market -- it was Phillip Baek's resume. Biomedical engineering at UT Austin, cancer research at Peking University, EP mapping at Boston Scientific, then a marketing analytics internship at Tropicana, then a Master's in psychological counseling at Columbia, and now an AI bioimage analysis startup. I've read a lot of founder bios, and this one doesn't tell a story -- it's a list of things someone tried. Compare it to Drew Houston, who built file sync tools because he kept forgetting his USB drive, or the Octopart guys who were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic parts. Those are people whose startups grew out of an obsession. Baek's path to microscopy image analysis looks more like someone surveying the landscape for an opportunity than someone who lived inside a problem until they couldn't stand it anymore.

I want to be fair about what's genuinely here. McBride getting a patent at 19 as a UIUC freshman is a real signal -- that kind of early technical output suggests someone with above-average building instinct. And the pairing of biomedical domain knowledge with ML engineering is structurally relevant for this problem. But I don't see evidence of either founder having actually sat in front of CellProfiler at 2am cursing at Python scripts to segment fluorescent images. The dossier explicitly notes that "neither founder has documented prior experience specifically in bioimage analysis software or computational biology research tools." When I ask the "live in the future" question -- were these founders living inside the future of research microscopy before they started the company? -- the honest answer is: probably not. Baek was adjacent, doing EP mapping and cancer research, but adjacent is not the same as immersed.

The idea itself worries me in a different way. "AI-powered image analysis for life sciences" sounds good to everyone. It sounds good to VCs, it sounds good to grant reviewers, it sounds good to TechCrunch. PathAI has raised $255 million. Flywheel has $125 million. Grand View Research publishes reports projecting the AI microscopy market to hit $3.38 billion by 2033. When an idea has analyst projections and nine-figure competitors, the returns have been competed away -- or at least the easy returns have. The specific angle here, targeting research labs with natural language pipeline assembly rather than clinical pathology departments, has some differentiation. But it's the kind of differentiation that any ML team with a couple of biologists could replicate in a few months. The barrier isn't the model -- Cellpose, StarDist, and general vision-language models are freely available. The barrier is supposed to be the curation of domain-specific validated nodes and the researcher UX. That's real work, but it's not the kind of deep regulatory schlep that kept everyone away from payments before Stripe.

The strongest bull case I can construct: research labs are stuck between free-but-painful open-source tools and expensive enterprise platforms bundled with microscope hardware. The structural gap is genuine -- hardware vendors can't easily unbundle, and open-source projects lack resources for productized AI workflows. If Alchemy nails the researcher experience and builds bottom-up adoption lab by lab, they could own this category before Leica or Zeiss respond. The timing is legitimately good -- vision-language models became capable enough for this in 2024-2025, and foundation model costs have dropped enough to make per-image inference economical. For this bull case to work, though, I'd need to believe these specific founders have the kind of obsessive domain understanding that lets them build something researchers love in its crappiest form. I'd need to see labs already using it and unable to go back. The dossier gives me a claim of "90% reduction in analysis time across labs" but no specific customer names, no usage data, no revenue signal, no evidence of anyone using this obsessively. The claim floats without anchor.

What I keep coming back to is the absence of stories. When truly resourceful founders describe what they've done, they generate notable anecdotes almost involuntarily -- the Airbnb guys selling cereal boxes, the Stripe brothers hand-migrating developers' payment code. There's nothing like that here. No prior shipped products, no public GitHub repos, no writing from either founder that would let me evaluate how they think. I can't find the "animal" quality when there's no animal behavior to observe. This might be a fine company that solves a real problem for research labs. But "fine" isn't the bar when I'm investing my own money. I need to see founders who make me think they'll figure it out no matter what goes wrong, and nothing in this dossier gives me that signal.
