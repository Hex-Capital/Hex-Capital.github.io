# 9 Mothers -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about 9 Mothers is the co-founder who isn't the CEO. Roman Khomenko studied computer science at Kharkiv National University of Radioelectronics, served in the Police of Ukraine, and has spent his career in ML and data science. Kharkiv has been under relentless drone bombardment since 2022. When someone from that city decides to build a counter-drone weapon system, I don't need to ask whether the problem is real to them -- their hometown is living it every single day. That biographical thread is exactly the kind of signal I listen for, the kind where the founder and the problem are fused at a molecular level. But here's my hesitation: Roman is the CTO, not the one I'd be hearing the full story from first. Russell Smith, the CEO, is a second-time YC founder whose prior company was a QA testing platform. His path from Rainforest QA to autonomous weapons is not explained by anything in the public record. I'd want to sit across from Russell and understand what pulled him from no-code testing into building systems that shoot down drones. If the answer is "the market is huge," that tells me one thing. If the answer is something I didn't expect, something rooted in a personal reckoning, that tells me something very different.

The acoustic ML angle is where I feel a flicker of that energy I look for -- that sense of something genuinely non-obvious hiding inside what might otherwise look like another defense tech startup. Most counter-drone systems use radar or RF sensing, which are well-understood, detectable, and jammable. 9 Mothers appears to be building around passive acoustic detection, using sound to identify and track drones. That's a legitimately weird technical bet. It's the kind of approach that makes traditional defense engineers uncomfortable, which is often exactly where the advantage lives. Passive detection means you don't broadcast your position. It means you can potentially operate in environments where radar would be contested or compromised. If the ML models are good enough, this could be a meaningful technical moat. But I want to be honest: the technique is replicable with sufficient R&D investment. The moat isn't the idea -- it's whether they can build the proprietary acoustic signature dataset and the field-tested models before someone better-funded decides to chase the same approach.

Here's where I push back on my own excitement: counter-drone is not an unpopular category anymore. Anduril just landed a $20B Army framework contract. DroneShield did A$216 million in revenue last year with 276% growth. Epirus has raised over half a billion dollars. Every VC in defense tech wants exposure to C-UAS. When I backed Anduril, Palmer Luckey was a controversial figure building defense technology that most of Silicon Valley thought was morally distasteful. That was genuinely anti-consensus. Today, building counter-drone systems is about as consensus as it gets in defense investing. The most promising companies are not popular -- and this category is extremely popular right now. The specific niche of low-cost, portable, tactical-edge systems is less crowded than the broader C-UAS space, and the acoustic approach adds genuine differentiation, but I can't pretend this has the same "everyone thinks you're crazy" energy that my best early investments shared.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? First, the acoustic ML approach would need to deliver detection performance that matches or exceeds radar-based systems at a fraction of the cost and form factor -- and that advantage would need to compound through proprietary data as units deploy. Second, the portable tactical-edge positioning would need to be genuinely unserved by Anduril and the primes, creating a wedge into the market that's too small for larger players to prioritize but large enough to build a real business. Third, Roman's ML expertise would need to translate from QA automation into real-time battlefield sensing -- a significant domain leap but not an impossible one given his DSP and data science depth. And fourth, they'd need to navigate the defense procurement labyrinth faster than their cash runway suggests is comfortable. If all four of those hold, you could see a company that becomes the standard-issue counter-drone system for infantry units and forward operating bases -- the thing every squad carries -- while Anduril serves the big integrated platforms. That's a real outcome and a real company. The team has the technical depth to attempt it, and the 14 active job postings with $150K-$250K salaries suggest they're building seriously.

The co-founder relationship is the strongest structural signal here. Russell and Roman worked together for eight years at Rainforest QA. Eight years. After watching HQ Trivia implode because two co-founders couldn't resolve their disagreements -- the right product at the right time destroyed by founder conflict -- I pay close attention to whether co-founders have actually been through difficult seasons together. Building and scaling a company over nearly a decade is exactly that kind of testing ground. They've survived crises, disagreements, and the daily grind together already. Bogdan Pyzh is the question mark -- his security consulting background at Leviathan is relevant, but there's no visible prior connection to Russell or Roman. Three co-founders with unclear alignment on one-third of the team is something I'd probe directly.

I land in a genuinely mixed place on this one. The co-founder bond between Russell and Roman, the acoustic ML weirdness, the hard regulatory space, and Roman's biographical connection to the problem are all real signals. But the category has become consensus, the CEO's personal stake in the problem is opaque, and there's no evidence of traction -- no revenue, no LOIs, no field tests, no named military partners. The economic access dimension that runs through my best investments -- Uber creating income for drivers, Affirm bringing transparency to credit -- doesn't really apply here. This is a company selling to governments, not creating opportunity for underserved populations. It's a legitimate company in a legitimate market, but it doesn't make me want to drop everything and write a check today.
