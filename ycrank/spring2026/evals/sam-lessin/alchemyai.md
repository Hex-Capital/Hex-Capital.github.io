# Alchemy -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Alchemy's dossier states, in plain text, that its core technical challenge "relies on prompt engineering and model orchestration rather than proprietary model architectures." I almost don't need to write the rest of this memo. That sentence is the AI kill test, pre-failed. Leica already has Aivia with 22 built-in applications and 20 pre-trained deep learning models sitting inside a distribution network that reaches every institutional microscopy lab on the planet. Adding a natural-language interface to Aivia is a product sprint, not a startup. PathAI has $490M in funding and FDA approval for AISight Dx. Proscia has 16 of the top 20 pharma companies as customers and is growing diagnostic cases 400% year-over-year. Alchemy is bringing a prompt-engineering wrapper to a knife fight against companies with billion-dollar distribution moats. Every time I see this pattern, I hear the same thing in my head: this is perfect for Leica.

AI plus life sciences is the consensus-hottest category in venture right now. Alchemy is not mispriced — it's priced exactly where the market puts every AI-for-biotech company in a top accelerator batch. There's no discomfort here, no category that makes sophisticated investors squirm. Nobody hears "AI microscopy analysis" and thinks "that's insane, pass." They think "oh, that's smart, lots of TAM." Which means the upside is already in the price. Compare this to Solana at four cents, when every crypto person told me Ethereum had won. Compare it to Eaze, when every mainstream VC wouldn't touch cannabis. Alchemy has the opposite dynamic — it's in a category VCs love, solving a problem everyone agrees exists, using technology everyone agrees works. There's no asymmetry. The risk-reward ratio is symmetric and boring.

Phillip Baek holds a BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin and an M.Ed. in Psychological Counseling from Columbia — relevant but not deep domain. Neither Baek nor Liam McBride has published research in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis. No Google Scholar results, no PubMed results. McBride's strongest prior is BoBo, a shared-living app that raised $20K in non-dilutive funding. These are early-career founders building in a space where their target customers — research PIs with decades of domain expertise — will immediately ask "who are you and why should I trust your pipeline over CellProfiler, which the Broad Institute maintains for free?" The sanity-insanity ratio doesn't activate here because the idea isn't insane. Automating tedious microscopy analysis is the most obvious possible application of computer vision to biology. It's a sensible idea from sensible people, and sensible-sensible is the combination that bores me.

Let me construct the bull case honestly. Alchemy targets a genuine gap: enterprise incumbents like PathAI and Proscia focus on clinical diagnostics, not general research microscopy. Academic researchers are stuck with CellProfiler and ImageJ, tools that require scripting and parameter tuning. A UC Santa Cruz PhD student reported Alchemy reduced H. pylori analysis from 3 hours to under 10 minutes. If Alchemy achieves bottom-up PLG in academic labs before incumbents notice, publication citations could create real switching costs. The potential data network effect — researchers sharing trained models — could compound over time. Maybe Alchemy becomes the GitHub of microscopy pipelines. The Clubhouse exception in my framework says I'll override durability concerns when I'm witnessing the birth of a genuine new primitive. But Alchemy doesn't meet that bar. Microscopy image analysis already exists as a behavior. Alchemy makes it faster, not new. There's no verb being created here. Nobody will "Alchemy" their images the way people "Venmo" each other money. And the Clubhouse exception literally failed — the incumbents replicated it and the company collapsed, which is exactly what would happen if Leica adds an LLM layer to Aivia.

Alchemy's two-person team is lean, and the absence of revenue data at pre-seed is a stage default I won't penalize. Capital discipline gets the benefit of the doubt. But capital discipline doesn't matter when the underlying business is a feature masquerading as a company. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Alchemy demonstrated a proprietary foundation model for microscopy — not orchestration of existing models, but a genuinely novel architecture trained on data no one else has — and had five paying institutional labs with annual contracts. That would shift both the durability and mispricing calculations. Without it, Alchemy is the exact bet I pass on every batch: an AI wrapper in a consensus-hot category where the value accrues to the platform, not the application layer.

Structural Durability and Consensus Mispricing are the two dimensions that lock this into Pass. Alchemy scores near the bottom of both because it combines the worst of two patterns I avoid: a pure-software tool with no moat AI can't commoditize, operating in a category where every investor already agrees the opportunity exists. The price reflects the upside. The moat doesn't survive Leica's next product cycle.
