# Sila -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Sila is the competitive landscape it's walking into. Glue raised $20M in October 2025 with near-identical positioning -- "agentic team chat" -- and is co-founded by David Sacks, who brings both operator credibility from PayPal and distribution reach across the entire Silicon Valley ecosystem. Dust.tt already has $7.3M ARR and $21.5M raised. Slack shipped Agentforce. Microsoft has Copilot embedded in Teams. Notion is adding agents. Glean has $200M ARR and $768M in funding. This is a two-person pre-product team entering a market where the category has already been named, funded, and partially captured by competitors with 10-100x their resources. When I look at markets, I'm looking for the non-obvious opportunity that other investors dismiss as too boring, too niche, or too small. "Agentic team communication" is the opposite of non-obvious -- it's on the cover of every enterprise software publication. Every B2B-focused fund has seen this thesis. That's the market structure that produces commoditized competition, not asymmetric returns.

The "why now" is real but consensus. LLM capabilities crossed a threshold for reliable agent participation in multi-turn conversations. MCP standardized tool integration. Remote work made messaging platforms mission-critical. These are genuine structural shifts. But when I invested in Harvey, legal AI looked like a science project to most investors -- the structural moment was visible only if you were tracking GPT scaling curves. Here, the structural moment is visible to everyone. Glue's pitch deck probably has the same "why now" slide. So does Slack's product roadmap. When the inflection point is obvious, the question shifts from "is there a market?" to "who has the distribution to capture it?" -- and a two-person team with no shipped product, no disclosed users, and no distribution strategy does not have a compelling answer to that question.

The bull case requires you to believe that Slack and Teams are structurally unable to rebuild their architectures around AI agents -- that the retrofitting problem is so deep it creates a genuine architectural gap that only greenfield entrants can fill. There's some logic here. Slack generates revenue per seat; making AI agents first-class participants could cannibalize that model. Teams is bundled with Microsoft 365, creating incentive to upsell Copilot rather than rethink the communication layer. If "AI-native messaging" turns out to be as categorical a shift as Slack was over email and HipChat, a greenfield platform could capture generational value. But this argument applies equally to Glue, which has a 12-month head start, $20M, and far more distribution firepower. The question isn't whether the architectural gap exists -- it's whether Sila specifically can exploit it, and I see no evidence of a distribution strategy, a unique technical approach, or a wedge that differentiates them from the other entrants pursuing the identical thesis.

The founders have shipped AI products -- a chatbot deployed across 16 Cornell classes and an AI course marketplace -- which shows they can build and deliver. That's not nothing at pre-seed. But both are fresh Cornell CS graduates (Class of 2025) with no enterprise experience, and their LinkedIn profiles still list saiera rather than Sila as of the research date. Building an AI chatbot for university courses is meaningfully different from building enterprise communication infrastructure that competes with Salesforce and Microsoft. The single-miracle test is concerning: to succeed, Sila needs to (1) build a product that matches or exceeds what Glue and Dust have already shipped, (2) convince teams to abandon Slack or Teams -- overcoming massive switching costs rooted in conversation history, integrations, and organizational habit -- and (3) make AI agents reliable enough for enterprise-grade communication. That's three miracles compounding, not one. When I see a plan that requires sequentially overcoming multiple low-probability obstacles, I pass.

The product-to-distribution trajectory has theoretical potential -- messaging platforms are among the strongest distribution channels in enterprise software, which is why Slack's app marketplace became so valuable and why Microsoft bundles Teams with 365. If Sila became the communication layer for a team, it could distribute workflow automation, analytics, and vertical-specific agent tools through that same surface. But this is entirely speculative at the pre-product stage, and the founders haven't articulated any multi-product vision in public materials. The category structure allows for platform expansion, but "allows for" is different from "is positioned for." Without evidence of distribution thinking from day one, this is a product company, not a platform company.
