# Sherpa -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Optimizely generates $400M in ARR selling manual A/B testing tools. Sherpa's bet is that an AI agent can automate that entire loop — identify drop-off points, generate variants, run tests, deploy winners — and that this automation represents a venture-scale opportunity. I've seen this pitch structure hundreds of times. Replace "A/B testing" with "recruiting" or "accounting" or "legal review" and you get the same slide deck: large TAM, underserved by technology, AI automates 40% of the workflow, 15-20% efficiency gains. Sherpa claims customers see 15-20% lift on their primary conversion metric within 30 days. That's useful. That's also exactly the kind of incremental improvement I pass on. If Sherpa fully succeeds — captures the entire $2.8B projected CRO market by 2032 — you get a successful SaaS company. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic human need becomes affordable. The world looks the same, except marketing teams are slightly smaller and websites convert slightly better. When I write a check, I need to believe the consequence of success is that the world is visibly different. Sherpa doesn't clear that bar.

Sherpa's core technical approach — using LLMs to generate page variants and run automated experiments — relies on what the dossier correctly identifies as "generally available foundation models." The barriers to replication are low at the technology layer. This is a wrapper around existing capabilities, not a technical discontinuity. Compare this to Rocket Lab building electric turbopump engines with 3D-printed combustion chambers — that was a non-linear approach to cost reduction that nobody else had. Sherpa's "single line of code" integration and LLM-powered variant generation is something Optimizely's engineering team could ship as a feature in a quarter. Intellimize already pursued ML-driven optimization and got acquired by Webflow for eight figures — not even nine — which tells you the market's ceiling for this capability as a standalone product. When I back a company, I need a technology wedge that creates an unfair advantage. Sherpa's 4.3 million website visits of learning data is a start, but it's not the kind of proprietary data moat that compounds into something unreplicable.

Ethan Kinnan and Norbu Sonam both came from Lindy, and their flagship case study — 40 experiments in 30 days yielding +30% total conversion lift — is from Lindy, their former employer. The bull case here is real: these are young founders (both graduated 2024) who moved fast, built a product, got into YC, and generated visible traction. Ethan's claim that he "automated every job he's ever had" shows the right instinct. Norbu's Microsoft Copilot and IBM internships give relevant AI engineering experience. But I don't see evidence of the rapid adaptation and first-principles reasoning that separates a learning machine from a competent executor. The product does what you'd expect given the available tools — applies LLMs to a known workflow. There's no pivot story, no "here's what surprised us," no evidence of the trajectory of capability growth I look for in founders. They're following a visible playbook, not reasoning from first principles about what shouldn't exist.

The strongest argument for Sherpa is the expansion path: start with CRO, extend to the entire digital marketing stack, become the autonomous marketing department. If Sherpa's agent eventually optimizes acquisition funnels end-to-end across the $7 trillion digital commerce ecosystem, the consequence magnitude grows. The 260 LinkedIn reactions and Everett Butler's testimonial about shipping "dozens of tests a week" suggest genuine product-market pull. And the timing is legitimate — LLM quality crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 that makes autonomous variant generation credible, while incumbent consolidation (VWO/AB Tasty merger, Intellimize/Webflow acquisition) creates distraction. I considered whether the DoorDash exception applies — we funded DoorDash despite no technical novelty because the consequence magnitude was planetary-scale. But DoorDash was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people. Sherpa is optimizing website conversion rates. These consequences are not in the same universe. No known exception in my framework overrides the fundamental mismatch: Sherpa is redistributing existing digital commerce spend, not creating new demand or making a basic human need free.

Sherpa's ambition level is squarely consensus. "Turn more website visitors into customers" — no informed observer would say "that can't be done." Every CRO incumbent is already pursuing AI-powered optimization. Sherpa is racing to automate a known process faster than Optimizely, VWO, and Mutiny ($72.6M raised) can add the same capability. That's an execution race, not a technology bet. I prefer technology risk to market risk, and Sherpa has the inverse — the technology is straightforward, and the risk is entirely about outrunning well-funded incumbents with existing customer relationships. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: if Sherpa demonstrated a proprietary technical capability — a novel model architecture, a unique data asset, a feedback loop that produces qualitatively different outputs from anything built on the same foundation models — that created a genuine 10x discontinuity rather than an automation of existing workflows. Without that, this is a 3-5x return bet at best, which in my framework is a bet not worth making.

The dimensions that lock this verdict are consequence magnitude and non-linear technical approach. Sherpa's consequence of full success is a profitable company in a $3B market — useful, not transformative. Sherpa's technology is available to every competitor with API access to the same foundation models. I don't invest in execution races for incremental outcomes. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Sherpa is designed for the middle.
