# Superset -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Superset is what's missing from the story: the specific moment of pain. Three ex-YC CTOs who were building developer tools -- they would have been running multiple AI coding agents. Probably frustrated by the workflow. But the pitch doesn't start there. It starts with a market observation: "every major AI coding tool shipped multi-agent capabilities in the same two-week window." That's a TechCrunch headline, not a personal frustration. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, he didn't lead with "the cloud storage market is projected to reach $X billion by 2030." He led with: I keep losing my files. The absence of a clear organic origin story isn't proof that one doesn't exist -- these founders probably did experience this pain -- but the fact that they frame it as a market timing play rather than a personal problem tells me something about how they think about it.

The schlep factor here is what concerns me most. Compare this to Stripe. The reason thousands of programmers knew payment processing was broken but nobody built Stripe was that the work was genuinely terrifying -- banking relationships, fraud systems, PCI compliance, money transmission licenses across 50 states. That schlep created a moat so deep that even after Stripe succeeded, few competitors could follow. What's the schlep in running multiple terminal sessions with Git worktree isolation? It's an Electron app with a diff viewer and some process management. That's real engineering work, but it's not the kind of ugly, regulatory, institutional complexity that causes smart people to unconsciously look away. Any competent team could reproduce the core functionality in a few sprints. And more importantly, the platforms themselves are already doing it -- Claude Code shipped Agent Teams the same month Superset launched. When the upstream platform can absorb your entire value proposition with a single feature release, you don't have a schlep moat. You have a timing advantage, and timing advantages evaporate.

The team itself is genuinely strong on the technical dimension, and I want to give that its due. Three co-founders who are all ex-CTOs, all builders. Kiet Ho built Onlook to 23.4k GitHub stars and number one trending on GitHub -- that's not luck, that's someone who knows how to ship developer tools that other developers actually want. The product is already at version 1.1.4 with 1,864 commits. These aren't people who write pitch decks; they write code. If I were scoring purely on "technical hacker founders who build," this team would score near the top. But there's an uncomfortable pattern here too: Kiet Ho and Avi Peltz both left their W25 companies -- just one batch ago -- to start this. Onlook is still active with 23.4k stars. That's not the Emmett Shear pattern, where a company died and the founders came back hardened. That's founders who left something working to chase something newer. Maybe they saw a bigger opportunity. Or maybe they gravitate toward what's trending.

The bull case is real and I want to engage with it seriously. If AI coding agents become the primary way software gets written -- and the evidence suggests they will -- then the developer who runs ten agents in parallel needs a control plane. Superset is positioning itself as Switzerland: agent-agnostic, editor-agnostic, local-first, open-source. That's the right positioning if you believe the agent landscape will stay fragmented, because no single agent vendor will build great orchestration for their competitors' agents. The 6,500 GitHub stars and claimed adoption at Amazon, Google, DoorDash, and Vercel suggest real pull. If Superset becomes what Docker became for containers -- the neutral orchestration layer that everyone adopts because it works with everything -- then the opportunity is enormous. For this bull case to materialize, two things have to remain true: the agent landscape has to stay fragmented (no single agent wins decisively), and native multi-agent features from individual providers have to remain inferior to a dedicated orchestrator. Both are plausible. Neither is certain.

But my actual read is that this is a consensus idea in the hottest category in tech, and that makes me wary. I said recently that the most impressive companies I've seen are not working on AI. That's not because AI isn't important -- it's because when every smart person in Silicon Valley is building in the same space, the competition is brutal and the edge cases multiply. Superset is competing against Cursor ($29.3 billion valuation, $2 billion-plus ARR), Anthropic's native Agent Teams, GitHub Copilot Workspace, and a dozen other well-funded teams all racing to solve the same problem. The product being free and open-source with no revenue model means they're fighting these giants without a business model -- which is fine for growth but means there's zero evidence that anyone will pay. The companies that win in developer tools usually find a way to be so essential that monetization becomes obvious. Right now, Superset is useful but not essential, because the problem it solves can be solved five other ways.

These are good builders in a real space with real early traction. But the idea reads more like opportunity recognition than organic discovery, the schlep is low enough that the platforms are already absorbing the functionality, and the competitive landscape is among the most crowded I've seen at any stage. I'd want to see evidence that this team has found something structurally defensible -- a workflow insight, a data advantage, an integration depth -- that the Cursors and Anthropics of the world can't replicate with a two-month sprint from their existing teams.
