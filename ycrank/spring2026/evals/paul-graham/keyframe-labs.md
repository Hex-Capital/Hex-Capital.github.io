# Keyframe Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Keyframe Labs isn't the product or the market — it's the pricing claim. $0.06 a minute when Tavus charges $0.32 and D-ID charges $0.56. That's not a marketing discount. You don't get 5-6x cheaper through better sales tactics or slimmer margins. That kind of cost difference, if real, implies a fundamentally different model architecture — something structural about how they do inference. It's the kind of advantage that, in my experience, only comes from someone who spent years inside the problem and figured out an approach everyone else missed. And in this case, that's exactly Parth Radia's story: seven years building photorealistic digital humans at AT&T Labs and Ericsson, including leading a 30-person team that built foundation models for voice and video synthesis on what Ericsson called a "database of humanity." He didn't read about this market in a trend report. He built the first generation of it inside a telecom giant and then left to build the version the corporation couldn't.

This is one of the strongest organic problem discovery stories I've seen in a pre-seed company. Parth's career reads like someone drawn to the same problem since his first research position — 3D telepresence at AT&T, digital human synthesis at Ericsson, now real-time video avatars at Keyframe. That's not trend-chasing. That's obsession. And Kaahan's background in real-time perception at Zipline — building camera-based detect-and-avoid for autonomous drones — is precisely the kind of complementary skill you'd design if you could choose your co-founder's resume. The sibling dynamic means they've been communicating since childhood, which eliminates the co-founder communication risk that kills so many startups. Technically, this founding team is about as well-matched to this specific problem as any I've encountered.

But here's where my framework gets uncomfortable. This idea doesn't repel anyone. It attracts everyone. Tavus just raised $40M from CRV and Sequoia. HeyGen is at $95M ARR. Synthesia has a $4B valuation and real-time avatars on their roadmap. Lemon Slice raised $10.5M in seed funding for nearly the same product. That's five well-funded competitors in a space that TechCrunch writes enthusiastic trend pieces about. When I look for investments, I want the opposite: problems that make smart founders say "ugh, no thanks." The schlep that repels competitors is the moat. AI video avatars are the anti-schlep — they're technically glamorous, visually impressive at demos, and easy to pitch to investors. Everyone's here already. The question is whether Keyframe's cost advantage is durable enough to function as a moat in the way that Stripe's willingness to wade through PCI compliance and bank integration spaghetti served as theirs. I'm not sure cost efficiency alone provides the same barrier. Architectural insights can be reverse-engineered or leapfrogged when foundation models improve, and they're improving fast.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If the proliferation of voice AI agents continues — and everything suggests it will — every deployed voice agent eventually wants a face. Keyframe's API-first approach (`await persona.connect()`) means they could become the Stripe of this layer: the infrastructure that voice agent developers reach for because it's cheap, fast, and trivially easy to integrate. At $0.06/min, Keyframe can serve high-volume use cases (enterprise training, healthcare check-ins, always-on customer support) that competitors have priced themselves out of. If Parth's seven years of architectural knowledge translate into a sustained cost curve advantage — the way TSMC's process knowledge compounds into an increasingly wide moat — then the pricing gap could widen rather than narrow as they optimize further. And Synthesia's channel conflict is real: their existing high-margin per-seat L&D business creates genuine organizational resistance to cannibalizing it with commodity API minutes. That's the classic innovator's dilemma opening.

What concerns me is the gap between founder credentials and visible traction. Eight anonymous testimonials, zero GitHub stars, no named customers, no revenue data, LinkedIn posts with 30-40 likes. For someone who spent seven years building exactly this, with deep industry relationships in the video synthesis space, I'd expect more signal that real users are obsessively using even a crappy early version. The Persona-1 to Persona-1.5-live iteration in three months is encouraging — it shows they're shipping and learning — but I can't see the user feedback loop driving that iteration. Are they building because users are pulling them forward, or because the technology is what they know how to build? In my experience, the best technical founders sometimes fall into the trap of optimizing the model when they should be optimizing distribution. I want to see the story of the first customer who used this so much they broke it. That story isn't in the dossier, and its absence is a mild negative because founders sitting on genuine product-market fit tend to radiate it.

This is a legitimately talented team with deep domain expertise building in a real and growing market. The pricing advantage is the most interesting signal. But the competitive landscape is too crowded for my taste — this isn't a bet on seeing what others can't see, it's a bet on executing better than well-funded competitors in a race everyone can see. I'd want to talk to Parth about specific customer stories, about what he learned at Ericsson that competitors don't know, and about whether the cost advantage comes from insights that compound or optimizations that can be replicated. Right now, the founder-market fit pulls me forward while the market consensus pulls me back.
