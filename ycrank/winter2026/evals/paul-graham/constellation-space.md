# Constellation Space -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is how Kamran Majid found this problem. He wasn't reading analyst reports about satellite constellations scaling to 70,000 nodes and thinking "that's a big TAM." He was at SpaceX, writing the simulation algorithms that prioritize low-latency ground assets for Starlink. He built the optimization toolset for the ground network. Then he worked at NASA on the GDC mission, building data engineering tools for predictive analyses. He was inside the satellite operations machine for years, and he saw exactly where it breaks. This is the kind of organic discovery I look for -- the idea didn't emerge from a brainstorming session, it grew from daily frustration with a system the founder personally built and operated.

The schlep here is genuinely ugly, and that's actually what makes me interested. Building physics-informed neural networks that combine ITU atmospheric propagation standards, orbital mechanics, and space weather models into a real-time prediction system handling 100,000 telemetry messages per second -- and then selling that to defense customers with 18-month procurement cycles -- is precisely the kind of problem that causes smart engineers to unconsciously look away. Thousands of engineers at SpaceX, Blue Origin, OneWeb, and Kuiper know that reactive monitoring degrades at constellation scale. They've all seen link failures cascade. But the path from "I see this problem" to "I'll build a company to solve it" requires wading through atmospheric physics, orbital propagation math, federated learning across security-classified boundaries, and government sales. That's a schlep of Stripe-like proportions. Every programmer knew payment processing was broken for a decade before the Collisons actually walked into the banks. Here, every satellite engineer knows ground-based orchestration is breaking, but nobody wants to deal with the physics-regulatory-defense tangle required to fix it.

The technical team is genuinely strong on paper. All four founders can build. Kamran coded SpaceX's ground network tools. Raaid wrote embedded flight software for Blue Origin's New Glenn. Omeed has published ML research and has working code on GitHub. Laith brings ECE depth from UT Austin. This isn't a team that needs to hire engineers to build a prototype -- they've already built a system ingesting 100K messages per second. The architecture choices (federated learning so operators don't share raw telemetry, physics-constrained neural networks rather than pure statistical ML) reflect genuine domain craft, not just stitching APIs together. I also like that all four founders overlapped at Capital One before this -- co-founder relationships that predate the startup are a structural requirement for me, not a nice-to-have. In one YC batch, nine companies that added co-founders they didn't know between interview and start all fell apart within a year. These founders knew each other.

What concerns me is the absence of evidence that anyone is pulling this product toward them. "Design partner phase with defense and commercial operators" with no names disclosed is the weakest form of traction signal. An NVIDIA partnership is a technology relationship, not a customer. I want to see what I saw with Airbnb's early hosts in NYC -- people who genuinely need this, who would suffer real economic harm if it disappeared. At pre-seed I don't expect revenue, but I expect at least one named operator who's using the system and would be upset without it. The defense/government sales cycle is also structurally at odds with my growth framework. I look for 5-7% weekly growth as the heartbeat of a working startup. Companies that sell to defense agencies on 18-month procurement cycles don't produce that signal for years. That doesn't mean the company is bad -- it means my standard measurement tools don't work well here.

The bull case is strong enough to keep me interested. If the federated learning architecture works across multiple constellation operators, it could create a data network effect that becomes genuinely defensible -- each new operator's models improve everyone else's predictions without exposing proprietary telemetry. That's the kind of compounding advantage I've seen in the best infrastructure companies. The timing is also right: Starlink operates thousands of satellites, Kuiper is deploying, OneWeb and Telesat Lightspeed are launching. For the first time, there are multiple large-scale commercial constellation operators who need vendor-neutral management tools. Five years ago this market barely existed. Five years from now, the incumbents might have caught up. The founders acknowledged honest limitations in their Launch HN post -- prediction accuracy degrading beyond 5 minutes, limited labeled data for rare failures -- which I read as a positive signal about intellectual honesty rather than a negative one about the technology.

Where I come down is that this company passes my first two tests decisively (organic discovery, real schlep) and my fourth test strongly (technical founders who build), but fails my third test (no visible evidence of user pull) and doesn't give me enough signal on my second (relentlessly resourceful -- impressive resumes, but no evidence yet of creative adaptation under startup pressure). The Kamran-at-SpaceX origin story is one of the more compelling organic discovery signals I've seen in this batch. But I've learned that organic discovery and technical depth are necessary conditions, not sufficient ones. I need to see at least one customer whose operations depend on this system before I write a personal check. This is a company I'd watch closely and revisit after their seed round, when the design partner relationships have either converted to real contracts or haven't.
