# Dayjob -- Paul Graham Evaluation

$450K in ARR seven months after first sale, at pre-seed, in skip hire scheduling. That number is the headline, and it earns the right to everything else I'm about to say about Dayjob. Most YC companies at this stage are showing me a prototype and a handful of design partners. George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks are showing me paying customers who measurably increased jobs per driver hour by 5-10% (Coastal Recycling testimonial). Revenue at this velocity is not something you can fake or buy with discounts in a market as operationally conservative as UK waste management. These are fleet operators who hate switching costs and distrust software vendors — if Dayjob is extracting $450K+ from them, the product is solving a problem that genuinely hurts.

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo and launched their grocery business in the UK — meaning he lived inside the operational mechanics of fleet scheduling, driver management, and real-time route changes. Fred Fooks ran supply chain analytics at Deloitte. But the real signal isn't their résumés; it's the sequence. Dayjob didn't start as Dayjob. They built Gaea first — a waste management ERP that generated $150K in revenue — and through eighteen months of selling ERP software to waste operators, they discovered that the actual bleeding wound was scheduling. Planners losing 65 hours weekly to spreadsheet logistics. So they pivoted in January 2025 and built the specific tool those operators were begging for. This is as close to "live in the future, then build what's missing" as you get in industrial software. They didn't identify a market opportunity in waste management; they waded into waste management, got their boots dirty with Gaea, and the scheduling problem found them. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive; George and Fred watched transport planners drown in spreadsheets. The origin structure is the same.

Dayjob's schlep factor is severe, and that's what I find most compelling about the competitive landscape. Skip hire scheduling — where you're juggling delivery stacking constraints, skip sizes, driver certifications, cancellations, and three different legacy ERP systems (Weighsoft, PurGo, Wastelogics) — is exactly the kind of domain that makes every smart engineer at a hackathon say "why would you work on that?" Routific and OptimoRoute optimize delivery routes in general. AMCS, the $225M-funded incumbent, bundles route optimization inside a full-stack ERP and faces a business model conflict in unbundling it. Dayjob occupies the gap: domain-specific enough to handle skip hire constraints that general tools ignore, but ERP-agnostic enough that operators don't have to rip out their existing systems. The VWS/PurGo co-marketing partnership announced in January 2026 is evidence this positioning works — an ERP vendor chose to partner with Dayjob rather than build scheduling natively. This parallel to Stripe is structural, not cosmetic: Stripe plugged into existing web stacks rather than replacing them, and the integration depth became the moat. Dayjob's three named ERP integrations are embryonic, but the pattern is correct.

The bear case is real, and I need to name it. Neither founder has public GitHub repos. Fred's background is Deloitte consulting. George's is sales and operations. Dayjob's team of five has a lead engineer (Joe Walton), but the founders themselves pattern-match more to smart operators who identified an underserved vertical than to obsessive hackers who couldn't stop building. I'm usually skeptical of this profile — my best bets have been on people who code compulsively and happen to find a business, not on people who analyze markets and happen to find a product. The UK skip hire market is also genuinely narrow; expansion to general waste collection, recycling, or continental Europe requires new ERP integrations and regulatory knowledge each time. And AMCS could decide tomorrow to invest in AI-driven real-time scheduling as a feature upgrade, which would pressure Dayjob's differentiation within AMCS's installed base. These are not hypothetical risks. But the $450K+ ARR overrides my pattern-matching concerns about founder archetype the same way ClassDojo overrode my "hackers building for hackers" preference — when the schlep is deep enough and the execution is this crisp, the competitive moat self-assembles because no one else wants to do the work. Stuart Pearce calling Dayjob "best route-planning software I've used in 10 years" is the kind of obsessive early-user signal I weight heavily.

Dayjob's idea doesn't truly repel before it attracts — no one hears "AI scheduling for skip hire" and says "that will never work." They say "that sounds boring," which is different. Boring repels founders, not investors. The Black Swan test requires that the idea seem wrong, not just unsexy. Dayjob seems obviously right once you hear about it, and "obviously right" usually means the returns have been competed away — except that in waste management, the competitive field is so thin that "obviously right" ideas can still be greenfield. The learning rate evidence is strong: from Gaea's $150K to Dayjob's $450K+ ARR in roughly a year of focused execution, with visible product evolution from generic waste ERP to specialized scheduling agent. That trajectory suggests founders who absorb customer feedback fast and redirect their energy without sentimentality.

The condition that would flip me from Invest to Pass: if Dayjob's net revenue retention drops below 100% in the next two quarters — meaning early customers churn or downsell — it would signal that the $450K ARR reflects initial enthusiasm rather than operational dependency. Conversely, what locks me in is the organic discovery dimension: the Gaea-to-Dayjob pipeline is not something you can manufacture. Eighteen months of building waste management software, discovering the real pain point, and pivoting into it with immediate traction — that sequence is the strongest evidence in this dossier, and it's the dimension where Dayjob scores farthest above the pre-seed default.
