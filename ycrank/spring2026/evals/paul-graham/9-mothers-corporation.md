# 9 Mothers -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Russell Smith built Rainforest QA from YC S12 through twelve years to $25.7M in revenue and $41.2M raised. That's the fact I anchor on before anything else about 9 Mothers. Most founders who come through YC once and build a real company — not a acqui-hire, not a pivot-to-consulting, but an actual revenue-generating business — learn something that's almost impossible to teach. They learn what it feels like when growth stalls and you have to grind through it. Smith did that for a decade. And now he's back with his Staff Data Scientist of eight years, Roman Khomenko, to build autonomous weapon turrets. The career leap from QA testing software to kinetic counter-drone systems is so jarring it almost demands an explanation — and the explanation, I suspect, lives in Khomenko's biography. He's from Kharkiv. His master's degree is from Kharkiv National University. Kharkiv has been under systematic drone bombardment since 2022. When your co-founder's hometown is being destroyed by the exact threat category your product addresses, you don't need a McKinsey slide deck to explain why you're building this.

Khomenko's technical credentials are precisely calibrated for 9 Mothers' problem: first place in the iMaterialist Kaggle challenge, production experience with PyTorch, YOLO, and OpenCV at Rainforest QA, where he built CV/ML pipelines and managed 50,000+ crowdsourced testers. Detecting and tracking a fast-moving FPV drone against a cluttered background is fundamentally a real-time computer vision problem running on edge compute — which is a compressed version of what Khomenko spent eight years doing at scale. Smith, meanwhile, lists ESP32 and Rust alongside his web languages on GitHub, with 212 repos and a prolific building habit that includes embedded systems work. These two didn't find each other on a co-founder matching platform. They built software together for eight years, and now they're applying the same working relationship to hardware that shoots drones out of the sky. The Smith-Khomenko pairing reminds me of the Stripe dynamic — I'd tracked Patrick Collison since Auctomatic, and what made my $15K check easy was the combination of proven shipping ability and a co-founder relationship that had already survived real pressure. 9 Mothers has that same skeletal structure: repeat founder plus deeply technical co-founder, working relationship battle-tested over years, attacking a schlep that makes polished founders recoil.

9 Mothers is building autonomous kinetic weapon systems — and that sentence alone is enough to repel 95% of YC-caliber founders. ITAR compliance, security clearances, DoD Directive 3000.09 on autonomous engagement authority, multi-year procurement cycles, the moral weight of building things that kill. This is schlep blindness territory. Most founders' unconscious minds won't even let them see this opportunity, which is exactly why I find it worth examining. The parallel I keep returning to is Boom Supersonic, where I made my largest angel investment ever in a pre-revenue aerospace company because the ambition scale and the "important for America" conviction overrode my usual heuristics about capital efficiency. 9 Mothers operates in the same register — point defense for American soldiers and allied forces against the drone swarm threat that Ukraine proved is real. The difference is that 9 Mothers' form factor play (small, portable, low-cost turret vs. Anduril's $60B enterprise platform or Epirus's $550M+ directed-energy systems) potentially creates a distinct market segment rather than competing head-to-head with the primes. A platoon doesn't need a Lattice-integrated network; it needs something a soldier can mount on a vehicle.

The bear case against 9 Mothers is formidable, and I'd be dishonest not to wrestle with it. Anduril has a $20B Army framework contract and $2B in revenue. Epirus has raised $550M+. Fortem Technologies has $28.5M in revenue and a strategic investment from Lockheed Martin. 9 Mothers' founders have zero defense industry experience — they come from QA software. Two of three founders have international backgrounds (Ukrainian), which may complicate the ITAR and security clearance process that defense hardware absolutely requires. The website returns a 403 error. There's no evidence of DoD contracts, LOIs, revenue, or even a working prototype beyond job postings and a LinkedIn announcement with 153 reactions. Hiring "government relations professionals" signals they know they need defense relationships but don't have them yet. The autonomous weapons policy landscape (DoD Directive 3000.09) adds regulatory risk specifically to kinetic systems that non-kinetic competitors like D-Fend Solutions ($73M raised, cyber/RF takeover approach) simply don't face. If 9 Mothers can't navigate clearance timelines and procurement bureaucracy, the technical quality of EDDA becomes irrelevant.

The bull case survives that assault, though, because of three specific facts. First, Smith already proved he can build and scale a company through YC — Rainforest QA's $25.7M revenue isn't a vanity metric, it's twelve years of selling to real customers, which means he understands the grind of enterprise sales cycles that defense procurement resembles at compressed scale. Second, Khomenko's ML stack (YOLO, PyTorch, OpenCV, edge computing) maps directly onto the core technical challenge of real-time drone detection and tracking — this isn't a generic AI team hoping the technology transfers. Third, the 14 open job postings at $150K-$250K salary ranges across AI, mechatronics, electrical, and systems engineering suggest 9 Mothers has raised meaningful capital and is building hardware, not just pitching it. What flips me to Pass would be evidence that the clearance complications are blocking product delivery, or that Anduril's CUAS subsidiary is actively targeting the small-form-factor segment with comparable unit economics. What would push me higher would be a confirmed DoD rapid acquisition pathway — a DIU contract or OTA agreement — demonstrating that the procurement barrier is being actively navigated.

The dimension where 9 Mothers scores highest is the repulsive quality of the idea itself — not the C-UAS market, which is consensus hot, but the specific act of building an autonomous kinetic weapon system as a startup. The dimension where it's weakest is the absence of evidence for rapid iteration and learning rate on the current product — I have career trajectories but no sprint-level data. The deciding factors for my score are founder quality (Smith's repeat-founder track record, Khomenko's elite ML credentials, their eight-year working relationship) and schlep magnitude (autonomous weapons as a category most founders can't even look at). Those two signals, combined, push 9 Mothers into territory where I'd write a modest check from my own money — not because I'm confident in the outcome, but because the founder-schlep combination creates the asymmetric return profile I look for. The gap in domain expertise is real but not disqualifying; the founders who built Stripe had no payments industry experience either.
