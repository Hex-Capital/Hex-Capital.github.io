# Alike -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Max Van Kleek has 9,012 Google Scholar citations and 25 years at MIT, PARC, Nokia, IBM, and Oxford researching human-AI interaction and privacy-preserving systems. That's not a resume — that's the kind of specific knowledge accumulation that takes a competitor decades to replicate. But here's where Alike breaks from the pattern that generates my highest conviction: Max is still a full-time Associate Professor at Oxford. A principal's identity is fused with the company. A CTO who maintains a tenured position at a university is structurally an agent — his downside is capped by the professorship he can return to, which means his skin in the game is fundamentally different from a founder who burned the boats. When I backed Travis at Uber or Aravind at Perplexity, there was no safety net. The dual commitment doesn't negate Max's knowledge — it dilutes the signal that this knowledge has been fully committed to productization.

The specific knowledge audit reveals a second gap. Addi Haran Diman completed a PhD in social data science at Oxford by age 21, studying group behavior and social networks — intellectually remarkable, and the domain is adjacent to coordination automation. But "studied how groups coordinate" is different from "built coordination tools for years and couldn't stop." Addi and Danial Hussain served as consecutive Oxford Students' Union Presidents — they understand how to organize humans at scale, which is relevant. But the path from Oxford student politics to "agent collaboration layer for enterprises" looks more like a market opportunity identified through proximity to AI research (via Max) than a problem the founders were already solving for themselves. Compare this to Ev Williams, who had already built Blogger and lived inside the public communication problem before Twitter. Alike's founders have relevant academic knowledge, but the company isn't the inevitable product of their combined life's work — it's an application of their knowledge to a hot category.

The leverage audit is where Alike's structural challenge becomes clear. Enterprise coordination automation should be pure code leverage — zero marginal cost per team, data flywheel that compounds as the system learns patterns. But three forces erode this architecture. First, Google's A2A protocol, now under Linux Foundation governance with 50+ partners including Salesforce, SAP, and Workday, commoditizes the agent-to-agent interoperability layer for free. Second, Slack is positioning as an "agentic OS" with 30 new AI agent features and auto-provisioning for all Salesforce customers — the incumbent owns the distribution and is building the feature natively. Third, Dust at $7.3M ARR with $21.5M from Sequoia and CrewAI with $18M from Insight Partners have massive head starts in overlapping territory. Alike's differentiation — coordination intelligence rather than agent orchestration or knowledge retrieval — is real but thin. The strongest structural argument in Alike's favor mirrors the one I made about Perplexity and Google: Slack's business model depends on message volume, so autonomous coordination that reduces messaging cannibalizes their core metric. Slack's team has the capability to build this but is agent to its own P&L. That principal-agent dynamic at the incumbent is the one structural opening I find genuinely compelling.

What concerns me most is the consensus signal. Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025. Every enterprise AI investor is salivating over agent coordination. You get paid for being right first, and to be first you can't wait for consensus — but this market has already reached consensus. Alike isn't contrarian; it's riding the most-discussed enterprise AI wave of 2026. The 20 design partners and 100-company waitlist from word of mouth suggest real pull, and named partners like Exus Renewables ($1B backing) and K-ID ($51M raised) are credible enterprises. But all traction is self-reported via the a16z Speedrun portal with no revenue, no pricing, and no independent verification. At pre-seed this is normal — what's less normal is the absence of any public-facing distribution (no Product Hunt, no company Twitter, no LinkedIn page, no app store listing). For a product replacing Slack-style coordination, the go-to-market surface area is remarkably small.

The bull case requires three things to be true simultaneously: Max's privacy-preserving coordination intelligence is genuinely novel research IP that cannot be replicated by Slack, Google, or well-funded competitors in 18 months; Max transitions from part-time academic to full-time CTO before the competitive window closes; and the coordination layer proves to be a standalone product category rather than a feature absorbed by incumbents. If all three hold, Alike's learned coordination patterns create real switching costs, the privacy architecture differentiates in regulated industries, and the Slack principal-agent conflict creates a structural opening that persists. Max's depth in privacy-preserving systems specifically could be the wedge — enterprise customers in healthcare, finance, and legal need coordination automation they can trust with sensitive communications, and a professor who has published extensively on privacy-preserving AI has more credibility here than any competitor's marketing page.

Alike lands in neutral territory primarily on two dimensions. The specific knowledge is real but incompletely productized — Max's research points toward this product but hasn't been forged into it through years of building, and the CTO's dual commitment undermines the principal signal that would elevate my conviction. The leverage architecture faces commoditization pressure from Google A2A and incumbent feature absorption that narrows the window for a standalone coordination layer. A team of four with a part-time CTO competing against Dust ($21.5M), CrewAI ($18M), and Slack's native agent features needs extraordinary specific knowledge to survive — and "relevant academic research" is a tier below "already built the underlying systems."
