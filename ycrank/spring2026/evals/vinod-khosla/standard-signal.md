# Standard Signal -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$100,000 minimum investment. That number tells me everything about Standard Signal's ambition before I read another word. When I backed Impossible Foods, the end-state was protein cheaper than animal agriculture — feeding billions. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, the end-state was energy too cheap to meter. Standard Signal's end-state is better risk-adjusted returns for accredited investors who can write six-figure checks. If Standard Signal achieves everything Michael Royzen dreams of — fully autonomous AI trading, dominant market-neutral performance, hundreds of billions in AUM — the world has a very profitable hedge fund. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic need becomes cheaper. No previously excluded population gains access to something they couldn't afford. The $5.7 trillion global hedge fund TAM that Standard Signal cites is existing capital being reallocated between managers, not new value being created. This is redistribution of existing financial spend, and my portfolio is built on the opposite: creation of entirely new demand.

Michael Royzen is the most compelling element of Standard Signal, and I want to be explicit about that. Second-time YC founder. Built Phind to 150 million searches. Started coding at age 12, featured by Wharton as a teenager, UT Austin Turing Scholar. The man has a demonstrated learning rate — he went from NLP research to production AI search at scale, and now he's entering quantitative finance with zero documented experience in the domain. That's exactly the outsider pattern I've backed repeatedly: Pete Beck building rockets without an aerospace degree, the Mach Industries kid building weapons systems at 19. Royzen's willingness to jump domains signals first-principles thinking over credential-following. His rate of learning across Phind's lifecycle — training what he calls "the best-performing coding LLMs of 2023-2024" — is real evidence, not a resume bullet. If he were applying this learning rate to a problem with planetary-scale consequences, I'd be writing a different evaluation. But he's applying it to making wealthy limited partners wealthier.

Standard Signal's technical approach — proprietary financial LLMs with chain-of-thought reasoning, 12ms execution latency, 2,847+ trading pairs — sounds like a genuine shift from Renaissance Technologies' statistical methods to LLM-based autonomous reasoning. The question is whether this represents a 10x discontinuity or a 2x improvement. The dossier reveals the answer: open-source AI hedge fund frameworks already exist on GitHub, Numerai manages $550 million in AUM using crowdsourced AI signals with 25.45% net returns in 2024, and Earthian AI is positioning as an "AI-native hedge fund" with autonomous reasoning models. Standard Signal is not the only team that noticed LLMs can analyze financial data. When multiple well-funded competitors are pursuing the same approach, the technology discontinuity has already been commoditized into an execution race. Renaissance Technologies, Two Sigma, and Citadel have massive data infrastructure and can hire the same caliber of ML engineers Royzen is recruiting. No structural barrier — not regulatory, not technological, not network-effect — prevents incumbents from adopting LLM-based trading. Standard Signal's moat depends on proprietary training data and accumulated track record, both of which take years to establish and can be replicated by firms with orders of magnitude more capital.

The bull case an advocate would make: Royzen is a rare second-time founder who has already built and shipped production LLMs at scale, entering a $5.7 trillion market dominated by firms using 20-year-old statistical methods. The DoorDash precedent applies — I backed Tony Xu's food delivery company despite it having no technical moat because the consequence magnitude of transforming food access was enormous and the founder was a high-velocity outsider. Standard Signal's Royzen fits the outsider-founder archetype even better than Xu, with deeper technical credentials. If LLM-based reasoning genuinely generates persistent alpha that statistical methods cannot, Standard Signal could become the next Renaissance — and at a $100K minimum, it's already more accessible than Medallion Fund's permanently closed doors. The advocate would argue that financial democratization follows performance: first prove the AI works, then lower minimums. I've dismantled this bull case in three words: consequence of success. DoorDash's success meant hundreds of millions of people could access restaurant food they previously couldn't. Standard Signal's success means a subset of accredited investors earn higher returns. The DoorDash exception required planetary-scale human impact to override technical novelty concerns. Standard Signal's impact ceiling is a reshuffling of who captures alpha in financial markets — a zero-sum game among the already-wealthy. The exception does not apply.

Standard Signal's unaudited Sharpe ratio claim of 2.0+ with no disclosed time period, no third-party verification, and no AUM data is precisely the kind of financial projection I've warned about for twenty years. Spreadsheets are fiction. A hedge fund's entire pitch IS a spreadsheet — the value proposition is literally a number on a return report. Every dimension of Standard Signal's success or failure reduces to financial performance metrics, which makes it structurally misaligned with how I evaluate companies. I don't calculate IRR. I don't evaluate Sharpe ratios. I ask whether the world looks different if this company succeeds. Royzen's ambition — autonomous AI trading — is shared by Numerai, Rebellion Research, Earthian AI, and every quantitative fund hiring ML engineers in 2026. No informed observer would call this improbable. They'd call it crowded.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Standard Signal pivoted to making institutional-quality investment management free or near-free for ordinary people — an AI wealth manager with no minimum, no fees, and performance matching top hedge funds — the consequence magnitude would shift from "better returns for the rich" to "democratized financial access for billions." That's the Curai Health model applied to wealth management, and it would activate my affordability thesis. As currently structured, Standard Signal is a hedge fund. A potentially good hedge fund. But a hedge fund is a bet on making money, not on changing the world, and I have never been interested in the former without the latter.
