# InLoop Robotics -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

InLoop Robotics is a frontier-hardware swing wrapped around a consensus category with no tiebreaker -- a 4-person team out of Karlsruhe and TUM pitching bimanual arms-as-a-service in a lane where Dexterity already sits at a $1.65B valuation with $291M in the bank (TechCrunch Mar 2025). The highest-load dimension for InLoop Robotics is that the category itself -- hardware-heavy RaaS in warehouses -- is exactly the unsexy, capital-intensive lane I look for. The lowest is governance: three co-founders, zero CEO designation in the dossier, and the cleanest precedent I have for that configuration is a portfolio failure I still wince about.

**Anduril (2017) is the high rung on my hardware-swing ladder** because Palmer Luckey was shipping defense hardware into a category that VCs called morally and operationally uninvestable, and the moat compounded with every shipped unit. InLoop Robotics is *of that family* on category alone -- bimanual arms, monthly subscription, no facility redesigns, "every failure becomes training data" per the YC page. Where InLoop Robotics diverges from the Anduril rung is the entry conditions: Palmer arrived with Oculus already sold to Facebook for $2B and a defensible reason no one else could build what he was building. InLoop Robotics arrives with $500K of YC paper against Dexterity's $291M and Symbotic's $22.5B backlog (SEC 8-K FY2025), and the technical bet -- general bimanual manipulation across hundreds of SKUs -- is the same bet Covariant made for ~8 years before getting acqui-hired by Amazon at $380M, below their prior $625M valuation. The category is right. The capital position and the timing are not.

**HQ Trivia (2017) is the failure I keep on my desk** because Yusupov and Kroll could not name a tiebreaker, and when the product-market thrill wore off the partnership ate itself in 2020-21. InLoop Robotics has three co-founders -- Sharfeddine, Feduniak, Rizali -- and the dossier does not name one as CEO, does not allocate operating authority, and lists Rizali on LinkedIn as "Aspiring Robotics Engineer | Computer Science Student." That is not a slight; it is a fact about who is going to make the call when the Bosch-trained ML engineer wants to ship an imperfect policy into a customer warehouse and the TUM-trained roboticist wants to wait. The HQ Trivia pattern was tactile virality strong enough to mask the governance crack until the product matured. InLoop Robotics does not have HQ Trivia's viral pull to buy time; it has paid pilots and a Safety Module, which means the unresolved-CEO question gets tested by month 9, not month 30.

**Warehouse robotics is the most-talked-about hard-tech category of 2024-2026**, which is the failure mode of the "magically weird" filter compounding the wrong way. Dexterity is at $1.65B, Pickle Robot pulled $87M in November 2024, Covariant got pulled into Amazon, Symbotic is public with a $22.5B backlog -- this is the inverse of Crusoe (~2018) burning flares for Bitcoin while sophisticated VCs laughed. Over the next 18 months, InLoop Robotics will be priced *against* the Dexterity comp, not against a blank category, which means the round terms compress and the data flywheel needs to outrun four better-capitalized teams chasing the same generalist-manipulation foundation models. "Find them before it becomes consensus" -- the InLoop Robotics dossier shows we are six to eight years past that point on warehouse manipulation.

**The lived-experience read on InLoop Robotics is the one place I would let a counterfactual change my mind.** Feduniak doing robot-learning research from age 18 is a real obsession signal, and Sharfeddine's Bosch/BMW ML tenure is genuine industrial exposure -- not McKinsey-deck exposure. What would have to be true to push InLoop Robotics from Pass to Invest on this axis: one of these founders worked a peak-volume warehouse shift, ran a 3PL operations floor, or shipped a manipulation policy into production at Bosch that survived contact with a real factory. The dossier does not show that. It shows ML researchers and a student-robotics initiative (RoboTUM), which is the academic adjacency, not the lived adjacency. The Palmer Luckey origin is shipping; the John Hanke origin is a decade of operating Keyhole. The InLoop Robotics origin is a thesis assembled at TUM.

**Niantic (~2015) is the parallel I want to make work on physical verifiability, and it almost does.** I played Ingress for three years before I wrote that check; I could *feel* the product in my hands and on my city blocks. InLoop Robotics has actual hardware in actual warehouses, multiple deployed robots with zero breakage per the company site, 300+ picks/hour per station -- that is more tactile than 90% of the YC software dossiers I read. But the Niantic test is whether *I* can install, ride, or photograph the product within 48 hours, and InLoop Robotics's arms live inside customer facilities I cannot walk into. The artifact is real; the public surface is invisible. That gap is the difference between a Flock Safety hotel-Wi-Fi serendipity and a deck description.

**My 36-month forecast on InLoop Robotics**: acqui-hired by Dexterity or a 3PL integrator for under $60M after stalling around $2-4M ARR, or pivoted to a teleoperation-services SaaS layer (selling the Safety Module to other robotics companies) after a difficult Series A. The mechanism is the signature -- right category, wrong configuration, wrong moment in the consensus cycle. Three co-founders without a tiebreaker burn six months on a strategy disagreement when Dexterity drops the price of a comparable RaaS station; the $500K runway hits a wall against $291M; the Covariant precedent gets repeated at a smaller dollar figure.

**The bull case that stings is Feduniak from age 18.** A founder who has been obsessed with robot failure since adolescence is exactly the kind of polymath signal that overrode my pedigree concern on DeepMind -- Hassabis was chess prodigy, neuroscientist, game designer compounding across domains, and the pedigree turned out to be a wrapper around an obsession. If Feduniak is the InLoop Robotics analog, and foundation models for manipulation really did cross a generalization threshold in the last 18 months (Nvidia Isaac/Inception backing is the weak version of this claim), then InLoop Robotics could be the team that ships the first one-policy-many-SKUs production deployment while Dexterity is still raising. What dismantles it is the dossier's own data: the moat as described -- "every failure becomes training data" -- is the *model* improving, not the founders compounding, and that is the exact pattern from my pass example where a polymath generalist could rebuild it in a quarter. The one verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a signed multi-station expansion contract from a non-Amazon mid-market 3PL (Saddle Creek, NFI, a regional fulfillment operator) at a stated monthly subscription price, paired with a public CEO designation among the three founders. Either alone is not enough.
