# Taiga -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Candid Health has raised $99.5 million. AKASA has raised $205 million. LunaBill, from YC's own Fall 2025 batch, already reports $764K in contracted ARR. Taiga enters a market where multiple well-capitalized teams with demonstrated traction are building AI-powered medical billing — and Taiga has no public revenue, no disclosed customers, and two founders who appear to be recent university graduates. The consensus investor sees "AI + healthcare billing" and gets excited because the TAM slide says $163 billion. I see the opposite signal. When every fund in the market is already backing this category — AI healthcare startups captured 62% of all digital health venture funding in H1 2025 — the contrarian premium has evaporated. You don't get paid for being right when everyone else is already making the same bet.

Nanda Guntupalli and Adam Wax grew up watching their physician parents struggle with billing. That's real. They've been friends since third grade — not a co-founder matching event, not a startup weekend pairing. The relationship predates the company by fifteen-plus years. Taiga's origin story passes my principal-agent filter cleanly: these founders aren't building billing software because they read a market report; they're building it because they sat at dinner tables where denied claims and coding errors were a source of genuine family stress. That's a principal's motivation, not an agent's. But here's the distinction I keep coming back to: motivation is not specific knowledge. Watching your parent lose revenue to denied claims gives you the emotional drive to solve the problem. It does not give you the payer-specific denial pattern expertise, the CMS coding edge cases, or the appeals workflow knowledge that would take a competitor years to replicate. Taiga's founders have the why. They don't yet have the non-trainable expertise that IS the company.

Taiga's leverage architecture concerns me more than anything in the dossier. The company positions itself as a "full-service agency" — the provider uploads visit data and Taiga handles everything downstream. Agency is labor leverage wearing a software mask. Every agency I've evaluated faces the same structural problem: as you add clients, you add operational overhead. Taiga's AI generates ICD-10 and CPT codes from clinical notes, but the physician must still review and approve. Taiga automates denial appeals, but someone must manage the exceptions. Compare this to Notion — pure permissionless leverage, zero marginal cost per user, scales to millions with a small team. Taiga's agency model is the opposite architecture. And the AI layer itself — parsing clinical notes into billing codes using foundation models — is explicitly reproducible. The dossier states it directly: "LLM-based medical coding and claims scrubbing are technically reproducible" and "core AI capabilities rely on foundation models accessible to any well-funded competitor." This is the AI wrapper pattern. Taiga is renting leverage from OpenAI or Anthropic, not building its own. The moment the foundation model provider ships a healthcare billing feature, or Candid Health trains on ten thousand times more claims data, Taiga's technical differentiation approaches zero.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Taiga targets approximately 200,000 physician-owned practices that large incumbents like Waystar ($8B valuation) and R1 RCM structurally cannot serve profitably — their cost structures are built around enterprise contracts. Taiga could own this underserved segment the way Gumroad owned independent creators that Shopify's enterprise motion ignored. Two technical co-founders, both CS-trained, building with AI-first economics could achieve unit economics impossible for incumbent billing agencies running human-staffed operations. The seven named EHR integrations — Athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, AdvancedMD, Tebra, Elation, Epic — create real switching costs once a practice is live. And the data flywheel is real in theory: every processed claim across every payer and specialty makes Taiga's denial-prediction models better, potentially creating a compounding advantage over time. If Taiga can reach a few hundred practices before competitors lock up the segment, the switching costs and data accumulation could compound into a genuine moat. I checked my Known Exceptions — none apply here. Taiga doesn't create a physically impossible-to-replicate capability like Varda, and the agency model doesn't generate marketplace network effects that could override absent specific knowledge the way Wish's marketplace briefly did. The bull case requires too many things to go right simultaneously: win the segment before Candid Health moves downmarket, build data advantages before foundation models commoditize the AI layer, and scale an agency without proportional headcount growth.

Taiga's access democratization angle is genuine but incremental — giving small practices sophisticated billing management previously available only to hospital systems with enterprise RCM contracts. AngelList unlocked something structurally new: retail investors accessing startup deals previously restricted to connected insiders. Taiga makes an existing service cheaper and more accessible to smaller buyers. That's a real business. It's not the structural access expansion that generates my highest conviction. The two-person team size is appropriate for pre-seed, and both founders can build, but Taiga's agency model raises a question no amount of AI can fully resolve: can Taiga reach meaningful revenue — say $10M ARR — with fewer than fifteen people? The service component of full-stack billing management may require customer success, onboarding specialists, and compliance staff that push the headcount denominator higher than a pure software play.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if Taiga showed me that their denial-prediction engine, trained on their parents' practices and a handful of early customers, already outperforms Candid Health's models on small-practice claim types — measurable, auditable, with specific payer-rejection-rate data. That would transform "childhood exposure" into genuine specific knowledge productized through proprietary data. Without it, Taiga is two motivated builders entering a well-funded market with reproducible technology and an agency model that fights against the leverage curve. The principal authenticity is the strongest signal here — 13/20 — but authenticity without specific knowledge is passion without a moat. The specific knowledge gap and the agency-model leverage architecture are the two dimensions that lock this evaluation into Neutral.
