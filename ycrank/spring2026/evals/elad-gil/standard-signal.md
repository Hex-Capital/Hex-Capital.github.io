# Standard Signal -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Standard Signal is structured as an LP hedge fund, not a software company. That single fact reshapes every dimension of my evaluation framework. Michael Royzen is deploying proprietary LLMs to trade 2,847+ pairs at 12ms latency with a claimed Sharpe ratio of 2.0+ — and I have no doubt the technical capability is genuine given his track record building production LLMs at Phind. But a hedge fund monetizes through management and performance fees on LP capital, not through software margins, not through platform switching costs, not through compounding infrastructure lock-in. Standard Signal is an end-user application of AI technology, not a layer that other businesses build on. When I invested in Stripe, I was backing payments infrastructure that thousands of companies would embed into their core workflows. Standard Signal sits at the opposite end of the stack — it consumes foundation models, market data feeds, and execution infrastructure that it doesn't control. If I'm choosing where to invest in the AI-finance value chain, I'd back the infrastructure layer (the data pipes, the compliance tooling, the risk management APIs) before I'd back a single fund using that infrastructure.

"AI-autonomous hedge fund" is not a nonobvious market — it's the most obvious application of AI that anyone in finance can articulate. Renaissance Technologies ($130B AUM), Two Sigma, and Citadel have been deploying machine learning to trading for over a decade. The dossier itself notes "no structural barrier (regulatory, business model cannibalization) prevents incumbents from adopting similar approaches." Open-source AI hedge fund frameworks exist on GitHub. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors — there was a genuine structural shift that consensus had missed. Standard Signal enters a market where every major quant fund is already racing to integrate LLMs, where the application of AI to trading is front-page consensus, and where well-capitalized incumbents have massive data advantages, decades of track records, and first pick of quantitative talent. The LLM capability threshold crossing from 2024-2025 is real, but it's fully priced into the competitive landscape. Standard Signal doesn't benefit from a nonobvious window — it's arriving after the window has been thrown wide open.

Michael Royzen's technical credentials are serious. Phind processed 150M+ searches, grew 5-10% week-over-week in DAUs, and Royzen built what he calls "the best-performing coding LLMs of 2023-2024." He's been shipping production AI systems since age 12. But Standard Signal requires him to simultaneously crack at least three independent problems: (1) generating persistent alpha from LLM-based trading strategies in a market where thousands of quantitative researchers at well-funded firms are pursuing the same approach, (2) building an institutional LP fundraising operation from scratch with no documented hedge fund, portfolio management, or quantitative finance experience, and (3) navigating SEC registration, Investment Advisers Act compliance, LP reporting obligations, and novel regulatory questions around AI-autonomous fiduciary duty — all as a solo founder with no compliance team or legal counsel publicly listed. That's three miracles, compounding odds. Each one alone would be a reasonable bet for a talented founder; together they push the probability to a level I can't underwrite. Compare Harvey, where the technology miracle was happening exogenously on the foundation model curve, and law firms were already proven technology buyers — one miracle, already underway. Standard Signal needs to build the alpha-generating technology AND the institutional distribution AND the regulatory infrastructure simultaneously.

$100,000 minimum LP investment and a presumed 2-and-20 fee structure means Standard Signal's revenue is fundamentally performance-dependent. If the models generate alpha, the 20% performance fee creates excellent unit economics. If alpha decays — and the dossier flags commoditization risk explicitly — LPs redeem, AUM drops, and revenue disappears. There are no switching costs, no compounding lock-in, no negative churn dynamics. LP "retention" is entirely a function of returns relative to benchmarks. This is the structural opposite of the high-margin, negative-churn SaaS businesses I look for. The claimed 2.0+ Sharpe ratio has no disclosed time period, no third-party auditor, and no context — institutional LPs require 12-24+ months of audited track records before allocating, which means Standard Signal faces a multi-year cold-start problem on fundraising regardless of actual performance.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Royzen is a proven LLM builder who demonstrated at Phind that he can train, deploy, and scale production models. The hedge fund industry entered 2026 with the highest inflows in almost two decades. Inference costs dropped 10-100x over 2023-2025, making continuous autonomous trading across thousands of pairs economically viable for the first time. If Standard Signal's proprietary models genuinely generate persistent alpha, the $5.7T global hedge fund TAM and the 9.12% CAGR growth create enormous scaling potential. And Numerai's $500M valuation on $550M AUM with 25.45% net returns in 2024 proves the market can reward AI-native fund structures. I've checked my known exceptions: the Anduril override applies when an overwhelming structural shift justifies multi-miracle risk, and the BioAge override applies when deep personal domain expertise overrides sector avoidance. Neither applies here — AI trading is consensus, not a generational structural opening, and Royzen's expertise is in NLP/search, not quantitative finance. The condition that would flip me to Invest: Standard Signal publishes a 12-month audited track record with consistent alpha and simultaneously hires a senior team member with institutional quant fund experience (someone who's been a PM or risk officer at a top-20 fund). That would collapse two of the three miracles into execution challenges rather than existential unknowns.

Standard Signal scores lowest on the two dimensions I weight most heavily: nonobvious market timing and infrastructure-layer positioning. The market is consensus, the positioning is anti-infrastructure, the miracle count is three or more, and the founder's genuine technical talent is deployed in a domain where he has no documented experience. Royzen may prove me wrong — talented builders sometimes do — but my framework is designed to identify structural advantages, and Standard Signal has structural headwinds on every axis I measure.
