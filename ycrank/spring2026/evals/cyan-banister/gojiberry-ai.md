# Gojiberry AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that stopped me cold before I even got to the founders' stories: the company was listed for sale on TrustMRR for $11.15 million on March 9th -- seven days after their Product Hunt launch, while they're sitting inside a YC batch. I've seen a lot of things in this business, but I've never seen a founder simultaneously pitch investors for growth capital and post a "for sale" sign in the window. When I backed SpaceX, when I wrote that first angel check, the thing that moved me wasn't the rocket science -- it was that Elon would have died on that launch pad before he sold. The founders who build enduring companies don't hedge like this. I don't know the full story behind the listing, and maybe there's a reasonable explanation, but it fundamentally undermines the conviction signal I need to see before I write a check with my own money.

When I try to hear the founder story here -- the story before the business -- I get competent French serial entrepreneurs who've bootstrapped and sold software companies. Pierre-Eliott made money in e-commerce as a student, Roman ran a Web3 game, Dylan built an attendance SaaS to 600K users and sold it. These are real accomplishments. But nobody on this team woke up at 3am burning to fix how small sales teams find warm leads. They pivoted to this product after their first idea -- a CRM auto-fill assistant -- attracted a 2,000-person waitlist and zero paying customers. That's a classic pattern of founders who are skilled at finding product-market fit through iteration, which is a genuine talent. But it's not the same thing as a founder whose biography makes them the only person who could build this specific company. When I met the Contraline founder, his years of academic research in male contraception made the connection between person and problem unmistakable. Here, three capable operators found a vein of demand and started mining it. The question is whether they'll keep mining when the ground shifts.

And the ground will shift, because this is the most consensus category I've seen in years. AI sales outreach tools are what everyone is building right now. Apollo just acquired Pocus for signal-based selling. Clay hit $100M ARR. Instantly, Lemlist, HeyReach -- bootstrapped competitors everywhere you look, all growing fast, all in adjacent territory. The best investments I've made looked ridiculous to other people at the time. Uber was illegal. SpaceX was considered delusional. Crusoe was mining Bitcoin on gas flares and people thought we'd lost our minds. Gojiberry AI is doing something that hundreds of well-funded teams are also doing, in a category that every VC in Silicon Valley considers obviously valuable. That's the opposite of the signal I'm trained to follow. When something is popular and easy to assess, the price is wrong and the moat doesn't exist yet.

I want to be fair about the bull case, because the execution here is genuinely striking. Growing from zero to $112K-$175K MRR with 1,000+ paying customers on $380 a month in marketing spend -- that's extraordinary capital efficiency. The 75% profit margin at this scale is rare. Five thousand cold emails a day, 800 LinkedIn DMs a week, five YouTube videos a week, all documented. If what you're investing in is pure execution horsepower, this team delivers. The argument would be that in a land-grab market, the fastest executor with the best data flywheel wins, and Gojiberry's compounding customer data from 1,000+ accounts creates a learning loop that new entrants can't replicate from scratch. The $99/mo price point structurally locks out enterprise incumbents who can't serve SMBs profitably. If these founders stay committed and the data advantage compounds, you could see a real outcome here. But "if these founders stay committed" is carrying a lot of weight in that sentence, especially when they've listed the company for sale.

There's no economic access story that moves me here. Helping small sales teams send more personalized LinkedIn messages is useful, and democratizing intent data from $25K/year down to $99/month is a real service. But this doesn't create economic opportunity for people who lack it -- it optimizes the workflow of people who already have jobs and computers and LinkedIn accounts. Compare that to Uber creating income streams for drivers, or Affirm giving transparent credit access to people locked out of the financial system. The impact here is incremental productivity, not structural access. And there's no regulatory moat to navigate -- LinkedIn platform dependency is a vulnerability, not a complexity advantage. If LinkedIn changes its terms of service tomorrow, the core product breaks.

Two of the three co-founders built and sold CoCo AI together, which is a real positive signal -- I always feel better when founders have survived a full company lifecycle before starting the next one. Dylan's track record with Edusign adds genuine technical credibility. The complementary skill split across CEO, CMO, and CTO is clean. But I keep coming back to that sale listing. It tells me something about how these founders think about this company -- as an asset to be optimized and flipped, not as a mission to be pursued through crisis. My entire framework depends on backing people who won't quit when things get hard. The long journey requires founders who are in it for reasons that go deeper than the economics.
