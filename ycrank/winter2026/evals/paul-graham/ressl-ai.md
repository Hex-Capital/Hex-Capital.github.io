# Ressl AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is how this idea was found. The YC description says it plainly: "We started as an AI consulting firm talking to businesses across every industry. After a month, the pattern was obvious — trades and field services had the most 'glue work.'" That's a consulting engagement that surfaced a market opportunity. It's not the worst origin story — they were at least inside real businesses observing real workflows, not reading market reports from a WeWork. But it's not the origin story that produces conviction. Drew Houston didn't survey people about file sync frustrations. The Collisons didn't interview businesses about payment pain and then decide to build Stripe. The strongest founders stumble into problems because those problems are woven into their own lives. Arushi Gandhi's prior company built CRMs for VC and PE firms. Neither founder has ever run an HVAC shop, managed a collision repair bay, or chased down a parts vendor. They observed the schlep from the outside. Observing is not the same as living.

The schlep itself, though — that part is real. Insurance coordination, CCCOne integration, vendor procurement, QuickBooks reconciliation across fragmented legacy stacks with no standardized APIs. This is ugly, tedious work that most AI startups would instinctively avoid in favor of cleaner, more glamorous verticals. Trades businesses are low-tech, hard to sell to, and require deep integration with software that was designed in the era of on-premise servers. The kind of grind that makes smart engineers say "I'd rather build a copilot for developers." So the problem passes the schlep test in isolation. But schlep blindness only works as a moat when most people are still blind to it. That's no longer the case here: Netic has raised $43 million at a $450M valuation, Avoca has $48 million, and WorkHero just raised a $5M seed — all targeting overlapping customer segments. When three or four well-funded companies are already in the ring, the schlep isn't hidden anymore. It's a category.

The co-founder origin raises a specific concern I've seen play out badly. Both founders met at Entrepreneurs First — a program that explicitly pairs strangers into founding teams. I've written about this failure mode: nine companies in one YC batch added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine imploded within a year. EF is a more structured version of that experiment, which may reduce the failure rate, but the underlying dynamic remains. Cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. The relationship should be stress-tested by years of shared context before you add the pressure of a startup. I don't see evidence of that here.

The bull case deserves honest examination. The $440K revenue figure with a four-person team is striking capital efficiency if it's attributable to the trades product. The PE roll-up distribution channel is genuinely clever — one relationship with a portfolio operator could unlock dozens of locations simultaneously, which is the kind of structural distribution advantage that scales without proportional sales headcount. And the agent-overlay positioning (sitting on existing tools rather than replacing them) is architecturally sound for this market. Trades businesses won't rip-and-replace their CCCOne or QuickBooks. A layer that automates the glue work between existing systems is how you actually get adoption in a low-tech-sophistication market. If the founders have built integrations that genuinely work across these messy legacy stacks, that integration complexity becomes its own moat over time.

But the revenue ambiguity is hard to look past. Ressl pivoted from "Devin for Salesforce" — and Salesforce-related content appeared as recently as mid-2025. The $440K number from Getlatka could include Salesforce consulting revenue, which would mean the trades product has substantially less traction than the headline suggests. At pre-seed I don't demand a growth chart, but I need to know what I'm measuring. Revenue from a product you've already pivoted away from tells me nothing about whether the current product has pull. And outside that number, there are zero organic demand signals: no Product Hunt listing, no community, no testimonials, no evidence of users who found the product on their own and would be upset if it disappeared.

The founders are technical — Abhishek leads the multi-agent architecture, Arushi has engineering internships and hackathon wins — so they can build. But I don't see evidence of the hacker mentality that produces elegant, distinctive technical work. No notable open-source contributions, no public technical writing, nothing that suggests they're building with unusual craft rather than competently assembling components. They're capable engineers who found a market through consulting. That's a perfectly fine company. It's not a company I'd bet my own money on at this stage, given the crowding, the manufactured origin, and the ambiguous traction.
