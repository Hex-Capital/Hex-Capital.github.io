# Orthogonal -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Stripe has already shipped its x402 protocol, processed roughly 50 million agent payment transactions, and co-developed the Agentic Commerce Protocol with OpenAI and Coinbase. That's not a future threat -- that's an incumbent with distribution to millions of developers who already has product in market. When I see a two-person pre-seed company building API payment aggregation and the largest payments company on earth is building the same category with OpenAI as a launch partner, the specific knowledge bar for the founders has to be extraordinarily high. Not "relevant experience" high. "They understand something Stripe's team structurally cannot see" high.

Christian Pickett worked on billing at Vercel and payments at Coinbase. Bera Sogut built reCAPTCHA and Maps APIs at Google, and he's a two-time ICPC World Finalist -- genuine technical talent. These are real credentials, and the founder-problem alignment is above average: one understands payment infrastructure from the inside, the other understands API systems at scale. But this is professional knowledge acquired through employment at well-known companies, not the kind of obsessive domain insight that takes years of independent exploration to develop. A talented engineer who spent two years at Stripe and two years at any API platform company could assemble the same mental model. Compare this to the Notion founders, who had been thinking about the relationship between tools and thought for the better part of a decade before anyone funded them. The Orthogonal founders are competent for this problem. They are not uniquely destined for it.

The leverage architecture is real in theory -- a unified API access layer where one integration serves every developer and agent is a genuine platform play, picks-and-shovels for the agentic era. That pattern maps to my portfolio: CoinList aggregated token launches, AngelList aggregated startup fundraising, Stack Overflow aggregated developer knowledge. But API aggregation marketplaces have been attempted for over a decade. RapidAPI (formerly Mashape) raised hundreds of millions pursuing this exact model for human developers and never became the dominant layer. The question is whether AI agents change the dynamics enough to make aggregation sticky in a way it wasn't for humans. Maybe -- agents calling hundreds of APIs per workflow create more aggregation pressure than a developer manually integrating three. But the MCP standard is open-source, the integration pattern is standardized, and the dossier itself notes the core product is "technically replicable." Sixteen curated API partners is a start, not a moat.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if agentic workflows become the default mode of software development -- and I think they will -- then the developer who needs their agent to discover, authenticate with, and pay for APIs across dozens of providers faces genuine friction. Someone will own that aggregation layer. The YC network gives Orthogonal a supply-side wedge that's hard to replicate quickly -- multiple YC companies already listed as partners. And the curated, trust-oriented approach may be the right product for developers who don't want on-chain USDC settlement and just want their agent to work. If Stripe's x402 remains blockchain-native and complex, there's a window for a simpler, fiat-native access layer. The CoinList analogy is instructive: CoinList won by being the curated, compliant alternative to the Wild West of ICOs. Orthogonal could be the curated, developer-friendly alternative to on-chain agent payment protocols. For this to be a great investment, Stripe would need to remain committed to on-chain settlement, the MCP ecosystem would need to fragment enough that aggregation creates real value, and Orthogonal would need to reach critical mass on both sides of the marketplace before better-funded competitors close the gap.

But the timing isn't contrarian -- it's consensus. Agentic payments is a category that Stripe, Mastercard, and multiple startups are pursuing simultaneously. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, smart people thought it was worthless. When I invested in Uber in 2010, ride-sharing was illegal. Here, everyone agrees agents will need payment infrastructure. The question is execution and distribution, not whether the category exists. That's a race, not a contrarian bet. And in races against Stripe, I don't back two-person pre-seed companies with my own money. The technology doesn't compound in a distinctive way -- there's no proprietary data graph, no novel algorithm, no physics-level insight. Each API integration is linear work, and the MCP standard means any competitor can build the same connectors.

I respect the founders' backgrounds and the clarity of the problem they've identified. But specific knowledge without structural defensibility is just a head start, and head starts against Stripe are measured in months, not years. This is a competent team in a real category that lacks the asymmetric upside I need -- the contrarian timing, the compounding technology, or the non-replicable founder insight that would make me write a check from my own account.
