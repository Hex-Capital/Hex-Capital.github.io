# Oximy -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most analytically interesting thing about Oximy is what their open-source repository reveals about the technology underneath: their "sensor" component is a fork of mitmproxy. That tells me everything I need to know about the depth of the technical moat. They've taken an existing network proxy, pointed it at AI tool domains, and built a categorization layer and dashboard on top. CrowdStrike already has agents on hundreds of millions of enterprise endpoints monitoring network connections. Zscaler already proxies enterprise traffic. Adding an AI tool domain list and a usage dashboard to either platform is a quarter's worth of engineering work, not a new company. When your core technology is a fork of something open-source, you are not building a defensible technology company -- you are building a product execution company in a race against incumbents who have distribution you will never match.

Let me apply my primary filter: if Oximy succeeds completely, what changes? Enterprises get a dashboard showing which AI tools their employees use, what it costs, and where data flows. That is a useful product. It is not a transformation of anything. Nobody's life changes. No industry gets disrupted. No incumbent system becomes obsolete. The financial services CISO sleeps slightly better, the CFO attributes AI spend more accurately, and the world continues operating exactly as it did before. This is the textbook case of a company that has reduced its probability of failure by choosing a manageable problem -- and in doing so, has made the consequence of its own success inconsequential. A successful Oximy is a $200M-$500M SaaS company that gets acquired by CrowdStrike or Palo Alto Networks. That is not the kind of outcome that justifies my attention.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The AI governance market is projected to grow from $2.5B to $68B by 2035, and the timing is genuinely favorable -- enterprise AI adoption is exploding while governance capabilities are scrambling to catch up. If Oximy becomes the definitive "system of record" for enterprise AI -- the way Salesforce became the system of record for customer relationships -- the outcome could be much larger than a feature acquisition. The founder shows real velocity: Naman built a COVID tracker to 10M users in four months, ran Easocare to $223K in revenue, and was a founding member at an Altman-backed AI company. His angel investor specifically cited "execution ability and product velocity." Processing millions of requests daily for financial services and healthcare enterprises at pre-seed is a legitimate traction signal. If the AI tool landscape fragments into thousands of tools (the 3,500+ taxonomy suggests this is already happening), then the monitoring layer could become genuinely indispensable -- not just useful, but required. The SOC 2 certification and on-premises deployment at this stage show the founder understands enterprise procurement. For a different investor evaluating execution-driven SaaS opportunities, this could be compelling.

But I am not that investor. The problem I have is not with the founder's execution -- it is with the ambition calibration. Every signal here points to a well-executed enterprise SaaS play in a growing market. That is precisely the profile I avoid. Nudge Security has $30M in capital, 200 customers, and 3x ARR growth already building overlapping capabilities. The competitive response is not "if" but "when" -- and "when" is measured in quarters, not years. The technology approach creates no structural barrier: any endpoint security vendor can replicate the AI tool detection capability because the detection mechanism (matching network connections to a domain list) is commodity engineering. The 3,500-tool taxonomy is a product advantage that erodes with every competitor who builds their own list.

Naman is clearly a fast-moving founder, but I see no evidence of contrarian courage here. Building an AI governance dashboard in 2025-2026 is not contrarian -- it is consensus. Every enterprise IT leader knows they need this. Every SaaS management vendor is pivoting toward it. The founder is not attempting something experts consider impossible; he is building what every analyst report recommends. Compare this to when I backed Juniper to challenge Cisco -- no major telecom wanted TCP/IP, and Cisco's own CTO said they would never build a TCP/IP router. That dismissal from experts was the signal. Here, every expert is nodding along. When the incumbents validate your approach rather than dismiss it, you are building something incremental enough to be non-threatening -- and non-threatening enough to be replicated.
