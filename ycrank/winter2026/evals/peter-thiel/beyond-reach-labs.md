# Beyond Reach Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Beyond Reach Labs is not the technology itself but the market thesis underneath it: that orbital power demand will grow from 50 megawatts to 500 gigawatts within a decade -- a 10,000x expansion. If you accept the current $600 million spacecraft solar panel market at face value, this is a competent hardware startup competing against Redwire and Northrop Grumman with a better mousetrap. If you accept the 10,000x thesis, this company is building the infrastructure layer for an entirely new civilization in orbit. The distance between those two readings is the distance between a pass and a conviction bet. And this is where I find the structure of the opportunity genuinely compelling: the founders are not optimizing within an existing market. They are wagering that the market itself will be created by forces outside their control -- Starship reducing launch costs, orbital data centers demanding power, lunar programs requiring sustained energy -- and that when that market arrives, the physics of deployable structures will be the binding constraint. That is a definite bet on a specific future, not an iterative exploration of customer needs.

The secret here is real, though it operates at two levels. The technical secret -- that metamaterial-based geometry change can break the fundamental length-stiffness-mass tradeoff that constrains every existing deployable array architecture -- is genuinely contrarian within aerospace engineering. Roll-out and fan-fold designs have dominated for decades, and most spacecraft engineers would view a 50x expansion ratio with structural rigidity as implausible until demonstrated. The deeper secret is strategic: the companies currently supplying solar arrays to satellites are building incrementally better versions of architectures designed for a world where orbital power demand is measured in kilowatts per spacecraft. If demand scales by orders of magnitude, those architectures become structurally inadequate -- not because they are bad, but because they were designed for a different era. Beyond Reach Labs is betting that the shift from kilowatt to megawatt-scale spacecraft power creates a discontinuity that favors an entirely new structural paradigm. This is not unlike what SpaceX understood about reusable rockets: the existing launch providers were not wrong about expendable rockets, they were solving a problem that was about to become irrelevant.

The founding team has the right composition for this specific problem. Collins spent seven years at SpaceX leading human-rated parachute systems across 30+ Dragon missions -- he understands the design-qualify-fly cycle for space hardware at the most demanding level, where failure means dead astronauts. Fogelson invented the core technology during his PhD at Carnegie Mellon under NASA NIAC funding and has already conducted microgravity validation. This is the combination I look for: one founder who has lived inside the production and qualification reality of spaceflight hardware, and one who created the underlying science. They have known each other for thirteen years, since freshman year at Penn. The advisory board -- Tom Cooley with 25 years at the Air Force Research Lab and Zac Manchester at MIT -- signals that serious people in the government-aerospace complex take this technology seriously. Collins leaving SpaceX to build this company is itself a signal of conviction; SpaceX is not a place people leave casually.

The monopoly path follows a pattern I recognize from Palantir and General Matter: government as the initial customer, with procurement dynamics that create deep switching costs once flight heritage is established. The $175 million in LOIs, while non-binding and from undisclosed counterparties, suggest that potential customers already see the need. ITAR restrictions, which appear as a risk factor, actually function as a competitive moat -- they limit the field to domestic players and make it harder for foreign entrants to replicate the technology. Once Beyond Reach Labs flies its demo mission in 2027 and accumulates orbital operational hours, government procurement processes will heavily weight that heritage. This is the Palantir dynamic: the integration becomes deeper, the switching costs increase, and the incumbent advantage compounds with every successful deployment.

The genuine risks are worth stating plainly. The technology has not flown in orbit, and the gap between a zero-gravity parabolic flight and sustained operation in LEO -- with thermal cycling, atomic oxygen, radiation, and micrometeorite impacts -- is enormous. Space hardware qualification kills startups regularly. The current TAM of $600 million is modest, and the 10,000x growth projection depends on orbital data centers, commercial space stations, and lunar programs that are themselves speculative. If those markets do not materialize on the projected timeline, Beyond Reach Labs is competing for a small pie against incumbents with decades of flight heritage and established government relationships. The two-person team with $200K in non-dilutive funding faces a capital-intensive path to flight qualification. These are not trivial concerns.

But the structure of this bet appeals to me. Hard technology that transforms the physical world. A genuine technical discontinuity -- 10x more power per launch mass -- originating from serious academic research with government validation. Founders with complementary expertise and a 13-year working relationship. A government-first go-to-market that creates compounding defensibility. And a market thesis that, if correct, makes existing approaches structurally obsolete. The company is not competing for market share in deployable solar arrays; it is positioning to own the power infrastructure layer of a space economy that does not yet exist. This is atoms-not-bits investing at its most literal. The question is whether the orbital power market materializes at the scale and speed the founders project. If it does, the company that solved the deployment physics first will be extraordinarily difficult to displace.
