# snag -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Three consecutive ventures with the same co-founder, all in urban housing. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas met at Pratt Institute, co-founded Knock Knock City (acquired by LuggageHero), co-founded OneRoof (raised $4.6M from General Catalyst, operated in 395+ NYC buildings), and now Snag. That's not a pivot — that's compound iteration. The strongest signal in the entire dossier is the co-founder relationship itself. Founders who survive an acquisition, a failed product, and a rebrand without splitting are principals, not agents. They stayed in housing when the smart career move was to chase AI wrappers. Principal commitment is revealed through behavior, not pitch decks. Sonmez and Georgantas have been behaving like principals for half a decade.

The specific knowledge question is where my enthusiasm tempers. Sonmez's Forbes 30 Under 30, three consumer ventures, and years operating in NYC housing give her real knowledge about GenZ rental behavior, university distribution, and the sublet trust problem. That knowledge led to the insight that Facebook groups and Craigslist are the actual competitors — not Zillow or Airbnb, whose business models structurally can't serve 1-3 month peer-to-peer sublets. The NYC Local Law 18 regulatory shift, which pushed demand from sub-30-day stays toward monthly sublets, is knowledge you accumulate by operating in the market, not by reading research reports. But I keep asking: if Sonmez and Georgantas left tomorrow, could a competent marketplace operator rebuild Snag's core insight within a year? Probably yes. The knowledge is real but replaceable — it's operational fluency, not the kind of irreducible expertise Aravind Srinivas brought to Perplexity from years inside Google Brain. Neither founder has public technical profiles or GitHub repos. The AI social-post-to-listing conversion is described as proprietary, but who built it? The dossier doesn't say. Specific knowledge that depends on an unnamed engineer is fragile.

The leverage audit is where Snag falls short of my highest conviction. Marketplaces are network-effect leverage — better than labor, weaker than pure code. AngelList, which I built, is structurally similar: aggregate fragmented supply, create trust through verification, match two sides. But AngelList scaled nationally from day one because startup investing isn't geography-bound. Snag's 30,000 users and 3,000 listings are all in NYC. Every new city requires rebuilding liquidity from scratch — recruiting universities, seeding supply, creating the density that makes a marketplace useful. That's labor leverage wearing a marketplace costume. The AI scraping component adds some code leverage on the supply side — if the model truly converts unstructured Facebook posts into structured listings automatically, geographic expansion becomes cheaper. But demand still requires city-by-city cultivation, and $6.5M in "booking requests" is GMV intent, not completed transactions or company revenue. The monetization model isn't even public. I can't evaluate leverage architecture when I don't know how the company makes money.

The access democratization pattern partially redeems the leverage weakness. Snag does give GenZ renters access to flexible housing that was previously locked behind knowing the right Facebook group or having the right roommate-of-a-roommate connection. Seven university partnerships — NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, Pace — create a distribution channel that systematically expands access to students who need it most. This fits my portfolio pattern: platforms that remove gatekeepers between people and something they want. But there's a distinction between democratizing access to a previously restricted resource (AngelList making angel investing available to everyone) and organizing an already-accessible but messy resource (aggregating sublet posts that were already public on Facebook). Snag is closer to the latter. Valuable, but not the structural access expansion that drives my highest conviction.

The bull case for Snag requires three things to be true simultaneously: (1) the AI supply aggregation genuinely enables multi-city expansion without proportional headcount, making this code leverage rather than labor leverage; (2) GenZ housing flexibility is a secular generational shift, not a cyclical preference that fades as this cohort ages into stability; and (3) the $6.5M booking requests and 40% month-over-month growth convert to real revenue at take rates sufficient to build a large business. If all three hold, this is a marketplace with Uber-like geographic network effects, a permanent demand tailwind, and AI-native unit economics. 30,000 users and a 4.7-star rating from 184 iOS reviews in five months suggest genuine organic pull — one of my invest signals. The 138 interested users on a single listing and 280 saves on the most-saved listing show demand density. Blueground ($352M raised, $750M valuation) validates the market but serves a different segment at premium prices — Snag could be the peer-to-peer layer below them.

Snag's most thesis-specific risk is the cap table and burn from the OneRoof era. $4.6M raised from General Catalyst for a neighbor-community app that became a sublet marketplace — that's investor capital consumed on a different product. Cap table complexity, existing investor preferences, and reduced runway create structural drag. The iOS app launched November 2025, meaning the current product is five months old on investor capital raised for a different vision. This isn't disqualifying — some of my best investments came from founders who iterated through multiple products. But it means the effective burn rate on this specific product is unknown, and the next funding round requires explaining the pivot to new and existing investors simultaneously.

The principal authenticity of Sonmez and Georgantas is the dimension that most pulls me toward investing — two designers who've built three companies together over five-plus years, all in the same problem space, with genuine organic traction in their latest iteration. The permissionless leverage architecture is the dimension that holds me back — city-by-city marketplace expansion with an unverified monetization model and an AI component whose depth I can't assess. If I could see the AI model's actual capability for multi-city supply aggregation and a clear monetization mechanism, this would cross into invest territory. Without that evidence, Snag is a well-executed marketplace with strong founders and a real insight, operating in a structure that doesn't produce the kind of asymmetric leverage I need to write a check.
