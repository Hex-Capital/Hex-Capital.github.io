# Clam (formerly Baseframe) -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Clam is the pivot. They entered W26 as BaseFrame, a hardware design validation platform, launched it publicly in January 2026, and then pivoted mid-batch to AI agent security. Pivots during a YC batch aren't inherently bad -- Justin.tv became Twitch, and that turned out fine. But the direction of the pivot matters. They moved from a niche technical problem toward one of the hottest categories in tech right now. When founders pivot toward the heat rather than away from it, I start asking whether they discovered a genuine problem or followed a trend. The answer here is genuinely ambiguous.

The organic backstory exists, and it's not nothing. Anshul was the founding engineer at HappyRobot, a production AI agent company that serves enterprises like DHL and Schneider and went on to raise a $44M Series B. He led AI evals, observability, and enterprise integrations -- which means he spent his days watching AI agents interact with real enterprise systems and seeing what goes wrong. That's the kind of lived experience that produces genuine problem discovery. Vaibhav worked on remote agent orchestration at Augment Code and data infrastructure at Sigma Computing. They took a Computer Security course together at Berkeley. The ingredients for this company existed in their backgrounds before the pivot. But if AI agent security was really the problem keeping them up at night, why did they start a hardware design validation company? The honest reading is that this problem was in their peripheral vision, not the center of it, and they pivoted to it when BaseFrame didn't gain traction. That's a weaker organic signal than someone who couldn't stop thinking about this problem from day one.

The architectural idea -- a network-level "Semantic Firewall" that intercepts all traffic from the AI agent rather than trying to constrain the model itself -- is genuinely clever. It's the right abstraction. If you can't trust the AI to follow instructions (and you can't), then you need to put the security outside the AI's control. The credential injection mechanism, where API keys are inserted at the network layer so the agent never sees them, is a real insight. But here's what I keep coming back to: is this a company, or is it a feature? The individual scanning capabilities -- PII detection, prompt injection detection, malicious code scanning -- are available in open-source tools like LLM Guard. The bundling and network-level deployment are differentiated, but the schlep here is moderate. This isn't Stripe dealing with banking regulations and money transmission laws. This is building a proxy server with good detection models. Any well-resourced security engineering team at Palo Alto Networks or Check Point could build this in a quarter.

Which brings me to the competitive landscape, and this is where I'd push back on the bull case that "the field has cleared." Yes, four standalone AI security startups were acquired by large cybersecurity incumbents in 2025. But they were acquired precisely because these incumbents want to own this capability. Check Point paid ~$300M for Lakera. Palo Alto paid ~$500M for Protect AI. These companies aren't going to deprioritize what they just spent hundreds of millions to acquire. They're going to bundle AI security into their existing enterprise security suites, which their massive sales teams already sell to every Fortune 500 CISO. Clam is a two-person startup competing against bundled features from companies with existing customer relationships and purchase orders. The consolidation wave didn't create an opening -- it created a wall. The one scenario where this works is if Clam becomes the standard for a specific ecosystem (OpenClaw developers) before the incumbents figure out that segment. But the product is currently tied to a single framework, and if OpenClaw builds native security, the positioning collapses.

The bull case I can construct: the founders are technical, lean, building it themselves, and Anshul has genuine domain experience from inside a production AI agent company. The timing is real -- AI agents are moving to production and the security tooling hasn't caught up. If they execute fast, build the best detection models from real traffic data, and expand beyond OpenClaw to become the default firewall for any AI agent framework, they could build meaningful switching costs before incumbents integrate their acquisitions. The network-level architecture could become the right standard. That's a plausible path. But it requires winning a race against some of the best-resourced companies in cybersecurity, with no traction evidence, a product that just launched post-pivot, and core capabilities that aren't proprietary. I need to see users pulling this product before I'd get excited. Right now it's a sound architecture looking for validation.

These are smart, technical founders with relevant backgrounds and the right instincts about where to put the security boundary. But the idea feels more like it was assembled from available ingredients than discovered through an inability to ignore the problem. The schlep is real but not the kind that creates structural barriers to competition. And the absence of any user signal for the current product -- combined with the pivot recency -- means I'm evaluating a hypothesis, not a company. I'd want to check back in three months and see if developers are actually adopting this.
