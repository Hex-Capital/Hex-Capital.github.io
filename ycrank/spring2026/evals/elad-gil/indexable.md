# Indexable -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Indexable is that it's entering a market that has already achieved full consensus. Six funded startups -- E2B with $32M+ and 88% of Fortune 100 signed up, Daytona at $31M with $1M ARR in two months, Modal at $111M and ~$50M ARR -- plus Cloudflare, Vercel, and Fly.io all launched sandbox products in the last twelve months. When I see this competitive density, I apply the market archetype test: is this market "too boring," "too niche," or "seemingly crowded" in a way that hides opportunity? AI agent sandboxes in spring 2026 are none of these. They're the infrastructure category every investor is chasing. My best returns have come from markets other investors dismissed -- PagerDuty when operations alerting seemed unglamorous, Coinbase when crypto seemed speculative. This market is the opposite: it's the thing everyone already wants to fund.

That said, there's a more interesting sub-question buried inside the competitive map. Most competitors -- E2B with Firecracker, Daytona with Docker, Cloudflare with V8 isolates -- are built on commodity virtualization primitives. They handle ephemeral code execution well but don't fork entire stateful environments including running databases, processes, and memory as a single atomic unit. Indexable built a custom VMM from scratch on KVM to do exactly this in 26 milliseconds. The technical depth here is genuine: a custom Linux 6.19 kernel with upstream-unsupported patches, RDMA-capable transport via libfabric, content-addressable storage with tiered deduplication. This is not a wrapper. This is closer to the kind of structural engineering moat I saw in Anduril -- where the hard thing itself becomes the barrier. The question is whether the performance and capability difference is a Google-entering-search moment (fundamentally better technology in a crowded category) or a "smart toaster" situation where the engineering sophistication doesn't translate into a wedge customers will pay for and switch to.

The bull case is that current AI agents mostly execute code in ephemeral containers, but the adoption curve is heading toward agents that manage entire application stacks -- databases, background processes, file state. When that capability threshold arrives, the ability to fork a complete running environment in 26ms becomes qualitatively different from what E2B or Daytona offer, not just faster. This would be the Harvey pattern: building ahead of a capability curve, positioning to capture share the moment the use case matures. If autonomous agents start managing database migrations, staging environments, and full-stack deployments -- which the trajectory of Claude Code and Codex suggests they will -- then stateful forking moves from "nice to have" to "essential infrastructure." For this bull case to hold, two things need to be true: the stateful forking use case needs to become the primary demand driver (not just ephemeral code execution), and Indexable needs to be ready with distribution when that moment arrives. Right now, neither condition is established.

And that's where the distribution gap becomes the structural concern. The founder's go-to-market is "early access DM" on Twitter with ~86 followers. The website returns HTTP 403. No Product Hunt launch, no press, no public documentation, no open-source repos under the org, no inclusion in any sandbox comparison article. Meanwhile E2B has an open-source SDK ecosystem driving adoption across the Fortune 100. I always evaluate whether founders think about distribution from day one -- "almost every company that ended up with tens of billions in market cap was aggressive about distribution from their earliest days." I see zero evidence of distribution thinking here. The founding team appears to believe the technology will speak for itself. That signal is concerning regardless of how impressive the VMM engineering is.

Andrew Gazelka's technical credibility is real. Building Hyperion -- a Minecraft engine in Rust targeting 10,000 concurrent players with 1,000 GitHub stars -- is a direct analog to the concurrent state management and VM forking challenges in sandbox infrastructure. His xAI tenure and prior exit demonstrate execution in high-stakes environments. The single miracle here is reasonably clear: can you build a VMM that makes full-environment forking fast and reliable enough to be qualitatively differentiated? The entire company appears organized around this technical challenge, which is how I want to see it. But with a 3-person team maintaining a custom VMM, custom kernel, and custom Nix fork, the engineering surface area creates concentration risk. Competitors using AWS-maintained Firecracker carry structurally lower maintenance overhead.

The structural moment for AI agent sandboxes is real -- the inflection happened in 2024-2025 with the scaling of autonomous coding agents. But recognizing the moment doesn't distinguish Indexable from the six other startups and three cloud providers who recognized it simultaneously. My framework requires a non-obvious market, and this market is fully obvious. The specific sub-segment of stateful forking is less crowded, but whether that sub-segment becomes the dominant wedge depends on the agent capability curve advancing to full-stack autonomy -- which is plausible but not yet validated by customer demand. I'm passing not because the technology is weak, but because entering a consensus-hot market with no distribution against incumbents who have 5-20x more capital and established customer bases requires a distribution miracle on top of the technical miracle. That's two miracles.
