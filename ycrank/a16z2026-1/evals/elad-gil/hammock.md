# Hammock -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, is the largest HSA expansion in two decades -- gym memberships became HSA-eligible up to $500/person starting January 2026, and the broader wellness-to-HSA pipeline got a clear regulatory greenlight. That's a genuine structural catalyst. But when I look at the capital already deployed against this exact shift -- Truemed raising $34M from a16z in December 2025, Flex pulling in $18M for merchant-side HSA/FSA infrastructure, Sika Health and Burst raising behind them -- I see a market where the structural window is real but fully recognized. Over $60M has been invested into HSA/FSA wellness plays in the last 18 months. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was a punchline. This isn't that. The HSA wellness thesis is now consensus fintech, and consensus market timing is the one thing I can't arbitrage. Hammock's consumer-side angle -- issuing its own card rather than integrating at merchant checkout -- is a genuine product positioning difference, but it's a distribution bet, not a structural insight that others have missed.

Where Hammock runs directly against my framework is the infrastructure-layer test. Flex is literally branding itself "Stripe for HSA/FSA" -- API-first payment infrastructure that merchants build on, exactly the kind of picks-and-shovels positioning I look for during platform shifts. Hammock is the application sitting on top: a consumer card with bundled LMN generation and an AI scanning agent. Consumer fintech applications can build large businesses, but they don't compound switching costs the way infrastructure does. The one structural exception here is HSA custodianship -- if Hammock holds the actual custodial relationship, that creates financial-account-level switching inertia, closer to a bank than to an app. Fidelity, HealthEquity, and Optum Bank hold most HSA assets today through employer distribution. Hammock would need consumers to voluntarily transfer balances to a pre-seed startup with no public custodial arrangement disclosed. That's a big ask, and the dossier doesn't clarify whether Hammock has a bank charter, trust company status, or custodial partner in place.

The miracle count concerns me. Hammock needs consumer acquisition to work (getting HSA holders to switch from Fidelity or HealthEquity to an unknown startup) AND it needs the LMN-generation model to survive IRS scrutiny at scale. These are independent risks. If the IRS decides that AI-facilitated bulk LMN generation constitutes rubber-stamping, Hammock's core value proposition -- unlimited LMNs through the membership -- collapses overnight. That's a regulatory miracle layered on top of a distribution miracle. Not three miracles, but definitely not one. Compare this to Harvey, where the technology miracle was happening exogenously through foundation model improvement, and law firms were already proven technology buyers. Hammock's miracles are endogenous -- the company has to make each one happen through its own execution.

Jesse Rose is the strongest signal in this dossier, and he's a genuinely strong one. Building Berry Street to $50M ARR with partnerships across 1,250+ insurance plans (Aetna, Anthem, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare) is directly relevant operating experience -- healthcare payments, provider networks, insurance eligibility workflows. Will Dennis overseeing a $1B ARR consumer health subscription at WeightWatchers and shipping product at WhatsApp to 2 billion users adds complementary scaling experience. This is the kind of operator-founder combination I look for: Rose brings the healthcare/regulatory complexity navigation, Dennis brings consumer product execution at scale. The clear CEO structure (Rose) avoids the co-founder ambiguity that stalls companies during scaling. If I were evaluating the team alone, I'd write a check. But I've seen great founders get crushed by a market that's already crowded with better-capitalized competitors, and Truemed's $34M war chest plus shared a16z backing creates real portfolio tension.

The bull case requires one thing to be true: that the HSA market is shifting from employer-controlled to consumer-controlled, analogous to 401(k) portability creating the retail brokerage market. If consumers start treating their HSA like a personal financial account they choose and manage independently -- not something their employer assigns -- then the consumer-side platform wins and the merchant-side infrastructure becomes a feature, not a business. Hammock's bundled model (card + LMN + investment + AI agent) could be the Schwab of HSAs. The $159B in assets growing to $208B by 2027 provides the scale for this to matter. Rose's Berry Street experience navigating insurance complexity makes him one of a very small number of founders who could actually execute on custodial HSA infrastructure. But this thesis requires a behavioral shift in how 40 million Americans relate to their HSA -- that's creating new behavior, not capturing existing behavior in a new medium. Most people don't think about their HSA until open enrollment.

The margin architecture, if Hammock executes, is actually well-designed for a fintech play: recurring membership revenue, interchange on card transactions, and potentially yield on custodial deposits. The membership model with unlimited LMNs creates a clear value proposition against standalone LMN services charging $50-$150 per letter. HSA custodial switching costs would produce strong retention dynamics. But these are all conditional on achieving custodial status and consumer traction -- neither of which exists yet based on the waitlist-phase product status. The $4,000 in average HSA-eligible expenses claimed on the website is unverified and could be modeled rather than measured.

Hammock has strong founders attacking a real market with a differentiated consumer-side approach. But the two dimensions I weight most heavily -- nonobvious market timing and infrastructure-layer positioning -- are where Hammock scores weakest. The HSA wellness market is now consensus with $60M+ deployed against it, and Hammock is an application, not infrastructure. Rose's Berry Street credentials nearly pull this into Invest territory, but the combination of obvious timing, application-layer positioning, and dual-miracle risk (consumer switching + regulatory survival) keeps me neutral. I'd revisit if Hammock secures a custodial arrangement and demonstrates consumer acquisition economics -- those would collapse the miracle count from two to one.
