# Polymath -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Polymath: this is one of the most crowded, well-funded, consensus-validated spaces I've seen in a while. Mechanize has Nat Friedman, Daniel Gross, Patrick Collison, and Jeff Dean behind it -- and is already working with Anthropic. Prime Intellect raised $70.4 million. Scale AI, with its billions in revenue and existing lab relationships, is expanding directly into RL environments. Wing VC is publishing consolidation timelines. Anthropic reportedly plans to spend over a billion dollars on RL environments. When I think about the companies that have defined my career -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe Energy -- the common thread is that nobody else wanted to fund them. The consensus hadn't formed yet. With Polymath, the consensus has very much formed. The market is real, but the arbitrage on insight is gone. Everyone sees this opportunity, and the players who got there first brought significantly more firepower. Two engineers with YC funding competing against teams backed by Jeff Dean and Patrick Collison, with Scale AI's existing customer relationships looming -- that's not the kind of asymmetric bet I'm drawn to.

When I try to understand these founders through their personal stories, I hit a wall. Dylan Ma came through UC Berkeley and Hume AI as a Senior ML Engineer. Naren Yenuganti went from Berkeley to Amazon's Planning and Optimization Technology team to Plaid. These are strong technical credentials. But I don't hear the biographical thread that tells me why *these two people* are uniquely compelled to solve *this specific problem*. The YC page mentions "years of experience post-training frontier models in industry," which is a professional qualification, not a personal stake. I'll note that Naren's earlier work -- designing a tongue-operated mouse for people with disabilities through Berkeley SkyDeck -- actually caught my eye. That shows genuine empathy and creative thinking about underserved needs. But that thread doesn't connect to RL environment generation for frontier AI labs. When I ask myself whether these founders arrived at this insight through some deeply personal or unusual path, or whether they saw a hot market and moved toward it, the dossier gives me no evidence of the former.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If Polymath's generative approach to environment creation truly works -- creating realistic, long-horizon environments from text descriptions alone -- that would represent a genuine paradigm shift from the labor-intensive model that Scale AI and Surge AI are built on. There's an innovator's dilemma argument here: Surge AI's $1.2 billion in revenue comes from human contractors, and automating that away would cannibalize their core business. A small, focused team unencumbered by legacy revenue could move faster on full automation. The Horizon-SWE benchmark, with its 20,000+ commit monorepo and production-grade multi-service environment, demonstrates real technical capability. And the market timing is undeniably right -- the shift from static benchmarks to agentic, long-horizon training environments is happening now. If I'm being fair, Crusoe Energy was also two engineers with a technical insight competing against established energy companies. But the difference is fundamental: Crusoe's idea was considered insane by everyone, while Polymath's idea is considered obvious by everyone who matters.

I also don't see the economic access dimension that pulls me toward my strongest investments. Uber created income for drivers. Postmates created income for delivery workers. Affirm brought transparent credit to people who couldn't get it. Polymath sells infrastructure to the wealthiest, most well-resourced companies on the planet -- OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind. That's fine as a business, but it doesn't create the kind of positive-sum economic outcomes that get me excited about writing a check from my own money. There's no invisible system being made visible, no underserved population gaining access to something they were previously locked out of. It's an infrastructure play in a hot space, and competent people are building it, but it doesn't carry the meaning I look for.

The co-founder dynamic is neutral -- both Berkeley graduates, likely connected through the AI/ML ecosystem there, which gives them shared context. But I have no data on how they handle disagreement, how long they've actually built together, or whether they've been through any real crisis. At two people and pre-seed stage, this is expected, but it also means I'm flying blind on the dimension I consider essential after the HQ Trivia lesson.
