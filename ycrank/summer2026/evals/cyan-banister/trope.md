# Trope -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Trope is two UBC undergrads selling AI agents into the ERP implementation partner channel, and the dimensional signature is that its highest note -- the genuine unsexiness of ERP as a category -- is not loud enough to drown out its lowest, which is that "Modernize ERP Implementations with AI" is the exact consensus lane where CoPlane already raised $14M (PYMNTS, 2025) and Microsoft Finance Copilot went GA in October 2025. This is a consensus AI overlay wearing an unsexy costume, not a magically weird bet, and Matthew Chow shipping Tesla Cybertruck software plus Victor Vannara's Devpost portfolio does not, by itself, give Trope the lived contact with the ERP implementation problem that my thesis actually requires.

Palmer Luckey shipped VR from his parents' garage before he built Anduril, and that autobiographical relationship to the hardware is what made me tattoo conviction into the defense category when peers had aesthetic objections. Trope's founders are the mirror-image case: Chow's LinkedIn shows Tesla Cybertruck and Zipline autonomous delivery, Vannara's public trail is a Devpost portfolio, and the trope.ai homepage name-checks Amazon, Schneider Electric, and Generac -- none of those are ERP implementation consultancies. The founder-market fit here is *adjacent enterprise software delivery*, not the roughneck-on-the-well-pad social license that made Chase Lochmiller credible at Crusoe. When Trope's dossier claims agents "take action across documents, configurations, and code" for SAP ABAP or NetSuite SuiteScript, I want to know which of these founders sat inside a Deloitte or Cognizant delivery pod and felt the specific pain of a fit-gap workshop -- the dossier says neither did.

CoPlane at $14M and Rewst at $104M cumulative (SiliconANGLE, 2024) already occupy the "AI automation on top of ERP" story, and Microsoft, SAP, and Oracle are shipping first-party Copilots inside the very suites Trope proposes to augment. My thesis is to find them *before* it becomes consensus, and Trope's competitive slate reads as already-consensus with $118M of adjacent capital deployed and hyperscaler platform substitution documented on the same page. Magically weird would look like a founder who worked five years inside Accenture's SAP practice and now argues that the entire billable-hour delivery model is fraud; instead Trope is the polite YC framing of a category Sequoia-adjacent funds are already circling. Two credible VCs passing on aesthetic grounds is a tell -- zero passing, and a Microsoft Copilot GA event two months before this batch, is a different tell.

Trope's co-founder question compounds over eighteen months in a specific way: the dossier names Chow and Vannara as co-founders, both UBC, with no CEO designation, no equity split, no operating agreement disclosed. In a partner-channel enterprise motion where the sales cycle runs six-to-nine months per Deloitte-tier account, the moment one co-founder wants to sell direct to CFOs and the other wants to keep the SI channel intact, there is no tiebreaker in the dossier to resolve it. HQ Trivia's Yusupov/Kroll dynamic did not detonate in year one; it detonated at scale. Trope has not yet had the conversation that would prevent it.

For the score on capital-intensity to flip a tier, Trope would need to be building the ERP configuration data model itself -- proprietary training on ABAP diffs, SuiteScript config artifacts, cutover-plan corpora accumulated across implementations -- and disclosing volume metrics that make the vertical model a compounding artifact. The dossier gestures at this ("[Inferred]: proprietary training data") but the "no customer count or data-volume figure is disclosed" line is doing all the work. ERP is genuinely unsexy in the way my Mindbloom and Vigil Labs sleeve is unsexy, which is why Trope earns some of this dimension. It does not earn the SpaceX or Crusoe end of the ladder because there is no physical asset, no regulatory moat, no capital-intensity that competitors cannot buy their way past with a $50M Series A.

Trope's product parallel is closest to the pass example I already wrote -- the B2B API whose value can only be evaluated via founder testimony. The trope.ai homepage exposes a "Get a demo" CTA and no pricing page; the @trope_ai handle exists but follower count is not retrievable; there is no artifact I can install, photograph, or hand to a friend within 48 hours. Flock Safety I could see -- the license-plate camera on the pole. Trope I cannot see. In a portfolio where physical verifiability is a repeatable sourcing method, an implementation-partner workflow tool with no observable surface starts below the midpoint by construction.

My 36-month forecast on Trope: flatlines at under $1M ARR against CoPlane and Microsoft ERP Copilot's channel gravity, and either winds down or gets acqui-hired by a mid-tier SI -- Cognizant, Infosys, or a Rossum-adjacent document-automation roll-up -- for founder talent under $10M by mid-2029. The mechanism is that partner-incentive misalignment on billable-hour compression prevents the SI channel from adopting quickly, while hyperscaler Copilot bundling erodes the standalone value narrative before Trope reaches escape velocity.

The strongest bear-case reversal I owe Trope: partner channel-conflict flips into leverage if end customers -- the CFOs paying the SI invoice -- start demanding implementation-time reductions and force Deloitte to adopt Trope defensively, which is the pattern that let Rossum reach a $100M Series A. If Chow's Tesla and Zipline production discipline translates into agent reliability benchmarks that beat SAP Joule on ABAP config edits, and Trope publishes those benchmarks, the founder-domain gap I flagged becomes irrelevant because engineering execution overrides pedigree. But this requires Trope to publish a physical, verifiable reliability number against a named ERP suite -- and the dossier's silence on benchmarks tracks the same silence on customers, revenue, and CEO designation. One verifiable condition would flip my verdict: a signed pilot with a named top-20 SI (Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant, Infosys, Capgemini) disclosed with reference-customer language, because that would prove Trope solved the channel-incentive problem the dossier does not address. Absent that, I pass.
