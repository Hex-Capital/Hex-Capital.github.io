# Sponge -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Sponge: three engineers who built Stripe's crypto payments infrastructure left to build payments infrastructure for AI agents -- and then Stripe launched x402 agent payments on Base in February 2026. Their former employer is now both their most likely integration partner and their most dangerous competitor. This is the kind of founder-incumbent entanglement I find deeply problematic. When I backed Juniper, Pradeep Sindhu came from Xerox PARC -- a research lab, not the company he was trying to displace. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he came from New Zealand, not Boeing. Sponge's founders are industry insiders from the dominant payments company, executing a variation of their employer's roadmap. They know exactly what Stripe considers possible, which means they also know what Stripe considers worth building. That constrains ambition in ways the founders may not even recognize.

The consequence-of-success question exposes the core issue. If Sponge wins -- if it becomes THE financial infrastructure for autonomous agents -- what changes? Agents can pay for things without a human entering a credit card number. That's genuinely useful. It's also middleware. The transformative event is agents themselves becoming autonomous economic actors; Sponge is plumbing that sits between the agent and the existing financial system. Compare this to what Square accomplished: Square didn't just add a new interface to existing payment rails -- it brought millions of previously excluded merchants into the card payment economy, fundamentally restructuring who could participate in commerce. Sponge adds an agent-shaped adapter to payment flows that already work for humans. The world doesn't operate differently if Sponge succeeds. Transactions get slightly more automated. That's a fine business, but it's not the kind of outcome that justifies tolerating a 90% probability of failure.

The competitive landscape reinforces my concern. Four funded competitors -- Skyfire ($9.5M), Natural ($9.8M), Payman ($13.8M), Nevermined ($7.02M) -- are all attacking the same category, all with substantially more capital. Stripe, Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, and Coinbase have all announced agent payment toolkits. When every major incumbent validates your category within a twelve-month window, that's not contrarian -- that's consensus. I look for investments where experts dismiss the idea as impossible. Nobody is dismissing agent payments as impossible; they're all racing to build it. The competitive question isn't whether this market will exist but whether a three-person YC pre-seed team can establish a two-sided marketplace network effect before Stripe's existing merchant base of millions gets connected to x402 agent rails. That's a race against distribution, not a bet on technology disruption.

Let me engage with the bull case honestly. The strongest argument for Sponge is the two-sided marketplace structure: Wallet + Gateway + Catalog could create network effects that single-product competitors and incumbent payment rails can't replicate. If Sponge becomes the place where agents discover and pay for services -- an app store for the agent economy -- that's a platform position, not just plumbing. The Amazon beta pilot and AgentMail integration suggest early supply-side traction. And the founders' deep Stripe Crypto experience means they understand stablecoin payment flows, compliance infrastructure, and multi-chain architecture at a level most teams don't. If the agent economy grows at the projected 46% CAGR and Sponge captures the marketplace layer, the consequence magnitude could be larger than I'm giving it credit for. The timing is also legitimately good -- building ahead of mass agent adoption while incumbents are still treating agent payments as feature additions to existing products, not standalone businesses. If I'm wrong about the consequence magnitude, it's because I'm underestimating how fundamentally different agent-to-agent commerce is from human-to-merchant commerce, and therefore underestimating how inadequate incumbent adaptations will prove.

But I keep returning to the pattern I've seen dozens of times: smart engineers from a dominant platform leave to build something adjacent, and the platform eventually absorbs their innovation because it has the distribution they'll never match. The founders have de-risked this bet in exactly the way I warn against -- they chose a domain they know perfectly, a technology stack they've already built, and a market that every major financial institution is already validating. The probability of building a viable business is higher than average. The consequence of success is a mid-scale payments company in a crowded category. They're too talented to spend their careers on infrastructure plumbing when the real transformation -- autonomous AI agents reshaping every industry -- is happening at a different layer of the stack.
