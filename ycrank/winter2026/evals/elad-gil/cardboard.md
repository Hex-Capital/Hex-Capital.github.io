# Cardboard -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at Cardboard is a crowded market. Descript has $101M in funding and roughly $55M ARR. OpusClip has $50M in funding, 10 million users, and $20M ARR. Kapwing has $10M in revenue. Adobe is layering AI into Premiere Pro. CapCut has ByteDance's distribution muscle. Multiple commenters on the Launch HN thread flagged "lots of players in the market doing this" -- including other companies in the same YC W26 batch. AI video editing is not a non-obvious market. It is the definition of a consensus bet. Every growth-stage VC can recite the TAM. Every accelerator has three companies in this category. When I ask "what structural change makes this possible now?", the answer -- multimodal LLMs crossed a video-understanding threshold, and WebGPU/WebCodecs matured for browser rendering -- is real but undifferentiated. Those same technology thresholds are available to every competitor simultaneously. Harvey worked because the team built legal AI workflow tools *before* GPT-4 existed, positioning ahead of the capability curve. Cardboard is launching into a market where the capability curve has already been recognized by dozens of well-funded players.

The bull case hinges on a Figma-like structural argument: Figma entered design tools when everyone said Adobe owned the market, and the browser-native architecture enabled collaborative workflows that desktop applications structurally couldn't deliver. Cardboard is building a browser-native NLE from scratch on WebGPU/WebCodecs rather than bolting AI onto legacy desktop architecture. If marketing teams shift from "one editor exports a final cut" to "the whole team iterates in the browser," the architectural advantage is real -- incumbents would need to rewrite their entire rendering stack to compete. The Teams tier with SSO and audit logs suggests they're at least directionally thinking about enterprise workflow adoption, not just individual creator tools. And the argument that "raw footage sits unused because editing is too expensive" is a latent-demand thesis -- the real TAM includes all the videos that marketing teams would produce if the cost approached zero. If Cardboard nails the agentic layer and the browser NLE simultaneously, the combination is genuinely hard to replicate. For this to work, the market would need to evolve the way collaborative design evolved -- away from specialist tools toward team workflows -- and Cardboard would need to be positioned as the Figma of video before the incumbents notice.

The problem is that this plan requires two miracles, not one. The founders explicitly acknowledge it: "We have to build two hard things at once." A professional-grade browser NLE is one hard thing -- it requires a custom renderer, multi-track timeline management, and performance parity with desktop applications that have had decades of optimization. A reliable agentic editing layer is another hard thing -- AI making autonomous creative decisions about shot selection, pacing, and transitions at a quality level that marketing teams will ship without substantial rework. And winning distribution in a market where Descript already has $55M ARR and name recognition is arguably a third. When I apply the single-miracle test, the path gets uncomfortable. Harvey's miracle -- model fidelity reaching legal-grade accuracy -- was being delivered by foundation model labs, not by Harvey. Harvey could focus entirely on workflow and distribution. Cardboard's miracles are internal: they need to build both systems themselves, with two people.

That said, the execution velocity is the one signal that gives me genuine pause. Thirteen product releases shipped between November 2025 and February 2026 with a two-person team. A working product with a custom WebCodecs/WebGL2 renderer. Eight named B2B customers including PostHog. One thousand early users. Pricing tiers live with a coherent structure ($60/$150/custom). The CTO built performance-critical browser applications at HackerRank for five years, which maps directly to the NLE engineering challenge. This is not a slide deck -- it is a shipped product iterating weekly. In my experience, that velocity with a two-person team signals the kind of builder intensity that can compress timelines others would consider impossible.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is structurally possible but unproven and unarticulated. If Cardboard becomes the video workspace for marketing teams, the customer relationship could distribute asset management, brand guidelines enforcement, performance analytics, and approval workflows -- the way Stripe distributed Atlas and Treasury through a payments integration. But I see no evidence the founders are thinking about this expansion yet, and the initial wedge in a market with this many funded competitors is the hard part. Distribution thinking at the earliest stage is one of my core signals, and "marketing teams will find us through content and HN launches" is not a distribution strategy that wins against a $55M ARR incumbent.

My framework is market-first, and this market is structurally wrong for how I invest. The technology enabling this company enables every competitor equally. The market is consensus, not non-obvious. The company requires two simultaneous technical breakthroughs plus a distribution win against established players. The execution velocity is legitimately impressive -- these are builders who ship -- but builders shipping fast in a crowded market with no structural moat end up in a feature war against teams with ten times their resources. I'd want to see this team apply their intensity to a market where the structural dynamics create asymmetric advantage, rather than one where they're in a footrace against Adobe's AI team, Descript's $55M revenue engine, and ByteDance's distribution.
