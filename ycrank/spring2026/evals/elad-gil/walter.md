# Walter -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Walter is a clean **single-miracle architecture** strapped to an **invisible revenue curve** -- and that pairing, in the AI cycle, is the exact profile where "something is fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss Show #863). Nikolas Keller's pitch isolates the miracle correctly (LLM accuracy on unstructured manufacturing documents in 270+ languages), but the dossier's headline traction signal -- "multiple 9-figure customers in packaging and defense" -- has no logos, no contract sizes, no MoM curve, and the YC description was written after a stated pivot from customer-support automation. That is not the **Harvey** (2023) profile of "just working." That is the false-signal profile.

**Anduril** (2022) is the HIGH rung on catalyst because Ukraine compressed Pentagon software-procurement inertia inside a 24-month window -- a specific, dated, exogenous shock peers misread as steady-state. Walter wants the same template: "LLMs crossed the accuracy threshold for non-English manufacturing documents." That catalyst is real, but it is the *same* catalyst Rossum, Hyperscience, Workist, Nanonets, and every Spring 2026 YC doc-AI company is riding. Where Anduril's catalyst opened a graveyard market, Walter's catalyst lowered the bar in a market that already had four well-funded incumbents -- Rossum at $100M Series A from General Catalyst, Hyperscience above $300M raised. The catalyst exists; the *re-opening* doesn't. This is "Stripe-after-PayPal" only if the back-office-document layer was previously closed, and it wasn't.

**Lyft** Series C is my standing reminder of what happens when the curve is missing and you reason from market structure alone -- I passed on the right framework and missed the wrong stock, but the framework still rejects pitches where revenue is asserted rather than disclosed. Walter's dossier names zero customer logos, publishes no pricing page, and offers only a 30-minute demo CTA with the CEO on Cal.com. In the AI cycle, real growth is easy to disclose -- when **Harvey** ran $3B → $5B → $8B in months, the numbers were the pitch (TechCrunch, Nov 2025). Walter's silence on MoM, on ARR, on named logos, paired with the LinkedIn-snippet evidence of a recent pivot from customer-support automation, is exactly the pattern I treat as enterprise-pilot tourism until proven otherwise.

Walter's founder-shipping signal compounds badly over the next 18 months: Keller is a CDTM dropout whose largest public artifact is a "tool to explore life paths of CDTM members," and Lukas Postulka has no verified public profile -- no GitHub, no confirmed LinkedIn, no prior shipped product. This is the mechanism that breaks **High Growth Handbook** (2018) math at the 20-employee mark: when you sell into SAP-integrated workflows at Rheinmetall-class buyers, the CTO has to negotiate integration scope against a vendor security team that has been doing this longer than he has been writing production code. Compare to Aravind Srinivas pinging me on LinkedIn and showing up the next week with the thing built -- Walter's dossier surfaces no equivalent rhythm artifact.

For the index-position dimension, the counterfactual that would flip Walter a tier is precise: if Walter were the *write-back layer that other agent vendors integrate against* -- the way Stripe became the rails every checkout touched -- the ERP integration list (SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics 365) would be a moat. As dossiered, Walter is *a* tenant in that workflow path, not *the* tenant. Rossum, Workist, and UiPath are in the same path with longer customer histories and finished SOC 2 audits, where Walter's SOC 2 and ISO 27001 are explicitly "in progress" (walter.one) -- a gating condition for the 9-figure buyers Walter claims to already have, which itself raises a small but real consistency flag in the dossier.

**Decagon** (2023) is the single-anchor parallel where Walter's architecture actually scores well: workflow infrastructure on top of a single validated LLM capability, in a vertical (manufacturing back office) my *AI Market Clarity* taxonomy would classify as "still wide open" rather than "winners declared." The miracle is isolated -- LLM accuracy on long-tail document schemas -- and the remaining work is GTM, integration depth, and customer-success muscle. This is the one dimension where Walter is genuinely the type of company I fund. It is not enough on its own.

My 36-month forecast: Walter reaches $3-8M ARR with 5-15 mid-market manufacturing logos, then gets acqui-hired by Rossum, Infor, or Epicor in the $30-80M range, or pivots horizontally to general agentic ERP automation under pressure from Workist's deeper manufacturing-vertical positioning. The mechanism is the founder-shipping gap colliding with incumbent integration density: Walter wins enough pilots to be interesting to a strategic, but cannot out-execute Rossum on enterprise procurement cycles long enough to reach the velocity inflection that justified my **Harvey** (2023) markup.

The strongest bear-case-on-my-pass: if the "multiple 9-figure customers" claim is literal and Walter has already written production code into SAP instances at Siemens-class or Rheinmetall-class buyers, then **Color Genomics** (2015) is the precedent -- workflow infrastructure on commodity sequencing built durable enterprise stickiness despite founder-team gaps in the underlying science, because the integration *was* the moat. The dossier could be understating verifiable enterprise traction the founders simply haven't disclosed publicly. Against that: the pivot recency from customer-support automation, the demo-only CTA, the in-progress SOC 2, and the absence of a single named logo are four independent signals that the "9-figure customers" claim is more aspirational than booked. The flip condition is narrow and verifiable: one named manufacturing or defense logo with disclosed six-figure ARR per logo within six months, plus completed SOC 2 Type II. Until then, Walter is the consensus AI-employee-for-X bet at consensus prices in a category with four better-funded incumbents -- "a worse version of the obvious bet."
