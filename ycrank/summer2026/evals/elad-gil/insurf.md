# Insurf -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Insurf is a tech-thesis-intact, everything-else-illegible pre-seed: the single-miracle (LLMs reading an EHR chart and producing a physician-attested appeal packet) is plausibly already validated by Counterforce Health's 70% reported reversal rate and Claimable's ~75%, but Insurf shows up with two co-founders whose LinkedIns won't load, no GitHub, no Twitter, no disclosed pilot count, and a "90-day Access Recovery pilot" page recruiting specialty practices through a calendly.com/chang-insurf/30min booking flow. That high/low pairing -- working underlying capability, zero legible execution rhythm -- is what I have to score.

Anduril (2022) is the load-bearing comparison on market re-opening, and it's where Insurf most disappoints me. Anduril's catalyst was Ukraine in a 24-month window exposing Pentagon software inadequacy -- a structural, regulatory, dollars-on-the-table re-opening that peers were misreading as a graveyard. Insurf's analogous catalyst is the 2025 PBS/NBC/Stateline/ProPublica wave of "AI-vs-AI appeals" coverage plus prior-auth's $35B administrative spend (Health Affairs Scholar, 2024) and 3.2M denials in 2023 (AMA, 2025). The problem: that's a derivative LLM-got-better catalyst, not a payer-side regulatory shift, and the dossier itself names at least five other entrants -- Counterforce, Claimable ($10M raised, Mark Cuban-backed), Fight Health Insurance, PenguinAI, Aegis Health -- racing into the same window. A catalyst that lets your five nearest competitors in is a tide, not a re-opening. Insurf gets some credit for picking provider-side rather than patient-side, but this is not the Anduril template.

Lyft Series C is the failure mechanism I keep checking Insurf against on revenue velocity. The Lyft pass was about a market where #2 captures a fraction of #1's economics and the dossier didn't show share inversion (20VC). Insurf's dossier has *no* revenue, no logos, no month-over-month, no claim-volume disclosed, and a "Pre-Demo Day Summer 2026" status -- which is stage-appropriate, except that Claimable already has $10M raised and Counterforce has a grant-funded distribution head start with 70% success rates published. "If you're an AI company and you're not seeing explosive growth quickly, something's fundamentally broken" (Tim Ferriss #863). Insurf has not been around long enough for that to be damning, but in a crowded field the silent dossier is itself the signal -- this is exactly the "false signal vs. just working" distinction (TechCrunch, Nov 2025), and Insurf hasn't yet shown which side it lives on.

Chang Lu and Bryan Chung on founder shipping: mechanism analysis. The Perplexity pattern is that Aravind pinged me on LinkedIn and showed up a week later with the thing we'd discussed built -- shipping rhythm is the only legible pre-seed signal once you strip pedigree. Insurf's dossier returns "WebFetch blocked, status 999" on both LinkedIns, no GitHub repos, no Twitter, no prior shipped artifacts named. Over 18 months this compounds against them in two ways: (1) recruiting senior engineering talent against Tennr ($162M raised, $605M valuation) requires founder legibility the dossier can't surface; (2) specialty-practice GTM rewards forward-deployed muscle, and we have no evidence either co-founder has built that muscle. The absent-evidence default applies and I'm not going to invent shipping history that public sources cannot find.

Coverage-decision graph: counterfactual on index position. For Insurf to occupy the Stripe-like index position on US health insurance denials -- the workflow path every specialty practice eventually integrates against -- the graph would need to densify before Tennr ($101M Series C Jun 2025, IVP-led) extends from referral parsing into appeals, and before Waystar/R1 build the cannibalizing internal version. Insurf would need to flip my placement on this dimension with: 25+ specialty practice logos within 6 months, a published payer-behavior dataset Surely can already price against, and a defensible BAA posture given HIPAA/physician-attestation exposure. The dossier discloses none of this and the second product Surely is structurally dependent on first-product scale Insurf has not yet demonstrated -- which is a multi-miracle smell on what should be a single-miracle bet.

Single-miracle architecture: parallel to Decagon (2023). Decagon's miracle was LLM accuracy at customer support; the rest was GTM execution in an open *AI Market Clarity* sub-category. Insurf's miracle is comparably contained -- chart-to-appeal-packet generation -- and the underlying capability is *already proven* by Counterforce's 70% and Claimable's ~75% reversal rates. This is the strongest thing in Insurf's dossier and the reason I'm not at a strong-pass: the technology risk is largely retired before Insurf wrote a line of code. The wobble is the Inveto-to-Surely product sequencing, which adds a second miracle (the graph reaches predictive density before payers' counter-AI compresses appeal success rates, per Stateline Nov 2025). Two miracles, compounded probabilities, and I'm marking down.

Forecast: in 36 months Insurf is either acqui-hired by a mid-tier RCM vendor (Waystar, Athenahealth, or Tennr extending into appeals) for under $25M, or quietly wound down after Claimable's $10M war chest and Counterforce's nonprofit distribution compress Insurf's specialty-practice CAC below pilot economics. Surely never ships at scale because the coverage-decision graph never densifies -- the two-product dependency was the giveaway. This is the dimensional signature playing out: tech worked, execution rhythm never became legible, crowded field absorbed the catalyst.

The strongest case against me is the Color Genomics (2015) override: workflow infrastructure on top of a hard layer beats betting on the hard layer, and Insurf going provider-side -- specialty practices, not patients -- is genuinely differentiated from Counterforce, Claimable, and Fight Health Insurance, all of whom target consumers. PenguinAI and Aegis Health are the real provider-side comps and neither has disclosed funding. If Insurf is the workflow layer specialty practices integrate against while patient-side tools handle the long tail, the graph thesis becomes the Coinbase-style "basket of every resolved claim" infrastructure bet. I'd take that argument seriously except for one fact in the dossier that wins: Tennr at $605M post is already in the provider-side document-parsing workflow with $162M to extend, and "appeals" is a natural adjacency from "referrals." Insurf is racing a funded incumbent into its own moat. Flip condition: Insurf discloses 15+ paying specialty practice logos with a >50% measured recovery rate within 6 months of YC Demo Day -- at that point the velocity signal would override the founder opacity and I would write the check at consensus seed price.
