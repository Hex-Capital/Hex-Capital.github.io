# Saffron -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me immediately about Saffron is that nobody disagrees with the premise. "Technical interviews need to adapt to AI" — try saying that at any engineering meetup in San Francisco and watch every head nod. That's the problem. When I hear an idea that makes smart people immediately say "yes, obviously," I get nervous, because it means every other smart team has also seen it. HackerRank already has optional AI tools. CodeSignal already has optional AI tools. Rounds.so built an AI interviewer. Aptora is doing AI-native assessments. This isn't a space suffering from schlep blindness — it's a space overflowing with people who noticed the same obvious gap at the same time. The sweet spot for startups is the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea." Saffron lives in the opposite quadrant: "seems like a good idea" and "probably is a good idea." That quadrant is where returns get competed to zero.

The founders are smart. MIT, Stanford, Harvey Mudd — all in CS and math, which is the right kind of background. Choji publishing at NeurIPS, ICML, and ICCV as an undergrad is legitimately impressive and means the multi-agent scoring system has someone who can actually build it. Yao shipped educational products that reached 50,000+ users, which tells me he can build things people use, not just things that demo well. Two founders share Jane Street exposure, which puts them in proximity to what rigorous technical evaluation looks like. These are clearly talented people. But talent and the animal quality are different things. I look for stories — tell me about a time everything went wrong and how you fought through it. The dossier has credentials where I want anecdotes. No public GitHub repos for two of three founders. No evidence of building Saffron in a dorm room before deciding it was a company. The founding story reads like smart students identifying a market gap, not like someone who kept getting frustrated by a broken process until they couldn't stand it anymore.

That origin story issue cuts deeper than aesthetics. Saffron is a tool for engineering hiring managers — people responsible for evaluating hundreds of candidates across multi-round interview loops. The founders are recent graduates. They've been on the candidate side of technical interviews, not the hiring side. Building for a customer you don't belong to isn't automatically fatal, but it means you're navigating by map instead of by feel. Drew Houston didn't interview a hundred people about file syncing; he kept forgetting his USB drive. The Octopart founders didn't research the electronic parts market; they were physics students who couldn't find components. When I look at Saffron, I see a well-reasoned thesis about where hiring is going, not a founder's raw frustration with a broken tool they use every day. That's a meaningful difference in how quickly they'll find product-market fit, because they'll need to learn what hiring managers actually value through customer conversations rather than from their own experience.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. Someone is going to own AI-era technical assessment, and the market timing is genuinely excellent — this is one of those moments where a fundamental change in how work gets done creates a gap in how people get hired to do that work. Saffron's specific implementation choices are smart: per-line human-vs-AI code attribution is a feature no one else offers and that every engineering manager will want once they see it. Integrating the employer's actual codebase rather than using toy problems is the correct insight — it makes the assessment measure real work, not puzzle-solving ability. At $20 per assessment with zero interviewer hours, the unit economics make Karat's human-interviewer model look like a relic. If these founders have the learning velocity to iterate hard during YC, talk to hundreds of engineering managers, and nail the specific scoring rubrics that make hiring decisions trustworthy, this could work. The data moat — accumulated patterns of how effective engineers interact with AI tools — would be genuinely defensible at scale. And the Anthropic dependency risk cuts both ways: being the assessment platform that's deeply integrated with the leading AI coding agent is also a distribution advantage, at least while the partnership holds.

But I keep circling back to the competitive dynamics. The browser-based IDE with session capture and multi-agent scoring is genuinely complex engineering, but it's the kind of complexity that attracts talented engineers rather than repelling them. It's fun complexity, not regulatory-compliance-and-bank-integration complexity. Replit, GitHub Codespaces, or Cursor could bolt on an assessment layer. HackerRank has $221 million in revenue and every incentive to add AI interaction tracking to their existing platform — that's a feature extension, not a pivot. When the schlep is building a cool technical system rather than doing something genuinely painful that nobody wants to do, you don't get the natural moat that protects Stripe or the companies tackling regulated industries. You get a race, and in a race, the incumbent with 2,000 enterprise customers usually wins.

I'd want to meet these founders and hear their stories before making a final call. There may be an animal quality the dossier doesn't capture — Yao's track record of shipping products that reach tens of thousands of users is the kind of signal that sometimes unfolds into something much bigger when you hear the full narrative. But based on what I can see, this is a competent team chasing a consensus opportunity in a space where well-funded incumbents are already moving. The idea is good. That's exactly my concern.
