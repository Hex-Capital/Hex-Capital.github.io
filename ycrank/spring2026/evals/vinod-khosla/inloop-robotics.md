# InLoop Robotics -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first thing that jumps off the page, and it tells a story I don't like. Nimble has $221 million and a billion-dollar valuation. Dexterity has $291 million and is valued at $1.65 billion. Pickle Robot has nearly $100 million. These are not incumbents with structural blind spots -- they are AI-native robotics companies attacking the same warehouse fulfillment problem with 500x more capital. When I backed Rocket Lab against SpaceX, SpaceX was not building small-sat launchers -- there was a genuinely different market to create. When I look at InLoop versus Nimble and Dexterity, I see the same market, the same customer set, the same fundamental manipulation challenge. The only differentiation is the deployment philosophy: ship imperfect, use humans as fallback, learn faster. That's a go-to-market insight, not a technology moat. And go-to-market insights are the kind of advantage that capital can overwhelm.

Let me ask my first question: if InLoop fully succeeds, does the world change? Warehouse fulfillment gets cheaper for small-to-mid-sized operators. That's a real commercial outcome -- maybe a few billion dollars of value creation. But nobody's life is fundamentally different. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means limitless clean energy for civilization, or Impossible Foods, where success means feeding ten billion people without animal agriculture. InLoop's consequence of success is: warehouses that currently employ people will employ fewer people at lower cost. I don't get excited about that. The consequence magnitude is bounded by the market it serves -- $13.5 billion by 2030 for the entire warehouse robotics category. Even capturing a meaningful share produces a good company, not a world-altering one. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. The ceiling here doesn't support the 50x case given the competitive dynamics.

The human-in-the-loop concept is the strongest piece of the thesis, and I want to be honest about what it could mean if I'm wrong. The bull case is compelling: every robotics company racing toward full autonomy is stuck in the lab, perfecting policies before they deploy. InLoop deploys immediately, collects real failure data in real warehouses, and trains on the edge cases that simulation can never replicate. If the learning rate is fast enough, the data flywheel compounds and InLoop's model becomes the best in the world precisely because it was the worst at the start. This is the Tesla self-driving playbook applied to manipulation -- ship hardware, collect data at scale, let the fleet train the model. If the teleoperator-to-robot ratio drops from 1:3 to 1:50 within 18 months, the unit economics become extraordinary and the data advantage becomes uncatchable. For this to work, two things must be true: the AI improvement curve must be steep enough that intervention rates drop dramatically with each deployment, and the teleoperator cost must remain manageable during the learning period. I see no evidence on either point -- no intervention frequency data, no improvement curves, no teleoperator staffing ratios. The concept is beautiful. The evidence is absent.

The founders present a mixed signal. Sharfeddine winning the 2025 BEHAVIOR Challenge is a genuine credential -- that's a competitive demonstration of robotic manipulation skill, not a resume line. Rizali co-founding Germany's largest student robotics initiative shows organizational ambition beyond individual contribution. These are talented young engineers. But talented young engineers from TUM are not the profile I get most excited about. My highest-conviction founder bets have been outsiders who don't know what can't be done -- a self-taught New Zealander building rockets, a teenage dropout building weapons systems. InLoop's founders are well-trained insiders to the robotics field. They're doing what their training prepared them to do, which is the opposite of the naive irreverence that generates non-linear outcomes. And one co-founder -- Feduniak -- is a complete diligence gap. No education, no work history, no credentials of any kind publicly available. That matters when I'm evaluating the gene pool.

The RaaS model with zero upfront CapEx is the right packaging for the SMB warehouse segment, and it does align with my instinct toward making expensive things affordable. Traditional automation at $100K+ per installation locks out most small operators. Removing that barrier is genuinely important. But this is affordable automation for commercial operations -- it's not making a basic human need 10x cheaper. The affordability play here is real but shallow compared to what gets me to write large checks. I funded Curai because AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost could make healthcare accessible to billions. InLoop makes warehouse picking accessible to thousands of smaller operators. The scale of human impact is fundamentally different.

This is a competent team with a defensible architectural insight in a market where capital and data advantages will determine the winner. I don't see the non-linear technical breakthrough that would let a $500K pre-seed company outrun competitors with 500x more funding. The consequence of success is commercial, not civilizational. The approach is clever but replicable -- the dossier itself notes that Sharfeddine forked Physical Intelligence's open-source framework, which means the foundation models are commoditizing. I'd need to see evidence that the data flywheel is actually compounding -- real intervention rate curves, real teleoperator economics -- before this moves from concept to conviction. Without that, I'm passing.
