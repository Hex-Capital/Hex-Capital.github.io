# Gikl, Inc -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is a leverage architecture problem hiding inside an inspiring analogy. "Coding assistants turned millions into software developers -- we're doing the same for science." That's a powerful frame. But it conceals a structural asymmetry that changes everything. Code has a built-in verification mechanism: you run it, and it either works or it doesn't. The compiler is the judge. In science, verification requires the very expertise the platform claims to democratize. A crowdsourced proof sketch on an open math problem still needs a mathematician to confirm it's valid. A hypothesis about protein folding still needs domain experts to evaluate whether it's novel, testable, and non-trivial. The leverage ratio in coding assistants is high because verification is cheap. The leverage ratio in science is constrained because verification is expensive -- and that cost doesn't disappear by adding AI agents to the loop. This is the central question ScienceSwarm must answer, and I don't see evidence they've confronted it yet.

On specific knowledge: Yamamoto is the more interesting founder for this bet. A physics PhD from Rice, publications in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, co-authorship with a Fields Medalist -- he has actually walked the 12-year training pipeline that ScienceSwarm claims to compress. That's lived experience, not book knowledge. He then spent nine years in Meta's core Llama group, which means he understands frontier model capabilities from the inside -- what they can actually do versus what demo slides suggest. That dual expertise (practicing scientist + AI systems builder) is rare and relevant. Vajda's background in scaling generative media at Meta is strong engineering credibility, but it maps less directly to the problem of scientific discovery. Together, they clearly have the technical depth to build sophisticated AI systems. The gap I see is between knowing how to build AI infrastructure and knowing how to design a community platform where non-experts produce valid scientific contributions. Stack Overflow worked because Spolsky and Atwood had spent years understanding the specific social mechanics of developer knowledge-sharing -- reputation systems, voting dynamics, the precise threshold where community moderation outperforms editorial curation. Building ScienceSwarm requires equivalent insight into how citizen science communities actually function, and I don't see that knowledge in either founder's background.

The competitive landscape reveals something important about positioning. Google's AI Co-Scientist is deployed at 17 DOE National Labs. OpenAI is building automated researchers. Sakana has $379M to throw at the problem. FutureHouse has $70M. These players are all betting on the same macro thesis -- AI transforms scientific discovery -- but they're building tools that replace human researchers with autonomous agents. ScienceSwarm occupies a third position: augmenting non-expert humans rather than replacing expert ones. In principle, this is exactly the kind of gap I look for -- the structural blind spot where big tech optimizes for one product category while a startup builds something fundamentally different. The problem is that this "third position" may be empty for a reason. The historical precedents for productive citizen science (Foldit, Polymath Project) are tiny in scale and predated modern AI. If frontier models become capable enough to do the science autonomously, the "human in the loop" becomes a bottleneck rather than an asset. And if they're not capable enough, then non-experts with AI assistance won't clear the bar either.

The strongest bull case: Yamamoto's specific knowledge actually does give him unique insight into where frontier models fall short -- and therefore where human intuition, even from non-experts, fills a real gap. Maybe he's seen from inside the Llama group exactly where LLMs fail at scientific reasoning, and he's designed ScienceSwarm to exploit those specific failure modes by routing human creativity to the precise junctures where AI gets stuck. The "built for AI agents" framing suggests the platform isn't just crowdsourcing from humans -- it's orchestrating human-AI collaboration at a systems level that requires deep understanding of both sides. If that's what's happening, this could be the protocol layer for scientific discovery -- the marketplace that sits between AI capability and human insight, growing more valuable as both improve. That's a compounding bet with genuine network effects. But the dossier shows zero traction, zero community, zero evidence the cold-start problem has been addressed. The platform's value is entirely prospective.

I keep coming back to the leverage test. The company that excites me creates a new form of leverage that didn't previously exist -- enabling a new type of person to do a new type of work. If ScienceSwarm actually works, it would be among the most powerful leverage multipliers ever built: turning curious non-experts into productive scientific contributors. That's not incremental. That's a phase change. But "if it works" is bearing enormous weight. No monetization model, no traction signals, no evidence the core hypothesis has been validated even at the smallest scale. The founders are clearly intelligent and technically formidable. But I'm investing in a hypothesis about the nature of scientific verification -- that AI can compress it enough to make crowdsourced science viable -- and neither founder's specific knowledge directly addresses that hypothesis. It's a bet I'd want to watch, not one I'd fund today from my own money. The founders should come back when they have ten examples of a non-expert producing a verifiable scientific contribution through the platform. That would change everything.
