# Veriad -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with what Veriad is actually building: a tool that checks whether ads comply with rules before they get published. If they succeed -- completely, totally succeed -- ads get reviewed in twenty seconds instead of two days. The advertising industry continues exactly as before. Enterprises continue spending a trillion dollars a year on ads. The compliance department just runs faster. This is precisely the kind of company where the founders have been so careful about reducing their probability of failure -- real market, known buyer, proven technology, clear pain point -- that they've engineered away any meaningful consequence of success. A faster compliance review process is not the same as replacing a trillion-dollar system. Nobody's world changes.

The dossier frames this as a "$100 billion wasted on ineffective ads due to non-compliance" problem. Let's examine that number honestly. Even if it's accurate, Veriad doesn't capture $100 billion -- it captures a SaaS fee for reviewing content. The value accrues to the advertiser, not to the compliance tool. This is the economics of a workflow automation vendor, not a platform that displaces an incumbent system. Compare this to what Square did to payment processing: Square didn't make existing POS terminals 30% faster. It created an entirely new category of merchant -- the coffee shop, the farmer's market vendor, the taxi driver -- that couldn't accept cards before. Square changed who could participate in the economy. Veriad changes how quickly a Fortune 500 marketing team gets approval on a banner ad.

The technology foundation gives me further pause. The dossier itself states the quiet part clearly: "The core capability -- applying LLMs to check content against rules -- has low technical barriers to replication." This is not Juniper building a TCP/IP router when Cisco's CTO said it was impossible. This is applying generally available LLM capabilities to a well-understood compliance workflow. Sedric has $22 million and is doing similar work for financial services. Haast has $7.2 million and clients like Telstra. IntelligenceBank just absorbed Red Marker -- the most direct historical competitor -- and can bundle compliance into their DAM platform at marginal cost. When your technology can be replicated by any funded competitor applying the same foundation models to the same rule-checking task, you don't have disruption potential. You have a feature.

I'll give the bull case genuine consideration, because the founders deserve it. Rohan and Anton connected through CoLoop, a YC S21 company, and they've both operated in enterprise sales at significant scale -- seven-figure deals, Fortune 500 clients. They built and sold internal compliance tools before forming Veriad, which means they discovered this pain point through direct customer interaction. That's real founder-market fit for a workflow problem. If they execute well on enterprise sales, embed deeply through DAM integrations, and accumulate proprietary compliance rule sets across industries and jurisdictions, they could build meaningful switching costs. Red Marker's acquisition does leave a gap in the standalone market. The timing of LLM capability crossing the threshold for reliable multimodal content analysis is genuine. And the regulatory environment is only getting more complex. If everything breaks their way -- land major accounts quickly, build compliance knowledge graphs that competitors can't replicate, establish the category before Sprinklr or Adobe build it as a feature -- this could be a $500 million compliance SaaS company. That's a real business. It's just not a business whose consequence of success justifies the kind of bet I make.

What the founders are demonstrating here is competence in enterprise sales execution, not contrarian courage or outsider imagination. "Building and selling internal compliance tools to Fortune 500 companies" and then productizing that experience is the textbook industry-insider playbook. Nobody thinks this is impossible. Nobody is dismissing them as crazy. The experts aren't threatened because this is exactly what the experts would build. When I backed Pat Brown, food industry executives laughed at a biochemist trying to make hamburgers from plants. When I backed Peter Beck, aerospace engineers dismissed a New Zealander with no pedigree building orbital rockets. Veriad's approach validates the existing compliance workflow rather than making it obsolete. That's the difference between disrupting an industry and servicing it.

The team pairing is sound for what they're building -- technical founder plus commercial founder covers the two critical functions of an enterprise SaaS company. But they're too smart to spend the best years of their careers making compliance review twenty seconds faster. I'd tell them: take that enterprise sales capability, take that technical skill in building AI agents, and aim at something where success means the world genuinely works differently.
