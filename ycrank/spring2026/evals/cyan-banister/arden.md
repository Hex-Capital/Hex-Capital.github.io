# Arden -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Aryaman Khanna left Databricks AI and David Lomelin left MIT mechanistic interpretability to point computer-use agents at SOX testing — and that sentence tells me what kind of company Arden is before I read another line. Arden's highest dimension is its raw building chops (Lomelin shipped Blueprint to 2,500 businesses before this, Khanna has Berkeley CS plus a Databricks badge), and Arden's lowest dimension is contrarian conviction — Midship raised $4.15M from Costanoa the same month Arden showed up in the YC P26 batch, and Oxus is in the YC family pitching the same automation. This is a credentialed, fast-shipping young team running into a category that is already consensus among sophisticated investors. That signature — high build velocity, zero contrarian texture — is the Pass Example I keep on file, almost word for word.

The contrarian dimension is where Arden fails my room-of-VCs test most cleanly. My HIGH rung here is Anduril — defense AI when Silicon Valley wouldn't take the meeting — and Contraline, where male contraception made VCs visibly squirm. The LOW rung is the Pass Example: "I was at Google and saw the opportunity," consensus AI in a consensus market. Arden sits one notch above the LOW rung only because SOX is unsexy enough that tourists won't show up, but the dossier nails the position: a direct AI-native competitor (Midship) with audit-domain founders from Deloitte, PayPal, and Faire already has named public-company logos including "a top social media platform and major fintechs," and a YC sibling (Oxus) is pitching the same workflow. Nobody is going to be uncomfortable at a dinner party hearing "AI agents for SOX." That's the tell.

The biographical forge is where I spend my opening question, and Arden's answer is the wrong shape. My HIGH rung is James Proud at Substrate — the founder I tattooed the company logo for, "forged in fire and came out unbroken." The LOW rung is the credentialed market-spotter from the Pass Example. Arden lands closer to the LOW rung: Khanna and Lomelin are clearly capable — the "19 year olds who applied to YC three weeks past the deadline" detail is the only piece of the dossier that has any forge texture, the kind of resourcefulness I respect — but neither founder has documented Big Four, internal audit, or SOX operating experience, and the audit-firm-lobby customer discovery story is market-spotting after the fact, not a biographical inevitability. Lomelin shipping Blueprint to 2,500 businesses is real building, and that keeps Arden out of the basement, but I don't yet hear a story about why SOX *had* to find these two specifically. Midship's founders, by contrast, lived inside the work.

The invisible-system test is where Arden looks superficially promising and then unwinds. My HIGH rung is Affirm attacking credit card opacity, Ownwell attacking property tax, Uber attacking the medallion cartel — systems that extract from people who don't understand them. The LOW rung is anything serving an already-transparent market. Arden looks like it might attack Big Four hourly billing — and there's a real channel-conflict insight buried in the dossier — but the subjects of SOX extraction are public companies and their CFOs, not vulnerable ordinary people without information or power. The opacity Arden is dissolving is internal compliance theater, which is a real cost, but it's not the kind of hidden tax that connects to my own biography. Closer to the MID-LOW rung than the high one.

Defensibility is where Arden has its second-best showing and where the dossier is most honest with me. My HIGH rung is Flock Safety's camera network and Substrate's semiconductor stack; my LOW rung is HQ Trivia and IRL — fad-shaped products with no compounding moat. Arden's 15+ enterprise integrations into Workday, Okta, NetSuite, ServiceNow, Active Directory, and AuditBoard is real switching-cost work, and SOC 2 Type I "in progress" is table stakes being assembled. But the dossier writes my own warning back to me: "the technical core (LLM agents + connector library + workpaper generation) is replicable by any well-resourced compliance-software team." That's not infrastructure at inflection — that's a feature race against Midship's $4.15M Costanoa round and AuditBoard's incumbent API access. Arden sits MID-LOW. Cascading impact is the cleanest LOW: serving Fortune 1000 internal audit teams is not Wonderschool expanding childcare or Postmates expanding delivery work.

My 36-month forecast on Arden: acqui-hired by AuditBoard or Workiva in the $30–80M range, or stalled below $2M ARR while Midship's audit-domain founders win the lighthouse logos and lock in Big Four channel acceptance. The mechanism is straightforward — in a consensus category where the technical core is replicable and a direct competitor has both the domain-credentialed team and the disclosed enterprise customers, the structural-moat winner is whichever team external auditors trust first, and trust in PCAOB AS 2201 workpapers is built by people who have lived inside Big Four engagements, which neither Arden founder has.

The strongest bull case — and I want to take it seriously because I have written checks against my own rules before — is that Arden is a 19-year-old building bet at a real LLM tool-use inflection, the KPMG 2025 SOX Survey confirms a talent-shortage tailwind that Midship's existence validates, and Lomelin's prior 2,500-business deployment proves shipping velocity that audit-domain Deloitte alums historically lack. RTFKT is my closest Known Exception precedent: I overrode "infrastructure-grade defensibility" because cultural timing was narrow and Nike was an identifiable acquirer. The bull move would be to call AuditBoard the identifiable acquirer and write a small check on velocity. The reason I don't: RTFKT had genuine cultural prescience at a category boundary nobody else saw, and Arden is in a category where two competitors and a YC sibling are visibly racing the same workflow at the same moment — that is not a narrow window, that is a crowd. The verdict flips if Arden lands a named Fortune 500 SOX customer with a public reference and a completed SOC 2 Type II within twelve months — that would be evidence of the audit-domain trust I currently see Midship building faster.
