# Modern -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Modern is a contested-schlep bet where the founders haven't personally lived the pain they're solving. Seb Poole's prior company was Frankie Health (workplace mental health, sold to Unmind February 2023). Aleks Tomovski ran internal developer platforms at Wix Code and Canva. Neither of those biographies has a person sitting inside an enterprise IT queue at 11pm trying to provision laptops, and the dossier on Modern does not contain a single sentence that says "we built this because we kept hitting X ourselves." That absence is the signature of the company.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the right HIGH-rung comparison for Modern on the schlep axis, because Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind is what made the wedge defensible -- every other YC applicant in that batch routed around the regulatory work, and the work itself became the moat. Modern sits in the same kind of zone on paper: ServiceNow displacement, SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, GDPR, integrations across thirteen named systems from Workday to Cisco Meraki, multi-quarter enterprise procurement. The mechanism is identical -- unpleasant work as moat. Where Modern diverges from **Coinbase** (2012) is that Modern is not alone in the schlep. Serval raised $127M from Sequoia and Redpoint at a $1B mark; Atomicwork raised $40.3M from Khosla; ServiceNow already paid $2.1B for Moveworks. The schlep is real, but in 2026 it is a crowded schlep, which is a different kind of asset than Armstrong had in 2012.

The lowest dimension for Modern is the organic-idea test, and the failure mechanism here is the one from the Pass Example -- founders who "spotted a market opportunity" rather than founders who built a tool for themselves. **Dropbox** (2007) is the canonical inverse: Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB stick at MIT, then shipped the demo, then I read the artifact on Hacker News. Modern's dossier reads the opposite way. The website opens with the line that legacy ITSM "was built to manage tickets" rather than eliminate them, which is competitive analysis, not autobiography. Poole's biography is enterprise mental-health sales (Disney, Uber, MLB); Tomovski's is developer tooling at Wix and Canva. Internal developer platforms touch IT-adjacent surfaces, but the dossier never claims Tomovski was the one drowning in access tickets at Canva. The idea may be correct, but it is not organic to either founder's own day.

On the animal-resourcefulness axis -- Poole has a prior exit, which is the closest thing in the dossier to a pre-funding artifact, and that is real. Frankie Health raised through to acquisition; before that he raised 20M+ AUD on a Hong Kong e-commerce venture in 2016. That is a track record. What is missing for Modern is the specific pre-check stunt -- the cereal boxes, the Android-wallet-after-rejection, the unscalable manual operation. The 62%, 74%, 80% customer testimonials on getmodern.ai are attributed to "Global IT team," "Series C healthtech," "Large retailer" rather than named logos, and that anonymization at YC-Spring-2026 stage is the tell. Animals leave tracks with names on them.

Hacker genealogy -- counterfactually, if Tomovski had a GitHub trail from his Wix Code or Canva internal-platform years, or if either founder showed up in an open-source ITSM project before founding Modern, the score would flip up a tier. Wix Code (now Wix Studio) is a serious developer platform, and the Canva internal developer platform is non-trivial infrastructure; Tomovski's resume is platform-engineering-real. But the dossier explicitly notes "No public repos found" for both founders, and Poole's degree is B.Com Finance from Monash. There is technical lineage in the team, but it is credential-shaped rather than hacker-tribe-shaped, and I over-index on the latter because of how the **Viaweb** (1995) Lisp lineage was forged.

Co-founder configuration for Modern is two people, which fits the pair-or-trio preference from "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010), with complementary coverage -- repeat-founder CEO plus platform engineer. The gap is the explicit note in the dossier: "No public data on co-founder history; no shared prior employer identified in Phase 3 searches." **Stripe** (2010) was brothers who had been hacking together their whole lives; **Reddit** (2005) was dorm roommates. Modern's pair could be Charleston-and-Chesky, who met at design school -- which is fine -- or it could be a pair stitched together for the company, which is a red flag from the same essay. The dossier doesn't resolve it.

My 36-month forecast for Modern: $2-5M ARR on mid-market healthtech and tech-company IT departments, then a tuck-in acquisition by ServiceNow or Atlassian in the $50-150M range, on the same playbook ServiceNow ran with Moveworks at $2.1B but at a fraction because Serval and Atomicwork will have absorbed the larger logos by then. That is not a black swan; that is a competent secondary outcome. The mechanism producing it is the signature -- a real schlep with a non-organic idea attracts founders who can sell and ship but who flinch at the unexpected ten-foot wall, because they didn't build it for themselves and won't smell the right next move.

The strongest objection is the Auctomatic exception. Poole sold Frankie Health to Unmind in February 2023; the Collisons sold Auctomatic before I funded **Stripe** (2010), and the prior-exit pattern was a structural override of my normal YC-process expectation. If Frankie-to-Unmind is the Auctomatic of Modern, the bear case dissolves. The reason it doesn't sting enough: Auctomatic was the Collisons hacking commerce APIs, and **Stripe** (2010) was the Collisons hacking commerce APIs again -- thematic and technical continuity. Frankie was workplace mental health; Modern is IT service management. The skill that transferred is enterprise sales, not the substrate. The verdict flips to Invest if Modern produces a named-logo customer with retained ARR at the YC demo day -- a Disney or an Uber under contract, the way Poole booked them at Frankie -- because that retires the testimonial-anonymity tell in a single stroke.
