# Human Archive -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Human Archive is that everyone wants to invest in robotics right now. Physical Intelligence just closed $1.1 billion. Goldman Sachs is publishing projections about humanoid robot shipments. Scale AI -- a company doing $2 billion in revenue -- pivoted into robotics data collection in September 2025 with hundreds of contractors. This is the opposite of unpopular and difficult to assess. This is consensus forming in real time, and I've learned that when the consensus forms, the price gets wrong and the structural advantage for early investors evaporates. The most promising companies I've backed -- SpaceX when private space was laughable, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded clinically insane -- were ideas people actively dismissed. Nobody is dismissing robotics data infrastructure in early 2026. The venture capital ecosystem is pouring billions into this category, which means four college dropouts competing against Scale AI's distribution machine and $13.8 billion valuation isn't a David-and-Goliath underdog story. It's a footrace where the other runners started with a massive head start.

When I evaluate founders, I always start with the same question: tell me your story, not the business. I want to hear about the specific hardship or obsession that makes THIS person the inevitable founder for THIS problem. And here, that thread is missing from what I can see. These are four smart students from Berkeley and Stanford who identified a market opportunity and moved fast. That's admirable -- and dropping out, relocating to India to build data collection operations, deploying custom sensor hardware across residential environments -- that demonstrates real execution instinct. Raj's background in farming, Samay's computer vision research at ML@B, Shloke's CHI publication in mechanical engineering -- these are competent people. But competence and opportunism aren't what I invest on. I invest on the founder whose biography and mission are inseparable, where the problem chose them as much as they chose it. Nothing in this dossier tells me why these four people are compelled to solve the robotics data problem specifically, versus the dozens of other AI infrastructure opportunities they could have pursued from Berkeley and Stanford.

The bull case deserves real engagement, because the structural argument is sound. Model companies like Generalist AI and Physical Intelligence face a genuine conflict -- they can't easily sell their proprietary training data to competing labs without undermining their own moats. That creates a real opening for a pure-play data provider who serves everyone. The custom hardware approach, if the multimodal alignment quality is meaningfully better than Scale AI's contractor-recorded video, could be a durable technical differentiator. And moving operations to India isn't just cost arbitrage -- it's access to diverse residential and manufacturing environments that American-based collection would struggle to replicate. If Human Archive genuinely builds the largest annotated multimodal robotics dataset with superior modality alignment, they could become the essential data layer for an entire generation of robot foundation models. That's a huge outcome. The question is whether I believe four early-career founders can outrun Scale AI's resources and survive the vertical integration risk from their own customers -- and whether the custom hardware moat is real or a temporary head start that any well-funded competitor can close in twelve months.

Four co-founders is a lot of people to navigate through the brutally hard years ahead. After HQ Trivia, I always probe how co-founders resolve disagreements, especially in structures where roles and authority might not be clearly delineated. Two Berkeley students, one Stanford student, and one operations-focused founder from Berkeley -- that's a team assembled from school networks, which can be strong or fragile depending on whether the relationships have been pressure-tested. They relocated to Asia together, which suggests alignment on the big bet, but I have no visibility into how they handle the smaller daily conflicts that compound over time. This isn't a red flag -- it's an absence of signal where I need positive signal to feel comfortable.

Where I do give this team credit is in their willingness to do something genuinely hard operationally. Deploying custom sensor hardware across residential and manufacturing environments in India, managing videographers and operations staff across Ahmedabad and Goa, building QA and anonymization pipelines for cross-border data -- that's messy, unglamorous infrastructure work that most YC founders wouldn't touch. It reminds me a little of the Crusoe team going to North Dakota to work with gas flares, choosing the hard physical thing when everyone else was building software. But with Crusoe, the founders had independently arrived at a physics insight nobody else saw. Here, the insight -- that robotics models need real-world multimodal data -- is widely shared. The execution is differentiated. The insight is not.

I'm passing on this one. Not because the founders aren't capable or the market isn't real, but because nothing here triggers the conviction signals I rely on. There's no biographical inevitability connecting these founders to this problem. The market is consensus, not weird. The competitive landscape includes a $13.8 billion incumbent that entered their exact space four months ago. And I can't see the economic access story -- they're selling data to well-funded frontier labs, not creating transparent systems or opening doors for people who've been locked out. Smart founders, real market, wrong investor.
