# Tepali -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start where I always start: if Tepali succeeds completely -- captures the entire medspa management software market -- what happens? Medspas run more efficiently. Thirty thousand small businesses schedule better, track inventory better, understand their ad spend better. The U.S. software TAM is $100 million today, growing to maybe $1.3 billion by 2034. That's a nice business. It is also precisely the kind of outcome where I stop evaluating. The consequence of success is a well-run vertical SaaS company in a sub-billion-dollar market. Nobody's life changes. No trillion-dollar system gets restructured. The medspa industry continues to exist exactly as it does today, just with better dashboards. I have spent forty years looking for companies where success means the world operates differently. A better operating system for Botox clinics does not meet that bar.

What concerns me more than the market size is the playbook. Vishnu built "all-in-one operational software for multi-location service businesses" at Dripos -- coffee shops. Now he's building all-in-one operational software for multi-location service businesses at Tepali -- medspas. This is the horizontal application of experience, not first-principles reinvention. I have seen this pattern a hundred times: take what worked in vertical A, apply it to vertical B, add the technology du jour. Today that technology is "AI-native." Five years ago it was "mobile-first." Ten years ago it was "cloud-native." The playbook is identical. I look for founders who are attacking something experts dismiss as impossible, who bring an outsider's irreverence to an established system. These founders are insiders executing a proven template in an adjacent vertical. That's competent entrepreneurship, and it will probably generate a reasonable outcome for a typical seed fund. But it's not the kind of bet that produces 100x returns, and it's not the kind of problem that demands a solution regardless of commercial viability.

The strongest bull case I can construct: the AI-native architecture is genuinely different from what Boulevard or Zenoti can bolt on. If the unified data model -- where the AI simultaneously sees ad spend, patient records, scheduling patterns, and inventory -- creates operational intelligence that makes medspas 30-50% more profitable, that's a real wedge. And the timing has some logic: 20%+ annual growth in medspa locations means thousands of greenfield operators making first-time software decisions with no switching costs and no legacy system to migrate from. The marketing-to-LTV attribution feature addresses a genuine pain point that current tools don't solve natively. If Tepali could prove that its AI layer generates measurably superior business outcomes -- not just consolidation convenience but actual revenue uplift -- it could become the default choice for new medspa locations. That story works for a $200-300M outcome. For most investors, that's compelling enough.

But I am not most investors. Boulevard has $188 million in funding and an $800 million valuation. Zenoti is a unicorn powering 30,000 businesses. Vagaro has $66 million in revenue. These incumbents are not sleeping -- Boulevard's $80 million Series D explicitly earmarked AI investment. The window for "we're AI-native and they're not" is measured in quarters, not years. Every component Tepali offers -- scheduling, CRM, charting, inventory, payment processing -- is a solved problem with multiple existing solutions. The AI assistant layer is the only differentiation, and it runs on the same foundation models available to every competitor. I learned from KiOR that technology achievement without durable economic advantage is a trap. Here, the technology isn't even a breakthrough -- it's the application of generally available LLM capabilities to a well-understood domain. The Lotus-to-Tepali rename during the batch adds a note of concern about conviction in the specific vertical, though pivots during YC are common enough that I wouldn't weight it heavily in isolation.

The founders are capable people -- CMU CS, NYU Stern, PJT Partners, Rilla, Dripos. They will build a competent product. They may well build a $20-30 million ARR business. And that is exactly the outcome I find inconsequential. They have reduced their probability of failure to the point where the consequence of success does not justify the magnitude of effort that building a company demands. I would tell these founders: you are too talented to spend the next decade optimizing medspa scheduling. Go find a problem where success means an entire industry becomes obsolete.
