# Salesgraph -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Gong has raised $584M and generates $300M+ in ARR. Clari raised $520M and merged with Salesloft in December 2025. People.ai has taken $198M. Salesgraph is a two-person team entering what might be the most heavily capitalized, consensus-attractive corridor in all of enterprise software — B2B sales intelligence — with a product built on API aggregation and LLM summarization, capabilities the dossier itself describes as "generally available technologies." When Lee Jacobs and I talk about avoiding "some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper," this is the archetype we're describing. Salesgraph connects to Salesforce, HubSpot, Gong, Clari, ZoomInfo, Clearbit, 6sense, Salesloft, Outreach, Slack, Zoom, and LinkedIn — ten-plus integrations maintained by two people — and synthesizes their data into pre-call briefs and business case documents. That's a feature, not a company. Any of those incumbent platforms could ship this in a quarter, and the Clari-Salesloft merger specifically created the kind of combined data surface that makes Salesgraph's cross-tool aggregation less necessary, not more.

Ricardo Nunez and Ruhan Ponnada were among the first ten employees at Mintlify, where they built an internal business case generator that reportedly helped close enterprise deals at higher ACV. Ruhan holds a BS in Computer Science from Georgia Tech and did product management at Microsoft Mesh. Ricardo brings full-stack engineering and sales experience from Mintlify. When I ask "tell me your story, not the business," what I'd hear from the Salesgraph founders is: we worked at a startup, noticed sales reps stitching together context manually, and built a tool to fix it. That's a market observation — it's how an employee at a growth-stage company identifies a workflow gap. It's not biographical inevitability. Nobody was forged in fire here. The problem didn't find these founders through lived experience; they encountered it through their jobs. I've seen this pattern hundreds of times: talented young engineers from a hot startup spinning out a tool they built internally. Sometimes it works. But the founders who survive the valley of death — and in this market, with these incumbents, there will absolutely be a valley — are the ones who can't walk away because the problem is woven into who they are. Salesgraph's founders could pivot to the next workflow problem without existential cost. That's the difference.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make for Salesgraph goes like this: the business model conflict between Gong and Clari means neither can build a truly cross-tool context layer — Gong won't deeply integrate Clari's data and vice versa — creating a structural opening for a neutral aggregator. The Mintlify prototype is real validation, not a slide deck. The $4.85B sales intelligence market growing to $10.25B by 2032 provides a massive tailwind. And the "why now" is genuine — Bain reported in 2025 that early AI deployments in sales boosted win rates by 30%, and Gartner projects 95% of seller research workflows will begin with AI by 2027. Salesgraph could become the Slack of sales context: the layer everyone builds on top of. That's a real argument. But it collapses when I apply my framework. Slack had network effects — every person added made it more valuable for everyone else. Salesgraph has API dependencies. If Salesforce changes its API terms, or Gong restricts third-party access (as platforms routinely do when they see a competitor building on their data), Salesgraph's entire value proposition degrades overnight. The two-person team maintaining ten-plus integrations isn't a feature — it's an existential fragility. And none of my known exceptions rescue this: there's no cultural inflection point (HQ Trivia exception), no narrow timing window with identifiable acquirers (RTFKT exception), no genre-defying weirdness (Superplastic exception). Salesgraph is a competent product in a crowded market.

Salesgraph targets mid-market and enterprise B2B sales teams in Dev Tools, SaaS, and Cybersecurity — professionals using Salesforce and Gong who need their pre-call prep automated. When I look for the invisible system beneath a business — the opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it — I find nothing here. The sales technology market is one of the most transparent, well-served categories in all of software. Sales reps aren't trapped in an information asymmetry; they're busy professionals who want a faster workflow. Compare Salesgraph to Affirm, which attacked credit card opacity — a hidden financial structure that extracted billions from consumers who couldn't see the true cost of their borrowing. Or Ownwell, which automated property tax appeals against a system designed to be too complex for homeowners to navigate. Salesgraph makes sales reps marginally more efficient at a job they already understand. There's no hidden tax being eliminated, no gatekeeper being disintermediated, no power being shifted from an institution to an individual. The cascading real-world impact is similarly absent — success would help well-compensated sales professionals at companies that already pay for Gong and ZoomInfo. Nobody's economic access expands. No new participants enter a market. This is optimization for the already-optimized.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Salesgraph demonstrated that its context layer generates proprietary deal-outcome data that no individual tool possesses — meaning the cross-tool synthesis creates genuinely new predictive signal about which deals close and why, with measurable lift over any single incumbent's analytics — and a named enterprise customer confirmed they'd built workflow dependencies around it that make switching costly. That would transform Salesgraph from an aggregation layer into an infrastructure layer with a data moat. Until then, the absence of defensibility signals, the consensus market positioning, and the career-derived rather than biography-derived founding story place Salesgraph firmly outside my investment framework. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Contrarian Conviction (Salesgraph operates in the most consensus-attractive space in enterprise software, competing against $1.3B+ in incumbent funding) and Invisible System (there is no opaque, extractive structure being disrupted — the market is transparent and well-served).
