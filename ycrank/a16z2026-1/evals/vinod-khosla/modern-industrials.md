# Modern Industrials -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Modern Industrials has a nearly identical twin. Ventura, backed by YC in W26, is automating quoting for industrial distributors with the same value proposition, the same vertical focus, and a CEO who already exited a wholesale distribution SaaS company. When two accelerator-backed startups launch simultaneously into the same narrow use case with no discernible technical differentiation between them, what you're looking at is a market where the core challenge — parsing unstructured quote requests and matching them to product catalogs — is addressable by any competent ML team with access to the same foundation models. The dossier states this explicitly: "The core technical challenge is addressable by multiple well-funded teams." That's not a technology wedge. That's a race to execute, and execution races in B2B SaaS produce 3-5x outcomes, not 50x outcomes.

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if Modern Industrials fully succeeds? Every lumber and building materials distributor in North America processes quotes instantly, 24/7, with zero manual data entry. The consequence is that distributors have better margins, faster turnaround, and fewer back-office headaches. That is a useful business. It is not a changed world. Nobody who couldn't afford a home before suddenly can. No basic human need becomes radically cheaper. The $450B distribution industry becomes incrementally more efficient, and Modern Industrials captures some fraction of that efficiency gain as SaaS revenue. This is precisely the category I pass on — AI that automates 40% of a workflow in a single profession. If you're going to take the risk of starting a company, build the thing that eliminates the cost structure entirely, not the thing that trims it.

The team is legitimately strong on paper. Ankit Bhargava built agentic AI systems at xAI and worked on Uber's surge pricing ML. Vatsal Bhargava led Google Ads bidding ML and shipped a side project to $10K MRR. Austin Mao was top 5% at McKinsey and ran growth at a Sequoia-backed startup. These are capable people. But capability applied to a modest ambition produces a modest outcome. I'd rather back a naive 19-year-old attempting to replace Lockheed Martin — as I did with Mach Industries — than three credentialed engineers automating quote processing for lumberyards. The team's strength is technical AI capability, but no founder has any documented experience in building materials or distribution. In a relationship-driven industry where sales cycles to traditional distributors require domain credibility, the outsider advantage I usually celebrate only pays off when the ambition justifies the learning curve. "AI Sales Engine for LBM distributors" does not provoke incredulity from anyone — it provokes a nod and a question about pricing.

The strongest bull case for Modern Industrials: the building materials distribution industry is genuinely massive, genuinely under-digitized, and the "gone live with national players" claim from Vatsal Bhargava's LinkedIn suggests they're already past the cold-start problem. If they lock in three or four national distributors before Ventura or Flitch do, switching costs compound — each deployment trains the AI on proprietary pricing rules and customer preferences, creating distributor-specific moats. The data advantage is real in theory. And the founding team's personal bond — brothers plus a 20-year friendship with Austin — could produce the resilience that startup teams need when things break. These are not trivial signals. But data moats in vertical SaaS take years to build, and with Ventura running the same playbook with deeper domain expertise (prior exit in wholesale distribution), Modern Industrials is fighting for data lock-in against a competitor who speaks the customer's language natively. The dossier marks this data moat as "unproven at current stage," and I agree.

Nothing about Modern Industrials maps to the bets I get most excited about. There is no path to radical affordability for end consumers — this is operational efficiency for intermediaries, not cost collapse for the people buying lumber. The technical approach is applying existing LLM capabilities to document parsing, which is the definition of my low-anchor example: "a YC company applying existing LLM APIs to automate a workflow with marginal efficiency gains — wrapper, not wedge." The ambition level is to be the best quoting software for lumber distributors, which no informed observer would call impossible or even particularly hard. Consequence Magnitude is the dimension that most decisively determines my verdict here — if Modern Industrials succeeds completely, I see a $500M-$1B SaaS company, and that outcome does not justify the attention cost of an investment when my portfolio is built around companies where success means the world is visibly different.
