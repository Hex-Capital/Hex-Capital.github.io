# Kuli -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Kuli is a competent operator-led B2B workflow tool whose highest signal -- Michael Hodara's actual time allocating $20M of paid ads at Aircall -- sits inside its lowest signal: an enterprise sales motion to mid-market consumer brands like Disney and Nivea that runs on labor leverage dressed up as code. The shape is familiar -- a real domain operator builds an AI agent for the buyer he used to be -- and it is not the shape my ledger compounds on. Kuli's "Calendly demo link tied to cofounder Jonathan" and "custom pricing tailored to mid-market and enterprise brands" tell me the distribution architecture before I read another line.

Twitter (2007) is the rung I measure permissionless-leverage against -- anyone posts, the platform charges nothing, marginal cost of replication is zero, and the 400x came from media compounding through the founder's hands without anyone's permission. Kuli sits two rungs below that. The dossier names a founder-led outreach motion through `founders@kuli.one`, custom enterprise pricing on request, and a comparison set -- CreatorIQ at $35K–$200K/yr, Aspire at $2,499/mo, Modash at $299/mo -- that defines the category as seat-based enterprise SaaS. That is capital and labor leverage requiring permission from procurement at Havas and Nivea. The free EMV/pricing calculators on kuli.one are a nod toward inbound, but they are lead-capture for the same SDR-adjacent motion, not permissionless distribution. There is an LLM in the stack; that does not make the *business* code-leveraged.

Single-sided SaaS subscriptions are the failure mechanism on criterion #2 -- each Modash customer is economically independent of the next, which is why Modash at $299/mo will never produce Uber-scale upside no matter how good the product gets. Kuli inherits this geometry directly. The dossier's "data flywheel from per-brand campaign outcomes" is marked `[Inferred]` and explicitly "unproven given 5-month operating history," and the product as described -- watching TikTok/Instagram/YouTube videos to surface lookalike creators -- does not get better for the Fortune 100 entertainment brand because Nivea also subscribed. Cross-brand data is contractually walled. No N-th-user-makes-N-minus-1-better mechanism appears in the Kuli dossier, which is the same wall Aspire and CreatorIQ already live behind.

On specific knowledge, Hodara is the strongest leg of the Kuli bet: EPFL Robotics MSc, ex-Aircall AI/GTM lead deploying $20M of paid ads via AI systems, Logitech voice/visual analysis on a $25M marketing budget. That is operator depth -- he was the buyer-adjacent persona before he was the seller. It does not clear the Spolsky/Atwood or Amjad Masad bar of "shipped a comparable product to scale," but it is materially above the "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity" floor I auto-discount. Hassan's 6 years at KPMG on M&A diligence reads as commercial fluency, not domain edge. Over 18 months the Hodara depth compounds into a real workflow product; it does not compound into a code-leverage moat.

For Kuli to clear the pivot-room criterion into the next tier, the company would need an articulated adjacency outside "creator marketing for consumer brands" -- ad-creative testing, performance prediction across paid channels, broader content intelligence for any brand running video. The dossier names none. The wedge is influencer/creator marketing teams at consumer brands specifically, and while marketing as a category is large, Kuli's starting niche is one slice of one function, with incumbents (CreatorIQ, Aspire, Modash) already holding the enterprise distribution. The "first idea never works" axiom needs pivot vectors in the dossier; Kuli's dossier needs them written in.

Notion (2013) is the founder-trio parallel that matters here -- Ivan Zhao and Akshay Kothari built side projects together before Notion, weathered a near-zero-cash rebuild, and the relationship absorbed the strain. Hassan's stated "ten years of side hustles together" with Hodara is the same signal, and no integrity flags appear in the Kuli dossier. Neither founder writes publicly or carries an organic X following from domain writing -- both Twitter handles return "not found" -- which is silence on the media-leverage side of the trio, not negative evidence on the integrity side. The 10-year-partner gate clears.

My 36-month forecast for Kuli: $3–8M ARR across 20–40 mid-market consumer-brand logos, then an acquisition by CreatorIQ or Sprinklr in the $30–80M range as the incumbent bundles AI video analysis into existing seats and Kuli's wedge compresses. The mechanism is the dossier's own commoditization risk -- "video-LLM capabilities... increasingly available via foundation-model APIs" -- combined with CreatorIQ's existing $35K–$200K/yr enterprise distribution. Hodara's operator depth produces a real product and real ARR; the leverage architecture caps the upside at acquihire-plus, not 1,000x.

The strongest bull case on Kuli is the **Perplexity** (2022) exception -- a single-sided product with no network compounding can override criterion #2 when criterion #1 (permissionless distribution) and criterion #3 (specific knowledge) both clear at top quartile. Aravind Srinivas brought Berkeley ML retrieval depth and Perplexity distributed via consumer search + API, permissionlessly. Kuli has the Hodara depth but does not have the Perplexity distribution -- the GTM is enterprise sales to brands, not a permissionless surface. Two gates do not clear; only one does. The condition that flips my verdict is concrete: if Kuli ships a self-serve product layer that consumer-brand marketers adopt without a sales call, and that adoption shows up as inbound revenue at $50K+ MRR within 12 months, the leverage architecture has changed and I re-underwrite. Until then, Kuli is the kind of competent B2B-AI bet that another investor on this council -- Gil, scoring on revenue velocity -- should evaluate, not me.
