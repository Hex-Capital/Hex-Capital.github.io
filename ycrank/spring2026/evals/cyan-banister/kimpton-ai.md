# Kimpton AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Jack Zumwalt and Mauricio Ortiz ran a $10M systematic crypto fund called Level III Capital for four years and built the research stack they couldn't buy, which is the operator-built origin I respect — but Kimpton AI is selling that stack into the most crowded buy-side AI category of 2026, sitting under Rogo's $750M Series C, AlphaSense's $4B valuation, and Hebbia's $159M war chest. The signature is clean: high on operator-as-user, low on magically weird. Kimpton AI is a competent founder team building a useful tool for themselves, parked dead-center of consensus.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril is my anchor for founder-from-lived-experience: a kid who shipped a hardware product to scale before he ever pitched defense. Kimpton AI's founders share the load-bearing mechanism — they were the user, they ran the mandate, they felt the 13F drudgery on their own desk, and they coded their way out of it before turning it into a product. Where Kimpton AI diverges from the Luckey rung is the texture of the lived experience: Zumwalt and Ortiz met at a Goldman Sachs intern event in 2018 and ran a *digital-asset* fund, not a long-duration equity book, so the buy-side mandate they encoded into `mandate.md` is a translation of an adjacent practice rather than a decade of buy-side scar tissue like John Hanke had with location data before Niantic. The fund existed; the four years are real; the Goldman shadow is what keeps this from being the upper rung.

HQ Trivia's failure mechanism was unresolved co-founder authority — Yusupov and Kroll never named a tiebreaker and the partnership fed the implosion. That pattern does not appear in Kimpton AI's dossier. Zumwalt is named CEO, Ortiz is CTO, Del Bosque is founding engineer; the hierarchy is delineated and the working relationship goes back to the Goldman 2020 cohort plus the four-year Level III run. Kimpton AI is the structural inverse of the HQ Trivia red flag, and that is one of the cleaner governance reads in this batch.

Mechanism analysis on "magically weird": this dimension compounds when the round is priced as if the category is uninvestable, and it decays when consensus closes in. Kimpton AI is on the wrong side of that compounding — Rogo closed a $750M post-money Series C in January 2026 against the same buy-side ICP, Microsoft just acquired Fintool, Aiera raised $25M backed by ten Wall Street banks, and the dossier itself names "several YC-batch peers" building agentic finance research. Six top funds are not yet visibly chasing Kimpton AI specifically, but the *category* is consensus, and category-consensus is what the thesis screens out — "find them before it becomes consensus" doesn't get an asterisk for "but the specific company is still unknown."

Counterfactual on capital-intensive/regulated swing: for Kimpton AI to flip from a 6 to a 10 on this dimension, the product would need to take on actual fiduciary recordkeeping liability, SEC Investment Adviser registration, or a custodial integration that competitors can't replicate by buying a FactSet seat. As written, Kimpton AI is a thin agentic layer over FactSet, Tiingo, Massive, Plaid, and Polymarket, with the regulatory exposure deferred by the "humans make the decisions" disclaimer. Vigil Labs sat in actually-regulated finance with a compounding compliance moat; Kimpton AI sits next to regulation without taking on its weight, and the moat sits with whoever owns the FactSet API contract.

Single-anchor parallel on physical-world verifiability: Postmates I bet on because the delivery happened in front of me at a restaurant — I could observe the artifact in the wild within 48 hours of hearing the pitch. Kimpton AI's surface area is a website that routes to "discovery calls through co-founder Jack Zumwalt," no Product Hunt, no app store, no Chrome extension, no public demo, no observable artifact a non-PM can install. The product may be excellent inside the four hedge funds that have seen it, but I cannot touch it, photograph it, or watch a stranger use it on a Tuesday. That is the developer-tools failure mode of this dimension.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Kimpton AI reaches $1-3M ARR by Q2 2029 selling to a long tail of mid-sized hedge funds, family offices, and RIAs, then gets tuck-in acquired by FactSet, Bloomberg, or Rogo before a Series B closes — most probably FactSet, because the data-vendor dependency means FactSet is structurally the acquirer of last resort for any agentic layer that adds seat-multiplier revenue to their own API. The mechanism is straightforward: consensus categories with thin moats above a fat data vendor consolidate into the data vendor, and Kimpton AI's `mandate.md` switching cost is markdown text any incumbent can ingest in a quarter.

The strongest opposing case is the operator-built default — the Carta pattern, where eShares looked like a boring cap-table tool until the founder-fit and the mandated-format lock-in compounded into the category default. The bull argument runs: Zumwalt and Ortiz lived the buy-side PM workflow at Level III for four years, the mandate-file abstraction is exactly the kind of opinionated primitive that becomes a standard, and the PM-vertical positioning beats Rogo's horizontal chat copilot and Hebbia's horizontal document agent because verticalization wins in finance. It stings because Carta's origin looked just as unremarkable in 2014. The dossier evidence that wins against it: Carta's Henry Ward had no $750M Series C competitor breathing down his neck in his founding year, and Kimpton AI's underlying data layer is rented from a vendor that captures the surplus. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed enterprise contract with a named multi-billion-dollar hedge fund or family office disclosed before the next round — not a discovery call, an actual seat-deployed mandate inside a fund whose name I would recognize.
