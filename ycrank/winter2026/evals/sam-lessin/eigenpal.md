# EigenPal -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

EigenPal is the platonic ideal of every AI startup pitch I've been publicly arguing against for two years. "AI-powered document processing for enterprises" might be the single most consensus application of large language models in the entire B2B software landscape. Google, Amazon, and Microsoft already bundle document extraction APIs into cloud contracts that enterprises are already paying for. Rossum has raised $114.5M and is doing nearly $45M in revenue. Hyperscience has raised $300M. And the underlying capability -- getting an LLM to read a messy scan and pull out structured data -- is becoming a commodity with every foundation model release. When I say "software is a business tool, not a business model," this is precisely the category I'm describing. The AI IS the product here, and that's the wrong side of the trade.

The founders are clearly competent. Blaszyk's engineering background at Elastic -- an enterprise data company -- gives him genuine domain context for document processing at scale. Novak's McKinsey and MIT credentials plus prior founding experience with Assetario show he can operate in enterprise B2B. They met at Imperial College, which means the co-founder relationship has real depth, not a startup weekend pairing. So the "sane" part of my filter checks out. But the "insane" part doesn't -- not in the way I need it to. Building a better document processing tool isn't insane. It's obvious. Every technical founder with enterprise experience looks at document workflows and sees the opportunity. The idea provokes zero skepticism from other investors. That's the problem.

Let me steelman the bull case, because I owe it the rigor. The on-prem deployment angle for regulated European banks is genuinely interesting. Data sovereignty requirements in finance and healthcare create a wedge that cloud-native competitors can't easily match. If EigenPal builds deep integrations into two or three major banks' loan processing pipelines, the switching costs become real -- not because of the AI extraction, but because of the workflow orchestration, compliance audit trails, and institutional inertia. The evaluation-first approach (test on historical documents before deploying) is a smart enterprise sales tool. And the SOC II Type 2 certification at pre-seed, if verified, shows unusual operational maturity. You could squint and see a version of this where the compliance infrastructure and on-prem deployment complexity become the moat -- a "Craftwork for document processing" where the operational burden of serving regulated enterprises IS the defensibility. But here's where the analogy breaks: Craftwork's moat is physical labor that software literally cannot replace. EigenPal's deployment complexity is just... deployment complexity. Any well-funded competitor can get SOC II certified and ship an on-prem version. The barrier is effort, not structure.

The commoditization risk is what kills this for me. The dossier itself lays it out plainly: "As foundation models improve, the core extraction capability becomes increasingly available via API calls to general-purpose LLMs without specialized IDP tooling." EigenPal's differentiation lies in the workflow layer, guardrails, and observability -- those are features, not primitives. They're building an application on someone else's infrastructure (foundation models), which means they're in a race against every cloud provider's next API update. When I run my "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" test, the answer is unambiguous: without AI, there is no EigenPal. That's a feature waiting to be absorbed, not a company. And there's no new primitive being created here -- no protocol that others build on, no layer that creates compounding network effects. It's a tool. Tools get commoditized. I watched this exact pattern with Birchbox -- narrative magnitude without structural moat produces a company that's too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound. The IDP market will produce real revenue for some players, but the structural winners will be the cloud providers who bundle extraction into existing contracts, not standalone startups competing on extraction accuracy.

One more thing nags me: Novak's prior company Assetario appears still listed as active. "3-time founder of B2B AI companies" at pre-seed, with a previous venture's status unclear, isn't automatically disqualifying -- but it does make me want to understand the commitment calculus. Combined with zero public revenue figures and unverified enterprise logos (McKinsey and Accenture could mean "we did a pilot" or "someone there used our free trial"), the traction signals are ambiguous enough that the downside cushion barely registers. This is a pass for me. Not because the founders lack capability, but because the structural logic of the business sits squarely in the category I believe AI is actively destroying.
