# Ontora -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The technology question here is simple and disqualifying: what does Ontora own that OpenAI doesn't? The core product is LLM-conducted interviews and RAG over enterprise documents. That's not difficult technology compounding over time -- it's an application layer assembled from commoditized foundation models. When I invested in Stack Overflow, the moat was a knowledge graph built over years by millions of developers, something that got harder to replicate with every answer posted. When I backed Alchemy, the moat was deep blockchain infrastructure that every Web3 developer depended on. Ontora's "Unified Context Layer" is a description on a website, not a technical moat. Any well-funded competitor -- and Celonis has $1.77 billion in funding and is already expanding toward AI-powered process intelligence -- can deploy the same LLMs against the same unstructured enterprise data. The dossier itself flags this: "barriers to replication are moderate." Moderate barriers at pre-seed become nonexistent barriers when incumbents decide to move.

The specific knowledge test is where Ontora most clearly falls short. Maximilian Arnold spent two years at Porsche building CoAnalyst, an agentic RAG system for SAP procurement data. That's relevant resume experience, not non-transferable insight. The distinction matters enormously. When Evan Williams built Twitter, he'd spent years on Blogger understanding how humans want to communicate publicly -- knowledge that emerged from genuine obsession, not a product engineering stint. Arnold's Porsche work tells me he can deploy enterprise AI competently. It doesn't tell me he understands something about organizational diagnostics that a thousand other AI engineers at Fortune 500 companies couldn't learn in six months. Iwanowitsch brings a business school background from HSG -- literally the kind of knowledge you acquire through standard education. And Korn has no confirmed public background at all. Three founders, none with deep consulting industry experience, none with years of process mining expertise, none with the kind of domain obsession that generates contrarian insight.

The leverage architecture has some merit -- AI interviews genuinely scale in a way human consultants don't, and the trillion-dollar consulting market is structurally unable to self-disrupt because incumbents can't automate away their own billable hours. That incentive misalignment is real, and it creates a legitimate opening. The bull case is that Ontora becomes the infrastructure layer for organizational intelligence -- accumulating cross-company process benchmarks that create a proprietary data asset over time. If every deployment adds to a knowledge base of how organizations actually work across industries, that compounds. The Vertiv testimonial from a strategy manager at a public company suggests the product delivers genuine value -- "what used to take months, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon." For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (1) the cross-company data moat materializes before Celonis or a well-funded AI lab replicates the approach, (2) enterprise buyers adopt AI-generated organizational diagnostics at scale despite the trust and procurement barriers, and (3) the founding team can navigate enterprise sales cycles with three people and no visible GTM resources. All three need to be true simultaneously.

The timing isn't contrarian -- it's consensus. "AI will automate consulting" is the most frequently pitched thesis in YC applications right now. Everyone agrees LLMs can conduct conversations and analyze documents. Gartner publishes reports saying 40% of consulting tasks are automatable. When the research firms are publishing your thesis, you're not early -- you're on time at best, and more likely late. The companies where I've made real returns -- Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Bitcoin when it was dismissed as nerd money -- those were bets where informed people actively argued I was wrong. Nobody argues that AI can't conduct interviews. They argue about execution, adoption, and timing -- but those are business execution questions, not the kind of fundamental disagreement that creates asymmetric returns.

This is a competent team applying available tools to a large market. That sentence describes roughly sixty percent of the YC batch in any given year, and it's precisely the profile I avoid. The founders aren't building from knowledge that can't be taught -- they're building from knowledge that every AI bootcamp teaches. The technology doesn't compound in any distinctive way. The market timing is consensus. I don't see the leverage architecture that would make this a platform rather than a feature. Pass.
