# Maquoketa Research -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything about whether this is a non-consensus bet. When Germany hands €9 billion in strike drone contracts to startups, when Ukraine orders thousands of loitering munitions from Helsing, when the U.S. Army launches PBAS and the Marines integrate three new munition types into infantry battalions -- the market has spoken. Autonomous attack drones are the most consensus defense-tech category of 2026. Anduril has raised $3.5 billion. Helsing is at €12 billion valuation. Stark got $500 million on the back of a demo where all four test strikes failed. Neros just secured Army PBAS selection with $121 million raised. Every defense-focused VC in the world is writing checks into this space. My filter is built to find sane people with insane ideas. A five-person team entering the most heavily funded, most government-validated, most obviously-needed military technology category is a sane person with a very, very sane idea. That's not what I invest in.

The one thing that tugs at me is the CTO's phased array and RF design background out of Berkeley Wireless Research Center. That's not a generic "we can build drones" credential -- phased array systems are hard, specialized, and directly relevant to the thing that actually differentiates mil-spec loitering munitions from the thousands of modified FPV drones flying in Ukraine today: electronic warfare resistance and autonomous guidance in GPS-denied, signal-jammed environments. If Hernandez can build guidance and communications systems at the component level that survive contested electromagnetic environments, that's a genuine hardware moat. It's the kind of physical-world capability that software cannot replicate and AI cannot commoditize -- exactly the "cherry on top" structure I look for, where the drone is the business and the electronics sophistication is what makes it defensible. I am sympathetic to this signal because it maps to my Craftwork thesis: the moat isn't the software layer, it's the operational and hardware capability underneath.

Here's the bull case I genuinely considered: defense primes structurally cannot build cheap expendable munitions because their business models depend on high-margin, low-volume platforms. That's a real structural insight -- Lockheed and Raytheon have profit-model cannibalization risk. And the well-funded drone startups (Anduril, Helsing) are building broad platforms, not specialized RF subsystems. If Maquoketa's phased array capability is genuinely differentiated, they could become a component-level primitive -- the RF backbone that other drone integrators need -- rather than competing as another end-to-end drone manufacturer. That would flip this from application to protocol. If Hernandez's BWRC research translates into EW-resistant guidance modules that other manufacturers want to integrate, you've got something closer to a new primitive in autonomous munitions. But there's no evidence of this strategy. The YC page says "autonomous one-way attack drones," not "EW-resistant guidance systems for the drone ecosystem." They're positioning as a complete-stack drone company, which means competing head-to-head with organizations that have three to four orders of magnitude more capital.

The structural problems compound from there. Defense procurement cycles run 12-36 months. This team has no disclosed contracts, no SBIR awards, no existing security clearances, and no defense industry relationships. Yeager's background is trading technology at SpiderRock -- capable engineering but zero defense procurement credibility. The third co-founder, Williams, has literally no discoverable public background. The team covers the right technical disciplines on paper (software, RF, mechanical), but building and selling attack drones to the DoD requires ITAR compliance, facility clearances, and navigating procurement bureaucracy that eats startups alive. YC's defense network helps, but it doesn't replace the years of relationship-building that companies like Neros and Anduril have already done. The pre-revenue period will likely exceed their runway, and their competitors are already in production.

I keep coming back to the fundamental question: does this company create something structurally new, or is it one more entrant in a gold rush? The loitering munition market is real and expanding, but the "infinity story" here belongs to the category, not to Maquoketa specifically. Their narrative can't expand beyond where Anduril's and Helsing's narratives already sit. And the TAM estimates in the dossier vary by an order of magnitude -- always a sign that the market is being sized to match the fundraising story rather than grounded in procurement reality. The U.S. SAM of $170 million in 2024 is the honest number, and splitting that against five funded competitors plus defense primes leaves very little room for a pre-seed entrant with no contracts. Physical-world defense hardware is my kind of moat, but only when paired with founder-market fit that borders on unfair advantage. I don't see that here.
