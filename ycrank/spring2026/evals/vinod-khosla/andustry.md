# Andustry -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me be direct: Andustry is the archetype of a company I pass on. AI that makes industrial procurement faster — from weeks to days — is a real efficiency gain. But when I ask my first question — "if this fully works, does the world visibly change?" — the answer is no. Procurement officers save time. Suppliers get more inbound leads. Industrial machinery still costs the same. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. The consequence of full success is a profitable B2B marketplace, and there are worse things to build, but I don't write checks for 3-5x outcomes. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. A more efficient way to buy CNC machines doesn't get me there.

The technology question is even more damning. LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are, as the dossier itself notes, "reproducible." There is no novel science here. No proprietary architecture. No physics breakthrough. This is wrapping existing LLM APIs around a procurement workflow — precisely the kind of AI-for-[vertical] company I see fifty times a year at Demo Day. The 500K+ supplier database sounds impressive until you ask whether it's proprietary or aggregated from public directories. No third party has verified it. If Matchory (with $8.86M), LightSource Labs (with $33M), or Xometry (with a $2.1B market cap and $686.6M in revenue) decide to build this exact feature, the software layer evaporates. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had built a fundamentally new engine — electric turbopumps, 3D-printed combustion chambers — that no competitor could replicate by hiring three engineers. Andustry's AI agents can be replicated by any team with API access to Claude or GPT and a weekend of scraping industrial directories.

The strongest bull case is the marketplace network effect. If Andustry locks in enough suppliers and buyers, the two-sided liquidity becomes the moat regardless of technology. Xometry proved industrial marketplaces can reach public-company scale. Johann Sturken's family background in industrial goods trading is genuine domain context — he understands the pain viscerally, not theoretically. The EUR 1M+ beta transaction volume shows something is working. And $810B in global industrial machinery procurement is genuinely underdigitized. If I squint, I can see a path where this becomes the dominant marketplace for off-the-shelf industrial goods, with data network effects that compound over years. But that path requires winning a market-share fight against better-funded competitors with no durable technology advantage — exactly the kind of execution-without-insight bet that doesn't match my framework. DoorDash was my exception to this rule, but Tony Xu was creating a new category of food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. Andustry is optimizing an existing B2B purchasing process for a narrower set of buyers.

The founders are credentialed — McKinsey, RWTH Aachen, TUM, NASA/DLR Design Challenge, Cambridge research — but credentials are not what I look for. I look for rate of learning, for what surprised the founder in the last three months, for evidence they've changed their mind about something fundamental. Nothing in this dossier shows me that. I see two well-educated European founders executing a logical business plan, not two irreverent outsiders attempting what the world says can't be done. When I backed Mach Industries, the founder was a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems because he didn't know it was supposed to be impossible. That naivety is an asset. Andustry's founders know exactly what's possible — they're building it competently, within known parameters. Competence is fine. It's just not what I fund.

This is a company targeting redistribution of existing spend, not creation of new demand. Industrial buyers already buy machinery. Andustry makes the buying process faster. Nothing becomes radically affordable. No basic human need gets transformed. The ambition level — "AI to source and sell industrial products" — is something every informed observer would say is achievable, which in my framework means the return profile doesn't justify the option price. Some problems are too important not to work on. Making procurement 5x faster is not one of them.
