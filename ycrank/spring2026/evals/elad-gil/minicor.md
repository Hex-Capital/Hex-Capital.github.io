# Minicor -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I noticed about Minicor isn't the AI angle -- it's the compliance certifications. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA at pre-seed, with a five-person team. That's months of work and real capital spent before writing a line of product code that ships to customers. Most pre-seed founders wouldn't do this. But it tells me these founders understand something important: the actual bottleneck for AI companies trying to sell into healthcare, automotive, and financial services isn't the AI model. It's the compliance and infrastructure wrapper around the model that lets you touch production systems of record without getting your customer sued. This is the Gusto pattern -- boring, operationally essential, regulatory-complexity-as-moat. The category doesn't attract prestige-seeking founders, which means less competition for the companies willing to do the unglamorous work.

On market structure timing, I see a real structural shift but I'm not sure it's nonobvious anymore. Anthropic released Computer Use in October 2024, and that genuinely created a new capability threshold -- AI models can now see and interact with screens in a way that was impossible two years ago. That's a concrete technology catalyst. But the "AI disrupts RPA" thesis is increasingly consensus: UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent, Automat raised $19.25M from Felicis, and there are multiple YC companies in adjacent spaces. Where Minicor's framing gets more interesting is the specific claim that legacy system vendors are *actively restricting* third-party API access, making UI-level automation the *only* integration path. If that's true -- and my experience with enterprise software suggests it often is -- then the market isn't "AI makes RPA better." The market is "AI companies hitting a wall trying to enter legacy industries need a managed infrastructure layer to connect to systems that deliberately have no API." That's a meaningfully different and less crowded framing than generic AI automation.

The infrastructure-layer positioning is where Minicor maps most directly to my thesis. They're building API-first: you define an automation, and you get a triggerable API endpoint. The MCP interface means AI coding agents can discover and invoke Minicor's capabilities programmatically. The customer isn't the enterprise running the legacy system -- it's the AI company building a product that *needs* to read and write to that legacy system. This is a picks-and-shovels play during a platform shift, which is exactly the pattern I look for. When Stripe was early, the value wasn't "Stripe processes payments" -- it was "Stripe is the payments API that every new internet company integrates into, and once integrated, never rips out." Minicor's path to that kind of compounding switching cost runs through the managed VM infrastructure, compliance layer, and production observability (video recordings, Slack alerts, screenshot debugging) that raw model APIs don't provide. The commoditization risk from Anthropic's Computer Use API is real -- anyone can call it -- but that's like saying anyone could call a payment processor's API in 2010. The value is in the managed production layer, not the raw capability.

The single-miracle structure is favorable. The technology miracle is happening exogenously -- foundation model computer-use capabilities are improving on their own curve, the same dynamic that made Harvey's bet on legal AI clean. Minicor doesn't need to build a better model; they need the models to keep getting better, which they will. Regulatory clearance is already handled (SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA). Distribution to AI companies via API and MCP is a developer-led motion, not a enterprise sales miracle. The one thing that needs to go right is production reliability at scale -- the gap between 93-96% click accuracy and the 99.5%+ that healthcare and financial services demand. Their architectural choice to store automations as deterministic code and use AI only for recovery and edge cases is smart engineering that reduces model dependency and narrows the reliability gap. But at 25,000 patients per day, even a 4% error rate produces a thousand failures. That accuracy ceiling is the real miracle they need to cross.

The founders are where my conviction weakens. Faizaan and Saheed have relevant domain experience -- Saheed spent three years at Ford and time at Morgan Stanley, both environments rife with legacy desktop systems. Their shared history building Software For Love since 2019 gives me confidence in the co-founder relationship. But neither has operated at a company during a hypergrowth phase, and there's no evidence of the decisive, team-building capability I look for when evaluating whether founders can navigate the 1-to-100 transition. At this stage, I weight market structure more heavily than founder pedigree -- I've seen strong markets carry competent-but-unproven teams further than great founders in bad markets. But it's a gap.

The bull case I'd need to believe: Minicor becomes the default infrastructure layer for every AI company that needs legacy system access, the way Stripe became the default for payments. The structural dynamics support it -- compliance moats deter casual entrants, on-premise deployments create switching costs, and the AI-company customer segment grows as more startups push into healthcare, automotive, and supply chain. If the accuracy ceiling gets crossed (which the exogenous model improvement curve supports), and the managed infrastructure layer proves sticky, this could be a category-defining platform. The "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" signal and "thousands of workflows a week" suggest real production usage, which is exceptional signal at pre-seed. The risk is that UiPath adds AI healing to its existing installed base faster than Minicor can build distribution from scratch, or that Anthropic's own tooling evolves to make the managed layer thinner. I'd want to see named customers and revenue before writing a larger check, but the infrastructure positioning and compliance moat earn this a conversation.
