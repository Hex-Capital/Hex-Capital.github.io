# Dayjob -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Dayjob is a documented-pivot team running a 20-30% optimization play -- George Postlethwaite and Fred Fooks earned the right to be funded by spending three years inside Gaea ERP depots since 2022, then pivoted in January 2025 to a scheduling agent that customers report delivers "8%+ efficiency gains" on ~100-jobs-per-day skip-hire fleets. The learning-rate evidence is real. The consequence-at-maximum-success is a UK waste operator hitting 11% more annual revenue. Those two facts, together, describe a competent vertical SaaS company, not a Khosla bet.

**Consequence ladder, top rung is Impossible Foods (2011)** -- where the success-state was the displacement of animal protein production, not "alt-meat captures 11% more shelf efficiency." The shared mechanism is collapsing an incumbent regime; that is what I underwrite. Dayjob's dossier names its consequence in customer-reported terms: 8% efficiency, 50% time-window hits, £100K incremental revenue per customer, $496K total ARR across three logos including Allstone and Coastal Recycling. None of those numbers names an incumbent regime that disappears. AMCS still books its $273M, Routeware still serves its 1,000+ haulers, diesel trucks still drive the routes -- just 8% better. That is the exact "20-30% better than incumbent" anti-pattern my Outlook Business framing was written to filter out, and the gap between Dayjob's success-state and Impossible Foods' success-state is the whole reason I score this dimension where I do.

**Capital-risk ladder, bottom rung is the GPT-4-on-HubSpot sales-summarizer I pass on in 30 seconds** -- the failure mechanism there is that any Tier-1 generalist can fund the deal because the science risk is zero, the capital required is small, and the moat is workflow integration rather than a falsifiable physical miracle. Dayjob fits that mechanism almost cleanly: the dossier itself concedes "route optimisation algorithms are well-known (OptimoRoute, Route4Me, Samsara already ship them)" and that "defensibility hinges on workflow integration depth rather than the optimisation model itself." Five engineers, $496K ARR, no plant, no model training run, no fleet to capitalize -- this is a Sequoia or Index seed, not a Khosla bet where I am supposed to be over-resourced for the risk no one else will touch.

**Founder-defiance dimension, mechanism analysis** -- Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching UK grocery and ran sales at Otta; Fooks did ESG and Supply Chain Analytics at Deloitte and AI/data at Capgemini. Over 18 months this profile compounds into operational competence inside waste/skip-hire workflows, which is genuinely useful. It does not compound into the "contradicted-their-own-field" stance that produced KiOR's defiance of petroleum orthodoxy or Commonwealth Fusion's defiance of "fusion is 30 years away" consensus. Dayjob's founders are domain-adjacent operators applying a now-conventional thesis (LLM agents on legacy ERPs) -- the expert-continuity pattern my TED Talk warned about, softened only by the depot ride-alongs.

**Learning-rate dimension, counterfactual** -- the Gaea-to-Dayjob pivot in January 2025 after roughly three years of building the wrong product is the single best signal in this dossier and the reason this dimension scores where it does. For it to flip a tier higher into Slash (2024) territory, the pivot story would need to include a publicly stated thesis update of the kind that justified my $100M Series C into a YC fintech -- not just "we built ERP, customers wanted scheduling," but a falsifiable bet the founders are now publicly making against their prior field. The dossier does not give me that articulation, so the pivot reads as a smart product correction rather than a founder rewriting their worldview.

**Capital-fit dimension, single-anchor parallel** -- contrast Dayjob with Genesis AI (2025), where I co-led a $105M seed because robot foundation models require capital depth that consensus VCs cannot stomach. Dayjob reached $359K+ ARR within seven months of launch with five people and no disclosed prior raise. There is no plant to build, no model to train, no fleet to capitalize. The capital deployment story is "more sales reps in the US," which is exactly the profile where I am over-resourced and a $5M check from Index does the same job.

**36-month forecast** -- Dayjob reaches $4-12M ARR concentrated in UK/US waste and skip-hire, and gets acquired by AMCS or Routeware for $25-70M as a defensive bolt-on to their existing AI-routing modules -- the mechanism is incumbent substitution risk converting into incumbent acquisition logic once Dayjob's three-logo ARR base proves the agent layer is a real feature rather than a real category. The Postlethwaite/Fooks operating discipline makes this a fine outcome for a £2-5M seed; it does not return a Khosla Ventures fund.

**Strongest opposing case** -- Slash (2024). YC pedigree, documented founder pivot, consumer-adjacent SMB market, $100M Series C from me despite consequence magnitude being modest. The pattern-match is real: Dayjob is YC-Spring-2026, has the Gaea-to-Dayjob pivot, has real customer love, and Tom Blomfield is the partner. If I overrode consequence for Slash, why not Dayjob? Because Slash's pivot was a public thesis update on consumer financial distribution, not a product correction inside the same vertical, and Slash's success-state plugs into a network-effect business banking flywheel that Dayjob's per-fleet SaaS contract does not have. The flip condition is narrow and verifiable: a signed pilot with a non-waste Fortune-500 industrial logistics operator -- construction aggregates, oilfield services, fuel distribution -- at $1M+ ACV within 12 months, demonstrating Dayjob's agent generalizes beyond skip-hire and earns the "Industrial Logistics" label its YC page already claims.
