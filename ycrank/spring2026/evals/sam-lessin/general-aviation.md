# General Aviation -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

General Aviation is what happens when a high-conviction aviation operator (Ben Frank — MIT Aero '14, pilot, A&P mechanic, three prior aviation companies) attacks a category that lives nowhere in my revealed sleeves and demands a regulatory schlep an order of magnitude longer than even Ro had to clear. The signature here is high founder-market fit fused to a near-zero category fit and zero distribution surface — a textbook "right operator, wrong asset shape for my book" pre-seed.

Founder-conviction is where General Aviation grades highest, so the ladder there matters most. The HIGH rung on this axis is my own **Drop.io** arc — bootstrapping a primitive against free incumbents because I refused to be talked out of it; the MID rung is **Sublime Security**, a founder with deep enterprise-security obsession but no public manifesto; the LOW rung is the canonical sanitized YC AI-agent deck with no founder writing anywhere on the internet. General Aviation sits between MID and HIGH: Ben Frank ran Rotabull, co-founded Seer Aerospace (acquired by Aviation Week), and was CCO at Rotor Technologies where he personally interviewed ~200 helicopter operators (MIT News, Feb 9 2024). That's a decade of paid obsession across parts, analytics, and autonomy — pilot and mechanic credentials on top — but I cannot find a single opinionated essay, thread, or screenshot from Frank on why ATC is structurally broken. Conviction is there in the resume; it is not yet legible in the public record, which is exactly the artifact I'd read before the meeting.

Founder-as-brand is where General Aviation grades lowest, and the ladder is brutal. The HIGH rung is **John Fish / Bookshelved**, a creator whose YouTube footprint was the entire underwriting; MID is **Marina Mogilko** with LinguaTrip's owned audience priced as a 30-year earnings stream; LOW is any pre-seed where the founder has no Twitter, no newsletter, no Discord, no community asset whatsoever. General Aviation is squarely at the LOW rung — the dossier finds no founder Twitter/X, a generic LinkedIn (linkedin.com/in/benjaminsfrank, 500+ connections), no GitHub, no company social presence, and Hiring: False. That's fine for a B2G ATC business that will be sold to the FAA over a five-year procurement cycle, but it eliminates the entire Slow Creator Fund lens (Axios, Feb 12 2025) and means General Aviation cannot be underwritten on owned distribution.

Category sleeve fit for General Aviation is the second-weakest axis. HIGH rung is **Solana** at $0.05/coin — a pre-consensus crypto seed inside my revealed sleeve; MID rung is **Allbirds** as DTC consumer where I was early in a category Sand Hill dismissed; LOW rung is the canonical AI-agent-for-vertical-X pitch I deselect on sight. General Aviation lands between MID and LOW: it isn't the AI-infra anti-sleeve I reject ("AI is just more internet," Lonsdale #113), but aerospace/satellite hard-tech isn't crypto, DTC, prescription health, or creator economy either — none of the **Aireon** / **Iridium** / **Starlink** comparables sit in my portfolio because aerospace infrastructure has never been one of my tacks.

Capital structure is where the **Ro** Known Exception gets stress-tested and ultimately fails for General Aviation. Ro is the HIGH rung — high-burn DTC pharma where 50-state prescription rails were the moat and only VC could clear the schleps; MID is a generic seed needing four rounds to default-alive; LOW is **Teamshares**' employee-ownership rollup where the structure itself is the alpha. General Aviation isn't Ro — it's worse on the dimension Ro was excused for. Ro's regulatory rails resolved in 2-3 years per state; ATC certification has consumed FAA's NextGen program for 20+ years and remains incomplete (CNN, May 8 2025), DO-178/DO-254 assurance is non-negotiable, and the buyer set is one sovereign ANSP per country fronted by primes like Peraton and Verizon ($2.4B FENS contract, CNN Feb 27 2025). Solo founder, team size 0, hiring off — General Aviation will need five-plus rounds and a decade before any ANSP pays a recurring service fee anywhere near Aireon's ~$20M/yr from NAV CANADA.

Round pricing is the one axis I have to grade on absence. The HIGH rung is **Solana**'s $0.05 seed when funds had quit crypto; MID is **Sublime Security** pre-consensus when SOC SaaS got all the late-stage dollars; LOW is the $30M-post YC AI-agent round with four tier-1s already committed. General Aviation sits above MID by category alone — "fix ATC by connecting aircraft to the internet" is not a hot YC consensus pitch and almost certainly clears at standard YC pre-seed terms with little competitive bidding — but the dossier carries zero price evidence and I refuse to reward silence on the dimension that is my entire alpha. The non-consensus category gets credit; the unverified round terms cap how much.

My 36-month forecast for General Aviation: Frank does not displace Aireon or win a prime ATC contract; instead General Aviation pivots into drone/UAM traffic management (UTM) — where the regulatory bar is one-tenth of crewed-aviation Part 91/121 and where Frank's Rotor Technologies network maps directly — and lands at <$2M ARR through a handful of UTM/Vertiport pilots and possibly an SBIR Phase II, while the "satellite ATC" line item gets absorbed into a Starlink or Iridium-Aireon product roadmap. The mechanism: solo founder + DO-178 assurance + ANSP procurement cycle is mathematically incompatible with seed-stage runway, so the only viable tack is into the adjacent UTM market where Frank's credentials over-index.

The strongest bull case against me leans on Ro: regulatory complexity IS the moat, the May 2025 DOT announcement created the political will, Verizon FENS is visibly failing (Musk himself called it out, CNN Feb 27 2025), and Ben Frank is a rare three-time aviation operator who's already had an exit (Seer → Aviation Week). The "venture moment" framing from my own Slow letter genuinely fits — only venture capital can fund the certification schlep. I take it seriously and reject it on one number: Ro had a 2-3 year regulatory horizon per state and direct-to-consumer revenue from day one; General Aviation has a multi-decade FAA certification horizon, zero non-government buyers, and team size 0. Ro had a sales motion before the moat closed; General Aviation has neither. I flip to Invest if Frank closes a named FAA SBIR or an ANSP LOI within 12 months, or if he publishes a public, opinionated technical memo on why a software/protocol layer atop Iridium or Starlink certifies faster than a new constellation — until then, this is a pass at the price I'd be asked to pay, and a maybe at half of it.
