# Fuchsia -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Fuchsia is a right-problem, no-animal-moment-yet company: Tony Gao and two unnamed Yale-dropout co-founders are running directly at FCC/FDA/CE/UL — exactly the kind of schlep "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) was written to surface — but the dossier contains no cereal-box equivalent, no pre-funding stunt, no demo-on-Hacker-News artifact. The customer-name drop ("products certified for Amazon, Apple, and Walmart" on getfuchsia.ai, 2026) is the kind of line that could mean anything from a real paid engagement to a consulting gig the founders ran in school, and the dossier doesn't tell me which.

On schlep fit, **Coinbase** (2012) is the right comp: Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind was the asset, and the regulatory exposure other YC S12 applicants routed around became the moat. Fuchsia is pointed at the same kind of unpleasant terrain — FCC parts 11/15/18/22/24/68/90/95, FDA 510(k), CE Marking, UL, ISO, RIA — the literal definition of work mainstream founders flinch from. Where Fuchsia diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) rung is that the schlep is being *delegated to AI agents* rather than personally absorbed by the founders; Armstrong personally read the BitLicense. The dossier gives me no evidence Tony Gao has personally sat in a UL lab and watched an EMC chamber test fail, which is the version of this story I'd want.

On animal resourcefulness, the **Pass Example** rung — the polished two-MBA AI-wrapper team with a glossy deck and no shipped artifact — is the failure pattern I check against. Fuchsia doesn't fully fit that template: Tony Gao has an ICRA 2026 paper ("Subsecond 3D Mesh Generation for Robot Manipulation," apollo-lab-yale.github.io) and the team is described as Yale dropouts with humanoid-deployment experience, which is real. But the dossier shows zero pre-funding *hustle* anecdote of the kind that flipped **Dropbox** (2007) or rescued **Coinbase** (2012). No stunt. No Obama O's. A cal.com link on the homepage and a claim of certifying products for Amazon/Apple/Walmart with no count, no contract value, no dates. Animals leave tracks, and I can't find them here.

The organic-idea question turns on whether humanoid-deployment work at Apollo Lab gave the **Fuchsia** founders their own scar tissue with FCC and UL. **Dropbox** (2007) emerged because Drew Houston forgot his USB stick repeatedly; **Stripe** (2010) emerged because the Collisons had been hacking commerce since **Auctomatic** (2007). Fuchsia's mechanism would have to be: a Yale humanoid robot had to clear UL 3300 or RIA R15.06 before a partner deployment, the founders did it manually, and they're now building what they wished existed. That story is *plausible* from the dossier — robotics research plus the "Yale dropouts with humanoid deployments" line — but it is never told in the first person. If it were told, I'd score this 8-10 points higher on dimension 2.

On hacker genealogy, the single anchor is **Reddit** (2005)'s Aaron Swartz co-authoring the RSS spec before the company existed: a verifiable artifact in a hacker-visible venue. Tony Gao's ICRA 2026 paper is the analogous artifact for Fuchsia — peer-reviewed robotics research is legitimate hacker output, even if it isn't the Lisp/competitive-programming flavor I over-index on (Patrick Collison's 2005 Young Scientist prize). Two co-founders are unnamed in the dossier, which is the standard pre-seed information gap and not a red flag by itself, but it caps how much credit I can extend.

Counterfactual on co-founder config: for the dimension to flip from neutral to high, the dossier would need to name the other two founders and document a 2+ year working relationship — a shared Apollo Lab stint, a co-authored paper, a humanoid-deployment project together. **Airbnb** (2009)'s Chesky/Gebbia roomed at RISD; **Stripe** (2010) is two brothers. Fuchsia gives me a team-of-three count and an [Inferred] Yale overlap, which is the absent-evidence default and nothing more.

My 36-month forecast: Fuchsia gets acquihired by UL Solutions, Intertek, or SGS for $8-25M as a documentation-AI tuck-in, with Saphira AI (YC S24, $500K pre-seed, 18-month head-start) winning the standalone safety-critical-robotics race. The mechanism is incumbent substitution — UL FY2024 revenue ~$2.9B against three overlapping YC AI-compliance entrants in 18 months (Saphira S24, HardwareCompliance March 2026, Fuchsia Spring 2026) means the buyer pool is small, motivated, and already talking to all three.

The strongest bear-case I owe myself is actually a bull-case here, because **Scale AI** is the live precedent: Alexandr Wang explicitly cited "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) as the trigger, and the data-labeling work he ran at was, on paper, no more defensible than AI-mediated compliance documentation. If Fuchsia is the Scale AI of FCC/FDA, I am dramatically underwriting it at 51. The dismantling: Wang shipped pre-funding manual ops at a scale the dossier documents (early Scale AI ran human labelers via Remotasks-precursor workflows before any institutional money); Fuchsia's pre-funding artifact is a customer-name drop with no count and a cal.com link. **One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest**: a single named hardware customer (not "Amazon, Apple, and Walmart" as a brand list) with a dated contract showing Fuchsia personally schlepped a product through an FCC TCB or FDA 510(k) before the YC check. Until then, this is a neutral.
