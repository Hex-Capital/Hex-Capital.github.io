# Orthogonal -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: Stripe has already processed roughly 50 million transactions through its x402 protocol and launched its Agentic Commerce Protocol with OpenAI and Coinbase. Mastercard shipped Agent Pay in April 2025. When the largest payments infrastructure company on the planet is already live in your exact category, you are not a startup with a non-consensus idea — you are a startup with a very consensus idea and a very large competitor. This is the opposite of my filter. The best investments happen when you find credible people doing something everyone else thinks is nuts. Nobody thinks agentic payments is nuts. Every major fintech company is racing into this space. That's a problem.

The founders are legitimately strong on the "sane" side of the equation. Christian Pickett built billing at Vercel and worked payments at Coinbase — that's directly relevant infrastructure experience. Bera Sogut is a two-time ICPC World Finalist who built APIs at Google including reCAPTCHA and Maps. They met at McGill and have been close friends since, which passes my co-founder relationship test (I'm always skeptical of speed-dating co-founder matches). If this were a genuinely contrarian bet, I'd be excited about this team. But the idea itself — unified API access and billing for AI agents — is table stakes. It's the definition of a "sane idea," which means the founder-idea combination doesn't produce the non-consensus signal I need.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart for me. Orthogonal is pure software middleware — an aggregation layer sitting on top of open protocols (MCP is open source, payment rails are commoditized, APIs are standardized by definition). There are no physical-world operations that software amplifies but cannot replace. No installed hardware. No regulatory position. No proprietary data that compounds. The two-sided marketplace has 16 integrations, which is nascent, and the catalog of curated YC-company APIs is a thin supply-side advantage that any well-funded competitor could replicate in weeks. This is exactly the category I've been shouting about — software that looks like a moat but isn't one. When I say software is a business tool not a business model, this is what I mean. The aggregation convenience is real but it's a feature, not a defensible business.

Let me steelman the bull case, because the narrative structure here is genuinely interesting. If you squint, Orthogonal could become the "Stripe for agents" — the default access layer through which all AI agents discover and consume APIs. That's an infinity story. If agents proliferate the way mobile apps did, and if every agent needs to call dozens of APIs, the aggregation layer that handles auth, billing, and discovery becomes critical infrastructure. The YC network gives them a warm-start advantage on supply, and the MCP integration with Claude Code, Cursor, and Codex gives them distribution on demand. The founders' combined backgrounds — payments infrastructure plus API engineering — are a precise match for the problem. If Stripe's on-chain approach proves too cumbersome for mainstream developers who want fiat-denominated simplicity, Orthogonal could own the off-chain lane. That's a real scenario. But here's why it doesn't get me over the line: Stripe isn't structurally locked into on-chain settlement. They could extend ACP to cover off-chain billing in a quarter. The moat here is a temporary product gap in Stripe's roadmap, not a structural barrier. And I learned from Birchbox that narrative magnitude without a defensible position produces the worst venture outcome — a company that's too promising to ignore but too fragile to compound.

The primitive-versus-application test seals it. Orthogonal isn't creating a new protocol. MCP already exists. Payment rails already exist. APIs already exist. Orthogonal is building a convenience layer on top of existing primitives — that's an application, not a protocol. Compare this to Venmo, which created a new primitive for peer-to-peer payments, or Aleo, which created zero-knowledge privacy as infrastructure. Those companies established new layers that other businesses built on. Orthogonal aggregates existing layers. That's a fundamentally different structural position, and it caps the upside in a way that the narrative doesn't reflect.
