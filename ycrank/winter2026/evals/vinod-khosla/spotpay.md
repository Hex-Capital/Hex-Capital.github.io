# SpotPay -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I see when I look at SpotPay is a crowded consensus. KAST raised $10 million. Dakota raised $12.5 million. Slash has $60 million and a $370 million valuation. Chipper Cash absorbed $305 million. Wise is public with a €8.6 billion market cap. They are all converging on the same thesis: stablecoins will restructure cross-border payments. SpotPay enters this field with $450,000. When I backed Juniper against Cisco, the insight was contrarian -- Cisco's own CTO said they would never build a TCP/IP router for public telecom. Nobody is saying stablecoin neobanks won't work. Visa is expanding stablecoin settlement. YC is accepting stablecoin payments. Stripe acquired Bridge. McKinsey is publishing reports on stablecoin growth curves. This is no longer a bet against the consensus -- it is the consensus. And when the consensus has already attracted $80 million in venture capital across direct competitors, a $450K entrant isn't making a contrarian wager. They're arriving at a party that started two years ago.

The consequence of SpotPay succeeding is a good neobank serving Caribbean and Latin American remittance corridors with lower fees. That matters to the individuals who use it -- 6-8% remittance fees are a genuine tax on some of the world's most vulnerable populations. But I need to distinguish between the consequence of *stablecoins disrupting remittances* and the consequence of *SpotPay specifically succeeding*. The former is significant and probably inevitable. The latter is a modestly differentiated consumer fintech product in corridors where multiple well-funded teams are pursuing identical outcomes. If SpotPay wins its corridors, it builds a solid business. It does not make incumbent approaches structurally obsolete -- it offers the same service at a lower cost on newer rails. That is exactly the kind of incremental improvement I warn against: reducing costs by some percentage rather than replacing how the system fundamentally operates.

Thomas César-Herriau as Brex engineer number four is the strongest signal in this dossier. He watched a fintech company scale from a dozen people to a $12 billion valuation, and he built foundational transaction and observability systems along the way. That is not trivial -- it is rare, deep infrastructure knowledge applied to a domain he knows intimately. Zsika's personal connection to the Caribbean and Latin American corridors adds authentic founder-market fit. But here is what concerns me: this team composition is *insider expertise applied to the insider playbook*. Thomas knows how to build fintech infrastructure because he built fintech infrastructure. Zsika studied entrepreneurship at Stanford and product management at Google. These are impressive credentials for executing a known model, not signals of the outsider thinking that invents new categories. The approach -- Visa card plus stablecoin settlement plus mobile app plus yield on balances -- is the standard stablecoin neobank architecture. I can find the same feature set described on KAST's website.

The strongest bull case for SpotPay requires believing three things simultaneously: that consumer stablecoin neobanking will consolidate around corridor specialists rather than global platforms, that Caribbean and Latin American corridors represent a sufficiently large and defensible niche to build a category-defining company, and that SpotPay's two-person team can outexecute competitors with 20 to 100 times more capital in a business that demands corridor-by-corridor regulatory licensing and payment rail integration. The timing of the stablecoin infrastructure curve is genuinely favorable -- supply grew 60x in five years, payment volume doubled in 2024-2025 alone. If SpotPay achieves network effects in specific corridors before the well-funded players get there, those corridors could become defensible. Thomas's infrastructure expertise could mean they ship faster with fewer people. But this bull case applies equally to half a dozen other stablecoin neobanks, and none of them are capital-constrained.

The technology here is assembled, not invented. SpotPay did not build the stablecoin rails, the Visa card issuance infrastructure, or the Apple Pay integration. They are orchestrating existing components into a consumer product. The dossier itself states that "building a Visa-enabled stablecoin wallet is technically reproducible by well-funded teams." When there is no proprietary technology and the differentiation comes down to corridor-specific execution, user acquisition cost, and regulatory licensing -- that is an operational competition, not a technology disruption. I invest in technologies that make existing approaches economically unviable. SpotPay is using a technology platform that others also have access to, competing on execution in a capital-intensive race it entered with the smallest war chest.

I pass. Not because the problem is unimportant -- remittance costs genuinely need to come down. And not because the founders lack capability -- Thomas's Brex pedigree is substantive. I pass because the ambition is calibrated to capture a slice of a trend rather than to create something the world has never seen. When everyone agrees that stablecoin neobanks are the future and the question is merely which team executes best in which corridors, that is a competition I leave to the growth equity investors. I look for founders attempting what others consider impossible, building in spaces where experts are actively dismissive. SpotPay is building what experts already endorse, in a category where capital has already crowded in. The probability of building a decent business may be reasonable. But the consequence of SpotPay's specific success -- as distinct from the broader stablecoin trend succeeding -- is not large enough to justify my kind of bet.
