# Korso -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Korso is that this is not a schlep blindness opportunity. Schlep blindness is when everyone knows the problem exists but unconsciously avoids it because the work is ugly. Manufacturing quoting is ugly — drawings arriving via WhatsApp, pricing scattered across spreadsheets, purchase orders tracked in someone's head. But the unconscious avoidance part? That's already over. Paperless Parts has $30M in funding. Uptool just raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI — at least five funded teams are working on essentially this problem right now. When Stripe started, the payment processing schlep was hiding in plain sight and nobody was touching it. The manufacturing quoting schlep is no longer hiding. It's a recognized market with recognized competitors and recognized investors. That changes the entire calculus.

The second thing — and this is the one that concerns me more — is the absence of any organic backstory connecting these founders to manufacturing operations. How did they find this problem? The dossier gives me nothing. Martin Pan spent time at GM as an automations engineer, which at least puts him inside a manufacturing company, but that's a large-company engineering role, not the experience of sitting in a 40-person machine shop drowning in RFQ emails at 6am. Daichi Hiraoka is an electrical engineer who did ML research on radiation therapy. Alex Liu dropped out of school and reads research papers. These are smart, technically capable people. But when I ask "did they stumble into this problem through their own lives, or did they sit down and try to think of startup ideas?" — the evidence points firmly toward the latter. I see no moment of organic discovery, no personal frustration that couldn't be ignored, no years of accumulated domain knowledge about how manufacturers actually operate. This pattern — talented engineers picking a vertical because AI makes it newly tractable — is exactly the "made up" idea failure mode. The idea exists because they decided to start a startup, not because they couldn't stop thinking about manufacturing quotes.

The strongest bull case would be this: the "why now" is legitimate, LLMs genuinely crossed a threshold for understanding unstructured manufacturing documents in 2024-2025, and the team's combination of electrical engineering, mechanical engineering, and software gives them real technical range for this domain. Two of the three founders — Daichi and Martin — worked together on Penn Electric Racing and are both from Irvine, so the co-founder relationship has roots. Young technical teams can move fast, and if they're willing to spend months embedded in machine shops doing the unscalable work of understanding how quotes actually flow, they could develop the domain intuition they currently lack. Andrew Miklas, their YC partner, co-founded PagerDuty — he understands infrastructure products. And the incumbents (Paperless Parts especially) predate the frontier LLM wave, which creates a genuine technology discontinuity. If Korso's founders are the ones who go deepest on the actual workflow — not just the quoting step but the full quote-to-order lifecycle including PO tracking and supplier communication — and if they do it with the obsessive user focus that turns SMB manufacturers into evangelists, the bundled approach could win despite the competition.

But that's a lot of "ifs," and the core problem remains: I don't see evidence that these founders have a relationship with this problem that their competitors lack. In a market with five funded alternatives, the question becomes "why this team?" The answer for Stripe was that the Collisons were programmers who personally hated the payments experience and built the first version themselves. The answer for DoorDash was that Tony Xu personally delivered food. What's the answer for Korso? An internship at GM and a racing team at Penn? That's not nothing, but it's not the kind of founder-problem fit that lets a three-person pre-seed team outrun a competitor with $30M and an established customer base. There's no public GitHub presence for any founder, no evidence of a working product beyond the website, and zero traction signals — no revenue, no users, no LOIs, no pilots. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of traction data, but I do notice when there's literally nothing to point to.

The honest assessment is that Korso looks like what happens when capable young engineers identify a real market that AI has made newly addressable and decide to build for it. The market is real. The technology timing is right. The schlep is genuine. But the founders don't seem to have a proprietary relationship with the problem, the competitive field is already crowded with better-funded teams, and I see no signal — organic discovery, creative resourcefulness, early user love — that would give me conviction this particular team will win this particular race. I'd need to see them do something that surprised me before writing a check.
