# RamAIn -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

RamAIn is building a better version of something Anthropic and OpenAI are already shipping as first-party features. That's the whole problem. When your core technology is a middleware layer sitting between a foundation model provider's API and an enterprise customer's desktop, you are not building a business -- you are building a feature that your own supplier will absorb. Anthropic launched Computer Use in public beta in October 2024. OpenAI shipped a competing CUA. These companies have every incentive to make their native computer-use capabilities production-grade, and the pre-training-on-specific-interfaces trick RamAIn is betting on is an obvious next step that any team at Anthropic or OpenAI could prioritize in a quarter. This isn't speculation -- it's the structural logic of platform providers eating their middleware.

The founders are clearly sharp. Vansh Ramani's ML credentials are real -- an ICLR 2025 publication, research at CMU's ML department, and a nearest-neighbor algorithm that got integrated into Meta's FAISS library. That's not noise. Shourya Vir Jain bootstrapped Genoshi to six-figure revenue and has McKinsey exposure, which gives him some enterprise sales intuition. So the "sane" side of my framework checks out. But the idea is not "insane" in the productive sense -- it's the most consensus play in AI right now. "AI agents for enterprise automation" is what every second YC company, every AI lab, and every well-funded startup is chasing. Simular has $27 million and an ex-DeepMind team with a Microsoft partnership. Browser Use raised $17 million from Felicis. Browserbase has $40 million. RamAIn is bringing a slingshot to a gunfight that's already crowded with well-armed combatants. A sane person pursuing a consensus idea is the configuration my framework penalizes most severely, because the returns are already priced in by the market's enthusiasm.

The strongest bull case would require believing that pre-training on specific enterprise UI interfaces creates a compounding data moat -- that the library of pre-trained interface models becomes a proprietary asset that scales with each customer deployment, eventually making RamAIn the "Rosetta Stone" for legacy enterprise software. If they accumulate hundreds of pre-trained interface models across healthcare EMRs, insurance claims systems, and procurement platforms, that corpus could theoretically become difficult to replicate. The "deploy in days" claim, if real, would represent a genuine unlock over traditional RPA's months-long implementation cycles. And the $35 billion RPA market is full of customers desperate for something better than UiPath's brittle scripts. But for this bull case to hold, you need to believe that neither Anthropic, OpenAI, Simular, nor any of the other dozen funded competitors will build the same pre-training capability -- and that's a bet against the entire trajectory of how foundation model providers expand their feature surface. I've seen this movie before with platform middleware companies, and the ending is always the same.

My "cherry on top" test kills this deal outright. Would RamAIn be a good business if AI didn't exist? Obviously not -- it IS an AI business, through and through. There are no physical operations, no regulatory moats, no protocol-layer positioning, no proprietary data that compounds independently of the AI models underneath. The business has no floor. Remove the AI capability and you have nothing. Compare this to Craftwork, where the painting crews exist regardless of whether the scheduling software uses AI -- that's a business where technology amplifies an operational moat. RamAIn has no such cushion. The pricing is listed as "Free" at $0, there are no disclosed customers, no revenue signals, no LOIs. The two-person team targeting enterprise verticals with long sales cycles (healthcare, insurance, finance) stretches credulity further.

The narrative of "AI employees" has theoretical magnitude -- you can imagine it expanding to encompass every knowledge worker workflow in every enterprise. But that's the same narrative that a hundred other companies are telling right now, and when the narrative is shared by the entire market, it stops being an "infinity story" and starts being an "everyone story." I need the narrative to be differentiated, not just big. The pre-training angle is an architectural choice, not a narrative moat.
