# Indexable -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is a flashing warning. Six funded startups, three cloud providers with sandbox features, and at least two open-source projects -- all building variations of "isolated environments for AI agents." When I see this pattern, I reach for the Girardian lens: every player in this market is imitating every other player. E2B raises $32 million and signs up 88% of the Fortune 100. Daytona raises $31 million and crosses $1 million ARR in two months. Cloudflare launches Dynamic Workers. Fly.io launches Sprites. The unanimous agreement that "AI agents need sandboxes" is the most consensus thesis in infrastructure right now. Where everyone agrees, there is no secret -- and where there is no secret, there is no path to monopoly, only a path to margin compression.

And yet. Something unusual is happening beneath this consensus surface. Andrew Gazelka did not take the easy path. Every competitor in this space uses off-the-shelf virtualization -- Firecracker, Docker, V8 isolates -- and wraps it in an API. Gazelka built a custom virtual machine monitor on KVM, wrote non-upstream patches for a custom Linux 6.19 kernel, built a content-addressable storage engine with tiered caching, and implemented RDMA-capable transport over libfabric. This is not a wrapper company. This is someone who looked at the available infrastructure and concluded it was structurally inadequate, then rebuilt from the hypervisor up. The decision to build a custom VMM at pre-seed, with three people, is either the mark of a founder with genuine conviction and a definite plan -- or it is a catastrophic misallocation of engineering resources. The answer depends entirely on whether the architectural bet is correct: that off-the-shelf virtualization cannot deliver true full-state forking (databases, processes, memory as an atomic unit), and that AI agent workflows will demand this capability as they mature.

The strongest bull case requires examining what "26-millisecond full-environment forking including databases" actually means. If you need to fork a running PostgreSQL instance with dirty pages in memory, pending transactions, and open file descriptors -- all as a single atomic snapshot that can be independently mutated -- you cannot do this with container-level isolation. Docker does not control the hypervisor. Firecracker gives you microVM snapshots but not database-level state coherence. This is the potential secret: the future of agent execution is not ephemeral containers but persistent, forkable state trees, and building this requires owning the entire stack from the VMM up. If this is correct, every competitor is structurally limited by their architectural foundation. They can optimize cold start times, but they cannot offer the capability that matters. Daytona's "fork/snapshot primitives" on Docker are fundamentally constrained in state fidelity. E2B's ephemeral sessions are the wrong abstraction entirely. The competitive landscape, in this reading, is illusory -- Indexable is the only company that can actually deliver what the next generation of agent platforms will require. This is the Stripe analogy: multiple payment companies existed, but Stripe's developer-first API was a structural advantage that the legacy architecture could not replicate. If Gazelka's custom VMM is the equivalent structural bet, the six-competitor landscape is irrelevant.

I do not fully buy this bull case, for two reasons. First, the magnitude of the advantage is unclear. The speed differential is 3-6x (26ms versus 90-150ms), not the 10x I look for. The capability differential -- full database-level forking -- may be genuinely qualitative, but this depends on whether agent platforms actually need this fidelity, or whether file-system-level snapshots are sufficient for 90% of use cases. If most agent workflows can tolerate the limitations of Firecracker snapshots, Indexable has built a technically superior product for a narrow use case while E2B owns the broad market. Second, and more fundamental: this market does not exhibit monopoly dynamics. There are no network effects in sandbox infrastructure. Switching costs are moderate but not structural -- a platform that integrates Indexable's fork API could migrate to Daytona's fork API without rebuilding their product. The content-addressable deduplication could create cost advantages at scale, but this is speculative and unproven. Without a clear mechanism through which early dominance compounds into structural defensibility, even a technically superior product risks becoming one of several adequate options in a fragmented market.

Gazelka's technical biography is impressive and directly relevant. Hyperion -- a Minecraft game engine in Rust targeting 10,000 concurrent players -- is precisely the kind of massively concurrent state management problem that maps to VM forking and sandbox orchestration. His xAI tenure gives him context on frontier AI workloads. The BountyBot acquisition shows he can ship product, not just build engines. The decision to build custom everything from the hypervisor up, at pre-seed, with three people, demonstrates the kind of definite conviction I value -- this is not a founder who is testing and iterating, this is a founder who has a specific architectural thesis and is executing it without compromise. But he is a solo listed founder with 86 Twitter followers and a website that returns 403. The surface area of custom infrastructure (VMM, kernel, storage engine, Nix fork, RDMA transport) against a three-person team creates severe concentration risk. If Gazelka is hit by a bus, the company's technical moat walks out the door with him.

The fundamental tension: this is a company with a genuinely differentiated technical approach competing in a market that violates my core investment criterion. The technology may be 0-to-1, but the market is definitively 1-to-n. Multiple funded players are fighting over the same customers with the same pitch. The path to monopoly would require either a dramatic capability gap that makes competitors structurally inadequate (possible but unproven) or a market consolidation event that leaves one winner (unpredictable and not investable). I respect the technical ambition and the founder's conviction, but I cannot invest my own money in a company whose market structure is a textbook case of mimetic competition -- regardless of how elegant the underlying architecture may be.
