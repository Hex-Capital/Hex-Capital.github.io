# Kinro -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

WTW -- Willis Towers Watson, a $30B+ insurance brokerage giant -- appearing in Kinro's "Trusted By" section is either the most impressive pre-seed client signal I've seen this batch or the most misleading. Let me take the bull case seriously for a second. If WTW is genuinely deploying Kinro's AI sales agents in production, that's a 3-person team selling infrastructure to one of the three largest insurance brokers on Earth. The SOC 2 Type II certification is real compliance work. Pierre-Alexandre Kamienny's DeepMind pedigree -- leading Gemini work for financial services specifically -- is about as targeted a founder-market credential as you could draw up. The 20%-to-40% conversion rate improvement and 48-second time-to-quote, if those numbers hold at scale, represent the kind of distribution efficiency that rewires an industry's economics. You could construct a story where Kinro becomes the Stripe of insurance distribution -- regulated financial plumbing that every carrier needs but none wants to build. I've made money backing financial rails before.

Here's the problem: Kinro is an AI application. Full stop. Every time I see "AI agents for [vertical X]," I run the kill test. Can WTW -- Kinro's own client, sitting right there in the "Trusted By" section -- build this internally? Of course they can. They have more carrier integrations than Kinro will have in five years. They have compliance teams that make Kinro's SOC 2 certification look like a science fair ribbon. They have distribution. OpenAI opened app submissions in February 2026, and twelve additional insurance apps are already in the pipeline. Aviva launched its own ChatGPT insurance app in March 2026. Kinro isn't finding something the market has dismissed -- Kinro is sprinting into a channel that every insurance company on the planet just noticed simultaneously. That's the opposite of mispricing. That's consensus with a two-month head start.

Pierre-Alexandre Kamienny is clearly brilliant -- five publications across ICML, NeurIPS, and ICLR, a PhD in reinforcement learning, and the DeepMind financial services work. Parth Ainampudi built ML infrastructure at Zoox for safety-critical autonomous systems. Corentin Hugot was first employee at an insurance startup and brings the broker-carrier relationships. These are sane, accomplished people. But what they're building is also sane. "AI sales agents for insurance" is exactly what a smart team with these credentials would build -- it's rational, it's well-timed, it's defensible on a slide. Nobody hears this pitch and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "that makes sense." And when every smart person in the room thinks something makes sense, the pricing reflects it. Sane founder, sane idea is the one combination that doesn't excite me.

Kinro's structural position reminds me of why I don't invest in dapps -- I invest in protocols. Kinro is building on OpenAI's platform, on Google's Gemini, on Anthropic's Claude. Those are the protocols. Kinro is the application layer. The multi-turn conversational optimization that Kamienny describes on LinkedIn is genuinely interesting technical work, but it's optimization within someone else's ecosystem. If OpenAI changes app store policies, increases revenue-share requirements, or launches native insurance features, Kinro's distribution strategy evaporates overnight. This is platform dependency layered on top of carrier dependency layered on top of regulatory dependency -- three levels of permission required just to operate. Compare that to Metropolis, where the parking infrastructure physically exists and no platform policy change can delete it. Kinro has regulatory compliance as a barrier, but WaniWani -- backed by Hexa, which is literally a studio model I'd normally dismiss -- already powered the first approved insurance app on ChatGPT through Tuio. The compliance moat is real but not unique to Kinro, and the competitive space is forming in months, not years.

The bull case advocate would say: "But the Slack exception! Kinro is creating the primitive of AI-native insurance distribution, just like Slack created real-time workplace messaging." I considered this. The difference is definitional. Slack created the behavioral primitive -- the thing that replaced email. The behavior itself was new. Kinro is using someone else's behavioral primitive (conversational AI) to distribute an existing product (insurance). The primitive here is the AI assistant. OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic built the primitive. Kinro is an app in their app store. That's not category creation -- that's channel optimization. What would flip me to Invest: if Kinro demonstrated that it owned proprietary carrier-binding infrastructure that no competitor could replicate within 18 months -- not compliance checklists, but actual exclusive technical integrations with specific carrier underwriting systems that create real switching costs. Evidence that WTW is paying meaningful revenue, not just piloting, would also change the math. But even then, the consensus mispricing problem remains -- this is a hot category, priced accordingly.

Kinro's capital discipline is unknowable at pre-seed beyond the basics: 3-person team, one open role at $120K-$300K, San Francisco. Nothing alarming, nothing distinctive. The $100B+ annual insurance commission market they cite is real but irrelevant -- TAM is never the constraint for a seed-stage company; the question is whether you can capture any of it with structural advantages, and Kinro's advantages are replicable. The Tracxn score of 16/100, ranked 56/57 in their category, is noise at this stage, but the signal buried in the competitive landscape is louder: WaniWani, Insurify, Avallon AI, RiskCube, plus twelve apps in the OpenAI pipeline, plus incumbents like Aviva building their own. Kinro is one of dozens of companies attacking AI insurance distribution right now. When I backed Solana at four cents, there was active consensus that it was worthless. When I look at Kinro, there's active consensus that AI insurance distribution is valuable. Those are opposite pricing environments, and I make money in the first one, not the second.
