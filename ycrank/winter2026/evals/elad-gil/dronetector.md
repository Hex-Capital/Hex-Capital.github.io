# DroneTector -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The structural moment here is undeniable and rare. Two years ago, counter-drone detection was an airport security niche -- a market most VCs would dismiss as "defense hardware, too slow, too capital-intensive." Then Ukraine demonstrated that $50 nano-drones are primary battlefield weapons at 1,600+ attacks per month, Red Sea Houthi drone strikes doubled global shipping costs, and suddenly every NATO defense ministry has an urgent procurement gap. What's changed isn't the technology -- millimetre-wave radar physics has been understood for decades -- it's that the threat profile shifted faster than existing radar systems could adapt. Traditional radar was designed to track aircraft, not objects with the radar cross-section of a bird. That mismatch between threat evolution and detection capability is the structural inflection.

This maps to a pattern I've seen before with Anduril: a defense technology vacuum created when incumbents can't or won't address an emerging threat category. Large defense primes -- Raytheon, L3Harris, Northrop -- optimize for multi-million-dollar platform programs. Building a low-cost tactical nano-drone sensor would cannibalize their existing radar lines. Software-only players like Dedrone (now Axon) can't detect autonomous drones that emit no RF signal. That's the gap DroneTector sits in. The regulatory and procurement barriers -- ITAR, defense certifications, NATO DIANA relationships -- create exactly the kind of compliance moat I've seen work in defense tech. But unlike Anduril, which had Palmer Luckey's brand and Founders Fund's capital, DroneTector is a 3-person academic team entering a market where DroneShield (AU$221M cash), Echodyne ($195M raised), and Robin Radar ($52M revenue) are already scaling. The counter-drone market is not non-obvious anymore -- it's consensus hot with $500M+ already deployed across competitors. The specific nano-drone detection angle is more differentiated, but the broader market awareness means pricing and distribution competition will be fierce.

The founder-market fit is genuinely strong. Matthew Moore didn't read about this problem and decide to start a company -- he literally built and validated 94 GHz Doppler radar for drone micro-Doppler detection during his PhD. That's the kind of deep technical credibility that matters in hardware. Thomas Doherty bringing a decade of optical physics plus the experience of founding Quantum Fabrix (which raised £2M from Paul Graham personally) adds operational credibility. Three PhDs in physics and computer science from St Andrews and Oxford is a team that can build the hardware. The concern is that Doherty was CEO of QFX until a new CEO was appointed in October 2025 -- just months before joining DroneTector for YC W26. That transition timeline is tight enough to warrant scrutiny, though not disqualifying.

The single-miracle test is where I get cautious. The obvious miracle is engineering a production-grade, field-deployable millimetre-wave radar at a cost point that defense and infrastructure customers will buy. Moore's PhD prototype validates the physics, but the gap between research hardware and manufacturable product is enormous in radar -- specialized components, supply chain, quality assurance, field reliability. That's one miracle. But there's a second: surviving 12-36 month defense procurement cycles as a pre-revenue startup with ~$500K in funding. These are sequential low-probability events, and I've written explicitly about why compounding miracles kills companies. NATO DIANA and DASA support help but don't guarantee contracts.

The bull case is compelling enough to take seriously: if millimetre-wave micro-Doppler is genuinely the right technical approach for nano-drone detection -- and Moore's peer-reviewed research suggests it is -- then DroneTector has a 3+ year head start on any competitor pivoting into W-band frequencies. The proprietary micro-Doppler signature database they'd build from deployments would compound over time, creating a data moat that gets harder to replicate. If the drone threat continues escalating (and every military analysis suggests it will), procurement cycles could compress dramatically -- wartime urgency changes the timeline math. A company that owns the nano-drone detection layer could expand into tracking, classification, and multi-sensor fusion -- becoming the detection infrastructure layer that defeat systems integrate with. That product-to-distribution trajectory from "radar hardware" to "counter-drone detection platform" is real, though harder to execute in hardware than software.

I'm in the upper range of neutral on this. The structural moment is real, the founder-market fit is strong, and the technical differentiation is genuine. But the market is no longer non-obvious -- it's crowded with well-funded competitors. The path from research prototype to production hardware requires capital and time that a 3-person team with YC-level funding doesn't obviously have. And the two-miracle problem (build production hardware AND survive procurement cycles) gives me pause. This is interesting enough to track closely, particularly if they secure a pilot contract with a NATO customer -- that would collapse both miracles into one and change the calculus entirely.
