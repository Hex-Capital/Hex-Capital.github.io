# Ardent -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Vikram Chennai pivoted into Ardent because he ran into the database-sandbox problem while operating Ardent AI -- the prior product he scaled to "$100K+ ARR in 6 months with 0 funding" before raising $2.15M in September 2025. That's the signature: a founder who *lived* the wedge through a prior product, dropped into the most consensus-saturated lane in 2026 venture -- AI infrastructure for coding agents, where Neon just sold to Databricks for ~$1B and Supabase already markets branching for agents in its docs. Ardent is the authentic-pivot founder in the crowded room. That pairing is the whole evaluation.

Anduril is the high rung on founder-lived-experience: Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus out of a garage before he ever filed a defense pitch, and the lived priority of the problem is what made the check legible. Ardent doesn't clear that bar -- Vikram is Cornell CS with a YC S23 interview and a finalist nod at the Cornell Spinout Awards, which is the wrong half of my outsider-trajectory pattern -- but the pivot mechanism does load: he was building Ardent AI, agents kept clobbering each other's writes against shared dev DBs, and the next product is the scar tissue of the prior one. That's a real lived moment, not a memo. Where Ardent diverges from the Anduril analogy is co-founder Evan, whose last name is not disclosed in the Launch HN post and whose LinkedIn does not surface in search -- "12 years in data engineering" is a sentence, not a person, and at pre-seed I want to know who the second name on the cap table actually is before I treat the founding team as load-bearing.

The pass example -- the consensus YC AI-agent darling with six VCs circling and a $200B TAM slide -- failed my "find them before it becomes consensus" filter because the lane was already priced as if the outcome was certain. Ardent walks into the same trap from a different door. Databricks bought Neon. Supabase ships branching tailored to AI agents. PlanetScale launched Postgres-compatible. Tembo and simplyblock Vela are the OSS shadow. The Launch HN post itself names Neon as Ardent's *primary branching engine* -- which means the moat is leased from the company most incentivized to close that door once Databricks finishes integrating. That isn't magically weird; that's wedge-between-giants, and the giants ship roadmaps quarterly.

On capital-intensity and unsexy-regulated swing, Ardent is the software-only default and the dossier doesn't argue otherwise: logical replication into Kafka, copy-on-write on a replica, Neon underneath. No hardware to touch, no regulator to charm, no roughneck to walk past. The 18-month mechanism is that cross-platform Postgres branching either becomes a feature inside RDS/Supabase/PlanetScale -- in which case Ardent's wedge compresses -- or Databricks decides Neon's API is for Databricks customers and Ardent's plumbing breaks. Neither outcome compounds in Ardent's favor; both compound against. This is the lane I usually discount, and the dossier gives me no reason to override.

CEO authority on Ardent would flip a tier only if the cap table and the operating agreement explicitly name Vikram as tiebreaker with Evan vesting on a four-year clock -- the inverse of the HQ Trivia (2017) Yusupov/Kroll failure I keep coming back to. LinkedIn lists Vikram as "Founder @ Ardent (YC X26)" and the Launch HN post is written in his voice, which is a soft positive, but two-person teams pre-seed with one co-founder publicly invisible is exactly the configuration where the conversation about tiebreaking hasn't happened yet. I'd want it on paper before I wired.

Flock Safety is the single anchor on physical-world verifiability: I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons before I read a deck, and the camera-on-a-pole was a tactile artifact a normal human could photograph. Ardent is a CLI install with six logos on the homepage -- Zenn Agents, Harvest, Open Ledger, Rose AI, Robynn AI, Clozers -- and a demo-booking funnel; better than a pure API black box because I can actually run the install, worse than Flock because the artifact is a developer abstraction that lives inside someone else's CI/CD. The Chevron endorsement from Abhay Singh is the most interesting signal in the dossier and the one I'd push hardest on in diligence -- not because Chevron pays yet, but because if it does, the artifact becomes legible in a way the homepage logos aren't.

My 36-month forecast: Ardent gets acqui-hired by Supabase, Databricks (via Neon), or PlanetScale for sub-$30M and the team folds into the acquirer's branching roadmap, or it stalls at $300K-$1M ARR and runs out of runway against the incumbent feature shipments. The mechanism is that the lived-experience founder caught a real problem but planted the flag inside a category where the giants own the substrate Ardent depends on -- when the substrate moves, the wedge moves with it.

The bull case stings here, and I want to say it cleanly: Vikram already pulled $100K ARR out of a prior product solo, he has lived through the exact pain his new product solves, the cross-platform-without-migration wedge is genuinely real for the long tail of RDS shops who will never move to Neon, and the Abhay Singh / Chevron endorsement is the kind of enterprise signal that doesn't show up on most pre-seed pages. If Ardent converts that Chevron endorsement into a signed $250K+ ARR contract within nine months, the verdict flips and I write the check -- because at that point the Neon dependency stops mattering, the founder has proven enterprise GTM twice in a row, and the wedge is wide enough that Databricks acquires *for* the customer base rather than the tech. That's the verifiable condition. Until then, the lane is consensus, the moat is leased, and I'm passing politely with the door cracked.
