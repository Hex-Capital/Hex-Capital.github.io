# Arzana -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Arzana isn't the product or the market -- it's the NYT headline: "These A.I. Dreamers Don't Fit the Stereotype." A 21-year-old from Okoboji, Iowa -- population 800 -- who taught himself Mandarin and learned discus from YouTube to earn a state ranking, teaming up with a D1 wrestler from Neenah, Wisconsin, earning $10,000 a year to sell AI to manufacturers in the heartland. My pattern recognition perks up. These are exactly the kinds of founders I gravitate toward -- outsiders who don't look like what central casting ordered. When I invested in SpaceX, nobody thought a South African web entrepreneur should be building rockets. When I backed Flock Safety, I noticed Garrett Langley's phone tethered to hotel WiFi -- the scrappiness was the signal. William Alexander and Marshall Kools have that same outsider energy, and the fact that they maintained a physical presence in Okoboji alongside San Francisco tells me they're building for people they actually know, not performing a thesis for Sand Hill Road.

But here's where I start to hesitate, and I have to be honest about it: AI automation for manufacturing is not an unpopular bet right now. It's becoming one of the most popular categories in enterprise AI. Endeavor AI raised $7 million from Craft Ventures. Canals AI has backing. Every VC I talk to is looking at "vertical AI agents for boring industries." My thesis depends on finding companies before consensus forms -- I'm looking for things that are difficult to assess and that other investors dismiss. Arzana's category is the opposite: it's easy to assess and everyone wants in. The founders are non-consensus, but the business is increasingly consensus. That's a meaningful tension in my framework. When I backed Crusoe Energy, the idea of mining Bitcoin on gas flares was so absurd that nobody else would fund it. Manufacturing AI automation in 2026 is not that kind of bet.

What I do find genuinely compelling is the invisible systems pattern. I've invested repeatedly in companies that make opaque processes transparent -- Carta did it for cap tables, Affirm for credit card fees, Flock Safety for neighborhood safety data. Arzana is doing something similar: the front office of American manufacturing runs on handwritten notes, PDFs, email threads, and spreadsheets that nobody outside the building ever sees. Cutting a quote cycle from five days to one day at Tier One Technologies isn't just efficiency -- it's making a previously invisible bottleneck legible and fixable. And they're doing this for mid-market manufacturers at $2,500 to $7,000 a month, which means companies that could never afford an SAP consulting engagement can now access the same operational intelligence. That economic access angle is real and it's the part of this company that most activates my conviction.

The founder-market connection is where I want to believe more than the evidence lets me. William grew up in Iowa near manufacturing, did some consulting for Italian manufacturers through the Brilla era, and has Mason Vander Pol -- also from Okoboji -- as his field engineer. That's a legitimate network advantage and it shows in the traction: 20-plus plants, nine named logos including Schneider National and Zeiss, real published pricing. For a pre-seed company with four people, that's strong commercial execution. But the path here was meal delivery to manufacturing AI, and the CEO has taken one coding class. I'm not someone who needs founders to have PhDs -- I didn't finish high school -- but I need the biographical thread that tells me this person will be solving this problem in ten years regardless of whether the company works. The self-teaching instinct is there, the scrappiness is there, the Midwestern roots create authentic access. What I'm missing is the story where William or Marshall watched someone they love struggle with a manufacturing quoting nightmare and decided they'd fix it or die trying. It may exist. I just don't see it in the evidence.

The bull case is genuinely interesting: if William Alexander is the kind of founder who absorbs domains the way he absorbed Mandarin and discus -- by sheer obsessive self-teaching -- then his lack of formal technical training is a feature, not a bug. The early traction suggests he can sell, and the Okoboji network gives him distribution advantages that a Stanford AI lab founder would kill for. If this team can deepen their ERP integration moat and accumulate manufacturing-specific training data faster than Endeavor can deploy their $7 million, Arzana could become the default operating system for mid-market American manufacturing. That's a large, consequential outcome. The question is whether I'm the right investor for a bet that requires believing in execution speed against a better-funded competitor in a category that's rapidly becoming consensus. In my experience, the best investments don't require me to out-analyze the competition -- they require me to see something nobody else sees. Here, I see what a lot of people see, just through a slightly different lens.

I respect these founders. The sacrifice is real, the traction is real, and the economic access angle resonates with how I think about capitalism as a tool for lifting people up. But my portfolio is built on companies that look wrong to almost everyone at the time I invest. Arzana looks right to almost everyone right now, and that makes me cautious.
