# Stage -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Stage is the cleanest possible governance story stapled to the loudest possible consensus wedge -- Charles Pan as named CEO, Dean Stratakos as CTO, two Stanford CS classmates who already worked together at Five Rings, attacking AI code review in the same quarter Cursor paid for Graphite and CodeRabbit closed at a $550M valuation. The team chart is the strongest thing in the dossier. The market chart is the weakest. That pairing is the whole evaluation.

CodeRabbit is the high rung on the "magically weird, non-consensus" ladder inverted -- $96M raised, $15M+ ARR growing 20% per month, Greptile pulling $25M from Benchmark, Cursor swallowing Graphite in December 2025, cubic shipping the YC X25 version of the same wedge. Stage is not finding a category before it becomes consensus; Stage is YC P26's eighth entry into a category that already has a $550M comp. "Putting humans back in control of code review" is a clever framing slide, but the dossier itself flags that buyers and capital are flowing to AI-auto-reviewers, not to better human-reviewer UX. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing -- and the game here is one where six well-funded incumbents can ship chapter-style diff grouping in a sprint. That is the opposite of the Crusoe pattern, where three credible VCs had already passed on aesthetic grounds and that was the tell.

SpaceX 2008 is the low rung Stage cannot touch on capital-intensive frontier swing, and I'm not pretending it should -- but the absence matters because the "ugly, regulated, capital-intensive, aesthetically transgressive" lane is where my edge actually compounds. Stage is a TypeScript CLI wrapping GitHub PRs. There is no roughneck to talk to, no well pad to walk on, no defense contract to chase, no regulatory body to befriend. The dossier explicitly notes GitHub, GitLab, Cursor and CodeRabbit all "have the engineering capability to add AI-organized diff views." When the moat is UI organization on top of a Microsoft-owned platform, every dollar of upside flows through someone else's distribution.

Founder-market fit on Stage compounds the wrong direction over 18 months -- Stratakos built an internal coding agent at Five Rings and Pan saw PR fragmentation at Yuzu Health, which is real exposure but not autobiographical contact with the problem the way Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus before Anduril. The dossier reads as two engineers who *noticed* AI code review was painful, not two engineers whose biography put them in front of it before everyone else. Over 18 months that gap matters because the founders who *lived* the problem will iterate past the founders who *analyzed* it; Stratakos's GitHub presence and the 204 stars on `stage-cli` are encouraging but not the Luckey-garage signal.

Counterfactually, Stage's "single CEO" dimension would flip a tier up if the dossier showed a documented operating agreement and founder-vesting protocol -- right now Charles Pan is named CEO and the team is two, so the HQ Trivia Yusupov-Kroll failure mode is unlikely on Day 1, but I have no evidence the tiebreaker conversation has happened in writing. Two Stanford classmates from the same employer is exactly the friendship-equity setup that goes sideways at Series A when one wants to sell and one doesn't. The score reflects "probably fine" not "structurally protected."

Stage's physical verifiability sits exactly where the Flock Safety Wi-Fi-name story does *not* -- I can `npm install` the CLI and I can read a Show HN thread, but the product is a developer abstraction whose value lives inside a reviewer's subjective sense of "this was faster." I cannot ride it the way I rode Uber in 2010, cannot photograph it the way Flock cameras sit on a pole, cannot feel the viral pull the way HQ Trivia hijacked my evenings. The 204 stars are real, modest, and consistent with a competent launch -- not consistent with the tactile-virality override that made me forgive the HQ Trivia governance mess.

Stage in 36 months is an acqui-hire to Cursor, GitHub, or CodeRabbit in the $5-25M range, or it is dead -- the mechanism is that chapter-style diff organization is a feature, not a company, and the incumbent with distribution will ship it natively within two product cycles. Stratakos and Pan land good engineering jobs at the acquirer. That outcome is consistent with the signature: clean governance, zero frontier risk, peak-consensus category.

The strongest bull case borrows from the HQ Trivia exception -- explosive product-led traction in a tactile artifact can override the surrounding red flags, and if Stage's chapter UX is so addictive that every Cursor user installs it the way every iPhone owner played HQ at 9pm, the consensus-category objection collapses. I want to believe it. The dossier kills it: 204 GitHub stars is a respectable launch, not a viral artifact, and the Show HN points and comments were not even retrievable. The category buyer is voting with capital for AI-auto-reviewers (CodeRabbit's 20%/month, Greptile-Benchmark, Cursor-Graphite), not for human-augment tooling. One verifiable condition would flip the verdict: Stage publishing a credible >50% reviewer-time-reduction A/B against GitHub native at a named enterprise customer within six months, the way Flock could point to a specific recovered vehicle. Until then, this is a pass with warmth -- Pan and Stratakos are exactly the kind of competent Stanford pair who will build something better next time, just not this time.
