# Robby -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the gap between the credentials and the domain. Three HBS grads -- one from Bain, one from a16z, one a founding engineer at Cloud Health -- building an upsell engine for HVAC technicians. The credentials are genuinely impressive. But impressive credentials are the opposite of specific knowledge. Specific knowledge is what you know that can't be taught in a classroom. Bain literally teaches associates to analyze PE-backed home services roll-ups -- that's the definition of trainable pattern recognition. The team recognizes this gap, which is why they're doing ride-alongs with technicians and studying for EPA 608 certification. That shows intellectual honesty. But acquiring domain immersion over six months is not the same as possessing the non-transferable insight that comes from running an HVAC business for a decade. When I backed Evan Williams at Twitter, he didn't need to shadow bloggers to understand public communication -- he was the guy who built Blogger. The pivot sequence sharpens this concern: Importrix (customs brokerage AI), Lifelong Health, and now Robby, all within roughly a year. That's three different domains explored by smart generalists looking for a wedge, not obsessives who've been circling one problem for years.

The leverage architecture is where this falls further from my framework. Robby combines CRM data with third-party sources to generate leads and talking points for technicians. That's useful automation. But it's not a new form of leverage -- it's making an existing sales process slightly more efficient. Compare this to what Replit does: it enables a completely new category of person to write software. Or what AngelList did: it gave individual angels access to deal flow that was previously locked behind institutional gatekeepers. Robby makes an HVAC technician slightly better at upselling a thermostat. The output-to-input ratio isn't transformative. There's no network effect between customers -- one plumbing company's data doesn't meaningfully improve recommendations for an electrician across town. The cross-customer learning potential exists in theory but the domains are narrow enough that the data advantage is incremental, not exponential.

The competitive positioning is consensus, not contrarian. "AI for revenue growth in an underserved vertical" is the modal YC pitch in 2026. No smart person is dismissing this idea -- ServiceTitan has already announced overlapping AI features (Atlas Campaign Recommendations, SMS Booking Agent). Arch is building AI-driven lead generation for the same ServiceTitan user base. This is the crowded lane where you don't get paid for being right because everyone already agrees it's right. The ServiceTitan dependency is the structural version of this problem: Robby is a feature sitting on top of a platform controlled by a $9B public company that has every incentive and capability to absorb this functionality. When I invested in Alchemy, it was building developer infrastructure for an ecosystem where no single platform could become the gatekeeper. Robby is building a revenue layer for an ecosystem where ServiceTitan *is* the gatekeeper.

The strongest bull case deserves honest engagement. Nine paying customers at pre-seed is real signal -- most YC companies at this stage have zero revenue. The "$40K closed within first hour" claim, if accurately attributed, suggests the product creates immediate measurable value that sells itself through ROI. If the data flywheel actually works -- if each technician visit generates proprietary intelligence that improves the next recommendation -- there's a compounding story. And if PE-backed home services consolidators adopt Robby as a standard part of their acquisition playbook (which Schwarzmann's Bain network could enable), the distribution story gets interesting. The path to a meaningful outcome exists. But it's a path to a $50-100M acquisition by ServiceTitan, not a path to a compounding platform business. That's the "optimized for early acquisition rather than compounding" pattern I actively avoid.

The team is smart, energetic, and shows integrity signals -- the ride-alongs, the EPA certification pursuit, the prior working relationship between Feroze and Joseph. I don't question their character or work ethic. But this is a case where intelligence and energy are applied to a domain the founders are learning rather than one they've inhabited. The technology doesn't compound in any distinctive way -- combining CRM data with third-party sources to generate sales prompts is technically replicable by any well-funded competitor in months. The timing isn't contrarian. The leverage is linear, not exponential. I'd pass and keep watching for the team's next move -- because the founders themselves might be a better bet than this particular problem.
