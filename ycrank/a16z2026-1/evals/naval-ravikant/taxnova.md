# Taxnova -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most structurally interesting thing about Taxnova is not the product — it's the principal-agent trap the incumbents are sitting in. EY, BDO, and Grant Thornton derive significant revenue from manually preparing R&D tax claims over three-to-six-month engagements. Building software that automates 75% of that process would cannibalize their own advisory fees. Taxnova's "advisor-agnostic" positioning — infrastructure for advisors, not a replacement — is the same structural play that made Perplexity viable against Google: the incumbent has the specific knowledge to build this but is an agent of their own revenue model, unable to self-cannibalize. George Nichkov and Maria Malykh are principals with no legacy revenue to protect, competing against agents handcuffed by their own P&L. That dynamic creates a real opening.

Where Taxnova falls short for me is the specific knowledge question. Nichkov's background is Oliver Wyman consulting and Gett product leadership — enterprise operating chops, not R&D tax obsession. Malykh managed nine R&D teams at Criteo, which gives her firsthand knowledge of how engineering organizations produce the artifacts that become tax claims, but that's proximity to the problem, not years of living inside it. The domain expertise lives in three additional team members from EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, and the IRS, plus Revolut's Head of Tax as an advisor. This is assembled knowledge, not productized founder knowledge. If Nichkov disappeared tomorrow, you'd hire another competent B2B operator. That's the test. Compare this to when I backed AngelList — I'd spent a decade as a founder and angel, and the product was my specific knowledge made into software. Taxnova is closer to a well-researched market opportunity staffed with the right hires than a founder who couldn't stop thinking about R&D tax until they built the solution.

The leverage architecture is genuinely good. Fifteen-plus read-only integrations across Jira, GitHub, Linear, Slack, Xero, and QuickBooks mean Taxnova's AI generates audit-ready documentation from existing engineering artifacts — the 75% of claim time spent on data collection drops to a thirty-minute setup. That's real zero-marginal-cost leverage within each jurisdiction. The compound interest test partially passes: each claim processed builds structured mappings between engineering activity and tax eligibility, creating a data flywheel that improves accuracy for the next customer. The jurisdiction expansion is where pure leverage breaks down — each new country requires bespoke tax logic, ongoing regulatory tracking, and potentially local partnerships. UK is live, US is "coming soon." SPRX/Onshore has $46M and is already entrenched in the US. Neo.Tax has $13M and Mercury distribution. Taxnova has $1M. Multi-jurisdiction complexity is both Taxnova's moat and its scaling constraint — the same feature that makes replication hard also makes expansion slow.

The traction deserves honest acknowledgment. $100K ARR at pre-seed with incident.io and Paddle as named customers, a $200K+ ACV enterprise pilot, and a completed channel pilot with a Top 5 R&D advisory firm — that's real pull, not manufactured growth. Dozens of early customers generating revenue before the February 2026 public launch. Malykh's building-in-public Substack signals some principal energy — people performing founder theater don't document the messy middle. But the co-founder relationship shows no shared prior employer, university, or visible pre-existing trust bond. That's not the red flag of a "find a co-founder" event, but it's not the deep trust foundation I prefer either.

The bull case I have to take seriously: R&D tax is a $10B market where HMRC inquiry rates jumped from 0.5% to 20% in three years, creating genuine urgency for audit-ready documentation. LLM capabilities crossed a quality threshold for technical narrative generation in the last eighteen months, creating a "why now" that's real, not manufactured. Taxnova's advisor-agnostic channel strategy could scale faster than direct sales — one Top 5 firm partnership could deliver hundreds of clients. The team of five generating $100K ARR with an enterprise pipeline suggests the leverage thesis is working in practice. And the access democratization is genuine: smaller tech companies that couldn't afford Big 4 advisory fees can now claim R&D credits they're entitled to. That pattern — unlocking restricted resources at scale — runs through AngelList, CoinList, and Udemy in my portfolio.

The risk that would flip me: Taxnova is entering the US market late against SPRX ($46M), Neo.Tax ($13M), and Boast ($30M). If the US expansion stalls, Taxnova becomes a UK-focused R&D tax tool with a ceiling dictated by the £7.6B UK claims market. The AI narrative generation layer is the most commoditizable part of the stack — the dossier itself notes this. The defensible layer is jurisdiction-specific tax logic and integration depth, which is real but requires ongoing engineering investment to maintain and expand. Evidence that would change my mind: if Nichkov or Malykh revealed years of personal engagement with R&D tax claims before founding Taxnova — some prior project, some obsessive side exploration — that would move specific knowledge from "assembled" to "innate" and shift my conviction significantly.

Taxnova is a well-constructed business with real traction and a smart structural positioning against agent-trapped incumbents. The leverage architecture works. The access democratization is genuine. But my highest-weighted criterion — specific knowledge productization — is the weakest dimension. The founders identified an opportunity and recruited expertise. That's competent entrepreneurship. It's not the same as a founder whose non-replicable knowledge IS the company. The $100K ARR and advisory firm pilot keep this from being a pass — there's enough signal to watch — but not enough founder-specific-knowledge density for me to write a check from my own money today.
