# 21st.dev -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what hits me immediately about 1Code: this is the most consensus-validated space in technology right now. Cursor is at $1.2 billion in ARR. Cognition raised nearly $700 million for Devin. GitHub Copilot CLI went GA in February. Cline shipped native subagents in February. Every major player is converging on exactly the same feature set -- parallel agent orchestration with a visual UI -- at exactly the same time. When I look at a deal and every investor in the Valley is already circling the category, that's usually the moment I walk away. The most promising companies tend to be unpopular and difficult to assess. AI developer tooling in early 2026 is neither. This is a category where consensus has already formed so completely that the billion-dollar incumbents are shipping competitive features monthly.

When I sit down with a founder, I don't want to hear about the market first. I want to hear the story -- how they got here, what happened to them that made this problem impossible to ignore. And what I see with this team is something that doesn't resonate with how I evaluate. Serafim describes himself as "one of the first full-time vibe-coders since October 2023." Nine products shipped in ten months -- a UI component registry, then Magic MCP, then 1Code. That's extraordinary execution speed, I won't deny it. But it's the opposite of the signal I trust. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research that everyone else dismissed. When I wrote my first check to SpaceX, Elon couldn't stop talking about making humanity multiplanetary -- it wasn't a pivot from a crypto protocol. The biographical thread that connects a founder to their specific problem, the thing that tells me they'll still be working on this when everything goes sideways, I can't find it here. Serafim's journey runs through crypto exchanges, stablecoin wallets, and meme token platforms before landing on developer tools. Sergey built clever products at speed -- Replai, Suggesty, a GPT-3 Chrome extension -- but these are demonstrations of technical fluency, not a story about why agent orchestration is the problem he was born to solve. These are talented builders surfing to where the energy is, which is a legitimate strategy, but it's not my kind of bet.

The bull case deserves honest consideration because the execution signal is genuinely impressive. A million users across their products. Three separate Product Hunt #1 launches. Five thousand GitHub stars on 1Code in just a couple months. Sergey monetized Replai at $1K MRR in its first month, which shows commercial instinct. The team has Sergey's enterprise engineering chops from Deel, Eyas's product discipline from Google and MIT, and Serafim's proven ability to build developer community. If the question is "can this team ship fast and attract developers?" the answer is clearly yes. What would have to be true for this to be a great investment is that the orchestration layer becomes a durable category distinct from IDEs -- that Cursor and GitHub can't just absorb this functionality the way Microsoft absorbed browsers. You'd need to believe that being agent-agnostic creates a structural advantage that IDE vendors can't replicate because of their business model conflicts. And you'd need the open-source community around 1Code to compound into a real moat before the window closes. It's a plausible story, and a different investor who weights execution velocity and market timing over the things I weight might write this check with confidence.

But I'm investing my own money, and I need to feel something beyond "this team is fast." I look for companies that create economic access or make invisible systems transparent -- Uber created income for drivers, Affirm made credit terms honest, Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities that lacked it. 1Code is a productivity tool for software engineers who are already among the best-compensated workers in the economy. It doesn't expand who gets to participate. It doesn't make an opaque system visible. And the space it operates in -- developer tools SaaS -- carries no regulatory complexity, no stigma, no barrier that would create the kind of moat I've learned to value from my IronPort days. The three co-founders have no documented shared history before this company, and with the velocity of pivots they've executed, I'd want to understand how they handle the disagreement that inevitably comes when growth stalls and they need to decide whether to pivot again or dig in. That's the HQ Trivia lesson I carry: product timing doesn't survive founder misalignment.

This team will probably build something that gets acquired or generates meaningful revenue. They're skilled, fast, and well-positioned in a growing market. But nothing about this opportunity activates the pattern recognition I've learned to trust. It's popular, it's easy to assess, and the founders are chasing the wave rather than being dragged into it by lived experience. I'm passing.
