# Hedge -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Hedge is a wholesale insurance brokerage with an AI ingestion layer pitched at retail agents placing hard E&S risks ? a workflow-arbitrage business whose highest dimension is a founder (Luke Button) taking his second swing at insurance in two YC batches and whose lowest is the absence of any path to radical affordability when your revenue model is a 10?15% commission skim on existing premium. That pairing is the whole story. Hedge is competing for share of the $90.3B US surplus-lines premium pool, not creating a category that changes how anyone lives.

Consequence magnitude is where Hedge fails my entry-point test most loudly. The HIGH rung on this ladder is OpenAI ? civilizational consequence justified a $50M check and an apology letter to LPs. The MID rung is DoorDash ? Tony Xu's logistics play transformed food access for hundreds of millions, the override case where I accepted execution risk because the consequence was planetary. The LOW rung is the YC pattern I publicly pass on every batch: "AI copilot for accountants, AI for recruiters, AI for real estate agents ? 40% efficiency in a single profession." Hedge sits one notch above that LOW rung, not below it, only because surplus-lines premium is a $90.3B pool rather than a $5B vertical SaaS niche. But the consequence of Hedge fully working is "retail agents place contractor property faster on Thursday afternoons." Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The customer testimonial on hedgespecialty.com about a quote returning in a morning instead of a week is exactly the framing that makes me lose interest.

Path to radical affordability is the other end of the diagnosis. The HIGH rung is Impossible Foods ? heme protein synthesized to drop the cost of protein below animal agriculture for billions of people. The MID rung is Rocket Lab ? small-sat launch made cheap enough that earth-observation companies that couldn't afford a Falcon 9 ride suddenly had access. The LOW rung is the enterprise-SaaS pattern that captures premium positioning without changing cost structure. Hedge sits at the LOW rung. Commission-based wholesale brokerage by definition does not make insurance cheaper to insureds ? it captures the agent-to-carrier intermediation fee. The 30+ direct carrier markets and CA license #6018729 give Hedge a place in the value chain; they do not collapse the cost of risk. Insurance becomes radically affordable when underwriting cost drops and loss ratios improve, neither of which is Hedge's core claim.

Non-linear technical approach: HIGH rung is Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers. MID is Impossible Foods' heme synthesis. LOW is the LLM wrapper around an existing workflow. Hedge sits between LOW and MID. The dossier labels Hedge "AI native" but the website "does not detail specific AI features publicly." Sixfold (founded 2023) and Cytora already do submission ingestion and risk-data extraction for insurers; the technical step Hedge is taking is not novel, it is contemporaneous. The non-linearity Hedge could claim ? a proprietary dataset of declined risks usable to train pricing models ? is flagged in the dossier as "unproven at this stage."

Founder learning machine is where Hedge actually scores best, and it is genuinely ambiguous. HIGH rung: the 19-year-old MIT dropout at Mach Industries building weapons systems against Lockheed. MID: Tony Xu's documented learning velocity at DoorDash. LOW: a domain expert reciting a rehearsed pitch. Luke Button is running his second insurance brokerage in two YC batches ? Fernstone (F25, direct AI brokerage to 100+ small businesses) and now Hedge (S26, wholesale placement to retail agents). If Fernstone was wound down because Button learned that direct-to-insured AI brokerage was the wrong wedge and the wholesale-placement layer is, that is exactly the rate-of-change I look for over three to six months. If Fernstone is still operating and Hedge is a parallel venture, that is a cap-table and time-allocation problem the dossier explicitly flags. Improbable ambition is similarly muted: the HIGH rung is Hermeus' Mach 5 aircraft; Hedge's stated goal of "30-minute median first response on hard risks" is operational, not audacious. No expert in the E&S market would say "that can't be done" ? CRC and Amwins could do it tomorrow with capital.

My 36-month forecast: Hedge reaches $5?20M in annualized commission revenue, gets acquired by CRC, Amwins, or Newfront in a $30?80M tuck-in for the carrier appointments and the AI ingestion stack ? a perfectly fine outcome for the founders and a 3?5x for whoever leads the seed, which in my framework is exactly the return profile I avoid. The mechanism is straightforward: workflow speed is not a structural moat against incumbents who already hold the carrier capacity; the AI layer gets replicated; Hedge converts to an attractive acqui-hire because Button and Rosa have proven they can ship in a regulated vertical.

The strongest case against this verdict is the Oscar Health exception. Oscar violated my "technology risk over market risk" preference and I funded it anyway because the affordability thesis in a calcified regulated industry was strong enough. Could Hedge be the same shape? The bull mechanism: if Hedge is operating with delegated underwriting authority as an MGA rather than as a pure broker, and if the submission flow becomes a proprietary loss-data set on hard-to-place risks, Hedge could become a synthetic carrier underwriting better than legacy E&S markets ? a real data flywheel against AM Best-rated incumbents. That is a different company than the one in this dossier. The dossier is explicit: "No public data on whether Hedge has delegated underwriting authority (MGA) or is purely brokering ? these are very different risk profiles." Until that question is answered with "MGA, with binding authority, with two years of loss data," Hedge is a commissioned middleman in a softening market (AM Best revised E&S outlook from positive to stable in November 2025). The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: confirmed delegated underwriting authority across at least three carriers plus loss-ratio outperformance vs. the E&S benchmark on a defined book. Without that, Hedge is share-of-existing-spend, and share-of-existing-spend is the red flag I most consistently honor.
