# Tensol -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Tensol is the pivot. They entered YC W26 as Solstis — "AI Agents for ERPs for Manufacturers" — and pivoted mid-batch to become a managed hosting layer for OpenClaw, the open-source agent framework. From my framework, this is a move in exactly the wrong direction. Solstis was attacking a boring, vertical, potentially defensible niche — the kind of market I've repeatedly backed (Samsara in industrial IoT, Gusto in payroll). ERP for manufacturers is messy, domain-specific, and unappealing to most VCs. Tensol, by contrast, repositioned into the single hottest consensus category in venture: horizontal AI agents. They went from a market that might have hidden structural depth to one where every investor on Sand Hill Road can recite the TAM slide from memory.

The structural moment question — "what changed in the last 12-24 months?" — has a clear answer: OpenClaw exploded from zero to 265,000 GitHub stars in roughly four months. That's a real phenomenon. But the critical distinction is between a structural moment that creates opportunity for a specific company versus one that creates opportunity for everyone. OpenClaw is MIT-licensed open-source. Any competent team can build a managed hosting layer — and multiple already have: Kimi Claw from Moonshot AI launched February 2026, TrustClaw is targeting enterprise, Perplexity has its Computer product. AWS, GCP, and Azure could add managed OpenClaw hosting as a service line with trivial effort. Tensol has no privileged relationship with the OpenClaw project — the creator joined OpenAI and transferred governance to a foundation. Compare this to Databricks, which was founded by the Apache Spark creators themselves, or Vercel, founded by the creator of Next.js. Those companies had structural positioning within their open-source ecosystems. Tensol is one of many third-party wrappers.

The market structure here triggers my "software-aware vs. software-driven" filter. Tensol's core offering is infrastructure management — spinning up VMs, injecting credentials, handling OAuth connections, providing audit trails. These are legitimate enterprise needs, but they're not proprietary technology. The 13,000+ skills in ClawHub and the persistent memory across deployments are interesting primitives that could theoretically create switching costs. But at weeks old, with a recent pivot, there's no evidence of accumulated organizational knowledge creating real lock-in for any customer. The defensive moat is aspirational, not structural.

I want to steelman the bull case, because there's a version of this that works. If OpenClaw becomes the dominant agent framework the way React became the dominant frontend framework, the "managed OpenClaw for enterprise" position is genuinely valuable. Vercel built a multi-billion-dollar business as the managed layer for Next.js/React. The 265K GitHub stars represent extraordinary developer mindshare, and enterprise teams that adopt OpenClaw will want someone to handle security, compliance, and deployment. If Tensol moves fastest on SOC 2, SSO, RBAC, and audit trails — and if they build the deepest integration library — they could establish enough switching costs before cloud providers enter. The "AI employee" framing is also strategically interesting as a distribution concept: if each agent becomes a persistent entity that accumulates company context, the per-agent pricing model scales naturally and the switching costs compound over time. For this to work, three things must be true simultaneously: OpenClaw must remain the dominant framework, Tensol must become the definitive managed provider ahead of well-resourced competitors, and AI agents must actually deliver measurable business value at scale. That's three miracles, not one — and the NBER study showing 90% of firms report zero productivity impact from agents suggests the third miracle is far from guaranteed.

The founders bring some positive signals. Oliviero's time at Stacksync (YC W24) shipping a workflow product used by enterprise customers is relevant experience, and the mid-batch pivot shows adaptability rather than stubbornness. Pratik's CMU AI engineering background and the McGinnis competition win demonstrate technical capability. But neither founder has the deep infrastructure or open-source ecosystem experience that this particular bet demands — building the enterprise layer for a fast-moving open-source project requires a specific operational playbook around community relations, contributor management, and platform strategy that I don't see evidence of here.

The technology cycle timing is ambiguous. Directionally, AI agents are where the adoption curve is heading — I believe that. But there's a meaningful difference between "agents will matter" and "agents work reliably enough today to justify managed infrastructure." The 90% zero-impact finding from NBER is the kind of data point that separates genuine inflection from premature deployment. Harvey worked because the capability threshold (GPT-4 for legal) was being delivered by external labs on a visible timeline. Here, the capability threshold — agents reliably executing multi-step business workflows autonomously — has no clear timeline for crossing. You can see the direction but not the arrival date, which makes infrastructure investment premature. This is closer to the VR/AR timing problem of 2015 than the legal AI timing of 2022.
