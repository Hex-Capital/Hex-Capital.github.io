# Asendia AI -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The pivot tells the whole story. This team built a B2C interview coaching product, got 286 upvotes on Product Hunt, and then pivoted to selling AI agents to staffing agencies. That sequence is diagnostic. Founders with deep specific knowledge don't pivot into their domain — they start there because they can't think about anything else. Aravind Srinivas didn't pivot into AI search from a consumer app. He was building search systems at Google Brain because that's where his obsessive curiosity led him. These founders found staffing agencies through market analysis, not through years of living inside the problem. If you told me both founders disappeared tomorrow, any competent team could rebuild this product in six months with the same API calls and the same ATS connectors. That's the specific knowledge test, and Asendia fails it cleanly.

The leverage architecture looks like software but behaves like services. Four distinct AI agents — sourcing, SMS outreach, video interviewing, phone screening — plus fifty ATS integrations, maintained by a three-person team that's already hiring. Each staffing agency needs onboarding, workflow customization, compliance verification. That's labor leverage dressed up as code leverage. And beneath the surface, every one of those agents is orchestrating calls to foundation model APIs. They're renting someone else's permissionless leverage. The moment OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google ships recruitment-specific capabilities — or the moment Bullhorn decides to build this into their ATS — the wrapper evaporates. Innovation without a moat is consumer surplus. The value flows to the foundation model providers and eventually to the agencies themselves.

The co-founders have a genuine pre-existing relationship — they've been building together since 2019 with The Coffee Show Podcast, and Badis built FAANG AI before the pivot to B2B. That's a real signal. I don't doubt their commitment to each other. But commitment to your co-founder is different from commitment to the problem. Their backgrounds are cloud infrastructure and semiconductor engineering. Rihab spoke at HR Technologies France — a conference appearance, not a decade of recruiting obsession. Neither founder has operated a staffing agency, managed a recruiting desk, or lived inside the specific pain of filling requisitions against tight timelines. They identified a market gap. That's what agents do. Principals don't identify gaps — they fill needs they've personally felt so acutely that building the solution was inevitable.

The strongest bull case: Paradox's acquisition by Workday genuinely created a structural opening. The largest independent conversational AI recruiting platform was absorbed into an enterprise behemoth, and Workday's agent problem — protecting existing per-seat ATS revenue — will slow their integration of Paradox's capabilities. A lean, fast-moving startup could capture the agencies that don't want to be locked into Workday's ecosystem. The 50+ ATS integrations are real work and create real switching costs once embedded. And the compliance certifications, if verified, represent a barrier that takes competitors months to replicate. If these founders develop genuine specific knowledge through deep immersion in staffing agency workflows over the next year — if they become the people who understand agency operations better than anyone in tech — this could compound into something defensible. But that's a hope, not a signal. I invest in what exists, not what might develop.

The compound interest test is where this falls apart most clearly. What compounds here? The data from interviews could theoretically train better screening models, but the dossier offers no evidence of proprietary model development. The ATS integrations are valuable but static — they don't improve with usage. The candidate database access is shared across competitors who tap the same LinkedIn and GitHub pools. Compare this to Notion, where every template shared by a user made the product more valuable to every other user, or to AngelList, where every deal funded attracted more investors and more startups. Asendia's advantages don't accumulate — they maintain. That's the difference between a tool and a platform.
