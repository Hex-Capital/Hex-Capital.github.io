# SpotPay -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about SpotPay isn't the founder story -- it's the competitive landscape, and it gives me pause before anything else. When I backed Uber, nobody wanted to fund ride-sharing. When I wrote my first check into SpaceX, people thought Elon was out of his mind. The signal I've learned to trust is the absence of other investors in the room. With SpotPay, I look around and see KAST with $10 million, Dakota with $12.5 million, Slash with $60 million, Chipper Cash with $305 million -- and Wise sitting at nearly nine billion in market cap moving toward stablecoin capabilities. The stablecoin neobank thesis is no longer something that makes people uncomfortable. It's becoming consensus. And consensus-based investing is fundamentally different from conviction-based investing. When this many well-funded players are converging on the same insight, the question stops being "is the idea right?" and becomes "can a two-person team with $450K outrun companies with fifty to a hundred times their capital?" That's not the kind of asymmetry I look for.

Zsika grew up between the Caribbean and Latin America. He felt the pain of fragmented cross-border finance in his own life. That biographical thread connecting the founder to the problem is real, and it's the kind of thing I always ask about first -- not the business, the story. And I appreciate that he's not building this from a spreadsheet; he's building it from memory. But when I pull on that thread a little more, the picture gets more complicated. His prior startup, Astros AI, was a monetization platform for mobile game studios -- a completely different domain. He went through Techstars with that company and then pivoted to cross-border payments. That's not disqualifying -- founders evolve -- but it makes me ask whether this is the problem he'd walk through walls for, or whether the stablecoin moment opened a window and his biographical connection made it feel like the right one to climb through. The distinction matters when you're competing against teams that have been building in payments infrastructure for years. His Stanford MBA and Google tenure are impressive credentials, but they tell me "well-prepared insider" more than they tell me "outsider who couldn't not build this thing."

Where SpotPay genuinely lights up for me is economic access. Remittance fees averaging six to eight percent globally -- and over eight percent in Sub-Saharan Africa -- are functionally a tax on some of the world's most vulnerable people. Immigrants sending money home to their families are losing meaningful income to opaque fee structures. This is the same pattern I recognized in Affirm, which made interest rates transparent when credit cards hid them, and in Uber, which I always saw as an income-creation tool for drivers more than a logistics play. If SpotPay can genuinely deliver stablecoin-powered transfers at a fraction of Western Union's fees, packaged in a simple neobank experience, the real-world impact on diaspora communities is substantial. That's the kind of positive-sum outcome I want my capital pointed toward. The Calypso Card, the yield on balances, the local payments -- it's the right product shape for people whose financial lives are split across borders.

Thomas César-Herriau as engineer number four at Brex is the strongest individual signal in this dossier. Building foundational payment infrastructure at a company that grew from a dozen people to a $12 billion valuation means he's actually done the hard technical work of compliance systems, transactional reliability, and card issuance at scale. That's not theoretical knowledge -- it's embodied expertise. And the team has shipped with intensity: app live in November, five updates in three months, Spanish language support by January, ATM withdrawals by February. That cadence tells me the engineering is real and the product instinct is present.

The bull case I have to honestly reckon with: the Caribbean and Latin American corridor is specifically underserved by the well-funded competitors. KAST is building globally, Chipper Cash is focused on Africa, Slash and Dakota have both gone B2B. If SpotPay can own a specific corridor with deep cultural authenticity -- Zsika's personal network, the Spanish language support, the "Calypso" branding that signals Caribbean identity -- there's a version of this where corridor-specific execution and community trust become a real moat. Stablecoin infrastructure costs have dropped dramatically, Visa is enabling stablecoin settlement, and the regulatory pathway through FinCEN MSB registration is clearer than it was even eighteen months ago. If everything breaks right, this could be the Chipper Cash of the Americas. But "if everything breaks right" is a lot of ifs for a two-person team competing against companies with orders of magnitude more resources in a space that's attracting capital like a magnet.

Ultimately, what I'm missing is the quality I can only describe as that jolt -- the feeling that this company exists in a category that doesn't yet have a name, that the founders arrived at an insight nobody else can see. SpotPay is a well-constructed product by capable founders in a real market with genuine impact potential. But it's not the kind of bet where I'm the first check into something others think is crazy. It's a bet that the best execution will win in an increasingly crowded race. And while I respect that race, it's not where my particular edge as an investor lives.
