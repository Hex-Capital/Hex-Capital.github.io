# Sponge -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Sponge is the founder composition: three engineers who built Stripe's crypto payment systems, stablecoin financial accounts, and money movement infrastructure are now building payments infrastructure for AI agents. That's the kind of direct domain transfer I weight heavily -- these founders didn't read about payments infrastructure, they built it at the company that defines modern payments. But the analytically interesting tension is that their former employer just launched x402 integration for USDC agent payments on Base in February 2026. Stripe is simultaneously Sponge's most natural infrastructure partner and its most dangerous potential competitor. When your ex-employer controls the merchant network of millions of businesses and starts building in your exact category, the question isn't whether the founders are capable -- it's whether the structural dynamics favor an independent startup or an extension of Stripe's existing distribution.

The market structural moment here is real but not non-obvious. When Mastercard unveils Agent Pay, Visa announces Intelligent Commerce, PayPal and Coinbase launch agent payment toolkits, and Stripe ships x402 support -- all within a 12-month window -- that's not a hidden inflection point. That's a consensus category. My best investments have been in markets that other investors dismissed: PagerDuty was "boring alerting," Gusto was "just payroll," Coinbase was "speculative crypto." Agent payments in March 2026 is none of those things. It's the hot category that every payment incumbent and at least four funded startups (Skyfire at $9.5M, Natural at $9.8M, Payman at $13.8M, Nevermined at $7.02M) are all pursuing simultaneously. The TAM slide showing $52 billion by 2030 is exactly the kind of static market sizing I'm skeptical of -- it tells you the category is big, but doesn't tell you why THIS company captures value versus the incumbents who already own merchant relationships.

The product-to-distribution architecture is where Sponge looks most compelling. The Wallet + Gateway + Catalog structure mirrors what made Stripe generational: a developer integration (Wallet SDK) that becomes the distribution channel for additional products (Gateway for businesses, Catalog as marketplace). If agents adopt Sponge Wallet as their default financial identity, that's a distribution channel for every service listed on the Catalog. If businesses integrate the Gateway, that's supply-side liquidity that makes the Wallet more valuable. This two-sided marketplace model is structurally different from competitors like Skyfire (enterprise payment network) or Payman (fiat rails for regulated institutions). The question is whether Sponge can achieve marketplace liquidity before better-capitalized competitors or before Stripe decides to build the full stack itself.

My concern is the multiple-miracle problem. Sponge needs: (1) AI agents to reach sufficient autonomy that enterprises trust them with real spending authority -- a behavioral threshold, not a technical one; (2) marketplace liquidity on both sides before competitors achieve it; (3) Stripe and Coinbase to remain at the primitives layer rather than building the full-stack agent commerce product. That's at least three sequential obstacles, and the first one -- enterprise trust in autonomous agent spending -- isn't in Sponge's control at all. Compare this to Harvey, where the single miracle (model fidelity for legal work) was being delivered by the foundation model labs, freeing Harvey to focus entirely on workflow and distribution. Sponge's critical dependency -- enterprise willingness to let agents transact autonomously -- is a cultural and regulatory shift that could take 18 months or five years.

The bull case is straightforward: the Stripe payments mafia thesis. PayPal alumni built the next generation of fintech companies. Stripe alumni building the next generation of agent financial infrastructure follows the same pattern. The incumbents are shipping payment primitives, not full-stack agent commerce platforms, and Sponge's complete product suite (Wallet + Gateway + Catalog) occupies a different layer. If the agent economy grows at anything close to projected rates, the team that establishes the default two-sided marketplace for agent transactions captures enormous value. The early integrations with AgentMail, Steel, and an Amazon beta pilot show the founders are executing quickly and building real supply-side partnerships. And the MCP integration for Claude is a smart distribution wedge into the fastest-growing agent ecosystem. If I'm wrong about the timing -- if enterprise trust in autonomous agent spending arrives in 12 months rather than 36 -- Sponge's founders and product architecture position them to capture a disproportionate share of value.

But I keep coming back to the market structure. This isn't a "too boring" or "too niche" market where I have a contrarian view. It's a consensus bet with well-funded competition and incumbent attention. The founders are strong, the product architecture is thoughtful, and the execution velocity is solid for pre-seed. But the structural dynamics -- competitive density, incumbent optionality, and a behavioral adoption curve that's outside the founders' control -- put this in the category of investments where I want to watch rather than act. I'd revisit in six months with evidence that enterprise agent spending is actually happening at meaningful scale.
