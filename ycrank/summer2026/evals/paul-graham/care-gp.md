# Care GP -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Care GP is a finance operator running a real schlep -- Melvin Chen, Oxford Engineering triple major, Goldman/Terra Firma/Samena PE, Forbes 30 Under 30, attacking Australian GP back-office admin -- and that pairing is the analytical signature. The schlep dimension is the highest rung Care GP touches; the hacker-genealogy and solo-founder dimensions are the lowest. Read those two together and what you have is a competent capital-allocator who picked a tedious problem and is executing it professionally, not a hacker who lived the pain and couldn't stop building.

On the schlep axis, **Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy: Brian Armstrong went toward the bitcoin compliance grind that every other YC applicant in his batch was unconsciously routing around, and that grind became the moat. Care GP is genuinely in this zone -- ISO27001, onshore encryption, Best Practice PMS integrations, clinical document routing where misallocating a pathology report has patient-safety consequences, Sonya at Yarrawonga collapsing six hours of reception into thirty minutes. The work is unpleasant in the right way. Where Care GP diverges from **Coinbase** (2012) is the moat geometry: Armstrong's compliance work compounded into a regulatory position competitors couldn't replicate cheaply, whereas Care GP's distribution sits inside Best Practice Software, and Best Practice has now shipped its own native admin agent and bundles Lyrebird for free. The schlep is real; the schlep-as-moat is rented.

On hacker genealogy, the pattern that kills companies in my book is the MBA-coded pass example -- glossy deck, credentials-led bio, no GitHub trail, no shipped open-source artifact, founder-as-allocator rather than founder-as-builder. Care GP's dossier triggers most of that pattern: Melvin Chen's bio leads with Oxford Valedictorian, Goldman, Terra Firma, Samena, Forbes 30 Under 30 -- the credential stack you'd assemble if you were optimizing for an LP deck, not a Hacker News thread. No GitHub found, no Twitter found, no Lisp-equivalent prior artifact in the **Stripe** (2010) Collison sense. Patrick Collison had won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work before he was 17; Melvin Chen had been doing PE at Samena Capital. Both are signals of intelligence, but they predict different companies.

The organic-idea test is where I run the counterfactual: what would have to be true for Care GP to score in the **Dropbox** (2007) Houston-USB-stick range on this axis? Melvin Chen would need to have been a GP, a clinic operator, or at minimum the spouse/sibling of one -- someone who personally watched Samantha-the-receptionist drown in faxes and built Samantha-the-agent to free her. The dossier names no such pre-history. The Startmate writeup frames it as "sabbatical to super agents," which is the language of an investor who picked a problem, not an operator who lived inside one. That's "spotted a market opportunity," not "I built this because I needed it."

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism analysis cuts both ways over the next 18 months. Care GP's 150+ clinics, "zero churn," self-described profitability, and 140% average MoM usage growth are not nothing -- they are, in fact, the strongest pre-seed traction numbers I've seen in this batch, and profitability at YC application is rare enough that it deserves weight. But the resourcefulness I underwrite is the cereal-box kind: a stunt nobody else would do, run by founders too broke or too stubborn to do it the normal way. Care GP's traction reads as professional B2B SaaS execution by a PE operator who knows how to close clinics -- replicable by Heidi Health's ~$138M war chest the moment they decide to extend from scribing into admin.

The solo-founder dimension is the single-anchor parallel where I don't bend: **Loopt** (2005) was the exception I made for Sam Altman, and the exception was force-of-personality so overwhelming that Jessica and I both flagged it independently. Care GP's YC page lists Melvin Chen alone running a 10-person team toward a stated 7,000-clinic national footprint, and the dossier surfaces no Altman-grade animal signal that would override "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010). Solo plus finance-background plus credential-led bio is the exact stack the essay tells me to flinch from.

My 36-month forecast: Care GP reaches roughly AUD $8-20M ARR inside Australia, gets acquired by Best Practice Software, Telstra Health, or MedicalDirector for AUD $40-100M as a defensive admin-agent tuck-in around 2028, and never breaks out of the AU geographic concentration the dossier already flags. The mechanism is straightforward -- the channel partner becomes the channel competitor, Heidi Health extends downward from scribing on its $138M, and Melvin Chen runs the sale the way a PE operator runs a sale, because that is the muscle he has built over a decade.

The bull case that stings: Brian Chesky was a design student, not a hacker, and **Airbnb** (2009) is in my Known Exceptions explicitly because the founders overrode my idea-skepticism with revenue produced by a stunt. Care GP has revenue, profitability, zero churn, and 140% MoM growth at pre-seed -- numbers Airbnb did not have when I wrote the check. If "the founders shipped the thing and customers stayed" is the test, Care GP passes harder than Airbnb did in 2009. The dossier evidence that wins against this bull case: Airbnb's cereal boxes were a stunt the founders would not have done if they had a Goldman exit option, and Melvin Chen does. The verdict flips if a co-founder with clinical or PMS-engineering depth is named in the YC interview and the dossier's "solo founder per YC page" turns out to be a listing error -- that one fact would lift the team configuration and the hacker-genealogy scores together and move Care GP into Invest range.
