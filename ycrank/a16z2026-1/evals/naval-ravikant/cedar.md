# Cedar -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Greg Gunn and Beier Cai claim $2.5–3M in contracted revenue with four people. If those numbers are even directionally accurate, Cedar is running at $625K+ revenue per employee before raising a real round. Midjourney hit $500M revenue with ~163 people. Cursor reached $500M ARR with fewer than 50. Cedar's ratio — if real — sits in that same structural class where AI leverage collapses the denominator to near-zero. The caveat is obvious: these are self-reported figures from a stealth company with no visible customers, no case studies, no independent validation. "Contracted revenue" can mean signed agreements that never convert to cash. But the signal is worth investigating because the founders' specific knowledge makes the number plausible in a way it wouldn't be for two MBAs who discovered recruiting was a big TAM.

The specific knowledge here is unusually dense. Gunn was founding business team at Hootsuite, scaling it from 5 to 1,500 employees and $0 to $200M+ ARR. Cai wrote Hootsuite's first line of code and rose to Senior Director of Engineering. They didn't read about how referral hiring breaks at scale — they lived inside that failure mode for years as their own company outgrew its ability to hire through trusted networks. Then they spent five years building Commit.dev, a professional network specifically for startup engineers, which gave them direct operational knowledge of how relationship graphs work as a product. Cedar is the third iteration of the same obsession: mapping and activating professional relationships at scale. The dossier says Stripe invested 100 people and $10M/year to maintain referral-based hiring. Gunn and Cai watched Hootsuite face that exact problem and are now building the tool that would have solved it. This is specific knowledge productized — the company IS their accumulated expertise from a decade in the intersection of scaling teams and professional networks.

The leverage audit reveals a structure I find compelling. Cedar's AI agents conduct 15-minute calls with employees, extracting private relationship data into a proprietary graph. Every call adds nodes. Every new customer makes the graph denser for all customers. This is a compounding data asset with genuine network effects — the kind of flywheel where time itself becomes a moat. The underlying technology (LLM-driven conversational extraction) is replicable — any competitor could build the call format. But the accumulated graph is not. This mirrors the AngelList dynamic I know firsthand: the tooling layer was never the defensibility; the network of founders, investors, and deals that accumulated over years was the asset no competitor could replicate by copying the software. Cedar's graph of private professional relationships — data that doesn't exist on LinkedIn, isn't scraped from public profiles, and can only be built one employee conversation at a time — is structurally the same kind of proprietary network asset.

The principal-agent problem at LinkedIn is Cedar's structural opening. LinkedIn owns the dominant professional graph but monetizes it through recruiter seats, job postings, and advertising. Building an agent-driven layer that extracts employees' private networks and routes referrals outside LinkedIn's platform would cannibalize LinkedIn Recruiter's revenue model. This is the same dynamic that let Perplexity challenge Google — the incumbent's search team has the knowledge to build AI-native search, but the ad revenue model makes them agents of their own P&L, unable to self-cannibalize. Gunn and Cai, as principals with no legacy revenue to protect, can build what LinkedIn's product team cannot politically ship. The advisor bench — Laszlo Bock (Google SVP People Ops), Tiffany Fenster (Stripe Head of Recruiting), Brendan Browne (LinkedIn/Replit VP Talent Acquisition) — suggests Cedar has direct insight into how the largest employers think about this problem, which compounds the founders' existing knowledge rather than substituting for it.

The co-founder relationship is the cleanest principal signal in this dossier. Gunn and Cai met as early employees at Hootsuite and have worked together for over a decade across three companies. This is the structural opposite of my red flag — co-founders who met at a startup weekend. A decade of shared context means they've already survived the conflicts that destroy most founding teams. The progression from Hootsuite (experienced the problem) to Commit.dev (built in the talent network space) to Cedar (productized the referral insight with AI) reads as a founder pair converging on their life's work, not chasing a market trend. The Commit.dev question is the one I'd probe in person: what happened to it, why did it not become this, and do prior investors create obligations that constrain Cedar? The $6M seed from iNovia and Georgian Partners doesn't just disappear.

The risk that would flip me is data: if the $2.5–3M revenue figure dissolves under diligence into soft LOIs or pilot agreements with no actual cash collected, Cedar's team-to-value leverage score collapses and the entire thesis weakens. The privacy and compliance risk — enterprise legal teams resisting AI extraction of employees' private networks, particularly under GDPR — could cap Cedar's addressable market to US-based, less regulated employers. And the core technology layer is not proprietary; the moat depends entirely on accumulating the graph faster than competitors. If Perfect ($23M seed) or a well-funded incumbent decides to add referral mapping, Cedar's time advantage could narrow before the graph reaches defensible density.

The two dimensions that lock my conviction: specific knowledge productization — a decade-long founder journey from Hootsuite's scaling pain through Commit.dev's network-building to Cedar's AI-driven graph extraction represents non-replicable expertise that IS the company's engine — and team-to-value leverage, where four people generating seven-figure revenue (if verified) demonstrates the kind of AI-era efficiency that validates my shrinking company thesis. The principal authenticity of a co-founder pair with 10+ years of shared history across three companies in the same domain removes the founder-risk that kills most pre-seed bets.
