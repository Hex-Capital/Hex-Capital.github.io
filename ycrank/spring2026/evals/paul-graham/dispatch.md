# Dispatch -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

They built a full-scale reentry heat shield in an apartment and then blasted it with rocket exhaust in the Mojave Desert. That's either the most interesting thing I've read today or the most misleading. But the *act* of building one in an apartment — that's the kind of thing I notice in YC interviews. When the Airbnb founders told us they'd funded themselves selling $40 cereal boxes, my reaction was: "You guys are like cockroaches. You just won't die." Building a heat shield in your apartment, claiming 100x cost reduction over incumbents, and dragging it to the desert to test it — that's a cockroach move. It doesn't prove the heat shield survives actual Mach 20+ atmospheric reentry (there's a substantial gap between simulated and real conditions, as Inversion Space discovered when their first orbital attempt failed due to a propulsion short circuit). But it does tell me something about these founders. Most aerospace engineers would insist they need a proper test facility and a few million dollars before touching hardware. These two just built the thing.

The founders' domain expertise is the strongest signal here, and it's exactly the right kind. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." Case was in the thermal group at Aerospace Corporation working on the Lunar Gateway program — and now he's building thermal protection systems for reentry vehicles. Mello led avionics at Astranis and built hardware-software systems at Zoox before it sold to Amazon. Between them they cover the two disciplines a reentry vehicle absolutely requires: thermal protection and avionics/GNC. More importantly, they worked together at Astranis, which matters enormously. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." Having built spacecraft together at a hard-tech startup is a real test of a working relationship. They're not two friends who had a brainstorm over drinks; they've shipped hardware together under pressure.

This idea also hits my schlep blindness framework in an interesting way. "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Reentry vehicles are among the most painful schleps in aerospace. It's intimidating in the same way Stripe was — everybody knew online payments were broken, but the schlep of dealing with banks and compliance and fraud kept people away for a decade. Lots of engineers can see that in-space manufacturing needs a return-to-Earth logistics layer, but most unconsciously filter it out because the path is terrifying. And the "picks and shovels" positioning — being the reentry logistics provider rather than vertically integrating like Varda — is the right wedge. "The way to do really big things seems to be to start with deceptively small things." If you own the downmass layer, you become essential infrastructure for everyone else's manufacturing ambitions.

But here's where my enthusiasm stalls. I've said many times that "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." That's true for most software companies. But this isn't most markets. Varda Space Industries has $329 million in funding and five successful reentry missions. They're not a hypothetical competitor — they're operational, they have cadence, and they've proven the technical feasibility that Dispatch is still working toward. Beyond Varda, there are at least seven other funded startups building reentry vehicles, including Reditus Space, a near-identical peer from the previous YC batch with the same "reentry-as-a-service" model. The market is becoming crowded before the market itself has fully materialized. And the capital intensity is severe: competitors have spent $40-125 million before their first flight attempt. Dispatch has ~$500K. "Founders often assume it will be easy to raise more money. But that assumption is often false, and worse still, the more you depend on it, the falser it becomes." Getting from a ground test to orbital demonstration requires bridging a $50-100M capital gap in a field where investors can already back companies with flight heritage.

The bull case is genuinely compelling if you squint at it the right way. If in-space manufacturing expands beyond Varda's pharma niche into semiconductors and advanced materials — and the TAM projections range from $5B to $23B by the early 2030s, suggesting genuine uncertainty about scale — then Varda's vertical integration becomes a limitation, not an advantage. They own the manufacturing payload, which means every new material category requires a new R&D investment from them. A pure logistics provider that can return *anyone's* payload is positioned more like AWS was to e-commerce: you don't need to be the manufacturer to capture the infrastructure layer. If the 100x heat shield cost claim holds at production scale, and if refurbishability works as advertised, Dispatch could offer unit economics that make them the default choice for the long tail of space manufacturers who don't want to build their own reentry capability. That's a real path to a very large outcome. The problem is that it requires multiple things to be simultaneously true — the market must expand dramatically, the cost claims must hold, and Dispatch must raise enough capital to reach orbit before better-funded competitors lock up the market.

I keep coming back to the question I ask myself about every YC company: is this a team that will find a way through no matter what? The apartment heat shield says yes. The domain expertise says yes. But the capital gap between here and orbit says this will be the hardest fundraising path imaginable, in a segment where investors can already write checks to companies with flight heritage. These founders aren't playing house — they've built real hardware and tested it. But the distance between "tested in the desert" and "returned a payload from orbit" is measured not just in engineering difficulty but in hundreds of millions of dollars. I'd want to see them get a government contract or a strategic partnership that validates the cost claims before I'd have real conviction.
