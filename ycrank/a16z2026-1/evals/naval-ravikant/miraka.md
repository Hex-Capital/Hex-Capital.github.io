# Miraka -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Miraka's core tension is visible the moment you apply the leverage audit. The company bills itself as an AI-powered cardiac care team, but the revenue mechanism is insurance-reimbursed clinical appointments — billable human hours. Every dollar of that $300K annual run rate required a credentialed clinician spending time with a patient. The AI improves which patients get flagged and when, but it doesn't replace the clinician in the loop. This is labor leverage with a technology layer on top, not permissionless leverage. Cadence, at a $1B valuation, chose the structurally superior architecture: license software to health systems, let them provide the clinicians. Miraka chose the opposite — deliver the care directly. That choice may produce better clinical outcomes (the claimed >90% readmission reduction versus Cadence's SaaS-only model), but it means every new hospital partnership requires hiring, credentialing, and managing additional clinical staff. "Managing other people is incredibly messy. It requires tremendous leadership skills." Miraka's growth plan is a headcount plan.

The specific knowledge story is more compelling than the leverage story. Nolan Abeyta and Kazuo Nakamura started building cardiac remote monitoring at Princeton in 2021 through Overwatch Health — not as a thesis project but through the eLab incubator, which requires genuine company formation. Jesse Abeyta brought Amazon AI experience and military biometrics work to the CTO role. The three of them have been iterating on the same problem — post-discharge cardiac care — for four to five years across at least three company names (Overwatch Health, Paralign, Miraka). That iteration history matters. They didn't read a McKinsey report about RPM market growth and decide to found a startup. They've been living inside hospital sales cycles, RPM billing codes, and clinical workflow integration since Nolan was twenty years old. Selling into NewYork-Presbyterian as "the youngest team to sell into a top-5 heart hospital" is specific knowledge made visible — you don't close that deal without understanding how cardiac departments evaluate vendors, how compliance reviews work, how to navigate institutional procurement. But the knowledge, while real, is operational rather than scientific. None of the founders are cardiologists or clinical researchers. Their edge is in hospital go-to-market and care coordination workflow, not in cardiac medicine itself. That's a narrower moat than Aravind Srinivas's years at Google Brain building the systems that became Perplexity's engine.

The principal authenticity signal is Miraka's strongest dimension from my framework. Four-plus years on the same problem through multiple pivots, a family member joining as CTO, selling into hospitals in their early twenties — these are not the behaviors of agents executing a fundable thesis. The Poshmark comparison is instructive: Manish Chandra had deep fashion marketplace knowledge but the business required operations teams to scale, and it underperformed my highest-conviction bets. Miraka shows even stronger principal commitment than Poshmark's founding story — but the same structural problem. Principal-level founders building a labor-leverage business still produces a labor-leverage business. The founder's passion doesn't change the company's cost function.

Miraka's access democratization angle is the one dimension where my framework gets genuinely excited. The industry captures only 10% of eligible patients for post-discharge cardiac care — Miraka claims 94%. If that number holds, they're taking a life-saving service from one in ten cardiac patients to nine in ten. That's not incremental improvement; that's structural access expansion. Cardiac patients discharged from hospitals and lost to follow-up die preventable deaths. Removing that access barrier — and doing it through insurance reimbursement so patients pay nothing — maps directly onto the pattern behind AngelList, CoinList, and Polymarket: give millions of people access to something previously restricted to thousands. The gatekeeper here is the hospital's outpatient cardiac rehab program, which lacks the operational capacity to capture more than a fraction of eligible patients.

The bull case I have to take seriously: $300K ARR with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments is real pre-seed traction in healthcare, where most companies spend two years in pilot purgatory. The CMS 2026 fee schedule lowered RPM billing thresholds, creating a structural tailwind. And the integrated care delivery model — providing clinicians, not just software — creates switching costs that Cadence's SaaS approach cannot match. Once Miraka's team is embedded in a hospital's discharge workflow and producing measurable readmission reductions, ripping them out means the hospital's HRRP penalties spike. That's a defensibility mechanism pure technology vendors lack. If the >90% readmission reduction survives third-party validation at NewYork-Presbyterian, Miraka could become the standard of care for cardiac post-discharge management despite the scaling constraints.

But I keep returning to the leverage architecture. Revenue scales with clinician headcount. CMS reimbursement rates are set by regulators — "regulators can't do any cost-benefit tradeoff, they always only restrict." Miraka's unit economics are determined by people it doesn't control: CMS sets the price, insurance companies approve the claims, clinicians must be hired and retained during a healthcare labor shortage. Compare this to Notion, where a small team serves millions with zero marginal cost per user. Miraka's AI component is a feature that makes clinicians more effective, not a product that replaces them. The compound interest test partially passes — the data flywheel on patient outcomes should improve predictions over time — but the delivery mechanism doesn't compound. Each new patient still requires human clinical time. This is the structural ceiling that prevents me from reaching Invest conviction despite genuinely strong founder signals.

The two dimensions that lock Miraka into the Neutral tier are Permissionless Leverage Architecture and Team-to-Value Leverage. The 71% month-over-month appointment growth and the NewYork-Presbyterian partnership demonstrate execution ability, and the principal authenticity is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed. But the business is architecturally designed to scale through hiring — the opposite of what produces asymmetric angel returns. I'd want to see evidence that AI can eventually replace clinical staff in the monitoring loop, not merely assist them, before writing a check.
