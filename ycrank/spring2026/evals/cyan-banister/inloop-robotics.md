# InLoop Robotics -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about InLoop Robotics is what I don't see: the story. When I look at these three founders, I find technically talented students from TUM Munich who co-founded a robotics club, won a manipulation challenge, and had internships at BMW and Bosch. That's a strong CV for a robotics engineering position. But when I ask my question — the one I ask every single founder, the one that surprises people — "tell me your story, not the business" — what comes back is a career trajectory, not a crucible. One founder's LinkedIn headline reads "Aspiring Robotics Engineer." Aspiring. That word tells me everything. These are builders who found a market to build in, not people who were broken by a system and decided to rebuild it. And the third co-founder, Stepan Feduniak, is essentially a ghost — no retrievable education, no work history, no public professional presence. That's not mysterious in the way that intrigues me; it's a diligence gap that concerns me, because I need to know who I'm betting on before I bet.

The technical concept, though — I want to be honest about what's genuinely interesting here. Shipping robots that are explicitly imperfect, that pause and ask a human for help, and then use that help as training data? That's the kind of idea that makes traditional robotics people wince. In a field obsessed with autonomous perfection, deliberately deploying incomplete policies and building a data flywheel from failure is a form of intellectual courage. It reminds me a little of how Crusoe started with Bitcoin mining as a first workload — a weird surface that was secretly building something infrastructural underneath. If InLoop's data flywheel works, every deployed robot becomes a training node, and the collective intelligence of the fleet improves with each human intervention. That's infrastructure being built beneath a "robots that need help" surface. I can see the ten-year version of this: a company whose AI models have absorbed millions of real-world edge cases from thousands of facilities, and the teleoperators have gradually been automated away. That's a real vision.

But here's where my conviction breaks down: this is not a hard space. Warehouse robotics is one of the most heavily funded sectors in the automation world right now. Nimble has raised $221 million at a billion-dollar valuation. Dexterity has raised $291 million at $1.65 billion. Pickle Robot has nearly $100 million. Amazon acqui-hired Covariant's entire team. When I wrote my check into Uber, 300-plus investors on AngelList had passed. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in the Valley. When I championed Contraline, VCs literally squirmed in their seats talking about male birth control. InLoop is entering a room where the chairs are already pulled up to the table and everyone's competing to sit down. The human-in-the-loop angle is differentiated within the space, but it's tactical differentiation in a consensus market — not contrarian conviction in a space others avoid. The concept itself, as the dossier notes, is "conceptually replicable," and the open-source frameworks that Sharfeddine has already forked on GitHub are lowering barriers for everyone.

I also can't find the invisible system here. My strongest investments attack opaque, extractive structures — the taxi medallion cartel, credit card opacity, the property tax labyrinth. Warehouse labor is genuinely difficult and often exploitative, but InLoop isn't making that system visible. They're replacing labor with robots. The $100K+ CapEx barrier for traditional automation is a real gatekeeping mechanism, and the zero-CapEx RaaS model does expand access to smaller warehouses that were previously locked out. That's meaningful. But expanding access to automation is not the same as revealing a hidden structure that extracts value from people who don't understand it. There's no "invisible tax" being exposed here — there's an efficiency improvement being offered.

For the bull case to work, you'd need to believe three things simultaneously: that the data flywheel creates a compounding moat faster than competitors with 100x more capital can build their own, that the teleoperator economics improve at a rate that keeps the flat-fee model viable at scale, and that the RaaS model for small-to-mid-sized warehouses represents a genuinely underserved market that the large players won't pursue. All three are plausible. None are probable enough for me to write a check with my own money. The founders are technically capable — Sharfeddine's BEHAVIOR Challenge win and Rizali's co-founding of Germany's largest student robotics initiative are real signals of building capacity. If they had a founder who'd spent years inside the warehouse system, who understood the pain of fulfillment operations from personal experience rather than market research, who had been told "no" by dozens of investors and was still building because they couldn't stop — that would change my calculus. But the forge I look for isn't here. I wish them well, genuinely. The approach is clever. But clever in a crowded, well-funded space without biographical inevitability is not where I make my bets.
