# Inth -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Christopher Burns shipped c15t into Vercel, Expo, Zed, Infisical, and Unkey before he ever incorporated Inth — 2.6M cumulative npm downloads of a headless consent SDK at the exact production-app layer where OneTrust's script-loader cannot reach. That is the dimensional signature of Inth in my portfolio: a contrarian secret sharp enough to articulate in one sentence ("compliance lives in the repo, not the dashboard"), paired with the structural-moat profile of pure Apache 2.0 software with no regulated customer and no atoms. Stripe-shaped wedge, not Palantir-shaped business.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy on the secret. The shared mechanism is the developer-installed substrate that incumbents cannot rebuild because rebuilding cannibalizes their existing distribution — Patrick Collison's secret was that Braintree and PayPal could not become API-first without destroying their merchant-services channel, and Inth's secret is that OneTrust and Usercentrics cannot become a headless OSS SDK without destroying their tag-manager telemetry and agency channel. Inth diverges from Stripe at the depth of the wedge: payments-API was a 10-year monopoly because the integration cost compounded against a regulated payments rail, whereas the c15t integration is "tree-shakable" by design and the shadcn/c15t fork already exists on GitHub. The secret is real and the named incumbent failure mode is specific (script-loader cannibalization at OneTrust, scan-only blindness at Privado) — but Transcend's $40M Series B in May 2024 is already pricing a thinner version of the same secret, which means Inth holds the contrarian thesis on a 24-month clock, not a 10-year one.

The pure-SaaS YC default is the pattern at the bottom of the atoms-heavy ladder — and Inth sits squarely inside it. The failure mechanism on that rung is that the moat hypothesis terminates at code, and code is the most replicable substance in the portfolio universe; the Founders Fund manifesto called this 140-characters territory for a reason. Inth's customer is the engineering org at a SaaS company, not the U.S. government, not the European Defence Fund, not an FDA-regulated counterparty. GDPR and Quebec Law 25 are regulatory tailwinds for *demand*, not for *moat* — every competitor faces the same regulator. There is no Anduril-grade integration depth or Palantir-grade customer trust accumulating here; there is a 6.5K-downloads-per-day npm package with an Apache 2.0 license.

On monopoly trajectory, the 18-month compounding question is whether the repo-resident data-lineage graph (Inth's "next layer beyond c15t" per the YC page) becomes a customer-specific asset that Transcend's $90M cannot replicate by simply forking c15t and bolting on their existing DSAR engine. The asymmetry runs against Inth: Transcend already sells engineering-led DSAR, the Apache 2.0 license invites them to embed c15t at zero cost, and Inth's per-app switching cost is "tree-shakable" by founder advertisement. Pricing power five years out looks like Osano's $5.9M of 2024 revenue, not OneTrust's $5.1B valuation.

On definite plan, the score would flip a tier only if Inth had published a specific 3-year technical commitment — say, "by 2028 every Inth repo emits a cryptographically signed RoPA artifact verifiable by a DPA without human review" — that the founder commits not to pivot from. What the dossier shows instead is sequenced expansion: rebrand from Consent.io to Inth in October 2025, DSAR SDK in Alpha, managed platform "in development." That is iteration-mode roadmapping with a launch-week cadence, not Musk's reusability whitepaper. Inth is closer to indefinite optimism than definite, redeemed partly because the sequence (consent → scanning → evidence) is at least coherent.

Christopher Burns at Everfund is the single anchor for founder lineage — and the Everfund parallel is closer to **Asana** (2010, Moskovitz from Facebook) than to **Affirm** (2014, Levchin from PayPal). Burns shipped a multi-tenant payments platform to 33+ countries, raised $2M, then wound it down; he is not a Levchin re-up, not a Thielverse operator, and Will De Ath is Head of Growth, not a co-founder CTO. The override that carried Asana — Moskovitz had shipped Facebook to a billion users — does not apply at this magnitude. What does apply is that c15t at Vercel, Expo, and Zed is a real shipped-to-scale OSS signal authored by Burns personally, which is the strongest pedigree datum in the dossier and the reason this is not a pure first-time-founder profile.

In 36 months Inth is either acquired by Transcend, Vercel, or Ketch for $30-80M on roughly $2-6M ARR of hosted c15t and managed DSAR, or it stalls under $1M ARR after Transcend embeds a c15t-equivalent into their existing engineering-led DSAR product and the Apache 2.0 license neutralizes the wedge. The mechanism producing both branches is the same: the secret is real but the moat around the secret is not, and the buyer of the company is one of three named incumbents who needs the developer distribution that Burns built.

The strongest bull case against my pass is that c15t at Vercel/Expo/Zed is the literal 2010 Stripe moment for compliance — a developer substrate that compounds into the default before the no-code incumbents notice, with the Inth managed control plane monetizing the lineage graph the way Vercel monetized Next.js. If that frame is correct, Inth is a fund-returner because privacy-as-code becomes the category and Inth owns the install base. I do not buy it, because Vercel monetized a hosting cost structure that Next.js naturally generated, whereas Inth's monetization is DSAR managed service against Transcend's $90M war chest with an Apache 2.0 license inviting that war chest to embed c15t directly — the shadcn/c15t fork is the canary. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed paid contract with a regulated counterparty (a bank, a national health system, a defense integrator) inside the next 9 months at six-figure ACV, which would convert the secret from a developer-fashion bet into a Palantir-shaped one. Absent that, Inth is a competent acquisition target, not a power-law check.
