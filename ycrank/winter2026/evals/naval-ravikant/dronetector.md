# DroneTector -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Matthew Moore built the actual radar. Not "studied the literature" or "worked adjacent to the problem" -- he designed, constructed, and experimentally validated a 94 GHz millimetre-wave Doppler radar system during his PhD at St Andrews, then published the peer-reviewed work proving it could extract micro-Doppler signatures from drone propeller blades. This is specific knowledge in its purest form. You cannot acquire this from a textbook or a six-month deep dive. The number of humans on Earth who have hands-on experience building W-band radar optimized for nano-drone classification is probably in the low dozens. Doherty complements this with a decade of optical and laser physics from Oxford's Clarendon Lab, plus the experience of co-founding Quantum Fabrix (which Paul Graham funded at seed -- that's a meaningful signal about the person). Frances de Mas brings automated reasoning -- relevant for the signal processing and classification layer. Each founder's knowledge took years of focused work to acquire. The specific knowledge bar is cleared convincingly.

Here's where I get stuck: the leverage architecture is wrong for how I think about compounding returns. This is a hardware company selling to governments. Every radar unit must be manufactured -- specialized components, custom millimetre-wave circuits, quality assurance for field deployment. Every customer requires navigating a 12-to-36-month defense procurement cycle with a three-person team. When I invested in Replit, one engineer's work serves millions of users simultaneously. When I backed Alchemy, a single API layer serves thousands of blockchain developers. DroneTector's output scales with physical units produced and defense contracts signed. That's not leverage -- that's manufacturing. The micro-Doppler signature database could theoretically create a data moat that compounds with each deployment, and that's the most interesting structural element here. But the core business model remains hardware units plus software licensing to governments. I've made exceptions for capital-heavy businesses when the founder's knowledge is extraordinary -- Opendoor being the example -- but even Opendoor had algorithmic pricing creating technological leverage within the capital-intensive structure. DroneTector's leverage story is thinner.

The timing question is nuanced. Counter-drone technology is emphatically not contrarian -- Ukraine demonstrated the threat, Red Sea attacks doubled shipping costs, Gatwick cost stakeholders over fifty million pounds. Five well-funded competitors are already scaling: DroneShield is public with AU$221M cash, Echodyne raised $195M from Bill Gates, Robin Radar is doing $52M in revenue. The market consensus is "counter-drone is critical." What's potentially non-consensus is the specific technical approach -- millimetre-wave micro-Doppler versus lower-frequency radar or RF detection. Most competitors operate at X-band or use software-based RF sensing. DroneTector's argument is that these approaches fundamentally cannot detect autonomous nano drones that emit no RF signal. If that's correct, the incumbents are solving the wrong problem. But "the right frequency band for the next generation of threats" is a technical contrarian bet within a consensus market -- it's not the kind of category-creating, timing-based contrarianism that generated my best returns.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If drone warfare continues to proliferate -- and every geopolitical signal says it will -- and if autonomous nano drones become the primary threat vector rather than RF-emitting commercial drones, then DroneTector's specific technical approach becomes the only one that works. The competitors' $195M and $221M don't help if their physics is wrong for the target class. Moore's knowledge becomes not just valuable but essential. NATO DIANA provides a real institutional pathway that most startups cannot access. The signature database compounds with deployment. Defense certifications create switching costs. And the team has the scientific credibility -- peer review, RAEng fellowships, Oxford and St Andrews pedigree -- to be taken seriously by procurement officers who dismiss most startups. If all this aligns, this is the kind of deep-science hardware bet I've been making more of since 2022 -- Extropic, Atomic Semi, Belfort -- where physics-first approaches create capabilities that software alone cannot replicate. The $3.36 billion detection sub-segment by 2030 at 29% CAGR provides the market oxygen.

Two specific concerns keep me at the margin. First, Doherty's transition from Quantum Fabrix -- he was listed as CEO until October 2025, and a new CEO was appointed then. The timeline to DroneTector's YC W26 batch is tight. I want to understand whether QFX has residual claims on his attention or whether the transition is clean. Second, the well-funded competitor convergence is real. Echodyne could extend into millimetre-wave frequencies -- they have the radar engineering team and the capital. Robin Radar already has 200+ units deployed in Ukraine and understands small-target detection. The question is whether DroneTector's specific W-band micro-Doppler expertise gives them enough lead time to establish deployment data and institutional relationships before the larger players catch up. For a three-person team trying to manufacture custom hardware and simultaneously navigate defense procurement, that's a demanding race.

I appreciate the physics, respect the specific knowledge, and believe the threat environment creates genuine urgency. But the leverage architecture -- hardware manufacturing plus government sales cycles -- constrains the return profile to something more linear than exponential. This is a bet on being right about the physics and surviving the procurement cycle long enough for the market to find you. Those bets can produce good outcomes in defense tech, but they don't produce the asymmetric, thousand-to-one returns I optimize my portfolio for. The specific knowledge is exceptional. The leverage is not.
