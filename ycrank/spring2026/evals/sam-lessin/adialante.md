# Adialante -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what caught my eye first: the incumbent kill dynamics. Siemens, GE, and Philips sell MRI machines for one to three million dollars a pop, and then they lock you into service contracts that print money for decades. Adialante walks up to a urology clinic and says "pay us per scan, we'll bring the machine to you, zero capex." That's not a technology pitch — that's a business model that the incumbents literally cannot copy without torching their own P&L. It's the same structural conflict that made the early Venmo pitch interesting: banks could have built P2P payments, but offering it for free would have cannibalized wire transfer revenue. When the dominant players are structurally unable to respond, that's where real value gets created. The question is whether the physics and the execution are good enough to actually exploit that gap.

On the physics, I'll admit this is more interesting than 90% of what YC puts through the healthcare track. FREE technology — eliminating gradient coils entirely from MRI — is not incremental. It's a fundamentally different architectural bet on how magnetic resonance imaging should work, published in peer-reviewed journals, developed at one of the world's best MRI research centers. A team that includes an ISMRM Rabi Award winner. That's not a weekend hackathon project. And the 1/10th size claim, if it holds, enables a form factor that makes mobile deployment practical in a way that hauling a Siemens MAGNETOM around in a tractor trailer never will. The physics creates the business model possibility. Without the physics, mobile per-scan MRI is just Alliance HealthCare with worse margins.

But here's where my enthusiasm hits a wall: Promaxo. They already have FDA clearance for in-office prostate MRI. They've sold 69 systems. They have $63-81 million in funding. They're building clinical evidence and an installed base right now while Adialante is pre-submission. The mispricing thesis requires that the market be wrong about something, and frankly, the low-cost MRI opportunity is not a secret. Hyperfine is publicly traded. Chipiron just raised $17M. Promaxo is deep into commercialization. This isn't Solana at four cents where everyone thought Ethereum had won and nobody was looking. This is a recognized market with well-capitalized competitors who have multi-year head starts. The mispricing is narrow — it exists in the specific intersection of "novel physics + per-scan mobile model" — but it's not the kind of extreme asymmetry that makes me pound the table.

The strongest bull case — what would need to be true for this to be a 10x outcome — is that the per-scan mobile model turns out to be the Amazon Prime of cancer screening. Every urology clinic in America has a standing order. The truck shows up Tuesday, scans twelve patients, moves on. No capital expenditure decision for the clinic, no infrastructure buildout, no hiring an MRI technician full-time. If guidelines continue pushing MRI-before-biopsy for prostate cancer, you've got a million-plus patients per year who need scans and a healthcare system that doesn't have nearly enough machines to serve them. The zero-friction adoption model could create network effects — more clinics using Adialante means more utilization, means lower per-scan costs, means more clinics signing up. And the physical operations moat is real. AI can't replace a truck with an MRI scanner. This is the Metropolis pattern: software amplifies but cannot substitute for physical-world infrastructure. If they nail FDA clearance and the mobile ops execution, this could be a very durable business.

The problem is that "if." Torres and Jenkins are academic researchers building what amounts to a mobile fleet logistics company. MRI physics is one hard problem. Fleet management, staffing, scheduling, multi-state regulatory compliance, equipment maintenance — that's a completely different hard problem, and I see zero evidence this team has ever operated anything at commercial scale. The co-founder relationship is organic, which I like — shared lab at UMN, not a founder-matching app — but I've watched enough deep-tech academic spinouts to know that the gap between "published the paper" and "built the business" swallows most of them whole. I'd want to see Torres demonstrate the calm commercial judgment that makes me trust a first-time founder with a capital-intensive physical operations buildout. The dossier doesn't give me that signal.

Capital-wise, the NSF SBIR Phase II funding is smart — non-dilutive money is the best kind of money — and the per-scan model has the theoretical virtue of generating cash flow per unit deployed rather than requiring massive upfront fleet investment. But let's be honest: this is a hardware company facing FDA approval, manufacturing scale-up, and fleet deployment. The capital requirements are real and unavoidable, and $2.4M raised against competitors with $60-260M is a deeply asymmetric fight. The question isn't whether they can be capital-disciplined; it's whether capital discipline is even compatible with the pace they need to move before Promaxo locks up the urology clinic market.

I find myself genuinely torn, which is worth noting — that doesn't happen with most YC healthcare companies. The physical moat is real. The incumbent conflict is structural. The physics is novel, not incremental. But the mispricing isn't extreme enough, the competition is too far ahead, and the team hasn't been tested outside the lab. This sits right at the edge of my conviction threshold without crossing it.
