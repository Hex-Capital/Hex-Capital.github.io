# Parasma -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Sean Cole shipped 200,000 human neurons playing Doom in roughly one week using Cortical Labs' CL1 via the Cortical Cloud Python interface, and got Scientific American, Tom's Hardware, Popular Science, ZME Science, Interesting Engineering, and 80.lv to cover it inside two months. That is the signature of Parasma: top-quartile specific knowledge with bottom-decile network mechanics, the inverse of the **Notion** (2013) pattern — Ivan Zhao had specific knowledge AND a compounding builder-tool; Cole has the specific knowledge but his product is a single-sided algorithm layer renting wetware from someone else's data center. Parasma is a founder-as-media-leverage bet on a permission-gated substrate.

**Stack Overflow** (2008) is the load-bearing analogy for the dimension where Parasma scores highest. Atwood and Spolsky were practicing developers writing for developers, and the specific knowledge was non-replicable by a Stanford CS clone. Cole's analog is real — he is the human who actually got cortical neurons to learn Doom, and six tier-1 outlets covered it because the demo is the kind of thing that travels on its own. Where Parasma diverges from the Stack Overflow rung is that Atwood and Spolsky's specific knowledge sat ON TOP OF infrastructure they did not have to ask permission to use; Cole's specific knowledge sits on top of Cortical Labs' CL1 hardware (~$35K/unit) and FinalSpark's Neuroplatform — both of which already ship Python APIs and have direct economic incentive to absorb the algorithm layer Parasma is building. The zmescience.com note that Cole had "little experience in biological computing" before the project is not the worry; one week to a working demo is the specific-knowledge signal. The worry is that the knowledge is wetware-vendor-shaped.

The **Pinterest** pass haunts me on the network dimension, but Parasma is the opposite failure mode — Pinterest had the compounding-user-value architecture and I missed it on taste; Parasma straightforwardly does not have it. The Nth Parasma customer does not make the (N-1) prior customers more valuable. There is no two-sided marketplace, no developer network, no creator graph — just an algorithm shipped to whichever lab or platform buys it. The **Perplexity** (2022) known exception is the right place to test whether I should waive this: Perplexity also failed the network test, and I overrode because criteria #1 (permissionless API distribution), #3 (Aravind's Berkeley retrieval-ML PhD), and pre-consensus AI timing all cleared at top quartile. Parasma clears #3 emphatically but fails #1 on the Cortical-Labs dependency, so the Perplexity override does not transfer.

Leverage architecture is where Parasma's geometry breaks under 18 months of compounding. Code is the product, but distribution runs through the CL1's Python interface that Cortical Labs itself controls — and Cortical Labs raised $11.6M with a Series A in March 2026, has a Melbourne wet lab, and already ships the abstraction layer Parasma is trying to own. The compounding mechanic on a healthy permissionless-code business is that every new user lowers the marginal cost of the next user and tightens the moat; on Parasma it is the opposite — every successful algorithm Cole ships teaches Cortical Labs and FinalSpark what they should build into their own SDK. This is closer to the **Anchorage Digital** known exception in shape, but Anchorage's permission was from a federal regulator that cannot itself enter the market. Parasma's permission is from a vendor that explicitly is the market and ships a competing layer today.

Big-market-with-pivot-room scores low because the founder's edge is biocompute-algorithm-specific. For Parasma's market-room score to flip from low to mid, the dossier would need a credible adjacent pivot vector — neuroscience research tooling, BCI training stacks, organoid-data SaaS — that Cole's MS in AI from Sussex plus the doom-neuron repo can underwrite without re-credentialing. The TAM line reads "No public data found" and the founder's specific knowledge is narrow enough that the niche-without-pivot-room axiom from Venture Hacks ("niche markets don't work because the first idea never works") bites here. Parasma needs the market to be wetware-algorithm-broad and the dossier gives me no anchor that it is.

The founder trio reads cleaner than the architecture. Cole ships — Doom in a week is energy; the University of Sussex Facebook post and the @vytalow X bio "Made some brain cells play Doom" is the exact founder-as-media-leverage pattern that runs through **Airchat** (April 2024) and **Quill Notes** (February 2026). Nothing in the dossier triggers the "if someone talks about how honest they are" filter from Knowledge Project #18. Solo-founder on hardware-adjacent product is the execution worry, not the integrity worry, and Parasma compares favorably to most YC pre-seed teams on the energy leg specifically.

My 36-month Parasma forecast: acqui-hired by Cortical Labs in the $5-15M range as the algorithm/developer-experience team that ships their next Cortical Cloud SDK, OR Cole pivots Parasma into a substrate-agnostic open-source neural-programming framework decoupled from CL1 and raises a proper $3-5M seed on doom-neuron-style organic GitHub adoption. The mechanism producing the acqui-hire outcome is direct from the dossier — Cortical Labs already ships the Cortical Cloud Python interface Cole used, and the rational vendor move is to buy the algorithm team that just generated their best PR cycle in 2026.

The bull case that stings: Cole's media velocity across six tier-1 outlets in two months is anomalous enough that he is becoming the category-defining figure in biocompute developer experience, and that founder-as-media-leverage pattern is exactly an Unstated Pattern I quietly fund. **Airchat** (April 2024) and **Quill Notes** (February 2026) both pattern-match. The bear answer is that Airchat and Quill ship code into permissionless distribution channels — X, the App Store as one of many — while Parasma ships code into Cortical Labs' wholly-owned Python interface, and the substrate vendor's incentive gradient points toward absorption, not partnership. One verifiable condition flips my verdict: Cole publishes a substrate-agnostic abstraction layer that runs identical algorithms across both CL1 AND FinalSpark Neuroplatform with organic GitHub adoption (1K+ stars on a non-Doom repo) within twelve months. That would be the **Replit** (April 2023) re-validation in wetware — code leverage that compounds across substrates rather than renting one. Until then, I pass and stay in touch.
