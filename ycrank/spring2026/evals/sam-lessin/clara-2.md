# Clara -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Clara: a founder who built the incumbent and is now trying to disrupt himself. George Favvas took Circle Medical to a hundred million in revenue with profitability. That's not a line on a pitch deck — that's a real business that actually works. And now he's back, basically saying "the thing I built is already obsolete, here's the AI-native version." That takes a specific kind of intellectual honesty. The problem is, every other investor in the room is looking at the same signal and reaching for their checkbook. AI healthcare is one of the most consensus-hot categories in venture right now. K Health raised $441M. Hippocratic AI raised $404M at a $3.5B valuation. Forward Health raised $546M. When the total competitive capital deployed in your category exceeds $1.3 billion before you've launched, there is no mispricing. The upside is in the price. The question isn't "is this a good company?" — it's "is this a good bet?" And a good company at a consensus price is a mediocre bet.

The primitive test is where Clara gets interesting but ultimately falls short. "AI-first, human-verified" primary care is a real structural inversion of the telehealth model — I'll give them that. Instead of a doctor doing the work and AI helping around the edges, the AI does the clinical reasoning and a human signs off. That's a meaningful flip. But is it a new primitive? Is it creating a behavior that didn't exist before? Not really. K Health is already doing AI triage with clinician handoff. Every telehealth company on earth is moving in this direction. Clara is executing on a thesis that the entire market already believes in. Nobody is going to "Venmo me" with Clara — they're going to get a virtual doctor visit that feels a bit more automated than the last one. It's better telehealth, not a new category. And better versions of existing things cap your upside at market share capture, not market creation.

The strongest bull case — and I genuinely considered this — goes like this: Favvas isn't some AI tourist who read about healthcare in a blog post. He's operated the exact business he's disrupting. He knows where every body is buried in telehealth operations: the state-by-state licensing hell, the insurance billing labyrinth, the clinical workflow nightmares. That operational knowledge is a legitimate moat that neither Hippocratic AI (selling B2B to health systems) nor any general-purpose LLM can replicate. The medical records integration from 150,000+ facilities creates real switching costs over time. And Carbon Health's bankruptcy in February proves the old capital-intensive clinic model is dying, which opens a lane. If you believe the AI layer is table stakes and the real moat is in clinical operations — that this is more like Metropolis (AI + physical infrastructure) than an AI wrapper — then Clara looks like the team best positioned to execute. I can construct that argument. But the market has already constructed it too, which is the whole problem.

What only I notice: this company triggers almost every one of my red flags simultaneously. It's an AI application-layer startup — "AI is like mobile, not disruptive." The idea is consensus-exciting among VCs — everyone loves AI healthcare. The competitive landscape requires massive capital deployment to win — the opposite of capital discipline. And the founder may have a dual-commitment issue, still listed as CEO of Circle Medical, a WELL Health subsidiary doing $100M+ in revenue. That last point is genuinely concerning. I've seen what happens when a founder tries to birth a new company while still operationally entangled with the old one. You can't serve two masters at the critical early stage. The sane-insane ratio is off too — Favvas is clearly sane, but "AI doctor" is not insane in 2026. It's on the cover of every healthcare trade publication. A sane person doing what everyone already expects is just... competent. Competent doesn't produce asymmetric returns.

The structural durability question is actually Clara's relative bright spot. The clinical operations wrapper — licensing, prescribing authority, insurance billing, regulatory compliance — these are real barriers that survive the AI kill test. Google can build a better medical LLM tomorrow, but Google is not going to staff up a fifty-state licensed clinical team and negotiate with Blue Cross. That part of the moat is genuine. But it's an operations moat, not a technology moat, and operations moats scale slowly and expensively. You're building a healthcare company that happens to use AI, not an AI company with structural leverage. That's a fine business. It might even be a great business. But it's not the kind of asymmetric bet I'm looking for at pre-seed. I want to buy SOL at four cents, and Clara is priced like ETH at two thousand.
