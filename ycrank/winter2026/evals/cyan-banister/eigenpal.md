# EigenPal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with EigenPal: this is possibly the most consensus AI application category I can think of right now. Enterprise document processing is on every cloud provider's roadmap, every consulting firm's AI transformation deck, and every accelerator's portfolio. Rossum has raised $114 million. Hyperscience has raised roughly $300 million. Google, AWS, and Azure all have document AI APIs bundled into contracts enterprises already hold. When I say the most promising companies are not popular and difficult to assess -- EigenPal is the inverse of that. Everyone understands this category. Everyone is building in it. The "why now" argument -- LLMs crossed a threshold for template-free extraction -- is exactly the same argument every other company in this space is making, which means it's not a differentiator, it's table stakes. I don't feel the jolt here. I feel the crowd.

When I try to hear the founders' stories, I find credentials where I'm looking for scars. Blaszyk has an MEng from Imperial and engineering stints at Elastic and Yelp -- technically relevant, genuinely capable. Novak has Imperial, MIT, McKinsey, Accenture, and describes himself as a "3-time founder." These are impressive backgrounds, but they're the backgrounds of people who identified a market opportunity, not people who couldn't sleep because a problem was eating at them. I don't hear the biographical thread that connects either founder to document processing as a calling. And the fact that Novak's prior company Assetario -- a revenue optimization SaaS engine for mobile apps, which is a completely different domain -- still appears active on Tracxn and The Org makes me wonder about full commitment. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else ignored. That biographical commitment is what carries a company through the inevitable crises. I'm not seeing the equivalent here.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. The compliance positioning -- SOC II Type 2, GDPR, CCPA -- is genuinely impressive for a pre-seed company and represents the kind of regulated-space navigation I respect. Working with two large European banks on loan automations is a concrete signal, not hand-waving. European banking is one of the most compliance-heavy verticals on the planet, and if EigenPal can build deep integrations there, the switching costs could become meaningful. The evaluation-first workflow testing and on-prem deployment are smart product decisions for this buyer. If these founders can execute on the regulated-enterprise wedge -- if the European banking beachhead turns into a real moat before Rossum and Hyperscience lock those accounts down -- this could be a solid enterprise SaaS business. The market is growing at 33% CAGR and there's room for multiple winners.

But "solid enterprise SaaS business" isn't what I write checks for. I'm looking for the company that creates a new category, that makes an invisible system visible, that gives economic access to people who don't have it. Enterprise document processing for $1B+ revenue companies is operational efficiency for organizations that already have plenty of resources. It doesn't create income for drivers or transparent credit for underserved borrowers. The customer logos -- McKinsey, Accenture -- are consulting firms, not the kind of scrappy end users whose lives change because of the product. And the unverified nature of those logos concerns me. No case studies, no named testimonials, no press releases. The difference between "McKinsey uses our product" and "someone at McKinsey tried our free trial" is enormous, and I can't tell which it is from the available information.

I respect the technical competence here. Blaszyk's open-source work on docx-js-editor with 443 GitHub stars shows real document engineering skill, and his contributions to Elastic's search infrastructure are directly relevant. These are capable builders. But capable builders in a crowded, consensus category with well-funded competitors and cloud platform risk -- that's not where I find my best investments. The weirdos mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota, the team building male contraceptive gel that every VC passed on -- those are the bets where I have an edge. In IDP, I have no edge, and neither does EigenPal against competitors with hundred-million-dollar war chests.
