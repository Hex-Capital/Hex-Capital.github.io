# Synthetic Sciences -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking thing about Synthetic Sciences is a painful irony: the founders have exactly the profile I look for -- an 18-year-old with 15 publications across ICML, NeurIPS, and ACL, research stints at CMU, Harvard, and MIT CSAIL, and a prior exit before he could legally vote -- and they have chosen to deploy that extraordinary talent into one of the most crowded, consensus-driven markets in technology. When I created the Thiel Fellowship, I was looking for people like Aayam Bansal. Precocious, technically brilliant, willing to drop out of a top CS program because the opportunity cost of staying felt intolerable. Vitalik Buterin had that profile. Dylan Field had it. But what distinguished those founders was not merely raw ability -- it was that they applied their ability to a contrarian thesis that the world had not yet recognized. Buterin saw programmable money when the consensus dismissed cryptocurrency as digital tulips. What does Bansal see that Google, Edison Scientific, Elicit, and Sakana AI do not?

I cannot find a secret here. "AI can automate scientific research" is not a contrarian truth -- it is the loudest consensus in technology. Google announced its own AI Co-Scientist in 2025. FutureHouse spun out Edison Scientific with $70 million and backing from Jeff Dean. Elicit serves 400,000 researchers monthly. Sakana AI is valued at $2.65 billion. When every major lab and a dozen startups are pursuing the same thesis, there is no hidden truth to exploit -- there is only a market where competitors will imitate each other into mutual margin destruction. The claimed differentiator -- a "data flywheel" where product usage generates process-level training data -- is itself the most consensus pitch in the AI agent ecosystem. Every agent startup in 2025 and 2026 claims a proprietary data flywheel. That is not a secret; it is a shared prayer.

The pivot from InkVell to Synthetic Sciences during the YC batch is, for my framework, a negative signal that compounds the competitive problem. InkVell -- helping researchers turn work into publication-ready documents -- was at least a focused tool with a specific wedge. It had the structure of a small-market-first strategy: own the manuscript preparation workflow, then expand into adjacent research tasks. Instead, the founders broadened to four modes (Research, Biology, Flywheel, Write), targeting the entire computational research lifecycle across ML, genomics, and protein design simultaneously. This is the opposite of what I look for. When Facebook launched, it was Harvard only. When PayPal launched, it was eBay power sellers only. When a pre-seed company with two people and $1.5 million targets everything from protein design to GPU orchestration to LaTeX formatting, I see ambition untethered from strategic discipline -- and more critically, I see indefinite thinking. The broadening suggests the founders are searching for product-market fit rather than executing a definite plan.

The bull case, which I want to take seriously because the founding talent is real: if Bansal and Gangwani are correct that process-level supervision data from real research workflows is the critical bottleneck to building capable research agents, and if their product is first to accumulate a sufficient corpus of research process traces, the resulting agents could become structurally superior to anything built on synthetic benchmarks or general-purpose training. This would be analogous to how Palantir's deployment data from intelligence agencies created models that no competitor without similar access could replicate. The 92% BixBench Verified claim, if genuine, would suggest the team has already built something technically differentiated. And 18-year-old founders who have already published at top venues and sold a company are precisely the socially unconventional, conviction-driven builders I favor. If they find their secret -- if they narrow their focus and discover the specific insight about research automation that no one else sees -- they could be formidable. But today, they have not articulated that secret. They have articulated a consensus thesis with a very broad product surface.

The underlying technology concern reinforces the competitive problem. The dossier itself identifies the core risk clearly: "wrapping LLM agents with research workflow tooling is reproducible by well-funded competitors." This is integration work, not a technological discontinuity. The frontier models powering the agents are commoditizing rapidly across providers. The GPU orchestration layer, credential syncing, and sandbox environments are engineering challenges, not proprietary breakthroughs. Nothing in the technical architecture suggests a 10x advantage that would make the prior approach structurally obsolete. Edison Scientific alone has 47 times the funding to build equivalent infrastructure. This is a market where the technology moat is shallow and the competitive dynamics are brutal -- precisely the structure I avoid.

I respect the founders' raw capability enormously, and I would not be surprised if Bansal builds something significant in his career. But this company, at this moment, is fighting a war on terrain that structurally prevents monopoly. The market is crowded, the thesis is shared, the technology is reproducible, and the strategic breadth works against the small-market dominance that creates early defensibility. I pass.
