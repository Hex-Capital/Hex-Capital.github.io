# Torus -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Marcus Lima is a legitimate industrial-domain operator running the exact leverage architecture I don't fund. Torus sells cross-document AI agents to Fortune 500 EPC firms through pilots that begin by asking prospects for "5-6 documents from current projects" and gate on SOC 2 Type 1 & 2 -- that is capital and labor leverage requiring permission from procurement, IT security, and Schneider-Electric-scale incumbents. Lima's Oxford MSc in Energy Systems Engineering and his Heimdal S21 tour with Altman and Benioff on the cap table are real specific knowledge; the distribution wrapper around that knowledge is the wrong shape for my ledger.

Twitter (2007) is the load-bearing analogy for how I score leverage: anyone posts, the platform charges nothing, the marginal cost of replication is zero, distribution is media itself. Torus is the inverse artifact -- an on-prem "sovereign" deployment tier, a SOC 2 gate still "in progress," pilots that require handing over confidential P&IDs before the product is even in the room. Where Twitter compounded through permissionless posting, Torus compounds through enterprise procurement cycles measured in quarters. The dossier names no open-source repo, no organic engineer following, no permissionless surface where the product distributes itself. Twitter's leverage was in the architecture; Torus's leverage sits inside a channel that AVEVA, Bentley, and Hexagon already own and will defend by shipping the same 50-category checker inside their existing EDMS suites once RTInsights-flagged AI features mature.

Vertical SaaS with a single-customer economic loop is the failure mode I flag as a red flag in criterion #2, and Torus sits inside it -- each Fortune 500 EPC customer's document set is economically independent of the next, and the "proprietary corpus of firm-specific procurement approvals" the dossier hopes will become moat is per-tenant lock-in, not network value. Uber's Nth driver made the (N-1) riders better off; Torus's Nth EPC customer makes no other EPC customer's consistency check more accurate unless Torus builds a cross-firm training pool, which the on-prem sovereign tier structurally forecloses. The compounding curve is missing at the architecture layer, and no amount of pilot conversion changes that geometry.

Marcus Lima having already scaled Heimdal into industrial construction customers with $9.7M raised at a $63M valuation is where Torus registers highest on my rubric -- specific knowledge you cannot train for, sitting exactly where the product is aimed. Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity is the single-anchor parallel: a founder with domain immersion deep enough that I waived criterion #2 for him. The disanalogy is that Srinivas paired specific knowledge with permissionless product-and-API distribution to individual researchers; Lima pairs specific knowledge with enterprise pilot programs. Specific knowledge is necessary and not sufficient -- it's the load-bearing pass on the founder, but it doesn't rehabilitate the leverage architecture below it. Rahul Thayil's LinkedIn surfacing Orion (Techstars '23) rather than Torus is a diligence tick to close, not an integrity flag.

The EDMS market at $3.95B growing to $7B by 2035 is not a market that gives you pivot-room -- it's the exact "niche market" pattern Venture Hacks warned about, dressed up with a $864B EPC-services number that Torus does not actually address. The adjacencies are all deeper into the same enterprise sale: pipeline operators, data-center EPCs, substation OEMs. Every pivot vector inside Torus's TAM leads to another Fortune 500 procurement office. The counterfactual that would flip this dimension a tier is if Torus repositioned toward the individual mechanical or process engineer as buyer -- a bottoms-up developer-tool-for-engineers motion analogous to Replit's -- because that would rebuild the leverage stack and open pivot-room simultaneously. The dossier shows no evidence of that motion.

Torus in 36 months is a $3-8M ARR vertical AI SaaS with three-to-five named Fortune 500 logos, acquired by AVEVA/Schneider or Bentley in the $30-80M range as a bolt-on AI feature for their existing EDMS suites -- a productive outcome for Lima's second act and a structural pass for me because the geometry caps at ~5-10x on a seed check rather than the 400x-to-4,000x math that pays for my portfolio system. The mechanism producing this ceiling is exactly the leverage architecture: incumbent channel ownership plus per-customer economics plus regulatory-adjacent switching-cost moats add up to a good business and a bounded outcome.

The strongest bull case is the Perplexity exception -- three of five gates clearing emphatically waived criterion #2 for Aravind Srinivas, and one could argue Lima's specific knowledge (top-quartile), the "reindustrialization capex surge" timing (pre-consensus-ish), and the multi-Fortune-500 pilot signal collectively reset the math for Torus the same way. The bear counter is that Perplexity distributed through code leverage to millions of individual users; Torus distributes through procurement to dozens of enterprise buyers, which is the architecture Anchorage got a waiver on only because I co-founded the category via MetaStable in 2014 and served as advisor. I have no such pre-consensus position in EPC document consistency. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Lima ships an open-source consistency-check engine or a self-serve product for individual process engineers that shows organic adoption before the next procurement pilot closes -- if that surface appears, the leverage architecture rewrites and I re-underwrite at a higher size.
