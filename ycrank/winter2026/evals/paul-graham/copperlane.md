# Copperlane -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The origin story here is unusually legit. Both founders grew up in what they call "mortgage families" -- Athan's parents at Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae, Brianna's at FHFA. They didn't discover mortgage origination by reading industry reports or brainstorming AI applications. They grew up at dinner tables where the $11,800 per-loan cost and the document-chasing misery were just how adults talked about work. That's organic problem discovery in the truest sense. It's not quite Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive -- they're one step removed from being the actual loan officers pulling their hair out -- but it's the kind of deep, lifelong exposure that can't be replicated by market research. The schlep factor is real and enormous: TRID, RESPA, ECOA, fair lending rules, state licensing, integration with legacy systems built by companies whose business model depends on human-intensive workflows. This is the kind of regulatory ugliness that makes smart engineers decide to build another developer tool instead. The problem has been hiding in plain sight for years precisely because the compliance burden scares everyone off.

The co-founder situation is the part that makes me nervous, and it maps uncomfortably well to a pattern I've seen kill companies. Both Athan and Brianna entered YC W26 with different companies and different co-founders. Both experienced breakups. They found each other mid-batch, discovered the shared mortgage background, and reformed as Copperlane. I've seen this movie before -- in one batch, nine companies added co-founders they didn't know well, and all nine collapsed within a year. But there are real differences here. They're from the same hometown. The shared domain background is deep and specific. And both of them demonstrated something important: when their original companies fell apart, they didn't quit the batch. Brianna wound down Q2Q -- which takes more intellectual honesty than most people have -- and kept going. Athan went through two co-founder breakups and kept building. That's not the same as two strangers pairing up at a hackathon. The foundation is thinner than I'd like, but the resilience signal is genuine.

The technology layer concerns me less than the business model dynamics. Yes, LLM-powered document reading is increasingly commoditized, and yes, Salesforce announced Agentforce for mortgage. But commoditization of the AI layer actually plays to Copperlane's advantage if they accumulate domain-specific training data faster than anyone else. The real question is whether a two-person team can run enterprise sales into conservative mortgage lenders while simultaneously building product and navigating regulatory compliance. Mortgage lenders don't adopt technology the way software companies do. The sales cycle is long, security reviews are painful, and the integration with existing LOS platforms like Encompass is a gating dependency. This is a schlep on top of a schlep, which is either a double moat or an impossible workload for two people fresh out of college.

The bull case is compelling enough to take seriously. If Copperlane nails the regulatory depth and builds trust with a handful of mid-size lenders early, the switching costs become formidable. Nobody wants to rip out a compliance-aware AI agent that's already trained on their edge cases. The incumbents -- Encompass, Empower -- face business model cannibalization: their revenue depends on the very human-intensive workflows that agents would displace. And Salesforce's horizontal approach rarely beats a vertical startup that lives and breathes one domain. The claim that they're already working with lenders processing hundreds of millions in volume is tantalizing, though too vague to hang a conviction on. If that's real contracted revenue rather than pilot conversations, this becomes a different bet entirely.

Where I land: the organic discovery is among the strongest I've seen in this batch -- growing up in mortgage families is exactly the kind of background that produces founders who understand the problem viscerally rather than theoretically. The schlep willingness is high. But the co-founder recency is a legitimate structural risk, the traction evidence is thin and unverified, and the path from two-person team to enterprise mortgage sales is a brutal execution challenge. I'd want to see them in six months -- if the co-founder relationship has held and they've converted those lender relationships into real revenue, I'd write a check then. Right now the conviction isn't quite there.
