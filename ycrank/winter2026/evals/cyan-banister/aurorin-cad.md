# Aurorin CAD -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what catches me first: a kid who apparently skipped college entirely, went straight from high school to SpaceX — three internships, Raptor engines, Dragon guidance systems, Starshield flight software — then did GPU driver work at Apple, and is now telling the world he's going to rebuild SolidWorks from scratch. Alone. That's either delusional or it's the kind of audacity I've spent my career backing. The honest answer is I don't know yet, because I don't have Michael Baron's story. And that's my problem. I want to know what happened between high school in — wherever he grew up — and walking into SpaceX. What drove a teenager to skip the path everyone told him to take? There's clearly something there: getting hired at SpaceX without a degree, repeatedly, suggests a level of raw technical ability and determination that's uncommon. It reminds me of my own path — teaching myself to code in public libraries, no credentials, just relentless self-education. But "reminds me" isn't enough. I need to hear it from him directly, unscripted, before I can calibrate whether this is biographical conviction or youthful ambition chasing a hot category.

The technical bet itself is genuinely interesting and has an element of contrarianism I respect. Everyone else in the AI-for-CAD space — Adam, Zoo, Shapr3D — is layering intelligence on top of geometry engines designed in the 1980s. Michael is saying: tear it all down and rebuild the kernel. That's structurally different, and it's the kind of bet where if you're right, you have a moat that compounds for years. It's analogous to what Carta did in a completely different domain — the cap table infrastructure was broken, everyone was patching spreadsheets, and Carta said "we're going to build the system of record from scratch." CAD kernels are a similar invisible system: every physical product in the world flows through software built on ancient geometry engines, and nobody outside engineering even knows these tools exist or how painful they are. Making that invisible infrastructure modern and accessible is a pattern I've invested into repeatedly without ever naming it as a thesis.

But I have to be honest about where this doesn't light up for me. The broader AI-for-engineering category is becoming crowded and consensus-y. Adam already generated 10 million social media impressions. Zoo has $5M in funding. When everyone is piling into a space, the weirdness premium — which is where I find my best returns — gets arbitraged away. The kernel-from-scratch approach is contrarian *within* the category, but the category itself isn't weird or dismissed anymore. Nobody's going to look at you funny at a dinner party for saying "I'm building AI for CAD." Compare that to when I wrote my first check into SpaceX and people literally thought Elon was insane, or when Crusoe was proposing to mine Bitcoin on gas flares. Those were ideas that made other investors uncomfortable. "Better, faster CAD with AI" doesn't make anyone uncomfortable — it makes them nod enthusiastically and ask about the TAM.

The bull case I want to engage with seriously: if Jared Friedman is right that AI coding has crossed the threshold making this possible for a small team, then Michael might be in a unique position — SpaceX-grade engineering discipline combined with Apple-level GPU expertise, applied at a moment when the tools to build this thing finally exist. The custom kernel becomes a flywheel: deeper AI integration than any plugin approach, GPU acceleration that legacy kernels can't match, and a technical moat that takes years to replicate even with AI assistance. If he can get to feature parity on core parametric modeling for hardware prototyping teams and the speed claims hold up, the bottoms-up adoption path could work — individual engineers falling in love with the tool and dragging it into their organizations, the way Onshape tried but with genuinely modern architecture. The $12B CAD market has massive inertia, but inertia protects insurgents who break through just as much as it protects incumbents. For this bull case to play out, Michael needs to recruit a world-class team quickly, ship a kernel that's reliable enough for real engineering work, and demonstrate that the full-stack approach delivers capabilities impossible through plugin architectures. That's a lot of "ifs," but each one is individually achievable, not requiring simultaneous miracles.

On the economic access dimension, there's a latent angle I can see but that isn't being surfaced: professional CAD tools cost $500 to $1,500 per seat per year, which prices out independent engineers, small hardware startups in emerging markets, and makers who could otherwise bring physical products to life. A free or dramatically cheaper AI-native CAD tool could unlock a class of creators who currently can't afford the tools of their trade. That's meaningful to me — it's the same pattern as Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm providing transparent credit. But Michael isn't framing it this way, and I'm not going to project my thesis onto a founder who may be thinking about this purely as a performance and AI play.

The solo founder question sits in an unusual place for me. After HQ Trivia, co-founder conflict is my sharpest red flag — and a solo founder eliminates that risk entirely. But the scope of this undertaking against a single pair of hands worries me differently. This isn't a consumer app where one brilliant engineer can ship version one and iterate. A CAD kernel demands precision that cascading errors can destroy. I'd want to understand Michael's plan for building a team before writing a check, and the fact that the YC page says "not hiring" at team size one gives me pause.

Where I land: this is a technically impressive bet by a clearly talented young engineer in a space I find structurally interesting. But I'm missing the biographical signal that anchors my highest-conviction investments, the category is trending toward consensus, and the economic access angle is latent rather than intentional. I'd want to meet Michael, hear his story unfiltered, and understand whether the audacity here is rooted in something personal or is primarily a technical thesis. Right now, I can't get to the conviction level I need to write a check from my own money.
