# Balance -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know before I read another line. Pilot has raised $325 million. Digits has raised roughly $100 million. Finally has raised $305 million. Puzzle, $66.5 million. Truewind, $17.5 million. Haven -- a pre-seed company with essentially the identical model of AI plus human accountants -- already has $1 million in revenue and 120 customers. This is not a market where someone has discovered a secret. This is a market where every venture capitalist in Silicon Valley has already agreed on the same thesis: AI will transform accounting. When every smart person in the room agrees, you are not looking at a secret -- you are looking at a consensus dressed up as insight. And consensus markets produce exactly one outcome: mimetic competition that destroys value for everyone involved. The participants imitate each other's features, match each other's pricing, and burn capital trying to differentiate products that are structurally identical. I have watched this pattern play out in food delivery, in ride-sharing, in dozens of SaaS categories. The accounting AI space is already deep into this dynamic.

The supposed differentiator -- "we are the accountancy, not the tool" -- is not a secret either. It is a positioning choice, and it is one that Haven has already made. Operating as the accountancy rather than selling software to accountancies is a business model decision, not a contrarian truth about the world. The dossier itself states plainly that "LLM capabilities for categorization and reconciliation are accessible to any well-funded team" and that "the AI + human review model is replicable." When your own market analysis concedes that your approach is replicable, you have conceded the monopoly question. There is no proprietary technology here. There are no network effects -- one SMB using Balance does not make the service more valuable to the next SMB. There are no meaningful economies of scale, because the model requires hiring qualified human accountants in every jurisdiction you serve. The conversational interface via Slack and WhatsApp is a UX convenience, not a technological moat that compounds over time.

The go-to-market strategy compounds the problem. Balance is operating across Denmark, the United States, and the United Kingdom from day one -- three distinct tax regimes, three sets of accounting standards, three regulatory frameworks. This is the exact opposite of the small-market dominance strategy that I consider essential. Facebook started at Harvard. PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Stripe started with developers. Each monopolized a tiny, concentrated market before expanding concentrically. Balance is trying to serve "tech, e-commerce, florists, and other sectors" across three countries simultaneously. This is competitive thinking -- trying to capture share across a broad market -- not monopoly thinking, which requires choosing a niche small enough to own completely.

The strongest bull case would require something like the following to be true: that the structural business model conflict facing both traditional accounting firms (whose revenue depends on billable hours) and software incumbents like Intuit (who cannot offer services without alienating their accounting firm partners) creates a genuine and durable opening for an integrated AI-plus-human player. If that structural gap is real, and if one company can establish itself as the default choice for SMBs abandoning their traditional accountant, the switching costs of embedded financial history could create a defensible position over time. Mathias Løvring's direct experience -- having founded an accountancy himself and served as CFO at Female Invest -- means he has lived inside the problem in a way that most founders in this space have not. The domain knowledge is genuine. If Balance could achieve dramatically better unit economics through AI leverage and use that margin advantage to price competitors out of the market, there is a version of this story that works.

But that version requires Balance to win a war of execution against competitors who collectively hold over $800 million in capital and significant head starts. Pilot has 1,000 customers and $27 million in revenue. Digits has $29.9 million in revenue with 11x growth. These are not theoretical competitors -- they are real businesses with real customers and real scale advantages. Nothing in Balance's approach -- no proprietary model, no unique data advantage, no technological discontinuity -- gives me reason to believe they will achieve the 10x superiority necessary to overcome these incumbents. The founding team has relevant experience but no evidence of the kind of definite, civilization-level vision I look for. A CFO background and McKinsey training produce operational competence, not the obsessive, socially unconventional conviction that drives 0-to-1 outcomes. The company is going from 1 to n -- building a better version of something that already exists, in a market where many others are building better versions simultaneously.

I pass. There is no secret here.
