# Coalition Systems -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I look for is whether someone on the team can build the product. Coalition Systems is pitching a secure coordination platform -- essentially real-time software for messaging, collaboration, and decision support across allied military organizations. The CTO, Freddie Wollen, is a mechanical engineer with expertise in energy systems and critical infrastructure. No public GitHub repos exist for any of the three principals. No prototype, no screenshots, no app store presence, no Product Hunt launch, no community -- nothing that suggests working software exists. I've written about this explicitly: a founding team that can't build the product themselves lacks the iteration speed that makes early-stage companies viable. When Patrick Collison was building Stripe, he was the kind of person who could sit down and write the code. When the AppJet team applied to YC, they built their product during the process. Coalition Systems has assembled a team optimized for navigating Washington, not for shipping software.

Andrew Badger's background is genuinely interesting -- a DIA case officer who deployed to Afghanistan in 2014, trained at the CIA's Farm, then advised McKinsey and Deutsche Bank on geopolitical risk. He has lived inside the defense coordination problem in a way that most founders haven't. But Badger holds the title "Head of Strategic Partnerships," not co-founder or CTO. His role architecture tells me this team views the challenge as primarily a sales and network-access problem rather than a building problem. The advisory relationship with H.R. McMaster and the co-authorship connection to former Acting DIA Director David Shedd are impressive door-openers. But doors opening is not the same as having something worth carrying through them. Vijay Pathak's prior venture, Quantum Politica, was an "AI-driven political strategy advisory" -- and his CES 2026 talk was titled "Beyond the Algorithm: Gen Z's New Digital Habits." Neither of these suggests someone obsessively focused on military coordination software. They suggest someone with broad intellectual interests who has selected defense tech as a promising vector.

The comparison that keeps surfacing is the anti-pattern I've described before: credentialed people from elite institutions who identify a market gap through analysis rather than lived frustration. Coalition Systems frames its problem in market-structure language -- "hyper-fragmented defense software stack," the positioning matrix against Palantir, Mattermost, and Second Front Systems. The origin story is conspicuously absent. When Drew Houston pitched Dropbox, the story started with forgetting his USB drive. When the Octopart founders applied to YC, they were physics students who couldn't find electronic parts. Coalition Systems' story starts with a 23-year-old from Luxembourg who won a NATO Youth Award and raised Silicon Valley money. That's a press narrative, not a problem-discovery narrative. The Silicon Luxembourg headline -- "At 23, This Luxembourger Just Raised Silicon Valley Money To Build The 'Slack for Defence'" -- perfectly captures what's being optimized for, and it isn't product-market fit.

The strongest bull case for Coalition Systems requires believing three things simultaneously: that the defense coordination problem is large enough and poorly-served enough to support a new entrant, that Pathak's Oxford AI/ML research and Badger's operational intelligence background will translate into a technical product neither has demonstrated they can build, and that $500K from a16z Speedrun provides enough runway to navigate the years-long FedRAMP and IL-5/IL-6 certification process before Mattermost -- already deployed to 20,000+ DoD users with $33.1M in annual revenue -- extends its platform to cover coalition-specific features. The schlep is real: building for classified environments with cleared personnel and SCIF requirements on a four-person team is genuinely the kind of painful, unglamorous work that repels most founders. That's the one signal here that activates my framework positively. Defense procurement is a schlep that creates natural moats. But a schlep only becomes a moat if you can actually do the schlep, and nothing in this dossier demonstrates that capability. The Stripe analogy is instructive: the Collisons succeeded not just because payments infrastructure was a schlep that repelled other developers, but because they were developers who could build the infrastructure. Coalition Systems has the schlep without the builders.

Pathak is 23 -- the age pattern I favor. I backed the Bindwell founders at 18 and 19, Patrick Collison as a teenager. But youth is only a positive signal when paired with demonstrated building ability. Collison had already shipped and sold Auctomatic. The Bindwell founders were synthesizing pesticide molecules. Pathak has an academic AI/ML profile and a political advisory startup. His Google Scholar profile exists but the dossier surfaces no published work or technical artifacts. The learning rate could be high -- an Oxford DPhil at 23 suggests raw intellectual capacity -- but I have no evidence of iteration, pivots, or the weekly compounding growth that I use as a compass for learning speed. Coalition Systems presents as a static pitch, not an evolving product.

I'm passing on Coalition Systems. The two dimensions that lock this decision are Organic Problem Discovery -- the absence of any founder narrative about personally experiencing the coordination problem, replaced by market-positioning language -- and the fundamental question of whether this team can build what they're describing. A secure, classified-environment collaboration platform is a serious engineering challenge, and the founding team's technical profile doesn't match the product's technical requirements. The network access is real and the problem space has merit, but I've learned to distinguish between teams that can open doors and teams that can build what goes behind them.
