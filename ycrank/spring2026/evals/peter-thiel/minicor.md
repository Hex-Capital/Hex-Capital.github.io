# Minicor -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: what is the secret? What does this company know that most intelligent people would actively dispute? And here I struggle, because the thesis that "AI vision models can replace brittle RPA scripts by reading screens instead of parsing DOM elements" is not a secret at all. It is the most obvious inference from the last eighteen months of foundation model development. Anthropic shipped computer use capabilities. OpenAI and Google followed. Every engineer who has watched a Claude agent navigate a desktop interface has had exactly this idea. When the technology demos *are* the thesis, there is no hidden truth -- there is only an implementation race. And implementation races are the province of competition, not monopoly.

The competitive landscape confirms this diagnosis. Skyvern, Browserbase, Browser Use -- three well-funded startups building AI-powered automation from slightly different angles. UiPath and Automation Anywhere, massive incumbents with billions in revenue and existing enterprise relationships, already incorporating agentic AI into their platforms. Microsoft Power Automate sits inside the enterprise stack of every Windows-dependent organization. Minicor's answer to this is "we do desktop and Citrix, not just browsers" -- but this is a deployment surface distinction, not a category creation. Stripe did not merely add a feature to existing payment processors; it created an entirely new paradigm of developer-first payments infrastructure that made the old approach structurally obsolete. Minicor is offering a better version of something that already exists, in a subsegment of a market where multiple players are converging on the same capabilities. The mimetic dynamics here are unfavorable: everyone is imitating the same foundation model demos, building toward the same endpoints, competing for the same enterprise budgets.

The pivot from Laminar -- a low-code integration platform -- to Minicor's desktop automation tells me something about the founders' epistemology. A pivot is, by definition, an admission that the prior thesis was wrong and a search for a new one. This is precisely the lean-startup methodology I find least compelling: iterate, discover, pivot. The sign-in portal still resolves to app.laminar.run. The GitHub organization is still laminar-run. These are not the marks of founders who had a definite vision of how the world should work and built relentlessly toward it. These are the marks of founders who are searching -- and searching is what you do when you do not yet have a secret. I do not doubt the team's engineering capability. Microsoft, Ford, Morgan Stanley -- these are credible technical backgrounds. But credible engineering talent executing on a consensus thesis in a competitive market produces, at best, a moderately successful company that gets acquired by UiPath for some fraction of a billion dollars. That is not a power-law outcome.

The strongest argument for Minicor is the healthcare wedge. SOC 2 Type II certification and HIPAA compliance are genuinely non-trivial -- they represent months of work and real operational discipline. The claim of processing 25,000 patients per day, if accurate, suggests production deployment in an environment where switching costs are high and risk tolerance is low. Healthcare organizations running Epic or Cerner on Citrix environments are not going to rip out a working automation system to try the next startup. This creates real, if narrow, defensibility. If Minicor can own EHR automation in a handful of large health systems before anyone else gets compliant and deployed, they could build a durable niche. The question is whether "durable niche" is what I invest for. It is not. A company that automates data entry into legacy EHR systems on Citrix -- even if it does so brilliantly -- faces a ceiling imposed by the very stagnation it exploits. As EHR vendors improve their APIs, as healthcare migrates (slowly) toward interoperability standards like FHIR, the screen-scraping layer becomes less necessary. The company is built on the persistence of technological dysfunction, not on a vision of technological progress. I invest in founders building the future, not founders monetizing the past's failure to arrive.

The deepest structural problem is foundation model dependency. Minicor's self-healing capability is not proprietary technology -- it is an application of Anthropic's and OpenAI's vision models wrapped in deployment infrastructure. The infrastructure layer -- Windows VMs, horizontal scaling, compliance -- is engineering work that any well-funded competitor can replicate. When your core technology belongs to your supplier, and your supplier is actively exploring vertical integration into your market, you are not building a monopoly. You are renting one. General Matter enriches nuclear fuel with proprietary centrifuge technology. Palantir built Gotham and Foundry as software platforms that no one else could replicate. Minicor calls an API. The distinction matters.
