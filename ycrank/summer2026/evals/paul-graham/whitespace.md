# Whitespace -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Whitespace is the cleanest version of a profile I see often and almost always pass on: the schlep is real, the animal is missing. Alex Tung and Leon Yao are pointed at a problem I respect -- order entry sitting in PDFs and inboxes, ERP integrations across Epicor and SAP and NetSuite and spreadsheets -- which the dossier itself concedes is "operationally intensive for a 2-person team" and is simultaneously the moat thesis. That is a Coinbase-shaped problem. What I cannot find in the Whitespace dossier is the Coinbase-shaped founder.

The schlep ladder is where Whitespace scores best, and the load-bearing analogy is **Stripe** (2010). The Collisons won because connecting a credit-card processor to a developer's app was unpleasant work everyone else routed around -- "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) named them by name. Whitespace is doing the distributor-side version of that same grind: living models of heterogeneous ERPs, inbox-native order capture, supplier ETA chasing, RMA intake. The dossier evidence pinning Whitespace to the upper half of this ladder is the integration surface itself -- Epicor, Infor, SAP, NetSuite, plus spreadsheets and inbox -- which is the kind of work a software-only YC applicant unconsciously avoids. Where Whitespace diverges from Stripe is that the Collisons had already shipped **Auctomatic** (2007) into this exact commerce surface; the Whitespace dossier shows no equivalent shipped artifact, only a feature list.

The animal-resourcefulness ladder is where Whitespace falls hardest, and the failure pattern I want to name first is the one from my Pass Example: a polished page, a benefits grid ("10 hrs saved/person/week, 5% order volume, 1% margin, 2-4 wk setup"), and customer logos -- Mercer & Co., Halton Foods, Brightway Retail -- that the dossier flags as appearing in "demo data contexts," not as paying customers. That is the inverse of the **Airbnb** (2009) cereal-box stunt. Chesky and Gebbia manufactured revenue from a thing that didn't exist; the Whitespace site manufactures social proof from customers that don't appear to exist. There is no pre-funding artifact named in the Whitespace dossier -- no Hacker News demo like Drew Houston shipped before **Dropbox** (2007), no rejected-then-rebuilt prototype like Brian Armstrong's Android wallet at **Coinbase** (2012), no unscalable stunt. Two founders, zero job postings, and a feature grid is what the dossier actually contains.

On organic founder-pain, the mechanism to watch over 18 months is whether the "grew up in families running wholesale distribution businesses" line in Whitespace's site copy translates into the kind of muscle-memory product instincts the Collisons had at **Stripe** (2010). Family-adjacent pain is real but it is not the Drew Houston USB-stick pain -- Whitespace's founders did not, as far as the dossier shows, sit at a distributor's desk last quarter retyping a PDF order into Epicor. Family-business exposure compounds into good instincts about who to sell to and what features matter; it does not compound into the same demo-first conviction that flips my read of a team.

On hacker genealogy, the score would flip a tier only if Leon Yao is confirmed to be the InstaDeep research engineer with NeurIPS and ICLR 2025 papers on RL and web automation -- that is exactly the technical lineage I over-index on, and web automation is on-point for the Whitespace agent stack. The dossier is explicit that this identity is unverified. Alex Tung's bio is BSc Business Economics from Exeter and Chief of Staff / Head of GTM at Ankar AI -- competent operator, not hacker. With no GitHub trail found for either founder and one founder identity unconfirmed, Whitespace lands where the default sits, with optionality upward if Leon turns out to be who he might be.

On co-founder configuration, the single-anchor parallel is **Stripe** (2010): two complementary founders, business and technical. Whitespace has two, the team is the right size for the stage, but the dossier states "No public data on co-founder history" -- I cannot tell whether Alex and Leon have the Collison-brothers chemistry or whether they paired up in spring 2026 for the YC application. That is the kind of thing the interview surfaces and the dossier cannot.

My 36-month forecast for Whitespace: stalls under $1M ARR and gets quietly acquihired into Epicor, Infor, or one of the funded vertical incumbents (Pepper at $128M raised, Choco at $301M) for under $25M, with Leon Yao landing in an applied-ML role. The mechanism is the high-schlep / low-animal pairing: the schlep is real enough that the better-capitalized competitors will close it, and Whitespace's dossier shows no pre-funding evidence the founders will out-grind a team with $50M of fresh Series C dollars from Pepper's February 2026 round.

The strongest objection comes from **Coinbase** (2012) itself: bitcoin compliance also looked like an unsexy schlep that bigger players should have won, and Armstrong won it from a 2-person start. Why not Whitespace? Because Armstrong shipped a working Android Bitcoin wallet after I'd already rejected him in writing -- that was the artifact that overturned the rejection. The Whitespace dossier contains the inverse evidence: customer logos that appear to be demo data, a feature list in lieu of a demo video, zero press, zero open-source footprint, no Hacker News presence. The condition that would flip my verdict is concrete and verifiable: a working Whitespace agent processing real orders for one named distributor with attributable revenue, plus confirmation that Leon Yao is the InstaDeep researcher. Until then, the schlep without the animal is the reverse of the pattern that has made me money. Pass, leaning Neutral.
