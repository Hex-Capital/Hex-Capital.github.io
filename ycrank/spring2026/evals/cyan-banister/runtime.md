# Runtime -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Runtime: this is one of the most consensus ideas I've seen in a while. AI coding agent governance, developer tools, enterprise guardrails -- every investor I know is looking for exactly this deal right now. And when every investor in town wants a piece of a category, that's not my signal. That's my exit sign. The most promising companies I've backed -- SpaceX when private rockets sounded delusional, Crusoe when they wanted to mine Bitcoin on gas flares -- those companies were unpopular. They were difficult to assess. Nobody else wanted to fund them. Runtime is the opposite: it's the kind of company a smart MBA student would design if you asked them "what should exist in the AI infrastructure stack in 2026?" The competitors tell the story here. E2B has raised $32M. Daytona has $30.7M. Galileo has $68M. This space is already crowded with well-capitalized players, and we're still in the earliest innings. That's not a weird pocket of dreamers nobody's found yet -- that's a land grab everybody can see.

When I think about Gus Trigos, I see a capable, accomplished builder. He taught himself C++ in high school, worked as a quant at BlackRock, founded Mentum through YC in S21, raised $4.2M from Google's Gradient Ventures and good angels, and navigated a pivot to an acquisition by Nuvocargo. That's a real track record. But when I ask my first question -- "tell me your story, not the business, how did you get here?" -- what I'm listening for is the thread that ties this person to this particular problem in a way that can't be replicated. Why coding agent governance? Gus's background is in quantitative finance and supply chain automation. The path from BlackRock derivatives trading to coding agent guardrails is intellectually coherent but biographically disconnected. He saw an opportunity in a hot market and moved on it quickly. That's smart, but it's not the same as the founder who's been obsessing over a problem for years because they lived inside it. Compare that to someone like the Contraline founder, who spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone would take him seriously -- that biographical commitment is what makes someone persist through the inevitable moments when everything falls apart.

The bull case deserves real engagement: the timing is genuinely right. Coding agents crossed from assisted to autonomous in 2025, and the governance gap is real -- organizations are either locking agents down entirely or letting them run wild. The structural argument that agent vendors won't build restrictive governance because it reduces their API call volume is clever and probably correct for the next 12-18 months. If Runtime moves fast enough, the switching costs from configured governance policies could create real stickiness. The $99/seat/mo pricing in a market where every enterprise team will eventually need some version of this could be enormous. And Gus's prior exit, while modest, proves he can navigate hard moments and find a landing. If you believe the governance layer becomes as important as the agents themselves, and if you believe a neutral third party wins over the agent vendors building their own controls, this could be a significant company.

But I keep coming back to what this company doesn't do for the people I care about. My best investments -- Uber, Postmates, Affirm, Thumbtack -- created economic access for populations that didn't have it. They made invisible systems visible and broke down barriers that kept people locked out. Runtime serves engineering leaders at companies that already have engineering teams. It helps PMs and designers at well-resourced startups use coding agents. That's productivity tooling for people who are already doing fine. It's a perfectly reasonable business, but it doesn't create the kind of positive-sum economic impact that gets me out of bed. And the defensibility question nags at me -- the governance layer has low technical barriers, the sandbox infrastructure is commoditizing rapidly, and the moment Anthropic ships team-level controls natively in Claude Code, a significant chunk of Runtime's value proposition compresses. The AGPLv3 license is smart but it's a legal moat, not a technology moat or a data moat.

This is a competent team building a real product for a real need in a market that's growing fast. For a lot of investors, that's enough. But I'm not looking for the thing that everyone can already see. I'm looking for the founder who's been told "no" fifty times and keeps going because this problem is personal to them, building something that most people think is crazy, in a space that creates genuine opportunity for people who need it. Runtime doesn't hit those notes for me.
