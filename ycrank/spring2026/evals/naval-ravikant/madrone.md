# Madrone -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Madrone is a capex hardware company selling chiller-replacement units into hyperscaler procurement cycles in Texas -- the structural opposite of what I fund. The dimensional signature is unmistakable: a real industrial market ($4.07B liquid cooling growing 31.5% CAGR per Globe Newswire May 2026) attacked through the most permission-gated distribution channel in the modern economy. When the leverage architecture is wrong, the market size doesn't save the check; it just makes the wrong-shape bet bigger.

Run the leverage check first, the way I always do. **Twitter** (2007) sits at the top of this ladder -- media as protocol, anyone posts, zero marginal cost, no gatekeeper. **Anchorage Digital** sits at the bottom rung I've actually written -- regulated custody, OCC charter, permission-as-moat -- and even Anchorage required a category-timing call rooted in my **MetaStable** (2014) co-founding conviction to override the rule. Madrone sits below Anchorage. Selling modular cooling hardware into hyperscaler facility teams is capex leverage requiring permission from the largest procurement organizations on Earth, with multi-year evaluation cycles, and Akshay Trikha and Erik Meike are a two-person team trying to run those parallel evaluations. Nothing in my 273-check ledger looks like this, and I have no MetaStable-equivalent dollar-cost-averaging conviction in industrial cooling to underwrite the override.

The lowest dimension is the leverage one above -- the highest is market size, which is still only mid-rung in my framework. **Uber** (2010) is the high rung: transportation as a category large enough to absorb the black-car-to-UberX pivot inside seed runway. The low rung is single-vertical SaaS that can't pivot without refinancing. Madrone sits in the middle: $13.6B cooling TAM growing to $46.3B by 2033 (openpr / Persistence Market Research) is genuinely big, but the pivot vectors are constrained because the asset is a physical thermodynamic unit. You can't ship a Madrone chiller to a different vertical the way Odeo's audio team shipped twttr; pivoting from "dew-point cooling for arid Texas data centers" to anything else means re-engineering hardware on capital you don't have at pre-seed.

On marketplace/network compounding, **Uber** is the high rung again, **Perplexity** (2022) is the documented exception where I waived this gate, and Madrone sits below Perplexity -- each unit Madrone sells is economically independent of the next, no Nth-customer lift, no compounding user-added value. The Perplexity exception required three gates clearing emphatically; Madrone clears one. On specific knowledge, **Stack Overflow** (2008) Atwood and Spolsky writing for the developers they were is the high rung; the low rung is "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity." Madrone's founders sit closer to the middle but on the wrong axis -- Meike's iPhone wireless power work at Apple is impressive hardware credentialing and Trikha's QuantumScape ML and Berkeley scaling-laws research is real, but neither has shipped HVAC, refrigeration cycles, or data center facility infrastructure per the dossier. Adjacent technical depth isn't the specific knowledge that closes hyperscaler facility deals. On the Buffett trio, no signals either way; I apply the default.

Forecast at 36 months: Madrone is acquired by Johnson Controls, Trane, or Carrier for sub-$200M following the **Alloy Enterprises**/JCI playbook (May 13, 2026 press release) and the JetCool/Flex playbook (November 2024) -- the incumbents have telegraphed they buy rather than build cooling startups, and a two-founder team without HVAC domain depth selling capex into multi-year hyperscaler cycles is exactly the acquisition shape those buyers prefer. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: real category, wrong leverage, capped by procurement physics into a strategic exit rather than a standalone scaling outcome. That's a respectable result for the founders and a poor fit for my asymmetric-payoff math, where one X downside is fine but I need the 1,000-10,000x upside geometry to clear, and a chiller-OEM acquisition doesn't deliver it.

The bull case that has to sting: **Anchorage** violated my leverage rule because regulated infrastructure created permission-as-moat, and Madrone could plausibly become permission-as-moat if the "different thermodynamic process" delivering 20°C supply water at 30% lower power genuinely works in Texas wet-bulb conditions and gets locked into hyperscaler facility specs. NVIDIA Inception membership, the AI-driven $1T infrastructure buildout, and a category where chip-level players (Corintis at ~$58M raised, 8-digit revenue per Sacra; Accelsius at ~$50M) are all attacking a different layer leaving Madrone's facility-water layer relatively uncrowded -- that's a real bull mechanism. It fails the same way Anchorage would have failed without my MetaStable-rooted category conviction: I don't have it for industrial cooling, the dossier surfaces no third-party validation of the 30% efficiency claim, and the Clearview exception that flexed integrity for "extreme specific knowledge" doesn't apply because the founders' knowledge is adjacent (ML, consumer hardware) not extreme (HVAC). The condition that flips my verdict: a signed pilot with a named hyperscaler -- Microsoft, Google, Meta, or a major Texas colocation operator like Lambda or Crusoe -- with third-party-measured efficiency data confirming the 30% claim at production load. Until that exists, Madrone is the wrong leverage shape for my check.
