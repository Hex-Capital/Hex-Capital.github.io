# Korso -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Five funded competitors are already building AI quoting for manufacturers. Paperless Parts has $30M and an established customer base. Uptool raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all in the mix. When four top-tier VC firms have already placed their bet on the same thesis, this is no longer a contrarian opportunity — it's consensus. You get paid for being right first. Korso is not first. The "why now" — LLMs crossed the threshold for parsing unstructured manufacturing documents — is real, but it applies identically to every competitor in this space. A real "why now" that five teams notice simultaneously is just a market signal, not an edge.

The specific knowledge question kills this for me. I look at these three founders — current or recent UPenn undergraduates with internship-level manufacturing exposure — and I ask: if they disappeared tomorrow, would this company's core insight survive? The answer is obviously yes, because the insight ("use AI to automate RFQ-to-quote workflows") is already being executed by at least five other teams. Martin Pan spent a stint as an Automations Engineer at GM. That's relevant background, not specific knowledge. Specific knowledge in manufacturing quoting would look like someone who ran a quoting operation for a decade, who knew which document formats break extraction models, who had pricing intuition so deep they could spot margin errors by feel. Ev Williams at Twitter had specific knowledge from years of building Blogger — the product was his knowledge, externalized. Korso's product could be built by any competent engineering team with access to the same LLM APIs. That's the definition of trainable, replaceable knowledge.

The leverage architecture concerns me almost as much. This looks like software leverage on the surface — SaaS, AI, RAG — but the reality of manufacturing B2B is that each customer has unique pricing logic, unique document formats, unique ERP integrations, and unique workflow quirks. The RAG system needs to ingest each manufacturer's pricing history individually. The multi-channel ingestion (email plus WhatsApp) adds integration surface area. Every new customer requires meaningful onboarding work. This is not Notion, where you build once and millions of knowledge workers self-serve. This is closer to Palantir's model — powerful software that requires per-deployment customization. Add the "Contact Sales" motion and enterprise sales cycles in manufacturing, and you're looking at a business that will need to scale headcount roughly in proportion to revenue. That's labor leverage wearing a software costume.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Manufacturing is genuinely under-digitized. Only 35% of procurement teams use AI for RFQs — the adoption window is wide open. If Korso moves fast and locks in a cluster of SMB manufacturers, the accumulated pricing data and customer document history creates real switching costs. The data flywheel could compound: more quotes processed means better pricing recommendations means higher close rates means more manufacturers adopting. Two of three founders share genuine history through Penn Electric Racing and their hometown of Irvine — that's not a "find a co-founder" event, it's an organic relationship. And manufacturing is a sector where most AI teams lack any domain exposure at all; even internship-level experience at GM puts them ahead of pure software engineers attempting this cold. For this to become a great investment, they would need to acquire their first ten customers so fast that the data moat becomes real before the better-funded competitors consolidate the market. Speed of execution, not technology, would be the wedge.

But I don't invest in execution races without a knowledge advantage. When the technology is broadly available, when the opportunity is consensus, and when better-capitalized competitors already have distribution, you need something they can't replicate. Specific knowledge. A founder who understands the problem at a level that takes years to reach. I don't see that here. These are smart, technical young builders who identified a market opportunity — and that's exactly what concerns me. The pitch deck leads with the market, not the obsession. Nobody on this team was automating quotes at 2am because they couldn't stop thinking about how broken the process was. They saw a large TAM and applied their engineering skills to it. That's agent behavior, not principal behavior. Innovation without a moat becomes consumer surplus — the value flows to the manufacturers and eventually to whoever wins the distribution game, which is unlikely to be the team with the least funding and the shallowest customer relationships.
