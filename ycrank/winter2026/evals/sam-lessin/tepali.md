# Tepali -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me immediately: Tepali is tagged "Artificial Intelligence, Fintech, SaaS, B2B, Healthcare" on the YC page. That's five buzzwords and zero structural insight. When I see "AI-native operating system for medspas," I'm seeing the exact archetype I've been publicly arguing against for two years. This is a software tool where the software IS the business, competing against Boulevard ($188M raised, explicitly investing in AI as of their July 2025 Series D), Zenoti (unicorn with 30,000 businesses), Mangomint ($20M revenue), and Vagaro ($66M revenue at $1B valuation). The founders are proposing to out-software well-capitalized software companies using better software. My question is simple: when Boulevard ships its AI features — which it has $80 million earmarked to do — what is Tepali's business?

The sane-person-insane-idea filter fails here, but not because the founders aren't capable. Chrisvin has a solid PJT Partners pedigree and operational experience at Rilla, and Vishnu built all-in-one software for multi-location coffee businesses at Dripos, which is structurally similar to what Tepali is attempting. That's the "sane" part, and it's real. The problem is the "insane" part is missing entirely. "AI-powered vertical SaaS for an underserved niche" is the single most consensus pitch pattern in Y Combinator right now. Nobody thinks this is crazy. Every VC understands the playbook: pick a growing vertical, consolidate fragmented point solutions, add an AI layer, charge per-location SaaS fees. That's not non-consensus — that's the median YC application. The fact that it may have pivoted from "Lotus" to "Tepali" during the batch doesn't help my conviction about depth of commitment to this specific vertical.

My "cherry on top" test is decisive here. Strip away the AI layer and ask: is this a good business without it? What you're left with is a two-person startup building scheduling, CRM, charting, inventory, and payroll software for medspas — competing against five entrenched players with hundreds of millions in funding, thousands of customers, and years of product-market fit. That's not a good business. It's an impossible business. The AI layer is doing all the work in the pitch, which means if incumbents integrate comparable AI capabilities (and Boulevard's $80M raise says they will), Tepali's differentiation evaporates. I've said it publicly and I'll say it here: the winner of the AI race depends on consumer distribution, and the incumbents have distribution. Apple, Google, Meta at the platform level — Boulevard, Zenoti, Vagaro at the vertical level. A startup's "AI-native architecture" advantage has a shelf life measured in quarters, not years.

The bull case, to be fair, has one genuine thread worth pulling: the marketing-to-LTV attribution feature. Connecting ad spend to patient lifetime value across Meta and Google Ads through a unified data model is a real pain point that none of the incumbents natively solve. If Tepali could establish this as a wedge — a narrow, high-value capability that pulls medspa owners into the broader platform — and do it specifically with the wave of new locations opening (20%+ annual growth means thousands of greenfield decisions where there's no incumbent lock-in), there's a version of this that works. Vishnu's Dripos experience building exactly this kind of consolidated platform for multi-location service businesses is the strongest founder-market signal in the dossier. But even in this best case, you're building a vertical SaaS business with a software moat in the era I believe software moats are dissolving. The ceiling is a nice acquisition by one of the incumbents, not an infrastructure-layer outcome.

Nothing about Tepali creates a new primitive. No one builds on top of medspa management software. The TAM for medical spa management software is $338 million growing to $1.3 billion by 2034 — that's a real market but a visible ceiling, not an infinity story. Compare this to Venmo creating a peer-to-peer payment primitive, or Helium building decentralized wireless infrastructure. Those are businesses where success enables an ecosystem. Tepali's success enables... better-run medspas. That's valuable to the medspas, but it doesn't compound. It doesn't create the kind of exponential narrative expansion I need to see at seed. This is a feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent with distribution, not a protocol waiting to be discovered by an ecosystem.
