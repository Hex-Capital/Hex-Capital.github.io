# Korso -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Epicor launched Prism -- an AI agent for automating RFQ workflows inside its own ERP -- sometime in 2026, which tells me something important about where Korso's market sits on the nonobvious-to-consensus spectrum. When the dominant ERP incumbent is already shipping a competing product, the structural window isn't hidden. Leverage AI has raised $14.9M for PO automation, Turian has 17 employees building agentic procurement AI out of Berlin, and Paperless Parts closed a $30M Series B in 2021 for CPQ tooling. The underlying capability shift -- frontier LLMs crossing the accuracy threshold for parsing unstructured manufacturing documents like RFQs and technical drawings -- is real and genuinely unlocks workflows that were stuck for years. That maps to my GPT Ladder framework: specific model capability thresholds opening specific markets. But the dossier also notes 80% of CPOs now name AI a top priority, and multiple funded startups are converging on manufacturing operations AI. Compare this to when I wrote the Coinbase seed check in 2012 -- crypto infrastructure was dismissed by every serious investor. Korso's market is opening, but it's opening visibly. The ERP vendor business model conflict adds a layer of structural insight I find more compelling: SAP, Epicor, and Oracle generate revenue from seat-based licensing on manual workflows, which means automating those workflows with an autonomous agent layer cannibalizes their own economics. That's a real structural tension, similar to why legacy payment processors couldn't build Stripe. But it's not enough to push this market into truly nonobvious territory when the incumbents are already responding.

Korso's infrastructure-layer positioning is the strongest dimension of the pitch. "The Intelligence Layer for Manufacturing" -- ERP-agnostic, sitting across systems rather than replacing them -- is architecturally the right abstraction. Korso claims long-running workflow orchestration with checkpointing for multi-week manufacturing processes, verification layers, dry-run validation, and full audit trails. That's purpose-built infrastructure, not a thin application wrapper around an LLM API. The per-account data accumulation -- pricing history, supplier patterns, customer preferences converted from "tribal knowledge into data" as Korso's thesis page describes it -- creates a compounding data moat per deployment. Once Korso is integrated with a manufacturer's ERP/CRM and trained on their specific workflows, the switching cost is real. This maps to the Gusto pattern from my portfolio: boring operational infrastructure in an unglamorous category where deep workflow integration creates negative churn. Payroll and manufacturing operations share a structural similarity -- both are operationally critical, both involve regulatory complexity (Korso's SOC 2 Type 1 and ISO 27001 certifications in progress signal this), and both attract fewer prestige-seeking founders, which means less competition for a company that does the hard integration work.

Martin Pan's General Motors experience gives Korso direct domain exposure to automotive manufacturing operations and supply chain workflows. Daichi Hiraoka's electrical engineering background at Penn Electric Racing -- where he led redesign of accumulator charger systems and designed circuits/PCBs -- provides hands-on familiarity with manufacturing processes and technical documentation. These are relevant domain credentials. But none of Korso's three co-founders appear to have prior startup founding experience, enterprise software sales backgrounds, or experience operating inside a company during its scaling phase. All three are recent graduates of Penn and UC Irvine. The dossier shows no designated CEO among the three co-founders -- all carry the "Co-founder" title on the YC page. I've said publicly that one of the big myths of Silicon Valley is that every founding relationship needs to be equal, and that you need somebody who can just make a call. Three equal co-founders in a domain that requires selling to conservative manufacturing enterprises, navigating complex ERP integrations, and building enterprise trust -- that's a structural concern. Enterprise manufacturing sales cycles are long and require credibility that's hard to manufacture (no pun intended) without either a track record or a senior hire who's done it before.

The bull case an advocate would make runs like this: Korso sits at the intersection of two of my strongest unstated patterns -- "boring but critical infrastructure" and "AI vertical applications in compliance-heavy industries." Manufacturing operations is a $34B market growing to $155B by 2030. The ERP agnostic positioning is structurally superior to Epicor Prism (locked to one ERP), Paperless Parts (human-operated CPQ, not autonomous), and Tacton (rule-based, not AI-native). The checkpointing architecture for multi-week workflows is technically non-trivial and differentiated. If Korso becomes the default intelligence layer across manufacturing ERPs, it's the Stripe of manufacturing operations -- a horizontal API layer that every manufacturer integrates into rather than builds themselves. The founders traveled across Asia talking directly to clients shortly after YC acceptance, showing the kind of urgency I value. And I've made exceptions before: Anduril required three simultaneous miracles but the structural market opening was generational. But Korso's market opening isn't generational -- it's a normal technology capability threshold being crossed, and multiple competitors have already recognized it. The Anduril exception doesn't apply because the market timing, while real, isn't overwhelming enough to override the multi-challenge path (ERP integration fragmentation + enterprise distribution to conservative buyers + competing against funded incumbents).

Korso's single-miracle viability is mixed. The core technology miracle -- LLMs reliably processing unstructured manufacturing documents -- is happening exogenously, which is favorable. Korso doesn't need to build a foundation model. But ERP integration across heterogeneous systems (SAP, Epicor, Oracle, JobBOSS, each with custom APIs and data models) is a significant engineering challenge that the company itself acknowledges: "Most AI automation breaks down at the ERP integration layer." That's not quite a second miracle, but it's a substantial execution challenge that compounds with the distribution challenge of selling to mid-market manufacturers who historically adopt technology slowly. On margin architecture, no pricing page exists and no revenue data is public, so I'm evaluating blind. The enterprise SaaS model with "Contact Sales" implies high gross margins if the product is software-delivered, and the per-account data accumulation should support strong retention. But with zero public customers, revenue signals, or community presence -- even by pre-seed standards -- there's no signal to calibrate against.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Korso demonstrates two or three live ERP integrations with paying manufacturing customers running autonomous workflows end-to-end, proving the integration layer actually works across heterogeneous systems. That would simultaneously validate the technology, confirm distribution capability, and prove the infrastructure positioning is real rather than aspirational. Without that evidence, Korso is a well-positioned concept in a market that is real but increasingly competitive, built by talented but unproven founders. The infrastructure-layer positioning is the strongest element of Korso's pitch -- it's the right architectural bet in a market where the incumbents are structurally conflicted. But infrastructure positioning without proven ERP integrations is a blueprint, not a moat, and the absence of any visible traction or designated decision-maker among three co-founders keeps this in neutral territory. The decisive dimensions are Nonobvious Market Timing (the market is opening but it's opening visibly to everyone, including Epicor) and Operator-Scaler Founder Profile (domain exposure without scaling experience or enterprise sales credibility).
