# Clam (formerly Baseframe) -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's a company where the idea is exactly as sane as the founders -- and that's the problem. Anshul Paul's experience as founding engineer at HappyRobot, where he led AI evals and enterprise integrations at a company now valued at ~$500M, gives him genuine firsthand knowledge of what breaks when you deploy AI agents in production. Vaibhav Agrawal's work on remote agent orchestration at Augment Code and data infrastructure at Sigma adds relevant systems engineering chops. These are credible people. But "build a security layer for AI agents" is not an insane idea -- it's the most obvious idea in enterprise software right now. Four companies doing approximately this were acquired for $180M-$500M in 2025. Every CISO at every Fortune 500 company is asking for this. When I say I want a sane person with an insane idea, I mean something that makes most investors uncomfortable. AI agent security makes every investor nod along vigorously. Sane person plus sane idea equals a crowded market where you're buying at consensus prices.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart for me. Clam is a pure software product -- a network-level proxy that scans for PII, prompt injection, and malicious code. Each of those capabilities individually is buildable by any competent security team, and open-source tools like LLM Guard already cover parts of the surface. More importantly, this fails my "AI kill test" in the most direct way possible: the very technology creating the security problem will progressively solve it. As AI models get better at following instructions, handling credentials securely, and resisting prompt injection, the gap that Clam fills narrows. Better models are the competitor. Meanwhile, Check Point, Palo Alto Networks, F5, and SentinelOne just spent a combined billion-plus dollars acquiring AI security companies and are integrating these capabilities into their existing enterprise security platforms with massive installed bases. A two-person pre-seed startup going head-to-head against bundled enterprise security suites is not the operational complexity moat I look for -- it's the opposite. There's no painting crew here. There's no physical infrastructure. The software IS the business, and that's the exact category where moats dissolve fastest.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement. The acquisition wave cleared the field of independent players, and incumbent integrations are notoriously slow -- 12-24 months of organizational entropy where product focus gets diluted into platform bundles. There's a real window where developers building on OpenClaw and similar frameworks want a standalone, best-of-breed security solution they can deploy in minutes, not a Palo Alto Networks enterprise procurement cycle. If Clam nails the developer experience and becomes the default "wrap your agent in security" tool for the ecosystem during this window, they could accumulate enough deployment density to become an acquisition target themselves. The "Semantic Firewall" framing -- network-level interception rather than prompt-layer tinkering -- is architecturally distinct and could position them as infrastructure rather than a feature. If the category continues to validate (and the TAM estimates ranging from $260M to $3.2B suggest real market formation), a focused independent player could matter. What would have to be true: OpenClaw and similar frameworks explode in enterprise adoption over the next 18 months, incumbents fumble their integrations, Clam achieves category-defining developer adoption, and native model improvements don't erode the need faster than the market grows. That's a lot of things that have to be true simultaneously.

But even the best case here is a $200-500M acquisition, not an infinity story. Security products are cost centers. They get bundled. The narrative ceiling is "we secure AI agents" -- which is a real market but not an elastic narrative that absorbs new developments and grows without limit. Compare this to Helium, which went from "IoT hotspots" to "decentralized wireless infrastructure" to "a new model for building telecom networks." Each expansion was more ambitious than the last. Clam's expansion path -- from OpenClaw security to multi-framework AI agent security to AI security generally -- narrows toward incumbents at every step rather than opening new territory. The pivot from BaseFrame (hardware design validation) to Clam during the batch adds uncertainty -- this product is weeks old, with no users, no revenue, no pricing page even visible. I give founders credit for having the conviction to pivot, but it means everything about this product is pre-validation.

The dependency on OpenClaw specifically is the kind of platform risk I find most concerning. If I'm building on someone else's infrastructure, I need to be building something that transcends that infrastructure -- a new primitive that other systems integrate with regardless of which AI framework wins. A firewall product that says "Run OpenClaw Securely in Minutes" on its homepage is an application, not a protocol. If OpenClaw ships native security features -- which they will, because every platform does -- Clam's core positioning evaporates. I'd rather fund a company building the underlying security primitives that OpenClaw itself would need to license than a product that wraps OpenClaw in a proxy layer.
