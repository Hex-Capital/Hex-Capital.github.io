# Prescience, Inc. -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Prescience, Inc. is a precocious-founder bet on an optimization-shaped market -- Rishab Jain was America's Top Young Scientist at 13 with two acquired companies behind him, and his brother Aditya walked away from residency after the Harvard M.D. in 2025 to build this thing, which is exactly the founder-defiance signature I look for; the problem is that the success-state Prescience, Inc. is pointing at -- $600 PMPM versus the $1,213 PEO market -- is a 50% price cut on employer health benefits, not a collapse of the PEO category. Strong founders, wrong consequence shape.

**Impossible Foods** (2011) is the right HIGH rung for consequence magnitude because the success-state was the disappearance of an industrial regime -- animal protein production -- not a discount on it. Prescience, Inc. cites the "$4.5T healthcare industry" on its YC page, which is the rhetorical tell of a company describing a market it wants to capture rather than a mechanism it wants to break. The Diamond Plan pitch -- $0 premium, $0 deductible, zero admin fees, $600 PMPM -- is a better mousetrap aimed at the 100-250 employee segment that Sana, Sidecar, Thatch, and SureCo are already aiming at. That is the Outlook Business inversion: Prescience, Inc. is leading with the metric (20-50% savings) and asking the consequence to take care of itself, when my framework requires the opposite. If Prescience, Inc. had written "we make employer-sponsored insurance obsolete by routing every American into individual ICHRA contracts," I would lean in -- collapsing the employer-as-insurance-buyer is a civilizational variable. "Cheaper PEO" is not.

The 30-second pass pattern from my Reference Voice -- "GPT-4 wrapper for sales calls, integrates with HubSpot" -- is the low rung on technical and capital risk, and Prescience, Inc.'s "Prescience Time Machine" described as "an AlphaGo-like system" steering patients toward "Move 37 health trajectories" lands closer to that pattern than I would like; the dossier explicitly notes "no benchmark, dataset disclosure, or peer-reviewed validation has been published." AlphaGo had a Nature paper and a measurable Elo. Prescience, Inc. has marketing language. The actual stack underneath -- LLM care companion, claims routing, ICHRA plumbing, Rippling distribution -- is software plus an insurance carrier partnership the dossier cannot even name. This is not the capital-hostile science that kept consensus VCs out of **QuantumScape** (early, IPO 2020) or **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019); this is the capital-light software profile any Tier-1 generalist can fund, and indeed Index, a16z, General Catalyst, and Koch Disruptive have already funded the adjacent shapes (Thatch $84.5M, Sidecar Health $328M / $1.2B valuation).

Founder-defiance is where Prescience, Inc. genuinely registers on my framework and is the dimension that prevents this from being a 30-second pass. Rishab Jain compounding ICOR Therapeutics and Qurios into a third company by his twenties is a learning-rate signal that maps to the **Slash** (2024) pattern I paid $100M Series C for -- demonstrated capacity to ship and exit, then attack something harder. Aditya Jain publishing the "Why I Didn't Apply to Residency" Doximity piece is a small but real instigator moment of the kind my TED Talk "World Needs 12 Good Instigators" was reaching for -- an M.D. publicly defecting from the prestige conveyor belt is rarer than the dossier makes it sound. Eighteen months from now, if the Jains have parlayed this credibility into a claims-data flywheel from even 5,000 covered lives, the founder dimension compounds into a moat. If they have not, the credibility decays into a Harvard-pedigree pitch deck.

Counterfactually, the consequence-magnitude score flips a tier only if Prescience, Inc. reframes from "operate an employer plan" to "make the employer-as-insurance-buyer obsolete" -- the **Impossible Foods** (2011) move of attacking the regime rather than competing inside it. Nothing in the getprescience.com manifesto or the YC description suggests Prescience, Inc. sees the deal that way; the named target logos -- Series D companies, FAANG, MBB, Goldman/JPM, Frontier Labs -- are buyers inside the existing employer-insurance regime, not evidence the Jains intend to dissolve it.

The capital-match dimension is the cleanest read: Prescience, Inc. at pre-seed with two founders, one of whom finished the M.D. in 2025, needs ~$2-5M to prove the operate-the-plan thesis with a handful of employers. That is a check size where I am structurally over-resourced; the **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) $115M Series A pattern that my fund is built for has no analog here. A $50M Khosla check into Prescience, Inc. today would be malpractice in both directions -- too much for the stage, too little to outflank Sidecar's $328M.

My 36-month forecast: Prescience, Inc. reaches roughly $1-3M ARR on 8-15 small employers, fails to displace Sana or Thatch in the 100-250 employee segment, and is acqui-hired by Rippling -- the partner already on the homepage -- for the AI Care Companion module at a price between $25M and $60M, returning ~2-4x on a seed round and effectively zero on a Khosla-sized check. The mechanism producing this is the dimensional signature itself: strong founders meeting a market whose success-state is a price cut, not a regime collapse, get bought for the team and the AI surface rather than scaling into a category-defining outcome.

The strongest case against my pass is that the Jain brothers are exactly the founder-market-fit profile -- Harvard M.D. plus a serial founder with two exits and ML research output -- that can navigate state insurance licensure, sign a real carrier partner, and turn even modest covered-lives data into a claims-routing model no ICHRA enablement platform has, because Thatch and SureCo deliberately do not operate the plan and therefore do not own the claims. If ICHRA expansion accelerates under the current regulatory tailwind and Prescience, Inc. is the only operator with a credible AI routing stack at 50,000+ lives, the data flywheel compounds into the **Glean** (key AI holding) exception -- domain-expert founders, software business, capital-scale consequence -- and I miss it. That case stings. It loses to the dossier on one point: **Glean** got the exception because its consequence-magnitude on Fortune-500 information asymmetry was independently large; Prescience, Inc.'s consequence is "employer health benefits cost 50% less," which does not clear the bar even if the moat materializes. The one verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a published actuarial study showing risk-adjusted total-cost-of-care reduction across 10,000+ covered lives with a named carrier partner -- absent that, Prescience, Inc. is a credible team building a 30%-better mousetrap in a market where four well-capitalized competitors are already mid-construction.
