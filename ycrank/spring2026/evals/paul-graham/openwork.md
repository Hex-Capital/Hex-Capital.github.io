# OpenWork -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Ben Shafii was rejected by YC ten times. That fact alone tells me more than the entire competitive analysis section of OpenWork's dossier. Ten rejections across what appear to be multiple different products -- Embedbase, Note Companion, Inbox, now OpenWork -- and he kept showing up. Most founders quit after two or three. The question is whether this represents the right kind of stubborn or the wrong kind. In "The Right Kind of Stubborn" I drew the distinction between persistence -- attached to goals, flexible on methods -- and obstinacy -- attached to your initial plan. Ben's trajectory across different-ai suggests persistence: he kept the goal (build something people use) but changed the product four times. That's the running-back quality, looking for openings rather than plowing straight ahead. And he ships -- 77 repos on GitHub, six pinned projects totaling ~18k stars, 1,062 releases on OpenWork alone. This is someone with the animal quality, someone who builds the way other people breathe. But I have no cereal-box stories here. No narrative of a creative workaround under impossible constraints. The dossier shows output volume, not resourcefulness under pressure, and those are different things.

OpenWork launched three days after Anthropic released Claude Cowork. Three days. On one hand, that's impressive execution speed -- Ben clearly had the infrastructure ready and moved fast. On the other hand, it tells me exactly how OpenWork's idea was discovered: by watching Anthropic. The website literally calls itself "the open source alternative to Claude Cowork." That framing is the opposite of organic problem discovery. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he didn't call it "the free alternative to corporate file sync." He built it because he kept forgetting his USB drive. The origin story was a personal frustration that turned out to be universal. OpenWork's origin story is a competitor's product launch that created an obvious gap. Ben Shafii has been building AI developer tools for years -- Embedbase, Obsidian plugins, the progression shows genuine residence in this space -- but OpenWork specifically reads as reactive positioning, not "live in the future, then build what's missing."

14.1k GitHub stars and 1.3k forks in a few months is real community traction, and I should take it seriously. An advocate would point out that OpenWork fits squarely in my "hackers building for hackers" pattern -- open-source developer tools, exactly the category where I backed Replit and Stripe and Retool. The Replit parallel is tempting: I found Amjad on Hacker News building an online IDE, a space that seemed small and nerdy, and sent Sam a gold-plated recommendation. OpenWork similarly targets developers with a tool that makes AI agents more accessible. But the parallel breaks down on a critical dimension. Amjad was obsessed with one problem -- making programming accessible -- for years before Replit existed. His product wasn't positioned as "the open-source alternative to" anything. OpenWork is defined by what it's against (Claude Cowork's $100-200/month pricing, single-vendor lock-in) rather than by a unique vision of what computing should become. "Free alternative to expensive proprietary tool" is the most consensus-friendly pitch in open source. Nobody hears it and says "that will never work." Everyone nods. And when everyone immediately agrees the idea is good, I get nervous, because it means the returns have already been competed away. OpenHands has 65k GitHub stars -- 4.6x OpenWork's traction -- in the same broad category. Eigent and Kuse exist as direct competitors. This is not a space where OpenWork discovered something others couldn't see.

The solo founder problem is one I take seriously, and OpenWork triggers it. The YC page lists one founder: Ben Shafii. Alexandre Shafii appears associated with different-ai and shares the surname, but isn't listed as co-founder. A team of four with one founder running a product that spans desktop app, browser automation, document processing, sales automation, and enterprise features -- that's a broad surface for one person to own the vision. I've written explicitly about why solo founders struggle: you need colleagues to brainstorm with, to talk you out of stupid decisions, and to cheer you up when things go wrong. The breadth of OpenWork's feature set magnifies this concern. Adding browser automation AND document processing AND sales qualification AND enterprise features simultaneously, with four people, suggests either unfocused expansion or a founder who hasn't yet found the core use case that makes everything else unnecessary. Combined with the OpenCode engine dependency -- OpenWork is "powered by OpenCode," an upstream project they don't control -- the technical risk surface is wider than the team can credibly cover.

Ben Shafii's shipping velocity deserves specific credit. 1,800+ commits as of March 2026, 1,062 total releases, stars growing from 12,200 to 14,100 in roughly a month -- this is a founder who iterates fast. The MIT license signals genuine earnestness; he's giving OpenWork away, not optimizing for extraction. His associate's degree from SUPINFO, his 10 YC rejections, his prior $3M+ raise -- none of this screams status-seeking. He builds because that's what he does. The earnest builder signal is real. But earnestness without focus is craftsmanship, not a startup. OpenWork's monetization path -- enterprise sales via cal.com with no pricing page, no public revenue, and an MIT-licensed core -- is the perennial open-source trap. The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: if OpenWork had evidence of even one enterprise customer paying real money, or a user cohort that uses it obsessively rather than just starring the repo, or a founder story that starts with "I was using Claude Cowork and it drove me crazy because..." rather than "Anthropic launched this thing and I built the free version in three days." Any of those would change the calculus. Without them, OpenWork is a competent hacker's well-executed reaction to a market event, not a founder-driven insight that only its creator could see.
