# Cedar -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Cedar's founding team presents one of the strongest co-founder prehistories I have seen at pre-seed: Greg Gunn and Beier Cai have worked together for over a decade across three companies, beginning when Cai wrote the first line of code at Hootsuite and Gunn helped scale the business from five employees to 1,500 and from zero to $200 million in ARR. They then co-founded Commit.dev, a professional network for engineers, spending five years inside the talent-matching problem before launching Cedar. This is precisely the kind of deep shared intensity I require — co-founders who have survived hypergrowth, a prior startup, and now a third venture together possess alignment that no accelerator matchmaking can manufacture. The Commit.dev experience, however, cuts in two directions: it demonstrates domain immersion, but it also suggests that Cedar is a second attempt at the professional graph problem, now with AI agents as the extraction mechanism. A founder executing a definite plan does not typically require two companies to find the right angle of attack.

The canonical question — "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — reveals Cedar's structural weakness. The claimed secret is that the most valuable hiring data lives inside employees' heads rather than on LinkedIn, and that AI agents can systematically extract it through 15-minute conversations to build a proprietary relationship graph. But the first half of this proposition is pure consensus: every head of talent acquisition at every company with more than 500 employees already knows that referrals produce the best hires. Stripe's reported $10 million annual investment in referral programs does not represent a market failing to recognize the value of referrals — it represents a market that already values referrals enormously and is actively spending against the problem. The second half — that LLM-driven conversational extraction is the right mechanism — is a tactical insight about AI capability thresholds, not a contrarian truth about the world. Perfect raised $23 million for agentic AI sourcing. ERIN, Teamable, and Boon each attack the referral workflow from adjacent angles. Moonhub was acquired by Salesforce for its AI recruiter. When I see five companies in a competitive landscape table, I see five companies that have all failed to escape competition. The dossier itself states that "the core technology (LLM-driven conversational extraction) is not proprietary, but the dataset is" — which is an admission that the moat depends entirely on accumulation speed, not on any structural barrier to replication.

The "killing LinkedIn" framing deserves scrutiny. LinkedIn's professional graph encompasses over a billion members and generates $15 billion in annual revenue backed by Microsoft's infrastructure. Cedar's claimed insight — that LinkedIn cannot build proactive referral extraction because it would cannibalize Recruiter seat revenue and antagonize enterprise customers — has some structural validity. Business model conflicts are real; Clayton Christensen built an entire framework around incumbents' inability to disrupt themselves. But LinkedIn is not the only threat. Any well-funded AI recruiting startup could replicate the 15-minute call format within months. The proprietary graph creates a time-based data advantage, not a structural monopoly. This is the Wish pattern in miniature — entering a market dominated by a massive incumbent, competing with multiple funded alternatives, and relying on execution speed rather than a genuine technological discontinuity that renders the existing approach obsolete.

The $2.5 million to $3 million in contracted revenue from a four-person stealth team is the single strongest signal in Cedar's dossier, and I take it seriously. Combined with advisors who are precisely the enterprise buyers Cedar targets — Laszlo Bock (former Google SVP People Ops), Tiffany Fenster (former Stripe Head of Recruiting), Brendan Browne (former LinkedIn VP Talent Acquisition) — this suggests that Cedar has solved initial distribution through network-driven enterprise sales. The founders are not building a product and hoping customers appear; they are selling into a network they have spent a decade cultivating. Distribution excellence of this kind can sustain a profitable business. But superior distribution alone does not answer my fundamental objection: superior sales in a competitive market produces a good company, not a monopoly, and the power law demands monopolies.

The bull case requires believing that Cedar's proprietary relationship graph achieves escape velocity — that once enough enterprises have mapped their employees' networks, the cross-company graph density creates a network effect so powerful that no competitor can replicate it, and that this graph eventually becomes the de facto professional identity layer, displacing LinkedIn not through frontal assault but through concentric expansion from the referral wedge. If the graph compounds across customers in the way Facebook's social graph compounded across campuses, Cedar could become the infrastructure layer for professional trust. But Facebook's network effect was constitutive — the graph WAS the product, and each user made it more valuable for every other user. Cedar's graph is derivative — extracted from employees who already maintain LinkedIn profiles and professional relationships independent of Cedar. The data enriches Cedar's matching capability, but it does not create the self-reinforcing user engagement loop that produces winner-take-all dynamics. The AI recruitment market ceiling of $1.2 billion by 2033 further constrains the power-law math; even monopolizing this category entirely would not produce fund-returner returns. Cedar would need to transcend recruitment and become something much larger — a possibility the founders may envision but have not articulated.

Cedar is a well-executed enterprise software company built by experienced operators with genuine domain expertise, strong distribution networks, and impressive early traction. By Paul Graham's criteria — founder determination, earnestness, domain insight — this team scores exceptionally. By mine, the company lacks the structural ingredients I require: a genuinely contrarian secret that the market disputes, a clear path to monopoly rather than competitive advantage, power-law return magnitude, and any physical-world technology barrier. The two dimensions most decisive for my verdict are Monopoly Secret — where the crowded competitive landscape and consensus nature of the referral insight score Cedar low — and Power Law Return Magnitude — where the $1.2 billion market ceiling prevents the extreme outcome that makes concentrated bets worthwhile.
