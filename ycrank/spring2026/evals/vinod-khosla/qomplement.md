# qomplement -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$200,000 to train a proprietary CUA model. That number tells me almost everything I need to know about qomplement's position. OpenAI, Anthropic, and BrowserUse — which already has 50,000+ GitHub stars — are all building computer-use agent capabilities as features of platforms backed by billions in compute. qomplement spent what amounts to a rounding error on a frontier lab's monthly AWS bill to build the same core mechanism. The local-first architecture is a genuine architectural choice worth respecting, but the underlying capability — an agent that clicks through desktop applications — is being commoditized in real time. When Anthropic ships computer use as a standard API feature, qomplement's technical differentiation evaporates. I've lived through this movie before: a startup builds something genuinely useful, then a platform player absorbs the capability and distributes it to a billion users for free.

"AI Agents for Document Filling." I read that on qomplement's YC page and my reaction is immediate: so what? A transportation company automated 10,000 invoices per month and saved $15,000. That's a real savings for that company, and I don't dismiss it. But the consequence of qomplement succeeding at planetary scale is that enterprises process invoices faster. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No basic human need becomes radically cheaper. No new category of human capability emerges. qomplement is redistributing existing enterprise spend — taking money that goes to data entry clerks and routing it to software subscriptions. That's the kind of outcome I describe when I explain what I pass on: a moderately profitable SaaS company. I want my money back 50 times or I want to lose it. qomplement is designed to generate the 3-5x return that's perfectly fine for most investors but represents a waste of my time and attention.

Kerim Taray and Andrés Garza García both attended Tecnológico de Monterrey, and both are described as "former CTOs at high-growth startups" by Semilla Ventures — but no startups are named, no scale is described, no evidence of what they built or learned. qomplement's product positioning has shifted from "OS-level AI agent for legacy software automation" to "AI Agents for Document Filling" to "Turn invoices and POs into system-ready data." That evolution could signal rapid learning and market discovery — exactly what I look for — or it could signal confusion about what the product actually is. With no public writing, no visible GitHub presence, no social media traction (16 Twitter followers), I have no way to assess their learning rate. And both founders share overlapping backgrounds — same university, same described role — which triggers my gene pool concern. A company becomes the people it hires, and a founding team optimized for familiarity over complementary capabilities starts with a compromised gene pool.

The bull case an advocate would make: qomplement's local-first, OS-level approach creates a privacy moat in regulated industries — healthcare, finance, transportation — where Rossum and Extend AI cannot go because they require cloud data transfer. The 15x cost advantage over cloud-based agents ($3 per interaction versus qomplement's local execution) could open a massive market of mid-market companies that Rossum ($114.5M raised, $44.9M ARR) serves at enterprise pricing. LatAm has enormous legacy-system prevalence, giving qomplement first-mover advantage in a region that frontier AI companies neglect. And the DoorDash exception applies — Tony Xu's company also lacked technical novelty but scaled on consequence magnitude and founder velocity. Here's why the DoorDash exception doesn't apply: DoorDash changed food access for hundreds of millions of people. qomplement changes document processing for enterprise accounting departments. The human impact differential is categorical, not marginal. The privacy moat is real but narrow — it only matters until frontier models offer on-device inference, which Apple, Google, and Qualcomm are actively shipping. The 15x cost advantage is meaningful today but built on a $200K model that will be outperformed by open-weight alternatives within 18 months.

qomplement's strongest dimension is radical affordability — 15x cheaper than cloud alternatives is a concrete, meaningful cost reduction, and I respect that the local architecture genuinely removes infrastructure costs that competitors carry. If qomplement demonstrated that this cost structure could make document processing effectively free for millions of small businesses globally — if the framing were "every corner shop in Mexico can afford AI back-office operations" — I'd lean differently. But the positioning targets enterprise departments, not radical democratization. qomplement is selling efficiency to companies that can already afford alternatives, not unlocking capability for those who can't.

One condition would flip me: if qomplement showed me that their OS-level agent generalizes beyond document filling to automating any workflow across any legacy desktop application — and that this generalization creates a platform with network effects where each automated workflow improves all others — that's a different consequence magnitude. That's replacing the need for enterprise software modernization entirely. Show me three enterprise customers using qomplement for three fundamentally different workflow categories with expanding usage, and the consequence calculation changes. Until then, qomplement is a competent document processing startup in a market where Rossum has $44.9M ARR and BrowserUse has $17M in seed funding. The consequence of success is insufficient, and the technical approach is being commoditized by players with three orders of magnitude more capital.
