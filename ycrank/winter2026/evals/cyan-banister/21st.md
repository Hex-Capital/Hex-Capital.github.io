# 21st -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about 21st.dev is that it's building in the single hottest category in technology right now. AI developer tools. Every fund in San Francisco has a thesis deck with "AI infrastructure" on slide three. v0.dev has $42M in ARR and Vercel's $863 million behind it. CopilotKit claims Fortune 500 adoption. There are new MCP servers and agent SDKs launching every week. When I look at a company, one of the first questions I ask myself is: would most investors pass on this? Would they find it weird, hard to assess, uncomfortable? With 21st.dev, the answer is no — this is exactly the kind of company that gets oversubscribed at demo day. That's not a compliment in my framework. The most promising companies I've backed — SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline — were ones that made other investors squirm. This one makes them salivate. And when everyone's salivating, the price is wrong and the insight has already been commoditized.

I want to know the founder's story before I look at anything else — how did Serafim Korablev end up here? He studied at Moscow State Academy of Physical Culture, which tells me he's probably self-taught in engineering, and I have deep respect for that. I taught myself to code at public libraries in Arizona. But then I look at the trajectory: Via Protocol (crypto routing, $1.5B transaction volume, shut down), GasPump (memecoin launchpad, acquired), Cutly (AI video editor, acquired), Rork AI, and now 21st.dev — nine products in ten months. That's extraordinary shipping velocity, and I don't want to dismiss it. But when I ask myself "why is THIS founder solving THIS problem," I can't find the biographical thread. The pattern I see is someone who's brilliant at riding waves — crypto when crypto was hot, memecoins during that window, AI tools now. That's a different archetype from what I invest in. I back founders who've been circling a specific problem for years, who can't NOT work on it because it's woven into who they are. Travis Kalanick's fury at the medallion system predated Uber by years. The Crusoe founders had an insight about waste gas that came from studying thermodynamics, not from market timing. I don't feel that biographical inevitability here.

Sergey Bunas is the more compelling founder story for me — senior engineer at Deel, built Suggesty which hit #1 on Product Hunt, created Replai. There's a genuine developer tools thread in his background. But Suggesty had 3K users and Replai had $12K ARR — these are side projects, not obsessions. And Eyas Kelani's situation gives me pause: he's listed as co-founder on Crunchbase but not on the YC page. That ambiguity around a founding team member is exactly the kind of thing I probe hard on after HQ Trivia. Three co-founders with no apparent prior working relationship, navigating a company that's already spread across four distinct products — component registry, Magic MCP, Agents SDK, 1Code — that's a lot of surface area for a team of three to cover without fracturing.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because it's real: 1.4 million developers have touched this product, 200K use it monthly, and the combined GitHub stars exceed 14K. The top-of-funnel strategy is clever — free component registry pulls developers in, then the agent infrastructure layer monetizes them. The MCP ecosystem positioning is smart, and being listed on six-plus aggregator sites creates organic discovery. If the MCP protocol becomes the standard interface between AI IDEs and tools, early positioning there could matter the way early App Store positioning mattered in 2008. And the team's ability to ship nine products in ten months, including two that were acquired, demonstrates execution capability that many pre-seed teams would envy. If I believed that velocity and distribution alone win markets, this would be a higher conviction bet.

But what I keep coming back to is that this company doesn't activate any of the signals I've learned to trust over 130-plus investments. It doesn't create economic access for underserved populations — it serves already well-compensated developers. It doesn't make an invisible system visible or transparent. It doesn't operate in a regulated or stigmatized space where complexity creates a moat — open-source developer tools have essentially zero regulatory barriers, which means zero regulatory moat. And it doesn't feel weird or unpopular or hard to assess. It feels like exactly what a smart, fast-moving technical team would build in 2026 if they were optimizing for what the market wants right now. There are plenty of investors who are great at evaluating that kind of bet — investors who prize execution speed, distribution metrics, and market timing. I'm just not one of them. I'm looking for the companies that make people uncomfortable, and this one makes people excited. Different signal entirely.
