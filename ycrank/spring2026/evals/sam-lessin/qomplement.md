# qomplement -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

$200,000 spent training a proprietary Computer Using Agent model — and that's supposed to be qomplement's moat. Let me put that in perspective: OpenAI and Anthropic are each spending billions building the exact same capability as a core feature of their frontier models. Anthropic literally ships "computer use" as a product. OpenAI has Operator. Microsoft has Copilot agents that interact with desktop applications. qomplement's $200K training run is a rounding error in the R&D budgets of companies building this as a platform primitive. This is the single most important fact in the dossier, and it's the one that kills the deal for me before anything else matters. Every time someone pitches me "we built the thing that OpenAI is building," I know how that movie ends.

BrowserUse has 50,000 GitHub stars and $17M in seed funding. Rossum has $114.5M raised and $44.9M ARR. Extend AI is cash-flow positive on $17M. qomplement is entering one of the most consensus-hot categories in enterprise AI — document processing with agentic automation. Every other VC at every demo day is excited about AI agents right now. Where's the mispricing? The entire thesis of my approach is that the risk needs to be in the price — you need the market to be wrong about the category for asymmetric upside to exist. The market is not wrong about AI agents for document processing. The market is extremely right about it, which is exactly why Rossum has nearly $45M in revenue and BrowserUse has 50K stars. qomplement isn't contrarian — it's late to a consensus trade. The LatAm enterprise wedge is real as a go-to-market tactic, but a geographic wedge in a hot category doesn't create the kind of narrative mispricing I need to get excited.

Kerim Taray and Andrés Garza García both graduated from Tec de Monterrey — Mexico's strongest engineering school — and are described as "former CTOs from high-growth startups" by Semilla Ventures, though no specific companies are named. Kerim calls himself a "contrarian" on his personal site. Here's my problem with the sanity-insanity calibration: qomplement's idea isn't insane. "AI agents that automate document entry in legacy software" is the most obvious, consensus-validated application of CUA technology. It's exactly what every enterprise AI pitch deck says. For the sane-insane ratio to work, I need a very rational person doing something that makes smart people say "that's impossible." Nobody hears "we automate invoice processing with AI" and thinks "that's fucking crazy." They think "obviously."

qomplement's one genuinely interesting angle — local-first, on-device execution compatible with 8-16 GB RAM machines for regulated industries where cloud data transfer is restricted — is a legitimate positioning choice, and I'll give it credit. The privacy-oriented architecture does create a wedge with healthcare and financial services customers who can't ship data to the cloud. The customer testimonial — 10,000+ invoices automated monthly, $15,000/month cost savings for a transportation company — shows the product works and delivers measurable ROI. But local-first is a deployment architecture, not a structural moat. As smaller open-weight models improve (and they are improving every quarter), any competitor can offer local inference. The $60K/month AWS infrastructure burn against undisclosed pre-seed funding means qomplement is in a race against commoditization with limited runway — standard YC $500K buys roughly 8 months of infrastructure alone.

Now, the best bull case I can construct: qomplement operates at the OS/desktop level, not just browser, which differentiates from BrowserUse and most CUA competitors. Legacy desktop applications in LatAm enterprises — SAP GUI, old ERP systems, custom Windows apps — represent a genuinely underserved market where cloud-native solutions can't reach. The 15x cost advantage over cloud models ($3 per interaction cited in Expansión) could matter enormously in price-sensitive LatAm markets. If frontier CUA models remain unreliable on complex desktop interactions for another 2-3 years, qomplement's domain-specific training data from actual enterprise deployments could compound into a real accuracy advantage. Does any Known Exception apply? Slack overrode my consensus mispricing concern because it was creating a genuinely new behavioral primitive. But qomplement isn't creating a new primitive — it's applying an existing one (CUA) to an existing workflow (document processing). Clubhouse overrode structural durability for a new social primitive — again, no new primitive here. The bull case doesn't hold because the core technology is on every frontier lab's roadmap, and the LatAm wedge doesn't create the kind of fundamental mispricing that would make me comfortable with that risk. The one condition that flips me: if qomplement showed me $50K+ MRR with 90%+ retention across 10+ enterprise accounts and a clear demonstration that frontier CUA models fail at their specific legacy desktop workflows, I'd reconsider — that would be evidence the commoditization timeline is longer than I think.

qomplement scores low on the two dimensions that matter most to me. Consensus mispricing is near-zero — this is an AI agent company in the hottest AI agent category, competing against players with 10-100x more capital. Structural durability fails the AI kill test directly — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Microsoft are literally building this exact capability into their platforms. The founders seem competent but the dossier lacks the specificity I need to score the sane-insane ratio above default. Pass.
