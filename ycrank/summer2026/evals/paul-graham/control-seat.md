# Control Seat -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Control Seat is a right-problem company with unproven-animal founders -- the highest dimension is the schlep (SCADA, IEC 62443, Allen-Bradley/Siemens/Beckhoff PLC integration, plant deployment), the lowest is hacker genealogy (Grodnick's GitHub has no notable stars, Shepard has no public repos). That pairing tells me more than the total score does. Two Dartmouth CS '26 grads with SpaceX/Tesla internships have correctly identified a zone of unpleasant work that Inductive Automation has been bootstrapping inside for two decades. What I don't yet see from Jack Grodnick and Warren Shepard is the cereal-box moment.

On the schlep axis, **Coinbase** (2012) is the load-bearing analogy: Brian Armstrong's appetite for bitcoin compliance grind was the moat, not the wallet UI. Control Seat is reaching for an equivalent moat -- AVEVA Wonderware and Siemens WinCC are decades-old desktop stacks defended by systems integrators, and the dossier's mention of critical-infrastructure regulation and IEC 62443 exposure is exactly the kind of overhead that scares software-only YC applicants out of the room. Where Control Seat diverges from the Coinbase pattern is that Armstrong arrived at YC having already built and shipped an Android Bitcoin wallet against his own rejection; Control Seat's public artifact is a launch video titled "Real-time internal dashboards" and a website offering a "competitive price guarantee" against integrator quotes. The schlep is correctly chosen. I have no evidence yet that Control Seat has personally bled inside it.

The lowest rung on the hacker-genealogy ladder is the MBA-AI-wrapper team from my Pass Example -- credentialed, no GitHub trail, hires engineers post-funding. Control Seat doesn't sit on that rung, but the dossier's silence here is louder than it should be for a company claiming "10k+ tags/sec, <100ms latency." Warren Shepard is Dartmouth's 2026 salutatorian with a mechanistic-interpretability research interest, which is the credential version of the signal I want; what I actually want is the Patrick Collison Young Scientist Lisp prize, the Aaron Swartz RSS specification, the Drew Houston Hacker News demo. Control Seat's founders have résumés where I look for tracks. Hackers leave GitHub trails, and Shepard's absence of public repos for a CS/Math salutatorian about to ship a real-time time-series platform is the kind of asymmetry I notice.

Founder-market fit on Control Seat is real but secondhand -- both founders interned at SpaceX, which runs custom industrial telemetry at volume, and Grodnick adds Tesla manufacturing exposure. That is closer to **Coinbase** (2012)'s Armstrong-from-Airbnb-engineering than to **Dropbox** (2007)'s Houston-forgetting-his-USB-stick. They've stood next to the problem; they have not personally suffered it as plant operators. Over 18 months this dimension compounds only if Grodnick and Shepard go live inside an actual plant the way Chesky and Gebbia photographed **Airbnb** (2009) listings door to door -- otherwise the SpaceX line on the resume decays into pattern matching by month nine.

On animal resourcefulness pre-funding, what would have to be true for Control Seat to flip a tier here is a named pilot -- not "in talks with" but a signed deployment at a specific mid-market manufacturer Grodnick personally drove a truck to. The dossier reports incorporation in January 2026, YC's $500K standard deal, zero open jobs, no Product Hunt, no Twitter, no revenue figure. That is the dossier of a company that got into YC on interview quality and credential stack. It is not yet the dossier of an animal. The default for absent evidence applies.

Co-founder configuration on Control Seat is one notch above neutral -- two Dartmouth classmates who both passed through SpaceX and incorporated together six months before YC. Compared to **Stripe** (2010)'s Collison brothers, who had been hacking commerce together since they were teenagers and had already exited **Auctomatic** (2007) by ages 19 and 17, Grodnick and Shepard are a credible academic pair without the years-of-shipping-together that makes the configuration load-bearing. Real, not decisive.

My 36-month forecast on Control Seat: $1-3M ARR by mid-2029, weighted toward services revenue from a handful of mid-market plant deployments, stuck competing against Ignition's ~$15K/server unlimited-tag licensing and unable to break into AVEVA's Fortune-100 base; outcome is either a vertical pivot (oil & gas remote telemetry is the obvious one) or a sub-$40M acquihire by Rockwell or AVEVA defending channel. Not a zero. Not a fund-returner. The mechanism is that SCADA replacement cycles span years, Inductive Automation took roughly two decades to bootstrap to ~$41M ARR with 373 employees, and Control Seat's two-person team enters that grind without the pre-funding animal evidence that would convince me they outlast the sales cycle.

The strongest bear case I have to dismantle is actually the bull case: Tulip Interfaces just hit unicorn status with a $120M raise in January 2026 in adjacent MES, the LLM-over-tag-data "why now" is genuine, and ex-SpaceX engineers may know more OT than the next ten YC applicants combined -- this could be the **Coinbase** (2012) of industrial control. The dossier evidence that wins against that bull case is the absence: no named customer, no pre-funding revenue stunt, no GitHub trail commensurate with the technical claims, and a services-heavy pricing posture ("competitive price guarantee" to beat integrator quotes) that signals Control Seat is already accepting integrator-margin economics rather than software-margin economics. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Control Seat names a signed deployment at a mid-market manufacturer with a real tag count and an unscalable story about how Grodnick or Shepard personally got it installed. Until then this is a neutral.
