# Maquoketa Research -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the story before anything else does. Anduril at $14B+ valuation. Helsing at €12B. AeroVironment with $990M in Army contracts. Stark and Neros each with $100M+ raised and active military procurement wins. This is not a market looking for its first credible startup -- it's a market where five well-capitalized players already hold contracts, have cleared ITAR hurdles, and are scaling production. When I backed Anduril, the structural vacancy was real: Google's Project Maven cancellation had created a defense AI gap with zero credible startup competitors. That vacancy no longer exists. Maquoketa is entering a market where the "market-ending move" dynamic may already be in play -- incumbents and well-funded startups have compounding distribution advantages through existing procurement relationships, security clearances, and production infrastructure. A 5-person pre-seed team competing against organizations with 100-1000x their capital isn't automatically disqualifying in software, where a small team can build something structurally better overnight. In hardware defense, where manufacturing scale, facility clearances, and multi-year procurement cycles are table stakes, the gap is much harder to close.

The structural moment in loitering munitions is undeniable -- Ukraine proved these are primary weapon systems, Germany awarded €9B+ to startups over legacy primes, the U.S. PBAS program is actively seeking vendors. But undeniable is the problem. This is consensus hot. Every defense-focused VC fund has this thesis. When I talk about non-obvious markets -- PagerDuty when operations alerting seemed boring, Coinbase when crypto seemed speculative -- I mean markets where the structural shift is visible to someone tracking adoption curves but invisible to the broader investor community. Loitering munitions in 2026 are the opposite: the inflection point is on the front page of every defense publication. The TAM estimates vary by an order of magnitude ($640M to $4B for 2025), which normally signals a market that's hard to size -- but in this case, every estimate is growing rapidly and the directional bet is obvious. Non-obvious markets are where asymmetric returns live. This market offers returns, but not asymmetric ones for a late entrant.

The CTO's phased array and RF design background from Berkeley Wireless Research Center is the strongest signal in the dossier and the reason I didn't dismiss this immediately. Phased array expertise is genuinely rare at the pre-seed level -- it's the kind of specialized capability that takes years to develop and can't be replicated by a software team bolting autonomy onto a commercial airframe. If Maquoketa's differentiation is EW-resistant guidance that performs in GPS-denied, jammed environments where commercial FPV drones fail, that's a real technical moat. The dossier's inference that this distinguishes them from FPV providers like Neros is plausible. But I'm applying my single-miracle test and counting at least three sequential breakthroughs required: build a technically superior autonomous system (hard), navigate defense procurement to land initial contracts without existing relationships or clearances (hard), and scale manufacturing to fulfill those contracts at competitive unit economics (hard). When I see a plan that requires multiple miracles, I pass -- compounding small odds means you're very likely to fail.

The strongest bull case: defense isn't winner-take-all. Germany gave contracts to both Helsing AND Stark. The U.S. military explicitly designs programs like PBAS for multiple vendors -- they want supply diversity. AeroVironment's production bottleneck (new factory not operational until late 2026/early 2027) creates a literal supply gap. The market may need 10+ credible suppliers, not 3. If Maquoketa can land one OTA or SBIR contract -- and YC's defense network plus Harj Taggar's connections could facilitate that -- the procurement flywheel starts turning. Stark won a €268M German contract despite failing all four test strikes, which suggests procurement relationships and production credibility matter as much as technical perfection. A team with genuinely superior RF and autonomy technology could find openings even in a crowded field. For this bull case to work, though, I'd need to see some evidence the team is moving -- a prototype, a DoD contact, a letter of intent, even a working website. The complete absence of any public signal is concerning not because pre-seed companies need traction, but because defense startups need to demonstrate they're navigating the procurement ecosystem. Zero signals at the most basic level -- the website returns a 403 -- makes it hard to assess whether this team is executing or still in the lab.

I keep coming back to the "software-driven versus software-aware" distinction, adapted for hardware. The question for any defense drone startup is: are you building a technology company that happens to make drones, or a drone company that happens to use technology? Anduril's Lattice operating system is software-driven -- the autonomy stack is the product, and the hardware is a delivery mechanism. If Maquoketa's phased array and autonomous guidance capabilities are genuinely software-driven, with proprietary algorithms that improve with deployment data and create switching costs, there's a path to defensibility. If they're building a better airframe with off-the-shelf autonomy, they're competing on hardware margins against players with massive manufacturing advantages. The dossier doesn't have enough technical detail to distinguish between these scenarios, and that ambiguity is itself a problem at the stage where the thesis needs to be crisp.
