# Galactic Resource Utilization Space, Inc. (GRU Space) -- Paul Graham Evaluation

This company requires not one miracle but at least five: ISRU brick-making must work on actual lunar regolith at scale, launch vehicles must hit cost and schedule targets, life-support systems must sustain guests on the Moon's surface, a legal framework for lunar commerce must materialize, and billions in capital must flow to a pre-revenue company over seven-plus years. I've written that when a startup needs multiple simultaneous breakthroughs, the probability collapses multiplicatively. Each miracle is independently uncertain. Together they make the 2032 hotel timeline something closer to science fiction than a startup plan.

The first thing I look for is how the founders found the problem. Did it grow from their own life, or did they sit down and try to think of the most impressive startup idea? Skyler Chan is clearly talented -- Berkeley EECS early graduate, pilot at 16, Tesla engineer, built a NASA 3D printer that went to space. But nothing in his background suggests he stumbled into lunar habitation through organic experience. The launch timing coincides precisely with the Trump-Isaacman Moon base announcement in January 2026. The company explicitly cited that announcement as spurring their public launch. This looks like an idea manufactured to ride a political tailwind, not one that emerged from years of living with a problem. The reservation page collecting $250K deposits for a hotel that won't exist until 2032 -- with the Trump family getting a Moon brick -- is the kind of thing that generates press, not the kind of thing that demonstrates genuine demand. Fifteen publications picked up the story because "Moon hotel" is inherently interesting to editors, not because users are pulling the product toward them.

I want to be honest about the bull case, because there's a version of this story that's compelling. Getting Dr. Kevin Cannon to leave his tenured position at Colorado School of Mines -- one of the world's foremost experts on lunar regolith ISRU -- to join a two-person startup is a genuinely impressive signal. Cannon isn't someone you recruit with a PowerPoint. His presence means the core science has credibility. If NASA's Moon base plans accelerate, GRU could position itself as the commercial habitation partner in a way that captures enormous government contract value. The niche is genuinely unoccupied -- ICON does infrastructure, Axiom and Vast do LEO stations, nobody is combining ISRU construction with surface hospitality. And if Starship actually delivers on its cost-per-kilogram promises, the economics shift dramatically. There's a world where this becomes the lunar equivalent of a Stripe-for-payments: the thing that was obviously needed but nobody wanted to tackle. In that world, Chan's audacity at 22 looks prescient rather than naive.

But that world requires every domino to fall in sequence over seven years, and the current evidence doesn't support confidence that this team can navigate that gauntlet. The solo founder problem is severe. I forced Drew Houston to find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox, and Dropbox was a software product one person could build in a dorm room. Building a Moon hotel is orders of magnitude more complex. Cannon is employee number one, not a co-founder -- the equity alignment and long-term commitment structure is different. Chan is 22 with a mission roadmap stretching to age 29 at minimum. The intensity of capital fundraising alone -- from $500K to the billions required -- will consume enormous founder bandwidth, and doing that as a solo founder while simultaneously managing the most technically ambitious construction project in human history is a setup for either burnout or dilution into irrelevance.

The deeper issue is that this isn't schlep blindness. Schlep blindness is when a problem is obviously solvable but nobody tackles it because the work is tedious and unglamorous -- dealing with banks for Stripe, photographing apartments for Airbnb, delivering food for DoorDash. The reason nobody is building a Moon hotel isn't unconscious avoidance of boring work. It's conscious recognition that the enabling technologies don't yet exist at the required scale. No entity has manufactured anything from lunar regolith on the Moon. GRU has demonstrated their geopolymer process only with simulated regolith on Earth. ICON, with five hundred million in funding and a NASA contract, hasn't managed on-surface construction either. This is a frontier science problem masquerading as a startup, and the gap between a 10-kilogram demo payload in 2029 and a functioning hotel with life support for four guests in 2032 is not a schlep -- it's a chasm.

I admire the ambition. Frighteningly ambitious ideas do face less competition precisely because they scare people off. But my Boom Supersonic investment -- my largest personal check -- was in a company attacking a problem where the underlying physics had already been proven by Concorde. The question there was execution and economics, not feasibility. Here, feasibility itself remains unproven, the founder is solo, and the path to even a minimal proof point requires years and capital that dwarf what a YC-stage company can access. This is a pass.
