# Klarify -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Klarify is what happens when a repeat-founder who shipped AI meeting notes at Circleback (YC W24, $2.5M seed) goes looking for the next vertical and lands on therapists -- a real schlep zone discovered by pattern-match rather than personal pain. Moody Abdul has no clinical background and neither does Alexander Bergholm; the dossier shows zero hours spent as a therapist, in a therapist's office, or married to one. So Klarify scores highest on the dimension I usually like (the work is genuinely unpleasant -- HIPAA + PIPEDA + PHIPA, 20+ note formats, CPT code extraction for insurance claims) and lowest on the one I usually like more (the founders did not live the problem). That pairing is the analytical signature.

The closest portfolio analog on Klarify's strongest axis is **Coinbase** (2012). Brian Armstrong's edge was not the wallet -- it was his appetite for the bitcoin compliance grind that every other YC applicant in his batch was routing around. Klarify's analog is "extracts relevant codes and completes insurance claims" plus tri-jurisdiction privacy compliance plus an EMDR/IFS/EFT/ACT format library that nobody builds because nobody wants to learn what BIRP and DAP even stand for. Where Klarify diverges from the Coinbase rung is that Armstrong went toward a schlep nobody had touched; Klarify is entering a schlep that Upheal ($14.35M raised, 70K providers) and Blueprint ($9M Series A, 1M+ sessions) have been grinding through for three years. The schlep is real but it isn't lonely, and a schlep moat only works when you're alone in the tunnel.

The pass example I keep on file is the polished AI-wrapper team that "spotted a market opportunity" -- product distance from founder biography is large, market-sizing leads the deck. Klarify's YC Launch page cites a "$40B US outpatient mental health market" and Moody's prior company built AI notes for meetings, not for therapy. The failure mechanism of the pass example is that when the product needs to turn -- when group practices demand SimplePractice write-back, when outcome-measurement becomes the wedge -- the founders without organic pain don't know which way to turn because they were never the customer. That pattern is present in Klarify's dossier: no founder has personally sat through a therapy session as a clinician, no founder forgot a CPT code at 11pm the way Drew Houston forgot his USB stick. The format breadth is impressive precisely because somebody had to research what those formats are -- which is the tell.

Klarify's resourcefulness dimension compounds well over 18 months if the 309,121 notes generated and the 2,154-to-6,000 therapist range represent real free-tier usage rather than signup vanity. Moody has the LinkedIn enterprise AE muscle ($20M in contracts) and Circleback gave him reps on AI-notes go-to-market; Bergie has shipped LLM infrastructure at Workday. This is not soft founders. But the dossier's traction inconsistency (2,154 on klarify.ca vs. ~6,000 on YC) and the absence of any disclosed paying-customer count means the animal moment is a self-serve flywheel claim, not a cereal-box stunt -- and I weight stunts higher than dashboards.

The counterfactual on hacker genealogy: for this score to flip a tier, Bergie's GitHub @bergholmalexander would need to surface a load-bearing open-source artifact -- the kind of thing Aaron Swartz brought to **Reddit** (2005) -- rather than a UBC degree and a Workday job title. The CV patent and the Finnish Defence Forces deep-learning research are real signals of technical seriousness, but they're credentials, not shipped hacker artifacts I can read. One technical cofounder plus one enterprise sales cofounder is a real configuration; it's just not the Collison-brothers configuration where both founders could rewrite the parser.

On co-founder configuration, the **Stripe** (2010) parallel doesn't quite hold for Klarify: the Collisons had been hacking commerce together since **Auctomatic** (2007), a multi-year shared-context relationship I could verify. Klarify's dossier explicitly says "no public data on shared prior employer or university" for Moody and Bergie. Four-person team, two named founders, met somewhere I can't see. The YC interview surfaces this in person; the dossier leaves it neutral.

My 36-month forecast: Klarify reaches $3-6M ARR by mid-2029 and is acquired by SimplePractice, TherapyNotes, or Headspace Health at a 4-6x revenue multiple, because the schlep moat is real enough to build a business but the organic-pain gap means the next product turn -- group-practice EHR write-back, outcome-measurement, supervision workflows -- gets won by Blueprint or Upheal who hit those problems by living next to them. Klarify becomes a feature inside a practice-management suite rather than the suite itself. The mechanism is the same one that capped Kiko: competent execution into a problem the founders didn't independently need solved.

The bull case stings because Moody is a repeat YC founder with shipped enterprise sales chops, and the **Stripe** (2010) known exception covers exactly this profile -- I funded the Collisons after Auctomatic without making them redo bootcamp. If Circleback's $50K ARR in four months is the right reference class, Klarify's free-tier funnel into a $16-$71/mo price ladder could compound into Upheal-shaped numbers faster than the seed timing suggests. What dismantles the bull case is that Auctomatic ended in a $5M exit at age 19 -- a real prior outcome -- whereas Circleback raised $2.5M and has not exited; the repeat-founder credit is provisional, not banked. And the dossier's open question about Moody's "current status at Circleback" is exactly the founder-concentration tell I read as ambivalence. The verifiable condition that would flip this from Neutral to Invest: Klarify discloses $1M+ ARR with >30% free-to-paid conversion within 12 months of the Spring 2026 batch, and Moody publicly resolves the Circleback dual-company question. Short of that, I lean toward "the best ideas look initially like bad ideas" cuts the other way here -- this idea looks reasonable, which is usually where I lose money.
