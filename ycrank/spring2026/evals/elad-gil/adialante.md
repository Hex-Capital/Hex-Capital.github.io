# Adialante -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Adialante's FREE technique -- eliminating gradient coils from MRI entirely -- is a legitimate physics breakthrough, peer-reviewed in *Magnetic Resonance in Medicine* (Torres et al., 2022) and patent-pending. I don't question the science. But when I apply my framework, the first question isn't "is the technology real?" -- it's "what structural shift is making this market accessible NOW that wasn't possible two years ago, and does consensus underestimate the window?" Adialante's answer is essentially: "We invented a new physics." The technology IS the catalyst, which means Adialante has to create its own structural shift rather than riding an exogenous wave. Compare that to how I evaluated Harvey: GPT capability was improving on its own curve regardless of whether Harvey existed, so the technology miracle was happening exogenously. Adialante has no equivalent tailwind. And critically, the broader market for accessible, low-cost MRI is not nonobvious -- Hyperfine is a public company, Promaxo has raised $72-81M, Openwater has raised $100M, and Ezra raised $44M before being acquired by Function Health. When four well-funded competitors are pursuing adjacent approaches, the market window is consensus, not contrarian. Adialante's specific physics are novel; Adialante's market category is not.

Adialante's multi-miracle problem is what moves this from "interesting but not my bet" to a clear pass. I count at minimum two independent breakthroughs required simultaneously: first, the FREE technique must produce diagnostic-grade images comparable to conventional MRI for prostate cancer screening (no clinical validation data has been publicly reported); second, FDA must clear a device with no predicate, likely requiring De Novo classification rather than a standard 510(k). Hyperfine -- building a simpler portable MRI with different physics -- took over five years and more than $200M to reach FDA clearance for brain imaging alone. Beyond these two miracles, Adialante must also manufacture custom MRI hardware at scale and build a mobile fleet logistics operation, each demanding substantial capital against a current funding base of roughly $3M. When I backed Anduril despite its multi-miracle profile, the override was a generational structural opening: bipartisan defense modernization consensus combined with legacy contractor inability to innovate. Adialante has no equivalent demand-side catalyst -- the MRI access gap has existed for forty years without a sudden forcing function on the buyer side.

Efraín Torres invented the core FREE technology during his PhD at the University of Minnesota under Dr. Michael Garwood, and Adialante has assembled a team with four or more PhDs spanning MR physics, systems engineering, and computational physics. The technical bench is genuinely deep. But Torres and co-founder Parker Jenkins are first-time founders with no evidence of operating at a scaling company. Torres's prior experience at Boston Scientific and Imaging Biometrics is relevant domain work, not hypergrowth operational experience. Adialante's challenge isn't just building a scanner -- it's building a scanner, manufacturing it, getting it through FDA, hiring technicians, deploying trucks, managing scheduling logistics for urology clinics, and scaling that fleet across metro areas. That's an operations-heavy, capital-intensive business that demands the kind of organizational scaling expertise I look for in founders tackling complex execution challenges. The High Growth Handbook was written precisely about these transitions, and nothing in Adialante's founding team suggests they've navigated one before.

Adialante's mobile-as-a-service model -- flat per-scan fee, no capital expenditure for clinics -- is a smart go-to-market framing that reduces adoption friction for urology practices. But from an infrastructure-layer perspective, Adialante is an end-to-end vertically integrated service, not a platform other businesses build on. There's no API, no developer ecosystem, no compounding network effects. Clinics use Adialante; they don't integrate Adialante into their core workflows the way they integrate an EHR or a billing system. The margin architecture is also uncertain: hardware manufacturing, vehicle fleets, and on-site technicians create a cost structure closer to a services business than a software company. At scale with high utilization, per-scan economics could work, but Adialante's current ~$3M in funding against a $3.5M seed target (UMN CTSI, Mar 2024) is dramatically undercapitalized for the capital-intensive path ahead. No revenue or scan volume data exists publicly.

The strongest bull case for Adialante runs through my "regulated market moat" pattern: once FDA clears the FREE system, the combination of patent-pending physics, regulatory clearance, and operational fleet creates a defensible position that's extremely difficult for new entrants to replicate. The clinical guideline shift -- NCCN increasingly recommending pre-biopsy prostate MRI -- does create genuine demand pull in urology practices. And I'll note the parallel to my own experience with Color Health: deep scientific founder expertise in diagnostics, attacking an access problem in healthcare. But Color was a software and services layer on existing sequencing infrastructure -- it didn't require building novel physics hardware from scratch, clearing FDA with no predicate, and operating a fleet of vehicles simultaneously. Adialante's path requires all three. The one verifiable condition that would flip me toward investment: if Adialante demonstrates diagnostic-grade clinical images comparable to conventional MRI in a published study AND receives FDA breakthrough device designation, the miracle count drops to one (operational scaling), which is manageable.

Adialante is a genuine scientific innovation searching for a venture-scale business model through a multi-miracle gauntlet. The technology is real, the need is real, the founders have authentic domain expertise. But the market for accessible MRI is consensus (not nonobvious), the company requires at least two independent breakthroughs before reaching product-market fit (violating my single-miracle filter), and the capital-intensive hardware plus fleet model sits at the wrong layer of the stack for my infrastructure thesis. The two dimensions most decisive for this pass: Nonobvious Market Timing (well-funded competitors pursuing the same market category) and Single-Miracle Viability (FDA De Novo plus clinical validation represent compounding independent risks that no amount of execution talent can compress into a single variable).
