# Hub.xyz -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I look for is what the founders know that can't be taught. And here, I can't find it. Armin studied industrial engineering. Tim worked financial data at Tiffany & Co. and attended business school. Léonie managed supply chains for fashion brands. None of them have built distributed systems. None have published on AI training data quality. None have public GitHub repositories. The company is hiring a "Founding Full Stack AI Engineer" at $70K-$100K with up to 2% equity — which means the core technical product hasn't been built by the founding team. This is the technology business, and the founding team doesn't include a technologist who built the thing. That's not a timing issue. That's structural.

The leverage architecture looks right on paper — a distributed node network collecting data at the edge, exposed as an API to enterprise AI teams. Infrastructure play. Picks and shovels. This is the shape I like: one engineering team's work serving millions of data consumers. But the leverage isn't novel. Grass raised $17.5M and sits at roughly a billion-dollar valuation doing the same thing. Nodepay has 1.5M active users. DePIN as a category has 600+ projects and $16B in market cap. When I invested in AngelList, there was no AngelList. When I invested in CoinList, there was no CoinList. Hub.xyz is entering a category that already has well-funded leaders running the same playbook — browser extension, bandwidth sharing, token incentives, AI data output. The "API for real-world training data" framing tries to differentiate by emphasizing structured output over raw bandwidth, but I see no technical evidence that the structuring layer exists yet or that these founders have the specific knowledge to build it.

The token economy raises my filter question: does this company need a token? A decentralized bandwidth network arguably benefits from token-based coordination — you need to incentivize geographically distributed node operators, and smart contracts can handle SLAs and payments. That passes the threshold test. But the airdrop campaigns, IQ Points earned via quizzes and Discord engagement, and a Token Generation Event timeline suggest the token is being used primarily as a user acquisition mechanism rather than as genuine protocol infrastructure. When I see communities of 500K users earning points by completing quizzes, I'm looking at incentive-driven vanity metrics, not product-market fit. The gap between crypto-native node contributors running browser extensions and enterprise AI teams paying for structured training data APIs is the entire business risk — and nothing in the dossier suggests anyone on this team has bridged that gap before.

The strongest bull case: AI training data genuinely is a bottleneck that's getting worse. Regulatory pressure on centralized scraping — the lawsuits against OpenAI, the NYT litigation — creates a real structural opening for consented, distributed collection. If the regulatory environment tightens enough, the compliant data pipeline becomes the only pipeline, and whoever owns the largest consented network wins. Hub.xyz has 500K nodes and a SwissBorg partnership giving access to 1M+ KYC-verified users. In this scenario, the supply-side network effect compounds — more nodes mean better geographic coverage, more real-time data, and harder-to-replicate freshness. The YC backing adds enterprise credibility. For this to work, though, the team would need to build a genuinely superior data structuring and quality layer on top of commodity bandwidth — and that requires deep AI infrastructure knowledge that I don't see on this founding team. The bull case depends on capabilities the founders haven't demonstrated.

One co-founder — Léonie Nguyen, listed as CBO — was appointed CEO of Meey Group, a Vietnamese proptech company, in October 2025. She's not on the YC page. I don't know her current involvement, but a co-founder taking a CEO role at an unrelated company during the critical pre-seed-to-seed phase is a signal I take seriously. Play long-term games with long-term people. If a co-founder's attention is divided at the earliest stage, the compounding breaks before it starts. Combined with the absence of a technical co-founder and the consensus-market positioning, this tips me toward pass. The company is building commodity infrastructure in a crowded DePIN category, without the specific knowledge advantage or the technical founding team that would give me conviction they'll be the ones to win.
