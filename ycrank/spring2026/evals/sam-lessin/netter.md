# Netter -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Netter is the Pass Example I wrote down at the start of the 2026 batch, dressed in slightly better clothes: two Mines Paris students, four people, a "Data Science Autopilot" pitch sitting directly between Palantir Foundry on the up-market side and Databricks/Snowflake on the warehouse side, with Carrefour/Tesco/Auchan logos on the homepage that no press release or case study URL corroborates. The faintest non-consensus angle in Netter is structural — a self-serve mid-market wedge under Foundry's FDE-heavy delivery — but everything else about Netter reads like the SaaS cap table I am specifically trying not to fund at consensus YC prices in 2026.

**Solana** at $0.05/coin in 2018 is the high rung on round-pricing for me: a category most generalist funds had quit, a price no one was bidding into, and a structure that wasn't even equity. Netter sits nowhere near that rung. Netter is selling into the most-bid category of the batch — AI-on-enterprise-data — against incumbents (Palantir FY24 $2.87B revenue, Databricks $14B+ raised) whose product roadmaps already include LLM-driven "chat with your data" features. The dossier flags a $12M "Seed" figure on StartupHub.ai that the YC standard $500K pre-seed deal directly contradicts; either Netter is genuinely a $12M round, in which case the entry price is already past the point I can underwrite, or the figure is wrong and we are looking at a standard SAFE into an oversubscribed AI-data pitch. Both versions of Netter fail my value-seed price test — the only world where Netter is mispriced in my favor is one where two tier-1 funds visibly passed and I have no evidence they did.

**John Fish / Bookshelved** is the low-rung failure pattern in reverse — Bookshelved worked because the asset on the cap table was a person with owned distribution before the round, and the structure paid for that asset directly. Netter has the opposite profile on every axis. Samuel Ladroue has no Twitter the search surfaced, no GitHub, no public writing on data platforms, no newsletter, no podcast — his LinkedIn headline isn't even retrievable. Amin Hamzaoui is "Student at Mines Paris and ENS Ulm." Distribution is founder-led demos through a Calendly link on netter.ai. There is no community pre-existing Netter, no opinionated essay on why ontologies-for-mid-market is non-obvious, nothing the Slow Creator Fund framework can attach to. The asset on offer is pure enterprise SaaS equity in a hot category, which is the exact instrument I am trying to avoid writing into.

On capital structure, Netter compounds the wrong way over 18 months: competing with Palantir Foundry on ontologies and Databricks on no-code pipelines requires field engineers, enterprise sales, and connector maintenance across 120+ integrations — a burn profile that necessitates a Series A within 12 months and a Series B inside 24, none of which is "default-alive in 24 months." There is no revenue figure, no pricing page on netter.ai, no signed contract value disclosed for Carrefour or Tesco or Auchan. Netter is being financed as a generic enterprise SaaS, and the regatta tacks ahead of the Series A are all priced above where I can make money.

For founder conviction, the counterfactual is what it would take to flip Netter from default-6/20 into Pinterest territory: Samuel Ladroue would need to have spent three years inside Carrefour's data org before this, or have published a manifesto on why Foundry's FDE model is structurally wrong for European mid-market retail, or have shipped an earlier ontology product that named customers used. None of that is in the dossier. The Mines Paris / ENS Ulm credentials are strong on quantitative training and weak on missionary temperament — they tell me Netter's founders can build the product, not that they have a non-obvious belief about it they won't be talked out of.

On sleeve fit, Netter sits where my **Joe Lonsdale podcast** position cuts hardest: "AI is just more internet" means an AI-data-platform layer at seed pricing is consensus exposure, not a venture moment. The one Slow analogue I'd reach for is **Ro** — high-burn, regulatory-complexity category where capital intensity was itself the moat — and Netter has none of Ro's regulatory schleps. Mid-market data tooling has no licensing regime, no 50-state prescription rail, nothing that makes Netter's capital intensity defensible against Snowflake adding the same feature in a quarter.

My 36-month forecast for Netter: does not reach $5M ARR, gets squeezed between Foundry's mid-market push and Databricks/Snowflake's LLM-chat features shipping by end of 2026, and either pivots into vertical workflow software for European retail (Carrefour/Tesco/Auchan footprint suggests this is the warmest path) or gets acqui-hired by a warehouse vendor at sub-$50M as an ontology-team tuck-in. The mechanism is straightforward — the wedge Netter claims is the wedge every major warehouse is closing simultaneously, and the founders have neither the distribution nor the structural moat to outrun the close.

The strongest bull case against my pass: those Carrefour, Tesco, and Auchan logos. If even one of those is a paid production deployment sourced through a Mines Paris alumni network, Netter looks less like the Pass Example and more like the **Pinterest** exception — operator-friend sourcing into a category I'd otherwise screen out. Three European retail majors as references for a four-person pre-seed is genuinely anomalous, and if real it implies a distribution channel that doesn't show up in any of the other places I'd look for it. What dismantles this for me is the dossier itself: no press release, no case study URL, no contract value, no public statement from any of the three retailers, and a $12M funding figure that fails primary-source verification. The verifiable condition that flips Netter from pass to second meeting is a single signed reference call from a Carrefour data exec confirming a paid production deployment and contract value — until then, Netter is the AI-data pitch I told myself I'd stop taking meetings on in 2026.
