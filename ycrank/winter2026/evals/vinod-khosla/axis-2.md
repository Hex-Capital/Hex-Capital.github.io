# Axis -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Axis is a familiar pattern that I've learned to walk away from: a massive domain paired with modest ambition. Commodities represent a third of global goods exports. The physical movement of metals, grain, and oil is one of the most consequential systems in the global economy. When I hear "commodities," I lean forward — this is the physical-world transformation I look for. But then I read what Axis actually builds: an analytics layer that helps existing traders at existing desks make marginally better-informed decisions. The trading houses still exist. The traders still sit at their desks. The bilateral OTC market still functions exactly as before. If Axis succeeds completely, the world doesn't operate differently — commodity traders just have a nicer screen to look at. The consequence of full success here is a profitable B2B SaaS company, not a structural change in how commodities markets work.

The founders are building a tool for an industry they come from. Eric Zhu was a quantitative trader; Ian Wang is described as "interested in trading" and comes from Yale's finance pipeline. These are insiders building for insiders. I've said many times that experience creates biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing. When you know what a commodity trading desk looks like, you build a better version of that desk. You don't ask whether the desk should exist. The outsider question — can AI agents replace the human trader entirely? Can you disintermediate the opaque bilateral OTC market with transparent AI-driven pricing? — those are questions insiders don't ask because they know all the reasons it "can't work." Axis has pre-constrained its ambition to what the industry considers plausible.

Let me give the bull case its due, because there is one. Bloomberg built a $12 billion annual revenue business by becoming the indispensable information layer for financial markets. Physical commodities — unlike equities — still operate in profound informational opacity. Trafigura, Vitol, and Glencore make billions precisely because they know what's happening across global supply chains before anyone else. An AI system that democratizes commodity intelligence could shift those information asymmetries. The timing is real: LLMs crossed a capability threshold in 2023-2024 that makes parsing unstructured broker reports and geopolitical analysis feasible at scale for the first time. And the founding team's quant trading background gives them the domain intuition to build something traders will actually use. If Axis captured the "Bloomberg of physical commodities" position, that's a multi-billion-dollar outcome. But Bloomberg succeeded through proprietary data terminals and network effects built over decades. Axis is building on third-party LLMs with no proprietary data moat, no network effect, and no evidence of a data acquisition strategy. The dossier shows zero traction signals, zero defensibility signals, and a website that returns nothing but a page title.

The technology disruption dimension is where this falls apart for me. The dossier itself identifies the core vulnerability: "General-purpose LLM providers could enable commodity trading firms to build in-house analytical tools." When your technology stack is fundamentally an LLM parsing unstructured text, and the LLM is controlled by a third party, and the trading houses you're selling to have massive IT budgets and direct relationships with OpenAI and Anthropic — you have a commoditization problem, not a technology moat. Kpler has $200 million in funding and Insight Partners behind it. Enverus was acquired by Blackstone with $854 million in estimated revenue. These incumbents have customer relationships, data assets, and the resources to bolt on an AI layer faster than a two-person startup can build enterprise credibility. Compare this to Juniper, where Pradeep Sindhu had a fundamentally different architecture — TCP/IP routing at scale — that Cisco's legacy systems couldn't replicate without rebuilding from scratch. That was structural disruption. An LLM wrapper on commodity data is not structural disruption; it's a feature that any well-resourced competitor can replicate.

I pass on companies like this regularly. The team is capable, the market is real, the timing is plausible — and none of it matters because the consequence of success is a modest analytics business in a concentrated market. The CTRM software market is $112 million growing at 6.4% annually. Even granting the broader market framing, Axis's serviceable addressable market is a niche within a niche. They've chosen a "manageable" segment — institutional physical commodity trading desks — and they're building a better tool for it. That's the definition of reducing risk until the consequences of success become inconsequential. These founders are too quantitatively talented to spend their careers making commodity traders 20% more informed. The problem worth solving in commodities isn't giving traders better screens — it's rethinking whether the opaque, relationship-driven, information-asymmetry-dependent structure of physical commodity markets should exist at all in an age of AI.
