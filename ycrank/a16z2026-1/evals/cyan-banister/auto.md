# Auto -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Dave Evans and Sam Hare each built separate camera companies — PlayCanvas and Seene — that were independently acquired by Snap in back-to-back years. Sam has a PhD in computer vision. Dave built the 3D engine powering Snap Games, with 14.7k GitHub stars on the PlayCanvas repo. Then they both spent years running Snap's camera product engineering from the inside. When I look at Auto's founding team, I see two of the most qualified people on earth to build a camera platform. That's not flattery — it's a statement of fact that makes what comes next harder to say, because domain mastery is not the same thing as biographical forge. When I ask my question — tell me their story, not the business — I hear a career arc, not a crucible. These founders spotted an opportunity from their professional vantage point inside Snap's camera stack. That's pattern recognition from privilege, not a wound that won't let them stop building. I don't see evidence that either Dave or Sam experienced something that made this problem find them rather than the reverse.

Auto's product — turning photos into persistent mini-apps called "Frames" — is a genuinely clever UX abstraction. One App Store reviewer said it replaced three existing subscriptions. But when I run my invisible system test, I come up empty. What opaque, extractive structure is Auto exposing? Google Lens is free. Apple Visual Intelligence is free and bundled into iOS. The "problem" Auto solves is app subscription fragmentation — that's inconvenience for iPhone owners, not a hidden tax exploiting people who can't see the markup. Compare this to Affirm attacking credit card opacity or Ownwell attacking the property tax system. Those companies made power structures visible. Auto makes calorie counting slightly easier. There's no structural shift in who holds information and who doesn't.

The contrarian conviction question is where Auto loses me most clearly. AI consumer apps are the most consensus investment category of 2026. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when they hear "AI-powered camera app." They nod and immediately ask how you compete with Google Lens's 1.5 billion monthly users, Apple's built-in Visual Intelligence on every new iPhone, and ChatGPT Vision's general-purpose multimodal capability backed by OpenAI's $157 billion valuation. Auto's competitors aren't scrappy startups — they're three of the most powerful technology companies ever built, and they're all offering their visual AI for free or bundled. The dossier itself states the quiet part: "The underlying multimodal AI models (GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude) are available via API to any developer." Building a photo-to-structured-action pipeline is achievable by any well-funded team. This isn't a space investors avoid — it's a space where the incumbents have already arrived.

Here's the strongest bull case, and I want to be honest about it because these founders deserve it. The Frame ecosystem — user-generated visual mini-apps that people create and share — could become a platform with real switching costs, the way Snap's lens ecosystem did. Dave Evans literally built the infrastructure for Snap's mini-programs and games platform; Sam Hare built the AR lens experience pipeline. If anyone understands how to create a camera-based content ecosystem, it's these two. The $9.99/month price point replacing multiple $5-10/month single-purpose apps is a clean consumer value proposition. And the timing window is real: multimodal AI crossed accuracy thresholds in late 2023, Google Lens usage grew 250% from 2023 to 2025, and Apple validated the camera-as-AI-interface with Visual Intelligence in December 2024. If the Frame ecosystem reaches critical mass before incumbents build their own version, Auto could own a category. But "before incumbents build their own version" is doing enormous work in that sentence — Google, Apple, and OpenAI are already iterating on visual AI products weekly.

The comparison that keeps surfacing for me is the gap between Auto and something like Flock Safety. Both involve cameras. But Flock operates in a space that makes civil liberties advocates nervous — surveillance technology most VCs wouldn't touch. Flock's camera network creates compounding data moats with every deployment, and it attacks a genuinely opaque system in community safety. Auto operates in a space everyone finds delightful and unthreatening, builds on commodity APIs, and has five App Store ratings. The Flock investment made my stomach tighten; Auto makes me think "that's nice." When my stomach doesn't tighten, I've learned that's information. The founders who build companies I write checks for aren't just qualified — they're operating in territory that requires courage to enter. Auto requires brilliance and execution speed, but not courage. For my own money, I need to feel that the founders are doing something the world hasn't given them permission to do. Dave and Sam have deep permission from their careers to build exactly this. That's the difference between a strong team and a team that's been forged. I wish them well — they'll likely build something impressive — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
