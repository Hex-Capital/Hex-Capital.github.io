# arc-prize-foundation -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumped out at me immediately: the co-founders of this nonprofit AGI benchmark also run Ndea, a for-profit AGI research lab that raised $43 million from Coatue. Ndea is listed as a *donor* to ARC Prize. So the structure is: Chollet and Knoop create the definitive benchmark for measuring AGI progress, run a nonprofit around it, and simultaneously run a for-profit company whose commercial value is directly informed by insights from that benchmark. This isn't a bug -- it's a sophisticated capital structure. But the question for me as an angel evaluating the *nonprofit entity* is blunt: where does the value accrue? The answer is Ndea. The benchmark is the primitive; the company that monetizes insights from the primitive is Ndea. I'm being asked to back the wrong side of this two-entity architecture.

Let me be clear about the founder caliber, because it's exceptional. François Chollet created Keras -- 63,900 GitHub stars, 71,000+ citations, TIME 100 AI. Mike Knoop co-founded Zapier, which is a $5 billion company generating $310 million in annual revenue, profitable since 2014. These are two of the most credentialed people in the AI ecosystem. The "sane" half of my filter has rarely been stronger. But here's where it breaks: the idea isn't insane. Creating an AGI benchmark is the most consensus idea in the AI research community right now. Everyone from Google to xAI to random ML researchers on Twitter agrees that measuring intelligence properly is important. When I say I look for "sane person, insane idea," I need the idea to provoke genuine skepticism from smart people. This provokes head-nodding. That's a different signal entirely.

My AI kill test produces a strange result here because this company doesn't just fail it -- it doesn't even apply. My framework asks: "Would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" If AI didn't exist, ARC Prize Foundation would have no reason to exist. There's no "cherry on top" -- AI isn't the cherry, it's the sundae, the bowl, and the table it's sitting on. Normally that's an automatic pass for me. But I want to steelman the counterargument, because the protocol angle is genuinely interesting. ARC-AGI is *becoming* a measurement standard. AI labs reference it publicly. The competition draws global participation. If you squint, this looks like Chollet and Knoop are building a new primitive -- a standard for intelligence measurement that the entire AI ecosystem orients around. That's the kind of infrastructure play I get excited about. Helium built a protocol for decentralized wireless; ARC could build a protocol for measuring cognition. The problem is that benchmarks are not protocols in the way I mean it. Protocols compound -- every new participant makes the network more valuable. Benchmarks get replaced. ImageNet mattered enormously and then it didn't. GLUE became SuperGLUE became something else. There's no lock-in, no network effect, no compounding dynamic that prevents the next measurement framework from displacing this one.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: if ARC-AGI becomes the *definitive* standard for measuring AGI -- the benchmark that determines which labs get funding, which models get deployed, which approaches get pursued -- then controlling that standard is genuinely infrastructural. Chollet's "On the Measure of Intelligence" paper is arguably the most influential framework for thinking about general intelligence. If ARC Prize Foundation becomes the NIST of AGI, the organization that everyone defers to for measurement standards, that's an enormous position. And the founders have the credibility to make it happen. Knoop's Zapier background means he understands how to build developer-facing infrastructure that becomes default. But even granting all of this, I'm back to the structural problem: it's a nonprofit. The financial narrative is capped. There's no equity path to an infinity story. The donations-based funding model means survival depends on the continued goodwill of AI labs -- the very entities whose products are being measured, creating an inherent tension. And the biggest financial upside from this work flows to Ndea, not to the entity I'd be backing.

The revenue cushion here is essentially zero in any meaningful sense. Donations from Tyler Cowen, Andy Fang, Dharmesh Shah, and AI labs fund operations. A million-plus dollars in prize pools is activity, not economics. Compare this to my Craftwork investment -- painting crews generate real revenue today, and the tech layer makes them more efficient. Or my Creator Fund structure -- Jonathan Katz-Moses had 75 million video views and a product line generating income before we wrote the check. ARC Prize Foundation has no revenue model, no path to revenue, and no downside cushion. If the AI labs stop donating, the organization stops functioning. That's the opposite of "infinity optionality plus downside cushion" -- it's bounded optionality with no floor.

I have enormous respect for Chollet and Knoop as individuals. If Knoop came to me with a for-profit infrastructure play leveraging his Zapier experience and Chollet's AI measurement framework, I'd lean forward hard. But the entity structure here means the value I care about -- compounding, defensible, uncapped -- lives at Ndea, not at ARC Prize Foundation. I'm being shown the primitive and asked to invest in the nonprofit wrapper around it while the for-profit entity captures the commercial upside. That's a structural mismatch with everything I look for.
