# Traverse -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Zachary Yu worked at Mercor. He saw how the RL environment business operates from inside one of the category's dominant companies -- a company now valued at $10 billion with roughly $450-500 million in annualized revenue. He left, started an unrelated company about AI for mortgage brokers, then pivoted mid-batch into building RL environments for non-deterministic domains -- a direct subset of what his former employer already claims to offer. René Girard would have recognized this immediately. The desire to compete was born from proximity to the rival. This is not a founder who discovered a secret about the world that others reject; this is a founder who observed a successful business model from inside and decided to replicate a variant of it. Mimetic desire generates mimetic competition, and mimetic competition destroys value. The entire trajectory -- from employment at the incumbent, to unrelated pivot, to re-entry into the incumbent's market -- is the Girardian pattern at its most transparent.

What is the secret here? Traverse claims that RL environments for non-deterministic work -- medicine, law, business strategy -- require fundamentally different verification approaches than coding environments. Is this contrarian? It is not. Every ML researcher alive understands that verifying a doctor's clinical judgment is harder than checking whether code compiles. Mercor already lists healthcare and law among its target domains. Applied Compute's ex-OpenAI team is building enterprise RL environments. Surge AI is generating $1.2 billion in revenue across the data spectrum and seeking a billion-dollar raise. The observation that "taste is hard to formalize" is not a secret -- it is a widely acknowledged open problem. A genuine secret would be a specific, non-obvious insight into *how* to formalize taste in a way that others believe is impossible. I see no evidence of such an insight. The company's own website acknowledges the problem is "largely unsolved," which is an honest admission that they do not yet possess the secret their business requires.

The pivot from Clice AI is the second structural problem. These founders began YC W26 building a communication OS for loan officers. They abandoned that thesis during the batch and adopted an entirely new one in a completely different domain. This is the lean-startup iterate-and-pivot methodology in action -- the founders did not arrive with a definite vision about how non-deterministic verification should work. They arrived with one idea, discarded it, and adopted another. I have consistently backed founders whose conviction precedes market validation -- Zuckerberg refusing a billion-dollar acquisition, Musk launching a fourth rocket after three failures. A mid-batch pivot from fintech communication tools to AI infrastructure is the opposite of that pattern. It signals that the founders are searching for a viable thesis rather than executing one they already hold. "Lean is code for unplanned" applies here with unusual precision.

The bull case, which I take seriously, runs as follows: the RL environment market is growing at 30%+ annually, frontier labs are spending tens of millions and that spend is projected to multiply 3-5x, and non-deterministic domains remain genuinely underserved because coding-focused incumbents built their infrastructure around deterministic verification. Yu's insider knowledge from Mercor gives him real understanding of buyer needs and incumbent blind spots. If Traverse developed a genuinely novel verification methodology for one domain -- say, medical reasoning -- they could become the specialized provider that labs turn to when their generalist vendors cannot deliver adequate quality. In this scenario, domain specialization creates a quality moat that commodity data providers cannot match. What would need to be true: the founders would need to recruit deep domain experts in medicine or law (neither founder has this background), develop a verification approach that is demonstrably superior to what Mercor and Surge AI can produce with their massive workforces, and sell to the three or four frontier labs that constitute the entire buyer universe -- all while competing against players with 100-1000x more capital. Every link in this chain is speculative, and the funding asymmetry alone makes the execution path perilously narrow.

The competitive landscape is damning by my framework. Mercor, Surge AI, Applied Compute, Idler, HUD -- five well-funded companies pursuing variations of the same thesis, targeting the same three or four buyers. This is not a market where a secret creates a monopoly; this is a Red Ocean where multiple players fight for lab procurement contracts. The absence of any proprietary technology, any unique dataset, any network effect, and any evidence of customer traction means there is no visible mechanism through which early advantage compounds into defensibility. The company is a two-person team with zero GitHub stars competing against a $10 billion company that already targets their market segment. There is no world in which this returns an entire fund.

I pass. The thesis is consensus, the market is competitive, the founders pivoted into the space rather than building from conviction, and the competitive funding asymmetry is three orders of magnitude. Competition in this market will be determined by scale, relationships, and capital -- precisely the dimensions where Traverse is weakest. A company without a secret is a company that will compete, and in this particular arena, the competition has already been decided by resource allocation alone.
