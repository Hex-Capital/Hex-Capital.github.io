# Pax Historia -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Let me start with what's genuinely impressive here: 35,000 daily active users in four months, organically, with a team of three. That's exceptional execution at any stage. Twenty million rounds played, 4,000 community-created presets, content creators posting about it unprompted on TikTok and X. These founders can build and ship. I respect that deeply. But execution velocity and consequence magnitude are fundamentally different questions, and my first question is always the same: if this succeeds spectacularly, does the world operate differently?

The honest answer is no. If Pax Historia becomes the dominant AI-powered strategy game, strategy gamers have a more creative, more emergent way to play strategy games. That's a good business -- maybe a great one. The strategy gaming market is $18 billion and growing. But nobody's food system changes. No trillion-dollar infrastructure gets displaced. No physical system becomes obsolete. This is entertainment, well-executed entertainment, but entertainment. When I backed Impossible Foods, the consequence of success was reclaiming billions of acres of arable land. When I backed OpenAI, it was transforming how every knowledge worker operates. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, it was replacing fossil fuel power generation. Pax Historia's consequence of success is that Paradox Interactive sells fewer copies of Europa Universalis. I've spent my career telling smart founders they're too talented to waste on problems this small -- and these founders are clearly talented.

The technology layer concerns me more. The core mechanic is wrapping third-party LLMs in a geopolitical game interface accessed via OpenRouter. A fan clone already exists on GitHub, built with Gemini, in what appears to be a weekend project. The VGTimes review documents real quality issues: AI models ignoring player orders, vulnerability to prompt manipulation, a "systemic paradox" where optimal play involves using external AI tools simultaneously. This is not Juniper building TCP/IP routers that made Cisco's architecture structurally obsolete. This is not Square creating mobile payment infrastructure that displaced traditional POS systems. There is no proprietary technology creating an economic impact that makes the existing approach unviable. Paradox and Firaxis still offer deeper mechanical gameplay, and they have decades of franchise loyalty. The 100 billion tokens processed weekly is a cost center, not a moat -- it represents dependency on third-party inference providers, not a technology advantage.

The strongest bull case requires me to squint and see this as the "Roblox of AI gaming" -- a platform where user-generated content creates network effects that compound into a defensible position. The 4,000+ presets with community wikis, a Fandom page, and organic content creation do echo early Roblox dynamics. If AI-generated emergent gameplay becomes the dominant paradigm in the broader $200 billion gaming industry, and if Pax Historia's UGC flywheel gives them the content density to maintain first-mover advantage against well-resourced competitors, the outcome could be substantial. For that to happen, several things must be true simultaneously: LLM inference costs must keep dropping, AI narrative quality must improve dramatically to match the mechanical depth strategy gamers expect, the UGC moat must compound faster than competitors can replicate it, and the monetization model ($1 for 15-20 minutes of gameplay, with only 6 minutes of free daily play) must convert free users at rates sufficient to cover enormous inference costs. That's a lot of simultaneous low-probability requirements -- the kind of multi-variable dependency I've learned to avoid after KiOR, where the technology worked but the economic assumptions didn't.

The founders don't fit my outsider-attacking-the-impossible pattern either. Eli Bullock-Papa has a solid engineering background from Virginia Tech and Capital One. Ryan Zhang's YC bio is "I like history." These are capable builders, not contrarian outsiders attempting what experts dismiss as crazy. AI gaming in 2026 is a consensus category -- everyone with LLM access is exploring it. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, aerospace experts said a New Zealander couldn't build a launch vehicle. When I backed Pat Brown, the food industry said you couldn't make meat from plants. Nobody is saying you can't make an AI strategy game -- a dozen teams are trying. The absence of expert dismissal is itself a signal that the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening to incumbents.

This is a company I'd expect to see in a consumer gaming fund's portfolio, not mine. The founders have demonstrated they can build something people want. The traction is real and the UGC dynamics are promising. But I invest in problems too important not to work on, with technologies that make existing systems economically obsolete. Pax Historia is a well-built game in a well-understood market using commodity technology. The probability of a good outcome may be reasonable, but the consequence of that success -- even in the best case -- doesn't reach the scale I need to write a check from my own money.
