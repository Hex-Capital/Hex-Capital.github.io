# Sentrial -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Sentrial isn't the founders or the product -- it's the crowd. AI observability is one of the most popular categories in developer tools right now. Braintrust just raised $121 million at an $800 million valuation. Arize AI has pulled in $131 million with Datadog and Microsoft among its backers. Langfuse got acquired by ClickHouse. Helicone, Maxim AI, and a dozen open-source alternatives are all competing for the same engineering teams. When I see a space where every VC in town is already writing checks and well-funded incumbents are expanding into the same niche, my instinct isn't excitement -- it's caution. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Sentrial is entering a space that is both extremely popular and relatively straightforward to evaluate, which is the opposite of where I've found my best investments.

When I try to apply my usual starting question -- tell me your story, not the business -- I come up mostly empty. Neel and Anay are UC Berkeley undergrads in computer science and data science. Neel interned at Sense working on agentic optimization. Anay interned at Accenture deploying agents and at IBM, and he co-founded DiscoverLabs, an ML-powered tool that hit 3.5K users in its first week of beta. These are smart, technically capable people who noticed a real gap while working with AI agents at their internships. But I'm not hearing a survival story. I'm not hearing about the hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this specific problem. They observed a pain point through professional exposure -- which is fine, and it's how a lot of companies get started -- but it doesn't give me the signal I rely on most. I backed Contraline because its founder had spent years in academic research that others ignored, driven by a conviction that came from deep personal investment in the problem. Here, I see relevant experience but not the biographical thread that tells me these founders will persist when Datadog bundles monitoring into existing enterprise contracts or when Braintrust's $121 million in funding lets them ship equivalent features in a quarter.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. The "why now" is real -- AI agents are proliferating fast, and the semantic failure modes they produce (infinite loops, hallucinations, tool misuse, user frustration) genuinely aren't caught by traditional monitoring. That's a legitimate gap. If Sentrial can build a data flywheel from accumulated production failure patterns -- learning from thousands of agent sessions what hallucination signatures look like, how loops begin, what tool misuse patterns precede user churn -- that could become a meaningful moat over time. And Anay's experience shipping DiscoverLabs to 3.5K users in under a week suggests he can build and distribute. If I squint, I can see a world where Sentrial's narrow focus on automated semantic detection and fix recommendations carves out a defensible niche that the broader platforms overlook because they're trying to do everything. But "if I squint" isn't the jolt of intuitive energy I felt with Niantic or the gut conviction I had writing my first check into SpaceX. This is an analytical maybe, not a felt yes.

What I don't see is any of the patterns that define my portfolio. There's no economic access being created for underserved populations -- this is a tool for well-paid engineering teams at companies that can afford AI agent infrastructure. There's no invisible system being made visible. There's no regulatory complexity creating a moat that deters competitors. There's no stigma or controversy scaring off other investors -- quite the opposite. And when I think about defensibility, which Lee and I look for even at the earliest stages, the dossier itself says "no defensibility signals found." Two undergrads with internship experience are competing against companies with a hundred times their capital in a space that Datadog is already investing in directly. That's not the kind of asymmetric bet where being the first check creates extraordinary value.

I wish Neel and Anay well -- they're clearly bright and they've identified a real technical problem. But I invest in misfits solving problems that consensus investors can't yet see, and AI monitoring in 2026 is as consensus as it gets. This is a pass for me.
