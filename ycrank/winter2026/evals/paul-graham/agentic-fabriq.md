# Agentic Fabriq -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me almost everything I need to know about the origin of this idea. Arcade.dev, founded by an actual former Okta executive, raised $12M to build what is essentially the same pitch. Composio has $29M. OASIS Security has $75M. Opti has $20M. SailPoint, a public company, already shipped Agent Identity Security as a product feature. When I count five funded startups and multiple incumbents all converging on the same problem simultaneously, I'm not looking at schlep blindness. I'm looking at a consensus opportunity. This is the kind of category where every VC in San Francisco nods along because the market research slides are obvious. When I said in 2025 that the most impressive YC companies I'd seen were not working on AI, I was pointing at exactly this dynamic: when everybody agrees an idea is good, the upside has already been priced.

The question I always ask first is: how did the founders find this problem? The dossier gives me no answer. Paulina and Matthew Xu are MIT AI researchers who dropped out to start a company. Their research backgrounds are in ML and computational science -- not in enterprise security, not in IAM, not in building AI agents that bumped into permissions problems. There's no moment where they describe hitting a wall themselves. No story about deploying an agent that accessed data it shouldn't have. The positioning -- "Okta for Agents" -- is the kind of crisp analogy that emerges from market analysis, not from lived frustration. Compare this to how Stripe started: the Collisons were programmers who spent years dealing with payment APIs and finally said "enough." The organic backstory was the idea. Here, the market thesis is the idea.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. The problem is genuinely real and growing fast -- 90% of AI agents being over-permissioned is a serious security gap, and the shift from chatbots to autonomous agents creates a new authorization surface that legacy IAM wasn't built for. Two MIT-trained technical founders who drop out at 18-19 have at least demonstrated conviction, and youth isn't disqualifying -- the Collison brothers were young too. If Agentic Fabriq has built something technically elegant that the well-funded competitors haven't, and if they can get into enterprises through YC's network before incumbents close the gap, there's a path to a real company. The "admin control plane" positioning is genuinely distinct from Arcade.dev's "action-level authorization" focus, which suggests they've thought carefully about where to plant their flag. For this to work, they'd need to be the kind of founders who are so technically gifted that their product is simply better than what $75M buys elsewhere -- and who can somehow sell to enterprise IT buyers despite being teenagers with no enterprise relationships. Both would need to be true simultaneously.

But I keep coming back to the structural problem. Enterprise security is not a category that rewards young first-time founders the way developer tools or consumer products do. Enterprise IT buyers want compliance certifications, SOC 2 reports, vendor stability, and reference customers. They buy from companies that look like they'll exist in three years. Two MIT dropouts in Sunnyvale with $500K competing against a former Okta executive with $12M and OASIS Security with $75M backed by Sequoia -- the distribution disadvantage is severe. This isn't like Stripe competing against PayPal, where developers could adopt the product bottom-up without procurement approval. Agent governance is inherently a top-down, admin-facing sale. The founders' youth, which would be an asset in a developer-tools category, becomes a liability in enterprise security sales.

I see no evidence of users, no revenue signal, no GitHub repos, no public code. The demo videos exist, which suggests something was built, but there's nothing to indicate whether the technical work is craftsman-quality or standard OAuth plumbing assembled into a dashboard. The MIT research credentials are real but point toward ML and computational physics, not the specific domain expertise (identity protocols, enterprise security architecture, compliance frameworks) that this product demands. When the underlying components -- OAuth flows, RBAC, token management -- are described even in the research dossier as "well-understood," the technical moat question becomes acute. What do these founders know about building agent identity infrastructure that the former Okta executive at Arcade.dev doesn't?

This is a real problem looking for the right team to solve it. These may not be that team. The absence of organic discovery, the consensus nature of the opportunity, the competitive funding gap, and the mismatch between founder profile and target buyer all point the same direction.
