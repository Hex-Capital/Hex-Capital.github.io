# Auto -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Dave Evans built a 3D engine, sold it to Snap. Sam Hare built a mobile 3D reconstruction system, sold it to Snap. Both spent years inside Snap building the camera-as-platform — Evans directing Mini-Programs, Games, and SnapKit engineering; Hare leading the entire Lens Experience engineering team responsible for every AR surface in Snapchat. Now they've left to build Auto, which turns each photo into a persistent mini-application called a Frame. The Frame concept is not a market-research insight. It is the literal synthesis of what Evans built (Mini-Programs) and what Hare built (Lens experiences) at Snap, recombined for the AI era. This is specific knowledge so concentrated it borders on unfair. Hare has a PhD in computer vision with published research and C++ repos on GitHub. Evans is a core contributor to PlayCanvas engine with 14.7k stars. These are not operators who hired engineers — they ARE the engineers. If you erased both founders, you'd lose the architectural intuition that makes the Frame abstraction feel native to camera behavior rather than bolted on. That's the specific knowledge test, and Auto passes it cleanly.

The principal-agent structure here mirrors what drew me to Perplexity. Google Lens processes 12 billion visual searches per month, but every query is monetized through ad-linked search results. Building a subscription mini-app platform would cannibalize that ad revenue. Apple Visual Intelligence is an OS feature engineered to sell iPhone 16 hardware — Apple has no incentive to open it into a user-generated ecosystem. Snap, where Evans and Hare spent years, optimizes for engagement metrics and advertiser relationships, not for turning each photo into a productivity tool. The incumbents have the specific knowledge to build this. Their P&L structures make them agents of their own revenue models. Evans and Hare, as principals with no legacy revenue to protect, can build what Snap's organizational physics prevented. You don't beat Google Lens by being a better search engine. You beat it by building a different category that Google's business model forbids it from entering.

The leverage audit reveals both strength and fragility. Auto is a three-person team that shipped version 1.0 on March 17, 2026 and iterated to version 1.2.6 within three weeks — builder velocity that validates the team-to-value ratio. The user-generated Frame system is the leverage multiplier: if it works, every user who creates a custom Frame is extending the product for free, the way Notion templates turned users into unpaid product managers. At $9.99/month or $99.99/year, the unit economics of a small-team software product are structurally sound. But the underlying multimodal intelligence comes from API calls to GPT-4V, Gemini, or Claude — Auto is renting its AI leverage rather than owning it. The specific knowledge here is in the platform layer (how to turn visual input into persistent task workflows), not in the model layer. That's defensible as long as the Frame ecosystem compounds faster than competitors can replicate the UX. It's vulnerable if it doesn't.

The strongest bull case for Auto is that camera-as-platform is a category that hasn't been claimed yet because the technology only became viable in 2024. Google Lens is search. Apple Visual Intelligence is a feature. ChatGPT Vision is a chatbot. None of them treat each photo as the seed of a persistent, task-specific application. The Frame abstraction is genuinely novel — and the two people on Earth most qualified to build it, based on a combined two decades of camera-platform engineering at Snap, are the ones building it. The UGC Frame ecosystem, if it reaches critical mass, creates switching costs that no incumbent can replicate without rebuilding their entire product architecture. One App Store reviewer already noted Auto "made three existing app subscriptions unnecessary" — that's the consolidation play at work.

The risk I'd need to watch is Apple. Auto is iOS-only, living inside a platform controlled by a company that ships a competing feature for free on every new iPhone. Apple has killed apps before by absorbing their functionality into the OS. If Apple expands Visual Intelligence to include persistent workflows or user-created modules in iOS 19, Auto's distribution advantage evaporates overnight. The generic name compounds this — "Auto" is nearly undiscoverable in App Store search. Five ratings at launch is expected for pre-seed, but the path from five to five million runs through Apple's territory, and Apple is both landlord and competitor. The evidence that would flip me from Invest to Pass: Apple announcing a Frame-like extensibility layer for Visual Intelligence at WWDC 2026.

Auto earns its score on two dimensions. Specific Knowledge Productization is the anchor — Evans and Hare aren't building in a domain they studied; they're building the product their decade of camera-platform work was training them to build. Two prior exits to the same acquirer in the same domain is a pattern I almost never see. Team-to-Value Leverage is the second driver — three technical builders shipping weekly iterations without hired engineers. The gap is in Permissionless Leverage Architecture: the API dependency on commodity AI models and the iOS platform lock reduce what would otherwise be a clean code-leverage play. I'd write a check. The founders' knowledge stack is non-replicable, and the incumbent principal-agent problem gives them a window that talent and capital alone cannot close.
