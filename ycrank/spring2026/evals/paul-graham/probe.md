# Probe -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Mahit Namburu is an ML inference researcher who declined a Together AI MLE offer to build incident-management software, which makes Probe the inverse of the pattern I keep paying for: hacker genealogy is the strongest signal in the dossier, and organic founder-pain is the weakest. That pairing tells me what kind of company Probe is before I read another line -- a competent ML person who picked a category because agents look good in it, not because being paged at 3am is the thing he hates most in the world. The YC page lists three people on the team and names exactly one of them; the other two are ghosts in the dossier, which is the second thing I notice and the second thing that bothers me.

Brian Armstrong's hand-built Android Bitcoin wallet is the canonical animal moment in my book -- he had been written off by YC and shipped a prototype to overturn the rejection, and that artifact, not the deck, is what flipped **Coinbase** (2012). I cannot find Probe's equivalent. The dossier's strongest pre-funding stunt is Mahit declining an MLE offer at Together AI, which is a career bet, not an animal moment; nobody at Y Combinator ever wrote a check on the strength of a job someone didn't take. No demo on Hacker News, no GitHub handle surfaced in search, no design-partner SRE team named, no incident corpus shipped. Armstrong wrote code after the rejection letter; Probe's dossier shows credentials before the application. Those are different categories of evidence.

Drew Houston of **Dropbox** (2007) lost his USB stick on a bus and went home and built the thing he wanted to use -- that is what organic means, and it is the test Probe fails most clearly. Mahit did LoRA fine-tuning at Databricks and machine translation at Apple; neither of those jobs involves carrying a pager. The dossier explicitly notes "no documented prior SRE/incident-response operating experience." Incident management is a domain you hate before you build a tool for it, and the founder who has not been woken up by PagerDuty at 4am building incident software is roughly the same shape as a sober person designing a hangover cure -- the artifact will be plausible and the empathy will be wrong. The two unnamed teammates might be ex-SREs and that would change everything, but the dossier does not say so, and I do not get to invent the missing founders.

Probe positions itself as "agent-native" against incident.io and Rootly, which is the schlep-blindness diagnostic working backwards. **Coinbase** (2012)'s moat was the compliance grind nobody else wanted; the work was offline, regulatory, and unpleasant. Probe's described work is autonomous remediation over heterogeneous logs -- which is a hard ML problem, but it is software-only, and the dossier names incident.io ($96M+ raised, $9M ARR, $400M valuation in April 2025) shipping the same AI-SRE pitch from inside an installed customer base. If the schlep were real, incident.io would be flinching from it; instead they raised $62M explicitly to do it. That is not schlep blindness, that is a crowded racetrack, and Probe is starting three laps behind a runner with a $96M war chest.

Mahit's SpecDec research at Berkeley plus the Databricks LoRA work is a real ML-engineering profile, and it is the dimension where Probe scores best -- the hacker-genealogy axis where Patrick Collison's Lisp prize sat before **Stripe** (2010). But the dossier shows no GitHub trail, only a Devpost handle, and I over-index on tribal markers; hackers leave commits. Counterfactually, if Mahit's GitHub showed a popular open-source SRE agent with a few hundred stars predating Probe, the hacker score moves up a tier and the organic score moves with it, because the artifact would be evidence of both. Without the trail, I am scoring credentials, and credentials are what the MBA-deck team in my Pass Example brought too.

The co-founder configuration is the dimension I cannot evaluate because the dossier will not let me. Three people, one name, no described relationship, no roommate history, no prior shipped project together. The **Airbnb** (2009) trio had years of Gebbia/Chesky RISD overlap before the cereal boxes; the **Reddit** (2005) pair were UVA dorm roommates. Probe's team chemistry is a black box, and absent evidence I sit at default. The YC interview will surface it; I cannot.

My 36-month forecast: Probe gets acqui-hired by incident.io or Rootly for $5-15M as a talent-and-tech tuck-in within 24 months, or pivots out of incident-management into a horizontal AI-agent developer-tools play where Mahit's LoRA-inference background actually fits the founder. The mechanism is straightforward -- incident.io raised $62M to ship exactly this, has the customers, and the cheapest way to neutralize a YC entrant in your category is to buy the founder before the data moat compounds.

The strongest case against me: the bull argument is that incident.io's $62M raise *proves* the category is real, LLM tool-use crossed the usability threshold in 2024-2025, and Mahit's inference-cost expertise is exactly the lever that makes autonomous-agent unit economics work -- the incumbent will be slow because per-seat ARR cannibalization is real, and the agent-native architecture is the thing that eats them. The **Coinbase** (2012) precedent holds here: Armstrong had no compliance background either; he learned the schlep on the job, and that became the moat. Fair. But Armstrong shipped the Android wallet *after rejection* to prove he would do the grind -- the artifact preceded the check. Probe's dossier shows no equivalent. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Mahit ships an open-source incident-triage agent on GitHub, names three engineering teams using it weekly in production, and publishes precision/recall numbers against the arXiv 2508.14231 benchmark. That is the Armstrong move. Until then, this is clever idea, unproven founder-animal, crowded category -- and that is the reverse pattern where I lose money.
