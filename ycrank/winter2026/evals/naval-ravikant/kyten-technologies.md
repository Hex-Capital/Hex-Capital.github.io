# Kyten Technologies -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is real. Cooper McBride and Lucas Maddox didn't read about aerospace battery packs — they shipped 6,000 of them into low earth orbit at SpaceX and Starlink. That's knowledge encoded in burned fingers and failed test cycles, not in textbooks. You can't train someone to understand the failure modes of battery packs in vacuum and thermal extremes. You can't teach the intuition for which cell chemistries behave badly under which vibration profiles. McBride progressed from Tesla intern to leading 30+ person engineering teams at SpaceX and Shield AI — that's not credential stacking, that's a progression that only happens when you accumulate real authority through real work. Maddox spent seven years at SpaceX/Starlink managing test and manufacturing at production scale. Together they probably know more about building and qualifying aerospace battery packs than any other two-person team you could assemble. This clears my specific knowledge bar convincingly. These founders couldn't have been trained to do this — they had to live it.

The leverage question is where my conviction stalls. Battery pack manufacturing is physical work. Each unit requires cells, assembly, thermal management hardware, BMS integration, testing. The company claims "software-defined automated manufacturing" — and if that's real, it introduces code leverage inside a hardware business, which is the Tesla manufacturing playbook applied to a niche where it might actually work. But the dossier provides no evidence this system exists beyond a website claim. Without it, Kyten is a high-quality contract manufacturer: great founders building a linear business where revenue scales with headcount and capital investment. I've made exceptions for capital-intensive businesses — Opendoor was one — but Opendoor had algorithmic pricing as its genuine software lever. What's Kyten's? The chemistry-agnostic positioning is interesting — they're the integration layer above cell manufacturers, indifferent to whether Amprius or Lyten wins the cell chemistry race. That's picks-and-shovels logic, and my portfolio leans heavily toward infrastructure plays. But infrastructure in atoms is structurally different from infrastructure in bits. Every API call through Alchemy costs nearly nothing at the margin. Every battery pack through Kyten costs materials, labor, and factory time.

The timing argument is the strongest bull case. The U.S. military plans to acquire 200,000+ drones while 90% of the battery supply chain runs through China. NDAA compliance is forcing reshoring. The Pentagon is developing a dedicated battery procurement strategy for 2026. This isn't speculative demand — this is a structural supply gap created by geopolitics, with explicit government procurement targets and timelines. The incumbents like EaglePicher are optimized for large prime contractor relationships with 12-month procurement cycles — their cost structures can't serve high-mix, low-volume, rapid-turnaround orders. That's a classic innovator's dilemma setup. What makes this subtly contrarian is that the investment dollars in this space have flowed overwhelmingly to cell chemistry innovation — Lyten raised $425M+, Amprius is publicly traded — while pack-level integration is considered the unsexy, commoditized layer. Kyten is betting that the bottleneck isn't better cells, it's getting qualified packs manufactured quickly. Most smart investors would disagree. That's worth something.

For this to be a great investment — the kind of 100x outcome I optimize for — several things would need to be true simultaneously. The software-defined manufacturing system would need to genuinely exist and create a 10x speed advantage that compounds with each new design iteration. The DoD drone procurement ramp would need to materialize on schedule and at scale. Kyten would need to secure early contracts that generate qualification data across multiple platforms, creating a design library that becomes a durable moat. And they'd need to raise growth capital fast enough to build manufacturing capacity before better-funded competitors (Packet Digital with $50M DoD, Lyten with $425M+) vertically integrate into pack manufacturing. The specific knowledge of the founders makes the first condition plausible. The geopolitical dynamics make the second likely. But the capital intensity makes the third and fourth genuinely difficult for a two-person pre-seed team. I don't see how they build meaningful manufacturing capacity on YC funding alone, and the path from here to a defensible position requires navigating defense procurement bureaucracy while simultaneously building a factory — two activities that are each individually consuming.

I respect the founders. The specific knowledge is among the deepest I've seen at this stage. But my framework asks: does this company create a new form of leverage that didn't exist before? Right now, the honest answer is: not yet, and maybe not ever. A faster contract manufacturer serving defense OEMs is a solid business — potentially a very profitable one. But it's not the kind of leverage-creating platform I optimize my portfolio around. If they showed me the software-defined manufacturing system working — genuinely automating the design-to-qualification pipeline in a way that turns each new customer into reusable code rather than bespoke engineering — I'd reconsider. That would transform this from a linear manufacturing operation into something with real code leverage embedded in a hardware business. Without that evidence, I'm passing with genuine respect for the founders' knowledge and the market timing.
