# Miraka -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Miraka's most striking feature is the gap between its traction and its structural position. $300K in annual run rate with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments, a named partnership at NewYork-Presbyterian, and a claimed 94% patient capture rate versus a 10% industry standard -- at pre-seed, from a team of three Princeton graduates. These are real numbers. Insurance is actually reimbursing them. Patients are actually being monitored. The question my framework asks is not whether this works, but whether it monopolizes. And here the answer becomes uncomfortable for the founders: Miraka has found a genuine operational insight -- that the bottleneck in cardiac post-discharge care is not monitoring technology but care delivery itself -- and that insight creates a good business, not a monopoly.

The secret test yields a mixed verdict. Cadence raised $144M to sell RPM software to hospitals. Biofourmis raised $465M to build FDA-cleared analytics. Health Recovery Solutions raised $85M for tablet-based monitoring. All of them built better tools and handed those tools to hospital systems that capture only 10% of eligible patients. Miraka's implicit claim is that these companies misidentified the constraint: the problem is not that hospitals lack monitoring technology but that nobody owns the patient relationship after discharge. By becoming the care team rather than licensing tools to one, Miraka achieves that 94% capture rate. This is a real insight -- it explains why hundreds of millions in competitor funding haven't moved the industry needle. But it is an operational insight, not a technological discontinuity. Any of Cadence's 500+ health system partners could decide tomorrow to hire clinicians and replicate Miraka's model. The insight creates competitive advantage through execution difficulty, not through structural barriers that compound over time. Execution difficulty is not a moat -- it is a temporary lead.

Nolan Abeyta, Kazuo Nakamura, and Jesse Abeyta share exactly the kind of prehistory I look for in founding teams: Nolan and Kazuo co-founded Overwatch Health together at Princeton's eLab in 2021, and Jesse served as CTO of that same company. They are attacking the identical problem they worked on before, with refined conviction. The family connection between Nolan and Jesse (shared surname, likely siblings) adds relational depth that most founding teams lack. This is a team that chose cardiac post-discharge care not because the TAM slide was compelling but because they lived inside the problem. Yet the trail from Overwatch Health to "Paralign" (Nolan's current LinkedIn title) to Miraka raises a question about the definiteness of the plan. A team on its second or third formulation of the same thesis is not necessarily iterating toward product-market fit in the lean-startup sense -- they may be sharpening a conviction. But the naming turbulence makes it harder to identify the irreversible commitment that signals definite optimism versus adaptive searching.

The power-law calculation is where Miraka fails my framework most decisively. A clinical services company that must recruit, credential, and manage healthcare workers to serve each additional patient faces linear cost scaling by structural necessity. Cadence reached a $1 billion valuation with a SaaS model precisely because software scales nonlinearly -- one platform serves 500+ health systems with marginal deployment cost. Miraka chose the harder path that produces better outcomes but worse economics. The US RPM market at $14 billion growing to $29 billion by 2030 is large enough, but it is also fragmented across dozens of funded players. Even the best-case trajectory for Miraka -- national expansion of their clinical model, dominant share of cardiac post-discharge -- looks like a $500 million to $1 billion outcome, not a $10 billion monopoly. In the power-law framework, an investment that produces 10x when you need 100x is structurally irrelevant no matter how well the company executes.

The strongest bull case: Miraka's 94% capture rate and claimed >90% readmission reduction represent such a dramatic improvement over the industry that hospital systems will preferentially route all cardiac patients through their platform, creating density-driven network effects -- more patients generate better predictive models, which produce better outcomes, which attract more hospital partnerships. If NewYork-Presbyterian's results hold and become publishable clinical evidence, Miraka could become the standard of care that payers mandate, achieving monopoly through clinical authority rather than technology lock-in. The CMS 2026 fee schedule lowering RPM billing thresholds provides regulatory tailwind. And the Palantir pattern -- embed so deeply in institutional operations that extraction becomes unthinkable -- could apply if Miraka becomes the cardiac care infrastructure at enough major health systems. What would have to be true: the clinical outcomes must survive peer review, the staffing model must find nonlinear leverage (perhaps AI reducing clinician-per-patient ratios dramatically over time), and hospital switching costs must prove genuinely prohibitive rather than merely inconvenient.

I respect what Nolan, Kazuo, and Jesse have built at pre-seed -- real revenue, real patients, real clinical outcomes at a tier-one hospital. But my framework requires me to distinguish between a company that works and a company that monopolizes. Miraka occupies a competitive market with $694 million in combined competitor funding, offers a services model that scales linearly with headcount, and holds an operational insight that is correct but replicable. The dossier describes a company that could become a successful cardiac care delivery business -- and in a framework that demands monopoly or nothing, "successful business" is the wrong category.
