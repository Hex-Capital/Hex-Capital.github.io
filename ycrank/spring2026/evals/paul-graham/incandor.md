# Incandor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Incandor is a competent Stanford pair pointing at a real schlep — bank fraud telemetry under SR 11-7 and BIPA — without a single pre-funding animal moment in the dossier to make me believe Matthew Yekell and Luc Rosenzweig will personally outwork BioCatch's 60+ patents and Permira's $1.3B checkbook. That high/low pairing is the whole evaluation: the *zone* is right, the *animal* evidence is missing, and at pre-seed the second one is the one I'm actually buying.

The highest rung on the resourcefulness axis is Brian Armstrong hand-building the Android Bitcoin wallet *after* I rejected his Coinbase application — a stunt that overturned a written no. The mechanism is simple: an animal founder leaves an artifact you can point to before the check clears. Incandor's dossier gives me Vista Equity Partners on Matthew Yekell's resume and NVIDIA/Apple/Epic Games on Luc Rosenzweig's, but zero artifact: no Hacker News demo, no pre-YC paying pilot, no public benchmark of the ">99% account-takeover accuracy" claim on incandor.com, not even a Product Hunt launch. The YC page has a launch video; that's table stakes, not a cereal box. Where Armstrong shipped working code to flip a rejection, Incandor shipped a marketing site with a "Request early access" button and an unverified accuracy number. Animals leave tracks, and I see none.

The pass-example failure mechanism — two-MBA team with a TAM slide and "we'll hire engineers post-funding" — is *not* what's happening at Incandor; Luc Rosenzweig is clearly the technical half and the NVIDIA input-stack pedigree maps cleanly onto capturing mouse/keystroke/touch telemetry. But the adjacent failure pattern *does* appear: the idea's origin story is missing. The dossier offers no anecdote of Matthew Yekell getting his Cboe account taken over, no Luc Rosenzweig moment of frustration with NuData's black-box scores. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick; the Collisons had been hacking commerce since they were teenagers. Incandor reads like founders who scanned the fraud category and chose the unsupervised-behavioral angle because it's the structurally clever wedge against label-trained incumbents — which it is — not because they lived inside the problem. Clever-idea, unproven-founders is exactly the reverse pattern I lose money on.

Incandor sits squarely in a schlep zone Sift and Feedzai already occupy: GDPR Article 9 biometric data, BIPA exposure, SOC 2 attestations not yet published, model-risk-management reviews under SR 11-7 that drag bank pilots into 9-12 month cycles. This is exactly the Coinbase-shaped grind I like. But naming the schlep zone and *running into it* are different things — the dossier explicitly notes "no SOC 2 or compliance attestation disclosed." Eighteen months from now Incandor either has two named bank logos that survived procurement, or it has a beautifully unsupervised model nobody at JPMorgan is allowed to deploy. The schlep compounds into a moat only if Matthew Yekell personally sits through the compliance reviews; if Incandor flinches, the zone becomes the grave.

On hacker genealogy, the single anchor is Patrick Collison winning Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp at 16 — that's the bar for "named technical artifact pre-startup." Luc Rosenzweig has one repository at github.com/Rosenzweig per the search snippet; Matthew Yekell has no GitHub found. NVIDIA and Epic Games are real shops and the input-handling adjacency is genuine, but employer prestige is credential, not artifact. The Stanford CS line is the same line on a thousand pre-seed dossiers in this batch.

The cofounder configuration would have to flip to push this score up a tier: if the dossier confirmed Matthew Yekell and Luc Rosenzweig had built something together at Stanford for two years pre-Incandor — a class project, a prior hack, a failed v0 — that's Huffman/Ohanian dorm-roommate territory and worth the full 10. What I have is "both attended Stanford CS," which is the weakest version of the long-relationship pattern. Pair, not solo, so not a red flag; chemistry unverified, so not a green one.

My 36-month forecast: Incandor reaches one or two named bank pilots, hits $300K-$800K ARR, fails to displace BioCatch on any tier-1 contract, and gets absorbed by Sift or LexisNexis for $15-40M in an acqui-hire-flavored deal — the Kiko outcome, not the Twitch one. The mechanism is the dimensional signature: the schlep zone is real enough to produce a few pilots but the missing animal evidence means Matthew Yekell and Luc Rosenzweig won't outgrind a Permira-owned incumbent with a decade of labeled bank fraud data through the years of regulated procurement required to win the category.

The strongest bear-case-against-pass argument is the Coinbase precedent: Armstrong's compliance appetite *was* the moat, and Incandor's label-free architecture is genuinely awkward for BioCatch and Feedzai to copy without cannibalizing their supervised contracts — a structural wedge I usually love. That stings because schlep-as-moat is *my* essay. But Armstrong rescued his rejected application with a working artifact; Incandor's dossier has the schlep zone and zero rescue artifact, and at pre-seed I'm buying the founder, not the wedge. I'd flip to invest tomorrow on one verifiable condition: a signed pilot with a named top-20 US bank or a published third-party benchmark of the >99% ATO claim. Without that, pass.
