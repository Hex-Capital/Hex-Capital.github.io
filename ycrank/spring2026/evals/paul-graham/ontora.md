# Ontora -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Max Arnold built agentic SAP process mining at Porsche that now runs across four Volkswagen subsidiaries. That's the strongest single fact in Ontora's dossier, and it's genuinely unusual for a pre-seed founder. Most 25-year-olds pitching enterprise process tools have read about the space; Arnold shipped production software inside one of the world's most bureaucratic manufacturing companies. When I backed Patrick Collison, part of what made the bet was that he'd already built and sold Auctomatic — he wasn't theorizing about developer tools, he'd made one. Arnold has a version of that: he lived inside enterprise process mining, saw its limitations firsthand, and is now building the thing he wished existed. Leon Iwanowitsch adds consulting-domain experience — he built AI automations at consultancies, so he knows the buyer persona from the inside. David Korn brings data pipeline engineering from finance. Ontora's founding team has genuine domain coverage across the technical, commercial, and data layers of the problem they're attacking.

But here's what bothers me. Ontora's origin story is that the three co-founders quit their jobs after a trip to Stockholm to visit fast-scaling startups. That's a "let's start a company" narrative, not a "I couldn't stop thinking about this problem" narrative. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive — the frustration preceded the company. The Octopart founders were physics students who couldn't find electronic parts. Ontora reads more like Arnold had strong domain experience, Iwanowitsch had consulting connections, and they identified a market opportunity at the intersection. Arnold's Porsche work gives this more substance than a typical market-research founding, but the dossier contains no moment of personal frustration, no story of a specific failure that haunted them. Ontora's problem statement is framed in TAM language — "$31.5 billion annually," "$2.4M yearly per 1,000-person company" — which is how consultants frame problems, not how obsessed builders frame them.

"AI agents that interview every employee to map how work gets done" — I want to be direct about this: nobody hears that pitch and thinks it's a bad idea. It sounds great. It sounds like exactly the kind of thing you'd see in a TechCrunch trend piece about AI disrupting consulting. The $500K-to-$50K cost reduction, the months-to-hours time compression, the 10%-to-100% coverage improvement — these are numbers that make every investor nod. And that's the problem. My entire framework for identifying outsized returns depends on the idea initially seeming wrong. Coinbase launched when serious people dismissed cryptocurrency. Airbnb launched when investors said strangers would never sleep in each other's apartments. Ontora launches into "AI replaces expensive consultants," which is the single most consensus-friendly AI narrative of 2025-2026. Celonis has $2.4B in funding and ~$771M in revenue; Skan AI has $57.6M raised. Ontora's conversational approach is a genuine twist — tacit knowledge that never appears in system logs — but it's an incremental differentiation, not a paradigm that repels before it attracts. Any team with access to frontier LLMs can build structured AI interviews. The moat has to come from somewhere other than the technology.

Ontora's Vertiv testimonial — "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" — is real signal. A Strategy Manager at a publicly traded $6B+ company used the product and reported transformative value. For a pre-seed company, landing even one genuine enterprise deployment demonstrates execution. But I notice something the dossier doesn't address: when Ontora sends AI agents to interview every employee about how they actually do their work, it's asking people to be honest with a machine that reports to their C-suite. The employees with the most valuable tacit knowledge — the ones doing creative workarounds, undocumented shortcuts, unofficial processes — are exactly the ones with the most to lose from transparent reporting. Ontora is building a surveillance tool that calls itself a knowledge tool. This isn't a technical problem that better LLMs solve; it's a human trust problem that may fundamentally limit the quality of what Ontora can extract. The dossier mentions no validation methodology or accuracy benchmarks for interview quality.

The bull case an advocate would make: Arnold's Porsche deployment across four VW subsidiaries is Stripe-level domain credibility for a pre-seed founder. Ontora attacks a schlep-heavy domain — enterprise procurement cycles, data sensitivity, organizational politics — that repels polished founders, creating a natural moat through complexity. The consulting displacement economics are genuinely compelling ($50K vs. $500K+). And Ontora's "Unified Context Layer" positions the company not just as a consulting replacement but as infrastructure for enterprise AI deployments — if 95% of GenAI pilots fail because agents lack operational context, Ontora becomes the foundation layer that makes them work. That's a platform play hiding inside a consulting sale. The team relocated from Germany to San Francisco, giving up careers at Porsche and established consultancies — that's commitment, not tourism. I take this seriously. But the advocate's case depends on Ontora building defensibility faster than Celonis can add a conversational feature to their existing platform, and on solving the employee trust problem at scale. Neither is demonstrated yet, and the idea's consensus appeal means they'll face well-funded competition from every direction.

Arnold's builder history — sterna.studio (renewable energy SaaS, 7 engineers), Berlin Senate Honor for STEM education, multiple hackathon wins (ETH InCube x Roche, Telekom XR Challenge second place for €50,000, Intel Liftoff) — shows someone who builds compulsively across domains. That's the earnest builder signal I weight. Iwanowitsch and Korn are thinner in the dossier, with no public GitHub repos and less visible building history. Ontora's learning rate is hard to assess — the product page lists four distinct modules (Process Discovery Agent, Meeting Agent, Unified Context Layer, API/MCP Server), which could indicate rapid iteration or premature feature sprawl. No weekly growth metrics, no pivot history, no before-and-after product evolution. At pre-seed this absence is expected, but it means I'm scoring on priors rather than evidence.

Ontora occupies an uncomfortable position in my framework: strong founder-market fit (Arnold's Porsche experience is legitimately excellent) wrapped around an idea that sounds good to everyone in a space where well-capitalized incumbents can replicate the core capability. The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific: show me evidence that Ontora's AI interviews extract information that employees demonstrably would not share with human consultants — that the AI medium itself is the unlock, not just the speed and cost. If the conversational approach produces qualitatively different knowledge (not just faster and cheaper knowledge), then Celonis can't replicate it by bolting on an LLM, and the consensus-sounding idea actually contains a non-obvious insight. Without that evidence, Ontora is a competent team executing a good idea in a crowded space — which is exactly the kind of bet I avoid because the returns have already been competed away.
