# Standard Signal -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Michael Royzen trained what he claims were "the best-performing coding LLMs of 2023-2024" and built Phind to 150M+ searches. That's real specific knowledge -- years of production LLM training that would take a competitor significant time to replicate. But Standard Signal isn't an AI search engine. It's a hedge fund. And the specific knowledge required to generate alpha in financial markets is not the same specific knowledge required to train a coding model. Renaissance Technologies' edge was never their software architecture -- it was Jim Simons' decades of mathematical insight about market structure, applied through code. Royzen has the code half. The dossier documents zero quantitative finance experience -- no quant roles, no finance research, no prior engagement with markets. Standard Signal's open job listings for "Quantitative Researcher" confirm the founder knows this gap exists. When I ask my foundational question -- if Royzen disappeared, would the company's core financial insight survive? -- the answer is that no documented financial insight exists yet to survive or not.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Royzen is a second-time YC founder, age ~22, who already built a production LLM system at scale. The thesis behind Standard Signal is that LLM reasoning now subsumes the financial analysis that previously required human quant researchers -- meaning LLM training expertise IS the new specific knowledge for generating alpha. One person monitoring 2,847+ trading pairs at 12ms latency is the shrinking company thesis pushed to its logical limit. If the claimed Sharpe ratio of 2.0+ holds under third-party audit, Standard Signal would validate that AI-native leverage can replace entire floors of analysts. The incumbents -- Renaissance, Two Sigma, Citadel -- face a version of the principal-agent problem I identified at Google when evaluating Perplexity: their organizations are optimized around human-designed statistical signals, and transitioning to LLM-autonomous trading would require rearchitecting core investment processes while protecting existing AUM. Royzen, as a principal with no legacy to protect, can move where their agents cannot.

But here is where my framework exposes what a sector-focused investor might miss. Standard Signal's leverage architecture is bifurcated in a way that Perplexity's never was. Perplexity is pure permissionless leverage -- zero marginal cost per query, scales to billions of users with code alone. Standard Signal's alpha generation is permissionless leverage -- AI replacing human analysts, beautiful. But the business scales through capital leverage, the most permissioned form there is. LP capital requires trust, audited track records (Standard Signal has none verified -- the 2.0+ Sharpe ratio is self-reported with no time period, auditor, or context), regulatory compliance (SEC registration, Investment Advisers Act, potentially CFTC), and institutional relationships. You cannot raise a hedge fund permissionlessly. Every dollar of AUM requires permission from someone with a checkbook and a compliance committee. Numerai, with $550M AUM and a crowdsourced tournament structure, at least attempted to make the signal-generation side permissionless. Standard Signal at $100,000 minimum investment is a traditional gated fund -- no structural access expansion, no middleman removal, no democratization.

Royzen's principal authenticity signal is the genuinely ambiguous dimension. He's been building since age 12 -- iOS apps, SmartLens at 16, Phind through YC S22. That's a builder's biography, not an agent's resume. But Phind raised $10.4M in December 2025 per Axios, and Royzen launched Standard Signal in Spring 2026 -- months later. The dossier flags that "circumstances of Royzen's departure from Phind are not publicly documented." A founder who leaves a recently-funded company to start something entirely different in an adjacent-but-distinct domain triggers my filter for people who are passionate about founding rather than about solving a specific problem. The strongest counter: maybe Royzen's LLM training work at Phind revealed something about financial reasoning that made this transition inevitable. But no evidence in the dossier supports that narrative -- no prior finance projects, no published financial ML research, no personal trading history documented. He didn't discover this problem through lived obsession. He appears to have identified it as an opportunity.

Standard Signal's team-to-value ratio is genuinely striking -- one person operating what claims to be an autonomous trading system across 2,847+ pairs. This is the Midjourney pattern (163 employees, $500M revenue) compressed further. If the system works, Standard Signal could generate institutional-scale returns with a team under five. But open-source AI hedge fund frameworks already exist (the dossier cites virattt/ai-hedge-fund on GitHub), and the concept of "AI-autonomous trading" is increasingly consensus -- not contrarian. Well-funded quant funds can hire LLM talent and combine it with decades of accumulated financial specific knowledge. When everyone at demo day is excited about the same category, my prior shifts negative. "AI hedge fund" in 2026 has the same energy as "AI wrapper" in 2024 -- the concept is obvious, the execution moat is unproven.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: a third-party audited track record of 12+ months showing the claimed Sharpe ratio of 2.0+ on live capital, combined with evidence that Royzen has hired or partnered with someone who brings the quant finance specific knowledge he lacks. That combination would resolve the bifurcated specific knowledge problem and prove the leverage architecture works in practice. Without it, Standard Signal is a technically capable founder applying real AI expertise to a domain where he has no documented edge, inside a business model that scales through permissioned capital rather than permissionless code. The AI is leveraged. The business is not.
