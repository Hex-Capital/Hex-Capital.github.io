# Ndea -- Paul Graham Evaluation

François Chollet didn't discover this problem. He defined it. He spent nine years at Google building Keras — which became the most widely used deep learning framework in the world — and that experience convinced him that the entire paradigm was pointed at the wrong destination. So he created ARC-AGI, the benchmark that measures what current systems can't do: genuine fluid reasoning, the kind of generalization a four-year-old manages effortlessly. Then he built the $1M competition around it. Then he left Google. The sequence matters. This isn't someone who noticed AGI was trending and decided to start a company. This is someone who spent a decade building the tools, articulating the theory, and constructing the measurement apparatus — and then concluded he had to build the answer himself. I have rarely seen founder-problem fit this deep. Chollet's relationship to the problem of machine intelligence is comparable to what the Collisons had with payments, except Chollet also wrote the test that proves the existing solutions don't work.

The organic discovery here is extraordinary, and the schlep is real — just not the kind I usually write about. Stripe's schlep was banks and regulations. Ndea's schlep is that the entire AI industry has invested hundreds of billions of dollars into scaling transformers, and pivoting to program synthesis would mean admitting that investment might be aimed at a ceiling. This is a structural version of schlep blindness: frontier labs aren't avoiding program synthesis because it's painful, they're avoiding it because acknowledging its potential threatens the value of everything they've already built. That's actually a stronger form of competitive protection than regulatory moats. OpenAI can't easily pursue Ndea's approach without undermining the narrative that powers their $30B+ in fundraising. Chollet sees this clearly because he's been arguing it publicly for years.

The Knoop pairing makes sense in the way that the best co-founder relationships do — the technical visionary and the operational builder who've already worked together. Knoop co-founded Zapier at YC S12 and scaled it to millions of users, which means he knows how to turn a technical insight into a business. They collaborated through the ARC Prize Foundation before founding Ndea, so the relationship predates the company. They've raised $43M from Coatue, Factorial, and Quiet Capital, which gives them years of runway at 15 people. The team composition looks right: a world-class researcher, a proven startup operator, and a growing group of specialists in an underexplored field.

Here's where my framework struggles. My most important test — "make something people want" — is unanswerable. There is no product. There are no users. There's no waitlist, no weekly growth rate, no path to ramen profitability. Ndea is a research lab betting that program synthesis is to AGI what deep learning circa 2012 was to narrow AI. If they're right, the upside is literally unbounded. If they're wrong, $43M evaporates over a few years of research that goes nowhere. I can't apply the 5-7% weekly growth test to a company whose product is a fundamental scientific breakthrough. This is the Boom Supersonic pattern for me — where the ambition is so large it overrides my normal measurement apparatus.

But there's a difference between Boom and Ndea that I need to be honest about. Boom was building something we knew was physically possible — supersonic passenger flight had existed. The question was whether you could make it commercially viable. Ndea is pursuing something that may not work at all through their chosen approach. The ARC-AGI 2024 competition showed program synthesis lifting scores from 33% to 55.5%, which is genuine progress but still far from the 85% human baseline. And even if they crack the benchmark, translating that into "a factory for scientific advancement" requires additional leaps that no one has demonstrated. This company needs at least two miracles: program synthesis must scale beyond constrained benchmarks, and the resulting capabilities must be commercializable. Each additional miracle multiplies the risk.

What would have to be true for this to be a defining investment? Program synthesis would need to be genuinely the right paradigm for AGI — not just a complement to deep learning, but a fundamental unlock. Chollet's decade of theoretical work would need to be directionally correct, not just intellectually coherent. And Ndea would need to maintain its talent advantage long enough to build something defensible before Google DeepMind or another well-funded lab redirects resources toward the same approach. If all three are true, this is one of the most important companies ever founded. The probability isn't high. But the founder who created both the problem definition and the measurement framework is the person you'd pick to attempt the solution. I'd write a small check on the strength of that founder-problem fit alone, knowing that most of my evaluation criteria are inapplicable to a company this early in the research cycle.
