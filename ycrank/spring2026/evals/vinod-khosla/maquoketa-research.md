# Maquoketa Research -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Maquoketa Research: everybody already agrees with them. Ukraine has ordered thousands of loitering munitions. Germany is writing €9 billion in contracts to drone startups. The U.S. Army launched PBAS. AeroVironment can't build Switchblades fast enough. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing in telecom would never happen. When I backed Impossible Foods, people thought plant-based hamburgers were a joke. When I wrote a $50 million check to OpenAI, AI was "laughable." The signal I look for is experts dismissing you. In autonomous attack drones, the experts aren't dismissing anyone -- they're handing out billion-dollar contracts. Maquoketa isn't entering a market that the consensus rejects. They're the sixth entrant into a market that the consensus has already validated and lavishly funded. Anduril has raised $3.5 billion. Helsing is at €12 billion valuation. Neros has $121 million and a U.S. Army PBAS selection. When the party is this crowded, the consequence of one more company showing up is fundamentally diminished.

The structural argument is real -- I'll grant that. Defense primes like Lockheed and Raytheon have profit models built on high-margin, low-volume manned platforms. Pivoting to high-volume, low-cost expendable drones cannibalizes their core business. That's the kind of incumbent structural paralysis I love: Cisco couldn't build the TCP/IP router because it would destroy their proprietary networking margins. And the evidence bears this out -- Germany awarded its strike drone contracts to Helsing and Stark, not to Rheinmetall. But recognizing this structural opening doesn't require Maquoketa specifically. Five well-funded startups already occupy this gap. The question isn't whether expendable autonomous drones will displace expensive manned systems -- that's settled. The question is whether a five-person pre-seed team with no disclosed contracts brings something that Anduril's thousands of engineers and Helsing's €1.37 billion war chest cannot.

The in-house phased array and RF design capability is the most interesting technical signal here. Hernandez's specialization at Berkeley's Wireless Research Center maps directly to electronic warfare resistance and autonomous guidance -- the capabilities that separate a mil-spec loitering munition from a modified commercial FPV drone. Most drone startups assemble commercial off-the-shelf components; building custom RF hardware is genuinely harder and potentially more defensible. But here's my concern: Hernandez is doing exactly what his training prepared him to do. A Berkeley EE from the Wireless Research Center building phased array systems for drones is not an outsider reimagining an industry. It's an expert executing the expert playbook. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree attempting orbital launch. That mismatch between the person and the industry is where I find the irreverence and first-principles thinking that produces category-defining companies. Yeager's background in trading technology at SpiderRock at least introduces some cross-domain perspective, but the team composition reads as technically competent within their domain, not as outsiders willing to break things the establishment considers sacred.

Let me engage honestly with the bull case, because there is one. If Maquoketa's phased array technology delivers meaningfully superior EW resistance -- the ability to operate in GPS-denied, heavily jammed environments where COTS-based drones fail -- that could be a genuine 10x capability advantage, not an incremental improvement. Modern battlefields are increasingly defined by electronic warfare; a drone that functions when competitors' drones go blind commands premium pricing and procurement priority. Militaries also want supply chain diversity -- they won't sole-source from one vendor. So even in a crowded market, there's room for a sixth supplier with differentiated capability. And AeroVironment's production bottleneck (new factory not operational until late 2026/2027) creates a literal time window. If Maquoketa can deliver units while AeroVironment is still building its factory, the timing could be perfect. For this to be a great investment, you'd need the phased array capability to translate into demonstrated battlefield superiority that commands contracts despite the team's lack of procurement relationships and security clearances. That's a lot of simultaneous requirements.

The timing trajectory is the company's strongest dimension, but it's consensus timing rather than pre-consensus. I look for exponential curves before they become obvious -- OpenAI in 2019, food tech in 2010, cleantech in 2004. Autonomous attack drones in 2026 are where AI was in 2024: everyone already sees it. The curve is real and accelerating, but the option value of being early has been captured by companies that entered 2-3 years ago. Maquoketa isn't skating to where the puck will be; they're skating to where it already is, alongside well-capitalized competitors who arrived first. Defense procurement cycles of 12-36 months compound this problem -- with no disclosed SBIR awards, OTAs, or contracts, the pre-revenue period could consume their entire runway before the first dollar arrives.

I pass on this one. Not because expendable autonomous drones aren't important -- they clearly represent a structural shift in how militaries project force. But because Maquoketa's specific success doesn't produce a consequence that's distinguishable from any of five better-funded competitors' success. The technology is competent, the timing is correct, and the team covers the core disciplines. But competent execution of a consensus thesis in a crowded field is precisely the kind of bet where you've reduced the probability of failure to the point where the consequence of your particular success is modest. Build a nice defense company, maybe. Transform how the world works, no. I look for founders attempting things the world considers crazy. Everyone considers autonomous attack drones smart money right now.
