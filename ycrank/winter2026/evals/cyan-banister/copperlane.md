# Copperlane -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what immediately caught my attention: both founders went through co-founder breakups during the same YC batch, then found each other and decided to start over together. On paper it sounds like a meet-cute -- two kids from mortgage families in the same hometown, both picking themselves up off the floor and choosing to keep building. But I've been burned by this exact pattern. HQ Trivia had perfect product-market timing and a transformative product, and it was destroyed entirely by founder conflict. The thing about co-founder relationships formed under pressure -- whether it's a batch deadline or a funding crunch -- is that pressure bonding is not the same as stress-tested trust. These two have known each other for weeks, maybe a couple months, and they're about to enter one of the most regulated, demanding sales environments in fintech. I need to see how they fight before I know whether they can build together, and there simply hasn't been enough time for that signal to emerge.

When I ask myself the question I always start with -- tell me how you got here, not the business -- what I hear is two exceptionally credentialed young people from elite programs (Princeton CS, UPenn M&T) whose parents work at Freddie Mac, Fannie Mae, and FHFA. That's genuine domain familiarity. They grew up at dinner tables where mortgage origination pain points were the ambient conversation. I respect that. But it's not the kind of biographical grit that makes me lean forward. There's no survival story here, no years of being told no, no outsider perspective forged through hardship. Compare this to how I felt meeting the Contraline founder, who had spent years in academic obscurity working on male contraception while everyone told him the market didn't exist. That biographical commitment -- where the founder and the problem are inseparable -- is what I'm listening for, and I don't hear it here. What I hear is smart insiders who identified a logical application of AI to a space they know through family. That's competence, not calling.

The idea itself is solid but thoroughly consensus. AI agents for mortgage document processing is exactly the kind of "AI for X" application that every VC I know is seeing fifteen versions of right now. Blend has $685M in funding, Maxwell is at $104M, Salesforce just launched Agentforce with mortgage-specific templates, and Zeitro is positioning as AI-first in the same space. The most promising companies I back tend to be unpopular and difficult to assess -- SpaceX when private space was absurd, Crusoe Energy when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded insane. "Let's use AI to automate mortgage intake" is neither unpopular nor difficult to assess. It's the obvious next step, and when something is obvious, the price of being right goes down while the competition goes up. The commoditization risk the dossier identifies is real -- the underlying capabilities (LLM document reading, form extraction, verification) are improving for everyone, not just Copperlane.

Let me engage honestly with the bull case, because there is one. The mortgage origination cost of $11,800 per loan is genuinely painful, and it ultimately gets passed to borrowers -- many of whom are first-time homebuyers stretching to afford their first home. If Copperlane succeeds, it could meaningfully reduce the cost of homeownership for millions of Americans. That resonates with my thesis about creating economic access. The regulatory complexity of mortgage (TRID, RESPA, ECOA, state licensing) is real and does create a moat that deters generalist AI tools -- I've always believed regulatory barriers are competitive advantages for founders who can navigate them. And the parents-at-Freddie/Fannie/FHFA network could provide informal regulatory knowledge and industry connections that most early-career founders simply cannot access. If the co-founder relationship solidifies, if they can navigate enterprise sales cycles with a two-person team, and if they can build sufficient domain-specific data advantages before Salesforce's horizontal agents reach acceptable quality, this could become the Veeva of mortgage origination. But that's a lot of "ifs" for a team that formed weeks ago.

The timing catalyst is legitimate -- LLM capabilities crossing the document reasoning threshold while origination costs are at record highs creates a genuine window. But good timing in a consensus category mostly benefits the best-resourced competitor, not the scrappiest. Blend has $685M and existing lender relationships. Salesforce has the CRM footprint. For Copperlane to win, they need something besides timing -- they need the kind of founder-driven intensity and domain obsession that lets a two-person team outmaneuver organizations with hundreds of engineers. I'm not seeing evidence of that intensity yet. What I see is a sensible bet by capable people, and sensible bets by capable people are exactly the kind of thing that other investors are better positioned to evaluate than I am.
