# Taxnova -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Taxnova is the competitive landscape, and it tells me something the founders might not want it to. SPRX has raised $46M. Boast.ai has $30M. Neo.Tax has $13M. That's nearly $90M of venture capital validating the proposition that R&D tax credits should be automated. When that much money has already flowed into a thesis, you're not in the sweet spot of "seems like a bad idea but is a good idea" — you're in the territory where smart people with large checkbooks already agree with you. Taxnova's differentiation is "global-first" coverage and an advisor-agnostic model, which are legitimate strategic positions, but they're positioning moves within a consensus market, not contrarian bets against it. The HMRC inquiry rate jumping from 0.5% to 20% is a compelling "why now" for the UK wedge specifically, but it's the kind of fact that appears in a well-researched market analysis, not the kind of insight that repels people before it attracts them.

George Nichkov comes from Oliver Wyman consulting and product leadership at Gett. Maria Malykh ran programs at Criteo coordinating nine R&D teams and previously co-founded awaitly. These are competent B2B operators with reasonable backgrounds for building enterprise software. But neither has a tax background. The tax expertise — EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, IRS — lives in the assembled team and advisory network, not in the founders themselves. I keep coming back to a question I can't answer from this dossier: how did George and Maria end up in R&D tax credits? There's no origin story of personal frustration. No moment where one of them filed a claim and thought "this is insane." The dossier describes the problem in market-size language — "$10B R&D tax market" — rather than through a lived experience. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, he could tell you the exact day he forgot his USB drive on the bus. When founders can't tell you the specific moment of frustration, it usually means the idea came from analysis rather than experience. That's not fatal, but it's the difference between building something you desperately need and building something you calculated should exist.

The $100K ARR with named customers like incident.io and Paddle is genuinely strong for a pre-seed company that launched publicly in February 2026. Real revenue from real companies is the hardest thing to fake. The pilot with a Top 5 R&D advisory firm and the enterprise deal at $200K+ ACV potential suggest Taxnova can sell. But I can't see the founder stories beneath these numbers. Did they personally do the first claims by hand before automating? Did they build the integrations in a weekend to close incident.io? The dossier gives me metrics without narratives, and narratives are what I use to evaluate determination. Maria's Substack documenting the building-in-public journey is a positive sign — it suggests reflective practice and genuine engagement with the process — but I'd need to read it to know if it reveals the obsessive quality I look for or just professional diligence.

Taxnova's advisor-agnostic positioning is the smartest strategic decision in this dossier. Where Boast.ai tries to replace advisory firms and risks triggering defensive responses, Taxnova builds infrastructure for them — selling picks and shovels during the gold rush. This mirrors the dynamic that made Stripe work: instead of competing with banks, Patrick and John built the layer that sat beneath them. But the parallel breaks down at the founder level. The Collisons were teenage programmers building payment APIs for programmers — hackers building for hackers in the purest sense. Nichkov and Malykh are consultants and program managers building compliance software for accountants and CFOs. The structural insight is similar; the founder-problem intimacy is not. Stripe's moat grew from developers who would never switch away from an API they'd integrated. Taxnova's moat depends on advisory firms continuing to choose their infrastructure over building in-house, which is a relationship dependency rather than a technical lock-in.

For the bull case: if Taxnova can lock in three to five major advisory firms as channel partners before SPRX or Neo.Tax expand internationally, the multi-jurisdiction complexity becomes a genuine moat. Each new country's tax code is a schlep that deters competitors — and that's real. The assembled team with Big 4 backgrounds could execute this faster than any pure-tech competitor. If the $200K+ ACV enterprise deal closes and becomes a template, Taxnova could reach $1M ARR within a year on a handful of large contracts, making the business capital-efficient despite the well-funded competition. The advisor channel could provide distribution leverage that a $1M pre-seed couldn't buy through direct sales. For this to work, the founders would need to demonstrate the kind of relentless execution on partnerships and jurisdictional expansion that turns a positioning advantage into an actual barrier — and I'd need to see evidence of that intensity, which the dossier doesn't provide.

Taxnova is a legitimate business built by competent operators in a real market with early traction that most pre-seed companies would envy. But nothing about it activates my framework. The founders didn't discover this problem organically — they identified a market opportunity and assembled a team to pursue it. The idea isn't contrarian — $89M in competitor funding proves the consensus already formed. The founder profiles are professional but not obsessive; capable but not the "animal" quality I'm scanning for. The two dimensions that most determine my verdict are Organic Problem Discovery, where the absent origin story and market-analysis framing push the score below my default, and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where the heavily funded competitive landscape confirms this is consensus territory. I'm passing — not because Taxnova won't succeed, but because it's not my kind of bet.
