# Fenrock AI (YC W26) -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I look at is how the founders found the problem. And what I see here concerns me. Charu Sharma's last company was NextPlay.ai -- an HR tech platform for mentorship programs at Fortune 500 companies. It was shut down because it couldn't scale revenue. Now she's building AI agents for financial crime compliance. There's no visible thread connecting those two things. No story about working inside a bank's compliance department and watching analysts drown in false positives. No experience filing SARs or navigating FinCEN requirements. The dossier reveals an MBA from Chicago Booth and time as an EiR at SignalFire and venture scout at Afore Capital -- roles where you see a lot of fintech deal flow. That's how you identify a large market with entrenched incumbents. It's not how you discover a problem organically. This has the signature of someone who sat down and asked "what's a big, painful market where AI agents could win?" rather than someone who stumbled into a broken workflow through lived experience.

The schlep in AML compliance is real and enormous. Banks deal with regulatory frameworks across jurisdictions, explainability requirements that make black-box AI unusable, audit trails that regulators actually examine, and procurement cycles that last a year or more. That's exactly the kind of ugly, regulatory-heavy problem that causes schlep blindness -- most founders unconsciously avoid it because the work is tedious and the sales cycle is brutal. But here's the distinction that matters: recognizing that a schlep exists is not the same as having been burned by it. The Collison brothers didn't study the payments market from the outside and decide it was underserved. They were programmers who personally hated integrating Stripe's predecessors. The organic origin was the foundation for everything that followed -- it gave them the conviction to endure years of banking negotiations that would have broken founders who merely thought payments looked like a good opportunity.

Let me give the bull case its due, because there is one. AML compliance is genuinely ripe for disruption by agentic AI in a way it wasn't even two years ago. The reasoning chains required for compliance analysis -- gathering evidence across databases, cross-referencing sanctions lists, drafting case narratives -- map well onto what modern LLM-based agents can do. The timing is legitimate. And Charu Sharma is clearly a capable operator: she was named VentureBeat's AI Entrepreneur of the Year in 2019, recruited to LinkedIn to grow Talent Solutions revenue, and managed to get General Catalyst's Hemant Taneja to fund a prior venture. If the unnamed second team member turns out to be a deep compliance domain expert or a strong technical builder, that would change the picture meaningfully. The company would also benefit from enormous switching costs once embedded -- compliance tools become load-bearing infrastructure that regulators themselves become familiar with.

But that bull case requires several things to be true simultaneously that I can't verify. The second founder's identity and background are unknown. No GitHub profile exists for Charu. Her background -- physics/economics undergrad, MBA, LinkedIn sales, venture capital -- suggests someone who is strong at fundraising and enterprise relationships, not someone who writes code. Six hundred speaking appearances and TEDx conferences organized is impressive for building a personal brand, but it's not building product. And the competitive landscape is brutal: Unit21 already advertises "AI Agents" for AML, ComplyAdvantage has $167M raised and $27M in revenue, Quantexa sits at a $2.6B valuation. These aren't hypothetical future competitors -- they're building the same thing right now with established customer bases and regulatory track records.

The combination I'm seeing -- a domain pivot from HR tech, no visible organic connection to the problem, no evidence of technical building capability, zero traction signals, a prior company that shut down rather than pivoted, and a crowded competitive field with well-funded incumbents already incorporating agentic AI -- doesn't match the pattern of companies I invest in. The strongest YC companies I've backed were built by founders who couldn't stop thinking about their problem because they'd lived inside it for years. I don't see that here. I see someone who correctly identified a large, painful market and positioned an AI narrative around it. That's smart, but it's not the same thing.
