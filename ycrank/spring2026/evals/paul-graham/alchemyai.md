# Alchemy -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me first about Alchemy isn't the product or the market -- it's the name. There's already an Alchemy in tech, a blockchain infrastructure company that's raised $345 million and even launched its own "AlchemyAI" tools. This is the kind of unforced error that makes me wonder about judgment. A truly resourceful founder would have either noticed this landmine during a five-minute Google search and picked a different name, or already have a strategy for fighting through it. The fact that neither is evident is a small signal, but it's the kind of small signal that compounds. "If someone seems slippery, or bogus, or a jerk, don't ignore it" -- and if someone seems careless about a basic positioning decision, don't ignore that either.

The idea itself sits in classic schlep-blindness territory. I wrote about this years ago: "Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." Researchers have been suffering through ImageJ and CellProfiler for decades, manually scripting pipelines, spending twenty hours on analysis that should take minutes. It's exactly the kind of unsexy, domain-specific tooling problem that sits unsolved because talented engineers would rather build the next social app. The structural dynamics are right too -- hardware vendors like Leica and Zeiss can't easily unbundle their analysis software from their microscope sales channel, and open-source projects lack the commercial incentive to build agentic natural-language interfaces. There's a real gap here. "It's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site," and the fact that vision-language models now make natural-language pipeline assembly feasible for the first time gives this a legitimate "why now." I don't dismiss the opportunity.

But here's what I keep coming back to: "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." Baek studied biomedical engineering and did EP mapping at Boston Scientific -- that's adjacent to microscopy, not inside it. He also has an M.Ed. in psychological counseling and a marketing internship at Tropicana, which reads more like someone exploring than someone obsessed. McBride is technically strong -- a patent at nineteen is a real signal, and UIUC's CS program is excellent -- but his documented interests are AI safety and hardware-software co-design, not bioimage analysis. Neither founder has documented experience building computational biology tools, working in a research imaging lab, or contributing to the open-source bioimage ecosystem they're trying to displace. When I think about Stripe, the Collisons understood payment integration pain because they'd built things that needed payments. When I think about Airbnb, the founders were literally sleeping on air mattresses in their users' apartments. I'm looking for evidence that these founders have *lived* this problem, and I can't find it.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: two technically capable young founders identified a genuine schlep through systematic market analysis, and their combined bio-engineering plus ML-engineering skill set is exactly shaped to solve it. If they're earnest, if they're doing things that don't scale with early lab customers, if they're the kind of people who would show up at a biology department with a laptop and say "give me your microscopy images right now and let me show you" -- the Collison-installation equivalent for bioimage analysis -- then this could work. The market is growing at fifteen percent CAGR, the incumbents are structurally disadvantaged, and a well-executed vertical AI tool with workflow lock-in could become the default. Grant-funded open-source tools with no commercial support cannot compete on UX when the alternative is describing your analysis in plain English. If these founders turn out to be cockroaches -- the kind who won't die no matter what -- this is a viable path.

But I have no evidence any of that is true. No traction signals: no customer count, no revenue, no user love quotes, no named pilot labs. No GitHub repos for either founder -- for a product that's fundamentally an ML engineering platform, this absence is notable. No shared history between the co-founders that I can see, and "the relationship between the founders has to be strong" -- startups do to that relationship what a dog does to a sock. No evidence of doing things that don't scale. The dossier gives me a sensible idea in a real market with a team that's approximately the right shape, but nothing that makes me lean forward in my chair. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people" -- and at pre-seed, I'm picking founders, not markets. I need to see determination, resourcefulness, earnestness. The signal here is simply too thin for me to have conviction.

The defensibility picture reinforces my concern. Every potential moat -- data flywheel from pipeline interactions, workflow lock-in via publications and SOPs, a library of validated domain-specific nodes -- is hypothetical. Today, any ML-capable team with biology consultants could assemble a similar product using open-source models like Cellpose and StarDist. The barrier is execution and domain curation, not technology, which means this is a race where the winner is determined by who understands the customer best. And I'm not yet convinced these founders have that understanding at the depth required.
