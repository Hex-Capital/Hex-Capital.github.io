# ZeroSettle -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most interesting structural fact about ZeroSettle is one that cuts both ways: Apple cannot build this, and RevenueCat won't. Apple would be cannibalizing $27 billion in annual commissions. RevenueCat would be torching its App Store relationship, the foundation of its 70,000-app business. That structural conflict is real, and it's the kind of gap that creates opportunity. But here's my problem: when the gap is this obvious — front-page court rulings, Stripe publishing how-to guides, Neon Pay raising $14M from a16z and Thrive, Paddle explicitly citing the Apple ecosystem opening — you're not looking at an "insane idea." You're looking at a land grab that every sophisticated payments player has already identified. Two former Apple engineers building a billing bypass SDK in the wake of Epic v. Apple isn't a contrarian bet. It's the most consensus trade in mobile payments right now.

Ryan Elliott and Gabe Roeloffs are credible. Both worked on operating systems at Apple, both attended Cal Poly SLO, Elliott holds an Apple patent. They know StoreKit, they know App Review, they know the internal plumbing of how Apple enforces its commission structure. That's the "sane" part, and it's legitimate — these are insiders who understand the system they're arbitraging. But the idea itself doesn't provoke skepticism from anyone. No VC hears "help developers avoid the 30% App Store tax now that the court says they can" and thinks that's crazy. They think it's obvious. And obvious opportunities with massive TAMs attract massive competition. The founder-idea pairing here is sane-sane, which is my least favorite configuration. Returns on consensus plays are competed away before the first check clears.

Where this really breaks for my framework is the moat question. ZeroSettle is a software SDK wrapping Stripe's payment infrastructure with migration campaigns and retention flows. The dossier itself acknowledges the core technology is "replicable." Stripe could build a turnkey version tomorrow — they've already published the plumbing guide. Paddle has $293M in funding, $91M in revenue, and explicitly named this market as a growth driver. RevenueCat has the distribution to 70,000+ apps and could pivot if the strategic calculus changes. ZeroSettle's Merchant of Record role creates some switching friction once subscribers are migrated, but that's a speed bump, not a wall. This is exactly the kind of software-as-the-moat business I've been warning about — the thing that looks defensible today but that a well-resourced competitor replicates in a quarter. The Birchbox lesson applies: if the software IS the business, the business is fragile.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: (1) the regulatory window stays open and Apple never gets to charge fees on linked-out purchases, (2) Stripe and Paddle choose not to build the turnkey migration-and-retention layer, and (3) ZeroSettle captures enough early developer relationships through its Founding Partner Program to build switching-cost lock-in before competition arrives. If all three hold, you could see meaningful revenue flowing through the platform — even 1% of Apple's $27B commission pool would be enormous. The MoR role and entitlement syncing do create genuine integration depth. But that's a lot of "ifs," and the December 2025 Ninth Circuit ruling explicitly left open whether Apple can charge some fee on alternative payment transactions. If Apple imposes even a 10-12% commission on linked-out purchases, the savings gap between 30% and ZeroSettle's 5% + $0.50 narrows to the point where many developers won't bother absorbing the risk of Apple relationship friction.

I also can't find the "infinity story" here. The narrative ceiling is "billing bypass tool for iOS developers" — that's a cost optimization play, not a platform creation story. Compare this to Venmo, which started as peer-to-peer payments between friends and expanded into a new financial primitive that PayPal acquired and built an ecosystem around. ZeroSettle is routing existing payments through a different pipe. There's no new primitive being created, no protocol that others build on, no compounding network effects. It's an application layer on Stripe's infrastructure. With zero public traction — no customers named, no revenue disclosed, no press, no Product Hunt launch — and a Founding Partner Program that gives the product away free for two years, there's no downside cushion either. The investment depends entirely on narrative momentum in a competitive market with unstable regulatory foundations.
