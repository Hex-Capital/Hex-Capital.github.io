# Seeing Systems -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Seeing Systems is that defense drones in 2026 are what AI was in 2024 -- everyone's already at the party. Defense tech VC funding nearly doubled to $49.1 billion last year. Ukraine produces four million drones annually. Stark has a €2.4 billion German armed forces contract. Shield AI sits at $5.6 billion. When I wrote my check to OpenAI in 2019, AI was "laughable" -- that's why the opportunity existed. When I backed cleantech in 2004, most VCs thought I was delusional -- that's why the category was available to create. The Le Maitre brothers are building into an accelerating market, yes, but one where the acceleration is already consensus knowledge and capital is flooding in at rates that compress every first-mover advantage. The question I have to ask is: what does Seeing Systems know that the $49 billion in defense tech capital doesn't?

The honest answer is: modularity. The specific thesis -- that drone platforms become obsolete within weeks due to electronic warfare evolution, and that swappable subsystems beat platform replacement -- is a genuine technical insight born from what's happening in Ukraine right now. The fiber-optic guided Banshee variant that achieves jamming immunity is a concrete engineering response to a real operational problem. This isn't hand-waving about AI; it's a hardware architecture decision that addresses the single biggest battlefield challenge facing drone operators today. And Stark -- their most direct competitor with $100 million raised -- failed all four strikes in UK military trials in October 2025. That's meaningful. But I have to ask the harder question: if modularity is the right answer, why won't Anduril, Shield AI, or any well-funded competitor adopt it within eighteen months? Modularity is an architectural choice, not a decade-long technology moat.

The traction is, I'll admit, genuinely striking for two brothers from Guernsey with $500K. UK Royal Marine Commandos, multiple other UK military units, four NATO forces, prototypes in Ukraine. The Forward Deployed Engineer model -- embedding with each military unit for real-time feedback -- is smart; it's Palantir's playbook applied to hardware, and it creates relationship lock-in that matters in defense procurement. Getting military operators to trust your hardware in contested environments at ages 25 and 28 is not something credentials produce -- it's something execution produces. Matthew's combination of Cambridge robotics research and Jane Street engineering rigor with Alexander's self-taught embedded electronics and prior EOD hardware experience covers the full stack in a way that feels deliberate rather than accidental. These are outsiders who've already demonstrated they can operate inside the military's trust architecture.

But here's where my framework forces a difficult conclusion. If Seeing Systems succeeds completely -- captures significant NATO drone procurement, scales the modular platform, deploys Aerie AI swarm coordination -- what's the consequence? They become a successful defense contractor selling expendable strike drones. A good business, perhaps a billion-dollar business. But the world doesn't operate fundamentally differently. The defense industry still procures through the same channels. Warfare still uses drones the way it was already trending. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means fossil fuel power generation becomes obsolete, or to Impossible Foods, where success reclaims half the planet's arable land. Seeing Systems' success means NATO has better, cheaper modular drones from this particular vendor rather than a competing vendor. The FPV strike drone segment is inherently commodity-trending -- the dossier acknowledges that hardware designs for basic FPV strike drones are "widely available." The consequence of success isn't inconsequential, but it's also not the kind of magnitude that makes me ignore a 90% failure probability.

The strongest bull case would require Aerie AI to become genuinely transformational -- not just swarm coordination software, but an autonomous warfare operating system that fundamentally changes force structure, enabling a handful of operators to control hundreds of drones with minimal training. If that software layer creates a data flywheel from real NATO deployments that no competitor can replicate, and if the modularity thesis proves correct such that fixed-design competitors keep hitting obsolescence cycles, then Seeing Systems could own the interface between human commanders and autonomous drone warfare. That's a consequence worth pursuing. But Aerie AI is listed as "in development," and Shield AI's Hivemind is already generating 30% of their revenue doing similar autonomous piloting at larger scale. The software moat needs to exist before I can underwrite it.

I respect the founders. Two brothers from a tiny island, self-taught and Cambridge-trained in complementary domains, who've gotten military hardware into the hands of elite forces before raising real money -- that's the kind of naive persistence that occasionally produces Rocket Lab. But Peter Beck was building something nobody else was building: orbital launch from New Zealand. The Le Maitre brothers are building in a space where four million drones a year are already being produced and billions in venture capital are chasing the same thesis. The problem of modern warfare is important. But the specific niche -- modular expendable FPV strike drones -- has been identified by too many well-capitalized players for the consequence of this particular team's success to reach the scale I need to see.
