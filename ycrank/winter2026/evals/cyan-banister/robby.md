# Robby -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's my honest reaction: three Harvard MBAs building AI for home services after ServiceTitan just IPO'd at $9 billion doesn't set off the signal I've trained myself to listen for. In my experience, the most promising companies are not popular, they're difficult to assess, and they carry real technology risk. Robby is the inverse -- it's applying the most popular technology category in venture (AI agents) to a market that was just validated by a blockbuster public offering. Every investor in the room can understand this pitch in thirty seconds. That clarity is a feature for most VCs and a warning sign for me. The deals I've made my career on -- SpaceX when private space was considered delusional, Crusoe when mining Bitcoin on gas flares sounded insane -- all shared the quality of making smart people uncomfortable. This makes smart people nod.

If I could sit down with Vineet, Feroze, and Joseph, my first question would be the one I always ask: tell me your story, not the business. And what I see in the dossier is impressive credentials but no biographical thread that binds any of these founders to the pain of running an HVAC company or working as a field technician. Joseph saw home services patterns at Bain consulting on PE acquisitions -- that's professional exposure, and it's valuable, but it's not the same as growing up watching your father's plumbing business leak revenue because nobody followed up on the water heater that was about to fail. Vineet was building an AI customs brokerage tool six months ago. Feroze and Joseph were working on a health-tech startup before this. Three product directions in roughly a year across the founding team tells me they're searching for a market, not solving a problem that won't let them sleep. When I backed Contraline, its founder had spent years in dedicated academic research on male contraception that everyone else ignored. That's inseparability between biography and mission. Here, the mission was discovered through a consulting engagement.

The bull case deserves real engagement. Nine named paying customers at pre-seed is legitimately strong. The claim of $40K closed within the first hour of launch, if verifiable, is the kind of outcome that makes customers sticky. And I genuinely respect that these founders are riding along with technicians, shadowing office positions, and studying for EPA 608 certification -- that kind of embodied domain immersion is exactly how I learn about markets, through experience rather than spreadsheets. It reminds me of how I found Uber through my driver Roger, not through market research. If Joseph's Bain pattern recognition across dozens of PE-backed home services companies gave him the same kind of felt understanding I get from using products myself, there's something real there. The product does make an invisible system visible -- field technicians observe equipment failures, upgrade opportunities, and safety issues every day that evaporate into the air because no system captures them. That pattern echoes what drew me to Carta and Flock Safety. And the data flywheel from accumulating visit-level intelligence could build genuine defensibility over time.

But I keep coming back to the ServiceTitan dependency, which feels structurally precarious. Robby syncs to ServiceTitan and positions itself as a complementary revenue layer. ServiceTitan has already announced Atlas Campaign Recommendations, Smarter Routing, and an SMS Booking Agent for 2026. When the dominant platform with $900 million in revenue decides to build AI features that overlap with your core value proposition, the question isn't whether they'll build it but when. I've seen this dynamic before -- building on top of a platform that controls your data access and could flip a switch. This isn't navigating a hard regulatory space that creates a moat; it's navigating platform risk that creates a ceiling. Arch is already offering overlapping AI lead generation for ServiceTitan contractors, which means even the third-party AI-for-home-services niche isn't uncontested.

The co-founder dynamics are actually the brightest signal here for me. Feroze and Joseph co-founded Lifelong Health together before Robby, which means they've already navigated the tensions of building something that didn't work out. All three bonded at HBS, and Vineet and Feroze went through the Pearce Fellowship together. After what I learned from HQ Trivia -- where the right product at the right time was destroyed by co-founder conflict -- I take prior working relationships seriously. These three have tested their relationship under pressure, which counts for something. But team resilience alone doesn't overcome my fundamental concern: this is a consensus bet by founders who found the market through analysis rather than obsession, in a space where the incumbent platform is actively building toward the same capabilities. I'd want to see what happens when ServiceTitan tightens its API or launches a competing feature -- that's the stress test that will reveal whether this team's commitment to home services is deep enough to survive.
