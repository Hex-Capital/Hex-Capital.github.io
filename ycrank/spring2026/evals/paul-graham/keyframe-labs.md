# Keyframe Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Parth and Kaahan Radia are brothers shipping real-time photoreal video at $0.06/min, and the signature here is awkward: Keyframe Labs scores its highest on the one dimension that costs almost nothing to clear — siblings co-founding, the Collison pattern by default — and its lowest on the schlep test, because Keyframe Labs is pure GPU infrastructure with no compliance grind, no manual ops, no unpleasantness to hide behind. That pairing describes a credentialed-engineer team in a category that already has Tavus ($24.1M raised), HeyGen ($95–100M ARR), and Synthesia ($146M ARR) sitting on top of it. It's the inverse of the profile I keep funding.

The Collison brothers at **Stripe** (2010) are the load-bearing analogy for Keyframe Labs's cofounder axis: two siblings with years of shared technical work, complementary depth, the kind of pair that doesn't fall apart in month nine. Parth at Ericsson leading 30 people on voice/video foundation models and Kaahan at Zipline shipping perception for autonomous drones is exactly the credible complementary coverage I want — generative model lead plus real-time systems engineer. Where Keyframe Labs diverges from the Collison rung is the pre-startup shipped artifact: Patrick and John had already sold **Auctomatic** (2007) for $5M at 19 and 17, and the Radia brothers' joint résumé is employer-credentialed, not founder-credentialed. Brothers ship the relationship; they have not yet shipped a company together.

The pass example I keep in my head — the polished AI-wrapper team with a TAM slide and no schlep — fails because the work described is pure software with no offline grind. Keyframe Labs sits closer to that failure mechanism than I'd like. Real-time photoreal video at 500ms latency is hard ML systems work, but hard is not the same as unpleasant, and "Schlep Blindness" (January 2012) was specifically about work other smart founders unconsciously route around. Tavus, HeyGen, D-ID, Synthesia, and Anam are not routing around real-time avatars; they are racing toward them. Keyframe Labs's $0.06/min price is a positioning bet against subscription incumbents, not a schlep moat. There is no Coinbase-style regulator to befriend, no DoorDash-style driver network to recruit by hand.

On animal resourcefulness, Keyframe Labs did ship two foundation models publicly — Cosmo 1.5 in October 2025 and Persona-1 in January 2026 — both announced on Parth's LinkedIn, which is more pre-funding artifact than most of what walks through the door. That clears my demo-first filter the way Drew Houston's Hacker News video cleared it for **Dropbox** (2007). What it does not clear is the cereal-box bar. The dossier contains no stunt, no unscalable improvisation, no "we paid for compute by doing X." Eight unattributed testimonials on the landing page is marketing, not animal evidence. Compounded over 18 months, the gap matters: animals improvise distribution; credentialed engineers wait for the API to convert.

On organic-idea fit, the counterfactual is clean — what would have to be true for Keyframe Labs to score a tier higher? Parth would need a story about being unable to find a video agent for some workflow he ran at Ericsson or AT&T and building Cosmo for himself. The dossier does not contain that sentence. Parth's AT&T Labs work on "world's first 3D call over production networks" is the closest thing, but that's prior employment continuity, not the USB-stick-at-MIT moment from **Dropbox** (2007). The Radia brothers are domain-native, which is rarer and more valuable than most people credit, but domain-native is a weaker signal than problem-bitten.

The hacker-genealogy axis is where Keyframe Labs reminds me of the inverse of **Reddit** (2005). Aaron Swartz co-authored the RSS specification before Huffman and Ohanian sat down with me; that's a tribal marker visible from a hundred yards. Parth led 30 people on petascale models at Ericsson and Kaahan published at CVPR and JAMA, which is real technical weight, but the dossier explicitly notes no GitHub account found for either founder and no Twitter presence. Hackers leave trails. The absence of a trail does not prove its absence in private — but my prior is that the trail is the thing.

My 36-month forecast for Keyframe Labs: acquihired by HeyGen or Synthesia for $30–60M as a real-time-model talent tuck-in, or grinding alone at $2–5M ARR while Tavus consolidates the developer-API real-time avatar category with its Sequoia and Scale Venture money. The mechanism is straightforward — the dimensional signature says "strong technical team, no schlep moat, well-funded direct competitor with the same positioning." That combination has historically resolved to acquihire, not to category dominance. The Radia brothers' credibility makes them an attractive acquisition target precisely because it makes them an unlikely independent winner.

The strongest case against my pass is the **Stripe** (2010) parallel, and I have to take it seriously because the surface pattern matches. Sibling co-founders, deep technical lineage, consumption pricing against subscription incumbents, developer-led distribution. If Parth's claim of ~500ms latency at "hundreds of simultaneous connections" is real and Tavus cannot match it, Keyframe Labs's $0.06/min embeds into agent platforms the way Stripe embedded into checkouts, and the incumbents' subscription ARR becomes the anchor that drags them under. What dismantles this case is that Stripe in 2010 had no Tavus — no Scale-Venture-backed competitor explicitly marketing "lifelike, real-time AI humans" via developer APIs with $24.1M in the bank — and the Collisons had Auctomatic as proof of shipped-company animal-quality before I wrote the check. I would flip my verdict on one verifiable condition: a named enterprise customer with a disclosed seat count or six-figure contract on Keyframe Labs's site within 90 days, replacing the eight anonymous testimonials. Until then, pass.
