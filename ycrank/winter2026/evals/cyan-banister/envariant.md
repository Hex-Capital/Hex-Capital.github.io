# Envariant -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out at me here isn't the founder or the technology -- it's the competitive landscape, and it tells a story I find deeply uncomfortable. Goodfire has raised over $200 million, is valued at $1.25 billion, and has Anthropic itself as a backer. Anthropic's CEO publicly declared mechanistic interpretability "among the best bets" for understanding neural networks -- and then open-sourced their own circuit tracing tools for free. MIT Technology Review named this a 2026 Breakthrough Technology. The EU AI Act is creating regulatory tailwinds. This is not an unpopular idea that's difficult to assess. This is one of the most validated, consensus-driven categories in AI right now. When I look at my best investments -- SpaceX when everyone said private rockets were lunacy, Crusoe when nobody wanted to touch Bitcoin mining on gas flares -- the defining characteristic was that other investors thought the founders were out of their minds. Nobody thinks AI interpretability is crazy. Everyone thinks it's important. That's precisely the kind of market where I lose my edge.

The dossier tells me almost nothing about who Varun Agarwal actually is. I know he studied CS and Biology at Stanford, did research at MIT, worked at Inceptive and NASA. Those are impressive institutional credentials -- but that's not what I'm listening for. When I sit down with a founder, I want to hear the story that explains why they couldn't NOT build this thing. What happened to them that made AI interpretability feel like a personal mission rather than a smart research direction? The dossier is silent on this, and I recognize that might be a limitation of what's publicly available rather than a reflection of the founder. But I evaluate through biographical narrative -- it's how I determine whether someone will keep going when everything falls apart. Without that thread, my primary assessment channel is blocked. A Stanford-MIT-NASA pedigree tells me he's brilliant. It doesn't tell me he's unbreakable.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Varun's interdisciplinary background -- straddling computer science, biology, and aerospace -- is genuinely unusual for this space, and the specific claim about antibody-binding prediction as a use case suggests he's thinking about interpretability not just for chatbots but for scientific foundation models where the stakes are different. If he's building the interpretability SDK that becomes essential infrastructure for biotech AI, robotics AI, and scientific computing -- not just the LLM safety tools everyone else is building -- that's a more defensible wedge. The model-agnostic SDK positioning versus Goodfire's platform approach is a real distinction: developer tools that embed into workflows create different switching costs than standalone exploration interfaces. And there's an honest argument that enterprise customers will want a neutral third-party interpretability layer rather than trusting the model provider to grade its own homework. If regulatory enforcement under the EU AI Act is aggressive, the demand for independent verification tooling could be enormous. I just don't see enough evidence that Envariant has the specific technical moat or the founder intensity to win that race against competitors with hundred-million-dollar head starts.

What I can't find here is what I've learned to look for across my portfolio -- a company that creates economic access or makes invisible systems transparent for people who currently lack power. In one sense, interpretability does make invisible systems visible -- it opens up the black box of AI. But this is transparency for AI engineers, not for communities or workers or consumers. When I backed Affirm, the transparency benefited borrowers who couldn't understand their credit card fees. When I backed Flock Safety, the data empowered neighborhoods. Envariant's transparency benefits the teams building and deploying models. That's valuable, but it doesn't give me the same visceral sense that this company is going to change someone's material circumstances for the better. It's infrastructure for other infrastructure builders, and while I've made those bets before -- Together.ai, Crusoe -- those had the weirdness factor and the founder intensity that made me feel the conviction in my body. I don't feel it here.

This is a technically capable person building a reasonable product in a validated market with enormous, well-funded competition. That's a fine description of many startups. It's not the description of the kind of company I write first checks into. I need the founder whose story makes my chest tighten, the idea that makes other investors nervous, and the sense that this person will outlast every obstacle because the problem is personal. I see smart, not weird. Credentialed, not forged. Consensus, not conviction.
