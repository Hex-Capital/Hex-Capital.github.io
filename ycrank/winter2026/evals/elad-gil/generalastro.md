# General Astronautics -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I see here is a multiple-miracle problem. To succeed, General Astronautics needs to simultaneously: (1) solve autonomous fluid handling in microgravity -- an unproven engineering domain where pipetting, reagent mixing, and sample prep all behave differently without gravity, (2) have commercial space stations actually become operational on schedule, which is an external dependency entirely outside their control, and (3) win procurement contracts from an addressable customer pool of maybe five to ten entities on the planet. Each of these is independently low-probability. Combined, they represent exactly the compounding small odds I've written about -- when your plan requires sequential breakthroughs across technical, market, and commercial dimensions, the math works against you regardless of how compelling any single dimension appears.

The structural moment question is where this falls apart for me. What has changed in the last 12-24 months that makes microgravity lab automation possible now? Varda completed its first pharmaceutical reentry missions, validating that microgravity manufacturing works scientifically. Launch costs continue declining with Starship development. Commercial stations from Axiom, Vast, and Orbital Reef are under development. But "under development" is the operative phrase -- these stations aren't operational, and space infrastructure programs are notorious for multi-year delays. This isn't a market where I can map a capability curve and project when it crosses a usability threshold the way I could track GPT scaling and predict when model fidelity would reach legal-grade accuracy. The ISS deorbits around 2030, which creates both urgency and a capacity gap -- there could be a period where orbital laboratory access actually *decreases* before commercial stations come online. Building robotic systems for platforms that don't yet exist feels like 2015 VR: the thesis might eventually prove correct, but the infrastructure timeline is measured in government procurement cycles and rocket engineering, not software release cadences.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If you believe commercial stations will be operational by 2028-2030, then being the platform-agnostic automation layer across all of them is a genuinely powerful position. It's the picks-and-shovels play during a gold rush -- don't build the station, don't own the manufacturing process, just provide the robotic labor that makes everything else possible. The technical barriers are real moats: microgravity fluid dynamics, autonomous manipulation without crew supervision, hardware that survives launch vibration and thermal cycling. A team that solves these problems first accumulates flight heritage and operational data that creates meaningful switching costs. Schork's SpaceX hardware experience and published soft robotics research, combined with Zhou's CMU Robotics background, cover the right technical surface area. And the market -- if it materializes at the projected scale -- has the kind of "too weird, too physical, too hard" profile that deters most software-focused investors, which is exactly where I've found some of my best returns. Anduril was a bet on defense hardware that most venture investors wouldn't touch, and the structural vacancy after Google's Project Maven cancellation made the timing right.

But here's the critical difference between this and Anduril: Palmer Luckey had already built and sold a company, had deep defense relationships, and most importantly, the customer (the US Department of Defense) already existed, was already spending hundreds of billions annually, and had an immediate, articulated need. General Astronautics is building for customers that are themselves pre-revenue startups or government programs still seeking funding. The customer base isn't just concentrated -- it's partially hypothetical. That's not a boring market hiding in plain sight like PagerDuty or Gusto, where every company running software or employing people was already a potential customer. The product-to-distribution trajectory is also constrained by this concentration: even if the robots work perfectly, "expand to all five orbital facilities" is not the kind of compounding distribution channel that separates good companies from generational ones.

Both founders are effectively recent graduates -- Schork is Caltech class of 2026, Zhou's graduation year isn't specified but the profile suggests similar timing. Schork's credentials are legitimately impressive for someone this early in their career: SpaceX Starlink hardware internship, published soft robotics research, competition wins in high school. But selling robotic systems to NASA, JAXA, and commercial station operators requires procurement credibility and industry relationships that typically take years to build. I see no evidence of shipped products, prototype demonstrations, parabolic flight testing, or customer conversations. At pre-seed, I don't penalize absence of traction, but in a hardware-for-space company, I need to see *something* that demonstrates the team can close the gap between academic robotics knowledge and flight-qualified autonomous systems. The GitHub profiles are sparse, with no notable public projects. This is a bet on potential, not demonstrated velocity.

I'm passing. The thesis is intellectually interesting -- if in-space manufacturing follows the adoption curve the market research projects, the automation layer is a real business. But the timing dependency on commercial stations, the extreme customer concentration, and the multiple-miracle structure put this outside my framework. The right time to revisit this category is when at least one commercial station is operational and conducting regular microgravity research -- when the market shifts from projected to demonstrated. Right now, this is a correct idea at a premature moment, and my experience says being early in hardware markets is functionally identical to being wrong.
