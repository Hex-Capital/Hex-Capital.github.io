# Clara -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what catches my eye immediately: George Favvas already built this company once. Circle Medical, YC S15, scaled to $100M revenue run rate with profitability. He sat inside the machine — the licensing headaches, the insurance billing labyrinth, the EHR nightmares — and he came out the other side knowing exactly where the bottleneck lives. It's the human clinician. Every telehealth company hits the same wall: you can't scale care when every interaction requires a licensed provider's full attention. Favvas isn't theorizing about this. He watched it happen in his own company. And now he's back with the same clinical lead (Caitlin Swift, who was an NP at Circle Medical) and the same product designer (Zeeshan Ahmed, also Circle Medical), building the version where AI does the clinical reasoning and humans become the verification layer. That operational DNA is real. This isn't a team that needs to learn healthcare — they've already paid that tuition.

But when I ask my first question — tell me the story, not the business — I get a gap. What happened to Favvas that made healthcare access *personal*? The dossier gives me a serial entrepreneur from Montreal with a Stanford certificate and a string of exits. That's an impressive resume. It's not a forge. Swift is closer — six years as a cardiology nurse, then an NP in chronic disease management. She's seen patients fall through the cracks of fragmented care. But the founding narrative here reads as "we built v1 and now we're building v2 with better technology." That's iteration, not inevitability. I don't doubt their competence. I question whether this is the problem they'd be solving without funding, or whether it's the natural next company to build given their prior company's constraints. There's also an unresolved question that bothers me: Favvas appears to still be listed as CEO of Circle Medical. Dual commitments during a company's most fragile phase is a real concern.

The invisible system test is where it gets interesting. American primary care *is* one of the most opaque, extractive systems I know. Your medical records are scattered across dozens of providers who don't talk to each other. Insurance billing is deliberately incomprehensible. The fee-for-service model rewards volume over outcomes — your doctor gets paid for the fifteen-minute visit, not for keeping you healthy. Clara's integration with 150,000+ facilities to aggregate a patient's full history is genuinely making something invisible visible — giving patients a longitudinal view of their own health that the fragmented system was designed to prevent. That resonates with me. But Clara isn't just illuminating the broken system — it's building a parallel one. That's more Uber than Affirm. Uber didn't make the taxi medallion system transparent; it routed around it entirely. That can work, but it's a different kind of bet, and it requires massive execution to build the replacement infrastructure before incumbents adapt.

Here's where my conviction weakens: this space is *hot*. K Health has $441M. Hippocratic AI has $404M at a $3.5B valuation. Forward raised $546M. AI healthcare is possibly the single most consensus-attractive investment category in 2026. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "AI is going to transform primary care" — they nod and reach for their checkbook. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. When I wrote my check into Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. The best ideas don't just look non-obvious — they provoke active resistance. Clara provokes enthusiasm. That doesn't mean it's wrong, but it means I'm not the right investor for it. My edge comes from being comfortable where others aren't. In a space where billions are already flowing, my pattern recognition doesn't add differentiated value.

The strongest bull case is hard to ignore, and I want to be honest about it. If anyone can pull off the AI-first inversion of primary care, it might be this exact team. Favvas has done the hardest parts already — state-by-state licensing, insurance billing infrastructure, clinical workflow design — and he's done it profitably, which almost nobody in telehealth can claim. The physician shortage isn't getting better (86,000 projected shortfall by 2036), and Carbon Health's bankruptcy in February 2026 suggests that capital-heavy clinical models are structurally failing. An asset-light, AI-first approach with a team that already knows where the operational landmines are buried? That's a credible path. The data moat from longitudinal patient records deepens with every user. If Clara can get to scale before K Health and Hippocratic AI figure out DTC, the switching costs become real — your next provider won't have ten years of your integrated medical history. This is a company I could see becoming very large. I just can't see what makes it a *Long Journey* bet versus a well-executed company in a crowded, well-funded category.

The cascading impact potential is genuine and worth naming. If AI-first primary care works, it doesn't just serve existing patients more efficiently — it creates access for the millions of Americans who functionally have no primary care provider today because of geography, cost, or wait times. That expansion of access to underserved communities is the kind of second-order effect I care about. But impact alone doesn't overcome my concern that this is a consensus bet in a consensus space, led by an operationally excellent team whose biographical connection to the problem is professional rather than personal.
