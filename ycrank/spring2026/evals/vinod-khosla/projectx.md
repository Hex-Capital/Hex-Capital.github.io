# ProjectX -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The number that jumps off the page is $204K in total funding against a competitive landscape where 95 companies have collectively raised $20.8 billion in GPU cloud infrastructure. Shadow has $100M+. CoreWeave has $5B+. The hyperscalers have functionally infinite capital. ProjectX is trying to win a GPU infrastructure fight with the budget of a used car. I have funded capital-intensive bets before — I have also lost hundreds of millions on them. KiOR taught me that brilliant technology in a capital-intensive sector without a clear path to self-sustaining economics is a cremation furnace for money. The difference between KiOR and Commonwealth Fusion Systems is that CFS had a physics breakthrough that fundamentally changed the cost equation. I need to see that kind of discontinuity here, and I don't.

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if this fully works? If any student in India — or anywhere — can open a browser tab and run Blender or Unreal Engine for a few cents an hour instead of buying a $3,000 workstation, that matters. Seventy-five percent of Indian students lack access to high-quality computing devices. That's hundreds of millions of people locked out of the tools that build the modern economy. The affordability thesis here is real and it maps directly to what excites me most — collapsing the cost of something essential. A kid in Kalyani, West Bengal running CAD software in a Chrome tab is the kind of future I want to fund. But I have to be honest about the consequence magnitude: this is access expansion, not category creation. Cloud desktops have existed for over a decade. Google Stadia tried to stream GPU workloads to browsers and shut down. OnLive tried it before that. The consequence of ProjectX succeeding is "more people use professional tools," which is meaningful but not "the world is fundamentally different." This isn't fusion energy. It isn't AGI. It's important infrastructure that makes existing capabilities more accessible.

The technical approach is where I struggle most. Sub-3-second cold starts for GPU-accelerated apps in browser tabs is genuine systems engineering — container orchestration, GPU virtualization, low-latency streaming all working in concert. That's hard. But hard is not the same as non-linear. The per-app streaming model versus a full persistent VM is an architectural choice, not a physics breakthrough. There's no proprietary science here, no novel algorithm, no data flywheel. WebGPU and WebCodecs matured as platform capabilities — ProjectX is riding that wave, not creating it. Any sufficiently motivated team at Google, AWS, or Microsoft could build this abstraction. And Google already bought Cameyo to move in exactly this direction. When I backed Rocket Lab, the Rutherford engine's electric turbopumps were a genuine technical discontinuity — something competitors couldn't easily replicate. I don't see the equivalent here.

The bull case deserves a real paragraph because the demand signal is interesting. Rs 20 Cr+ ($2.4M) in signed LOIs against $204K in funding is a striking ratio. A pilot with IIT Bombay. Thirty thousand in their community. These founders are 23-24 years old, from West Bengal — not the IIT-Stanford pipeline that produces every other Indian SaaS founder. That outsider energy is something I notice. Rounak Adhikary started a company at 19, bootstrapped enough revenue to fund early ProjectX development, got into Draper, Stanford ASES, and now YC. The trajectory is real. If the right abstraction layer — per-app GPU streaming with usage-based pricing — achieves the kind of product-market fit that makes capital chase the company rather than the other way around, this could work the way Dropbox worked on top of AWS. You don't need to own the GPUs if your software layer is sticky enough. The founding team restructuring — three of four original co-founders replaced — could signal ruthless self-improvement rather than dysfunction. I'd want to understand which interpretation is correct before writing a check.

But the balance of evidence keeps me in neutral territory. The ambition is to execute better on a known problem, not to attempt something the world considers impossible. Nobody says "streaming GPU apps can't be done" — they say "it's hard to make it economically viable at scale." That's a market and execution challenge, not a technology challenge, and I have always preferred technology risk to market risk. The competitive moat is thin. The capital requirements are enormous relative to what they have. And the founding team churn at pre-seed — three of four original co-founders departed between 2024 and 2026 — introduces uncertainty that I can't resolve from public information alone. The affordability vision is genuine and it's the strongest thing about this company. But a genuine vision in a capital-intensive commodity market without a clear technical discontinuity is not enough for me to write a check.
