# Drip -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Mike Levin is a UCLA CS 2026 grad with ex-GitHub metered-billing experience pointing a 2-person team at a category occupied by Ironclad ($200M+ ARR), Conga ($198.2M ARR), and Salesforce Revenue Cloud — and the live usedrip.ai, the YC page, and the drippay.dev redirect each describe a different product to a different buyer. The signature of Drip is a domain-credible billing engineer in search of a wedge, wrapped in the most consensus-shaped SaaS cap table I've seen this batch. The highest-loading dimension is founder conviction, because Levin's metered-billing résumé is the one non-generic fact in the dossier; everything else — round shape, sleeve, distribution, structure — reads as the canonical 2026 YC AI-CRM pitch.

Drop.io is the high rung on conviction for me because I lived it: bootstrapping a primitive against free incumbents for years before the Facebook outcome required missionary temperament that showed up in public artifacts long before the round. Drip has the rare credential half — Levin actually built metered billing at GitHub, which is genuinely scarce inside the YC billing-adjacent cohort — but it has none of the public surface that pre-pitch conviction leaves behind. No Twitter, no essays, no opinionated take on why Conga's CLM stack is structurally wrong, and a GitHub (@MichaelLevin5908) with no notable starred repo. Worse, Drip is currently running three different positionings across three domains — "enterprise deal execution" on YC, "dripos customer history" on usedrip.ai, "Memra customer memory for B2B founders" on drippay.dev — which is not Wizard-of-Oz iteration; that's landing-page A/B testing against three different buyers. Missionary founders narrow; mercenary founders broaden. Drip is broadening.

The canonical Pass Example I wrote — "$25K MRR AI agent for enterprise vertical, $30M post, four tier-1s in" — is exactly Drip's category sleeve minus the traction. "AI is just more internet" (Joe Lonsdale podcast #113, 2024) is the operating frame here: Drip is a thin LLM-context layer indexing Gmail/Slack/iMessage/Calendar/Stripe locally on a Mac, which is technically a 2-person-team-can-replicate observation the dossier already makes explicitly. That pattern — undifferentiated SaaS-on-top-of-LLM in an incumbent-occupied category — is the exact anti-sleeve from my 2025 WTF VC deck. The DealHub $61.6M revenue line and the Ironclad $200M+ ARR line aren't market validation for Drip; they're the wall a 2-person team has to climb against buyers who already bought a multi-year procurement-suite implementation. No crypto sleeve, no DTC consumer sleeve, no creator sleeve, no holdco. Drip is wearing the uniform of the cohort I most actively re-weight away from.

On round pricing: no dossier evidence on price, lead, or oversubscription — applying my absent-evidence default of consensus-priced YC seed and lowering it further because the category itself (enterprise AI billing-context) is one I'd expect to attract competitive seed bids in Spring 2026. The mechanism that compounds against Drip over 18 months is that incumbent CPQ/CLM platforms ship the same "context layer" as a feature inside the suite they've already sold to procurement — Salesforce Agentforce Revenue Management is the live example in the dossier — and Drip's local Mac-app data-access dependency on chat.db, Gmail API, and Slack scopes is exactly the kind of platform-policy surface that breaks unilaterally.

On capital structure: Drip is the inverse of Teamshares. Teamshares earned the cap-table room because the employee-ownership rollup was an instrument no other fund would underwrite; Drip is a standard SAFE on a horizontal-context-layer pitch that will need at least four more rounds to compete with Conga's $549M raised. There is no revenue-share angle, no holdco wrapper, no community pre-sale, no founder-as-brand royalty — nothing the financing instrument can do that a generic SaaS seed can't. For the score to flip a tier on capital structure, Drip would need to be running the deal-execution service manually as a paid concierge for 6+ months against named Fortune 500 procurement orgs — the actual Wizard-of-Oz pattern, not three landing pages.

On founder-as-brand: no Twitter, no newsletter, no Discord, no community. The Slow Creator Fund thesis (Axios, Feb 12 2025) doesn't even register here — Drip is a pure B2B sales-led play with no owned distribution channel, scored at the absent-evidence floor.

36-month forecast: Drip pivots once more off the current three-way scope split, lands at sub-$500K ARR against the incumbent wall, and either acqui-hires into DealHub or a mid-tier CPQ vendor for Levin's billing chops, or shuts down quietly by mid-2028 — the scope thrash across usedrip.ai, drippay.dev, and the YC page is the leading indicator.

The strongest bull case is that Levin's metered-billing experience at GitHub is genuinely rare in the YC billing cohort, and that the three-domain scope thrash is actually pre-product-market-fit search, not pivot-shopping — Venmo took "so many turns of the crank" and the Drop.io arc tacked through three positionings before Facebook bought it. If Drip's actual operating mode is running deal-execution as a manual concierge for one named enterprise customer while shipping the Mac app as customer-history bait, that's the exact Fin / Wizard-of-Oz template I wrote into. The dossier evidence that wins against this: Fin ran one product manually for paying users; Drip is running three landing pages for three buyer personas with no signed LOI from any of them, which is search-by-marketing, not search-by-manual-service. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict is one signed LOI with a named procurement org at ≥$50K ACV, or Mike Levin publishing a public take on why the Conga/Ironclad CLM model is structurally wrong — either would convert the GitHub credential into the missionary signal it currently lacks.
