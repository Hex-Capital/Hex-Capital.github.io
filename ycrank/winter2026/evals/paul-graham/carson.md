# Carson -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Carson is the tagline: "more capable than Cowork, more secure than OpenClaw." That's not a description of a problem. That's a description of a competitive gap. Claude Cowork launched in January 2026. OpenClaw went viral in late January 2026. Carson is in the Winter 2026 batch. The arithmetic tells you how this idea was born — not from years of living with a broken workflow, but from watching two products launch and noticing the space between them. When a startup defines itself as the intersection of two competitors' weaknesses, the idea was manufactured from market analysis. The strongest startup ideas don't position against incumbents; they describe a problem so clearly that the incumbents become obviously inadequate by comparison. Stripe wasn't "PayPal but for developers." It was "accepting payments on the internet is inexcusably hard." That's a different kind of sentence entirely.

The team complicates this judgment in an interesting way. Milan Bhandari co-founded Bolto at YC S23 and scaled it past $1M in revenue — that's not nothing. He's a repeat YC founder who has actually shipped and sold enterprise software. Three of the four founders worked at Palantir, which means they've lived inside the enterprise security apparatus — they know what SOC 2 compliance actually requires, what fine-grained authorization looks like at scale, what audit trails mean to a CISO. That's genuine domain depth. And Ketan Agrawal did ML engineering at Robust Intelligence, which was literally an AI security company before Cisco acquired it. So the schlep they're tackling — making AI agents enterprise-ready with real security controls — is a schlep they understand from the inside. Most AI agent startups skip this work because it's boring and painful. The Carson team wouldn't skip it because they've spent their careers in exactly this territory. That matters.

But there's a distinction I keep coming back to. Knowing how to do the hard thing is different from discovering the hard thing through your own pain. The Palantir experience gives them a capability advantage — they can build enterprise security controls that a team of fresh CS grads cannot. What it doesn't give them is the organic founder-problem bond that makes companies survive the long middle years. When I ask "would these founders have built this product even if they hadn't decided to start a startup?" the honest answer is probably no. The product exists because AI agents suddenly became a category in January 2026 and these founders recognized they had the right skills to fill a gap. That's intelligent opportunism. It's not the same thing as building something you can't stop thinking about.

The bull case deserves a full hearing. Enterprise AI agent adoption is genuinely accelerating, and security will be the bottleneck. Every CISO in the Fortune 500 is going to ask "how do we control what these agents can access?" before approving deployment. Anthropic has limited incentive to build deep vertical security for sales workflows — it fragments their horizontal platform. OpenClaw's creator joined OpenAI, creating real uncertainty about its future. A team of Palantir-trained engineers building the enterprise security layer for AI agents could be exactly the right bet at exactly the right moment — like building the Okta for the agentic era. And Bhandari's track record proves he can execute a B2B go-to-market. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that enterprise AI agent security becomes a standalone category rather than a feature that Anthropic or Salesforce adds to their existing products. I can see the argument, but it requires the security layer to be deep enough that it's genuinely hard to replicate — and right now Carson is describing product design choices (dynamic UIs, authorization controls), not deep technical moats.

The competitive landscape also concerns me. Clay has $210M in funding and $100M ARR in adjacent territory. Artisan and 11x have raised enormous rounds in AI sales specifically, and while both have stumbled badly (LinkedIn bans, inflated metrics, CEO resignations), the capital flowing into this space means Carson is entering a field where well-funded competitors can add enterprise security as a feature rather than buying a separate platform. The absence of any traction signal — no users, no revenue, no waitlist, no community, no press — is expected at pre-seed, but it also means there's zero evidence yet that the "dynamic task-specific UI" approach actually resonates with sales teams more than a chat interface does.

Four technical founders from Harvard and Stanford, all engineers, all with enterprise experience — that's a team I'd normally get excited about. But the idea needs to be as strong as the team. Right now Carson feels like a smart team that identified a market gap rather than a team that discovered a problem they couldn't ignore. If Bhandari had told me he spent two years at Bolto trying to deploy AI agents for HR and kept hitting enterprise security walls until he realized someone needed to build the security layer itself — that would be an organic discovery story. Instead, the origin appears to be "Cowork and OpenClaw launched, we can see the gap." Smart analysis. Not the same thing as lived frustration.
