# Alchemy -- Elad Gil Evaluation

CellProfiler shipped in 2005. ImageJ dates to 1997. The core UX of research microscopy image analysis — scripting pipelines, tuning parameters, assembling workflows by hand — hasn't fundamentally changed in two decades. Alchemy's bet is that LLM-driven orchestration of computer vision pipelines via natural language cracks open this stuck workflow. The structural catalyst is real: agent-based tool orchestration hit a capability threshold in 2024-2025 that makes this feasible for the first time. A UC Santa Cruz PhD student going from 3 hours to under 10 minutes on H. pylori analysis is a genuine 98% time reduction, and that's meaningful value. But here's my problem: this timing is not nonobvious. AI applications in scientific workflows are among the most discussed categories in the current ecosystem. Every foundation model lab, every major life sciences instrument company, every AI startup scanning for vertical applications can see this same window. When I backed Perplexity in 2022, AI search was a category that investors had written off after a decade of failures — the structural shift was invisible to consensus. Alchemy's market is more "overlooked niche" than "consensus-rejected graveyard," and that distinction matters for how much alpha the timing carries.

Alchemy's positioning fails my infrastructure-layer test cleanly. Stripe became the payments API that thousands of companies integrated into their core stack. Harvey is embedding in law firm workflows with compounding switching costs. Alchemy is building a workflow application — researchers upload images, get results, export them. There's no API-first architecture described, no platform that other tools build on, no evidence of becoming infrastructure that life sciences companies depend on. The dossier mentions a potential data advantage from a growing model library as researchers train and share CV models, but this is speculative at pre-seed with no evidence of network effects forming. The core technical capability — LLM-driven orchestration of open-source segmentation tools like Cellpose and CellProfiler — relies on generally available foundation models and prompt engineering rather than proprietary model architectures. Leica could ship a natural-language interface for Aivia's 22 built-in applications and 20 pre-trained deep learning models faster than Alchemy can build a comparable model library from scratch.

Phillip Baek's BS in Biomedical Engineering from UT Austin gives Alchemy some domain familiarity, and Liam McBride's MS in Computer Science from UIUC covers the ML pipeline side. But neither founder has published research in microscopy, computational biology, or bioimage analysis — no Google Scholar or PubMed results were found. Compare this to how I evaluate founder-market fit in life sciences: when I co-founded Color Health, my biology PhD from MIT gave me scientific conviction that pure market analysis couldn't replicate. When I backed BioAge despite generally avoiding biotech, it was because the founders brought a computational approach to drug discovery that mapped to infrastructure-layer thinking. Alchemy's founders have relevant educational backgrounds but lack the deep domain credibility that research lab PIs — the target buyers — typically require before adopting a new tool and citing it in publications. The BoBo shared-living app ($20K non-dilutive funding) is a student project, not evidence of scaling experience or decisiveness under pressure.

The bull case deserves construction. Alchemy could become the default research microscopy platform the way Figma became the default design tool — capturing an entire workflow category through superior UX on top of a structural capability shift. If the model library grows with each researcher's contributions, Alchemy builds a data flywheel that free tools can't match. Academic labs spend billions collectively on research, and even cost-sensitive PIs will pay for a tool that eliminates hours of pipeline configuration per experiment. The incumbents — PathAI ($490M raised, FDA-approved AISight Dx), Proscia ($130M raised, 400% YoY case growth), Aivia (Leica/Danaher distribution) — are all focused on clinical diagnostics and large pharma, leaving general research microscopy underserved. Alchemy could own that gap. I've checked my Known Exceptions: the Anduril override applies when a generational market structure shift justifies multi-miracle risk, and the BioAge override applies when deep personal domain expertise generates independent conviction. Neither trigger here. The market structure shift in microscopy analysis is real but incremental, not generational. And I don't have the kind of personal scientific conviction about this specific approach that would override the framework.

Alchemy's single-miracle test lands in ambiguous territory. The technology miracle — LLMs capable of reliable domain-specific CV pipeline orchestration — is happening exogenously, which is favorable. But Alchemy also needs a distribution miracle: convincing cost-sensitive academic researchers accustomed to free, well-maintained, publication-standard tools (CellProfiler is maintained by the Broad Institute, napari by the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative) to adopt and pay for a commercial alternative. That's two independent things that need to go right. On margin architecture, no pricing information exists — the website returned only a title line — and the target market of academic labs is one of the most price-sensitive buyer segments in enterprise software. The $3.13B live cell imaging TAM (MarketsandMarkets, 2025) includes hardware and reagents; the software-only SAM for AI research microscopy tools is materially smaller and undefined.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: Alchemy demonstrates that 10+ research labs are actively using the platform weekly with measurable retention, AND the founders articulate and begin executing a platform strategy where trained models are shared across labs with compounding network effects that open-source tools structurally cannot replicate. That would shift the infrastructure-layer score significantly and provide evidence that the distribution miracle is already underway. Without it, Alchemy is a well-timed application-layer tool on top of generally available components, in a cost-sensitive market, built by early-career founders without deep domain publication credentials — and that combination doesn't clear my bar for deploying personal capital.
