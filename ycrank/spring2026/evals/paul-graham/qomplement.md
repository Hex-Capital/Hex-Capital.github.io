# qomplement -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about qomplement is the way the idea is framed. "90% of enterprise data is document-bound." That's a market statistic, not a problem statement. When Drew Houston described Dropbox, he didn't say "the portable storage market is $X billion." He said he kept forgetting his USB drive. When the Collisons described Stripe, they didn't cite the payments TAM -- they described the specific pain of integrating with payment processors as developers who'd done it themselves. qomplement leads with the market because the market is where the idea came from. The "filling vs. extraction" distinction is the kind of insight you arrive at by studying the competitive landscape and looking for whitespace, not by personally suffering through a workflow so broken you couldn't stand it anymore.

Now, there is a thread worth pulling. Kerim Taray worked at Contalink, a cloud accounting automation platform in Mexico. If he spent time there watching customers manually re-key extracted invoice data into internal spreadsheet templates -- cursing at the screen, making errors, wasting hours -- that would be an organic discovery story. Accounting workflows do involve genuine document-filling pain. But the dossier doesn't tell that story. No one describes the specific moment they saw this problem. The Codemix profile talks about "turning companies into multi-agent systems," which is the kind of grandiose AI-native framing that makes me think the founders started with the technology and went looking for a problem, not the other way around. The X/Twitter bio mentions "turning invoices and POs into system-ready data" -- fine, but that's Nanonets' pitch from 2020.

The deeper issue is that the schlep here is thin. Compare this to Stripe. Everyone knew payments were painful, but the reason nobody built Stripe wasn't technical -- it was the regulatory, banking, and fraud schlep that made smart people's unconscious minds flinch. With document filling, what's the schlep? Handling diverse PDF templates and spreadsheet formats? That's an engineering problem, and it's the kind of engineering problem that gets easier every six months as LLMs improve. Rossum has $111 million and $45 million in revenue. Reducto has $108 million from a16z. Nanonets has $42 million and 10,000 customers. Any of these companies could add a "fill this form" feature as a natural extension of their extraction pipeline. The reason nobody has built a standalone document-filling company isn't schlep blindness -- it's that filling is a feature of extraction platforms, not a separate product category. That's a crucial distinction. Schlep blindness means the problem is so painful that everyone avoids it. Feature blindness means the problem is real but too small to be a company.

The bull case would require two things to be true. First, that document filling is genuinely harder and more varied than it appears -- that enterprise internal forms are so diverse, so idiosyncratic, and so entangled with legacy workflows that building a product to handle them at scale is a multi-year engineering challenge that extraction vendors won't prioritize. Second, that the founding team has technical depth we can't see from public sources -- that Andres Garza Garcia is a strong engineer, that they've built something genuinely impressive under the hood, and that their Contalink-adjacent experience gives them customer relationships and domain intuition that will compound. If both of those are true, qomplement could carve out a real niche in enterprise document workflows before the extraction giants notice. But I have no evidence for either. The CTO has no public profile, no GitHub, no visible work. The product website doesn't even render. Sixteen Twitter followers. This is a team asking me to bet on a market thesis, not on demonstrated capability or organic founder-problem fit.

The team is young -- recent CS graduates from Monterrey with a bootstrapped marketplace behind them and some adjacent enterprise software experience. That's not disqualifying at pre-seed. But combined with a hot, crowded category (document AI), a feature-not-a-company risk, and zero evidence of user pull, the picture doesn't add up to conviction. The founders may be capable, but this idea needed to come from a place of deeper pain and the team needed to show me something already built and already wanted. Right now this looks like smart engineers who noticed an underserved niche in a growing market and built toward it. That's not how the best companies start.
