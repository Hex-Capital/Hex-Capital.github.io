# Arzana -- Paul Graham Evaluation

William Alexander grew up working for a manufacturing sourcing company in Okoboji, Iowa, and Arzana is what that childhood produced — an overlay for RFQ and PO paperwork that the Iowa shops he watched as a kid still process by hand. That is the most important sentence in the Arzana dossier. The second most important sentence is the New York Times line that neither Alexander nor Marshall Kools had a tech background before Arzana. So Arzana is the inverse of my modal bet: organic founder pain in a schlep zone, attacked by non-hackers. That pairing — high on origin, low on genealogy — is the analytical signature, and it is the same signature that made me wrong about **Airbnb** (2009) for the first thirty minutes of the interview.

On animal resourcefulness, the rung I keep returning to is **Airbnb** (2009) and the cereal boxes — founders generating revenue via a stunt I would not have thought of, before any institutional check arrived. Arzana is not at that rung, but it is closer to it than the dossier first suggests: Alexander and Kools paid themselves roughly $10K a year while logos like Zeiss, Koike, Tier1, and Iowa Mold appeared on the site, and the NYT picked them up before YC's Demo Day. That is "default alive" behavior plus pre-funding distribution, and Zeiss is not a logo a manufacturing-sourcing kid from Okoboji lands by accident. Arzana diverges from the **Airbnb** (2009) rung because the resourcefulness here is enterprise hustle, not a coined-stunt — there is no Obama O's moment in the dossier, just a quieter version of the same trait.

The lowest rung is hacker genealogy, and the failure mechanism I worry about is the one from my pass example: a pitch where the founders plan to "hire engineers post-funding" because they themselves cannot build the artifact. Arzana is not quite that — Alexander has a Stanford CS half-degree — but the NYT *explicitly* writing that neither founder had a tech background prior to the company is exactly the trail I look for and do not find. No GitHub for Alexander. No GitHub for Kools. No open-source contribution, no Hacker News presence, no demo video that pre-dates the YC application. Compare to Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before **Stripe** (2010), or Drew Houston's Hacker News demo before **Dropbox** (2007). On this dimension Arzana looks like a company that will need to hire its hacker, and the integration surface area — Epicor, JobBoss, NetSuite, SAP, Salesforce, HubSpot — is exactly the kind of work where a non-shipping CEO discovers in month nine that the "99.9% accuracy" claim was a sales artifact.

Arzana's schlep score compounds over the next eighteen months in a way the headline number understates. Manufacturing back-office automation requires reading messy PDFs from job shops, mapping parts numbers against legacy ERP schemas, and showing up at MD&M trade shows in person — the LinkedIn post about MD&M is itself a schlep tell. Korso and Smartbase in the same P26 batch are routing around the full integration grind by picking narrower slices (quote follow-up only, order-entry only); Arzana is taking the whole quoting + order-entry + customer-updates surface, plus an "Arzana ERP" option. That is the **Coinbase** (2012) compliance-grind shape applied to ERPs. If Arzana survives the integration work, the schlep becomes the moat.

The counterfactual on the organic-idea dimension: for this score to flip down a tier, Alexander's Iowa manufacturing background would have to be retconned marketing rather than load-bearing biography. The dossier evidence against that reading is the customer concentration — Iowa Mold and Milltown Paper are not logos a Stanford grad cold-emails into; they are logos a kid from Okoboji calls his dad's network for. The organic-idea filter from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) — "something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing" — clears two of three for Arzana; the "themselves can build" leg is the wobble, and it wobbles because of the hacker-genealogy gap, not the origin story.

On co-founder configuration the single-anchor parallel is **Reddit** (2005)'s Huffman and Ohanian — two cofounders who met in college and built a thing they wanted to read. Arzana has Alexander and Kools, Stanford classmates, co-founded 2025, with Kools a D1 wrestler doing the operating role. Two people, complementary, met in school — this is the **Reddit** (2005) shape on configuration, just at one year of working relationship rather than four. The dossier does not give me evidence either way on whether they actually like each other, which is the failure mode I named in "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010), and the YC interview will decide it.

My 36-month forecast: Arzana reaches $3-8M ARR by mid-2029 on professional-services-heavy enterprise deployments to mid-market manufacturers, gets squeezed by a more technical YC P26 peer (most likely Korso) on net-new logos, and either sells to Epicor or NetSuite for a low-nine-figure outcome or pivots into a vertical AI-services consultancy for industrial supply chains. The mechanism is the hacker-genealogy gap meeting the integration sprawl: the founders cannot personally close the technical debt on a 10-ERP overlay, the "99.9% accuracy" claim degrades under heterogeneous PDF intake, and a strategic acquirer pays for the customer list and the Iowa-shaped distribution channel rather than the platform.

The strongest case against my forecast is the **Airbnb** (2009) precedent itself, and it stings: I have a known exception for funding teams whose idea I had mentally rejected when the founders pass the animal test. Alexander getting Zeiss onto the customer wall while paying himself $10K a year is the 2026 version of selling Obama O's at $40 a box. If Kools is the kind of D1 wrestler who treats ERP procurement officers the way Chesky treated investors in 2008, the non-hacker objection collapses — **Airbnb** (2009)'s Blecharczyk was the only technical one of three and the company still ran. The dossier evidence that holds my forecast against this bull case is the NYT's explicit framing of both founders as non-technical, where **Airbnb** (2009) at least had Blecharczyk in the room. The verifiable condition that would flip me to invest: Alexander personally ships a working public artifact — a free PDF-to-ERP parser, a GitHub repo with non-trivial integration code, or a public live demo against a real Epicor instance — within 90 days of Demo Day. Without that, Arzana is a Neutral on the high edge, and I let someone else lead.
