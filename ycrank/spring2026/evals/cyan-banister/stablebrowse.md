# StableBrowse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

StableBrowse is a browser-for-AI-agents infrastructure play with four cofounders, an unsexy HIPAA wrapper around insurance/healthcare/mortgage use cases, and the unfortunate honor of being born into the most consensus-saturated YC category I can think of right now -- a lane where Browserbase has already raised $67.5M, Kernel $22M, TinyFish $47M, and Hyperbrowser is sitting inside YC itself. The signature here is a regulated-vertical wrapper bolted onto a deeply consensus infrastructure category, which is almost the geometric opposite of the bets I write. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, and the game StableBrowse is playing looks exactly like the game it appears to be playing: catching up to incumbents on developer-led API distribution.

Anduril is the load-bearing analogue for the founder-origin dimension, because Palmer Luckey had already shipped Oculus to scale from his garage before he turned to defense hardware -- the biography put him in front of the problem before the company existed. StableBrowse's narrative says the founders "started after frustrations themselves while building browser agents," which is the analytical-discovery origin, not the lived one. Jay Mehta's eight years of distributed-systems work and Deepit Shah's Amazon-plus-UIUC CS resume tell me StableBrowse's team can build the thing; they do not tell me any of the four cofounders grew up inside an insurance claims office or a hospital prior-authorization queue, which is the kind of biography that would let StableBrowse actually walk onto those workflows the way Chase Lochmiller could walk a well pad.

Crusoe is the failure mechanism I'd lead with on the consensus dimension -- nobody else would write the check because flaring gas to mine Bitcoin sounded laughable, and the laughter is what kept the round cheap and the cap table clean. StableBrowse is the inverse pattern. The dossier names Browserbase at a $300M valuation, Kernel with Accel leading, TinyFish with ICONIQ Capital leading, plus an open-source Browser Use framework eating the bottom of the market. The "magically weird" filter requires that sophisticated investors have visibly passed on aesthetic grounds; StableBrowse instead enters a battlefield where sophisticated investors have visibly committed roughly $130M in the last twelve months. That is not a rebuttal I can talk my future self into.

StableBrowse lists four cofounders -- Sarthak Awasthi, Jay Mehta, Deepit Shah, Somansh Shah -- and the dossier names no CEO. This is the HQ Trivia warning before HQ Trivia even shipped, except worse, because two-way tiebreaker ambiguity becomes a four-way mess where any two can outvote any one. The Tim Ferriss line was "who's the tiebreaker?" and StableBrowse has not answered it on a public surface that I can find. Four-way founder governance at pre-seed in a category that will demand rapid pivot decisions when Browserbase ships a competing graph layer is the structural setup for the kind of slow-motion authority crisis that ended GameCrush.

The HIPAA claim plus the named insurance/healthcare/mortgage verticals is the one place StableBrowse picks up real points from me, because it pushes the company toward the regulated-and-unsexy lane that Vigil Labs and Mindbloom occupy -- compliance moats compound with time and the incumbents may not chase you into them. Eighteen months out, though, this dimension only compounds if StableBrowse actually closes the SOC 2 Type II that is "in progress," lands a named carrier or EHR, and converts the per-site execution graph into a switching cost for that specific buyer. Absent a signed counterparty in the dossier, the regulated angle is a pitch posture, not a moat.

Postmates is the single-anchor parallel for physical-world verifiability -- I bet because the delivery happened in front of me, on a sidewalk, and I could photograph it. StableBrowse is a B2B API with no Product Hunt presence, no Chrome Web Store artifact, no app I can install on my phone tonight in San Francisco and hand to a friend over coffee tomorrow. The self-reported "98% success rate" and "70-80% fewer tokens" are unaudited founder testimony, which is the form of evidence I systematically distrust. I cannot touch StableBrowse.

My 36-month forecast is that StableBrowse either gets acqui-hired by Browserbase or one of the regulated-vertical browser-agent players for the team and the graph IP at sub-$1M ARR, or quietly winds down once the seed extension conversation lands in front of investors who can see the same competitive map I see. The mechanism is straightforward: StableBrowse is a consensus-category infrastructure entry without a founder biography that locks them into a defensible vertical, and the incumbents will either ship a semantic-graph layer themselves or buy the cheapest competent team building one.

The strongest objection is HandUp -- I broke the commercial-defensibility rule for a founder whose biography mirrored mine, and the bull case for StableBrowse asks me to break the consensus-category rule because the persistent semantic knowledge graph for insurance carriers and EHR portals is genuinely a different product than Browserbase's session infrastructure. The bull case stings because Browserbase founders themselves might not want to climb the regulated-vertical compliance ladder, leaving StableBrowse a real wedge. It does not survive contact with the dossier, though: HandUp had a founder whose homelessness mirrored mine, and StableBrowse has four cofounders whose biographies do not visibly intersect insurance, healthcare, or mortgage operations at all -- the override requires a lived origin and there is none on the page. I'd flip my verdict if StableBrowse produced one named carrier or EHR contract worth more than $200K ARR plus a designated CEO with operating control, both within the next two quarters -- that combination would convert the regulated-vertical posture into a moat and resolve the governance ambiguity in a single move.
