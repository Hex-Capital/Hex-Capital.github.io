# Manicule -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Naman Bansal was 13 when he started freelancing technical content for PromptLayer and Brightdata, and 15 when he and Shreyans Jain recorded their first YC founder intro from their bedrooms in Agra — four years before they actually got into the batch. By the time Manicule walked into P26, the two of them had already pushed it to $58K MRR with 100% MoM growth across three months, with Supermemory, Greptile, Reducto, Mastra, Agentmail and Rootly paying. That is the signature: animals on resourcefulness, organic on the pain, weak on hacker symmetry — one of the two cofounders is a writer, not a coder. This is a teenage pair who shipped revenue from named customers before any institutional check, which is the only pre-funding artifact that matters to me.

Airbnb (2009) is the ladder rung Manicule is leaning on. The mechanism is the same: founders generating revenue from a stunt-shaped distribution that other applicants in their batch would not have thought to run. Chesky and Gebbia sold Obama O's at $40 a box; Naman freelanced docs for dev-tool companies from age 13 and parlayed those relationships into Manicule's first six logos. Where Manicule diverges from Airbnb is that the cereal boxes were a one-time funding stunt and the marketplace was the company — here, the freelance grind *is* the company, which is both more durable as cashflow and lower-ceiling as outcome. I would put Manicule between Dropbox's demo-video moment and the Airbnb cereal-box moment on this axis. $58K MRR at 18 is animal. Drew Houston was 24 when he posted his demo.

The lowest rung is hacker genealogy, and the failure mechanism I worry about is the one that took down a lot of agency-shaped businesses in the early 2010s: when the writer-founder is the louder voice and the engineer-founder gets pulled into customer work, no software ever ships. The Collisons at Stripe (2010) were both hackers — Patrick won Ireland's Young Scientist for Lisp work in 2005 — and so the question of "will they productize" never came up. Manicule has Shreyans Jain, who was founding engineer at Supermemory and ships under github.com/CodeWithShreyans, and that is a real hacker tell. But Naman is a writer who has been paid to write for six years. The team is asymmetric in a way Stripe was not, and the dossier's mention of a third "founding engineer" (Ritik Sahni) tells me they feel the asymmetry too.

The schlep test is more interesting than it first looks. Documentation is exactly the kind of work that hackers route around — they hate writing docs, dev-tool companies have devrel roles "open for months at $300K+" and still can't fill them, and that hiring failure is the schlep zone Manicule is colonizing. The 18-month compounding mechanism is whether Manicule's agent-plus-human workflow becomes a tooling layer that other agencies cannot replicate, or whether Mintlify ($67M raised, $45M Series B in April 2026 at a $500M valuation) decides to add services and crushes the margin. The former is Coinbase-shaped; the latter is Wufoo-shaped at best. The dossier shows no proprietary software shipped yet, only workflows.

The organic-idea test is where I'd flip a tier if one fact changed. Shreyans Jain was founding engineer at Supermemory before Manicule existed, and the dossier shows a 30% answer-success improvement in 23 days on Supermemory's own docs — which means he personally felt the docs-quality pain inside an AI company and then went and built the thing that fixes it. That is the Dropbox USB-stick pattern translated to devrel. If Naman had a parallel pain story — if the dossier said he had been a frustrated dev advocate inside a YC company before this — I would push organic-pain into the top quartile. As written, half the founder pair has a clean organic story and the other half has a freelance income story, which is a weaker but still real claim.

The cofounder configuration is the cleanest signal in the dossier. School friends in Agra, four years of working together pre-YC, complementary writing-plus-engineering coverage. This is the Reddit dorm-roommate pattern (Huffman and Ohanian) rather than the hackathon-pair anti-pattern. The thing that would worry me — pairs who met for the startup — is the opposite of what is on the page.

36-month forecast: Manicule reaches $5-12M ARR as a profitable AI-native devrel boutique, ships a thin proprietary tooling layer that Naman and Shreyans use as the productization wedge, and either stays independent at a Wufoo-shaped outcome or gets bought by Mintlify or Common Room for $40-100M as the services arm those platforms can't build culturally. Mechanism: the animal-test signal at 18 with $58K MRR is real, but the absence of a hacker-symmetric cofounder pair caps the productization probability, which caps the outcome below fund-returner.

The bear case stings, and it is the case I want to write into the angel-check terms. Manicule's six named customers are all early-stage AI dev tool companies — Supermemory, Greptile, Reducto, Mastra, Agentmail, Rootly — which is one correlated funding cohort. If AI dev tools cool, Manicule's MRR cools with them. Draft.dev got to $60K/mo as a bootstrapped human-only agency and stayed there; that is the gravitational floor for the services-shaped version of this business, and Manicule is already past it. Mintlify at $500M with $67M of dry powder can absolutely decide to add services and target the same buyer. The Founder Mode counter is that Naman and Shreyans have been founder-mode operators since they were 13 and 15 respectively, and the asymmetric founder pair is partially repaired by hiring Ritik. The dossier also lists three revenue figures — $58K MRR, $15K MRR-and-profitable, $150K+ ARR — and the variance there is the diligence question I would ask in the meeting, not a kill shot. The condition that would flip me to a pass: if at month 12 Manicule's customer list is still those same six AI-dev-tool peers and no proprietary tooling has shipped, the ceiling concern won and I was wrong. The condition that would flip me to a top-decile-of-batch conviction: a Mintlify-shaped customer on the roster, meaning the platforms themselves are paying Manicule. I write the check.
