# Ontora -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at Ontora: a company sitting in the middle of the most consensus space in technology right now — AI agents for enterprise — and positioning itself against one of the most genuinely opaque industries in the world, management consulting, without seeming to recognize the deeper structural fight they could be picking. The trillion-dollar consulting market is absolutely an invisible system. McKinsey charges millions for diagnostics wrapped in proprietary frameworks that are often common sense in expensive slides. Clients can't evaluate the methodology because the methodology is the product. There's real information asymmetry being exploited — executives pay for political cover as much as insight, and the consulting firms know it. That structural reality is something I'd get excited about if the founders were framing this as a revolution against opacity. But "help managers regain control of their organisations" is a feature pitch, not a war cry. The invisible system is there. The founders just don't seem to see it.

When I ask my first question — tell me the founder's story, not the business — I get silence. Maximilian Arnold went to CODE University, Stanford, ETH Zürich, and built an agentic RAG assistant at Porsche for SAP procurement data. That's technically relevant and it's a strong credential set. But it's a career path, not a forge. He saw the opportunity from inside a corporate AI team and decided to productize it. Leon Iwanowitsch has a business school background at HSG and organized hackathons. And David Korn — a co-founder — is essentially invisible. No confirmed LinkedIn, no public background, no trace. Three founders with no visible shared history, no shared employer or university overlap, and one who's a ghost in public data. That pattern worries me. I broke my own rule once on co-founder dynamics with HQ Trivia and it was the most instructive failure of my career. I'm not saying this is the same situation, but three co-founders with no visible connective tissue and one who can't be found — that's a question I'd need answered before I could get comfortable.

The bull case deserves a full paragraph because the structural opportunity is real. Consulting firms face a genuine incentive trap — they cannot automate their own diagnostic process without destroying the billable-hour model that generates their revenue. Gartner says 40% of consulting tasks are automatable, and the incumbents are structurally blocked from pursuing that automation. That's the kind of misalignment that creates a window for a startup. Arnold's Porsche experience is directly relevant — he built essentially this system inside an enterprise context. The Vertiv testimonial from a real strategy manager at a public company suggests the product actually delivers on its promise: months of work compressed to an afternoon. And the timing is right — LLMs genuinely crossed a quality threshold for open-ended conversational interviews in 2024-2025 that makes this possible for the first time. If Ontora could own the organizational knowledge layer across hundreds of enterprises, the cross-company benchmarking data could become a real moat over time. That's the infrastructure play hiding underneath.

But I keep running into the same wall. When I apply the ten-year imagination exercise — where does this technology end up in a decade? — I can see it, but I can also see Celonis building it as a feature. Celonis has $1.77 billion in funding, $771 million in revenue, and is already expanding from structured process mining into AI-powered process intelligence. They have the enterprise relationships, the data, the distribution. The underlying technology here — LLM-based interviewing with RAG over enterprise documents — uses broadly available foundation models. There's no proprietary model, no patent, no data moat yet. Lee Jacobs would call this what it is: an AI wrapper with enterprise ambitions but no structural defense against well-funded competitors replicating it in a quarter. The founders are smart and credentialed, but credentials without a biographical connection to the problem and consensus technology in a hot market — that's career optimization, not the kind of conviction that survives the valley of death. I wish them well and genuinely hope they prove me wrong about the consulting disruption — but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
