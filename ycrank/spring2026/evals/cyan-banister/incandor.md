# Incandor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Incandor is a regulated-finance API play whose entire value lives inside bank integrations I will never get to touch, photograph, or hand to a friend -- the highest signal sits in the unsexy-regulated lane (banking fraud, BIPA exposure, SR 11-7 model risk), and the lowest sits in physical verifiability, because Matthew Yekell and Luc Rosenzweig are shipping an enterprise API gated behind "Request early access." That pairing is my analytical signature for Incandor: a category I would normally lean toward, wrapped in a product shape I can't observe in the wild.

On the dimension where Incandor scores best -- the capital-intensive/unsexy-regulated swing -- the natural HIGH rung is **Vigil Labs** (August 2025), where the moat compounds inside compliance perimeters most generalist investors won't enter. Vigil's mechanism is that regulated-finance buyers price legal risk above feature velocity, so a focused player with native SR 11-7 and SOC 2 posture stacks switching costs. Incandor lives in the same lane on paper -- GDPR Art. 9 biometric data, BIPA, banking procurement -- but the dossier shows no SOC 2 attestation, no published privacy posture, no DPA terms, and "Not hiring" on the YC page; the regulated-swing thesis only loads if the founders are already three months into the audit, not announcing intent. That gap is what separates the Vigil archetype from a Stanford CS pair pointing at a regulated TAM.

Where Incandor scores worst -- physical verifiability -- the **Pass Example** failure mechanism is exactly this: "I cannot install it, photograph it, or hand it to a friend." **Flock Safety** got sourced because I read a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons cafe; **Niantic** got sourced because I had been playing Ingress since 2012. Incandor's surface area is a JavaScript SDK that captures mouse dynamics and keystroke timing inside someone else's banking app -- I cannot ride it, scan it, or watch a stranger use it. The dossier offers a ">99% account-takeover accuracy" claim with no benchmark and no customer logo, which is the developer-tools-abstraction shape my framework treats as evidence-by-founder-testimony only.

Matthew Yekell's Vista Equity Partners and Cboe Global Markets tenure plus Luc Rosenzweig's NVIDIA/Apple/Epic Games tenure reads as a competent founder-market fit pairing on the *selling* and *systems-telemetry* axes -- but neither biography contains a moment where Yekell or Rosenzweig was personally defrauded, ran a mule investigation, or sat inside a bank fraud-ops room. Over 18 months that absence compounds the wrong way: every BioCatch and Feedzai sales conversation will surface a war story the Incandor founders cannot match from lived experience, and the "no labels required, works from session one" technical claim has to do all the work the biography isn't doing. Stanford CS plus Vista plus Cboe is the exact "discovered the problem in a memo" silhouette my Pass Example warns against, even when the memo is well-researched.

For the magically-weird dimension to flip a tier on Incandor, two or three credible VCs would need to have publicly passed on Incandor for aesthetic or moral grounds -- the **Crusoe** (~2018) tell -- and the founders would need to articulate why those investors misread the category. Instead the dossier shows a YC Spring 2026 cohort slot, David Lieb as YC partner, behavioral biometrics ranked a $3.45B market growing 26.95% CAGR per Mordor Intelligence, and Feedzai just printing a $2B valuation in October 2025. Incandor is sitting inside the consensus tailwind, not outside it; the lane is hot, and BioCatch's $1.3B Permira exit in May 2024 already telegraphed the comp to every Tier-1 fund. That is the opposite of "find them before it becomes consensus."

The single-CEO axis is the only place Incandor cleanly clears my **HQ Trivia** (2017) post-mortem heuristic without help: Yekell is named CEO and Rosenzweig is named CTO on the company site, titles distinguished rather than co-equal, and the two-person Stanford CS pairing is the structurally cleanest version of a co-founder team. There is no dossier evidence of unresolved tiebreaker authority, no 50/50 language, no third co-founder muddying the equity stack. I don't get a documented founder-vesting protocol either, so I'm scoring above the absent-evidence default but below the top quartile -- the dossier shows me the title hierarchy without showing me the operating agreement.

My 36-month forecast on Incandor: acqui-hired into Sift, LexisNexis Risk Solutions, or Feedzai for under $40M, or stalls below $1M ARR as banking procurement cycles eat the runway before BioCatch's 60+ patents and Permira's balance sheet ship a label-free SKU of their own. The mechanism is the signature -- regulated lane that rewards incumbents with audit history, behind a product shape I cannot independently verify, sold by founders whose biographies don't carry the fraud-war-story currency that closes enterprise deals against entrenched competition.

The bull case that should sting: the label-free, "from session one" architecture is genuinely orthogonal to BioCatch's supervised-model contracts, and incumbents can't cannibalize their own labeled-data moat without admitting it depreciated -- which is exactly the structural blind spot **Affirm** exploited against legacy underwriters. If Yekell and Rosenzweig ship a SOC 2 Type II inside 12 months and land two top-25 US bank pilots, the unsupervised approach becomes the default greenfield architecture and the incumbents are stuck retrofitting. I dismantle it on the founder axis: Affirm had Max Levchin, a founder whose biography *was* the moat against legacy credit; Incandor has Vista Equity Partners and Epic Games, which is competence without lived problem ownership. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed pilot with a named top-50 US bank or top-10 EU bank disclosed before the end of YC Demo Day -- not "Request early access" copy, an actual counterparty name with a press release. Until then, Incandor is a pass.
