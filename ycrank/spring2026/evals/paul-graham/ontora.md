# Ontora -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Ontora: this idea sounds good. That's the problem. "Replace expensive management consultants with AI agents" is exactly the kind of idea that every smart person at a Stanford dinner party would nod along with in 2026. It has none of the quality I've learned to look for -- the quality where smart people hear the pitch and think you're crazy. Facebook sounded like a site for college students to waste time. Airbnb sounded like people would sleep in strangers' beds. Those sounded bad. "Automate the trillion-dollar consulting industry with AI" sounds *great*, which usually means you're about to discover why it hasn't been done yet, and the reasons are harder than the technology.

The strongest signal in this dossier is Maximilian Arnold's work at Porsche building CoAnalyst -- an agentic RAG system for SAP procurement data projected to save millions across the VW Group. That's not a line on a resume; that's someone who lived inside the exact problem of extracting operational intelligence from enterprise data. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise," and Arnold has it. He didn't think up this idea -- he noticed it, which is the verb I want to hear. The technical analogy between CoAnalyst and Ontora is tight enough that I believe he understands both the promise and the schlep. Building integrations across CRM, email, documents, and meeting transcripts inside large enterprises is the kind of painful work that triggers schlep blindness in most founders. Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve that level of integration pain. Arnold apparently can see it because he's already done a version of it.

But then I look at the rest of the team and the picture gets murkier. David Korn is a ghost -- no confirmed LinkedIn, no public background, nothing. For a three-person startup, that means I can't evaluate a third of the founding team. And I see no visible overlap between any of the founders -- no shared university, no shared employer, no shared project. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." I have no evidence this sock is strong. Leon Iwanowitsch brings a business school background from HSG and some hackathon organizing, which is fine, but "fine" doesn't move me at this stage. What I want at pre-seed is founders who are formidable and earnest, doing something that borders on obsessive. I want the cockroach quality -- the founders who fund themselves selling cereal boxes, who grab your laptop and install their product on the spot. The Vertiv testimonial is the closest thing to a hustle signal here, and it reads more like a successful product demo than evidence of a paying customer relationship.

The bull case is real, and I want to lay it out honestly. If Arnold's Porsche experience translates into an ability to navigate enterprise procurement and security reviews -- the actual hard part of selling to large organizations -- then Ontora could be attacking a segment where incumbents are structurally unable to compete. McKinsey can't cannibalize its own billable hours. Celonis is optimized for structured event logs, not messy human conversations. The timing genuinely is now -- LLMs crossed the interview-quality threshold in the last eighteen months, making this approach technically feasible for the first time. And the Vertiv reference, thin as it is, comes from a strategy manager at a $27 billion public company, not a friend doing a favor. If this team can turn that reference into a repeatable enterprise sales motion, they're sitting on a wedge into an enormous market. The question is whether three people with no GTM hires and no visible sales infrastructure can actually pull that off.

What holds me back is the absence of signals I weight most heavily. No evidence of traction beyond demo phase. No revenue. No scrappy determination stories. No signs of doing things that don't scale -- going out and getting users one by one, providing a level of service no big company can match. The commoditization risk is also real: LLM-based interviewing plus RAG over enterprise documents is a technique, not a proprietary technology. Every major AI lab and a dozen well-funded startups are building similar capabilities. Celonis has $1.77 billion in funding and is explicitly moving toward AI-powered process intelligence. Ontora needs to be moving faster than I see evidence of. At pre-seed I'm picking the founders, and while Arnold impresses me technically, I need more signal on the full team's determination before I'd write a check from my own account.

This is a competent team with relevant domain experience working on a real problem in an enormous market. But competence in a consensus market isn't what generates the asymmetric returns I look for. I need either a contrarian insight that makes the idea look bad to most investors, or founder determination so fierce it compensates for the competitive landscape. I see neither clearly enough here.
