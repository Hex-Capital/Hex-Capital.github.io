# FullSeam -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about FullSeam: this is one of the most consensus ideas I've seen in a long time. "AI employee for finance and accounting" — I could walk into any demo day in San Francisco right now and trip over five companies saying nearly the same thing. Auditoria.ai has $60.5 million and is already doing exactly this across AP and AR. Stampli has $148 million. Vic.ai has $52 million. Fazeshift just raised a seed. Everyone and their cousin is building AI agents for back-office finance work. When I say the most promising companies are not popular, I mean it as a filter — and FullSeam is on the wrong side of that filter. The idea is instantly legible to any investor, easily pitchable, and sits squarely in the hottest category in B2B SaaS. That accessibility is a warning sign for me, not a selling point.

The founders are clearly capable. Three UIUC grads who built TaxProper through YC S19, grew it to process over a billion dollars annually in property tax payments, and sold it to Opendoor. That's real execution. Tom Dowling is a Rhodes Scholar who advised the mayor of Chicago and got appointed to a state commission by the governor. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is a polished, credentialed trajectory, not a survival narrative. The connection to this problem is operational frustration: their accounting team at TaxProper was overwhelmed during growth. That's a legitimate pain point, but it's the kind of pain hundreds of founders at scaled startups have experienced. Compare that to how I found Uber — years of discussions with my husband about the taxi medallion racket, understanding viscerally that the system was designed to exclude people. Or Contraline, where the founder had spent years in academic research on a problem nobody else would touch. FullSeam's founders are solving a problem they encountered, not one that forged them.

The strongest case for FullSeam is the co-founder dynamic and execution advantage. After HQ Trivia taught me that even perfect timing and product can't survive founder conflict, I probe co-founder relationships harder than anything else. And this team has already survived the entire journey together — founding TaxProper, scaling it, navigating the Opendoor acquisition, working through post-acquisition integration, and then choosing to start over together. That's rare and valuable. They've had the fights. They know how each other operates under pressure. If I were evaluating this company purely on team resilience, I'd be very excited. The multi-system integration breadth — nine platforms spanning accounting, billing, banking, and CRM — also shows ambition. Point solutions tackle AP or AR alone; FullSeam's approach of spanning both plus cash application and reconciliation is a harder build that creates compound integration barriers if they execute.

But through my lens, the bull case still doesn't get me there. This company doesn't create economic access for anyone who lacks it. It doesn't make an invisible system visible — AP/AR processes are already visible, just inefficient. Compare this to Carta, which replaced opaque spreadsheet cap tables with transparent equity data, or Affirm, which exposed hidden credit card fees. FullSeam makes an existing system faster, not more transparent or accessible. The people it serves — finance teams at growing companies — already have good jobs and access to tools. There's no underserved population being empowered here. And the space, while requiring some financial data integrity and audit compliance, isn't the kind of regulated frontier that deters other investors. This isn't navigating FDA pathways like Contraline or defense procurement like Anduril — it's well-mapped territory with well-funded incumbents who know the regulatory terrain better than a pre-seed startup.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? The repeat-founder team would need to execute with such speed and product quality that they carve out the SMB and mid-market segment before Auditoria.ai moves down-market or before QuickBooks and Xero ship native AI automation features. The multi-function approach would need to create genuine switching costs as AI agents learn each customer's GL coding rules and vendor patterns. And the LLM inflection point would need to be durable enough that the accuracy threshold for autonomous financial entries gets crossed in the next 12-18 months, creating a land grab where speed-to-market matters more than capital. All of those things could be true. But they're execution bets in a crowded market, not the kind of non-consensus, difficult-to-assess opportunity where I've historically generated my best returns. A good VC will fund this company — it's just not my kind of bet.
