# Traverse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that these founders entered YC building "the communication OS for loan officers" and pivoted mid-batch to RL environments for non-deterministic work. That's not a minor course correction -- that's a complete domain change, from fintech communication tools to AI training infrastructure. Now, I've seen pivots work. Justin.tv was a lifecasting webcam before it became Twitch. But the pivot that concerns me isn't the fact of change -- it's what the change reveals about the origin of the idea. When Emmett Shear pivoted from Kiko to Justin.tv, he was still working in the same broad space of online video. When these founders jumped from mortgage broker tooling to RL environments, the only connecting thread is "we're at YC and need to work on something bigger." That's the startup idea machine at work, not organic discovery.

The strongest counter-argument is Zachary Yu's time at Mercor. He was a backend engineer at a company that's now valued at $10 billion and deeply embedded in the RL environments market. He would have seen what frontier labs are buying, where the product gaps are, and specifically that non-deterministic domains remain underserved. That's real insider knowledge, and it's the closest thing to organic problem discovery in this dossier. It's not quite Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's better than brainstorming from a TechCrunch article. The question is whether six months as a backend engineer at Mercor gives you the depth to out-execute Mercor itself -- which now has $350 million in funding and explicitly lists healthcare and law among its target domains.

Here's what I'd want to believe for the bull case: that building verifiers for subjective domains is genuinely hard in the way that creates moats, not just hard in the way that prevents anyone from succeeding. Traverse's own website says this problem is "largely unsolved." But there's a crucial distinction between a schlep and an unsolved research problem. Stripe's challenge was a schlep -- everyone knew payment processing was broken, and the solution involved tedious bank integrations, compliance paperwork, and fraud detection. Painful, ugly, but tractable. If non-deterministic verification is an open research question -- if nobody yet knows how to reliably determine whether a lawyer's argument is persuasive or a doctor's diagnosis is correct -- then Traverse doesn't need schlep willingness, it needs a scientific breakthrough. Two-person teams can out-schlep incumbents. Two-person teams rarely out-research teams backed by $100 million (Applied Compute) or $350 million (Mercor). The company is framing a research frontier as if it were a schlep opportunity, and those are different animals.

On the founders themselves: they can clearly build things. Yu's side projects show genuine hacker instincts -- tunesform.com (a cursor for jazz lead sheets with 10K+ users) is exactly the kind of weird, specific thing a real builder makes because they personally want it. Lance Yan was ML Lead at Waterloo's AI organization at what appears to be age 18, which is precocious. Both have active GitHub profiles. If I were evaluating them purely as technical founders, I'd be reasonably impressed. But technical ability is necessary, not sufficient. The question isn't whether they can build -- it's whether they've found the right thing to build, and whether they found it for the right reasons.

The competitive landscape here is the kind that should make any angel investor pause. Mercor at $10B valuation. Surge AI bootstrapped to $1.2B in revenue. Applied Compute with $100M from ex-OpenAI researchers. Idler already closing multimillion-dollar lab contracts. This is not a market hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness -- this is a market where the smartest, best-funded teams in AI are already competing aggressively. When I said the most impressive YC companies I'd seen recently were not working on AI, this is exactly what I meant. Consensus categories attract capital faster than startups can build defensibility. Two founders with $500K in YC funding competing against five well-capitalized incumbents for contracts from perhaps four or five frontier labs -- that's a narrow path through hostile territory. The buyer concentration alone (essentially Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google DeepMind) means a single procurement decision at one lab could make or break the company.

I keep coming back to the pivot. The best founders I've backed worked on problems they couldn't stop thinking about. The Airbnb founders were renting out their own apartment. The Dropbox founder was losing his USB drive. These problems preceded the company. With Traverse, the company preceded the problem -- they were already in YC, pivoted away from their original idea, and landed on RL environments because the market is obviously large and Yu had relevant experience from Mercor. That's a rational choice, not an obsession. And startups run on obsession.
