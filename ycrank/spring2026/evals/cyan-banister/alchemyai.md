# Alchemy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what concerns me right away: this is an AI layer on top of openly available tools and models, in one of the most heavily funded spaces in venture right now. Lee and I have talked about this pattern a lot. AI applied to life sciences is not a contrarian bet — it's practically consensus dinner-party conversation in San Francisco. PathAI has raised a quarter billion dollars. Leica bought an AI imaging company three years ago. Every microscope manufacturer is shipping AI features. Every foundation model company is building vision capabilities. When I write a check, I want to feel that uncomfortable twinge that means most of the room disagrees with me. I don't feel that here. I feel the room nodding along.

When I ask my first question — tell me the founders' stories, not the business — the dossier gives me something that doesn't quite connect. Phillip Baek studied biomedical engineering, did electrophysiology mapping at Boston Scientific, spent time at Peking University on cancer-related weight loss research, interned in marketing analytics at Tropicana, and then got a master's in psychological counseling at Columbia. That's a genuinely eclectic journey, and I respect the range — but I can't find the thread that leads inevitably to microscopy image analysis. The counseling degree especially interests me. In another context, that credential might be the signal — if he were building mental health tech, I'd see the forge. Here, it reads like someone still searching for the thing that found them. Liam McBride is clearly sharp — patent at 19, strong UIUC credentials in CS and ECE, interests in AI safety. But sharp is not forged. Neither founder has documented experience specifically in bioimage analysis, computational biology tools, or even research microscopy workflows. The dossier says it directly. This reads like two capable people who spotted a market gap and brought adjacent skills to it. That's not the same as founders who lived inside a broken system until they couldn't stand it anymore.

The strongest bull case, and I want to be honest about it: the research microscopy workflow genuinely is a mess. I understand that. Open-source tools like CellProfiler and ImageJ require real scripting expertise, and most bench scientists didn't go to grad school to learn Python. The enterprise options are expensive and tethered to hardware ecosystems. The "why now" is legitimate — vision-language models and agent frameworks make natural-language pipeline assembly newly feasible. If Alchemy nails the researcher experience and embeds its pipelines into publication SOPs, the switching costs could become real over time. Labs that cite Alchemy-built analysis in published papers create a kind of lock-in that's hard to replicate. And the hardware vendors have genuine channel conflict that prevents them from unbundling a standalone SaaS tool. There's a structural gap in the market. For all of that to work, though, I'd need to believe these specific founders — without deep bioimage domain experience — can navigate one of the most specialized, opinionated user bases in science. Researchers have strong tool preferences. Earning their trust without having been one of them is a steep climb.

What I keep coming back to is the invisible system question. When I backed Affirm, the hidden structure was obvious — credit card companies were extracting value through opacity, and most consumers had no idea what they were actually paying. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessments were a black box that punished people who didn't know how to appeal. Microscopy image analysis is frustrating, but it's not opaque. The tools are open-source. The code is visible. The models are published. Nobody is being exploited by hidden information asymmetry — researchers are just spending too much time on manual work. That's a productivity problem, not a structural injustice. I've made my biggest bets when I can point to a specific invisible tax being eliminated from people's lives, and I can't identify one here.

The defensibility picture also gives me pause. Right now, Alchemy is assembling pipelines from available models — Cellpose, StarDist, and whatever vision-language models are current. The dossier itself flags that "any ML-capable team with life science domain knowledge could build similar pipeline-assembly tooling." A curated library of validated nodes could become defensible over time, but at pre-seed, that library is small and the moat is aspirational. Meanwhile, foundation model capabilities are advancing fast enough that the interface advantages of today could be table stakes in eighteen months. I've been burned by companies that relied on consumer experience without structural moats — HQ Trivia taught me that painful lesson. This isn't the same category, but the underlying concern is the same: if the product can be replicated by a well-resourced team in a quarter, the investment thesis needs something else holding it up.

I wish them well. The market is real, the timing of AI capabilities makes the product newly possible, and Baek's biomedical background gives them a starting point in the domain. But when I run my ten-year question — where does this technology end up? — I see a feature more than a company. The big microscope vendors will integrate natural-language analysis. Foundation models will get good enough at bioimage interpretation that the pipeline assembly becomes commoditized. Without founders who are biographically locked into this problem, without a contrarian thesis that makes other investors flinch, and without a hidden system being exposed, this isn't a Long Journey bet.
