# Approxima -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that Approxima didn't exist a few months ago. The founders entered YC W26 as SideKit -- a mobile SDK for analytics and feature flags -- and pivoted mid-batch to "self-healing software" powered by AI agents. Their GitHub organization still contains only SideKit repositories. Their website returned a connection error. This tells me something important about how the idea was found: it wasn't found. It was chosen. And there's a big difference.

When I ask "how did they discover this problem?", the honest answer appears to be: they were inside YC, SideKit wasn't gaining traction, and they looked around at what's working right now. AI agents that write and fix code is the hottest category in tech. Every founder in this batch has thought about it. That's exactly the pattern I've warned about for twenty years -- smart people sitting down to think of startup ideas rather than stumbling into problems they can't stop thinking about. Drew Houston didn't decide "file sync seems like a good market." He kept forgetting his USB drive. The Collisons didn't read a report about payment infrastructure pain. They'd been dealing with it as developers. I see no equivalent organic moment in Approxima's story. Two Waterloo CS students with Databricks and Microsoft internships are certainly qualified to have opinions about software maintenance -- but "software teams spend too much time on bug fixes" is an observation anyone could make from reading Hacker News. It's not a problem that was hiding in plain sight due to schlep. It's a problem every AI company and their investors are already staring at directly.

And that's the deeper issue: where's the schlep? Stripe worked because the schlep was enormous -- dealing with banks, compliance, fraud, international regulations. That regulatory and institutional complexity is what kept thousands of smart developers from building it. With Approxima, the hard part is making the AI work accurately. That's a technology challenge, not a schlep. And it's the exact same technology challenge that Cognition ($10.2B valuation, $73M ARR), Factory AI ($300M valuation), Augment Code ($252M raised), OpenHands, and the foundation model companies themselves are all working on simultaneously. When Anthropic and OpenAI are shipping their own coding agents -- Claude Code, Codex -- the value of a two-person wrapper diminishes quickly. Sweep AI, a YC S23 company, positioned themselves in almost exactly this niche -- automated bug-fix PRs via LLMs -- and has since abandoned it for JetBrains IDE tooling. When your closest predecessor tried your positioning and walked away, that's worth understanding before you walk in.

The strongest argument for investing would be something like this: the founders are genuine technical builders (Waterloo CS, Databricks, competitive programming), the pivot itself shows adaptability, and "self-healing software" could be a narrower, more defensible wedge than general-purpose coding agents. Maybe Cognition and Factory are too broad, and there's room for a product that does one thing -- autonomous maintenance -- exceptionally well. If these founders have deep insight into how production systems break, drawn from their Databricks and Microsoft experience, and if they can build something that integrates into CI/CD pipelines so tightly that it becomes infrastructure rather than a tool, there could be something here. The problem is real. Every engineering team does spend too much time on maintenance. But for this bull case to work, I'd need to see the founders articulate a specific, opinionated insight about why existing approaches fail -- not a generic pitch about "routine maintenance." I'd need to see a working product that demonstrates technical craft beyond calling an LLM API. And I'd need evidence that at least one engineering team is pulling this toward them organically. I see none of these signals today.

The founders themselves are the brightest spot. They're both CS graduates from Waterloo, which produces genuinely strong engineers. Selvaraj shipped production code at Databricks. Pronev competed in programming contests. They clearly can build software -- they built SideKit's iOS and React Native SDKs. The co-founder relationship appears solid; they were classmates and co-founded together. But being able to build is necessary, not sufficient. The question is whether they're building something that emerged from genuine conviction or from the pressure to find something that works during a YC batch. A mid-batch pivot to the consensus hottest category in tech looks more like the latter.

I keep coming back to a simple test: if AI coding agents weren't the topic on everyone's lips right now, would these founders still be building this? Everything I can see suggests the answer is no. They'd still be working on SideKit, or they'd have pivoted to something else entirely. That's the signature of a manufactured idea, not an organic one. And in a market where your competitors have raised, collectively, over $700 million and the foundation model providers are building the same capability directly, a manufactured idea with no visible product or traction isn't a bet I'd make with my own money.
