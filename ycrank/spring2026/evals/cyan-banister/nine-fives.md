# Nine Fives -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Noah Levy spent years as a Senior RF Engineer building satellite hardware at SpaceX. Andrew Kurtz worked test automation on the Falcon 9 first stage at SpaceX before moving to Aurora Flight Sciences. Between them, Nine Fives has roughly two decades of aerospace RF and mechanical engineering experience — and I'd bet my favorite tattoo they've both spent nights cursing at GPIB cables and proprietary driver installs while trying to get a qualification test running before a launch window. That's real. When I ask "tell me the story," I want to hear about what happened to someone that made them incapable of ignoring this problem. Nine Fives' founders have the professional version of that — they lived the pain — but I don't see the biographical inevitability that separates "I experienced this frustration at work" from "my entire life prepared me to be the only person who could solve this." MIT, Cornell, SpaceX — these are formidable credentials. SpaceX in particular is its own forge; surviving that environment shapes you in ways that carry. But the founding narrative here reads as career-derived insight, not as a story where the problem found the founders through some crucible beyond their professional trajectory.

Keysight pulls in $5.4 billion a year, and National Instruments was acquired by Emerson for $8.2 billion — and a meaningful slice of that revenue comes not from the instruments themselves but from the proprietary software, drivers, and platform dependencies wrapped around them. Nine Fives is attacking that lock-in directly: plug in via PoE or USB-C, get a browser UI and REST API in ten seconds, no drivers, no vendor software. That structure — where incumbents extract value through artificial complexity that most buyers can't fully see — is an invisible system. I've backed companies that expose exactly this kind of hidden tax: Affirm showed people what credit cards were actually costing them, Carta made cap tables legible when they'd been deliberately opaque. Nine Fives is doing something structurally similar for RF test benches. The dossier's inference that "unbundling individual instruments with open APIs directly cannibalizes the high-margin software/driver ecosystem" describes a genuine business model conflict that protects Nine Fives from incumbent response — Keysight can't easily open-source their control layer without gutting their own margins. That's a real structural insight. But I need to be honest: the people being taxed here are well-funded aerospace labs and telecom companies, not ordinary people navigating systems they don't understand. The pattern is present; the visceral resonance is diluted.

Nine Fives' two shipping products — the POE-ATTEN-6G and POE-SWITCH-6G — represent something I respect: real hardware on real shelves at pre-seed, with published datasheets and reference manuals. That's not a deck and a dream. But the 6 GHz frequency ceiling is a concrete limitation when 5G mmWave testing operates at 24-40 GHz and competitors like Vaunix reach 40 GHz. And two products versus Mini-Circuits' 60,000-plus catalog items creates a bundling gap that no amount of API elegance closes overnight. The defensibility question comes down to whether Nine Fives can build a broad enough networked instrument portfolio fast enough that test scripts written against their REST API create compounding switching costs — each new instrument deepening the lock-in of the platform rather than the individual device. That's infrastructure logic, and it's the right logic. But today, the moat is thin: the dossier correctly notes that "adding a REST API to an existing product line is an engineering project, not a fundamental barrier" for Mini-Circuits or Vaunix. No patents found. The inflection is real — Open RAN, 5G testbeds, DevOps-style test automation all create demand for API-first instruments — but Nine Fives hasn't yet built the compounding asset that would make them structurally irreplaceable.

The strongest bull case for Nine Fives goes like this: these are SpaceX-forged hardware engineers who are already shipping product, attacking a $4B+ market where the dominant players are structurally unable to cannibalize their own software margins, at the exact moment when the entire test industry is shifting toward software-defined, headless, API-first workflows. If Nine Fives executes on catalog breadth, the REST API becomes a de facto standard for networked test instruments — a Flexport-like layer of transparency over an opaque incumbent ecosystem. The timing with government-funded 5G testbeds and Open RAN interoperability labs couldn't be better. I feel the pull of that argument. My Flexport investment was exactly this pattern: software-native infrastructure unbundling an opaque, legacy-locked industry. But what made Flexport a Long Journey bet was the sheer scale of the hidden system and the founder's obsessive biographical connection to freight. Nine Fives' serviceable market is "likely in the low hundreds of millions" for networked programmable attenuators and switches — that's a real business, but it's not a market structure that produces outlier venture returns without expanding dramatically into adjacent instrument categories. And the space doesn't make anyone shift in their chairs. RF test equipment is overlooked, not stigmatized. Nobody is telling Nine Fives "this is impossible" or "this is dangerous" — they're telling them "this is niche." That's a fundamentally different signal than what I heard when I wrote my check for Anduril or Flock Safety.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Nine Fives has signed integration partnerships or LOIs with major test system integrators (like Astronics or Teradyne subsidiaries) or defense primes, signaling that the API-first approach is being adopted as a platform standard rather than a point product. That would indicate the compounding infrastructure dynamic is emerging, which would change the defensibility score substantially and suggest the catalog will expand with pull rather than push. Without that, Nine Fives is two SpaceX engineers selling two well-designed instruments into a niche market where the incumbents could respond with firmware updates. I respect the craft, I respect the domain expertise, and I believe the invisible system they're attacking is real. But my highest-conviction bets require a biographical forge that goes beyond professional frustration and a space that makes the room uncomfortable — and Nine Fives, for all its technical merit, lands in a zone where the founders are strong but not inevitable, and the market is real but not contrarian.
