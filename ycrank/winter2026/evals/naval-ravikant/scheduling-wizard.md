# Scheduling Wizard -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the business model, and it worries me. "Departments outsource scheduling entirely rather than operating software." That's not a technology company -- that's a consulting firm with proprietary tools. My entire framework distinguishes between leveraged and unleveraged work: code scales without permission, services scale with headcount. Three people serving 19 departments across 14 hospitals means each founder is personally responsible for roughly six department relationships, encoding constraints, producing schedules, handling exceptions. That's renting out expertise by the hour, which is the opposite of the leverage architecture I look for. The Scheduling Programming Language is the seed of something interesting -- a domain-specific language that could eventually automate the human out of the loop -- but right now it's wrapped inside a service delivery model that requires proportional human effort for each new client. When I invested in Replit, the product was code leverage: one engineer's work reaching millions of users. Here, one engineer's work reaches six departments.

On specific knowledge: Oberly's background in applied mathematics from Cambridge, operations research at the Department of Defense, and Minor League Baseball scheduling is more relevant than most pre-seed founders bring. Constraint optimization is genuinely his domain. But I distinguish between knowledge that can be taught in a graduate program and knowledge that emerges from years of obsessive immersion in a problem space. These are 2024 JHU graduates who accessed hospital scheduling through campus proximity, not through years inside the healthcare system understanding why chief residents make the specific tradeoffs they make, why certain political dynamics within departments make scheduling intractable, or what breaks when a schedule meets reality at 3 AM. The mathematical foundation is solid but teachable -- any strong operations research PhD could acquire equivalent capability. The healthcare-specific insight layer appears thin.

The market tells a consensus story, not a contrarian one. QGenda's $2.5B acquisition by Hearst validates physician scheduling as valuable real estate. Lightning Bolt holds the #1 KLAS rating five times over. YouShift from YC W25 has Bain Capital Ventures backing and is pursuing the same AI-powered approach with a self-serve model that has inherently better unit economics. When I see a market where the exit has already happened, the incumbents are well-resourced, and a fellow YC company one batch ahead is competing for the same customers with a more scalable architecture -- I'm not looking at contrarian timing. I'm looking at a crowded field where the pricing already reflects the opportunity. The best returns come from being right when everyone else thinks you're wrong. Here, everyone agrees healthcare scheduling is broken and AI can fix it.

The strongest bull case: those 14 hospital names are genuinely impressive for a pre-seed team. Mass General, Johns Hopkins, UT Southwestern, UCSF -- these are not easy institutions to penetrate, and doing so at 22 years old suggests real sales ability and credibility. If the SPL accumulates enough constraint logic across enough departments to reach a tipping point where adding a new department becomes nearly zero-marginal-cost, the managed service flips into a software-margin business with high switching costs and a growing data moat. The department-by-department land-and-expand motion within hospital systems could compound. And the fact that incumbents sell self-serve software means they'd have to cannibalize their own model to offer a managed service -- a structural advantage rooted in business model mismatch. That path exists. But it requires the founders to solve the leverage problem they've designed into their own business model, and I see no evidence yet that the automation is approaching that threshold.

I keep returning to the fundamental question: what does this team know that can't be taught? The mathematical chops are real but replicable. The healthcare access is circumstantial. The managed-service insight -- that program directors want someone else to handle scheduling, not another tool to learn -- is a genuine observation about buyer psychology, but it's an observation any smart person spending time in hospitals would make within a few months. I don't see the deep, non-transferable knowledge that made me write checks for founders who understood something about their domain that surprised me. This is competent execution in a validated market with a business model that fights against scale. Pass.
