# Auxos -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Auxos is the canonical 2026 batch shape: three Team USA fencers from Columbia, Duke, and UPenn building "simulated audiences for customer research" into a market where Aaru already cleared a $1B Series A in December 2025 and bootstrapped Evidenza is already serving Salesforce and GE. The high dimension is founder lineage — Kerry Lu built simulation infrastructure at Amazon Ads modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic, which is real. The low dimension is the contrarian secret, because Auxos does not have one. That pairing names what Auxos is: a competent engineering team running the consensus play in a category venture capital has already crowned.

Stripe (2010) is the high rung on the contrarian-secret ladder — Patrick Collison named developer-experience as the specific architectural failure mode of legacy processors after PayPal had "won." The pass-example AI-for-X pitch is the low rung — Stanford CS team, $50B TAM slide, no named incumbent blind spot. Auxos sits at the low rung. The dossier's stated incumbent failure mode is "traditional research takes 2-6 weeks at high cost" — that is not a secret, that is a price-performance complaint any of the six named competitors makes verbatim. Aaru, Evidenza, Viewpoints.ai, Synthetic Users, Brox.ai, and Synthelos all already claim the same wedge. What does Ashton Daniel believe about market research that Aaru's $1B-valuation team actively rejects? The dossier surfaces nothing. That absence is the signal.

SpaceX (2008) is the high rung on the atoms-heavy moat ladder — reusable launch capex no software competitor could replicate. Asana (2010) is the mid rung, the known exception where pure SaaS got a check anyway because Dustin Moskovitz had shipped Facebook to scale. The low rung is software-only in a commoditizing space. Auxos is the low rung without the Moskovitz override. The underlying capability — LLM-driven persona simulation — is reproducible by anyone with frontier API access; PyMC Labs has already published the recipe, and weavely.ai offers free ChatGPT-based versions. Nielsen Norman Group's explicit warning that synthetic users are valid only for "hypothesis generation, not final design decisions" caps the contract size before Auxos opens its first enterprise conversation. No regulated customer, no hardware capex, no certification depth — none of what made Palantir (2003) or Anduril (2017) load-bearing in my book.

Palantir (2003) anchors the monopoly-trajectory ladder; Asana (2010) is mid as a workflow-SaaS exception; the low rung is commoditizing AI SaaS. Auxos sits at the low rung — six direct competitors are named in the dossier, one of them at a $1B headline valuation, another bootstrapped to 100+ enterprise customers including Salesforce and GE. That is not a path to monopoly pricing; that is a path to feature-velocity competition with better-capitalized incumbents. On definite plan, SpaceX is the high rung with a 15-year reusability roadmap, the lean-startup pitch is the low rung, and Auxos's three-verb product framing of "Reach / Simulate / Learn" with no named multi-year technical milestone sits closer to the low rung — this reads as iteration mode, not the kind of definite optimism Zero to One was written to defend.

Affirm (2014) anchors founder lineage with Max Levchin's PayPal Mafia re-up; Stripe (2010) is mid with the Collison credentialed-outsider profile; first-time founders with no shipped product is the low rung. Auxos sits between mid and low, closer to mid. Lu's Amazon Ads simulation infrastructure is genuinely relevant — that is the one dossier specific that prevents me from scoring this as a pure pass-example. Jerry Wu's Meta Reels work cited at $100M+ incremental revenue is senior IC pedigree, not founder pedigree. Ashton Daniel was a BCG consultant, not an operator who shipped a product to scale, and his NCAA fencing championship is the kind of competitive-achievement signal I'd weight in a Thiel Fellowship application but not as a substitute for the contrarian thesis the dossier does not contain.

Auxos in 36 months is acquired by a market research incumbent — Qualtrics, SurveyMonkey, or a synthetic-research roll-up — at sub-$30M, or shut down. ARR caps below $2M before Aaru's capitalization and Evidenza's enterprise footprint compress the wedge. The mechanism is the signature: competent engineering with no contrarian secret in a category that already has a $1B headline competitor produces a feature, not a company. This is the indefinite-optimism profile — three smart fencers iterating against a moving consensus.

The strongest objection is the Asana exception. Lu's simulation infrastructure at Amazon Ads modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic is non-trivial, Wu's Meta ranking-infra work shipped at scale, and Daniel's BCG network does map to Fortune 500 marketing buyers who write the actual checks. If aggregate-level simulation accuracy is the moat and the Auxos team can ship a 10x calibration system per-customer creating switching costs, the operator-pedigree-overrides-thesis path applies. The Asana exception fails here for one reason: Moskovitz had founded and shipped Facebook to billions of users — he was the operator, not a senior IC at someone else's product. Lu and Wu were excellent engineers at someone else's scaled product, which is a different signal. The dossier shows zero customer logos, zero LOIs, zero revenue against six funded competitors who are already past that gate. I flip to invest if Auxos signs a named Fortune 500 enterprise contract at $100K+ ACV within six months — that would name the wedge the dossier cannot. Until then, pass.
