# Asendia AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out to me about Asendia AI isn't the product or the market -- it's the pivot. This team built a B2C interview coaching product, launched it twice on Product Hunt, got decent traction with it, and then turned around and repositioned as a B2B agentic platform for staffing agencies. That pattern tells me something important, and it's not necessarily what the founders would want me to hear. When I see a pivot from consumer to enterprise in a space like AI recruiting -- where every other startup in YC's batch probably has "AI agent" somewhere in their description -- I have to ask: did these founders discover a deeper truth about the market, or did they chase the B2B SaaS narrative because the B2C unit economics weren't working? I don't have enough information to know, but the absence of a biographical thread connecting either founder to the staffing agency world makes me lean toward the latter.

When I evaluate founders, I want to hear the story before the business. Tell me how you got here. With Rihab and Badis, what I see are two technically capable people -- Google, Microsoft, Infineon, TU Munich -- who built something adjacent to this space (an interview prep tool) and then moved into a crowded enterprise market. Their resumes are strong. But a strong resume is not the same thing as a founder whose life led them to this specific problem. Neither founder appears to have worked inside a staffing agency, experienced the brokenness of that system firsthand, or arrived at this insight through the kind of personal, lived frustration that I look for. Compare this to how I found Uber -- years of conversations with my husband about the medallion system, riding with my driver Roger, feeling the unfairness of a broken industry in my bones before I ever saw a pitch deck. That biographical inseparability between founder and problem is my strongest signal, and I don't see it here.

The market itself is my bigger concern. AI recruiting tools are perhaps the most consensus category in the current startup landscape. hireEZ has raised $76 million. Paradox raised over $250 million before Workday swallowed it. Humanly, Fetcher, and dozens of others are all building overlapping capabilities. The most promising companies are not popular -- they're difficult to assess, and other investors tend to pass on them. Asendia AI is the opposite of that. Every VC I know is looking at AI recruiting plays right now. The "agentic" framing is fashionable, not contrarian. When I backed Crusoe Energy, nobody understood what mining Bitcoin on stranded gas even meant. When I wrote my check into SpaceX, people thought private rocketry was delusional. Those were bets against consensus. An AI recruiting platform for staffing agencies in 2026 is a bet with consensus, and that's where I tend to lose money.

The strongest bull case I can construct: Paradox's acquisition by Workday genuinely created a structural gap for standalone AI recruiting platforms. The 50+ ATS integrations represent real technical work that creates switching costs. The team's enterprise pedigree at Google and Microsoft gives them credibility in B2B sales. And if they can build a true data flywheel from screening interactions -- proprietary candidate scoring that improves with volume -- there's a defensibility story that could emerge over time. A founder with deep staffing agency operational experience who pivoted into this space with the same technical execution would be genuinely interesting to me. But that's not this team. They're outsiders to the staffing world who are building sophisticated technology for a customer they haven't been. The compliance claims -- SOC 2, GDPR, HIPAA from a three-person pre-seed team -- also make me uneasy. Either they're overstating their certification status or they've allocated scarce early resources toward checkbox exercises rather than customer discovery.

On the positive side, the co-founder relationship has a real foundation -- they started a podcast together in 2019, which means they've been collaborating for years before this company. That's a meaningful signal. I learned from HQ Trivia that even the right product at the right time gets destroyed by founder conflict, so I always want to see evidence that co-founders have weathered time together. Rihab and Badis have that. But a solid co-founder relationship in a consensus market solving a problem you haven't lived doesn't add up to a check from me. I need to feel that jolt of energy about what's possible, and an AI tool that helps staffing agencies fill roles faster just doesn't stir that in me. It's a reasonable business. It's not a Long Journey bet.
