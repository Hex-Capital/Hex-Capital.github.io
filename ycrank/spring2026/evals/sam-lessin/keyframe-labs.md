# Keyframe Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Parth Radia spent five years at Ericsson building the exact product he's now pitching — photorealistic digital humans with real-time voice and video synthesis, a 30-person team, Ericsson's first generative AI commercial product shipped (Ericsson blog, 2023). That's legitimately impressive domain depth. I don't see that often. But here's my problem with Keyframe Labs: the market already knows this is a good idea. Synthesia just raised $200M at a $4B valuation in January 2026. HeyGen is at $100M ARR (Sacra, October 2025). Tavus closed a $40M Series B with Sequoia and Scale specifically to build real-time conversational video — the exact segment Keyframe Labs is targeting — and they've been at it for three-plus years with $64.2M total raised (BusinessWire, November 2025). When I look at this deal, I don't see mispricing. I see a category where every sophisticated investor in the room is already bidding. The upside is in the price. My whole game is finding the thing others have dismissed, and nobody has dismissed AI video avatars — it's one of the hottest segments in the current batch landscape.

Keyframe Labs is positioning as an API layer — "turn agents into lifelike video calls" — which means they're building on top of existing LLM and voice-agent stacks, adding a visual component. Their GitHub shows a fork of livekit/agents, confirming the transport infrastructure is shared, not proprietary. This is definitionally an application layer, not a protocol layer. I invest in protocols. Protocols compound. Applications get commoditized. The question I always ask is: "Can an incumbent add AI to their existing product and replicate this within 18 months?" Synthesia, sitting on $530M in funding and $146M ARR (Sacra, September 2025), has the engineering resources, the avatar library (230+ avatars, 140+ languages), and the enterprise distribution to ship a real-time conversational feature. HeyGen at $100M ARR has the same option. When the big async video players decide real-time matters — and Tavus is already proving the market — Keyframe Labs' two-person team is going to be fighting for oxygen against companies with 100x their resources and existing customer relationships. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about. Real-time conversational video is a feature for Synthesia, not a company in its own right.

The primitive test is where Keyframe Labs almost gets interesting but ultimately falls short. Adding a photorealistic face to an AI agent conversation is an upgrade, not a new behavior. Nobody's going to "Keyframe" someone. Compare this to Venmo, which created the behavior of peer-to-peer mobile payments — a verb, a social norm that didn't exist. Or Patreon, which created the act of paying creators directly as a subscription behavior. Keyframe Labs is making existing AI chat interactions richer, like going from radio to television. That's meaningful technology, but it's not category creation. The underlying interaction — talking to an AI agent for support, training, sales — already exists and is already deployed at scale. Keyframe Labs adds fidelity, not a fundamentally new primitive.

On the sanity-insanity ratio: Parth Radia is clearly sane — VP of Generative AI at Ericsson, AT&T Labs research scientist, shipped commercial products. Kaahan Radia is clearly competent — five years building perception systems for autonomous drones at Zipline, CVPR and JAMA publications. The likely sibling co-founder relationship actually scores well in my framework; I trust deep pre-existing relationships over incubator matchmaking. But the idea itself isn't insane — it's obvious. When I get excited, it's because a very reasonable person is pitching me something that makes everyone else in the room say "that's crazy." Real-time video for AI agents makes everyone in the room say "yeah, obviously." The calibration here is sane people with a sane idea, which in my framework is just... boring. It's a well-executed play in an already-identified market opportunity. Keyframe Labs' $0.06/min pricing is an interesting cost wedge, but competing on price in a compute-intensive category against Tavus ($64.2M) is not a structural advantage — it's either unsustainable margin compression or an efficiency lead that erodes as model architectures converge.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Parth Radia has literally more hands-on experience building this specific product than almost anyone on earth. The real-time conversational segment is architecturally distinct from async video — different model architectures (streaming inference vs. batch rendering), different infrastructure (WebRTC vs. CDN), and Synthesia/HeyGen pivoting would cannibalize their per-video pricing models. Keyframe Labs could be to real-time conversational video what Solana was to high-throughput blockchains — a technically superior new entrant that the market dismissed because incumbents seemed unbeatable. And the timing is perfect: the explosion of voice agents from Vapi, Bland, and Retell creates a massive installed base of agents that need faces. I take this bull case seriously. But the Solana parallel breaks down on two dimensions: Solana was a new Layer 1 consensus mechanism — a protocol primitive — while Keyframe Labs is an application-layer API. And Solana was priced at essentially zero because the market had written off alternative L1s. Keyframe Labs is entering a category where Sequoia is already writing $40M checks. The structural position and the pricing are both wrong for my framework. What would flip me: if Keyframe Labs demonstrated a proprietary model architecture that Tavus and Synthesia provably cannot replicate within 18 months — a measurable quality or latency gap that compounds rather than converges — and if the real-time segment specifically were being avoided or underpriced by the market rather than actively funded by top-tier VCs.

Keyframe Labs is a competent team in a real market building a real product. That's exactly the profile that returns 2-3x — not the asymmetric 100x+ outcome I'm looking for. The two dimensions that lock this as a Pass: Consensus Mispricing (9/30) because the AI avatar category is consensus-hot with Sequoia, Scale, and hundreds of millions in competitive funding already deployed, and Structural Durability (4/15) because this is an AI application layer that incumbents with distribution will replicate. Parth Radia's domain expertise is genuine, but a brilliant founder in a consensus-priced category where the structural moats don't exist is a bet where the risk is not in the price — it's hidden behind the excitement.
