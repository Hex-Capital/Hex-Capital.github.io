# Trellis -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Lodovico Benvenuti built AI agents at Conduit, a YC W24 company doing AI guest messaging across 77,000+ properties. Jan Sahagun sold software at Besty AI, Guestway, and Boom — three STR tech vendors, with Boom being the closest positional competitor to what Trellis is now building. Both founders left direct competitors to start Trellis in the same market they just departed. I've seen this pattern before, and it's not the pattern I look for. When I backed Uber, it wasn't because the founders had previously worked at taxi dispatch companies — it was because the product had already escaped into the world and was changing a real person's life. Trellis's founders assembled from inside the vendor ecosystem, which gives them knowledge but not what I'd call a forge. Lodovico did scale a property management company to roughly $2M in revenue and 150+ listings — that's genuine operator experience, and it means he felt the fragmentation problem in his own business. That lifts him above a pure career-optimizer. But when I'd ask "tell me your story," I expect I'd hear "I worked in this industry and saw the gap." That's founder-market fit. It's not biographical inevitability.

Trellis claims on their YC page that "software is being commoditized" and that they reached feature parity with competitors in two weeks. I sat with that claim for a while because it cuts in a direction the founders may not intend. If Trellis can replicate Guesty's features in two weeks, what stops Guesty — sitting on $409M in funding and a $900M valuation — from building Trellis's AI agent layer in a quarter? Hostaway closed at $543M and a billion-dollar valuation in December 2024. Boom, which Jan previously worked at, raised $12.7M and is already positioned as AI-native with its own agentic manager called "BAM." Trellis is entering a space where nearly a billion dollars in combined competitor funding is actively pursuing the same AI-native thesis. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs — it's a space they're writing large checks into. There is no stigma, no regulatory frontier, no political discomfort. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was toxic in Silicon Valley. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs squeamish. Trellis is building operational software for vacation rentals. Nobody is uncomfortable with that thesis at a dinner party.

Trellis describes the problem as "5–10 disconnected software tools with no unified data layer" causing "reactive operations, declining reviews, lower rates, and homeowner churn." That's a real pain point — I don't doubt it. But it's a workflow fragmentation problem, not an invisible system exploiting information asymmetry against people who don't understand it. When I backed Affirm, Max Levchin was attacking the opaque structure of credit card lending — hidden fees, compounding interest that consumers couldn't parse, a system designed to profit from confusion. When Ownwell automates property tax appeals, it exposes a bureaucratic system that extracts value from homeowners who don't know they're overpaying. Trellis's target customer — professional STR operators managing 10 to 150+ units — knows exactly what their problem is. They know their tools don't talk to each other. They know they're spending too much time on operations. The system isn't hidden from them; it's just annoying. That's a legitimate business to build, but it doesn't activate the part of my framework where I develop the deepest conviction.

The bull case an advocate would make: Lodovico is a rare combination of operator (150+ listings, ~$2M revenue) and AI engineer (built the actual agents at Conduit that served this market). Jan owns STR Tech Report, a media platform that provides direct distribution into the exact buyer persona Trellis needs. Together, Trellis has product, engineering, and distribution covered by a six-person team already showing an 84.5% AI automation rate on their dashboard and claiming operators save 20 hours weekly. The 200+ integrations create switching costs once adopted. The AI inflection point is real — LLMs reaching production-grade reliability for multi-step agentic workflows in 2024–2025 creates a genuine architectural opening that legacy PMS platforms built on traditional SaaS codebases cannot easily exploit without cannibalizing their own per-listing revenue models. This is an innovator's dilemma moment. I checked my known exceptions: RTFKT validated that narrow timing windows can substitute for infrastructure moats. Could Trellis be catching the AI-native PMS wave at precisely the right moment? The problem is that RTFKT's timing window was genuinely narrow — digital fashion before the NFT collapse, with Nike as an identifiable acquirer. Trellis's timing window is wide open and crowded. Boom is already there. Conduit is expanding. Guesty and Hostaway are adding AI features. There is no narrow window that only Trellis can squeeze through.

Trellis's aspirational data network effect — where "every workflow that works, every SOP that survives contact with guests, every resolution pattern" feeds back into the system — is described in the dossier as exactly that: aspirational. They're currently operating one property management company's operations. The cross-company knowledge network doesn't exist yet and may never compound meaningfully if competitors with 77,000+ properties (Conduit) or enterprise-scale customer bases (Guesty, Hostaway) build their own data flywheels first. My biggest wins — Crusoe Energy, Flexport, Carta — were infrastructure bets where the first mover had a structural reason to win, not just a temporal one. Crusoe had stranded energy assets that nobody else was capturing. Flexport had logistics network effects. Carta had cap table switching costs in a market with no real competitor. Trellis has integrations with public APIs that every competitor can also access, operating in a market with four well-funded competitors all converging on the same AI-agent-for-STR thesis.

One verifiable condition would flip me: if Lodovico showed me that the data Trellis accumulates from cross-operational orchestration — across communications, tasks, workforce, smart homes, bookings, and accounting simultaneously — creates resolution patterns that are structurally impossible for single-domain competitors like Conduit (messaging only) or Boom (PMS replacement) to replicate, and if early design partners demonstrated measurably superior outcomes (higher review scores, lower homeowner churn) that prove the cross-operational data layer is the moat rather than a feature, I'd reconsider. That would transform Trellis from a workflow tool into infrastructure. But today, with one customer and an aspirational network effect, I don't see that evidence. Trellis is competent founders building a real product in a real market — but it's a consensus AI play in a well-funded space, solving a visible problem without a hidden system to disrupt. The biographical forge is career-derived, not life-forged. The defensibility thesis undermines itself. This isn't my kind of bet.
