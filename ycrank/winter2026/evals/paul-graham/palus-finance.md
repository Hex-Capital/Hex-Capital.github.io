# Palus Finance -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that catches my attention about Palus is the pivot. They came into YC with a consumer savings product and switched to startup treasury management because they noticed their own YC funding sitting in an account earning less than it should. That's organic problem discovery. They didn't read a report about the treasury management market; they opened their bank statement and did the math. When other companies in their batch said "yeah, same problem," they had the sense to follow the signal. That's exactly the kind of idea that grows from lived experience rather than a brainstorming session. It reminds me of how the best YC ideas often emerge after founders arrive — the batch itself becomes a laboratory.

But here's where I get stuck: the schlep factor is weak. The reason Mercury and Brex haven't offered agency MBS portfolios isn't that the problem is too ugly for them to contemplate. It's that it would cannibalize their MMF fee arrangements and complicate their banking UX. That's an innovator's dilemma, which is a real structural advantage — but it's not schlep blindness. Schlep blindness is when the problem is so tedious and painful that smart people literally can't see it. Everyone in fintech can see this opportunity. The yield spread is right there in the numbers. What's protecting Palus isn't that nobody wants to do this; it's that the incumbents currently make more money not doing it. That protection evaporates the moment Mercury decides the opportunity is big enough to justify a new product tier, and with $650M in annualized revenue and 200K+ business customers, they can move fast when they choose to.

The core business is essentially a financial strategy choice — agency MBS instead of money market funds — wrapped in a startup-friendly interface. The strategy itself is "well-understood in institutional finance and is not proprietary," which the dossier admits plainly. Sam Lushtak's experience building investment research platforms for asset managers gives him exposure to this world, which is meaningful. But there's a difference between building tools that help other people manage $3 trillion and actually managing money yourself under SEC scrutiny with a two-person team. The regulatory schlep of being a registered investment advisor handling client securities is real, but I'm not convinced these founders have the depth of financial services operating experience to navigate it at scale. This isn't like Stripe, where the founders were programmers solving a programmer's problem with technical elegance. This is consultants applying institutional finance knowledge to a smaller customer segment.

The bull case is worth engaging with seriously. If the rate environment keeps compressing MMF yields while MBS spreads hold, that 1-1.5% premium becomes proportionally more valuable — a startup with $5M idle could capture an extra $50-75K annually, which is real money. The YC network is an ideal distribution channel for exactly this product, and the Plaid integration means no bank switch is required. If Palus accumulates enough AUM to generate data on startup cash drawdown patterns, they could optimize duration matching in ways that create a genuine information advantage. And the innovator's dilemma protecting them from Mercury might last longer than I expect — large companies are remarkably slow to cannibalize profitable business lines, even obvious ones. If Palus gets to $500M AUM through the YC network alone, the 0.25% fee generates $1.25M in revenue with a two-person team. That's well past ramen profitable.

But I keep returning to the same concern: this is a financial arbitrage, not a technology company. The product is choosing a different column on a spreadsheet. The yield advantage depends on macro conditions outside anyone's control. The moat is thin. And the path to venture-scale returns requires billions in AUM from a customer segment — pre-Series B startups — that is small and transient by nature. Startups spend their cash. The best customers churn as they succeed. That's a structural headwind that no amount of execution can overcome. The founders are competent and the discovery was organic, but the problem they found isn't painful enough or hard enough to build something that compounds over decades.
