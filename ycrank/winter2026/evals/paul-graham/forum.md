# Forum -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I always ask is: how did the founders find this problem? When Owen Botkin was trading long/short equities at Balyasny, he presumably noticed that social media buzz and cultural attention move stock prices. That's a real observation. But the leap from "attention affects markets" to "let's build a regulated exchange where people trade attention indices" is the kind of conceptual hop that happens in a brainstorming session, not the kind of discovery that happens when something is genuinely broken in your life. Nobody is lying awake at night unable to hedge their attention exposure. Brands manage cultural relevance with entire departments, agencies, and analytics tools. The fact that there's no direct financial instrument to trade on "attention" might not be a market failure at all -- it might be because the underlying signal is too noisy and gameable to support a credible market. Sometimes there's no product in a space because there shouldn't be one.

The competitive landscape tells you something important about the idea's origin. When I see Noise raising $7.1M from Paradigm to build essentially the same thing on-chain, Versus raising $1.5M for cultural prediction markets, and Kalshi sitting at $11B with the regulatory infrastructure already built -- I worry that "attention trading" is a trend-derived idea that multiple smart people arrived at simultaneously by reading the same signals. That's the opposite of schlep blindness. In schlep blindness, an obvious problem sits unaddressed because everyone unconsciously flinches from the pain of solving it. Here, I see multiple teams racing toward a market that may not have organic demand. Stripe worked because millions of developers already needed to process payments and the existing tools were terrible. Who already needs to trade attention indices? The question largely answers itself.

The regulatory strategy deserves careful examination because it's the entire moat thesis. Forum wants to be CFTC-regulated, which is genuinely hard -- the process is expensive, slow, and uncertain. That's a real schlep. But it's the wrong kind. Stripe's regulatory schlep protected a solution to a problem everyone already had. Forum's regulatory schlep gates a product whose demand hasn't been established. You're spending your limited pre-seed runway pursuing regulatory approval for a market that might not have participants when it opens. That's a dangerous sequence: build the exchange before proving anyone wants to trade on it. The liquidity cold-start problem compounds this. Even if you get CFTC approval, you need enough traders on both sides to make meaningful markets. At pre-seed with virtual currency only, there's no way to validate that real-money demand exists.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. If attention truly becomes a distinct, tradable asset class -- the way derivatives on weather or carbon emissions became legitimate markets -- then the first regulated US exchange to offer it would be enormously valuable. Owen's Balyasny background gives him genuine intuition about exchange mechanics and financial product design. Joseph's NASA and GEICO engineering background is solid. They were high school debate partners, which means the co-founder relationship predates the startup by years -- that's one of the strongest team signals I can see here. The timing argument has legs too: the CFTC is becoming more permissive, prediction markets just had their breakout year with $44B in volume, and the IAB just standardized attention measurement. If you believe the category will exist, these founders are plausible people to build it. But "if the category will exist" is doing an enormous amount of work in that sentence. The entire investment depends on a market-creation bet, not a market-capture bet, and those are fundamentally different risk profiles.

What I keep coming back to is that Forum requires at least two miracles: the CFTC has to approve attention-based event contracts as a new category, and then enough traders have to care about attention indices to create liquid markets. Each miracle individually is uncertain. Together, the probability drops considerably. Compare this to Airbnb, where the founders only needed one miracle -- that strangers would trust each other enough to share living spaces. The demand for affordable accommodation was already proven. Forum is asking me to believe both that regulators will bless a novel instrument and that traders will materialize to use it. I pass on companies that need two miracles.
