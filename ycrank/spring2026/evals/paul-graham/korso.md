# Korso -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Korso is a competent engineering trio with one genuine manufacturing-floor veteran and zero hacker-culture trail — that is the signature, and the dossier specific that pins it is Martin Pan's prior automations-engineering role at General Motors sitting next to "No public GitHub repos found" for all three founders. That pairing tells me what kind of company Korso is: domain-credible, technically capable, and invisible to the filter I actually trust to surface outliers. The Penn Electric Racing thread between Daichi Hiraoka and Martin Pan gives me a real team — but a real team aimed at a market where Lumari (YC) and Turian ($3.78M raised, Berlin) are already swinging at the same RFQ/PO workflow.

On animal resourcefulness, Brian Armstrong's hand-built Android Bitcoin wallet after his **Coinbase** (2012) rejection is the canonical pre-funding artifact — a thing the founder shipped before the institutional check, evidence the animal showed up before anyone asked it to. Korso's dossier has no equivalent. The korsoai.com sample dashboard shows "$2.4M pipeline, 47 active quotes, 89% close rate" attributed to "Northbridge Metals" and "Blue Harbor Components" — fictional companies. I am not bothered that a pre-seed company has no revenue; I am bothered that the surface they chose to show the world is a mockup with invented logos instead of one real manufacturer Martin Pan got into a paid pilot by walking into a factory. Cereal boxes were the **Airbnb** (2009) tell because they were unscalable, embarrassing, and revenue-shaped; Korso's tell is the opposite — polished, scalable-looking, and revenue-pretend.

The lowest-rung pattern on hacker genealogy is the polished MBA AI-wrapper team in my Pass Example — the deck without the demo, the credential without the artifact. Korso is not that team; Daichi Hiraoka and Martin Pan are real engineers from UPenn with a formula-racing build history together. But the pattern still partly shows up: no GitHub for any of the three, no open-source contribution, no Hacker News footprint, no competitive-programming win, no Lisp-prize equivalent to Patrick Collison's Young Scientist work before **Stripe** (2010). Penn Electric Racing is engineering effort; it is not hacker tribal signal. When I cannot find a GitHub for the technical co-founders of an AI-agent company in 2026, I do not assume the trail is hidden — I assume it is thin.

On organic founder-pain, Martin Pan's GM automations-engineering line is the one fact in this dossier I keep returning to. It is not nothing. Drew Houston's USB stick at **Dropbox** (2007) was a daily annoyance he hit before he built; Pan walked plant floors before he built Korso. The compounding question over 18 months is whether that one founder's pain converts into the kind of unreasonable product taste that lets Korso ship a workflow Lumari cannot — or whether the GM exposure is too narrow to generalize across SAP, NetSuite, Dynamics, Sage, SYSPRO, Plex, and Acumatica simultaneously. The dossier lists seven ERPs; one founder has experience inside one manufacturer. That gap widens, not narrows, as Korso tries to ship.

On schlep, Korso would have to land three named mid-market manufacturer logos with paid pilots and signed IT-security reviews by month 12 to flip this dimension from "category fit, execution unproven" to "schlep moat earned." The category is real schlep — enterprise sales into SAP shops is grind nobody at the polished end of YC wants to run. But **Coinbase** (2012) earned its schlep moat by Brian Armstrong personally absorbing compliance pain, not by listing "compliance" on a feature page. Korso's korsoai.com lists "verification layers, scoped tools, dry-run validation, and audit trails" as architecture — those are the words a team uses before they have eaten the schlep, not after.

On co-founder configuration, compare Korso to **Airbnb** (2009): three founders, prior shared build history, complementary coverage. Korso has three founders, Hiraoka and Pan with two-plus years on Penn Electric Racing together, and Alex Liu from UC Irvine with no described prior relationship to the other two in the dossier. That is a stronger configuration than most pre-seed teams I see — not a hackathon pair, not a solo founder, not three strangers. It is the one dimension where Korso clearly clears my bar.

My 36-month forecast: Korso lands sub-$2M ARR or gets absorbed in a $20-60M feature-acquisition by NetSuite, Plex, or an ERP implementation-partner roll-up — most likely the acquihire end of that range, because Lumari is the same bet with a louder hacker pedigree and Turian already has $3.78M and a 2022 head start in Europe. The mechanism is straightforward: a three-engineer team without a hacker-trail differentiator, attacking a workflow that four named competitors are also attacking, with no shipped customer evidence at launch, gets compressed between Lumari's velocity above and SAP's native agent features below. The acquihire framing is exactly the **Kiko** (2005)-shaped tell I treat as data about ambition ceiling.

The strongest case against my pass is that Martin Pan's GM background is real organic-pain signal and the manufacturing-ERP space is genuinely schlep-coded — this could be the boring-looking **DoorDash** (2013) bet where revealed founder quality, not category, drives the outcome, and I should re-check the **Airbnb** (2009) Known Exception that lets me override idea-level skepticism when the founders surprise me. The bear evidence wins on one fact: **Airbnb** (2009) overrode my skepticism with cereal boxes, a specific pre-funding stunt I could name; Korso's equivalent surface is a fictional-customer dashboard, which is the inverse of an animal moment. I would flip to invest if Korso ships three real named manufacturer logos with paid pilots — not "Northbridge Metals" — within twelve months, or if Pan publishes working agent-orchestration code that visibly beats Lumari on the long-running-workflow reliability claim. Until then, pass.
