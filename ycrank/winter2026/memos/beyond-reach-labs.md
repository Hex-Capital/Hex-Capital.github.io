## The Idea

**Problem:** Satellites and in-space infrastructure are fundamentally power-constrained. The collective satellite fleet today uses approximately 50 MW of power, but demand is projected to scale to >500 GW by 2035, driven by orbital data centers, commercial space stations, mega-constellations, and lunar outposts (YC launch page). To generate more power, operators must launch larger solar arrays, but as arrays get longer, their first bending mode frequency drops, causing vibration interference with spacecraft attitude control. This forces a tradeoff: build heavier (stiffer but more expensive to launch) or shorter (less power). Current solutions include rigid panel arrays (e.g., ISS-style), roll-out arrays (Redwire ROSA), and fan-fold designs (Northrop Grumman UltraFlex). All face this fundamental length-stiffness-mass tradeoff, which limits how much power a single launch can deliver.

**Approach:** Beyond Reach Labs has developed two proprietary deployable structural concepts — Pop-Up Extending Trusses (PETS) and Hierarchical High-Expansion Ratio Deployable Structures (HERDS) — that change geometry in space to achieve large expansion ratios while maintaining structural rigidity (company website; Fogelson et al., IEEE Aerospace Conference 2023). A prototype demonstrated a 50x expansion ratio (company website). The arrays launch at dining-table size and deploy to football-field scale in orbit, delivering 10x more usable power without increasing launch mass or volume. The technology originates from Mitchell Fogelson's PhD research at Carnegie Mellon, funded by a NASA NIAC grant, and has been tested in microgravity via a NASA-funded zero-gravity flight experiment (Fogelson personal website).

**Differentiation:** Compared to Redwire's ROSA (roll-out blanket design, flight-proven on ISS), Beyond Reach Labs claims a different structural paradigm — metamaterial-based geometry change rather than roll-out or fan-fold mechanisms — achieving higher expansion ratios while remaining rigid. Compared to Northrop Grumman's UltraFlex (fan-fold), the company claims 10x longer reach. Compared to Astrix Astronautics (inflatable deployment), Beyond Reach Labs uses rigid structural elements rather than gas inflation. Compared to cell-level innovators like mPower Technology (flexible mesh solar cells) and Solestial (self-healing silicon cells), Beyond Reach Labs differentiates on the structure/deployment mechanism rather than the photovoltaic material.

## Defensibility

The company's core technology is protected by patents (described as "patented deployable design" on the YC page and company website; specific patent numbers not publicly disclosed). The technology originates from Mitchell Fogelson's PhD research at Carnegie Mellon on mechanical metamaterials, representing years of R&D investment. The two proprietary concepts (PETS and HERDS) are published in peer-reviewed literature (IEEE Aerospace Conference 2023), establishing prior art. Technical advisors include Tom Cooley (25 years at AFRL, former Chief Scientist of Space Vehicles Directorate) and Zac Manchester (MIT professor, director of the Robotic Exploration Lab), providing institutional knowledge and credibility with government buyers (company website).

Today's defensibility rests on patent protection and specialized domain expertise. Over time, flight heritage (once the Q2 2027 demo is completed) would create switching costs, as space customers heavily weight demonstrated in-orbit performance in procurement decisions.

**Market structure:** Incumbent deployable array manufacturers (Redwire, Northrop Grumman, Airbus) have existing product lines with flight heritage and sunk R&D. However, their architectures (roll-out, fan-fold, rigid panel) face the fundamental length-stiffness-mass tradeoff that Beyond Reach Labs claims to solve with a different structural paradigm. For incumbents to replicate the metamaterial-based approach, they would need to develop new IP outside their existing patent portfolios and potentially cannibalize their current product lines. Redwire, the closest incumbent competitor, is publicly traded with $335M revenue but a $226.6M net loss in 2025 (Redwire SEC filing), which may constrain investment in entirely new architectures.

**Commoditization risk:** The deployable structure concept builds on published academic research (HERDS paper, IEEE Aerospace 2023), meaning the theoretical principles are in the public domain. However, the engineering implementation — converting metamaterial theory into flight-qualified hardware — requires specialized mechanical engineering expertise, extensive testing, and qualification cycles. Other academic labs (e.g., Caltech's Space Solar Power Project, DARPA NOM4D program participants) are working on related large-scale deployable concepts. Startups like Astrix Astronautics (inflatable deployment) represent alternative approaches to the same expansion-ratio problem.

## Key Risks

**Technology readiness gap:** The core technology has been demonstrated in prototype (50x expansion ratio) and in microgravity (zero-g flight), but has not been deployed in orbit. The first in-space demo is targeted for late Q2 2027. Space hardware qualification is a multi-year process with high failure risk; vibration, thermal cycling, atomic oxygen, and radiation exposure in LEO/GEO present failure modes not captured in ground testing.

**Incumbent flight heritage advantage:** Redwire's ROSA arrays are already operating on the ISS with verified performance data. Northrop Grumman's UltraFlex has flown to Mars. Government and commercial satellite customers typically require flight heritage as a procurement prerequisite. Beyond Reach Labs must successfully complete its demo flight and accumulate operational hours before competing for major contracts, creating a multi-year gap during which incumbents continue to extend their heritage advantage.

**LOI-to-revenue conversion uncertainty:** The $175M+ in LOIs (YC launch page) represent non-binding expressions of interest, not committed contracts. LOIs in the space industry frequently do not convert to purchase orders, particularly for pre-flight-heritage technologies. The identity of the LOI counterparties is not disclosed, making independent verification impossible.

**Supply chain and manufacturing scale-up:** Transitioning from a 2-person team with prototype-level production to manufacturing flight-qualified deployable arrays at scale requires building or contracting aerospace-grade manufacturing, quality control, and testing infrastructure. The company has ~$200K in non-dilutive funding plus YC standard deal; significant additional capital will be required to reach the demo flight and initial production.

**Regulatory and export control:** Space hardware is subject to ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) and EAR (Export Administration Regulations) in the United States. As a dual-use technology with potential military applications (powering military satellites, lunar outposts), the company may face export restrictions that limit its addressable market to U.S. and allied-nation customers.
