# The Token Company -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's a company building cost-optimization middleware for a resource that's deflating at roughly 10x per year. That's the signal I can't get past. LLM inference costs are on one of the steepest deflationary curves in the history of computing — comparable to Moore's Law at its most aggressive — and The Token Company is building a business whose primary value proposition is making that already-plummeting cost 66% lower. This is structurally analogous to the macro assumption that destroyed my KiOR investment: I bet on biofuel economics that depended on oil reaching $150-200 per barrel, and when oil went the other direction, the technology achievement was irrelevant. The Token Company's consequence-of-success calculation embeds an assumption that LLM tokens will remain expensive enough to justify a compression middleware layer. The trajectory of the market says otherwise. If this succeeds completely — every LLM application on earth routes through their API — the result is a thin margin infrastructure business in a market where the absolute dollar savings shrink every quarter. That's not a problem whose consequence of success justifies the effort.

The company's pivot to claiming compression *improves* output quality, not just reduces cost, is the one move that could rescue the thesis. If shorter prompts consistently produce better LLM outputs — and the 268K-vote blind arena and benchmark gains (+2.7pp on financial QA, +4.0pp on reading comprehension, +5% purchase amount lift) are real and generalizable — then the value proposition decouples from token pricing entirely. A quality layer doesn't deflate with cost. That's a genuinely interesting technical claim. But here's what I notice that a generic analyst would miss: if prompt compression reliably improves LLM output quality, the LLM providers themselves will internalize it instantly. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are not going to leave a systematic quality improvement sitting in a third-party middleware layer. Microsoft has already built the open-source version (LLMLingua, 4.4K GitHub stars, integrated into LangChain and LlamaIndex). Apple has published hierarchical compression research. A NAACL 2025 survey documents the breadth of existing academic approaches. The concept isn't contrarian — it's consensus. When the incumbents and the academics are already working on your exact approach, you're optimizing within the system, not disrupting it. Nobody's CTO told these founders their idea was impossible. That silence is the wrong signal.

Let me give the bull case its full weight. Otso Veister? is 18 years old, programming since age 7, building production ML models in PyTorch and CUDA, writing systems code in Rust, winning national competitions in physics and problem-solving. The technical velocity here is real — this kid's rate of learning over the past decade is in the top fraction of a percent for his age. If he were directing that learning rate at a problem whose consequence of success matched his talent, I'd be paying very close attention. The named customer (Pax Historia, 193B tokens/month) demonstrates the product works and creates measurable business impact. The angel investors from Hugging Face, Supercell, and Wolt suggest people who understand ML infrastructure see something here. And the agentic workflow explosion — multi-step chains generating massive context — means total token consumption is rising even as per-token costs fall. There's a version of reality where token volume growth outpaces price deflation and the absolute dollar savings market grows. If the quality improvement claim holds across diverse workloads, and if agentic workflows create structural prompt bloat that compression permanently addresses, this could be a solid infrastructure business.

But a solid infrastructure business is exactly the ceiling I see. The consequence of maximum success is that LLM API calls cost less and perform marginally better. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. No physical reality changes. No industry operates fundamentally differently. This is a better analytics dashboard for inference costs — well-executed, technically sound, and, in the framework I've used for forty years, inconsequential. The founders have reduced their probability of failure by choosing a safe, validated problem space where Microsoft Research, Apple, a YC batchmate with EPFL PhDs, and multiple academic groups are all converging. That convergence is de-risking, and de-risking is the enemy of consequential outcomes. Otso Veister? is building the wrong company for his talent. He should be working on something that terrifies him and matters.
