# Meridian -- Paul Graham Evaluation

"AI operating system for consulting firms" is the kind of phrase that should make you nervous. Not because it's wrong, but because it's too right. Everyone nods. No one flinches. When I read through Meridian's positioning, I couldn't find the moment where a knowledgeable person would say "that's a terrible idea." Consulting firms losing knowledge when employees leave? Of course. AI that searches documents and generates decks? Obviously useful. $23.2 billion TAM in knowledge management? The number itself is a warning sign — it means the market is already defined, already measured, already being attacked by Glean at $200M ARR, by Kenley out of YC S24, by Guru, by Bloomfire, by Interloom with its $16.5M seed. Meridian's idea sits squarely in the quadrant of "seems like a good idea" and "is a good idea." That quadrant pays well for incumbents with distribution. It pays poorly for seed-stage startups because the return premium has been competed away before they arrive.

The founding team — Kashyap Nathan, Chris Farrington, Shanay Champaneri — have resumes that read beautifully on paper. Palantir, OpenAI, Sandia National Labs, DARPA, Robinhood. Georgia Tech and Cornell. These are real credentials. But when I look closer, I see internships and research positions, not full-time roles where you own outcomes. Farrington's LinkedIn still says "Incoming SWE Intern — Deloitte," which suggests the consulting domain expertise comes from a summer internship at best. Nathan's "FDE at Palantir" could be an internship or a six-month stint. The dossier gives me credentials but not stories. When I evaluate founders, I'm looking for the X-ray — the specific narrative of a problem they hit, a system they hacked, a constraint they turned into an advantage. Truly resourceful founders generate these anecdotes involuntarily; the absence of any such story in Meridian's public materials is itself a mild negative.

The organic discovery question is where Meridian loses me most. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. When I read that Meridian's problem framing leads with "42% of job-specific expertise disappears when someone departs" and "$10M in lost productivity for a typical 500-person firm," I hear market research, not lived frustration. Compare this to Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive on the bus. Houston's origin story takes ten seconds and you immediately understand why he had to build Dropbox. Meridian's founders came from Palantir and OpenAI — enterprise AI deployment shops — and chose consulting as their target vertical. That's "identified an opportunity" thinking, not "lived in the future and built what's missing." If any of them had spent two years as an actual consultant, drowning in SharePoint folders and losing institutional knowledge every time a senior manager left, the pitch would sound different. It would start with anger, not statistics.

Meridian claims ~$700K in contracted revenue four months in, with 3x month-over-month growth and competitive bake-off wins against Databricks and Palantir. If true, this is genuinely impressive and would override several of my concerns — because "make something people want" is the ultimate test and revenue is the hardest-to-fake signal. But every one of these numbers is self-reported on the a16z Speedrun portal with zero independent verification. No customer testimonials, no press coverage, no third-party validation. "Contracted revenue" is also not the same as collected revenue — it could mean signed LOIs or pilot agreements that haven't converted. The YC batch departure adds ambiguity: they got into F25 and then left. Maybe they had enough traction to not need it. Maybe something else happened. I can't tell, and the absence of explanation is concerning. The bull case for Meridian depends almost entirely on this traction being real and durable, and right now I'm being asked to take it on faith from student founders I've never met.

The strongest argument for Meridian is that these are technically capable young builders — exactly the demographic I've bet on with Patrick Collison, Sam Altman, and the Bindwell founders. Nathan dropped out of Georgia Tech, which shows conviction. The team has worked at places where you learn to deploy AI at enterprise scale. If the $700K is real and the growth rate holds, the data moat thesis becomes interesting — behavioral data on how consulting work actually gets done could be genuinely proprietary. But when I backed young founders before, the common thread was that they were hackers building for hackers (Stripe, Replit) or had discovered the problem through personal obsession (Bindwell's teen founders in pesticide chemistry). Meridian is building for consulting managing partners — a buyer persona none of these founders appear to have inhabited. That distance between builder and buyer is a pattern I've seen fail more than succeed.

Meridian sits in the space between "interesting team" and "wrong problem selection" for my framework. The founding team's youth and technical credentials earn some baseline respect, but the idea lacks the contrarian ugliness that creates moats, the problem discovery lacks the organic anger that produces deep product taste, and the traction data lacks the verification that would let me override those concerns. I'd want to see Meridian's founders pivot to something closer to their own lived experience — the way I'd redirect a smart team toward the problem they actually feel rather than the one they researched.
