# Rex -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Rex is what happens when two operators who lived inside a finance-ops platform for four years aim at a real schlep, but arrive without the hacker tells I usually need to write the check on my own money. Merlin Kafka and Lewis Blackwood spent four years scaling Sequence (a16z-backed) to Series A, and Rex's claim to manage ">$500M in receivables from F100 companies" pre-YC is unusually concrete for Summer 2026 -- but Lewis read Economics at Cambridge, Merlin's lineage is CPO not CTO, and the dossier surfaces no Hacker News footprint, no shipped open-source artifact, no competitive-programming prize. The signature is operator-grade schlep coverage paired with a missing hacker ceiling.

Coinbase (2012) is the load-bearing analogy on Rex's strongest axis: schlep zone. Brian Armstrong volunteered for bitcoin compliance work nobody else in the batch would touch, and the unpleasantness itself became the moat. Rex is doing the 2026 equivalent on a smaller scale -- triaging inboxes, posting to supplier portals, integrating across NetSuite, SAP, Dynamics, Workday, and Oracle Fusion, and pricing on outcomes so they eat the SLA risk themselves. That last clause is what separates Rex from the AI-wrapper pile; "pay for outcomes only" means the founders have personally signed up for the long tail of exception failures across heterogeneous ERPs. Where Rex diverges from Coinbase is the regulatory grade of the schlep -- O2C exceptions are operational schlep, not a federal-license schlep, and SOC 2 Type II merely "pending" is the wrong direction on the one compliance gate F100 buyers actually demand.

The MBA-deck pass case I described in my reference voice -- glossy team, no GitHub, "we'll hire engineers post-funding" -- is the failure pattern I check Rex against on hacker genealogy, and Rex partially trips it. Lewis Blackwood does have a GitHub handle (@lewisblackwood), which is something, but the dossier explicitly notes star counts are not retrievable and surfaces no notable open-source contribution; Merlin Kafka has no GitHub at all in the search. Compare against Patrick Collison's Young Scientist Lisp prize before Stripe (2010) or Aaron Swartz co-authoring the RSS spec before Reddit (2005) -- those are the tribal markers that flipped me. Rex's CTO is a competent engineering lead by employment history, not a hacker by community footprint, and that gap is the single largest constraint on my willingness to underwrite a frighteningly-ambitious ceiling here.

Animal resourcefulness for Rex is real but operator-shaped rather than cereal-box-shaped. The Airbnb (2009) stunt I keep retelling worked because Chesky and Gebbia invented revenue out of nowhere with no permission and no precedent; what Rex has instead is ">$500M receivables managed" before YC plus #1 of 2,500 teams in the Vercel AI Accelerator with a Vercel Ventures check from Guillermo Rauch personally. Over eighteen months that compounds differently than a stunt -- it compounds into named-logo case studies if the founders push for them, or into vendor-of-record sleep if they get comfortable. The dossier's failure to disclose a single F100 logo by name is the tell I'd want to resolve before Demo Day.

Idea-organic-to-founder-pain is where Rex's score would have to flip for me to move tiers. Counterfactual: if Merlin had been a marketing hire at Sequence rather than CPO, the "saw firsthand that at-scale O2C still runs on manual labour" line in Rex's YC blurb would read as positioning rather than as biography. He was CPO for four years inside a B2B billing platform, which is roughly the most direct adjacency a finance-AI founder can have without literally being the CFO of a F100. That is genuine organic origin from "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) -- though it is organic-by-adjacency, not organic-by-personal-pain the way Drew Houston's lost USB stick was organic to Dropbox (2007).

Stripe (2010) is the single-anchor parallel on cofounder configuration, but only structurally, not in kind. The Collisons had two years and a prior $5M Auctomatic outcome together; Rex's Kafka and Blackwood have four years building Sequence from zero to Series A together, with clean CPO/CTO complementarity. That is a stronger working-relationship base than most pairs I see at Summer 2026 application time, and it neutralizes my usual cofounder-chemistry risk.

My 36-month forecast for Rex: $12-30M ARR concentrated across 8-15 F100 accounts, acquired by HighRadius or BlackLine for $150-400M, or alternatively raising a credible Series B at a $300-500M valuation -- but not becoming a Stripe-scale outcome, because the hacker ceiling that turns operational competence into category-defining product isn't in this team. The mechanism: operator-founders with deep domain access close real enterprise revenue fast, but the same operator instincts that win SAP integrations rarely produce the ten-year second-act product that escapes the incumbent's gravity.

The strongest objection is the Airbnb (2009) known exception itself -- I had mentally rejected that idea and funded the animals anyway because the cereal-box stunt overrode my skepticism. The bull case for Rex argues that ">$500M receivables managed" pre-YC plus Vercel's #1/2,500 selection IS the 2026 animal signal, that demanding GitHub stars from a CTO with four years scaling Sequence's engineering is me fighting a 2007 war, and that enterprise integration breadth across five named ERPs is the new schlep equivalent of Coinbase's compliance grind. The bear-case rebuttal that wins: that $500M number is self-reported with zero F100 logos disclosed, the homepage "342 customers / $18.4M" dashboard the dossier flags as possibly illustrative is exactly the disclosure-hygiene tell I distrust, and Sequence raised a $20M Series A in December 2025 to "automate revenue operations -- billing and invoice automation," which is the same product surface from the same investor ecosystem -- founders building adjacent to their prior company's expanding scope is a relationship-and-non-compete problem that compresses Rex's option value. Flip condition: one F100 logo disclosed publicly by name plus SOC 2 Type II completed within six months, and I move to Invest.
