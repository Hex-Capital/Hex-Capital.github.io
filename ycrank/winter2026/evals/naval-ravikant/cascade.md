# Cascade -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I see isn't the founders or the technology -- it's Raindrop. A company with $15M from Lightspeed, already training "small, custom models that adapt to the unique shape of each AI product" for production monitoring. That's not an adjacent competitor. That's the same thesis with thirty times the capital. Behind Raindrop sits Braintrust at $80M and an $800M valuation, Arize at $131M with Uber and the U.S. Navy as clients, and a trail of acquisitions -- ClickHouse absorbing Langfuse, Snyk absorbing Invariant Labs, Anthropic acqui-hiring HumanLoop. When the category is consolidating before you've shipped a visible product, the market has already priced in the optimism. Anyone chasing this market at pre-seed in early 2026 is arriving to a party that's well underway.

On specific knowledge: Adam researched agentic safety and graph reasoning at BAIR. Haluk built production monitoring at Netflix and Amazon and studied failure mode taxonomies at BAIR. The combination is legitimate -- one researcher, one production engineer, both from a top lab. But I have to ask the harder question: could a competent recruiter assemble this same skill combination in three months? BAIR produces dozens of AI safety researchers per year. Netflix and Amazon produce thousands of monitoring engineers. The domain overlap is real but the knowledge is transferable. I don't see the decade of obsessive curiosity that made Evan Williams uniquely qualified for Twitter, or the design philosophy that made Ivan Zhao irreplaceable at Notion. What non-obvious insight do these founders have about agent failure modes that would surprise someone who's spent six months studying the same papers? The dossier doesn't surface one. The GitHub repo returns a 404. No public writing, no thought leadership, no contrarian framing of the problem. This looks like capable recent graduates entering a trending category, not founders whose specific knowledge forced them toward an inevitable company.

The leverage architecture is standard B2B SaaS. Each customer gets custom evaluator models trained on their production data -- which creates per-customer value but no cross-customer network effect. There's no protocol, no marketplace, no platform dynamic where one user's participation makes the system more valuable for others. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question-and-answer pair made the entire knowledge graph more valuable for every developer. Cascade's models are siloed by customer, which means the defensibility story is really about switching costs, not compounding leverage. Switching costs from ML models are real but modest -- a new competitor with better models can overcome them in a single quarter.

The bull case deserves honest articulation. Agent reliability is genuinely the bottleneck for enterprise AI deployment. If 40% of enterprise applications embed agents by 2027, every one needs monitoring infrastructure purpose-built for non-deterministic multi-step systems. Datadog and New Relic are architected for deterministic request-response patterns -- retrofitting them for agent workflows is structurally hard. A focused team with the right research background could build something meaningfully better than what the incumbents bolt on. And if Cascade's custom evaluator models genuinely learn company-specific behavioral norms that improve with production volume, the data flywheel could become real. YC gives them distribution into exactly the engineering teams deploying agents today. For this to work, they'd need to move faster than Raindrop, build evaluator models that are demonstrably superior, and lock in a cohort of design-partner customers before the category gets fully absorbed. It's possible -- but it requires outrunning better-funded competitors with the same playbook, and the acquisition pattern in this space suggests that even successful execution leads to a $50-100M acqui-hire, not a compounding independent company.

The category's acquisition trajectory is the structural problem I can't solve. When ClickHouse, Coralogix, Anthropic, and Snyk are all buying AI monitoring companies, the market is telling you that this capability gets absorbed into larger platforms rather than standing alone. That compresses the outcome distribution in exactly the wrong direction for my portfolio -- I need investments where nine go to zero and the tenth goes 1,000x. An observability tool that's likely acquired for $50M before reaching escape velocity is optimized for the wrong game. I want founders building for decades, not positioning for absorption.
