# Blueprints -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Blueprints is what happens when a real market re-opening meets a thin distribution position: prediction-market volume crossed $10B/month in March 2026 (TRM Labs), Ryan Morrissey and Bence Redmond have shipped a working visual block-builder plus an LLM strategist on top of co-located infra, and yet Blueprints sits as a hosted GUI bolted onto a single venue — Polymarket — that already publishes its own agents SDK at github.com/Polymarket/agents. The signature is catalyst-rich, index-poor. That's the kind of company that catches the wave but doesn't get to keep the beach.

Anduril (2022) is the rung I keep reaching for on the catalyst dimension: Ukraine compressed Pentagon software inadequacy into a 24-month re-opening that peers were still calling a graveyard. Blueprints has a real analog — Polymarket plus Kalshi went from "niche crypto curiosity" to ~$24B combined monthly volume by April 2026 (Pew, 2026) with 840K unique wallets, and 14 of the top 20 most profitable Polymarket wallets are now bots extracting from retail (Yahoo Finance, 2026). That is a specific recent catalyst, not steady-state TAM math. But Anduril's catalyst was paired with a workflow position that incumbents would *eventually integrate against*; Blueprints' catalyst is paired with a venue that has already shipped the open-source primitives that compete with Blueprints' own GUI. The catalyst is real and I'll score it accordingly, but the catalyst alone is what gets the meeting, not the check.

Lyft Series C is the failure-pattern I want to test Blueprints against on distribution position — I passed because #2 in a winner-take-all network-effect market captures a fraction of #1's economics, and the dossier showed no path to share inversion. Blueprints isn't quite #2 in network-effects, but the structural problem rhymes: Polystrat (backed by Valory's $13.8M financing) launched February 2026 with a self-custody autonomous agent that already outperforms 37% of human Polymarket participants and ran 4,200+ trades in its first month, while Blueprints has processed $500K total volume across 250+ users in a comparable window. Simmer is *literally* positioned as an "agent harness" — the exact word in Blueprints' tagline. TurbineFi, Rainmaker, and Bankr round out the bot terminal layer. When the venue itself runs builders.polymarket.com and ships first-party tooling, Blueprints is a thin GUI fighting four funded competitors plus the platform owner — that is not an index on a compounding category, that is a knife fight on someone else's floor.

On revenue velocity: $500K cumulative trading volume processed is not revenue, and at $20/mo Pro the unit economics imply that even 1,000 paying users gets Blueprints to ~$20K MRR — fine for a YC pre-seed snapshot, nowhere near the Harvey-style $3B→$5B→$8B "just working" signal I look for in AI dossiers. The mechanism that compounds or fails here over 18 months is straightforward: prediction-market retail traders only return to automated tooling if their PnL is positive, and retail PnL on Polymarket is structurally negative against latency-advantaged arbitrage bots that extracted ~$40M between April 2024 and April 2025. Blueprints' growth curve is gated by whether its hosted users can beat bots that Blueprints itself is not — and the dossier publishes no benchmark of "Prince"-generated strategy outcomes. Silence on growth in an AI dossier is a tell.

On founder shipping rhythm: Morrissey dropped out of the University of Limerick ISE programme after a Stripe internship and recruited Redmond out of an AWS SDE internship to build the first version of Blueprints — that is shipping behavior, not a credential parade, and it nudges me up from the default. The counterfactual that would flip this dimension a tier: a named prior shipped artifact at scale, or evidence that Morrissey and Redmond pushed three meaningful product surfaces between YC acceptance and Demo Day. The dossier shows a built product with visual builder, LLM Prince, backtest harness, and co-located execution — that's real surface area for two people — but it's the first artifact, not the third, and the Perplexity-template requires a shipping rhythm I can see across iterations, not a single working v1.

On single-miracle architecture, Blueprints is the parallel to Color Genomics inverted — Color was workflow on commodity sequencing, one miracle, GTM and product execution from there. Blueprints requires (a) the "Prince" LLM to translate plain-English theses into strategies that beat market consensus, (b) retail users to outperform latency-advantaged execution bots that already dominate, (c) Polymarket to choose not to vertically integrate hosted no-code despite shipping the agents repo, and (d) the CFTC/state regulatory posture on automated prediction-market trading to stay benign. That is four independent miracles compounded, and the NFX framework collapses fast under that math.

Forecast at 36 months: Blueprints is either acquired by Polymarket or Kalshi for talent at sub-$15M, or it's pivoted into a multi-venue analytics/copy-trading layer (Rainmaker's positioning) at sub-$2M ARR, or it's dead. The mechanism is the index-position failure compounding faster than the catalyst tailwind: Polymarket either ships first-party hosted tooling or favors one of the funded competitors via builders.polymarket.com, and Blueprints' $500K volume base doesn't reach escape velocity before that happens.

The strongest opposing case is the Decagon (2023) parallel — open AI market, real catalyst, workflow infrastructure, early days, two competent shippers, and prediction markets really did just re-open. A bull would argue the category is growing fast enough that even #3 captures a real business, that Polymarket's open agents repo is *enabling* not *competing* (the way Stripe's docs enabled rather than killed every adjacent fintech), and that Morrissey's Stripe DNA plus Redmond's AWS infra DNA is exactly the workflow-on-hard-thing template I funded with Color. That case stings because the catalyst dimension genuinely is the highest-weighted in my framework. But Decagon shipped into a category where the venue (the enterprise customer's own support stack) couldn't ship competing tooling; Blueprints ships into a category where the venue already did. The verdict flips if Blueprints hits $200K MRR within six months at 30%+ MoM growth AND ships verified multi-venue support (Kalshi plus on-chain) that breaks the Polymarket dependency — that combination would convert the catalyst into an index position and I would re-engage at the seed.
