# Origin -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first question I ask about any startup is how the founders found the problem. With Origin, the answer is conspicuously absent. I see two CS undergrads from UIUC — strong technical backgrounds, NVIDIA experience, competition wins, ML research — who are now building AI models for regulatory DNA design in gene therapy. But nothing in their histories connects them to genomics, gene therapy, or even biology in any lived-experience sense. Yash did robotics and computer vision research. Malhar did disease modeling with ML and worked at an AI company. These are impressive credentials, but they're the credentials of people who could have ended up working on any technically interesting problem. When I can't find the moment a founder personally collided with the problem they're solving, my default assumption is that the idea was generated rather than discovered — the output of a brainstorming session where someone asked "what's a hard problem where AI could be transformative?" and landed on gene therapy.

The underlying problem is real, I should be clear about that. Everyone working in cell and gene therapy knows that regulatory DNA elements — the promoters and enhancers that control when and where therapeutic genes express — are a bottleneck. It's been obvious for years. In that sense, this has the shape of a schlep-blindness opportunity: an important problem hiding in plain sight because tackling it requires bridging deep ML with wet-lab biology, which is tedious and slow and falls outside the comfort zone of both ML researchers and bench biologists. The schlep is genuinely there. But schlep blindness works as an investment signal when the founders encountered the schlep through their own work and decided to push through it anyway — like how the Collison brothers dealt with payments as developers before building Stripe. Here, I can't tell if the founders are pushing through a schlep they lived or one they identified from the outside.

The bull case is worth stating precisely, because if it's right, this is a big outcome. If Axis really does unify generation and prediction of regulatory DNA in a way that no other model does, and if Origin can build a proprietary wet-lab-validated dataset that compounds over time, they'd occupy a narrow but critical layer of the gene therapy stack that well-funded competitors like Dyno and Asimov don't currently address. Pharma companies would rather license specialized tools than build internal AI genomics capabilities. The advisory board — Manolis Kellis at MIT/Broad, Nicole Paulk at UCSF, Rashid Bashir at UIUC — is unusually strong for a four-person pre-seed company and suggests real scientific credibility. And Malhar publishing in Nature Scientific Reports in high school signals the kind of precocious drive that sometimes produces exceptional founders. If this team can get pharma partners running real therapeutic programs on their designs, the data flywheel kicks in and the moat becomes real. That's a legitimate path.

But here's what concerns me most: their primary benchmark is Google DeepMind's AlphaGenome, and their training data is the publicly available ENCODE V4 Registry. When your competitive differentiation rests on a 6.7% improvement over a model built by a company with functionally unlimited resources, and both of you are training on the same public data, your moat is thin. Origin acknowledges this — they say the moat is the proprietary wet-lab dataset. But they're four people with CS backgrounds. How exactly are they running wet-lab experiments at sufficient scale to build "the largest proprietary dataset of synthetic regulatory sequences across diverse cell-states"? That claim is aspirational, not operational. The gap between in silico benchmarks and clinical-grade regulatory elements involves years of biological validation, animal studies, and regulatory navigation that this team isn't currently equipped for.

On the technical-hacker dimension, they score well. These are clearly builders — they created the Axis model themselves, it demonstrably works, and the architectural decision to unify generation and prediction in a single transformer backbone shows genuine craft. That matters. But technical ability alone doesn't overcome the absence of organic founder-problem fit and the lack of any evidence that users are pulling this product toward them. There's a free API. No one is publicly using it, paying for it, or talking about it outside the founders' immediate network. A LinkedIn post from the CTO's mother getting 1,070 likes is social proof of a family network, not product demand.

I'd need to see two things to change my mind: first, a credible story for how these specific founders arrived at this specific problem — ideally involving actual work in a gene therapy context that exposed the regulatory element bottleneck firsthand. Second, even one pharma or biotech partner running a real therapeutic program using Origin's designs. Without those signals, this is smart people doing impressive technical work on a manufactured idea in a space where DeepMind is their neighbor and the bridge from benchmarks to biology is longer than the founders seem to realize.
