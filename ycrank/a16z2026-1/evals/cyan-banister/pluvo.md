# Pluvo -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Pluvo is not the founders or the technology — it's who else is in the room. Datarails raised $70M in January 2026 with $175M total and a $550M valuation. Abacum raised $60M in June 2025. Anaplan was acquired by Thoma Bravo for $10.7B. Every major VC in enterprise software is pouring money into AI-powered FP&A right now. When I see that kind of consensus enthusiasm, my instinct is to walk the other direction — not because the market is wrong, but because consensus markets don't produce the asymmetric outcomes I'm looking for. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "we're building AI for finance teams." They nod. They write checks. And that's exactly the problem for someone like me who needs the room to be uncomfortable.

My first question for any founder is: tell me your story, not the business. Alexandre Labréche is a former financial analyst and professional hockey player who worked at Ford. That's an interesting life — hockey requires physical courage and competitiveness, financial analysis gives domain context. Vanessa Galarneau spent ten-plus years applying ML to finance at Norton Rose Fulbright. Sebastian Fallenbuchl built a 30K LinkedIn audience. Andrew Ingram has solid engineering credentials from Ciena and Statistics Canada. What I don't see in any of these biographies is the moment the problem found them — the personal encounter with FP&A brokenness that made this company inevitable rather than opportunistic. The pivot history tells a story too: Rain Technologies built Budget Manager for public-sector budgeting starting in 2019, then reoriented toward Pluvo in 2024. Iteration toward product-market fit is not the same thing as biographical inevitability. These are competent, domain-credentialed operators who identified a gap when LLMs made a new product possible. That's a legitimate founding story, but it's not a forge.

Where I struggle most is the invisible system test. Pluvo's stated problem is that "CFOs, FP&A teams, and finance directors rely on a handful of domain experts to interpret financial data." That's a knowledge bottleneck inside organizations, and solving it is valuable. But compare this to what Affirm did to credit card opacity, or what Carta did to cap table mystery — those companies exposed extractive structures that took advantage of people who lacked information and power. Pluvo's users are CFOs and finance directors at growth-stage companies. These are among the most financially sophisticated, well-compensated people in any organization. Making their workflow faster is efficiency; it's not the same as redistributing power from gatekeepers to the people being gatekept. When I think about Carta — which I consider a strong invisible-system investment — the magic was that employees could finally understand what their equity was worth. Pluvo doesn't have an analogous structural revelation. It makes existing power more efficient rather than challenging who holds it.

I want to be honest about what's working. The $430K ARR in three months with dozens of customers across North America is real signal — not the kind of number you can manufacture. Greg Henry, the CFO of 1Password, and Charly Kevers from Carta invested as angels. When target buyers put their own money in, the product has escaped the building. That's the kind of encounter-based validation I usually trust. And "codifying how your company actually works into a single governed model" — if that sticks, it creates genuine switching costs proportional to the institutional knowledge a customer encodes. The deterministic computation approach versus pure LLM prediction is a real technical choice that could matter. If Pluvo executes perfectly, the bull case is that they become the system of record for how mid-market companies think about their finances — not just where the data lives, but how decisions get made. That would be infrastructure, not a wrapper.

But executing perfectly against Datarails with 400 employees and 70% year-over-year revenue growth, against Abacum with $101M raised and customers in 31 countries, requires something more than good product and early traction. It requires either a structural advantage that compounds faster than their competitors' resources, or a contrarian insight that the well-funded players are wrong about something fundamental. I don't see either in the dossier. Pluvo's "decision layer" positioning is smart, but "LLM-powered FP&A analysis is replicable" — the dossier's own assessment — and agent orchestration quality is a temporary moat at best. The four co-founders with ties to Ottawa and the University of Ottawa suggest a strong working relationship, and I don't see the 50/50 split red flag since Labréche is clearly CEO. But the dual headquarters in Ottawa and San Francisco with 14 people is a coordination tax during the exact phase where speed matters most against larger competitors.

Pluvo is a well-executed company in a consensus market with domain-credentialed founders who lack the biographical crucible that drives my highest-conviction bets. The $430K ARR and CFO angel investors are meaningful — they tell me the product works. But my framework isn't optimized for "the product works in a competitive market." It's optimized for founders who were forged to solve a problem that makes other investors nervous, attacking a hidden system that exploits people who don't understand it. Pluvo doesn't trigger either signal. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are the absence of biographical forge — competent domain expertise without inevitability — and the consensus-attractive competitive landscape that is the opposite of the hard, stigmatized spaces where my returns concentrate.
