## The Idea

**Problem:** Current AI benchmarks become saturated quickly—top models score 88%+ on MMLU, and SWE-bench scores jumped from 4.4% to 71.7% in a single year (DataCamp LLM Benchmarks guide, 2025 via search snippet). These benchmarks measure memorization and pattern matching rather than genuine generalization. AI labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI), researchers, and regulators lack a durable, agreed-upon yardstick for measuring progress toward artificial general intelligence. Existing benchmarks typically test narrow capabilities and do not capture the ability to solve truly novel problems.

**Approach:** ARC Prize Foundation creates the ARC-AGI benchmark series—tasks requiring visual and abstract reasoning on problems the AI has never seen before, measuring "fluid intelligence" (the ability to adapt to novel challenges efficiently). The foundation operates a multi-pronged model: benchmarks (ARC-AGI-1, ARC-AGI-2, ARC-AGI-3), annual global competitions with substantial prize pools hosted on Kaggle, research grants, and community engagement (Discord, newsletter, university tours). ARC-AGI-3, launching March 25, 2026, introduces interactive reasoning across 1,000+ levels in 150+ game-like environments, marking the first major format change since ARC's 2019 introduction (arcprize.org).

**Differentiation:** Unlike MMLU, GPQA, or SWE-bench—which test knowledge recall or domain-specific coding—ARC-AGI tasks are designed to resist brute-force scaling and require genuine abstraction. Compared to Epoch AI's FrontierMath (mathematical reasoning) or LMArena's Chatbot Arena (human preference voting), ARC-AGI focuses specifically on general fluid intelligence and novel problem-solving. The benchmark has been adopted as a reference by OpenAI (used to evaluate o3), Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI (arcprize.org). Stanford HELM evaluates models across multiple dimensions (fairness, robustness, toxicity) but does not specifically target generalization to novel tasks. ARC-AGI-3's interactive agent-based format further differentiates it from static benchmarks.

**Business Model:** ARC Prize Foundation is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Through 2024, the organization was self-funded by its founders (arcprize.org blog, January 2025). It accepts tax-deductible donations and has named individual donors including Tyler Cowen, Andy Fang (DoorDash co-founder), Dharmesh Shah (HubSpot co-founder), and Aaron Levie (Box CEO) (arcprize.org). AI lab partners include Ndea, xAI, Google, Prime Intellect, and Nous (arcprize.org). A fundraising initiative was announced for mid-January 2025 (arcprize.org blog). As a nonprofit, revenue generation is not the primary objective; sustainability comes from donations, grants, and lab partnerships.

**TAM/SAM:** No public TAM/SAM data found for the AI benchmarking nonprofit segment specifically. The broader AI safety and evaluation market is nascent and does not have standardized market-sizing estimates from named research firms.

**GTM / Distribution:** Distribution occurs through Kaggle competition hosting (reaching 1,400+ teams annually), direct partnerships with frontier AI labs who use ARC-AGI as an internal evaluation metric, an active research community (Discord, newsletter, GitHub), academic engagement (university tours at UC Berkeley and others), and high-profile events (ARC-AGI-3 launch party at YC featuring a fireside between François Chollet and Sam Altman, moderated by Deedy Das) (@arcprize on X, March 2026).

## Defensibility

**Benchmark authority and network effects:** ARC-AGI has become a de facto reference benchmark adopted by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and xAI (arcprize.org). This adoption creates a self-reinforcing cycle: as more labs benchmark against ARC-AGI, its results become more comparable and cited, which encourages further adoption. The annual competition has attracted 1,430–1,455 teams across two years (arcprize.org), building a research community around the benchmark. François Chollet's original ARC-AGI paper and dataset have 4,700+ GitHub stars (GitHub, via search snippet), establishing the benchmark's academic pedigree. The 6,700-subscriber newsletter and 4,443-member Discord (as of research date) form a dedicated community.

**Founder credibility as IP:** The benchmark is grounded in Chollet's published theoretical framework on measuring intelligence ("On the Measure of Intelligence," 2019). This intellectual foundation, combined with Chollet's standing (TIME 100 Most Influential in AI, 2024; creator of Keras with 63.9K GitHub stars), creates a reputational moat that is difficult to replicate.

**Market structure:** AI labs face a coordination problem: they need credible third-party benchmarks but cannot create them without perceived bias. A nonprofit with no commercial AI product is structurally positioned as a neutral evaluator. Labs building their own benchmarks face credibility challenges (as illustrated by Epoch AI's controversy over undisclosed OpenAI funding, TechCrunch, January 2025). ARC Prize Foundation's nonprofit status and founder independence from any single lab provide structural neutrality.

**Commoditization risk:** Creating an alternative benchmark is technically feasible—any research group can publish evaluation tasks. However, achieving community adoption and lab buy-in comparable to ARC-AGI's current position requires sustained effort, credibility, and the coordination problem described above. LMArena (formerly LMSYS Chatbot Arena) demonstrated that benchmark organizations can achieve significant scale, raising $100M at a $600M valuation (X/@lmarena_ai, May 2025), suggesting the space supports multiple players. Epoch AI, funded by Open Philanthropy with $11M+ in grants (Open Philanthropy grants page), operates in the adjacent space of AI capability tracking and benchmarks.

**Competitive landscape:**

1. **LMArena (formerly LMSYS Chatbot Arena)**: $100M seed raised at $600M valuation, led by a16z and UC Investments (X/@lmarena_ai, May 2025). Revenue unknown. Originated at UC Berkeley. Differentiator vs. ARC Prize: measures human preference via blind pairwise comparison voting (crowdsourced), whereas ARC-AGI measures generalization on novel reasoning tasks. LMArena evaluates conversation quality; ARC-AGI evaluates fluid intelligence.

2. **Epoch AI**: Nonprofit funded by Open Philanthropy with $11M+ in total grants ($6.9M in 2022, $4.1M in 2025) (Open Philanthropy grants page). Revenue: nonprofit/grant-funded. Creates FrontierMath and tracks AI capability trends. Differentiator vs. ARC Prize: broader scope covering compute trends, training data, and AI forecasting; FrontierMath focuses on mathematical reasoning rather than general fluid intelligence.

3. **Stanford CRFM (HELM)**: Academic project at Stanford's Center for Research on Foundation Models. Funding through Stanford university resources. Revenue: not applicable (academic). Differentiator vs. ARC Prize: evaluates models across 42 scenarios on 7 dimensions (accuracy, fairness, toxicity, etc.); focuses on holistic evaluation including safety and bias rather than generalization to novel tasks.

4. **Hugging Face Open LLM Leaderboard**: Part of Hugging Face ($235M Series D at $4.5B valuation, August 2023, via search snippet). Revenue: Hugging Face reported $70M ARR (2023, via search snippet). Differentiator vs. ARC Prize: focuses on open-source model evaluation using the EleutherAI Evaluation Harness across standard benchmarks; does not create novel benchmarks targeting general intelligence.

**Why now:** [Inferred]: Several converging factors opened this opportunity: (1) Frontier AI models began saturating existing benchmarks (MMLU 88%+, SWE-bench jumping from 4.4% to 71.7% in one year), creating urgent demand for harder, more meaningful evaluations; (2) The emergence of "reasoning" models (OpenAI o1/o3, late 2024) prompted new questions about whether scaling alone leads to generalization, directly validating ARC-AGI's thesis; (3) The shift toward AI agents (2025–2026) requires interactive, multi-turn benchmarks like ARC-AGI-3, which existing static benchmarks cannot address; (4) Regulatory attention on AI capabilities (EU AI Act, US executive orders) increases demand for credible, independent measurement of AI progress.

## Key Risks

**Benchmark saturation risk:** ARC-AGI-1 SOTA jumped from 33% to 55.5% in a single competition cycle (arcprize.org, 2024), and OpenAI's o3 scored significantly higher in testing. If ARC-AGI-2 and ARC-AGI-3 are solved faster than anticipated, the foundation must continuously create harder versions, which is resource-intensive for a 4-person nonprofit team. Mitigation: ARC-AGI-2 was designed to resist brute-force approaches, and ARC-AGI-3 introduces interactive environments that are structurally harder to game.

**Funding sustainability as a nonprofit:** The organization was self-funded by its founders through 2024 and announced a fundraising initiative in January 2025 (arcprize.org blog). Prize pools of $1M+ per competition and ongoing operations require sustained donations and lab partnerships. Unlike LMArena, which raised $100M as a commercial entity, ARC Prize Foundation's nonprofit structure limits its financing options to donations and grants. Named individual donors (Andy Fang, Dharmesh Shah, Aaron Levie) and lab partnerships provide partial mitigation.

**Founder attention split:** Both Chollet and Knoop are simultaneously co-founding Ndea, a separate for-profit AI lab (TechCrunch, January 2025). Knoop also remains on Zapier's board. The ARC Prize Foundation has a 4-person team led operationally by Greg Kamradt as President, but the intellectual direction depends heavily on Chollet's continued involvement, which must be balanced against Ndea commitments.

**Competitive displacement from well-funded entrants:** LMArena raised $100M in seed funding (May 2025) and Epoch AI receives multi-million-dollar grants from Open Philanthropy. These organizations could expand into ARC-AGI's domain of generalization benchmarking with greater resources. Hugging Face, with its $4.5B valuation, could also invest in novel benchmarking formats. Mitigation: ARC Prize's specific focus on fluid intelligence and Chollet's theoretical framework are difficult to replicate, and the nonprofit positioning provides neutrality advantages.

**Potential conflict of interest between ARC Prize and Ndea:** ARC Prize benchmarks are designed to measure AGI progress; Ndea is a for-profit lab attempting to build AGI. Both share the same two co-founders (Chollet and Knoop). If Ndea's systems are evaluated on ARC-AGI benchmarks, questions of independence and impartiality could arise. No public governance policy addressing this separation was found.
