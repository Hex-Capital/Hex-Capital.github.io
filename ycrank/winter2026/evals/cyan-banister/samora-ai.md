# Samora AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Kartik Sawhney's story stopped me cold. Born blind in India, told he couldn't study science because no blind student ever had, he fought the system until national policy changed. Then Stanford CS. Then Microsoft's AI research division. Then he built I-Stem, reaching half a million people with disabilities across 80+ countries. When I hear a story like that, my first instinct is to lean all the way in — this is exactly the kind of person I back. Someone who had to rebuild the rules of their own world before they could build anything for anyone else. The thread from his lived experience with accessibility and language barriers, through I-Stem's multilingual work with underserved populations, directly into Samora's voice agents handling dialects and code-switching for UNICEF and the Government of India — that's not a manufactured narrative. That's a life that produced a company. I don't need to ask Kartik "tell me your story, not the business" because his story IS the business.

The economic access dimension here is where I get genuinely excited. When UNICEF uses Samora to voice-enable career discovery for young people with limited connectivity in their own languages, or the Government of India reaches citizens across dozens of dialects through voice agents instead of call centers that only speak Hindi and English — those are invisible systems becoming visible. That's the same pattern I've seen in Affirm making credit transparent, in Flock Safety making public safety data accessible, in Carta pulling cap tables out of opaque spreadsheets. Samora is making institutional services reachable for populations that were locked out by language barriers. That matters to me in a way that "we automate your call center 40% cheaper" never will.

But here's where my conviction stalls, and I have to be honest about it: voice AI agents is one of the most crowded, consensus categories in tech right now. Bland AI has raised $65 million. Synthflow has $30 million. Vapi has north of $20 million and a YC pedigree of its own. Retell AI is already processing over 10 million minutes per month. Everyone from enterprise sales teams to dental offices is deploying voice agents. This is the opposite of unpopular and difficult to assess — it's one of the easiest pitches to understand in the current market. My entire thesis is built on the premise that the most promising companies look bad to most investors, and Samora's category looks great to everyone. The specific multilingual, managed-service, institutional-buyer angle is genuinely differentiated today, but I've learned that differentiation built on top of someone else's foundation models has a half-life. When OpenAI ships better multilingual capabilities — and they will — the moat narrows. The entire stack is OpenAI: Whisper for transcription, GPT for reasoning, their TTS for voice output. That's a dependency, not a defensible position.

The strongest bull case would go like this: the institutional relationships and domain expertise in deploying voice technology across developing-world populations are the actual moat, not the technology. Bland AI's enterprise sales team isn't calling UNICEF. Vapi's developer community isn't building for Government of India use cases. The I-Stem network — 80+ countries, 500K+ people served — is a distribution channel into exactly the institutional buyers who need this most and who other voice AI startups can't reach. If the $1.1M revenue figure is real (and I note it's from Getlatka, which is self-reported), that's meaningful traction for a pre-seed company with institutional customers. The managed-service model is structurally misaligned for developer-first competitors to replicate without cannibalizing their core positioning. And the co-founder team has years of working together at I-Stem with clear role differentiation — Kartik on vision and institutional relationships, Vineeth on engineering, Shakul on AI — which is exactly the kind of tested dynamic I look for after the HQ Trivia lesson.

I keep coming back to the tension between an extraordinary founder in a consensus market. If Kartik were building something that made other investors uncomfortable — if this were in a stigmatized category, or if the idea sounded ridiculous on first hearing — I'd be writing a check tonight. Instead, he's building in a space where dozens of well-capitalized competitors are all racing toward similar capabilities. The multilingual institutional angle is the wedge, and it's a real one, but I'm not sure it's weird enough or defensible enough to overcome the capital asymmetry. I'd want to meet the team, feel their energy, and understand whether the institutional pipeline is as deep as the customer logos suggest before committing my own money. This is a company I want to root for, and a founder whose grit is beyond question — but my framework demands that the opportunity itself be non-obvious, and voice AI in 2026 is about as obvious as it gets.
