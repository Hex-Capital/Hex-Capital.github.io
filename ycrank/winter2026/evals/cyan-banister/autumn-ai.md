# Autumn AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that hits me about Autumn AI is the tagline: "Stalk your prospects at scale." That tells me everything about where this company lives. This is a sales intelligence tool in a market where ZoomInfo does $1.25 billion in revenue, 6sense has raised $426 million, Apollo.io has $150 million in revenue, and Demandbase is north of $200 million. Every venture capitalist in San Francisco understands this category. Every sales team already has a stack of tools doing some version of this. When I look for companies that are unpopular, difficult to assess, and have real technology risk -- the three characteristics I believe the most promising companies share -- Autumn AI checks none of those boxes. This is consensus investing territory, and that's just not where I operate.

I wanted to find the founder story here, because that's always where I start. Vishnu built sourcing technology at Sierra Ventures, which gives him real professional exposure to the problem of tracking prospects through scattered signals. Shiv presented at NeurIPS and did an AI residency at SandboxAQ. Both graduated from Columbia CS. These are clearly brilliant, technically capable people -- and I don't question their intelligence for a second. But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- "how did you get here, and why does this problem belong to you?" -- what I hear is smart engineers who identified a market gap, not founders whose life experience forged an unbreakable commitment to this specific problem. Vishnu saw the pain of manual prospect research while working at a VC firm. That's valid. But it's professional observation, not the kind of biographical thread that kept Travis Kalanick fighting through years of regulatory battles or made Contraline's founder dedicate a decade to male contraception. At the earliest stages, I need to believe the founder would keep going when everything falls apart, and that conviction usually comes from somewhere deeper than a prior job.

The defensibility question genuinely concerns me. The dossier is honest about this: no proprietary datasets, no patents, no network effects. The core technology -- monitoring public APIs, applying NLP to extract intent signals -- is technically reproducible. And the data sources themselves are fragile: LinkedIn, X, and GitHub have all been progressively restricting API access and scraping. Your product's signal coverage could degrade overnight because of a platform policy change you have no control over. When I think about the companies in my portfolio that created real moats -- Carta made cap table data sticky because once you're managing equity on the platform, switching costs are enormous; Flock Safety accumulates proprietary camera data that gets more valuable over time -- I don't see the equivalent mechanism here. The inferred defensibility pathways (signal-processing logic, user-defined ICP configurations, data flywheel) are hypothetical, not demonstrated.

To be fair to Vishnu and Shiv, here's what would make me wrong: if the LLM-driven approach to real-time signal aggregation turns out to be genuinely 10x better than what incumbents can bolt on -- if the product becomes so embedded in sales workflows that teams can't go back to cycling through tabs -- and if the founders' four years together at Columbia has built the kind of resilient partnership that can survive the grind of competing against billion-dollar incumbents. The "why now" argument has merit: LLMs have crossed a threshold that makes unstructured signal extraction cheap and accurate for the first time, and the incumbents' data-licensing business models create a real structural conflict with building around freely available public signals. If Autumn can move fast enough to own the workflow before ZoomInfo ships a feature, there's a world where this works. But that's an execution race against well-capitalized competitors in a space with no regulatory moat, no stigma barrier, and no technological risk that would slow the giants down. That's not the kind of bet that gives me the jolt.

What I look for -- and what I don't find here -- is a company that makes an invisible system visible for people who need that transparency, or that creates economic access where none existed. Uber created income for drivers. Affirm replaced hidden credit card fees with transparent interest rates. Flock Safety brought public safety data to neighborhoods that couldn't afford it. Autumn AI helps sales teams at B2B companies prospect more efficiently. That's useful software, and I don't begrudge anyone building it. But it optimizes an existing workflow for people who already have resources and good jobs. It doesn't expand access or shift power. The co-founder relationship is the strongest signal I see -- four years together at Columbia before founding, which is the kind of tested partnership I look for after the HQ Trivia lesson taught me that even perfect timing and product can't survive founder conflict. But one strong signal isn't enough when the rest of the picture points away from my thesis.
