# Care GP -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Melvin Chen is selling AI agents named Samantha, Veronica, and Bill into Australian GP back offices through a Best Practice Software partner listing -- which means Care GP's leverage architecture is the exact inverse of what I fund, and the dimensional signature is a clean-credentials solo founder building a permissioned-distribution vertical SaaS in someone else's channel. The 150-clinic traction and the "profitable" line are real, and they would land harder with Elad than with me; I read architecture, not velocity, and Care GP's architecture is labor-and-capital leverage routed through a gatekeeper who is also a competitor.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung on permissionless leverage -- media as protocol, zero marginal cost, no gatekeeper between Dorsey's product and the next user. Care GP's distribution is the structural opposite: the Best Practice Software partner page is the channel, and Medical Republic (2026) reports Best Practice has shipped its own scribe and AI admin agent and natively bundles Lyrebird. That is not permissionless leverage; that is rent paid to a platform that has decided to compete with its own tenants. Care GP's ISO27001 certification and the onshore-encryption story are exactly the credentials that matter when you're selling through a permissioned channel into a regulated buyer -- which is to say, Care GP is optimizing for the wrong leverage stack relative to my entry point, and Melvin Chen's 7,000-clinic national plan runs through the same gate Best Practice can close.

The "ex-PE identified market opportunity" pattern is the LOW rung on specific knowledge -- founder pedigree as thesis, no shipped artifact in the domain, the anti-pattern of Atwood and Spolsky building Stack Overflow as practicing developers. Care GP's dossier traces Melvin Chen through Oxford Engineering, Terra Firma, Samena Capital, Goldman Sachs, and Forbes 30 Under 30 Finance & VC -- credentials, not domain immersion. No prior healthcare operator role is disclosed, no GitHub, no public technical writing about clinical workflow, no lived-problem ownership. Specific knowledge in primary care admin is non-trainable in the way Ivan Zhao's productivity-tool obsession at Notion (2013) was non-trainable; Care GP reads as the inverse case where finance fluency identified a market opportunity and the team is now learning the domain in production.

Mechanism analysis on network: Care GP is single-sided SaaS where Sonya at Yarrawonga Medical Clinic getting six hours back does nothing to make the next clinic's Samantha deployment more valuable. Over 18 months, that means the 140% MoM usage growth compounds linearly per logo, not super-linearly across logos, which is the Uber (2010) math I underwrite for and the math Care GP cannot produce. The 175% MoM on Samantha specifically is intra-account expansion, not cross-account network value -- a healthy SaaS curve, not a marketplace curve.

Counterfactual on market: Care GP's 7,800-practice Australian TAM flips a tier only if Melvin Chen credibly articulates expansion into UK NHS practices or US independent primary care -- both regulatory regimes where the PMS integration work, the ISO certification analog, and the channel relationships start from zero. The dossier names neither; the 7,000-clinic plan is Australia-only. That single-vertical, single-geography posture is exactly the "first idea never works -- you always have to change the idea" failure mode Venture Hacks (2007-10) warned about. Pivot room inside primary-care admin is narrow because the workflow taxonomy is the product.

Single-anchor parallel on the founder trio: Care GP reads like a cleaner version of the credentials-stack I usually pass on -- Forbes 30 Under 30, Oxford Valedictorian, Goldman, no contested exits, no integrity flags I can find. Set against Notion (2013), where Ivan Zhao came with a decade of personal-productivity obsession but no Goldman line, Care GP inverts the trade: legible credentials, illegible specific knowledge. Solo-founder status on a 10-person team scaling toward 7,000 clinics is the other note -- "picking a co-founder is your most important decision" (Venture Hacks ~2007-10), and Care GP has deferred that decision permanently. Intelligence and energy read fine; the 10-year-partner test is neutral, not affirmative.

36-month forecast: Care GP gets acquired by Best Practice Software or by Heidi Health for sub-AUD $80M before reaching a Series B, because Heidi's ~$138M war chest and Best Practice's native agent collapse Care GP's channel into a build-vs-buy decision and the cheapest exit clears. Not a zero, not a unicorn -- a tidy strategic acquisition that returns capital to Startmate and YC and validates Melvin Chen's PE instinct for finding clean exits, which is consistent with the signature: clean operator, wrong leverage stack, capped upside.

The bull case stings because Care GP is actually profitable at the pre-seed stage, which almost nothing in YC S26 will be, and Sonya's "six hours into thirty minutes" plus Josh Casey's "cycle time cut by half" at Green Square Health are the kind of unprompted customer love that historically drags reluctant angels into checks they later thank themselves for. Anchorage Digital is the Known Exception precedent -- I funded a regulated, permissioned-distribution business because the category-timing call overrode the leverage rule. Care GP would need the same override: agentic primary-care admin as a category-timing call strong enough to flip the channel-conflict risk into a moat. The dossier does not support that override because Heidi at ~$700M valuation and Best Practice's native agent mean Care GP is not pre-consensus on category -- it is mid-consensus and out-capitalized. I'd flip the verdict if Care GP shipped a documented developer-API or open integration layer that other PMS vendors adopted independent of Best Practice, because that would convert the architecture from permissioned-channel-SaaS to code-leverage primitive. Until then, this is a pass.
