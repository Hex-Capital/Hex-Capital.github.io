# Keyframe Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me first: Parth Radia spent five years inside Ericsson building photorealistic digital humans on a petascale dataset, leading a team of thirty, and before that he built what's described as the world's first 3D call over production networks at AT&T Labs. That's nearly a decade of institutional R&D pointed at exactly this problem. And then he left to build a two-person startup doing the same thing at one-tenth the price. That's a specific kind of founder -- someone who watched a giant corporation sit on transformative technology and couldn't stand it anymore. When I see a $0.06/min price point against competitors charging $0.20 to $0.56, I don't see a pricing gimmick. I see someone who spent years understanding the architectural waste in how this technology gets deployed and built a fundamentally different inference stack. The Persona-1 architecture -- two-stage flow-based image transformer running at 2x realtime on consumer GPUs -- is a specific, falsifiable technical claim. If it's real, it's the kind of infrastructure advantage I've seen compound in companies like Crusoe, where cost structure *is* the moat.

But when I ask my first question -- tell me their story, not the business -- what I get back is a career trajectory, not a biographical crucible. Parth went from UCLA to AT&T Labs to Ericsson to founding Keyframe. That's a perfectly logical path for a talented engineer who saw an opportunity. Kaahan brings complementary depth from Zipline's real-time perception systems and published ML research. These are serious technologists. But I don't hear the story of someone who was *broken open* by this problem. I don't hear why photorealistic video avatars keep this person awake at 3am beyond "I know how to build them better than anyone." Technical mastery is necessary but it's not the forge I look for. The founders who survive the inevitable valley of death in a startup are usually the ones who can't walk away because the problem is personal, not professional. I'd want to sit across from Parth and hear what happened at Ericsson that made leaving inevitable -- was it just corporate frustration, or was there something deeper about who gets access to this technology and who doesn't?

The thing that gives me real pause is the competitive landscape. This is not a space that makes people uncomfortable. Tavus just raised $40M from CRV and Sequoia. HeyGen is doing $95M ARR. Synthesia is valued at $4B. Lemon Slice raised a $10.5M seed. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley -- VCs literally wouldn't take the meeting. When I wrote my Uber check, 300 investors on AngelList had already passed. Those were signals that the consensus was wrong, which is where I find asymmetric returns. Real-time video avatars are the opposite -- this is consensus AI, well-funded and actively competed for by top-tier firms. That doesn't make it a bad business. It makes it a business where the first mover doesn't win structurally, where capital matters enormously, and where Keyframe's $500K in YC funding is going against $64M+ at Tavus alone. The pricing advantage is real, but cost leadership in a commodity market is a treadmill, not a fortress.

The strongest bull case for Keyframe is the infrastructure-at-inflection argument. Voice AI agents are proliferating through platforms like LiveKit and Vapi, and every one of them is a potential Keyframe customer who just needs to call `await persona.connect()`. If you squint, this is picks-and-shovels for the agent economy -- the rendering layer that sits beneath every conversational AI that wants a face. And if Keyframe's architectural efficiency is genuinely 5-9x better than competitors, that's not an API wrapper; that's proprietary infrastructure. The ten-year question gives me something interesting: if the agent economy grows as fast as voice AI suggests, the company that owns the cheapest, fastest face-rendering pipeline becomes essential plumbing. Synthesia's channel conflict -- cannibalizing high-margin per-seat licenses for commodity minutes -- could genuinely keep the largest incumbent out of the real-time segment for years. That timing window is real. But I've been burned by this pattern before: impressive technology in a crowded market where capital concentration matters more than architectural elegance. The invisible system I look for -- the opaque structure extracting value from people who don't understand it -- just isn't here. This is B2B infrastructure selling to sophisticated buyers who understand pricing perfectly well. Nobody is being exploited by avatar API costs; companies are simply comparison shopping.

The sibling co-founder dynamic (both Radias, likely siblings) is actually something I watch carefully. It's different from two strangers splitting 50/50 -- siblings usually have organic hierarchy that doesn't need to be negotiated. But I'd still want to know who's the clear CEO and whether the equity split reflects that. The anonymous testimonials are concerning at this stage -- eight quotes from unnamed companies could mean early design partners under NDA, or it could mean the product hasn't found real champions willing to put their name behind it. At pre-seed I don't penalize absence of traction, but I do notice when a company has testimonials without attribution while simultaneously showing zero GitHub stars on their public repos and minimal LinkedIn engagement. These are small signals, but they add up to a picture of a product that hasn't yet escaped the building.

I respect what these founders have built technically. The cost advantage is real engineering, not marketing spin. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. My highest-conviction investments are in founders whose lives left them no choice but to build, operating in spaces that make other investors flinch, attacking hidden structures that exploit ordinary people. Keyframe is talented engineers building competitive infrastructure in a well-funded consensus market. I wish them well -- the technical foundation is there -- but I need to feel that pull in my gut that says this team will survive the inevitable near-death moment because they simply cannot stop, and I need to see a world being remade beneath the surface, not just a price point being undercut.
