# Menza -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Menza is that it's building in one of the most crowded, most consensus categories in tech right now. AI-powered data analytics. Every fund deck I see has some version of "AI replaces your data team" in it. ThoughtSpot has over $300 million in revenue. Julius AI just raised $11 million from Bessemer. Shopify itself is building an AI assistant called Sidekick that does a version of this inside its own platform. When I look at a deal and every sophisticated investor in the room is nodding along -- that's actually my cue to pay closer attention to why I shouldn't be there. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Menza is neither of those things. It's legible, it's trendy, and it sits in a space where the underlying technology -- LLMs querying structured data -- is commoditizing in real time. That doesn't mean it can't be a good business, but it means the weirdness signal I rely on to identify asymmetric returns is completely absent.

Now let me talk about the founders, because that's always where I start. Qasim Munye has a genuinely interesting trajectory -- a qualified medical doctor from King's College London who taught himself machine learning, built ShortlyAI as a side project while in medical school, and got it acquired by Jasper in 2021. That's a real exit. The self-taught angle resonates with me deeply -- I taught myself to code at public libraries, so I recognize the kind of person who just figures things out. Starting businesses at 14, selling discount codes on eBay -- that's entrepreneurial wiring, not opportunism. Mariam Ahmed brings Goldman Sachs experience in financing structuring and portfolio optimization, which maps directly to the reporting and analytics domain. These are competent, resourceful founders. But here's what I'm not hearing: the story that makes this problem theirs. Why DTC analytics? Qasim built an AI writing tool before -- a very different problem. Mariam worked in investment banking. The path from those experiences to "we need to replace the data team for small e-commerce brands" feels like market identification, not lived compulsion. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception -- the problem was biographical, not strategic. I'm not sensing that same thread here.

The economic access angle is the strongest part of the thesis for me. Replacing a £300K data team for small brands that can't afford one -- that does something I care about. The Keto Shop discovering a 10% revenue leak from stockouts, Evolve Beauty saving 60 hours monthly on reports -- those are real outcomes for real small business operators who otherwise would be flying blind. I've always believed that the best businesses create access where gatekeeping existed before, and there is a version of Menza that democratizes sophisticated analytics the way Affirm democratized transparent credit. But I'd put this in the "nice efficiency gain" category rather than the "fundamental shift in economic access" category. These DTC brands aren't underserved in the way that Uber drivers lacked income or Thumbtack tradespeople lacked customers -- they're mostly well-resourced enough to run ads on Meta and sell on Shopify, they just don't have perfect visibility into their data. That's a pain point, not an injustice.

What would have to be true for this to be a great investment? Menza would need to become the default data layer for every sub-$50M DTC brand, creating a network effect where their models get smarter across the vertical -- understanding seasonality patterns, ad-spend benchmarks, and stockout risks better than any horizontal tool could. The 650+ integrations represent real engineering work and real switching costs once a brand is connected. If Menza can accumulate enough vertical-specific intelligence before ThoughtSpot or Shopify's native tools catch up, there's a narrow window to own this category. The repeat founder with an exit is a real asset -- Qasim has been through the cycle before. And the fact that they have 12 named customers with specific testimonials at pre-seed is solid execution signal. If I were a consensus-style investor optimizing for "good team, good market, good timing," I'd probably write this check. But that's exactly the kind of investing I've spent my career moving away from.

The space requires no regulatory navigation, there's no stigma to overcome, and the technology risk is low -- which in my framework translates to low barriers for competitors, not safety. The defensibility question is acute. When Shopify can add an AI analytics tab to every merchant dashboard overnight, when Meta can surface the same insights inside Ads Manager, when any well-funded team can wire up the same LLM-to-database pipeline -- the moat is thin. I learned at IronPort that the best positions in technology are the ones where complexity itself is the barrier. I don't see that structural complexity here. The co-founder relationship appears complementary -- medicine/ML plus CS/finance from the same university ecosystem -- but I have no evidence of how they handle crisis or disagreement, which after HQ Trivia is something I always want to understand before writing a check.
