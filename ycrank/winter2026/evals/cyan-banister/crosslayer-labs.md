# Crosslayer Labs -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what immediately grabs me about Crosslayer Labs: these three people didn't just study Internet security -- they wrote the rules. Literally. The MPIC standard they invented now governs how every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate gets issued on the planet. Google, Apple, Amazon, Let's Encrypt, Cloudflare -- all of them implemented what this team built. That's an extraordinary credential. My years at IronPort taught me what real security infrastructure looks like, and I can tell you that getting a global mandate adopted by the CA/Browser Forum isn't something you stumble into. It requires years of grinding through institutional politics, technical validation, and bureaucratic navigation that makes the FDA look fast. The moat here is genuine and deep -- you can't fake fifteen peer-reviewed papers and a standard that secures 700 million websites.

But when I sit across from founders, the first thing I ask is "tell me your story -- not the business." And what I'm listening for is the hardship, the personal stakes, the reason this particular human being can't NOT solve this problem. With Crosslayer Labs, I hear a different kind of story. These are Princeton academics -- a tenured professor with an ACM award, a research engineer who stayed at his alma mater, a PhD student working under her advisor. They're insiders at one of the most elite institutions in the world. That doesn't make them less capable -- clearly they're brilliant -- but it doesn't give me that biographical signal I rely on. The founders who keep me up at night with excitement are the ones who got told no fifty times, who came from the outside, who are solving something because life left them no choice. Academic motivation, even deeply committed academic motivation, is a different fuel than existential necessity.

The piece that does connect for me is the "making invisible systems visible" pattern. This company is doing for cross-layer Internet attacks what Flock Safety did for neighborhood crime data and what Carta did for cap table opacity. Right now, if someone hijacks your BGP routing to intercept credentials while simultaneously compromising your JavaScript supply chain, most security tools are watching individual layers and missing the coordinated assault. Crosslayer Labs correlates signals across DNS, BGP, TLS, and JavaScript to catch attack patterns that no single-layer tool detects. That's a real insight, and my IronPort instincts tell me it's technically sound. But here's my hesitation -- cybersecurity is the opposite of a contrarian bet right now. EASM is a well-funded category with Censys at $75 million raised, IONIX at $50 million, SecurityScorecard approaching a billion in valuation. I back companies that other investors think are crazy. Nobody thinks cybersecurity is crazy.

I should note that Long Journey Ventures is listed as an existing backer, which means my fund has already made a bet here. Setting that aside and evaluating fresh: the bull case is real. If cross-layer attacks become the dominant attack vector in healthcare and fintech -- which the rising sophistication of state-sponsored attackers suggests they might -- then the team that literally wrote the standard for defending against them is the team you want. The MPIC mandate they helped create is doing the market education for free, raising awareness of exactly the threat vector their product detects. That's a beautiful feedback loop. And the navigating-complexity dimension is strong -- these founders have operated in the most institutionally dense corner of Internet governance and won. If they can translate that institutional credibility into enterprise sales relationships, they have a genuine path. The question I can't answer from a dossier is whether Henry Birge-Lee can sell. Can he walk into a CISO's office and create urgency? Can Grace build a production platform that meets enterprise SLA requirements? The academic-to-commercial transition is where brilliant technical founders either transform or stall, and I don't have enough signal here to judge.

The co-founder dynamic gives me pause too -- not a red flag, but a question mark. A professor and his two graduate students starting a company together carries a built-in power asymmetry that's different from co-founders who chose each other as equals. How do Henry and Grace push back when Prateek disagrees? And is Prateek fully committed, or is he keeping his Princeton professorship as a safety net? "Chief Scientist" titles on a three-person team sometimes signal part-time commitment. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I always want to understand how the hard conversations happen. Multi-year research collaboration is a foundation, but academic collaboration and startup co-founding test relationships in fundamentally different ways.

This is a competent team in a real market with genuine technical differentiation, and I respect the depth of expertise enormously. But it doesn't activate my primary investing instincts. It's not weird enough, the founders aren't outsiders enough, and cybersecurity SaaS for enterprise security teams doesn't create the kind of economic access that draws me to a company. If I didn't have a framework, I might call this an obvious yes based on credentials alone. But credentials are what consensus investors use to evaluate, and I've learned that my best investments came from a completely different signal.
