# Sequence Markets -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know before I examine anything else. Talos has raised $196M and achieved a $1.5B valuation. FalconX raised $430M at an $8B valuation. CoinRoutes has been operational since 2017. 1inch raised $190M for decentralized aggregation. And now Sequence Markets, a five-person pre-seed team, proposes to build smart order routing for digital assets -- the exact category these companies already occupy, using a concept (SOR) that traditional equities markets solved decades ago. When incumbents validate the approach rather than dismiss it, I get nervous. Nobody at Talos or FalconX is laughing at the idea of multi-venue order routing. They built it years ago. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing in telecom would never happen, that was my buy signal for Juniper. Here, the incumbents would say "yes, that's exactly what we do, and we've been doing it with hundreds of millions of dollars and institutional client relationships." That's the opposite signal.

Let me apply my first filter: if Sequence Markets succeeds completely -- captures significant market share, builds the best execution layer in crypto -- what changes? Trading becomes somewhat more efficient. Price slippage decreases. The infrastructure layer works better. That's a real business outcome, but it's not a civilization-scale consequence. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. Compare this to what Square did -- it didn't route existing payments better, it created an entirely new category of merchant that could accept cards. It restructured who participates in commerce. Sequence is optimizing the plumbing within an existing system. The consequence of total success is a useful middleware company, not an industry transformation. They've narrowed the scope to ensure viability, and in doing so, they've made success inconsequential by my standards.

Peter Bai's background concerns me further. TMX Group, University Pension Plan Ontario managing $12.8B, focus on "quantitative finance, market microstructure, and execution quality." This is exactly the profile I described when I warned about founders who have industry experience that constrains their thinking -- he knows what market microstructure looks like, which means he's building the version of it that his experience tells him the market needs. He's not approaching crypto execution from some wildly orthogonal angle. He's not a biochemist wondering why trading infrastructure can't work differently from first principles. He's a market structure professional building market structure technology. The AI angle from his Western AI leadership adds some texture, but "AI-powered routing" in 2026 is table stakes, not contrarian. Every fintech pitch deck has AI in it now. There's nothing in this dossier that suggests either founder is attempting something the consensus considers impossible.

The strongest bull case would require believing that the intersection of centralized and decentralized venue routing creates a genuinely new category -- not just better SOR, but a unified execution layer that makes the fragmented crypto market function like a single liquidity pool. If tokenized real-world assets explode in the next three years and every asset class -- equities, bonds, real estate, commodities -- starts trading across both traditional and decentralized venues, then the company that owns cross-venue execution infrastructure could sit at the center of a multi-trillion-dollar transition. In that scenario, Sequence's early positioning across both CEX and DEX venues could be genuinely prescient, and Peter Bai's traditional finance background becomes an asset for bridging the two worlds. The 22.6% CAGR in the broader crypto exchange market supports the directionality. But even granting all of this, I'd need to see evidence that Sequence has a technology trajectory that bends differently from incumbents -- a rate of change in routing quality or venue integration speed that the funded competitors can't match. The dossier offers no such evidence. Sub-millisecond latency and AI routing are claims, not demonstrated advantages over Talos or CoinRoutes. And nothing prevents those incumbents from expanding downmarket to serve the "retail and institutional" audience Sequence is targeting.

One co-founder's public tagline is "I like building cool projects." I have no visibility into Muhammad Awan's technical background, education, or prior work. For a company whose entire value proposition rests on engineering excellence -- sub-millisecond latency, AI-powered routing across dozens of venues -- the absence of any visible technical depth in the founding team's public profile is a gap I can't ignore. Gene pool engineering requires knowing what risks you're building around. The dossier shows no advisors, no notable investors beyond YC, and no evidence of deliberate team assembly to address the venture's critical risks: regulatory complexity, exchange integration maintenance, and algorithmic routing superiority against well-funded competitors.

This is a competent team building a known product category in a competitive market. They've identified a real inefficiency -- crypto liquidity fragmentation -- but the solution architecture is well-understood and already deployed at scale by multiple well-capitalized companies. I pass on companies where the consequence of success is a functional business rather than a transformed industry. Sequence Markets is exactly that.
