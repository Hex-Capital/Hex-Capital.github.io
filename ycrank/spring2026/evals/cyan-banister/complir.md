# Complir -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

107,489 products monitored across 40+ markets, seven named Scandinavian retailers onboarded, and ~45% month-over-month growth — all from a company founded in May 2024 with €1.7M raised. Complir's execution velocity is real, and the system it's attacking is genuinely opaque. Retailers trying to sell a toy or a cosmetic across borders today face a labyrinth of market-specific labeling rules, testing requirements, risk assessments, and documentation obligations — and they navigate it with spreadsheets, consultancies billing by the hour, and manual regulatory research that takes months per product launch. The EU Commission calls compliance "one of the top 3 barriers preventing European companies from exporting to new markets." That's not a market gap someone spotted in a slide deck. That's a hidden tax being levied on every company that tries to move a physical product across a border. When I look at Complir through the lens I used for Affirm — where Max Levchin made credit card opacity visible — or Ownwell — where property tax extraction became transparent — I see the same structural pattern: an extractive, opaque system that profits from information asymmetry, now being automated and exposed.

Gustav Bang, Tine Kühnel, and Marc Kaa Brejner are the founding team, and here's where I pause. Gustav has 10 years of startup experience and led Legora's Denmark operations. Tine spent a decade designing data-driven B2B platforms with a DTU master's in Design & Innovation. Marc built large-scale systems for Danish government authorities. These are competent, experienced operators — the fact that Complir was forward-deployed with Flying Tiger Copenhagen one month after founding tells me they had deep pre-existing relationships in the Nordic retail ecosystem. But when I ask my opening question — "tell me your story, not the business" — I get professional résumés, not a biographical crucible. I don't see evidence that regulatory compliance pain found these founders the way flare gas found the Crusoe team in North Dakota oil fields. Gustav's public quote — "We aim to become the default compliance infrastructure for global retail" — is a positioning statement, not a mission that someone's life story made inevitable. The absence of that biographical narrative doesn't mean it doesn't exist, but it is, for me, a significant gap. I default to 12 on founder forge when the story isn't there, because the story IS the data.

Complir operates in regulatory compliance for physical products — a space that is boring, complex, and unglamorous. That's worth distinguishing from the kind of contrarianism I look for. Boring is not the same as stigmatized. Nobody shifts in their chair when you say "product compliance automation" the way they do when you say "defense AI" or "male birth control" or "Bitcoin mining with flare gas." Assent has raised $350M+ and reached $100M+ ARR in adjacent compliance territory. Compliance & Risks has 300+ enterprise clients. Reglyr, backed by F4 Fund, launched a directly competing AI-powered platform in April 2026. This is a recognized, investable category with established players — the opposite of the 300-investors-said-no signal I got from Uber. Complir's differentiation — ERP/PIM/PLM integrations, Adobe plugins, Scandinavian retailer relationships — is real but operational, not contrarian. No one is calling this idea crazy. No one is uncomfortable funding it.

The Carta parallel is where Complir earns its strongest marks from me. Carta made cap table management — an opaque, spreadsheet-driven administrative burden — transparent through software that integrated into startup workflows and created switching costs with every new round of financing. Complir is building the same structural moat in product compliance: once a retailer's ERP, PIM, and PLM systems are connected, once the supplier workspace is populated, once 107,000+ SKUs are mapped to regulatory obligations across 40 markets, ripping Complir out becomes genuinely painful. The EU's General Product Safety Regulation, enforced from December 13, 2024, is the inflection point — the CHIPS Act equivalent for this category, creating acute time-bound pressure that pushes retailers from manual processes toward automated solutions. Complir's structured regulatory knowledge engine across 40+ markets and 20+ product categories represents domain-specific IP that requires years to replicate. But Reglyr's April 2026 launch as a direct competitor, plus the ever-present risk that SAP or Oracle adds a compliance module, puts a ceiling on how much moat credit I can give at this stage.

The bull case an advocate would make is compelling: Complir has more real traction than 95% of pre-seed companies I see — named enterprise customers including Flying Tiger Copenhagen and COOP, 45% MoM growth, 13 employees on €1.7M, and angel investors including former Coop Group CEO Kraen Østergaard Nielsen and GS1 board member Per Thau. The regulatory inflection (GPSR) is structural, not cyclical. The invisible system being attacked — consultancy-mediated compliance that extracts value through information asymmetry — maps directly to my highest-conviction pattern. The ten-year question yields a real answer: if Complir accumulates regulatory mappings across every product category and every market, it becomes the compliance infrastructure layer for global retail, the way Flexport became logistics infrastructure. I've checked my Known Exceptions — RTFKT showed me that timing at a narrow window can substitute for deep moats, and Complir's GPSR timing is genuinely narrow. This is not a frivolous pass.

But I keep coming back to the forge. The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: if Gustav, Tine, or Marc can tell me a story about being personally trapped inside the compliance system — a product they couldn't launch, a retailer they watched get crushed by regulatory opacity, a moment where this problem became personal rather than professional — that changes my calculus. The invisible system score is high because the system IS genuinely opaque and extractive. The infrastructure defensibility is real and at a genuine inflection. But without the biographical forge, without the contrarian weirdness that makes other investors uncomfortable, Complir reads to me as a well-executed enterprise SaaS company in a recognized market category — competent, timely, and likely to build a solid business. It does not read as a Long Journey bet. The two dimensions that lock my tier are the founder's biographical forge (absent) and contrarian conviction (this is consensus-fundable regtech, not a space that provokes discomfort). Complir's strongest dimension — the invisible system it attacks — is genuine, and it's the reason this lands in Neutral rather than Pass.
