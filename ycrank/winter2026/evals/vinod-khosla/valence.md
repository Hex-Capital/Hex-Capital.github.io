# Valence -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here is a team of Optiver, DRW, Citadel, and Cubist alumni -- some of the most sophisticated quantitative trading infrastructure people you'll find at the pre-seed level -- and they've pointed all of that talent at building a smart order router for prediction markets. Let me be direct about why this bothers me. Smart order routing, co-location, cross-venue aggregation -- this entire technology stack has existed in equities and futures markets for two decades. They're applying established infrastructure patterns to a new asset class. That's not invention, it's porting. The dossier itself acknowledges this: "The core technology (order routing, aggregation, co-location) is established in traditional financial markets." When the founders' own competitive analysis concedes the technology isn't novel, I have to ask what exactly we're investing in.

The consequence of success here is what stops me cold. If Valence fully succeeds -- becomes the dominant aggregation layer for prediction markets globally -- what changes? Professional traders get marginally better execution across Kalshi and Polymarket. Some arbitrage gets captured more efficiently. The world continues to operate exactly as it does today, with a slightly more liquid prediction market ecosystem. Compare that to what the underlying exchanges are attempting: Kalshi is trying to create an entirely new information market that makes economic forecasting more accurate. Polymarket demonstrated during the 2024 election that prediction markets can outperform polling. Those are consequential ambitions. Valence is building the plumbing between them. The transformational consequence belongs to the exchanges, not to the middleware. I've seen this pattern many times -- talented people choosing the layer of the stack where failure probability is lowest, and in doing so, ensuring that success is inconsequential. These three founders are too smart to spend their careers making professional prediction market traders 15% more efficient.

The team profile reinforces my concern. All three attended UT Austin. All came through the same quantitative trading pipeline -- Optiver, DRW, Citadel, Cubist. All have competitive programming credentials. They know exactly what a trading infrastructure company looks like because they've worked inside the best ones. That's the kind of experience I'm most skeptical of in founders. They're not outsiders reinventing how information markets work; they're insiders replicating the infrastructure they've already seen. Daniel built an order book in Rust for a university club. Neo built "FTX Markets" as a prototype. Arthur qualified for USAMO. These are technically gifted people executing a playbook they already know. Where's the irreverence? Where's the naive confidence that leads someone to attempt something the industry says is impossible? When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand with no aerospace pedigree -- and he built a commercially successful launch vehicle precisely because he didn't know what was supposed to be impossible. Valence's founders know exactly what's possible, which constrains what they'll attempt.

The strongest bull case deserves examination. Prediction markets are genuinely exploding -- from 4,000 monthly active users to 600,000+ in a single year, $44 billion in notional volume, Kalshi revenue up nearly 1,000% year-over-year. Fragmentation is increasing as Robinhood, DraftKings, and FanDuel all launch competing prediction products. If this asset class becomes as large as derivatives or sports betting, the aggregation layer could be enormously valuable. The claimed traction -- $200M+ monthly volume, 1B+ contracts, 50% peak market share on individual events -- would be remarkable if verified, suggesting the team can execute. And the timing argument is real: more venues mean more fragmentation, which means more value in aggregation. The question is whether this follows the trajectory of Bloomberg in fixed income, which started as an information terminal and became the backbone of an entire market. If prediction markets become a trillion-dollar asset class and Valence becomes the institutional interface, the consequence of success grows substantially. I genuinely considered this path. But Bloomberg succeeded because bond markets had no electronic infrastructure -- Bloomberg built the rails. Prediction markets are born digital. Kalshi and Polymarket already have APIs, already have electronic order books. The aggregation value-add is narrower, and the existential risk is that any exchange can revoke API access at any time. You're building a business on someone else's permission. That's not a foundation for a category-defining company.

The timing is the one dimension where I give Valence real credit. They're positioned at the leading edge of a genuine market explosion, and the prediction that fragmentation will increase as more consumer platforms launch is sound. But good timing with insufficient ambition produces a decent business, not a transformational one. The technology rate of change here is in the underlying prediction market ecosystem, not in Valence's own technology. Co-location and smart order routing aren't accelerating -- they're mature capabilities being applied to a new venue. Valence is surfing someone else's exponential curve without building its own.
