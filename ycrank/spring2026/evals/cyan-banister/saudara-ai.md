# Saudara AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that grabs me first about Saudara AI isn't the founders or the AI — it's the tariff clock. US tariffs on Chinese goods hit 54%, and overnight every DTC brand in America is asking the same question: "Where else can I make this?" Indonesian tariffs sit at 32%, imports from non-China Asia surged 40% year-on-year in 2025, and suddenly a $30B+ export corridor that barely registered in Silicon Valley is the emergency alternative. That's a real inflection. The question I'm sitting with is whether Saudara AI is building infrastructure at that inflection — the kind of thing Crusoe was building when they put data centers next to gas flares — or whether they're a well-timed service riding a policy wave that could reverse with a single executive order.

When I ask my first question — tell me the founders' story, not the business — I get Jennifer Prasetyo's family manufacturing network stretching back 50-plus years in Indonesia, and Edward Haryono's trajectory from Indonesian heritage through Duke and Amazon into this venture. There's genuine founder-market fit in the cultural bridge these two represent. Jennifer's family connections are the kind of asset you cannot purchase or replicate from a WeWork in San Francisco. But I keep looking for the forge — the moment the problem became personal, became impossible to ignore. What I find instead is a Senior Technical PM at Amazon and an engineer at Microsoft Quantum and Meta Reality Labs who recognized, correctly, that their heritage gave them an edge in a market that's suddenly hot. That's smart. It's strategic. But it's not the same as a founder who lived inside the broken system and couldn't sleep until they fixed it. When I invested in Affirm, Max Levchin wasn't spotting an opportunity in lending — he'd spent years watching credit card companies hide the true cost of borrowing from ordinary people, and it enraged him. I don't see that rage here. I see clear-eyed positioning.

There IS an invisible system underneath cross-border sourcing — I want to be honest about that. If you're a small American brand trying to find a factory in Surabaya, you're navigating a maze of middlemen, ghost suppliers, opaque pricing, and language barriers that exploit your ignorance at every turn. Traditional sourcing agents sit at the center of that opacity and extract 3-10% for information they hoard. That pattern — gatekeepers profiting from asymmetry — is exactly the kind of thing I've built my portfolio around disrupting. But I need to separate the opportunity from the execution. Alibaba already pulled back the curtain on global sourcing for millions of buyers. What Saudara is really saying is that Indonesia specifically remains opaque even after Alibaba, and that on-the-ground family connections plus AI can crack it open in a way that horizontal platforms can't. It's a narrower version of the thesis, and narrow can be powerful if it compounds — but narrow plus policy-dependent makes me nervous.

The bull case I keep turning over: the tariff shift is creating genuine urgency, and urgency creates willingness to pay. Sourcify, a comparable YC company, already reached $1.9M in revenue doing Asia manufacturing sourcing — proving the model. Saudara's family network is something Accio's two million users and SourceReady's 1.2 million suppliers cannot replicate overnight, because relationships in Indonesian manufacturing are built in person, in Bahasa, over decades, not through platform algorithms. If the founders can convert those relationships into a data layer — supplier reliability scores, pricing benchmarks, quality histories — that accumulates with every transaction, they're building something that gets harder to displace over time. And the tariff differential isn't just a moment; it reflects a deeper structural reorientation of global supply chains that may persist for a decade. If that's the world we're entering, the first trusted bridge between American brands and Indonesian factories wins. That's a real case, and I don't dismiss it.

But when I run my ten-year question — where does this technology go? — I struggle. In ten years, is Saudara AI an infrastructure company that every brand passes through to access Southeast Asian manufacturing? Or is it a services business with a family contact list and an AI skin? The AI layer — supplier matching, communication automation, status tracking — is the kind of thing Accio could build in a quarter with their resources. The family network doesn't scale like Flock Safety's camera network or Carta's cap table switching costs. Each new customer doesn't structurally make the platform harder to displace in the way my best infrastructure bets do. And the single-country concentration means a tariff change, a political disruption, or a natural disaster in Indonesia puts the entire thesis at risk. There's no geographic diversification and no path to it without abandoning the core advantage.

This isn't a company I have conviction against — the founders are capable, the timing is real, and the market need is genuine. But it doesn't activate my framework. The biographical forge isn't there. The space isn't contrarian — supply chain diversification from China is the most consensus trade thesis of 2026. The invisible system is present but narrow. The defensibility is relational, not infrastructural. I'd tell these founders they're building something real, and someone should fund it — but I'm not the right investor. This is a smart services business looking for a technology exit ramp, and my best bets have always been technology businesses that looked deceptively like something weird.
