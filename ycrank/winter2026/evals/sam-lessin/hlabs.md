# Hlabs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what immediately catches my attention: a solo founder with $500K is trying to build the American industrial supply chain for robotics components. That's either the kind of operational insanity that produces Teamshares-scale outcomes, or it's a capital-intensity trap that chews up a founder before they can reach scale. The answer hinges almost entirely on whether Paul Hetherington is a sane person with an insane ambition or whether the resource mismatch between vision and capital is itself the signal.

On the sane side of the ledger, Hetherington is genuinely credible for this specific company. An MEng in integrated mechanical and electrical engineering from Bath, embedded systems research on UAVs and ultrasound tomography, a commercial drone certification, and then six years running Mystic AI — a developer-facing ML infrastructure platform — through YC W21. That last piece matters most: he's already built infrastructure tools for technical users. He knows what developer adoption looks like. He's a second-time YC founder who pivoted from ML infrastructure to hardware infrastructure, and the combination of embedded systems engineering plus developer platform experience is exactly what you'd want in someone building "plug and play robotic primitives." The insane part isn't the thesis — everyone knows robots need components — it's the ambition to out-execute both Chinese manufacturers and established industrial suppliers as a one-person company. Most investors would look at RobStride backed by Sequoia in Beijing and say "you're bringing a soldering iron to a gunfight." That dismissal is what creates the non-consensus opportunity.

What I notice that most investors would miss is the structural position this company occupies relative to AI. Run my standard test: does AI commoditize this business or strengthen it? The answer is unambiguously "strengthen." Every advance in AI capabilities accelerates the demand for physical robots, which accelerates demand for robotic components. AI cannot manufacture an actuator control board. AI cannot ship hardware to a defense customer who needs ITAR-compliant US-sourced components. This is the "cherry on top" dynamic in its purest form — Hlabs would be a viable business in a world without AI, but AI is pouring rocket fuel on the demand side while doing nothing to erode the supply-side moat. Contrast this with every "AI-powered" SaaS tool in the current YC batch: those companies are building where AI creates the value AND where AI enables every competitor. Hlabs is building where AI creates the demand but physical manufacturing creates the defensibility. That asymmetry is exactly what I look for.

The primitive aspiration is real but needs honest assessment. "Plug and play robotic primitives" with open-source software is structurally an attempt to become the layer that robotics companies build on — the way Venmo became the primitive for peer-to-peer payments. If Hlabs' control boards and software APIs become the standard development stack for US robotics prototyping, every new robot company that starts with their components increases the switching cost for the ecosystem. 40+ companies ordering within four months of launch is an early signal of this dynamic. But hardware primitives compound differently than software or crypto primitives. There are no network effects in buying an actuator board the way there are in using a payment protocol. The lock-in comes from software integration and documentation, not from the hardware itself. Open-source reference designs for robotic actuators already exist on GitHub. The question is whether the integrated hardware-software-support stack creates enough friction to prevent commoditization before the ecosystem matures. HEBI Robotics has been attempting a similar modular actuator play since 2014 out of Carnegie Mellon and has raised only $580K — either that validates the difficulty of the market or it indicts the opportunity.

The bull case deserves its full articulation: If the humanoid robotics wave produces fifty serious companies instead of five, every one of them needs components. The narrative path from "actuator control boards" to "the Foxconn of American robotics" is coherent and expansive — it's a genuine infinity story. Each product line expansion (compute modules, cameras, wireless, bespoke production designs) makes the platform stickier and the narrative larger. Defense procurement alone could provide a revenue floor via ITAR-compliant sourcing requirements, and US trade policy is actively constructing moats against Chinese component suppliers that Hlabs gets for free. If Hetherington can attract a co-founder and raise a proper hardware seed round in the next six months, the capital-intensity problem becomes solvable, and the 40+ pre-existing customer relationships become the foundation for a real business. The timing convergence — humanoid wave, tariff regime, falling component costs — is unusually favorable.

Solo founder on a capital-intensive hardware company remains my primary concern. This is the dimension where the sane-insane calibration gets uncomfortable. Hardware businesses demand simultaneous execution across manufacturing, logistics, quality control, inventory management, and customer support — and that's before you add the multi-product roadmap Hetherington has announced for Q1 2026. One person cannot do all of this. The company isn't currently hiring. At $500K of funding, the margin for error on hardware inventory and manufacturing tooling is essentially zero. This isn't a software company where a solo founder can ship product from a laptop — every misstep in manufacturing or supply chain management burns physical capital. I've watched enough hardware companies die in the gap between prototype enthusiasm and production-scale execution to take this risk seriously.

On balance, Hlabs fits my framework in ways that most YC companies don't. It's a physical-world infrastructure play where AI increases demand without increasing competition. The founder has specifically relevant credentials. The early traction is real. The narrative ceiling is high. But the solo-founder-plus-hardware combination, the capital intensity relative to resources, and the fact that "robotics infrastructure" is becoming warm rather than cold as a thesis all create drag on my conviction. This is a company I'd want to meet the founder on before writing a check — the operator credibility question can only be fully answered in person. The structural positioning is compelling enough to warrant serious engagement.
