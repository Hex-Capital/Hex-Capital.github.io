# Squid -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out about Squid is the embedded customer position: a two-person pre-seed company has National Grid DSO's FlexPortal running on its platform, with press coverage in three energy trade publications and a named testimonial from National Grid's Head of Flexibility Markets. That's remarkable for any stage, let alone pre-seed. It tells me these founders have deep relational capital in the UK utility sector -- which is unsurprising given that both previously worked at National Grid and Octopus Energy, co-founded VoltQuant serving those exact customers, and are now running it back with Squid selling to the same organizations. The insider access is undeniable. But here's my problem: insider access to a defined enterprise market is a distribution advantage, not a structural thesis. This is the Birchbox lesson applied to enterprise -- the founders' relationships got them in the door, but relationships alone don't build a moat that compounds over decades.

When I run the sane-person-insane-idea filter, the calibration is lopsided. The "sane" signal is among the strongest I've seen at pre-seed: second-time energy founders, Cambridge engineering pedigree, Head of Technology at Octopus Energy, AWS AI/ML experience, BCG consulting discipline, and they already built and sold a product to these exact customers. These are credentialed operators who deeply understand the problem space. But where's the "insane"? Browser-native grid planning software is a logical modernization of an acknowledged problem. Everyone in the energy sector agrees that planning workflows are fragmented across spreadsheets and PDFs. Envelio built a digital twin SaaS for this. Camus raised $25M for adjacent grid software. Plexigrid got $11.6M. GE Vernova has PlanOS. The idea provokes head-nodding, not head-scratching -- and in my framework, consensus problems attract consensus capital and consensus competition. A sane person with a sane idea is a fine business, but it's not the asymmetric bet I'm looking for.

The structural moat question is where my real skepticism crystallizes. Strip away the language about "live, versioned grid models" and "AI-powered stress testing" and what you have is enterprise SaaS sold to utilities. The dossier itself characterizes the technical complexity of building accurate network models with versioning as "moderate." SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications are table stakes for selling to utilities, not a moat -- every competitor in this space either has them or will get them. The switching costs from embedded data are real but not unique to Squid; any grid planning tool that a utility adopts as its system of record creates similar lock-in. Meanwhile, E.ON acquired Envelio in 2021 and has the balance sheet and utility relationships to distribute an overlapping product across Europe. GE Vernova bundles planning tools with hardware and SCADA systems. When I ask "does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper?" the answer is uncomfortable: AI will make it easier, not harder, to build grid modeling and simulation tools. The domain knowledge required is real but finite -- it's engineering knowledge that can be encoded, not physical operations that resist digitization.

Now, the bull case I have to genuinely engage with: if Squid's versioned grid model becomes the canonical planning layer for distribution network operators, it could evolve from application to infrastructure. The analogy would be GitHub for grid planning -- the versioned model becomes the collaborative primitive that all planning workflows depend on, creating genuine protocol-layer lock-in. The UK's RIIO-ED2 regulatory framework is forcing DNOs to modernize planning processes through 2028, creating a procurement window. And the electrification wave -- EVs, heat pumps, distributed generation -- is creating unprecedented planning complexity that legacy tools genuinely cannot handle. If Squid nails the UK market and the versioned model concept becomes a standard that utilities globally adopt, the narrative expands from "grid planning tool" to "the infrastructure layer for the energy transition." That's a real infinity story. But it requires Squid to survive the competitive gauntlet of GE Vernova, E.ON/Envelio, Camus, and Plexigrid with a two-person team and 6-18 month utility sales cycles -- and it requires the versioned model to become a genuine standard rather than just a feature of one vendor's product.

What I keep coming back to is that Squid occupies an awkward position in my framework. It's not a physical-world business with a tech layer -- it's a pure software business selling into the physical world. Craftwork has painting crews that AI can't replace. Metropolis has installed parking hardware. Squid has a web app. The domain complexity of grid planning provides some insulation from casual competition, but it doesn't provide the kind of AI-resistant moat I look for. The founders clearly have operational credibility and embedded customer relationships that give them a meaningful head start, and the FlexPortal deployment is a genuine proof point. But I'm not seeing the non-consensus bet. I'm seeing excellent execution on a consensus opportunity in a competitive market where well-capitalized incumbents are already playing. For my own money, I need either a crazier idea or a deeper structural moat -- ideally both.
