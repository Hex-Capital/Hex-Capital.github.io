# Petrarch -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Petrarch is a company that has spotted a real schlep -- buying orphaned datasets out of wind-downs and cleaning their provenance for AI-lab buyers -- and staffed it with three undergraduates whose dossier contains no pre-funding artifact, no stunt, no shipped demo. That pairing -- a genuine schlep zone attached to founders who read on paper as credentialed rather than resourceful -- is Petrarch's analytical signature. The Harvard econ/history major, the Harvard construction-ops student, and the college sophomore ISEF winner have described what the marketplace *will* do; the dossier does not tell me what any of them has already done to make it real.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the ladder rung Petrarch is reaching for on the schlep axis, and it is the strongest part of the story. Brian Armstrong won that batch because he wanted the bitcoin compliance grind -- the KYC filings, the state money-transmitter licenses -- that every other batch-mate was routing around. Petrarch's problem shape rhymes: chain-of-title from insolvent Delaware C-corps, GDPR-compliant anonymization of end-user data the shutdown company may not have had rights to transfer, bankruptcy-trustee relationships that require showing up in person to unglamorous rooms. Datarade won't do this; Scale AI won't do this; Gretel side-steps it entirely by generating synthetic data. Where Petrarch diverges from Coinbase is that Armstrong had already hand-built an Android Bitcoin wallet to prove he'd do the grind; Petrarch's dossier shows one $25/hr Business Operations Intern posting and a "Coming 2026" splash page. Naming the schlep is not the same as walking into it.

The pass example in my file -- the two-MBA AI-wrapper team with a glossy deck and a hire-engineers-later plan -- fails because no founder has personally suffered the problem, and that pattern shows up in Petrarch too. Ian Lee sourced construction contracts at CBRE and DPR for Google and UCSF; Samuel Hahn is studying economics and history; Sudhish Swain models chemotherapy metabolism. None of them, per the dossier, has ever tried to buy a dataset from a dying startup, sold a dataset to an AI lab, or run a two-sided marketplace. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick; the Collisons had been hacking payments since **Auctomatic** (2007). Petrarch's founders spotted an opportunity in the market rather than in their own workflow, and that is the exact inversion of the "How to Get Startup Ideas" (November 2012) filter.

Mechanism analysis on animal resourcefulness: over 18 months, a Petrarch that lacks a pre-funding stunt has to *manufacture* one, and the compounding is unforgiving. Buying a dataset from a bankruptcy trustee requires showing up with wire-ready capital and a licensing agreement drafted by a lawyer who has done Chapter 7 data assignments before; brokering it to Anthropic or OpenAI requires a warm intro Petrarch does not appear to have. The Airbnb cereal-box moment isn't a nice-to-have anecdote -- it's the moment I learn whether the team can generate cash from an idea nobody else will fund. Petrarch's dossier is silent on that moment, and silence at pre-seed is the moment itself.

Counterfactual on hacker genealogy: for Petrarch to move up a tier on this axis, I would need to see Sudhish Swain's ISEF 2023 computational-modeling code published under a confirmed GitHub handle, or a shipped tool -- a provenance-diligence script, a dataset-fingerprinting demo -- posted anywhere before the YC check. The dossier lists "GitHub: Handle unconfirmed" for the CTO and "No public repos found" for the other two. Patrick Collison had a Lisp prize the world could inspect before **Stripe** (2010); Aaron Swartz had the RSS spec before **Reddit** (2005). Absence of a GitHub trail from a self-declared CTO is not a neutral fact -- hackers leave trails.

Single-anchor parallel on cofounder configuration: **Airbnb** (2009) was a trio with years of RISD and roommate history; Petrarch is a trio where two founders share a Harvard affiliation and the third is at a different institution as a sophomore, with no confirmed prior shared project. Three-person teams work when the coverage is complementary and the relationship is long. Petrarch might have that -- the YC interview would tell -- but the dossier gives me two Harvard LinkedIn URLs and no evidence they built anything together before applying.

My 36-month forecast on Petrarch: dead or quietly pivoted to a data-licensing consulting practice by mid-2029, under $500K ARR, no strategic acquirer, because the sourcing side is episodic (bankruptcy datasets are adversely selected -- the worst companies die first, so their data is the least valuable) and the buying side is being eaten by synthetic-data generation (Gretel-style) and first-party labeling (Scale AI at $1.5B ARR). The schlep is real; the team, on this dossier, does not yet look like it will run at it hard enough to compound.

The steel-man is Ian Lee. Sourcing distressed real-estate deals at CBRE and Lincoln Property is *unusually* good preparation for sourcing distressed data assets from bankruptcy trustees -- both are unglamorous, relationship-driven, offline pipelines, and almost no YC applicant has that background. This could be the Petrarch version of Chesky asking about cereal boxes: an unconventional pre-funding skill nobody else in the batch has. It stings because I've watched real-estate operators quietly out-execute software people on physical-world sourcing before. But it does not clear the bar: real-estate sourcing is not chain-of-title legal diligence on end-user personal data under GDPR, and Petrarch's dossier contains zero evidence of a first acquired dataset, a first LOI from an AI lab, or a shipped provenance-verification tool. One verifiable condition would flip Petrarch to Invest: publicly naming one acquired dataset from a named wind-down with a signed licensing chain-of-title and one signed LOI from a named AI lab before end of 2026. Until then, pass.
