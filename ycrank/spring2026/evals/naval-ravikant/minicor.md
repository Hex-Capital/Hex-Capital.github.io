# Minicor -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The architecture tells me almost everything I need to know about this bet. Minicor stores automation as deterministic code and deploys AI only for recovery and edge cases. That's not a wrapper — that's an engineering decision that reveals how the founders think about durability. Most teams in this space are building castles on Anthropic's API. Minicor is building the castle out of code and using the API as a repair crew. The distinction matters because it means their product doesn't degrade when model providers change pricing or capability. But it also raises the question I can't escape: the core unlock — AI that can see and interact with screens — was handed to the entire market by Anthropic in October 2024. The managed infrastructure layer (VMs, compliance, observability, self-healing orchestration) is where they're placing their bet, and that's a real layer, but it's an operational moat, not a knowledge moat. Operational moats erode. Knowledge moats compound.

On specific knowledge: Saheed spent three years as a senior engineer inside Ford's legacy systems, with Morgan Stanley and Kinaxis before that. Faizaan worked at Phoenix, a YC company focused on system connectivity. They didn't study legacy enterprise software from the outside — they lived inside the machinery. That's meaningful. But it's specific knowledge about the *problem*, not the *solution*. Compare to someone who spent five years building computer vision for UI interaction at UiPath and then left to build something fundamentally better. Minicor's founders know why legacy desktop automation breaks, and they know which enterprises are in pain. That knowledge is harder to acquire than most investors appreciate — you cannot learn it from a market research report. But it's also the kind of knowledge that any engineer who spent three years at a Fortune 500 could accumulate. It doesn't create the non-replicable asymmetry I look for in my highest-conviction bets.

The leverage architecture has a split personality. On the positive side: five people, SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA compliant, twenty-five thousand patients per day at architecture scale. That's extraordinary output per person. Getting compliance certifications at a five-person pre-seed company is not a checkbox — it's months of disciplined engineering work that most startups postpone until Series A. The MCP interface turning legacy desktop apps into triggerable API endpoints is genuine infrastructure leverage — it converts something previously requiring human operators into something a coding agent can call programmatically. On the negative side: UiPath has already shipped an AI Healing Agent. Automation Anywhere is converging on the same capabilities. And any team with access to Anthropic's Computer Use API can start building tomorrow. Minicor's response — that the managed infrastructure layer is the real product — is correct but insufficient. Infrastructure layers get commoditized by the platforms they sit on. Anthropic could ship managed VMs for computer use the way AWS ships managed everything.

The principal-agent signals are the strongest part of the case. These founders met organically in a university class in 2019, built a non-profit together during the pandemic, and started working on Minicor in 2023 before deciding to raise. That's the pre-existing trust and shared history I look for — not co-founders who met at a networking event. Saheed leaving a senior role at Ford to build this tells me something about commitment. The co-founder relationship has been tested across multiple projects over five-plus years. That's durable. But I don't see evidence that either founder was obsessively building desktop automation tools on their own before this became a company. The origin story reads more like "we identified an opportunity from our experience" than "we couldn't stop building this."

The bull case is stronger than it first appears. If you believe — and I mostly do — that AI agents will flood into healthcare, automotive, supply chain, and financial services over the next three years, then every single one of those agents needs a way to talk to legacy systems of record that have no APIs. Minicor is positioning itself as the bridge. The "API for legacy desktop apps" is a genuine unlock that enterprises cannot build themselves and that incumbent RPA vendors are poorly structured to deliver. UiPath sells to enterprise IT departments on multi-year contracts; Minicor sells to AI companies through developer-first APIs. That's a different buyer, a different sales motion, and a structural blind spot for incumbents whose agents are optimized for their existing customer base. If the AI-into-legacy wave materializes at scale, Minicor's early compliance certifications and production deployments become compounding advantages — every workflow processed generates training data for their self-healing system, and every SOC 2 audit makes the next enterprise deal faster. The 93–96% accuracy claim, if it holds, is a real technical edge over the 80–85% baseline. In healthcare, that delta is the difference between usable and unusable.

Where I land: this is a well-executed company attacking a real problem, built by principals with genuine enterprise experience and impressive team-to-value leverage. But the specific knowledge density doesn't reach the threshold I need for my highest conviction. The founders know the pain; they don't own the solution at a level that would take competitors years to replicate. And the commoditization pressure from both model providers above and RPA incumbents below creates a compression risk that the managed infrastructure layer may not survive. I'd want to see this company in twelve months — if they've built a proprietary data flywheel from production workflows that makes their self-healing measurably better than anything built on raw model APIs, the knowledge moat will have emerged from execution rather than from the founders' backgrounds. That's a real path, but it's a bet on future compounding, not on present advantage.
