# Docura Health -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Docura Health is that nobody would call this idea insane. Using AI to summarize medical records and draft compliance reports for physicians -- that's not the kind of thing that makes other investors shift uncomfortably in their chairs. It's sensible. It's obvious. And that's exactly the problem from where I sit. The companies that have defined my portfolio -- SpaceX when private rocketry was a punchline, Crusoe when "Bitcoin mining on gas flares" made people laugh -- all shared the quality of being genuinely unpopular and difficult to assess. Docura Health is neither. It's a vertical AI workflow tool in a space where well-funded companies like Wisedocs ($20M raised, $14.4M revenue, 116 employees) and the Datavant/DigitalOwl combination ($40.8M raised) are already operating. The specific niche -- physician-facing report generation rather than carrier-facing summarization -- is a clever positioning choice, but it's a go-to-market wedge, not the kind of structural insight that makes my stomach flip.

I want to hear Akhil's story, and what I find is a gap where I need a thread. He's a UC Berkeley CS graduate with internships at Rivian, Salesforce, Zuora, and Cisco. His GitHub shows OCR and document-processing projects, which tells me he can build the technical layer. But where is the biographical connection to workers' compensation? To medico-legal evaluation? To the experience of an injured worker waiting months for a claim settlement, or a physician drowning in thousand-page records? I don't see it. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception before anyone thought it was investable -- his life and the company were inseparable. When I recognized what Travis Kalanick could be at Uber, it was because I'd spent years in conversation about the medallion system and felt the problem viscerally through my own driver Roger. With Akhil, I see a technically capable recent graduate who identified a workflow inefficiency analytically. That's not disqualifying at pre-seed, but it's not the signal I build conviction around. I want founders where the problem chose them, not the other way around.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. The niche targeting is smart -- California has roughly 1,900 active QMEs, and by owning that physician workflow with AMA-compliant impairment ratings, Docura could build real switching costs that the carrier-focused competitors won't easily replicate. The claim that providers have "doubled their case volume" is meaningful productivity improvement if it holds. Workers' comp is a $57 billion market with deep inefficiency, and 80% of claims could theoretically be automated according to Deloitte. If Akhil can establish himself as the default tool for California QMEs and then expand state by state, adapting to each jurisdiction's regulatory framework, there's a path to a real business. The regulatory complexity of AMA Guides compliance and state-specific workers' comp rules could become a genuine moat over time. And choosing a hard, unsexy, regulated vertical rather than chasing consumer AI hype shows a certain discipline I respect.

But I keep coming back to what this company doesn't do for me. It doesn't create economic access for underserved populations -- the primary beneficiaries are well-compensated physician-evaluators, not injured workers or economically marginalized people. You could argue that faster report turnaround accelerates claim settlements for workers who need the money, but that's an indirect and optimistic chain of causation, not the direct economic empowerment I saw with Uber drivers or Affirm borrowers. It doesn't make an invisible system visible -- it makes an existing professional workflow faster. And the core technology risk is low to nonexistent. LLM-based document summarization and structured report generation is reproducible. Any well-resourced competitor -- and there are several with ten to sixty times the capital already raised -- could build this. The defensibility lives in domain-specific compliance and workflow embedding, which take time to accumulate and which a solo founder with no healthcare background will need to earn the hard way, one physician relationship at a time, in a trust-based market where credentials matter.

The solo founder dynamic is worth addressing directly. I've learned from HQ Trivia that co-founder conflict can destroy even the best-timed products, so I don't automatically penalize solo founders -- they avoid that specific failure mode. But workers' comp report generation for physicians is a market that requires simultaneous excellence in product engineering, regulatory compliance, and relationship-based sales to a conservative customer base. Doing all three alone, with a team of one and no documented healthcare network, is a structural challenge that goes beyond the normal pre-seed constraint. This is the kind of regulated, trust-dependent market where I'd want to see either deep domain relationships or a co-founder who brings the clinical and regulatory credibility the technical founder lacks.
