# Atlas Discovery -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Atlas Discovery is a research-credentialed AI/bio bet inside a YC batch that already contains a near-identical Yale spinout, and that pairing — a competent founder pedigree at the top of my ladder, a tactile-artifact and non-consensus score at the bottom — tells me what kind of company this is before I ever open the deck. Sanjukta Bhattacharya has a bioRxiv paper on discrete diffusion for gene expression and a Los Alamos AI research stint, which is real; Atlas Discovery is also the third or fourth "virtual cell" pitch in this specific cohort window, with Recursion's BioHive-2, Insitro at $1.0B, Tahoe at $72M, and CellType (YC W26, Cell2Sentence 27B with Google DeepMind) already on the field. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing — and the game here is not whether Atlas Discovery's model works, it's whether a solo-named PhD student in Bristol gets to consolidate a category that Illumina just commoditized with a Billion Cell Atlas in January 2026.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus out of his parents' garage before Anduril; John Hanke ran Keyhole into Google Earth before Niantic — the test on dimension one is whether the founder has *already done* a comparable thing to scale in an adjacent domain, not whether they have the right research line. Sanjukta Bhattacharya's discrete-diffusion-for-gene-expression paper and her Edinburgh master's under Antonio Vergari on probabilistic circuits put her closer to the DeepMind-Hassabis polymath override than to the analytical-PM pass pattern, which is exactly why Atlas Discovery does not crater this dimension — there is a research trajectory that maps to the pitch. But the dossier names *only* Sanjukta on the YC page, with two unnamed teammates, and a still-active PhD at the University of Bristol while the company sits in San Francisco; the lived-experience claim is "I publish in the field," not "I sat in a pharma trial-design meeting and watched a Phase II fail." That's good but not Luckey-good.

The consensus YC AI-agent darling I pass on at slide 3 has the same shape as Atlas Discovery on dimension two, and the failure mechanism is the same: the round is priced as if the outcome is already decided. Atlas Discovery is in YC Summer 2026 with Pear and Glasswing already on the cap table, in the same batch shape as CellType (YC W26) doing essentially the same Cell2Sentence-flavored virtual-cell foundation model with Yale and Google DeepMind backing it. Recursion is publicly traded with $500M of cumulative milestone payments; Insitro has raised $1.0B; CZI, Arc, and Google DeepMind all have public virtual-cell programs. The "magically weird" filter wants me to find Atlas Discovery before it becomes consensus — the dossier itself documents that the consensus formed roughly eighteen months ago. There is no aesthetic, regulatory, or moral pass I can point to from a sophisticated investor that Atlas Discovery would be correcting.

On dimension three, the mechanism that compounds over eighteen months is unresolved authority — a co-founder team of three with one publicly named member and a part-time geographic split between Bristol and SF will hit its first unresolvable disagreement (model strategy vs. BD strategy vs. publication strategy) inside year one. Sanjukta is the only founder named on the YC page for Atlas Discovery; whether she's the unambiguous CEO with tiebreaker authority is exactly the question the HQ Trivia post-mortem taught me to ask before signing, and the dossier doesn't answer it. Default applies, scored conservatively.

For dimension four, the counterfactual that would flip Atlas Discovery a tier is owning the wet lab — if the dossier said "Atlas operates a perturbation foundry like Tahoe" or "Atlas has an exclusive license to a pharma's frozen Phase II tissue archive," I would treat it like Crusoe-of-clinical-data and the score would jump. As written, Atlas Discovery is a software layer over data its peers can also access; Illumina's January 2026 Billion Cell Atlas and open-source scGPT/CellFM/scLong publications mean the training-data barrier is falling for everyone, not just Atlas Discovery. Capital-intensive in *category*, yes — clinical drug discovery is unsexy and regulated — but Atlas Discovery itself is not running the unsexy capital-intensive operation, it's running the model.

The single-anchor parallel on dimension five is HQ Trivia, which I bought because I could *play* it on my phone at a Tuesday lunch and feel the addictive pull in my own hand — Atlas Discovery has no such artifact. The product is a foundation model whose output is a prediction about a clinical trial; a normal human cannot install it, ride it, scan it, or photograph it in their life within 48 hours. The hiring inbox exists, the bioRxiv paper exists, the @splicewiring Twitter handle exists, but the dossier records no demo, no design partner named, no signed pharma LOI, no benchmark a third party can verify. This is the developer-tools-abstraction shape, just in biology.

Thirty-six month forecast: Atlas Discovery raises a seed in 2026, fails to differentiate from CellType, Tahoe, and Valence Labs on a published benchmark by 2027, signs one mid-tier pharma research contract under $1M ARR, and is either acqui-hired by Recursion or Insitro for the model team, or quietly shuts down — the mechanism is that the category consolidated around capitalized incumbents while the data layer commoditized underneath, and a three-person team with one named founder cannot out-iterate $1.5B of competing capital on a model whose moat is the training corpus.

The DeepMind exception is the strongest case against my pass, and it should sting: Hassabis arrived with elite credentials and no lived-experience origin and I funded him anyway because the polymath compounding was the override. Sanjukta Bhattacharya's diffusion-for-gene-expression line is genuinely novel research, and "age of the polymath" is exactly the regime I said is coming — if biological foundation models hit a scaling-law inflection in 2027 and Atlas Discovery has the right architecture, the model itself is the moat. But the dossier evidence wins the other way: DeepMind in 2013 had Hassabis, Suleyman, *and* Legg publicly named with a clear research lead; Atlas Discovery has one named founder, two ghosts, an ongoing Bristol PhD, and a YC batchmate (CellType) executing the same thesis with Yale + Google DeepMind affiliation. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Atlas Discovery publishes a clinical-response-prediction benchmark that beats scGPT and Tahoe-trained baselines on a held-out pharma dataset, with all three co-founders named and a single CEO declared. Until then, pass — politely, with one foot in love, because the research is real.
