# InsForge -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Supabase has $500M+ in funding, 80K+ GitHub stars, and has already begun shipping MCP support for AI agent interaction. InsForge is building what amounts to an interface abstraction on top of the same backend primitives -- Postgres, S3-compatible storage, OAuth -- that Supabase already operates at scale. The central question for InsForge isn't whether agent-native backend infrastructure matters (it does) but whether a semantic layer over commoditized primitives constitutes infrastructure or a feature. When I backed Stripe, the answer was clear: Stripe built the payment rails that other businesses integrated into their core transaction loops, creating compounding switching costs that made rip-out progressively more painful. InsForge's "semantic layer" and `skill.md` workflow reference are clever interface design, but the dossier's own risk assessment states it plainly: "any BaaS provider could build an agent-optimized interface; the barrier is execution speed and community adoption rather than deep technical moat." That's a feature race, not an infrastructure moat.

InsForge's market timing is directionally correct but not nonobvious. AI coding agent adoption is accelerating -- 51% of professional developers use AI tools daily per MarketsandMarkets 2025 data, and MCP standardized the agent-tool interface in late 2024. Every developer tools investor I talk to sees this shift. Supabase sees it. Firebase will see it. When I wrote the seed check for Perplexity in 2022, AI-powered search was still considered a failed category after a decade of disappointments -- that was a nonobvious window. When I backed Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed. InsForge's thesis -- "AI agents need backend infrastructure they can operate programmatically" -- is a thesis that's already consensus among the developer tools community. InsForge's own Launch Week generating 1.5M+ X impressions and hitting #1 on GitHub Trending confirms that the market is aware and excited, which is great for adoption but terrible for nonobviousness. The structural shift is real; the contrarian insight is absent.

InsForge's single-miracle test reveals compounding challenges. The exogenous miracle -- AI agents becoming capable enough to handle backend operations -- is already underway, and InsForge's self-reported MCPMark benchmarks (1.7x higher accuracy vs. Supabase at 47.6% vs. 28.6%) suggest meaningful differentiation today. But InsForge needs a second independent miracle: building sufficient community lock-in and brand before Supabase, with 10x InsForge's GitHub stars and 250x its funding, closes the agent-optimization gap. Supabase adding MCP support isn't a hypothetical risk -- it's already happening. InsForge's 7,700 stars and 4,000+ databases are impressive for a pre-seed company with a 6-person team, but network effects in open-source BaaS favor the incumbent with deeper documentation, more integrations, and a larger contributor ecosystem. The race requires InsForge to win on execution speed against a well-capitalized competitor that's already moving -- that's a second miracle.

Hang Huang and Tony Chang bring complementary backgrounds -- Huang's MBA from Yale and product experience at Amazon and Riot Games paired with Chang's systems engineering from Databricks and Meta, plus 10 hackathon wins that signal rapid prototyping ability. But neither founder has operated at a company during its hypergrowth scaling phase (10 to 1,000+ employees), which is the experience I weight most heavily for navigating the 1-to-100 transition. Chang graduated from UIUC in 2024, making him very early-career. InsForge's six YC rejections before acceptance demonstrate persistence and iteration speed -- they doubled their database count from 2,300 to 4,000+ between November 2025 and January 2026 -- but the repeated rejections also suggest the product-market framing took considerable refinement to land. For a company that needs to outrun Supabase, I want to see founders who've previously navigated the organizational and go-to-market scaling challenges they're about to face.

InsForge's $25/mo Pro tier and $300/mo revenue (at the time of their 5th YC rejection) imply roughly 12 paying customers against 4,000+ hosted databases -- a conversion rate under 1%. The generous free tier (50K MAU, 500MB database, 5GB bandwidth) is standard for PLG developer tools, but InsForge's pricing architecture is tier-based rather than outcome-based. In my "seats to cognition" framework, the highest-value AI-era companies price on units of work delivered, not static resource allocation. InsForge's overage pricing ($0.00325/MAU, $0.125/GB) captures some usage-based upside, but the core model looks more like traditional BaaS pricing than the outcome-oriented architecture I look for. At pre-seed I don't penalize this heavily -- most companies haven't optimized pricing yet -- but it's not a positive signal either.

The strongest bull case for InsForge: the human-to-agent interface shift could be as fundamental as desktop-to-mobile, and purpose-built agent-native infrastructure could structurally outperform retrofitted solutions. InsForge's 47.6% accuracy versus Supabase's 28.6% on backend tasks (their MCPMark benchmark) might reflect genuine architectural advantages that bolted-on MCP support can't replicate. If that's true, InsForge could become the default backend infrastructure for the agent-native development paradigm the way Supabase became the default for the open-source Firebase alternative wave. But I can't verify this -- the benchmarks are self-reported, no third-party validation exists, and the accuracy gap (47.6% vs. 28.6%) is still below 50%, meaning agents fail more often than they succeed on InsForge's own platform. The condition that would flip me to Invest: independent third-party benchmark validation showing sustained accuracy advantages that hold as Supabase iterates on its MCP support, combined with $10K+ MRR demonstrating that the agent-optimization layer drives meaningful paid conversion. Without that evidence, InsForge is a competent team executing on a consensus thesis with thin infrastructure positioning against a well-capitalized incumbent already converging on their differentiation.
