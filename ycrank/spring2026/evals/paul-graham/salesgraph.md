# Salesgraph -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Salesgraph's origin is the most interesting thing about it: Ricardo Nunez and Ruhan Ponnada built a business case generator at Mintlify -- where both were among the first 10 employees -- and it worked. It helped close enterprise deals at higher ACV. They didn't sit down and brainstorm startup ideas in the sales intelligence space. They lived inside an early-stage sales process, felt the pain of stitching together Gong recordings and CRM records before every call, and built the tool they needed. That's the real version of "live in the future, then build what's missing." It's the same origin structure as Dropbox -- Houston forgot his USB drive, these two forgot their pre-call prep. I weight this heavily because the story has the right shape: frustration, then prototype, then the prototype working well enough that they realized it could be a company. Salesgraph scores genuinely well on organic discovery.

Gong at $300M+ ARR, Clari merging with Salesloft, People.ai at $198M raised, Aligned tripling revenue in 2024 -- Salesgraph is entering one of the most crowded, well-funded segments in B2B SaaS. And here's what concerns me most: nobody hears "we aggregate sales context and generate pre-call briefs" and says "that's crazy." Everyone nods. Every sales VP immediately understands the value proposition. The competitive landscape has already validated this idea multiple times over. In my framework, that's the tell. The best startup ideas initially sound bad to smart people. Salesgraph's idea sounds good to everyone, which means the return premium has already been competed away. Gong or the Clari-Salesloft combination could ship a comparable feature in a quarter, leveraging their existing install base and data access. Salesgraph's core technology -- API aggregation across 10+ tools plus LLM-based summarization -- uses entirely commodity components. Nothing in the dossier suggests a technical moat that would survive a determined incumbent sprint.

Ricardo's 79 GitHub stars across 28 repos and Ruhan's prior project Mabbu (an auto-diagramming tool) tell me these are builders who ship things. Salesgraph has two technical co-founders with shared working history at Mintlify -- that's a functional founding team. But I'm looking for the "animal" quality, and I don't find it in this dossier. There are no stories of creative workarounds under constraint. No evidence of doing things that don't scale to get their first users. No prior exits. No moment where Salesgraph's founders did something that made me think "these people will find a way through any wall." The absence of those stories at the pre-seed stage isn't disqualifying -- my default assumption is moderate resourcefulness for YC-caliber founders -- but it means I can't distinguish Salesgraph's team from dozens of competent two-person teams I've seen with similar profiles. Mintlify tenure establishes domain exposure but not the relentless resourcefulness that would make me bet on Salesgraph against Gong's $584M war chest.

Salesgraph also cuts against my strongest unstated pattern: I back hackers building for hackers. Stripe, Replit, Retool, Wordware -- my portfolio skews dramatically toward developer tools. Salesgraph is building for sales reps, not developers. The ClassDojo exception in my portfolio happened because education represented extreme schlep blindness -- selling to school districts through bureaucratic procurement that repelled every other startup. Enterprise sales tools are the opposite of a schlep-blind market. It's where every ambitious B2B SaaS founder already looks. There's no competitive moat created by the work being too unglamorous or painful for others to attempt. Salesgraph's integration work across Salesforce, HubSpot, Gong, Clearbit, ZoomInfo, 6sense, Salesloft, and Outreach is genuinely tedious, but it's the same tedium every sales tool company already manages. That's commodity schlep, not strategic schlep.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Salesgraph's founders discovered this problem organically, built a working prototype before starting the company, and both can build the product themselves. The Mintlify prototype reportedly contributed to closing deals at higher ACV -- that's early evidence of "make something people want." Gartner projecting 95% of seller research workflows will begin with AI by 2027 suggests the timing window is real. LLMs make cross-tool context synthesis newly feasible at a quality level that prior-generation NLP couldn't achieve. And the specific wedge -- generating buyer-ready three-page business cases, not just pre-call notes -- is a concrete deliverable that neither Gong nor Clari produces today. If Salesgraph can achieve 5-7% weekly growth with mid-market dev-tools sales teams (their stated target vertical), the integration depth across 10+ tools would create genuine switching costs. The founders' shared tenure at Mintlify means they've already survived the co-founder stress test of building together under early-startup pressure. The condition that would flip me: if Salesgraph showed me three paying customers who independently said "I can't do my job without this" -- the obsessive-small-cohort signal I look for -- I'd reconsider. Obsessive usage in the crappiest version one is the proof that a consensus-sounding idea actually has a non-obvious insight underneath.

Salesgraph's decisive weakness is the intersection of two dimensions: the idea scores well with everyone who hears it (6/20 on contrarian quality), and the founders, while competent, haven't demonstrated the exceptional resourcefulness (14/30) that would make me bet on them against billion-dollar incumbents in a consensus market. The organic discovery at Mintlify is real and I respect it -- but the problem they discovered is one that Gong, Clari, and a dozen well-funded startups have also discovered. Salesgraph needs either a much scarier idea or much scarier founders. Right now it has neither.
