# Saffron -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's the tension that makes Saffron analytically interesting: the underlying shift they're building on — AI agents becoming the default way engineers write code — is genuinely consequential. That shift will restructure how software gets built, who builds it, and what "engineering skill" even means. But Saffron's response to that tectonic shift is to build a better hiring quiz. They're standing at the edge of a revolution and opening a testing center. The wave is real. The surfboard is too small.

Let me start where I always start: what happens to the world if Saffron fully succeeds? Technical hiring gets faster and cheaper. Companies save money on interviewer hours. Candidates get evaluated on skills that matter. That's... fine. HackerRank already does $221M in revenue and the world hasn't noticed. Nobody's life is fundamentally different because coding assessments improved. The consequence of Saffron's success is a profitable SaaS company in a $3.5B market — the exact outcome profile I have no interest in. I want to either lose my money or change how people live. "Better HR tooling" does neither.

The technical approach deserves honest examination. Per-line code attribution — classifying every line as human-written, AI-generated, or AI-modified — is clever and genuinely novel. The multi-agent scoring system and full session replay of AI interactions are real product innovations. Nobody else captures the granular human-AI interaction data they're collecting. But this is integration engineering, not a technology discontinuity. They're assembling existing capabilities — browser IDEs, Claude Code's API, LLM-based evaluation — into a well-designed product. That's a 2-3x improvement in assessment quality, not a 10x leap. And here's what concerns me more: their entire product depends on Anthropic's Claude Code. A single API pricing change or Anthropic deciding to build an assessment layer themselves — which would be trivially adjacent to their existing developer tools business — and Saffron's foundation shifts underneath them. Platform dependency at this level, on a company that's actively expanding into developer tooling, is a structural vulnerability I can't ignore.

The strongest bull case is the data flywheel. Every assessment captures how real engineers interact with AI agents — what they prompt, what they accept, what they reject, how they iterate. That dataset doesn't exist anywhere else at scale. If Saffron accumulates enough volume, their scoring models become meaningfully better than anything a competitor could build from scratch. Combined with $20/assessment versus $500+ in traditional process costs and zero interviewer hours versus eight-plus, there's a legitimate disruption of Karat's entire human-interviewer model. The Jane Street background of two founders gives them firsthand experience with what elite technical hiring actually requires — that's real founder-market fit. And the timing is genuinely right: AI-assisted coding went from optional to default in 2024-2025, creating a measurement gap that incumbents built around algorithmic puzzles cannot easily close. If you believe this transition period lasts a decade and Saffron captures the defining dataset for evaluating AI-augmented engineering skill, you could build a case for a significant outcome.

But I keep returning to a deeper problem that only becomes visible when you project the AI capability curve forward. The same technology that makes Saffron's product necessary — AI agents that write production code — is the technology that, at sufficient capability, eliminates the need to hire most software engineers at all. I've said publicly that 80% of jobs will be done by AI. If that's even half right for engineering specifically, the technical assessment market doesn't grow to $3.5B — it contracts to near zero. Saffron is optimizing for a transitional moment. They're building the best tool for evaluating humans in a world that may rapidly need fewer humans in the loop. The founders are smart — MIT, Stanford, Harvey Mudd, NeurIPS publications — but I see no evidence they've grappled with this paradox or positioned the company to evolve beyond it.

The team has strong credentials but no evidence of the learning velocity I prioritize over pedigree. I can see their resumes. I can't see their rate of change. What surprised them? What did they learn at YC that changed their approach? What did they try that failed? These are the signals that separate a first-principles thinker from someone executing a well-reasoned plan. The absence of that evidence, combined with the bounded consequence magnitude and incremental (not discontinuous) technical approach, puts this clearly outside my framework. Competent founders, real market timing, defensible product — and none of the asymmetric upside I need to write a check.
