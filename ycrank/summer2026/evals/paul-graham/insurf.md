# Insurf -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Insurf is a real schlep wearing a crowded coat. Prior authorization at $35B of US administrative spend (Health Affairs Scholar, 2024) is exactly the kind of unpleasant compliance grind that I'd normally lean toward -- the **Coinbase** (2012) pattern -- but the dossier on Chang Lu and Bryan Chung gives me nothing else to grab onto. No shipped artifact named. No GitHub. No prior-life anecdote. Just a Calendly link and a 90-day pilot. That mismatch -- a Coinbase-shaped problem with a dossier shaped like the AI-wrapper team from my Pass Example -- is what I have to resolve.

**Coinbase** (2012) sits at the top of my schlep ladder because Brian Armstrong wanted the regulatory ugliness everyone else was routing around, and the moat compounded year after year as the schlep deepened. Prior auth has the same compliance shape -- HIPAA business-associate paperwork, physician attestation, EHR plumbing -- and on paper Insurf is running at it. But here's where Insurf diverges from the **Coinbase** (2012) rung: when Armstrong applied, no other YC company in his batch wanted the bitcoin compliance grind. When Insurf applies, Counterforce Health, Claimable (already $10M raised, Mark Cuban-backed), Fight Health Insurance, PenguinAI, and Aegis Health are all chewing on the same bone (Stateline, Nov 2025; PYMNTS, 2026). Schlep blindness only works when other smart founders are still blind. By Summer 2026, five competitors mean the schlep has been seen. The contrarian premium is gone; what's left is execution, and execution needs founders I can see.

The AI-wrapper team from my Pass Example failed because no founder had personally suffered the problem -- a glossy deck on top of someone else's pain. Insurf's dossier reads the same way on founder-market fit: "No verifiable domain expertise found in public sources" for Chang Lu or Bryan Chung, no healthcare prior, no payer or RCM background, nothing. I asked the **Dropbox** (2007) question -- where is the lost USB stick? -- and the dossier returned silence. Maybe Chang or Bryan worked at a denial-management vendor and it's just not on LinkedIn. Maybe one of them watched a parent get denied. I can't know, but I score what's in front of me, and what's in front of me is a healthcare workflow product where neither founder's biography contains healthcare.

Animal resourcefulness over 18 months compounds when the founder ships something before the check, then ships something weirder after. Drew Houston's Hacker News demo for **Dropbox** (2007) became the door-to-door house-call energy that powered the first 1,000 users. Insurf's pre-funding artifact, as disclosed, is a website and a Calendly funnel pointing at calendly.com/chang-insurf/30min. That's not animal evidence; that's table stakes. Animals leave tracks -- a stunt, a screenshot, a thread, a manually-onboarded specialty clinic with a name attached. The dossier has none. Absence is itself the signal.

For hacker genealogy I'll use the counterfactual: what would have to be true for Insurf to clear the **Stripe** (2010) bar on this axis? Patrick Collison's Lisp prize wasn't decorative -- it predicted that the commerce-API would be built with the kind of taste that produces a developer-default. For Insurf to flip from 3/15 to a tier higher, I'd need to see one of: an open-source EHR parser shipped by Chang or Bryan, a competitive-programming history, a non-trivial side project on GitHub, a Hacker News post about insurance-claim parsing that predates the company. Insurf's dossier explicitly returned "No GitHub repos found" for both founders. The counterfactual is unmet, and the absent-evidence default actually overstates what I see.

On co-founder configuration, the single-anchor parallel is **Reddit** (2005): Huffman and Ohanian were dorm roommates with years of shared shipping before YC. Insurf has the right shape -- a pair, not a solo -- but "No public data on co-founder history found" is the entire entry. I'll stay near default; YC's in-person interview surfaces this and I can't from a dossier.

The 36-month forecast: Insurf either acquihires into a mid-tier RCM vendor (Waystar or similar) below $20M, or it dies quietly as Claimable's $10M warchest and Counterforce's free tier compress what specialty practices are willing to pay for AI-drafted appeals. The mechanism is the signature pairing: real schlep, invisible founders. The schlep was supposed to be the moat; with five competitors it becomes just hard work, and hard work without an animal in the driver's seat gets out-executed by whichever entrant has the more obsessive founder.

The strongest bull case is **Tennr** -- adjacent referral automation, $605M valuation in Jun 2025 (Fortune), and a reminder that healthcare-document AI is real money. If Insurf's coverage-decision graph compounds even half as well as Tennr's referral parser, the prior-auth wedge into Surely for plan pricing is genuinely "frighteningly ambitious" (March 2012) -- a two-sided dataset flywheel I should want. The bear answer is that Tennr's founders had a shipped artifact and a story before the round; Insurf's dossier has neither, and "coverage-decision graph" is currently a phrase on a homepage, not a measured asset. The verdict flips if Chang or Bryan publishes a concrete pilot result -- e.g., one named specialty practice, an appeal-reversal rate above Counterforce's 70%, or a HIPAA BAA posture I can verify. Until then, pass.
