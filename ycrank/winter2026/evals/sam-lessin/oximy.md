# Oximy -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Oximy is the structural irony at its core: it's a pure software business whose entire existence depends on AI proliferation, built in a category where every incumbent with endpoint distribution can replicate the product as a feature. The "sensor" component -- the technical foundation of the whole platform -- is a fork of mitmproxy. Not a novel protocol. Not a new primitive. A wrapper around an open-source network proxy with an AI tool taxonomy bolted on top. When I see that architecture and then hear "system of record for enterprise AI usage," I hear a company describing a feature as if it were infrastructure. CrowdStrike's Falcon platform already monitors every network connection from enterprise endpoints. Adding a categorization layer that flags AI tool domains is a product sprint, not a competitive moat. Zscaler, Netskope, SentinelOne -- they all have the same structural advantage. They already own the endpoint relationship. This is the Birchbox problem wearing enterprise drag: narrative magnitude without structural defensibility.

Naman Ambavi is clearly a high-velocity builder. Google Code-In finalist at what I'd guess was around age 17, founded a healthtech company at 19, built a COVID tracker that reached 10 million users in four months, then joined Induced AI as a founding member. A repeat investor followed him from Easocare to Oximy specifically citing his execution speed. That's credible. But credible execution doesn't satisfy my framework alone -- I need the idea to be genuinely non-consensus, something that provokes confusion or skepticism from other investors. Enterprise AI governance is the exact opposite of that. Every CISO in every enterprise is having this conversation right now. Nudge Security raised $22.5 million in November 2025 for an overlapping thesis with 200 customers and 3x ARR growth. This is a "sane person, sane idea" -- and in my framework, that's actually worse than a sane person with an insane idea, because the returns are already being arbitraged by the market. Every VC in the room wants AI governance plays. That consensus excitement is a signal I should stay away.

The "would this business exist without AI" test kills it for me. My entire screening mechanism for AI-era companies is whether AI is the cherry on top of an already-good business, or whether AI IS the business. Oximy fails this test categorically -- without enterprise AI adoption, there is no product, no market, no company. That's fine for some investors, but it's the opposite of what I look for. I want businesses where AI makes operations cheaper or better but doesn't constitute the entire value proposition. A painting company with scheduling software gets stronger as AI improves dispatch optimization. A parking infrastructure company with checkout-free tech benefits from better computer vision. Oximy has no business without the AI wave, which means it's fully exposed to whatever the AI landscape does next -- including the scenario where the major platform vendors (Microsoft, Google, Apple) build governance tooling directly into their enterprise AI products, which they absolutely will.

The strongest bull case is regulatory compulsion. If the EU AI Act and similar frameworks create mandatory compliance requirements for AI tool inventory and data flow documentation, the category becomes non-discretionary enterprise spend. In that world, the first company with SOC 2 certification, on-premises deployment, and coverage of 3,500+ tools could lock up early enterprise accounts before incumbents prioritize the feature. The "processing millions of requests daily for enterprises in financial services and healthcare" claim, if real, suggests meaningful early deployment in exactly the regulated verticals where compliance mandates bite hardest. If Ambavi can convert regulatory urgency into long-term contracts with switching costs baked in through MDM deployment across thousands of endpoints, the data on AI usage patterns across those enterprises could compound into something genuinely proprietary. That's a real scenario. But it requires winning a race against Nudge Security (better funded, more customers, growing 3x annually) AND outrunning the feature roadmaps of endpoint security giants who already own the customer relationship and the endpoint agent. That's a lot of "AND" for a solo founder with a 5-person team at pre-seed.

The protocol-layer test is definitive here. Oximy's GitHub describes "open standards and system-boundary tooling" -- language that gestures toward a primitive. But the reality is a monitoring application built on someone else's open-source proxy, shipping a proprietary dashboard. There's no new financial primitive, no decentralized network, no protocol that other businesses build on. It's a SaaS application in the most traditional sense: deploy our agent, view our dashboard, pay our subscription. When I backed Venmo, I saw a new primitive for how money moves between people. When I backed Aleo, I saw privacy as infrastructure that every application would need. I don't see a new primitive here. I see a well-timed feature in an overheated market -- exactly the kind of bet I've trained myself to avoid.
