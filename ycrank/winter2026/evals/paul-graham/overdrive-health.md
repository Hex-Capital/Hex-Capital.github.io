# Overdrive Health -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Overdrive Health isn't the AI or the billing market -- it's that they bought an ambulance billing agency. Most pre-seed founders build a product and go looking for customers. These founders went and acquired the customers first, then started deploying AI into the operation. That's a genuinely unusual move. It reminds me of how DoorDash's Tony Xu started by personally delivering food -- except instead of doing the work themselves, these founders bought an entire company already doing the work, then set about automating it. The result is that at pre-seed, they have ~20 providers and $50M+ in annual claims flowing through their systems. That's rare. It also means they're not building AI in a vacuum -- they're building it while operating the actual workflow, which forces the kind of honest feedback loop that pure SaaS companies never get.

But when I ask the question I always ask first -- how did they find this problem? -- the answer gives me pause. Both founders built AI agents at EliseAI, watched that company scale past $100M ARR, and then went looking for a new vertical to apply the same playbook. That's not the same as a doctor who's been fighting insurance companies for fifteen years and finally snaps. It's not Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. It's more like: "We know how to build AI agents. Medical billing is a large, painful, automatable market. Let's go." The domain expertise is real -- EliseAI expanded into healthcare, so they weren't starting cold. But the idea emerged from capability looking for application, not from someone who couldn't stand the status quo. That's a weaker foundation than I'd like.

The schlep, though, is enormous. Medical billing is one of those problems that makes smart people's eyes glaze over. HIPAA compliance, CMS regulations, insurance company phone trees, denial management, fax machines that still exist in 2026 because healthcare moves at the speed of regulation. Ambulance billing is the ugliest corner of an already ugly space -- even routine claims require hours of manual follow-up. This is classic schlep-blindness territory. Nobody fantasizes about fixing ambulance billing. The founders chose this deliberately, and the acquire-and-operate model means they're neck-deep in the operational muck, not building a dashboard for someone else to deal with it. That willingness to run the actual billing operation -- to own the outcome, not just sell tools -- is the kind of thing that deters most competitors and most founders.

What concerns me is the competitive crowding. At least three other YC companies -- Amby Health, CareSwift, LunaBill -- are attacking adjacent parts of the same problem. When YC funds multiple companies in the same narrow vertical across consecutive batches, it usually means the category has stopped being invisible and started being consensus. Schlep blindness works as a moat only when other people are still blind. The moment LLMs made it feasible to automate insurance phone calls, a dozen teams noticed the opportunity simultaneously. That doesn't invalidate the opportunity, but it significantly reduces the structural advantage that comes from being the only one crazy enough to try.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. If these founders are right that full-stack operation beats SaaS tools in medical billing -- if owning the workflow end-to-end creates a data and execution advantage that copilot-style competitors can't match -- then the acquire-and-automate model is brilliant. Revenue per FTE "nearly doubling" after deploying AI is a strong operational signal. The ambulance niche is deliberately narrow, which is how the best companies start. They could acquire more agencies, automate each one, and expand into other specialties. The EliseAI playbook gives them pattern recognition that first-time founders in healthcare wouldn't have. If the full-stack model works and they can replicate it, this could become a very large business. Both founders are clearly technical -- Harvard CS and Jane Street for Daniel, engineering lead roles for Michael -- even if I can't see their code. The pieces are there.

But I keep coming back to the origin. The strongest companies I've funded were built by people who were solving their own problem. These founders are solving someone else's problem, well, using tools they happen to be good at building. That's not fatal -- plenty of great companies are built by outsiders with transferable skills. But it means the motivation has to sustain through years of dealing with ambulance billing, which is a different kind of grind than building developer tools or consumer apps. The operational model also worries me structurally: running a billing agency with human staff is a fundamentally different challenge than building software. The founders need to be managers and makers simultaneously, which is the tension I've warned about. At pre-seed, they need to be writing code, not managing billing specialists.
