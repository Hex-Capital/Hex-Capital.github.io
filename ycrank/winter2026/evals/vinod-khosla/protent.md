# Protent -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Protent is that two founders who appear to be in their early twenties have somehow secured deployments with police departments in Atlanta, Chicago, and St. Louis. Government procurement is the hardest sales cycle in technology — twelve-to-twenty-four month RFP processes, CJIS compliance, budget committee approvals — and these two have cracked it at pre-seed with $500K. That's a genuine signal. It tells me something about founder hustle that the dossier's LinkedIn metrics and follower counts don't capture. But hustle alone doesn't answer my first question: if Protent succeeds completely — every police department in America running their platform — does the world change in a way that justifies the attempt?

The honest answer is: not at the scale I look for. The problem is real — fewer than 1 in 200 incidents caught in real time is a damning statistic. But Protent's solution is a better intelligence layer on top of existing surveillance infrastructure. The cameras stay. The dispatch systems stay. The policing model stays. Protent makes it all more effective, perhaps dramatically so. That's a real business — the AI video surveillance market is heading toward $12-17 billion by 2030 — but it's not a paradigm shift. Compare this to what excites me: technologies that make the incumbent approach *economically unviable*, not technologies that make the incumbent approach work better. When Juniper built TCP/IP routers, Cisco's entire legacy architecture became obsolete. When Square built mobile payments, traditional POS terminals became artifacts. Protent makes existing surveillance networks smarter, but it doesn't replace them. The consequence of full success is a more efficient police force, not a fundamentally different approach to public safety. That's the gap.

The technology itself concerns me for a different reason. Computer vision, NLP, and sentiment analysis are increasingly commoditized — the dossier acknowledges this directly. Lumana AI has raised $64 million and deploys vision-language models with a positioning that overlaps significantly with Protent's. Verkada is doing $1 billion in annualized bookings and has the resources to build any feature Protent ships. When your differentiation rests on "specificity of escalation-pattern models trained on law enforcement scenarios" and "government relationships," you're in a feature war, not a technology war. A feature can be replicated by a well-funded competitor in a quarter. I've learned this lesson painfully — technology that achieves novelty without structural economic advantage is vulnerable. The question isn't whether Protent's escalation detection works; it's whether it works in a way that a $700 million-funded Verkada can't replicate once they decide to pursue the public safety vertical.

The bull case deserves honest engagement. If Protent's early deployments generate a data flywheel — real escalation patterns from real law enforcement scenarios — that flywheel could compound into a defensible advantage that later entrants can't match. First-mover data in government is genuinely sticky. The founders' technical composition is complementary: Srihan's Lockheed Martin reinforcement-learning video intelligence maps directly to the core product, and Abhisheik's NLP research maps to the sentiment-analysis layer. If they can recruit a government sales leader who knows how to navigate federal procurement and expand from municipal police to DHS, FBI, and DOD contracts, the SAM expands meaningfully. And the timing is legitimately favorable — vision-language models just crossed the real-time multi-feed threshold, and bodycam mandates have created an ocean of unmonitored video. There's a version of this story where Protent becomes the Palantir of video intelligence for law enforcement. That's a large outcome.

But that version requires navigating a political minefield that gets more treacherous as you succeed. Predictive surveillance by law enforcement isn't a technology problem the experts dismiss as impossible — it's an application that a growing segment of the public believes *shouldn't exist*. San Francisco, Boston, and Portland have enacted restrictions on AI surveillance. The YC LinkedIn post generated substantial critical commentary about dystopian surveillance. This is fundamentally different from the contrarian signal I look for. When Cisco's CTO told me TCP/IP routing was technically impossible, that was an expert being wrong about technology. When privacy advocates say predictive policing shouldn't be deployed, they're raising ethical objections that could translate into regulation. The more successful Protent becomes, the louder the backlash grows. That's a ceiling on consequence magnitude that doesn't exist for my best investments.

The founders are technically competent insiders executing an understood approach — Lockheed Martin defense intelligence applied to law enforcement. That's the opposite of the outsider-with-contrarian-naivety profile that has produced my best returns. They're not attempting something the consensus considers crazy; they're building an obvious application of mature technology for an obvious customer. The risk they've reduced — government sales at pre-seed — is impressive operationally, but it means they're optimizing for probability of success in a defined market rather than pursuing a consequence of success that would terrify a more cautious team. I respect the execution. I just don't see the magnitude.
