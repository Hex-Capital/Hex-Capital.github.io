## The Idea

**Problem:** Small, low-cost drones have become a serious security threat to airports, critical infrastructure, and front-line defense. Even $50 drones now present major risks. Existing detection systems were not designed for small, low-radar-cross-section targets and suffer from high false-positive rates while missing real threats. The 2018 Gatwick Airport drone incursion cost stakeholders over £50M (Converge Challenge profile, May 2024). Ukraine experienced more than 1,600 drone attacks per month in 2024 (Converge Challenge profile, May 2024). Drone usage in the Red Sea has doubled shipping costs globally since November 2023 (Converge Challenge profile, May 2024).

**Approach:** Milliray builds high-frequency millimeter-wave radar systems designed from the ground up to detect low-signature targets, including nano drones. The radar exploits micro-Doppler signatures — the unique radar returns generated by rotating propeller blades — to differentiate drones from confusing objects like birds and to distinguish between drone models (IET Radar, Sonar & Navigation, 2024). The radar is integrated with camera and acoustic sensor arrays to provide a multi-sensor detection, tracking, and identification capability (YC launch post). The company's tagline is "Detect. Track. Identify. Integrate." (dronetector.com).

**Differentiation:** Most existing counter-UAS radar systems operate at lower frequencies (e.g., X-band) that struggle with very small targets. Milliray's 94 GHz millimeter-wave radar operates at a much higher frequency, yielding finer resolution on small objects (ResearchGate publications, Matthew Moore). Competitors like Blighter use solid-state e-scan radar optimized for micro-Doppler but at lower frequencies; Dedrone (now Axon) relies primarily on RF signal detection rather than radar; DroneShield uses a multi-sensor approach but does not specialize in millimeter-wave radar. Milliray's purpose-built high-frequency approach is designed to detect targets that evade these conventional systems.

**Business Model:** No pricing page is publicly available (dronetector.com). [Inferred]: Most likely monetization path is hardware unit sales of radar systems plus potential recurring revenue from software/integration services, following the industry model of companies like DroneShield, which is transitioning to a 30% SaaS revenue mix. Defense and critical infrastructure customers typically procure through multi-year contracts with maintenance and support components.

**TAM/SAM:** The global anti-drone market was valued at USD 3.11 billion in 2025, projected to reach USD 16.45 billion by 2034 at a CAGR of 19.79% (Fortune Business Insights, 2026). Markets and Markets estimates USD 4.48 billion in 2025, growing to USD 14.51 billion by 2030 (Markets and Markets, 2025). The counter-UAV market overall is projected to expand by over $30 billion between 2025–2035 (GlobeNewsWire / Research and Markets, Feb 2026). DroneShield estimates the total addressable market at US$63 billion, with 44% (US$28.2 billion) in civilian applications (DroneShield investor materials via search snippet). [Inferred]: The serviceable addressable market for radar-based detection specifically (excluding kinetic defeat, electronic warfare, and directed energy segments) is a subset, likely in the single-digit billions.

**GTM / Distribution:** The company has secured backing from the UK Ministry of Defence's Defence and Security Accelerator (DASA) and NATO DIANA (YC page), both of which serve as validation and potential procurement channels. The product is described as "deployment-ready" (dronetector.com). [Inferred]: Most likely initial distribution path is direct sales to defense ministries and critical infrastructure operators in NATO-aligned countries, leveraging the DASA and NATO DIANA relationships as beachheads. The UK and broader European defense market is the likely starting point given the team's UK origins and institutional relationships.

## Defensibility

All three founders hold PhDs in directly relevant technical domains — millimeter-wave radar physics, laser/optical physics, and automated reasoning (YC page). The CEO's published research on 94 GHz drone micro-Doppler simulation and detection represents specialized domain knowledge (IET Radar, Sonar & Navigation, 2024; ResearchGate). The company is a spin-out of the University of St Andrews Millimetre Wave & EPR Group (St Andrews Research Portal), which provides access to specialized research infrastructure.

NATO DIANA selection and DASA backing provide institutional validation that can create switching costs in defense procurement pipelines, where vendor qualification is a significant barrier. The company is described as a "Scottish deep-tech spin-off" positioned as a sovereign European radar technology provider (Defence Finance Monitor, LinkedIn post).

**Market structure:** Defense procurement creates structural barriers for incumbents like Raytheon and Northrop Grumman to address the small-drone detection niche. [Inferred]: Large defense primes optimize for high-value platform programs (aircraft, missiles); building a specialized sub-$100K radar for nano-drone detection is economically unattractive relative to their cost structures and sales cycles. Additionally, European defense customers increasingly seek sovereign technology providers to reduce dependence on US systems, a structural advantage for a UK-based company with NATO DIANA backing.

**Commoditization risk:** Millimeter-wave radar hardware at 94 GHz requires specialized RF engineering expertise, but the underlying physics is well-understood. Companies like Blighter (UK), Robin Radar (Netherlands), and larger defense primes could develop competing millimeter-wave products. The primary barriers are the combination of hardware design, signal processing algorithms for micro-Doppler classification, and the cost/time of building and validating a new radar system from scratch. Robin Radar has already demonstrated drone detection at 12 km with software upgrades (Defense News, Sep 2025), though at different frequency bands.

**Competitive landscape:**

1. **DroneShield (ASX:DRO)** — AI-powered multi-sensor counter-drone platform. FY2025 revenue AUD 216.55M, up 276% YoY; first profitable year at AUD 3.52M net profit (Investing.com, Feb 2026). Pipeline of A$2.3B (DroneShield FY2025 slides). Publicly listed. Broad product suite including detection, tracking, and defeat. *Differentiator vs. Milliray:* DroneShield offers an integrated detect-and-defeat platform at scale; Milliray focuses specifically on high-resolution millimeter-wave radar for the hardest detection targets.

2. **Dedrone (acquired by Axon, May 2024)** — Software-centric RF and multi-sensor drone detection. Had raised $127M total before acquisition (CNBC, May 2024). Now part of Axon ($2.1B revenue in 2024; Axon investor relations). *Differentiator vs. Milliray:* Dedrone emphasizes RF signal detection and software analytics; Milliray builds purpose-designed radar hardware for low-RCS targets that RF detection may miss (e.g., autonomous drones not emitting RF).

3. **Robin Radar Systems (Netherlands)** — Bird and drone detection radar. PE backing from Parcom (Oct 2024); 100 IRIS radars contracted by Dutch MoD; 200+ radars deployed in Ukraine (Defense News, Sep 2025). Revenue undisclosed. *Differentiator vs. Milliray:* Robin Radar operates at different frequency bands and originated in bird detection; Milliray's millimeter-wave approach targets the smallest nano drones at higher resolution.

4. **Blighter Surveillance Systems (UK)** — Solid-state e-scan ground radar. Deployed in 35 countries including UK MoD in Afghanistan and US DoD in Iraq (Blighter website). Funded via loan from BOOST&Co (2019); revenue undisclosed. *Differentiator vs. Milliray:* Blighter's A400 radars detect nano drones at up to 3.6 km; Milliray's millimeter-wave radar claims to detect targets that evade even these systems.

5. **Fortem Technologies (US)** — Radar-based detection plus TrueView intercept drones. Raised $17.8M from investors including Lockheed Martin Ventures; ~$30M predicted revenue (PR Newswire, Jan 2025). Cleared for direct US Army sales (The Defense Post, Nov 2025). *Differentiator vs. Milliray:* Fortem combines detection with kinetic defeat (interceptor drones); Milliray focuses on detection/tracking only.

**Why now:** [Inferred]: Several converging factors opened this opportunity in 2024–2025: (1) The Ukraine conflict demonstrated mass drone warfare at unprecedented scale, with 1,600+ monthly drone attacks creating urgent demand for detection capabilities. (2) Over $1 billion in counter-drone purchases closed in December 2025 alone (Defense Security Monitor, Jan 2026), indicating defense budgets are now allocating significantly to this domain. (3) High-profile incidents at civilian airports and critical infrastructure (Gatwick 2018, ongoing Red Sea drone attacks) pushed detection from a military-only concern to a dual-use market. (4) NATO DIANA's launch in 2023 created a new institutional pathway for European deep-tech defense startups. (5) Miniaturization of drones down to nano-scale has outpaced existing detection technology, creating a specific gap for high-frequency radar.

## Key Risks

**Dual-use procurement cycle risk:** Defense sales cycles are typically 12–24+ months from initial engagement to contract award. Milliray's primary institutional relationships (DASA, NATO DIANA) are validation programs, not guaranteed procurement contracts. Converting these into revenue-generating orders requires navigating complex defense acquisition processes, which is particularly challenging for a 3-person team without dedicated business development resources.

**CTO attention fragmentation:** Thomas Doherty is listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix (LinkedIn), an ion trap quantum computing venture, alongside his CTO role at DroneTector/Milliray. Running two deep-tech companies simultaneously poses execution risk for a 3-person startup where each founder's full bandwidth is critical.

**Incumbent convergence risk:** Robin Radar already extended its IRIS radar detection range from 5 km to 12 km via software upgrade (Defense News, Sep 2025) and has 200+ units deployed in Ukraine. DroneShield achieved AUD 216.55M revenue in FY2025 with 276% growth (Investing.com). Well-funded and operationally proven competitors could add millimeter-wave capabilities to existing platforms through acquisition or R&D, compressing Milliray's differentiation window.

**Hardware scaling risk:** Millimeter-wave radar at 94 GHz requires specialized RF components and manufacturing processes. Scaling from research prototypes to production-grade units at competitive unit economics is a known challenge in mmWave hardware. The company describes itself as "deployment-ready" but has no publicly disclosed production customers or manufacturing partners.

**Brand fragmentation:** The company operates under two names — Milliray (YC page, milliray.com) and DroneTector (operating website dronetector.com, UK Companies House registration, X/Twitter handle, email addresses). The milliray.com domain redirects to dronetector.com. This dual-branding may create confusion in early-stage customer engagement and SEO.
