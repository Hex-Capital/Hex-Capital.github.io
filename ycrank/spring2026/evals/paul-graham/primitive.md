# primitive -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that I already wrote a check to AgentMail. Same problem space -- email infrastructure for AI agents -- but AgentMail has $6M from General Catalyst, 500+ B2B customers, and a nine-month head start. So the question isn't whether agent communication infrastructure matters. It clearly does. The question is whether Primitive has found something AgentMail missed, and whether Ethan Byrd is the person to exploit that gap. The dossier doesn't give me a convincing answer to either question.

The origin story is the part that should be strongest here and isn't. Byrd built Actual AI -- agents for engineering managers, $3.2M raised, 32 active pilots. That's exactly the kind of background where you'd organically discover that email infrastructure is broken for agents. If he spent two years wrestling with Gmail APIs and SendGrid webhooks trying to make his agents communicate, and finally said "this is insane, I'll just build the infrastructure myself," that would be a genuine schlep-driven discovery. The problem is I can't confirm any of that. The relationship between Actual AI and Primitive is "not publicly documented." His LinkedIn still says Founder & CTO at Actual AI. Did Actual AI fail? Did he pivot? Did he hand it off? The fact that he had a co-founder at Actual AI and is now solo raises more questions than it answers. There's a plausible organic origin story here, but plausible isn't the same as demonstrated.

And even if the origin is organic, I'm not seeing the schlep. This is where Primitive loses me. Stripe worked because the schlep was enormous -- banking relationships, PCI compliance, fraud detection, money transmission licenses across fifty states. The regulatory and institutional complexity was so painful that thousands of programmers who knew payments were broken still wouldn't touch it. Email-to-webhook routing is... not that. It's a clean engineering problem. Mailgun and SendGrid have offered inbound parse webhooks for years. The "email as an API primitive" insight is a reframing of existing capability, not a navigation of painful complexity that deters competition. When the schlep is low, the barrier is low, and that's exactly what we see: AgentMail, Composio, Infraforge, and the looming possibility of AWS or Google just adding an "agent mode" toggle to their existing email services. The webhook-first architecture might be architecturally cleaner than AgentMail's mailbox-centric approach, but architectural elegance is a feature, not a moat.

The strongest bull case would require two things to be true simultaneously. First, that the agent communication layer is winner-take-most -- that network effects emerge as agents from different platforms need to talk to each other, and whoever becomes the default routing layer captures disproportionate value. Second, that Primitive's webhook-first design is fundamentally better suited to agent-native communication than mailbox-based approaches, and this becomes obvious to developers once they try both. If both are true, the 500+ customers AgentMail has today are building on the wrong abstraction, and Primitive could leapfrog them the way Stripe leapfrogged PayPal's merchant integration model. The timing supports this -- Gartner's projection of 40% agent penetration by 2026 means the real volume hasn't arrived yet, and being right about the architecture matters more than being first when the market is about to expand by an order of magnitude. But "could leapfrog" requires evidence I don't have: a working product, developers choosing it over AgentMail, or at minimum a technical prototype that demonstrates why webhooks-first is categorically better.

Byrd's technical credentials are real. Google, Meta, AWS, Microsoft -- he's seen infrastructure at scale. But his GitHub tells a different story: 15 repos, mostly forks, 12 followers. Where is the code? For a solo technical founder building developer infrastructure, the prototype should be the pitch. Drew Houston had a working demo. The Collison brothers had a working API. Byrd has job postings for founding engineers at $220K-$320K. That salary range at pre-seed, before any visible product or revenue, doesn't signal the lean, ramen-profitable mentality I look for. It signals someone planning to build a team before building the thing. At this stage, the founder should be the team.

I respect the ambition of going after infrastructure that every AI agent will need. And the timing tailwind is genuine -- agent proliferation is creating real demand for communication primitives. But a real market with low schlep and a funded competitor that's already winning is not where I put my own money. If Byrd had shown me a working product that developers were already choosing over AgentMail, or if he could articulate a specific technical insight from his Actual AI experience that makes Primitive's architecture categorically superior, this would be a different conversation. Right now I see a plausible idea in a hot category without the organic backstory, the schlep barrier, or the early user pull that would make me write a check.
