# Silmaril -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Five AI security startups acquired for a combined $1.4B+ in 2024–2025 — Protect AI by Palo Alto Networks for $500M, Robust Intelligence by Cisco for $400M, Prompt Security by SentinelOne for $180M, Lakera by Check Point, CalypsoAI by F5 for $180M — and Silmaril walks into a market where every independent competitor just got absorbed into a platform security suite. This is the most structurally interesting thing about Silmaril: the exits already happened. The incumbents validated demand, then cleared the field. When I built IronPort's email security technology, the same dynamic was playing out — Cisco eventually bought us for $830M because defending communication infrastructure was too important to leave to startups, but before that acquisition there was a window where independent companies could build something with real conviction. Silmaril is sitting in that window for AI agent security. The question is whether they're building infrastructure that compounds, or a feature that gets bundled.

Aum Upadhyay built the security and privacy framework at AWS that "prevented over $1.8B in damages." Eduardo Velasco was an Amazon tech lead shipping low-latency ML models generating $400M in annual revenue. Together they claim "$2.2B in combined impact" from their prior work. These are strong credentials for the problem. But when I ask my opening question — tell me your story, not the business — I hear Amazon. I hear credentials. I don't hear what happened to these founders that made Silmaril inevitable. What personal encounter with AI vulnerability drove them to leave Amazon and bet their careers on this? The dossier is silent. Maybe that story exists and just hasn't been told publicly yet. At pre-seed, many founders haven't. But the absence matters in my framework. The founders who built Crusoe Energy weren't energy consultants who saw a market gap — they had stood in North Dakota oil fields watching fire get thrown away. Silmaril's founders built security systems at Amazon, which is relevant and impressive, but relevance is not forge. The clear CEO/CTO delineation (Aum as CEO, Eduardo as CTO) avoids the co-founder red flag that destroyed HQ Trivia, and their shared Amazon tenure suggests a working relationship stress-tested in production environments.

Silmaril's self-healing mechanism — autonomous agents that continuously red-team customer applications, generate synthetic attack data via "simulations and nested RL environments," and retrain a compact classifier to block novel patterns "in under an hour" — is technically distinct from the static guardrails it replaces. Silmaril claims 99% detection versus roughly 50% for "leading guardrails," with 20ms latency overhead described as 10x lower than current SOTA. Security professionals in the YC LinkedIn comments questioned "validation methodology and deterministic reproducibility in an adaptive system," and those concerns are legitimate — the benchmarks lack independent verification. But the architectural insight is sound: asking "does this execution lead to a harmful outcome?" instead of "does this input look malicious?" is the right frame for multi-agent workflows with tool access, MCP connectors, and confused-deputy attack patterns. Silmaril's data flywheel — each deployment generating proprietary attack intelligence that trains the classifier — mirrors the compounding moat I backed at Flock Safety, where every camera installed improved the network for all customers. The difference: Flock Safety was operating in surveillance tech that made civil liberties advocates nervous. Silmaril is operating in AI security, which makes VCs reach for their checkbooks.

That's the gap in my thesis. Silmaril is not a contrarian bet. AI security is the definition of consensus-attractive in 2026. Straiker AI raised a $21M seed from Lightspeed and Bain Capital Ventures. Every cybersecurity platform is acquiring in this space. When I wrote my SpaceX check, rockets were blowing up and investors were running. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically radioactive. When I championed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically recoil. Silmaril makes nobody uncomfortable. It's solving a real problem that every enterprise AI team already knows they have. That's not disqualifying — not every investment needs to be contrarian — but it means Silmaril doesn't activate the part of my pattern recognition that fires when I see a founder operating in a space others refuse to enter.

Silmaril also lacks the "invisible system made visible" signature that runs through my strongest bets — Uber attacking the taxi medallion cartel, Affirm attacking credit card opacity, Ownwell attacking property tax opacity. Silmaril defends AI applications from prompt injection, which is valuable infrastructure, but it doesn't expose a hidden, extractive structure that exploits information asymmetry against ordinary people. The customers are enterprises building AI agents, not vulnerable individuals navigating opaque systems. There's a second-order case — securing AI agents protects the end users who interact with them, and the customer testimonial citing "8 breaches prevented, $28M in damages blocked" in two months suggests real harm avoidance — but the company's relationship to structural transparency is indirect.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Silmaril is infrastructure at an inflection point, which is my highest-conviction unstated pattern. Agentic AI adoption is exploding. The attack surface is growing faster than defenses. Five competitors got acquired and absorbed into slow-moving platform companies that will deprioritize innovation post-integration. Silmaril's RL-based self-healing approach is genuinely novel — not a wrapper, not an incremental improvement, but an architectural rethinking of how to defend AI systems. The founders have precisely the right credentials. The IronPort-to-Silmaril thread connects to a domain I've followed for two decades. Garry Tan personally shared Silmaril on LinkedIn. All true. But credentials without biographical forge, infrastructure in a consensus-attractive space, and B2B security without a hidden system to disrupt — these are the gaps that keep Silmaril in neutral territory for me. The one condition that would flip me: if I sat with Aum and Eduardo and heard a personal story that explained why AI security isn't their career but their mission — something that happened to them, not something they observed at Amazon — I'd reassess everything. Biographical inevitability changes the math, because founders who MUST solve their problem are the ones who survive the near-death moments that this market will inevitably deliver when Palo Alto Networks and Cisco start bundling their acquired AI security capabilities against standalone point solutions.

Silmaril scores strongest on infrastructure defensibility and market timing — the data flywheel from autonomous red-teaming, the 20ms latency moat, and the post-acquisition market vacuum are real structural advantages at a genuine inflection point. Silmaril scores weakest on founder biographical forge and contrarian conviction — the two dimensions I weight most heavily. A competent team building important infrastructure in a hot market is a good company. It's not a Long Journey bet.
