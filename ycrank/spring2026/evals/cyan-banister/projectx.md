# ProjectX -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Two of ProjectX's three original co-founders — Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das, listed on Crunchbase as co-founding the company in 2022 — are no longer on the YC page, replaced by Bishal Karmakar and Sourya Majumder before the Spring 2026 batch. I've been burned by this exact pattern. HQ Trivia was the right product at the right time, and it was destroyed entirely by founder conflict. When I see pre-seed co-founder turnover, the first thing I want to know is: what happened, and is the equity clean? The dossier offers no explanation — just the fact of the change. Rounak Adhikary, born in 2002 in Kalyani, West Bengal, is clearly the surviving founder who has been building ProjectX since age 20. That continuity counts for something. But the structural risk of early team fractures is real, and at this stage there's no way to evaluate it from the outside.

Rounak Adhikary bootstrapped early ProjectX development by founding Woldix, a web development agency, at age 19 and channeling the revenue into R&D. That's resourcefulness I recognize — I know what it looks like when you fund your survival and your vision from the same hustle. The Tiger Launch win at Princeton and the WTC Innovation Award at IIT Bombay among 17,000 startups show competitive validation. But when I ask my first question — "tell me your story, not the business" — what I hear back from the dossier is: a technically ambitious young person in West Bengal saw that cloud desktops were limited to single-session, single-OS paradigms and decided to build something better. That's a market observation, not a biographical forge. I don't see the moment where the problem of computing access found Rounak in a way that made solving it inevitable. Maybe it's there — maybe growing up in Kalyani with limited hardware access is the crucible, and the $12K in Indian government grants and the $204K Google Cloud seed reflect a founder who has been scrapping for every resource because they lived the compute poverty they're trying to solve. But the dossier doesn't surface that narrative, and I've learned that when founders don't lead with their personal stakes, either the story isn't there or they haven't found it yet.

ProjectX is building a cloud OS where every application runs in its own isolated virtual computer with dedicated GPU, supporting Windows and Linux in the same browser session. That's genuinely ambitious systems engineering — Bishal Karmakar's Golang, Rust, C++, and Kubernetes depth on GitHub suggests the technical chops are real. But when I run my invisible system test, I come up empty. Cloud computing is not an opaque, extractive structure. AWS, GCP, and Azure publish their pricing. Citrix and VMware are well-understood enterprise products. There's no hidden tax being exposed, no information asymmetry being disrupted, no gatekeepers hoarding access that ProjectX reveals. The value proposition is "your computer should do more things at once" — that's a UX improvement on a transparent market, not a structural revelation. Uber attacked the taxi medallion cartel. Affirm attacked credit card opacity. ProjectX attacks... the single-cursor paradigm. I feel the difference in my body.

ProjectX operates in what might be the most consensus-attractive space in tech right now: cloud infrastructure with AI agent workflows. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "cloud desktop with GPU acceleration for AI agents." Neverinstall raised $1.21M, Vagon raised $542K, Workspot raised $84.65M — this space is well-populated and well-funded, with Microsoft Azure Virtual Desktop and Amazon WorkSpaces as incumbents who could add per-app isolation features without rebuilding their stacks from scratch. The "why now" argument — GPU costs dropping, AI agent workflows emerging, Kubernetes GPU support maturing — applies equally to every competitor. When 300 investors passed on Uber, that was signal; when ProjectX's space is attracting capital from every direction, that's consensus. I look for the magically weird, the idea that provokes discomfort in a room of sophisticated VCs. ProjectX provokes nodding.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: ProjectX is building genuine infrastructure at the intersection of cloud computing and AI agent workflows — per-app GPU isolation with sub-second cold starts is a technically differentiated architecture that incumbents would need to re-architect their desktop streaming stacks to match. Rounak has been building for four years with minimal resources, self-funding through a services business, winning competitive grants from Google Cloud ($204K), Microsoft, and AWS (over $60K combined). The DaaS market is growing at 11-19% CAGR toward $174 billion by 2034. The AI agent co-working angle — human and AI operating side by side in the same session — could be a timing wedge that creates a new category before incumbents react. YC acceptance by partner Andrew Miklas validates the technical thesis. A defender would point to RTFKT as precedent: a narrow timing window in a consensus-adjacent space can still produce outsized returns if the category boundary is genuinely novel. But RTFKT had Nike as an identifiable acquirer and a cultural moment that was visibly cresting. ProjectX's timing wedge — AI agents needing their own compute environments — is speculative, and the website returning a minimal loading page nearly two years after a June 2024 public beta announcement undermines the claim that execution is ahead of the market. No revenue, no customer logos, no user counts, zero job postings on the YC page. The technical vision is compelling on paper, but the gap between vision and visible traction is wider than I'm comfortable with.

One condition would flip me to Invest: if Rounak can show me that the co-founder departures led to a stronger, cleaner team (not a fractured one), AND that paying enterprise customers are already using Infinity for multi-OS AI agent workflows — real revenue, not credits — that would validate both the biographical resilience and the market timing thesis. Without that, ProjectX is a technically ambitious team building in a consensus space with no biographical inevitability, no invisible system to disrupt, and team stability questions that echo my most instructive failures. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Founder's Biographical Forge (no evidence of personal stakes in the problem beyond career ambition) and Contrarian Conviction (cloud infrastructure for AI is where everyone wants to be, not where no one will go).
