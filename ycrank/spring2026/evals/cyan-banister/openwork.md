# OpenWork -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what tells me everything about this company's structural position: within *days* of Anthropic launching Claude Cowork in January 2026, at least four separate teams — including LangChain, a well-funded developer tooling company — shipped nearly identical open-source alternatives using the same name and the same underlying SDK. That's not a market being created. That's a race to the bottom in a category with no barriers. When I backed Uber, three hundred investors had passed — not three competitors shipping the same product the same week. The speed of replication here isn't a signal of market demand; it's a signal of commodity. And the fact that Kuse AI, bootstrapped, already has $9M ARR in sixty days while OpenWork has no documented revenue tells me that execution speed in this space rewards the fastest builder, not the most differentiated one.

When I sit across from a founder, I want to hear their story before their business. With Ben Shafii, I see a competent open-source builder — Associate's degree, engineering manager at a web3 company, Orange DAO fellow, multiple repos with meaningful community adoption. That's a real track record of shipping. But I don't see the biographical crucible. Where's the moment this problem found *him*? His prior work spans web3, AI note-taking tools, embedding frameworks — he builds things that developers want, and that's a skill, but it's not a forge. When Claude Cowork launched and he saw the opportunity to build an open-source alternative, that's pattern recognition from reviewing eight thousand startup applications at Orange DAO. It's career intelligence. It's not someone who was broken by vendor lock-in and rebuilt themselves around the conviction that agentic workflows must be open. I'd want to hear what I'm missing, because the dossier gives me no story — just a résumé.

The invisible system test fails here, and that matters to me more than most investors would understand. Claude Cowork charges $200 a month. That's transparent. It's Mac-only. That's transparent. There's no cartel, no hidden tax, no information asymmetry exploiting people who don't know better. When Affirm attacked credit card opacity, the entire system was designed to obscure what consumers actually paid. When Ownwell attacked property tax assessments, homeowners literally didn't know they were being overcharged. Anthropic's Cowork is expensive and limited, sure — but everyone can see the price tag and the platform constraint. Making something cheaper and more accessible is valuable, but it's not the same as exposing a hidden structure. OpenWork is attacking a visible wall, not an invisible one.

The strongest bull case — and I want to be honest about it, because I've been wrong before — is the Kubernetes analogy. If agentic desktop workflows become the primary way knowledge workers interact with AI, and if the market fragments across dozens of model providers, then the vendor-neutral orchestration layer could become critical infrastructure. OpenWork's 12.5K stars in two and a half months is real community pull, and Anthropic is structurally incentivized to *never* make Cowork model-agnostic because it drives their Max subscription revenue. That strategic gap could be permanent. If Ben can convert open-source adoption into an enterprise platform with audit trails, managed deployment, and team administration — the Red Hat playbook — there's a path to something substantial. The question is whether the MIT license and the three-plus competitors doing the exact same thing let him get there before someone with more resources captures the same position. When I look at my portfolio, the companies that became infrastructure — Flexport, Carta, Flock Safety — had structural reasons why the first mover won permanently. An MIT-licensed wrapper on someone else's SDK, where LangChain has already published a competing repo, doesn't give me that structural inevitability.

This is a well-executed open-source project in the most consensus-attractive category in venture capital right now. AI developer tools, model-agnostic platforms, open-source alternatives to proprietary products — no investor in the world shifts uncomfortably hearing this pitch. They nod. They compete for the deal. That's exactly the opposite of the signal I look for. My biggest returns came from places where people thought I'd lost my mind — private rockets when they were blowing up, defense tech when it was politically radioactive, male birth control when it made VCs physically wince. OpenWork makes VCs comfortable, and that's the clearest signal I have that this isn't my kind of bet.
