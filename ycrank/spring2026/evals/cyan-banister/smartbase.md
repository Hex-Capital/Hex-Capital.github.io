# Smartbase -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Sam Goldman has founded three companies across three unrelated domains in roughly three years: Sphinx Labs in smart contract DevOps ($2M raised), Anytrace in AI support engineering (YC S25), and now Smartbase in manufacturing purchase order automation (YC Spring 2026). When I sit across from a founder, my opening question is always about their story — not the pitch, not the TAM slide. I want to know what happened in their life that made this problem impossible to walk away from. Smartbase's founders describe sleeping at a factory and working side-by-side with manufacturing employees, which shows genuine hustle — the kind of resourcefulness I respect because I know what it means to figure things out with nothing. But hustle on a factory floor is research, not biography. Sam went from web3 to AI support engineering to manufacturing PO automation in three years. Taira Fujioka was the youngest SDE 2 at AWS SageMaker Inference, which is impressive technical credentials, but it connects to building ML infrastructure, not to understanding why a machinist in Ohio is still hand-keying purchase orders from faxes. These are smart, capable founders searching for a problem worth solving — not people whose life story made this problem find them.

Smartbase automates purchase order entry for manufacturers, converting POs from paper, email, and fax into ERP orders using AI extraction and integrations across nine named systems including SAP, NetSuite, and Epicor. The company projects savings of roughly $33,750 per year per facility based on 500 POs processed monthly. This is a real problem — manufacturers genuinely waste hours on manual data entry. But when I apply my invisible system test, Smartbase doesn't pass. Every manufacturer already knows they're spending money on clerks to type in orders. There's no hidden tax here, no information asymmetry exploiting people who don't understand the system. Compare Smartbase to Affirm, which revealed the opacity of credit card interest structures, or Ownwell, which exposed how property tax assessments systematically overcharged homeowners who couldn't navigate the appeals process. Those companies made something invisible suddenly visible and shifted power from gatekeepers to ordinary people. Smartbase makes something tedious faster. That's valuable engineering, but it's not structural disruption of a hidden system.

Conexiom has raised $170M, generates approximately $75M in annual revenue, and serves 16 of the top 20 industrial distributors. Smartbase's positioning — targeting SMB manufacturers that Conexiom's enterprise sales motion and pricing can't reach — is a legitimate structural gap. I've seen this pattern work: the incumbent builds upmarket, leaving a segment underserved because their unit economics don't support downmarket expansion. But the space itself isn't one that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable. Manufacturing automation isn't politically toxic like Anduril's defense tech. It isn't stigmatized like Contraline's male birth control. It isn't "magically weird" in a way that provokes discomfort at a dinner party. Workist raised $11.6M for overlapping capabilities in Europe. OrderEase has been at this since 2014. Buddy targets the same workflow. This is a real market with real competitors and real investor appetite — which means it's consensus. I'm looking for the deals where 300 investors said no, not the ones where the competitive landscape already has four named players and an incumbent with $170M in backing.

Smartbase's defensibility story is the strongest dimension in this evaluation, and it's genuinely interesting. Nine named ERP integrations — Acumatica, Epicor, Infor, Dynamics 365, NetSuite, QuickBooks, SAP, SYSPRO, Sage X3 — combined with custom business rules per customer, create real switching costs once a manufacturer is onboarded. Each deployment trains Smartbase's extraction models on that manufacturer's specific PO formats and business logic, building a compounding per-customer data advantage. The "why now" is credible: LLMs have made document understanding viable at SMB price points, and ERP vendors have expanded their API ecosystems. This is the seed of an infrastructure play — if Smartbase becomes the translation layer between the messy analog world of manufacturing purchasing and the structured world of ERP systems, that's a position that compounds. But I can't ignore the bandwidth reality: two people maintaining nine ERP integration surfaces is a fragility risk. Conexiom has dedicated integration teams funded by $75M in revenue. And the core AI extraction technology — the part that reads a faxed PO and turns it into structured data — is increasingly commoditized through LLM APIs, as the dossier acknowledges. The moat lives in the ERP integration depth, not the AI, and depth across nine systems with two engineers is a promise, not yet a fact.

The strongest bull case an advocate would make: Smartbase is onboarding first customers onto five-figure annual contracts at pre-seed — that's real revenue signal, not vaporware. The founders demonstrated the kind of primary research (factory floor immersion) that builds genuine domain understanding. Manufacturing is one of the last large industries to be penetrated by modern software, and the LLM inflection point creates a narrow window where a nimble startup can build ERP integration depth before incumbents like Conexiom move downmarket. YC admitted these founders twice, which is a capability signal. And the $3.6 billion purchase order software market growing at 9.8% CAGR provides sufficient scale for a venture outcome. This is a reasonable case, and the five-figure contracts are the most persuasive data point — customers writing five-figure checks to a pre-seed company validates real pain. But when I check my known exceptions — HQ Trivia (cultural timing override), RTFKT (narrow inflection window), Superplastic (genre-defying weirdness) — none apply here. Smartbase is a competent B2B SaaS play in a real market, not a culturally prescient or category-defying bet that warrants overriding my framework. The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Sam or Taira had a biographical connection to manufacturing — a family business that struggled with this exact problem, a formative experience watching a factory fail because of order processing errors, anything that made this problem personal rather than discovered through market research. That biographical inevitability is what separates the founders I back from the ones I wish well.

Smartbase lands as a Pass driven primarily by two dimensions. The founder's biographical forge scores low because three ventures across three unrelated domains (web3, AI support, manufacturing) within three years signals market-gap searching rather than mission inevitability — and neither founder has documented manufacturing experience beyond their recent primary research. The invisible system dimension scores below my neutral default because the evidence actively shows this is efficiency automation in a visible workflow, not disruption of an opaque structure. Smartbase is building something real and useful, and the five-figure contracts prove manufacturers feel the pain. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. I'm looking for the founders whose story IS the data, building in spaces that make other investors flinch, attacking systems that exploit people who can't see them. Smartbase is solving a known problem competently. My portfolio was built on the unknown ones.
