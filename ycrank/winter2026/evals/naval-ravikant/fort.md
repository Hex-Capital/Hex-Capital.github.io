# Fort -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge question here is inverted in a way I find structurally concerning. Miranda, Paul, and Zac are genuine Tesla-grade hardware engineers -- 4680 battery cells, Robotaxi sensing architecture, SpaceX satellite structures. This is knowledge you cannot acquire in a classroom or a weekend bootcamp. It takes years inside a high-performance manufacturing organization to understand what it actually means to ship atoms at scale. The problem is that Fort's differentiation doesn't live in the hardware. A wrist-worn band with an IMU and optical heart rate sensor is well-understood consumer electronics. Fort's actual value proposition -- automatically classifying 50+ exercises, estimating per-rep velocity, predicting proximity to failure from wrist motion -- is a machine learning and signal processing challenge. None of the three founders show evidence of deep ML expertise. No public GitHub repos. Paul's autonomy architecture work at Tesla is the closest adjacency, but systems architecture for autonomous vehicles and signal classification for bicep curls are different problems operating at different scales of complexity. These founders have exceptional knowledge about the *medium* of building (hardware), not about the *substance* of what they're building (exercise science and motion classification). When I invested in Stack Overflow, it was built by developers who understood developer knowledge infrastructure from the inside. Fort is built by hardware engineers who happen to lift weights.

The leverage architecture is where this investment breaks down for me entirely. Consumer hardware is the opposite of leverage -- every unit sold requires a unit manufactured, shipped, supported, and potentially returned. Fort's business model ($289 device + $79.99/year subscription with a free tier) is un-leveraged at both ends: the hardware requires physical production, and the subscription is priced too thin to generate meaningful recurring revenue per user. Compare this to Whoop, which moved to a $0-hardware, $30/month pure subscription model precisely to maximize software leverage. Fort is competing against a company that has already solved for the leverage problem Fort hasn't even identified. The data Fort collects could eventually become an asset -- per-rep kinematics across millions of sessions would be genuinely valuable training data. But that requires a scale of deployment they cannot reach at $289/unit against competitors with hundred-million-dollar revenue bases. I invest in companies that create new forms of leverage. Fort creates a product.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. Whoop acquired Push in 2021 and killed the standalone hardware, integrating only a simplified muscular load estimate. This left a real gap -- no consumer wearable today provides automatic per-rep velocity tracking and proximity-to-failure estimation for strength training. The timing signal is real: strength workouts on Whoop grew 122% year-over-year, and the longevity movement has shifted mainstream fitness culture toward resistance training. If Fort ships a product that actually works -- that genuinely detects exercises and estimates RIR with useful accuracy -- they would own a category that every major wearable company has either ignored or abandoned. The Tesla pedigree means this team can probably ship hardware, which is where most hardware startups die. And a team that built battery cells, autonomous sensing systems, and rocket structures has the mechanical and electrical engineering chops to miniaturize a multi-sensor wearable to 30 grams with 7-day battery life. If accuracy proves out and the product ships on time, the category ownership window could last 18-24 months before a Whoop or Garmin reacts. That window is real but narrow.

What keeps me on the sidelines is the absence of compounding defensibility. The dossier states plainly: "The core technology (IMU-based exercise detection from wrist motion) is not patented in a way that prevents replication. Multiple academic papers and prior products (Push Band, RepOne) have demonstrated velocity-based training measurement from wearable sensors." This is an execution race, not a compound interest bet. Fort has no network effects -- my workout data doesn't make your workout data more valuable. No protocol dynamics. No developer ecosystem. Just a hardware device with software that has to be more accurate than whatever Whoop ships next with Push's acquired IP and vastly more training data from their existing user base. I backed Extropic because thermodynamic computing is speculative physics that no amount of capital can shortcut -- the science itself is the moat. Fort's science is known; the implementation is the variable. And implementation advantages erode quickly against $454M in competitor funding.

The founding team shows integrity signals I respect -- Zac prototyping on nights and weekends before committing full-time, Miranda honestly discussing the complexity stack of hardware startups on a podcast rather than hand-waving it away. The Tesla provenance suggests a culture of building things that actually work in the physical world. But long-term orientation in a consumer hardware business requires a clear path to leverage, and I don't see one here. This is a team I'd want to invest in building infrastructure -- a sensor fusion platform, a motion classification API that other fitness companies license, a developer toolkit for wearable ML. Instead, they're building a consumer device. The specific knowledge is real but misaligned with the core challenge, the model lacks leverage, and the technology doesn't compound in ways that create durable barriers. I'd take the meeting, tell them I admire the engineering pedigree, and suggest they think about whether the picks-and-shovels version of this business -- the motion intelligence layer underneath every wearable -- is the higher-leverage bet.
