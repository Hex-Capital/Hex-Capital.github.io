# Terminal Use -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Terminal Use is a tension I've been thinking about a lot lately. The founders' origin story is genuinely organic -- all three worked at Palantir, and Vivek led what's described as one of the largest agent deployment use cases across US hospitals. They didn't read about AI agents being hot and brainstorm infrastructure ideas. They lived inside the problem of getting agents to work reliably in production, with persistent filesystems, versioning, and all the ugly plumbing that entails. That's exactly the kind of discovery path I look for. But the market they're walking into has stopped being invisible. E2B has $32 million. Daytona has $24 million. Modal has $111 million and $50M in ARR. OpenAI is bundling agent hosting directly into their SDK. When I said the two most impressive YC companies I'd seen recently weren't working on AI, this is part of what I meant -- not that AI infrastructure is unimportant, but that when everyone can see the opportunity, you're no longer benefiting from schlep blindness. You're competing on execution speed against well-capitalized teams attacking adjacent territory.

The organic discovery is real, though, and I want to be precise about why. There's a meaningful difference between "ephemeral sandboxes for running code snippets" (what E2B and Daytona focus on) and "persistent infrastructure for long-running background agents that manipulate filesystems across tasks." Terminal Use's specific primitives -- filesystem forking, task-pinned versioning, manifest-based sync with checksums -- reflect the kind of problem you'd only care about if you'd actually tried to run agents in production at scale. A team that brainstormed "AI infrastructure" would build another sandbox. A team that spent years wrestling with Palantir's agent deployments across hospitals would build exactly what Terminal Use is building. The specificity of their technical choices is evidence of organic discovery in a way that market positioning alone isn't.

The "Vercel for background agents" framing is interesting but hides a structural gap. Vercel worked because Guillermo Rauch built Next.js first. The framework created captive demand for the deployment layer. Terminal Use doesn't own a framework -- they're building the deployment layer for other people's frameworks (Claude Agent SDK, Codex SDK). That means they need to be better than what the framework providers themselves offer. OpenAI has already launched Frontier with hosted runtimes. Anthropic could do the same tomorrow. Being framework-agnostic is a feature until the framework providers decide hosting is their business. This is the multiple-miracles problem in disguise: Terminal Use needs the agent market to grow massively AND the framework providers to not bundle hosting AND their specific niche (persistent filesystem agents) to remain distinct from ephemeral sandboxes. That's a lot of things that all have to be true.

The strongest bull case is that this team is exactly right for this problem at exactly this moment. Three Palantir engineers who've already built production agent infrastructure, who went to university together and became close friends at work -- that's the co-founder relationship pattern I care about most. They've clearly built a working product: SDK in Python and TypeScript, CLI-based deployment, documentation site, and Woz running as a Slack product with real pricing tiers. These are hackers who make things. If background agents become the dominant computing paradigm over the next five years -- and Gartner's projection of 40% of enterprise apps embedding agents by 2026 suggests momentum -- then whoever owns the orchestration layer captures enormous value. The specific niche of persistent, long-running agents is underserved by the existing competitors, who mostly optimize for ephemeral execution. If Terminal Use gets developer adoption in this niche before it gets absorbed, they have a real business.

But the traction signals give me pause. Zero GitHub stars across four public repositories. No community. No press. No visible users. For a developer tools company, GitHub adoption is oxygen. When Replit was early, developers were already using it and telling other developers about it. When Stripe was early, the Collisons were doing installations on developers' laptops. There's no evidence yet that anyone outside the team wants this enough to use it, let alone depend on it. The Woz product has pricing but no evidence of customers behind those prices. A pricing page without customers is just a hypothesis about what people might pay. I need to see evidence that developers are pulling this product toward them -- organic adoption, word of mouth, someone building something important on top of it. Without that, I'm betting entirely on the team and the market timing, and the market timing cuts both ways because it's attracting so much well-funded competition.

This is a competent technical team with genuine domain expertise attacking a real problem they discovered organically. Under different competitive conditions -- if they were one of three companies in this space instead of one of a dozen -- I'd be significantly more excited. The Palantir background and co-founder relationship are strong. The product specificity is strong. But zero traction evidence combined with a category that's attracted hundreds of millions in competitor funding makes this a bet where the odds don't clear my bar. The schlep was real when they started; it's visible to everyone now.
