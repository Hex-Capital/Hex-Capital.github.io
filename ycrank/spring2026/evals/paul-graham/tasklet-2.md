# Tasklet -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Andrew Lee already sold Firebase to Google in 2014 and Jonny Dimond was the tech lead for Firestore inside Google — Tasklet is what happens when a proven infrastructure pair, twelve years deep into working together, kills their own email product because users kept asking for the agent runtime hidden underneath it. That is the signature here. The highest rung is the cofounder configuration, which is about as good as a two-person team can be short of the Collison brothers. The lowest rung is the schlep test: Tasklet sits in the horizontal-automation lane next to Lindy ($53.6M raised), Gumloop ($20M), Relay.app ($8.2M), and Zapier Agents, and nothing in the dossier names a schlep mainstream founders are unconsciously routing around.

Stripe is the load-bearing analogy for the cofounders dimension, because Patrick and John Collison had been hacking commerce together since **Auctomatic** (2007) before they built **Stripe** (2010), and the prior shared artifact was what I underwrote. Andrew Lee and Jonny Dimond have the same shape but longer: Firebase together, Google together after the 2014 acquisition, Shortwave together from 2020, then Tasklet carved out of the Shortwave runtime in 2025. Where Tasklet diverges from Stripe is that the Collisons were 19 and 17 when I bet on them and the ceiling was open — Lee and Dimond are second-act founders with $9M already raised at Shortwave and a $175M Tasklet valuation set by USV and Lightspeed, which means the asymmetry I love is already partly priced.

The schlep ladder is where I worry. The **Pass Example** failure mode is the AI-wrapper team pitching a horizontal product with no offline grind — and Tasklet's category description, "cloud agent operating system for knowledge work," reads alarmingly like that frame even though the founders are not MBAs. Does the failure pattern apply? Partially. Tasklet's actual work — 32 integrations shipped in a single release, browser-based computer-use reliability for sites without APIs, the MCP plumbing — is real engineering schlep, and the dossier notes computer-use is "an industry-wide unsolved reliability problem." But it is not the **Coinbase** (2012) kind of schlep that scares other founders away; it is the kind every well-funded competitor is already doing. The moat from grind compounds only if rivals flinch, and Lindy and Gumloop are not flinching.

On animal resourcefulness, the mechanism that matters over the next eighteen months is whether Tasklet's $5M ARR with >1,200% growth since January 1, 2026 compounds because Andrew Lee personally drags integrations and customers across the line, or because the Shortwave user base did the early lifting and the curve flattens once that cohort saturates. The dossier shows Lee shipping changelog tweets at @startupandrew and a 4.8 Product Hunt rating across 144 reviews — founder-led, but not the cereal-box stunt. I score this between the Dropbox demo rung and the Airbnb cereal rung, closer to Dropbox.

On organic idea, the counterfactual that would push Tasklet's score up a tier: if Jonny Dimond's agent infrastructure had been open-sourced and pulled out of Shortwave by external developers before the founders themselves productized it, that would be the **Dropbox** (2007) USB-stick pattern in pure form. Instead, Shortwave users asked for the agent without the email client (per the Shortwave blog), and the founders responded — organic, but solicited rather than self-discovered. Still well above the absent-evidence default.

On hacker genealogy, Firebase is the parallel I keep returning to — it is itself a serious technical artifact at the level of the **Stripe** (2010) API, and Jeff Dean writing a check is a tribal marker no MBA-coded team gets. Dimond's GitHub @jdimond with Haskell repos (firehose, a Firebase clone in Haskell) is exactly the trail I look for. This is the cleanest dimension after cofounder configuration.

My 36-month forecast for Tasklet: $40-80M ARR with an acquisition by Google, Salesforce, or Microsoft in the $400M-1.2B range, rather than a standalone defining outcome. The mechanism is the one Andrew Lee himself articulates on the Cognitive Revolution podcast — "always bet on the models" — applied to his own horizontal agent UI. If ChatGPT generates the right UI on the fly, Tasklet's runtime becomes a feature inside a foundation model company's product, and the proven Firebase-Google relationship makes the acquirer obvious.

The bear case I have to dismantle is my own ceiling-thinking from "Frighteningly Ambitious" (March 2012): if I cap Tasklet at acquihire-coded outcomes because Andrew Lee already has a Google acquisition on his resume, I am committing the **Kiko** (2005) error in reverse — penalizing a founder for prior outcome shape rather than reading the current animal. The Collisons had Auctomatic on their record when I funded Stripe and that exit did not cap them. What wins the argument against the bull case is the specific commoditization claim Lee makes in his own voice and the $175M post-money already set by USV — the upside compression is mechanical, not a judgment about the team. The condition that would flip my verdict to Strong Invest: if within twelve months Tasklet ships an integration or computer-use capability that OpenAI's and Anthropic's native agent products demonstrably cannot replicate at parity, the schlep dimension reclassifies from 7 to 12 and the ceiling reopens.
