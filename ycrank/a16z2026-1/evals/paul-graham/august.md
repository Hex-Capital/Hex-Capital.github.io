# August -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Eight hundred thousand dollars in annual recurring revenue inside three months, selling to commercial banks. That number stopped me. Banks are the slowest-buying organisms in the enterprise ecosystem — procurement committees, compliance reviews, vendor risk assessments, all before they'll let you touch a test environment, let alone production data. For a three-person team out of Tel Aviv to hit $800K ARR that fast, something unusual had to happen. The most likely explanation is also the most concerning: August's single named deployment is at one "$170B+ bank," which means this probably isn't a broad sales motion but a single large contract, likely enabled by a pre-existing relationship. One deal is not traction. One deal is a relationship. The question is whether Bar Ittah and Tom Tankilevitch can turn that relationship into a pattern.

The founders have real credentials. Tom built the AI backbone of a Unit 8200 intelligence unit, then moved to Iguazio — acquired by McKinsey for ~$50M — then led a twenty-person engineering team at Port, which hit an $800M valuation. Bar was on the founding team at Xperiti, which Ipsos acquired in 2023, and was first employee at OnFire AI. Both have been through exits. Both have operated inside high-stakes AI infrastructure. That's competence. But competence is not the same as the animal quality I look for — that slightly unhinged intensity where someone takes the problem so seriously they forget to eat. The dossier gives me résumés, not stories. I don't know how Bar and Tom handle being blocked. I don't know what they built on a weekend because they couldn't wait for Monday. The $800K ARR in three months is itself a kind of resourcefulness proof — you don't close enterprise banking deals at that velocity without being good — but I'm inferring the quality from the outcome rather than seeing it directly.

What bothers me most is the organic discovery question. How did two AI infrastructure engineers from Unit 8200, Iguazio, and Port end up building autonomous banking operations software? Bar's Xperiti had a fraud prevention angle, which is fintech-adjacent, but fraud detection and commercial loan underwriting are different specialties in the way that cardiology and orthopedics are both medicine. The dossier contains zero origin story — no moment of personal frustration with banking workflows, no "we built this because we kept seeing banks waste millions on manual credit analysis." The website says "Banking's last piece of software" and "Powering banks of the future," which is marketing language, not the language of someone who lived inside the problem. Compare this to how Drew Houston talks about forgetting his USB drive, or how the Collison brothers describe the pain of integrating Stripe's predecessor libraries. August's positioning sounds like it was designed in a strategy session. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas, and when I look at August, I see a team that identified a large, growing market — 91% of banking executives calling AI a strategic priority — and designed a product to serve it. That's the opposite of my framework.

The Stripe comparison is instructive but ultimately cuts against August. Stripe worked because Patrick and John Collison were developers building for developers — they were the customer, they understood the pain viscerally, and the seven lines of code to integrate Stripe payments existed because they personally hated the hundred lines required by existing solutions. August is AI engineers building for bankers. That customer distance means they have to learn the domain from the outside, which is possible but slower and more error-prone than organic discovery. And unlike Stripe, which entered a space most developers thought was "done" (PayPal existed), August is entering a space where everyone already agrees the opportunity is enormous. nCino is public at $500M+ revenue. Numerated was acquired by Moody's. Heron raised $16M. FundMore.ai grew revenue 1,504% over three years. When I hear about August's autonomous banking AI, nobody's first reaction is "that will never work." Their first reaction is "of course, everyone's building that." The schlep of banking operations — the regulatory density, the compliance barriers, the integration complexity — does create real barriers, and the 23% Accenture figure on banks moving AI past pilot shows execution is genuinely hard. But schlep alone doesn't make an idea contrarian. It makes it difficult. Those are different things.

The bull case for August requires believing three things: first, that the $800K ARR represents genuine product-market pull rather than a single relationship-driven deal; second, that the "proprietary context layer" — the claim that usage compounds bank-specific knowledge — creates a real data flywheel that compounds into a switching-cost moat; and third, that Bar and Tom's AI infrastructure backgrounds translate into banking domain expertise fast enough to outrun both incumbents and the dozen other AI-for-banking startups. If all three are true, August could become the default AI operations layer for mid-to-large commercial banks, which would be a very large outcome. The fact that existing incumbents like Temenos and FIS earn professional services revenue from manual processes creates exactly the kind of business model conflict that prevents them from cannibalizing themselves. That structural advantage is real. But I'd need to see August close a second and third bank to believe the first one wasn't an anomaly.

August scores well on founder competence and execution speed, but the absence of organic problem discovery and the consensus nature of "AI for banking" are significant gaps in my framework. The dimension that most determined my score is Organic Problem Discovery — not because the founders lack capability, but because I can't find evidence they stumbled into this problem through lived experience rather than market analysis. The $800K ARR signal keeps me from passing outright; something is working, and founders who can close enterprise banking deals in three months with three people are doing something right. But the idea itself, stripped of the traction number, is the kind of thing that sounds good to everyone — and in my experience, that's the tell.
