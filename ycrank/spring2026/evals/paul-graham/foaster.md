# Foaster -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that this is a consulting firm. "AI-native consulting firm" is right there in the tagline. A human consultant scopes the engagement. A human consultant reviews the output. A human consultant delivers the recommendations. The AI conducts the interviews -- which is genuinely clever -- but the delivery model is still a person selling time to enterprises. That's not a startup. That's a consultancy with better tools. The path to scaling this requires hiring more consultants, not shipping better code. When I see a company whose growth depends on becoming a professional services organization rather than a product organization, that's a structural problem. The thing that makes startups powerful is leverage -- one team building something that serves millions. Consulting is the opposite of leverage.

The origin of this idea concerns me more. How did two ENSAE Paris statistics students in their early twenties discover that management consulting engagements are too slow and expensive? Neither founder has worked at McKinsey or BCG. Neither has been on the client side of an expensive consulting engagement, as far as the evidence shows. What they *did* have was a hammer: Combes built Repondia, an AI system that handled 60,000+ phone calls for French restaurant groups. They wrote LLM behavioral benchmarks. They understood that AI agents could conduct structured conversations at scale. So they went looking for a domain where that capability would be valuable and landed on consulting. That's the AI startup version of what I've written about before -- working backward from a technical capability to a market application, rather than forward from a genuine problem you've been living with. The fact that "AI consulting" is possibly the single hottest category in enterprise software right now only reinforces my concern. When I look at the competitive landscape -- Distyl AI with $202 million, Skan.ai with $57.6 million, Aily Labs with $101 million, PromptQL with $136 million -- this is the definition of a crowded consensus category. Five hundred million dollars in funded competitors is not what schlep blindness looks like. Schlep blindness looks like an empty field with an obvious problem nobody will touch. This field is packed.

The strongest bull case is that Combes has already built the core mechanism. If Repondia genuinely handled 60,000+ customer calls at scale, that's meaningful execution evidence. The leap from restaurant customer calls to employee interviews is smaller than it appears -- both require AI agents that navigate unstructured conversation, extract structured information, and handle the messiness of real human communication. And the consulting market is genuinely vulnerable to disruption through an innovator's dilemma: McKinsey cannot offer a service that is 3-5x cheaper without destroying its own leverage model. The operational map as a persistent data asset -- interview the whole organization once, then sell follow-on analyses against that baseline -- is a real switching-cost story if they can land clients. And Dabadie building 400K+ user communities as a teenager and getting recruited by Nadal's Academy at 18 suggests someone with serious interpersonal hustle, which matters in founder-led enterprise sales. Their LLM benchmark work getting shared by OpenAI's president is intellectually impressive and indicates a capacity for original thinking about AI agent behavior. These are smart, ambitious founders.

But the gap between "smart and ambitious" and "relentlessly resourceful" is exactly what the startup crucible tests, and these founders haven't been through it yet. Selling AI-augmented consulting to enterprises when you're 23, have never worked in consulting, and are competing against firms with hundreds of millions in funding -- that requires a specific kind of credibility and grit that I can't assess from the dossier. The Repondia claim is the single strongest evidence point, but it has no independent verification -- no web presence, no press, no Crunchbase listing. That's not disqualifying at pre-seed, but it means the most important data point is unverifiable. And I keep returning to the services model problem. Even if they execute brilliantly, the ceiling on a consulting business is lower than the ceiling on a product business. The promised evolution from per-engagement fees to a persistent platform is the standard consulting-to-SaaS narrative, and it almost never happens because the incentives pull in opposite directions -- customization for each client versus standardization for scale.

I'm passing on this one. The idea was manufactured from a technical capability, not discovered through pain. The category is consensus, not contrarian. The business model has a services ceiling. The founders are clearly intelligent and have relevant technical skills, but they're entering an enterprise sales motion without domain credibility, against competitors with orders of magnitude more capital. If they had one paying customer who was dependent on the product -- one enterprise that had gone through the full interview cycle and was coming back for a second engagement -- my calculus would shift. But right now I'm looking at a well-executed application of generally available AI capabilities in a crowded market, built by founders who found the market through research rather than through frustration. That combination has rarely worked in my experience.
