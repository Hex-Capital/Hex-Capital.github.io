# Lamina Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Lamina Labs isn't the product claim — it's the gap it's sitting in. Billions of dollars are flowing into generative AI video right now. Sora, Runway, Synthesia, HeyGen — everyone wants to generate cinematic footage from text. But generative video is fundamentally probabilistic. It hallucinates. And for STEM education, hallucination isn't a minor quality issue; it's a category error. You cannot teach someone calculus with an animation where the derivative curve is approximately right. It has to be exactly right. This is the kind of problem that hides in plain sight because the AI video hype is so loud that nobody stops to ask: "Wait, who's building the boring, precise version for teachers?" That's a schlep blindness signal. Not a strong one — but a real one.

The question I always ask first is: how did these founders find this problem? And here, the dossier gives me nothing to work with. There's no story of Sudip spending three days trying to animate a proof for an MIT class. There's no account of Rohan struggling to visualize astrophysical models for a Caltech presentation. What I see instead is market-gap language: "Billions are going into Hollywood-style AI video. Nearly no one builds animation designed for educational explanation." That sentence reads like it was composed for a pitch deck, not extracted from lived experience. It's possible — even likely — that two STEM students at MIT and Caltech have personally felt the pain of making precise educational visualizations. But the absence of that story in any public-facing communication is itself a signal. Founders who discovered a problem organically tend to lead with the story of discovery. When they lead with the market gap, I worry the idea was manufactured.

The technical credentials here are genuinely strong for pre-seed. Sudip has a C++ project on GitHub — ghostclaw, a compact OpenClaw implementation with 30+ AI provider integrations. That's systems-level work, not tutorial code. Rohan has published astrophysics research in a real journal, won an Honorable Mention at the International Olympiad on Astronomy and Astrophysics, and earned a Bronze Medal at the International Computer Olympiad as what must have been a young teenager. The combination — a systems programmer and a physicist with competitive programming credentials — maps directly onto the core technical challenge: converting natural language into mathematically exact visual representations at speed. If these two have actually built a deterministic rendering pipeline that produces correct STEM animations in under eight seconds, that's a meaningful piece of engineering, not a wrapper around an API.

The bull case is that this is Stripe for educational content — an obvious problem hiding behind a schlep that nobody wants to touch. Manim has 35,800 GitHub stars, which tells you the demand for mathematical animation is real and large. But Manim requires Python scripting. If Pictor genuinely replaces that coding step with a text prompt while maintaining mathematical precision, and does it in seconds rather than hours, that's the kind of 100x improvement in a specific workflow that creates fanatical users. Teachers and course creators who currently spend days on a single animation would switch immediately. The founding team has exactly the right technical background to pull this off. And the two-person team using AI coding tools as force-multipliers fits the YC model of staying lean and moving fast. If I'm wrong about the manufactured-idea concern — if these founders have a genuine, deep connection to this problem that just isn't visible in the public record — this could be a very good bet.

But several things hold me back. First, the product is in waitlist stage with zero public evidence of user pull. No testimonials, no usage metrics, no "someone found this and won't stop using it" signal. At pre-seed I don't demand traction, but I look for any organic pull — and I see none. Second, the commoditization risk is real. Manim is open-source and well-established. Someone could build an LLM wrapper around Manim in a weekend and get 80% of the value. The barrier is the quality of the text-to-scene conversion and the rendering speed, but those advantages erode as LLMs improve. Third, the co-founders attend different universities on different coasts and have no documented shared history beyond being Nepalese — I've seen too many companies fall apart when co-founders don't have deep pre-existing relationships. Fourth, both founders are undergraduates. That's not disqualifying — we funded Reddit when Steve and Alexis were barely out of college — but it means they haven't yet been through the kind of sustained adversity that reveals whether someone is truly relentlessly resourceful or just talented and enthusiastic.

What I'm left with is a technically capable team building in a genuine gap, but without the organic origin signal that gives me conviction. The schlep is real but narrow, and the moat is uncertain. I'd want to see Pictor actually working — generating a correct animation of, say, a Fourier transform from a text prompt — before I could assess whether the "under 8 seconds" and "no hallucinations" claims translate into something that creates the kind of user dependency I look for. Right now, this is a plausible idea with strong founders, not yet a company that's earned my personal check.
