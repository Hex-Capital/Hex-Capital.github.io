# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Milliray is that the CEO literally built the 94 GHz radar system that is the product. Matthew Moore's PhD research, published in IET Radar, Sonar & Navigation, is on simulating and validating drone micro-Doppler signatures at exactly the frequency his company uses. This is not a founder who read a market report and hired engineers -- this is the scientist who created the technology in the lab and is now trying to commercialize it. That's an extremely high "sane" score. Three PhDs from St Andrews and Oxford, all Royal Academy of Engineering Enterprise Fellows, with complementary expertise in millimeter-wave physics, laser/optical systems, and automated reasoning for classification. On the founder credibility axis, this team is close to the Mysten Labs archetype -- deep infrastructure builders spinning out of a research institution. But here's where my framework creates friction: counter-drone detection is not an insane idea. It is possibly the most consensus defense investment thesis on the planet right now. DroneShield just posted AUD 216 million in revenue with 276% year-over-year growth. Over a billion dollars in counter-drone purchases closed in December 2025 alone. NATO and DASA are actively funding anyone in this space. When your TAM slide shows $16 billion by 2034 and every defense publication is writing about your market, you are not in non-consensus territory -- you are in a gold rush.

The specific 94 GHz millimeter-wave approach adds some technical differentiation within the market, but it doesn't make the bet structurally contrarian. The pitch is "our radar is better at detecting small drones than their radar" -- that's a feature advantage within a validated category, not a primitive-layer insight that reshapes how security works. Compare this to Helium, which proposed that regular people would deploy wireless hotspots and create a decentralized telecom network -- an idea that sounded genuinely deranged for seven years until it didn't. Or Venmo, where the thesis was that Americans would text each other money from their phones in 2009. Milliray's thesis -- that governments need better drone detection -- would get a head nod from literally every VC and defense analyst I know. The "insane" component is really "three Scottish PhD students can outbuild Raytheon, DroneShield, Robin Radar, and Axon's Dedrone division," which is an execution bet, not a structural insight.

Where Milliray does activate my framework is on the AI-resistance axis, and this is worth spending time on because it's the dimension that separates this from the pile of AI-wrapper companies I see daily. You cannot download a better radar. You cannot prompt-engineer millimeter-wave signal processing into existence. The physical hardware, the RF engineering at 94 GHz, the manufacturing know-how -- these are moats that get stronger as AI makes software cheaper, because AI improves the classification algorithms running on top of the hardware without enabling a purely software competitor to replicate the sensor itself. This is structurally identical to what I see in Craftwork -- the painting crews can't be replaced by software, but the dispatch software makes the crews more efficient. Milliray's radar can't be replaced by an LLM, but ML makes the radar's classification better. That's the "cherry on top" pattern I seek. Add defense procurement barriers -- vendor qualification cycles, security clearances, sovereign technology preferences in NATO countries -- and you have switching costs that compound over time. If they can get into procurement pipelines, the moat deepens with every deployment.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: (1) the 94 GHz frequency advantage creates a detection capability gap that existing competitors cannot close through software upgrades to their current hardware, (2) the European sovereign defense tech movement creates a structural preference for a UK-based provider over American alternatives like Fortem or Australian DroneShield, and (3) the DASA and NATO DIANA relationships convert into real procurement within 18 months rather than remaining as validation theater. If all three are true, this is a Seneca-type investment -- physical-world defense tech with genuine operational moat in a market with explosive demand. The narrative could expand from "drone radar" to "sovereign European sensor infrastructure for aerial security," which has real elasticity. And the timing is undeniably excellent -- $30 billion in market expansion projected over the next decade, with governments writing checks today, not in some speculative future.

But I keep coming back to two concerns that are specific to how I evaluate bets. First, the CTO is listed as CEO of Quantum Fabrix, a separate quantum computing venture. At a three-person startup where every founder's bandwidth is existential, this is not a minor detail -- it signals exactly the kind of attention fragmentation that kills deep-tech companies in the commercialization phase. Building hardware is brutally operationally intensive, and you need every founder fully locked in. Second, there is zero revenue signal. Not even a pilot deployment, an LOI, or a named customer evaluation. "Deployment-ready" is a claim on a website, not a traction signal. For a market this hot with this much active procurement spending, the absence of any commercial signal at all is notable. DroneShield, Robin Radar, and Fortem are shipping product and booking revenue. If Milliray's technology is truly superior, someone should be paying to test it by now.

This lands for me as a competent team in a real but consensus market with genuine hardware moat characteristics but no commercial validation and one meaningful founder-commitment red flag. I'd take a meeting because the AI-resistance profile and physical-world moat are exactly what I look for. But I would not write a check today without evidence that the NATO DIANA and DASA relationships are converting into something tangible, confirmation that Doherty has fully committed to Milliray over Quantum Fabrix, and ideally a pilot deployment with a named defense customer. The structural logic is sound; the execution signals aren't there yet.
