# Smart Bricks -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Smart Bricks is a solo founder company. Mohamed Mohamed is the only founder identified in any source, leading a team of 20 from a career that runs Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, BCG -- the four horsemen of institutional prestige. I've said many times that starting a startup is too hard for one person. You need someone to brainstorm with, to talk you out of stupid decisions, to cheer you up at 2am when the deploy breaks. Mohamed's team of 20 may include strong people, but no named technical co-founder or executive appears in any public source. That's key-person risk, yes, but it's worse than that -- it's a signal about the kind of company this is. When I look at Stripe, I see two brothers who'd been programming together since childhood. When I look at Smart Bricks, I see a solo operator who raised capital and hired a team. Those are different organisms.

Mohamed's background is precisely the profile I described when I wrote about how the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. He left BCG in July 2024 and founded Smart Bricks. His company positions real estate as "the only trillion-dollar asset class that doesn't already have its own AI-native computing stack." That sentence is a market analysis, not a frustration. Compare it to Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, or the Octopart founders who couldn't find electronic parts for their physics lab. Those stories start with "I had this problem and it was driving me crazy." Mohamed's story starts with "I identified a market gap." He spent years at Blackstone and Goldman evaluating real estate investments professionally, which gives him domain adjacency -- but domain adjacency through institutional finance is different from the organic discovery I look for. He experienced real estate investing from inside Goldman's deal team, not from the perspective of the individual investor struggling with "PDFs, WhatsApp threads and incomplete data" that Smart Bricks claims to serve.

The idea itself fails my Black Swan test completely. "AI for real estate" is not an idea that repels before it attracts -- it's an idea that attracted $392M to EliseAI, $373M to Roofstock, and $130M to Cherre before Smart Bricks wrote its first line of code. When I read that Smart Bricks made TechCrunch's Top 200 and the company description could run verbatim in a proptech trend report, I know this idea sits in the zone where returns have already been competed away. The best startup ideas make people say "that will never work." Nobody says that about AI-powered real estate analytics in 2026. Everyone nods. That's the tell.

The strongest bull case for Smart Bricks: Mohamed's Blackstone and Goldman network gives him distribution into exactly the institutional and HNW investor channels that could become early customers. The $12M revenue run rate, if accurate, would be extraordinary for a company founded eighteen months ago on $5M -- the kind of signal that would make me reconsider everything else. And the multi-jurisdiction regulatory complexity across Dubai, London, New York, and Miami could function as a schlep moat if they build genuine compliance infrastructure. But that revenue figure is self-reported with no independent verification and no definition of what "revenue" means -- it could be gross transaction value flowing through the platform, not fees earned. For a company founded in July 2024 with $5M raised and 20 employees, claiming $12M in actual revenue would be so unusual that the absence of any third-party corroboration makes me discount it heavily. If it's real and means platform fees, I'm wrong about Smart Bricks. But I'd need to see the books.

Smart Bricks' communication style reinforces my concern about earnestness. "Autonomous agents automate up to 99% of the workflow." "Intelligence infrastructure." "AI-native computing stack." This is the vocabulary of pitch decks optimized for investor pattern-matching, not the direct, specific language of founders who understand their problem deeply enough to explain it plainly. Mohamed has no public GitHub, no Product Hunt listing, no Hacker News presence. His credentials are Forbes 30 Under 30, advisor to a $200B sovereign wealth fund, angel investor in 30+ startups. These are status markers. When I funded the Airbnb team, their credential was selling cereal boxes out of their apartment to keep the lights on. The founders I back pass through professional and cross over into obsessive about the problem itself -- not about the fundraising milestones or the accelerator selections.

My verdict is Pass. The two dimensions most decisive: Organic Problem Discovery, where Mohamed's consulting-to-startup trajectory and market-analysis framing actively contradict the "live in the future, then build what's missing" framework I consider essential; and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts, where over $900M in combined funding to direct competitors confirms this is consensus territory with compressed returns. The sole founder structure and absence of visible technical building capability compound these concerns. Smart Bricks may succeed as a business -- Mohamed is clearly capable and well-networked -- but it doesn't pattern-match to the kind of bet I make with my own money.
