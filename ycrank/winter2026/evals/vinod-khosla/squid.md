# Squid -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most striking thing about Squid is not what the founders have built -- it's who they are and who they're selling to. Both Conor Jones and George Kolokotronis worked at National Grid and Octopus Energy. They co-founded VoltQuant, which served National Grid and Octopus Energy. Now they've founded Squid, which serves... National Grid and Octopus Energy. In most investment frameworks, this is lauded as "extraordinary founder-market fit." In mine, it triggers a very specific alarm: these are industry insiders executing the industry's playbook. They know exactly what distribution network operators want because they sat inside those organizations. And what DNOs want is a better planning tool -- not a fundamentally different way to distribute electricity. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a New Zealander with no aerospace pedigree who didn't know what the industry considered impossible. That ignorance freed him to attempt things Lockheed engineers never would. I don't see that quality here. I see two talented operators who understand the pain of managing grid data in spreadsheets and are building the obvious solution.

Let me apply my first test: if Squid succeeds completely, what changes? Utilities plan their distribution networks in a browser instead of spreadsheets and desktop software. Planning cycles get faster. Assumptions become transparent. That's useful. National Grid's FlexPortal launch -- "powered by Squid" at pre-seed, which is genuinely impressive execution -- confirms the product solves a real problem. But the consequence of that success is a better enterprise workflow tool in a $3.7 billion market. Nobody's life changes. No incumbent industry gets structurally displaced. The grid itself still operates the same way. Compare this to Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means making fossil fuel power generation obsolete, or even to Square, where success meant making traditional POS infrastructure economically unviable. Squid's success means utilities use a nicer interface for grid planning. I've passed on dozens of B2B SaaS companies with this exact profile: real problem, competent team, working product, and a consequence of success that amounts to a modest efficiency gain in a specialized market.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to engage with it honestly -- goes something like this: the energy transition is the defining infrastructure challenge of this century, and the binding constraint is not generation or storage but the grid itself. Every EV charger, heat pump, and solar panel connects through distribution networks that were designed for one-way power flow. If Squid becomes the planning layer that every utility uses to navigate this transition, they're sitting at the chokepoint of trillions of dollars of infrastructure investment. The National Grid deployment isn't a pilot -- it's a production system at one of the world's most important grid operators, achieved by two people. If you squint, this looks like Palantir for grid infrastructure: not flashy technology, but becoming the indispensable decision layer for critical systems. The timing tailwind is real -- Ofgem's RIIO-ED2 framework is forcing UK DNOs to modernize, EVs are proliferating faster than grid capacity, and AI has crossed a threshold where browser-native modeling can compete with desktop engineering suites. This is a legitimate "why now" that isn't manufactured.

But I don't invest in bull cases that require squinting. The technology here -- browser-native delivery, versioned data models, AI-powered stress testing -- is solid product engineering, not a structural disruption that makes the incumbent approach uneconomic. Moving from desktop to browser is a delivery mechanism improvement that GE Vernova or Envelio could replicate in a product cycle. The AI layer is unspecified in the dossier. Envelio, already owned by E.ON with access to major European utilities, offers overlapping digital twin capabilities. Camus Energy has $25M and 500% ARR growth in adjacent territory. The competitive landscape here is not a moat -- it's a crowded field where a 2-person pre-seed company is competing with an E.ON subsidiary and a $33 billion GE division. The VoltQuant-to-Squid evolution could signal learning rate, and I'd want to understand what specifically they learned that changed their approach. But from the outside, it looks more like repackaging the same relationships with a refined product than a fundamental rethinking.

The timing trajectory is the one dimension where Squid genuinely scores well by my framework. Grid modernization is accelerating on an exponential curve driven by electrification, and the regulatory window in the UK is open now. But strong timing in a market where the consequence of success is incremental doesn't change my calculus. I need both: an exponential curve AND a consequence of success that transforms an industry. Squid has the former but not the latter. This is a competent team building a needed tool at the right moment. For most investors, that's sufficient. For me, the question remains: are these founders too smart to spend their careers on a problem this contained? The grid needs transformation, not optimization. I'd want to see Squid articulate a vision where their planning layer becomes the foundation for something that makes today's grid architecture obsolete -- not just easier to manage.
