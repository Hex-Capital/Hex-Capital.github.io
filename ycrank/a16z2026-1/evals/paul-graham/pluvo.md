# Pluvo -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Pluvo is that everyone agrees it's a good idea. AI for FP&A. AI agents that do variance analysis for CFOs. Datarails just raised $70M for essentially the same thesis. Abacum raised $60M. Anaplan got acquired for $10.7B. When I see a startup entering a space where hundreds of millions of dollars are already validating the category, the return premium has been competed away. The best startup ideas look like bad ideas to most people — that's the whole point of "Black Swan Farming." "AI for finance teams" could appear unchanged in a TechCrunch trend piece. Nobody hears this pitch and says "that will never work." They say "oh, interesting, tell me more." That's the tell.

Pluvo's founding team is four people: Alexandre Labrèche (CEO, former financial analyst and hockey player), Andrew Ingram (CTO, software engineering degree from Ottawa), Vanessa Galarneau (CFO/COO, 10+ years in ML for finance at Norton Rose Fulbright), and Sebastian Fallenbuchl (Chief Growth Officer, 30K+ LinkedIn audience). What strikes me is how thoroughly this team was assembled to cover business functions — a CEO, a CTO, a CFO/COO, and a growth person. This is how you'd staff a startup if you read a book about how to staff a startup. Compare it to the way Stripe started: two brothers who were both programmers, both obsessed with the same ugly infrastructure problem. Pluvo's team is professionally composed but there's no GitHub presence for any founder, no evidence of compulsive building, no story where someone stayed up all night because the problem wouldn't leave them alone. The "animal" quality — that state where someone passes through professional into obsessive — I don't see it in this dossier.

The origin story is revealing in its absence. The legal entity is Rain Technologies Inc., founded 2019, which built "Budget Manager" for public-sector budgeting before pivoting to Pluvo in 2024. That timing matters. 2024 is precisely when every company with access to an LLM API pivoted to "agentic AI" for whatever vertical they happened to be near. Galarneau spent a decade doing ML for finance. Labrèche was a financial analyst. They have genuine domain proximity — this isn't outsiders playing tourist. But domain proximity is not the same as organic discovery. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he forgot his USB drive on the Chinatown bus and couldn't stand it anymore. The dossier tells me Pluvo's team "identified" that "planning tools today stop at data aggregation and visualization" — the language of market analysis, not the language of personal frustration that keeps you awake at 3am. The distinction matters because people who discover problems organically understand the problem at a depth that market researchers never reach.

The bull case deserves a real hearing. $430K ARR in three months is impressive if real — CFOs are notoriously skeptical buyers who control their own budgets. Greg Henry (CFO of 1Password) and Charly Kevers (CFO of Carta) invested as angels in the seed round, which means actual target buyers put their own money in. That's close to the signal I look for: people wanting the product so much they'll bet on it in its earliest form. The team has five years of working together through a product pivot, which shows the kind of flexibility I value — persistence about the goal, adaptable on the method. And the "verified numbers" versus LLM prediction distinction suggests Ingram's engineering team has thought carefully about where deterministic computation matters, which is a real technical insight in a space full of LLM wrappers. If the $430K ARR is real and growing at 15-20% month-over-month, Pluvo could outrun Datarails and Abacum the way a small fast animal outruns a large slow one.

But here's where my framework pushes back: this is a 14-person company with $5M in seed funding operating in a space where Datarails has 400+ employees, $175M in capital, 70% year-over-year revenue growth, and a $550M valuation — all targeting the exact same CFO buyer. Abacum has $101M and customers in 31 countries. Pluvo's advantages (AI-native architecture, agentic approach, faster iteration) are real but temporary. Every incumbent is racing to add exactly these capabilities. The competitive moat Pluvo describes — "codifying how your company actually works into a single governed model" — is a switching cost that only materializes if customers stay long enough to encode deep institutional knowledge. At three months in, that moat is a blueprint, not a wall. This isn't Stripe tackling PCI compliance and bank integrations that repelled every other developer for a decade. This is a hot space getting hotter.

What would change my mind is evidence I don't see in this dossier: a founder story that reveals the animal quality, weekly growth numbers showing the 5-7% compounding trajectory, or a technical moat that competitors genuinely cannot replicate — not just "we built AI-native" but something architecturally defensible that takes years to reproduce. The CFO angel investors are the strongest signal here, and if I could talk to Labrèche and Galarneau for ten minutes and hear how they think about the problem — not their pitch, but their actual mental model of what's broken in FP&A — I might revise upward. But on paper, Pluvo reads as competent operators executing well on a consensus idea in a crowded market. The two dimensions that most determine my verdict are Idea That Repels Before It Attracts (this idea attracts everyone, which is exactly my concern) and Organic Problem Discovery (domain experience exists but the origin story reads as market identification, not lived frustration). The traction is real enough to make me pause, but traction in a consensus-hot category doesn't create the kind of asymmetric return I'm looking for with my own money.
