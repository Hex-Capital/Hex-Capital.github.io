# Grade -- Paul Graham Evaluation

What catches my attention first is how these founders found this problem. They didn't read a report about the creator economy hitting $205 billion and decide to build something. They were running consumer apps, using creators as their growth channel, paying those creators out of spreadsheets, and discovering that when they switched from flat fees to performance-based compensation, their growth accelerated — but the payment logistics became unbearable. Lotanna was a content creator himself before founding XPO, an influencer fintech startup, before founding Creator Check, a creator screening tool. This is someone who has been circling this exact problem from three different angles over multiple years. That's what organic problem discovery looks like. The idea wasn't conceived in a brainstorming session. It grew out of years of accumulated frustration.

The schlep here is real, though it needs to be properly scoped. "Creator payments" broadly isn't a hidden problem — Lumanu has $20.6M, Trolley has $44.4M, and both are already in this space. If Grade were just another way to send money to creators, the schlep would already be occupied. But the specific schlep of performance-based pay is different. When compensation is variable — per view, per conversion, per engagement metric — you need a system that connects performance data to payment execution across 190 countries, multiple payout rails, and different compliance regimes. That's the schlep within the schlep. Traditional payroll assumes fixed amounts. AP tools assume invoices. Neither handles "pay this person $0.003 per view across 800 creators in 47 countries every two weeks." Most people building in the creator payments space unconsciously gravitate toward the simpler version of the problem — flat fee disbursement. Grade is tackling the version that requires stitching together performance APIs, payment rails, and variable compensation logic into a single workflow.

The founding team's track record is substantive. James sold a startup at 22, built Pray Screen to $40K MRR in two months and sold it, built FindMyVax for 55,000 Canadians, and built a campus social platform capturing 20% of Ryerson's student body. Lotanna raised over a million pounds for XPO, sold two SaaS businesses, and built Bible Buddy to 10K active users. They've collaborated on four mobile AI apps together before Grade. This is a team that ships and exits — repeatedly. What I don't have is a cereal-box story. I haven't seen them navigate a moment where everything was falling apart and they invented something lateral. Their resourcefulness signal comes from breadth and consistency of execution rather than from surviving a specific crucible. That's not a red flag at pre-seed, but it means I'm evaluating potential for resourcefulness rather than proven resourcefulness under extreme pressure.

The traction is where this gets genuinely interesting. $1M+ disbursed, 790+ creators paid, $380K processed in 30 days at 120% month-over-month growth on payout volume. Named customers include Valid (which manages $30M+ in ad spend) and Astra AI (2.1M+ users). For a pre-seed company with ~$500K in funding, processing real money through the platform for real businesses is meaningful. That's not a waitlist. That's not a prototype. Companies are routing actual payments through Grade right now. The 120% MoM growth on payout volume, if sustained even at a decaying rate, suggests they're finding product-market pull rather than pushing product into an uninterested market.

The competitive capital disadvantage is the honest concern. Lumanu and Trolley collectively have $65M in funding. The core technology — connecting performance APIs to payout disbursement — is technically reproducible by anyone with API access to Stripe, Wise, and PayPal. Grade's advantage is a product design insight (integrated performance-tracking-to-payment), not a deep technical moat. If Lumanu decides to add performance-based pay rules, they could build something comparable within a quarter. The "Grade" name also creates unnecessary friction — it's a common English word that a Nordic HR company already occupies on LinkedIn. These are solvable problems, but they're real. The question is whether execution speed and founder-market fit can outrun capital advantage. The founders' deep domain experience and working product give them a head start, but the race will get harder.

If this works, it works because performance-based creator compensation becomes the default model — replacing flat fees the way SaaS replaced perpetual licenses — and Grade becomes the infrastructure layer that makes that transition possible. The founders are positioned to see that shift before others because they lived through it themselves. The schlep of connecting variable performance data to global payment execution at scale is genuinely deterrent. The traction suggests real demand. What makes me want to write a check is the organic origin and the evidence of pull. What keeps the check modest is that the moat is product design, not technology, and the competitive landscape is already funded.
