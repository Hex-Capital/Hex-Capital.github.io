# Alchemize -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Alchemize is a regulated-services labor-leverage business wearing AI clothing — Samuel Fu and Robert Hou are building a *brokerage*, not a product, and a brokerage that cannot file a single CBP entry without a licensed human whose 2025 exam pass rate is sub-15%. The dimensional signature is brutal: the lowest score is on the only dimension I read first (permissionless leverage), and there is no compensating spike anywhere else. Alchemize sits in the structural opposite quadrant from where my checks land.

The leverage ladder runs from **Twitter (2007)** at the top — media, anyone can post, zero marginal cost, no gatekeeper — through **Notion (2013)** in the middle as code-leverage with product-led pull, down to **Anchorage Digital** at the bottom as my own exception case where regulated custody required an OCC charter and I waived the rule because the crypto-category timing call was strong enough to flip permission-into-moat. Alchemize sits *below* Anchorage on this ladder, not at it. The dossier names the gate explicitly — "operating as a customs brokerage requires a licensed broker" — and unlike Anchorage in 2014-2021, there is no pre-consensus category bet underneath; customs brokerage is a 2.88% CAGR business that has existed for a century, and Flexport already shipped an AI Customs Suite in October 2025 with a claimed 0.2% auditor error rate. This is capital-and-labor leverage requiring permission from CBP, not code-and-media leverage compounding the founder's specific knowledge. My thesis files this under "labor and capital require permission" (nav.al/product-media) and walks away.

The specific-knowledge ladder is the second wound. **Stack Overflow (2008)** sits at the top — Atwood and Spolsky were practicing developers writing the developer Q&A site they personally needed; **Notion (2013)** in the middle with Ivan Zhao's adolescent-onward obsession with personal productivity tools; at the bottom is the "ex-FAANG PM identified market opportunity in $X TAM" pattern I file as a red flag. Alchemize lands at the bottom rung. Samuel Fu's LinkedIn headline is "Building AI-enabled customs brokerage" with a Columbia degree and an unconfirmed Netflix mutual-connections signal; Robert Hou's background was not retrievable; neither has a documented customs license, freight role, or trade-compliance prior. This is the memo-founder profile responding to "trade policy chaos" framing rather than a founder who lived inside an HTS classification problem for a decade. Compare to Aravind Srinivas at **Perplexity (2022)** — Berkeley ML PhD specifically in retrieval — where I waived the marketplace rule precisely *because* the specific-knowledge gate cleared at top quartile. Alchemize cannot waive anything on this gate.

The marketplace ladder runs **Uber (2010)** → **Stack Overflow (2008)** → **Perplexity (2022)** as the single-sided exception, and Alchemize sits at or below the Perplexity rung as a single-sided B2B services contract — Power-of-Attorney filings lock customers in transactionally but the Nth importer does not make the (N-1)th importer's entries any cheaper or better. The pivot-room ladder runs **Twitter (2007)** pivoting off Odeo down through **Uber (2010)** going black-car-to-UberX down to vertical-SaaS niches with no adjacent path; Alchemize's $5.48B US TAM at 2.88% CAGR with a 10.45% digital sub-segment is a narrow vertical, and the site's gestures toward "procurement reconciliation" do not constitute the pivot vectors that let Twitter survive a podcasting collapse. The Buffett trio — intelligence, energy, integrity — has no public negative signal on Fu or Hou, so I default neutral; silence is not punished here, but it is not credit either.

My 36-month forecast for Alchemize: acqui-hire by Flexport, KlearNow, or a legacy broker (Livingston, C.H. Robinson) at sub-$25M for the team and the small book of importer relationships, with ARR somewhere between $800K and $3M at the time of sale. The mechanism is mechanical — Flexport already controls the importer relationship and now ships the AI feature; KlearNow has $69M to spend; Amari AI is selling the AI-workforce shovels to every legacy broker simultaneously. Alchemize's wedge is "we are the broker, not the software," but the broker-license barrier is held by every competitor, the customer relationship is owned by incumbents, and the 2.88% market growth means escape velocity requires share-take from players who are mobilizing on AI in 2025-2026.

The strongest bull case worth steel-manning is the Anchorage analogy — that the customs broker license is itself permission-as-moat, and the sub-15% exam pass rate plus aging workforce (avg age 43+) creates a labor-scarcity arbitrage where an AI-leveraged broker captures the spread between human-broker cost and software-speed delivery. This case stings because the underlying labor economics are real and Alchemize would, in that frame, be an arbitrage on a regulated bottleneck. But Anchorage required a category-timing call I made via **MetaStable (2014)** five years before the OCC charter; Alchemize has no equivalent pre-consensus category underneath it — customs brokerage in 2026 is a fully-narrated category with Flexport, KlearNow, Amari, and Altana already named. The bull case also requires founder-product-market fit on the regulatory layer (a licensed broker on the cap table or in the seat), and the dossier discloses neither. The condition that flips my verdict: Samuel Fu or Robert Hou turns out to be a licensed customs broker with 5+ years of CBP filing experience, *or* the round names a co-founder/early hire with that profile — the specific-knowledge gate alone, cleared at top quartile, would let me reconsider the leverage architecture under a regulated-moat exception. Until then, this is a pass.
