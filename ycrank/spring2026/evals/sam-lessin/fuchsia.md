# Fuchsia -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Tony Gao and two unnamed Yale-dropout co-founders are pitching AI agents that draft FCC/FDA/CE/UL technical files -- the most archetypal "AI agent for [regulated vertical]" pitch the Spring 2026 batch could produce, and Fuchsia is arriving eighteen months after Saphira AI (YC S24, $500K pre-seed September 2024) and two months after HardwareCompliance.com's March 2026 launch with an overlapping AI-agent workflow. The dimensional signature here is competent robotics technicians (Apollo Lab, ICRA 2026 co-authorship) running the single most consensus YC structural play in the batch, which means Fuchsia's only real positive dimension is founder conviction on robotics and every other dimension fails the price-discipline and sleeve filters I built the fund around.

Round pricing is the dimension that kills Fuchsia before anything else. The HIGH rung on my round-pricing ladder is Solana at $0.05/coin in 2018-2019 when crypto funds had quit the category entirely -- the mechanism is that I was paid to underwrite an asset two tier-1s had publicly walked away from, and the markups happened *after* my entry point cleared. Fuchsia is the structural inverse of that mechanism: three YC-funded AI-compliance entrants in eighteen months (Saphira S24, HardwareCompliance.com March 2026, Fuchsia Spring 2026) means YC partners have already pattern-matched the category as fundable, the deck-template is set, and any seed price on Fuchsia is being paid into a category where two earlier entrants are already absorbing the early-customer share. The dossier shows no tier-1 visibly passing, no founder concession on price, no strategic ambiguity in the asset -- it is the pass example I wrote into the persona file almost verbatim, just with "FCC certification" swapped in for "enterprise vertical."

The Pass Example founder in my framework -- "We're building an AI agent for [enterprise vertical], $25K MRR, three months out of YC, four tier-1 VCs already in" -- fails because the financing structure is a generic SaaS cap table that needs five more rounds before default-alive, and that exact failure mode appears in the Fuchsia dossier without modification. No pricing page on getfuchsia.ai, founder-led cal.com booking link as the entire GTM motion, two-sided lab marketplace cold-start to underwrite on top of the AI documentation layer, and zero indication that Fuchsia could be bootstrapped to $50M revenue without follow-on. Fuchsia is not Teamshares-shaped, not Mogilko-shaped, not a holdco, not a revenue-share -- it is the SaaS template I publicly call "no longer investible."

Tony Gao's ICRA 2026 paper on subsecond 3D mesh generation for robot manipulation is genuine technical depth, and that is the only dimension where Fuchsia clears the floor of my framework. The mechanism that would compound this over eighteen months is Tony's Apollo Lab network giving Fuchsia first-look access to humanoid-robotics deployments hitting UL/FDA certification walls before any incumbent has the documentation corpus to serve them. The problem is that Tony's conviction is documented in robotics perception research, not in compliance -- there is no public Tony Gao essay on why FCC parts 15/18 are broken, no three-year obsession with 510(k) submission failure modes, no opinionated thread on why UL's billing-hours model collapses under humanoid deployment volume. The conviction is real but it's pointing at a different problem than the one Fuchsia is selling.

Counterfactually on founder-as-brand: for Fuchsia's distribution score to flip a tier, Tony Gao would need either an owned audience of hardware engineers (a Substack on certification failure modes with 5K+ subscribers, a robotics-compliance podcast with named guests from Boston Dynamics or Figure) or the company would need a Discord/forum where hardware founders already congregate before encountering the product. The dossier shows neither -- @getfuchsia handle with unretrievable follower count, LinkedIn page with unretrievable follower count, zero Product Hunt presence, no press coverage. This is the exact opposite of the John Fish / Bookshelved profile that the Slow Creator Fund was designed to underwrite, and Fuchsia's GTM is a cal.com link on the homepage.

Category sleeve is where Sublime Security is the single load-bearing parallel and where Fuchsia fails the comparison. Sublime cleared my enterprise-security filter because it entered before the next consolidation wave in email-security and underwrote a category most security investors had abandoned for late-stage SOC SaaS. Fuchsia's category is not pre-consensus AI-compliance -- it is mid-consensus, with Saphira eighteen months ahead, HardwareCompliance.com two months ahead, and UL Solutions ($2.9B FY2024 revenue) and Intertek already cross-selling documentation services into existing test-lab customer relationships. The "AI is just more internet" line from the Lonsdale podcast applies in full: this is commodity AI exposure priced as venture exposure, not a venture moment.

My 36-month forecast for Fuchsia: acquihired by UL Solutions or Intertek for under $30M as a documentation-automation tuck-in, or dead after Saphira AI and HardwareCompliance.com split the early SaaS layer and Fuchsia runs out of runway before the lab marketplace reaches liquidity. The mechanism is that the AI-documentation layer is a feature incumbents can layer onto existing testing relationships faster than Fuchsia can build the lab supply side from a cold start, and the eighteen-month head start Saphira has on safety-critical hardware closes the most defensible subsegment before Fuchsia gets there.

The strongest bull case I owe Fuchsia is the Ro exception: a regulatory-heavy category where capital intensity itself becomes the moat because only VC-funded teams can clear the multi-jurisdiction licensing schleps. If humanoid robotics deployment volume hits UL/FDA certification walls in 2027-2028 and Tony Gao's Apollo Lab network gives Fuchsia first-look at Figure/1X/Tesla Optimus compliance pipelines, the robotics research depth becomes the bottleneck-clearing asset incumbents can't replicate. The dossier evidence that wins against this case is that Fuchsia is not positioning as robotics-specific -- the homepage spans FCC, FDA, FAA, CE, UL, ISO, RIA across the full TIC stack, which is the broad commodity play Saphira already narrowed to safety-critical robots eighteen months earlier. The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Fuchsia announces a named humanoid-robotics customer (Figure, 1X, Apptronik, Agility) with a signed multi-year certification contract and reframes the company as humanoid-compliance-specific -- at that point the Sublime Security category-bet analogy starts loading and I reconsider at the next round.
