# RefortifAI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me first: DRM has lost every single arms race it's ever entered. Music. Movies. Games. Denuvo gets cracked in days now. The entire history of software-only copy protection is a graveyard of companies that thought they could win an asymmetric fight against motivated adversaries with physical access to hardware. And now someone's proposing to do software-only DRM for AI model weights -- where the adversary literally has the GPU running the inference and can inspect memory at will. The dossier itself flags this: "Software-only DRM has historically been broken in adjacent domains." That's not a risk factor, that's the base rate. You need a very compelling reason to believe this time is different, and I don't see one.

The mispricing question cuts hard against this company. AI security is one of the hottest categories in venture right now. Palo Alto Networks dropped $500M+ on Protect AI. F5 bought CalypsoAI. HiddenLayer raised $56M. Every enterprise CISO has "AI security" on their budget. This is emphatically not a category the market has dismissed -- it's a category where capital is flooding in. Yes, the specific niche of "weight-level DRM" is less crowded than "prompt injection guardrails" or "ML supply chain scanning," but that's not the same as mispriced. When a $500M acquirer already owns the adjacent category and can trivially extend into your niche, your niche isn't mispriced -- it's on someone else's roadmap. Palo Alto's AI security division is one product decision away from offering weight protection alongside everything else they already do. That's not contrarian territory, that's a feature in someone else's platform.

The primitive test fails here too. DRM is not a new concept -- it's one of the oldest concepts in software. Applying it to AI weights is a new application of an existing approach. Nobody's going to describe a new behavior by saying "the RefortifAI of X." Compare this to what made Venmo interesting: it created the behavior of casual peer-to-peer payments. People started saying "Venmo me." That's a primitive. This is "we applied an existing security paradigm to a new file type." It's incremental, not generative. The "anti-cheat for AI models" framing is clever marketing, but anti-cheat systems are famously locked in perpetual arms races that require massive ongoing investment -- Riot's Vanguard team is enormous. Can a 3-person startup sustain that kind of continuous security R&D while also maintaining compatibility with rapidly evolving inference engines across multiple GPU architectures?

Now, the bull case, because I want to be honest about what would have to be true for this to work. If the proliferation of commercially licensed models -- not open-source, but proprietary weights that companies want to deploy on-premise -- becomes a $10B+ market, and if the hardware-level solutions (NVIDIA confidential computing, cloud TEEs) remain too inflexible for edge and multi-cloud deployments, then a software-agnostic DRM layer that drops into existing inference stacks could become the Widevine of AI. The team has the right technical DNA for this specific problem: RTL hardware design from TI and Tenstorrent, security research under Somesh Jha (who's a real name in the field), and AI engineering from KLA's semiconductor inspection group. That's not an accidental team -- they have the systems-level chops to build a hardened runtime. And the drop-in vLLM/SGLang compatibility is a smart adoption wedge. If they could demonstrate near-zero latency overhead on obfuscated weights, that would be a genuine technical moat. But I've seen zero benchmarks, zero traction signals, and the website returned a 403. The bull case requires multiple things to be simultaneously true: hardware solutions stay limited, software obfuscation stays ahead of attackers, a 3-person team keeps pace with vLLM's development velocity, AND model providers choose this over just going API-only.

The structural durability problem is what really kills this for me. NVIDIA is building confidential computing directly into their GPU architecture. When the hardware solution arrives -- and the semiconductor companies are investing billions to make it arrive -- a software-only approach becomes obsolete overnight. This is the opposite of what I look for. Metropolis works because you can't commoditize parking garages with software. Solana works because a consensus mechanism IS the infrastructure. RefortifAI is a software layer that sits on top of hardware it doesn't control, trying to protect against adversaries who have physical access to that hardware. That's a structurally losing position. The founders are clearly technical and competent, but competent people building in a structurally disadvantaged category is still a pass. I want sane people pitching insane ideas, and "better DRM" is neither insane nor, frankly, exciting enough to overcome the structural headwinds.
