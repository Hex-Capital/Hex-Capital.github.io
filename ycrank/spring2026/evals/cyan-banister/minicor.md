# Minicor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that strikes me immediately about Minicor is how consensus this category is right now. "AI agents that automate desktop workflows" -- I could walk into any demo day in San Francisco this week and hear that pitch five times. Browserbase just raised $40 million. Browser Use raised $17 million at seed. Skyvern came through YC two batches ago. UiPath is a public company with $1.4 billion in revenue trying to bolt AI onto their existing product. When I think about the investments that have defined my career -- SpaceX when private space was considered lunacy, Uber when ride-sharing was illegal, Crusoe when people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares was a prank -- the common thread is that *nobody else wanted in*. The best opportunities I've found were the ones where I had to explain to people why I wasn't crazy for writing the check. With Minicor, there's no explaining needed. Everyone already agrees AI automation is the future. That's the problem.

If I were sitting across from Faizaan and Saheed, my first question would be the one it always is: tell me your story. Not the business -- how did you get here? And what I see in the dossier is two competent enterprise engineers who previously built a low-code integration platform called Laminar, pivoted recently to desktop automation, and still have their sign-in portal running on app.laminar.run. That pivot pattern -- from one B2B infrastructure play to the hottest AI category of 2025-2026 -- is exactly the signal I've trained myself to be cautious about. I look for founders whose biography and company mission are inseparable, where the problem found them rather than the other way around. Saheed's journey from Lagos, building payment systems in Nigeria and eventually landing at Ford and Morgan Stanley, shows real resourcefulness and grit. Faizaan co-founding Software For Love as a non-profit suggests genuine values. But I don't see the biographical thread that says "I *must* solve desktop automation in healthcare." The problem feels found through market analysis, not through lived experience. That distinction matters enormously to me at the earliest stage.

The strongest bull case here is real, and I want to be honest about it. If that 25,000 patients/day number on their website represents actual production throughput -- not aspirational capacity -- then these founders have accomplished something remarkable for a pre-seed company founded in 2024. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certification aren't things you stumble into; they represent months of genuine compliance work and signal that healthcare customers are taking them seriously. The desktop and Citrix specialization is a legitimately underserved niche -- browser-only AI startups can't touch it, and incumbents like UiPath are locked into their deterministic scripting paradigm. Healthcare's legacy desktop dependency isn't going away for decades, which means the addressable surface is durable even as other industries migrate to cloud-native SaaS. If I were a more analytically-driven investor, I could see constructing a compelling case around this moat. The compliance certifications create switching costs, the desktop specialization creates differentiation, and the healthcare vertical creates regulatory barriers that deter casual competitors.

But I'm not that kind of investor. I invest through felt conviction, not constructed theses. And when I apply the filter I actually use -- would I drop everything to work for these founders? Does this company create economic access for people who currently lack it? Is there a "jolt" when I imagine its full potential? -- I come up short. This is enterprise efficiency tooling. The value accrues to healthcare organizations processing patient data faster, not to patients or underserved communities. It doesn't make an invisible system visible in the way Carta made equity transparent or Affirm made credit terms honest. It makes an already-visible workflow run without breaking. That's valuable -- it's just not the kind of value that gets me out of bed.

The co-founder dynamic is actually the bright spot here. Faizaan and Saheed met at the University of Ottawa, co-founded Software For Love together before starting this company, and have complementary enterprise backgrounds across Microsoft, Ford, and Morgan Stanley. After HQ Trivia taught me that even the perfect product dies when founders can't resolve conflict, I pay close attention to whether co-founders have been tested together. These two have. That matters, and I don't want to dismiss it. But a strong co-founder relationship in a consensus category still leaves me in the same place -- confident in the team's ability to execute, unconvinced this is where I should deploy my own capital.

The foundation model dependency also gives me pause. Their self-healing capability sits on top of vision models from Anthropic or OpenAI. If those providers ship first-party desktop automation products -- and Anthropic's computer use capabilities already point in that direction -- Minicor faces the classic platform risk of building on infrastructure that could swallow them. The compliance certifications and deployment specialization provide some insulation, but a well-funded competitor with the same compliance budget could replicate that layer. This isn't the kind of defensibility moat I look for -- it's a head start, not a structural advantage.

I'm passing on this one. Minicor is a competent team building in a real market with genuine early signals of traction. But it doesn't activate my framework. The category is too hot, the biographical connection too thin, the economic access signal absent, and the weirdness factor nonexistent. I wish them well -- the healthcare desktop automation niche is real and someone will build a significant business there. It's just not the kind of bet I write checks for.
