# Quanto -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Anderson Petergeorge didn't read a McKinsey report about the CPA shortage and decide to build a company. He was a practicing CPA who scaled a bookkeeping firm to 40 clients in six months using AI tooling, hit the capacity wall himself, and built the platform to break through it. That practice-to-product journey is the specific knowledge signal I look for. The knowledge Anderson carries — how messy client books actually look, which onboarding steps consume 80% of a CPA's time, what makes a firm owner say yes to a new tool — can't be extracted from an interview or a customer discovery sprint. It was earned one reconciliation at a time. Kajanth Nithiyananthan adds a complementary layer: his Atomic Build studio was already a 7-figure business building AI tech specifically for accounting firms before Quanto existed. That's two founders whose pre-existing expertise converges on the exact problem. I deduct slightly because Anderson's prior venture was Tanuki Pet, an unrelated e-commerce play — the accounting obsession has a start date, even if it's now genuine.

The leverage audit reveals a split personality. Quanto's AI agents — client outreach, QuickBooks diagnostics scoring books 0-100, automated reconciliation and month-end close — are classic zero-marginal-cost software. Each new firm onboarded doesn't require Quanto to hire another engineer. $800K contracted ARR on a team of six is $133K per person, which is the right kind of ratio at pre-seed. But the leverage is partly rented. Quanto's core product is built on QuickBooks' API — Intuit owns that surface area and has its own AI investments. Rented leverage is not permissionless leverage. If Intuit changes API terms or ships native AI onboarding tools, Quanto's product surface shrinks overnight. The distribution model compounds the concern: the website directs prospects to book discovery calls, which means human-touch sales, which means the growth path may require salespeople, which means labor leverage creeping in alongside the code leverage.

The principal-agent dynamics in Quanto's market are genuinely interesting and reminiscent of what I saw with Perplexity facing Google. Intuit, Thomson Reuters, and Wolters Kluwer sell to the accounting firms' clients or sell compliance software to large practices. Building AI that helps mid-market firms acquire and onboard clients faster would cannibalize these incumbents' existing partner channel economics. Anderson, as a principal with no legacy revenue to protect, can build what Intuit's product managers — agents of their own P&L — cannot justify. Basis, with $138M raised and a $1.15B valuation, targets the top 25 U.S. firms — a different market segment entirely. Digits at $29.9M revenue in 2024 is replacing the general ledger itself, not augmenting it. Quanto's positioning in the mid-market, layering onto existing QuickBooks workflows rather than replacing them, gives it a structural opening that the larger players' strategies leave exposed. Botkeeper shutting down in 2026 hands Quanto a pool of displaced firm customers at exactly the right moment.

The co-founder relationship gives me pause. No shared employer, no university overlap, no evidence of pre-existing trust between Anderson and Kajanth. The dossier infers the connection came through Toronto's startup ecosystem or Kajanth's accounting-AI studio work. Co-founders without pre-existing bonds are agents to each other — the internal principal-agent problem starts before the first customer. This isn't disqualifying, but it's a friction source I'd want to probe directly. Anderson's background — first in family to attend college, parents are Sri Lankan refugees, Harvard then Oxford — signals someone who builds out of drive rather than optionality. Running a real accounting practice before building the platform is the kind of revealed preference I weight heavily over stated passion.

The strongest bull case for Quanto centers on a structural comparison to what I experienced building AngelList. AngelList took a restricted resource — access to startup investing, previously gatekept by personal networks — and used software to expand who could participate. Quanto takes a different restricted resource — the capacity of an accounting firm, previously gatekept by the supply of hireable CPAs — and uses AI agents to expand it. The 75% of CPAs reaching retirement age isn't a temporary trend; it's a demographic fact that makes the capacity constraint permanent and worsening. A firm that can serve 40 clients with Quanto instead of 15 without it has experienced genuine access expansion. The $5M pipeline, even at the reported ~16% conversion rate, suggests the problem resonates with firm owners who feel the constraint daily.

The risk that would flip me is QuickBooks dependency crystallizing into platform risk. If Intuit ships native AI onboarding tools for QuickBooks Accountant — or restricts API access to third parties building on their workflow layer — Quanto's product collapses to the prospecting component alone, which is commoditized AI outreach. I'd want to see Quanto's roadmap for building proprietary data assets (the book health scoring benchmarks, pricing intelligence across firms) that exist independent of the QuickBooks integration surface. The competitive threat from Basis and Digits is real but structurally different: Basis is upmarket, Digits replaces the ledger. Quanto's wedge is the mid-market firm that uses QuickBooks and wants to grow. That wedge holds as long as the platform dependency doesn't break it.

The two dimensions that lock this into Invest rather than Neutral: Anderson's practice-to-platform journey producing genuine specific knowledge (not top-quartile singular, but meaningfully non-replicable at the intersection of CPA practice and AI product building), and the $800K contracted ARR on six people demonstrating that the leverage architecture works at current scale. The two dimensions that prevent Strong Invest: rented leverage on QuickBooks' platform, and a competitive landscape where Basis and Digits have 100x-200x more capital. Quanto's bet is that mid-market speed and founder-specific knowledge outrun well-funded competitors moving downmarket. That's a bet on specific knowledge plus principal commitment against capital — the exact structure I've seen win before.
