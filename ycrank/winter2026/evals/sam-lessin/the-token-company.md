# The Token Company -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that immediately jumps out: this company's core value proposition has a built-in expiration date. LLM inference costs are declining roughly 10x per year for equivalent performance. The Token Company sells compression at $0.05 per million tokens. Run that math forward two years and the tokens themselves cost almost nothing -- meaning the absolute dollar savings from compressing them converge toward zero. Building a business around making something cheaper when that something is already racing toward free is like launching a long-distance cost arbitrage service in 1999. The founders acknowledge this and try to pivot the narrative to "compression actually improves output quality," which is a genuinely interesting technical claim. But if it's true -- if removing tokens from prompts consistently makes LLM outputs better -- then OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google will build it into their own inference pipelines within a product cycle. You don't get to be middleware forever when the platforms on either side of you have every incentive and capability to absorb your function.

Let me apply my sane-person-insane-idea filter. Otso Veister? is clearly a talented kid -- 18 years old, programming since age 7, national awards in physics and problem-solving, ML engineering skills in PyTorch and CUDA. That's the kind of raw technical horsepower I respect. But "sane" in my framework doesn't mean "smart." It means operator credibility specifically relevant to the business being built. Anatoly Yakovenko's years at Qualcomm doing systems engineering made you believe he could actually build a high-throughput blockchain. An 18-year-old with a hackathon win and a two-month internship at Planmeca doesn't yet have the kind of battle scars that make me believe he can sell enterprise infrastructure middleware to companies processing hundreds of billions of tokens monthly. And the idea itself? "Compress LLM prompts to save money" is not insane. It's obvious. Microsoft Research published LLMLingua at EMNLP '23. Apple has published on hierarchical dynamic prompt compression. There's a NAACL 2025 survey paper documenting the breadth of existing approaches. A direct YC batchmate -- Compresr -- is doing the same thing with a four-person team of EPFL PhD researchers. When your idea is so consensus that your own accelerator batch has a competitor, you've failed the contrarian test entirely.

This company is the photographic negative of everything I look for. My "cherry on top" test asks: would this business be compelling if AI didn't exist? Remove AI and The Token Company literally doesn't exist -- there's no business underneath the software layer. That's the reddest of red flags in my framework. Craftwork is a great painting company that happens to use software. Metropolis operates parking infrastructure that happens to have a tech layer. The Token Company is pure software middleware sitting between a user's prompt and someone else's LLM API. There's no physical-world complexity, no regulatory position, no installed base that creates switching costs. The proprietary bear-1 and bear-1.1 models are ML artifacts that a well-funded competitor or a motivated academic lab can replicate -- and Microsoft already has. The "LLM providers won't build this because it cannibalizes their token revenue" argument sounds clever until you realize that providers are already cutting prices aggressively because total market expansion matters more than per-token margins. If compression improves quality, they'll ship it themselves because better outputs drive more usage.

The strongest bull case, and I want to take it seriously: what if the claim that compression *improves* LLM output quality is real and durable? The 268K-vote blind arena showing preference for compressed prompts, the +5% purchase lift at Pax Historia, the benchmark gains on financial QA and reading comprehension -- if these hold across diverse workloads, the value proposition shifts from "save money" to "get better results," which doesn't degrade with falling token prices. In that world, The Token Company becomes a quality layer, not a cost layer, and the narrative expands significantly. But this bull case requires believing that a two-person team in their first company can maintain a quality edge over Microsoft Research, Google DeepMind, and whatever Anthropic's internal prompt optimization team is doing. That's not an "insane idea backed by a sane person" -- that's hoping a structural advantage emerges from a position of resource asymmetry against the best-funded ML organizations on earth.

There's no protocol here, no new primitive that an ecosystem builds on. One customer's compression doesn't improve another customer's experience. There's no network effect, no compounding data advantage that creates winner-take-most dynamics. This is an optimization utility -- useful, maybe, but it lives and dies as an application on someone else's platform. The narrative doesn't expand; it actually contracts as token costs decline and LLM providers improve their own inference efficiency. Compare this to Venmo, which created a new payment primitive that became infrastructure for an entire generation's financial behavior, or Helium, which built a decentralized wireless protocol that other applications compose on top of. The Token Company compresses tokens. That's the story at founding and that's the story at exit, and the ceiling is visible from here.
