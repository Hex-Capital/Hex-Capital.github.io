# Sellara -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

When I ask founders "tell me your story," the answer I dread most is "I was at [Big Bank] and saw how inefficient everything was." Charles-André Jolly covered 180 institutional clients at Citi across Macro Credit Derivatives. Spencer Secord spent six years on Citi's Cross Asset and Fixed Income desks building workflow automations integrated into core risk systems. These are credentialed people who watched a problem up close and decided to leave and fix it. That's domain expertise — real, verifiable domain expertise — but it's not a biographical forge. Nobody was broken and rebuilt by this problem. Nobody lived inside a system that exploited them. The founding story of Sellara is career optimization dressed as mission: smart people who spotted a market gap from their trading terminals. When I hear that story, I know these founders might build a solid business. But I don't feel the inevitability I felt hearing about Crusoe's founders watching gas flares burn in North Dakota fields, or the Uber driver who handed me a card because the product had already changed his life before any investor validated it.

Sellara operates in what might be the single most consensus AI category of 2026. Forty-four percent of finance teams are projected to use agentic AI this year. Beam AI already has roughly $4.5 million in revenue doing adjacent BFSI automation in the same city. Blueflame AI closed a $5 million Series A for investment research AI. Every venture fund I know is actively looking for "AI agents for financial services" — it's practically a checkbox on pitch day. Nobody shifts in their chair when they hear this idea. Nobody passes because it sounds too weird or too dangerous. The 300-investor-pass test that Uber went through? Sellara would get term sheets from half the room. That's not a signal I chase. My career was built on writing the first check when others wouldn't write one — SpaceX when rockets were detonating, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive, Contraline when male birth control made VCs physically cringe. Sellara is the opposite of that discomfort. It's the kind of deal where the hardest question you'll face at a dinner party is "which AI-for-finance startup did you pick?"

The comparison that keeps surfacing in my mind is Affirm — another fintech company, another attempt to improve financial systems. But the structural parallel breaks down at the exact point that matters to me. Max Levchin built Affirm to expose the hidden compound interest that credit card companies buried in fine print, extracting billions from consumers who couldn't see the real cost. That's an invisible system made visible — power shifting from gatekeepers to ordinary people. Sellara automates booking, allocations, reconciliations, and compliance for banks, broker-dealers, and hedge funds. The people doing these workflows understand them completely — they just find them tedious. There is no hidden tax. There is no information asymmetry being weaponized against the vulnerable. Sellara is efficiency software for the most sophisticated financial operators on the planet. When I was fifteen and homeless, navigating systems designed to be opaque to people without power, the institutions Sellara serves were on the other side of that wall. I don't begrudge companies that serve the powerful, but my framework doesn't light up for them either.

The strongest bull case for Sellara is the defensibility emerging beneath the surface. SOC II and ISO 27001 certifications at pre-seed are genuinely unusual — most startups at this stage haven't invested that kind of time and capital into compliance infrastructure. Once Sellara's agents are configured inside a regulated institution and integrated with Bloomberg and internal risk systems, the switching costs become real; nobody at a bank rips out a validated, compliant AI vendor lightly. If the claimed 60%+ outperformance over leading LLMs on financial tasks reflects proprietary model training on domain-specific data rather than prompt engineering on commodity models, that's a compounding moat. Secord's background building workflow automations at Citi and then rebuilding them from scratch at Sellara suggests the team understands what breaks in production at these institutions. For this to become a Long Journey-scale outcome, the proprietary model advantage would need to be real and validated, the Citi Rolodex would need to convert into paying contracts before Beam AI or Accenture's consulting arms saturate the same buyers, and the enterprise sales cycle — which the dossier notes can exceed twelve months in regulated finance — would need to compress enough that $500K in Speedrun capital doesn't run dry before revenue arrives.

Sellara is a competent team building a reasonable product in a real market at a genuine inflection point. None of that is sufficient for me to write a personal check. The two dimensions that locked my verdict are the absence of biographical forge — Jolly and Secord's Citi tenure is credential, not crucible — and the active presence of consensus rather than contrarian conviction. I can't find the invisible system, I can't find the founder who was forged by the problem, and I can't find the room of VCs shifting uncomfortably. I wish them well, but this isn't a Long Journey bet.
