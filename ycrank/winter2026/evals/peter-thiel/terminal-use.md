# Terminal Use -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything. E2B has raised $32 million. Daytona has raised $24 million. Runloop has $7 million. Modal Labs has $111 million and is reportedly in talks at a $2.5 billion valuation. OpenAI has launched Frontier with hosted agent runtimes bundled directly into their platform. And now Terminal Use enters with a YC check and four repositories on GitHub -- each with zero stars -- to build "Vercel for background agents." This is not a market with a secret. This is a market where every venture capitalist in San Francisco has already written the same memo about the same opportunity. When five well-funded companies and a model provider are all racing toward the same thesis, the Girardian dynamics are already in motion: imitation breeds competition, competition destroys margins, and no one achieves monopoly. The question I ask first is always "what important truth does this company see that no one else sees?" Here, the answer appears to be: nothing that E2B, Daytona, Runloop, and Modal Labs don't also see. "AI agents need production infrastructure" is not a secret -- it is a headline.

The specific architectural claim -- that persistent filesystems and long-running background agents require fundamentally different infrastructure than ephemeral sandboxes -- is a product distinction, not a contrarian insight about the world. It's the kind of feature-level differentiation that sounds meaningful in a pitch deck but dissolves under competitive pressure. Daytona already offers "stateful sandboxes." E2B is expanding beyond ephemeral execution. Modal is building out its platform. These are well-understood engineering patterns: Docker containerization, checksums for file sync, git-native versioning, auto-scaling. A competent infrastructure team at any of these companies -- or at AWS, for that matter -- could build filesystem forking and task-pinned versioning in a quarter. The technology is solid engineering work, but it is not a 10x discontinuity. It does not make the old way structurally obsolete; it makes it somewhat more convenient. And "somewhat more convenient" is the operating range where competition grinds everyone to zero.

The "Vercel for X" framing itself betrays the problem. When a company defines itself by analogy to an existing company applied to a new domain, it is making a 1-to-n argument by construction. "We'll do what Vercel did, but for agents" is not zero to one -- it is the explicit admission that someone else already went from zero to one and you are copying their model. Vercel itself succeeded because it captured a specific developer workflow (Next.js deployment) before the category existed, creating genuine switching costs through framework integration. Terminal Use has no equivalent lock-in mechanism. The Slack-based Woz product creates some workflow embedding, but Slack integration is a distribution channel, not a structural moat -- it can be replicated by any competitor in weeks.

I should note the strongest argument for this company, because the founders deserve that. All three came from Palantir, which I co-founded and which I understand better than most. Palantir's engineering culture produces people who understand how to deploy complex software in hostile, high-stakes enterprise environments. Vivek Raja led one of the largest agent use cases across US hospitals -- that is not trivial domain expertise. He has seen the production gap firsthand at the kind of scale where most developers have never operated. The team's shared Palantir context means they have a working relationship forged under pressure, which matters. If you believe that the agent infrastructure market will consolidate around a winner the way developer platforms sometimes do -- and that the persistent-filesystem, long-running-agent niche is the right wedge -- then a Palantir-trained team with direct deployment experience is well-positioned to execute. The timing is genuinely favorable: Claude Agent SDK and Codex SDK launched recently, creating immediate demand. Gartner's projection that 40% of enterprise applications will embed AI agents by 2026 suggests the wave is real. The bull case is that Terminal Use's specificity (filesystem-native, long-running, framework-agnostic) carves out precisely the right niche before the market consolidates, and Palantir-grade engineering quality becomes the differentiator when every competitor is building similar features.

But I do not find this bull case persuasive. The structural problem is that Terminal Use occupies a middleware layer squeezed between model providers above (who are bundling hosting) and cloud providers below (who sell the raw compute). OpenAI has already moved to bundle agent runtimes with its SDK -- this is exactly what happens when your value proposition sits between the platform and the customer. Anthropic could do the same tomorrow. The company's framework-agnostic positioning is a defensive response to this risk, but framework-agnosticism is a feature, not a moat. And the massive TAM projections ($183 billion by 2033) confirm my concern: when the projected market is enormous and the thesis is consensus, you are not describing a monopoly opportunity. You are describing a competitive market that will attract capital from every direction until returns are ground to nothing. This is not a company built around a secret. It is a company built around a widely recognized market opportunity, entering late relative to better-funded competitors, with technology that is competent but not discontinuous. The founders' Palantir background gives me respect for their capability, but capability deployed into a crowded market without a monopoly path is capability wasted.
