# matforge -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The single most interesting thing about Matforge is a claim I can't fully verify: that Akash Ramdas discovered materials during his Stanford PhD that were adopted into Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. If that means what it sounds like -- that the two largest logic foundries on Earth looked at this person's work and said "yes, we're building with this" -- then Akash may be one of a very small number of humans with the credibility to sell AI-driven materials discovery to semiconductor fabs. That relationship is the kind of thing money genuinely cannot replicate. Periodic Labs can raise $300 million and CuspAI can take Samsung's money, but neither of them has a founder who already put materials into the pipeline at TSMC. The dossier flags that this could be academic roadmap citations rather than commercial procurement commitments, and that ambiguity matters enormously. The difference between "Intel cited my paper" and "Intel is qualifying my material for production" is the difference between an interesting PhD and a generational founder-market fit. I'd need to hear Akash tell me exactly what happened -- walk me through the moment Intel said yes, what the process looked like, whether there's an ongoing relationship or a one-time citation.

What gives me real pause is that AI-for-materials is not a weird idea right now. It's the opposite. Over a billion dollars flowed into this category in 2025 alone. When a16z leads a $300 million seed round for Periodic Labs and Lila Sciences raises $550 million, this is consensus investing at industrial scale. I've spent my career trying to find the pockets of dreamers and misfits before the money shows up, and the money has already shown up here in force. The semiconductor-specific focus is a smart narrowing -- nobody else is exclusively targeting fabs and datacenters -- but it's a strategic subdivision of a hot category, not the kind of thing that makes people uncomfortable when you describe it at a dinner party. Nobody's going to tell Akash he's crazy for applying AI to materials discovery in 2026. Contrast that with Crusoe Energy, where the founding insight -- mine Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota -- made people genuinely question whether the founders had lost their minds. That discomfort is the signal I look for, and I don't feel it here.

I also can't do what I most need to do with these founders, which is hear their story. Not their Stanford credentials or their IIT-JEE rank -- those are impressive, and Advaith's All India Rank 330 out of 1.3 million students tells me something about raw intellectual horsepower. But I want to know what happened before the credentials. Why materials? Why semiconductors? Is there something in Akash's biography that made this problem personal -- not just academically interesting, but the thing he'd keep working on if every investor on Earth said no? The best founders I've backed had a biographical thread connecting them to their problem that went deeper than their resume. Travis had years of obsession with the taxi medallion racket before Uber existed. The Crusoe founders independently arrived at their gas-flare insight from lived experience with energy waste. I don't see that thread here, and its absence means I'm evaluating credentials rather than conviction -- which is a fundamentally different exercise for me.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about it. If Akash genuinely has warm relationships at Intel and TSMC R&D, and if the prediction-to-synthesis gap that MIT Technology Review flagged is something his specific background uniquely equips him to bridge, then this two-person team could build an extraordinarily defensible position. The customer base is tiny -- fewer than ten major foundries globally -- which means you don't need a sales army, you need deep trust with a handful of decision-makers. And the co-founder pairing is genuinely complementary: Akash brings the domain depth (semiconductor materials, 15,000+ materials screened in his PhD) and Advaith brings production-grade agentic AI experience from Persona AI and Luma Labs. They've known each other since IIT Madras in 2015, co-authored a book together, and chose complementary graduate paths that now converge perfectly on this problem. That's the kind of co-founder alignment that survives hard moments. For the right investor -- someone who evaluates primarily on technical credentialing and market structure -- this could be a strong bet. The structural argument is sound: incumbent materials suppliers have no incentive to discover their own replacements, fabs are capacity-constrained on exploratory R&D, and the AI capability threshold for autonomous scientific workflows just crossed in the last eighteen months.

But I'm not that investor. I invest from felt conviction, not structural analysis, and I need to feel that biographical connection between founder and problem before I write a check from my own money. The consensus capital flooding into AI-for-materials also works against my framework -- I'm looking for the deals that other investors find too strange or too early, and this category is neither. The hard-space complexity is genuinely appealing to me (semiconductors are one of the most intricate supply chains humans have ever built, and navigating fab qualification processes requires exactly the kind of regulatory comfort I developed at IronPort), but it's not enough on its own. I'd want to meet Akash and Advaith, hear the story behind the story, and understand whether the Intel/TSMC relationship is as deep as it sounds. If it is, and if their personal narratives reveal the kind of grit I can't see in the dossier, I'd reconsider. But based on what I can evaluate today, this is a pass for me -- a respectful one, because the founder-market fit credentials are genuinely rare.
