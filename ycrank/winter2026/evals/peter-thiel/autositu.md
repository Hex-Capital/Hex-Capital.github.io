# AutoSitu -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know. PermitFlow has raised $91 million. CivCheck was acquired by Clariti. CodeComply.Ai is selling seats at $3,000 a year. Archistar has $22.7 million and $6.7 million in revenue. Blitz AI claims to be the "most widely adopted AI plan review platform in American local government." AutoSitu is entering a market where at least five funded companies are pursuing overlapping theses with overlapping technology. This is the opposite of a secret -- it is a consensus dressed in slightly different go-to-market clothing. When every VC in the ecosystem can independently arrive at "AI will accelerate municipal permitting," the insight has zero information content. The mimetic dynamics here are textbook: companies imitating each other's positioning, differentiating on which side of the permitting desk they sit on, competing for the same municipal budgets with the same underlying LLM capabilities. Nobody wins this game at monopoly margins.

The claimed differentiation -- targeting the municipal reviewer side rather than the developer side -- is a positioning choice, not a secret. AutoSitu argues that embedding agents into the city's cross-department workflow is structurally different from PermitFlow's developer-facing approach. But this is a distinction without a moat. The core technology -- LLM-based extraction and reasoning over regulatory text -- is commodity infrastructure. Any well-funded competitor can build the same multi-department coordination layer. The dossier itself acknowledges this directly: "The core technology (LLM-based document analysis against regulatory text) is reproducible. Differentiation depends on depth of jurisdiction-specific code coverage." When the dossier written about your company concedes that your technology is reproducible, the 10x superiority test fails on its face.

The founder combination is genuinely thoughtful. Lin's Harvard GSD training in urban planning gives him the domain fluency that most AI-for-government startups lack -- he understands how zoning codes actually work, not just how to parse them. Zhai's seven patents and production AI experience at GM and McLaren suggest real engineering capability, not academic posturing. This is the strongest signal in the dossier. But founder-market fit solves the competence question, not the monopoly question. Competent founders in a competitive market build good businesses that earn mediocre returns. The question is not whether these founders can build a working product -- clearly they can, given 1,500+ plan reviews and 11 named city engagements. The question is whether that product creates a position no one else can occupy.

The bull case would require believing that jurisdiction-specific regulatory data is a genuine compounding moat -- that the first company to deeply encode San José's specific code interpretations across departments becomes structurally impossible to displace. There is something to this argument. Municipal codes are genuinely fragmented, each city's ordinances are idiosyncratic, and the tacit knowledge embedded in how reviewers actually interpret ambiguous provisions is hard to replicate from the outside. If AutoSitu achieves deep integration into a critical mass of cities, the switching costs could become real -- ripping out an AI system that has learned your city's interpretive norms across five departments is operationally painful. The Palantir analogy would be that government integration itself becomes the moat. But Palantir's secret was that anti-fraud algorithms could transform intelligence -- a non-obvious insight that most experts rejected. AutoSitu's insight -- that AI can speed up permitting -- is something the Mayor of Seattle is issuing executive orders about. When government officials are publicly demanding what you're building, you do not have a secret. You have a market with a line of competitors at the door.

Eleven named city engagements at pre-seed is legitimately impressive execution, and it suggests these founders can navigate procurement cycles that destroy most startups. But breadth of engagement is the opposite of the small-market monopoly strategy. Facebook owned Harvard completely before expanding. AutoSitu is spreading across San José, Seattle, Long Beach, Boston, Coral Gables, Los Angeles, Dallas, San Mateo, Clark County, Honolulu, and San Francisco simultaneously. This is competitive thinking -- cover as much ground as possible before rivals do -- not monopoly thinking. A monopoly thinker would own one city so completely that every other city calls asking how to replicate it.

I pass. The founders are capable, the market is real, and the traction is genuine. But this is a 1-to-n company -- applying known AI capabilities to a known problem in a market where multiple well-funded players share the same thesis. There is no secret here, and without a secret, there is no monopoly, and without a monopoly, the returns converge to what the competitive structure allows, which in government SaaS is modest.
