# Minicor -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out at me about Minicor is the schlep. I wrote years ago that "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps," and managing Windows VMs running legacy desktop applications for healthcare and automotive companies is about as schlepy as it gets. Nobody at a hackathon dreams up "I want to orchestrate Citrix sessions interacting with dental practice management software." The reason Stripe sat there for a decade waiting to be built was that dealing with banks and payment infrastructure was intimidating and boring. Minicor's version of that is dealing with Epic EHR interfaces, CDK Global dealer management systems, and SOC 2 Type II audits — at a five-person pre-seed company. The fact that they already have SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance tells me something important about these founders: they did the hard, unglamorous work that most teams at this stage wouldn't touch. That's not a compliance checkbox — it's a determination signal.

The founders fit a pattern I've come to trust. Faizaan and Saheed met in a UX class in 2019, built a non-profit together during the pandemic, and started working on Minicor in 2023. That's about five years of working together before going through YC. I've said that startups do to the relationship between founders what a dog does to a sock — if it can be pulled apart, it will be. These two have been through enough together that I'd bet the sock holds. More importantly, their domain expertise maps directly to the problem. Saheed spent three years as a senior engineer at Ford and previously worked at Morgan Stanley — exactly the kind of legacy enterprise environments where the desktop applications Minicor targets live. Faizaan worked at Phoenix, a YC W21 company, doing system integration work. They didn't think up this idea sitting in a coffee shop. They noticed it because they lived inside the pain. "The verb you want to be using with respect to startup ideas is not 'think up' but 'notice.'"

The architecture decision is worth examining. They store automation as deterministic code and use the AI agent only for recovery and edge cases. This is the kind of insight you arrive at by actually building the thing both ways and watching it break. Pure AI agents are unreliable for production healthcare workflows — 80-85% accuracy means thousands of errors per day at the scale they claim (25,000 patients/day). Pure RPA is brittle and breaks every time a vendor updates their UI. The hybrid is the non-obvious answer, and the claimed 93-96% accuracy, if it holds in production, is the difference between usable and unusable in regulated environments. At 25,000 patient interactions daily, each percentage point of accuracy represents hundreds of fewer errors — which in healthcare isn't a product metric, it's a patient safety threshold.

The obvious objection is commoditization. Anthropic shipped Computer Use in public beta in October 2024. Anyone can build desktop automation now. UiPath has already added an AI Healing Agent feature. So why won't this get crushed? The answer is the same reason Stripe wasn't killed by the existence of payment APIs — the hard part isn't the API call, it's everything around it. Managed Windows VMs, Citrix deployment, HIPAA compliance, per-application tuning, observability with full video recordings, Slack failure notifications. The model layer is commodity; the infrastructure and compliance layer is the schlep that competitors would rather not do. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors." UiPath is bolting AI onto a legacy RPA platform. Minicor built from scratch for this specific use case. Those are fundamentally different products even if the marketing decks sound similar.

What gives me pause is the size of the initial wedge. They're selling to AI companies that need legacy system access — a customer segment that is itself nascent. If AI company adoption in legacy industries grows slowly, Minicor's near-term market could be quite small. But I've learned to see narrow-and-deep as a feature at this stage. "You can either dig a hole that's broad but shallow, or one that's narrow and deep, like a well." The broader vision — becoming the integration layer for every legacy desktop application across healthcare, automotive, supply chain, financial services — is genuinely large. The way to do really big things does seem to be to start with deceptively small things. And the "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" claim, if accurate, suggests they've already proven the architecture works at meaningful throughput.

These are first-time founders without exits or exceptional pedigrees, and I won't pretend otherwise. But I've funded enough startups to know that the founders who end up building the biggest companies aren't always the ones who look most impressive on paper. What matters is whether they're relentlessly resourceful — and getting SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA compliance, and production-scale deployment as a five-person team founded in 2024 suggests they are. The relationship is battle-tested, the domain expertise is genuine, and they're willing to do work that makes other founders' eyes glaze over. That combination is harder to find than a more polished resume.
