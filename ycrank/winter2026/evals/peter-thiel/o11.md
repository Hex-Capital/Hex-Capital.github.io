# o11 -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

"Microsoft Copilot, But It Actually Works." That is o11's own tagline on Product Hunt. I want to dwell on this because it reveals everything about what this company is and is not. When a startup defines itself as an improved version of the incumbent's product, built inside the incumbent's platform, distributed through the incumbent's marketplace, it has not escaped competition -- it has embraced it as its identity. This is mimetic rivalry in its most structurally pure form. o11 looks at Microsoft and says: we will do what you do, but better, inside your house, using your tools, for your customers. René Girard would have recognized this immediately -- the model and the rival are the same entity. The startup has chosen to define itself through the thing it opposes, which means it can never differentiate from it. Every improvement o11 ships is a feature request Microsoft can absorb. Every customer o11 wins is a customer Microsoft has a structural path to reclaim by improving Copilot, adjusting API permissions, or simply bundling deeper vertical features into the existing $30/month Copilot subscription. This is not zero to one. This is 0.95 to 1.05.

What is the secret here? The implied thesis is that Microsoft cannot adequately serve finance, consulting, and legal knowledge workers with its horizontal Copilot offering, and that a specialized third-party add-in will permanently occupy this vertical niche. But this is not a truth that most smart people disagree with -- it is the consensus thesis of every enterprise SaaS investor in San Francisco. "Vertical beats horizontal" is the most agreed-upon framework in B2B software. The market already reflects this: Gamma has $100M ARR building AI presentations, Plus AI occupies the exact same add-in niche for Slides and PowerPoint, Templafy has raised $147M for enterprise document management, and Macabacus serves finance professionals with Office formatting tools. There is no secret here. There are multiple well-funded players pursuing the same thesis in the same market, which is the exact signature of a competitive Red Ocean. When the landscape looks like this, no one has a contrarian insight worth investing in.

The strongest signal in this dossier is Ajay Misra. Leading AI at Cal AI, a product that reached $40M ARR and 10M users before acquisition by MyFitnessPal, is a genuinely impressive credential for someone this young. His Mayo Clinic research publications add technical credibility. Aryah Oztanir at 19 with the Morehead-Cain scholarship and multiple prior ventures shows the kind of raw energy and ambition I respect. These are capable young founders. But capability deployed in the wrong direction does not create value -- it destroys it through opportunity cost. The founders describe their product as solving problems for "bankers, lawyers, and consultants" despite having no direct experience in finance, law, or consulting. Their conviction appears oriented toward building a better product rather than toward a specific vision of how knowledge work will be fundamentally restructured. "We make better charts inside PowerPoint" is an engineering goal, not a thesis about the future. I see no evidence that either founder has articulated a view of the world that intelligent people would argue against.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that Microsoft will permanently neglect vertical optimization for its most lucrative enterprise customers, that the Office Add-in platform will remain open and permissive toward a competitor that explicitly markets itself against Microsoft's flagship AI product, and that o11's execution advantage in finance-specific features creates switching costs deep enough to resist both Microsoft's resources and Gamma's $91M in funding. If all three are true, o11 could build meaningful revenue in a niche -- perhaps becoming the Macabacus of the AI era, a durable but subscale vertical tool. But even this optimistic scenario produces a modest outcome, not a power-law return. The company that dominates AI-powered knowledge work will either be Microsoft itself or something that makes Microsoft Office structurally obsolete -- not something that lives inside Office as a dependent add-in. The last mover in this market will be whoever controls the platform, and o11 does not control the platform.

The go-to-market also fails the beachhead test. Featured logos span Wells Fargo, Bank of America, Yale, Mayo Clinic, Duke, and UNC -- that is finance, healthcare, and higher education in a single breath. "Hundreds of companies across the globe" with no disclosed revenue, for a product priced at $20/month, suggests broad free-tier adoption without concentrated dominance in any single segment. Compare this to PayPal's deliberate monopolization of eBay power sellers, or Facebook's absolute saturation of Harvard before expanding to a single additional campus. o11 is going after a large, generic market from day one, which in my framework signals competitive rather than monopoly thinking. The pricing umbrella created by Microsoft Copilot at $30/user/month caps o11's value capture -- customers will always benchmark against the platform-native alternative.

I have invested in young founders before -- Zuckerberg was 19, Buterin was 19. Age is not the issue. The issue is that those founders possessed secrets about the world that the consensus actively rejected. Zuckerberg understood that real-identity networking would create an unbreakable monopoly when the prevailing wisdom favored anonymity. Buterin understood that programmable money would enable decentralized computation when most computer scientists dismissed cryptocurrency as a toy. o11's founders believe they can build better AI features inside Microsoft Office than Microsoft can. This is not a secret. It is a wager on execution speed against the most well-resourced software company in history, inside that company's own ecosystem. I pass.
