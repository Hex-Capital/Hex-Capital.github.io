# Byteport -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: an 18-year-old drops a full-ride scholarship at Georgia Tech to build enterprise file transfer infrastructure and sell it to defense contractors and satellite operators. There's an audacity to that decision that I recognize -- the willingness to walk away from the safe path when you believe you've seen something others haven't. And his Fermilab and CERN experience is genuinely relevant. The CMS experiment at the LHC moves petabytes across a global network, and if you've lived inside that data infrastructure as a research associate, you've felt the pain of TCP bottlenecks in your bones. That's real founder-market fit through direct experience, not from reading a market report. The question I keep coming back to, though, is whether technical proximity to the problem is the same thing as the kind of biographical commitment I look for -- the thing that keeps you going when your company is in crisis at 2 AM and every rational person would quit.

When I evaluate founders, I start with their story, not their business. Jayram Palamadai's trajectory -- Illinois Mathematics and Science Academy, Fermilab research associate, CERN, Netflix -- is intellectually extraordinary. He's clearly brilliant, and the dropout decision from a full-ride shows conviction. But I'm listening for something specific: the hardship that forged the person, the obstacle that makes this founder uniquely equipped to survive the inevitable near-death experiences every startup faces. I think about how Travis Kalanick had years of battle scars from previous startups when I met him, or how the Crusoe founders had arrived at their insight through a path nobody else would have walked. Jayram's path is impressive but it reads as a trajectory of achievement, not adversity. That doesn't disqualify him -- some of the best founders I've backed were simply extraordinary at a young age -- but it means my primary evaluation channel isn't giving me a strong signal.

The company itself doesn't trigger what I look for in terms of non-consensus conviction. UDP-based file transfer acceleration is a known technique with known players -- IBM Aspera has been doing this for over a decade, Signiant is doing $42.5 million in revenue, and there are open-source protocols like UDT and QUIC chipping away from below. The developer-first, SDK-driven approach to disrupting Aspera's bloated enterprise model is a reasonable strategy, and it reminds me of patterns I've seen work in other infrastructure categories. But "faster, cheaper, more developer-friendly version of an existing enterprise product" is one of the most consensus investment theses in Silicon Valley. This isn't the kind of idea that makes other investors uncomfortable or confused. Nobody hears "faster file transfer" and thinks you're crazy. The best investments I've made looked bad to most investors at the time -- this one looks sensible to most investors, which is actually the less promising signal in my experience.

The bull case I want to genuinely engage with: this could be infrastructure at a major inflection point. AI training clusters are generating data at scales that didn't exist three years ago. Autonomous vehicle fleets, satellite constellations, robotics -- these are all producing data volumes that cross a threshold where standard transfer becomes a material bottleneck. I backed SpaceX when private space was absurd, Crusoe when flare gas computing was a joke, and Together.ai as AI infrastructure was emerging. If Jayram has actually built a protocol that delivers on the "50x faster than TCP" claim, and if the AI data movement problem grows as fast as it might, this could be the kind of infrastructure play that compounds in value as the underlying market explodes. The multi-language SDK approach creates real switching costs once embedded. I can see the version of this story where Byteport becomes the default data movement layer for the AI stack, the way Aspera became default for media but across a much broader surface area. That's a genuinely large outcome.

But even granting that bull case, three things hold me back. First, there's no evidence of any traction -- no revenue, no named customers, no LOIs, no working prototype in customer hands. A LinkedIn post with 660 likes isn't market validation. Second, the target customers -- defense networks, satellite ground stations, autonomous drone fleets -- have procurement cycles and compliance requirements that would challenge a well-resourced team, let alone a solo 18-year-old founder. My comfort with regulated and complex spaces comes from seeing founders who can actually navigate those spaces, and I see no evidence of that capability here. Third, and most fundamentally for how I invest: this company doesn't create economic access or make invisible systems visible for people who need it. It makes enterprises more efficient at moving data. That's valuable, but it's not the kind of positive-sum outcome that draws me in. When I backed Uber, I could see immediately how it would create income for drivers. When I backed Affirm, I could see how transparent credit would help people trapped by hidden fees. I don't see that dimension here.

The solo founder question deserves a direct comment. I don't have a blanket rule against solo founders, but the specific combination -- 18 years old, solo, selling enterprise infrastructure to defense and satellite companies -- creates a credibility and capacity gap that concerns me. This isn't a consumer app where a brilliant solo technical founder can ship and iterate. Enterprise infrastructure sales require building trust with procurement teams that are evaluating vendor stability and longevity. I'd want to see either a co-founder with enterprise sales experience or early customer relationships that demonstrate Jayram can bridge that gap on his own. Neither is present in the dossier.
