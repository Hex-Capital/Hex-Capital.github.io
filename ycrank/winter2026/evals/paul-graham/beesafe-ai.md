# BeeSafe AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about BeeSafe is where the idea came from. These founders didn't read that pig-butchering losses hit $10 billion and think "fraud is a big market." They spent years inside UCSD's systems security lab — under Stefan Savage and Geoffrey Voelker, who've been studying the economics of cybercrime for over a decade — and they built the academic system that defines this entire approach. Their paper "Victim as a Service" is literally the first published framework for automated scam engagement. The idea grew out of their research lives the way Dropbox grew out of Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive. Different flavor of organic — academic rather than economic necessity — but unmistakably genuine. You don't spend four years getting a PhD in adversarial NLP because you're chasing a market trend.

The schlep here is what really gets my attention. Everyone in fraud prevention looks at the transaction layer — behavioral biometrics, payment anomalies, risk scores. That's the comfortable place to build. BeeSafe does something most founders wouldn't even consider: they deploy AI agents that impersonate victims and sustain weeks-long conversations with active criminals. Think about the layers of difficulty that involves. Legal complexity around engaging with criminal actors. Platform terms-of-service compliance. Building AI that can convincingly maintain rapport with humans whose entire profession is manipulation. Selling an "offensive intelligence" product to bank compliance teams who have never purchased anything like it. And then the adversarial adaptation problem — scammers will learn to detect these agents, creating an ongoing arms race where only the team with the deepest expertise survives. This is exactly the kind of multi-dimensional schlep that causes smart founders to unconsciously look the other way. BioCatch, Featurespace, Hawk — none of them will build this because it sits entirely outside their existing sales motion. Their reps sell to fraud-ops teams; BeeSafe's product sells to threat intelligence buyers. That's structural protection, not just a feature gap.

The team is deeply technical in the right way. Three PhDs across NLP and security, with research stints at Google AI, Microsoft Research, Salesforce, and Censys. They built CHATTERBOX themselves. Daniel has 38 public repos on GitHub. Ariana has published at IEEE S&P and SIGCOMM. These aren't business school graduates who plan to hire engineers — they are the engineers. The co-founder relationships are also solid: Daniel and Nikolai share CMU roots, all three converged at UCSD under the same advisors, and they co-authored the foundational paper together. This isn't a team that met at a startup weekend. They've been intellectually collaborating for years. My one hesitation is the academic-to-startup transition. Publishing papers and shipping product are different disciplines. I've seen brilliant researchers struggle when the metric shifts from citation count to revenue growth.

Where I have less confidence is in the evidence of real demand. They claim their intelligence has "enabled financial services and government agencies to intercept scammers mid-transaction," and they report tens of thousands of scammer conversations and thousands of extracted mule accounts. Those are operational metrics, which tells me the system works. But I don't have named customers, revenue figures, or any indication of what financial institutions are willing to pay for this intelligence feed. The $305K SBIR grant shows the government found the research valuable enough to fund — that's a real signal. But at pre-seed, I want to see even one customer who would be genuinely upset if BeeSafe disappeared tomorrow. That signal is absent or at least not visible.

The bull case is compelling enough that I'd write a small check. The timing is right — LLM capabilities crossed the threshold required for sustained adversarial conversations only in the last two years, making this impossible before current-generation models. The data moat compounds: every scammer conversation generates intelligence that makes the system better, which generates more intelligence. Competitors can't bootstrap this dataset without the operational know-how to safely engage with criminal actors, which these founders refined through years of academic work. And the problem is getting worse fast — 66% year-over-year growth in pig-butchering losses means the buyers' urgency is increasing. If BeeSafe can land two or three major bank contracts and demonstrate measurable fraud prevention, the pipeline of demand will be enormous. The narrow product scope that looks like a risk is actually correct strategy at this stage — start with the specific problem nobody else is solving, prove it works, then expand.

What keeps this from a higher score is the absence of that pull signal I look for. The system works — they've proven that technically. But I can't tell yet whether enterprise buyers are reaching for this product or whether BeeSafe is pushing it uphill. The difference between those two modes is everything. Stripe didn't just build a better payments API — developers were so desperate for it that the Collisons could walk up to strangers and say "give me your laptop." I want to see BeeSafe's version of that moment. The ingredients are here — genuine organic discovery, massive schlep, deep technical founders, novel approach with no direct competitors. What's missing is the evidence that buyers feel the same urgency the founders feel.
