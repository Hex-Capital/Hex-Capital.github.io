# Minicor -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The schlep here is spectacular. Automating Windows desktop applications by spinning up VMs and clicking through UIs -- dealing with EHRs, dental practice management systems, automotive dealer software -- this is exactly the kind of work that makes every smart engineer's unconscious mind look away. I wrote about this phenomenon in "Schlep Blindness": your brain literally prevents you from seeing startup ideas that involve tedious, ugly, regulated complexity. Minicor sits right in the center of that blind spot. Every AI company trying to sell into healthcare or automotive knows they need to connect to these legacy systems. Every one of them looks at the problem and thinks "surely someone else will solve this." Nobody wants to deal with Citrix environments, HIPAA compliance, and vendor UIs that change without notice. That's the hallmark of a problem with structurally limited competition -- not because it's contrarian, but because it's painful.

The organic discovery passes my test. Saheed spent three years as a senior engineer at Ford and time at Morgan Stanley -- two of the heaviest legacy-enterprise environments on the planet. Faizaan worked at Phoenix, a YC company focused on connecting systems. These are founders who lived inside the problem, not people who read about RPA market sizes and decided to build a startup. They met in 2019, built a non-profit together during the pandemic, and started Minicor in 2023. The idea grew from years of firsthand frustration with exactly these systems. It's not as visceral as Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, but it's the genuine article -- a problem discovered through work, not brainstormed at a whiteboard.

What catches my attention is the SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA certifications at pre-seed with five people. That's not something you pursue recreationally. Getting SOC 2 Type II is its own schlep -- months of documentation, process formalization, and auditor interaction. You do that because customers are telling you they can't go live without it. That's a form of user pull that's more convincing than waitlist numbers. Combine it with "25,000 patients/day at same architecture scale" and "thousands of workflows a week across healthcare EHRs" and you have evidence that real systems in production depend on this. The wording is careful -- "at same architecture scale" leaves ambiguity about whether this is actual daily throughput or demonstrated capacity -- but the compliance certifications anchor the claim in something concrete.

The positioning is smart in a way that reminds me of how the best infrastructure companies start. They're not competing with UiPath for enterprise RPA budgets. They're selling to AI companies that need legacy system connectivity -- a different buyer with a different purchase motion. That's a picks-and-shovels approach: instead of building AI applications for healthcare, build the infrastructure that lets other AI companies reach healthcare systems of record. The MCP interface for coding agents is the right distribution bet. When your customer is another developer, you sell through documentation and APIs, not enterprise sales teams.

The real risk is commoditization. Anthropic shipped Computer Use as a public beta, and any team with API access can now build desktop automation. UiPath is already adding AI self-healing to its existing platform. Minicor's argument is that the managed layer -- VMs, compliance, observability, scaling across Citrix environments -- is the actual product, not the AI model. That's a reasonable argument, and it maps to real complexity that a raw API cannot replicate. But it means the moat is operational rather than technological, and operational moats take time to deepen. The 93-96% click accuracy claim is notable, but in healthcare, a 4-7% failure rate across 25,000 daily patient interactions means hundreds of errors that need reliable self-healing. The margin between "good enough" and "production-grade" in healthcare is where this company will either build a real moat or hit a wall.

Both founders are clearly technical -- software engineering degrees, working codebases, a product in production. They've been building together since 2019, which matters more than people think. In one YC batch, nine companies added co-founders they barely knew and all nine imploded within a year. Faizaan and Saheed have seven years of shared history. That's the kind of foundation that survives the inevitable conflicts of a startup. I don't see a dramatic "relentlessly resourceful" moment in their story yet -- no cereal boxes, no laptop-grabbing installations -- but the trajectory from non-profit to YC to production healthcare deployments with compliance certifications suggests founders who execute consistently rather than episodically.

If I'm wrong about this company, it's because the commoditization risk is worse than I think -- that Anthropic or Microsoft ships a managed desktop automation product that makes the infrastructure layer trivial, or that UiPath's AI Healing Agent reaches parity and their distribution advantage overwhelms Minicor's technical edge. But the schlep of actually dealing with these specific legacy systems -- the Athenas, the CDK Globals, the WellSkys -- is so deep and so application-specific that I suspect general-purpose tools will remain insufficient for years. The compound schlep of Windows VMs plus compliance plus vendor-specific knowledge plus production reliability is exactly the kind of unsexy moat that compounds quietly while everyone else builds chatbots.
