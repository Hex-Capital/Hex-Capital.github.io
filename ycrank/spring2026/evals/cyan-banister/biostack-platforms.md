# BioStack Platforms -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I notice about BioStack isn't the idea -- it's the absence. Team size listed as zero. No LinkedIn company page. A website footer pointing to Connecticut while the YC page says San Francisco. No disclosed data partnerships beyond a UCSF logo and a Skydeck badge. When I look at a company, I'm looking for signs of life -- the kind of obsessive, burning energy that radiates outward before anyone asks to see it. What I see here is a well-constructed shell around an idea that hasn't yet been inhabited by the kind of founder energy I need to feel before I write a check.

And that leads me to the question I always ask first: tell me about the founders, not the business. Sanat Mishra has an impressive research arc -- IISER Mohali to Carnegie Mellon to Stanford, cancer genomics, bioinformatics across four elite institutions. Parth Patwa brings serious ML engineering credibility from AWS with nearly 1,800 Google Scholar citations and publications at CVPR and AAAI. These are accomplished people. But accomplishment and grit are different things. I don't have a story here. I don't know what broke in Sanat's experience at Stanford or Yale that made him say "the whole system of biomedical data is wrong and I'm going to fix it." I don't know what Parth saw at Amazon that convinced him to leave a comfortable position and bet on this. When I backed the Crusoe founders, their story of stumbling onto gas flares in North Dakota and seeing wasted energy as computation was inseparable from who they were. With BioStack, I have two technically qualified researchers who could be working on any number of healthcare AI problems. The biographical thread connecting these specific founders to this specific problem isn't visible to me.

The idea itself has intellectual merit. Framing biomedical data as reinforcement learning training environments rather than static datasets is a genuinely smart insight about where frontier AI is headed. As models move beyond pre-training into post-training and RLHF, the demand for structured, workflow-aligned biological data will grow. That's a real inflection point. But here's my problem: healthcare AI data isn't unpopular. AI drug discovery companies raised $3.8 billion in 2024. There are over 530 companies operating in this space. Tempus alone is generating $1.27 billion in revenue. This isn't the kind of bet where other investors look at you like you've lost your mind -- it's a hot category with a slightly differentiated angle. The RL environment packaging is novel, but it's a technical differentiation within a consensus market, not the kind of fundamentally non-obvious opportunity that gets me excited. When I invested in SpaceX, people thought private rocketry was delusional. When I backed Contraline, male contraception made VCs physically uncomfortable. BioStack doesn't provoke that reaction.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because it deserves serious consideration. If the shift to RL-based post-training in biology is as fundamental as the shift from static to dynamic data was in other domains, then the company that builds the canonical training environment layer could become essential infrastructure. Tempus and Flatiron are oriented toward diagnostics and pharma partnerships -- their business models actually conflict with selling training data to external AI labs. That's a real structural opening. And the combination of a biology-domain researcher with an ML infrastructure engineer is the right pairing for bridging these two worlds. If BioStack secures exclusive data access agreements with even a handful of major medical centers, the compounding data advantage could become a genuine moat. With 85% of generative AI spend in healthcare flowing to startups, the window is open. But this bull case asks me to invest on timing and technical insight alone -- and at the earliest stage, I invest in people and my conviction about whether that person can solve this particular problem. The people here are credentialed, but I can't see the fire.

The co-founder dynamic also gives me pause. No shared prior employer or university between Mishra and Patwa. After HQ Trivia, I always want to understand how co-founders found each other and how they fight. Two people from different institutions, different countries of origin, different academic trajectories -- that can be a powerful complement or an untested partnership. I have no signal either way, and that's not nothing. The team size of zero on the YC page and the geographic discrepancy between San Francisco and Connecticut suggest a company still finding its operational footing, which is fine for pre-seed, but combined with the absent co-founder narrative, it leaves me without the resilience signal I need. On economic access and real-world impact -- BioStack serves AI labs, pharma, and biotech startups. These are well-resourced institutional buyers. The downstream impact on patients is real but indirect and years away. This isn't a company that creates economic opportunity for people who currently lack it. It's infrastructure for other infrastructure.

I'm passing on this one. The founders are technically capable, and the timing insight about RL training environments in biology is sharp. But I don't have the biographical thread, I don't feel the weirdness, and the market they're entering is crowded with well-funded competitors who could replicate the software layer if the RL environment framing proves valuable. The proprietary data access that would make this defensible isn't demonstrated -- it's hypothesized. I need to feel that jolt of conviction, and it isn't here.
