# Armature -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Armature is an infrastructure-positioned developer tool entering a market where the structural window is already half-closed by incumbent eval platforms -- that high/low pairing is the entire story. The dossier reads as a competent SF team (Théodore Otzenberger, 5 years Palantir; Louis Scremin, Roland Berger + Joko) shipping a "Cypress for agents" wedge that genuinely embeds in CI, but doing so in 2026, not 2024 -- after DeepEval already added MCP metrics for args correctness, task completion, and tool correctness in 2025. The product is real. The window is late.

Nonobvious Market Timing is where Armature places lowest on my ladder, and it's the dimension I weight heaviest. My HIGH rung here is Perplexity -- I wrote that seed before ChatGPT launched because the AI capability curve was crossing a threshold consensus hadn't priced. My LOW rung is the consumer social apps I pass on every YC batch -- no exogenous catalyst, stable platform layer. Armature sits closer to the LOW rung than I'd like: MCP standardization and harness proliferation (Claude Code, Codex, Gemini) are a real structural shift in the last 12-24 months, but the shift is no longer nonobvious. The dossier itself names Braintrust, Langfuse, LangSmith, Latitude, and DeepEval all expanding into agent territory, with DeepEval shipping MCP metrics in 2025. When the LangChain State of Agent Engineering reports 89% observability adoption versus 52% eval adoption, that gap is now visible to every Series A eval vendor with a roadmap. Armature isn't reading a curve consensus missed -- it's racing consensus to ship.

Infrastructure-Layer Positioning is where Armature places highest, and this is what keeps me from a clean pass. My HIGH rung is Stripe -- the payments API thousands of businesses built on, compounding switching costs. My LOW rung is Wish, the consumer marketplace with no API and no B2B layer that collapsed 99% post-IPO. Armature sits in the upper-middle: connecting a customer's MCP/CLI to a continuous test runner, auto-discovering tools, and embedding in regression workflows is structurally the right shape -- this is testing infrastructure, not an end-user app. Closer to Gusto than to Wish. The question is whether the layer is defensible once Braintrust adds the same execution-style multi-harness runs through their existing CI merge-blocking integration. Armature's positioning is correct; its layer is contested.

Single-Miracle Viability is the dimension where Armature most clearly fails my framework. Harvey was one miracle -- foundation model capability improving exogenously while legal firms were already proven buyers. Anduril was the override -- multi-miracle but justified by generational structural shift. Armature is closer to Anduril's miracle count without Anduril's market structure: it needs (a) auto-discovery quality across heterogeneous customer MCPs, (b) the harness/model integration treadmill maintained by 3 people against a market that's adding harnesses faster than vendors can integrate them, and (c) distribution that beats incumbents who already have the eyeballs. No generational catalyst overrides three miracles here.

Operator-Scaler Founder Profile sits between my MID rung (Brex's young operators) and my LOW rung (first-time founders without scaling exposure). Otzenberger's 5 years at Palantir gives forward-deployed enterprise instincts, and Scremin's Roland Berger Paris/Singapore plus Joko Business Ops covers GTM mechanics. Neither has a documented prior exit or eval-tooling specialization, and the third team member isn't publicly named despite the YC page listing team size 3. Decent for the wedge; not the Parker-Conrad-rebuilding-Rippling profile that would override the timing concern. Margin Architecture & Retention Dynamics is the dimension that quietly bothers me most: Armature priced Starter at $49/mo and Pro at $119/mo -- pure seat tiers in 2026, when my own "seats to cognition" framework says the winners will price per agent run, per resolved test, per detected regression. Abridge bills per encounter. Decagon bills per resolution. Armature bills per seat. That's the wrong pricing primitive for the wave they claim to ride.

My 36-month forecast: Armature reaches $1-3M ARR on the back of YC batch demos and the Palantir/Joko investor network, then gets acquired by Braintrust or Langfuse for the multi-harness execution layer and the integration matrix at a sub-$50M outcome -- or pivots into MCP-specific compliance testing for a regulated vertical (financial services, healthcare) where the integration treadmill is narrower and the payer is clearer. Mechanism: contested infrastructure layer + multi-miracle execution + seat-based pricing in a cognition-priced era compresses the outcome distribution toward acqui-hire well before the structural window for an independent category leader closes.

The bull case stings more than I'd like. Latitude already publicly argued that agent evaluation is architecturally distinct from LLM evaluation -- failure surfaces at tool call arguments, state propagation, goal alignment drift -- which means the prompt-eval incumbents may not actually win this layer just by adding metrics. If Armature ships harness integrations faster than Braintrust can refactor for execution-style runs, "Cypress for agents" is a real category and the first credible runner takes it. Cypress survived alongside Jest. The dossier's claim that Armature founders "deployed MCPs used by millions of customers" -- if substantiated -- means they've felt the testing pain firsthand, which is exactly the founder-market fit signal Paul Graham would weight. None of that flips me, because the seat pricing tells me they don't yet see the pricing primitive shift, and DeepEval's 2025 MCP metrics tell me consensus has already moved. What would flip me: Armature signs three named design-partner logos with usage-based or per-test-run pricing in the next two quarters, and at least one is an enterprise that previously evaluated Braintrust. That would prove both the wedge and the pricing instinct.
