# Cascade -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Cascade is the competitive landscape, and it worries me. Braintrust just raised $80M at an $800M valuation. Raindrop has $15M from Lightspeed. Arize has $131M. ClickHouse acquired Langfuse. Snyk acquired Invariant Labs. Anthropic acqui-hired HumanLoop. This isn't a market hiding in plain sight — it's a market where everybody showed up at the same time. The structural advantage I look for in investments is the schlep that keeps smart people away. AI agent observability has the opposite dynamic: it's attracting smart people and capital at a rate that suggests the opportunity has already been priced. When every VC in San Francisco is excited about a category, I get less excited, not more.

What's worse, Raindrop's publicly described approach — "training small, custom models that adapt to the unique shape of each AI product" — is nearly word-for-word what Cascade claims as its differentiator: training custom evaluator models from production data. When your stated moat is identical to a competitor's stated moat, and that competitor has $15M and Lightspeed behind them, you don't actually have a moat. You have a description of a moat. The question becomes: what do these two founders know that Raindrop's team, Braintrust's team, and Arize's team don't? I don't see the answer in the dossier.

On origin: both founders come from BAIR Lab, which gives them legitimate domain context. Haluk built production monitoring infrastructure at Netflix and Amazon, and researched failure mode taxonomies for AI agents. Adam worked on agentic safety and graph reasoning. These aren't random people who read a Gartner report and decided "AI agents are hot." They've been in the relevant technical trenches. But there's a difference between knowing a problem academically and stumbling into it because it broke something you were trying to build. Stripe didn't start because the Collisons studied payment infrastructure at a research lab. It started because they were hackers trying to accept payments and couldn't. The best founders I've funded can point to the specific moment the problem became personal. Here, the path from BAIR Lab research to "let's start a company in agent reliability" feels more like academic-to-market translation than organic discovery. That's not damning, but it's not the signal I get most excited about.

The bull case is real, though, and I want to be honest about it. If production AI agents become as pervasive as Gartner projects — 40% of enterprise applications by late 2026 — then the reliability tooling market will be enormous, and the companies building it now will have a timing advantage. The founder-market fit is genuinely strong: production monitoring engineering plus AI safety research is exactly the skill complement you'd want for this product. And the "custom evaluator models trained on your data" approach, while not unique, could create genuine switching costs if executed well — your models get better with your data, making you reluctant to start over with a competitor. If one of these two founders turns out to be relentlessly resourceful in the way that matters, they could win a slice of this market through sheer execution. The team composition is sound. My concern isn't about whether they can build it — it's about whether the market structure rewards being a two-person pre-seed team when your competitors have 50x your capital.

On the technical side, these are clearly builders — UC Berkeley CS, BAIR Lab, Netflix, Amazon. I believe they can write the code. But I can't verify it. The GitHub repo referenced on Adam's personal site returns a 404. The website didn't render product content. There's no demo, no documentation, no visible prototype. For a company whose entire value proposition is technical, the absence of any public technical artifact is a gap. At pre-seed I don't demand much, but I want to see something built. Dropbox had a working demo video. Stripe had a working API. Even at the earliest stage, the best technical founders ship something visible.

The deeper issue is that this doesn't feel like a startup that needs to exist — it feels like a startup that could exist. A lot of teams could build AI agent observability. A lot of teams are. The companies I invest in tend to be the ones where the founders are building something they need so badly that they'd build it whether or not it was a company. I don't see that signal here. I see technically capable people in a hot market with a reasonable product thesis. That's not nothing. But it's not the thing that makes me reach for my checkbook.
