# Zibra Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Zibra Labs is a technically excellent plumbing team pointed at an audience whose success leaves the world unchanged: Ibrahim Rabbani and Zac Policzer come straight out of Anyscale's Ray Core and LinkedIn's Venice — as serious a distributed-systems pedigree as you'll find at pre-seed — and they have aimed it at making hedge fund backtests cheaper. The dimensional signature is high engineering capability married to vanishing consequence magnitude, and that's the wrong shape for my checkbook regardless of how clean the multi-cloud scheduler is.

Consequence magnitude is where Zibra Labs collapses, and this is where I start every evaluation. The high rung on this ladder is OpenAI — a $50M check that required an apology letter to my LPs because the consequence of AGI was civilizational. The mid rung is Rocket Lab, where cheap dedicated small-sat launch creates thousands of new businesses in earth observation, IoT, and defense that couldn't exist before. The low rung is the YC pitch I pass on every batch: "AI copilot for accountants, 40% workflow automation." Zibra Labs sits squarely on that low rung. The dossier names the customer explicitly — "quant trading firms running strategy backtesting" — and the win condition is that Bridgewater and Two Sigma run more signals per day. If Zibra Labs fully succeeds, hedge funds get marginally more profitable. No one's life changes. Nobody gets healthcare, energy, food, or education they didn't have before. This is redistribution of existing compute spend among the most capitalized financial firms on earth, which is precisely the anti-pattern I flagged in my Red Flags: "targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand."

Non-linear technical approach is where Zibra Labs scores its best, and even here the placement is mid-low. The high rung is Commonwealth Fusion Systems — new plasma physics, a step-function change in energy. The mid rung is DoorDash, which I admitted as a Known Exception because the technical novelty was minimal but consequence was planetary. The low rung is the LLM-API-wrapper. Zibra Labs sits between mid and low: 50,000-node clusters with <50ms dispatch over spot instances at 6.4M parallel tasks is genuine distributed-systems engineering, but the substrate is Ray, which the founders' own bios link to as 42.4k-star open-source. AWS Batch + Coiled is a published reference architecture for this exact workload. Anyscale, the founders' former employer, sits one product-roadmap meeting away from owning this niche. There is no proprietary scheduler benchmark, no novel hardware, no data moat — just very good engineering on a layer the hyperscalers and my friends at Anyscale can replicate.

Founder-as-learning-machine is the dimension where Zibra Labs reveals it doesn't fit my outsider thesis. The high rung is Mach Industries — a 19-year-old MIT dropout building weapons systems against Lockheed Martin, naive enough not to know what couldn't be done. The mid rung is Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, a self-taught New Zealand engineer with no aerospace degree. The low rung is the credentialed industry insider running a known playbook. Rabbani and Policzer are the credentialed insiders of the field they're competing in — they helped build Ray at Anyscale, which is the dominant commercial player in the adjacent market. The OpenAI Known Exception lets me override outsider preference when consequence is civilizational. Zibra Labs gets no such override; the consequence floor is too low to forgive the establishment-pedigree problem.

Improbable ambition: high rung Hermeus (Mach 5 passenger aircraft); mid rung Rocket Lab; low rung Pass Example. Zibra Labs's stated ambition — better backtesting infrastructure for quants — provokes zero incredulity. Ask any infra engineer at AWS, Anyscale, or Citadel whether multi-cloud spot scheduling for backtests is achievable in 24 months and they'll say yes. That answer is the failure of the dimension. Radical affordability: high rung Impossible Foods (protein cheaper than animal agriculture); mid rung Rocket Lab (10x launch cost reduction unlocks new buyers); low rung enterprise SaaS premium pricing. Zibra Labs makes spot-instance compute cheaper for some of the wealthiest financial firms in the world. That isn't affordability in my framework. Affordability means a basic human need collapses to near-zero marginal cost — healthcare, education, energy, legal access. Hedge fund AWS bills are not on that list.

In 36 months Zibra Labs is either acquired by Anyscale or AWS for $30M–$80M, or it reaches $3M–$8M ARR with five to fifteen quant-fund logos and trades as a profitable lifestyle infrastructure company. The mechanism producing this forecast is the dimensional signature: deep distributed-systems competence applied to a wealthy but bounded buyer set, on a substrate the founders' own former employer can absorb. That outcome is fine money for the founders. It is not my outcome shape — I want to either lose my money or make 50x.

The bull case stings: hedge funds and prop shops are the highest-margin compute buyers on earth, Citadel alone is reported to spend hundreds of millions on compute, and the Anyscale relationship gives Zibra Labs warm intros into Bridgewater and Two Sigma — a quant-infra company really could compound to $100M ARR by simply being the best at one workload. I'd dismantle it this way: even if that bull case fully plays out, the terminal value is a vertical infrastructure business in a regulated, secretive customer base — the same redistribution-of-existing-spend pattern I just flagged, with a $1B–$2B exit ceiling that doesn't justify the option price in my portfolio construction. The condition that flips my verdict: if Rabbani and Policzer pivot Zibra Labs into AI training compute for civilizationally consequential workloads — biotech foundation models, fusion simulation, synthetic biology pipelines — the same scheduler suddenly sits beneath problems too important not to work on, and the consequence magnitude rebuilds. Until then, Zibra Labs is a pass.
