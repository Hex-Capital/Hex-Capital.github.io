# Harbor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Harbor is something I've seen a hundred times: competent founders who lived a pain point, built a product that solves it ten times better than the status quo, and are executing a well-structured go-to-market in a clearly defined market. And my immediate question is the one I always ask before anything else -- if Harbor succeeds completely, captures the EDC market, displaces Medidata and Veeva from their perch, what happens? Clinical trials get set up in days instead of months. Data entry goes from hours to minutes. Sponsors save maybe 20-25% of their trial budgets. That's valuable. Medidata was acquired for $5.8 billion, so there's real money in this market. But the clinical trial system itself remains fundamentally intact. Patients still wait years for drugs to clear the same regulatory gauntlet. Nobody's rebuilding the architecture of how we prove medicines work -- we're just running the existing architecture on better software. The consequence of success is the next Medidata. That's a good business. It's not the kind of outcome that justifies my attention.

Here's where I have to interrogate my own reaction, because there's a genuinely interesting signal buried in Harbor's approach. The 10x speed improvement is real -- a 1,600-subject trial configured in one week versus the industry-standard eight to ten weeks, setup compressed from two months to two days according to their customers. And the flat monthly pricing versus per-subject/per-site charges creates a classic innovator's dilemma for Medidata and Veeva, whose entire revenue model depends on the complexity and manual labor that Harbor eliminates. If Veeva adopts AI that removes the need for professional services and per-subject billing, they cannibalize their own $2.36 billion revenue base. That's real structural tension. The problem is, both incumbents have already announced AI features -- Veeva's AI Agents roll out across R&D in 2026, Medidata's embedding AI into study planning now. These are not incumbents dismissing the technology the way Cisco's CTO dismissed TCP/IP routing when I backed Juniper. These are incumbents actively racing to absorb the same capability into their existing platforms. The window for an AI-native challenger exists, but it's narrowing quarter by quarter.

Albert Cai spent four years inside clinical trial operations at Biolinq, watching coordinators spend hours on manual data entry, navigating FDA regulatory pathways firsthand. He knows this problem intimately. That's exactly the kind of founder most VCs love -- deep domain experience, lived the pain, built the solution. And it's exactly the kind of founder profile that gives me pause. Albert is an insider executing the industry's own wish list. Everyone in clinical trials knows EDC systems are terrible. Everyone wants AI-powered data capture. There's no contrarian thesis here, no idea that experts reject as impossible. When your customers' testimonials say "we faced 10-12 week timelines elsewhere; Harbor delivered in nine days" -- that's a sales pitch, not a paradigm shift. Nathan Leung was employee number one at Jupiter, a YC S19 company in our own portfolio at Khosla Ventures, so I have some indirect signal on his engineering caliber. He can build. But building a better version of a system everyone already agrees needs improvement is not the same as building something the world didn't know it needed.

The strongest bull case goes like this: clinical trials are the bottleneck for all of drug development, and drug development is a $100 billion-plus annual expenditure. If Harbor's approach compresses timelines by months across tens of thousands of trials, the second-order effects on time-to-market for therapeutics are enormous -- measured not just in dollars saved but in patient-years gained. An AI-native EDC built from scratch might be architecturally superior to anything Medidata or Veeva can retrofit onto their 25-year-old platforms, the way Juniper's clean-sheet TCP/IP routers outperformed Cisco's legacy architecture. The regulatory compliance infrastructure -- 21 CFR Part 11 validation, immutable audit trails -- creates a genuine moat that generic LLM startups can't easily replicate. And at $2,000/month flat, Harbor could capture the entire long tail of small and mid-size trials that Medidata and Veeva don't bother serving, then expand upmarket with proven AI accuracy. For this bull case to hold, you'd need the incumbents' AI efforts to be mediocre (bolt-on features rather than architectural rethinks), the regulatory environment to increasingly accept AI-extracted data, and Harbor's extraction accuracy to compound into a data flywheel that becomes genuinely hard to replicate. It's plausible. But it requires Harbor to outrun two well-capitalized incumbents who are already sprinting, which is a fundamentally different dynamic than building into a market where incumbents are asleep or dismissive.

I keep coming back to the core issue: Harbor has reduced their probability of failure -- real customers, working product, regulatory compliance, sensible pricing -- to the point where the consequences of success are, by my standards, modest. A $5-10 billion clinical trial software company. That's the ceiling. The floor is getting absorbed by one of the incumbents or squeezed out as Veeva and Medidata bundle their own AI features into existing enterprise contracts at zero marginal cost to the customer. This is a company I'd expect to see succeed in a conventional venture portfolio. It is not a company where I lie awake thinking about what the world looks like if they win, because the world looks mostly the same -- just with faster CRF generation.
