# Arzule -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that catches my attention here isn't the product or the market -- it's the pivot. A few weeks into YC W26, these founders were building open-source observability for multi-agent systems. That product actually mapped to their technical backgrounds: Jeffrey built multi-agent arbitrage systems, Nikhil had experience with AI data pipelines. Then they pivoted to B2B partnership intelligence -- a domain where neither founder has any documented experience in partnerships, SaaS go-to-market, or channel strategy. When I invest, the first question I ask is always "tell me your story -- not the business." I'm listening for the moment where I can feel that the founder's biography and the company's mission are the same thing. Here, the biographical thread runs toward multi-agent systems and quantitative research, and then abruptly redirects to partnership management for growth-stage SaaS companies. That's the opposite of what I look for. It feels like they found a market gap and decided to fill it, which is a perfectly rational thing to do, but it's not the signal I invest on.

I'm also not getting any weirdness from this. B2B partnership intelligence is a sensible, logical SaaS play in a market with established players -- Crossbeam with $117 million raised, PartnerStack with $40 million. Nobody is going to call you crazy for wanting to help SaaS companies find better partners. Nobody is going to reject this idea fifty times before someone finally says yes. And that's actually the problem, from my perspective. The ideas that have produced my best returns -- SpaceX, Crusoe Energy, Contraline -- were ideas that made smart people uncomfortable. They were difficult to assess. They had a quality that repelled consensus capital. Arzule is the opposite: it's immediately understandable, fits neatly into existing market maps, and competes against incumbents who could add AI discovery features as a natural extension of their current data assets. When I imagine the full potential of this company, I don't feel that jolt of energy. I feel the mild satisfaction of seeing a competent team address a real workflow problem. Those are very different feelings.

Let me give the bull case its due, because I want to be honest about what could make this work. If Nikhil and Jeffrey have stumbled onto something real about how LLMs can analyze unstructured ecosystem data -- feature releases, integration announcements, product changes -- and build a living intelligence layer that genuinely predicts which partnerships will generate revenue, that's a valuable capability. The timing argument is real: LLMs make this kind of continuous monitoring possible in a way it wasn't even two years ago. They already have early customers in DevTools, HR, and Finance, which suggests the product is resonating with someone. And the incumbents -- Crossbeam and PartnerStack -- are built around managing existing partnerships, not discovering new ones. If Arzule can own the upstream discovery layer and build a proprietary dataset of partnership outcomes, the data compounds and gets harder to replicate. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that partnership discovery is a fundamentally different product category from partnership management, that the data moat accrues fast enough to matter before incumbents react, and that two technical founders with no GTM experience can sell effectively to VP-level partnership leaders at growth-stage companies. It's plausible. But plausible isn't what moves me.

What's missing for me is any dimension of economic access or real-world impact for people who need it. When I backed Uber, I saw it as a poverty-alleviation tool -- Roger, my driver, was earning a living in a way the medallion system had prevented. Affirm gave people transparent credit. Postmates created income for delivery workers. Arzule helps growth-stage SaaS companies optimize their partnership revenue. That's fine, but it's not creating access for anyone who currently lacks it. It's not making an invisible system visible to people who are excluded from it. It's an efficiency tool for companies that are already doing well. I don't begrudge anyone building that, but it doesn't activate the part of my brain that writes checks.

The co-founder dynamic is the one area where I'd give them some credit -- they navigated a pivot together, which means they had to agree to abandon their original idea and align on a new direction under pressure. That's a real test. But I don't have enough information about how they make decisions, how they argue, or what their conflict resolution looks like. After HQ Trivia, I probe those dynamics hard before I invest, and here the record is simply too thin to evaluate. What I can see is two technically strong founders, both from good schools, both with relevant engineering internships -- but no biographical grit that connects them to the specific struggle they're asking customers to trust them with.
