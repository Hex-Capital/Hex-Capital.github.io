# Torus -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Marcus Lima ran Heimdal, direct-air-capture concrete, YC S21, raised $9.7M at $63M with Sam Altman and Marc Benioff — and now Torus is document-consistency AI agents for EPC firms. That pairing is the signature: a founder who lived on well pads and cement kilns is now selling a Legora clone into Fortune 500 procurement inboxes. Torus's highest dimension is who Lima is; its lowest is that I cannot install, ride, scan, or photograph Torus in my own life. That gap — real founder, intangible artifact — is the whole story.

Anduril-Luckey is the HIGH rung on lived-experience, because Palmer had shipped Oculus at consumer scale before he ever wrote a defense contract, and the biography did the load-bearing work. Torus rhymes with that mechanism because Lima built Heimdal in industrial materials — Oxford MSc in Energy Systems Engineering, and he actually got physical product into cement customers, which is the closest an EPC-adjacent founder can come to walking on a well pad. Where Torus diverges from the Anduril rung is that Lima was never the engineer receiving the RFI at 2 a.m. because the P&ID said 425 psig and the datasheet said 450; he is adjacent to the pain, not underneath it. That is the difference between "of the place" and "visiting" — Chase Lochmiller could talk to a roughneck at Crusoe because he had grown up next to that world; Lima has operated near it, which is a legitimate check but not the tattooed-conviction version.

Physical verifiability is where Torus collapses to the floor. The Pass Example failure mechanism is that a normal human cannot touch the product — the consensus YC AI agent I passed on was pure software abstraction, no artifact, no observable moment. Torus is exactly that shape: an enterprise cross-document consistency layer for AVEVA/Bentley/Hexagon workflows, delivered via email threads that draft procurement approvals. I cannot use Torus at a Four Seasons café the way I read the Flock Wi-Fi name; I cannot ride it the way I rode Uber in 2010; I cannot play it the way I played Ingress into Niantic. When the pilot ask is "send us 5-6 documents from your current project," the product exists only inside the customer's SharePoint — that is the definition of an artifact I cannot photograph.

The "magically weird" filter fails Torus by construction — "Legora for physical engineering firms" is the shape of a category YC will fund ten times this batch and next. AI vertical SaaS wrappers into unsexy industrials is now a consensus lane, not a contrarian one, and I have watched this exact pitch shape land in a dozen decks in 2025-26. What partially rescues Torus is that the *customer set* — substations, pipelines, treatment plants — is genuinely unsexy in the way the Hustle Fund profile means it, and Gustaf Alstromer is a competent YC partner but not a signal that the round will be underpriced.

The CEO authority dimension compounds badly over 18 months if not resolved now. Lima is clearly titled CEO, which is fine, but the dossier flags "founder identity ambiguity" — Rahul Thayil's LinkedIn surfaces Orion (Techstars '23, $3.5M Nov 2025), and no source in front of me confirms the same Thayil is Torus's CTO versus a name collision. If it is the same person, the question is whether Orion has been shut down or whether the CTO is running two companies; either answer needs diligence before docs. There is no documented tiebreaker protocol, no equity split disclosed, no operating agreement mentioned — this is the "they haven't had the conversation yet" default and it is 20 months from being a HQ Trivia-style feud.

Counterfactual on capital-intensity: for Torus to flip a tier here, the sovereign on-prem deployment would have to become the *sales cycle itself* — meaning EPC firms buy Torus specifically because the model runs behind their firewall on classified substation designs, and no SaaS competitor can serve that segment. That would echo the moat pattern of Anduril's classified-environments posture. As currently described, Torus's SOC 2 Type 1 & 2 is "in progress" and the on-prem tier is a feature bullet, not the whole go-to-market — so the frontier-hardware analog does not activate.

My 36-month forecast: Torus reaches $3-8M ARR across two or three Fortune 500 EPC accounts, then gets acquired by AVEVA (Schneider Electric) or Bentley as an AI-features tuck-in in the $80-150M range — a respectable outcome for Lima, not a defining company. The mechanism that produces this: incumbent bundling wins because AVEVA already sells the EDMS suite into these accounts and RTInsights already flagged AI classification as the direction of the category, so Torus's cross-document check becomes a feature rather than a platform.

The bull case that stings: Lima is a repeat founder who already ran the Sam Altman / Marc Benioff cap-table gauntlet at Heimdal, he understands industrial procurement cadences that pure software founders do not, and U.S. reindustrialization capex into data centers and grid is a real tailwind that could pull Torus into the AI-native EDMS category winner. The Known Exception to check is HandUp — I overrode commercial-defensibility because the founder mirrored my origin — but Lima is Oxford + QVARTZ / Bain, not a Tucson-homeless-equivalent biography, so that override does not apply. The DeepMind polymath exception also does not fit; Lima is a competent industrial operator, not a cross-domain compounder. What would flip me to invest: verifiable proof that Torus has a paid, deployed contract (not pilot) with a named Fortune 500 EPC — an ENGIE, a Bechtel, a Fluor — inside 90 days, and Thayil's identity resolved cleanly. Absent those, this is a rebuttal I am giving Lima, not a hard no, and I will re-look at Series A if the artifact has become touchable by then.
