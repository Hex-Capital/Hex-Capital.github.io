# Congruent -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Here's a problem that has been sitting in plain sight for years: radar sensors in autonomous vehicles throw away 99% of their raw data before it reaches any AI model, and nobody in the radar industry seems to notice this matters. Every major radar company -- Arbe, Uhnder, Zadar -- is competing to build better point clouds. They're all polishing the same abstraction while the industry is moving to end-to-end neural networks that consume raw sensor data directly. Cameras have raw data access and simulators. Lidar has raw data access and simulators. Radar has neither. And radar is the only depth sensor that costs $50 and already sits in 90% of vehicles. This is a genuine case of what I'd call schlep blindness applied to a technical architecture: the problem is obvious once you state it, but solving it requires simultaneously redesigning radar hardware, building a physics-based generative simulator, and navigating automotive qualification standards. That's enough accumulated ugliness that smart people unconsciously look away.

What's encouraging is how the founders found this problem. Clement Barthes wasn't reading TechCrunch articles about autonomous driving. He was Lead Perception Software Engineer at Zendar, one of the handful of radar startups specifically building for autonomous vehicles. He was the person trying to feed radar data into perception pipelines and hitting the wall. Evan Carnahan was doing a PhD at UT Austin on radar signal processing and physics-learned models for radar data. They were both inside the problem before they decided to build a company around it. That's the origin story I look for -- not "we identified a market opportunity" but "we kept running into this wall and finally decided to break through it." The fact that their founding ML scientist specializes in diffusion-based generative models -- exactly the technology needed for the radar simulator -- suggests the team was assembled around the specific technical requirements of the problem, not assembled first and then aimed at whatever seemed hot.

The schlep here is genuinely multi-layered, which is both the strongest bull case and the biggest concern. To win, Congruent needs to build automotive-grade radar hardware (hardware manufacturing schlep), create a physics-based radar simulator trained on real-world data (ML research schlep), get through AEC-Q automotive qualification (regulatory schlep), and sell to OEMs with multi-year design cycles (enterprise sales schlep). Each layer deters a different class of competitor. The incumbent radar chip companies (NXP, TI, Infineon) could add raw data interfaces but won't build the simulator. The radar startups (Arbe, Uhnder) have the capital but are architecturally committed to point clouds. Pure software companies can't touch the hardware. This stacked schlep creates a natural moat -- but it also means Congruent is essentially attempting four hard things simultaneously with two people and pre-seed capital. That's where my concern sharpens.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If end-to-end training wins -- and Tesla's FSD v12, Wayve's $1B raise, and the broader industry trajectory suggest it will -- then every AV developer using radar suddenly needs exactly two things: raw radar data and a radar simulator. Nobody else is building both. The timing feels right rather than early. This could be a picks-and-shovels play for the end-to-end era, the way Stripe was picks-and-shovels for internet payments. The addressable market is large, the problem is structural, and the founders have the specific expertise to solve it. If I'm wrong about the risks, this could be enormous.

But two things keep me from reaching for my checkbook. First, the market dependency: Congruent's entire thesis requires that radar remains in the autonomous driving sensor suite as the industry shifts to end-to-end architectures. Tesla removed radar and went camera-only. If the industry follows Tesla rather than diverging, Congruent's market shrinks dramatically. The founders would argue radar is essential for adverse weather and safety, and most OEMs agree today, but that's not guaranteed. Second, this is a hardware company at pre-seed with zero evidence of user pull -- no pilot programs, no LOIs, no conversations with OEMs that we can see. Deep domain expertise in radar perception is necessary but not sufficient for selling hardware into automotive supply chains. I have no evidence these founders can navigate the commercial side, and I have no evidence that any customer is pulling this product toward them yet. The technical insight is strong. The commercial execution is entirely unproven.

The team is clearly technically exceptional for the problem. Two PhDs with direct radar perception experience, plus a founding ML scientist with the exact generative modeling skills needed for the simulator. This isn't a team that needs to hire the people who build the core product -- they are the people who build it. That matters. But I notice no GitHub repos, no public demos, and no evidence of a working prototype beyond the YC launch. At pre-seed that's not unusual, but for a company whose core differentiator is technical, showing working technology would be a stronger signal than describing it.

I'd want to see this company again when they have a working radar module producing raw data, a simulator generating synthetic training data that an AV company has actually used, and at least one serious conversation with an OEM or tier-one. The organic discovery is real, the schlep is genuine, and the technical team matches the problem. What's missing is any evidence that the world is reaching back -- that someone on the other side actually wants this badly enough to engage with a two-person pre-seed startup building hardware. That's the gap between an interesting technical insight and a startup that's working.
