# FinalDose -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

FinalDose is a frontier-biotech swing with no artifact I can touch — three Oxford PhDs claiming a DNA-sensing protein kill-switch that "unlocks 80% of cancers existing drugs cannot reach," and that high-capital, high-regulation, no-tactile-product pairing is the exact analytical signature that tells me what kind of company FinalDose actually is. It's the SpaceX 2008 profile minus the rocket I could watch on a launch pad — capital-intensive and aesthetically transgressive on the science, but I cannot install it, photograph it, or hand it to a friend, and Jeff Liu, Li-Yao Huang, and Steven Lin are not going to put a working version in my hands inside 48 hours or 48 months.

SpaceX 2008 is the rung I keep reaching for on the capital-intensive axis, and FinalDose lives one step below it. SpaceX got my first-ever angel check because rockets were "uninvestable" — too expensive, too physical, too unsexy for software-trained VCs — and that aesthetic refusal *was* the underpricing. FinalDose sits in the same lane (oncology therapeutics burn $100M+ to clinic, Crunchbase shows no Series A, the company is incorporated as FINALDOSE LTD #16816175 in the UK with five people), and the regulated-biotech swing absolutely fits the "too hard or too unsexy" filter that gave me Crusoe and Mindbloom. Where FinalDose diverges from the SpaceX rung is that the moat is publishable science — a DNA-sensing protein's structural change repurposed as a kill switch is exactly the kind of mechanism Beam, Intellia, and Scribe could read in a preprint and run at, especially Scribe with the Sanofi NK-cell partnership already wired. Capital-intensive, yes; defensible-by-time-and-data, not yet.

Dev-tools APIs are what I refuse on the physical-verifiability axis, and FinalDose has the same problem dressed in a lab coat. A B2B API I cannot install fails my Flock Safety test — I sourced that company from a Wi-Fi network name at the Four Seasons because I could *see* the founder's product in the world — and FinalDose's pipeline is preclinical, with no in vivo data, no disclosed delivery modality (LNP? AAV? the dossier is silent), no IND, and a 3-5 year timeline to anything a human body experiences. The pattern is identical to the dev-tools pass: value can only be evaluated via founder testimony, and the "7-day hit validation cycle" on the YC Launch page is a process claim about a screen, not an artifact a normal human can touch. This is the dimension where FinalDose loses the most against my framework, and I cannot wave it away by pointing at the biology.

(Mechanism analysis on lived experience.) Jeff Liu spent five years at the MRC Weatherall Institute of Molecular Medicine running CRISPR screens for druggable cancer targets, and co-founded Vivid Dx ($15M raised) before FinalDose — that is a real adjacent-product trajectory, the Palmer-Luckey-shipped-Oculus-before-Anduril shape, except in the wet lab. Over the next 18 months, that compounds if Liu can convert academic screening fluency into in vivo proof-of-concept on a single hard target like MYC; it inverts if FinalDose's pitch language ("destroying all cancers, unlocking 80% of targets") keeps overshooting what the bench will defend, because the gap between "we ran a screen" and "we have a therapeutic" is where most academic spinouts evaporate. Liu has been close enough to the problem to know the difference; the dossier does not yet prove he respects it in how he markets FinalDose.

(Counterfactual on magically-weird.) For FinalDose's non-consensus score to flip a tier upward, two credible VCs would need to have publicly passed on aesthetic or moral grounds — the way they passed on Crusoe burning gas flares — and Liu would need to articulate the misread. The dossier shows the opposite: Garry Tan as YC partner, a YC Launch post, Dealroom coverage, and a pitch framed in the most consensus-aspirational language in oncology ("cure all cancers"). This is not Crusoe-weird. It is biotech-aspirational, which is the most crowded narrative tier in the YC Spring 2026 batch, sitting in a category where Beam, CRISPR Therapeutics, Intellia, Scribe, and KSQ have each raised between $100M and $1B+ to chase variants of the same dream.

(Single-anchor parallel on CEO clarity.) Anduril named Palmer Luckey as the operating principal from day one and never let "co-CEO" enter the room, which is the structural inverse of the HQ Trivia post-mortem I keep citing. FinalDose has titled itself cleanly — Liu CEO, Huang CSO, Lin CTO — and the three co-founders share an Oxford lineage in adjacent disciplines rather than overlapping ones, which reduces the tiebreaker risk that killed GameCrush. Equity splits and operating agreements are not disclosed, so I cannot fully credit the rung, but the titling alone is more disciplined than half the YC pre-seed teams I see.

Thirty-six-month forecast: FinalDose raises a $25-50M Series A from a generalist biotech fund (a16z Bio, ARCH, or Lux) on mouse-model efficacy data for one tumor type, no human dosing, and an MYC- or KRAS-targeted lead candidate; alternatively, FinalDose stalls at in vivo delivery and quietly winds down without a clinical candidate. The signature — frontier-biotech-capital-intensive paired with zero tactile artifact — produces exactly that outcome: enough scientific credibility and unsexiness-premium to clear a Series A on data, not enough physical-world traction to compound into the Beam/Intellia tier without a delivery breakthrough that the dossier does not yet hint at.

The bear case that stings is that FinalDose is the Stanford-Harvard-Goldman pattern I explicitly avoid, in academic costume. Three Oxford PhDs. Problem discovered in research memos, not autobiography — none of the three founders has publicly disclosed a personal contact with cancer. Pitch language ("cure all cancers") is the single most consensus-aspirational sentence in oncology. The DeepMind exception — polymath cross-domain compounding overriding pedigree — does partly apply here, since Liu (oncology), Huang (biochemistry/CRISPR), and Lin (computational biology/ML) map onto three disciplines fusing into one platform, and Liu's Vivid Dx round is a real prior-venture credential most YC pre-seed teams cannot match. But DeepMind is an exception, not a rule, and the absence of any "moment I felt the problem" anecdote is the silence I weight heavily. The single verifiable condition that would flip me from neutral to invest: FinalDose publishes in vivo efficacy in a humanized mouse model on an undruggable target (MYC, TP53, or APC) with off-target specificity benchmarks, within 18 months. Until that artifact exists, I am watching, not writing.
