# Clawvisor -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Clawvisor is the kind of company my framework finds hardest to fund: a credentialed operator's infrastructure SaaS in a category that already has six funded competitors before the product is GA. Eric Levine sold Berbix to Socure for ~$70M in June 2023, which is a real outcome and proves he can ship in regulated identity ? but the dimensional signature here is "insider founder, infrastructure wedge, no consequence explosion." The high rung on Clawvisor's profile is the founder ledger; the low rung is what happens if Clawvisor wins. That pairing produces $50?150M outcomes, not Juniper-style 2,500x.

Consequence magnitude is where I start, and Clawvisor pins low on that ladder. OpenAI is the high rung ? civilizational consequence, which is why I wrote a $50M check despite an apology letter to LPs. Rocket Lab is mid ? a $28M bet that re-opened space access for thousands of small-sat operators. The low rung is the AI-copilot-for-vertical-X pattern I pass on weekly: 40% efficiency in one profession. Clawvisor sits just above that low rung. If Clawvisor's gateway works perfectly, AI agents get a safer credential vault and a cleaner audit trail ? Auth0 already ships Token Vault for AI Agents at auth0.com/ai, and WorkOS shipped FGA. The world is incrementally less compromised. No basic human need becomes free. Nothing visibly changes for billions of people. The Grand View number ? $22.56B in 2024 to $322.39B by 2033 for "agentic AI cybersecurity" ? is a TAM slide, and TAM slides are fiction.

Non-linear technical approach is the second-lowest rung. Commonwealth Fusion Systems is the high rung ? new high-temperature superconductor magnets, plasma physics, step-function. Impossible Foods is mid ? heme synthesis, real chemistry but a bounded breakthrough. The low rung is the LLM-API-wrapper that I dismiss without a meeting. Clawvisor sits closer to the low rung than I'd like: 14+ adapters in Go, an "API gateway for purpose-based authorization." That is an architecture choice, not a discontinuity. Composio already has 500+ integrations, Nango has 700+, and Arcade.dev raised $12M in March 2025 marketing itself as the MCP runtime with task-time auth. Clawvisor's "purpose verification on every API call" is a policy layer ? replicable in a quarter by any of the funded competitors. The dossier explicitly notes "no proprietary data, patents, or named enterprise customers disclosed." There is no Rutherford engine here.

Founder as learning machine is where Clawvisor scores highest, and even here I have to override my stated preference. Mach Industries' 19-year-old MIT dropout is the high rung ? naive outsider attacking Lockheed. Peter Beck of Rocket Lab is mid ? self-taught New Zealand engineer. The low rung is the rehearsed-resume founder reading from a playbook. Eric Levine is the wrong shape for my thesis: ex-Google, ex-Airbnb Trust & Safety, prior YC S18 with a $70M exit, currently a YC Visiting Group Partner reporting to Garry Tan. He is the established wide receiver, not the best athlete. The Berbix exit proves he can execute and exit ? but it also calibrates the likely Clawvisor outcome to roughly the same magnitude. I score this mid-band, not high, because consequential founders surprise me; Levine is doing exactly what his resume predicted.

Improbable ambition pins low. Hermeus building Mach 5 passenger aircraft is the high rung ? the kind of audacity that makes industry experts laugh. The low rung is incremental SaaS. Clawvisor's stated goal ? "the authorization layer for AI agents" ? is a serious infrastructure category, but it is not improbable. Six funded teams (Composio at $29M, Arcade at $12M, Nango at $7.5M+, Anon at $6.5M, plus Auth0 and WorkOS) are racing the same lane. Path to radical affordability is the lowest rung of all. Curai delivers AI primary care at near-zero marginal cost; Impossible Foods aims to make protein cheaper than animal agriculture. Clawvisor's pricing model is undisclosed but the framing ("sales@clawvisor.com," enterprise audit trail) is classic B2B SaaS premium positioning. Nothing expensive becomes free.

My 36-month forecast for Clawvisor: acquihired by Auth0/Okta, WorkOS, or HashiCorp for $40?100M as a feature inside an identity platform, with Levine landing as a VP of Agent Security ? a near-replica of the Berbix-to-Socure trajectory. The mechanism is mechanical: incumbent identity vendors already ship agent-auth modules, the gateway architecture is replicable, and Levine has demonstrated he runs the 5-year-build-and-sell playbook. That outcome would return 2?5x on a $500K pre-seed check, which is fine for most investors and useless to me.

The bull case I have to take seriously is the OpenAI exception: when the founder is the *exact* domain expert ? Levine ran Trust & Safety abuse detection at Airbnb and built a forensics engine for spoofed identity documents at Berbix ? maybe the consequence magnitude of agent security is bigger than I'm pricing. If 80% of jobs are done by AI by 2030, as I've publicly predicted, then every one of those agents needs authorization, and the gateway that enforces purpose at the call layer becomes critical infrastructure. The bear answer that wins: that future does not produce one Clawvisor. It produces an MCP-level standard that Anthropic, Auth0, and the Linux Foundation Agentic AI Foundation co-author, and the gateway becomes a protocol feature. Arcade is already a Gold member of that foundation; Clawvisor isn't mentioned. The verifiable condition that flips me to invest: Clawvisor signs three named Fortune-500 design partners at $500K+ ACV within 12 months *and* pivots from "yet another auth gateway" to authoring a piece of the MCP authorization spec ? evidence that Clawvisor is shaping the standard, not getting commoditized by it.
