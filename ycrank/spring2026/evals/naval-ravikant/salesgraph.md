# Salesgraph -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Salesgraph is a clean founder-fit story bolted onto a permissioned distribution architecture, and the pairing tells you exactly what kind of bet this is: Ricardo Nunez and Ruhan Ponnada were first-10 at Mintlify, they shipped the internal business-case generator that became Salesgraph's conceptual seed, and they are now building a Slack-native overlay that breathes through Gong, Salesforce, HubSpot, Outreach, and Salesloft APIs. The founders cleared my specific-knowledge gate before they incorporated. The business architecture sits on the wrong side of the four-leverage taxonomy.

I run the leverage check first, the way I always do, and Salesgraph fails it cleanly. The HIGH rung on this ladder is **Twitter** (2007) — anyone can post, the platform charges nothing, the marginal cost of replication is zero, and the distribution is media-as-protocol with no gatekeeper. The shared mechanism I look for is *permissionless propagation* — a user, a developer, or a creator adopts the product without anyone's blessing and brings the next user with them. Salesgraph diverges on the exact axis: the dossier names twelve named integration partners as the *distribution body* of the product, and any one of them — Gong, Salesforce, Agentforce pricing changes, Clari+Salesloft post-merger API terms — can compress unit economics or feature parity by a board memo. The GTM is "[Inferred]: founder-led enterprise sales leveraging Mintlify alumni network." That is labor and capital leverage. That is the leverage type that requires permission (nav.al/product-media). I have a structural underweight on it that predates this dossier.

The LOW rung on the marketplace dimension is the single-sided SaaS subscription where each customer is economically independent of the next, and Salesgraph sits squarely on that rung. There is no two-sided supply, no cross-customer data flywheel disclosed, no developer or creator network where the Nth user makes the (N-1) prior users more valuable. The "proprietary deal-graph dataset" the dossier hypothesizes as a future moat is intra-tenant data, not cross-tenant compounding — every Salesgraph buyer's deals stay inside that buyer's instance, by enterprise procurement requirement. This is the opposite of the **Uber** (2010) and **Stack Overflow** (2008) math that produced the 10,000x outcomes underwriting my whole portfolio theory. Without that math, the upper bound of Salesgraph's geometry is bounded by sales-enablement category economics, which the dossier itself describes as a $6.13B-to-$25.65B market already occupied by a merged $450M-ARR Clari+Salesloft and a $300M+-ARR Gong.

Specific knowledge is where Salesgraph actually earns points, and the mechanism matters: Ricardo and Ruhan didn't identify this market from a McKinsey deck — they were the engineers who built the predecessor artifact inside Mintlify, a YC-backed enterprise dev-tools seller, which means they have lived the *exact* enterprise sales motion they are now tooling. Over an 18-month horizon, that compounds — they know which Gong fields matter, which Salesforce custom objects break in practice, what a mutual action plan actually needs to look like for an enterprise dev-tools deal to close. That is the **Stack Overflow** (2008) Atwood-Spolsky pattern in miniature: practicing operators in the domain. The score here would flip into the top quartile if the dossier disclosed either a $100K+ pilot with a named Mintlify-network customer or a shipped open-source artifact that pulls organic developer attention; neither is present.

The market-pivot dimension is where I apply the counterfactual test: what would have to be true for Salesgraph's score to climb a tier? The dossier names the Sales Enablement Platform TAM at $7.20B in 2026 expanding to $25.65B by 2034 at 17.20% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights), which is large but architecturally *bounded* — every adjacent pivot vector available to Salesgraph (forecasting, coaching, CRM-native, vertical revenue agents) lands inside the same enterprise-procurement-gated buyer. For the score to climb, the founders would need to articulate a pivot path *outside* the per-seat sales-rep envelope — into developer-facing revenue tooling, into a creator-economy adjacency, into a builder product that monetizes the founders' actual specific knowledge of shipping dev-tools-to-enterprise. The dossier shows no such path.

On the Buffett trio, Salesgraph reads neutral and clean — single-paragraph parallel to **Notion** (2013) on this axis only: two co-founders with prior shared shipping history at the same employer, no contested exits, no integrity-volunteering in any public surface returned by search, BS CS Georgia Tech 2023 plus Microsoft/Pioneer/Mabbu prior for Ruhan. I'd want to spend ten years with engineers who shipped together inside a YC company; nothing in the dossier suggests otherwise. The trio passes. The trio is not the bottleneck here.

My 36-month forecast for Salesgraph: reaches $500K-$2M ARR on mid-market revenue teams sourced through the Mintlify alumni network, then either acqui-hires into Clari+Salesloft or Outreach for $8-25M as the merged incumbents consolidate the orchestration layer, or pivots into a narrower vertical revenue-agent product for dev-tools-selling companies — the exact slice where the founders' specific knowledge is non-replicable. The mechanism producing this forecast is the high/low pairing itself: specific knowledge floors the outcome above zero, permissioned leverage architecture ceilings the outcome below the 10,000x math my portfolio is constructed around.

The strongest bull case against my pass is the **Anchorage Digital** precedent in my own override list — there, permissioned architecture flipped into permission-as-moat when the regulatory clause itself became leverage, and a steelman could argue Salesgraph's deepening API integrations and proprietary deal-graph dataset could similarly invert into a switching-cost moat against the Clari+Salesloft+Gong stack. The reason this doesn't carry: Anchorage's permission was a Treasury OCC charter granted to roughly two entities; Salesgraph's "permission" is Gong's standard integrations marketplace alongside every other orchestration vendor in the Momentum.io 2025 buyer's guide. The leverage isn't scarce. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a publicly disclosed signed contract with a named enterprise customer where Salesgraph is the system-of-record for at least one revenue workflow (not an overlay) by Q4 2026, paired with organic developer pull around an open-sourced piece of the deal-graph schema. Until that lands, Salesgraph is a competent team building permissioned middleware in a category where the incumbents already compound — exactly the bet I structurally don't write.
