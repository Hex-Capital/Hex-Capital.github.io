# Alchemy -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The payer problem is what I notice first. I've talked publicly about why I've never invested in EdTech -- there's just not a payer in the education market. Academic research labs have structurally similar dynamics. PIs run on grant funding, they're accustomed to free tools (CellProfiler has been the standard for nearly two decades), and the budget approval process for new software subscriptions in university settings is adversarial. Alchemy is building for "life science researchers," and the dossier infers academic labs as the initial segment. That immediately triggers my filter for markets where the value recipient and the check-writer are different entities with misaligned incentives. The researcher wants better analysis; the grants office wants to minimize line items; the PI has been using ImageJ since grad school. Pharma and biotech R&D departments are the obvious escape hatch -- real budget authority, willingness to pay for productivity tools that shave months off drug discovery timelines. But the product appears designed bottom-up for individual researchers, not top-down for pharma procurement. The GTM sequence matters enormously here, and I see no evidence they've thought through which payer to prioritize.

On market structure timing: the capability shift is real but not nonobvious. Vision-language models crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 that makes natural-language-to-pipeline assembly feasible for the first time. Two years ago, you couldn't describe a microscopy analysis task in plain English and get a working pipeline back. That's a genuine structural change. But here's the problem -- every investor in the AI ecosystem recognizes this. "AI for bioimage analysis" is one of the most obvious applications of current vision model capabilities. When I backed Perplexity, AI search was a category that most investors thought had already failed. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto was fringe. The highest-alpha opportunities are markets where consensus is wrong, not markets where consensus is right but nobody's executed yet. The microscopy analysis market has been growing steadily at ~14% CAGR -- it wasn't stuck like legal tech was before Harvey. There's no structural barrier being removed; there's an incremental technology improvement making existing workflows faster. That's a real business opportunity, but it's not the kind of nonobvious window that generates outsized returns.

The infrastructure-layer question is where this falls short of my threshold. Alchemy is an application sitting on top of foundation model infrastructure -- a contested and rapidly evolving layer that the company doesn't control. When I invested in Stripe, the compelling logic was that thousands of businesses would build their payment flows on Stripe's API, creating compounding switching costs. Alchemy could develop some platform characteristics over time -- a library of validated processing nodes, reproducible pipelines embedded in publications -- but right now it's a tool, not a layer. The dossier itself flags the commoditization risk clearly: "Any ML-capable team with life science domain knowledge could build similar pipeline-assembly tooling." Foundation model providers could add microscopy-specific capabilities, open-source projects like napari could integrate LLM-powered interfaces, and hardware incumbents like Leica/Danaher are already moving to bundle AI analysis with microscope sales. When you're building an application on infrastructure someone else controls and your differentiation is UX rather than data or workflow lock-in, you're in a structurally vulnerable position.

The strongest bull case requires me to imagine a different version of this company. If Alchemy targets pharma R&D and CROs from day one -- clear payers with budget authority and acute pain around manual image analysis bottlenecks -- the payer problem disappears. If the "20 hours to 5 minutes" claim is demonstrable in customer pilots, that's a 240x improvement that creates genuine pull regardless of competitive dynamics. If they build a validated node library with enough domain-specific curation that switching costs compound, the application could evolve into something closer to infrastructure. And the structural gap is real: hardware-bundled vendors can't easily unbundle SaaS analysis, and open-source projects lack the incentive to build productized agent workflows. There's a genuine wedge here. But this bull case requires Alchemy to navigate from where they appear to be (bottom-up academic tool) to where they'd need to be (pharma workflow infrastructure), and that's a GTM pivot, not just execution. I don't see evidence the founders have the domain relationships or enterprise sales capability to make that transition.

On the team: both founders are recent graduates without documented experience in the specific domain they're targeting -- bioimage analysis software and computational biology tooling. Baek's biomedical engineering background and Boston Scientific experience provide adjacent domain knowledge, and McBride's UIUC CS credentials and early patent activity signal technical capability. But neither has operated at a scaling company, and the co-founder relationship shows no visible shared history. I'd want to see either deeper domain expertise in the exact problem space or evidence of rapid iteration speed with actual research lab customers. The M.Ed. in Psychological Counseling on the CEO's resume is unusual -- it might indicate versatility, but it doesn't signal the kind of focused domain obsession I'd want from a founder building specialized life science tooling. At pre-seed I give founders the benefit of the doubt on execution capability, but the market structure concerns here are significant enough that exceptional founder-market fit would need to compensate, and I don't see it.

The single-miracle test reveals the compounding risk: the technology working accurately across diverse microscopy modalities (fluorescence, brightfield, electron microscopy each have fundamentally different characteristics) is one challenge, but distribution into labs that default to free tools and converting budget-constrained researchers into paying customers are additional, independent challenges. That's at least two miracles required simultaneously. If the technology miracle were the only one -- as it was for Harvey, where law firms were already proven technology buyers -- I'd be more optimistic. But layering a distribution miracle on top of a technology miracle in a market with entrenched free alternatives pushes this outside my comfort zone.
