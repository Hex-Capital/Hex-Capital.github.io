# Foaster -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's my problem with Foaster in one sentence: every other investor at demo day is nodding along. "AI will disrupt consulting" is maybe the single most consensus take in venture right now. Distyl AI just raised $202M. Aily Labs took $101M. PromptQL has $136M+. There are at minimum five well-funded companies attacking variants of this exact thesis, and that's just what showed up in a quick scan. When the market has already poured half a billion dollars into your category and the tags on your YC page read "Artificial Intelligence, B2B, Enterprise Software" — congratulations, you've described the most oversubscribed zip code in venture capital. The upside is in the price. There is no asymmetric bet here. You're buying at market.

The bull case is real and I want to take it seriously. Management consulting is a $300 billion industry built on a labor arbitrage that hasn't changed since the 1960s — partners sell the work, junior analysts do the work, everyone bills by the hour. McKinsey literally cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper product without destroying its own economics. That's a textbook innovator's dilemma. And these founders have a relevant technical credential: the CTO built Repondia, which he claims handled 60,000+ AI-conducted calls, and together they published LLM behavioral benchmarks that OpenAI's president shared. That's not nothing. If you squint, there's a version of this where the per-client operational map becomes a persistent data asset with genuine switching costs — the consulting equivalent of owning the customer's workflow graph. In that world, you'd be investing in infrastructure, not a service. But that world requires a series of things to go right that I can't observe yet, and the dossier gives me zero traction signal — no revenue, no named customer, no LOI, no case study with an identifiable client. For a consulting business, the engagement IS the product. No engagements, no evidence.

The primitive test kills it for me. "AI interviews employees faster than humans" is not a new behavior — it's a faster, cheaper version of an existing behavior. Process mapping, AI roadmap design, cost reduction — these are the exact service lines McKinsey already sells. Foaster delivers them with agents instead of 24-year-olds in suits. That's an execution improvement, not a category. When Venmo launched, there was no word for what it did. Nobody was "Venmo-ing" each other before Venmo existed. People were absolutely doing management consulting before Foaster. Nobody is going to say "we got Foastered" the way they say "Venmo me." This is the kind of company that competes on being better in an existing market, and competing on execution in a market with $300B incumbents who have brand, trust, and 50-year client relationships is a losing proposition, especially for two 23-year-old students without a single consulting industry credential between them.

And then there's the AI kill test, which this fails clean. The entire technology stack — LLM-based interviews, data synthesis, recommendation generation — runs on generally available foundation models. The dossier says it explicitly. McKinsey has a 700-person AI practice. Bain just partnered with OpenAI. Any of these firms can build an internal interview agent, bolt it onto their existing client relationships and brand trust, and sell it as a premium feature rather than a standalone product. This is exactly the "Adobe for AI" pattern — every time someone pitches me a workflow automation tool built on top of GPT, I ask who already owns the customer relationship and can add this as a feature. In consulting, McKinsey owns the relationship. Accenture owns the relationship. Hell, even Salesforce could bolt an employee-interview agent onto their existing enterprise deployment. Foaster is a feature waiting to be absorbed. The operational map switching cost is the best counterargument, but a data asset built on one engagement isn't a moat — it's a deliverable.

The founders are clearly smart. Twelve million views on LLM benchmarks, OpenAI's president amplifying their work, the CTO's claimed experience running 60K AI calls — these are real intellectual signals. But "smart young founders with a sensible idea in a hot market" is the most common profile at YC. I need sane people doing insane things. Nothing about "make consulting cheaper with AI" qualifies as insane. It's the opposite — it's obvious. It's what everyone expects to happen. The ratio is off. I want to invest in things that make smart people uncomfortable, and AI consulting makes every smart person nod enthusiastically.
