# qomplement -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about qomplement is that they pivoted in the wrong direction. In May 2025, they described themselves as "the only OS agent that controls your computer — like having the smartest AI built right into your operating system." That's a big swing. A general computer-using agent that actually works would be enormously consequential — it would reshape how hundreds of millions of people interact with software. By March 2026, they'd narrowed to "AI agents for document filling." They went from attempting to automate all computer work to filling out PDF forms and Excel templates. Most investors would call that "finding focus." I call it retreating from consequence. The version of this company that scared me — in the good way — is the version they abandoned.

Let me apply my primary filter: if qomplement fully succeeds at document filling, what changes? Operations teams at mid-market companies process invoices faster. One transportation client saves $15,000 a month. Scale that across the IDP market and you get a profitable SaaS company worth maybe $500 million in a generous scenario. That's fine. It's also exactly the kind of outcome I refuse to spend time on. Nobody's life is materially different. No cost structure collapses. No new category of human capability emerges. When I invested in Impossible Foods, the consequence of success was feeding billions without animal agriculture. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems, it was limitless clean energy. "Enterprises fill out forms 15x cheaper" is not in that conversation. The consequence ceiling is too low to generate the asymmetric return profile I need.

The technical approach confirms this. qomplement is running quantized LLMs locally on 8–16 GB machines and wrapping them in a visual workflow builder with database connectors. That's competent application engineering. It is not a technology discontinuity. The "15x more economical" claim comes from avoiding cloud inference costs — a deployment decision, not a breakthrough in how AI processes documents. There's no proprietary model architecture, no novel training methodology, no science advantage. Nanonets has $100 million in revenue and $42 million raised. Reducto just closed $108 million. Rossum has $104.6 million in funding. These are well-capitalized extraction companies for whom adding a "fill" step downstream is a quarterly feature, not an existential pivot. qomplement's position as a filling specialist is less a moat than a head start measured in months.

The strongest bull case: the founders are young outsiders from Monterrey, Mexico — exactly the immigrant-outsider profile I've funded before. Kerim Taray's experience at Contalink (YC S21) gave him direct exposure to enterprise document workflows in Latin America, and the local-first architecture reflects a genuine insight about serving regulated, cost-conscious enterprises in emerging markets. The pivot itself is evidence of learning — they tested a broader thesis and narrowed to where customers were paying. There's a world where qomplement becomes the document automation standard for Latin American enterprise, expands north, and builds workflow stickiness that extraction vendors can't easily replicate. If I believed the document filling niche could sustain a company worth 50–100x my investment, I'd find enough founder signal to write a check. But that's the problem: even the optimistic scenario is a 5–10x return, and those don't justify the attention cost. I'd rather lose my money entirely on something where success reshapes an industry.

The affordability angle is the one dimension where qomplement scores above average for me. Making AI document processing accessible to Latin American companies that can't afford Nanonets or Rossum pricing — that resonates with the part of my thesis about making expensive things cheap. But "cheaper enterprise SaaS for emerging markets" is not the same as making a basic human need free. It's a business strategy, not a mission that changes the world.

I respect the founders for getting into YC, for pivoting based on real customer feedback, and for building a product that solves a genuine pain point. But I don't invest in pain points. I invest in consequences. If they'd kept building the general computer-using agent — the thing that could actually replace entire categories of knowledge work — I'd have leaned forward in my chair. Instead, they're filling out PDFs. Pass.
