# Moda -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing about LLM observability: it's the consensus play disguised as infrastructure. Every VC pitch I've sat through in the last eighteen months includes a slide about "the picks and shovels of AI" -- and monitoring is always exhibit A. Braintrust just raised $80 million at an $800 million valuation. LangChain sits at $1.25 billion. Langfuse got acquired by ClickHouse. When the market has already produced multiple well-funded winners in your exact category before you've left private beta, you're not pursuing an insane idea. You're late to a consensus one. Moda's pitch -- behavioral monitoring for AI agents -- is the kind of thing that sounds clever in a YC application but fails the most basic filter I apply: is this non-consensus? The answer is emphatically no. "AI agents need better monitoring" is what every engineering manager building with LLMs already believes. There is no contrarian insight here.

The founders are competent -- Waterloo CS, Shopify engineering, Pranav Bedi's stint at Cerebral Valley doing LLM observability work -- but competence in a crowded consensus space is exactly the wrong combination for my framework. I need sane people chasing ideas that make other investors uncomfortable. Nothing about "we built a monitoring dashboard for AI agents" makes anyone uncomfortable. It makes VCs nod approvingly, which is the death signal. Compare this to Teamshares telling investors they were going to buy hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership -- that made people squirm. That's the discomfort I'm looking for. Here, the founders' backgrounds lend some credibility to execution but don't create the founder-idea mismatch that generates outsized returns.

The structural moat problem is where this really falls apart for me. Moda is pure software monitoring software -- it's SaaS observing SaaS. The dossier itself concedes that "the underlying approach -- running LLM-based classifiers over agent conversation logs -- is technically reproducible." That's not a moat admission buried in the fine print; it's the entire story. Worse, the platforms Moda monitors -- OpenAI, Anthropic, Bedrock -- have every incentive and technical capability to build behavioral analytics natively. When I say software moats are dissolving, I mean exactly this: a monitoring layer that runs classifiers over API outputs is a feature, not a company. Anthropic could ship a "behavioral health" dashboard tomorrow and eliminate the need for a third-party tool. This is the opposite of Craftwork, where the moat is crews with paint rollers and route-optimized scheduling that no software competitor can replicate.

The bull case would require believing that agent behavioral monitoring becomes its own enduring category -- a new Datadog for autonomous systems. If AI agents proliferate to the point where every company runs dozens of them interacting with customers, and the failure modes are sufficiently complex that a specialized monitoring layer compounds proprietary detection capabilities over time, then the first mover with the deepest failure-pattern taxonomy wins. The data flywheel argument has some theoretical merit: more conversations analyzed means better classifiers means more accurate alerts means more customers. If Moda could establish itself as the standard behavioral monitoring primitive before the incumbents pivot, there's a path to real infrastructure status. But this argument requires that OpenAI and Anthropic leave monitoring to third parties permanently, that $260M-funded LangChain doesn't just add behavioral detection as a feature tab, and that the underlying model APIs remain stable enough for a monitoring layer to build durable value on top. I don't believe any of those three things.

This company sits at the intersection of every category I avoid: pure SaaS, AI-first where AI is the entire value proposition, application-layer with no protocol position, and a hot market where the pitch implicitly leads with TAM size. Remove AI agents from the equation and Moda has zero value -- it fails the "cherry on top" test completely. The business doesn't exist without the AI wave, which means it's riding a trend rather than building structural value. And within the AI wave, it's building on the platforms most likely to absorb its functionality. The "infinity story" here narrows rather than expands -- as the market matures, monitoring gets commoditized and bundled, not unbundled and elevated.
