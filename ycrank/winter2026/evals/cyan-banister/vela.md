# Vela -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to with Vela: this is the least weird AI use case I can imagine. "Let AI schedule your meetings" is literally the first thing every technology demo has promised since Siri launched in 2011. Clara Labs tried it and got acqui-hired into oblivion. x.ai tried it and faded away. Now with LLMs making the underlying technology accessible to essentially any competent engineering team, there will be dozens -- maybe hundreds -- of teams building scheduling agents this year. When I look at my best investments, they share a quality of being deeply unpopular at the moment I wrote the check. SpaceX was lunacy. Uber was illegal. Crusoe was burning gas in North Dakota for Bitcoin. Nobody was competing for those deals because nobody else wanted them. Vela is the opposite -- it's a category where Google, Microsoft, and Apple are all shipping AI calendar features into products that already own the calendar layer. That's not a pocket of dreamers and weirdos. That's a consensus stampede.

What genuinely impresses me is Gobhanu. Building SWIFTmfi at fourteen years old -- disbursing $400,000 in interest-free microloans to over three thousand women across seventy-seven countries -- that's not a line on a resume. That's a kid who looked at a broken system and decided to fix it with his own hands. The Diana Legacy Award, leaving Wharton summa cum laude, the Obama-Cheney Voyager scholarship -- this is someone with real gravity. And Saatvik finishing a dual CS degree at Carnegie Mellon in two years with highest honors, filing patents at sixteen, building on AWS's ML Supercompute team -- the technical credibility is there. These are clearly extraordinary young people with the kind of raw capability that makes you sit up. But when I ask my first question -- the one I always ask -- "tell me your story, not the business, tell me how you got here" -- I'm listening for the thread that connects the founder's life to this specific problem. And I can't find it. Gobhanu's story is about microfinance and economic empowerment for women in the developing world. That's a mission that could have produced a company I'd write a check for immediately. Instead, it produced an AI scheduling tool for enterprise recruiters at Netflix and LinkedIn. The biographical thread is severed. These founders chose this problem because it's commercially sensible, not because it kept them up at night.

The one dimension where Vela genuinely lights up for me is the sibling co-founder relationship. After watching HQ Trivia implode because two co-founders couldn't resolve a fifty-fifty split, I started paying close attention to how founding teams handle conflict. Gobhanu and Saatvik have been building together since they were teenagers in Oman -- they co-founded SWIFTmfi, they've navigated the pressure of international recognition, and they both made the decision to leave elite graduate programs to go all-in on Vela. Family businesses have a conflict resolution muscle that most co-founder pairs simply don't have. These two have been stress-tested across years and geographies. That matters, and it's something I weight heavily.

Let me be honest about the bull case, because there is one, and it's real. The traction claims -- enterprise logos including Netflix, LinkedIn, Scale AI, AWS, "thousands of interviews scheduled each week" -- at pre-seed, that's remarkable execution. If those numbers are accurate, these founders have demonstrated they can ship product and acquire sophisticated customers fast. The Paradox acquisition by Workday genuinely does open a market gap for non-Workday shops. And the multi-channel approach -- actually negotiating across email, SMS, WhatsApp, Slack, Teams, and phone rather than just sending a booking link -- is a different product architecture than what Calendly or Clockwise offers. If Vela can accumulate enough organizational scheduling context to become genuinely hard to rip out, there's a data flywheel story here. The question is whether that flywheel spins fast enough before an incumbent ships something good enough natively. I don't have enough conviction that it does.

What I don't see is a company that makes an invisible system visible or creates meaningful economic access for people who lack it. Vela saves time for already-employed recruiters at well-resourced technology companies. That's valuable -- I'm not dismissing it -- but it's not the kind of structural empowerment that draws me to a bet. When I backed Uber, I saw a poverty-alleviation tool that would create income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. When I backed Affirm, I saw transparent credit replacing predatory hidden fees. Vela is a productivity optimization, and a solid one, but it doesn't carry that same weight of expanding who gets to participate in the economy. Gobhanu's SWIFTmfi work shows he understands that mission deeply -- which makes it all the more striking that Vela doesn't embody it.

I respect what this team has built in a short time, and I wouldn't be surprised if Vela becomes a successful business. But this isn't my kind of bet. It's a consensus category, the founder-problem biographical connection is absent, the defensibility against incumbents who own the calendar layer is uncertain, and the real-world impact dimension that I need to feel excited doesn't activate here. I'd pass and tell Gobhanu that when he's ready to build something as ambitious and unlikely as what he did at fourteen with SWIFTmfi -- something that makes people uncomfortable, something most investors would dismiss -- I want to be his first call.
