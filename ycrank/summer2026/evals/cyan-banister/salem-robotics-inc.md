# Salem Robotics Inc -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Caleb Horan and Janak Panthi are two PhD researchers out of UT Austin's Nuclear and Applied Robotics Group with 8+ years combined at Los Alamos, and Salem Robotics is what happens when the people who have actually walked contaminated pads with a radiation detector decide the paper compliance record should be a robot's job instead of a human's. The signature here is a very tall founder-lived-experience column sitting on top of a very short physical-verifiability column — Salem Robotics is a domain-authentic bet stuck in pre-deployment demo purgatory, where the moat is the biography and the missing thing is a robot I can watch clear a room.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril in 2017 is the rung that makes Salem Robotics legible to me on founder origin — Luckey had shipped Oculus before the defense pivot, and the pattern is founders who arrived at the regulated hard problem from a prior body of work in the exact substrate. Horan spent five years of year-round research internships at Los Alamos and published VLM-based autonomous task planning on Boston Dynamics Spot for radiation inspection; Panthi has six years of quadruped product development plus nuclear facility deployments plus a U.S. Army background; the two of them co-authored peer-reviewed work on multi-robot radiation surveys inside *active* nuclear facilities (arXiv 2402.15008). Where Salem Robotics diverges from the Luckey rung is scale of prior artifact — Oculus was a real consumer product that shipped to millions; Salem's founders shipped academic deployments and a PhD portfolio, not a company. That is why Salem Robotics sits above my mid rung but below the Anduril anchor: lived, published, in-facility, but not yet load-bearing at commercial scale.

The Flock Safety story is the one I keep coming back to on physical verifiability — I found Flock because I read the founder's Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café, which means the artifact was already alive in my physical environment before the deck arrived. Salem Robotics fails that test structurally: the website says the end-to-end system was "demonstrated in high-fidelity simulation," the five commercial nuclear plant relationships are unqualified as pilot-vs-paid, and by definition I cannot walk into a reactor containment building and observe a Spot doing an autonomous survey the way I could ride an Uber in 2010 or play Ingress in 2012. This is not the pattern where I sit at a hotel and stumble into the company; this is a category where verification lives behind NRC clearances. The Salem Robotics dossier does not overcome that with any observable field artifact — that is the honest deduction.

Nuclear, regulated, radiological, DOE-partnership-gated — Salem Robotics is exactly the "too hard or too unsexy" lane I fund and most of the council will not. This is the sleeve that produced Crusoe on flared gas and Mindbloom on ketamine; the moat compounds in domains other investors refuse to enter. Salem Robotics as a hardware-agnostic software layer running on other people's quadrupeds is slightly less capex than a first-party robot company, which trims the Anduril-grade capital intensity, but the vendor qualification and radiological safety clearances at commercial nuclear plants are a compounding regulatory moat that a generalist AI-agent team cannot rebuild in a quarter. This dimension is where Salem Robotics is most obviously mine.

On magically-weird — 18 months from now, if Gecko Robotics is still executing on its $1.25B-valuation infrastructure-and-defense footprint including the USAF Nuclear Weapons Center contract, does Salem Robotics look like the contrarian nuclear-inspection bet or does it look like the number-two vendor in a category the market already priced? The category is unsexy, which is my lane, but Gecko at $347M raised and a Series D in June 2025 means the *category itself* is no longer laughed at the way flared-gas Bitcoin mining was in 2018. Salem Robotics is workflow-specific-software-in-a-known-hard-market, not Crusoe-tier "wait, you want to do WHAT." That is why this dimension scores in the middle, not the top.

Caleb Horan is the named CEO and Janak Panthi is the named CTO — the titles are clean and the roles are delineated, which is the structural inverse of the HQ Trivia Yusupov/Kroll and GameCrush post-mortems I actually invoke on this axis. Salem Robotics has two co-founders from the same lab with the same LANL trajectory, which is co-founder-relationship risk in the "they've never disagreed under pressure" sense, but there is no 50/50 equity language, no shared-CEO structure, no telegraph of unresolved authority. I score this as clean-with-a-caveat rather than clean-with-a-crisis.

My 36-month forecast: Salem Robotics converts one to two of the five commercial nuclear plant relationships into paid deployments, lands a modest DOE task-order contract via the LANL alumni network, reaches $500K–$1.8M ARR, and is either acquired by Gecko Robotics for the radiological-workflow IP plus the two founders, or raises a small Series A on DOE contract volume without breaking out of the "second-place vertical vendor" position. The mechanism is straightforward — nuclear procurement cycles are 18-36 months minimum, Salem Robotics' hardware-agnostic wedge is real but narrow, and Gecko's incumbent-substitution risk is a live threat because a $1.25B company can afford to add a radiological survey module before Salem Robotics reaches escape velocity.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that Salem Robotics gets pulled into the SMR-buildout wave — U.S. reactor license renewals plus small modular reactor construction plus DOE decommissioning at $7.38B and 5.65% CAGR means survey frequency is genuinely expanding, and a hardware-agnostic compliance-reporting layer could become the reference dataset for NRC/DOE reporting the way I speculated in the moat section. If Horan and Panthi convert the LANL relationships into a sole-source DOE prime contract, the ceiling moves from "acquired by Gecko" to "the compliance layer for the U.S. nuclear renaissance." My honest counter is that HandUp is my precedent for overriding capital-defensibility on mission alignment, and I do not have a nuclear-adjacent biography to justify that override here — the exception doesn't fire. The verifiable condition that flips me from Invest to Strong Invest is a named, signed, paid pilot at one of the five commercial nuclear plants disclosed by end of Q2 2027 — a real robot doing a real survey on a real reactor floor, not another simulation.
