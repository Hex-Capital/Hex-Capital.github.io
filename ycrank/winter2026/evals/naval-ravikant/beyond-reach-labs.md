# Beyond Reach Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The specific knowledge here is almost unreasonably deep. Mitchell Fogelson didn't study deployable space structures -- he invented a new category of them. His PhD produced the HERDS and PETS concepts, funded by NASA NIAC, validated in microgravity on NASA's dime, and published in peer-reviewed literature before the company existed. This is knowledge that took years of obsessive work at the intersection of mechanical metamaterials, structural optimization, and orbital mechanics. You cannot hire for this. You cannot acquire it through a six-month deep dive. Fogelson is one of perhaps a dozen people on Earth who understand how to make a rigid structure expand 50x while maintaining the stiffness properties spacecraft need. Pele Collins complements this with seven years of hands-on space hardware at SpaceX -- not writing software for rockets, but leading the engineering and production of the parachute systems that returned NASA astronauts from orbit. He knows the design-qualify-fly cycle from the inside. Together, these two have been friends since their freshman year at Penn in 2013 -- thirteen years of shared context. When I backed Extropic, it was because the founders understood thermodynamic computing at a level no one else did. The knowledge profile here is comparable: PhD-originated physics insight paired with production-hardened aerospace execution.

The leverage question is where this gets uncomfortable for my framework. Space hardware is not code. You cannot serve a million customers from a single deployment. Every array must be manufactured, qualified, and physically launched. This is not Notion, where one engineer's work multiplies across millions of knowledge workers. The leverage here operates at the physics layer -- 10x more usable power per kilogram of launch mass -- which is genuine but bounded. Each array delivers disproportionate output per unit of launch cost, and if Starship compresses $/kg to orbit by another order of magnitude, the demand for power infrastructure in orbit becomes enormous. But the business model remains hardware procurement: build arrays, sell to satellite operators and government agencies. Linear revenue scaling with production capacity. My portfolio has moved toward deep-science hardware since 2022 -- Atomic Semi, Extropic, Belfort -- precisely because I believe compute and power scarcity are the defining constraints of this era. The same logic applies in orbit. Power is the bottleneck for everything that matters in space: data centers, stations, lunar infrastructure. But I'm honest that this is a picks-and-shovels bet on orbital infrastructure, not a leverage-creating platform.

The contrarian timing is genuinely interesting. Most investors think about space as launch cost reduction -- the Starship thesis. Almost nobody is thinking about what happens after you can cheaply put things in orbit: you need to power them. The jump from 50 MW of orbital power today to the hundreds of gigawatts needed for orbital data centers and lunar outposts is a phase transition that hasn't been priced into anyone's mental model. The incumbents -- Redwire with ROSA, Northrop with UltraFlex -- are optimizing within the existing paradigm of roll-out and fan-fold architectures. Beyond Reach Labs is claiming a fundamentally different structural approach using metamaterials. If they're right, the incumbents can't get there by iteration; they'd need to develop entirely new IP. That's the kind of non-consensus positioning that produces asymmetric returns. Smart aerospace engineers look at this and say "interesting but unproven in orbit" -- which is exactly what they said about reusable rockets.

The bull case is compelling enough that I need to state it clearly: if orbital power demand follows anything close to the projected trajectory, and if the metamaterial-based approach achieves flight qualification, this company owns the dominant architecture for the next generation of space power infrastructure. The $175M in LOIs suggest real customer pull, even accounting for the typical inflation in non-binding space industry letters of intent. The advisors -- a former AFRL Chief Scientist and an MIT professor who co-advised the PhD -- provide direct channels to the DoD and NASA procurement apparatus. A successful Q2 2027 demo flight would create flight heritage that compounds: each successful deployment makes the next contract easier to win. In a world where every satellite constellation, space station, and lunar outpost needs dramatically more power, the company that solves the stiffness-mass tradeoff owns a foundational layer of space infrastructure.

The risks are real and specific to hardware: the technology has never survived orbit. Thermal cycling, atomic oxygen, radiation -- these are failure modes that destroy ground-tested prototypes. The gap between a zero-g flight experiment and a functioning orbital deployment is enormous. LOIs converting to contracts requires flight heritage that doesn't exist yet. And the capital requirements to reach that demo flight, starting from a two-person team with ~$200K in grants plus YC's standard deal, will require significant additional fundraising before any revenue materializes. This is a multi-year, capital-intensive path with binary risk at the demo flight stage. I've made bets like this before -- The ePlane Company was a small check on physics-enabled disruption outside my comfort zone. This is the same category: a speculative, physics-first bet where the founder's specific knowledge is undeniable, the timing thesis is credible, and the downside is bounded to 1x while the upside is genuinely uncapped if orbital power demand follows the curve.

The thirteen-year founder relationship, the willingness to leave SpaceX and a fusion energy company to pursue a PhD-originated technology, the NASA and NSF validation of the core research -- these all signal long-term orientation over quick-exit thinking. Nobody builds deployable space structures for a fast flip. The integrity filter passes without notable concern.
