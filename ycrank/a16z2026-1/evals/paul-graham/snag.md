# snag -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Three companies together. That's the first thing I'd focus on with snag. Selin Sonmez and Niko Georgantas met at Pratt, co-founded Knock Knock City (acquired by LuggageHero), worked together post-acquisition, then co-founded OneRoof in 2020 and raised $4.6M from General Catalyst for a neighbor community app in 395+ NYC buildings. When that didn't work, they pivoted to a sublet marketplace — now snag — and launched the iOS app in November 2025. Five months later: 30,000 student users, 3,000 listings, $6.5M in booking requests in 30 days growing 40% month-over-month. That co-founder durability across three ventures is genuinely rare. Most co-founder relationships shatter under the stress of a single failed product, let alone two pivots. But durability and resourcefulness are different qualities, and the dossier is curiously absent of the kind of creative-workaround stories that distinguish relentlessly resourceful founders from merely persistent ones. They had $4.6M from General Catalyst for OneRoof — that's not selling cereal boxes to survive. I don't see evidence of building under real constraint.

The technical founder question bothers me more than anything else in this dossier. Both Selin and Niko are designers — BID from Pratt, UX at IBM, product design at Red Antler. No public GitHub repos for either. Their former CTO, Cem Ozer, left OneRoof to start Sovereign Labs. The app is published under "OneRoof App, Co." on both app stores, and snag's claimed differentiation is a "proprietary AI model" that converts social media posts into structured listings. But who built the AI model? A founding team that can't build the product themselves lacks the iteration speed I consider essential at this stage. The dossier acknowledges the AI is replicable by "any team with ML capability" — so the technical moat is nonexistent, and the founders building it don't appear to be the ones with ML capability.

Here's what snag gets right: the NYC sublet market is a genuine schlep. Local Law 18 killed Airbnb-style short-term rentals under 30 days, pushing demand into exactly the 1-3 month sublet window snag targets. Airbnb's business model is optimized for nightly tourist stays; Zillow is built around 12-month leases and broker economics. Neither incumbent can easily serve this gap because their unit economics and regulatory positioning make it a poor fit — that's the structural barrier snag benefits from. Seven university partnerships with NYU, New School, Pratt, Baruch, NYIT, UAlbany, and Pace give snag a campus-by-campus distribution channel. The growth numbers in a single city are legitimate early marketplace signals. But the idea itself — an affordable housing marketplace for GenZ — sounds good to absolutely everyone. Nobody's first reaction is "that will never work." Blueground raised $352M, June Homes raised $50M before being acquired, Kopa went through YC, LeaseBreak has 100K+ registered users. The space is validated, which in my framework means the return premium has been competed away. The best startup ideas make smart people say you're crazy. Snag makes smart people nod.

The Airbnb comparison illuminates the gap. When Brian and Joe came to us, they'd been rejected by every VC. Their idea — strangers sleeping on air mattresses in your apartment — actively repelled investors. That repulsion was the signal. And the cereal box story wasn't just a cute anecdote; it was proof these founders could convince anyone of anything, which turns out to be the core skill of marketplace building. Selin's prior exit at Knock Knock City and Forbes 30 Under 30 recognition show consumer product execution, but the origin story of snag is a pivot from a funded community app, not a moment of personal frustration with finding a sublet. The dossier doesn't contain a single line where either founder describes their own housing pain. The manifesto talks about GenZ being "locked into 12-month leases designed for an older generation" — that's market framing, not lived experience.

The bull case for snag is real and I want to state it clearly. 40% month-over-month growth in booking requests is strong — if even a fraction converts to completed transactions, the marketplace flywheel is turning. The co-founder relationship surviving three ventures is among the most durable I've seen in any dossier. Selin has actually shipped and sold a product before (Knock Knock City acquisition), which puts her ahead of most first-time founders. The 4.7/5 App Store rating with 184 ratings suggests users genuinely like the product. And NYC is arguably the single best city on earth to launch a sublet marketplace — the density, the student population, the regulatory tailwind from Local Law 18. If snag can achieve real marketplace liquidity in New York and prove a monetization model, the city-by-city expansion playbook is well understood. What would change my mind: evidence that the founding team includes someone who actually built the AI system, a clear revenue number (not "booking requests"), and a concrete story of how they acquired their first 100 listings through sheer founder hustle rather than repurposing OneRoof's existing building relationships.

The $6.5M "booking requests" metric deserves scrutiny. Booking requests are not bookings. They're not revenue. They're intent — the top of a funnel whose conversion rate is unknown and whose monetization mechanism isn't public. For a marketplace that appears free to list and browse, the path from request volume to company revenue requires a transaction fee or service fee on completed sublets, and snag hasn't disclosed either the fee structure or the completion rate. This is the kind of metric ambiguity that makes me ask harder questions, not softer ones.

Snag lands in a zone where the founders are competent and persistent, the market is real, and the early traction is promising — but nothing about this company triggers the specific signals I invest on. The idea doesn't repel anyone. The founders are designers, not builders of the core technology they claim differentiates them. The problem discovery came through a business pivot, not lived frustration. The growth is encouraging but measured in a metric that obscures whether anyone is actually paying for anything. The two dimensions that most determine my score: Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — snag fails this test entirely, sitting in a consensus-validated space with multiple well-funded competitors — and Founder Resourcefulness — where three ventures together and a prior exit show persistence, but the absence of constraint-driven creativity stories and the reliance on $4.6M in prior funding prevent a high score.
