# Maquoketa Research -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I notice first about Maquoketa Research: defense drones are no longer the weird bet. When I was early to Anduril, defense tech was genuinely unpopular -- most of Silicon Valley wouldn't touch military contracts, and the founders building in that space were doing something the ecosystem considered distasteful. That discomfort was signal. Today, Anduril is valued at $14 billion. Helsing is at €12 billion. Germany just handed €9 billion in strike drone contracts primarily to startups. Every VC I know has a defense thesis now. So when I see a pre-seed team building autonomous attack drones in Spring 2026, my first reaction isn't excitement -- it's wariness. The weirdness premium that made defense tech an outsider bet has been fully absorbed by the market. This feels like founders entering a category because the consensus has already formed, and that's the opposite of where I want to deploy my money.

What I really want to know -- and what I can't find anywhere in this dossier -- is why these specific people are building attack drones. Tell me about Evan Yeager's story. He studied EECS at Berkeley, worked at SpiderRock doing trading technology. Alejandro Hernandez did electrical engineering at Berkeley's Wireless Research Center, specializing in phased array and RF systems. Aidan Williams -- I can find almost nothing. None of them appear to have military service, combat experience, time in conflict zones, or the kind of personal encounter with the problem that would make me believe they'll persist through the inevitable valleys. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception -- his biography and the company's mission were inseparable. Here, I see talented engineers who have the skills to build the product, but I don't see the biographical thread that tells me this is the problem they were born to solve. That absence is significant in my evaluation because the founders who survive defense procurement cycles, ITAR headaches, and the emotional weight of building weapons systems are the ones who feel called to it, not the ones who recognized a market opportunity.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Hernandez's phased array and RF specialization from BWRC is genuinely unusual for a company at this stage -- most pre-seed drone startups are assembling commercial off-the-shelf components, not designing advanced guidance electronics in-house. If their RF capability translates into meaningful electronic warfare resistance or autonomous targeting that outperforms what FPV drone providers like Neros can offer, that's a real technical moat. The market timing is undeniably compelling: AeroVironment can't scale Switchblade production fast enough, the new factory won't be operational until late 2026 or early 2027, and the DoD is actively seeking new suppliers through programs like PBAS. A team that genuinely builds a complete airframe-to-electronics stack with advanced autonomy -- during a production bottleneck from the incumbent -- could win early contracts and establish the procurement relationships that compound over time. If these founders have the kind of quiet, deep conviction that doesn't show up in a dossier search, and if they chose this problem because something in their experience compelled them toward it, this could be a meaningful company.

But I keep returning to the competitive reality. Five funded competitors with active military contracts and $100 million or more each in capital. AeroVironment has $990 million in Army contracts. Anduril has a broad defense portfolio and software-defined autonomy. Helsing has 6,000 units ordered by Ukraine. And Stark Defence secured a €268 million German contract despite failing all four test strikes -- which tells you something uncomfortable about how procurement relationships can matter more than product quality. A five-person pre-seed team with no disclosed contracts, no SBIR awards, no evidence of existing security clearances, and no public presence is entering this arena with a slingshot. I've seen startups win in defense -- that's what the German drone contracts proved -- but those startups had hundreds of millions in funding and government relationships by the time contracts were awarded. The path from here to there requires surviving a 12-to-36-month pre-revenue procurement cycle on pre-seed runway, while building hardware that requires ITAR compliance and possibly facility clearances. Nothing about that is impossible, but nothing about this team's visible profile tells me they've been through the kind of fire that prepares you for it.

This company also doesn't activate my economic access lens. Uber created income for drivers. Affirm created transparent credit. Flock Safety brought public safety data to communities that lacked it. Attack drones serve an important national security function, but they don't create economic opportunity for underserved populations or make an invisible system visible in the way my highest-conviction investments do. That doesn't make it a bad company -- it means it doesn't pull on the thread that makes me write checks from my own money with the kind of visceral conviction I need.

I'm passing on Maquoketa Research. The technical talent is real, and the market moment is genuine. But I invest in founders whose personal stories explain why they can't NOT solve this specific problem, and I invest when the idea still looks uncomfortable to most of the market. Neither condition is met here. Defense drones in 2026 are consensus, and these founders -- however capable -- haven't shown me the biographical fire that tells me they'll endure the brutal slog of defense procurement against competitors with thousand-to-one funding advantages.
