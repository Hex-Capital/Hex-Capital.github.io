# Bits -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking data point in this entire dossier isn't about Bits -- it's about SimpleClaw. A wrapper startup that hit $30K MRR in two weeks, then listed itself for sale at $225K within a month. Multiple other wrapper founders are simultaneously exiting. The industry has already coined the term "90-day wrapper window." When the founders of companies in your category are rushing for the exits before the paint dries, that's the market telling you something structural about defensibility. This isn't a market where first movers compound advantages -- it's one where the product itself can be replicated faster than you can build a brand.

The "why now" for Bits is real but potentially self-defeating. OpenClaw's explosive adoption (250K GitHub stars in 60 days) combined with the ClawHavoc supply-chain attack created genuine demand for security-hardened managed hosting. That's a textbook structural moment -- except the market it created has 143 startups generating a combined $320K monthly. That's roughly $2,200 per startup per month. This is the opposite of a non-obvious market at an inflection point. It's a consensus gold rush where every developer who can spin up a Docker container sees the same opportunity. When I invested in PagerDuty, nobody else was excited about operations alerting. When I look at OpenClaw hosting, everybody and their VPS provider is offering one-click deployment -- Hostinger, DigitalOcean, Amazon Lightsail, Cloudflare. The crowding is the signal.

My framework asks whether a startup requires exactly one miracle. Bits has the opposite problem: it requires zero miracles. There's no hard technical challenge, no regulatory barrier to clear, no marketplace liquidity to bootstrap. Security hardening an open-source project is valuable work, but it's configuration, not invention. And a startup requiring zero miracles probably isn't a defensible startup -- if anyone can do it, everyone will, and the dossier confirms they already have. Meanwhile, Bits faces a platform dependency that introduces risk without introducing moat: OpenClaw's creator is leaving for OpenAI, the project has been force-rebranded twice due to trademark issues, and governance is transferring to an unknown foundation. Building your entire company on a platform in governance upheaval is accumulating risk, not reducing it.

The strongest bull case would be that OpenClaw becomes the defining AI agent framework -- the Kubernetes of autonomous agents -- and that managed hosting becomes a massive, durable business category the way MongoDB Atlas or Vercel emerged from their respective ecosystems. If that happened, an early security-focused player with YC backing could capture a meaningful position. The founders have relevant backgrounds: both built AI agent infrastructure at Console, Bailey Wickham's GitHub shows genuine systems engineering depth (Rust, Go, C -- fuzzer, DNS client, container runtime), and they shipped a working product quickly. If you believe the AI agents market really grows at 49.6% CAGR to $183 billion by 2033, even a thin slice is enormous. But this bull case requires that OpenClaw specifically wins the framework wars, that managed hosting retains value as the ecosystem matures (rather than being absorbed by cloud providers as a feature), and that Bits specifically outcompetes Emergent ($100M raised, 104 employees) and every other wrapper. That's three sequential conditions, each uncertain -- and I don't fund multi-condition bets.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is the dimension where this company most clearly falls short of what I look for. Klaus is a managed hosting instance. What's the second product? What's the third? Stripe started with payments but the developer API became a distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Treasury. Figma started with design but the user base became a channel for FigJam. Klaus starts with hosting and... continues hosting. There's no articulated vision for how the customer relationship compounds into additional product distribution. The "batteries-included" framing describes a well-configured product, not a platform with expansion potential. At best, you could imagine expanding into agent monitoring, cost optimization, or workflow orchestration -- but nothing in the dossier suggests the founders are thinking about this, and the commoditized competitive landscape means any expansion would face the same low-barrier dynamics.

I pass. The market is consensus, the barriers are near-zero (the market's own participants are confirming this by listing their companies for sale), the platform dependency introduces uncontrolled risk, and the product has no visible trajectory toward becoming a distribution channel. The founders are competent builders in a relevant domain, but competent builders in a commoditized wrapper market produce commoditized outcomes.
