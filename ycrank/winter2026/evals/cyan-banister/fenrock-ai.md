# Fenrock AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The thing that strikes me immediately about Fenrock AI is that this is one of the most crowded spaces I've seen in years. Bretton AI just closed $75 million in February, Hawk AI has pulled in over $134 million, ComplyAdvantage has been at this for a decade with $108 million behind them, and even within YC's own alumni network, Flagright is building something very similar. When I look at a market and every major fund in the ecosystem has already placed a bet, the weirdness has been completely arbitraged away. This is the definition of consensus investing. I have a pretty clear rule about this: the most promising companies are not popular. AI-for-compliance is enormously popular right now. That doesn't mean it's a bad business — it means it's not my kind of bet. The opportunity for asymmetric returns at pre-seed in a category where a competitor already has 1.2 million completed investigations and institutional regulatory relationships is fundamentally different from the opportunities I chase.

Now, Charu Sharma has a genuinely interesting biography. The Indian National Bravery Award from the Prime Minister and President of India — I want to know that story. Scaling Osana Salud to six million patients across Latin America with $26.5 million raised, backed by General Catalyst's CEO, building a 100-person team — that's real. She knows how to operate, how to sell into enterprises, and she's clearly someone who has been through the fire more than once. The NextPlay.ai closure is not something I hold against her — I've seen founders grow enormously from shutting something down honestly, and the fact that she posted about it publicly on LinkedIn suggests she processes failure with the kind of transparency I respect. But here's where my framework hits a wall: I can't find the biographical thread connecting Charu to financial crime compliance. Her companies were in healthtech and HR tech. Why AML? Why sanctions screening? When I sit down with a founder, the first thing I ask is "tell me your story — not the business." I need to hear how your life made this problem yours. Right now, this looks like a talented operator who identified a large market and pivoted toward it, which is a perfectly rational thing to do but it's not the signal that gets me to write a check with my own money.

The second co-founder situation genuinely concerns me. "Michael M" appears in search snippets but isn't listed on the YC page, has no discoverable full name, no biographical details, no public profile. After HQ Trivia, I learned that co-founder dynamics are the single most predictive factor in whether a company survives crisis — and in a regulated enterprise space where sales cycles are long and capital is being burned, crises will come. I can't assess what I can't see. The opacity around this person is itself a signal I can't ignore.

The strongest bull case here is that Charu's enterprise sales DNA — three years at LinkedIn selling to large organizations — combined with her operational scaling experience at Osana could make her exactly the right person to build a compliance automation company. Sometimes the outsider brings fresh eyes to a calcified industry. If "Michael M" turns out to have deep regulatory and compliance expertise, this team could be formidable. The regulatory moat is real once you're embedded — once a bank's examiners accept your audit trails, switching costs are enormous. And the market is legitimately massive at $9 billion projected by 2035. If Fenrock could find a wedge that the well-funded competitors are missing — perhaps a specific geography, a specific tier of financial institution, or a novel approach to audit explainability — there's a path to something meaningful. But that's a lot of "ifs" stacked on top of each other, and I don't see the differentiation articulated anywhere in the public record.

What I uniquely notice is that this company doesn't create economic access or make an invisible system visible — the two patterns that run deepest in my portfolio. Compliance automation is an enterprise cost-reduction play. It helps banks process alerts faster. That's valuable, but it's not Uber creating income for drivers or Affirm making credit transparent for consumers. The people who benefit most are compliance departments at financial institutions, not underserved populations. I've learned to trust the investments where I can feel the human impact downstream, and I'm not feeling it here.

I respect Charu's track record and resilience. In a different market structure — one where this category was being ignored, where she was the only weird person who saw the opportunity — I'd lean in much harder. But she's entering a battlefield where the other combatants have hundreds of millions in funding, established regulatory relationships, and years of head start. Without a deep domain connection to the problem or a genuinely differentiated approach I can identify, this is a pass for me.
