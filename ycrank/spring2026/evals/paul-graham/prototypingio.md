# Prototyping.io -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Prototyping.io is what I'd call a menu-pick: two capable engineers, Revanth Bodepudi (UT Austin MS Aerospace, ex-Schlumberger) and Prerit Oberai (UIUC, ex-Microsoft AI), building exactly the company Nicolas Dessaigne wrote into the YC Spring 2026 RFS — "produce parts dramatically faster." When the company you're funding is named in the RFS coverage as the example of the RFS, you have to ask whether the founders found the problem or the problem found the founders. Prototyping.io's highest dimension is that manufacturing genuinely is a schlep; its lowest is that the dossier contains zero evidence anyone on the team ever uploaded a CAD file at midnight, waited four days for a quote, and got angry enough to build the thing themselves.

On Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — Prototyping.io's strongest dimension — Stripe sits at the top of my ladder, because PCI compliance and bank integrations were the kind of work that made every developer I knew physically recoil. Bindwell sits in the middle: pesticide chemistry repels, but two teenagers walked straight into it. The bottom rung is the consultant deck about "B2B workflow automation" from my Pass example. Prototyping.io sits between Bindwell and Stripe in theory — CNC machining, 5-axis milling, ±0.0001" tolerance, partner shops with grease on the floor — but the schlep has been entered already. Xometry did $686.6M FY2025 revenue; Hadrian raised $730M; MakerVerse is already shipping "AI price calculation with manual engineering review." When the schlep has $1.6B valuations attached, it's no longer repelling anyone. Prototyping.io is in a now-consensus schlep, which is a strictly worse position than an unconsensus one.

On Organic Problem Discovery — Prototyping.io's weakest dimension — Dropbox is the high rung: Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, and the company is the ghost of that frustration. Octopart is the middle rung: physics grad students who couldn't find the right capacitor. The low rung is the consultants who "identified an opportunity" through customer interviews. Prototyping.io sits between Octopart and the consultants, closer to the consultants. Oberai's bio claims he "worked across different industries observing fragmented hardware-build processes" — this is the language of observation, not pain. Bodepudi was at Schlumberger, which is closer, but no specific moment of frustration is on record. Compare this to how Drew talked about Dropbox in 2007 — he could tell you which flight, which thumb drive, which file. Prototyping.io's origin story, as best the public record shows, starts with the YC RFS, not with a part that didn't ship.

On Founder Resourcefulness Under Constraint, Airbnb's cereal boxes are the high rung; Patrick Collison shipping Auctomatic before Stripe is the middle rung; my Pass example — the deck-with-50-customer-interviews — is the low rung. Prototyping.io sits between the middle and the low rung. The site is waitlist-only. There is no demo video, no part gallery, no "we machined this on weekends in a friend's garage." For a company claiming end-to-end CAM automation across 3/4/5-axis milling, turning, and EDM at ±0.0001", the absence of a single shipped part on the homepage is the kind of silence I notice. Truly resourceful founders generate anecdotes; these two might have them, but the dossier shows none.

On Founder Learning Rate, Sam Altman at Loopt anchors the high rung — 19, no business experience, learning so fast I championed him for YC president. Patrick Collison's Auctomatic-then-Stripe arc is the middle rung. The low rung is founders whose product hasn't visibly changed in a year. Prototyping.io has no public iteration history — Spring 2026 batch, waitlist site — so I'm forced to default. Bodepudi's MRSEC bulge-test research and Oberai's Microsoft AI work suggest a baseline ability to learn hard things, which is why I won't go below the default. On Earnest Builder Identity, Watsi sits at the top, Replit in the middle, the buzzword-deck company at the bottom. Prototyping.io's homepage reads "AI-native manufacturing platform" — positioning language, not obsession language. No personal blog post wrestling with CAM automation, no Hacker News thread arguing about toolpaths. Earnestness is hard to read from a dossier, but the surface area I can see is marketing, not mission.

In 36 months, Prototyping.io is a $10-25M acqui-hire by Xometry, Fictiv, or Protolabs as a small CAM-automation team — not dead, not a unicorn, but absorbed. The mechanism is simple: the schlep dimension says manufacturing is real, so capable engineers building real CAM software have salvage value; the organic-discovery dimension says they don't have the missionary conviction to outlast the capital cycle when Hadrian's $730M war chest expands sideways into their lane. Engineers without an origin story tend to take the offer.

The strongest case against this forecast is the Boom Supersonic exception — that frighteningly ambitious hardware ideas justify capital-efficiency violations, and that Bodepudi and Oberai might be Blake Scholl in disguise. The dossier doesn't support it. Boom worked because the founder's missionary conviction was visible in every public statement; Prototyping.io's public surface is a contact form. Hadrian, the closer comparable, has $3M revenue against $730M raised — the capital bar to play vertically is enormous, and Prototyping.io has not disclosed whether they're going asset-light (margin war with Xometry) or vertical (capital war with Hadrian). Both paths require something the dossier doesn't show: founder-driven obsession that converts capital into compounding advantage. I'll flip to Invest if I see one specific thing — a video of Prototyping.io's software taking a CAD file from a real hardware-startup customer and producing a part on a real machine, end-to-end, no human in the loop, with the customer on camera saying it shipped in 24 hours. That's the artifact that would tell me the founders live in the future they describe. Until then, pass.
