# Ressl AI -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most important analytical question about Ressl AI is one the dossier can't cleanly answer: how much of that $440K revenue belongs to the current trades product versus the prior Salesforce administration business? The company announced itself as "Devin for Salesforce" in December 2024, and Salesforce-related content appeared as recently as mid-2025. If the bulk of that revenue is from the trades product built in the last few months, it's genuinely impressive capital efficiency — $440K from a 4-person team selling to low-tech trades businesses would be a strong execution signal. If it's carryover from consulting or Salesforce work, the trades business is essentially unvalidated. This ambiguity sits at the center of the entire evaluation, and I can't resolve it from public data.

The structural moment for AI agents in trades is real but increasingly consensus. When I think about my best investments, the market looked wrong at the time — PagerDuty seemed boring to everyone, Coinbase seemed speculative. Trades back-office automation would have fit that "too boring" archetype eighteen months ago. But ServiceTitan's December 2024 IPO at an $8.3B market cap made home services software a recognized category. Netic raised $43M at a $450M valuation backed by Founders Fund. Avoca raised $48M. This is no longer a market most investors would dismiss — it's a market multiple well-funded teams are actively pursuing. The capability threshold for multi-step AI agents crossed in 2024-2025, which is the genuine "why now," but every competitor sees the same threshold. When I map this against my framework for non-obvious markets, the trades AI space has moved from "hidden" to "emerging consensus" — still early enough for winners to emerge, but the asymmetric pricing that comes from being non-obvious is largely gone.

The piece that interests me most is the PE roll-up distribution channel. Arushi Gandhi built CRMs for private equity firms at Fundinc, which gives her direct relationships with the buyer persona she's now targeting. A PE firm that owns thirty collision shops or HVAC businesses wants margin improvement across its entire portfolio — one relationship could deploy Ressl across dozens of locations simultaneously. That's a distribution wedge that's structurally different from selling shop-by-shop. It's reminiscent of how enterprise infrastructure companies can skip the bottoms-up grind when they find an institutional buyer who acts as a force multiplier. If the PE channel works, the product-to-distribution trajectory opens up: you start with estimating agents, expand to procurement, insurance coordination, and scheduling across an entire portfolio. The question is whether that overlay approach — sitting on top of CCCOne, QuickBooks, and various ERPs — creates enough integration depth to generate switching costs, or whether it remains a thin layer that any well-funded competitor can replicate. Each new customer potentially requires custom integration work against software Ressl doesn't control, which looks more like a services margin structure than a software one.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things: that the $440K is primarily trades revenue (proving execution velocity), that the PE roll-up channel creates exponential distribution (solving the competitive capital disadvantage), and that workflow-specific data accumulated from operating inside trades businesses creates a compounding moat over time. If all three are true, Ressl has a path to becoming the back-office operating system for fragmented trades businesses — a "boring but critical" infrastructure play where the low-tech sophistication of the customer base creates natural stickiness. The agent-on-existing-software positioning also exploits a structural tension with ServiceTitan: ServiceTitan can't easily build a product that works on top of competitors' software without cannibalizing its own platform-replacement business model. That's a real opening.

But I keep returning to the multi-miracle problem. For Ressl to win, they need AI agents to work reliably across diverse legacy software stacks (miracle one), they need to out-execute Netic ($43M) and Avoca ($48M) with a fraction of the capital (miracle two), and they need the PE distribution channel to actually convert at scale (miracle three). Any one of these is achievable; requiring all three sequentially is the compounding-low-probability pattern I avoid. The pivot from Salesforce — with the timing suggesting the trades focus is only months old — adds execution risk on top of the competitive risk. First-time founders without deep trades industry experience, entering a market that now has multiple well-funded incumbents, with an integration-dependent approach that may limit gross margins — the structural setup doesn't give me the conviction I'd need to write a check from my own money, even acknowledging the genuine cleverness of the PE distribution angle and the real market timing.
