# Andustry -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the absence of a builder. I scan the founding team for a technologist and find a mechanical engineer from RWTH Aachen and TUM who consulted at McKinsey, and a technology management graduate who worked at a SaaS acquisition shop. No GitHub repos. No evidence of either founder having written the core product themselves. The dossier describes an "AI-agent platform" with Smart Supplier Search, Automated Outreach, Smart RFQ Processing -- but who built it? If the answer is contractors or a development agency, this is a structural deficiency I cannot look past. In the technology business, you invest in technology teams. A procurement marketplace powered by LLMs where neither founder has demonstrated they can build the LLM layer is a company renting its core capability rather than owning it.

Johann Sturken's family background in industrial goods trading is the strongest signal in this dossier -- and it's a real one. Knowledge absorbed through dinner-table conversation and warehouse visits since childhood cannot be taught at RWTH or McKinsey. He understands the trust dynamics, the opacity of pricing, the relationship-dependency of industrial procurement in ways a smart generalist doing customer discovery for three months simply would not. His mechanical engineering education and aerospace experience at Airbus and McKinsey A&D reinforce this -- he understands the physical world of manufacturing, not just the abstraction of it. But specific knowledge of the problem is necessary, not sufficient. Evan Williams didn't just understand public communication -- he had been building tools for it. Johann understands industrial procurement, but the evidence suggests he's been analyzing it (consulting) rather than building for it. Het Dave's SaaS-to-AI transformation background at Aries Global is relevant but generic -- it's a skill set, not specific knowledge about this domain.

The leverage architecture concerns me. Industrial machinery procurement is high-value, low-frequency, and relationship-dependent. Each transaction for a mid-size machine involves specification matching, compliance verification, trust evaluation, and often physical inspection. This is not like Notion, where one engineer's work serves millions of knowledge workers doing fundamentally similar tasks. Each industrial RFQ is bespoke. The AI agents can automate outreach and quote comparison -- that's genuine but incremental leverage, more like a productivity tool for procurement managers than a platform that creates a qualitatively new capability. The marketplace dynamics are theoretically present, but two-sided networks in industrial procurement face a cold-start problem that's particularly vicious: buyers won't use a platform without trusted suppliers, and suppliers won't onboard without buyer demand. EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is a positive signal for a two-person team, but it doesn't prove the flywheel is spinning.

The bull case worth engaging: industrial procurement is one of the last major B2B categories to be digitized, and the "why now" of LLMs crossing the threshold for reliable specification extraction and multi-supplier negotiation is genuine. Johann's family background gives him distribution into the German Mittelstand -- the hidden champions of European manufacturing who remain stubbornly analog. If the 500K+ supplier database is genuinely verified and proprietary (not scraped from Thomasnet or Kompass), it becomes a compounding data asset. If Andustry can reach density in a specific machinery category -- say, CNC machines in DACH -- before Matchory or LightSource expand there, they could own the niche. The bet would be that the industrial vertical is different enough from generic procurement that a focused, domain-native team beats horizontal platforms that treat industrial goods as just another category. For this to work, Johann's family network would need to be the wedge for initial supply-side density, and the AI layer would need to get measurably better at industrial spec matching through proprietary transaction data. It's a plausible path, but it requires the founders to be right about defensibility in a way that contradicts the evidence on commoditization.

Here is why I pass: this is a consensus play in a consensus category with a team that lacks a core technologist. "AI agents for procurement" is what every B2B AI startup is pitching in 2026 -- there are at least five funded competitors doing overlapping work, some with ten to fifty times more capital. The LLM-based software layer is explicitly reproducible; the dossier acknowledges this. The supplier database's proprietary status is unknown. I don't see what this team knows that Matchory's team with $8.86M, or LightSource's team with $33M, cannot learn. Johann's family background gives him an edge in understanding the problem -- but understanding a problem and building a defensible technology business around that understanding are different things. I need a founder whose specific knowledge extends to the solution architecture, not just the problem space. Without a technical co-founder who has built the core AI infrastructure, this is a domain-expert team that will be outbuilt by better-funded, more technical competitors.
