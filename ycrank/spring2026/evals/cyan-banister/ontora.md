# Ontora -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Max Arnold built agentic SAP process mining at Porsche and deployed it across four Volkswagen subsidiaries before starting Ontora. That's genuine domain competence — he has already proven the core technical capability works inside a Fortune 500 procurement organization. Leon Iwanowitsch built AI automations at consultancies, meaning he knows the buyer persona and the delivery model Ontora aims to replace. David Korn built data pipelines in finance. Three talented, complementary founders with relevant professional experience. But when I ask my first question — tell me your story, not the business — what I hear is: three university friends quit their jobs after a trip to Stockholm's fastest-scaling startups. That's inspiration tourism, not biographical inevitability. Max saw process mining work at Porsche and decided to productize it. Leon saw consulting inefficiency and decided to automate it. These are smart career moves, not the story of someone who lived inside a broken system and couldn't stop themselves from fixing it. Ontora's founders chose this problem; the problem didn't choose them.

Ontora positions itself as a $50K replacement for $500K McKinsey engagements, completing in hours what consultants take months to deliver by interviewing 100% of employees rather than sampling 10%. The structural argument is real — McKinsey, BCG, and Bain face genuine business model cannibalization risk because they can't automate away their own billable hours without destroying their revenue model. But this is the most consensus thesis in venture capital right now. Every investor I know is looking at "AI disrupts expensive professional services." Celonis has raised $2.4B. Skan AI has raised $57.6M. Glean has raised roughly $800M. Ontora is entering one of the most crowded, well-funded spaces in tech. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you describe this company at a dinner party — they nod and say "of course, that makes total sense." When three hundred investors passed on Uber and I wrote my check anyway, that was conviction in a space that made people uncomfortable. Ontora's thesis requires zero courage to articulate.

Ontora claims 80% of operational knowledge is tacit and undocumented, and their AI agents surface it through structured employee interviews. But I keep asking myself: visible to whom? The answer is C-suite leaders and operations executives. Ontora extracts knowledge from employees — the people who actually hold it — and delivers it upward to management. Compare that to Affirm, which made credit card opacity visible to the *consumer* being exploited. Or Ownwell, which made property tax assessments transparent to the *homeowner* being overcharged. Ontora's transparency flows in the wrong direction for my framework. There's no hidden tax on vulnerable people being eliminated. There's an operational inefficiency being resolved for executives who already have consultants on speed dial. The "invisible system" here is a company's own workflows — and the people it's invisible to are the people running the company, not the people being ground up by it. When I was fifteen and homeless, the systems that hurt me weren't invisible to the people at the top. They were invisible to me. Ontora solves the version of the problem that the powerful already have budget to address.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Ontora's "Unified Context Layer" and API/MCP Server could position it as infrastructure for enterprise AI — not just a consulting replacement but the operational knowledge substrate that makes every other AI deployment effective. Their stat that 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver zero measurable impact because agents lack operational context reframes Ontora as the missing layer in enterprise AI stacks. If every company needs its processes mapped before any AI agent can be effective, Ontora becomes infrastructure at an inflection point — the pattern behind Crusoe Energy, Substrate, and my strongest wins. The switching costs from an integrated context layer are real: once Ontora has mapped your organization and fed that knowledge into your AI tools, ripping it out means rebuilding the entire knowledge base. And the Vertiv testimonial — "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" — suggests the product genuinely works. But here's why the Crusoe parallel breaks down: Crusoe was magically weird. Bitcoin mining with waste gas in North Dakota oil fields made every sophisticated investor I know cringe. Ontora makes every investor nod. Crusoe's founders were building in a stigmatized space nobody else would touch. Ontora's founders are building in the single most competitive AI category of 2026. And the context layer infrastructure thesis is speculative — the current product is a consulting-replacement tool, and the core capability (LLM-driven interviews plus synthesis) could be replicated by any well-funded team with access to frontier models. Celonis could add conversational discovery to their platform next quarter.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: if Ontora's founders could show me that their organizational process maps have become the integration point that other AI tools depend on — that companies are routing Copilot, internal agents, and automation tools through Ontora's context layer — that would prove the infrastructure thesis and create the compounding defensibility I need to see. Right now it's a consulting automation tool in a consensus market. If it becomes the operational knowledge graph that enterprise AI runs on, the ten-year question gets interesting. But today, Ontora is a well-executed product by competent domain experts in a space where I see no biographical forge, no contrarian courage, and no invisible system being disrupted on behalf of the people who need it most. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Founder's Biographical Forge — a trip to Stockholm startups is not a crucible — and Contrarian Conviction — AI disrupting consulting is the most consensus bet in the market.
