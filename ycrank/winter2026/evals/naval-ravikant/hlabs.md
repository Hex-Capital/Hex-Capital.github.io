# Hlabs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting structural question here is whether the robotics market is going to look like the early internet — many builders, massive demand for shared infrastructure — or like the smartphone market — a few vertically integrated giants who manufacture their own components. Hlabs is a picks-and-shovels bet. I systematically favor infrastructure over applications. Alchemy over any individual DeFi app. Stack Overflow over any individual developer. AngelList over any individual startup. The pattern recognition fires immediately: if dozens of robotics companies need actuator controllers, compute modules, and sensing systems, the company supplying all of them captures more total value than any single robot builder. But the bet only works if the market fragments. If Tesla, Figure AI, and two or three others absorb the entire humanoid robotics opportunity and vertically integrate their supply chains, there's no ecosystem of small builders to sell components to. That's the central risk, and it's not one you can engineer away.

On specific knowledge: Paul Hetherington has real hardware DNA — MEng in integrated mechanical and electrical engineering, UAV control research, embedded systems work, commercial drone certification. That's not nothing. But his most recent six years were spent running Mystic AI, a serverless ML deployment platform. He's pivoting from software infrastructure to hardware manufacturing. The question I always ask is: what does this founder know that can't be taught? A smart engineer with embedded systems training could identify that robotics teams struggle with fragmented component supply chains — that insight doesn't require years of lived obsession. Compare this to the founder who has been hand-soldering actuator boards in a garage for a decade and discovered through painful iteration exactly which design tradeoffs matter. The second-time YC founder credential matters for execution — he knows how to ship, how to fundraise, how to navigate the YC network — but it's operational knowledge, not domain-specific knowledge about robotic component manufacturing that would take a competitor years to replicate. The pivot from ML infra to robotics hardware makes me ask whether the passion is for the specific problem or for infrastructure businesses in general.

The leverage architecture is where this gets complicated. My framework says: code as leverage is infinite; hardware as leverage is bounded. Every control board shipped requires manufacturing, inventory, quality control, logistics. That's labor-proportional scaling, which I generally avoid. However, there's a software layer here — open-source APIs and development tools — that could create the same dynamic Arduino created: a developer community that locks into your ecosystem and drives recurring hardware purchases. If the software becomes the standard interface for robotic prototyping, the hardware becomes the monetization mechanism for an open-source platform. That's a better leverage story than pure hardware margins. But it's not proven yet, and open-source actuator reference designs already exist in the wild (Open Dynamic Robot Initiative, OpenTorque). The leverage ratio is lower than what I typically require, though not fatally so for an infrastructure play.

What would make this a great investment? The bull case is precise: the humanoid robotics wave is creating a Cambrian explosion of robot builders. Dozens of startups, university labs, and defense contractors all need the same core components. Chinese manufacturers face tariff headwinds and ITAR restrictions that structurally exclude them from the US defense customer segment. Hlabs becomes the default component stack for US robotics teams the way Nvidia became the default compute stack for AI. 40+ orders in roughly four months — pre-YC, pre-funding, solo founder — is genuine market pull, not manufactured demand. If the market does fragment into many builders rather than consolidating into a few, this is a $1B+ infrastructure company. The open-source software creates ecosystem lock-in, the US manufacturing creates regulatory moat, and the integrated hardware-software stack creates switching costs that commodity actuator manufacturers can't replicate. If all that's true, I'm wrong to hesitate.

But I keep returning to the structural constraints. Solo founder on a hardware company is not a red flag I can ignore. Hardware is operationally brutal — managing manufacturing, supply chain, quality control, customer support, and multi-product development simultaneously requires a team, not a person. He's shipping a single product (FOC actuator control board) and has announced three more for Q1 2026 (Jetson-powered main board, camera system, wireless module). That's an ambitious hardware roadmap for a company with one employee and $500K in capital. Capital intensity is the enemy of angel returns — every dollar I put in gets diluted by the next round needed to fund inventory and manufacturing capacity. RobStride has Sequoia's backing and China's cost structure. This is a race where the competition has structural cost advantages and deeper pockets. The defense/ITAR moat is real but narrows the addressable market. I don't see the asymmetric upside profile I need — the failure modes are numerous (solo founder burnout, capital starvation, market consolidation, Chinese price competition) and the success case requires everything to go right simultaneously.

The timing is interesting but not deeply contrarian. "Picks and shovels for the robotics revolution" is a thesis every hardware-aware investor has already articulated. The specific execution — US-manufactured, open-source, developer-friendly components — has differentiation, but smart people are not dismissing this idea. They're actively looking for this exact company. That means the pricing will reflect optimism, and the competitive set will grow. I want to be right when everyone else is wrong. Here, I'd be right when everyone else is also right, which means the returns accrue to whoever executes best against well-funded competition, not to whoever had conviction earliest.
