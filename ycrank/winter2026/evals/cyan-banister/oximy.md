# Oximy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Oximy: this is one of the most popular categories in enterprise software right now. Every CISO I talk to is asking about AI governance. Every VC in the Valley has a thesis slide about "shadow AI" and enterprise compliance. The TAM projections are huge, the analyst reports are breathless, and Nudge Security just raised $22.5M to do something very similar. When I see a pre-seed company entering a space where well-capitalized competitors already exist and every investor already agrees the market is real -- that's the opposite of the signal I've learned to trust. The most promising companies are not popular. They are difficult to assess. Oximy is neither. It's easy to assess and very popular, and that makes me uneasy in exactly the way consensus deals always do.

I wanted to understand Naman's story -- not the business, the person. What I found is a clearly talented young builder. Google Code-In finalist at 17, started Easocare at 19, built a COVID tracker to 10M users, worked at Induced AI alongside people backed by Sam Altman. That's impressive execution velocity, and the fact that a prior investor came back for Oximy tells me Naman earns trust through delivery. But what I'm listening for -- that biographical thread where the founder's life experience and the company's mission become inseparable -- I don't hear it. He encountered the AI governance gap while working at an AI startup. That's a professional observation, not a wound. When I backed Contraline, the founder had spent years in academic research on male contraception that everyone else thought was a dead end. When I backed Uber, I'd spent years in the back of taxis thinking about the medallion racket. The connection between founder and problem needs to feel like destiny, not like market timing. I don't doubt Naman can build product quickly, but I question whether he'll endure the brutal multi-year enterprise sales grind in this specific category when the going gets truly hard.

My IronPort background actually makes me *more* skeptical here, not less. I spent years in security operations, and I know exactly what Oximy's technical core is: a network-level traffic interceptor deployed as a desktop agent. Their open-source "sensor" is a fork of mitmproxy -- a perfectly fine tool, but not proprietary technology. CrowdStrike already has agents on most enterprise endpoints. Zscaler already proxies network traffic. Adding AI tool detection to their existing capabilities is a feature sprint, not a new product build. The 3,500-tool taxonomy and the spend attribution logic are real product work, but they're execution advantages, not technical moats. When I think about defensibility, I look for something that gets harder to replicate over time. Here, the data moat could develop if Oximy achieves broad deployment, but the incumbents have a massive distribution advantage that makes that race very difficult to win.

The strongest bull case I can construct: the "why now" is genuinely powerful, the desktop agent catches shadow AI that agentless tools miss, the company is already processing millions of requests for enterprises in financial services and healthcare, and a focused AI-governance-only player might outrun broader SaaS management platforms by going deeper faster. If Naman can close a handful of marquee enterprise logos before Nudge Security and the endpoint security giants fill this gap, and if the data advantage compounds into something defensible, this could be a meaningful company. Enterprise buyers want best-of-breed tools in moments of category formation, and Oximy's AI-specific focus is a real wedge. I can see a path where this works. But it requires winning a sprint against better-funded competitors in a consensus category -- and that's a bet on execution speed in enterprise sales, which is not where I typically find outsized returns.

What I notice that a generic analyst might miss: this product makes AI usage visible, but visible to management and compliance teams -- to people who already hold power. My portfolio gravitates toward companies that create transparency for people who lack it. Uber gave economic agency to drivers. Affirm gave borrowers honest interest rates. Carta let employees actually understand their equity. Oximy gives CISOs a dashboard. That's useful, but it doesn't create the kind of economic access that gets me out of bed. If anything, it's a surveillance tool that monitors how workers use AI -- which sits uncomfortably against my instinct to back companies that empower individuals rather than institutions.

Naman is clearly someone with high execution velocity and real technical chops. A repeat investor validating his ability to deliver is meaningful. But this is a consensus play in a hot market, built on commoditizable technology, without the biographical depth or anti-consensus weirdness that defines my best investments. I'd be excited to see what Naman builds in a category where his personal story and the problem are inseparable -- where the idea sounds crazy to everyone else and he's the only person who can see why it works. This isn't that company.
