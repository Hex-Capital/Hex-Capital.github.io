# Ontora -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ontora is the commoditization problem. The core technology stack -- LLM-based conversational interviewing, RAG over enterprise documents, unstructured text analysis -- is built entirely on broadly available foundation models. The dossier itself flags this: differentiation "would need to come from domain-specific prompt engineering, integration depth, and accumulated organizational process data rather than proprietary model technology." When I evaluate infrastructure investments, I look for companies building a layer that creates compounding switching costs over time -- Stripe's payments API, Figma's collaborative design layer. Ontora's "Unified Context Layer" and "API & MCP Server" use infrastructure language, but the actual product is a diagnostic application that generates reports. Other businesses don't build on Ontora. It ingests your data, runs interviews, produces findings. That's a point solution, not an infrastructure layer. And point solutions built on commodity technology in hot markets get replicated fast.

The market timing question here is revealing, but not in Ontora's favor. Yes, there's a real structural shift: LLM quality crossed a threshold in 2024-2025 that makes open-ended conversational interviewing at scale feasible for the first time. That part is legitimate. But "AI disrupts management consulting" has been one of the most discussed theses in technology since GPT-4 launched. This is not a nonobvious window -- it's consensus. When I invested in Coinbase, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors. When I wrote the seed check for Perplexity, AI-powered search was considered a failed category. The consulting disruption thesis is on the cover of business magazines. The specific angle of unstructured data versus Celonis's structured event-log approach adds some nuance, but not enough to qualify as a market that most investors have dismissed or haven't noticed. My framework is built to identify structural shifts where consensus is wrong. Here consensus is directionally right, which means competition will be intense and the timing advantage that creates outsized returns is absent.

The incumbent dynamics concern me more than the startup competition. Celonis has $1.77B raised, $771M in revenue, and is already expanding into AI-powered process intelligence -- that's a well-funded adjacent player moving directly toward Ontora's position with established enterprise distribution. But the bigger issue is the consulting firms themselves. The "cannibalization" argument -- that McKinsey and BCG won't build this because it undercuts billable hours -- is a popular but incomplete analysis. I've watched incumbents in other markets. The sophisticated ones don't ignore platform shifts; they use new technology to increase leverage per consultant, expanding margins while maintaining client relationships. McKinsey has invested billions in AI capabilities. They have the client relationships, the trust, and the domain expertise. A three-person team with no visible GTM hires selling organizational diagnostics to C-suite buyers is going up against incumbents with decades of enterprise trust, and that's before counting the pure-play competitors like Worktrace AI (ex-OpenAI team, $9.3M seed).

The strongest bull case for Ontora: the $1T consulting market has been structurally resistant to technology disruption, and if this team can establish that "100% employee coverage in hours instead of months" genuinely delivers -- which the Vertiv testimonial from Julien Kang suggests is at least directionally real -- the value proposition is a 100x improvement on speed and coverage. If Ontora accumulates cross-company process benchmarks from enterprise deployments, that data asset could eventually constitute a real moat. And the consulting incumbents' incentive complexity around cannibalizing their own revenue model could create a longer window than I'm crediting. For this to be a great investment, three things would need to be true simultaneously: (1) the unstructured-data approach to process mining becomes the category standard over Celonis's structured approach, (2) the team cracks enterprise sales and builds trust for AI-driven organizational diagnostics before well-funded competitors do, and (3) consulting firms are slower to respond than I expect. That's three independent bets, and my single-miracle framework says compounding those odds is exactly where startups die.

Max Arnold's Porsche experience building CoAnalyst -- an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data -- is directly relevant to Ontora's technical approach and is the strongest founder signal in the dossier. But building an internal AI tool at a large company and selling enterprise organizational diagnostics to C-suite buyers are fundamentally different challenges. The team has no visible enterprise sales experience, no GTM hires, and a third co-founder (David Korn) with no confirmed public background. For a product that requires procurement cycles, security reviews, and executive buy-in at mid-to-large enterprises, this is a real capability gap. I've seen strong markets carry mediocre teams, but enterprise sales in a sensitive category -- where you're asking employees to candidly discuss their organization's problems with an AI -- requires a level of trust-building and sales sophistication that technical founders typically struggle with in the early stages.

The margin architecture is unclear but potentially problematic. Organizational diagnostics may be a periodic purchase (annual assessment?) rather than a continuous workflow, which creates a usage pattern closer to consulting engagements than recurring SaaS. If the revenue model ends up being project-based rather than subscription-based, you lose the negative-churn compounding that makes enterprise software investments attractive. The "fraction of the cost" framing against consulting projects suggests they may be pricing as a cheaper alternative rather than building a new value-capture model -- and competing on price against incumbents with deeper relationships is not a winning strategy.
