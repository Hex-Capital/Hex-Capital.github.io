# Miraka -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ninety percent of cardiac patients who qualify for post-discharge care never receive it. That number stopped me. When I look at Miraka, I don't initially see the biographical forge I always look for — I see a system. A system where someone has a cardiac event, gets discharged from the hospital, and falls into a void where care is technically available, insurance technically covers it, and the billing codes technically exist, but the patient has no idea any of it applies to them. The hospital gets penalized under HRRP when that patient bounces back to the ER. The patient suffers a preventable readmission. Miraka's 94% capture rate versus the 10% industry standard is the clearest articulation of an invisible system being cracked open that I've seen in healthcare this year. This is structurally similar to what Affirm did with credit card opacity — the terms existed, the consumer protections existed, but the system was designed so that ordinary people couldn't access what was already theirs. Miraka is doing that for cardiac patients and the care they're already entitled to.

The biographical forge, though, is where I get stuck. My first question is always "tell me your story," and what I see from Nolan Abeyta, Kazuo Nakamura, and Jesse Abeyta is three smart people who found a market gap through a Princeton incubator in 2021. I don't see the cardiac event in a family that kept someone up at night, the parent navigating a broken post-discharge system, or the personal encounter with the 90% failure rate that made this problem find them. What I do see — and this matters — is persistence. They've been building in cardiac post-discharge care for five years through Overwatch Health, then Paralign, now Miraka. They didn't pivot to building an AI wrapper when that became the easy fundraise. Nolan, who's barely out of Princeton, and Kazuo, who graduated a year before him, somehow sold into NewYork-Presbyterian — a top-5 heart hospital — and got their first patient there by February 2026. That's resourcefulness under constraint, which I recognize. Jesse's Amazon AI background and military exoskeleton biometrics work gives the team technical credibility. But resourcefulness is not the same as inevitability, and I can't tell from this dossier whether these founders would be working on cardiac care if there were no funding involved.

Where Miraka loses me is the contrarian test. Cardiac remote patient monitoring has $694M of known competitor funding sitting in the room — Cadence at $144M and a billion-dollar valuation, Biofourmis at $465M before its CopilotIQ acquisition, HRS at $85M. This is not a space that makes sophisticated investors shift in their chairs. Nobody's uncomfortable writing checks into healthcare AI. The differentiation — being the clinical team rather than licensing software — is real and structurally interesting, but it's a positioning choice within a consensus market, not a contrarian entry into a space others avoid. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs visibly squeamish. Miraka makes VCs reach for their checkbooks, not squirm. That's a fundamentally different signal.

The strongest bull case for Miraka rests on the clinical delivery model as infrastructure. If the >90% readmission reduction holds under third-party scrutiny, every hospital partnership becomes a switching cost — you don't rip out the team keeping your readmission penalties down. The CMS 2026 fee schedule lowering RPM billing thresholds is a genuine regulatory inflection, and Miraka is positioned to capture it. The $300K ARR with 71% month-over-month growth in billable appointments is meaningful pre-seed traction, and the insurance-reimbursed model means they've already solved the hardest part of healthcare distribution: getting paid. If Nolan and Kazuo can replicate the NewYork-Presbyterian playbook across five more top cardiac programs, the data flywheel — continuous wearable and EHR data improving predictive models — could create a moat that Cadence's SaaS approach structurally cannot match, because Cadence sells tools while Miraka owns the clinical outcome. For this to become a defining company, the clinical staffing model needs to scale non-linearly, perhaps through AI augmentation that lets fewer clinicians manage more patients — and the outcome data needs peer-reviewed validation that turns a company claim into a clinical standard.

The risk that would flip me, even from where I stand, is the linear scaling trap. Miraka has to hire clinicians to grow. Every patient requires human clinical time. In a healthcare labor shortage, that's not just expensive — it's a hard ceiling. Cadence's SaaS model doesn't have this constraint. Unless Miraka's AI meaningfully reduces the clinician-to-patient ratio over time — and I see no evidence yet that it does — this is a services business with a technology layer, not an infrastructure company with a services wrapper. My biggest wins have been infrastructure bets where each new customer makes the product harder to displace and cheaper to operate. Miraka's current model does the first but not the second. The impact story is undeniable — making post-discharge cardiac care reach the 90% who currently fall through the cracks is exactly the kind of access expansion I look for, and the fact that it's insurance-covered means income isn't a barrier. But impact alone doesn't make an investment when the defensibility and founder forge signals are incomplete.

Miraka lands in my mixed-signal zone. The invisible system — 90% of eligible cardiac patients abandoned by a broken post-discharge infrastructure — is one of the clearest I've evaluated, and that dimension carries real weight in my framework. But the founder story reads as market-opportunity-driven rather than biographically inevitable, and the competitive landscape is consensus-funded rather than contrarian. I'd want to sit with Nolan Abeyta and hear what happened in his life that made cardiac care inescapable for him — not the Princeton incubator story, the real one. If there's a forge underneath the credentials that this dossier doesn't capture, the score moves. As it stands, the invisible system alone isn't enough to override what's missing.
