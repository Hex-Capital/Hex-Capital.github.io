# Amboras -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

"The Shopify of the AI era" — that's how Amboras positions itself on its about page. When I hear a pre-seed company claim to be the next version of a $100B public company in one of the most hotly funded categories in tech, my first instinct isn't excitement. It's concern. Shopify has 4.8M+ merchants and just shipped its "Renaissance" update with AI-native features including Shopify Magic and agent-led commerce. Durable has raised $26.5M. Wix is public. Swap just closed a $100M Series C. Every venture fund in the world wants to write a check into "AI + e-commerce" right now. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when Amboras walks into the room — they nod along. That's the opposite of the signal I look for. My biggest wins — SpaceX when rockets were exploding, Anduril when defense tech was politically radioactive, Contraline in male birth control — all started with investors saying no. Amboras sits squarely in a consensus-attractive space where investors are competing for deals, and the YC tags confirm it: Generative AI, B2B, E-commerce. Three categories that every accelerator and every fund is chasing.

Amin and Imad Mokadem are brothers who attended ETH Zurich and built an e-commerce brand together to $400K+/month in revenue. Imad built his first Shopify store to sell board games he designed as a student. They "spent considerable time and money working with developers" to manage their store, and that frustration became Amboras. I respect this — they lived inside the problem, they felt the pain, they built a prior product (EcomCoder, which hit #14 on Product Hunt with 97 upvotes) before pivoting to a standalone platform. That's real domain experience. But when I ask my first question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is "we ran a successful store and saw a market opportunity." That's an operator's insight, not a biographical forge. Nobody survived something, nobody was broken and rebuilt, nobody is incapable of walking away from this problem. The brother dynamic adds another layer of concern: the dossier doesn't identify a clear CEO. I broke my own rule once with HQ Trivia — two co-founders, no clear delineation, and it failed entirely because of founder conflicts despite being the right product at the right time. Amin and Imad are brothers, which could mean deeper trust than typical co-founders, but it could also mean the power dynamic is even harder to resolve when things get difficult.

Amboras claims 1,000+ stores on its platform and a 3.9% average conversion rate. These are compelling numbers — if real. The dossier flags that these figures appear only on amboras.com with no third-party verification, and the testimonials feature merchants from Milan, London, Amsterdam, Mumbai, Barcelona, and Rome, which is an unusual geographic spread for a San Francisco pre-seed company. No revenue signal exists in any public source. The prior product, EcomCoder, was a Shopify plugin; Amboras represents a pivot to a standalone platform, meaning the 1,000+ stores figure belongs to a product that's months old at most. I've been burned by this pattern before — IRL fabricated its user metrics entirely. I'm not saying Amboras is doing that, but unverified traction claims with no independent confirmation and a suspiciously global testimonial base give me pause rather than conviction.

The invisible system test is where Amboras falls hardest for me. I grew up navigating opaque structures that exploited people who didn't understand them — that's why I backed Affirm against credit card opacity, Ownwell against property tax opacity, Carta against cap table mystery. Amboras operates in e-commerce, which is one of the most transparent, well-documented, well-functioning markets in technology. Shopify's pricing is public ($39–$2,300/month per emergent.sh). The app ecosystem is open. Merchants understand what they're paying for developers and agencies — they just find it expensive and complex. There is no "hidden tax," no information asymmetry being disrupted, no power being shifted from gatekeepers to users. Amboras is solving an operational convenience problem, which is legitimate, but it's not the structural revelation that defines my highest-conviction bets.

The defensibility question concerns me most. Amboras is "powered by Anthropic's Claude AI" according to Extruct's profile. The core technology — LLM-powered store generation, A/B testing, SEO optimization — uses generally available AI models. The dossier itself states: "Any well-funded e-commerce platform or AI builder could replicate individual features." Lee Jacobs put it plainly when he said we don't invest in "B.S SaaS companies or AI wrappers." Amboras has a theoretical data flywheel — each store generates conversion data that feeds optimization models — but that flywheel requires massive scale to become a moat, and Shopify already has behavioral data from 4.8M+ merchants. A dedicated "Shopify Autopilot" product would replicate Amboras's entire value proposition within an ecosystem that merchants already trust and depend on. The channel conflict argument (Shopify would cannibalize its app store revenue) is real but temporary — incumbents resolve channel conflicts when existential threats emerge.

Let me steelman the case for Amboras: founders with $400K+/month in direct e-commerce experience, a genuine pain point (Shopify's plugin sprawl costs real money and time), legitimate timing (LLM capability thresholds crossed in 2024-2025 making autonomous optimization feasible), early traction of 1,000+ stores, and a structural conflict that prevents Shopify from moving fast. The RTFKT precedent in my portfolio shows I'll invest in consumer-adjacent plays at narrow timing windows even without infrastructure moats. Could Amboras be riding a similar narrow window where AI-native platforms capture merchants before Shopify adapts? The condition that would flip me: if Amin or Imad Mokadem showed me verified GMV flowing through Amboras stores — real merchants generating real revenue through autonomous AI optimization that demonstrably outperforms their prior Shopify setup — and if one of them was clearly the CEO driving the company's vision while the other served a defined complementary role, I'd reconsider. But right now, Amboras is a consensus AI bet in a transparent, well-funded market with no invisible system to disrupt, no contrarian conviction required, and commoditization risk from both incumbents and the foundation model providers whose APIs power the product.

Amboras has competent founders with relevant experience solving a real problem. I don't doubt the pain exists. But the two dimensions that most define my framework — contrarian conviction and invisible system disruption — both score well below my threshold. The space actively attracts investors rather than repelling them, and the market it serves is already transparent and functional. Those two gaps, combined with the AI wrapper defensibility concern, make this a pass for me.
