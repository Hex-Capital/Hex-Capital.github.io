# Inviscid AI -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the problem with Inviscid AI from my chair: this is an AI company whose entire value proposition IS the AI. Physics-informed neural networks that replace traditional CFD solvers -- that's the product, the moat claim, and the pitch all in one sentence. My first question with any company is always "would this business be good if AI didn't exist?" With Inviscid AI, the answer isn't just no -- it's that the company literally wouldn't exist. There's no physical operation underneath, no regulatory position, no protocol that other systems depend on. The AI isn't a cherry on top of a killer business. The AI is the sundae, the cherry, and the bowl. That's a feature, not a company -- and features get absorbed by incumbents with distribution.

The sane-person-insane-idea calibration is off in the wrong direction. Ziming Qiu is a 5th-year PhD student at NYU with ML publications -- credible but not the kind of operator credibility that makes you lean forward. Kabir Jain's background is essentially a blank page publicly beyond "likes physics and AI." But the bigger issue is the idea itself: accelerating CFD with neural networks is one of the most consensus applications of ML in computational science right now. NVIDIA built an entire framework for it (Modulus). PassiveLogic has $125M+ pursuing physics-based digital twins with backing from NVIDIA Ventures and Johnson Controls. Cadence already ships a Reality Digital Twin Platform for data centers. This isn't a case where everyone thinks the founders are crazy -- it's a case where everyone agrees this is a good idea, and the well-capitalized players are already executing on it. A sane person with a sane idea gives you consensus returns at best and a competitive bloodbath at worst.

The structural moat question is where this falls apart completely. Physics-informed neural networks are published academic research -- the methodology is in the open literature, multiple research groups work on it, and the tooling is commoditizing fast. No patents were found. The company's GitHub organization has one follower and a single forked repository with zero stars. The per-deployment data flywheel is real in theory, but any competitor who connects sensors to a building gets the same data. Meanwhile, the competitive field reads like a venture capital arms race: PassiveLogic ($125M+), BrainBox AI ($103M, acquired by Trane who has global HVAC distribution), EkkoSense (deployed at 300+ data center sites), 75F ($45M with Carrier and Breakthrough Energy as investors). A two-person pre-seed team entering this landscape needs either a radically different approach or a radically better team. I see evidence of neither.

The strongest bull case would require believing that the speed gap between traditional CFD vendors (built for design-time simulation sold to engineers) and real-time operational optimization (sold to facilities managers) creates a genuine business model chasm that incumbents can't cross. There's something to this -- Cadence and ANSYS sell per-seat licenses to engineering departments, not per-building SaaS to facilities operators. That's a real channel conflict. And data center thermal management is an urgent, growing problem as AI workloads increase compute density. If Inviscid AI laser-focused on data center cooling, established itself as the real-time simulation layer between IoT sensors and building management systems, and built enough deployment-specific knowledge to outperform generic tools, you could sketch a path to becoming essential infrastructure. But the case studies sprawl across HVAC, coastal infrastructure, and storm surge forecasting -- three entirely different verticals with different buyers, different physics, and different sales motions. With two people. That's not a wedge strategy; that's a research lab looking for a market.

The narrative has theoretical elasticity -- "real-time physics simulation for all physical systems" is a big story. But it's also PassiveLogic's story, and Cadence's story, and arguably NVIDIA's story. When your infinity narrative is shared with an $80B+ public company, the narrative isn't yours. The absence of any revenue signal, named customers, or production deployments means there's no downside cushion -- this investment would be pure narrative bet, and I need that narrative to be non-consensus and structurally defensible. Neither condition holds here.
