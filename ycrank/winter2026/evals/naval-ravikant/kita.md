# Kita -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is a gap between the specific knowledge the founders possess and the specific knowledge this problem demands. Carmel Limcaoco is from Manila -- that's genuine geographic context you can't acquire from a pitch deck. Rhea Malhotra won Stanford's Firestone Medal in computer science and published at ICRA, Humanoids, and RSS. These are individually impressive credentials. But the knowledge that matters here is how emerging market lending actually works: what a Philippine e-wallet fraud pattern looks like, why Indonesian payslip formats break extraction pipelines, how underwriting officers at Manila-based lenders actually decide to extend credit. Carmel knows the Philippines as someone who grew up there. She doesn't appear to know it as someone who built or operated lending infrastructure. Rhea's CV research is world-class but it's in robotics and manipulation, not document understanding or financial signals. The technical skills transfer, but that transfer is available to any strong CV researcher who decides to work on documents. When I backed Stack Overflow, Jeff Atwood and Joel Spolsky didn't just know software development -- they had been living inside the developer knowledge problem for a decade. I don't see that depth of domain immersion here.

The leverage question is where I spend the most time. "70x faster document processing" is an efficiency metric, not a leverage metric. Efficiency makes an existing process cheaper. Leverage enables a qualitatively new activity. The interesting question for Kita is whether faster document processing unlocks lending to populations that were previously un-lendable -- where the unit economics of manual review made small-ticket loans in the Philippines structurally unprofitable. If Kita collapses the cost of underwriting enough to make a $200 loan in Cebu viable for the first time, that's genuine leverage creation -- a new type of borrower gains access to credit that didn't exist before. But neither the founders nor the dossier frame it this way. The messaging is about making existing lenders faster, not about enabling fundamentally new lending. That's an optimization business. Valuable, but not the kind of leverage architecture I look for.

The bull case is real, and I want to articulate it honestly: the six-figure paid pilot secured the week the prototype was built is an unusually strong pre-seed signal. It suggests acute pain. If Kita executes on the data flywheel -- linking document-level signals to actual repayment outcomes across many lenders -- it builds something that resembles a shadow credit bureau for markets where no credit bureau exists. That would be infrastructure at the protocol layer, with compounding defensibility that gets stronger with every loan outcome fed back into the system. The structural disincentive for Ocrolus ($252M revenue, US-focused) to build localized models for Philippine e-wallet records is real. And the VLM timing is right -- vision-language models crossed a reliability threshold in 2024-2025 that makes this technically feasible for the first time. If everything compounds correctly, Kita becomes the Alchemy of emerging market lending: the picks-and-shovels layer that every fintech and lender plugs into. That's the kind of infrastructure play I systematically back.

But three things pull me back from conviction. First, the VLM commoditization risk is fundamental. The core extraction technology -- using vision models to parse documents into structured data -- is becoming a commodity capability. Any lender with an engineering team can call Claude or GPT-4V with a well-crafted prompt and extract data from a Philippine bank statement. Kita's moat has to be the lending-specific feedback loop and localization layer, not the extraction technology itself, and that flywheel is entirely prospective at this stage. Second, the contrarian positioning is weak. Emerging market fintech has been a recognized investment category since Nubank's rise. AI-powered document processing is consensus. The specific intersection is somewhat under-served, but no smart person actively dismisses this idea -- it's a logical application of obvious technology to a known problem. You don't get paid for being right about something everyone already agrees makes sense. Third, the market is geographically fragmented in a way that undermines the leverage thesis. Philippine documents, Indonesian documents, and Mexican documents have different formats, languages, regulatory requirements, and fraud patterns. Scaling from Manila to Jakarta to Mexico City isn't network-effect expansion -- it's essentially rebuilding localization for each market. That's linear effort, not compound leverage.

The founders have intelligence and energy -- Stanford pedigrees, the Firestone Medal, Apple internships, prior founding experience. No integrity red flags. Their co-founder relationship predates Stanford, which is a genuine signal of alignment. But when I apply the test I use for every deal -- "do you like them, can they teach you something, and are the economics right?" -- I find myself uncertain on the second question. I want founders who can teach me something I don't know about a domain. These founders can teach me about computer vision and robotics, and about growing up in the Philippines. I'm not sure they can teach me something surprising about how credit works in emerging markets, because I don't see evidence that they've accumulated that non-obvious insight yet.

This is a competent team building useful infrastructure in a real market. The early traction is promising. But it doesn't pass my specific knowledge + leverage test at the threshold I need. The founders' knowledge is geographic and technical -- both genuine but neither deeply lending-specific. The leverage is efficiency-based, not category-creating. The timing is logical, not contrarian. I'd watch this team closely -- if they develop genuine underwriting insight through their data flywheel and start framing themselves as the credit infrastructure layer for unbanked markets rather than a faster document processor, the thesis could shift substantially.
