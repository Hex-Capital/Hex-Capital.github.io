# Milliray (formerly DroneTector) -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The question I keep coming back to with Milliray is whether building a better radar constitutes the kind of consequence magnitude that justifies my attention. Drones are genuinely reshaping warfare — 1,600 attacks per month in Ukraine, doubled shipping costs in the Red Sea, £50 million in losses from a single Gatwick incursion. The problem is real and urgent. But urgency is not the same as transformation. If Milliray succeeds completely — captures significant share of the radar detection segment, deploys across NATO — the world has better drone detection at airports and military installations. That matters. Lives are saved. But the world still operates the same way. Compare that to what happened when Juniper succeeded: the entire networking paradigm shifted from legacy telco to TCP/IP. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they'd never build a TCP/IP router — and that's exactly why I knew the technology would make their architecture obsolete. Milliray isn't making radar obsolete. They're making a higher-frequency radar. DroneShield is already doing AUD 216 million in revenue with 276% year-over-year growth in this exact market. The existence of a publicly traded company growing that fast in your space is evidence the market is real, but it's also evidence that the category is being served. Milliray's success narrative is "we detect what others miss" — a feature advantage, not a paradigm shift.

What concerns me most is that these founders are the definition of domain experts executing their own research. Matthew Moore's PhD is literally on 94 GHz Doppler radar for drone micro-Doppler detection. He built the hardware, published the papers, and spun out the company. That's impressive craftsmanship, but it's the opposite of the outsider pattern that produces my best returns. Peter Beck didn't come from aerospace — he came from New Zealand with what the industry considered laughable credentials, and that freed him to build Rocket Lab in ways that aerospace veterans wouldn't attempt. Pat Brown was a biochemistry professor, not a food industry executive. Moore, Doherty, and Frances de Mas are three PhDs building precisely what their doctoral research predicted they would build. They know exactly what this industry considers possible, which means they also know what it considers impossible — and that set of constraints will shape their ambition whether they realize it or not. I see no evidence of contrarian thinking, abandoned assumptions, or rapid evolution of strategy. This is a straight line from university lab to startup, which produces good companies but rarely produces the kind of category-defining leaps I invest for.

The technology itself is genuinely differentiated — 94 GHz millimeter-wave radar yields finer resolution on small targets than the X-band systems that dominate the market, and micro-Doppler signature analysis is real, published science. But the differentiation is a matter of degree, not kind. Robin Radar already extended its detection range from 5 to 12 kilometers via software upgrade and has 200+ units deployed in Ukraine. Blighter detects nano drones at 3.6 km. These are established, funded competitors operating at different frequency bands. There's nothing preventing Robin Radar or DroneShield from adding a millimeter-wave module to their existing multi-sensor platforms — and they have the revenue, the customer relationships, and the procurement track record to deploy it. Milliray's 94 GHz advantage is real today but not structurally defensible over a 3-5 year horizon. The underlying physics is well-understood; the moat is execution speed, not fundamental science. And execution speed in defense procurement is determined by institutional relationships and contract history, where incumbents hold every advantage over a three-person team.

The bull case I have to honestly engage with: drone warfare is the most significant transformation in military conflict since precision-guided munitions, and it's happening on an exponential curve. Nano-drone swarms that are invisible to current detection systems could become the dominant weapon within five years. If that happens, Milliray's 94 GHz radar may be the only technology capable of seeing what's coming — and in defense, being the sole provider of a critical capability means price insensitivity and sovereign mandate procurement. European defense budgets are surging, NATO DIANA is actively channeling startups into procurement pipelines, and over $1 billion in counter-drone purchases closed in December 2025 alone. A sovereign European radar company backed by DASA and NATO DIANA, in a market growing at nearly 20% CAGR, could ride the defense spending wave to a multi-billion-dollar outcome. This is plausible. But it requires Milliray to navigate 18-24 month procurement cycles while competing against DroneShield's $2.3 billion pipeline, and to do so with three people, one of whom is simultaneously running a quantum computing company. The CTO dual-venture issue isn't incidental — in a three-person deep-tech startup, every founder's full bandwidth is load-bearing. If Doherty is splitting time with Quantum Fabrix, the team effectively has two and a half founders building specialized radar hardware. That's a meaningful execution risk.

The timing is the strongest dimension here. The drone threat curve is accelerating, defense budgets are expanding, and the gap between nano-drone capability and detection capability is widening. Milliray is positioned at the right point on that curve. But being on the curve is different from being ahead of it. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, AI was something most VCs dismissed — I was four years early. When I invested in cleantech in 2004, the category didn't exist in venture. Counter-drone is already a consensus investment thesis. DroneShield's 276% revenue growth made the front page of every defense investor's newsletter. Milliray isn't inventing a category — they're entering one that's already attracting billions in capital.

I respect the team's technical depth and the genuine urgency of the problem they're solving. But I invest for consequence magnitude that transforms industries, and I invest in outsiders who bring perspectives that insiders can't imagine. Milliray is three insiders building a better version of existing technology in a market that's already being served by well-capitalized competitors. It's a good company. It's not my kind of bet.
