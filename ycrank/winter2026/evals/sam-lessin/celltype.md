# CellType -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about CellType is not the science — it's that David van Dijk co-developed Cell2Sentence with Google DeepMind, published it open-source under Apache 2.0, and then turned down Google to commercialize it as a two-person startup competing against Isomorphic Labs (Google's $600M drug discovery spinout built on the same intellectual lineage). This is the structural question that dominates my analysis before anything else: did van Dijk create a methodology that requires *him* to commercialize, or did he publish a paper that Google will absorb on its own timeline? Because I've seen this movie before. Birchbox published the subscription box model into the world — the methodology was trivially replicable, and Katia and Hayley couldn't defend the position no matter how brilliant they were. The core IP here is literally Apache 2.0. Google has the compute, the data partnerships, and a $600M vehicle (Isomorphic Labs) purpose-built for this exact domain.

On the sane-person side, van Dijk is one of the most credentialed YC founders I've encountered. 11,589 citations, Yale Associate Professor, NSF CAREER Award, papers in Cell, Nature, ICML, NeurIPS, and a personal endorsement from Sundar Pichai calling Cell2Sentence "an exciting milestone for AI in science." The co-founder Vrkic built software for CERN's Large Hadron Collider and has foundation model training experience. These are deeply serious people. But here's my problem: "AI for drug discovery" is possibly the single most consensus investment category in biotech right now. Recursion raised $461M. Insilico raised $408M+. Isomorphic Labs raised $600M. Atomwise raised $220M. When every major tech company and dozens of well-capitalized startups are pursuing the same market thesis, the "insane idea" premium has been completely arbitraged away. What I'm looking at is a sane person with a sane idea — which in my framework is actually a *worse* signal than it sounds, because it means you're buying at market price into a category where the winners will be determined by capital and distribution, not by being early and contrarian.

The structural moat problem here is the one I'm most allergic to. CellType is an AI-first company where AI IS the core value proposition. My test is simple: would this business exist if AI didn't exist? The answer is categorically no. This is the exact opposite of my "cherry on top" framework — there is no business underneath the AI to provide a floor. And the specific AI capability (Cell2Sentence) is published, open-source, and co-developed with the entity best positioned to compete with it. The argument for a future data moat — that pharma partnerships will generate proprietary validation data that compounds — is legitimate in theory but hasn't materialized. One MOU with Senhwa Biosciences for a six-month pilot is a signal, not a moat. The "agentic pipeline orchestration layer" that CellType keeps as proprietary could be differentiating, but that's precisely the kind of software layer that gets commoditized fastest in the current AI environment.

The bull case I have to take seriously: van Dijk may be one of five people on Earth who can push this specific frontier forward fastest. The open-source publication established the *field*, and being the originator gives CellType a permanent head start in knowing where to take it next. Each pharma partnership generates tacit knowledge — not just data — about how to translate computational predictions into actionable drug development insights. If computational biology enters a "winner captures the field" dynamic similar to early search (where the best model wins exponentially, not linearly), then the originator of the paradigm at a YC pre-seed valuation is spectacularly cheap. And the narrative ceiling is genuinely infinite — "simulate all of human biology" scales from cancer immunotherapy to every disease, every drug, every clinical trial. If drug discovery is one of those rare domains where AI creates genuinely new capabilities rather than commoditizing existing ones, I'm wrong about the structural setup, and this is a generational company.

But I don't think I'm wrong. The pattern I see — academic publishes breakthrough, starts company to commercialize it, gets outgunned by the platform companies that have distribution and capital — is one of the most reliable failure modes in technology. Van Dijk's relationship with Google adds a specific wrinkle: he co-built this with them. Google has the models, the compute, the Gemma architecture Cell2Sentence runs on, and a purpose-built entity (Isomorphic Labs) to deploy it. The academic-to-commercial transition with a two-person team and no one with pharma commercial experience compounds this risk. Drug discovery deals take years to close, require regulatory expertise, and demand relationship depth with pharma procurement that citations alone don't provide. I respect the founder enormously. I just think the structural setup — AI-native company, open-source core IP, consensus market, platform dependency on Google — hits too many of my avoidance criteria simultaneously.
