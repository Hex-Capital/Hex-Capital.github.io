# Smart Bricks -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Smart Bricks claims that "real estate is the only trillion-dollar asset class that doesn't already have its own AI-native computing stack." I want to be precise about what kind of statement this is: it is an observation that every proptech investor, every McKinsey partner, and every real estate conference keynote speaker already agrees with. It appeared, nearly verbatim, in a dozen pitch decks I saw last year. A genuine secret makes knowledgeable people uncomfortable or dismissive — it does not generate nodding heads in a room full of VCs. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is "AI can transform real estate," we are not in the territory of secrets. We are in the territory of consensus dressed in contrarian language. The dossier lists EliseAI ($392M raised, $100M+ ARR), Cherre (~$130M raised), Roofstock ($373M raised), and Reonomy ($140M raised, acquired) — four well-funded companies attacking adjacent pieces of the same thesis. The existence of this competitive landscape is the clearest possible signal that there is no monopoly secret here.

Mohamed Mohamed's credentials at Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, and McKinsey's AI practice give him genuine fluency in both institutional capital allocation and technology strategy — that combination is not common. His advisory relationship to ADQ, a $200B sovereign wealth fund, and his network of angel investors from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Blackstone suggest real distribution leverage. Forbes 30 Under 30, a 20-person team built in under two years, a16z Speedrun selection — these are signals of execution capability. But execution capability in a competitive market is the precise combination that destroys value. A determined, well-connected founder running hard in a market with five funded competitors is doing exactly what my framework warns against. The parallel to Wish is uncomfortably direct: Wish had execution, had scale, had capital — and had no monopoly structure. It IPO'd at $14B and crashed over 90%. Smart Bricks' simultaneous launch across Dubai, London, New York, and Miami compounds the problem — this is the opposite of Facebook starting at Harvard. Instead of monopolizing a single concentrated market before expanding concentrically, Smart Bricks is fighting four distinct regulatory regimes, four sets of local competitors, and four different customer cultures simultaneously. That is not definite optimism. That is diffusion.

The self-reported ~$12M revenue run rate demands attention. If this figure represents actual platform fees — not gross transaction volume — from a company founded in July 2024 on a $5M raise, it would be among the most exceptional pre-seed trajectories I have encountered. But no independent source verifies this claim, the metric's definition is unspecified, and the dossier itself flags the tension between a $5M raise and a $12M run rate. I treat unverified self-reported metrics with the same skepticism I apply to founders who describe their market as "$500 billion" without specifying what fraction they can monopolize. If this number is real and represents recurring platform revenue, my assessment of distribution effectiveness would shift materially. But extraordinary claims require independent verification, and none exists.

The technology moat question is where Smart Bricks fails most decisively against my framework. The dossier states plainly: "LLM-based property analysis is technically reproducible; the core models use publicly available foundation models." This is a software wrapper around someone else's intelligence layer. The "1M+ proprietary and public data feeds" sound impressive until you ask what percentage is genuinely proprietary versus aggregated from public sources that any competitor can access. Smart Bricks is not manipulating atoms — it is not building surgical robots, enriching uranium, or fabricating chips. It is applying commodity AI to a fragmented market. "We wanted flying cars, instead we got 140 characters" — and now we are getting AI-powered real estate dashboards. The commoditization risk the dossier identifies is not a risk; it is the current structural reality. A well-funded competitor — EliseAI with its $392M war chest, or a new entrant from Zillow, CoStar, or any large brokerage — could replicate Smart Bricks' core analytical capability in months.

The strongest bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that the $12M run rate is genuine recurring revenue, that the cross-border data network creates compounding advantages no single-geography competitor can match, and that Mohamed Mohamed's institutional network locks in sovereign wealth fund and family office customers with switching costs that grow over time. If all three are true, Smart Bricks could become a Palantir-like platform embedded so deeply in institutional real estate workflows that displacement becomes prohibitively expensive. But Palantir had a genuine secret — that the intelligence community needed software to fuse structured and unstructured data in ways that existing tools could not — and it had the CIA as its anchor customer, creating government-grade switching costs from inception. Smart Bricks has no equivalent anchor, no equivalent secret, and no equivalent structural lock-in. The Palantir comparison illuminates precisely what is missing.

Smart Bricks scores lowest on monopoly secret and physical-world technology moat — the two dimensions that most determine whether I write a check. Mohamed Mohamed is a capable founder with real institutional credibility, and if the revenue figures are independently verified, the distribution story improves meaningfully. But my framework is explicit: a brilliant founder in a competitive market with reproducible technology is still structurally uninvestable. The proptech category is crowded, the AI layer is commodity, and the multi-geography strategy disperses rather than concentrates. Pass.
