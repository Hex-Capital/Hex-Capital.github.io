# FinalDose -- Paul Graham Evaluation

FinalDose is a credentialed Oxford trio running at a frightening biological schlep -- "destroying all cancers" via a DNA-sensing kill-switch -- where the highest dimension is the size and ugliness of the schlep itself and the lowest is the pre-funding animal evidence I need to believe they will personally outlast it. Jeff Liu, Li-Yao Huang, and Steven Lin have the academic shape of people who could build the thing FinalDose claims to build. What I cannot find anywhere in the dossier is the cereal-box moment.

The schlep dimension is where FinalDose sits highest, and the load-bearing analogy is **Coinbase** (2012): Brian Armstrong walked toward bitcoin compliance grind nobody else in his batch wanted, and the grind became the moat. FinalDose is walking toward something an order of magnitude uglier -- $100M+ to first IND, in vivo specificity across billions of bases, an undisclosed delivery modality (no LNP, no AAV named on finaldose.ai as of May 2026), and a 3-5+ year preclinical timeline before any human sees this drug. Where FinalDose diverges from the Coinbase rung is that Armstrong had personally built the Android wallet to demonstrate appetite for the regulatory grind; FinalDose's dossier shows me Oxford pedigrees and a 7-day hit validation cycle, both of which are upstream of the grind, not evidence the founders will live inside it for a decade.

The lowest dimension is animal resourcefulness, and the failure mechanism I keep seeing in passed companies is the one from my own pass example -- a credentialed team with a strong-on-paper bio but no shipped artifact that scared the founders themselves before the institutional check arrived. FinalDose's strongest pre-funding fact is Liu's prior company Vivid Dx raising $15M, which tells me he can close investors but not that he is an animal; raising $15M from a sepsis-diagnostics seed is a credentialed-money signal, the opposite of the Obama O's cereal boxes that flipped **Airbnb** (2009) for me. No Hacker News demo, no GitHub for any of the three founders, no scrappy pre-YC revenue, no anecdote of an unscalable stunt. The dossier is silent here, which I read as absence, not as missing data.

On organic-idea fit, the mechanism over the next 18 months matters: Liu spent 5 years at the MRC Weatherall Institute running CRISPR screens for druggable cancer targets, and Huang built a Cas9 discovery-to-validation framework -- so the FinalDose problem statement (undruggable MYC, TP53, APC) is exactly the problem these two stared at in their day jobs. This is not market-spotting; this is the **Dropbox** (2007) pattern where Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB stick, except expressed in academic-lab terms. The thing I cannot tell is whether FinalDose is the tool they wished existed for themselves, or whether the kill-switch idea arrived later as a publishable angle on existing tools. The former compounds into Stripe-shaped conviction; the latter decays into a thesis project.

The counterfactual on hacker genealogy: FinalDose's score flips a tier upward if I find one of these three has a non-academic technical trail -- an open-source contribution to a CRISPR analysis library, a Kaggle prize, a Hacker News post that hit the front page, anything that says hacker rather than PhD. The dossier reports no GitHub account for Liu, Huang, or Lin. Steven Lin's Wellcome Trust scholarship for ML-on-coevolution is genuine technical work, but it is academic-shipped, not community-shipped, and my Unstated Pattern here -- the Lisp-literate, Young-Scientist-prize, RSS-co-author hacker signal that ran through Patrick Collison and Aaron Swartz -- is conspicuously absent. Biotech founders typically lack this trail, which is fine, but it means I have to lean harder on the other dimensions and the other dimensions are not yet carrying the weight.

On co-founder configuration, the single anchor is **Airbnb** (2009): Chesky/Gebbia/Blecharczyk were a RISD-roommate trio with explicit deep prior collaboration, and that chemistry showed in the cereal-box improvisation. FinalDose is a three-person team where all three did Oxford PhDs in adjacent fields, which is good shape on paper, but the dossier says only that they "worked in adjacent CRISPR/genomics research at Oxford" -- not that Liu and Huang ran experiments together, not that Lin built Liu's screening pipelines, nothing operational. Three Oxford PhDs is closer to the assembled-for-the-startup pattern than to the dorm-roommate pattern.

FinalDose 36-month forecast: still preclinical, no IND filed, no revenue (none possible in oncology on this timeline), and either raises a $20-40M Series A from a specialist biotech fund -- F-Prime, ARCH, Flagship -- on the strength of in vivo mouse efficacy in one tumor model, or fails to clear the A bar and quietly winds down without a press cycle. Acquisition by Beam or CRISPR Therapeutics is unlikely at this stage because the platform is too early to acquire and too undifferentiated against base editing to license; FinalDose either earns specialist-biotech survival capital or it does not.

The strongest case against my caution invokes my own Known Exception list: I funded **Airbnb** (2009) over my own gut-level rejection of the idea, and the cereal-box stunt is what flipped me. The bull would say FinalDose is a "Frighteningly Ambitious Startup Ideas" (March 2012) bet -- the ceiling on programmable cancer therapy is the ceiling, and I wrote that the No. 1 portfolio company beats the next 199 combined, so I should be indifferent between Wufoo and fusion if the founders pass the animal test. The dismantling: the Airbnb exception was overridden by founder evidence I could see and touch -- $40 cereal boxes, 1,000 units sold, a stunt I would not have invented. FinalDose's dossier offers me Liu's prior $15M raise as the closest analog, and a fundraising track record is the polar opposite of a stunt. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a public preprint, demo, or Hacker News post in which the FinalDose team shows in vivo selective cell death on a TP53-mutant model in mice, shipped by Liu or Lin personally before the next priced round. That would be the demo-before-deck artifact, in biotech form, and I would write the check the day it dropped.
