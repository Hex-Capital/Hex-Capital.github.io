# Kimpton AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that immediately catches my eye about Kimpton is the origin story: three founders who ran a quant fund together, built their own trading infrastructure over multiple years, and then realized the tools they'd built for themselves might be a product. That is almost textbook "live in the future, then build what's missing." Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive. These guys kept needing better research and execution tools for their fund and eventually built them. When I hear that Kimpton started as Level III Capital's internal stack, I think: these founders understand the problem viscerally, not theoretically. They weren't sitting around brainstorming startup ideas. They were trading real money with real tools and got frustrated enough to build better ones.

The co-founder relationship is also the kind I trust. Jack and Mauricio overlapped at Goldman Sachs before co-founding Level III Capital in 2021. Adrian attended the same university as Mauricio and joined as a partner at Level III. Four years of working together building quantitative trading systems -- not at a cushy corporate job where you can avoid conflict, but at a small fund where disagreements surface fast and either get resolved or explode. That's battle-tested. And all three are technical builders. Nobody on this team needs to hire someone to build the product. Jack did detection engineering and DevOps at Goldman. Mauricio did infrastructure engineering. Adrian was at SpaceX and then building cloud infrastructure at Vistra Corp. This is a team that can ship.

Here's my problem, though: "AI for investing" is the opposite of an idea that repels before it attracts. It's an idea that attracts everyone immediately. The dossier lists five funded competitors and notes that "LLM-based financial chat interfaces are proliferating." AlphaSense is at $500M+ ARR and a $4B valuation. Reflexivity just raised $30M with Druckenmiller backing them. Composer launched "Trade With AI" in October 2025. When I see a space this crowded, my instinct is that the returns have already been competed away. The best investments I've made were in categories where most smart people thought the founders were wrong -- Airbnb renting out air mattresses, Coinbase building on top of what seemed like internet funny money. Nobody thinks "AI tools for investors" is a bad idea. That's the tell. The "Cursor for investing" framing is borrowed cleverness -- it's positioning by analogy rather than from first principles, which is itself a small warning sign about how the founders think about what they're building.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If you believe that the transition from "internal quant trading infrastructure" to "productized investing platform" preserves most of the technical advantage, then Kimpton has something genuinely hard to replicate: years of battle-tested financial computation that was built under the constraint of needing to work with actual money. The schlep of integrating 2,500+ brokerages via Plaid, building sandboxed Python execution environments for Monte Carlo simulations, and orchestrating 27 financial tools is not trivial. Most competitors are building chat wrappers on financial data; Kimpton is building an execution environment. That's a meaningful distinction. And the price disruption story -- replacing $24K/year Bloomberg terminals with a $200/month tool -- is the kind of magnitude shift that creates real companies. If these founders can nail the product for the prosumer segment, the market is enormous.

But I keep coming back to three concerns. First, the transition from "tools we built for our quant fund" to "the IDE for all investors" is a bigger leap than it looks. What professional quant traders need and what a retail investor with a Schwab account needs are fundamentally different -- the organic discovery that makes the origin story compelling may not transfer to the target customer. Second, five months after public launch, there's no disclosed traction -- no revenue, no user counts, nothing except social media followers in the hundreds. That's not damning at pre-seed, but it's not encouraging either, especially in a category with this much competition. Third, the regulatory question is unaddressed. Proposing structured trades and running a prediction engine while having no RIA registration is the kind of thing that can kill a fintech company overnight. Mezzi, a much smaller competitor, already registered as an SEC RIA. The absence of regulatory preparation here worries me.

These are competent founders with genuine domain expertise building in a space they understand from lived experience. That profile deserves respect. But competent founders with domain expertise in a consensus space is exactly the kind of bet where returns get competed to mediocrity. I'd need to see evidence that users are choosing Kimpton over the alternatives -- some signal of obsessive early adoption, the kind where people are using a crappy version one because they can't live without it. That evidence isn't in the dossier, and without it, this is a pass for me. The organic discovery is real. The idea just isn't contrarian enough.
