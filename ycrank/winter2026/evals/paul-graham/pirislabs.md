# Piris Labs -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Piris Labs is the magnitude of the mismatch between what they're attempting and the resources they have to attempt it with. Their direct competitors — Lightmatter, Ayar Labs, Celestial AI — have raised between $500M and $850M each. NVIDIA has poured $4 billion into photonic manufacturing firms. Piris Labs has $500K. In software, that kind of asymmetry is manageable because code is cheap to produce. In photonic hardware, it's potentially fatal. And yet this is also the exact pattern of Boom Supersonic, where I wrote my largest personal check: a frighteningly ambitious hardware play where the ambition magnitude itself deters competition. The question is whether the founders have the right kind of crazy.

The schlep here is among the deepest I've seen in a YC company. Photonic interconnects for AI inference isn't just hard — it requires knowledge that most engineers literally cannot acquire without years of PhD-level physics training. Ali Khalatpour developed the first room-temperature terahertz semiconductor laser and built NASA's GUSTO optical engine. His entire career has been constructing the physics that this company needs. Keyvan Moghadam built AI infrastructure at Meta and Twitter/X, which means he's lived inside the memory wall problem at the exact companies where it's most acute. When a photonic physicist and an AI infrastructure engineer look at inference bottlenecks, they see different aspects of the same problem they've each been working on for a decade. That's not manufactured from a brainstorming session. That's two people whose professional lives converged on a problem that almost nobody else has the background to even attempt. The schlep here is so steep it's practically invisible — not because people avoid the work, but because they can't even comprehend what the work involves.

What I can't see, though, is the specific moment of organic discovery. How did these two meet? When did they realize their combined expertise pointed at this particular product? The dossier shows no prior employer or university overlap between the co-founders. If they met at a networking event and decided to start a company together, that's one of my explicit red flags — co-founders who meet specifically to start a startup tend to fall apart within a year. The backgrounds are perfect; the relationship is opaque. That gap matters.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. Their decision to build a full-stack inference service rather than sell photonic components is strategically sharp. Lightmatter and Ayar Labs are component vendors — they sell interconnects to data center operators. Piris Labs wants to be the data center operator, or at least the inference service. That's the difference between selling picks and running the mine. If their photonic interconnects actually deliver 5x lower latency and 2x lower cost per token, they don't need to convince skeptical OEMs to integrate novel hardware — they just need to demonstrate that their inference API is faster and cheaper than the alternatives. Start with one rack, serve a handful of AI companies, prove the economics. The inference-as-a-service model is also more capital-efficient at small scale than manufacturing and selling hardware. And the acquisition dynamics are strong: NVIDIA bought Groq for $20B, Marvell bought Celestial AI for $3.25B. Building a working photonic inference demo might be enough to become an acquisition target before they ever need to match their competitors' war chests.

But I keep coming back to the multiple-miracles problem. They need a technological breakthrough at production scale (miracle one), hundreds of millions in additional capital to build it (miracle two), and they need to accomplish both before NVIDIA vertically integrates photonics into its own stack (miracle three). Each additional miracle multiplies the risk. With Stripe, the miracles were all execution — dealing with banks, navigating regulations — and the Collisons could do that from a laptop. With Piris Labs, the miracles require physical infrastructure that $500K cannot build. The performance claims — 5x latency, 10x power, 2x cost — are unvalidated. Moving from Khalatpour's lab demonstrations to production-grade photonic interconnects serving real inference workloads is an engineering chasm that has swallowed well-funded companies.

The technical founder quality here is exceptional — genuinely top-tier. 572 citations, NASA hardware, breakthrough laser physics. If I were scoring purely on "is this founder an animal at their craft," Khalatpour would rank in the top percentile of any YC batch. Moghadam's systems background at Meta and X provides the AI infrastructure knowledge the software stack requires. Both founders are PhD engineers who build things themselves. That combination is rare and real. But world-class talent doesn't automatically produce a viable company when the capital requirements are three orders of magnitude beyond current funding and the competitive landscape includes NVIDIA's balance sheet.

I'd watch this company carefully. If they demonstrate a working photonic inference prototype — even at small scale — the calculus changes dramatically. Right now, the founders are the asset. The product is a claim. And the gap between claim and demonstrated capability is where hardware startups go to die.
