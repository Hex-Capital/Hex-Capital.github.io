# Auxos -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Auxos is a Pass Example case before the dossier finishes opening: CEO Ashton Daniel "ran surveys, panels, and interviews" at BCG and is now selling AI-flavored surveys, panels, and interviews back to BCG-shaped buyers. That is the domain-expert-continuity profile my TED Talk warned against -- the UC Berkeley 28,000-prediction study, the McKinsey 1986 forecast of fewer than 1M U.S. cellphones by 2000 (actual: 109M) -- and the central feature of Auxos is that it does not break any economic mechanism. It captures a slice of one. The $56B market research sector (ESOMAR via Research World) is real, but the success-state of Auxos is "market research, faster and cheaper," not "market research, no longer needed because the underlying decision class has collapsed." That is the inverted question I refuse to answer with a check.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence -- I wrote $50M into a no-revenue structure because the success-state redrew the labor frontier of $15 trillion of U.S. GDP (Fortune 2026-03-06). Auxos's success-state, by its own marketing copy, is "847 packaging tests, 2,104 ad creative A/B tests" -- which is a productivity multiplier on an existing CMO workflow, not a civilizational variable. The mechanism that makes OpenAI load-bearing here is consequence asymmetry: a 10% probability of changing the world beats a 90% probability of capturing a category. Auxos inverts it -- a plausible probability of capturing a slice of the $56B market research sector, with no scenario where the consequence of winning is distinct from Qualtrics shipping the same feature in 2027. The dossier even names the channel-conflict incumbent -- Qualtrics, Dynata -- but does not name the regime that disappears.

The LOW rung on founder-defies-expert-consensus is my own Pass Example: "I spent eight years at Salesforce, now I'm using GPT-4 to summarize sales calls." Auxos sits squarely on that rung. Ashton Daniel's BCG tour "ran surveys, panels, and interviews ... for Fortune 500 companies" -- that is not a founder who has publicly contradicted his field; that is a founder selling his field's playbook with a faster backend. Kerry Lu built "ad simulations modeling 40% of US e-commerce traffic" at Amazon Ads, and Jerry Wu built Instagram ranking infra credited with "$100M+ of incremental revenue per year" at Meta -- impressive infrastructure résumés, but the resulting product is a research-panel replacement, not a defied orthodoxy. Nowhere in the Auxos dossier is there a public position the founders have taken that their industry rejects. That silence is the signal.

On technical and capital risk that repels consensus VCs, the Auxos stack is LLM persona prompting layered on recruited interview data -- the dossier itself flags that "core LLM persona-prompting is replicable." This is the stack-shallow pattern I avoided across **Cognition** (2024) and **Glean** by demanding proprietary depth. Over 18 months, the Auxos moat compounds only if per-customer interview corpora produce measurable accuracy lift versus generic synthetic-user APIs -- and there are zero published benchmarks. The mechanism that defeats Auxos in month 18 is not technical failure; it is Aaru shipping a "with real interview anchoring" toggle on top of $50M of Series A capital (TechCrunch Dec 2025) and Simile doing the same on $100M (ai2.work 2026). Consensus VCs already funded the category at scale -- which means Auxos is not repelling consensus, it is arriving after consensus.

On founder learning rate, the score flips a tier only if the dossier surfaces a documented pivot of the **Slash** (2024) variety -- the publicly-told Kanye West moment that justified my $100M Series C. None exists for Auxos. Three months post-YC, three co-founders who "have known each other for 15 years" from Team USA fencing, one linear plan since inception. I do not penalize aggressively because the company is too young to have pivoted -- the absent-evidence default applies -- but I also cannot give Auxos the multiplier on consequence that learning-rate evidence would unlock.

Required-capital profile is the cleanest mismatch in the Auxos dossier. The GTM is a Cal.com link to the CEO (cal.com/ashton-daniel) and founder-led outbound -- this is $5-10M-to-$1M-ARR commodity SaaS economics. My $115M Series A lead in **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) and my $105M co-led seed in **Genesis AI** (2025) exist because no consensus VC would write those checks. Any Tier-1 generalist will fund Auxos's seed. I am over-resourced for this deal by an order of magnitude, which means even if I were excited about consequence (I am not), the structural fit is wrong.

My 36-month forecast for Auxos: acqui-hired by Qualtrics, SurveyMonkey, or Dynata for under $30M, or absorbed into Aaru/Simile as a tuck-in for the real-interview-anchoring feature -- with under $2M ARR at the time of exit. The mechanism is straightforward: Aaru at $1B headline (TechCrunch Dec 2025) and Simile at $100M Series A (ai2.work 2026) will productize "anchored synthetic respondents" inside their existing enterprise motions, the incumbents will buy the cheapest team that demonstrates the workflow, and Auxos -- pre-seed, three co-founders, no disclosed customers -- is the cheapest team.

The bull case for Auxos that stings: the founding trio is unusually well-matched on simulation infrastructure (Kerry Lu's Amazon Ads 40%-of-US-e-commerce simulator is a serious credential, Jerry Wu's Instagram ranking infra is serious), and the **DoorDash** (2013) Known Exception proves I have written checks into capital-light consumer categories when founder ambition and consequence framing aligned. Could Auxos be a DoorDash-shaped exception -- consensus market, exceptional execution, $72B IPO? The dossier evidence wins against that bull case: DoorDash's success-state reshaped consumer-commerce flow at population scale; Auxos's success-state is faster A/B tests for CMOs, with named, better-capitalized competitors already 18 months ahead on funding. The Known Exception requires consequence magnitude to override; Auxos has neither. The single verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a signed pilot with a named Fortune 100 enterprise plus a published benchmark showing Auxos twins predict real-respondent outcomes within a measurable error band that beats Aaru's synthetic baseline by a margin the buyer will pay for. Absent that, I pass in 30 seconds -- exactly as my Pass Example says I should.
