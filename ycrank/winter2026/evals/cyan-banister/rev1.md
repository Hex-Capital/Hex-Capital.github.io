# REV1 -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about REV1 is how many other people have already had the same idea. DraftAid, a YC W24 company, has been building essentially the same product since August 2023 and claims 210,000 drawings generated and $150K in paid pilots. Leo AI has raised $9.7 million for a broader AI copilot that overlaps. SolidWorks itself shipped a beta Auto-Generate Drawing feature in its 2025 release. When I see a space where incumbents, well-funded startups, and a direct YC-network competitor are all converging on the same pain point, that tells me the consensus has already formed. The most promising companies I've backed -- SpaceX, Crusoe, Contraline -- were investments that looked ridiculous to other people. Nobody was racing to compete with them because nobody else believed the opportunity existed. REV1 is solving a real problem that everyone already agrees is worth solving, and that's precisely the kind of deal where I know the price is wrong and the differentiation window is closing.

When I look at the founders, I see strong credentials -- Tesla, Apple, UC Berkeley, AWS -- but I don't hear the story I'm always listening for. Alex Rivero has genuine domain experience creating manufacturing drawings and founded a CNC router venture with seven prototypes, which shows entrepreneurial instinct. Louis Liu solo-built Tesla's Supercharger planning platform, which is impressive engineering. They complement each other well: the mechanical engineer who's lived the pain point paired with the ML researcher who can build the automation. But domain expertise isn't the same thing as biographical grit. I don't see the hardship that forged an unbreakable commitment to this specific problem. Creating tedious GD&T drawings at Tesla and Apple is frustrating, sure -- but it's a professional inconvenience experienced by well-compensated engineers at elite companies, not a wound that demands healing. I want founders whose life story and their company's mission are inseparable, and what I see here is two talented engineers building a sensible tool from a sensible observation.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. Alex has actually sat at a workstation placing dimension annotations on rotor drawings for the Cybertruck program. He knows the 300 pages of GD&T rules in his bones, not from a market research deck. The hardware boom in robotics, EVs, and defense is creating genuine throughput pressure on manufacturing documentation. A CAD-agnostic tool that works across SolidWorks, Siemens NX, and PTC Creo simultaneously could carve out a real position that no single incumbent has incentive to build -- Dassault isn't going to make a tool that works beautifully with their competitor's software. If REV1 can accumulate proprietary training data from customer-specific drawing conventions and build deep PLM integrations, the switching costs could compound. The technical accuracy requirement in aerospace and medical devices -- where a wrong tolerance annotation can be safety-critical -- could become a genuine quality moat. For an investor looking for a solid enterprise SaaS play with clear product-market fit in engineering workflows, this might be exactly right.

But that investor isn't me. I'm not the right check for this company. There's no economic access story here -- this tool serves the engineering departments of well-resourced hardware companies, not populations that lack access to opportunity. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to people who need transparency; it makes an already-visible but tedious workflow faster for people who already have good jobs. The space isn't stigmatized or regulated in a way that creates a natural moat against competition -- anyone with ML capability can attempt this, and several already are. The "weird factor" is essentially zero. If I walked into a room of VCs and described "AI that automates engineering drawings from 3D models," nobody would look at me like I was crazy. They'd nod and ask about competitive differentiation. That nod is the signal I've learned to avoid.

The co-founder dynamic seems healthy -- former Tesla colleagues with complementary skills and overlapping Robotaxi-era context. I don't see the red flags I've learned the hard way to watch for. But healthy co-founder dynamics alone don't override the absence of everything else I look for in a bet.
