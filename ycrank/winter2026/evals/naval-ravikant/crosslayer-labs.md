# Crosslayer Labs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

This team invented a global Internet standard. Not "contributed to" — invented. Henry Birge-Lee coded the first Multi-Perspective Issuance Corroboration implementation in 2016. By March 2025, the CA/Browser Forum mandated it for every publicly-trusted HTTPS certificate on Earth. Prateek Mittal's research shaped the Tor network and Let's Encrypt — he holds the ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award, which is the computing equivalent of a Fields Medal for early-career researchers. Grace Cimaszewski published the analysis of the KLAYswap attack that demonstrated exactly why cross-layer monitoring matters. When I ask "what does this founder know that can't be taught?" — the answer here is that they know how attacks propagate across DNS, BGP, TLS, and JavaScript layers because they literally wrote the academic literature defining these attack surfaces. You could give a smart generalist five years and they wouldn't reach this depth. This is specific knowledge at the protocol layer of the Internet, which is about as non-teachable as it gets.

The infrastructure instinct is right. Crosslayer Labs sits at the monitoring layer underneath application-layer security tools — the same structural position that made Alchemy valuable relative to individual DeFi apps, or that made Stack Overflow valuable relative to individual developer tools. Cross-layer signal correlation is a picks-and-shovels play for Internet integrity. The competitors — Censys, IONIX, CrowdStrike Falcon Surface — scan broadly for exposed assets. Crosslayer correlates signals across protocol layers to detect impersonation attacks that single-layer tools miss by design. That's a genuinely different capability, not a feature difference.

Where my framework gets uncomfortable is leverage. Enterprise security SaaS with a Calendly demo link and a sales-led motion targeting healthcare, crypto, and fintech verticals — this is a business that scales with headcount on the sales side. Each new enterprise customer requires a sales cycle, education about cross-layer attacks that most CISOs haven't prioritized, and ongoing customer success. There's no evidence of self-serve distribution, no viral mechanic, no protocol-level network effect. The correlation engine itself is code leverage — one engineering team's work protecting many customers simultaneously — but the distribution channel is linear. The strongest security companies eventually develop brand gravity that reduces sales friction, but that takes years. I would want to understand whether this could evolve toward a platform where the monitoring data itself becomes leverage — accumulated attack pattern signatures across customers creating a data moat that makes the product better for everyone. That path exists but is unproven.

The contrarian timing element is unusually strong because the founders engineered it themselves. They spent years getting MPIC adopted as a standard, which educated the entire certificate authority ecosystem about BGP-layer attacks. Now they're building the monitoring tool that detects these attacks beyond the certificate issuance stage. Most founders ride a wave; these founders created the wave and are now surfing it. The tension, though, is that cybersecurity as a category is consensus — it's one of the hottest enterprise software markets. The specific niche of cross-layer impersonation detection is genuinely contrarian (most security teams don't monitor for it), but the broader market positioning as "another EASM tool" could drown the signal in noise.

The academic-to-commercial transition is the risk I'd spend the most time digging into. Prateek Mittal is a tenured Princeton professor — will he leave tenure to build a company, or is this a fractional commitment? The best deep-science companies I've backed (Extropic, Liquid AI) have founders who burned the boats. Three researchers with 15+ publications and zero commercial traction — no revenue, no named customers, zero job postings — could mean they're early in a deliberately sequenced plan, or it could mean the product is still closer to a research prototype than enterprise software. The knowledge moat is extraordinary, but knowledge without commercial execution is an academic paper, not a company. I'd want to see the founders demonstrate they can sell, not just publish. The integrity and long-term orientation signals are positive — spending years on standards work before commercializing is the opposite of chasing hot markets — but the question is whether that patience translates into commercial urgency when it's needed.

The bull case is compelling: if cross-layer Internet attacks become a recognized threat category (the way ransomware became one), the team that invented the countermeasure standard and has the deepest cross-layer expertise on the planet is the obvious winner. The technology compounds — each monitored customer adds attack pattern data that improves detection for all customers. The MPIC mandate creates a regulatory education tailwind. And the competitors would need years of fundamental research to match the team's depth in cross-layer correlation. This could be the defining company in a category that doesn't fully exist yet, which is exactly the kind of bet that looks wrong early and obvious later. I'd write a check — a smaller one than my conviction on the founders warrants, because the leverage architecture concerns me — and push hard on the GTM question.
