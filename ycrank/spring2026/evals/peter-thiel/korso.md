# Korso -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Korso is the textbook 2026 YC archetype I wrote the Pass Example against — an "AI agents for manufacturing RFQs" pitch where the dimensional signature is a competent execution team (Martin Pan shipped automations at General Motors) paired with the conspicuous absence of a contrarian secret (the dossier names Lumari, Turian, Smartbase, and qomplement targeting the identical RFQ/PO/supplier-coordination workflow inside the same eighteen-month window). That pairing is the diagnosis: Korso is mimetic energy with a domain-credible founder, not intellectual courage with an operator-pedigree override.

Stripe (2010) is the load-bearing analog on the contrarian-secret axis because Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent failure mode — PayPal had structurally optimized for consumer trust and could not ship a developer-first API without cannibalizing the merchant-services revenue that defined the company. Korso's dossier never names that kind of failure mode. SAP, Oracle, NetSuite, Dynamics 365, Plex, SYSPRO, and Acumatica appear in the Korso dossier as *integration targets*, not as institutions with a specific architectural blind spot Korso exploits. The closest the dossier comes is the inferred "long-running workflows that span weeks and checkpointing that never loses progress" — but that is an engineering claim about agent reliability, not a secret about why Lumari (also YC, $500K raised, ex-Google/Tesla/Stripe team) or Turian ($3.78M, Cherry Ventures) cannot ship the same thing. When the contrarian thesis collapses into "we have better checkpointing," there is no secret, only a feature.

Quora (2010) is the cautionary mechanism on monopoly trajectory: Adam D'Angelo walked into a market where Stack Overflow and Yahoo Answers already existed, and only his Facebook-CTO pedigree plus a knowledge-graph network-effect hypothesis bought the override. Korso has neither override. The dossier lists four direct YC-or-funded competitors (Lumari, Turian, Smartbase, qomplement) plus AWS Bedrock AgentCore procurement agents shipping from the hyperscaler side, and the founding team's pedigree (UPenn EE, UPenn MechE/Robotics, UC Irvine) is not Moskovitz-from-Facebook-grade. The Korso pricing-power story terminates at "switching costs from deep ERP integration" — but seven-ERP coverage means Korso is shallow across all of them, not deep in any one, and shallow integration is exactly what AWS Bedrock and SAP's own Joule agents will commoditize within eighteen months.

Korso's definite-plan dimension fails by mechanism: an agent-platform thesis that targets seven ERPs and five communication tools on a three-engineer team in a pre-seed posture *is* indefinite optimism in operational form — the surface area itself signals "we will learn from customers which integration matters." Compare the SpaceX (2008) commitment to reusability as a five-to-fifteen-year architectural lock that Musk explicitly refused to pivot from; Korso's dossier surfaces no equivalent technical commitment Daichi Hiraoka, Alex Liu, or Martin Pan will not abandon when the first design partner pulls them toward a single-ERP wedge.

The counterfactual on founder lineage is instructive — for Korso to flip from neutral to invest on this axis, Martin Pan would need to have shipped a meaningful automation product at General Motors that ran in production at scale, or one of the three would need a prior YC outcome or a credentialed-outsider signal (Olympiad, PhD, acquired side project). The dossier shows Pan as an "automations engineer at GM" and Penn Electric Racing alum — real manufacturing-floor exposure, which is the strongest single signal Korso has, but not shipped-to-scale operator status. This is the dimension where Korso scores highest in my framework, and it still does not clear the bar that Affirm (Levchin) or Asana (Moskovitz) cleared by themselves.

The Asana (2010) parallel applies on atoms-heavy moat and exposes why Korso fails where Asana passed. Asana was pure SaaS in a consensus workflow category — exactly Korso's structural profile — and the override was Moskovitz having shipped Facebook to scale. Korso is pure-software middleware over other people's ERPs with no regulatory certification depth, no hardware capex, no government customer, and no operator-pedigree override of Moskovitz magnitude. The Founders Fund manifesto called this the 140-characters mode of capital allocation; Korso is the manufacturing-vertical instance.

My 36-month forecast on Korso: acquihired by Lumari, Turian, or an ERP incumbent (most likely Acumatica or Plex) for under $30M, with ARR plateauing below $2M before the transaction. The mechanism is direct competition from a YC-channel peer (Lumari) with stronger founding pedigree compressing Korso's GTM motion, while AWS Bedrock AgentCore and SAP Joule commoditize the agent-layer value proposition from above — a two-sided squeeze on a team without the contrarian thesis or operator pedigree to break out of it.

The strongest bull case against my pass: cross-ERP orchestration in a fragmented mid-market manufacturing base is a real coordination problem that no single ERP vendor will ship because each vendor's roadmap is single-tenant by commercial design, and Martin Pan's GM floor experience could surface workflow insight that Lumari's ex-Google/Stripe team genuinely lacks — the Anduril (2017) precedent says domain-pedigree-plus-incumbent-channel-conflict can compound into a category. The dismantling: the Anduril override required Palmer Luckey having shipped Oculus to a billion-dollar exit *plus* a regulated-customer moat structurally invisible to software competitors. Korso has neither leg of that triple — Pan's GM tenure is IC-level not founder-level, and ERP middleware has no regulatory moat. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Korso landing a named Fortune-500 manufacturer (Boeing, Caterpillar, Lockheed) on a six-figure paid pilot within twelve months with documented switching costs the customer cites as the reason for incumbency. Absent that, pass.
