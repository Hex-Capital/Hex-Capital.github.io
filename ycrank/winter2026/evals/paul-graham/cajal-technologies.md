# Cajal -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is where this idea came from. Both founders participated in Entrepreneurs First's Bridge program — a residency held in a castle in Germany where smart people come together specifically to generate startup ideas. That's almost exactly the origin story I warn against in "How to Get Startup Ideas." The best startup ideas are not conceived in programs designed to produce startup ideas. They grow from the founder's own life, from a problem they personally couldn't stop thinking about. When I look for how Luke and Pedro arrived at "autonomous formal verification for applied science," I find no breadcrumb trail of personal frustration. Luke was studying meta-reinforcement learning in neuroscience at Cambridge. Pedro was building air quality monitors and paper plane optimizers in Spain. Neither has a documented history with Lean, dependent type theory, or theorem proving. The problem they're tackling is real, but the path to it looks like market research, not lived experience.

Here's where it gets interesting, though. Formal verification genuinely is a schlep. It's tedious, unforgiving, requires extreme mathematical precision, and most smart people unconsciously avoid it. The kind of work where you need every single step to be correct, where one error invalidates everything, where progress is measured in lemmas per month rather than features shipped. That's the texture of a problem that hides in plain sight. But schlep blindness only works as an investment thesis when the founder who sees the schlep is someone who has been living inside the domain long enough to see what others filter out. Stripe worked because the Collisons were programmers who had personally suffered through payment integration. The question for Cajal is: who here has personally suffered through a failed formal verification attempt and thought "there has to be a better way"? From the public record, neither founder. They identified a valuable schlep from the outside, which is very different from discovering one through experience.

The domain expertise gap is the most concerning signal. Building a multi-agent system that autonomously discovers and formalizes mathematical proofs in Lean is not a weekend hack. It requires deep knowledge of dependent type theory, proof tactics, and the specific failure modes of automated reasoning. Luke's reinforcement learning background is adjacent — RL is part of the training pipeline — but "adjacent" is not "sufficient." Pedro's projects (PCB design, A* routing algorithms) demonstrate broad engineering capability but nothing specific to formal mathematics. Compare this to what I'd want to see: founders who had spent two years writing Lean proofs by hand and kept thinking "why am I doing this manually?" Or a former Lean contributor who understood the proof assistant's internals cold. The team can clearly build software. Whether they can build *this* software is the question I can't answer from what's available.

The bull case is worth articulating because the problem really does matter. If Cajal can build what they describe, the data flywheel is compelling — every proof verified adds to the corpus, and verified mathematical datasets are scarce and valuable. The "why now" is genuine: Lean 4 has matured, LLM reasoning has crossed a threshold, and formal verification for applied domains (quantum computing, finance, cryptography) is an underserved market that's distinct from what Harmonic is pursuing with competition mathematics. If these founders turn out to have hidden depth in this domain — if Cambridge gave Luke access to formalization expertise that doesn't show up on GitHub — this could be the kind of picks-and-shovels play I usually love. Verified training data and RL environments for proof assistants is infrastructure that other AI labs need, and infrastructure businesses are my sweet spot. But "could be" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

The competitive landscape amplifies my concern. Harmonic AI has raised $295 million to pursue "mathematical superintelligence" with formal verification in Lean 4. Cajal's differentiation — applied science rather than competition math — is coherent in theory but fragile in practice. What stops a $295M-funded team from expanding into applied domains once they've built their core reasoning engine? Category focus only works as a moat when you have deep domain expertise that generalists can't replicate. I don't see evidence of that expertise here. And the co-founder origin — meeting through a structured entrepreneurship program rather than through shared obsession with a problem — is one of the specific red flags I track. Not fatal on its own, but combined with the domain gap and the manufactured-idea signal, it forms a pattern.

I don't see enough signal to invest my own money. Two smart, technical people building ambitious software in a real problem space — that's the median YC application, not the outlier. What's missing is the organic backstory, the domain depth, and any evidence that users are pulling this product toward them. At 107 Twitter followers and zero documented customers or revenue, I have nothing to falsify the hypothesis that this is a well-executed brainstorming-session idea rather than a genuine discovery. I'd want to talk to them and ask the simple question: "Why you? Why are you the people who should build this?" If the answer reveals years of hidden context in formal verification that didn't make it into the public record, I'd reconsider. But on what's in front of me, this is a pass.
