# Litmus -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Litmus is an anti-heat AI wrapper — two Columbia seniors, Shaivi Rau and Elena Zhao, selling repo-generated coding assessments at $60K ARR into a category where CodeSubmit already lists at $99/month. The dimensional signature is a company whose highest score is on round-heat (the deal isn't visibly consensus-oversubscribed because HR-tech assessment is boring) and whose lowest score is founder-as-brand (zero Twitter, zero GitHub, no newsletter, no owned distribution channel that pre-dated the LLC). That pairing — cold round, cold founders — isn't the Solana pattern of "everyone quit the category so I can buy cheap." It's the "no one showed up because there's no asset here yet" pattern, which reads differently on the price ladder.

On round dynamics, the reference rung is **Sublime Security** — a pre-consensus enterprise security seed I wrote into while the security VCs were still marking up SOC SaaS Series Bs. The load-bearing mechanism was that incumbents (HackerRank, CodeSignal, Karat) were legibly boring enough that tier-1 seed capital wasn't crowding the category, and a new architectural take could get bought at a real price. Litmus superficially inherits that: HackerRank at G2 4.6 with 553 reviews and CodeSignal at ~$87M raised are the legacy library-of-problems archetype Litmus wants to displace with per-customer repo-generated assessments. But where Sublime had a novel detection architecture, Litmus is a foundation-model wrapper — "ingest repos + generate assessments + grade with LLM" is a weekend build for any engineering team with Claude Code access, and Litmus's own dossier admits the only defensible piece is the Ashby/Greenhouse/Lever ATS wiring and 3,000 graded submissions of calibration data. That's not a mispricing; that's a commodity round dressed as a niche one.

**Marina Mogilko** is the reference on founder-as-brand: millions of YouTube subscribers, twenty years of compounding earning power, and I underwrote $1.7M for 5% of 30-year earnings because the *person* was the asset. Litmus fails that filter on every axis I check for — Rau has no public Twitter, Zhao has no public Twitter, neither has public GitHub repos, and the only press artifact is a Columbia Spectator alumni feature from May 2026 that reads like a campus profile, not owned distribution. The origin story — Rau's Series A-founder brother struggled to hire, so she built assessments — is a plausible narrative arc, but it's a story about a problem, not a body of public thinking about the problem. The Slow Creator Fund exists precisely because founder-brand is a step-function feature when present; Litmus doesn't clear the step.

Capital structure compounds badly here on an 18-month horizon: Litmus at $60K ARR with per-seat SaaS pricing (inferred, no pricing page published as of July 2026) needs to clock $500K–$1M ARR to raise a credible Series A into a hiring-tech market where CodeSignal reportedly raised ~$87M and Karat took $150M+. That's four financing tacks minimum before the regatta starting line, each requiring the previous tack to have compressed CAC and expanded ACV against incumbents who already own the ATS relationships Litmus is integrating into. The math doesn't bend toward default-alive without a category-defining wedge, and the wedge is currently "we capture Claude Code prompts" — a feature, not a company.

Counterfactual on founder conviction: for Litmus's Criterion 3 score to jump a tier, either Rau or Zhao would need to have been writing publicly about AI-era engineering evaluation for 18+ months before YC S26, or one of them would need to have run engineering hiring at scale as an operator (not as a Two Sigma incoming intern). Neither is in the dossier. The "watched my brother hire" origin is the kind of sanitized founder-market-fit narrative every YC coach helps polish, and I've stopped pretending it substitutes for the missionary temperament that carries a company through five turns of the crank.

**Sublime Security** is again the single-anchor parallel for category sleeve — enterprise security with a real architectural angle sat inside a Slow-convictioned sleeve. Litmus sits in HR-tech-assessment, which is not a sleeve I've been building — my HR-adjacent portfolio is thin, and the AI-wrapper layer of it puts Litmus squarely in "AI is just more internet" territory (Joe Lonsdale podcast, 2024). This is the Pass Example category: "AI agent for [vertical], $25K MRR, out of YC, standard cap table." Litmus is a hair earlier and slightly cheaper by implication, but it's the same shape.

**Forecast (36 months):** Litmus ends up at sub-$500K ARR and gets acqui-hired by Ashby or Greenhouse for a low-single-digit-million talent-and-integration deal, or dies quietly when the two co-founders take Meta/Two Sigma return offers after the S26 batch demo momentum fades. The mechanism is the LLM-wrapper commoditization risk the dossier itself names: any of HackerRank, CodeSignal, or a scrappier YC W27 team ships repo-context assessment generation as a feature, ATS integrations become table stakes, and Litmus's calibration data on 3,000 assessments isn't a moat against a competitor with 30,000.

**Strongest bear-case objection to my Pass:** the bull argument is that Litmus already shipped native integrations with all three modern ATSs (Ashby, Greenhouse, Lever), named Neo Scholars as a paying client, and is at $60K ARR with two Columbia seniors who haven't graduated — that's real distribution and real revenue-per-founder-hour, and I've historically underwritten weirder Wizard-of-Oz operator profiles. The Known Exception to check is **Ro** — I gave regulatory complexity a pass on Criterion 2 because only VC capital could clear 50-state prescription rails. Does hiring have an equivalent structural moat that would justify the same override? No. ATS integrations are OAuth and a webhook, not a regulatory schlep, and hiring-market cyclicality (which the dossier itself flags) makes the budget line procyclical rather than moated. The condition that would flip my verdict: Litmus lands two named Series B+ engineering orgs (think Ramp, Vercel, Anthropic) as paying multi-seat customers at $50K+ ACV each within the next nine months, proving the per-customer assessment corpus is actually sticky in the accounts I care about — until then, Pass.
