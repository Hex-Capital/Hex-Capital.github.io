# Ontora -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Max Arnold built agentic SAP process mining at Porsche and deployed it across four Volkswagen subsidiaries. That's not a pitch deck bullet — that's a founder who spent a year inside one of the world's largest industrial procurement systems learning how enterprise processes actually work. When I evaluate Ontora's specific knowledge density, Arnold's Porsche work is the anchor. He understands the problem of organizational opacity from the inside. But here's the tension: SAP log-based process mining and LLM-driven conversational interviews are adjacent disciplines, not the same discipline. Arnold knows how to mine structured system data for process inefficiencies. Ontora's core product asks employees open-ended questions and synthesizes tacit knowledge that never appeared in any system log. The gap between those two capabilities is real. Leon Iwanowitsch built AI automations at consultancies — relevant to the buyer persona, less so to the core technology. David Korn built data pipelines in finance. Ontora's founding team understands the problem space better than 90% of pre-seed teams, but the solution technology sits on commodity LLM capabilities that any team with API access can replicate.

McKinsey charges $500K+ for a three-to-six-month engagement that samples roughly 10% of employees. Ontora claims to deliver equivalent insight in hours at ~$50K with 100% coverage. The structural opportunity here is a textbook principal-agent trap for incumbents: McKinsey's partners are agents of the billable-hour revenue model. They cannot build a tool that replaces a $500K engagement with $50K software any more than Google's search team could build Perplexity while protecting their ad revenue. McKinsey reported ~40% of projects are AI-related — they're selling AI *to clients*, not automating their own diagnostic methodology. Ontora's three founders, as principals with zero legacy revenue to protect, can move where McKinsey's agents structurally cannot. This is the same dynamic that made me invest in Perplexity — but with a critical difference. Aravind Srinivas had built the exact systems at Google Brain and OpenAI that powered his product. Ontora's founders have domain adjacency, not domain identity. The Perplexity analogy breaks at the technology layer.

The leverage audit reveals Ontora's central vulnerability. Hundreds of simultaneous AI interviews with zero marginal cost per conversation — that's permissionless leverage in form. But Ontora is renting its leverage from foundation model providers, not owning it. The core capability — structured LLM interviews plus synthesis — could be replicated by Celonis ($2.4B raised, ~$771M revenue) adding a conversational layer to their existing process mining platform, or by Skan AI ($57.6M raised) extending their computer-vision task mining with interview capabilities. Worse, the foundation model providers themselves could ship "interview your organization" as a feature. When I backed Notion, the team had rebuilt their product from scratch and owned every layer of the collaboration stack. Ontora's interview technology sits on someone else's infrastructure with no proprietary model, no disclosed validation methodology, and no accuracy benchmarks. Innovation without a durable moat sends all the value to the customer and eventually to the foundation model provider.

Ontora's founding story gives me pause on principal authenticity. The three co-founders quit their jobs after a trip to Stockholm's fastest-scaling startups organized by Max. That's an inspiring narrative for a blog post, but it signals that the founding impulse came from seeing other founders succeed — not from an inability to stop working on organizational knowledge extraction. Arnold was gainfully employed at Porsche doing related work. Iwanowitsch was building AI at consultancies. No evidence exists that any of them were already prototyping Ontora's core interview technology on nights and weekends, or that they'd been obsessing over tacit knowledge capture before deciding to start a company. Compare to Sahil Lavingia continuing to build Gumroad as a solo founder after failing to raise — that's principal behavior revealed through sacrifice. Ontora's founders made a coordinated career bet after a field trip. That's not a disqualification, but it's not the signal I optimize for.

The Vertiv testimonial — "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" — is a genuine product-market-fit signal at the pre-seed stage. One named enterprise customer willing to be quoted publicly matters more than claimed TAM figures. Ontora's three-person team running AI agents that can interview an entire organization is the right structural shape — if they reach $10M ARR with fewer than fifteen people, this becomes a Cursor-shaped story. The access democratization angle is moderate: Ontora reduces cost 10x within the existing enterprise buyer segment ($50K vs. $500K), but $50K is not democratizing operational intelligence to the small businesses or teams who could never afford consultants. It's making an enterprise tool cheaper for enterprises. My portfolio pattern favors companies that expand access to new populations — AngelList gave every founder access to angel investors, not just cheaper access for founders who already had it.

The bull case writes itself: structural consulting disruption via principal-agent dynamics, 10x cost compression, AI-native architecture with a three-person team, and a founder (Arnold) with real deployed enterprise process mining experience across four VW subsidiaries. If Ontora builds a proprietary data flywheel — aggregated anonymized process intelligence across hundreds of organizations creating benchmarking advantages no competitor can replicate — this becomes a compounding asset, not a wrapper. The condition that would elevate my conviction: evidence that Ontora's interview methodology produces insights that materially differ from what a skilled prompt engineer could extract using off-the-shelf LLMs in a weekend. If the synthesis layer embodies genuine specific knowledge about organizational dynamics that took years to develop, the commodity objection dissolves. Right now, I see a competent team with adjacent domain expertise building on rented leverage in a real market. The enterprise consulting disruption is structural and inevitable — the question is whether Ontora owns enough of the technology stack to capture the value, or whether they're the first mover who educates the market for Celonis or Microsoft Copilot to harvest.
