# Zolvo -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The principal-agent problem at the incumbents is the most interesting thing about this market. nCino at $541M in revenue and Finastra at $1.8B built their businesses serving large banks -- banks that employ thousands of back-office workers on legacy servicing contracts. Selling those banks a product that eliminates their own workforce is a structural impossibility. The sales team at nCino is an agent of their existing customer relationships, unable to cannibalize them. Meanwhile, 12,500 mid-market commercial lenders with $10M-$600M in assets are servicing loans with spreadsheets. The gap exists not because nobody noticed it but because the entities positioned to fill it are incentivized not to. That's a real opening. Salient recognized an adjacent version of this in consumer and auto lending and hit $25M ARR in two years. The commercial segment remains untouched because it's operationally messier -- reconciliation, verification, collections, and reporting across heterogeneous loan portfolios. A startup principal with no legacy customers to protect can walk straight into that gap.

Now the specific knowledge question. Rodriguez closed Domu's first million in revenue selling voice AI to financial institutions. Montes built the AI infrastructure behind 50,000-100,000 daily calls and has NLP publications at ACL and EMNLP. These are real credentials -- but they're credentials in AI engineering and fintech sales, not in commercial lending operations. The specific knowledge that would make this company structurally defensible is deep understanding of how a $200M-AUM commercial lender actually reconciles payments, verifies borrower compliance, handles collections on commercial real estate or equipment loans, and satisfies bank examiners. That knowledge lives inside operations managers who've spent a decade inside these institutions. I don't see evidence that either founder possesses it. They observed the problem from an adjacent vantage point at Domu -- which is how many good ideas start, but it's market research, not the kind of non-replicable knowledge stack that made Aravind Srinivas at Perplexity irreplaceable. If Rodriguez and Montes disappeared, another competent AI team could rebuild this with the same commercial APIs.

The leverage architecture is sound in theory. AI agents replacing manual reconciliation and verification are pure code leverage displacing labor leverage -- this is the right side of the transition. But the implementation rests on OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude -- rented infrastructure, not owned. The defensibility would need to compound through customer data accumulation and workflow-specific fine-tuning. In financial services, that compounding is real because every lender's data has proprietary structure, and once you're integrated into their servicing pipeline, switching costs are meaningful. The question is whether you get to enough customers fast enough for the data flywheel to matter before Salient -- with $70M and 0% churn in adjacent lending -- decides commercial is their next adjacency. The $6K in revenue within four weeks is an honest signal. Not transformative, but it proves they can ship and close. The $1B+ pipeline in AUM is pipeline, not contracts -- but at pre-seed, having pipeline at all means someone is picking up the phone.

The bull case I have to take seriously: Rodriguez relocated to San Francisco with $3,000. That's a principal's bet, not an agent's calculation. Montes is 22 with dual engineering degrees, research publications, and production AI at scale -- that's the profile of someone whose specific knowledge in AI systems is still compounding rapidly. If their Domu experience gave them even partial maps of commercial lender workflows, and they iterate fast enough with real customers, the domain knowledge gap closes through shipping. The Domu founders investing in Zolvo is a co-investor signal -- people who built a YC company in the adjacent space are putting their own money in. Tom Blomfield, who built Monzo, is their YC partner. The fintech-specific mentorship pipeline is real. And mid-market commercial lenders are the right beachhead -- small enough to sell to without enterprise procurement cycles, large enough to generate meaningful revenue per customer.

But my framework is clear on one thing: when the core AI is commercially available and the founding team's specific knowledge is in engineering rather than domain operations, you're in a race against every other competent team that notices the same gap. Salient's existence proves the market. It also proves that well-funded competitors are one pivot away. The regulatory overhead in commercial lending -- SOC 2 is only the beginning, bank examiners will want much more -- favors teams with institutional relationships and compliance expertise that these young founders would need to acquire. I don't see the non-replicable knowledge asymmetry that turns a good idea into a power-law outcome. This is a capable team attacking a genuine market gap with adequate tools. That's a reasonable bet for many investors. For my framework, the absence of founder-specific knowledge that IS the company keeps this in neutral territory.
