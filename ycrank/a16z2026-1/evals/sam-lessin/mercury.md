# Mercury -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Mercury is the platonic ideal of the deal I pass on. No-code multi-agent orchestration is not a contrarian bet — it is the single most consensus category in venture capital right now. Every demo day I attend, someone is pitching me "we coordinate AI agents so you don't have to." CrewAI has raised $24.5M. Relevance AI just closed a $24M Series B. Lindy has ~$50M in the bank. The market has priced maximum optimism into this exact thesis. Where is the mispricing? When I seeded Solana at four cents, every smart person told me Ethereum had won and I was throwing money away. When I look at Mercury, every smart person tells me agentic AI is the future and I should be excited. That's the opposite signal. If every other investor at the table wants in, the upside is already in the price — there's no asymmetry left for me.

The AI kill test is where Mercury dies. Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma built a layer that sits on top of Claude Code, Devin, and gstack — it's right there on their homepage. They are orchestrating other people's agents. You know who else can orchestrate those agents? The companies that built them. Anthropic ships tool-use APIs. OpenAI already has an Agents SDK with native multi-agent orchestration. Microsoft Copilot Studio does multi-agent coordination inside a platform that already has enterprise distribution. Mercury is the exact case I describe when I say "every time you get pitched Adobe for AI" — the orchestration feature is perfect for the incumbents who own the agents. Software companies that had moats are learning they aren't moats anymore, and Mercury hasn't even built a moat yet.

The Palantir FDE backgrounds are the one thing here that gives me any pause. Stefano spent almost four years deploying complex AI systems at enterprise scale, and Naveen has the same pedigree. FDEs at Palantir do real deployment work — they sit in the field with customers and bridge the gap between powerful tools and operational reality. That's exactly the problem Mercury claims to solve. These are competent, grounded people. But competent, grounded people building the most obvious idea in tech is not the ratio I look for. I want sane people pitching insane ideas. "No-code multi-agent orchestration" is not insane — it's what half the pitch decks in Silicon Valley say right now. A sane person with a sane idea is just... fine. Fine doesn't produce asymmetric returns.

Let me steelman this. The bull case is that multi-agent coordination becomes a genuine new primitive — that managing teams of AI agents becomes as fundamental as managing databases or managing containers. If Mercury becomes the "Kubernetes of AI agents," that's a protocol-level position, not an application. And the Palantir experience means Stefano and Naveen understand enterprise deployment pain in their bones. Maybe the no-code angle targeting non-technical operators is a real wedge that developer frameworks like CrewAI can't serve. But here's what would have to be true: the agent providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Cognition) would have to permanently cede the orchestration layer and never build it themselves. That's a bet against the platform owners internalizing their own coordination problem. I don't make bets that require platform owners to be stupid.

Mercury also has a practical problem that reveals a deeper strategic weakness: the name collision with Mercury Financial, a $3.5B+ fintech serving 200,000 startups. Every founder in their target market already associates "Mercury" with their banking platform. The .build TLD is a band-aid on a brand wound. But the name problem is a symptom — the real issue is that Mercury has no defensibility signals at all. No public traction beyond a free alpha and a waitlist. No revenue. No proprietary data. No network effects. No physical-world moat. The dossier marks "No defensibility signals found in public sources," and every inferred moat (orchestration data, switching costs, template marketplace) is speculative and replicable. Lindy with $50M in the bank and an existing user base can add multi-agent orchestration as a feature update.

The decisive dimensions are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability — and Mercury fails both. The category is priced for perfection by every major VC, which means there's no asymmetric risk/reward for me. And the product sits at the application layer on top of third-party AI agents with no moat against those same agents building native orchestration. I invest in protocols, not wrappers. Pass.
