# Datost -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The single most striking thing about Datost isn't the idea or the founders — it's the platform geometry. They're building an AI data analyst inside Slack. Slack is owned by Salesforce. Salesforce also owns Tableau. Datost is, in effect, building a product that could cannibalize Tableau's revenue, distributed through a platform controlled by Tableau's parent company. This is the startup equivalent of opening a restaurant inside someone else's house and hoping they never get hungry. Salesforce has not yet unified Slack and Tableau into a single AI analyst — but the incentive structure is screaming at them to do it. When I think about "it's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors," I'm thinking about organic market competition. Platform owners are a different animal entirely.

The idea itself concerns me in a way that's hard to articulate precisely but I'll try. "AI data analyst in Slack" sounds good. It sounds like something you'd pitch to investors and they'd nod along. That's actually the problem. The best startup ideas — the ones that produce asymmetric returns — look bad initially. Facebook sounded like a way for college kids to waste time. Airbnb sounded insane. "AI data analyst in Slack" sounds like a feature, not a company. It sounds like something a product manager at Salesforce has already put on a roadmap somewhere. When everyone can see why an idea is good, the returns get competed away. The "own computer" angle — giving the agent a sandboxed execution environment rather than just generating SQL — is a genuine architectural insight. But it's the kind of insight that buys you six months, not six years, because the moment it proves valuable, every competitor implements the same pattern.

On the founders: Maceo and Jason are both 21, both worked at Traba, both software engineers. The shared employer is a meaningful signal — I've always said "the relationship between the founders has to be strong" and co-workers who choose to start something together have at least passed the basic compatibility test. But I'm looking for something more here and not finding it. Where's the evidence of relentless resourcefulness? The Airbnbs sold cereal boxes. Stripe's founders grabbed laptops out of people's hands. What have these founders done that's unreasonable? Their GitHub has one demo repo with zero stars. Their LinkedIn profiles still list intern-level titles. They're not data analysts themselves — they're engineers building for a user persona they've observed but haven't inhabited. "What you need is expertise in your own users," and I don't see evidence of that deep user empathy here. They could have it and it's just not visible in the public record, but at pre-seed, the founder signal is almost the only signal, and this one is quiet.

The bull case deserves a fair hearing. Here it is: bottom-up Slack distribution could bypass the enterprise sales cycle that would otherwise kill a two-person team. Someone asks a data question in a Slack channel, the bot answers it brilliantly, three other people in the channel see it and start using it. Viral within the organization, spreads across organizations through job-changers. The market is massive and growing fast — AI in data analytics projected at 29% CAGR. And maybe these two young engineers have exactly the technical chops to build the most reliable, most deeply integrated version of this before the incumbents figure it out. Zuckerberg was 19 and had no enterprise experience. For this bull case to work, two things would need to be true: (1) the Slack-native form factor creates such strong habit formation that organizations resist switching to an incumbent's version, and (2) Salesforce moves slowly enough on unifying Slack + Tableau + AI that Datost gets 18-24 months of clear air. I find (1) plausible but (2) a coin flip at best, and I don't love betting my own money on a competitor's organizational dysfunction.

The commoditization risk is severe and structural. Text-to-SQL is a capability, not a product. Every frontier LLM can do it. Microsoft is shipping Copilot in Power BI. Google is shipping AI in Looker. Salesforce is shipping Einstein everywhere. Datost's argument for defensibility rests on "data context accumulation" — the idea that as the agent learns an organization's schemas, terminology, and patterns, switching costs increase. That's real, but it's slow-building, and the switching cost only kicks in after months of deep usage. Before that point, the product is competing on raw capability against teams with vastly more resources. And the enterprise trust barrier — SOC 2, security reviews, database access permissions — is a brutal schlep for a two-person team with no enterprise sales experience. I wrote about schlep blindness because important problems sit unsolved when the schlep is intimidating. But the flip side is that sometimes the schlep is genuinely prohibitive for a team at this stage.

This is a competent team in a real market with a reasonable idea. But "reasonable" is the operative word, and in my experience, reasonable ideas with commodity technology facing incumbent platform owners don't produce the asymmetric returns that make angel investing work. I need to see either extraordinary founders or a genuinely contrarian insight, and I'm not seeing enough of either to write a check.
