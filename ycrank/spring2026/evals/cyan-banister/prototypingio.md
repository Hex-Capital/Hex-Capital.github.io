# Prototyping.io -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me first: this is one of the most crowded pre-seed landscapes I've seen in a while. Xometry is doing $687M in revenue with AI-powered instant quoting already at scale. Protolabs is at $533M and growing CNC revenue 25% year-over-year. Then there's a fresh wave — RMFG has shipped over 100,000 parts, Jiga has NASA and Siemens as customers with $19M raised, Toolpath has $10M for sell-side AI. And Prototyping.io is pre-launch, two people, gated waitlist, zero public traction. I don't get uncomfortable when I hear "AI-powered manufacturing platform" — I hear it five times a week. Nobody's squirming in their chairs at this pitch. This is consensus AI in a consensus-hot sector, and that's exactly the pattern I try to avoid. My biggest wins came from places that made people flinch. SpaceX was insane. Anduril was politically toxic. This? This is a well-populated opportunity that every AI-aware investor in the Valley is already excited about.

When I ask "tell me their story," what I get back here are resumes. Strong resumes, sure — Revanth earned an IIT silver medal in mechanical engineering, did aerospace research at UT Austin, worked at Schlumberger. Prerit published at ACM and IEEE, built AI systems at Microsoft, was a founding engineer at a stealth startup. These are capable people with complementary skills. But there's no biographical crucible I can find. There's no moment where this problem found them — no story of a part failing in the field because nobody caught the DFM issue, no personal frustration that became an obsession. They "recently joined forces," which tells me two smart engineers looked at a market and saw an opening. That's career optimization, not mission inevitability. I've learned the hard way that in markets this competitive, you need founders who would be working on this problem if every investor on earth said no. I don't see evidence of that here.

The strongest bull case goes something like this: the DFM-first approach is genuinely differentiated. Most competitors are marketplaces or factory operators that quote and fulfill — they catch manufacturability issues after the order is placed, or not at all. If Prototyping.io builds the best AI-driven design feedback loop, catching problems before metal gets cut, that creates a wedge that could become a data flywheel. Every CAD file analyzed teaches the system more about what works across processes, materials, and geometries. Revanth's deep manufacturing domain knowledge means the DFM models could be structurally better than what a pure-software team would build. And the market is enormous — $400B globally in machining alone. If they become the place where hardware engineers go first, before they order anywhere, they'd own the top of the funnel. That's a real thesis, and I respect the technical pairing. But when I pressure-test it, I keep landing in the same place: Xometry processes millions of parts and just acquired Thomasnet for $300M. Their training data advantage is orders of magnitude larger than anything a pre-launch startup can accumulate. The DFM analysis layer, as the dossier itself notes, "could be replicated by incumbents with sufficient training data." I don't see the structural reason the first small mover wins here against incumbents with that kind of data gravity.

There is something in the manufacturing procurement system that touches my "invisible system" instinct — hardware engineers uploading files to five different vendors, waiting days for opaque quotes, discovering too late that their design can't be made the way they imagined. That's real information asymmetry, and it does extract value from people who don't understand the manufacturing side. But here's the thing: Xometry already made that system significantly more visible. AI instant quoting is not new. Protolabs offers rapid turnaround. The structural opacity is already being attacked by well-capitalized players. Prototyping.io is adding a layer of visibility to an already-disrupted space — that's an incremental improvement on transparency, not a structural revelation. When I backed Affirm, credit card pricing was genuinely hidden from consumers and nobody was fixing it. That's not the situation here.

I wish these founders well — the credentials are real, the market is real, and DFM-first is a legitimate wedge. But this isn't a Long Journey bet. There's no biographical forge pulling them through the inevitable valley of death against Xometry's $687M revenue engine. There's no contrarian weirdness that makes other investors uncomfortable. And the defensibility picture at this stage, against this competitive set, doesn't give me conviction that I can hold through the hard years. I need to believe in ten-year infrastructure being built beneath a weird surface, and what I see here is a capable team entering a well-mapped market through a sensible but replicable angle.
