# Clair Health -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The core bet at Clair Health is one of the more audacious scientific claims I've seen at pre-seed: that you can infer estrogen, progesterone, LH, and FSH levels continuously from a wrist-worn device using proxy signals like skin temperature, HRV, and electrodermal activity — no blood, no urine, no saliva, no microneedles. Every other company in this space has decided you need to touch the actual molecule. Level Zero Health raised $6.9M to put microneedles on your skin. Eli Health raised $20M for saliva samples. Clair Health is saying none of that is necessary — just wear the bracelet. That's either a genuine breakthrough or it's a claim that will collapse under clinical scrutiny. Their validation dataset is 40 women across 127 cycles, and accuracy drops from 95.2% on regular cycles to 84.3% on irregular ones — which is exactly where women need this data most. Forty women is not a study; it's a starting point. No peer-reviewed publication exists. The science underneath this company is simultaneously the most exciting and most fragile thing about it.

When I ask about Jenny Duan and Abhinav Agarwal — not the business, the people — I see a 21-year-old Stanford Symbolic Systems grad who volunteered at Rose Haven and built endometriosis apps at a hackathon, and a 23-year-old Stanford CS grad who built a non-invasive glucose monitor at KOS AI and won a best paper at ICAIRME. Abhinav's technical transfer from glucose inference to hormone inference is real capability — that's domain-specific engineering that doesn't grow on trees. But when I look for the biographical forge, the moment where this problem grabbed Jenny by the throat and wouldn't let go, I find domain interest rather than domain inevitability. She cares about women's health. That's clear. But "I cared about this at Stanford" reads differently to me than "I couldn't get a diagnosis for years and the system failed me" or "I watched my mother suffer through undiagnosed perimenopause while doctors dismissed her." The problem may have found her — I just can't see the evidence of it in this dossier.

Where Clair Health genuinely activates my framework is the invisible system underneath. Women's hormonal health has been one of the most opaque structures in medicine for decades — symptoms dismissed as anxiety, PCOS taking an average of seven years to diagnose, perimenopause treated as a mystery rather than a measurable biological process. The system extracts real costs from women who don't have access to the data about their own bodies: missed fertility windows, unexplained mood shifts, misdiagnoses. Blood draws cost money, require appointments, and deliver snapshots instead of continuous data. Clair Health is attempting to make that entire opaque structure transparent by putting continuous hormone data on a woman's wrist. That's the Affirm parallel — Max Levchin attacked credit card opacity by making lending terms visible; Jenny Duan is attacking hormonal opacity by making biological data accessible. The structural parallel is strong. The founder parallel is not: Levchin was a repeat founder with a PayPal exit and deep financial conviction. Duan is 21 with a hackathon project and a nonprofit volunteer stint.

The contrarian question cuts both ways for Clair Health. The approach — inference from proxy signals — is genuinely contrarian within the medical and scientific community, and that's the kind of discomfort I pay attention to. But the space? FemTech is not making VCs shift in their chairs anymore. It's a $60B market with 16-18% projected CAGR. Natural Cycles has raised $99.5M. Eli Health just closed a $12M Series A. This is consensus capital flowing into a consensus category. The "weirdness" lives in the specific technical bet, not in the market. And the unverified traction claims trouble me — $800K in D2C revenue and $18M in B2B contracts from a page that now 404s, for a device that hasn't shipped yet and targets late 2026 delivery. When fundraising narrative outpaces building substance, that's a pattern I've been burned by. I'm not saying these numbers are false, but I can't evaluate what I can't verify, and a 404'd source page is not nothing.

The bull case is real and I want to name it honestly: if Clair Health's inference approach works at scale — truly works, across diverse populations, skin tones, BMIs, irregular cycles — then this is the only company in the space that can reach hundreds of millions of women with zero friction. Microneedles won't scale to mass consumer adoption. Saliva tests won't be done daily. A bracelet that looks like jewelry and tells you your progesterone is rising? That scales to every woman on Earth. The "female biology world model" becomes infrastructure that compounds with every user, and the first company with a working non-invasive wearable hormone monitor becomes the platform — not just a product. Abhinav Agarwal's prior work at KOS AI on the Argus glucose monitor is exactly the kind of technical pedigree that makes me think this team might actually pull it off. And the on-device processing with zero-knowledge encryption could become a genuine regulatory moat as health data laws tighten. If I saw a peer-reviewed validation study and proof that the accuracy holds on irregular cycles, my score would move substantially.

The dimensions that lock my tier are the biographical forge gap and the consensus nature of the space. Clair Health is building in a category where capital is eager, not reluctant — and my biggest wins have come from writing the first check when everyone else was running the other direction. The founders are capable and the mission is real, but I need to feel the inevitability in the founder's story, and I need to see the science validated beyond 40 women, before I write a check from my own money.
