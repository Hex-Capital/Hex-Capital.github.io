# Panta -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Harper raised $47M in February 2026 building the same thing. That's the first fact I have to reckon with. When I backed Juniper to challenge Cisco, nobody else was crazy enough to fund a TCP/IP router for public telecom networks. When I wrote the OpenAI check in 2019, the AI space was laughable and unfunded. The signal I look for is that the experts dismiss it, the outsiders haven't crowded in, and you're skating to where the puck will be. Panta is entering a category where a well-funded YC predecessor is already serving 5,000+ businesses, Newfront just got acquired for $1.3 billion, COVU has raised $50 million, and multiple YC companies are attacking adjacent angles. This isn't a contrarian bet. This is a consensus opportunity with a dozen well-capitalized players arriving simultaneously. The dossier itself states it plainly: "The core technology (LLM-based agents navigating portals and handling documents) is not unique." When the technology isn't unique and the market timing is consensus, you're in a footrace, not an invention.

Now let me ask the question I always ask first: if Panta succeeds completely, what happens to the world? The $80 billion U.S. insurance brokerage market gets automated. Forty thousand brokers are displaced. Businesses that waited two weeks for coverage get it in days. That's a meaningful economic impact — I won't pretend otherwise. A $135 billion E&S market growing at 12.5% annually is large enough to produce a significant company. But the consequence is operational — making an existing intermediary layer faster and cheaper. The carrier system remains. Underwriting remains. The insurance product remains. Nobody's life is fundamentally different; the paperwork just moves faster. Compare this to what I look for: Impossible Foods aimed to make animal agriculture obsolete. Commonwealth Fusion Systems aims to make fossil fuel power generation obsolete. Panta aims to make insurance brokers faster. The magnitude of the consequence is bounded by the efficiency gain, not by system transformation.

The E&S specialty focus is the strongest element of the thesis, and I want to give it proper weight. Hard-to-place risks — hazmat trucking, oil and gas, cannabis, construction — are genuinely underserved. Traditional brokers struggle with these placements because the complexity is high, the carrier appetite data is fragmented, and the cycle times are punishing. If Panta can build a proprietary dataset on which carriers accept which risks at what price across hundreds of specialty categories, that data compounds into a real moat. The 38% price reduction on that oil and gas laboratory placement is suggestive — not because 38% is transformational, but because it hints that better carrier matching, done at speed, unlocks genuine economic value that incumbents leave on the table. The bull case is that Panta becomes the definitive intelligence layer for hard-to-place commercial risk — not just a faster broker, but the entity that knows more about E&S carrier appetite than any human or institution. If that proprietary dataset achieves escape velocity before Harper moves upmarket into specialty risks, there's a real defensible position. The 127 reviews at 4.9/5 within roughly two months of launch suggest customers are responding to something real.

The founders are technically strong — Vincent Chen shipped agentic AI systems to billions of users at Google, Frank Wang built systems at Apple and contributes meaningfully to Rust and Gleam ecosystems. Both obtained their insurance broker licenses, which shows willingness to immerse in the domain rather than build from the outside looking in. But this is the standard FAANG-to-startup pipeline executing the consensus playbook of 2025-2026: take LLM agent capabilities, apply them to a manual workflow, capture the efficiency margin. I don't see the outsider audacity that characterized my best bets. Peter Beck building rockets from New Zealand with no aerospace pedigree. Pat Brown, a biochemist, declaring he would replace the meat industry. These founders are doing exactly what their résumés predict they would do. That's competence, not courage.

The timing question cuts both ways. Yes, LLM agents are just reaching the capability threshold for complex, judgment-intensive, multi-channel workflows like specialty insurance placement. That's a genuine enabling moment. But it's a moment that Harper identified twelve months earlier, and that the broader market has now priced in with hundreds of millions in aggregate funding across AI insurance startups. The rate of change in underlying AI capability is favorable — I still believe we're on an exponential curve in agent reliability — but Panta isn't ahead of the curve. They're riding it alongside a crowded field. When I invested in OpenAI in 2019, the rate of change was accelerating but almost nobody saw it. Here, everyone sees it.

I'll acknowledge what would change my mind: if I could sit with Vincent Chen for an hour and watch how he thinks about the hardest unsolved problems in autonomous placement — the edge cases where AI judgment fails, the carrier relationships that can't be automated, the regulatory landmines ahead — and if I saw the kind of rapid first-principles evolution that tells me this founder is learning faster than the problem is growing, I'd reconsider. The E&S specialization is genuinely harder than broad SMB insurance, and harder problems sometimes produce more durable companies. But from what I can evaluate today, this is a competent team executing a consensus approach in a crowded market with a well-funded leader. The consequence of success is a good business, not a transformed industry. And I don't write checks for good businesses — I write checks for attempts to change how the world works.
