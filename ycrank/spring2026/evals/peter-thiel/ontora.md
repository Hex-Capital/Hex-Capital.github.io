# Ontora -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first question I ask about any company is: what is the secret? What important truth does this team see that most intelligent people actively reject? Ontora's implicit thesis is that AI agents can replace management consultants by conducting stakeholder interviews and organizational diagnostics autonomously. This is not a secret. It is the most widely held consensus in technology today. McKinsey is deploying its own AI tools. BCG has internal platforms. Gartner publishes reports estimating that 40% of consulting tasks are automatable. Every AI startup pitch deck in 2025-2026 includes some version of "we replace expensive professionals with AI agents." When every smart person in the room agrees with your thesis, you do not have a secret -- you have a crowded market. And a crowded market means competition, and competition means no monopoly, and no monopoly means this investment fails the most fundamental test.

The competitive landscape confirms the diagnosis. Celonis has raised $1.77 billion and generates $771 million in annual revenue doing adjacent process mining. Worktrace AI, founded by an ex-OpenAI PM, raised $9.3 million to observe employee work patterns. Leena AI has $40 million to build conversational AI for enterprises. Polsia claims $450K ARR doing autonomous company operations. Ontora's differentiation -- that it analyzes unstructured conversational data rather than structured event logs -- is a feature, not a moat. It is the kind of distinction that dissolves within eighteen months as Celonis adds LLM capabilities to its existing platform and as every foundation model provider enables RAG over enterprise documents. The underlying technology stack -- large language models conducting interviews, retrieval-augmented generation over corporate data -- is available to anyone with an API key. This is precisely the Girardian trap: multiple companies imitating each other into mutual destruction, competing on execution speed rather than structural advantage. When I look at this landscape, I see food delivery for enterprise software.

The bull case deserves serious examination, because the structural vulnerability of consulting firms is real. McKinsey, BCG, and Bain generate revenue through billable human hours -- their business model creates genuine incentive misalignment against automating themselves out of existence. If Ontora could achieve consultant-quality diagnostics at one-tenth the cost, the incumbents structurally cannot respond without cannibalizing their core revenue. The Vertiv testimonial -- "what used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon" -- suggests the product genuinely works. And Maximilian Arnold's experience building CoAnalyst at Porsche, an agentic RAG system for SAP procurement data, is directly analogous domain work. For this to be a great investment, three things would need to be true simultaneously: the quality gap between AI-conducted and human-conducted organizational diagnostics would need to narrow faster than incumbents can adapt, Ontora would need to build a proprietary data asset from aggregated cross-company process benchmarks that creates compounding returns to scale, and the founding team would need to demonstrate the kind of definite conviction that turns a product into a category-defining company. I see evidence for the first proposition. I see speculation for the second. I see nothing for the third.

The founders present as competent young European technologists -- Arnold from CODE University, Stanford, and ETH Zürich with enterprise AI experience at Porsche; Iwanowitsch from St. Gallen with business and entrepreneurship context. This is a solid background for building an enterprise product. But I look for something beyond competence. I look for the kind of intellectual framework and stubborn conviction that refuses to be deflected by consensus. Zuckerberg at nineteen had a specific vision of how real-identity networking would restructure human social behavior. Musk at SpaceX had a physics-derived plan for reusable rockets that every aerospace engineer called impossible. What is Ontora's vision beyond "AI is cheaper and faster than consultants"? The dossier reveals no philosophical framework, no refusal of easier paths, no articulation of how the world fundamentally changes if they succeed. The third co-founder, David Korn, has essentially no public footprint at all. "Building in Stealth" is not a vision -- it is the absence of one. I need founders who can tell me exactly what organizational management looks like in 2035 if they are right, and I do not see that here.

The market entry strategy compounds the concern. Ontora targets "mid-to-large enterprises" broadly -- there is no evidence of a deliberate beachhead, no small market they intend to completely own before expanding. One testimonial from a strategy manager at Vertiv is a proof point, not a monopoly. The enterprise sales cycle for organizational diagnostics involves procurement teams, security reviews, and C-suite buy-in, and they have three people with zero job openings. Without a specific niche they can saturate -- a particular industry vertical, a particular company size, a particular operational problem -- they are competing broadly in a market where well-capitalized incumbents have distribution advantages they cannot match. The playbook should be obvious: Facebook started at Harvard, PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Where is Ontora's Harvard?

This is a company applying commodity technology to a consensus thesis in a market where well-funded competitors are already converging. The founders have relevant experience but no visible secret about the world, no monopoly mechanism, and no evidence of the kind of definite conviction that builds category-defining companies. I pass.
