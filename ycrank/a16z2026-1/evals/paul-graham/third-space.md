# Third Space -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Dhruv Chopra ran a Brooklyn live music venue for ten years. That's the fact I keep coming back to. Elsewhere does 600+ events a year, draws 300,000 attendees annually, and operates in one of the most punishing regulatory environments in America — New York nightlife. You don't survive a decade in that business without being relentlessly resourceful in ways that don't show up in a pitch deck. When COVID shut down every venue in America, Chopra worked with Senator Schumer on the Shuttered Venue Operations Grant and with Mayor de Blasio to strike down NYC's Cabaret Law. Those aren't the actions of someone who sits around waiting for conditions to improve. That's someone who, when the front door is locked, goes around to the loading dock. The fact that he experienced 300-500% premium increases as an operator and now sits on trade association boards representing 5,000+ businesses — that's not market research. That's a decade-long origin story.

The problem discovery here is the cleanest I've seen in insurance. Drew Houston built Dropbox because he kept forgetting his USB drive. Chopra is building Third Space because he has been personally paying ruinous insurance premiums for years and hearing the same pain from thousands of other operators through his board seats at the NY Hospitality Alliance, BABAR, and the North Brooklyn Chamber of Commerce. He didn't identify this opportunity by reading Insurance Journal — he lived it. Liquor liability payouts hit $250,000 average in 2024, up 30% over five years, and lawsuits climbed 20% since 2020. Forty-three states have dram shop laws exposing venues to downstream litigation. When the dossier says Chopra "represents 5,000+ businesses through trade association boards," that's not a distribution strategy appended to a pitch — it's the embedded context from which the entire company grew organically.

Vinai Rachakonda is the right CTO for this build. Senior tech lead at Ramp, founding engineer at DoltHub, prior stints at SpaceX and CTRL-Labs (acquired by Facebook). The Ramp experience matters most — he built financial products at scale in a regulated fintech environment, which is structurally identical to what Third Space requires. The pairing reminds me of what worked with Stripe: Chopra brings the domain obsession and the distribution relationships (like a venue operator's version of the Collisons' developer community), while Rachakonda brings the engineering chops to build the data pipeline that ingests camera footage, POS data, and incident reports. My concern is that these two met through South Park Commons rather than through years of working together. Untested co-founder relationships are a real risk — I've listed it as a top startup killer — but at least they've chosen each other deliberately rather than being matched by an algorithm.

Third Space has a structural parallel to ClassDojo that most people would miss. ClassDojo violated my usual pattern of backing developer tools, but it worked because selling to school districts involves such painful schlep — bureaucratic procurement, parent relationships, teacher adoption — that no one else would touch it. Insurance brokerage for nightlife venues is the same category of schlep: state-by-state licensing, carrier relationships, liquor liability complexity, and selling to small business owners who are notoriously hard to reach through conventional SaaS channels. Harper raised $47M for horizontal AI brokerage serving 5,000+ businesses, which is the "obvious" play. Third Space's bet is that the nightlife vertical requires such specialized data — camera footage, shift logs, incident reports — that a horizontal platform can't replicate the underwriting intelligence. That's a real bet, and it's the right kind of contrarian: specific enough to repel most founders, but grounded in a genuine data differentiation rather than a branding exercise.

The strongest argument against investing is carrier dependency. Third Space can aggregate all the venue operational data it wants, but if insurance carriers won't accept that data as the basis for differentiated pricing, the core value proposition — lower premiums for engaged venues — evaporates. No carrier partnerships have been announced. The $3M in LOI premiums from 50+ locations is promising as a demand signal, but an LOI from a bar owner who wants cheaper insurance is the easy side of a two-sided market. The hard side is convincing carriers to underwrite nightlife differently based on camera and POS data they've never used before. If Third Space can close even one carrier partnership that validates the data-driven pricing model before their June 2026 launch, that would substantially change my conviction level. The other risk — that Harper could add a nightlife vertical module — is real but less concerning. Horizontal platforms always look threatening on paper, but the depth of domain knowledge required to interpret nightlife operational data (what does a spike in incident reports at 2am actually mean for risk?) is harder to bolt on than most people think.

The dimension that most decisively pulls Third Space into investment territory is organic problem discovery — Chopra didn't choose this problem from a menu, he lived inside it for a decade. The dimension that keeps it from scoring higher is the absence of specific resourcefulness stories in the startup context itself. Running Elsewhere through COVID and navigating SVOG legislation is impressive, but I want to see what happens when Third Space hits its first carrier rejection or regulatory roadblock. The LOIs suggest real demand. The June 2026 launch date means I'd be betting on execution I haven't witnessed yet. But the schlep is deep enough to create a genuine moat, and the founder-market fit is among the strongest I've seen in insurtech. I'd write a small check.
