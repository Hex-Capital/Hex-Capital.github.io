# Alchemy -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The dossier's own risk section delivers the verdict before I do: "Any ML-capable team with life science domain knowledge could build similar pipeline-assembly tooling." That's not a risk factor — that's a business description. Alchemy is a natural-language interface on top of open-source segmentation models like Cellpose and StarDist, wrapped in a SaaS subscription. Strip away the AI and what's left? Nothing. There's no physical infrastructure, no proprietary data flywheel, no protocol that other tools build on, no regulatory position. The software IS the business, and that's the exact category I've spent the last two years arguing is getting destroyed by the next wave of foundation model improvements. When I look at the YC tags — "AI-powered Drug Discovery, Machine Learning, SaaS" — I see three words that individually make me cautious and together make me reach for the pass button.

The founder-idea calibration here is inverted from what I look for. Phillip Baek and Liam McBride are reasonably credentialed — biomedical engineering from UT Austin, CS from UIUC, a patent at 19 — but "AI to make microscopy image analysis easier" is not an insane idea. It's an obvious one. The AI in microscopy market is projected at 14.83% CAGR through 2033. PathAI has raised $255 million. Leica acquired DRVISION specifically to bolt AI analysis onto their microscope hardware. Every microscope manufacturer is integrating AI features. This isn't a founder pair where a sane person is pursuing something the market thinks is crazy — it's competent people pursuing something the market already agrees is inevitable. When I backed Solana, most of Silicon Valley thought building yet another Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter was delusional. When I backed Teamshares, people couldn't understand why you'd want to buy hundreds of small businesses through employee ownership. Those ideas provoked genuine confusion. "Let's use AI to analyze microscope images faster" provokes head-nodding, which means the returns are already priced into the competitive landscape.

The structural moat question is where this really falls apart for me. Run my standard test: does this business get stronger or weaker as software gets cheaper? It gets weaker — demonstrably and obviously. As vision-language models improve, the specific capability of assembling analysis pipelines from natural language becomes easier for anyone to replicate. The dossier identifies a "potential" data advantage from accumulated image-model pairings, but marks it as not existing at meaningful scale. The "potential" workflow lock-in from embedded pipelines is real but fragile — if a better tool appears, researchers switch. This is Birchbox's ghost: a UX layer over commoditized inputs with no compounding defensibility. Meanwhile, Danaher can bundle equivalent AI features into Leica hardware at zero marginal cost to the customer. Zeiss and Nikon are doing the same. The channel conflict argument — that hardware vendors can't easily sell standalone SaaS — is real but cuts both ways: it means the standalone SaaS has to compete against "free" bundled analysis, not just against other standalone tools.

I'll steel-man the bull case because it deserves consideration. If Alchemy's "library of verified processing nodes" became the de facto reproducibility standard for bioimage analysis — cited in papers, embedded in SOPs, required by journals — that starts to look less like an application and more like infrastructure. Research has a genuine reproducibility crisis, and a platform where validated pipelines are shared and built upon could create network effects that open-source tools can't replicate because they lack a commercial entity coordinating validation. The gap between expensive enterprise pathology tools (Visiopharm at $7.1M revenue serving pathology departments) and free-but-requires-scripting open-source tools (CellProfiler, QuPath) is real, and the first company to nail researcher UX in that gap could build meaningful switching costs. If that happened, you'd be looking at something closer to my Airtable exception — SaaS that functions as a new primitive for a specific category of knowledge work. But the dossier shows zero evidence the founders are thinking about infrastructure or standards. There are no named customers, no LOIs, no traction metrics of any kind, and the founders lack specific bioimage analysis experience — Baek's closest credential is EP mapping at Boston Scientific, which is adjacent but not the same domain. The distance between "upload images and AI analyzes them" and "we're building the reproducibility infrastructure for global research microscopy" is enormous, and nothing in the evidence suggests they're bridging it.

The absence of any traction signal compounds my concern. At pre-seed I don't expect revenue, but I expect something — a named lab using the tool, a letter of intent from a CRO, a waitlist, repeat usage data from a pilot. The dossier found literally nothing: no customer count, no revenue signal, no user metrics, no press coverage. The claim of "90% analysis time reduction" floats without any anchor to specific users. Combined with the name confusion problem — sharing a name with a $345M blockchain company whose own "AlchemyAI" product creates direct SEO competition — and I'm looking at a company that hasn't yet demonstrated it can reach its target users, let alone retain them against free alternatives and bundled incumbents.
