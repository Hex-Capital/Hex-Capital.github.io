# GovGuard -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Everlaw has $80.8M in revenue and already does AI-powered redaction for FOIA at the federal level. Granicus bought GovQA in 2021. CivicPlus bought NextRequest in 2022 for a combined war chest north of $290M from Insight Partners alone. So the first thing I'm looking at with GovGuard is: what exactly is the pitch here? "We're going to build AI search-and-redact for local government FOIA requests." OK. You know who's going to build AI search-and-redact for local government FOIA requests? Granicus. CivicPlus. Everlaw moving downmarket. Every incumbent with existing municipal distribution is looking at GovGuard's demo and thinking "we should add that feature by Q3." This is the Adobe problem I keep coming back to — GovGuard is building an AI application layer on top of an existing workflow where the incumbents already own the customer relationship. The AI doesn't disrupt the incumbents; it's a gift to them.

Federal FOIA requests hitting 1.5M in FY2024 — up 25% — with backlogs surging 33% to 267,056, and one organization alone filing 100,000+ requests via AI tools — that's a real pain point. I'm not disputing the problem. The "why now" is genuinely acute, especially with federal workforce reductions gutting the staff who process these requests. But a real problem with an obvious AI solution is not a contrarian bet. It's consensus. Every govtech-adjacent VC can read the Brechner Center report and draw the same straight line from "backlogs up 33%" to "someone should automate this." GovGuard isn't operating in a category that makes investors uncomfortable — cannabis made investors uncomfortable, crypto in 2017 made investors uncomfortable. GovGuard is in a category investors find boring, which is different. Boring doesn't create mispricing. It creates indifference, and indifference with good reason — B2G sales cycles of 6-18 months and municipal procurement bureaucracy are real structural barriers that make the risk/reward mediocre, not asymmetric.

Aditya Sabharwal and Gleb Hulting co-founded BoardWatch together before GovGuard, which is worth something — I care about co-founder relationships that predate the current company. But the sane-person-insane-idea ratio is off. The idea is entirely sensible. AI automation for government document processing is the kind of thing that writes itself on a whiteboard. Nobody hears "we automate FOIA redaction with AI" and says "that's fucking crazy." They say "yeah, someone should probably do that." That's the wrong reaction for my framework. GovGuard's founders have technical backgrounds — Aditya's MS CS from UIUC — but no visible government or legal domain expertise, no public GitHub repos, no social media presence to assess temperament. The dossier shows zero paying customers, 2 YC Launch upvotes, and a team size listed at 0. I can't evaluate founder calibration when there's this little signal.

GovGuard's claimed differentiation — that incumbents like Granicus and CivicPlus are ticket-management systems while GovGuard automates the substantive fulfillment work — is the strongest element of the bull case. The innovator's dilemma argument: these incumbents built their businesses on workflow routing, and rebuilding as AI-native fulfillment engines would cannibalize their existing product positioning. The state-code exemption library across 50 states creates ongoing maintenance cost that deters casual entrants. And government switching costs, once you're in, are genuinely sticky. If GovGuard landed 5-10 municipal contracts and built a reference customer base before incumbents shipped their own AI features, they could establish a beachhead that's defensible through procurement inertia alone. The condition that would flip me: if GovGuard had signed LOIs or active pilots with 3+ municipalities and could demonstrate that Granicus/CivicPlus are structurally unable to ship comparable AI fulfillment within 18 months due to architectural debt, I'd revisit. But none of that evidence exists today.

GovGuard's structural durability problem is terminal for my framework. The company explicitly states it doesn't train models on municipal data, which means no proprietary data flywheel. The state-code exemption library is a database, not a moat — any competent legal engineering team can build one. Compare this to Metropolis, which I seeded and is now at roughly $5B — Metropolis controls physical parking infrastructure that no software layer can replicate. GovGuard controls nothing physical. It's a software layer that processes other people's documents stored in other people's systems (Microsoft 365, Google Drive, Dropbox). When I ask "can an incumbent add AI to their existing product and replicate this within 18 months?" the answer is unambiguously yes. Granicus has thousands of municipal customers, an existing FOIA module, and PE money. They will ship AI redaction. The question is when, not if. GovGuard's best-case outcome is acquisition by one of these incumbents for a modest multiple — which is fine for a small fund, but I'm not writing checks for acquisition outcomes when the acquirer can just build it.

Pass. GovGuard fails my AI kill test — this is an application-layer AI tool in a category where well-funded incumbents (Granicus, CivicPlus, Everlaw) already own distribution to the target customer. The idea is sensible, not insane, which means the sanity-insanity ratio is flatlined. There's no primitive being created here — FOIA processing is an existing workflow getting automated, not a new behavioral category. The consensus mispricing that defines my best investments is absent: GovGuard isn't in a category the market has dismissed for wrong reasons; it's in a category the market has correctly identified as structurally challenging for startups. The one condition that changes my mind is tangible evidence that the incumbents' architectural debt makes AI-native FOIA fulfillment a 3+ year rebuild for them, combined with GovGuard having locked in municipal contracts that create switching-cost moats before that rebuild ships.
