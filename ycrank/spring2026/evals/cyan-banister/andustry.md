# Andustry -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I see when I look at this deal: AI-powered procurement is the hottest consensus category in B2B software right now. LightSource Labs just raised $33M. Matchory closed a EUR 6M seed in January 2025. Cavela pulled in $8.6M. MESH Works is processing $884M in RFQs. And the research itself says AI adoption in procurement is expected to hit 86% by end of 2026. When I hear those numbers, I don't lean in — I lean back. The best ideas never look obvious at the start, and this one looks obvious to a lot of people with a lot of money. Nobody is uncomfortable writing a check into "AI for procurement." Nobody shifts in their chair. That's not where I find my investments. That's where I find crowded markets.

Now, the founder question — tell me their story. Johann Sturken grew up in a family that trades industrial goods. He got mechanical engineering degrees from RWTH Aachen and TUM, consulted at McKinsey in the aerospace and defense practice, did strategy at Airbus. That's a coherent path, and the family connection gives him genuine proximity to the problem. He's seen this broken system from the inside of a family business. I respect that. But when I evaluate biographical forge, I'm asking whether this person was *forged* — whether something happened to them that made walking away from this problem impossible. Johann's story reads as someone who grew up comfortable inside the system and saw an opportunity to improve it. That's intelligent pattern recognition, not biographical inevitability. Het Dave brings SaaS-to-AI transformation experience, but no visible thread connecting him to industrial procurement. These are two smart, credentialed people who found a market gap. I invest in people who found their life's mission.

The bull case deserves serious engagement, because the EUR 1M+ in beta transaction volume is a real signal at pre-seed — actual transactions flowing through the platform, not vanity metrics. If Andustry can build genuine supplier network density in industrial machinery specifically, the two-sided marketplace dynamics could create defensibility that pure AI tools can't touch. Xometry proved this works for custom manufacturing and grew into a $2.1B public company. The 500K+ verified supplier database, if genuinely proprietary with trust scores and certification data built from real transaction feedback, could become the kind of compounding data asset I look for. Industrial machinery is one of the last un-digitized procurement categories, and the founder with a family legacy in the space has authentic context that a generic AI team wouldn't. If the marketplace flywheel catches — more buyers attract more suppliers, more supplier competition drives better prices, better prices attract more buyers — this could be the Flexport of industrial goods. That path exists.

But I keep running into the wall that Lee Jacobs articulated better than I can: "not some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper." The dossier states plainly that "LLM-based RFQ generation and supplier matching are reproducible." That's not my inference — that's the structural reality. If the AI layer is commodity, and the supplier data might be aggregated from public directories rather than proprietary, what exactly is the moat? The marketplace network effect is the answer, but it's pre-scale, and well-funded competitors are building the same network in adjacent segments. When I apply the ten-year question — where does this go in a decade? — I can see a version where Andustry becomes essential infrastructure for industrial buyers worldwide. But I can also see a version where Google, Microsoft, or one of the $33M-funded competitors absorbs this capability as a feature. The gap between those two futures is where conviction lives, and I don't feel it here.

There's something about industrial procurement that does activate my "invisible system" antenna — buyers really are navigating an opaque, fragmented market where information asymmetry benefits entrenched middlemen and legacy relationships. Weeks waiting for a first quote is a genuine hidden tax on businesses that don't have procurement departments or established supplier networks. That's real. But Andustry's approach is to make the process *faster*, not to fundamentally restructure the power dynamics. Affirm didn't just speed up lending — it exposed what credit cards were hiding. I need to see that same structural revelation here, and I don't yet.
