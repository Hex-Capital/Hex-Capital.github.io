# Lab0 -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Rocketlane, a $105M-funded PSA platform with 750+ customers, banned Lab0 from their Propel conference on April 19, 2026. That's the most interesting fact in this dossier. Incumbents don't gatekeep against irrelevant startups — they ignore them. When a well-capitalized company actively excludes a three-person pre-seed team from an industry event, it means Lab0 has built something that threatens the incumbent's narrative. I've seen this pattern before: the established players dismissing Stripe as "just another payment API" while quietly copying its approach. But the Stripe parallel only goes so far. When I backed Patrick Collison, I'd watched him ship Auctomatic as a teenager. I had years of founder data. With Lab0, I have a conference ban and some resumes. The ban tells me the product might be real. It doesn't tell me whether the founders are.

Lakshya Gupta co-authored "World of Workflows" (arXiv 2601.22130, January 2026), a 234-task benchmark showing that frontier LLMs suffer from "dynamics blindness" — they can't predict cascading side effects in enterprise systems like ServiceNow. That's a genuine research contribution, not a marketing paper. Lab0's founders have mapped the exact technical frontier of the problem they're solving, which is more homework than most pre-seed teams do. But here's what nags me: mapping a frontier is different from having lived on it. None of Lab0's three founders appear to have worked as forward-deployed engineers or system integrators. Lakshya was a founding engineer at Emergent, Sujay founded Ravvio in BFSI sales AI, Onkar built production systems at Oracle and Mudrex. They're studying enterprise implementation from the outside — through a benchmark paper — rather than building what was missing from their own daily work. When Drew Houston kept forgetting his USB drive, that frustration was the seed of Dropbox. When the Octopart founders couldn't find electronic parts for their physics lab, that was organic discovery. Lab0's origin story reads more like "we analyzed the $451.6 billion system integration market and identified an automation opportunity." The research depth is real, but the problem discovery feels academic rather than visceral.

"AI agents that automate enterprise software implementation" — does anyone react to Lab0's pitch with "that will never work"? I doubt it. Three major PSA vendors launched AI agent products within six months: Rocketlane's Nitro, Certinia's Veda, Kantata's Expertise Engine. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks in late 2025. The entire enterprise software industry is saying "yes, AI agents should automate implementation." When everyone agrees an idea is good, the returns have usually been competed away. Lab0's specific approach — replacing the consultant entirely rather than augmenting them — has a slightly harder edge, and automating the schlep of enterprise deployment (SOC 2 compliance, API integrations, configuration management) does repel most AI founders who'd rather build the next coding assistant. But the category itself is consensus. Lab0 isn't in the sweet spot of "seems like a bad idea but is a good idea" — it's in the adjacent territory of "seems like a good idea and is a good idea but is really hard." That's a different bet, and one where execution against well-funded incumbents matters more than insight.

Sujay Srivastava previously founded Ravvio, an AI startup for sales and customer engagement in BFSI — that's a data point I value. Prior shipping experience, even from a company whose outcome isn't documented, tells me more than academic credentials. Lab0 has a repeat founder, which maps to my pattern of tracking builders across companies. But I don't know what Sujay learned from Ravvio. I don't have the pivot stories, the creative workarounds, the "we ran out of money and here's what we did" narratives. The dossier gives me Sujay's CAT 99.94 percentile and his quantum computing thesis — signals of raw intelligence, not of the relentlessly resourceful quality I need to see. Onkar Borade brings production engineering depth across Oracle, Mudrex, and Samespace, and Lab0 benefits from having three co-founders with complementary skills (research, startup experience, systems engineering). All three attended IITs, with Lakshya and Onkar sharing an IIT Bombay connection that suggests a genuine co-founder relationship. These are technically capable people. But technically capable is table stakes at YC.

The bull case for Lab0 is real: a $451B market dominated by billable-hour consulting firms whose business model structurally prevents them from automating themselves, a founding team with published research on the exact technical bottleneck (dynamics blindness in enterprise AI), a repeat founder in Sujay, and an incumbent frightened enough to ban them from a conference. If Lab0 can compress a 6-month Accenture engagement into weeks, even for a narrow vertical, the value capture is enormous and the data flywheel from "continuous learning from every rollout" could create a genuine moat. The Bindwell parallel applies — young technical founders tackling an unsexy domain with AI — and I backed those teenagers at 18 and 19. But Bindwell had a pivot story I could evaluate (from selling AI tools to licensing pesticide molecules), and I could see the founders' flexibility in real time. Lab0 gives me no equivalent window into how these founders think under pressure. The condition that would flip me: if I sat with Lakshya, Sujay, and Onkar for ten minutes and they told me stories — not about their research or credentials, but about problems they'd hacked their way through — and one of them had personally done implementation work, manually configuring enterprise software to understand every pain point their agents would need to solve, I'd move to Invest quickly. The market is right. The schlep is real. I need founder stories I don't have.

Lab0 sits in a zone where the market thesis is strong but the founder signal is thin — not negative, just thin. No traction, no revenue, no user love metrics, no evidence of doing things that don't scale. The research paper elevates Lab0 above pure resume-ware and demonstrates genuine intellectual engagement with enterprise workflow complexity, which is why I score earnest builder identity slightly above my default. But the organic problem discovery gap is the dimension that most differentiates Lab0 from the kind of bet I make with my own money. The founders chose this problem from analysis, not from lived frustration, and that distinction matters because enterprise implementation has a thousand edge cases that only someone who's lived through them would anticipate. Lab0 is competent founders in a real market with a real technical contribution — but without the founder-problem intimacy that turns a good bet into my kind of bet.
