# CatchBack Cards -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that strikes me first about CatchBack Cards is the gap between the organic origin and the manufactured solution. Joseph Zhang was a Pokémon card flipper. He quit his job to sell cards. That's about as organic as it gets -- you don't leave McKinsey and AWS to sell Pokémon cards unless you genuinely love it. But then I look at what he built and I see cryptographic randomness proofs, a Nexus Layer 1 mainnet partnership, and tags that read "Crypto / Web3, NFT." There's a disconnect. The problem was discovered organically. The solution was shaped by where he last worked. When you spend time at a blockchain infrastructure company, everything starts looking like it needs on-chain verification. The schlep in trading cards is real -- fragmented markets, opaque pricing, 13% fees -- but the actual hard part isn't proving randomness. It's building liquidity against Whatnot at $11.5B, navigating brand licensing with Pokémon and the major sports leagues, and handling the physical logistics of shipping cards. Zhang found the right problem but is tackling a secondary schlep while the primary one looms untouched.

The crypto framing worries me specifically. In 2026, tagging a consumer collecting company as "Crypto / Web3, NFT" is the equivalent of leading your pitch with the technology rather than the user. Mainstream collectors -- the people who actually drive trading card volume -- don't care about cryptographic verification. They care about price, selection, and trust. Trust can be built many ways. Blockchain is one of them, and probably the one that alienates the most potential users right now given how consumer sentiment toward NFTs has cratered since 2022. The Nexus partnership and podcast appearances suggest the company is spending social capital in the crypto ecosystem rather than in the collector community where the users actually live.

The competitive landscape here is the opposite of schlep blindness. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight because nobody wants to do the work. Whatnot raised $975M. Fanatics already runs a mystery box program called Under Wraps with official league licensing. Alt has $106M for card investment. Loupe does live-streamed pack openings. When you count five well-funded competitors doing variations of the same thing, you're not in a market where the problem scared everyone away. You're in a market where everyone showed up. My instinct when I see this many players is that the easy part -- identifying the opportunity -- is done, and what remains is an execution war against companies with orders-of-magnitude more capital and existing distribution. The zero-fee marketplace and buyback guarantees sound good for users but mean CatchBack is subsidizing growth in a capital-intensive way, competing against companies that can outspend them indefinitely.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. $13K MRR with 700 users at pre-seed is genuinely meaningful -- people are spending real money. The Steph Curry collaboration, if it materializes, could provide the kind of distribution spike that changes a company's trajectory overnight. And there's an argument that the asynchronous, customizable mystery pack format is a distinct enough product wedge that it isn't really competing head-to-head with Whatnot's live streams or TCGPlayer's search listings. If CatchBack nailed the "pack rip" experience on mobile the way that DoorDash nailed last-mile logistics when everyone said food delivery was dead, the crowded market wouldn't matter. For this to work, though, the crypto layer would need to become invisible infrastructure rather than a marketing identity, the Steph Curry partnership would need to drive real collector adoption, and the company would need to find a capital-efficient path through the buyback economics. That's not three miracles, but it's close.

The co-founder situation concerns me. In a two-person company, both founders matter enormously. Sophia Kim's background is completely undocumented -- no LinkedIn, no GitHub, no public information at all. I've seen companies fall apart when a co-founder turns out not to have the skills or commitment required. And I've seen it enough times that the absence of any information about a co-founder in a two-person startup is itself a signal. Joseph Zhang has strong credentials -- Gates Scholar, ISEF finalist, built real software at AWS and Nexus -- but I want to know who he's building with and why, and right now that's a blank.

This is a company with a genuine organic founder-problem connection and real early revenue, building in a market with enormous, well-funded competition, using a technology framing that narrows rather than expands its addressable audience. Zhang is clearly technical and clearly loves this domain. But the path from $13K MRR selling mystery packs to a venture-scale outcome runs directly through Whatnot, Fanatics, and eBay -- and I don't see what makes CatchBack the one that wins that fight.
