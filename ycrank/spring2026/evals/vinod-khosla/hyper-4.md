# Hyper -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Hyper is two ex-Matic Robots autonomy engineers building a shared memory layer for AI tools — competent founders, adjacent technical background, attacking a category where Glean already prints $200M ARR. The dimensional signature is clear: middling on founder profile, near-zero on radical affordability, and that pairing tells me exactly what kind of company Hyper is. It's the AI copilot for AI-first teams. It's my Pass Example, dressed in different clothes.

Consequence magnitude is where Hyper sits closest to my Pass Example rung. The HIGH rung on this ladder is OpenAI — I wrote a $50M check and an apology letter to my LPs because the consequence of AGI was civilizational. The MID rung is DoorDash, where the consequence of universal food access for hundreds of millions overrode my technology-risk preference. The LOW rung is the AI-copilot-for-accountants pitch I reject reflexively — "we automate 40% of their workflow" — moderately profitable SaaS, nobody's life fundamentally changes. Hyper sits a hair above that LOW rung. The pitch in the dossier — Hyper "silently learns from every update in every tool your team uses" so that AI-first teams stop re-prompting context — is a workflow tax reduction for engineering teams that already pay Glean $200M/year collectively. If Hyper fully succeeds, AI-first SMBs save context-switching minutes. The world does not visibly change.

Path to radical affordability is the lowest rung Hyper hits. The HIGH rung is Impossible Foods — protein at eventually lower cost than animal agriculture, a basic human need collapsing toward free. The MID rung is Rocket Lab — small-sat launch from millions to a price small companies can budget for. The LOW rung is per-seat enterprise SaaS that captures value through premium pricing without changing cost structure. Hyper plants itself on the LOW rung. There is no pricing page on heyhyper.ai per the dossier fetch, but the inferred per-seat SaaS targeting AI-first SMB/mid-market teams is not collapsing the cost of a basic necessity — it is taxing the productivity of teams already paying for Notion, Cursor, and Claude Code seats. Healthcare, energy, education, legal access — Hyper touches none of them.

Non-linear technical approach: the HIGH rung is Rocket Lab's Rutherford engine with electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers — a step-function on cost. The LOW rung is the LLM-wrapper SaaS that ships a vector store over webhook ingestion. Hyper is on the LOW rung. The dossier itself names the primitives — "LLM extraction over tool webhooks, vector store, retrieval injection" — and notes Dust ($21.5M raised, $7.3M ARR) and a long tail of competitors already shipping overlapping capability. There is no Rutherford engine here. Improbable ambition: the HIGH rung is Hermeus Mach 5; the LOW rung is the YC vertical-AI-copilot pitch. Hyper's "self-driving company brain" tagline gestures at audacity, but Glean's existing $7.2B valuation and Notion AI shipping external connectors per the dossier mean no informed observer would say "that can't be done" — they would say "Glean and Notion are doing it." That places Hyper barely above the LOW rung.

Founder as learning machine is Hyper's strongest dimension and the only one that reaches my MID rung. The HIGH rung is the 19-year-old Mach Industries dropout taking on Lockheed; the MID rung is Peter Beck, self-taught New Zealand engineer; the LOW rung is the domain expert with rehearsed answers. Shalin Shah and Kanyes Thaker are Berkeley '21 ex-Matic Robots engineers — autonomy lead and perception researcher respectively, with sensor-fusion DNA that is conceptually adjacent to fusing tool surfaces into shared memory. The March 2026 Product Hunt launch as an iOS voice notetaker followed by a May 2026 YC framing as a cross-tool team brain is the only learning-rate signal in the dossier — a possible pivot from a 96-upvote consumer voice product to a B2B knowledge layer. That is a real signal, but it is not a 50x signal. Adjacent ML talent without a documented prior exit and without a first-principles insight that contradicts the Glean playbook puts Hyper between my MID and LOW rungs.

Hyper in 36 months: acqui-hired by Notion or Glean for under $30M, or quietly dead, as Glean's 9-month $100M-to-$200M ARR slope drags team-shared memory features into the platform Hyper is trying to be a layer above. The mechanism is simple and matches my Red Flag for "redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand" — Hyper is fighting for a slice of a budget Glean is already collecting at scale.

The bull case that should sting: my own DoorDash exception. Operationally hard, technically unremarkable, but the founder velocity and consequence magnitude overrode my technical-novelty bar — Keith Rabois led the seed and the company IPO'd at $72B. Could Hyper be the same? No. DoorDash's consequence was planetary food access; Hyper's consequence is faster context for engineering teams. The two scales do not compare. The Oscar Health exception fails too — Oscar attacked healthcare affordability in a regulated incumbent industry, and Hyper attacks productivity in an unregulated one where Glean already has $610M of capital and a 2-3 year integration head start. The OpenAI exception requires civilizational consequence; Hyper does not have that. One verifiable condition would flip me: if Hyper ships a proprietary cross-tool team knowledge graph that demonstrably compounds — measurable retention curves where context quality improves week-over-week in a way Glean's connector-based architecture structurally cannot match — and pairs that with a 10x affordability collapse for SMB teams priced out of Glean. Until then, this is the SaaS bet I pass on so I can fund the fusion plant.
