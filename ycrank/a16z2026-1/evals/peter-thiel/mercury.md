# Mercury -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Stefano Delmanto and Naveen Sharma were Forward Deployed Engineers at Palantir. I co-founded Palantir. I know what FDEs do — they embed inside the most consequential institutions on Earth, build bespoke analytical systems under operational pressure, and create switching costs so deep that the customer cannot imagine operating without the platform. The FDE role is one of the most demanding positions in technology precisely because it requires bridging the gap between technical capability and real-world deployment. Mercury's founders possess that training. And yet Mercury itself is the structural inverse of what made Palantir a monopoly. Where Palantir built proprietary ontological software that became inseparable from government intelligence operations, Mercury builds a no-code orchestration layer atop other companies' agents — Claude Code, Devin, gstack, all named on their homepage. The founders learned monopoly-building at Palantir and then started a company with no visible path to monopoly. The skill is real; the application squanders it.

The secret test fails immediately and decisively. "Non-technical users need to coordinate multiple AI agents" is not a contrarian truth — it is the loudest consensus in enterprise AI right now. Four well-funded competitors have already raised a combined $132 million pursuing adjacent theses: CrewAI at $24.5 million, Relevance AI at $37 million, Lindy at approximately $50 million, Dust at $21.5 million. OpenAI ships a multi-agent orchestration SDK. Microsoft builds agent coordination into Copilot Studio. Google adds it to Vertex AI. When I ask "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" and the answer is something a dozen funded startups and three platform incumbents already believe, there is no secret. There is only competition. The dossier itself confirms this: "No defensibility signals found in public sources at this stage." Mercury's competitive landscape slide — comparing itself against five similar companies — is the red flag I described in Zero to One. The existence of that comparison is itself the diagnosis.

Mercury's positioning as a "no-code" interface for multi-agent orchestration deserves scrutiny because it reveals the depth of the commoditization problem. The no-code wrapper is a feature, not a technology. Lindy already serves non-technical users with no-code agent building and $5.1 million in revenue as of October 2024. Adding multi-agent coordination to Lindy's existing product is an incremental feature addition for a team with fifty million dollars and an established user base. Mercury must build the entire product, acquire users from scratch, and maintain compatibility with rapidly evolving third-party agent APIs — all while the agent providers themselves (Anthropic, Cognition, OpenAI) can build native orchestration that renders the orchestration layer redundant. This is the nightmare scenario for any company that wraps someone else's infrastructure without possessing proprietary technology of its own. When the platforms move, the wrapper dies.

The bull case requires one specific thing to be true: that multi-agent orchestration is so complex and so domain-specific that the coordination layer itself becomes the product — that Mercury accumulates proprietary workflow data, delegation patterns, and context-routing intelligence that no competitor can replicate. If agent coordination turns out to be a genuinely hard systems problem rather than a thin routing layer, and if Mercury's Palantir-trained team solves it first with sufficient depth, the switching costs from persistent memory and agent configurations could create real lock-in. Stefano's description of running a five-agent AI sales organization using Mercury's own platform suggests the team understands operational deployment, not just product demos. The a16z Speedrun selection from 19,000 applicants provides initial distribution. But this bull case requires Mercury to outrun four funded competitors, avoid disintermediation by the agent providers whose tools it orchestrates, and overcome a name collision with Mercury Financial — a $3.5 billion fintech platform at mercury.com that will structurally dominate every search query for years.

Palantir succeeded because it built proprietary technology so deeply embedded in the CIA's operational workflow that no competitor could displace it — the switching cost was not inconvenience but operational paralysis. Mercury builds atop agents it does not control, serves business users who face minimal switching costs, and operates in a market where the consensus thesis attracts capital faster than any single team can execute. The Palantir comparison illuminates precisely what Mercury lacks: proprietary technology, government embedding, and a customer whose switching costs approach the structurally impossible. The founders carry the right training from the right institution, but they have chosen to compete rather than to monopolize. Monopoly Secret and Power Law Return Magnitude are the decisive dimensions — a 7 and an 8 respectively — because without a genuine contrarian insight enabling structural dominance, and in a fragmented market that resists winner-take-all dynamics, no amount of founder capability changes the outcome. Competition remains, as ever, for losers.
