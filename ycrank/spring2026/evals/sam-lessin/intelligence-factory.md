# Intelligence Factory -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Intelligence Factory is two genuinely credentialed robotics researchers — Yash Sinha out of ETH Zurich's Robotic Systems Lab and Jalaj Shukla out of UPenn GRASP — pointing a tactile-data thesis at the single most consensus-priced sleeve in 2026 venture, where Skild AI just printed $14B in January and Physical Intelligence cleared $5.6B the same month. The dimensional signature is unmistakable: the highest rung is founder-market fit on raw pedigree, the lowest is the price of the regatta they're entering. That pairing is the canonical Pass Example in my own deck — a YC AI seed where the founders are smart and the round is a price I literally cannot make money at.

The Pass Example I wrote into the deck almost word-for-word describes Intelligence Factory's competitive posture: "AI agent for [vertical], three months out of YC, tier-1s already in." Swap "robot manipulation" for "AI agent" and the frame holds — Skild AI raised $1.4B at $14B, Physical Intelligence took $470M at $5.6B, Figure sits at $39B, and 1X is reportedly raising at $10B. This is not Solana at $0.05/coin in 2018 when funds had quit crypto; this is the inverse — the most over-bid category in venture, where Intelligence Factory's $80K–$200K founding-engineer post has to compete against checks written by companies with three orders of magnitude more capital. The price discipline test fails before I read the team page. AI is just more internet, robots are just more AI, and the seed entry price into a sleeve where the leaders are at $14B is the worst-of-both: priced as if Intelligence Factory has already won, structured so it almost certainly won't.

Teamshares is the canonical capital-structure rung — a financing instrument no other fund could underwrite, around an asset class no one else priced correctly. Intelligence Factory's failure mode runs in the opposite direction: a foundation-model robot brain is structurally the highest-burn shape a venture company can take, and the dossier shows no revenue, no LOI, no licensing customer, and a comparable in Physical Intelligence that has raised $470M and remains pure research. Default-alive in 24 months is not a question one can ask Intelligence Factory in good faith — they need five-plus tacks of $50M+ each to reach a model release competitive with π-zero, and at YC seed pricing that cap table gets washed out by the Series A from someone like Lachy Groom before Sinha or Shukla finish a tactile dataset of meaningful scale.

Founder conviction — the dimension where Intelligence Factory rates highest — compounds or fails on artifacts I can read before the meeting. Sinha was CTO at Kaio Labs, ran F1 work at Sauber, and sat inside ETH RSL; Shukla did GRASP Lab research on multimodal manipulation policies and a DRDO Research Fellowship. That is real domain density — the kind of resume Paul Graham would lean on first. But my filter is "writing is thinking," and Intelligence Factory's founders have no public Twitter, no GitHub, no essay, no opinionated thread on why tactile-first beats vision-language pretraining. Over 18 months the absence compounds against them: every robot-brain founder pitching a Series A in 2027 will have published benchmarks, demo videos, and a manifesto on contact-rich manipulation, and Intelligence Factory's landing page is one sentence. Pedigree is the prior, public conviction is the posterior, and the posterior is missing.

The counterfactual on round non-consensus: what would have to be true for Intelligence Factory to clear my price-discipline screen? It would require either (a) the round to come in below $8M post — which the dossier neither confirms nor denies, but Comma Capital plus Allen Gannett plus YC at SF robotics in 2026 implies the standard $15M–$20M post — or (b) two tier-1 robotics funds to have visibly passed, which the dossier does not document. Neither condition holds in the evidence, so I cannot underwrite the round as mispriced. The flip would need an actual data point on entry valuation; absent it, I apply the silence-equals-consensus default, which in 2026 robotics is aggressive.

Marina Mogilko is the single anchor for the founder-as-brand axis, and the comparison is brutal for Intelligence Factory: she came with millions of subscribers and a profitable LingaTrip, which is why $1.7M for 5% of 30-year earnings was a deal I could be the only bid on. Intelligence Factory's founders have no follower count retrievable, no Twitter handle, no GitHub presence, no Discord, no newsletter — the dossier searches return literal absences. There is no asset here to structure non-standard exposure around. The Slow Creator Fund cannot touch this; the holdco instrument cannot touch this; it is a SAFE into a SAFE-shaped company in a SAFE-shaped category, which is precisely the deal flow where my alpha is zero.

My 36-month forecast: Intelligence Factory is acqui-hired into Skild AI, Physical Intelligence, or Figure for the tactile data-collection hardware and the GRASP/ETH-trained engineering team, at a price that returns 1–3x to the YC SAFE and roughly zero to anyone entering at the seed post, with no independent foundation-model release shipped. The mechanism is capital asymmetry: a $14B incumbent with $30M of revenue can hire Shukla's tactile pipeline outright before Intelligence Factory raises a Series A on terms that survive dilution, and the regatta tacks needed to reach a competitive model release exceed the financing windows available to a 5-person team without owned distribution.

The strongest bull case — and I want to steel-man it honestly — is that tactile/force is a genuine data-modality gap in vision-language pretrained π-zero and Skild Brain, that contact-rich manipulation is where every existing foundation model visibly fails, and that a proprietary human-demonstration hardware rig collecting force data is the kind of illegible-asset wedge I should be excited about. Comma Capital signals the George-Hotz-adjacent operator network, which is exactly the channel my public framing under-states. The Ro override could apply: if tactile data collection turns out to require capital-intensive operator scaling that only VC dollars unlock, capital intensity becomes the moat. The dismantle is that Covariant, Embodied, and a dozen imitation-learning startups pitched the same hardware-data-as-moat thesis and got acqui-hired by the better-capitalized incumbents, and Intelligence Factory's dossier shows no benchmark, no model release, and no LOI to suggest they've crossed the threshold where tactile-first beats vision-language-plus-tactile-bolted-on. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Intelligence Factory publishes a tactile manipulation benchmark where their policy beats π-zero by 2x on a named contact-rich task, OR shows a signed LOI with a tier-1 robotics OEM at $1M+ ACV — either fact arrives, and I re-underwrite the round.
