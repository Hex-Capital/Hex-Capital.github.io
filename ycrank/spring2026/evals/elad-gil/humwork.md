# Humwork -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Tendem has $72M from Bezos Expeditions, 10,000+ experts, and is building the exact same product — MCP-based human expert access for AI agents. That's the fact I can't get past with Humwork. When a well-capitalized competitor with 10x your supply-side density is already in market offering the same core mechanism, the burden shifts entirely to "what structural advantage does the smaller player have?" Humwork claims a narrower focus on real-time agent debugging with a 30-second matching SLA, but narrow focus is a positioning choice, not a moat. Tendem can narrow its focus tomorrow. The dossier shows no proprietary technology, no exclusive expert relationships, no patent protection on the matching algorithm. Humwork's 1,000+ verified experts versus Tendem's 10,000+ is a 10x supply-side deficit in a business where supply density IS the product quality.

MCP — Anthropic's Model Context Protocol, released late 2024 — is the structural catalyst Humwork rides. It's real. Agent proliferation is real. The need for human fallback when agents stall is real. But none of this is nonobvious. YC Spring 2026 was "dominated by autonomous agent pitches" per Analytics Drift. Every developer building with Claude Code or Cursor has experienced agents looping on edge cases. The insight that "agents sometimes need humans" is approximately as nonobvious as "websites sometimes need customer support." Compare this to when I wrote checks into Coinbase in 2012 — crypto infrastructure was dismissed as fringe by mainstream investors, and the structural shift in digital asset rails was invisible to consensus. Humwork's market timing is consensus timing. The structural shift is visible to everyone who's used a coding agent for fifteen minutes.

Humwork's infrastructure positioning triggers one of the patterns I watch most carefully: this is an application built on someone else's infrastructure layer. MCP is Anthropic's protocol. Anthropic could build first-party expert escalation into Claude — the dossier flags this platform dependency risk explicitly and notes "no mitigation found." When I backed Stripe, Stripe WAS the infrastructure layer — it defined the API that others built on. Humwork doesn't own the protocol layer; it's a service that plugs into a protocol controlled by a company that could vertically integrate at any point. That's not where I want to be positioned. The expert marketplace itself has network effects — more experts enable faster matching, attracting more agent operators — but expert marketplaces are two-sided services businesses, structurally more like Upwork than like Stripe. The switching costs are low: an agent operator can swap MCP servers with a config change.

The strongest bull case for Humwork: the 130% net retention in software engineering and 87% resolution rate are genuinely strong early signals for a pre-seed company. Yash Goenka is a repeat YC founder (S25 and P26) who's been building LLM products since 2021, and Humwork's prior product (Qualify.bot) involved real-time AI-to-human interaction in time-sensitive loan qualification — structurally similar to this problem. The "boring operational plumbing" angle has merit: nobody prestigious wants to build an expert dispatch network, which means less competition from prestige-seeking founders. And the accumulated resolution data could train matching algorithms that improve over time, creating a data flywheel. If Humwork could establish itself as the default expert escalation layer before Tendem or platform incumbents lock the category, the network effects could compound. I've seen this pattern work in unglamorous categories — Gusto in payroll, Checkr in background checks. But those companies operated in regulated markets where compliance created structural barriers to entry, and they weren't competing against a $72M-funded direct competitor from day one. Humwork's category has no regulatory moat, and the MCP integration layer is technically replicable by any company with an expert network.

Yash Goenka pivoted from Qualify.bot to Humwork in approximately nine months — both operating under Orange AI Inc. That's fast even by YC standards. Repeat YC acceptance (S25 to P26) signals partner confidence, but the pivot velocity raises a question I always ask: has the founder found a market they'll grind on for a decade, or are they pattern-matching to the hottest category? Neither Yash nor Rohan Datta has operated at a company during its scaling phase — Rohan spent five years at Microgrid Labs, Yash built multiple small startups. They're technically capable builders, but I haven't seen evidence of the organizational scaling instincts I look for — the ability to hire, fire, and build management layers that my High Growth Handbook framework treats as the core CEO function from 50 to 500 employees.

Humwork's margin architecture is the dimension where the business model reveals its structural constraint. Paying human experts on every resolution creates a services-margin business, not a software-margin business. Expert marketplaces historically operate at 20-35% take rates — Upwork's gross margins hover around 75% but that's because they charge fees on both sides of transactions where the labor cost isn't their burden. Humwork is closer to a managed marketplace where expert quality and availability are the product promise, which typically compresses margins. The 130% net retention in software engineering is the strongest metric in the dossier, but net retention at pre-seed scale with a small denominator is noisy. The verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: if Humwork demonstrated gross margins above 60% at $500K+ ARR — proving software leverage in the matching layer rather than pure services pass-through — AND showed that its resolution data created measurable matching quality advantages over Tendem, I'd reconsider. That would indicate an infrastructure-layer value capture mechanism rather than a commoditizable services business.

Humwork operates in a real market with a real structural tailwind, and the founders are technically competent repeat builders. But the market timing is consensus, the positioning is application-layer on someone else's infrastructure, the competitive landscape includes a direct competitor with $72M and 10x supply-side density, and the margin architecture is structurally constrained by human labor costs on every transaction. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Nonobvious Market Timing — where the evidence shows a fully consensus market with an incumbent already present — and Infrastructure-Layer Positioning — where Humwork is building a service on Anthropic's protocol rather than owning the layer itself. Pass.
