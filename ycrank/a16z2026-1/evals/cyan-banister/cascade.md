# Cascade -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out about Cascade is the traction velocity: $183k in contracted ARR from zero in three months, fifteen firms signed, and the first three clients doubling their spend. These aren't hobby shops — we're talking about firms that built JFK, LaGuardia, Four Seasons hotels, and nuclear reactors. That's a real signal. At pre-seed, you almost never see enterprise customers of that caliber adopting this fast. Hiring a Head of Growth who was Procore's #2 sales rep and drove $3M ARR gives Cascade an immediate distribution wedge into the exact network where construction software gets bought. I respect the execution here — Hannia Zia clearly knows how to ship product fast, which tracks with her Google Pay India record of scaling from zero to 100 million users.

But when I ask my first question — tell me the story, not the business — Cascade leaves me wanting. Hannia's biography is impressive: Yale CS, Google across four products, Pakistan's pandemic response reaching 500,000 users, Forbes 30 Under 30 Asia. She's clearly a world-class product builder. And Joana Ferreira brings serious ML depth from Google Search and Amazon, plus she grew up in a family of carpenters, which gives her at least a childhood proximity to the trades. They worked together at UnlikelyAI for years before founding Cascade, so the co-founder relationship is tested. But I'm looking for the biographical crucible — the experience that made these specific founders incapable of *not* solving this problem. What I see instead is two talented technologists who identified a large, underserved market. That's smart. It's not inevitable. Hannia's forge was in payments and government tech. Joana's was in ML infrastructure and fintech. Neither was living inside the AEC procurement system, monitoring twenty government portals at 2am, losing bids because they couldn't staff a proposals team. The carpentry thread is real but thin — growing up around carpenters and understanding the architecture firm RFP lifecycle are very different worlds.

The space itself doesn't make me lean forward in the way my strongest bets do. AI-powered vertical SaaS for a specific industry workflow is the single most consensus investment thesis in 2026. Every fund I talk to wants this exact pattern — take an LLM, point it at a fragmented industry with manual processes, wrap it in a vertical platform. Cascade executes this pattern well, but executing a popular pattern is different from doing something that makes sophisticated investors squirm. When I backed Anduril, defense AI was politically radioactive. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs physically uncomfortable. Cascade's pitch — "AI helps construction firms find and win more projects" — would get enthusiastic nods at any investor dinner. That's not disqualifying, but it means the company has to earn conviction through other dimensions of my framework, and those dimensions don't strongly activate either.

I look for an invisible system being disrupted — an opaque structure extracting value from people who can't see it. The government RFP ecosystem is genuinely fragmented across thousands of portals, and I can see how that fragmentation advantages large firms with dedicated business development teams over smaller shops that can't afford to monitor everything. There's a version of the Cascade story where they're democratizing access to government contracts for smaller AEC firms — making a gatekept system visible and navigable. I like that thread. But I want to be honest: government RFP portals are publicly accessible. The system isn't deliberately opaque or extractive the way credit card terms are (Affirm) or cap table terms were (Carta). It's scattered and inefficient, which is a real problem, just not the same as structural exploitation. Compare this to Flexport, where Ryan Petersen spent years inside the logistics system watching freight forwarders profit from information asymmetry — he lived inside the hidden structure before he decided to tear it open. Cascade identifies the fragmentation from the outside, which is analytically correct but emotionally different.

Where Cascade does earn my attention is defensibility. The "data layer for all construction projects" aspiration, if achieved, would create genuine infrastructure-grade switching costs. The ERP integrations with Deltek Vantagepoint, Ajera, and Costpoint aren't trivial — those are specialized construction ERPs that took real engineering to connect. As firms build proposal history, capability profiles, and compliance records inside Cascade, the platform gets stickier and smarter. The expansion revenue from early clients (doubling spend) validates this flywheel starting to turn. AutogenAI's $65.3M is a real threat from the horizontal direction, and Workorb AI ($2.6M raised) is building the closest direct competitor, but Cascade's three-month ramp suggests they're building vertical depth faster than anyone else in the space. The question is whether government portal scraping — the upstream data source — is fragile enough that format changes or access restrictions could break the pipeline. That's an infrastructure risk inside an infrastructure play.

If I'm wrong about passing on Cascade, here's what would have to be true: the AEC procurement workflow is so painful and so resistant to horizontal tools that the first vertical platform to nail it becomes the category-defining system of record — the way Veeva became inevitable for life sciences CRM, not because it was contrarian but because no horizontal tool could serve the domain's compliance and workflow needs. The $183k cARR in three months, the client caliber, and the Procore sales network could be early evidence of that kind of pull. If Hannia and Joana build the compounding data layer they describe and AEC firms' switching costs lock in before AutogenAI can verticalize, Cascade could own this category regardless of whether it fits my thesis. Strong execution in a large underserved market can overcome the absence of contrarian conviction — I just don't write those checks as often, because execution bets without structural weirdness produce fewer outcomes at the scale I'm looking for.

Cascade is a well-built company with exceptional early traction, a clear market, and competent founders — but it doesn't activate my framework. The founders are talented operators who found a market gap, not survivors who were forged toward an inevitable mission. The space is consensus-attractive, not contrarian. The system being disrupted is fragmented, not deliberately opaque. I'd watch the expansion revenue closely and revisit if the data moat compounds to the point where the defensibility story becomes self-evident, but today this is a pass from my portfolio.
