# matforge -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking signal here is something I rarely see at pre-seed: a founder whose PhD research has already produced materials adopted into Intel and TSMC interconnect roadmaps. That's not a prototype, not a pilot, not a letter of intent -- it's the two largest logic foundries putting his materials on their technology planning documents. If that claim holds up as genuine roadmap inclusion rather than an academic citation in a survey paper, it collapses the typical "will enterprises actually use this?" question I ask of every pre-seed company. Akash Ramdas has already answered it once. The question is whether an AI-automated version of what he did manually during his PhD can answer it repeatedly and at speed.

The structural moment is real and specific. AI chip power and thermal dissipation are doubling annually, and sub-3nm interconnects are hitting physics walls that existing materials cannot solve within traditional R&D timelines. This isn't a static TAM story -- "the semiconductor materials market is $67 billion" -- it's a dynamic one: the rate at which new materials are needed is accelerating while the traditional discovery pipeline remains stuck at 10+ year cycles. That gap between demand velocity and supply velocity is exactly the kind of structural inflection I look for. The semiconductor materials vertical is also genuinely non-obvious to most AI investors, who gravitate toward the consumer-facing or software-layer applications. This market is "too physical" and "too hard" -- it requires understanding fab process integration, not just computational chemistry. That's a market archetype I've consistently found underpriced.

Where I get uncomfortable is the miracle count. Harvey's pattern was clean: the single miracle -- model fidelity for legal reasoning -- was being delivered on a predictable scaling curve by OpenAI and Anthropic. Harvey just had to build workflow and distribution ahead of that curve. Matforge's pattern is messier. MIT Technology Review noted in December 2025 that there's been no "ChatGPT moment" in materials discovery, and the critical bottleneck is bridging computational prediction to physical synthesis and validation. That bridge isn't being built by external foundation model labs on a visible curve. Matforge potentially needs two sequential breakthroughs: AI agents that can reliably identify viable semiconductor material candidates, AND a path to physical validation that doesn't require the kind of wet-lab infrastructure only the $300M+ funded competitors (Periodic Labs, Lila Sciences) can afford. If I reframe it -- Akash has already bridged prediction to synthesis once in his PhD, and the AI just accelerates the prediction side -- it's closer to a single miracle. But that reframing requires believing the synthesis validation step is a checkbox, not a bottleneck, and the MIT review suggests otherwise.

The competitive dynamics deserve careful mapping. Periodic Labs raised $300 million from a16z to build broad AI-for-science with physical labs. Lila Sciences has $550 million. CuspAI has $154 million and already lists semiconductors as a focus area with Samsung Ventures backing. These are not paper competitors -- they have capital to build the physical infrastructure that may be required to close the prediction-to-synthesis gap. Matforge's counter is vertical depth: none of these platforms have a founder whose materials are on Intel and TSMC roadmaps, and semiconductor process integration expertise is genuinely hard to hire. This is the Samsara pattern -- a focused vertical play against broad-platform competitors where domain specificity creates an integration moat. But Samsara had capital to match its ambition. Two people and YC funding against competitors with combined $1 billion in the bank is a hard position, even with superior domain fit.

The bull case I'd need to believe: this is a "boring but critical" enterprise infrastructure play where the CEO's existing Intel/TSMC relationships convert to a first contract within 12 months, that contract validates the AI-driven approach, and the concentrated customer base (fewer than 10 major fabs globally) actually becomes an advantage -- one deal with TSMC could make the company. The product-to-distribution trajectory would follow: start with contract R&D for interconnect metals, then expand to thermal interface materials, then dielectrics, then become the embedded materials discovery platform these fabs can't function without. If Akash's roadmap relationships are real commercial relationships and not just academic citations, the path to that first contract is shorter than any competitor's. And if the agentic AI approach can compress discovery timelines from years to months even partially, the value proposition at $67-81 billion in semiconductor materials spend is enormous. That's the structural case, and it's a real one.

But I can't get past the execution gap between where they are today -- two people, no public product, no traction signals, no lab infrastructure -- and where they need to be to capture this opportunity before the mega-funded competitors pivot into their lane. The distribution story is underdeveloped beyond the CEO's existing relationships. The technology cycle positioning is early-to-correct rather than precisely timed -- the AI capability for materials discovery hasn't crossed its threshold yet, and being too early in a capital-intensive market with well-funded competitors is more dangerous than being too early in a pure-software market. I'd want to see a working product and at least one signed pilot with a major fab before committing capital. This is a company I'd track closely and revisit at seed.
