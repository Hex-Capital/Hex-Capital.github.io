# Fern (Formerly Ishiki Labs) -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Fern: the founders are quintessential insiders. Amit trained multimodal LLMs on Meta's LLaMA team and built video assistants for Ray-Ban smart glasses. Robert built the low-latency inference and orchestration stack underneath that same product for four years. They are not outsiders who don't know what the industry considers impossible -- they are the industry. They know exactly what Meta shipped, what Meta couldn't ship, and they're building the commercial version. This is the pattern I've warned about for decades: experienced engineers with "a set of biases that prevent you from making mistakes but also prevent you from inventing." When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a New Zealander with no aerospace connections. When I backed Pat Brown, he was a biochemist who didn't know what the food industry deemed impossible. Fern's founders know precisely what Meta deemed possible, and they're executing on it. That's not the same thing as invention.

The consequence-of-success question is where this evaluation gets decided, and it gets decided quickly. If Fern succeeds completely -- becomes the dominant real-time AI meeting participant -- what changes in the world? Sales reps get better support during calls. Follow-up emails draft themselves. Knowledge workers receive contextual prompts while talking. This is a productivity improvement to a $2.5-3.7 billion market. That's a nice business. But nobody's life is fundamentally altered. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. Animal agriculture doesn't get disrupted, fossil fuels don't get replaced, healthcare doesn't get reinvented. The consequence of success is that Zoom calls become more efficient. I've passed on hundreds of companies where the team was strong, the market was real, and the technology worked -- because the magnitude of what success delivers doesn't justify the effort of the best people spending their best years on it.

What concerns me more is the competitive topology. Otter.ai is at $100M ARR. Fireflies.ai has a $1 billion valuation. Read.ai raised $81M. Fathom grew revenue 90x in two years. This is not a category that hasn't been opened to venture capital -- it's one of the most crowded AI application markets in existence. And the platforms themselves are moving: Microsoft Copilot already provides real-time meeting intelligence inside Teams, Google is building Gemini into Meet. When incumbents and platform owners validate your approach rather than dismissing it, that's typically a signal that the innovation is incremental enough to be non-threatening. I want to hear Cisco's CTO say "we'll never do that" -- not see Microsoft already doing it.

Now, the strongest argument for Fern: "social awareness" in AI -- knowing when to speak, understanding conversational dynamics, distinguishing who's being addressed -- could be a foundational capability layer that extends far beyond meetings. If you squint, this is the wedge into every context where AI interacts with humans in real time: customer service, healthcare consultations, negotiations, education. The founders' Meta Reality Labs experience building exactly this for wearable devices is among the most directly relevant technical backgrounds I could imagine for this problem. Amit's 298 citations across CVPR and NeurIPS, combined with Robert's systems engineering on low-latency inference, means the team composition genuinely addresses the core technical risks. If "social awareness" becomes the critical differentiator between AI that humans tolerate and AI that humans trust, Fern could own that layer. That's a real thesis. But the company as described isn't pursuing it -- they're "starting with sales," which is the classic narrowing of ambition to reduce probability of failure. They've chosen the safe beachhead in a crowded market, which is precisely how you end up with consequences of success that are inconsequential.

The timing is favorable but obvious. Foundation models crossed the real-time latency threshold in 2024-2025 -- GPT-4o and Gemini Live proved sub-second voice interaction is technically feasible. Multimodal capabilities matured. Enterprise willingness to add AI to meetings is established. All true, but all visible to every AI startup and every platform company simultaneously. This isn't 2019 when I wrote the OpenAI check and AI was "laughable." This is a moment when every well-funded company in the space can see the same trajectory. Skating to where the puck will be requires being there before the crowd -- and the crowd has already arrived at AI meeting assistants.

I respect the technical depth here and I believe the founders can build the product they describe. But I invest in the magnitude of transformation, not the competence of execution. Fern is two talented Meta engineers making meetings incrementally smarter in a market with four well-funded competitors and platform owners building the same features natively. The technology works, the market exists, the team is qualified. And I stop right there -- because if this fully succeeds, the world still operates the same way, just with marginally better sales calls.
