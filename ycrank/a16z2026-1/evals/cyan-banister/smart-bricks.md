# Smart Bricks -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Smart Bricks self-reports a ~$12M revenue run rate on a $5M pre-seed raise for a company founded in July 2024, and not a single independent source — press, third-party database, investor announcement — corroborates that number. The metric's definition isn't even specified: is it gross transaction value flowing through the platform, platform fees collected, or actual recurring revenue? When I see a fundraising narrative that dramatically outpaces what I can verify, every survival instinct I've built fires at once. I've been burned by this before — IRL fabricated user metrics, told a beautiful story, and the building underneath was hollow. I'm not saying Mohamed Mohamed is doing that. I'm saying the absence of verification on such an extraordinary claim, combined with the fact that a16z Speedrun led a modest $5M round (if you're genuinely doing $12M run rate eighteen months after founding, your round should be much larger), creates a dissonance I can't resolve from the outside.

When I ask my first question — tell me Mohamed's story, not Smart Bricks' business — what I get back is Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey's AI practice, BCG, London Business School. That's a résumé, not a story. There's no biographical moment where real estate opacity personally cost him something, no evidence that this problem found him rather than the other way around. He observed the inefficiency from an advisory perch at institutions that profit from the very opacity Smart Bricks claims to disrupt. Forbes 30 Under 30, angel portfolio of 30+ startups, advisor to ADQ — these are credential markers that would impress most investors. They don't impress me the way a founder who taught themselves to code while navigating a broken system does. Mohamed is clearly accomplished and well-networked, and his institutional experience at Blackstone gives him genuine insight into how real assets are evaluated at scale. But the distance between "I saw the opportunity at Goldman" and "this problem broke me and I have to fix it" is the distance between career optimization and biographical inevitability.

The invisible system question is where Smart Bricks has its strongest case, and I want to be honest about that. Real estate is genuinely opaque — Mohamed's own words about "PDFs, WhatsApp threads and incomplete data" and investors who "lack visibility into opaque fee structures" describe a real extractive structure. Brokers extract commissions that buyers don't fully understand. Information asymmetry between institutional and individual investors is massive. This is the kind of hidden tax I recognize from my own experience with systems that exploit people who don't have the information to fight back. But then I look at who Smart Bricks actually serves: investors buying property across Dubai, London, New York, and Miami, with golden visa programs featured on the website. These are not vulnerable people being exploited by a system they can't navigate — these are high-net-worth individuals and institutional allocators who already have capital and access. The Affirm comparison is instructive: Max Levchin built transparent lending for consumers who were being buried by credit card opacity they couldn't see. Smart Bricks is building better analytics for people who are already wealthy enough to invest in cross-border real estate. The opacity is real; the power dynamic being disrupted is not.

AI-native real estate investing does not make anyone shift uncomfortably in their chair. When I backed Anduril, defense tech was politically toxic in Silicon Valley. When I backed Contraline, male birth control made VCs visibly squeamish. Smart Bricks operates in a space where every major accelerator — Google AI Accelerator, NVIDIA Inception, Microsoft GrowthX, a16z Speedrun, Techstars, 500 Global — is eagerly writing checks. EliseAI has raised $392M at a $2.2B valuation in an adjacent space. Roofstock raised $373M. Cherre raised over $100M. This is consensus capital flowing into a consensus category. The contrarian signal I look for — the 300 investors who passed on Uber, the years of rejection that prove a founder is building from mission rather than market — is entirely absent. Smart Bricks is swimming with the current, not against it, and the commoditization risk is real: LLM-based property analysis is technically reproducible, and a well-funded competitor like EliseAI could expand into the investment use case with resources that dwarf Smart Bricks' $5M raise.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if the proprietary data moat is real — 1M+ data feeds, 1,000+ variables per property, continuous learning from completed transactions — Smart Bricks could become the Bloomberg Terminal for real estate, infrastructure that every investor needs and no one can easily replicate. Mohamed's network (Blackstone alumni, ADQ advisory relationship, angel investors from OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepMind, Airbnb) could create distribution advantages that pure-play proptech competitors lack. And if the $12M run rate is legitimate, the product has already escaped the building in a way that demands attention. For this to work, the data advantage needs to be genuinely proprietary rather than a sophisticated aggregation of public feeds, the revenue needs to be real and recurring, and Mohamed needs to be the kind of operator who can build through the regulatory complexity of four distinct jurisdictions simultaneously as a sole founder. That's a lot of "needs to be" without verification.

Smart Bricks' decisive gap is at my entry point: Mohamed Mohamed's biography reads as a market opportunity identification, not a biographical crucible that made him incapable of working on anything else. The 8/25 on contrarian conviction reflects that no investor has said no to this thesis — the accelerator pipeline alone proves it's consensus. The 12/20 on invisible systems acknowledges real estate opacity is genuine while recognizing that the customer served is not the person most harmed by it. This is a competent team in a real market with an approach that requires no courage to articulate at a dinner party, and that's not a Long Journey bet.
