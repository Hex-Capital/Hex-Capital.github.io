# Alchemy -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Alchemy is a structural payment problem that reminds me why I've never invested in EdTech. Academic research labs -- the stated initial customer -- operate on grant funding with near-zero discretionary software budgets. The person who benefits from faster image analysis (the grad student or postdoc) is not the person who controls the budget (the PI, who answers to the grant agency). CellProfiler is free. QuPath is free. napari is free. ImageJ has been the default for decades and costs nothing. Convincing researchers to pay for analysis software when grant-funded alternatives exist is a distribution miracle on top of whatever product miracle is required. This is not the "too boring" market archetype where I've found returns -- PagerDuty and Gusto served companies with real operating budgets and a willingness to pay for reliability. This is a market where the payment structure itself is broken, which is one of my explicit avoidance criteria.

The "why now" is real but entirely consensus. Yes, vision-language models crossed a capability threshold in 2024-2025 that makes natural-language-to-pipeline assembly feasible for bioimage analysis. But every investor in the YC demo day audience knows this. AI applied to life sciences is one of the most crowded investment categories right now -- the Grand View Research report they'd cite to size the market is the same report twenty other startups are citing. When I invested in Harvey, legal AI looked like a science project to most people; the gap between perceived impossibility and actual capability trajectory was enormous. With bioimage AI, there is no perception gap -- everyone already sees the opportunity. A non-obvious market where I have information advantage is where the asymmetric returns live. A consensus market where the TAM slide comes from an analyst report is where returns go to die.

The commoditization risk here is severe and directly linked to the structural moment. The same foundation models that make Alchemy possible -- open-source segmentation models like Cellpose and StarDist, general-purpose vision-language models for pipeline assembly -- are available to anyone. The dossier correctly identifies that "any ML-capable team with life science domain knowledge could build similar pipeline-assembly tooling." When the enabling technology is commodity and open-source, the moat has to come from either distribution or domain-specific data accumulation. Alchemy has neither at this stage. Meanwhile, Leica/Danaher acquired DRVISION (now Aivia) back in 2021 -- the hardware incumbents are already building AI analysis capabilities and can bundle them with microscope sales, which is how labs actually buy things. An independent SaaS tool fighting against bundled-with-hardware distribution has a structural disadvantage, not a structural advantage.

The strongest bull case would require three things to be true simultaneously: (1) the productized UX advantage over free tools is large enough that labs will pay despite budget constraints, following the pattern of GitHub replacing self-hosted git or Slack replacing IRC; (2) the company accumulates enough domain-specific pipeline data to build a moat that general-purpose models cannot replicate; and (3) hardware vendors move slowly enough on AI integration that Alchemy captures meaningful market share before bundling kills standalone pricing. If all three hold, the expansion path -- from analysis tool to data management platform to collaboration layer to publication pipeline -- could create real product-to-distribution dynamics. A research lab's entire imaging workflow embedded in one platform would have genuine switching costs. But that's three sequential requirements, not one miracle. The single-miracle framework exists precisely to catch plans that look plausible in narrative form but stack low-probability events multiplicatively.

On the founding team: Baek has biomedical engineering training and medical device experience at Boston Scientific, which gives him adjacent domain credibility. McBride has strong CS credentials from UIUC with an early patent, covering the ML engineering side. But neither founder has documented experience in bioimage analysis software, computational microscopy, or research tool development -- the specific domain they're targeting. The pairing is sensible in broad strokes but lacks the kind of deep domain specificity I'd want to see. When I backed Color Health, the team had direct experience in the exact regulatory and lab operations challenges they were attacking. Founder-market fit here is approximate rather than precise, and in a market with entrenched open-source tools and sophisticated technical users, approximate domain knowledge slows the iteration cycle with customers who know their workflows intimately.

I'm passing. The market has a real workflow problem, but the structural dynamics work against this specific approach: consensus opportunity, commodity enabling technology, broken payment structure in the initial customer segment, hardware-bundled incumbent distribution, and a plan that requires multiple things to go right simultaneously. None of the signals that trigger my conviction -- a non-obvious inflection, a distribution wedge, a regulatory moat, a single identified miracle -- are present here.
