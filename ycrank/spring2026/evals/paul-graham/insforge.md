# InsForge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Hang Huang applied to Y Combinator six times before getting in. That fact is the first thing I'd want to talk about over dinner, because it cuts both ways and the direction it cuts tells you almost everything. Six applications is not normal persistence — it's the kind of behavior that either signals the "right kind of stubborn" or its evil twin, obstinacy. The distinction I draw in "The Right Kind of Stubborn" is whether the founder is attached to the goal (good) or attached to the approach (bad). InsForge had $300/mo in revenue at the fifth rejection. If Hang and Tony Chang spent those years iterating on the product, learning from each rejection, and fundamentally rethinking their approach, that's the Airbnb cereal box story in slow motion — founders who refuse to die. If they resubmitted the same pitch six times hoping for a different partner on interview day, that's a different diagnosis entirely. The dossier doesn't contain the detailed stories of creative workarounds I'd need to distinguish these cases. What I can see: they built 7,700 GitHub stars, 4,000+ databases, and a #1 Product Hunt launch while being told "no" five times by YC. That's building without external validation, which is real. But I'm scoring the stories I don't have as much as the traction I can see.

Tony Chang's résumé activates my "hackers building for hackers" pattern — Databricks networking infrastructure, Meta ads ML infrastructure, UIUC CS, 10 hackathon wins, 43 GitHub repos in Rust and Python. InsForge is developer infrastructure, and Tony has built developer infrastructure professionally. That's the kind of direct domain overlap I look for. If I were evaluating Tony alone, I'd see a young, technically sharp builder in the mold of the founders I tend to back — someone who could plausibly build whatever InsForge needs to become. Hang Huang brings Yale MBA, Amazon, and Riot Games experience but has no public GitHub presence. InsForge's semantic layer and MCP integration are software engineering challenges, not business strategy challenges. The CEO/CTO split works when the CEO has enough technical fluency to make product decisions at the speed agents demand. I can't evaluate that from a dossier, but the absence of any Hang-authored technical artifact is a data point.

InsForge's core problem — "AI agents can't operate backends" — is where my framework starts raising flags. Nobody hears that framing and says "that's crazy." In 2026, saying "we're making infrastructure AI-native" is like saying "we're making payments work on mobile" in 2012 — it's so obviously right that the consensus has already priced it in. InsForge hit #1 on Product Hunt with 639 comments, generated 1.5M+ X impressions during launch week, and trended #1 on GitHub. That's universal enthusiasm, not the confused skepticism that preceded Coinbase or the outright rejection that preceded Airbnb. When everyone agrees your idea is good, either you're too late or the returns have been competed away. The insight that agents need better backend interfaces is correct, but correctness isn't the same as defensibility. Supabase — $500M+ raised, $5B valuation, 80K+ GitHub stars — has already announced MCP support. InsForge's "semantic layer" is a software abstraction over Postgres, S3, and OAuth. That's a feature Supabase can ship in a quarter, not a moat that takes years to build. Stripe succeeded against incumbents because PCI compliance and bank integrations were a multi-year regulatory schlep. InsForge's "schlep" is making API documentation more parseable by LLMs. Those are categorically different barriers.

InsForge's origin story is conspicuously absent from the dossier. I see market framing — "AI coding agents struggle with backend operations" — but no moment of personal frustration. No "I was building an app with Cursor and spent three hours trying to get it to configure my Postgres." No "Tony was at Databricks watching engineers waste cycles on exactly this problem." The problem is described in the language of market research, not lived experience. When Drew Houston built Dropbox, the origin was a specific, personal irritation — he kept forgetting his USB drive. When the Octopart founders built their search engine, they were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic parts. InsForge's problem statement reads like it was derived from observing the AI agent trend and asking "what infrastructure do these agents need?" — which is exactly the method I warn against in "How to Get Startup Ideas." The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. The Databricks and Meta connections give Tony proximity to the problem domain, but proximity isn't the same as organic discovery.

The bull case for InsForge is genuine: the 6x YC persistence, Tony Chang's directly relevant infrastructure experience at Databricks, 7,700 GitHub stars with real community engagement (615 forks, 3,546 commits), and the timing thesis that AI agent adoption is accelerating at 48.1% CAGR. InsForge's database count doubled from 2,300 to 4,000+ in roughly two months through a building-in-public strategy — that's visible momentum. If the agent ecosystem fragments enough that no single BaaS incumbent captures the agent-native interface layer, InsForge's head start in context engineering and community could compound into a real position. An advocate would point to the Stripe parallel: a small team building developer infrastructure that incumbents (PayPal, banks) were too organizationally constrained to build. But the Stripe parallel actually undermines the case. Stripe's moat was regulatory complexity — years of PCI compliance work, bank relationships, money transmission licenses. InsForge's moat is a software abstraction layer. Supabase adding MCP support is not PayPal trying to become Stripe; it's a much smaller architectural change. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that InsForge has discovered a deep, structural reason why incumbent BaaS platforms *cannot* optimize for agents without fundamentally breaking their human-developer experience — a genuine architectural incompatibility, not just "they haven't prioritized it yet."

InsForge lands in Neutral territory because the company has one strong signal (founder persistence over six YC applications plus real open-source traction) layered on top of two significant weaknesses: the idea generates consensus enthusiasm rather than skepticism (7/20 on repulsion), and the origin story reads as market-derived rather than organically discovered (10/25 on organic discovery). The resourcefulness score (19/30) reflects genuine evidence of building through rejection, tempered by the absence of the specific creative-workaround stories that distinguish the best founders I've backed. Tony Chang's technical profile earns the "hackers building for hackers" nod, but InsForge is building a feature on top of commodity infrastructure in a market where the $5B incumbent is already converging on their differentiation. That's not the bet I make with my own money.
