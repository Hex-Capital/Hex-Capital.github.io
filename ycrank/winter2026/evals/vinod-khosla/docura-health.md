# Docura Health -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

California has approximately 1,900 active QMEs. That number stopped me cold. When I evaluate a company, my first question is always: if this succeeds completely -- total market domination, everything goes right -- does the world operate differently? Docura Health's maximum success scenario is that nearly two thousand physicians in California write their workers' comp reports faster. Expand nationally, expand to adjacent evaluation types, and you're still talking about a workflow improvement for a small population of specialists within a $673 million global medico-legal software market. The consequence of success here is a well-functioning vertical SaaS company. Not an industry transformation. Not a system replacement. A productivity tool for a niche within a niche.

I have nothing against niche markets as starting wedges -- Square started with a dongle for micro-merchants nobody else would serve. But Square's wedge opened into the entire small business financial stack, because there were tens of millions of unserved merchants and the technology created a capability that didn't exist before. Docura Health's wedge opens into... a slightly larger set of physicians writing slightly different medical-legal reports. The ceiling is visible from the ground floor. The broader workers' comp market is $56.7 billion, but Docura isn't disrupting workers' comp -- it's making one step in the process 5x faster. Medical services account for 60% of workers' comp costs, but that cost isn't driven by slow report writing; it's driven by medical treatment itself. Automating the report doesn't change the economic structure of the system.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: Docura is a wedge into becoming the AI infrastructure layer for all medical-legal proceedings, not just workers' comp evaluations. If the platform accumulates enough structured medico-legal data and builds deep compliance intelligence across state regulations, it becomes the operating system for how medical evidence enters legal proceedings -- disability claims, personal injury, malpractice. In that scenario, you're capturing value across a much larger surface. The claim that providers "doubled their case volume" is genuinely interesting as a unit economic signal -- if a physician can process twice the caseload, the ROI sells itself and word-of-mouth in a small professional community could drive rapid penetration. And the timing is real: LLMs crossed the reliability threshold for medical document comprehension in 2024-2025, and 60% of workers' comp professionals now rank automation as a priority. But this bull case requires the technology to be defensible, and the dossier itself acknowledges it isn't -- "the core AI technology (document summarization + structured generation) is not proprietary." Wisedocs already has $14.4 million in revenue, 116 employees, and training data from 100 million documents. They could build this feature in a quarter.

The founder is a UC Berkeley CS grad with relevant technical background -- his GitHub shows OCR and document-processing projects, which maps directly to the medical record extraction problem. He's an outsider to healthcare and law, which in my framework is not a negative. Pat Brown knew nothing about the food industry. Peter Beck had no aerospace pedigree. The outsider advantage is real. But the outsider advantage matters when the outsider is attempting something the industry considers impossible. Nobody in the workers' comp industry thinks AI report generation is impossible -- they're actively asking for it. This isn't contrarian; it's consensus. There's a meaningful difference between an outsider who sees what insiders refuse to see, and an outsider who builds a better tool for what insiders already want. Akhil is doing the latter. The solo founder dynamic in a regulated, relationship-driven sales environment with compliance complexity across fifty states adds execution risk that compounds the concern.

What I keep coming back to is calibration of ambition to problem scale. This is a smart person building a competent product for a real pain point. And I would tell him exactly what I tell every talented founder working on something too small: you're too capable to spend your prime building years optimizing report generation for two thousand physicians. The workers' comp system has genuine structural problems -- misaligned incentives between insurers and injured workers, fraudulent claims costing billions, return-to-work programs that don't work. Those are problems whose consequences of success would justify the effort. An AI tool that writes a first draft of a QME report in five minutes instead of three hours is useful. It is not consequential.
