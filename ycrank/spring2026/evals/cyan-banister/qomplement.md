# qomplement -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I want to know about any founder is how they got here -- not the market analysis, not the TAM slide, but the personal thread that makes this problem theirs. And with qomplement, that thread is thin. Kerim Taray went to Mercersburg Academy in Pennsylvania, studied CS at Tec de Monterrey, co-founded a nearshoring platform, worked at Contalink -- a YC S21 accounting fintech. That's a competent resume for a young founder. But when I ask myself "why is this person the one who's going to fight through the inevitable crises to make document filling work?" I don't get a clear answer. The connection is functional -- he saw document pain at an accounting startup -- not biographical. Andres Garza Garcia is even more opaque: first venture, no public history, no narrative I can latch onto. These seem like smart engineers who spotted a gap, and that's fine, but it's not the signal that makes me write first checks. I backed SpaceX because Elon's intensity made the impossible feel inevitable. I invested in Contraline because the founder had spent years in academic research that everyone else ignored. Here I'm looking for that biographical commitment and I'm not finding it.

What does catch my attention is the pivot itself -- narrowing from a general-purpose computer-using agent down to filling out PDF forms and spreadsheets. In a world where every AI company is racing toward the biggest, most general-purpose vision, these founders went smaller and more specific. That takes a certain kind of discipline. Document filling is genuinely unglamorous. No investor gets excited telling their LPs they backed the company that populates cells in Excel templates. And the "reverse" positioning -- everyone does extraction, nobody does filling -- occupies a less crowded niche. It's not the kind of thing that makes me feel a jolt of energy, the way Niantic or Crusoe did, but it has a quiet contrarian quality. The local-first architecture, where data never leaves the user's machine, pushes slightly further against consensus in a cloud-everything world.

The strongest signal for me is the economic access story. Kerim's quote about wanting "AI accessible for Latin American companies without system replacements or disproportionate costs" hits directly on something I care about. I invested in Uber because it created income for drivers who had no other flexible work option. I backed Postmates and Affirm because they opened up economic participation for people who were locked out. qomplement is doing something adjacent -- bringing AI document automation to mid-market Latin American enterprises that can't afford Nanonets or Rossum, running 15x more economically than cloud alternatives on hardware these companies already own. The transport client automating 10,000+ invoices per month and saving $15,000 monthly is a real proof point. That's the kind of impact I look for -- making an expensive, opaque capability transparent and accessible to people who couldn't reach it before.

But here's where I get stuck: is this a company or a feature? Nanonets is at $100M in revenue. Rossum has $104.6M raised. Reducto just closed a $75M Series B. These are well-funded extraction platforms that already process the same document types qomplement targets. Adding a "fill" capability downstream is an incremental feature for any of them, not a company-building effort. The local-first deployment model creates some differentiation, but it also creates real support headaches at scale -- asking enterprise IT teams to provision 8-16 GB of RAM on individual machines is swimming upstream against the managed-cloud tide. The bull case would require me to believe that (a) document filling is genuinely hard enough to sustain an independent company, (b) the local-first architecture creates a durable compliance moat in regulated industries, and (c) the Latin American enterprise market is large enough and underserved enough to build a real business before extraction vendors notice the niche. For this to be great, the founders would need to demonstrate the kind of relentless conviction that keeps them building even when Microsoft or UiPath extends into their lane. I haven't seen enough to believe that yet.

The co-founder dynamic gives me neither alarm bells nor reassurance. Both graduated from the same university in the same timeframe -- shared context is good, but I've learned from HQ Trivia that what matters is how founders fight, not how they met. With a four-person team at pre-seed, there's simply no evidence in either direction. I don't see the red flags that would make me walk away, but I also don't see the kind of tested resilience -- a crisis survived, a hard pivot navigated together, a failed prior venture that forged their partnership -- that gives me real confidence for the long journey ahead.

I'm passing on this one. The economic access angle for Latin American enterprises is genuinely compelling, and the contrarian move of going narrow when everyone else goes broad shows some intellectual independence. But my primary filter is the founder's story, and I can't find enough biographical depth to build conviction around. These are young, capable engineers solving a real problem, but I don't feel the biographical inevitability that tells me they'll persist through the hard years. The competitive moat is thin, the pivot is recent, and the defensibility story depends on workflow stickiness that hasn't been demonstrated at scale. If Kerim sat across from me and told me a story that connected his life to this problem in a way I couldn't ignore -- the kind of story that makes me forget the competitive landscape for a moment -- I might feel differently. But based on what I can see, this is a solid company that doesn't fit my particular way of investing.
