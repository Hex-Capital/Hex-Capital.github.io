# Interfaze -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The thing that jumps out about Interfaze is the gap between the ambition of their published research and the reality of their traction. They have an IEEE CAI 2026 paper arguing that the future of AI is task-specific small models orchestrated by a controller layer — which is a genuinely interesting architectural thesis. But their top GitHub repo has 25 stars. Firecrawl, which covers just one of their several claimed capabilities (scraping), has 50,000. That ratio tells you something important: the intellectual argument may be sound, but the product hasn't yet created the kind of organic pull that distinguishes real demand from plausible-sounding infrastructure. As I've said, "startups take off because the founders make them take off" — and 10 million API requests over nine months of beta is usage, but it's not takeoff.

The CEO, Yoeven Khemlani, has a prior exit that deserves credit — Stayr grew from $1M to $3M ARR before selling. That's not nothing. It proves he can build something people will pay for and see it through. But it's a proptech company in Singapore, not developer infrastructure for AI. The question I'd be asking in the interview is whether he's building Interfaze because he lived inside this problem — because he personally hit the wall of integrating five different AI APIs and thought "this is insane" — or because he saw an opportunity in the market from the outside. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise." The CTO, Khurdula, brings genuine ML research credentials from Purdue, and the IEEE publication is a real differentiator at pre-seed. But I see no evidence of prior shared work between these two founders before JigsawStack. No common employer, no common university, no indication of how they met or how long they've worked together. "Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." That's an open question here, not a red flag, but it's an open question I'd want answered.

I keep testing Interfaze against the "schlep blindness" framework: is consolidating OCR, STT, classification, and scraping into a single API the kind of painful, unglamorous problem that everyone else unconsciously avoids? Stripe worked because nobody wanted to deal with banks and PCI compliance and the regulatory mess of moving money — "it's a lot more intimidating to start a startup like this than a recipe site." Interfaze's schlep is different: it's maintaining multiple specialized models simultaneously, keeping them performant, and making the routing layer smart enough to be invisible. That's genuinely hard engineering. But it's not the kind of structural barrier that kept a Stripe-shaped hole in the market for a decade. The individual components — OCR engines, speech-to-text models, web scrapers — are available as point solutions and increasingly as features within frontier LLMs. The integration value is real but the moat is thin. OpenAI-compatible endpoints are clever distribution — developers can swap in Interfaze with minimal code changes — but that sword cuts both ways. Easy in, easy out.

The strongest bull case: if small model architectures prove structurally superior for deterministic tasks — more consistent, cheaper, faster — then Interfaze is building the right thing at the right moment. The "why now" is legitimate: small language models reached production quality in 2024-2025, MCP and tool-use protocols created standardized interfaces, and frontier LLM pricing remains high for tasks that don't need general intelligence. If you buy that thesis, Interfaze could become what I'd call a "narrow deep well" — it doesn't need to be everything to everyone, it just needs to be dramatically better for developers who need deterministic outputs. The $1.50/$3.50 per million token pricing is aggressive compared to frontier models. And 10M API requests suggests someone is using this for real work. For this to be a great investment, you'd need to believe that (a) deterministic task routing is a durable architectural advantage rather than a feature frontier models will absorb, and (b) this team can build developer community fast enough to establish switching costs before Firecrawl expands into adjacent capabilities. I'm not convinced on either count, but I can see the path.

The brand confusion between JigsawStack and Interfaze bothers me more than it probably should. The email is interfaze@jigsawstack.com. The GitHub org is still jigsawstack. The SDKs carry the old name. This isn't a dealbreaker — "most successful startups end up doing something different than they originally intended" — but sloppy execution on something this visible makes me wonder about attention to detail elsewhere. Developer tools companies live and die on trust and polish. When a developer sees mismatched branding across your docs, your GitHub, and your email domain, it creates a subtle erosion of confidence that compounds.

Net: competent founders building a reasonable product in an enormous market, but I'm not seeing the asymmetric signal that makes me want to write a check. The idea doesn't have the "looks like a bad idea but is actually good" quality that characterizes the best early-stage bets. It looks like a sensible idea — which means it looks sensible to everyone, including Firecrawl's team and the frontier model providers who are steadily adding multimodal capabilities. "It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors" — but that's when you have something users love so much that competitors can't replicate the pull. I'd need to see evidence of that love, and I don't yet.
