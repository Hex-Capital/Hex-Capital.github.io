# RamAIn -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells me everything I need to know about the market structure. Simular has $27M and a Microsoft partnership with an ex-DeepMind team building full desktop automation. Browser Use raised $17M from Felicis for open-source browser agents. Anthropic and OpenAI ship first-party computer-use APIs. And then there's RamAIn -- a two-person team of IIT Delhi students with no revenue, no customers, and pricing listed as "Free." The question isn't whether AI-powered RPA is a real market. It obviously is. The question is whether this is a non-obvious market at a structural inflection, or whether it's the most consensus bet in enterprise AI right now. It's the latter. When I look at the dossier and see four funded competitors, two foundation model providers building competing capabilities, and a $35 billion RPA TAM slide -- that's the opposite of the pattern that has generated my best returns. PagerDuty succeeded because operations alerting seemed boring and niche. This market has every investor in Silicon Valley excited about it simultaneously.

The pre-training approach -- teaching agents UI policies in advance rather than reasoning from screenshots at runtime -- is an architecturally interesting choice. If it delivers materially better speed and reliability, accumulated interface knowledge could create a data advantage over time. That's the bull case: RamAIn builds a compounding library of pre-trained interface models that become increasingly difficult to replicate, similar to how Google Maps' ground truth data accumulated into an unassailable lead. The CTO's credentials support this possibility -- an ICLR 2025 publication, a nearest-neighbor algorithm integrated into Meta's FAISS library, and time at CMU's ML department. These are real technical chops, not resume padding. If the pre-training technique works at production scale, the company would need to build faster than both the foundation model providers (who could add interface-specific optimization natively) and well-capitalized competitors (who could adopt the same architectural approach). That's a plausible but narrow path.

The problem is this company requires multiple miracles, not one. First miracle: the pre-training approach must deliver a step-function improvement in speed and reliability over runtime VLM inference -- and there's no public benchmark or case study validating this claim. Second miracle: they need to sell into regulated enterprise verticals (healthcare, insurance, finance) with a two-person team and no visible sales infrastructure. Third miracle: they need to do both before Anthropic or OpenAI adds pre-training as a native feature of their computer-use APIs, which would collapse RamAIn's middleware layer overnight. When I see three sequential breakthroughs required, I see compounding low-probability events.

The distribution story is particularly thin. The target vertical list -- procurement, insurance, healthcare, revenue cycle management, finance, retail, pharmacy, logistics -- reads like a brainstorm of every possible enterprise buyer, not a focused go-to-market wedge. The strongest enterprise AI companies I've backed chose one vertical, dominated it, and expanded from there. Harvey started with elite law firms. Stripe started with developer-first payments. RamAIn lists eight verticals with zero customers, zero revenue, and no evidence of a distribution strategy beyond "deploy in days." The product is free. There's no signal that any enterprise buyer has validated the approach, even informally. At pre-seed I don't need revenue, but I need evidence that someone outside the founding team believes this works -- a signed LOI, a design partner, a named pilot. Nothing here.

The founders are technically capable -- Vansh Ramani's ML research profile is genuinely strong for a pre-seed CTO, and Shourya Vir Jain bootstrapping Genoshi to six-figure revenue shows he can build something customers pay for. But both are still undergraduates at IIT Delhi, and neither has operated in the enterprise environments they're targeting. Selling into healthcare procurement or insurance workflows requires understanding compliance requirements, vendor evaluation processes, and integration with legacy systems that you can only learn by working inside those organizations or selling to them repeatedly. The founder-market fit is strong on the technical dimension and weak on the enterprise go-to-market dimension -- which matters because the technical problem may be the easier half.

I'd need to see the pre-training approach validated with at least one production deployment, a focused vertical strategy rather than eight simultaneous targets, and evidence that the speed advantage is durable against foundation model providers adding similar capabilities. Without those signals, this is a technically interesting team in a consensus-hot market facing well-funded competition and platform risk from their own infrastructure providers. That's not my kind of bet.
