# Foaster -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what I keep coming back to: "AI replaces management consulting" might be the single most popular idea in Silicon Valley right now. Every pitch competition, every demo day, every AI fund thesis deck -- automating McKinsey is in there somewhere. And that's the problem for me. The most promising companies I've backed were the ones nobody else wanted to touch. When I wrote that first check to SpaceX, people thought private rocketry was lunacy. When we found Crusoe Energy, people thought mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota was a punchline. Those companies were unpopular. They were difficult to assess. Foaster is neither. It's a clearly articulated, well-understood value proposition in a category that already has $202 million competitors (Distyl AI), $101 million competitors (Aily Labs), and hundreds of millions more flowing into adjacent plays. The consensus has already formed here, and that means the price of being right is high and the edge from being early is gone.

If I could sit down with Raphael and Alexandre, the first thing I'd ask is: tell me your story. Not the business -- how did you get here? And what I see in the dossier is genuinely impressive resourcefulness. Raphael built a 245,000-follower Nadal fan account at age 11 and parlayed that into a real role at the Nadal Academy by 18. That's the kind of hustle I notice -- a kid who figures out how to turn obsessive passion into something real. Alexandre built an AI system that handled 60,000 customer calls for French restaurants, which maps directly to what Foaster's agents need to do. And their LLM benchmark research on social intelligence -- the Werewolf paper that OpenAI's president shared -- shows they think about how AI agents interact with humans in ways that go beyond surface-level prompt engineering. These are clearly talented, ambitious young people. But what I don't see is the biographical thread that connects them to the consulting problem they're solving. They're 23-year-old statistics students at an elite French university. When did they sit through a broken consulting engagement? When did they watch a company hemorrhage money on McKinsey analysts who interviewed 5% of the organization and called it insights? The founder's story and the company's mission need to be inseparable for me, and I can't find the seam where these founders' lives made this particular problem impossible for them to ignore.

The economic access question is where this really falls outside my framework. When I look at my best investments, there's a thread: Uber created income for hundreds of thousands of drivers. Postmates put money in delivery workers' pockets. Affirm gave people transparent access to credit. Thumbtack connected skilled tradespeople with customers who needed them. These companies created economic opportunity for people who didn't have it before. Foaster saves enterprises 3-5x on consulting fees. That's a perfectly valid business proposition, but it doesn't create access for anyone who was locked out of something. If anything, it accelerates the displacement of junior consulting analysts -- the exact opposite of the positive-sum economic dynamic I look for. Making an organization's operational reality visible to its leadership is interesting, and there's a faint echo of what Carta did for cap table transparency. But Carta made equity visible to employees and founders who were being kept in the dark. Foaster's transparency flows upward to management, not outward to the people who need it.

Let me give the bull case its due, because there is one. Alexandre Combes has actually done the hard thing already -- building AI that talks to humans at scale across 60,000 calls. That's not a slide deck, it's operational experience. The innovator's dilemma argument is real: McKinsey literally cannot offer a 3-5x cheaper service without destroying its own leverage model. The operational map as a persistent data asset could create genuine switching costs. And the "why now" timing -- frontier LLMs finally being capable of conducting nuanced qualitative interviews -- is legitimate. If these founders can land a few transformative case studies with mid-market companies through YC's network, the compounding effect of those proprietary organizational maps could build something defensible. The right version of this company -- the one where they've run a hundred engagements and their reasoning layer has learned things about organizational dysfunction that no competitor can replicate -- could be genuinely formidable. But the path from here to there runs straight through a field of well-funded competitors who are already further along, and I don't see the biographical conviction that would sustain these founders through the years of enterprise sales cycles and competitive pressure that path requires.

I'm passing. Not because the founders aren't talented -- they clearly are -- but because this company sits at the intersection of several things I actively avoid: a consensus category, no biographical connection between founders and problem, no economic access thesis, and a competitive landscape where being pre-seed against $200 million competitors requires the kind of founder obsession that can only come from personal pain. I'd be happy to be wrong about this one, and if Raphael told me a story I haven't heard yet about why consulting is personal for him, I'd listen. But on what I can see, this isn't my kind of bet.
