# Corelayer -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Corelayer: this is a company entering one of the hottest categories in enterprise software right now, and that immediately cools my interest. incident.io just raised $62 million in April 2025 explicitly to build AI agents that resolve incidents. Shoreline.io was acquired by Nvidia. BigPanda got acquired. FireHydrant got absorbed by Freshworks. When there's that much capital and consolidation activity in a space, the weirdness has been priced out. The most promising companies are not popular -- and AI-for-incident-response is very popular right now. This feels like the kind of deal where every infrastructure-focused VC in town is already circling the category, which means the insight advantage has been arbitraged away. I'm looking for founders who've arrived at something the rest of the market considers ridiculous, and "AI should help debug production systems" is not a statement anyone finds ridiculous in 2026.

When I think about the founders, I see impressive professional credentials but I'm missing the story I always listen for first. Mitch went from Princeton astrophysics to Goldman Sachs data infrastructure. Shipra went from Oracle cloud to Goldman Sachs. They've clearly lived the problem -- debugging systems processing hundreds of billions of rows daily is real operational pain, and I don't doubt they understand the technical surface area deeply. But Princeton to Goldman to YC is one of the most well-trodden paths in Silicon Valley. I don't see the biographical thread that tells me these two are solving something that's burned into who they are at a personal level. The best founders I've backed -- whether it was Travis at Uber or the Crusoe team in North Dakota -- had stories where you could feel the inevitability of them solving this particular problem. Here I see competent engineers who noticed an efficiency gap at their employer and decided to productize it. That's a perfectly valid origin story for a B2B company, but it doesn't give me the conviction signal I build my portfolio around.

The bull case is real, and I want to be honest about it. If you're an enterprise software investor who cares about founder-market fit and defensible compliance moats, this company has genuine strengths. They already have SOC 2 Type I certification as a two-person team, which shows unusual operational maturity. The confidential compute and on-prem deployment options for regulated industries are not trivial to replicate -- I know from my IronPort days how much work goes into building security infrastructure that passes muster at financial institutions. The incumbents face legitimate business model cannibalization: PagerDuty and Datadog profit from the complexity that Corelayer aims to eliminate, which means they'll hesitate to build this themselves even though they could. And the Goldman Sachs network gives these founders a direct sales channel into exactly the buyer they need. If they land two or three Fortune 100 financial institutions in the next eighteen months, the integration depth and compliance switching costs could create a real moat. A different investor with a different framework could make a compelling case here.

But it's not my framework. This company doesn't create economic access for people who lack it -- it saves Fortune 100 firms money on their $200K-a-year engineers' time. That's efficiency optimization for the already well-resourced, not the kind of positive-sum economic outcome I look for. It's not making an invisible system visible to people who were previously locked out. And the regulated-industry focus, while genuinely complex and something I respect from my security background, is being pursued by founders who are industry insiders navigating familiar terrain rather than outsiders bringing a perspective that incumbents can't see. The compliance work is the strongest dimension here for my framework, but it's not enough to overcome the consensus positioning and the absence of the biographical grit signal that drives my highest-conviction bets.
