# Carrot Labs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here is the first and most important thing to see. Braintrust has raised $121 million. Arize has raised $131 million. LangChain has raised $260 million. Langfuse was just acquired by ClickHouse. DeepEval has 13,000 GitHub stars. And now Carrot Labs -- two founders, standard YC funding -- proposes to enter this same arena with a variation on the same thesis everyone already shares: that AI agents in production need better evaluation and optimization tooling. This is not a market with a secret hiding in it. This is a market where a half-dozen well-capitalized companies have already converged on the same insight, which means the insight is not an insight at all -- it is consensus. When every intelligent person in Silicon Valley agrees that "AI agents need better tooling," the correct conclusion is not that this is a great market to enter. The correct conclusion is that whatever profits exist in this market will be competed away by companies imitating each other into mutual destruction.

The question I always ask is: what important truth does this company see that others do not? Carrot Labs' implicit claim is that evaluation alone is insufficient -- you need closed-loop continuous optimization and fine-tuning. But this is not a contrarian position. Braintrust's $80 million Series B explicitly cites "new infrastructure products for production-grade AI." Every observability vendor is adding optimization capabilities. The "continuous learning" framing sounds distinctive in a pitch deck, but the underlying technical work -- prompt refinement, retrieval tuning, fine-tuning via foundation model APIs -- is accessible to any competitor with engineering resources. There is no proprietary algorithm here that I can identify, no fundamental technical breakthrough, no capability that did not previously exist. The fine-tuning APIs that make this product possible are owned by OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. Carrot Labs is building an integration layer on top of infrastructure controlled by others -- a position that invites commoditization from above (the model providers add it natively) and from the side (better-funded competitors add the same features).

The strongest bull case would require believing that Carrot Labs' specific implementation of the optimization loop creates compounding data advantages -- that each customer's proprietary workflow data makes the platform increasingly irreplaceable, generating switching costs that accumulate faster than competitors can replicate them. The YC page gestures at this: "capturing your proprietary know-how in the model so it gets more valuable and harder to copy as you grow." If this flywheel is real and operational, it could create per-customer lock-in analogous to Palantir's government integrations, where each year of deployment deepens the moat. But there is no evidence this flywheel exists beyond marketing copy. No customer counts, no retention data, no case studies with named companies. The claimed performance metrics -- 43% latency reduction, 18% quality improvement -- are unverified website assertions. And crucially, even if the per-customer flywheel works, it does not create cross-customer network effects. Each deployment is siloed. A competitor offering the same closed-loop optimization to a new prospect starts from the same baseline. This is not Facebook, where the value of the network to each user increases with every additional user. This is consulting-like customization dressed in platform language.

The founders present a mixed signal. Yuta Baba brings relevant experience -- senior data scientist at Snowflake working on forecasting algorithms, which maps to the ML optimization core of the product. His prior founding of CareVo demonstrates entrepreneurial willingness, though the domain pivot from Japanese senior care to AI agent optimization is jarring. I do not see evidence of the obsessive, years-long focus on a specific technical problem that characterizes the founders I back most aggressively. Christopher Acker's professional background is essentially undocumented publicly, which is concerning for a company selling deeply technical optimization tooling to engineering teams. Neither founder has visible public GitHub contributions, technical publications, or evidence of the kind of intellectual frameworks that would suggest they see the world differently from the dozens of other teams building AI developer tools. When I backed the Collison brothers at Stripe, they had been writing code since childhood and had already sold a company. The technical credibility was self-evident. Here, it is not.

The market entry strategy compounds my concern. Carrot Labs targets "production-grade agent deployments" broadly -- any AI-powered SaaS product, internal agent, workflow automation system, or tool-using copilot. This is horizontal positioning against a massive, generic market from day one. Where is the Harvard? Where is the eBay power seller? A two-person company cannot out-execute Braintrust's team and $121 million in capital across every use case simultaneously. The path to survival -- let alone monopoly -- requires identifying a specific, narrow segment where the closed-loop optimization approach delivers such dramatically superior results that those customers cannot imagine using anything else. I see no evidence of that segmentation. The sales-led GTM via a Calendly booking link suggests founder-led selling, which is appropriate at pre-seed, but without a defined beachhead, those sales conversations will be scattered across industries and use cases, generating breadth without depth.

This is a pass. Not because the founders are incompetent or the market is unreal -- AI agent optimization is a genuine need that will generate real revenue for someone. But "a genuine need that will generate real revenue for someone" is the definition of a competitive market, not a monopoly opportunity. The secret is missing. The technology is integrative rather than discontinuous. The competitive dynamics are Girardian in the worst sense -- multiple well-funded companies converging on identical positioning, each imitating the others' feature sets. If I am wrong, it will be because the continuous fine-tuning flywheel creates deeper per-customer lock-in than I expect, and because Carrot Labs finds a specific vertical wedge that their larger competitors overlook. But that is a hope, not a thesis, and I do not invest in hopes.
