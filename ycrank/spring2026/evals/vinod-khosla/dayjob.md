# Dayjob -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

$450K ARR in seven months selling AI scheduling to skip hire operators is legitimately impressive execution — I want to acknowledge that upfront because what follows is not a critique of competence. Dayjob has found real customers (Allstone, Coastal Recycling) paying real money to solve a real problem. Marc Williamson at Coastal Recycling reports a 5-10% increase in jobs per driver hour. That's a genuine efficiency gain. But here's where my framework diverges from most investors: I don't start with "is this a good business?" I start with "what happens to the world if Dayjob fully succeeds?" If Dayjob captures every skip hire operator in the UK, then Europe, then every waste management fleet globally — the consequence is that waste trucks run slightly more efficient routes. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The world looks the same, with marginally better logistics. That's the ceiling, and it's the ceiling that determines whether I write a check.

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching the grocery business, which means he understands fleet scheduling and last-mile operations at scale. Fred Fooks brings Oxford Engineering Science and Deloitte supply chain analytics. The pivot from Gaea — their earlier waste management ERP that generated $150K in revenue — to Dayjob's scheduling agent shows genuine learning. They spent eighteen months inside waste operations, identified that scheduling was the acute pain point, and rebuilt around it. That's a real signal of adaptation. But Dayjob's founders are domain operators executing well within a known problem space, not outsider thinkers attempting something the industry says is impossible. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was building electric turbopumps and 3D-printed combustion chambers in New Zealand — a non-linear technical approach that made the entire small-sat launch category possible. Dayjob is applying existing AI capabilities to route optimization, a problem that Routific, AMCS, OptimoRoute, and FarEye have all been working on for years. The dossier itself states the core AI scheduling technology "is not inherently defensible." That's not a wedge — that's execution speed, and execution speed without a technology discontinuity puts you in a race you eventually lose to the incumbent with $225M in funding.

The bull case someone would make to me: Dayjob's ERP integration strategy — plugging into Weighsoft, PurGo, and Wastelogics rather than replacing them — is a smart distribution wedge that AMCS can't easily replicate because unbundling route optimization would cannibalize AMCS's platform lock-in. The VWS partnership formalized in January 2026 gives Dayjob channel access to PurGo's entire installed base. And skip hire is just the entry point — Dayjob could expand to general waste collection, recycling fleets, bulk haulage, and eventually all industrial logistics. The DoorDash exception in my framework shows I'll override the technical novelty requirement when consequence magnitude is enormous and the founder shows extraordinary learning velocity. Could Dayjob be a DoorDash-style exception? No. DoorDash's consequence was transforming food access for hundreds of millions of people — a planetary-scale behavior change. Dayjob's expansion path leads to better fleet scheduling across industrial verticals, which is valuable but not transformative. The DoorDash exception requires consequence magnitude that justifies overriding every other criterion, and Dayjob doesn't clear that bar.

Dayjob's claimed metrics — 11% annual revenue boost, 66% fewer late deliveries, 25% less admin time — are exactly the kind of numbers I see in every vertical AI copilot pitch. A 5-10% efficiency gain per driver hour is a 2x improvement at best if you're generous, not a 10x step function. The route optimization software market at $8.5B growing to $21.5B by 2030 is real, but Dayjob is competing for share of existing operator budgets, not creating new demand. There is no path to radical affordability here — waste collection doesn't become ten times cheaper because routes are better optimized. Operators become slightly more profitable, which is fine, but making waste operators' margins better is not making an expensive necessity free for anyone.

The one condition that would flip me to Invest: if Dayjob demonstrated that its scheduling agent could autonomously run an entire fleet operation — not optimize routes for human planners, but replace the planning function entirely and expand to replace dispatch, driver management, and customer communication — creating a path where a single operator could run a fleet that currently requires a team of ten. That would be a consequence worth betting on. As it stands, Dayjob is a well-executed vertical SaaS company designed to generate 3-5x returns, which in my framework is a competent business but not a bet I make with my own money. I want to either lose my capital or make fifty times my capital. Dayjob is engineered for the middle, and the middle is where I don't play. The two dimensions that lock this verdict: consequence magnitude (skip hire scheduling efficiency does not change how millions live) and non-linear technical approach (applying existing AI to route optimization is incremental, not discontinuous).
