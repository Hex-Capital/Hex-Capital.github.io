# smol machines -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

BinBin He worked on Firecracker at AWS — the exact microVM technology that Lambda and Fargate run on — and then left to build smol machines, a desktop-native version of the thing his former employer structurally cannot ship. AWS built Firecracker for Linux/KVM cloud multi-tenancy; extending it to macOS Hypervisor.framework for developer laptops would conflict with Firecracker's core mission. Docker shipping full hypervisor isolation would undermine the container abstraction its $393M+ ecosystem depends on. So there's a real structural gap here, and BinBin is one of maybe a few hundred people on Earth with the systems-level expertise to fill it. Sub-200ms cold boot — 225x faster than QEMU — is a genuine technical achievement built on libkrun, and the timing with AI coding agents (Claude Code, Cursor, Codex) needing sandboxed execution environments gives smol machines a demand curve that didn't exist two years ago. I want to be honest about what's working before I explain why I'm not writing a check.

BinBin He's biography reads as professional expertise, not biographical inevitability. Ex-AWS containers, Illinois Institute of Technology, FIRST Robotics mentor, prolific Rust/systems open-source contributor with ERA (303 GitHub stars) and envmap (125 stars). That's a strong engineering resume. But when I ask my first question — tell me his story, not the business — what I hear is "I was at AWS and saw the opportunity to bring microVMs to developer desktops." That's career optimization dressed as a startup. I don't see evidence that the "works on my machine" problem found BinBin through personal pain, that he'd be working on smol machines without funding, or that his life story made him incapable of ignoring this problem. Fu Qiao, the co-founder listed on smol machines' YC page, has essentially no public biographical footprint — no prior employment, no education, no documented technical contributions. When half the founding team is invisible to me, I can't evaluate what I can't see. The YC page lists team size as 1 despite two founders, which raises its own questions about commitment and role clarity.

2,500 GitHub stars and 97 forks in smol machines' early days demonstrate real developer pull — that's not nothing. The `.smolmachine` artifact format could create switching costs if adoption scales, and the structural reasons Docker and AWS can't easily replicate smol machines' specific approach are legitimate. But libkrun, the core VMM, is open-source and maintained by Red Hat's containers organization. BinBin built a polished CLI on top of someone else's foundational layer, and any team with deep systems programming expertise could do the same. Compare this to Crusoe Energy, where the founders built portable data centers to capture waste gas — physical infrastructure with compounding network effects in specific geographies. Smol machines' defensibility is UX polish and a packaging format, not infrastructure that gets harder to displace with each customer. The AI agent sandboxing inflection is real and it's the strongest argument for smol machines' timing, but the moat remains thin.

Here's what I keep coming back to: the "works on my machine" problem is one of the most transparent, well-documented problems in all of software engineering. Every developer knows exactly what it is, exactly what causes it, and exactly what their options are — Docker, VMs, containers, Nix. There's no hidden tax being extracted from people who don't understand the system. There's no information asymmetry, no opaque structure, no gatekeepers hoarding access. When I invested in Affirm, the thesis was that credit card companies were hiding the true cost of borrowing behind deliberately opaque terms — that's an invisible system extracting value from vulnerable people. When I backed Ownwell, property tax assessments were a black box exploiting homeowners who didn't know they could appeal. Smol machines is an engineering improvement in a market where the problems and tradeoffs are already fully legible. My portfolio conspicuously lacks investments in well-served developer tooling categories for exactly this reason — I need a hidden structure to disrupt, and dependency management doesn't have one.

The bull case an advocate would make: smol machines sits at the convergence of a timing inflection (AI agents needing sandboxed execution) and a structural gap (no incumbent can serve the macOS developer desktop with hypervisor-grade isolation). BinBin He has exact domain expertise from building Firecracker at AWS. The "Electron for VMs" analogy — ship an entire VM with the software the way Electron ships an entire browser with the web app — could redefine how desktop and CLI software is distributed. If AI agents become the primary way code is written and executed, smol machines could become invisible infrastructure beneath every coding tool. I take this seriously. But I've watched three investments fail — HQ Trivia, GameCrush, IRL — because surface-level product appeal masked the absence of structural defensibility. Smol machines has real technical substance that those didn't, but it shares the vulnerability: a well-funded competitor (Docker launching VM mode, Apple adding native VM management to Xcode, or AWS open-sourcing a desktop Firecracker variant) could replicate the core value proposition because the underlying VMM is not proprietary. The condition that would flip me: if BinBin He showed me a personal story that makes this problem his life's work rather than his career's next chapter, and if smol machines had a proprietary data or infrastructure layer that compounds beyond the open-source CLI — a registry, a distribution network, enterprise workflow lock-in — I'd look again.

Smol machines scores highest on infrastructure timing — the AI agent sandbox demand is genuine, the incumbent structural barriers are real, and 2,500 GitHub stars validate developer interest. It scores lowest on biographical forge and invisible system disruption, which are my two highest-weighted criteria and together account for 50 of 100 points in my framework. A competent systems engineer building a faster VM in a transparent market with an open-source dependency on someone else's VMM — that's a real product, but it's not a Long Journey bet. I wish BinBin well, but my money follows founders whose stories told me they had no choice.
