# Korso -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first: five funded startups are already building AI quoting tools for manufacturers. Paperless Parts has $30M and an established customer base. Uptool just raised $6M from Khosla, Eclipse, Bessemer, and Kleiner Perkins — that's not a whisper, that's a shout. Gendra, Mercura AI, Endeavor AI are all in the mix. And the underlying technology — RAG over documents, LLM extraction from PDFs and emails — is available to anyone with an API key. When I invested in SpaceX, nobody else would touch private rockets. When I backed Flock Safety, most VCs wouldn't go near surveillance tech. Those were spaces where being the first check meant something structural. AI for manufacturing quoting is a space where being the first check means you're in a footrace with half a dozen well-capitalized teams, and the track is made of commodity infrastructure. Nobody at a dinner party shifts uncomfortably when you say "we're automating RFQ processing." They nod and ask about your GTM.

When I sit with founders, my first question is always about their story — not the business, the person. What happened to you that made this problem yours? With Korso, what I see are three talented UPenn engineering students: Daichi with ML research chops, Martin with a stint at GM's automations team, Alex who dropped out and reads research papers. They're clearly smart. But I don't hear a story about someone who lived inside the dysfunction of manufacturing quoting and couldn't walk away from it. Martin's GM experience is the closest thread, but it's a recent internship-level role — not years of watching a broken system grind people down. The founding story reads as "we identified a market opportunity" rather than "this problem found us and wouldn't let go." That gap matters to me. The founders I back tend to be the ones who would be solving this problem in their garage even if no one wrote them a check.

The dimension I keep returning to is what I call the invisible system — is there an opaque structure here that extracts value from people who can't see it? Manufacturing quoting is slow, manual, and annoying. But it's not hidden. Manufacturers know their costs. Buyers receive quotes. The inefficiency is visible to everyone involved — it's just tedious. Compare this to what Affirm did to credit card companies, where the entire business model depended on consumers not understanding their interest terms, or what Ownwell does with property tax assessments, where homeowners are systematically overcharged because the appeals process is deliberately opaque. Korso is solving a real workflow problem, but it's an efficiency play, not a transparency revolution. There's no gatekept information being liberated, no extractive middleman being disintermediated. That doesn't make it a bad business — it just means it doesn't light up the part of my brain that drives my highest-conviction bets.

Let me steelman this, because I owe every founder that honesty. The bull case is real: manufacturing is genuinely underdigitized, and that 35% AI adoption stat for procurement suggests early innings. The WhatsApp ingestion angle is actually a smart insight — a lot of manufacturing communication, especially among smaller shops and international suppliers, happens on messaging platforms that enterprise tools ignore. If Korso moves fast, lands a handful of manufacturers, and accumulates enough pricing history and customer relationship data, the switching costs could become meaningful. Manufacturing buyers are sticky once you're embedded in their workflow. And the "why now" is legitimate — frontier LLMs crossing the threshold for reliable document extraction from engineering drawings and messy email chains is a genuine capability inflection. A scrappy three-person team in YC could potentially outrun the bigger competitors on speed and focus. But even in the bull case, I'm looking at a good B2B SaaS outcome, not the kind of infrastructure-at-inflection bet that defines Long Journey's portfolio.

I keep thinking about what Lee said — "not some B.S SaaS company or AI wrapper." I don't think Korso is BS, and I don't think it's a thin wrapper. But the moat question haunts me. The dossier itself flags it: "Differentiation will depend on depth of manufacturing-domain training, integration quality, and speed of customer acquisition rather than proprietary model architecture." That's a polite way of saying the technology is table stakes and the race is won on sales execution. I've learned the hard way — HQ Trivia, GameCrush — that products without structural defensibility can evaporate no matter how good the initial execution is. At pre-seed I give more benefit of the doubt on moats, but here I see a team with internship-level domain experience going up against Paperless Parts with years of customer data and Uptool with blue-chip VC backing and a head start. The forge hasn't happened yet for these founders, and the space isn't hard enough or weird enough to reward the kind of conviction I bring to my best investments.
