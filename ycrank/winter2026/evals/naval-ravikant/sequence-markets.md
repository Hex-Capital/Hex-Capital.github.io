# Sequence Markets -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Smart order routing is a solved problem. It was solved in traditional equities two decades ago. Every major brokerage, every electronic trading platform, every institutional execution desk runs some form of SOR. Applying it to crypto markets is an adaptation, not an invention. When I ask "what does this founder know that can't be taught?" about an SOR company, the answer needs to be something about the microstructure of fragmented digital-asset markets that a competent quant from Jane Street or Citadel couldn't figure out in six months. Peter Bai has relevant experience -- TMX Group, macro trading at a $12.8B pension fund -- but this is knowledge acquired through a standard finance career pipeline. It's the kind of background I'd expect from any strong Ivey graduate who spent a few years in capital markets. It's not the decade-long obsession that produces non-teachable insight. And Muhammad Awan's public profile is essentially a blank page -- "I like building cool projects" is a YC tagline, not evidence of specific knowledge. I can't evaluate what I can't see, and the absence of a documented technical background for the co-founder of a latency-sensitive execution platform is not a neutral signal.

The leverage architecture is real but unremarkable. Software that routes trades across venues is inherently leveraged -- code serves many traders simultaneously. But this is the baseline leverage of any software company, not a new form of leverage that didn't exist before. When I backed AngelList, it created a category of capital leverage that founders literally couldn't access through any prior mechanism. When I backed Replit, it made programming accessible to people who would never have used a terminal. Sequence Markets is offering better execution of something traders already do, through a mechanism (SOR) that already exists in well-funded competitors. Talos at $1.5B, FalconX at $8B, CoinRoutes operational since 2017, 1inch aggregating DEX liquidity with $190M raised. The company claims $15B+ in aggregated liquidity and sub-millisecond latency, but these are table-stakes claims in this category. The infrastructure layer positioning is directionally right -- I systematically prefer picks-and-shovels over application-layer plays -- but when the picks-and-shovels market already has multiple well-capitalized toolmakers, being another shovel manufacturer isn't contrarian.

The strongest bull case: Talos and FalconX have enterprise sales cycles and high minimums that structurally exclude smaller active traders and mid-market institutions. If Sequence can be the Stripe of crypto execution -- dead-simple API, non-custodial, instant integration -- it could build from below while incumbents focus on whale accounts. The non-custodial architecture is smart; it sidesteps the heaviest regulatory burdens. And fragmentation is genuinely accelerating -- more DEXs, more chains, more venues means the routing problem compounds. If the AI-powered routing claims are real, execution data could create a flywheel where more volume produces better routing intelligence, which attracts more volume. The "why now" is also plausible: institutional adoption is accelerating, tokenized real-world assets create new execution needs, and regulatory clarity is improving. For this bull case to work, they'd need to prove that the gap between enterprise execution platforms and self-serve routing is durable, that incumbents can't simply launch a self-serve tier, and that their routing quality is measurably superior. None of these are proven, and the history of "we'll serve the underserved segment" in financial infrastructure is littered with companies that got squeezed from above.

The timing isn't contrarian -- it's consensus. "Crypto needs better execution infrastructure" is a thesis that every institutional crypto investor has already validated with hundreds of millions in deployed capital. The efficient market hypothesis may fail because humans are herd animals, but when the herd has already stampeded into your category and funded four competitors north of $19M each, the inefficiency you're exploiting isn't informational. I don't see what Sequence knows that Talos, CoinRoutes, and FalconX don't. The SOR concept isn't novel, the market is recognized, and the technology -- while requiring real engineering -- doesn't involve the kind of fundamental scientific or architectural insight that compounds into an unreplicable advantage. This is a category where execution quality matters but can be matched by any well-funded team with exchange integration experience.

On integrity and long-term orientation, I see no red flags but also no strong signals. The YC acceptance is a mild positive. The "controlled rollouts, quality over quantity" language suggests some discipline. The location discrepancy between New York and Toronto is minor but worth noting -- it hints at a company still figuring out its operational identity. Five people, pre-seed, no public revenue -- all normal for this stage. But at this stage, the founders' conviction and specific knowledge need to carry the entire investment thesis, and neither founder has demonstrated the kind of domain depth that makes me want to bet on them for a decade.
