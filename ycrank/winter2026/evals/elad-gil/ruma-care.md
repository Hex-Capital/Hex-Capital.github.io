# Ruma Care -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The first thing that jumps out about Ruma Care is the payment structure alignment. In the buy-and-bill model, providers purchase biologics at $80-150K per patient per year *before* knowing if insurance will reimburse -- and denials hit 37% of the time. That means clinics are absorbing catastrophic financial losses from paperwork failures. The person who benefits from better PA automation is the same person writing the check. This is the structural opposite of the broken payment dynamics I avoid -- the reason I've never invested in EdTech is precisely that the payer and beneficiary are misaligned. Here, the alignment is almost uniquely strong. A rheumatology clinic losing six figures on a single denied biologic has extreme willingness to pay for a tool that prevents that outcome.

The "why now" is solid but layered. CMS-0057-F, finalized January 2024, mandates FHIR-based PA APIs by January 2027 and forces faster decision turnaround with specific denial reasoning. That's a regulatory inflection with a hard date -- not a vague "the market is moving" claim. Separately, LLM capabilities have crossed the threshold where extracting payer submission criteria from unstructured documents and auto-filling forms is actually feasible at production quality. Two years ago this required brittle rule-based systems. The combination of regulatory digitization mandate and AI capability threshold creates the window. But here's the double edge: the FHIR mandate also lowers integration barriers for everyone, including Epic and Oracle building native PA modules. The regulatory catalyst that opens the window could also commoditize the technical layer over time.

The market fits my "too boring" archetype cleanly -- prior authorization paperwork is about as unglamorous as operations alerting was when I backed PagerDuty. Most investors dismiss it as healthcare admin. But the biologics niche has structural economics that distinguish it from generic PA automation. The competitors bear this out: Cohere Health ($200M raised) serves payers, not providers. Waystar offers PA as one module in a billion-dollar RCM platform optimized for breadth, not depth. Develop Health is broader across all medication access. Nobody is going deep on the specific biologics-at-infusion-clinics vertical. That said, this isn't as non-obvious as I'd like -- the general PA automation space has attracted serious capital, and Ruma's niche, while defensible, may be legitimately constrained in scale. The dossier has no SAM estimate for biologics-specific PA, and that absence matters. If there are only a few thousand infusion clinics in the U.S., the initial addressable market may be smaller than investor expectations require.

The founder-market fit is where I get genuinely interested. Christina Huang has been a biologics patient for seven years and experienced insurance denials firsthand -- that's not market research, that's lived pain. Meng Fei Shen embedded himself as a medical assistant at a rheumatology clinic to experience PA workflows directly, which is the kind of operational immersion I respect from my own experience building Color. They've already shipped: live pilots in California and Nevada, 50% denial rate reduction, 20x staff time reduction per PA, a free Formulary Navigator launched in January 2026, and a symposium co-hosted with Stanford Medicine. For a two-person pre-seed team, that's real execution velocity. The Formulary Navigator specifically is a smart distribution move -- offering a free tool that delivers immediate value while collecting data on provider queries and coverage rules. That's using a product as a distribution channel, not just waiting for word-of-mouth.

The bull case I have to genuinely engage with: if Ruma becomes the definitive intelligence layer mapping every medication-diagnosis-insurance combination to approval criteria, the denial pattern data compounds into a moat no competitor can easily replicate without processing equivalent PA volume. From biologics, they expand to all specialty medications, then potentially to the broader specialty clinic operating system -- scheduling, billing, copay management. That's the Gusto trajectory: start with one boring workflow, become the platform for the entire practice. The regulatory tailwind forces adoption on a specific timeline. In this scenario, the "too boring, too niche" perception creates exactly the kind of gap between how the market looks today and where the adoption curve heads.

Where I land: this has legitimate thesis fit on the "boring but critical" pattern, the regulatory-moat pattern, and founder-market fit. But I have two concerns that keep me from higher conviction. First, the CMS FHIR mandate is a double-edged catalyst -- it enables Ruma but also enables competitors, and EHR vendors with existing clinical relationships could build native PA tools more easily once FHIR standardizes the integration layer. Second, I don't see strong evidence that the founders have thought through the product-to-distribution trajectory beyond the Formulary Navigator. The expansion from biologics PA to a broader clinic platform requires new payer rule mappings, different workflow integrations, and potentially different buyer personas -- that starts to look like more than one miracle. I'd want a conversation about their specific expansion thesis before writing a check.
