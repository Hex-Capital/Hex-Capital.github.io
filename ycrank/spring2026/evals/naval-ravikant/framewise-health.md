# Framewise Health -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Framewise Health is a permission-gated business wrapped around a founder with real lived specific knowledge -- Tane Kim watched 500+ patient interactions across a dozen institutions before dropping the Brown BS/MD -- and that pairing is the analytical signature: the dimension I weight hardest (leverage architecture) fails, and the dimension that usually saves a borderline check (specific knowledge) carries weight Framewise Health cannot cash. A patient-discharge video pipeline that pulls FHIR data from Epic, Oracle/Cerner, athenahealth, ModMed, eClinicalWorks, and NextGen, then sells to hospitals, is a capital-and-labor-leverage business. I run the leverage check first, and Framewise Health fails it first.

Twitter (2007) is the HIGH rung on leverage architecture I keep returning to -- media as protocol, marginal cost of replication zero, no gatekeeper between the founder and the audience. Framewise Health distributes through six EHR vendors and sells into hospital procurement; every customer requires a HIPAA review, a SOC 2 review, and an Epic integration sign-off. That is the inverse of the Twitter mechanism. The Framewise Health deck (per the website) targets "hospitals, specialty groups, medical devices, and pharma" -- four buyer categories that all require SDRs, BAAs, and 12-month sales cycles. The dossier confirms the Red Flag I underline twice: labor-leverage signaling in a business that needs code leverage to underwrite asymmetric upside. The clinician-review step in the workflow is also labor leverage -- the very thing the AI was supposed to remove sits inside the pipeline as the safety wrapper.

The LOW rung on network value is single-sided vertical SaaS where each customer is economically independent of the next, and Framewise Health sits squarely on that rung. A hospital buying personalized discharge videos for its discharged patients does not make the next hospital's videos better; there is no two-sided compounding, no developer ecosystem, no creator graph. The dossier hints at "engagement data could train downstream personalization models" but tags it [Inferred] and unproven -- which is the dossier admitting Framewise Health has no demonstrated network. Without the Nth-user-makes-the-(N-1)-users-more-valuable geometry, the 10,000x upside math the Knowledge Project sketch depends on never starts.

Tane Kim's 500+ patient interactions and the Brown BS/MD route is the strongest dimension in the Framewise Health dossier, and it is genuinely non-trivial -- this is closer to the Ivan Zhao-at-Notion mechanism (lived problem ownership from adolescence) than to the ex-McKinsey-PM pattern I flag. Over 18 months that specific knowledge compounds in one direction only: clinical-content judgment calls about what an AI-generated discharge instruction can and cannot say without inviting FDA SaMD classification. David Cui's computer-vision research at Brown plus the Freya (YC S25) voice-AI internship is competent but not category-defining. Framewise Health's specific-knowledge profile is real, but it is medical-empathy specific knowledge driving a regulatory wrapper, not a distribution wedge -- it deepens the moat one inch and widens it none.

For the market and pivot-room dimension to flip from below-midpoint to top-quartile, the Framewise Health dossier would need to describe a path out of FDA-regulated medical-instruction delivery into an adjacent code-leverage market -- consumer health media, a developer API for healthtech builders, an open-source FHIR-to-video pipeline. Nothing in the dossier signals that pivot vector. The $14.55B US patient engagement TAM (MarketsandMarkets, 2025) is real, but it is a TAM measured inside the same regulatory box Framewise Health is born into; the first idea rarely works, and the second idea here lands in the same Epic procurement queue as the first.

The founder trio reads clean -- Tane Kim and David Cui met at Brown freshman orientation, no contested exits, no self-advertised honesty -- and I will not punish silence here. The Anchorage Digital exception is the question I owe myself: does Framewise Health rhyme with the regulated-moat-as-leverage flip? It does not. Anchorage rode a pre-consensus crypto-custody category I had been dollar-cost-averaging into since MetaStable (2014); I have no comparable pre-positioned thesis in AI patient education, and the December 2024 Commure-Memora consolidation tells me the incumbents are already buying this category before Framewise Health has a logo.

The 36-month forecast: Framewise Health either gets acqui-hired by Commure or a regional EHR partner for under $25M after eight to twelve hospital pilots fail to convert to enterprise contracts, or it pivots to a developer-facing FHIR-to-multimedia API and raises a flat-to-down seed extension in 2027. The mechanism producing this outcome is straightforward -- the same FHIR-vendor dependency that makes Framewise Health's integration story credible at pre-seed makes the substitution risk overwhelming once Epic or athenahealth ships native AI patient-education video, which the dossier flags as direct substitution risk.

The bull case that stings: Tane Kim's lived clinical exposure plus the clinician-review wrapper could compound into regulatory specific knowledge that incumbents underestimate -- the Anchorage pattern where regulation becomes the moat -- and AI-generated multilingual discharge instructions across 75+ languages is a problem the EHR incumbents have shown no urgency to solve in twenty years of Epic's roadmap. That is a real argument. It loses to two facts in the Framewise Health dossier: zero named customer logos or LOIs and a distribution architecture entirely dependent on hospital procurement that no permissionless-code mechanism can shortcut. I would flip to Invest on one verifiable condition -- Framewise Health publishing three named hospital-system contracts with paid-pilot revenue inside 12 months, which would prove the regulatory wrapper is the leverage rather than the cost.

Pass. The downside is one X, but at angel size I deploy that X where the upside geometry exists. Framewise Health's upside geometry is bounded by the same procurement queue that bounds the incumbents.
