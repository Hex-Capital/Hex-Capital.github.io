# Maywood -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Maywood: it's exactly the kind of company that every other investor would want to fund. Wharton Summa Cum Laude CEO from Blackstone, two MIT EECS co-founders, one from BCG and one from a major hedge fund, building AI for investment banking — a sector with $5 trillion in annual deal volume. Drake Goodman's LinkedIn announcement got 952 likes. That number tells me everything I need to know about where this sits on the consensus-to-conviction spectrum. When I think about the companies that have defined my portfolio — SpaceX when everyone said private rockets were lunacy, Crusoe when people laughed at mining Bitcoin on gas flares — those founders couldn't get 952 people to agree on anything. The fact that nearly a thousand finance professionals immediately validated this direction is the opposite of the signal I look for. The most promising companies are not popular. Maywood is popular.

When I sit down with a founder, I don't want to hear about the business first. I want to hear the story — the real one. How did you get here? What broke you and remade you? With Maywood, the biographical thread I can trace is: Drake went to Wharton, worked at Blackstone, saw that M&A deal execution was painfully manual, and decided to build software to fix it. Kent went to MIT, worked at BCG automating enterprise processes, and his brother called him. Esteban went to MIT, worked at Balyasny on AI transformation, and his long-time friend Drake called him. These are impressive people making a rational career move. But there's no survival story here, no hardship that forged an unbreakable connection between these founders and this problem. When I backed the Contraline founder, what moved me was years of solitary academic research on a problem everyone else dismissed — that's biographical commitment you can't fake. Leaving Blackstone after a few years to start an AI company in 2025 is brave, sure, but it's not the same thing. It's professional optimization, not existential necessity.

I also have to be honest about what this company does and who it serves. My investing life has been shaped by backing companies that create economic access — Uber gave income to people who needed it, Postmates opened up delivery work, Affirm made credit transparent for people who'd been exploited by hidden fees. Maywood helps investment bankers at middle-market and large banks "double capacity" and "double deal count with the same headcount." That's a real efficiency gain, and those bankers probably appreciate it, but it doesn't expand opportunity for anyone who currently lacks it. It doesn't make an invisible system visible to people who've been shut out. It makes an already-visible system faster for people who are already well-compensated. That's a fine business, but it doesn't activate the part of my thesis that matters most to me.

The bull case is real, and I want to be fair to it. The team's founder-market fit is genuinely exceptional — Drake lived the pain at Blackstone, Kent brings the AI research depth with neural stylistic transfer work at MIT, and Esteban bridges the gap between financial organizations and AI infrastructure. They've already achieved SOC 2 Type II certification at pre-seed, which is non-trivial and creates a genuine barrier in regulated financial services. They're working with several banks and have customer testimonials citing doubled capacity. The regulated buyer dynamic — long procurement cycles, extensive security reviews — is exactly the kind of complexity I've learned to value, because it deters competitors and creates switching costs. If the question is "can this team execute in this space," the answer is clearly yes. And the brother dynamic between Drake and Kent is something I actually like — after HQ Trivia taught me that 50/50 co-founder splits without trust can destroy even perfect products, I've developed an appreciation for family-run teams where conflict resolution patterns are already baked in. Adding Esteban as a long-time friend reinforces that foundation.

But when I step back and ask myself the question I always ask — would I drop everything to work for these founders? Do I feel that jolt, that visceral excitement about where this could go? — the honest answer is no. This is a well-executed, credential-rich, consensus-friendly AI productivity tool for one of the best-paid professions on earth. It will likely raise money easily, grow into a solid business, and maybe get acquired by Datasite or one of the incumbents. But it doesn't have the quality that draws me in — that sense of a few dreamers and weirdos arriving at an insight nobody else can see. Every smart finance-plus-AI team in New York is looking at this same opportunity right now. The defensibility depends not on some weird structural insight but on execution speed and enterprise relationships, which is a fine strategy but not the kind of bet I write my own checks into.
