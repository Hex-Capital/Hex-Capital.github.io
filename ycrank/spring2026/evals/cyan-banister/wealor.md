# Wealor -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Karim Bouri and Pierre Depuydt met at École Polytechnique and UC Berkeley, and they are pointing a forward-deployed-engineering pitch at the single most-contested AI vertical of 2026 — Jump just took $80M from Insight, Zocks took $45M from Lightspeed and QED, Orion shipped Denali AI in February, Addepar shipped Addison in March, and Anthropic itself released RIA plug-ins. Wealor's analytical signature for me is clean governance pointed into a knife fight already in progress: the highest-scoring dimension is that there is a named CEO and a named CTO with no apparent ambiguity, and the lowest is that "AI-native operating system for wealth managers" in Spring 2026 is the textbook definition of consensus. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but I cannot find the game underneath this one.

Crusoe (~2018) is the ladder rung Wealor needs to be measured against on weirdness, and the gap is the whole evaluation. Crusoe was three uninvestable things stapled together — crypto, oil and gas, North Dakota — which is exactly why the round was underpriced and why Chase Lochmiller's adjacency to oilfield operators was load-bearing. Wealor is the inverse mechanism: an AI workflow layer for RIAs is being chased by Jump ($105M raised), Zocks ($65M raised), Vise ($124.5M raised, $24M revenue), Altruist's Hazel at $60/seat, and the two largest incumbents that already sit on $5.8T and $9T of advisor AUM respectively. The Wealor dossier does not describe a single sophisticated investor who passed on aesthetic grounds, because nobody is passing — they are all bidding. Find them before it becomes consensus; this one became consensus before Wealor incorporated.

HQ Trivia's failure mechanism was two founders without a tiebreaker, and that pattern is conspicuously *absent* in Wealor — Karim is unambiguously CEO and Pierre is unambiguously CTO on the YC page, both with distinct functional lanes (Karim sold financial products and ran ML at CANAL+; Pierre ran finance at Tiffany & Co. and was Deputy Section Chief in the French military). I have no documented operating agreement or vesting protocol in the dossier, so I cannot push Wealor into the top rung of this dimension, but the failure mode that took down HQ Trivia and GameCrush — peer-status co-founders fighting over product DNA — does not present in Wealor. This is the one place Wealor scores well, and it scores well by *not* tripping a red flag rather than by exhibiting a positive signal.

Karim Bouri's biography is the only Anduril-shaped fragment in the Wealor dossier — he "sold financial products to wealth managers" before founding the company, which is buyer-adjacent in the way Palmer Luckey was hardware-adjacent before defense. The mechanism that compounded for Luckey was years of shipping Oculus before turning the same physical instincts onto autonomous defense; Karim's exposure is one rung down — he sold *into* wealth managers but did not *operate* as a financial advisor inside an RIA or a family office. Eighteen months from now, that distinction will show up in product feel: an operator who has personally reconciled a quarterly performance report at month-end builds a different OS than someone who pitched portfolio products to that operator. Pierre's Tiffany-and-BCG-and-Polytechnique stack is the "Stanford or Harvard, Goldman Sachs or McKinsey" silhouette I am most allergic to, and it does not add lived advisor exposure to balance Karim.

Counterfactual on the regulated-and-unsexy dimension: for Wealor to clear into Vigil Labs territory on the capital-intensive-regulated axis, the dossier would need a concrete compliance posture — SEC Investment Advisers Act recordkeeping architecture, a named compliance hire, a signed pilot with a family office over $1B AUM willing to put advisor-AI in front of fiduciary workflows. None of that is present. Wealor is software-only sitting horizontally across other vendors' stacks, which is the worst geometry against Orion and Addepar, because incumbents collapse the integration value into a native release the moment the category proves out — Denali AI in February and Addison in March are precisely that collapse already happening.

Flock Safety is the single-anchor parallel on physical-world verifiability, and it is brutal for Wealor. I sourced Flock from a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café because the product existed in the physical world before the deck did. Wealor has team size 0 listed on the YC page, no Product Hunt launch, no public app, no observable demo, no retrievable LinkedIn or Twitter footprint, no named customer. A normal human cannot install Wealor, photograph it, or hand it to a friend within 48 hours; I cannot ride this thing the way I rode Uber in 2010 or play it the way I played Ingress in 2012. The dossier offers founder testimony where I require a tactile artifact.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Wealor gets acqui-hired by Orion, Addepar, or Altruist for under $30M as a tuck-in talent purchase, or it folds and the founders return to France — it does not reach $5M of independent ARR. The mechanism is the one Wealthmanagement.com already described in April 2026: incumbents at $5.8T and $9T AUM are shipping native AI inside the platforms RIAs already pay for, and the well-funded note-taker wedge (Jump, Zocks) is expanding horizontally with multi-year runway. A pre-seed two-founder forward-deployed-engineering shop arriving in Spring 2026 is arriving to a room where the chairs are already taken.

The strongest bull case is that Karim is actually the one founder in the cohort who has personally sold into the RIA back office, which is rarer than it sounds — most AI-for-advisors companies are built by ex-FAANG engineers who learned the buyer from a memo, and a forward-deployed-engineering motion run by someone with real buyer-side scar tissue can win Palantir-style multi-million-dollar enterprise contracts that the note-taker wedge cannot. I take this seriously; it is the only thing keeping Wealor out of the Pass-Example bucket. But it does not survive the dossier evidence: Karim *sold* to wealth managers, he did not *work* inside one, and that is the difference between Palmer Luckey shipping Oculus and a defense-curious PM reading a McKinsey report on autonomy. The verifiable condition that would flip me: a signed pilot disclosed publicly with a named family office over $1B AUM, or a single RIA reference customer with seven-figure ACV, inside the next six months. Absent that, Wealor is the consensus AI-agent darling pattern wearing a French accent.
