# General Astronautics -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about General Astronautics: this company is building robots for laboratories that don't exist yet, aboard space stations that haven't been completed, to serve a manufacturing market that hasn't materialized. That's not three layers of technology risk -- it's three layers of *market existence* risk. When I backed SpaceX, private space was considered absurd, but the customers were identifiable (the US government needed launches and was paying for them). Here, the customers are themselves startups and programs-in-progress -- Axiom, Vast, Orbital Reef are all still building their stations. You're asking me to bet on a company whose revenue depends on other companies succeeding at their own extraordinarily hard problems first. That dependency chain gives me real pause.

Now, I always start with the founders, not the market. Tell me your story -- not the business. And what I see with Bram Schork is genuinely impressive technical precocity. Winning an international soft robotics competition at fourteen, getting published in a peer-reviewed journal, landing a SpaceX internship on Starlink laser hardware -- that's a kid who's been building things his whole life. I respect that deeply. But what I'm listening for is the hardship that forged the commitment. Why space robots? Why this problem, for you, right now? And from everything I can find, the answer is: because it's intellectually fascinating and they have the technical chops. That's a fine reason to start a company, but it's not the biographical thread I look for. The founders I back the hardest -- the ones I'd drop everything to work for -- are solving problems that came from their own lived experience, their own pain. Schork and Zhou are solving a problem they identified from the outside, through academic and internship exposure. And Zhou is nearly invisible to me -- CMU Robotics is world-class, but I have almost no signal on who this person is, what drives them, or how they think. I can't evaluate a founder I can't see.

The honest bull case here is worth sitting with. If Schork is the kind of generational technical talent that his early robotics competition wins suggest, and if Zhou brings the autonomy and AI chops from CMU to complement the mechanical and aerospace side, they could be positioned at exactly the right moment. Commercial space stations will need automation -- astronaut time at $130K per hour is obviously unsustainable for any kind of manufacturing scale. Being the general-purpose robotics platform that works across multiple stations, rather than being locked to a single vertically integrated manufacturer like Varda, is a genuinely interesting strategic position. And the microgravity fluid handling problem is hard enough to create a real technical moat if they solve it first. I can see the version of this where they become the essential automation layer for orbital manufacturing the way Opentrons became essential for terrestrial lab automation. The space is legitimately hard, regulatory complexity would deter most early-stage companies, and the NVIDIA Inception membership suggests they're serious about the AI side of autonomous operation.

But I keep coming back to the things that don't fit my framework. This isn't an anti-consensus bet -- space robotics is a well-recognized category with hundreds of millions flowing into adjacent competitors. GITAI alone has raised $129 million and could pivot into this exact niche tomorrow. Varda has $329 million and just closed a Series C. Redwire is a public company doing $304 million in annual revenue. The idea isn't unpopular or difficult to assess -- it's a crowded neighborhood where the startup is the smallest player by orders of magnitude. And fundamentally, this company doesn't create economic access or make invisible systems visible for underserved populations. It serves NASA, JAXA, and billionaire-backed space station operators. There's nothing wrong with that, but it doesn't activate the part of my thesis where I see investing as a mechanism for positive-sum economic outcomes.

The co-founder dynamic is a question mark I can't resolve from the outside. Two founders from different universities with no shared prior work experience that I can identify -- I'd want to understand how they found each other, how they divide authority, and what happens when they disagree about something fundamental. My HQ Trivia lesson taught me that even perfect timing and product can't survive unresolved founder conflict, and with a two-person team in a capital-intensive space that will take years to reach meaningful revenue, the relationship between these two people is everything.

I'm passing on this one. The founders are technically talented but early-career, the market is consensus rather than contrarian, and the dependency on other companies' timelines introduces a compounding risk I can't underwrite at the pre-seed stage. If I met Bram Schork and he told me a story that changed my understanding of who he is and why this problem chose him -- something I couldn't find in any dossier -- I'd revisit. That's happened to me before. But based on what I can see today, this isn't my kind of bet.
