# Sazabi -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that immediately jumps out about Sazabi is the collision between a genuinely organic founder journey and one of the most consensus categories in tech right now. Sherwood Callaway built observability infrastructure at Brex, founded an AI company that got acquired, then ran engineering on "one of the largest agentic systems in production" at 11x. He lived the exact trajectory that makes this problem real -- traditional infra, then AI agents, then discovering the monitoring tools couldn't keep up. That origin story is authentic. But "AI observability" is precisely the kind of category where every VC is already excited and every smart engineer has already thought "someone should build that." My wariness about crowded consensus categories exists for a reason: when Langfuse gets acquired by ClickHouse, LangSmith hits a billion-dollar valuation, Arize raises $70M, and Datadog is bolting on AI features, the idea has been priced. The question is whether Callaway's specific technical thesis -- "logs are all you need" -- is contrarian enough to escape the consensus trap.

The organic discovery here is real, which matters. Callaway didn't read a market research report and decide AI observability was hot. He was literally operating a large agentic system and saw the gaps firsthand. That's the difference between a Stripe-like origin and a brainstorming-session origin. But there's a crucial distinction I keep coming back to: schlep blindness means the problem is hiding in plain sight because the work is so painful that people unconsciously avoid it. AI observability is not hiding. It's the opposite -- it's the most visible problem in the AI infrastructure stack. Everyone sees it. Five well-funded companies are already attacking it. The schlep exists (those SOC 2 and ISO certifications for a 10-person team are genuinely tedious to achieve, and dealing with log ingestion at scale is hard engineering), but the schlep is not deterring competitors. Stripe worked partly because the banking and regulatory pain scared everyone away for a decade. Here, the pain is attracting capital, not repelling it.

The bull case is the "logs are all you need" thesis. If Callaway is right that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces from logs alone, that's a genuine technical insight that restructures the entire observability pricing model. Datadog charges per host, per trace, per metric -- their business model depends on the three-pillar architecture being necessary. A company that collapses three pillars into one creates a business model barrier that makes it hard for the incumbent to respond without cannibalizing revenue. That's the kind of structural insight that could matter. The compliance certifications at pre-seed also signal enterprise seriousness -- most 10-person teams skip that entirely, and it suggests Callaway understands the enterprise sales cycle he's entering. And his investor list reads like a who's-who of the AI developer tools ecosystem: angels from Anthropic, Vercel, Brex, Replit, LangChain. If those are genuine product endorsements and not just network investing, the distribution advantage is meaningful.

But I can't get past two things. First, the solo founder. Only Callaway is listed, running a 10-person team. Cofounders are for a startup what location is for real estate. I made Drew Houston find a co-founder before accepting Dropbox, and Houston was building something with far less competition. A solo founder entering a market against Datadog ($2.1B revenue), a $1.25B LangSmith, and a ClickHouse-backed Langfuse needs a co-founder who brings complementary strength -- whether that's a deep systems engineer, or someone with enterprise sales DNA. Maybe one of the 10 employees is effectively a co-founder, but the absence of any named co-founder in public materials is a flag. Second, the product is waitlist-only with zero public traction. At pre-seed that's not unusual, but combined with a 10-person team, it raises a question about burn rate and default-alive trajectory. Ten people with no revenue is not lean. Rapid hiring after fundraising is one of the biggest killers of startups, and a 10-person pre-product team suggests the company may be building ahead of demonstrated demand rather than iterating tightly with early users.

Callaway is clearly a builder -- Dev Bootcamp background, engineering roles at Crunchbase and Brex, founded and shipped Opkit to acquisition. His GitHub shows active projects. He's not a PowerPoint founder. But I don't see evidence of the hacker intensity that characterizes the founders who win in deeply competitive markets. When Patrick Collison grabbed someone's laptop and installed Stripe on the spot, that was a founder whose technical craft was inseparable from his distribution strategy. What's Callaway's equivalent? With a waitlist and no public users, I can't see it yet.

This is a competent founder with genuine domain experience attacking a real problem -- but in a market where "real problem" is the consensus view and capital is flooding in from every direction. The "logs are all you need" thesis could be the differentiating insight that makes this work. But that thesis is unvalidated, the market is consolidating fast, and I don't see the structural advantages that would let a pre-product solo-founded company outrun competitors with $70M+ in funding and thousands of existing customers. I'd want to see evidence of the thesis working in production, a named co-founder, and any signal of organic user pull before writing a check.
