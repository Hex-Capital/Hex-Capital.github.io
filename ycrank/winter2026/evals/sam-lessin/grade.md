# Grade -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out immediately: Grade is a software layer sitting on top of PayPal, Wise, Venmo, Payoneer, and Revolut — someone else's payment rails, end to end. The company's own dossier acknowledges that "connecting performance APIs to a payment disbursement layer is technically reproducible." That's not me editorializing. That's the factual assessment. When I look at my best investments — Venmo creating a new peer-to-peer payment *primitive*, Solana building a new consensus *protocol*, Aleo establishing zero-knowledge privacy as *infrastructure* — what they share is that they became the layer other people build on. Grade is an application on six other companies' infrastructure. The moment any of those rails decides to offer a "pay creators by performance" feature, or the moment an AI agent can orchestrate the same API calls Grade orchestrates, this company's product design advantage evaporates. This is the structural diagnosis I keep making on CNBC: software companies that had moats are discovering they aren't moats anymore. Grade hasn't even had time to build the moat it's about to lose.

The founders are genuinely capable — I want to be clear about that. Lotanna built XPO (influencer fintech), Creator Check (creator screening), and sold two SaaS businesses in travel. James sold a startup at 22 and built Pray Screen to $40K MRR before selling it. They've worked together across Creator Check and four mobile AI apps. This is a pre-existing co-founder relationship with shared operational scars, which matters — I've seen too many accelerator speed-dating teams implode at the first real crisis. The domain fit is real: they literally lived the creator payment pain while scaling consumer apps and discovered that switching from flat fees to performance-based compensation accelerated growth but broke their payment workflows. The problem is that "sane person with a sane idea" is my *worst* configuration. Creator payment infrastructure isn't contrarian — it's a well-recognized need in a $205 billion market that's already attracted $85 million in venture capital to direct competitors. Lumanu has $20.6M and $2.1M in revenue. Trolley has $44.4M. Nobody hears "payroll for creators" and thinks "that's crazy." They think "that's obvious." And when the idea is obvious, the returns are already priced in.

The bull case I'd want to engage with honestly: these founders are executing fast. $380K+ in payouts processed in a single month with 120% month-over-month growth is real signal. Five named customers. 790+ creators paid across 190+ countries. If they can build an aggregated "creator performance rating" dataset across all their customers — a shared intelligence layer where Company A's data about Creator X improves Company B's decisions — that could become something closer to a genuine data primitive. A marketplace intelligence network for creator compensation would be structurally interesting, and nobody else is explicitly building that. If the performance data compounds and creates switching costs that deepen with every payout cycle, the application could morph into infrastructure. That's the path to something I'd fund.

But I don't think the evidence supports that path today. The dossier identifies the potential data moat as "[Inferred]" and "unproven." The current product is a workflow tool: add creators by email, define payment rules, execute bulk payouts. That's a feature set, not a protocol. Any company with Stripe and a competent engineer — or increasingly, any company with an AI coding agent and an afternoon — can replicate that workflow. The "cherry on top" test fails completely here: remove the software layer and there is no business. The software *is* the business. That's the Birchbox pattern I learned the hard way — narrative magnitude in a hot market without structural defensibility produces a company too successful to die quickly but too fragile to compound. The creator economy is the narrative. The payment processing on borrowed rails is the reality. And AI makes the gap between those two things wider, not narrower, because the specific value Grade provides — automating the link between performance data and payment execution — is precisely the kind of structured workflow that AI agents will handle natively within existing platforms.

The traction numbers are the strongest element of this pitch, and I respect founders who show up with real volume rather than just a deck. $1M+ disbursed is meaningful at pre-seed. But traction in a commoditized category doesn't solve the moat problem — it just means you're running faster on a treadmill that Lumanu and Trolley are also running on, with 40-90x more capital behind them. The competitive capital disadvantage is real and the structural differentiation — "we do performance-based pay, they do flat fees" — is a product feature, not a business model moat. Adding variable compensation logic to an existing payout platform is a sprint, not a pivot.
