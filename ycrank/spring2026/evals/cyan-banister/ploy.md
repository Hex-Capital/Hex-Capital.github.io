# Ploy -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Bryant Chou spent twelve years as Webflow's co-founder and CTO building a platform that powers about 1% of the most active sites on the internet, and now he's solo-founded Ploy to bundle a site builder, visitor de-anonymization, outbound, and ad creative into one stack — which is to say Ploy is the cleanest possible founder-CEO profile (solo, deep, identifiable) walking straight into the most crowded consensus lane of Spring 2026. That high/low pairing is Ploy's whole signature: an A+ operator in a room that already contains Mutiny's $72M of post-pivot war chest, Default's fresh $4M from 8VC, and Webflow itself — Chou's own prior company — rebranding as an "agentic web marketing platform" with AI credits in every Workspace plan as of May 13, 2026. I'm never playing the game I seem to be playing, but this is one of those decks where the game I seem to be playing and the game I'm actually playing are the same game, and I don't love it.

Ploy's highest rung is the founder ladder. The load-bearing analogue is John Hanke at Niantic — a builder who spent a decade inside Keyhole and Google Earth before shipping the location-AR thing he was always going to ship, where the prior product was the *adjacent* product, not the *same* product. Chou clears that bar for half of Ploy: the Web Engine is the literal extension of twelve years at Webflow, and a founder who built site infrastructure to ~1% of the top sites has earned the right to redo it agentically. Where Ploy diverges from the Hanke template is that the Grow Engine (visitor ID, outreach, lead scoring) and Ads Engine (creative + attribution) are not Chou's lived domain — those are categories Warmly raised $17M into and Default raised $10.6M into respectively, and Chou is extending into them by inference, not by autobiography. I score the lived-experience axis high but not maxed because two-thirds of Ploy's promised surface area is analytically discovered, not personally inhabited.

The lowest rung is the consensus ladder, and the failure mechanism I keep coming back to is the YC AI-agent darling from my Pass Example — the deck where six top funds are already chasing because the agentic-GTM thesis has crystallized in the same quarter the company launched. That exact pattern is alive in Ploy's dossier: Mutiny killed its 8-figure SaaS on April 8, 2026 to relaunch agent-first; Default 2.0 added an AI routing agent on the same wave; Webflow positioned itself as "agentic web marketing platform" in 2026. Five sophisticated venture-backed operators converged on the same substrate in the same two months, and Ploy is the sixth wearing a founder logo I'd otherwise tattoo on. "Find them before it becomes consensus" — Ploy *is* the consensus. The round will be priced as if the outcome is already certain, which is the opposite of where my checks land.

Ploy's governance is the easiest line on the page: one founder, Bryant Chou, named CEO, no co-founder, no 50/50 disaster waiting to surface in a Yusupov-Kroll-style feud two years in. That alone earns near-max on the tiebreaker axis — the structural inverse of the **HQ Trivia** (2017) post-mortem I quote at people who don't ask. If Ploy fractures in 36 months, it will not fracture on who controls the cap table; it will fracture on whether a 14-person team can ship parity with three venture-funded specialists simultaneously.

The capital-intensity lane is where Ploy is most thesis-mismatched. To flip Ploy from a 4 to a 9 on this axis, the dossier would need to show that Ploy is somewhere doing something other investors find aesthetically gross — a defense-adjacent attribution layer, a regulated-industry compliance moat on the visitor-ID side, an on-prem agent for some unsexy buyer. None of that is in the dossier. This is pure-software B2B marketing SaaS, the opposite of the Crusoe-Anduril-Vigil Labs sleeve where the moat compounds because rivals refuse to enter.

Physical verifiability is structurally absent in the way developer-tools abstractions are always absent for me. Ploy is waitlist-gated at ploy.ai, the on-page demo figures ("23 visitors identified, 12 ICP matches, $1K pipeline") are illustrative dashboard mockups not customer outcomes, and there is no Product Hunt launch, no Chrome extension, no app store presence. Contrast with **Flock Safety** (~2018), where the camera was on a pole I could photograph. I cannot touch Ploy in June 2026, which by my rule means nothing tangible has shipped at scale yet.

My 36-month forecast: Ploy gets acqui-hired by Webflow itself for $80M–$250M somewhere between months 18 and 30, as the AI-agent layer Webflow's stock needs and as Chou comes home with the talent and the IP. That's the mechanism the signature predicts — a respected solo operator with deep adjacency to a public incumbent, in a consensus category where three competitors will outspend him, almost always ends up as a feature acquisition by the incumbent whose alumni network shipped the product. Not a zero, not a Niantic — a Webflow line item.

The strongest bull case against me: Chou's distribution is real. He can call every Webflow customer personally, and the "bundle the commoditizing layers" thesis is the right thesis because each point solution (Mutiny, Warmly, Default) is in fact commoditizing in real time. The Niantic precedent says a builder with twelve years in the adjacent stack ships the next platform regardless of how crowded the lane looks at month zero — Ingress was unfashionable in 2012, too. Where this bull case loses to the dossier is the *founder origin* test: Hanke at Keyhole was solving location, then solved location-AR; Chou at Webflow was solving site-build, and now claims to solve visitor-ID + outbound + ads + site-build, which is not the same shape of adjacency. The flip condition I'd actually need: Chou personally articulates which two credible VCs passed on Ploy in this round on aesthetic or contrarian grounds (not on his pedigree) and why they're wrong. If that passage exists, I reopen the file. Until then, I pass with one foot in love.
