# Forum -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what strikes me immediately about Forum: prediction markets are the hottest category in fintech right now. Kalshi just raised a billion dollars at an $11 billion valuation. Polymarket did $2 billion. The sector moved $44 billion in trading volume last year. And now Forum is entering this space as a two-person pre-seed team with virtual currency and no regulatory approval, competing against Noise which already has $7.1M from Paradigm building essentially the same "attention trading" product. When I see a category this crowded, this competed, this validated by consensus capital -- that's the opposite of the signal I trust. The most promising companies are not popular. Forum is building in one of the most popular categories in venture right now, and the "attention" angle isn't weird enough to separate it from the pack. Noise, Versus, Opinion -- multiple teams independently arrived at "trade on culture." That's consensus forming, not conviction leading.

When I think about Owen and Joseph, I respect pieces of their story. Owen traded long/short equities at Balyasny, which is a serious multi-manager hedge fund -- he understands exchange mechanics, position management, market microstructure. Joseph built systems at NASA. They were high school debate partners who both dropped out of college to pursue this. Those are real credentials and a real relationship. But when I ask myself the question I always ask -- tell me how you got here, not the business -- I don't hear the answer that makes me lean forward. Why attention? Why is Owen Botkin the person who HAS to build the financial instrument for cultural relevance? Trading equities at a hedge fund teaches you how markets work, but it doesn't explain the obsession with this specific problem. The biographical thread connecting these founders to attention as an asset class isn't visible to me. Compare that to how I felt when the Crusoe founders explained why they were out in North Dakota burning gas -- there was an inescapable logic between who they were and what they were building. I'm not seeing that connective tissue here.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If Forum achieves CFTC-regulated Designated Contract Market status, that would be a genuine structural moat. Kalshi spent years and enormous resources getting there, and it locked out competitors. Owen's hedge fund background gives him real fluency in how regulated financial products get designed and approved. The timing is interesting -- CFTC is becoming more permissive, the IAB released attention measurement standards in late 2025, and there's a plausible argument that "attention" becomes a recognized asset class the way carbon credits or weather derivatives did. If you squint, Forum could be the CME of cultural relevance -- the regulated, institutional-grade venue where brands hedge their cultural exposure. That's a compelling vision. But today it's entirely aspirational. The demo runs on virtual currency. The regulatory application hasn't been filed or approved. And Kalshi, which already has the DCM designation and $260M in revenue, could launch attention contracts as a product line extension next quarter with trivially more effort than Forum has invested in its entire existence.

I also notice what this company doesn't do for the people I care most about. Uber created income for drivers who needed it. Postmates created income for delivery workers. Affirm gave transparent credit to people locked out of traditional lending. Forum creates a speculation vehicle for traders who want to bet on whether a celebrity's relevance goes up or down. I'm not seeing how this makes an invisible system visible or creates economic access for underserved populations. The "brands can hedge their cultural exposure" argument serves large corporations, not people who need new pathways to economic participation. That dimension matters deeply to me, and its absence here is felt.

The co-founder dynamic is probably Forum's strongest signal from my perspective. High school debate partners who both made the significant, irreversible decision to drop out of top universities -- that tells me they trust each other enough to bet their futures together. Debate teaches you to argue both sides of an issue, resolve disagreements through structured reasoning, and handle high-pressure competitive situations. That's not nothing, and it maps better to the co-founder stress-testing I look for after what happened with HQ Trivia. But it's untested in the specific crucible of building a company through crisis, and I'd want to understand much more about how they navigate disagreement before writing a check.

This is a pass for me. Competent founders in a real market, but I don't feel the biographical conviction, the idea isn't anti-consensus enough in a world where five companies are simultaneously building attention prediction markets, and I can't see who gets lifted up economically. The regulatory ambition is genuine, and if these founders had a deeper personal connection to the problem and were operating in a less crowded field, I'd look harder. But this feels like chasing a hot market with a clever angle, and that's exactly the pattern I've learned to avoid.
