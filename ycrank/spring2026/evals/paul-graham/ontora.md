# Ontora -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Ontora is that "AI will replace management consultants" is one of the most consensus ideas in technology right now. Every person who has ever received a six-figure invoice from McKinsey has had this thought. Every founder who watched ChatGPT conduct a passable conversation has imagined scaling that to organizational interviews. This is the opposite of schlep blindness. Schlep blindness is when a problem is obvious but your unconscious won't let you see the solution because the work is too ugly. Here, both the problem and the solution are obvious. When an idea is this consensus, the question becomes pure execution -- and at pre-seed with three people and a waitlist, execution hasn't been tested yet.

Maximilian Arnold's background gives me some hope. He built CoAnalyst at Porsche -- an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data that was projected to save millions across VW Group. That's not nothing. It's technically adjacent to what Ontora does: applying AI agents to enterprise operational data locked in siloed systems. So there's a genuine thread of experience running from his prior work to this company. But "adjacent" is the key word. The strongest founder-problem fits I've seen are direct, not lateral. Drew Houston didn't build a file backup tool at a big company and then decide to start a consumer sync product -- he kept forgetting his USB drive. The question with Arnold is whether he experienced the pain of organizational diagnostics firsthand at Porsche, or whether he had a hammer (enterprise AI agents) and went looking for the biggest nail (a trillion-dollar consulting market). The dossier doesn't answer this, and the absence of that organic origin story is itself a signal.

The co-founder situation gives me pause. Leon Iwanowitsch studied at HSG, which is a top European business school and a pipeline to consulting firms -- so he may have genuine exposure to how consulting engagements work. That's plausible founder-market fit for the business side. But David Korn is essentially invisible: no confirmed LinkedIn, no Twitter, no GitHub, no public background of any kind. And there's no visible overlap in where the founders studied or worked before starting Ontora. I've seen this pattern before -- co-founders who connected through a startup network or hackathon, formed a team around an idea rather than around years of shared context. In one YC batch, nine companies added co-founders they didn't know well, and every one of those teams fell apart within a year. I'm not saying that's what happened here, but the absence of evidence about the co-founder relationship is a concern, not a neutral data point.

The bull case is worth taking seriously. The Vertiv testimonial is specific and attributed to a real person at a public company: "What used to take our operating team months of interviews and analysis, Ontora delivered in a single afternoon." If that's accurate, the product works and delivers dramatic time compression on a real enterprise workflow. The schlep of actually executing this -- integrating with CRM, email, documents, conducting hundreds of AI interviews, mapping process dependencies -- is genuinely substantial, even if the idea itself isn't hidden. And there is a real structural advantage in attacking consulting firms: McKinsey cannot build this product without cannibalizing their own billable-hour revenue model. That kind of incumbent incentive misalignment is exactly the gap that creates openings for startups. If Ontora can nail enterprise distribution -- Tom Blomfield as YC partner helps here -- the ceiling is very high. The management consulting market is a trillion dollars, and even capturing a thin slice of the diagnostic portion would be enormous.

But one testimonial and a waitlist is thin evidence for a company claiming to automate what armies of MBAs do. The underlying technology -- LLM-based interviewing combined with RAG over enterprise documents -- uses broadly available foundation models. There are no public repositories, no visible technical artifacts, no evidence of proprietary model work or novel architecture. Arnold's hackathon wins and Porsche experience suggest technical ability, but I want to see code, not credentials. The commoditization risk is real: any competent team with access to GPT-4 or Claude can build an interview bot that ingests company documents. The moat would have to come from accumulated organizational process data across deployments, and that data network effect doesn't exist yet. Right now, I see a team with relevant-but-indirect experience in a consensus market with a product that works (per one testimonial) but hasn't demonstrated the kind of organic user pull that would make me write a check from my own money.
