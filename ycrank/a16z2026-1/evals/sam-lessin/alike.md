# Alike -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Every VC I talk to right now is losing their minds over "agentic AI." Gartner says multi-agent system inquiries surged 1,445% in a single year. Google shipped A2A as an open protocol with Salesforce, SAP, and Workday already signed on. CrewAI raised $18M, Dust raised $21.5M and is doing $7.3M ARR. Salesforce is literally auto-provisioning AI-powered Slack for every new customer and calling it an "agentic OS." So when Alike walks in pitching "the agent collaboration layer for enterprises," the first question isn't whether they can build it — it's whether the price already reflects the upside. And the answer is: absolutely it does. This is the most consensus category in enterprise tech since "cloud" in 2015. There's no mispricing here. There's a bidding war.

Max Van Kleek's credentials are the single most interesting thing in this dossier — 9,012 Google Scholar citations, MIT CSAIL PhD, 25 years across PARC, Nokia, IBM, and Oxford in exactly the intersection of human-AI interaction and privacy-preserving systems. That's rare and real. Addi finishing a PhD at 21 — started university at 13 — signals abnormal raw processing power. But the sane-insane ratio is inverted at Alike. The people are genuinely impressive. The idea is not remotely insane. "AI agents should coordinate autonomously in enterprises" is something Marc Benioff is saying on earnings calls. When the CEO of Salesforce agrees with your thesis, you're not contrarian — you're a feature request. I want a sane person pitching something that makes me uncomfortable, not something that makes a Fortune 500 CEO nod along.

The structural durability question kills this for me. Alike is pure software sitting between other people's agents and other people's tools — calendar, email, Slack, meeting notes. No physical operations. No protocol-level lock-in. The dossier's own competitive analysis tells the story: Google's A2A is the protocol (open source, Linux Foundation, 50+ partners). Alike is an application layer on top of someone else's primitive. That's exactly backwards from how I invest. I backed Solana, not a Solana dapp. I backed the protocol, not the wrapper. Alike is the wrapper. The "learned coordination patterns create switching costs" argument is theoretical — it doesn't exist today and by the time it could, Salesforce will have Slack doing exactly this natively with infinitely more data. The dossier floats a clever argument about Salesforce having a business model conflict because coordination automation reduces message volume, but Salesforce charges per seat, not per message. They'd happily automate coordination if it makes Slack stickier.

The co-founder dynamics raise a flag I specifically watch for. Addi and Danial are consecutive Oxford Students' Union Presidents who met through campus politics, not through building and shipping product together. Max is a full-time Associate Professor maintaining his academic post while serving as CTO. I've said many times — the best investments come from founders I've watched across multiple ventures, people whose judgment I've seen under real commercial pressure. Here I see three people from the same university ecosystem, two with zero prior startup experience, and a CTO with divided attention at a moment when CrewAI and Dust are sprinting with full-time teams and $18-21M each in the bank. The Oxford network is real distribution for landing design partners — the 20 signed partners and 10 live deployments likely trace to those connections — but selling to 10 design partners who know you from university is categorically different from winning enterprise contracts against Salesforce's sales machine.

The bull case for Alike requires believing three things simultaneously: that multi-agent coordination is genuinely different from what Slack can build natively, that Max's academic IP in privacy-preserving HCI creates a technical moat that well-funded competitors can't replicate, and that enterprise privacy requirements become so stringent they force adoption of a purpose-built solution over incumbent features. If all three are true, Alike has a real shot at building the coordination layer between agents the way Venmo built the payment layer between people. But each of those premises is shakier than it looks. Slack has every incentive to build coordination natively. Google A2A commoditizes interoperability as an open standard. And privacy requirements, while real, are a feature of enterprise software — not a company. The parallel to Venmo breaks because Venmo created a behavioral primitive in a space with no incumbent. Alike is creating an application feature in a space where every incumbent is already building.

Alike scored lowest on consensus mispricing and structural durability — the two dimensions that most determine whether I write a check. A consensus-hot AI application layer with pure software moats, competing against an open Google protocol and Salesforce's native roadmap, with first-time founders from academia — this is almost a checklist of my avoidance criteria. The academic talent is real, but talent in the wrong structural position doesn't produce venture returns. Pass.
