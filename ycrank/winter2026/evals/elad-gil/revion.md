# Revion -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells you everything about where this market sits on the adoption curve. Five funded startups — Toma ($17M from a16z), STELLA ($34.7M+ with Reynolds and Reynolds as strategic partner), Mia Labs ($29M, 350+ dealerships), Flai ($4.5M from First Round), and now Revion — all building voice AI for automotive dealerships, plus Tekion at $4B valuation announcing its own agentic AI platform for the same vertical. When a16z and First Round are already writing checks into your category, and an incumbent with 30,000+ dealership customers is building the same capability in-house, this is not a non-obvious market. It's becoming consensus. The structural moment — voice AI reaching production quality for domain-specific environments — is real, but the window where this was a contrarian bet closed roughly 18 months ago.

Revion's most interesting angle is the technician-in-bay workflow, which is genuinely differentiated from the phone-call-handling products that dominate the competitor set. Turning spoken repair narratives into OEM warranty-compliant Complaint-Cause-Correction documentation while the technician is under the hood — that's a different technical problem than answering inbound service calls. It requires automotive jargon recognition in noisy shop environments, structured output mapping to operation codes and labor hours, and compliance with OEM-specific formats. None of the funded competitors appear to be building this. If I squint, the technician workflow has some of the "too boring for most investors" quality I look for — it's deep operational plumbing inside dealership fixed operations, which is itself the profit center most outsiders don't understand. When I backed PagerDuty, nobody thought operations alerting was exciting either, but every company running software needed it. The parallel here would be: every dealership service department needs structured repair documentation, and it's currently done by hand.

The problem is the dual-product strategy. Revion is simultaneously building technician workflow tools and dealer communication AI agents — two distinct products serving two different end users (technicians vs. BDC staff) with different technical requirements. On a four-person team, this looks like a two-miracle plan. The technician voice-to-repair-order product requires domain-specific speech models tuned to automotive jargon in noisy environments. The phone-call automation product competes directly against Toma, STELLA, and Mia, each with 5-8x more capital. When I apply the single-miracle test, I'd want to hear: "Our one miracle is making voice-to-structured-repair-order work at production quality in a shop environment, and everything else is a checkbox." Instead, they appear to be fighting on two fronts simultaneously. The bull case is that winning the technician workflow creates a wedge into the dealership relationship, and the communication product distributes through that same relationship — a product-to-distribution play. The nine named DMS/CRM integrations (CDK, Tekion, Xtime, MyKaarma, Cox Automotive, Keyloop, CitNOW, Salesforce, RTC) suggest middleware positioning that could become a distribution channel. But at pre-seed with four people, building middleware across nine integration partners while also building two product lines is a resource allocation question I'd want answered.

The execution signals are the strongest part of this story. For a four-person pre-seed team, they've shipped a working product deployed at real dealerships, generated case study data (30,000+ billable hours recovered at Ewing Automotive Group, 15% increase in repair order count, 12,000 calls handled with 50% BDC staffing reduction), secured partnerships with $500M+ and $700M+ revenue dealership groups, built nine platform integrations, and earned testimonials from a former Ford UK Chairman and a Mercedes-Benz Fixed Operations Director. That's an unusual level of operational velocity for a YC W26 company. The CTO, Hanbo Xie, joining Terra at 18 and shipping through Imperial, Goldman, and IBM Research suggests the technical execution is credible. Zaki GW's investment banking and Multiverse GTM background gives the enterprise sales capability, though neither founder has deep automotive domain experience — they've compensated with industry relationships rather than personal history in the sector.

The strongest bull case: the technician-in-bay workflow is genuinely greenfield — no well-funded competitor is building it — and the $81B dealership service market is undergoing simultaneous pressure from aging vehicle fleets (12.6-year average) and technician labor shortages. If Revion focused exclusively on the technician product, treated it as their single miracle, and used the dealership relationship as a distribution channel for adjacent products over time, this could become embedded operational infrastructure in the way Gusto became payroll infrastructure. The DMS incumbents (CDK, Reynolds and Reynolds) have deep lock-in but have not innovated on the workflow layer. A voice-first tool that technicians actually use on the shop floor, integrated into the existing DMS, could create switching costs that compound. But that requires focus — picking the technician product as the wedge and being disciplined about the single miracle — rather than simultaneously competing with better-capitalized startups on the phone-call product.

I'm landing at neutral. The market's structural moment is real but no longer non-obvious. The technician workflow differentiation is genuinely interesting but gets diluted by the dual-product approach. Strong execution velocity for the stage, but the competitive dynamics are challenging — particularly with Tekion building AI agents from a position of existing dealership penetration. I'd want to see the team commit fully to the technician-in-bay product as their single wedge before writing a check.
