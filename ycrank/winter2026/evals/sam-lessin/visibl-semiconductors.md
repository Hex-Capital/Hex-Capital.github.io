# Visibl Semiconductors -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Visibl isn't Visibl itself -- it's ChipAgents sitting there with $74 million in funding, 50 semiconductor companies deployed, and an advisory board that reads like a reunion dinner of former Synopsys, Cadence, and Mentor Graphics executives. When I see a two-person pre-seed team entering a space where a competitor has already raised Series A money at that scale and locked in customer relationships across the industry, the burden of proof shifts dramatically. The "coordination layer" positioning is a genuinely clever wedge -- the insight that Synopsys can't build a tool orchestrating Cadence workflows without undermining its own capture strategy is structurally real -- but clever positioning doesn't survive a capital disadvantage of this magnitude unless you're operating on fundamentally different terrain. Visibl isn't operating on different terrain. They're building software for the same customers, solving adjacent problems with the same underlying technology.

Here's where my framework gets harsh. When I run the "cherry on top" test -- would this business exist if AI didn't exist? -- the answer is an unambiguous no. Visibl IS AI agents for chip design. Remove the AI and there's nothing left, no physical operations, no installed base, no protocol that other tools integrate with. This puts it squarely in the category I avoid most aggressively: AI-first companies where AI constitutes the entire value proposition. Apple, Google, and the EDA incumbents themselves are better positioned to deliver AI coordination features because they have the distribution. Synopsys DSO.ai and Cadence Cerebrus are already pushing AI capabilities into their tool suites, and while they're currently focused on PPA optimization rather than cross-workstream coordination, the step from "AI that optimizes within our tools" to "AI that coordinates across our tools" is an incremental feature release for a company with $80 billion in market cap, not a startup-scale opportunity.

Jordon Kashanchi brings legitimate semiconductor credibility -- designing custom AI silicon at Microsoft for Azure Maia, plus stints at Arm and Intel, with a Master's from UT Austin in Integrated Circuits and Systems. That's the "sane" half of my equation, and I take it seriously. He's personally lived the coordination pain he's trying to solve. But the "insane" half is missing entirely. AI for chip design is one of the hottest categories in the EDA ecosystem right now. Bloomberg projects AI could add $6 billion to the EDA market through 2030. ChipAgents, Silimate, Chipmind, Maieutic -- every angle of this market has funded companies pursuing it. This is consensus with a capital C, and consensus means the returns are already priced in. My whole framework is built around non-consensus bets -- Venmo when nobody thought peer-to-peer mobile payments mattered, Solana when the idea of yet another Layer 1 blockchain during crypto winter seemed delusional. "AI agents for semiconductor coordination" in 2026 does not provoke genuine skepticism from investors. It provokes competitive term sheets.

The bull case I want to genuinely engage with: the "coordination layer" framing could, if executed perfectly, become something closer to a protocol -- a neutral standard that sits across the fractured EDA ecosystem, precisely because no incumbent can build it without advantaging their competitors. If Visibl established itself as the Switzerland of chip design coordination, the switching costs from ingesting full design context (code, specs, scripts, tool outputs) across a team's entire workflow could create real lock-in. That would make it less like a SaaS tool and more like infrastructure. The Airtable exception in my own portfolio proves I'm willing to invest in things that look like SaaS but function as new primitives. For this to work, though, Visibl would need to get deeply embedded in customer workflows before ChipAgents expands laterally from verification into coordination, and before Synopsys or Cadence decide that a cross-tool coordination feature is worth the strategic tradeoff. With two people, no public customers, and a product offered as a free download, the timeline math doesn't favor them.

The enterprise semiconductor sales cycle compounds every other problem. On-prem deployment -- which is necessary given IP security requirements -- means months of procurement, security review, and integration work for each customer. For a two-person team, this creates a brutal arithmetic: long sales cycles, high deployment costs, and limited bandwidth to service accounts while simultaneously building product. Craftwork, one of my favorite portfolio companies, solves a similar operational-complexity challenge but with physical painters generating revenue from day one. Visibl has no equivalent revenue floor. No public customers, no revenue signal, and a free download tier that suggests they're still searching for product-market fit. At pre-seed I don't demand revenue, but I need some cushion against narrative collapse, and I don't see one here.

This is a well-positioned application in a consensus market, built by a team with one strong domain founder and one generalist, competing against dramatically better-capitalized alternatives. The software IS the business, and that's exactly the category where I believe moats are dissolving fastest. Pass.
