# Asendia AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Rihab Lajmi left Tunisia at eighteen, studied engineering in Germany, got hired at Microsoft Munich and then Google Cloud, and quit Google at twenty-seven to start a company. Badis Zormati took the same path — Tunisia at eighteen, electrical engineering at TU Munich, six years building AI systems at Infineon. Those biographical facts describe real determination. Crossing continents alone as teenagers, landing at elite institutions, then walking away from secure careers — that sequence is not something passive people produce. And yet when I look at what Asendia AI is actually building, I see a gap between the founders' evident grit and the kind of organic problem obsession I need to see before writing a check. Rihab experienced recruiting from the candidate side — she went through "dozens of interviews herself" at Google, Microsoft, and Amazon. But Asendia AI sells to staffing agencies, not job seekers. Her thirty-five recruiter interviews during early development are market research, not lived frustration. The way to get startup ideas is not to interview people who have the problem. It's to have the problem yourself.

"AI for recruiting" is probably the single most consensus application of large language models in enterprise software right now. Paradox raised $253M before Workday acquired it. HireVue has $89M in annual revenue. Humanly just closed a $25M Series B. Sense HQ hit a $500M valuation. Fetcher raised $41M. Asendia AI is entering a category where competitors have collectively absorbed over half a billion dollars in venture capital. When I apply Peter Thiel's Venn diagram — the intersection of "seems like a bad idea" and "is a good idea" — Asendia AI sits entirely outside that sweet spot. Nobody reacts to "AI phone screening for staffing agencies" with skepticism or confusion. They react with "oh, like Paradox?" This is an idea that attracts before it repels, which in my framework means the return premium has already been competed away. The specific differentiator — live voice screening versus text chatbots — is real but incremental, not structural. Voice AI quality crossed a usability threshold in 2024-2025 with commodity APIs from OpenAI and ElevenLabs, which means every competitor can add voice screening as a feature rather than ceding the category.

Asendia AI's pivot from "FAANG," a B2C job-seeker coaching product, to its current B2B staffing agency platform is the most informative data point in the dossier, and it cuts both ways. When I gave Alexis and Steve the idea for Reddit, the pivot worked because I was redirecting exceptional founder raw material toward a better target. Asendia AI's pivot looks different — it reads as a market-driven reorientation after the B2C product didn't scale, not a founder following an obsession to its natural endpoint. All of Asendia AI's Product Hunt traction — the #2 Product of the Day, the 1.3K followers — belongs to the prior product. The current B2B product has approximately ten paying clients across US and European markets, which is genuine pre-seed traction but also means Asendia AI is effectively restarting go-to-market against entrenched incumbents with a hundred times its capital. The positive reading of the pivot is that it shows the flexibility I prize — persistent about building a company, flexible about the form. The negative reading is that the staffing agency problem was selected from a menu, not discovered in the wild.

Fifty ATS integrations and SOC 2, GDPR, and HIPAA certifications from a three-person team — that is real execution. Those compliance certifications alone represent months of work and real engineering discipline, and the integration breadth creates genuine switching costs once agencies route workflows through Asendia AI. I'll give Asendia AI credit for this: getting ten paying enterprise clients across two continents with three people and compliance infrastructure in place is not the output of a lazy or unfocused team. Badis's six years of AI engineering at Infineon gives me some confidence that the technical foundation is sound, even though neither founder has public GitHub repositories — an absence I notice because the builders I tend to back leave artifacts everywhere. The couple co-founder dynamic is a risk I can't ignore. Co-founder conflict is one of the top startup killers, and when co-founders are also romantic partners, both relationships are loaded into the same failure mode. Asendia AI's founders have clearly built a working partnership through the B2C phase and the pivot, but I've seen too many couple-founded companies where the professional disagreements and personal ones become indistinguishable.

The strongest bull case for Asendia AI runs through the Workday-Paradox acquisition. When Workday absorbed Paradox in August 2025, it likely redirected Olivia's development toward Workday's enterprise HCM suite, creating a genuine channel conflict with standalone staffing agencies who don't use Workday's core platform. Asendia AI is positioned to catch the customers who fall out of that integration, and its staffing-agency-specific focus — Bullhorn integrations, blue-collar and healthcare verticals, multi-language voice screening — addresses a real underserved niche. If Asendia AI can compound its ten clients into a hundred in the next twelve months while the Workday integration creates confusion among Paradox's former standalone customers, there's a path to a defensible vertical SaaS business. The bear response is that Humanly, Sense HQ, and Fetcher are all positioned to catch those same displaced customers with ten to fifty times more capital and established customer bases. Asendia AI's voice-AI differentiation is a feature, not a moat — and features get copied by better-funded competitors in quarters, not years.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Asendia AI's ten paying clients are retaining and expanding — specifically, net revenue retention above 120% over two consecutive quarters — because that would demonstrate the data flywheel is producing measurably better screening outcomes, which is the only durable moat available to Asendia AI in a commoditizing voice-AI landscape. Without that signal, I'm looking at a competent team in a consensus market with formidable competition. The two dimensions that lock Asendia AI into the Neutral tier are Organic Problem Discovery (the staffing agency problem was found through market research and a pivot, not lived experience) and Idea That Repels (over $500M in competitor funding proves this category attracts rather than repels capital and talent). Asendia AI's founders have real grit, but grit applied to a consensus idea in a crowded market produces a grind, not a breakout.
