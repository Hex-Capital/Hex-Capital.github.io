# GovGuard -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

FOIA -- the Freedom of Information Act -- was built to make government transparent. And it has become, itself, one of the most opaque systems in American civic life. Clerks searching decade-old archives by hand, redacting documents with physical markers, taking 44 days to fulfill a simple request while backlogs swelled 33% to 267,056 pending cases in FY2024. GovGuard sits at the center of a genuinely beautiful irony: the law designed to force government accountability has calcified into a bureaucratic barrier that keeps citizens from information they're legally entitled to. When I trace the invisible system here -- who benefits from this opacity? -- the answer isn't a cartel or a gatekeeper extracting rent. It's institutional inertia itself. The incumbents confirm it: Granicus acquired GovQA in 2021 and CivicPlus acquired NextRequest in 2022, and both built ticket-management systems -- essentially Jira for records requests -- that digitize the queue without touching the actual work. GovGuard proposes to automate the substantive fulfillment: search, redaction, exemption citation, response drafting. That's not incrementally better workflow software; it's a different product category. The invisible system score here is genuinely high because GovGuard attacks the gap between a citizen's legal right and their practical ability to exercise it.

Aditya Sabharwal and Gleb Hulting co-founded BoardWatch together before GovGuard, and Gleb previously founded EGO Logistics. Aditya's LinkedIn reads "BioE & CS @ UIUC," which tells me these are early-career technical founders who've built together before. That prior working relationship matters -- it's not a cold 50/50 split, and the co-founder red flag is partially mitigated by the shared history at BoardWatch. But when I ask my opening question -- tell me their story, not the business -- the dossier gives me almost nothing. No evidence that either founder worked in government, navigated FOIA as a journalist or activist, grew up watching a parent drown in municipal paperwork, or had any biographical encounter with public records that made this problem find them. The dossier explicitly notes "no public evidence of direct prior government or legal-sector domain expertise." GovGuard's founders appear to have identified a market gap, not lived inside a broken system. I've watched this pattern before -- credentialed technical founders spotting an underserved vertical without the scar tissue that tells you how the system actually works behind closed doors. Government buyers are unforgiving of that gap; they can smell outsiders.

The timing convergence deserves serious attention, and an honest bull case starts here. GovGuard enters at a moment when three forces are colliding simultaneously: FOIA requests surged 25% YoY to 1.5 million in FY2024, AI-assisted filing tools enabled one organization alone to submit 100,000+ requests, and federal workforce reductions in 2025 gutted processing capacity. A federal agency (ARPA-H) explicitly sought AI redaction software in 2025. GovGuard's competitive landscape is structurally weak -- Everlaw has AI redaction capabilities but targets enterprise at $80.8 million revenue and a $2 billion-plus valuation, leaving local governments underserved. The built-in state-code exemption library spanning 50 states creates genuine domain complexity that casual entrants won't replicate easily. If GovGuard lands even a handful of municipal customers and proves the product works, the switching costs of re-integrating archives and retraining staff create real stickiness. I can construct a version of this where GovGuard becomes the Carta of public records -- regulatory complexity as moat, information asymmetry as the structural problem, and deep integration with institutional workflows creating compounding defensibility. Carta attacked cap table opacity for startups; GovGuard attacks records opacity for citizens. The parallel holds structurally.

But Carta's Henry Ward lived inside the cap table problem as a founder before he built the solution. Max Levchin at Affirm understood lending from the inside. The Crusoe founders witnessed gas flaring in North Dakota oil fields firsthand. GovGuard's founders, as far as the public record shows, walked into government transparency from a computer science program at UIUC. The contrarian dimension is moderate -- GovTech is genuinely hard to sell into and most VCs avoid the space due to procurement friction, which provides some deterrent against competition. But GovGuard doesn't provoke the visceral discomfort I look for. Nobody shifts in their chair when you describe automating FOIA compliance. Compare this to Flock Safety's surveillance cameras, where civil liberties concerns made investors actively uncomfortable, or Anduril's defense AI, which was politically toxic. GovGuard is solving a real problem in a difficult-to-penetrate market, but the difficulty is operational (long sales cycles, SOC2 and FedRAMP requirements, fragmented buyers across 90,000 local government entities), not ideological. Operational difficulty deters lazy competitors; ideological difficulty deters nearly everyone.

GovGuard shows zero visible traction -- 2 YC Launch upvotes, no revenue, no customer counts, no LOIs, no pilots, no press coverage, 0 job postings. At pre-seed I don't penalize the absence of metrics categorically, but I need at least one signal that the product has escaped the building -- a clerk who used it and told a colleague, a municipal IT director who said "finally." GovGuard's cascading impact potential is real: if this works, every citizen's legal right to government records becomes practically exercisable rather than theoretically guaranteed, which is the kind of access expansion that I consider authentic impact. But impact potential without founder-domain connection and without any customer signal remains theoretical. I checked my Known Exceptions -- HQ Trivia's cultural-moment override, RTFKT's narrow-window override, Superplastic's creative-weirdness override -- and none apply to a B2G infrastructure play. No exception rescues this evaluation from its core gap.

The one verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that either Aditya or Gleb has a personal encounter with the FOIA system -- as a requester, a records custodian, a civic technologist, or someone who witnessed the system fail someone they cared about -- combined with at least one municipal pilot producing measurable time savings. GovGuard's invisible-system thesis is strong enough that the biographical gap is what holds me back, not the market or the product concept. The problem is real, the timing is acute, and the competitive landscape is genuinely vulnerable to an AI-native entrant. But I invest in founders forged by the problems they're solving, and GovGuard's founders, based on everything publicly available, found this problem through analysis rather than autobiography. The founder biographical forge, weighted heaviest in my framework at 30 points, is what keeps GovGuard in neutral territory despite a compelling structural thesis on the invisible-system dimension.
