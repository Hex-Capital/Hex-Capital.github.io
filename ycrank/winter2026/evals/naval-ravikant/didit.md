# Didit -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing that strikes me about Didit is not the founders or the technology -- it's the pricing. Free core KYC, $0.03 per email verification, $0.15 per ID check. This is a company whose primary differentiation is being cheaper. And in my experience, when your moat is price, you don't have a moat -- you have a margin compression problem waiting to happen. Sumsub built $85.6M in revenue on $7.5M raised. Persona has $141M in revenue and $417M in capital. These aren't sleeping incumbents; they're fast, well-funded, and growing 75% year-over-year. Didit is bringing a knife to a gunfight and the knife is labeled "we charge less." The question is whether there's something underneath the pricing that compounds.

On specific knowledge: Alberto Rosas has genuine ML capability -- the StyleGAN2 TensorFlow implementation with 143 stars on GitHub tells me he can build models, not just call APIs. Alejandro built AI systems at Oracle. These are real technical skills applied to identity. But "can build ML models" is a transferable skill, not domain-specific knowledge. The identity verification problem they're solving didn't emerge from years of obsessive study of fraud patterns or regulatory architecture -- it emerged as infrastructure for Gamium, a metaverse/SocialFi project with a token. They built identity verification because their Web3 platform needed it, then pivoted when B2B KYC proved to be the better business. That's smart opportunism, but it's the opposite of what I look for: a founder who has been marinating in a specific problem domain so long that their insights can't be replicated by a smart generalist with six months of research. Alex Pinilla has built biometric ID systems since 2018, which is the strongest domain signal on the team -- but he's a developer, not a founder driving the vision.

The leverage architecture is where this falls apart for my framework. Identity verification is inherently a per-transaction service. Each user gets verified individually. There's no network effect -- my verification doesn't make your verification better. There's no protocol-level leverage -- no permissionless coordination mechanism that compounds. The MCP server for AI agents is interesting -- enabling autonomous agents to run KYC workflows could create a new category of programmatic identity -- but it's positioned as a feature, not a thesis. Compare this to Stack Overflow, where every question and answer made the knowledge graph exponentially more valuable, or Alchemy, where every developer building on the platform deepened the infrastructure moat. Didit is an API that processes documents and faces. It's software, so it scales better than a services business, but the leverage ratio is linear, not exponential.

The bull case deserves genuine consideration. The traction is real and impressive for a team that's raised only $2M: 700+ B2B customers growing 20% month-over-month, 300%+ net revenue retention by month 6, near-profitable. Those are not the metrics of a confused pivot -- those are the metrics of a team that found product-market fit and is executing with discipline. The free tier as a PLG funnel is a smart distribution mechanism. 14,000+ document types across 190 countries represents accumulated work that creates switching costs. And the "why now" is genuine: deepfake technology has made identity fraud an arms race, and legacy providers with opaque pricing and multi-vendor integration complexity are losing to simpler, cheaper alternatives. If I believed that the identity verification market would consolidate around the cheapest provider -- the way AWS won cloud computing partly on price -- this would be a strong bet. The capital efficiency is particularly notable. Near-profitability on $2M is the kind of discipline that lets a company survive long enough to find its real edge.

But I keep coming back to the structural question: what does this company know that can't be taught? What form of leverage does it create that didn't exist before? "We consolidated four vendors into one API and made it cheaper" is a valid business, but it's not the kind of insight that produces 1,000x returns. The market is consensus -- everyone agrees identity verification is important and growing. The timing is consensus -- everyone knows deepfakes are a problem. The competitive landscape is crowded with well-funded players offering substantially similar capabilities. And the Gamium origin story -- Web3 metaverse with a token, then pivot to B2B KYC -- triggers my concern about founders who are passionate about building companies rather than about a specific problem. The $GMM token airdrops tied to Didit's growth add a layer of complexity that enterprise buyers will question. This is a competent team running a real business in a big market. But competence in a commodity market compounds linearly. I invest for exponential.
