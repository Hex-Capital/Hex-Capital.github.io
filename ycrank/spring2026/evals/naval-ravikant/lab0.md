# Lab0 -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Rocketlane raised $105M, has 750+ customers, and just excluded Lab0 from their Propel conference. That exclusion is the most interesting data point in this entire dossier — not because it validates Lab0's product, but because it reveals that a well-capitalized incumbent already perceives Lab0 as a competitive threat with zero public traction. Rocketlane's defensive posture tells me more than any pitch deck could. When incumbents start gatekeeping distribution channels against a 3-person pre-seed team, the structural tension is real. The $451.6 billion system integration market runs on labor leverage — bodies multiplied by billable hours. Accenture, Deloitte, Wipro — their entire revenue model is the antithesis of permissionless leverage. Lab0 is attempting to convert the world's largest labor-leverage market into AI leverage. That's the right structural move.

Lakshya Gupta's "World of Workflows" paper (arXiv, January 2026) identified "dynamics blindness" — frontier LLMs failing to predict cascading side effects in enterprise systems. That's a genuine technical insight, and it likely informs Lab0's approach to implementation automation. But one research paper is not years of obsessive domain engagement. None of Lab0's three founders spent time as forward-deployed engineers or system integrators. Sujay Srivastava previously founded Ravvio in BFSI sales automation — a different domain entirely. Onkar Borade engineered at Oracle and Mudrex — solid builder credentials, but general-purpose. Lab0's founders identified this problem through research and market analysis, not through the kind of lived experience that creates non-replicable knowledge. If Gupta disappeared, could a strong AI engineer with enterprise workflow data rebuild Lab0's core insight? Probably yes, within months. That's the specific knowledge test, and Lab0 doesn't pass it cleanly.

Lab0's leverage architecture is where my framework gets genuinely interested. The structural parallel to Perplexity is unmistakable — both attack massive markets where the incumbent's business model creates a principal-agent trap. Google's search team has the knowledge to build AI search but the ad revenue model makes them agents of their own P&L. Accenture's consultants have the implementation knowledge but automating their work destroys billable hours. Lab0, like Perplexity, is a principal with no legacy revenue to protect, moving where agents structurally cannot. The difference: Aravind Srinivas had years at Google Brain and OpenAI building the exact systems that made Perplexity possible. Lab0's founders have proximity to the problem, not immersion in it. The leverage architecture is sound — AI agents replacing human consultants is zero-marginal-cost leverage at its purest — but the foundation underneath it is thinner.

The bull case an advocate would make: Lab0 is three IIT technical builders with zero open job postings, building AI agents to automate a $451.6B labor-intensive market where every incumbent is structurally prevented from self-disruption. Gupta's research demonstrates technical understanding of exactly why current LLMs fail at enterprise workflows. The Rocketlane exclusion is a competitive signal that money can't buy. Certinia just launched Veda, Kantata launched Expertise Engine, ServiceNow acquired Moveworks — every major player is racing to add AI to implementation, confirming the timing. Lab0 is the only entrant aiming to eliminate the human layer entirely rather than augmenting it. Three people shipping against a field of 750-customer incumbents IS the team-to-value leverage signal. This is the Perplexity structure applied to consulting — principal versus agents, AI leverage versus labor leverage, small team versus corporate armies.

The bear case dismantles this on specific knowledge and commoditization risk. Lab0 faces ServiceNow (post-Moveworks acquisition), which owns the enterprise workflow data that Lab0 would need to build its agents effectively. Salesforce and Microsoft can embed implementation agents directly into their platforms — they don't need Lab0 as a middleware layer. The paper on dynamics blindness is a useful diagnostic, but it's published research, not proprietary knowledge — every competitor can read it. Enterprise sales cycles will strain a 3-person team's runway before the product can prove itself. And fundamentally, enterprise implementation is contextual, messy, relationship-heavy work where "continuous learning from every rollout" is an aspiration, not a demonstrated flywheel. Lab0 may be building a wrapper around frontier model capabilities that the model providers themselves will ship natively — the exact AI wrapper pattern I consistently pass on, dressed in enterprise clothing.

Lab0 sits at a genuinely interesting intersection — the leverage architecture is compelling, the incumbent principal-agent dynamic is structural, and the market timing is confirmed by every competitor simultaneously launching AI agent products. But specific knowledge is my highest-weighted criterion for a reason. The compound interest test remains uncertain: Lab0 claims continuous learning from every rollout, but with no public traction data, this is a claim, not evidence. The access democratization angle is real but secondary — if Lab0 works, companies that couldn't afford Accenture's implementation fees get access to enterprise software deployment, removing the system integrator gatekeeper. The condition that would flip me to Invest: evidence that Gupta or Srivastava spent meaningful time doing enterprise implementations themselves — as consultants, as forward-deployed engineers, as anything that gave them the visceral understanding of why implementations fail that you can only get from living inside the problem for years. The paper suggests they understand the problem technically. I need to see they understand it operationally.

Lab0 has the structural architecture I look for — AI leverage displacing labor leverage, principals versus agents, a lean team attacking an enormous market. The leverage dimension and team density score well. But specific knowledge productization is my highest-conviction filter, weighted at 30 points, and Lab0's founders identified this opportunity through research rather than through irreplaceable domain experience. That's the gap between a neutral evaluation and a check.
