# CatchBack Cards -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells the entire story before I even get to the product. Whatnot is valued at $11.5 billion and doing $6 billion in GMV. Fanatics — a $31 billion company — already operates "Under Wraps," a mystery box program with official licensing from every major sports league. eBay acquired TCGPlayer for $295 million. Alt has raised over $100 million for card investment infrastructure. This is a market with at least five well-capitalized players all pursuing the same pool of collectors. When I see this many funded competitors fighting over the same customers, I see a Girardian nightmare of mutual imitation. Companies copying each other's features, subsidizing users to switch, burning capital to match each other's engagement mechanics. No one achieves monopoly in this configuration. The question is not whether CatchBack has a good product — the question is whether any structural path to monopoly exists, and the answer is clearly no.

What is the secret here? The dossier implies it is that mystery packs need cryptographic randomness verification — that collectors cannot trust opaque pack sellers, and verifiable fairness will unlock a better experience. But this is not a truth that most intelligent people actively reject. Ask any collector whether they would prefer verified randomness, and they will say yes. Ask any technologist whether cryptographic proofs can verify randomness, and they will say obviously. There is no disagreement to exploit, no contrarian bet to make. This is a feature — a sensible, useful feature — not a secret about the world that creates a monopoly. Worse, the founders open-sourced the card selection code, which means even this modest technical differentiation is freely available to every competitor. Whatnot could implement the same verification layer in a quarter and deploy it to their existing base of millions of users.

The founding team has legitimate credentials. Joseph Zhang is a Gates Scholar, ISEF finalist, former AWS engineer — the raw intellectual horsepower is evident. Quitting a stable career to sell Pokémon cards takes a certain kind of conviction. But the conviction I look for is civilizational, not hobbyist. "I quit my job to sell Pokémon cards" is not "I have a specific plan for how this industry will be fundamentally restructured in ten years." The vision as articulated — custom mystery packs with cryptographic verification and a $1 marketplace — is a product roadmap, not a theory of the future. And Sophia Kim, the co-founder in a two-person team, has no publicly documented background whatsoever. In a company this small, both founders matter enormously. The McKinsey on Zhang's résumé is not encouraging either — consultants are trained to analyze existing frameworks, not to hold a singular vision against all opposition.

The strongest bull case would require several things to be true simultaneously: that cryptographic verification becomes a consumer-facing trust standard that collectors actively demand, that this standard creates switching costs deep enough to retain users against Whatnot and Fanatics, that the Steph Curry partnership delivers meaningful distribution to sports card collectors, and that $13K MRR with 700 users represents the early signal of explosive organic growth rather than the ceiling of a niche product. If all of these converge, CatchBack could become the default trust layer for digital card trading — a narrow but defensible position. The $13K MRR is real revenue, and at pre-seed that deserves acknowledgment. But even in this optimistic scenario, I cannot see how the company achieves the kind of 10x or 100x returns that justify an angel investment. The upside case is "a good small business in a competitive market," not "a monopoly that returns the fund."

The crypto and Web3 tags compound the problem. The founder's prior employer was Nexus, a blockchain infrastructure company, and the Nexus Layer 1 partnership feels more like a legacy of Zhang's professional network than a strategic necessity for consumers. After the NFT collapse, attaching blockchain branding to a consumer product narrows the addressable market rather than expanding it. Mainstream collectors do not want on-chain anything — they want cards. This is not 2021. The technology here is solving a trust problem that matters, but the blockchain framing transforms an understandable value proposition into something that triggers justified skepticism from the very audience they need to reach.

This is a 1-to-n company in a market already saturated with well-funded 1-to-n competitors. There is no secret, no monopoly path, and no technological discontinuity sufficient to escape competition. The founders are capable but not building something that would matter at the civilizational scale. I pass.
