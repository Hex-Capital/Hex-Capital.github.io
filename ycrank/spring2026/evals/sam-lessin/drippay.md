# Drip -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Stripe paid one billion dollars for Metronome in January 2026, and three months later Drip is raising a pre-seed to sell roughly the same shape of product. That sentence is the entire evaluation. When the market has already marked the category to a billion-dollar strategic exit, every pre-seed in the space is priced at the consensus. The risk is not baked into the price of Drip ? the upside is. My whole game is finding four-cent SOL when everyone believes Ethereum won. Drip is the four-hundred-dollar SOL trade: buying after the thesis is proven, hoping to catch the next leg.

Mike Levin worked on GitHub's metered billing platform, which is the single best thing in this dossier. Founder-market fit on the metering engine is real ? he has shipped exactly this kind of infrastructure at scale. That is the "sane" half of the equation. But where is the insane half? Drip's pitch is "Stripe Billing maxes out at 1,000 events/sec, we do 100,000+" and "prepaid balance so you save on card fees." That is a sensible performance/pricing pitch to a validated buyer in a validated category with a validated exit outcome. Nothing about it makes a smart person at the bar say "that's fucking crazy." It makes them say "yeah, that's a reasonable wedge." Reasonable wedges in reasonable categories are fine businesses and bad venture bets.

Metronome, Orb, Lago, Flexprice, Zenskar, Schematic, and now Stripe-with-Metronome-inside ? that's the competitive set on page two of the memo for Drip. Lago is open source and YC-backed, meaning the metering primitive is already commoditizing toward free. So Drip has to defend on the settlement/prepaid-balance layer, which is the newer and less-proven sliver. And that sliver happens to be the piece Stripe already owns the rails for. If you're pitching me a billing company whose differentiation is "we route the money better than Stripe," you need to explain why Stripe ? which just spent a billion dollars telling us it cares about this exact problem ? won't ship this feature in the next twelve months. The AI kill test here isn't really an AI test; it's a Stripe test. And Drip fails it cleanly. This is pure software with no physical operation, no protocol, no creator relationship, no regulatory license. It's the melting-ice-cube SaaS I keep warning about.

The prepaid-balance model is actually the most interesting wrinkle on Drip, and I want to give the bull case its due. Holding float on-platform creates switching costs ? customers with $50K pre-loaded don't casually migrate. Batching ten thousand micro-charges into one settlement genuinely saves money on interchange. There is a real wedge here for AI-agent companies billing fractions of a penny per token. An advocate would say: Metronome sold for $1B precisely because this category is bigger than anyone realized, Drip is attacking the one seam Metronome never addressed (settlement economics), and Mike Levin is uniquely qualified to build it. Fine. Now tell me what happens when Stripe ? which just absorbed Metronome's metering engine and already operates the payment rails ? adds a "prepaid credits" SKU. The seam closes. The Known Exception that could save this is Slack: consensus-hot deal where I overrode the mispricing rule because the primitive was real. But Slack replaced email. Drip replaces a line item on a Stripe invoice. That's not a primitive.

Lucas He as CTO is unverified in the dossier ? the LinkedIn match to a TikTok ML engineer could not be confirmed for Drip. Two founders with no documented shared history, one with strong domain fit and one who is a cipher, building high-throughput financial infrastructure that will need money-transmitter analysis state by state if the prepaid-balance thing scales. This is the synthetic co-founder pattern I actively avoid. Compare to Kortina and Magdon-Ismail at Venmo ? years of shared history before the company existed. For Drip, I don't have that, and the memo explicitly flags "founder concentration / unverified CTO profile" as a risk.

Capital discipline: no evidence in the dossier on Drip's burn plan or fundraising trajectory. Team size listed as zero, which is a YC-profile artifact rather than signal. I default to moderate. The category itself, though, is a land-grab category ? Metronome, Orb, and Lago all raised meaningful capital to stand up billing infrastructure, and Drip will almost certainly need to match that spend to build the integrations and compliance surface. This isn't the kind of business you bootstrap to profitability on a tight seed.

One verifiable condition flips me on Drip. Show me three named AI-company customers processing real volume on the prepaid-balance product, with retention data indicating the float-based switching cost is actually sticky ? not just "we integrated Drip alongside Stripe." If Drip is already doing seven-figure GMV through the settlement layer with named logos, the consensus-pricing concern gets overridden by proof the wedge is real and the incumbent hasn't closed it. Absent that, Drip is a competent team building a sensible product in the single hottest billing category on the board three months after a $1B comp printed. The two dimensions that lock this for me are Consensus Mispricing (the price already reflects the category, there is no asymmetry to harvest) and Structural Durability (Stripe owns the rails and just bought the metering layer). Pass.
