# 9 Mothers -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me immediately: a QA software founder and his data scientist are building autonomous weapon systems. Russell Smith spent a decade building Rainforest QA — a no-code testing platform — and Roman Khomenko was his lead AI data scientist there. Now they're building a counter-drone kill system for the U.S. military. That's not a pivot, that's a teleportation. When I backed Raj on Solana, I'd watched him across three companies *in crypto infrastructure*. The domain continuity was the signal. Here, I see an eight-year co-founder relationship — which is genuinely valuable, that's a real bond forged under real pressure — but zero visible defense industry experience across the entire founding team. Pyzh has security consulting at Leviathan, which is cybersecurity, not weapons procurement. Nobody on this team has shipped hardware to the DoD, held a clearance (that we know of), or navigated an ITAR compliance process. The relationship is real. The domain match is not.

The pricing problem is the killer for me. Defense tech in Spring 2026 is where AI copilots were in 2024 — every investor at every demo day is salivating. Anduril just raised at $60 billion. DroneShield is doing $216 million in revenue growing 276% year-over-year. The U.S. Army just signed a $20 billion framework contract for counter-UAS. When I look at this space, every sophisticated investor I know is trying to get into defense. That means the price already reflects the upside. The entire thesis of counter-drone systems being important is *consensus* — it's on the front page of every defense publication, it's in the DoD budget, it's what Garry Tan is tweeting about. There's no narrative mispricing here. Nobody has "written off" tactical C-UAS. The market has fully priced in the demand signal from Ukraine.

The bull case deserves a real paragraph because the underlying market dynamic is legitimate. If you squint, there's a "Toyota gap" argument: Anduril is going upmarket with billion-dollar integrated platform deals, defense primes sell $10M radar systems, and there's an opening for a $50K-$100K portable autonomous counter-drone unit that gets deployed at 100x the volume. The acoustic-AI approach is genuinely interesting — passive detection that doesn't emit RF signatures, which matters when your enemy is looking for your electronic emissions. And Khomenko being Ukrainian isn't just a credential, it's skin in the game at a visceral level that no Stanford MBA can replicate. If they're right that acoustic ML on edge hardware can detect and classify Group 1 drones cheaper and more portably than anything Anduril or DroneShield fields today, and if they can navigate DoD procurement as outsiders, this could be a real business. The "if" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

Where 9 Mothers actually scores well on my framework is structural durability. This is hardware. It requires ITAR compliance, government certifications, physical deployment, field testing in contested environments. No LLM is going to commoditize an actual weapon system. This is the Metropolis pattern — AI amplifies the product but the physical-world operations and regulatory barriers create moats that software alone cannot replicate. I respect that. If this were in a mispriced category with domain-expert founders, the durability alone would get me interested.

But it's not, and they're not. Fourteen active job postings at $150K-$250K salaries tells me this pre-seed company is already burning aggressively, and defense hardware is going to demand many more rounds of capital before a single DoD procurement dollar arrives. Government procurement cycles run 12-36 months *after* you have a working system, established relationships, and the right contract vehicles. This is a company that will absolutely need to raise again — probably multiple times — before revenue materializes. That's the factory farm model applied to hardware, which is worse than the factory farm model applied to software because the iteration cycles are longer and the feedback loops are slower. And the category is *not* creating a new primitive — counter-drone systems exist, acoustic drone detection exists, portable C-UAS exists. EDDA is a better version of things that already exist, competing on cost and form factor. That's an execution bet in a crowded category, not a new behavioral pattern.

The honest summary: real market, real co-founder bond, real physical-world durability, but consensus pricing, no domain expertise where it matters most, incremental rather than primitive-creating, and capital requirements that will stretch across many rounds. This is a bet I'd expect a defense-focused fund like Theory Forge to make — and they already did. It's not my kind of bet.
