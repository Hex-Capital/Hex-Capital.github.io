# Indexable -- Elad Gil Evaluation

E2B has raised $21M and claims 88% Fortune 100 adoption. Daytona has raised $24M and hit $1M forward ARR in under three months. Modal sits at a $1.1B valuation on $87M raised. Blaxel reports millions of daily agent requests on $7.3M in seed funding. That's $139M+ in combined capital already deployed into AI agent sandbox infrastructure, with at least two competitors showing real revenue traction. Indexable enters this market with roughly $500K from YC and a 3-person team. The first question I ask about any company is whether a structural shift is creating a window that consensus has underestimated — and here, consensus has not only recognized the window but has already funded five companies to climb through it. The AI agent sandbox market is one of the most obviously hot infrastructure categories in 2026. VentureBeat covered E2B's Series A, PRNewswire covered Daytona's, TechCrunch profiled Modal — this is not a market that needs to be explained to investors. Indexable's challenge is that the structural catalyst (AI agents needing sandboxed execution) is real but fully recognized, which places the market timing score well below what my framework requires for high conviction.

Indexable's custom VMM on KVM, with millisecond forking of full guest state — files, processes, memory, databases — is the deepest infrastructure play in this category and the strongest signal in the dossier. E2B, Daytona, and Blaxel all wrap Firecracker; Indexable builds its own hypervisor from scratch. The modified Linux 6.19 kernel with upstream-unsupported patches, the content-addressable storage engine with cross-tenant deduplication, the libfabric-based storage fabric enabling TCP-to-RDMA migration, and the Nix-in-Rust build system represent a genuine infrastructure-layer bet rather than an API wrapper over someone else's runtime. This maps to how I think about Stripe: Stripe didn't build on top of PayPal's API, it built the payments layer itself. Indexable is attempting the same architectural choice — build the actual execution layer rather than abstract over Firecracker. The content-addressable deduplication could eventually create a multi-tenant efficiency advantage as more tenants share common base images, which is a proto-network effect. Indexable scores strongly on infrastructure-layer positioning because it is building a platform that other companies would integrate into agent execution workflows, creating compounding switching costs. The tension is that every competitor in this space is also an infrastructure company, so the question is whether the deeper layer wins — and history suggests it sometimes does (AWS over hosting providers) and sometimes doesn't (higher-level abstractions beating raw infrastructure).

Andrew Gazelka's technical credentials are well-matched to the problem. Hyperion, his Minecraft engine supporting 10,000+ concurrent players with 1,000+ GitHub stars, demonstrates exactly the kind of high-performance networked systems engineering, low-latency state synchronization, and Rust expertise that building a custom VMM demands. His MTS role at xAI means he's seen AI infrastructure requirements at a frontier lab. The BountyBot acquisition, while small and undetailed, shows he can ship a product to exit. But Indexable's solo-founder structure concentrates key-person risk in a company whose technical surface area — custom hypervisor, modified kernel, storage engine, build system — is enormous for a 3-person team. I've seen great systems engineers struggle with the 1-to-100 transition, and nothing in the dossier indicates organizational scaling experience. Gazelka's background is that of a gifted IC, not an operator who has navigated a company through hypergrowth. For the single-miracle test, Indexable needs the custom VMM to deliver fork semantics that Firecracker-based solutions structurally cannot replicate — if it does, developer adoption follows capability, collapsing the distribution challenge into the technology challenge. That's one miracle, which is favorable. But maintaining a divergent kernel fork against upstream Linux's advancement is a recurring engineering tax that could consume the team's bandwidth, making execution on that single miracle harder than it appears.

The bull case deserves serious articulation: Indexable may be the only company in this space building at the right architectural layer for what comes next. If AI agent workflows evolve from single-turn execution to deeply branching exploration — where an agent needs to fork an entire running environment, try five approaches simultaneously, and roll back four of them — then Firecracker's checkpoint/restart model hits a fundamental wall. You can't truly fork a Firecracker microVM with full in-memory state; you can only snapshot and restart, which has different performance characteristics and semantics. Indexable's custom VMM could be the architectural foundation that next-generation agent frameworks require, and the five competitors with $139M+ are building on the wrong abstraction layer. This mirrors the Anduril pattern — legacy contractors (here: Firecracker wrappers) can't match the capabilities of a ground-up technical approach. But the Anduril analogy breaks down on timing: Anduril entered when defense tech was considered uninvestable by Silicon Valley, creating a nonobvious window. Indexable enters when AI agent infrastructure is the most consensus bet in venture capital. The absence of nonobvious market timing is what separates this from an Anduril-class investment.

Indexable's margin architecture is impossible to evaluate — no pricing page (ix.dev returns 403), no revenue data, no customer signals of any kind. The inferred usage-based model at market rates ($0.05-$0.08/vCPU-hour) would produce healthy software margins if the custom VMM doesn't require disproportionate infrastructure spending. But this is entirely hypothetical at this stage. What I can evaluate is the competitive pricing pressure: E2B at $0.083/vCPU-hour and Daytona at $0.0504/vCPU-hour are already establishing price anchors that Indexable will need to match or undercut, which compresses margin upside unless Indexable's fork semantics command a premium. The condition that would flip me to Invest is specific and verifiable: show me a major agent framework — LangChain, CrewAI, or an internal tool at an AI lab — that has attempted branching agent workflows on E2B or Daytona, hit architectural limitations of Firecracker's checkpoint/restart model, and validated that Indexable's true fork semantics solve the problem. That would shift the market timing from "obvious and crowded" to "everyone thinks the market is served, but the next generation of demand structurally cannot be met by current solutions."

Indexable's total score is driven by two dimensions pulling in opposite directions. Infrastructure-layer positioning is strong — a custom VMM is a deeper bet than any competitor, and the content-addressable storage with cross-tenant deduplication could compound into a real moat. But nonobvious market timing is weak — five competitors with $139M+ in combined funding, E2B's 88% Fortune 100 adoption, and extensive press coverage make this one of the most consensus infrastructure categories in 2026. My framework weights market timing highest for a reason: I've watched technically superior companies get outrun by better-capitalized competitors in obvious markets. Indexable's technology depth is genuine and impressive, but it's building the best mousetrap in a market where the trap buyers already have five options and are signing contracts. I need to see evidence that the current solutions are architecturally inadequate before this becomes an investment — without that, Indexable is a strong technical team arriving late to an obvious party.
