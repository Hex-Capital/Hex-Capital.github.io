# Vela -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive graveyard here tells you everything about the structural challenge. Clara Labs raised $12.6 million to build an AI email scheduling assistant, burned through it, and got acqui-hired by Twitter in 2019. x.ai raised over $100 million for essentially the same idea and shut down. Both failed because the NLP wasn't good enough -- you needed humans in the loop, which destroyed the unit economics. Now LLMs can actually handle multi-turn scheduling negotiation reliably, and Vela is taking another swing. The question isn't whether the technology works now -- it does. The question is whether LLM maturation creates a structural moment for a *standalone scheduling company* or whether it simply hands this capability to every incumbent simultaneously. When I backed Harvey, the insight was that model fidelity was being delivered by the foundation labs, which meant Harvey could focus on workflow, distribution, and enterprise trust in a $900 billion market. Here, the model fidelity is also being delivered by the foundation labs -- but so is the scheduling capability to Google, Microsoft, Calendly, and every ATS vendor. The asymmetry runs in the wrong direction.

My "software-driven vs. software-aware" test is the real issue. When Google ships conversational scheduling through Gemini integrated with Google Calendar -- which is architecturally trivial for them -- what happens to Vela's standalone value proposition? The multi-channel angle (WhatsApp, SMS, phone, Slack, Teams) is the strongest counter-argument, because Google and Microsoft each control only one or two channels. But multi-channel integration is an engineering effort, not a structural moat. Any well-funded team can build WhatsApp and SMS integrations. Compare this to Coinbase, where regulatory compliance created a multi-year barrier that protected the business while it scaled. Or PagerDuty, where deep integration into engineering on-call workflows created real switching costs. Vela's scheduling context data could theoretically compound into switching costs over time -- learning an organization's stakeholder priorities and scheduling preferences -- but scheduling preferences are far less sticky than financial relationships or operational runbooks. You can switch scheduling tools in an afternoon.

The bull case deserves real engagement. Paradox's acquisition by Workday at a $1.5 billion implied valuation validates that AI scheduling coordination in recruiting is genuinely valuable, and the acquisition itself creates a structural vacancy: non-Workday shops (which is most of the enterprise market) lose access to Paradox's capabilities as it becomes tightly coupled to Workday's ATS. If Vela can capture that displaced demand, the timing window is real and specific. The enterprise logos -- Netflix, LinkedIn, AWS, Scale AI -- suggest meaningful traction for a pre-seed company, and "thousands of interviews scheduled each week" is substantial volume that generates the kind of edge-case training data no new entrant can easily replicate. The founding team has shipped and deployed a working product with SOC compliance, which is unusual execution velocity at this stage. If the market evolves such that multi-party scheduling negotiation across disparate communication channels becomes its own category -- distinct from booking links (Calendly) and calendar optimization (Clockwise) -- Vela could own that category. The sibling co-founder pair with a long co-founding history (SWIFTmfi since age 14) actually mitigates co-founder risk, which I take seriously.

But here's what I keep returning to: scheduling coordination is a task, not a platform. Stripe started with payments but became the distribution channel for Atlas, Billing, Radar, and Treasury because every financial interaction creates an ongoing relationship. Figma started with design but became the distribution channel for collaboration because designers live in the tool all day. Scheduling is episodic and transactional -- you interact with the tool for 30 seconds, then you're done until the next meeting needs scheduling. There's no natural surface area for product expansion. The multi-channel integration layer could theoretically become a "communications orchestration platform" for recruiting teams, but I see no evidence the founders are thinking about this trajectory, and even if they were, that's a fundamentally different company than the one they're building. The product-to-distribution path is narrow.

The technology cycle positioning also concerns me. Yes, LLMs crossed a capability threshold that makes conversational scheduling negotiation viable without human fallbacks. But that same threshold was crossed for everyone simultaneously in 2023-2024. This isn't like Harvey building ahead of GPT-4's arrival -- that was anticipatory positioning where you could capture market share before the capability existed. Vela is building *after* the capability exists, which means they're competing in real-time against every AI-enabled calendar product and every new entrant who noticed the same opportunity. The dossier lists five funded competitors, and that likely understates the field. When the enabling technology is broadly available and the application is obvious (everyone understands scheduling is painful), you're not in a non-obvious market at an inflection point -- you're in a consensus market during a gold rush.

I respect the execution. The founders left elite programs (Wharton, UChicago Booth), shipped a multi-channel product, landed recognizable enterprise logos, and achieved SOC compliance -- all at pre-seed scale. Gobhanu's time at Perplexity gives relevant AI product intuition. But execution alone doesn't overcome market structure. The scheduling coordination market is neither boring-enough-to-be-overlooked (everyone knows scheduling is painful) nor structurally protected from incumbents who own the underlying calendar layer. This feels like a company that could build a solid business but faces a ceiling imposed by the feature-vs-product dynamic -- which historically leads to an acqui-hire outcome, not a generational company. Clara's path repeated.
