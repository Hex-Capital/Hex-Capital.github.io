# Mireye -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Mireye is a cleanly-governed pair of data engineers — Ansh Chokshi out of Seismic ML and Shashwat Kapoor out of Tools for Humanity and Lyra Health — selling an MCP wrapper over US federal geospatial data in a category where Google Maps Platform, Mapbox, CARTO, and xMap all shipped MCP servers in the same 2025-2026 window. The dimensional signature here is brutal: the one place Mireye scores above my default is that Chokshi is listed as CEO and Kapoor as Co-founder so I know who breaks the tie, and the place it scores lowest is that I cannot touch, ride, scan, or photograph a single thing Mireye has built. That pairing — clean cap table, intangible product, consensus category — is the exact silhouette of my Pass Example, not my Invest Example.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril (2017) is the HIGH rung on founder-lived-experience because he had already shipped Oculus from a garage before he ever pointed a camera at a border; the lived contact predated the company. The mechanism that makes Anduril load-bearing here is biography-as-permission-to-operate: Palmer could walk onto a defense customer because he had spent a decade inside the adjacent hardware culture. Mireye's dossier gives me Chokshi's Poker Pit at 21 doing $250K ARR pre-acquisition and Kapoor's six years of data-infra plumbing, both real accomplishments, but neither founder has an autobiographical relationship to terrain, land cover, USGS, NOAA, Census, parcel data, FEMA flood maps, or any other corner of the geospatial stack they propose to normalize. They are competent generalist data engineers who saw an MCP shaped hole. I read that as the analytical-not-lived founder profile, and on this axis it lands closer to my Pass Example than to Palmer.

HQ Trivia (2017) is the LOW rung on CEO clarity — Yusupov and Kroll never resolved the tiebreaker and the company unwound in feud. The failure mechanism was two people of equal stature with no documented authority. Tracing that pattern in Mireye, I find the opposite: the YC page names Chokshi CEO and Kapoor co-founder, and the absence of co-CEO language or 50/50 framing is itself a small positive signal. I have no operating-agreement detail and no founder-vesting disclosure, so I cannot push this above the upper-mid range, but Mireye is at least not pre-loading the HQ Trivia failure mode into Day 1.

Magically-weird, eighteen-month mechanism: MCP itself is a 2024 Anthropic standard, and by 2026 the dossier already documents CARTO MCP Server, Mapbox MCP Server, Google Maps Platform MCP grounding, and xMap MCP across 200+ countries. That is not a "find them before it becomes consensus" market; that is the consensus already arriving in Mireye's lane before Mireye has shipped a public customer logo. The mechanism that compounds against Mireye over the next eighteen months is not competition — it is that every incumbent has the same agent surface, deeper data, and existing developer distribution, so Mireye's wedge erodes weekly rather than monthly.

Capital-intensive and unsexy, counterfactual: for Mireye's score on this axis to flip a tier, the dossier would need to show Mireye operating its own data-collection layer — drone flights, LIDAR captures, parcel-licensing deals with specific counties, exclusive FEMA or USGS partnerships, anything where the moat compounds with time and capex. What the dossier actually shows is "federal-grade geospatial ground truth" sourced from public-domain US datasets that any team with comparable ETL can replicate. There is no Crusoe-style well-pad here, no Anduril-style hardware, no Mindbloom-style regulated wedge — just a software API over public data.

Niantic is my single anchor on physical-world verifiability: I bet on Niantic because I had been playing Ingress on my phone since 2012 and could feel the pull in my hand before any metric existed. Mireye is the photographic negative of that test. The dossier describes an API endpoint and an MCP server; a normal human cannot install Mireye, ride it, scan with it, or hand it to a friend at brunch. Even Flock Safety's camera on a pole was a thing I could photograph. Mireye is developer-tools abstraction whose value can only be evaluated via founder testimony or integration counts, and the dossier has neither.

Thirty-six-month forecast: Mireye either acqui-hires into Mapbox, CARTO, or a vertical insurance/real-estate platform for talent and the ETL pipeline, or it pivots out of horizontal MCP infrastructure into a single vertical — most likely property-and-casualty underwriting or wildfire/flood risk — where federal-dataset normalization is genuinely painful and a small team can own a customer. If Mireye is still horizontal in mid-2029, it is below $500K ARR and quietly winding down, because Google Maps Platform's MCP grounding will have eaten the developer-self-serve channel.

The bull case that should sting: Kapoor's six years at Tools for Humanity is not a generic data-eng line — Worldcoin's identity-and-location infrastructure is exactly the kind of high-throughput geospatial-adjacent plumbing that teaches you what incumbents get wrong, and Chokshi already proved he can build zero-to-revenue under his own steam. The MCP standardization wave is real, and the agent-side buyer wants a citation-bearing endpoint that Mapbox's display-first heritage struggles to serve cleanly. Maybe Mireye is the focused outsider that out-executes the incumbents the way small, focused teams always do. The dossier dismantles this for me though: zero named customers, zero LOIs, zero integration counts, US-only coverage against xMap's 200 countries, and four incumbents already in market with the same MCP surface. I am not seeing the founder-of-the-place signal that Crusoe had on its well pads. The single verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed pilot with a named insurance carrier, FEMA contractor, or wildfire-risk underwriter at a disclosed dollar figure — anything that proves Mireye is being pulled into a vertical where the data normalization actually hurts. Absent that, I pass and keep watching Chokshi for the next thing.
