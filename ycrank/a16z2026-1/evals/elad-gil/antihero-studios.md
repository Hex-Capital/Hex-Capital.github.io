# Antihero Studios -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Frank Yan driving Brawl Stars' revenue from its all-time low to 8.8x growth is one of the strongest single operator credentials I've seen at any stage of venture. Combined with Brice Laville Saint-Martin's creative direction on Clash Royale and Andre Parodi's decade as Senior Technical Director at King, Antihero Studios has assembled a founding team with direct operational fingerprints on eight separate billion-dollar-per-year franchises. That's not marketing language -- these are the people who shipped and sustained some of the most commercially successful mobile games ever made. If I were a games-focused investor, I'd be writing a check based on the team sheet alone. But I'm not a games-focused investor, and the reason why maps directly to how I evaluate market structure.

Start with what structural change makes Antihero Studios possible now versus two years ago. The company cites two catalysts: Apple's IDFA deprecation disrupting performance marketing, and the extraction shooter genre's validation on PC/console via ARC Raiders' $500M revenue and 14M copies sold. Neither passes my nonobvious test. IDFA changes landed in 2021 -- every mobile gaming investor, every mobile studio, and every ad-tech company has been adapting to post-IDFA reality for five years. That's consensus, not a structural window. The extraction genre's PC/console validation is real, but "proven genre on PC, bring it to mobile in a casual format" is the oldest playbook in mobile gaming. It's how Supercell, King, and dozens of studios built their businesses. There's no technology threshold being crossed, no regulatory opening, no cost curve collapse creating a new category. Antihero Studios is executing a well-understood strategy with exceptional talent -- but the structural timing is obvious, not nonobvious.

The infrastructure-layer test is where this opportunity structurally diverges from how I invest. MISFITZ is a single consumer game -- a free-to-play top-down extraction shooter for iOS and Android. There's no API other businesses build on, no developer tools, no platform layer accumulating switching costs. The aspiration to build Antihero as "a player-facing brand like A24 or Liquid Death" is creative, but A24 took a decade and hundreds of films to build that brand equity, and no mobile games studio has achieved comparable brand-driven distribution. Even the 750-creator program generating 250 content pieces during pre-alpha is marketing, not infrastructure. Players can leave for the next game with zero friction. This maps to my Wish/ContextLogic experience: consumer-facing, application-layer bets can generate explosive initial growth but lack the structural defensibility that protects value when growth decelerates. Wish collapsed 99% post-IPO because growth velocity without infrastructure positioning offers no floor.

The single-miracle count concerns me more than the team's ability to execute. Antihero Studios needs the extraction genre to translate to mass-market mobile audiences (Arena Breakout's $50.5M revenue came 95% from China's hardcore FPS players, not the casual Gen Z audience MISFITZ targets). Simultaneously, the company needs an organic/brand-driven distribution model to work at mobile scale without significant user acquisition spend -- the dossier notes no mobile game studio has accomplished this. And cosmetic-only monetization needs to generate meaningful revenue, which requires not thousands but millions of daily active players. That's at least two independent breakthroughs, arguably three. The pre-alpha engagement data -- 70,000 players, 50 minutes average daily playtime, and the genuinely striking stat that eliminated players were 6% more likely to restart than successful extractors -- demonstrates the team knows how to build compelling gameplay loops. But pre-alpha testers are self-selected enthusiasts. The miracle is whether this translates to mass-market retention and organic distribution without paid acquisition.

The strongest bull case for Antihero Studios is that the founder profile is so exceptional it partially compensates for structural concerns. Frank Yan didn't just work on Brawl Stars; he redesigned its monetization system and spoke at GDC about removing loot boxes, demonstrating the kind of principled game design that builds long-term player trust. Brice brings Supercell's creative and brand DNA. Andre has built technical infrastructure at King's scale (Candy Crush's hundreds of millions of players). The team has already attracted 13 people in 18 months, including hires from Ubisoft and Socialpoint. If any team can prove that a casual extraction shooter works on mobile AND that organic distribution can replace paid UA, it's this one. The $4.5M seed from a16z Speedrun and Laton Ventures is appropriate capital for proving these hypotheses before committing to scale spend.

But I keep returning to the competitive structure. Tencent operates Arena Breakout on mobile AND owns Supercell, which runs Brawl Stars -- the two closest competitors and the two most relevant distribution networks for exactly the audience Antihero Studios is targeting. If MISFITZ demonstrates that casual extraction works on mobile, Tencent can respond with functionally unlimited resources, existing player bases, and established creator relationships. The structural barrier the dossier identifies -- business-model cannibalization for incumbents -- is real but temporary. Tencent has historically been willing to launch competing products within its own portfolio when new categories emerge. The defensibility rests on execution speed and creator network, which are real but not structural moats. I pass on Antihero Studios because the two dimensions most central to my framework -- nonobvious market timing and infrastructure-layer positioning -- both score near the bottom of their ranges. The team is genuinely world-class, and the early engagement signals from 70K pre-alpha players are strong. But I've seen exceptional operators get crushed by market structures that don't favor new entrants, and consumer gaming against Tencent is one of the hardest market structures in technology. The absence of a structural shift and the absence of any infrastructure or platform layer make this the wrong shape for my portfolio, regardless of how talented the builders are.
