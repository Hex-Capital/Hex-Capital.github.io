# Maywood -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most telling thing about Maywood isn't what they're building. It's where they chose to sell it. Most AI founders in 2025 unconsciously filtered out investment banks as customers for the same reason most programmers in 2010 filtered out payment processing: the schlep is enormous. To get a working product inside an investment bank, you need SOC 2 Type II certification, single-tenant deployment, GDPR and CCPA compliance, and the patience to survive procurement cycles that can take longer than some startups live. Maywood already has the SOC 2 Type II. At pre-seed. That's not the kind of thing that happens by accident. It signals a team that walked into the regulatory thicket with their eyes open, which is exactly what schlep willingness looks like in practice.

Drake Goodman was at Blackstone doing PE deals. That's real proximity to the problem — he saw from the inside how M&A execution runs on manually assembled documents, copy-pasted figures, and eighty-hour weeks of formatting pitch decks and CIMs. The nuance is that Blackstone PE is buy-side, and Maywood's primary target is sell-side bankers who actually create the CIMs. Drake received these documents; he didn't grind through producing them at 2 AM. So the organic origin is genuine but slightly displaced — he saw the problem clearly from across the table rather than from the exact chair where the pain is worst. That matters because the deepest founder-problem fit comes from having personally suffered the workflow, not just observed it. Still, this is meaningfully different from a team that read a McKinsey report about AI in banking and decided to build something. The idea grew from actual deal experience, and the brother dynamic — Drake at Wharton/Blackstone, Kent at MIT doing neural stylistic transfer research — suggests the specific combination of finance domain knowledge and AI capability that makes the idea buildable by this particular team, not just conceivable.

The Stripe parallel is hard to ignore and I don't think it's superficial. Two brothers. Financial infrastructure. A schlep that competitors avoid. The Collisons walked into banks; the Goodmans are walking into banks. But there's an important difference in the idea's visibility. When Stripe launched, every programmer already knew payments were broken — the idea was hiding in plain sight due to schlep blindness. "AI for M&A documents" is not hiding in plain sight. It's a legible opportunity that any team reading about LLM capabilities could identify. I'd estimate at least a dozen teams have pitched "AI for investment banking" to VCs in the last eighteen months. That makes this more of an execution bet than a schlep-blindness bet. The schlep is real, but the idea isn't invisible — it's just hard to execute. Those are related but distinct advantages.

What would make this a great investment? If the firm-specific style learning creates genuine switching costs. Every bank has its own conventions for CIMs — fonts, language patterns, how they present comps, the rhythm of a management presentation. If Maywood gets embedded deeply enough that its outputs genuinely match a firm's voice after processing dozens of deals, that's a data moat that compounds over time. The customer testimonial claiming doubled deal capacity with the same headcount is exactly the kind of economic dependency signal I look for — if true, that bank literally cannot go back to manual workflows without halving their output. That's real pull. The problem is I can't verify it. "Several banks" without names, testimonials on the company website without attribution — at pre-seed this is normal, but it means I'm taking the claim on faith.

The technical team is credible on paper. Two MIT EECS graduates — Kent with AI research and Esteban as CTO with hedge fund AI experience — suggests the core product was built by founders, not outsourced. But I can't find a single public GitHub repo, technical blog post, or demo from any of the three founders. For an enterprise product selling to banks, proprietary code is expected and public repos would be unusual. Still, the hacker signal is weaker than I'd like. I want to see evidence of craft, not just credentials. The active hiring of AI researchers at $150-250K tells me they're investing in the technical core, which is the right priority.

My main hesitation is category heat. "AI for finance" is consensus exciting in 2026. The most impressive YC companies I've seen recently aren't working on AI at all. When every VC nods approvingly at your category, the upside has already been partially priced. Maywood's specific niche — generative document creation for M&A, not just another chatbot overlay — is narrower and more defensible than the broad category suggests. But Datasite has $2 billion in funding and is acquiring AI capabilities aggressively. The window for an independent company to build enough switching costs before an incumbent acquires or replicates is real but not infinite. This is a race, and races favor well-funded incumbents unless the startup's product is dramatically better. The early bank deployments suggest Maywood may be building that gap, but I'd want to see the product myself before being sure.
