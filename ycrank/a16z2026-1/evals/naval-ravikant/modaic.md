# Modaic -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Modaic's technical identity is captured in a single LinkedIn headline: Farouk Adeleke describes himself as "dspy infra." That phrase tells me everything I need to know about where specific knowledge lives in this company — and it's not inside Modaic. DSPy is an open-source Stanford framework with an MIT license. Building infrastructure on top of someone else's open-source project is renting leverage, not owning it. When I backed Perplexity, Aravind Srinivas had years of Google Brain and OpenAI experience building the exact retrieval-augmented systems that became the product. The knowledge was inseparable from the founder. At Modaic, the core optimization primitives are freely available to anyone who reads the DSPy documentation. Tyrin-Ian Todd trained transformer models at the MIT Media Lab and contributed to HuggingFace's datasets library — real ML competence, but general-purpose competence that thousands of engineers share. Neither founder has spent years inside enterprise decision automation, content moderation at scale, or confidence calibration systems. They identified DSPy as a framework worth building on, which is market analysis, not specific knowledge productization.

The principal-agent diagnostic raises a sharper concern. Farouk launched Spydr — "GitHub for LLM context" — on Product Hunt in June 2025, reaching #12 daily. Tyrin co-founded Dyle, an AI customer success agent. Modaic is the third distinct product between them in roughly twelve months. I have a specific filter for this: "If you're more passionate about founding a business than the business itself, you can fall into a ten year trap." Two prior ventures abandoned within a year signals founders searching for product-market fit across domains rather than founders who couldn't stop working on a particular problem. The co-founders met through the DSPy community, not through years of shared engagement with the problem of AI decision alignment. Compare this to Sahil Lavingia continuing to build Gumroad alone after failing to raise a Series A — that's a principal whose identity is fused with the work. At Modaic, the work follows the framework trend, not the other way around.

The leverage architecture has one genuinely interesting structural element: confidence-based routing that creates per-customer feedback datasets which improve model instructions over time. Every human correction compounds into better automated decisions. That's a data flywheel, and data flywheels are the closest thing to compound interest in software. But the dossier's own commoditization assessment is honest: "Any well-funded competitor could build a similar pipeline within 6-12 months." Braintrust, at $800M valuation with $116M raised, already counts Dropbox as a customer — and Modaic claims Dropbox as a "design partner." These unverified partnerships are the weakest form of traction signal. The architectural gap between observability (Braintrust's current product) and active optimization (Modaic's positioning) is real but not permanent. Braintrust has the capital and customer base to close it. Modaic's two-person team must outrun a competitor with fifty times the resources and an established enterprise sales motion.

The bull case requires believing three things simultaneously: that DSPy becomes the dominant paradigm for declarative AI programming, that Modaic captures the optimization layer before Braintrust or Patronus AI ($20M raised) extends into it, and that the claimed Accenture-Dropbox-Vercel partnerships convert into paying customers who generate enough feedback data to create switching costs. If all three prove true, Modaic occupies a genuinely new category — DevOps for self-improving AI agents — with per-customer data moats that deepen over time. The two-person team with pure code leverage could reach meaningful revenue without hiring, which is exactly the team-to-value ratio I look for. Todd's MIT AI & Decision Making background and Adeleke's production engineering experience at Wayfair are complementary skills for building this. The 116 GitHub stars across their org suggest real developer interest, even if modest.

Modaic's strongest dimension is team-to-value leverage — two technical builders shipping SDK, terminal agent, and RPC bridge simultaneously. That's the Cursor-style small-team-high-output pattern I've been tracking since 2012. But team efficiency without specific knowledge is execution speed on someone else's insight. Innovation built on open-source optimization primitives, without a proprietary knowledge layer that would take competitors years to replicate, ultimately produces consumer surplus — the value flows to the customer and eventually to whoever controls the underlying framework or the enterprise distribution channel. The serial pivoting, the DSPy dependency, and the unverified partnerships all point in the same direction: a competent team that has not yet found the problem they were born to solve. I pass.
