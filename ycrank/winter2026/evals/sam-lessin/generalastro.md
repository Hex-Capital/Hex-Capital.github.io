# General Astronautics -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The first thing I notice about General Astronautics is that the idea isn't insane -- it's hard. Those are fundamentally different things in my framework. Nobody hears "autonomous robots for microgravity labs" and says "that's crazy, why would anyone want that?" They say "obviously that's valuable, can you actually build it?" When I backed Venmo, people thought peer-to-peer mobile payments were a solution in search of a problem. When I backed Coinbase, regulated cryptocurrency exchange was an oxymoron to most of Silicon Valley. Those were insane ideas anchored by sane operators. Building lab robots for space stations is a consensus-hard engineering challenge with obvious economic justification -- the $130K/hour astronaut cost is in the pitch for a reason. Consensus-hard is a much worse bet for an investor than consensus-crazy, because consensus-hard attracts well-funded competitors the moment the timing is right, while consensus-crazy repels them until it's too late.

The founder pairing compounds this concern. Bram Schork's technical pedigree is genuinely impressive for his age -- winning international soft robotics competitions as a teenager, publishing in peer-reviewed journals, SpaceX Starlink hardware experience. Shibo Zhou brings CMU's robotics program, which is world-class. But both founders are essentially at or near graduation, with internship experience rather than full-time operator roles. The "sane" in my framework doesn't mean "smart" -- it means credibly battle-tested in a way that makes you believe they can execute the specific insane thing they're proposing. Selling robotic systems to NASA, Axiom, and Vast requires navigating multi-year government procurement cycles, building relationships with program managers who've been in the industry for decades, and demonstrating flight heritage that doesn't exist yet. Anatoly Yakovenko brought years of Qualcomm systems architecture to Solana. Brian Armstrong had shipped production systems at Airbnb. The gap between "talented engineering student" and "operator who can close seven-figure government contracts" is not trivial, and the customer base here is so concentrated that the sales motion is basically the entire business.

Here's where my framework gets interesting on this one, though. The physical-world moat analysis is genuinely strong. Robots operating in microgravity, handling fluids where surface tension dominates over gravity, performing delicate laboratory protocols without crew supervision -- this is exactly the kind of operational complexity that AI amplifies rather than commoditizes. You cannot replace a physical robot on a space station with an LLM. AI makes the autonomous control systems better, but the moat is in the hardware, the fluid dynamics engineering, the reliability requirements of operating where a technician can't just walk over and fix things. This is a legitimate "cherry on top" business -- it would be valuable without AI, and AI makes it better without enabling competitors to replicate it cheaply. That's the Craftwork pattern applied to orbit. If I squint, I can see the structural logic.

But the protocol-layer test kills most of my enthusiasm. General Astronautics is building an application on someone else's infrastructure. They don't own the station, the launch vehicle, the manufacturing process, or the customer relationship with pharmaceutical companies buying the output. They're a tool vendor in Axiom's or Vast's ecosystem. Compare this to Varda, which owns the entire vertical stack from spacecraft to reentry to pharmaceutical output. Varda is building a new manufacturing primitive. General Astronautics is selling picks to someone else's gold mine -- and that someone else might decide to build their own picks once the mine is proven. The platform-agnostic positioning sounds like a strength in the pitch, but in practice it means you're a commodity supplier to platforms that have every incentive to bring automation in-house once it becomes mission-critical. There's no network effect, no protocol lock-in, no compounding dynamic. Revenue scales linearly with bespoke procurement wins from a customer base you could count on one hand.

The bull case deserves serious engagement. If commercial space stations come online roughly on schedule -- Axiom's first module attaching to ISS, Vast and Orbital Reef building independently -- every single one needs lab automation, and General Astronautics could become the de facto standard through early deployment. The ISS deorbiting around 2030 creates genuine urgency. The microgravity fluid handling challenge is a real technical barrier that protects early movers. And the narrative is genuinely elastic: microgravity lab robots expand to orbital manufacturing automation, then to lunar and Mars surface operations. That's an infinity story with real physics underneath it. If you believe the founders can solve the microgravity manipulation problem and navigate procurement cycles despite their age, this is a generational space infrastructure opportunity at pre-seed pricing. What would have to be true: the founders would need to demonstrate flight-analog testing results within 12 months, secure a development contract with at least one station operator, and build relationships with NASA program managers who can champion their technology. Those are achievable milestones, but they're sequential dependencies in a domain with long timescales and a two-person team competing against GITAI's $129M war chest.

I'm passing. The founders are talented but too early in their careers for the sales motion this business requires. The idea is hard but not contrarian. The business is an application-layer tool vendor with extreme customer concentration and timeline dependency on third-party space stations being built on schedule. The physical-world moat potential is real and the narrative magnitude is compelling, but those two positives don't overcome the structural position problem: this company will always be building on someone else's platform, and the protocol-layer compounding I look for in my best investments isn't available here.
