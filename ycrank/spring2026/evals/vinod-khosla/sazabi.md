# Sazabi -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what strikes me first about Sazabi: this company enters a market where the puck has already arrived and five teams are fighting over it. LangSmith hit a $1.25 billion valuation. Arize raised $70 million. Langfuse got acquired by ClickHouse. Laminar came through YC two batches ago. Datadog -- a $50 billion public company with $2.1 billion in annual revenue -- is bolting AI observability onto its existing platform. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's CTO told me they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I invested in OpenAI, VCs thought AI was laughable. The signal I look for is experts dismissing the idea and outsiders not yet crowding in. With AI observability, the experts are not dismissing it -- they are racing to build it. That tells me the window for a category-defining outsider bet has likely closed, or at minimum, the entry cost has gone from one million dollars of option value to a bruising fight against well-capitalized incumbents from day one.

The consequence of success question is where Sazabi falls short of my threshold. If this company wins -- captures meaningful share of AI-native observability -- the result is a good SaaS business. Engineering teams debug AI-generated code faster. Incidents get resolved more quickly. That matters to CTOs. It does not matter to civilization. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. Observability is plumbing -- essential, valuable, monetizable plumbing, but plumbing. Datadog built a $50 billion company on this plumbing, and the world operates the same way it did before Datadog existed, just with better dashboards. I am not looking for the next Datadog. I am looking for the company that makes what Datadog monitors irrelevant.

The one element that caught my attention is the "logs are all you need" thesis -- the claim that AI can reconstruct metrics and traces entirely from logs, delivering three pillars for the price of one. If that is technically true at production scale, it does not just improve observability; it structurally undermines Datadog's pricing model, which charges separately for metrics, traces, and APM. That is the kind of economic disruption I care about -- making an incumbent's revenue architecture untenable. But this thesis is unvalidated. The product is waitlist-only. No public customers, no revenue, no evidence that the approach works at the scale and latency that production systems demand. KiOR taught me that a compelling technical thesis without validated unit economics is a trap. "Logs are all you need" is intellectually interesting. It is not yet evidence.

Sherwood Callaway is competent. He built observability infrastructure at Brex, founded Opkit and got it acquired by 11x, then led engineering on one of the largest agentic systems in production. That is an impressive operating resume. But it is precisely the resume of an industry insider executing the industry's established playbook. He is an observability engineer building an observability tool. He is an AI agent developer building AI agent monitoring. He knows what the field considers possible, which means he likely also knows what it considers impossible -- and may not attempt it. My highest-conviction investments have been in people who did not know what was supposed to be impossible: Pat Brown the biochemist making hamburgers, Peter Beck the New Zealander building rockets, Pradeep Sindhu the Xerox PARC scientist building routers. Callaway's angel investors from Anthropic, Vercel, LangChain, and Replit validate his network and credibility within the AI tooling ecosystem. But endorsement from the ecosystem is not the same as the ecosystem being wrong and you being right -- it is the ecosystem agreeing with you, which in my experience is a signal of incremental rather than disruptive ambition.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. AI coding agents are on an exponential adoption curve. Within two to three years, a material fraction of production code will be agent-generated. When that crosses a threshold, observability changes categorically -- you need to understand agent decisions, not just application metrics. If Sazabi defines the telemetry schema for agent decisions and that schema becomes the standard, the data moat and switching costs could be substantial. The SOC 2 and ISO certifications -- unusual for a ten-person pre-product company -- suggest they are engineering for enterprise readiness from the start. If the "logs are all you need" thesis validates, they could offer observability at a fraction of Datadog's cost with superior AI-era capabilities. That is a path to a five to ten billion dollar outcome. But even in this best case, I am funding a more efficient version of existing infrastructure, not a transformation of how the world works. The timing is favorable but consensus. The technology is interesting but reproducible. The founder is capable but not contrarian. This is a company that has thoughtfully reduced its probability of failure -- enterprise certifications, waitlist validation, ecosystem angel backing -- which is precisely the pattern that, in my experience, reduces the consequences of success to something inconsequential relative to the founder's talent.

I pass. Callaway is too smart to spend his career making monitoring dashboards better. He should find a problem that terrifies him.
