# Lance -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The Computer Use Agent approach — vision-based screen navigation of legacy hotel PMS software instead of API integrations — is the most architecturally interesting decision here, and it reveals both the opportunity and the constraint in one move. It's a leverage arbitrage: sidestep the integration bottleneck that kills vertical SaaS plays in hospitality, get deployed fast, build switching costs before the incumbents notice. Smart as a wedge. But a core technical differentiator that depends on the visual consistency of Oracle Hospitality's UI is not compounding technology. It's a temporary arbitrage on broken infrastructure. When the PMS vendor ships a redesign, Lance's automation breaks without warning. When the PMS vendor ships their own AI agent that uses their own APIs — the ones Lance doesn't have access to — the arbitrage collapses entirely. Contrast this with Stack Overflow, where every question and answer made the knowledge graph harder to replicate. Lance's technology gets more fragile with each PMS update, not more durable.

The specific knowledge question is where this investment stalls for me. Caleb Chan built voice and AI agent technology at Google and Salesforce — that's relevant, but it's knowledge acquired through the standard institutional pipeline. Any well-funded competitor can hire voice AI product leaders from Google. Gavin Brennen "grew up around Marriott operations" — this is proximity, not the kind of deep operational understanding that produces insights a hotel GM would find surprising. There's a meaningful difference between having been around hotels and having run hotel operations with the burden of a P&L, understanding why specific workflows break under pressure, or having a theory about guest communication that contradicts conventional industry wisdom. Gatik Trivedi's profile is the strongest: Stanford speech and language research, agent infrastructure work at GI Partners, LLM evaluation at Knowtex (YC S22), and a prior healthcare startup exit. But his specific knowledge is in AI systems, not hospitality. The team assembly is competent — AI product plus hospitality familiarity plus agent infrastructure — but it reads like three smart generalists who identified a good market, not domain obsessives who couldn't stop thinking about this problem. When I backed Twitter, Evan Williams had spent years inside the evolution of public communication. I don't see that depth of domain obsession here.

The leverage architecture has a ceiling. At the call-handling layer, yes — one AI voice system can answer thousands of calls with near-zero marginal cost. That's real software leverage. But deployment is heavier than it appears. Each hotel runs different PMS software, different operational workflows, different request routing. The Computer Use Agent must be adapted to each visual interface. Lance Pad requires on-property behavioral change — replacing walkie-talkies means changing how staff coordinate, which is a change management problem, not a software problem. This is implementation-heavy enterprise software disguised as a product demo. It doesn't create a new category of leverage the way Uber turned every car owner into an entrepreneur or Replit turned every browser into a development environment. Lance automates existing hotel call handling. That's incremental efficiency, not a new form of capability.

The bull case is real and I want to engage with it honestly: 50+ hotels across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands at pre-seed is genuinely impressive distribution. These are risk-averse buyers — landing them at all signals product-market pull that most pre-seed companies would kill for. The voice-first positioning exploits a structural gap: competitors like HiJiffy, Duve, and Akia built around text and chat, while hotels still run on phone calls. Being voice-native when the incumbents are text-native is a legitimate wedge — today. The staffing shortage is structural and won't self-correct, sustaining demand regardless of macro conditions. If Lance embeds deeply enough across hundreds of properties, the accumulated operational data — call patterns, request types, resolution workflows, property-specific quirks — becomes a compounding asset that new entrants can't shortcut. The path to a strong outcome runs through deep operational embedding plus data flywheel. If these three founders execute at the pace their early traction suggests, this could be a meaningful hospitality infrastructure company.

But I can't escape the timing problem. AI agents for vertical industries is the consensus category of this moment. Every investor agrees that voice AI will transform hotel operations. That agreement is already priced into the competitive landscape: Canary Technologies sits at $600M valuation with $180M raised and AI voice as a shipping feature across 20,000+ hotels. Duve has $85M and is expanding AI capabilities. When the well-funded incumbents are actively building what you're building with an order of magnitude more capital and distribution, you're not making a contrarian bet — you're entering an execution race. I got paid on Uber because ride-sharing was illegal in most cities when I invested. I got paid on Bitcoin because the consensus was that it was nerd money. Here, the consensus and Lance agree: AI voice will help hotels. The question becomes "why Lance versus Canary's next quarterly release?" — and that's a knife fight over execution speed and enterprise relationships, not a leverage play with asymmetric upside. The voice AI layer itself is commoditizing fast as OpenAI, ElevenLabs, and Vapi drive costs toward zero. Lance's value then compresses to the operational execution layer, which is the hardest part to sell and the hardest part to scale.

This is a capable team building a useful product in a market with genuine demand. The traction signals are strong for pre-seed. But the absence of deep non-transferable founder knowledge, the implementation-heavy deployment model, and the fundamentally consensus timing prevent this from matching the kind of bet I look for. I want companies where the founder knows something that can't be hired, the technology compounds with each user, and the smart money thinks they're wrong. Lance has hirable knowledge, fragile technology, and the smart money agrees with them. That's a fine business. It's not my kind of bet.
