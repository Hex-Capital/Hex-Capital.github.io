# Laurence -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Laurence is that the problem they're solving already has five well-funded companies attacking it. Pacvue does $50M in revenue. Quartile manages $2B+ in ad spend. Teikametrics has raised $65M. This is not a problem hiding in plain sight because of schlep blindness. Everyone can see it. Everyone is already working on it. When I look at a crowded category like this, the bar for entry shifts from "did you find a real problem?" to "do you have a structural reason to win that the incumbents can't replicate?"

How did the founders find this problem? Matthew Chen spent three months building software tools for Amazon Ads agencies before founding Laurence. That's deliberate market exploration -- going looking for problems in a growing category. The problem didn't find the founders. The founders went shopping for a problem. Now, the quant trading lens is a genuine insight. Leo Gierhake led research at Jump Trading overseeing billions in live trading volume. The recognition that Amazon ad bidding is structurally identical to stochastic optimization problems in quantitative finance is architecturally sound. This isn't just applying "AI" to an existing workflow. It's a real cross-domain transfer where knowledge from trading floors reveals an approach that e-commerce engineers wouldn't naturally reach. But an insight born from adjacent expertise is different from an insight born from personal pain. One gives you a technical edge. The other gives you the obsessive determination to grind through years of ugly work. The Collisons didn't just see that payments were broken -- they'd been frustrated by it as programmers for years before they walked into the banks.

The traction, however, is the strongest signal here and genuinely good for pre-seed. Ten million dollars in managed ad spend, up from $2.5M -- roughly 4x growth during what appears to be the YC batch period. Named clients: Because Market, Finn, Flings. Measurable results: 40% reduction in advertising cost of sales, 15-20% profit increases, and some clients reporting 2x sales growth. These are real businesses getting real improvements from a working product. That's evidence of wanting that you can't manufacture. And the advisor Aaron Apple -- who built and exited a top-75 Amazon 3P seller and ran a PPC consultancy managing eight figures in annual ad spend -- provides both domain credibility and a distribution channel into exactly the right buyers.

The bull case is clear: if reinforcement learning genuinely produces 40% better results than rule-based systems, the incumbents face a real architectural disadvantage. Pacvue is bootstrapped and profitable with no incentive to rebuild its core engine. Perpetua was absorbed into Ascential's corporate structure. Trading firms produce a specific kind of technical thinking -- comfort with stochastic processes, continuous decision-making, explicit profit constraints -- that's genuinely hard to hire for in the e-commerce software world. The data flywheel compounds as more managed spend feeds better models. And Amazon's ad market is heading toward $70B, so even a thin slice is enormous.

But here's what keeps me from conviction. The distance between "ML-based optimization" -- which Quartile already does -- and "RL-based optimization" is far smaller than the distance between manual spreadsheets and any form of automation. Quartile doesn't need a fundamental architectural overhaul to close this gap. They need to hire a few quant researchers. At $50M in revenue, Pacvue can afford that. When your moat is a computational technique rather than a network effect, a regulatory license, or years of accumulated operational schlep, you're in a race where proving the approach works is the same as showing competitors the blueprint. Compare this to Stripe: Stripe's moat wasn't the insight that payments should be developer-friendly. It was the grinding years of banking relationships, regulatory compliance across dozens of countries, and fraud infrastructure that nobody wanted to build. The ugly operational work was the moat. Here the schlep is computational, and computational schleps are exactly what well-resourced competitors tackle comfortably.

The founders are technically excellent -- Cornell Math/CS, Google, Meta, Jump Trading. The team includes founding engineers from Meta and a previous YC company. They're clearly hackers who build their own product, and the product is producing measurable results. That matters. But technical excellence deployed in a crowded market without an organic origin story is a configuration I've seen produce good companies, not great ones. The outlier returns come when the founders are working on something they would have built regardless of whether it was a good startup idea -- when the problem chose them, not the other way around. I don't see that compulsion here. I see smart people who recognized a growing market and brought a sophisticated approach. Sometimes that's enough. But I'm not writing a personal check on "sometimes."
