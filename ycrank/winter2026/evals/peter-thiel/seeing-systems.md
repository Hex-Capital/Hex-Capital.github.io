# Seeing Systems -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape surrounding Seeing Systems is the first thing I notice, and it tells me almost everything I need to know. Defense tech venture funding reached $49.1 billion in 2025 -- nearly doubling from the prior year. European defense venture capital grew 132% year-over-year. Stark has raised $100 million. Shield AI sits at $5.6 billion. Anduril, which Founders Fund backed precisely because it was creating a category that didn't exist, now commands $30.5 billion. And Ukraine produces four million drones annually. When every fund, every government, and every ambitious engineer on two continents is rushing into the same thesis -- "cheap drones are the future of warfare" -- you are looking at the textbook anatomy of mimetic competition. Everyone is imitating everyone else's conviction that this market is enormous and inevitable. The consensus nature of the thesis is the problem. A thesis that every defense minister, every VC, and every military commentator repeats on podcasts is not a secret. It is the opposite of a secret.

What, then, is Seeing Systems' specific insight? The modularity thesis -- that drone subsystems should be swappable rather than requiring full platform replacement as electronic warfare countermeasures evolve -- is the closest thing to a contrarian claim here. Most drone companies, including Stark with its fixed-design kamikaze platforms, build for manufacturing scale rather than field-level reconfigurability. If the EW environment truly evolves on a weeks-long cycle, and if modularity is the only architecture that survives that cycle, then fixed-design competitors are building products that are structurally obsolete upon delivery. This would be a genuine secret. But I'm not convinced it rises to that level. Modularity is an engineering design choice that any well-capitalized competitor can adopt -- it is not proprietary technology in the way that SpaceX's reusable rocket architecture was, where the physics and manufacturing learning curves created compounding advantages that widened with every launch. The company claims "2x lifecycle cost reduction," which is explicitly not the 10x threshold that creates monopolistic advantage. Two times better invites competition; ten times better eliminates it.

The strongest signal in this dossier is the traction relative to the team's size and capital. Two brothers from Guernsey, ages 25 and 28, with $500,000 in YC funding, have placed their hardware into the hands of UK Royal Marine Commandos and four NATO forces. They are shipping prototypes to Ukraine for iterative battlefield feedback. Meanwhile, Stark -- with two hundred times their capital -- failed all four strikes in UK military trials. This asymmetry is worth examining. It suggests either genuine technical superiority in the specific domain of field-deployable strike drones, or deep relationship capital with UK military operators that the brothers built through Alexander's prior EOD hardware work. The Forward Deployed Engineer model they've adopted is explicitly the Palantir playbook, and I know from direct experience that embedding engineers with end-users creates relationship lock-in and product-market fit that remote development cannot replicate. But Palantir's secret was not the Forward Deployed Engineer model -- the model was a tactic in service of a deeper insight about what data fusion could do for intelligence analysis. The tactic alone does not constitute a strategy.

The bull case for this company requires believing three things simultaneously: that modularity is not just a design choice but a structural moat that compounds over time as the company accumulates more module variants and more field data than competitors; that Aerie AI, the swarm coordination software currently listed as "in development," will become a genuine proprietary technology layer creating network effects across multi-drone deployments; and that early institutional trust with UK forces will convert into procurement contracts before the company runs out of runway. If all three prove true, this could become the defense equivalent of Stripe's early trajectory -- dominating a specific niche (UK expeditionary forces using modular FPV drones) and expanding concentrically to NATO allies. The fiber-optic guided variant of Banshee, which provides jamming immunity in EW-contested environments, is the most technically distinctive element and addresses a problem that RF-dependent competitors structurally cannot solve without adopting similar physics. If EW countermeasures continue accelerating -- and the Ukraine data suggests they will -- fiber-optic guidance becomes increasingly valuable while radio-frequency approaches become increasingly worthless. That is a genuine technological wedge.

But the fundamental question remains: does this company have a path to monopoly, or is it entering a market that will produce fifteen adequately funded competitors, each winning contracts from different NATO members based on national industrial policy rather than technical superiority? Defense procurement is notoriously fragmented by country -- Germany backs Stark and Quantum Systems, the UK backs domestic companies, France protects its defense industrial base. This fragmentation prevents any single company from achieving the kind of winner-take-all dynamics that create power-law returns. Palantir overcame this through software that was genuinely impossible for competitors to replicate, with integration depths that created multi-year switching costs. Hardware companies in commoditizing markets rarely achieve comparable lock-in. I need to see evidence that the Aerie AI layer creates the kind of software moat that transforms this from a hardware vendor into a platform -- and that software does not yet exist.

The Le Maitre brothers demonstrate real founder-market fit -- Matthew's Cambridge robotics research and Jane Street engineering discipline paired with Alexander's hands-on military hardware experience covers the full stack. Their traction is genuinely impressive at this stage. But impressive traction in a consensus market with a competitive structure that fragments along national boundaries does not satisfy my core investment criteria. I am looking for companies where success creates monopoly, and I do not see the monopoly mechanism here. The secret is not contrarian enough, the technology is not discontinuous enough, and the market structure militates against the kind of winner-take-all outcome that justifies power-law investing.
