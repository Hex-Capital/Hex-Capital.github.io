# Booko -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what stops me cold with Booko: I can't do the thing I always do first. My opening move with every founder is "tell me your story -- not the business." I want to know how you got here, what broke you, what forged you, what makes you the only person alive who should be solving this exact problem. With Booko, I'm staring into complete darkness. Neither Will Hall nor Arjun Saluja has a discoverable professional history, public profile, or any trace of domain background in dynamic pricing, machine learning, fitness, wellness, or booking platforms. The dossier couldn't even confirm whether the Arjun Saluja who attended Dartmouth and studied mechanical engineering at Columbia is the same person. This isn't a founder who is private -- this is a founder who is invisible. And my entire evaluation method depends on biographical transparency. When I can't see the person, I can't feel conviction about whether they'll survive the inevitable crisis. Every great investment I've made started with a founder's story that made the business make sense. Here, I have a business looking for a story.

The idea itself is the opposite of what gets my blood moving. Dynamic pricing for perishable time slots is not magically weird -- it's logically obvious. Airlines solved this in the 1980s. Hotels followed. Uber brought it to rides. The insight that a Tuesday 5pm yoga class is perishable inventory identical in economic structure to an unsold airline seat is correct, but it's the kind of correct that any MBA student would arrive at in a case study. Nobody is going to call this insane or dismiss it as impossible. It won't make other investors uncomfortable. And that's precisely why it doesn't trigger the signal I've learned to trust over 130+ investments. The companies that made my career -- SpaceX, Uber, Crusoe -- were ones where smart people told me I was losing my mind. Booko is a company where smart people would nod and say "yeah, that makes sense." In my experience, universal nods at the seed stage mean the opportunity has already been understood, and the question becomes pure execution against well-resourced incumbents.

I want to be honest about what would make me wrong here, because I've learned that my framework has blind spots. The bull case is real: Mindbody charging 20% plus 3.5% on every dynamically priced booking is a genuine structural vulnerability. That's a business model conflict where the incumbent profits from the fee rather than from the merchant's success, and a lean overlay player could exploit that gap the way Affirm exploited hidden credit card fees by offering transparent pricing. If these founders have a personal connection to this pain -- if one of them watched a family member's studio fail because they couldn't fill off-peak slots, or if they built the ML models out of frustration with their own booking experience -- that biographical thread would change everything. The product itself shows surprising technical maturity for a two-person pre-seed team: per-client ML models, SOC 2 and HIPAA compliance, API integrations with major platforms, and a claimed 94.2% prediction accuracy. Someone with real capability built this. And 20% revenue uplift, if it holds across a meaningful customer base, is the kind of value proposition that sells itself. The fitness and wellness vertical alone is worth billions, and every adjacent service business -- tax accountants, tutors, consultants, event venues -- faces the same perishable inventory problem.

But even granting all of that, the defensibility concerns keep me at arm's length. Booko sits as a pricing overlay on top of booking platforms it doesn't control. Mindbody could restrict API access tomorrow, or simply lower its 20% fee to 5% and eliminate Booko's structural advantage. The per-client ML models create some switching costs, but they're moderate -- a competitor with booking data access could retrain in weeks. There's no network effect, no data moat that compounds across clients (their own DPA prevents cross-client training), and the core algorithmic challenge of predicting slot utilization from historical patterns is well-understood. This is a company whose moat is execution speed, not technical differentiation -- and execution-speed moats erode quickly when incumbents with $500M in revenue decide to pay attention. The Carta comparison illustrates what I look for: Carta made cap tables transparent in a way that created a genuine data network effect across the entire startup ecosystem. Booko makes pricing more efficient for individual businesses, but each implementation is isolated.

The economic access dimension gives me a small pulse of interest -- if studios discount off-peak slots by 4-9%, price-sensitive consumers get access to fitness and wellness they couldn't otherwise afford. That's a real, if modest, version of the pattern I saw in Uber creating income for drivers and Affirm opening credit access. But it's a secondary effect of a pricing optimization tool, not the company's animating mission. Nobody at Booko seems to be on fire about democratizing access to wellness -- they're on fire about revenue uplift percentages. And without being able to see the founders' stories, I can't tell whether that fire has the kind of biographical fuel that sustains a company through its darkest moments.
