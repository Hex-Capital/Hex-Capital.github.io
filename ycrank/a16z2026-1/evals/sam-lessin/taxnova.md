# Taxnova -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Taxnova is building an AI tool that automates R&D tax credit preparation by pulling data from Jira, GitHub, and accounting systems. Let me say the quiet part loud: every Big 4 partner reading that sentence just thought "we should build that." EY, BDO, Grant Thornton — these firms literally invented R&D tax advisory as a service line. Their consultants wrote the rules, trained the auditors, and bill $200-400 an hour doing exactly what Taxnova automates. The team even lists EY, BDO, Grant Thornton, and IRS alumni as their domain experts. So Taxnova hired people from the incumbents to build software that the incumbents will inevitably build themselves. The "advisor-agnostic" positioning — working alongside advisors rather than replacing them — is a tell. When your go-to-market strategy is "we're a feature for the people who will eventually compete with us," you're building on rented land.

The mispricing test is brutal here. SPRX/Onshore just closed a $31M Series B in February 2026. Boast.ai has eight-figure revenue and 1,000+ customers on $30M raised. Neo.Tax raised $13M back in 2022. The market has loudly, repeatedly validated R&D tax credit automation as a category. Every sophisticated investor already sees this. Taxnova is entering the US market — the biggest prize — with "coming soon" on their website while competitors with 10-40x more capital have multi-year head starts. There is no narrative mispricing to exploit. The risk isn't in the price because the upside already is. Compare this to how I got into Solana at four cents — Ethereum consensus had priced alternative L1s at zero, creating a massive gap between market perception and underlying reality. With Taxnova, the market perception and reality are aligned: R&D tax automation is a real, growing, competitive market. No gap, no asymmetry.

George Nichkov and Maria Malykh are clearly competent operators — Oliver Wyman, Gett, Criteo backgrounds, strong B2B instincts. $100K ARR on $1M raised with named customers like incident.io and Paddle is genuinely solid execution at pre-seed. The Top 5 advisory firm pilot shows they can sell to enterprise. But here's my problem: nothing about this idea is crazy. "We use AI to automate a manual, data-heavy professional services workflow" is the single most consensus idea in B2B SaaS right now. Every YC batch, every accelerator, every angel syndicate is seeing twenty versions of this pitch applied to different verticals. When I say I want a sane person pitching an insane idea, I mean the ratio matters. Nichkov and Malykh are sane — but R&D tax automation is also sane. Sane-sane is boring. Nobody's going to tell them "what you're doing is fucking crazy" because it's obviously sensible. Obvious sensibility is the enemy of asymmetric returns.

The AI kill test is where Taxnova falls apart structurally. This is a software tool that generates text documents (technical narratives) from structured data (Jira tickets, Git commits) using LLMs, then applies jurisdiction-specific tax rules. The jurisdiction-specific logic is real complexity — the UK HMRC inquiry rate jumping from 0.5% to 20% means audit defensibility matters — but it's codifiable knowledge, not a physical moat. Metropolis seeds parking infrastructure that no LLM can replicate. Patreon built direct creator-audience financial relationships that compound with usage. Taxnova's 15 read-only integrations and tax rule engine are exactly the kind of workflow automation that Deloitte's innovation lab ships as an internal tool. The "data accumulation" defensibility story — learning qualification patterns over time — is the same story every AI SaaS company tells, and it's usually worth about six months of head start before the next entrant trains on the same data patterns.

The strongest bull case for Taxnova is the multi-jurisdiction wedge. If SPRX, Neo.Tax, and Boast are all US-only or US-Canada, and Taxnova builds deep compliance logic across UK, EU, and Asia-Pacific before anyone else, they could own the "global R&D tax" category for multinational tech companies — the highest-ACV customers. The $200K+ enterprise pilot ACV supports this positioning. For this to work, Taxnova would need to be right that multi-jurisdiction is a genuine moat rather than a feature that well-funded competitors add in 18 months, and that the advisory firm channel creates real distribution leverage rather than a dependency that evaporates when those firms build their own tools. The UK HMRC audit spike is a real "why now" — but it's a regulatory tailwind that benefits every player in the space, not a structural advantage unique to Taxnova.

Capital discipline is Taxnova's relative bright spot: $100K ARR on a $1M raise, team of five, lean burn. That ratio of traction-to-capital is respectable. But discipline born from having $1M isn't the same as discipline born from philosophy. The question is what happens when they need to compete with SPRX's $46M war chest for US enterprise customers — does the discipline hold, or do they join the fundraising treadmill? At this stage, I can't distinguish between "built to survive" and "hasn't yet faced the temptation to blitzscale." The deciding dimensions here are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability. The market is right about this category and has already priced the opportunity into well-funded competitors. The product is a software workflow that incumbents can and will replicate. Competent team, real traction, wrong kind of bet for me.
