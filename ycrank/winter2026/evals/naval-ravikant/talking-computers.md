# Talking Computers -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is that Gartner reports a 1,445% surge in multi-agent system inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025. CrewAI has raised $24.5M. Relevance AI has raised $24M. Microsoft merged AutoGen into its enterprise Agent Framework with GA targeted for Q1 2026. Google launched the A2A protocol. Anthropic launched MCP. This is not a market where you get paid for being right first. This is a market where consensus has already formed, capital has already arrived, and the incumbents are building the exact infrastructure layer that Talking Computers claims to occupy. When I invested in Bitcoin in 2014, people thought I was an idiot. When I invested in Uber, ride-sharing was illegal. Nobody thinks multi-agent AI is stupid right now -- they think it's the future. Which means the asymmetric upside has already been compressed into the price of every deal in this space. A two-person pre-product startup entering a category where Microsoft has effectively unlimited resources is not a contrarian bet. It's a consensus bet with unfavorable odds.

The specific knowledge question is where this falls apart for me. Parsa has systems programming skills in Rust, compiler development background, an internship at TextQL, and contributions to package management tooling. Zayaan has ML inference optimization experience from Modular and a stint at Yutori, an AI agent startup. These are genuinely strong technical credentials for Waterloo undergrads. But they are teachable credentials. Rust programming, GPU optimization, TensorRT -- these are skills you can acquire through courses, internships, and open-source contribution. What I don't see is non-transferable knowledge about how AI agents should coordinate. Neither founder has spent years inside the problem of multi-agent communication -- they've spent internship rotations adjacent to it. The question I always ask is: what does this founder know that a smart generalist couldn't learn in six months? I can't find the answer here. When I backed the Notion founders, Ivan Zhao had spent years obsessed with the relationship between tools and cognition -- that obsession couldn't be replicated by hiring a senior PM. These founders have relevant skills, but skills and specific knowledge are not the same thing.

The bull case would require believing that agent-to-agent communication is a genuinely new coordination problem that needs a neutral, vendor-agnostic platform layer -- something like what AngelList did for startup fundraising, creating infrastructure that no single player had incentive to build. If Facility becomes the interoperability standard that sits above Google's A2A, Anthropic's MCP, and Microsoft's Agent Framework, letting heterogeneous agents from different providers coordinate in a shared workspace, that could be an infrastructure play with real network effects. Every additional agent type that connects makes the platform more valuable. Every workflow pattern observed creates data advantages. And the incumbents -- Google, Microsoft, OpenAI -- each have platform lock-in incentives that make it structurally difficult for them to build truly neutral interoperability layers. That's a real opening. But for this bull case to work, the founders would need to demonstrate deep understanding of protocol design, distributed systems coordination, and enterprise workflow patterns -- the kind of specific knowledge that would make their approach non-obvious and hard to replicate. The dossier shows no evidence of that understanding, and the website is a title page.

The pivot signal concerns me. Parsa's GitHub profile still lists him as "CTO @ Speedtrain (YC W26)" as of March 2026, suggesting a name change or pivot during the batch. Pivots are fine -- Twitter itself started as Odeo. But a pivot combined with no public product, no traction signals, no customer data, and a placeholder website tells me the founders are still searching for their specific insight into this space. They haven't yet found the non-obvious truth about agent communication that would justify building here rather than contributing to one of the existing frameworks. The product name "Facility" and the tagline "making computers talk" are evocative but generic -- they describe the category, not a specific architectural opinion about how the category should work.

I want to invest in companies where the founder's specific knowledge produces a contrarian architectural bet that compounds over time. Here, I see competent early-career engineers entering the hottest consensus category in tech, competing against the best-resourced companies in the world, with no public product and no evidence of non-teachable domain insight. The technical skills are real but replicable. The market is real but crowded. The timing is not contrarian -- it's peak consensus. This is a pass.
