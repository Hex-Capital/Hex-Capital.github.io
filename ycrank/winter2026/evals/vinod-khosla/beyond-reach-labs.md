# Beyond Reach Labs -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Beyond Reach Labs is a tension that tells me everything about how to evaluate them: $175 million in letters of intent against a two-person team that hasn't flown hardware in orbit. Either the demand signal is screaming so loudly that customers are lining up before the technology is proven -- which would be extraordinary -- or these LOIs are the vapor that fills every space startup's pitch deck. I've seen both. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck had no aerospace pedigree and no flight heritage, but the demand for small-launch was structural and undeniable. The question here is whether orbital power demand is equally structural or whether the "500 GW by 2035" projection is a fantasy number designed to make a component business look like a platform opportunity.

Let me start where I always start -- the consequence of success. If Beyond Reach Labs works, what changes? Satellites get more power per kilogram launched. That's meaningful, but it's an enabling component, not a system-level transformation. Compare this to Impossible Foods, where success meant reclaiming half the planet's arable land, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means replacing fossil fuel power generation entirely. Here, success means better solar arrays for spacecraft. The consequence scales up only if you believe orbital data centers, commercial space stations, and lunar outposts are coming at massive scale -- and that belief requires Starship economics to materialize, orbital computing to make economic sense versus terrestrial alternatives, and sustained government commitment to lunar infrastructure. The consequence of success is derivative: it depends on an entire speculative ecosystem materializing. That's not disqualifying -- I've bet on ecosystems before -- but it means the magnitude of the prize is conditional in a way that a direct industry disruption is not. The current spacecraft solar panel market is $600 million. Even the broader space power market is under $7 billion by 2034. These are not trillion-dollar systems being replaced. They're components in a supply chain that might become enormous, or might not.

The technology itself is genuinely interesting. Metamaterial-based geometry change achieving a 50x expansion ratio while maintaining structural rigidity -- that's a different paradigm from Redwire's roll-out blankets or Northrop Grumman's fan-fold designs. If the PETS and HERDS architectures deliver what the prototype suggests, they don't just improve existing deployable arrays; they make the current length-stiffness-mass tradeoff irrelevant. That's the kind of disruption I look for: not 20% better, but structurally different. The NASA NIAC grant, zero-g flight testing, and peer-reviewed publications establish that this isn't just a PowerPoint claim. But -- and this is where KiOR's ghost haunts me -- technology achievement without demonstrated unit economics at production scale is a trap I've personally funded to the tune of $160 million. The 50x expansion ratio works in a prototype and in zero-g parabolic flight. Whether it survives thermal cycling, atomic oxygen, radiation, and the vibration profile of an actual launch remains unproven. The demo flight in Q2 2027 is the gate, and until it's through, this is a science project with patents.

What gives me pause from my framework is the founder profile. Collins spent seven years at SpaceX leading Dragon parachute systems. Fogelson completed a PhD at CMU specifically on deployable space structures. These are deep domain insiders, not outsiders bringing fresh eyes to a calcified industry. My pattern has been that the most transformative companies are built by people who don't know what the experts consider impossible -- Pat Brown in food, Peter Beck in aerospace, the OpenAI team in AI. Collins and Fogelson know exactly what the aerospace industry considers impossible because they've lived inside it. That said, the complementarity is strong: one founder invented the technology, the other knows how to build and qualify flight hardware through the design-fly cycle that kills most space startups. They've known each other thirteen years. The advisor network -- AFRL Chief Scientist, MIT professor who co-advised the PhD -- maps precisely to the government-buyer channel they'll need. This is a deliberately constructed team for this specific problem, even if it's not the outsider profile I instinctively prefer.

The bull case is real and I want to articulate it clearly. If Starship drives launch costs to $100/kg or below, orbital computing economics shift fundamentally, and every satellite constellation operator suddenly needs ten to fifty times more power than current arrays deliver. In that world, the company that solved the deployment problem owns a critical bottleneck in space infrastructure -- the equivalent of owning the power grid for a new industrial revolution. The incumbents (Redwire with a $226 million net loss, Northrop Grumman with a $40 billion parent that moves slowly) may be structurally unable to cannibalize their existing product lines fast enough. The $175 million in LOIs, if even partially real, suggests sophisticated buyers already see this future arriving. And the timing -- DARPA's NOM4D program, multiple commercial space stations in development, Starship approaching operational flights -- suggests we're at the inflection point where patient technology development meets explosive demand. This is the Rocket Lab pattern: back the hard-tech company before the market validates it, and if the market arrives, the returns are extraordinary.

But I keep coming back to the core question. Is the consequence of success large enough to justify the attempt at this scale of ambition? This is a component business targeting a sub-billion-dollar market today, betting that the market grows ten to a hundred times. My best investments have been in companies where success transforms how humanity does something fundamental -- how we eat, how we generate energy, how we think. Better solar arrays for satellites, even dramatically better ones, don't reach that threshold unless you squint hard at a speculative future. I'd take the meeting. I'd want to see how much these founders have evolved their thinking since joining YC, and whether the LOIs represent genuine procurement intent or the non-binding interest that every space startup collects. But at this moment, the consequence magnitude is conditional on too many external variables for me to write a check with high conviction.
