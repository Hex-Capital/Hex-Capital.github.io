# Alchemy -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice is what's missing from the story. Baek studied biomedical engineering at UT Austin and did EP mapping at Boston Scientific. McBride got an MS in CS from UIUC and filed a patent at 19. Both of these are respectable backgrounds. But when I look for the moment where one of them was hunched over a microscope at 2am, manually annotating cell images for the third night in a row, thinking "this is insane, there has to be a better way" -- I can't find it. Neither founder has documented experience in bioimage analysis, computational biology software, or research microscopy. Baek's work is adjacent -- medical devices, cancer research -- but adjacency and lived experience are different things. The idea pattern here looks like two smart people combining their respective expertise (biomedical domain + ML engineering) and landing on "AI for microscope images" through a process that resembles brainstorming more than organic discovery. I've seen this pattern many times. It's how smart people generate plausible-sounding ideas that look good on a YC application but lack the founder-problem fusion that sustains you through years of painful iteration.

The schlep factor cuts both ways. On one hand, life science image analysis does involve real complexity -- diverse imaging modalities, the need for validated and reproducible pipelines, the trust barrier where researchers won't cite AI-generated results unless they're confident in the accuracy. That's genuine difficulty. On the other hand, this isn't Stripe-level regulatory schlep. The hard part of this market isn't the analysis technology -- capable segmentation models like Cellpose and StarDist are freely available -- it's the distribution. Academic researchers have been using ImageJ since 2004 and CellProfiler since 2005. These tools are free, grant-funded, deeply embedded in published protocols, and carry the institutional trust that comes from thousands of peer-reviewed papers. Convincing budget-constrained labs to pay for a SaaS tool when free alternatives exist is its own form of schlep, and I see no evidence these founders understand or have engaged with that challenge yet.

The bull case requires you to believe three things. First, that the timing is right -- vision-language models now make natural-language pipeline assembly genuinely feasible for the first time, which is probably true. Second, that Baek's biomedical background represents deeper domain knowledge than what's visible publicly, and that the organic discovery just isn't documented. That's possible. Third, that bottom-up adoption in research labs can work like Slack spreading through engineering teams -- one grad student tries it, gets results in five minutes instead of twenty hours, and the whole lab switches. If all three are true, the structural gap between open-source tools (no commercial entity to build agentic UX) and enterprise platforms (locked to microscope hardware sales) is a real opening. A company that threads that needle could build meaningful workflow lock-in as labs embed Alchemy pipelines in their published methods sections. But three simultaneous assumptions is a lot. Each one individually is reasonable; the conjunction is speculative.

What concerns me most is the competitive landscape layered on top of the organic discovery gap. PathAI has raised $255 million. Leica/Danaher already acquired an AI microscopy company (DRVISION/Aivia) in 2021. The open-source ecosystem is vast and free. When you're entering a market this crowded without the specific domain experience that would give you an unfair advantage in understanding what researchers actually need -- not what a market report says they need, but what they reach for at the bench -- the odds are steep. McBride's technical credentials are solid (patent at 19, UIUC MS in CS), and I'd bet the product works. But a product that works and a company that wins are separated by the same gap that separates most YC applicants from the ones that break out. The co-founders have no visible shared history before this company, which is another flag -- though at pre-seed with a two-person team, I weight this less heavily than I normally would.

The "20 hours to 5 minutes" claim on their YC page is marketing. I need to see the researcher who tells me this unprompted. The dossier surfaces zero public traction signals -- no customer counts, no revenue, no press, no Product Hunt launch, no accessible website content. At pre-seed I don't expect much, but I expect something. Even a single named lab running on the platform would change my read.

I'm passing. The idea is reasonable but doesn't have the organic roots or schlep magnitude that would make me want to bet my own money. When I funded Dropbox, Drew Houston could describe the exact moment he forgot his USB drive on the Chinatown bus. When I funded Stripe, the Collisons had been programming since childhood and had personally suffered through payment integration. Here, I see competent founders who landed on a sensible market, not founders who are building something they can't not build.
