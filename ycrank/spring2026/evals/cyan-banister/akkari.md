# Akkari -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Jeffrey Byun and Henry Lee are on their third company together, with Michael Moore — who ran a 30-person GTM team and 2,500 contractors at OrderAhead — owning exactly the operational seam Akkari is now selling into. That is a real founder-market fit signal, the kind I do not get to see often at pre-seed. The problem is that Akkari is pointing this lived operator experience at the single most consensus arena in YC Spring 2026: B2B AI agents, where Decagon already prints a $4.5B mark and Sierra a $15.8B mark. So the diagnosis for Akkari writes itself — right people, wrong arena for my book.

Palmer Luckey at Anduril is the high rung on lived founder experience: he had shipped Oculus before he ever touched defense, and that was the load-bearing reason I tattooed that conviction. Akkari does not ride that high — Byun's MIT MBA and Morgan Stanley fixed-income path is the pedigree pattern I usually screen against — but Moore is the closest thing in this dossier to a Luckey-shaped artifact. He actually ran the scattered-commitments-across-Slack-and-email problem at OrderAhead at the scale of 2,500 contractors. That is autobiographical contact with the problem, not a memo. Akkari diverges from Luckey because the OrderAhead/BLADE pair is operator-credible, not garage-weird; Byun and Lee already had Coinbase and SV Angel on a $4.3M BLADE round in 2019, which is a different texture than a founder nobody will return a call to.

The pass example — two ex-FAANG PMs, four-logo team page, oversubscribed AI-agent seed — is the low rung on magically weird, and Akkari sits uncomfortably close to it. Fidji Simo, Tony Xu, Eric Wu, Othman Laraki, Tuesday, Liquid 2, Orange Collective: this is the angel stack of a deal that everyone in San Francisco has already heard about. Crusoe in 2018 cleared this dimension by having three credible VCs pass on aesthetic grounds before I wrote — Akkari has the opposite signal, with Fidji Simo (OpenAI) anchoring a round in a category Sierra just marked at $15.8B in May 2026. The thesis is to find them before it becomes consensus, and customer-ops AI agents became consensus roughly eighteen months before Akkari incorporated. I am never playing the game I seem to be playing — and the game Akkari seems to be playing is the one everyone is already at the table for.

On CEO authority, Akkari's mechanism is that Byun/Lee have now co-founded three companies together — OrderAhead, BLADE, Akkari — which is a stress-tested governance artifact, not a Day-1 promise. The HQ Trivia 2017 failure mode was two founders who had never argued and lost; Byun and Lee have argued through a Square acquisition and a $1.2B-monthly-volume crypto derivatives platform together. Moore as third co-founder with a clearly distinct operator lane reduces the 50/50 tiebreaker risk further. This dimension is not where Akkari breaks.

If a polymath generalist could rebuild Akkari in a quarter, the defensibility comes from the model, not the founders — and the score flips down a tier. The dossier admits no public moat signals beyond the angel list and gestures at "per-account memory could compound" as inferred. For Akkari to break a tier higher on the unsexy-regulated dimension, the product would need to attack customer ops inside a regulated vertical — clinical trial sponsors, regulated finance back-office, defense supplier ops — where the autonomous-execution accuracy bar is enforced by a regulator, not by a CSM's patience. Nothing in the akkari.io feature list suggests that turn; "drafting emails, creating Linear/GitHub tickets, provisioning access" reads as horizontal software-only.

Compare Akkari's physical-world verifiability to Niantic, where I had been playing Ingress since 2012 before I wrote the check — I could feel the artifact in the park. Akkari sits behind akkari.io/auth with an access-request gate, no public demo, no observable artifact in the wild, no Product Hunt launch. I cannot install it, hand it to a friend, or photograph someone using it on a sidewalk. That is the developer-tools-abstraction failure mode the rubric warns me about, and the dossier does not give me anything tactile to override it.

My 36-month forecast: Akkari reaches $4–12M ARR with mid-market customer-ops buyers, gets acquired by Salesforce, HubSpot, or Decagon itself for $80–250M as a talent-and-team acquihire, and returns 2–4x to angels at YC-priced entry — a fine outcome for Byun and Lee's third exit, an unremarkable outcome for the seed sleeve. The mechanism is that the consensus arena gets settled by the capitalized incumbents (Decagon $361M+ raised, Sierra $950M Series C) before Akkari can build per-account memory deep enough to compound switching costs, and operator-quality teams in saturated AI categories tend to exit horizontally rather than break out.

The strongest bear-case-against-my-forecast — the bull case — is the DeepMind exception: Akkari is a polymath operator team where Byun + Lee have already shipped two companies together, one acquired by Square in 2017, and Moore owns the exact GTM-ops scar tissue the product is solving. Repeat founders in hot AI categories do produce Together AI–shaped outcomes, and Fidji Simo's involvement is real distribution into OpenAI's enterprise wedge. I dismantle it on the consensus signal: Akkari is post-consensus on dimension two, and my own thesis says aesthetic-passes-by-credible-VCs is the entry signal, not Fidji Simo signing the SAFE. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a named enterprise lighthouse — DoorDash via Tony Xu, Opendoor via Eric Wu — running Akkari on a regulated or revenue-critical workflow with published execution-accuracy data inside twelve months. Until then, this is a pass with respect.
