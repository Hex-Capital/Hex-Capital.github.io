# Hub.xyz -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hub.xyz is that it is trying to serve two masters, and I have rarely seen that work. On one side, they pitch enterprise AI teams: "the API for real-world training data." On the other, they operate a crypto-token-incentivized bandwidth network where users earn IQ Points redeemable for $HUB tokens at a future TGE. One of these is an enterprise infrastructure business. The other is a DePIN speculation game. When your primary user acquisition mechanism is airdrops and Discord engagement campaigns, you are not building the next great data infrastructure company -- you are building a token distribution event with an API bolted on. I have watched this hybrid identity fail in cleantech (promise the mission, fund it with carbon credits, lose the thread) and I have watched it fail in crypto. The enterprise buyer evaluating training data APIs does not care about your token economics, and the crypto-native node contributor does not care about your SLA guarantees. These are fundamentally different customers with different motivations.

Now let me address consequence of success, because that is always my starting question. If Hub.xyz wins -- completely, dominantly wins -- what changes? Enterprises get training data from a distributed bandwidth network instead of Scale AI's human annotators or their own web scraping pipelines. AI models train on fresher, more geographically diverse data. That is a real value proposition, and the market is growing at 22% CAGR toward $16 billion. But this is a supply chain optimization for an existing process, not a transformation of how the world works. Nobody's industry collapses. No trillion-dollar system becomes obsolete. The consequence of success is that Hub.xyz becomes a good data vendor in a growing market. I tell founders: if you are too smart to waste your career on a problem this small, find something that matters at civilization scale. AI training data sourcing is important, but it is a means to an end, not the end itself.

The competitive landscape is what makes this particularly uncomfortable. Grass has raised $17.5 million and sits at roughly a billion-dollar valuation running essentially the same model -- distributed bandwidth sharing for AI training data. Nodepay has 1.5 million active users, triple Hub.xyz's 500,000 nodes, on $7 million in funding. There are 600 DePIN projects as of late 2024. This is not a contrarian bet where the experts dismiss you and the outsiders have not crowded in. This is a consensus category with well-funded competitors already ahead. When I backed Juniper, Cisco's CTO publicly declared they would never build a TCP/IP router. When I backed Impossible Foods, the food industry thought plant-based meat was a joke. Where is the equivalent dismissal here? The market has already validated the approach, priced it, and funded three versions of it. Hub.xyz is not skating to where the puck will be -- the puck is already there, and multiple players are already fighting for it.

The strongest bull case I can construct: AI training data becomes the critical bottleneck for frontier models, centralized web scraping becomes legally untenable due to the wave of copyright lawsuits in 2024-2025, and user-consented distributed collection emerges as the only legally defensible sourcing model. In that world, Hub.xyz's 500,000 nodes and API-first positioning could make them the standard interface between AI labs and real-world data. The regulatory tailwind is genuine -- when OpenAI and others face billion-dollar lawsuits over training data provenance, a consented-collection model has structural legal advantages. If Hub.xyz could prove that their structured, multimodal output is meaningfully superior to Grass's raw bandwidth contribution, and if they convert enterprise AI teams into paying API customers, there is a path to a significant business. But even in this optimistic scenario, I see a competitive data vendor, not a category-defining company. And critically, no enterprise customers, LOIs, or revenue are publicly documented -- the demand side of this business is entirely hypothetical.

The team composition raises further concerns. One co-founder, Léonie Nguyen, was appointed CEO of Meey Group, an entirely separate Vietnamese proptech company, in October 2025. Her operational involvement with Hub.xyz is unclear, but taking the CEO role at another company is not a signal of commitment. The remaining founders have industrial engineering and business school credentials -- respectable but not the outsider profile I look for. There is no deep AI/ML technical founder visible; they are actively hiring a "Founding Full Stack AI Engineer," which tells me the core technical capability is not yet on the team. For a company whose entire value proposition depends on data structuring and AI pipeline quality, this gap is structural, not incidental. I spend more time helping my portfolio companies recruit than on anything else in my calendar because getting the first ten hires right is everything -- and this team has not yet assembled the technical core needed to win a data quality competition against better-funded competitors.
