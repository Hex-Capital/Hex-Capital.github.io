# Zatanna -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The pivot trajectory tells me everything I need to know about how this team calibrates ambition. They started with lawtech focused on jury trials -- a consequential problem in a system that hasn't been redesigned in centuries. Then they moved to healthcare admin agents -- a sector that consumes $4.3 trillion in the US alone, where administrative waste is a genuine crisis. And they landed on... scraping infrastructure. They moved *down* the consequence ladder with each iteration. A team that had the instinct to attack healthcare and legal systems ultimately chose to make web scraping 10x faster. I understand why -- scraping is a cleaner technical problem, the demand signal is immediate, and the product-market fit search is more tractable. But this is precisely the pattern I warn founders about: reducing the probability of failure until the consequence of success becomes inconsequential. If Zatanna captures the entire web scraping market -- every dollar of a ~$1 billion TAM -- nobody's life changes. No industry is disrupted. No physical system is transformed.

The strongest bull case requires you to believe that AI agents will explode the scraping TAM by 10-100x, and that protocol-level infrastructure becomes the critical bottleneck. If every AI agent needs real-time web data to function, and browser-based scraping is too slow and expensive to serve millions of concurrent agent requests, then Zatanna's sub-500ms, sub-1MB-per-request architecture becomes essential plumbing for the agent economy. In that scenario, you're not investing in a scraping company -- you're investing in core infrastructure for the next computing paradigm. There's a version of this that is genuinely consequential. But Zatanna hasn't articulated this vision; their website describes faster scraping, not agent infrastructure. And Firecrawl -- YC-backed, $14.5M raised, 81K+ GitHub stars, open-source with community momentum -- is already positioned on this exact curve with far more distribution. Bright Data at $300M ARR could acquire this capability in a quarter. The window for a pre-seed company to own this category is narrow and closing.

The technology itself is real but not novel. Protocol-level HTTP scraping -- replicating TLS fingerprints, reverse-engineering authentication flows, mimicking browser traffic at the request level -- is a known technique used by data brokers and sophisticated in-house teams. Alex Blackwell's experience at Pikkit on anti-bot bypass is genuine domain expertise, and the performance claims (6-20x speed improvement, 200x memory reduction versus headless browsers) represent meaningful differentiation. But each integration requires bespoke reverse-engineering per target site, and each breaks when the target updates its defenses. This is a maintenance treadmill that scales linearly with coverage, not a compounding technology moat. It's closer to a services business with a software wrapper than to infrastructure that achieves network effects or economies of scale.

What I'm not seeing here is a founder who is attempting something the consensus considers crazy. Nobody dismisses web scraping as impossible -- it's a proven market with established players and known techniques. There's no outsider bringing first-principles thinking to an entrenched industry. These are capable engineers building a technically superior version of an existing product category. The team composition is sensible: a CTO with anti-bot domain expertise, a CEO with backend and fintech experience, a data science co-founder. But sensible team construction for a modest opportunity doesn't meet my bar. I invest in problems too important not to work on. Faster web scraping is important to developers, but it's not a problem that demands a solution the way eliminating animal agriculture demands one, or the way fusion energy demands one. These founders are too smart for a problem this contained -- the healthcare pivot they abandoned had a hundred times the consequence magnitude of where they ended up.
