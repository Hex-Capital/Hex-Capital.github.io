# Unifold -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that immediately catches my attention here is the origin arc. These founders built wallet-as-a-service infrastructure at Streambird, had it acquired by MoonPay, helped onboard 30 million users across platforms like Phantom and Polymarket — and then came back to attack the problem they saw still wasn't solved. They didn't brainstorm this idea. They built the adjacent layer of the stack, shipped it at scale, and watched from the inside as on-chain deposit flows remained fragmented and painful. That's organic discovery of the purest kind. A prediction market that's reportedly top-3 in weekly transactions behind Kalshi and Polymarket reached out to them — not the other way around. When the customers come find you before you launch, something real is happening.

The schlep here is genuine and multi-dimensional. Cross-chain routing alone is tedious. Add gas abstraction, compliance checks integrated with centralized exchanges, per-user deposit address provisioning, and settlement across dozens of different blockchains — each with its own protocol quirks — and you have the kind of ugly infrastructure work that makes most crypto developers unconsciously look away. This is structurally similar to what made Stripe work: thousands of programmers knew payment processing was broken, but dealing with banks and fraud and regulation was a schlep nobody wanted to touch. Here, thousands of crypto developers know deposit flows are broken, but dealing with chain-specific integrations and compliance and gas mechanics across an ever-expanding landscape of L1s and L2s is the schlep. The "Stripe for crypto deposits" framing isn't just marketing — it has structural merit.

The technical team is exceptionally strong for a three-person pre-seed company. Timothy Chung has 231 GitHub repos and a CS background from Cambridge and Columbia. Quang Huynh was the fourth engineer at Instabase and has six years building crypto and fintech products. Hau Chu has a dual MS/MA from Cornell Tech and was a senior blockchain engineer at both MoonPay and Solana Labs. All three are builders. They've already shipped SDKs across React, React Native, iOS, and Android. This isn't a PowerPoint — it's a working product built by people who've done this before. Two of the three co-founders have worked together across multiple companies (Streambird, Polymarket, Instabase), which is the kind of co-founder relationship durability I look for.

The most serious concern is MoonPay. The founders' prior company was acquired by MoonPay, which means MoonPay now owns the wallet infrastructure these founders originally built, has $757 million in funding, and has existing developer relationships that could be leveraged to build a competing on-chain deposit product. On paper this looks devastating. But there's a classic innovator's dilemma dynamic here: MoonPay's business model is optimized for fiat-to-crypto conversion margins (up to 4.5% per card transaction). Building on-chain deposit orchestration would require a fundamentally different technical stack while potentially cannibalizing their existing revenue streams. Large incumbents rarely make that pivot cleanly, especially when their incentives point elsewhere. The fact that these founders left MoonPay to build this suggests they saw from the inside that MoonPay wasn't going to solve the on-chain problem — and that insight, earned through direct experience, is worth something.

The traction is thin but directional. Private beta, no revenue, a free tier with no paid pricing. But the inbound from a top-3 prediction market is a real signal — that's a company processing meaningful transaction volume that identified Unifold as solving their problem. Named early adopters Alpha Arcade and Lofty.ai (a YC S19 company), plus ecosystem partnerships with Algorand and MegaETH, suggest the developer-led GTM is getting traction with the right audience. The worry is vertical concentration in prediction markets, which face regulatory uncertainty. If Unifold can expand beyond that initial wedge into DeFi protocols and fintech platforms more broadly, the deposit orchestration problem is genuinely horizontal.

This is the kind of company I invest in: founders who discovered the problem through lived experience, tackling a multi-dimensional schlep that deters competition, with the technical chops to build it themselves. They've already had one exit. The co-founders have a durable working relationship. The risk is real — MoonPay could wake up, and crypto markets are cyclical — but the organic founder-problem fit and the depth of the schlep tip me toward writing a check.
