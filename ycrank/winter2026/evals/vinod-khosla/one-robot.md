# One Robot -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's what I notice first: these founders built robots at Industrial Next, hit a wall where evaluating a new policy took longer than training it, and decided to build the tool that solves that specific bottleneck. That's excellent engineering instinct. It's also exactly the kind of pattern that produces a useful product and a modest company. They've identified a real pain point, scoped it tightly, and are executing against it. Most investors would call this disciplined. I call it a consequence-of-success problem. If One Robot succeeds completely -- if they become the dominant simulation layer for VLA evaluation -- what changes? Robotics teams iterate faster. That's valuable. It's not civilization-scale. The virtual robot simulator market is $1.6 billion growing at 6.6%. Even if you believe the broader $35 billion robotics simulation number, One Robot is capturing a slice of a slice -- task-specific world models for contact-rich manipulation among teams training VLAs. They've narrowed their aperture to reduce failure probability, and in doing so, they've constrained the consequence of success to something I'd describe as a solid business, not an industry transformation.

The technology is genuinely interesting. Learned world models versus hand-crafted physics engines is a real paradigm shift for robotics simulation, especially for deformable objects and contact-rich tasks where MuJoCo and Isaac Sim consistently fail. Sixty-second autoregressive rollouts from a single wrist camera input -- if that works reliably at the fidelity needed for policy transfer -- is a capability that didn't exist eighteen months ago. The architecture is correct: physics engines will never capture the complexity of textile manipulation or sub-millimeter assembly the way learned models trained on real robot data can. I respect the technical insight. But here's the problem: NVIDIA Cosmos is free and has 3 million downloads. World Labs just raised $1.23 billion. The learned-world-model approach is not One Robot's secret -- it's the direction the entire field is heading. Their differentiation is task-specific data and integration depth, which is real but fragile. That's a consulting moat, not a technology moat. Each customer engagement requires custom data collection and model training. That doesn't scale the way a platform does, and it doesn't compound the way a network effect does.

The bull case is worth examining honestly. VLAs for industrial robotics could be where large language models were in 2019 -- tiny today, exponential tomorrow. Physical Intelligence's Pi 0.5, NVIDIA's Gr00t, the flood of research -- the rate of change in VLA capability is accelerating. If VLAs become the standard approach for manufacturing manipulation within three years, every robotics company in the world needs a simulation layer, and One Robot has first-mover advantage with the deepest domain expertise. The founders lived this pain point since 2020 at Industrial Next. Hemanth has the ML depth (Georgia Tech, JPL, Google, published robotics research) and prior entrepreneurial experience (bootstrapped Crescer AI to profitability). Elton built production AI infrastructure at Tesla during the Model 3 ramp and on Dojo. They've worked together for years. If the VLA ecosystem explodes, One Robot is positioned at the bottleneck. That's a real argument. But it requires the VLA market to expand by an order of magnitude, world models to prove superior to improving physics engines, and a two-person team to outrun NVIDIA's platform and World Labs' $1.23 billion. Three simultaneous conditions, each uncertain.

What concerns me most is the insider dynamic. Both founders are deeply credentialed in robotics and ML -- Tesla, Google, NASA JPL, Georgia Tech. They know what the robotics industry considers hard and what it considers feasible. That knowledge makes them effective engineers and gives them immediate customer access. It also gives them the biases I've watched kill ambition at dozens of companies: they know the boundaries, so they build within them. Task-specific world models for manipulation evaluation is the thing their industry peers would nod at and validate. When the experts validate your approach, you're usually solving a problem at the scale the experts consider reasonable. Compare that to backing someone who says "we're going to make physical robot testing entirely obsolete for all of robotics" -- that's the kind of statement that gets you laughed out of a robotics conference and gets you funded by me.

The timing trajectory is the strongest dimension here. The VLA curve is real and accelerating. They're building before the market has fully formed, which is when I like to enter. But I enter early markets when the consequence of success is transformational -- when the company, if it works, restructures how an industry operates. One Robot, if it works, makes VLA development 10x faster for a small number of robotics teams. That's consequential for those teams. It's not consequential for the world. I'd want to see these founders expand their ambition -- not just faster evaluation of manipulation policies, but becoming the synthetic reality layer that makes physical prototyping obsolete across all of robotics. That's a different company with a different consequence of success. As currently scoped, this is a well-built tool with a narrow ceiling.
