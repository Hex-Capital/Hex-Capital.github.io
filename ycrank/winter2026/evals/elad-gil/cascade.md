# Cascade -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The competitive landscape here tells a clear story, and it's not the one Cascade needs. Braintrust raised $80M at an $800M valuation in February 2026. Raindrop raised $15M from Lightspeed in December 2025 with a stated approach -- "training small, custom models that adapt to the unique shape of each AI product" -- that is functionally identical to Cascade's description of training custom evaluator models from production data. Arize has $131M in total funding with enterprise clients including Uber, DoorDash, and the U.S. Navy. Meanwhile, ClickHouse acquired Langfuse, Snyk acquired Invariant Labs, Anthropic acqui-hired HumanLoop, and Coralogix acquired Aporia -- all in the past year. When I look at this landscape, I see a category that has already moved past the formation stage and into consolidation. That's a structurally different market from what a pre-seed two-person team can attack.

The "why now" for AI agent reliability is real -- agents are moving to production, Gartner's projection of 40% enterprise application embedding by 2026 is directionally correct, and the failure modes (hallucinations, tool call errors, planning breakdowns) are genuinely different from what traditional APM handles. But this is exactly the kind of structural moment that is visible to everyone. When I invested in PagerDuty, operations alerting seemed boring and niche -- investors couldn't see why it mattered. When I backed Harvey, legal AI looked like a science project. AI agent observability in 2026 looks like exactly what it is: critical infrastructure for the next wave of enterprise AI. Every investor I know can see this market. That's the problem. My best returns come from markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors. This market triggers excitement.

The founders have relevant technical credentials. Haluk's production monitoring experience at Netflix and Amazon combined with BAIR Lab research on failure mode taxonomies for AI agents is a strong pairing for this problem domain. Adam's BAIR Lab work on agentic safety and graph reasoning maps directly to the product. As a founder-market fit signal, this is above average -- they've worked on both the infrastructure side and the research side of the problem they're solving. But founder-market fit without a distribution edge or a differentiated technical approach is necessary but not sufficient. The dossier describes Cascade's approach as "training custom evaluator models from production behavior" -- which, as noted, Raindrop describes in nearly identical terms with 30x more capital.

The strongest bull case would require believing two things. First, that the AI agent observability market is large enough to support multiple winners -- a reasonable argument given the projected market size and the fact that traditional monitoring has Datadog, New Relic, and Splunk coexisting. Second, that Cascade's specific implementation of custom evaluator models creates a meaningfully different product than what Raindrop, Braintrust, or Arize deliver -- perhaps the BAIR Lab research on failure mode taxonomies translates into a genuinely novel evaluation architecture that competitors haven't built. If Cascade has developed a technique for closed-loop learning from agent traces that produces measurably better detection of planning breakdowns and memory drift, that could create real switching costs through a data flywheel. The "boring but critical infrastructure" pattern could emerge here -- where Cascade embeds so deeply into production agent workflows that it becomes the reliability layer enterprises can't remove. That's the PagerDuty path. But PagerDuty didn't face five well-funded competitors executing similar approaches when it started.

The product-to-distribution trajectory is theoretically present -- agent observability could expand into evaluation, safety compliance, governance, and eventually become the trust infrastructure layer for all enterprise AI. That multi-product arc exists in the market structure. But there's no evidence the founders have articulated this expansion path, and the website didn't render substantive content at the time of research. No public product, no documentation, no demos, no customers. For a company in YC W26, this level of pre-product visibility makes it difficult to assess execution velocity. The GitHub repo referenced on the founder's personal site returns a 404. These aren't disqualifying signals at pre-seed, but combined with the competitive dynamics, they don't provide enough evidence to override the market structure concerns.

I'm passing. The structural moment is real but consensus, the differentiation is stated but unproven, and the competitive landscape has already consolidated around better-funded players executing similar approaches. This isn't a company requiring multiple miracles -- the single miracle of building better custom evaluator models than competitors is coherent. But it's entering a fight where the opponents have orders of magnitude more resources, and nothing in the dossier tells me why Cascade wins that fight.
