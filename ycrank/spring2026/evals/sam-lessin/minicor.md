# Minicor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are all shipping computer-use capabilities directly into their foundation models — and Minicor is building a managed layer on top of those exact APIs, claiming 93–96% click accuracy versus the base models' 80–85%. This is the single most important fact in the dossier, and it's the fact that kills the deal for me. Minicor is an AI application-layer company sitting directly in the blast radius of foundation model improvement. Every quarter Anthropic ships a better computer-use model, Minicor's accuracy gap shrinks. Their own website acknowledges the 80–85% baseline — they're measuring themselves against a moving target that's moving toward them. This is the "Adobe for AI" problem I keep banging on about: the platform providers are the real incumbents here, and Minicor's orchestration and self-healing layer is a feature they'll absorb, not a company they'll acquire.

Browserbase just raised $40M at a $300M valuation for browser infrastructure. UiPath is public. Automation Anywhere has raised $6.8B+. "AI-powered RPA" is one of the hottest consensus categories in enterprise software right now — every VC with a pulse is looking at this space. Minicor's desktop-and-Citrix focus is marginally less fashionable than browser automation, and I'll give them a sliver of credit for choosing the dirtier, less sexy corner of the problem. But "slightly less consensus within a wildly consensus category" is not mispricing. The risk is not baked into the price here — the price reflects the hype. When I seeded Solana at four cents, the entire market had written off non-Ethereum L1s. Nobody has written off AI automation. Quite the opposite: everybody's writing checks.

Faizaan Chishtie and Saheed Akinbile are clearly competent — University of Ottawa software engineering grads who co-founded Software For Love together, meaning they have a genuine pre-existing relationship, which I always look for. Saheed's Ford and Morgan Stanley stints touch target verticals. Faizaan has prior YC experience from Phoenix (W21) and shipped Homeboard to mid-five-figure ARR. These are sane, capable engineers. The problem is that what they're building is also sane. "Make RPA self-healing with AI agents" is exactly what every smart enterprise software person on the planet thinks should exist. Sane people, sane idea — that's the wrong ratio. I get excited when a very reasonable person tells me something that sounds fucking crazy and then calmly explains why it's inevitable. Minicor's pitch sounds... obviously correct. Everyone agrees. That's the problem.

Minicor's one customer processing 25,000 patients per day is genuinely impressive traction for a pre-seed company, and I don't want to dismiss it. The bull case writes itself: Epic and Cerner are actively restricting API access, healthcare systems are locked into legacy Windows desktop applications, SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance create real barriers for competitors, and the on-prem/Citrix deployment model creates switching costs once workflows are embedded in production. Six customer logos on the website. A pivot from Laminar that was driven by encountering the actual problem in the field — that's the right reason to pivot. If I'm constructing the strongest case for Minicor, it's this: healthcare legacy system integration is a $35B+ market where the pain is real, growing, and vendor-driven, and Minicor got there first with compliance credentials. The Metropolis parallel tempts me — Metropolis used AI to manage physical parking infrastructure that can't be replicated by software alone, and you could argue Minicor is managing "digital infrastructure" (legacy desktop systems) that also can't be easily replaced. But the parallel breaks down: Metropolis owns or operates physical parking assets. Minicor wraps API calls to someone else's AI model and points them at someone else's software. The thing underneath them can change at any time.

Minicor's structural durability is where the deal truly falls apart. The legal entity is still Laminar Run, Inc. — they pivoted recently, which means current-product traction is early and the prior Laminar integration platform metrics don't transfer. More critically, Minicor's differentiation versus raw foundation model computer-use is an accuracy gap (93–96% vs. 80–85%) and a managed infrastructure layer. The accuracy gap is a temporary artifact of model capability curves. The managed infrastructure — observability, orchestration, compliance — is valuable but not venture-scale defensible. Any well-funded company can get SOC 2 and HIPAA certification. And here's the vendor countermeasure risk the dossier flags: Epic has a long history of controlling its ecosystem, and the 21st Century Cures Act is already forcing EHR vendors toward interoperability. If Epic opens APIs — even partially — Minicor's entire wedge evaporates. The one condition that would flip me to Invest: if Minicor showed me that they'd built proprietary workflow data from their 25,000-patient-per-day customer that creates a compounding accuracy advantage the foundation models can't replicate — a data flywheel, not just a wrapper. I see no evidence of that in the dossier.

Minicor scores low on the two dimensions I weight highest: consensus mispricing and primitive creation. This is a better version of existing RPA in the hottest AI category in enterprise software — it's not a new primitive, it's not mispriced, and it sits squarely in the path of foundation model commoditization. The founders are solid but the idea lacks the insanity I need to get excited. Capital discipline looks fine at pre-seed — five people, real customer, no signs of bloat — but that's table stakes, not a reason to write a check. Pass.
