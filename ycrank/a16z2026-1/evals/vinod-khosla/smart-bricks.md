# Smart Bricks -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Smart Bricks self-reports a ~$12M revenue run rate on a $5M pre-seed raise, eighteen months after founding. If true, that would be extraordinary — a capital efficiency ratio that would make any investor look twice. But the metric is unverified, its definition is unspecified (gross transaction value? platform fees? ARR?), and no independent source — not TechCrunch, not a16z's own announcement, not a single press outlet — corroborates it. I have not made IRR calculations on a spreadsheet since 2004 because spreadsheets are fiction. Self-reported traction numbers without independent verification are the same species of fiction. When a company's most impressive data point cannot be confirmed, I set it aside and evaluate what remains.

What remains is an AI wrapper for real estate investing. Strip the buzzwords — "AI-native computing stack," "autonomous agents," "1,000+ variables per property" — and Smart Bricks is applying existing LLM capabilities to automate real estate due diligence for wealthy investors in Dubai, London, New York, and Miami. The dossier's own commoditization risk section states it plainly: "LLM-based property analysis is technically reproducible; the core models use publicly available foundation models." Mohamed Mohamed told Wamda that competitors "are building on top of a tech stack, but this product has built the stack itself." Building a data aggregation pipeline across 1M+ feeds is engineering work, not a scientific breakthrough. There is no step-function technology here — no novel architecture, no proprietary model, no physics insight. EliseAI has raised $392M and runs at $100M+ ARR in adjacent proptech. Cherre has raised $130M for CRE data management. Smart Bricks is entering a category with well-funded incumbents and no technical wedge that makes it 10x better rather than incrementally different.

The consequence question kills this for me before I even get to technology. If Smart Bricks fully succeeds — captures every real estate investor in its target markets — what changes? Wealthy people find investment properties faster. Portfolio returns improve by some margin. Transaction cycles compress from months to minutes. Fine. But nobody's life fundamentally changes. Contrast this with Impossible Foods, where success means feeding billions sustainably at lower cost, or Commonwealth Fusion Systems, where success means energy too cheap to meter. Smart Bricks' success means a better Bloomberg terminal for property investors. The company explicitly targets golden visa programs and premium markets. This is not democratization — it is optimization for the already-privileged. When I look at the affordability endgame, Smart Bricks moves in the wrong direction: more efficient investor acquisition in top-tier markets could drive property prices up, not down. The consequence of success is a profitable fintech company, which is a fine outcome for most investors but not the kind of bet I make.

Mohamed Mohamed's resume — Blackstone, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey AI practice, BCG, LBS — is exactly the profile I discount. When I backed Peter Beck at Rocket Lab, he was a self-taught engineer from New Zealand building rocket engines in his garage. When I backed Mach Industries, the founder was a 19-year-old MIT dropout. Mohamed is the establishment: top consulting firms, top banks, Forbes 30 Under 30. He is applying known frameworks from institutional investing to a new medium. That is domain expertise, not first-principles thinking. I see no evidence of pivots, no description of what surprised him, no signal of rapid learning or intellectual irreverence. The company has 20 people and no named co-founder, no CTO, no identifiable technical lead in any public source. Key-person risk is elevated, and the gene pool of the founding team — the single most important decision — is invisible.

The bull case would require three things to be true simultaneously: the $12M revenue figure is real and represents genuine platform revenue, the proprietary data feeds compound into an unassailable moat over time, and the platform genuinely democratizes real estate investing for a population currently excluded. If all three hold, Smart Bricks could become the Robinhood of real estate — and that consequence magnitude would change my calculus. But the evidence for each is thin. The revenue is unverified. The data moat is "developing" by the dossier's own characterization. And the target markets and golden visa positioning suggest HNW clientele, not democratization. The product-vs-platform ambiguity — the company describes itself as both an "applied AI lab" and a consumer marketplace with property listings — suggests the founders themselves haven't resolved what they're building.

Smart Bricks fits the pattern I see constantly in YC batches and accelerator cohorts: AI for a specific vertical, large TAM, underserved by technology, automating the workflow. My response is always the same. A 40% or even 99% efficiency gain in real estate acquisition for existing investors is incremental. It does not change the world. The two dimensions that lock this as a Pass are consequence magnitude — optimizing wealthy people's property portfolios does not clear my threshold — and the absence of non-linear technology, confirmed by the dossier's own acknowledgment that the core approach uses publicly available foundation models. I want to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. Smart Bricks is designed to generate moderate returns in a competitive fintech category, and that is not a bet I make.
