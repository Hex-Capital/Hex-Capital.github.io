# modern -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

ServiceNow paid $2.85 billion for Moveworks. Two-point-eight-five billion dollars. For an AI layer that resolves IT tickets autonomously — which is exactly what Modern is building. When the $180B+ market cap incumbent in your category buys your direct predecessor for nearly $3B, that's the market screaming at you: "This is our feature now." Modern is entering a space where the biggest player literally just acquired the capability Modern sells, Automation Anywhere grabbed Aisera for ~$1.5B doing the same thing from a different angle, and Ravenna pulled $15M from Khosla and Madrona. Every sophisticated enterprise investor on the planet has already priced AI-ITSM as a validated, exciting category. There is no mispricing here — the upside is fully baked into every term sheet in this space. When I look at Modern, I don't see a contrarian bet. I see a company swimming in the most consensus-hot current in enterprise software.

Modern's core claim — "AI agents that take tickets from request to resolution" — is what Moveworks was doing since 2016. Moveworks had eight years of iteration, hundreds of enterprise customers, and deep integrations before ServiceNow swallowed it. Modern's differentiation page on getmodern.ai basically says "we're like Moveworks but independent and faster to deploy." That's not a new primitive. Nobody is going to say "they Modernized their IT department" the way people say "Venmo me." When I backed Venmo, Andrew and Iqram were creating a behavior that literally didn't exist — peer-to-peer mobile payments between friends. Modern is automating a workflow that already exists, that multiple companies have already automated, and that the category incumbent now owns the best version of. "Define workflows in plain language" is a UX improvement, not a category birth.

Seb Poole has two exits — a refurbished-device platform sold in 2020 and Frankie Health acquired by Unmind in 2023 after raising $1.5M from E15VC. That's legitimate operational credibility. He raised ~20M AUD from investors including Alibaba for an e-commerce venture in Hong Kong. Aleks Tomovski brings multi-geography DevOps and SRE experience across Melbourne, Vilnius, Denver. These are competent, experienced operators. But my framework isn't about founder quality in isolation — it's about the ratio. Sane person, insane idea. Modern's founders are sane, but the idea is also sane. It's one of the most obviously good ideas in enterprise software. Every IT leader I know wants autonomous ticket resolution. That's the problem — when everyone agrees the idea is good, you're not getting compensated for the risk. A sane person pitching a sane idea is a pricing trap.

Modern fails the AI kill test in the most literal way possible. ServiceNow didn't just theoretically threaten to build this — they spent $2.85B buying it. Zendesk has AI-native resolution capabilities. Salesforce has Agentforce. Microsoft has Copilot actions for service management. Modern's website lists integrations with ServiceNow, Zendesk, Slack, Microsoft Teams, Workday, Salesforce — every single one of those integration partners could build Modern's core functionality as a feature toggle. The managed-services angle ("Modern Managed") is the one structural wrinkle that caught my eye, because operating someone's IT service desk creates a human-in-the-loop moat that pure software can't replicate. But managed services scale linearly with headcount, which is the opposite of what you want in a venture-backed company. It's a margin trap dressed up as a moat. Metropolis works as a physical-world bet because parking infrastructure is a capital-intensive, location-locked asset. Answering someone's IT tickets is not.

The bull case deserves honest construction: Moveworks and Aisera's acquisitions genuinely orphaned their non-ServiceNow, non-Automation Anywhere customers. Modern could capture enterprises that want independent, vendor-neutral AI-ITSM without locking into ServiceNow's ecosystem. The "live in under a day" deployment claim, if real, compresses enterprise sales cycles dramatically. SOC 2 Type 2 and ISO 27001 at this stage is unusual discipline. And Seb Poole's repeat-founder track record means Modern won't die from rookie mistakes. I checked my known exceptions: Slack violated Consensus Mispricing because it was creating a genuine new communication primitive. Modern is not doing that — it's automating existing workflows in a category where two companies were just acquired for a combined ~$4.35B. The Slack exception doesn't apply because there's no new behavioral primitive here. The one condition that would flip me: if Modern showed me $500K+ ARR from enterprise customers who specifically chose Modern over ServiceNow's Moveworks integration, with documented reasons tied to vendor independence — that would prove the orphaned-market thesis is real and the pricing gap exists. Without that, Modern is a feature competing against platforms.

Modern's capital discipline is unremarkable in either direction — two-person team is lean, pricing is "coming soon," no public revenue, 218 LinkedIn followers. At pre-seed with a YC batch, that's table stakes, not signal. The real issue isn't burn rate; it's that Modern would need to raise aggressively to compete in enterprise sales against ServiceNow's distribution machine and Ravenna's $15M war chest, which means the "last money you'll ever raise" test is almost impossible to pass in this competitive structure.
