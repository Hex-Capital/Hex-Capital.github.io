# Foaster -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Raphaël Dabadie built 400,000-member tennis communities at age 11 and became an agent on Rafael Nadal's team at 18. Alexandre Combes built Répondia, where AI agents handled 60,000+ restaurant calls for major French restaurant groups. These are young people — roughly 23, ENSAE Paris classmates — with the kind of precocious intensity that makes me sit up in an interview. When someone accomplishes things like that before they can legally drink, it usually means they have the animal quality. And their LLM benchmark work — the Werewolf paper got 300,000+ views on X, OpenAI's president shared it, xAI's co-founder personally reached out to get Grok 4 added — demonstrates genuine research depth, not just hustle. Foaster's founders are the kind of people I'd want to keep tracking across multiple companies, the way I tracked Patrick Collison from Auctomatic to Stripe.

But Foaster itself makes me uneasy in exactly the way I've learned to pay attention to. The problem statement cites MIT 2025 data, S&P Global data, and McKinsey data to establish that enterprises struggle with AI deployment. That sentence structure — citing market research to prove a problem exists — is diagnostic. When Drew Houston forgot his USB drive, he didn't need McKinsey to tell him file syncing was broken. When the Octopart founders couldn't find electronic parts, they didn't cite a TAM slide. Foaster's origin reads like two talented people who spent a year in the enterprise AI space, noticed a market gap through observation and analysis, and built a product to fill it. The French corporate registry confirms the story: Foaster Technologies shares the same registration number as Répondia (983585290), meaning they pivoted from restaurant AI calls to enterprise AI consulting. That pivot shows flexibility — the running-back quality — but the destination feels chosen from a menu rather than discovered through personal frustration.

"Your AI-native partner for AI transformation" could appear verbatim in a TechCrunch trend piece. The tags on Foaster's YC page — Artificial Intelligence, B2B, Enterprise Software — are the most consensus-friendly combination possible in 2026. Nobody hears "we automate management consulting's data-gathering phase with AI agents" and says "that will never work." Everyone says "oh, that makes sense." That reaction is my single most reliable negative signal. The AI consulting market is projected to grow from $11 billion to $91 billion by 2035. Every consulting firm, every process intelligence company, every AI startup is converging on this space. Celonis has $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in 2023 revenue. McKinsey has already partnered with Wonderful AI ($284 million raised) for enterprise AI agent deployment. Foaster is a two-person team competing at the intersection of all these players. The competitive landscape alone doesn't kill a company — Stripe faced PayPal and every bank — but Stripe's advantage was schlep blindness. Online payments infrastructure repelled other developers because of PCI compliance and bank integration complexity. AI consulting repels nobody. It attracts everyone.

The bull case deserves its due. Foaster has a working product with a real case study: 280 people interviewed in 5 days at a 350-employee firm, 1,200+ processes mapped, delivered in 9 days versus months for traditional consulting. LIPTON Teas and Infusions appears as a client on LinkedIn as of April 2026. The insight that "people are surprisingly candid with AI interviewers compared to human consultants" could be genuinely non-obvious — if true at scale, it means Foaster captures better data than McKinsey associates, not just faster data. Alexandre's Répondia experience (60,000+ AI-handled calls) proves the team can build and deploy conversational AI agents that work in production, not just in demos. The pivot itself shows the learning velocity I prize — they found the bigger application for their core capability. And at 23, these founders have decades of compounding ahead. A Known Exception check: ClassDojo overrode my "hackers building for hackers" pattern because the schlep of selling to school districts created a natural moat. Could Foaster's enterprise sales schlep function similarly? It could — but ClassDojo's idea also initially repelled investors (K-12 classroom management seemed too unsexy to fund), while Foaster's idea attracts them. The exception doesn't apply.

Foaster's learning rate shows in the pivot trajectory — restaurant calls to enterprise consulting in roughly a year — and in the rapid delivery of a working assessment product with real client engagements. The LLM benchmark work (two papers with combined 12 million+ views, engagement from Google DeepMind, Mistral AI, and NVIDIA researchers) reveals genuine intellectual curiosity about how language models behave, which is the earnest builder signal: they care about AI capabilities for their own sake, not just commercially. But Foaster's positioning language — "AI-native partner for AI transformation" — leans heavily on the buzzword register that makes me skeptical about whether the founder communication reflects deep understanding or performed expertise.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Foaster showed me that AI interviews consistently surface operational insights that human consultants systematically miss — not just faster, but categorically different data — and that this insight gap creates measurable ROI for a specific, repeatable customer type. That would transform Foaster from "cheaper McKinsey" to something with a genuine information advantage, which is the kind of non-obvious secret that could sustain a company against well-funded competition. Right now, the dossier shows speed ("10 days versus months") but not qualitative superiority. The founders are worth watching. The idea, in its current form, is not worth my money.
