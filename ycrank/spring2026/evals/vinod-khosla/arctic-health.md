# Arctic Health -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Arctic Health is, at its highest ambition, a faster way for a dermatology group in Tulsa to get enrolled with Aetna -- and that sentence is the entire problem. Sam Slezek, Raghav Mehrish, and Michael Clark have built browser agents that traverse 300+ insurance networks and all 50 states + DC to compress a 60-90 day credentialing cycle into something shorter. The success-state, fully imagined, is a U.S. credentialing software & services market of $267.72M in 2024 (Grand View Research) that grows 6.95% a year and gets a new vendor at the SMB tier. No incumbent regime collapses. No civilizational variable moves. This is the **pass example** from my own framework -- "we use GPT-4 to summarize sales calls and integrate with HubSpot" wearing a healthcare costume.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence, and I wrote the $50M check before the moat was clear because the success-state redrew what cognition itself costs. Arctic Health's success-state, by contrast, redraws which vendor processes a CAQH form for a 12-provider practice in Ohio. The dossier itself names the ceiling: a $14.9B global *credentialing services* market by 2032 (8.9% CAGR), which is what a single one of my portfolio outcomes is supposed to be worth, not the entire TAM. When I ask my Unique Lens question -- *what economic mechanism breaks if Arctic Health works?* -- the honest answer is that Medallion's SMB tier gets uncomfortable. That is not consequence; that is competitive repositioning inside an existing $267M software line.

**Range Fuels** (~2007) failed because the capital plan understated the burn required for the science to work; the inverted failure mode is what Arctic Health embodies -- the capital plan is *appropriately small* because there is no science. $500K from Menlo Ventures, Fortitude, and YC is the right number to build browser agents on top of a commodity LLM stack, and the dossier flatly admits "Browser-automation + LLM stack is increasingly accessible" with Assured ($6M, Fierce Healthcare) already racing the same wedge. Arctic Health does not repel consensus VCs on capital risk; it attracts them precisely because the risk is bounded software-execution risk that any Tier-1 seed fund underwrites in a Tuesday partner meeting. This is the dimension where my edge disappears entirely.

Slezek's resume -- Scale AI, Instacart, Amasia, Morgan Stanley covering insurance -- and Mehrish's Zenefits tenure are exactly the founder-market-fit story the dossier wants me to applaud, and exactly the pattern my TED Talk flagged: experts extrapolating the past. Mehrish coming from **Zenefits**, an HR-compliance-automation company, into healthcare-compliance-automation is linear extrapolation in pure form. None of the three founders has publicly contradicted their field, taken a controversial position, or earned the "too important not to do" framing. Over 18 months this dimension compounds negatively: domain-expert founders in commodity-technology markets converge on the median product, and Assured plus an inevitable Medallion-SMB skew will arrive at feature parity before Arctic Health can build a true data moat.

For Arctic Health's learning-rate score to flip a tier upward, the dossier would need a Slezek pivot story -- a documented "we were wrong about X" moment from his Scale AI or Amasia tenure, or a Mehrish public thesis update after Handstamp -- and there is none. Handstamp is a prior company, not a pivot; the dossier offers no narrative of failed thesis and revised hypothesis. I apply my Entrepreneur-magazine "learning rate above everything else" criterion and find only resume continuity, which is the inverse signal.

**Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) absorbed my $115M Series A lead because the roadmap required capital depth no generalist would touch. Arctic Health's $500K seed is the photographic negative of that pattern -- I am over-resourced by roughly two orders of magnitude for this deal, and any reasonable check from me would distort the cap table without buying conviction-defining ownership. The dossier shows no path where Arctic Health needs >$50M to a first commercial milestone; credentialing SaaS reaches $1M ARR on $5-10M, period.

My 36-month forecast: Arctic Health reaches $2-6M ARR serving SMB practices, gets acquired by Tebra, athenahealth, or Medallion itself for $40-90M, or merges defensively with Assured before Series B. The producing mechanism is straightforward -- commodity stack plus domain-expert founders plus a TAM ceiling of $267M U.S. equals a feature acquisition, not a category company. This is not a failure outcome; it is the *median* outcome, which in my framework is its own indictment.

The strongest bull case invokes my **Glean** exception: Arvind Jain was a paradigmatic ex-Google domain expert and I funded him anyway because consequence magnitude on Fortune-500 information asymmetry overrode the consensus-credential prior. A Glean-believer would argue Slezek's Scale AI plus Morgan Stanley insurance coverage is the analogous credential, and that credentialing-as-infrastructure could become the system-of-record for U.S. provider data -- the Certify ($40M Series B 2025) thesis at SMB scale. The case stings because it is the actual exception in my portfolio. It loses on one fact: Glean's success-state is information access for the Fortune 500 -- measured in hundreds of billions of GDP-relevant productivity -- while Arctic Health's success-state is a $267M software line where Medallion has already raised $130M (Tracxn) and Verifiable $50.1M (TechCrunch 2023) and Certify $54.5M cumulative. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: a signed enterprise LOI with a top-10 U.S. payer (UnitedHealth, Anthem, Aetna) or PBM repositioning Arctic Health from SMB-credentialing-SaaS to payer-side provider-data infrastructure -- the move that would justify reading this through the Glean lens rather than the pass-example lens.
