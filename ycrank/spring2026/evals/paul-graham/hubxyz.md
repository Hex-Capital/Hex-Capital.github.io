# Hub.xyz -- Paul Graham Primary Source Research: Structured Quote Record Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hub.xyz is something I've warned about for years: "Whenever you hear anybody talk about 'growth hacks,' just mentally translate it in your mind to 'bullshit.'" Hub.xyz has 500,000 "users" — but these aren't people paying for a product. They're people running bandwidth-sharing nodes because they earn IQ Points redeemable for $HUB tokens at a future Token Generation Event. That's not making something people want. That's bribing people to participate in your infrastructure so you can report a big number. When the Airbnb founders sold cereal boxes, they were doing something desperate to fund the company they were obsessed with building. When a DePIN project airdrops tokens to attract node operators, they're optimizing a flywheel of speculative incentives. These are not the same activity. One reveals determination; the other reveals a go-to-market playbook borrowed from every other project in a 600-project DePIN sector.

The founder situation concerns me more than anything else here. "The relationship between the founders has to be strong. Startups do to the relationship between the founders what a dog does to a sock: if it can be pulled apart, it will be." Léonie Nguyen, listed as Co-founder and CBO, was appointed CEO of Meey Group — a Vietnamese proptech company — in October 2025. She doesn't appear on the YC page. This looks like a co-founder departure that happened before the company even entered YC. Meanwhile, of the two remaining founders, Armin has an industrial engineering degree and LP positions at venture funds; Tim has a luxury retail background at Tiffany & Co. and business school credentials. Neither has visible technical depth in AI, ML, distributed systems, or data infrastructure. There's no CTO. There's no CEO title on anyone. There are no public GitHub repos. For a company whose entire value proposition is "API for real-world training data," I need to see someone who has been living inside the AI training data problem — someone who passes what I used to call the "animal test," someone obsessive about this specific domain. "The component of entrepreneurship that really matters is domain expertise. Mark Zuckerberg did not succeed in Facebook because he was an expert in startups." I don't see domain expertise here in either AI data or distributed systems.

The strongest bull case would require believing three things simultaneously: that Hub.xyz's distributed collection produces meaningfully differentiated data that centralized scraping cannot, that the team can build a structured API product sophisticated enough for enterprise AI teams, and that they can outexecute Grass (which has roughly 10x their funding and a ~$1B valuation doing essentially the same thing). If all three were true, the AI training data market is real — $3.2B today growing at 22%+ CAGR — and a distributed approach has a genuine "why now" given the regulatory friction building around web scraping. The idea that you could build a Stripe-like API for training data, where enterprises plug in and get fresh multimodal data streams, is not inherently bad. But ideas can't save you. "Good people can fix bad ideas, but good ideas can't save bad people." The idea needs founders who are obsessively, earnestly building the product — not founders managing a token economy and airdrop campaign.

What I keep coming back to is the pattern I called "playing house": "A characteristic mistake of young founders is to go through the motions of starting a startup." Hub.xyz has the surface elements — a YC batch, a strategic partnership with SwissBorg, a half-million node operators, a token, an airdrop campaign, a job posting for a Founding Full Stack AI Engineer. But where are the enterprise customers? Where's the revenue? Where's even a single LOI or case study? The company positions itself as an "API for real-world training data" for enterprises, but the entire go-to-market is crypto-native community building. That's building the supply side with mercenary participants while hoping demand materializes. The supply side network effect — more nodes means better data coverage — only matters if someone is paying for the data. And token-incentivized node operators are the least sticky supply base imaginable; they'll migrate to whichever DePIN project offers better token economics tomorrow. This is not the kind of compounding that produces superlinear returns.

I'll pass. The market is genuine, but this team hasn't demonstrated the domain depth or the earnest obsession with their users that I need to see at this stage. When there's no traction, no revenue, and no enterprise validation, you're picking the founders — and these founders don't give me conviction that they're the right ones to win a race against better-funded competitors in a replicable model.
