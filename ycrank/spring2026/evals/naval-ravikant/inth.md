# Inth -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Christopher Burns ships an Apache 2.0 consent SDK called c15t that is sitting in production at Vercel, Expo, Zed, Infisical, and Unkey, doing 2.6M cumulative npm downloads and ~6.5K/day -- Inth is a permissionless-code-leverage builder-tool with a single-sided distribution shape, not a network. That is the Perplexity silhouette in my ledger, not the Twitter one: criterion #1 and criterion #3 carry the check, criterion #2 gets waived under the known-exception precedent. The question Inth forces me to answer is whether the leverage architecture and the founder's specific knowledge are top-quartile enough to override the missing compounding-user mechanic, the same trade I made on **Perplexity** (2022).

**Stack Overflow** (2008) is the right HIGH-rung analog for Inth on permissionless leverage, because Atwood and Spolsky distributed a developer-tool by shipping it where developers already were, not by hiring SDRs or buying app-install ads. Inth runs the same play through c15t -- the SDK is `npm install`-able, Apache 2.0 licensed, headless, tree-shakable, and the 1,691 GitHub stars / 141 forks / 2.4M downloads over 365 days arrived without paid acquisition. Where Inth diverges from the **Stack Overflow** rung is that the underlying business -- privacy compliance evidence for GDPR/CCPA/Quebec Law 25 -- terminates in a regulator-or-buyer audit, which is permission-adjacent in a way Q&A pages never were; the leverage is permissionless on the distribution side but the monetization brushes against the same gatekeeper geometry that put **Anchorage Digital** in my exceptions list. I score the dimension high but not maxed because the wedge is OSS and the contract layer above it is not.

**Perplexity** (2022) is the LOW-rung failure mechanism to watch on network-compounding: a single-sided builder-product where the Nth user does not make the (N-1) prior users meaningfully more valuable, only the founder's data flywheel does. Inth has exactly that shape -- the consent SDK installed in Vercel's repo does nothing for the SDK installed in Expo's repo, the DSAR managed platform (still "in development" per the LinkedIn company page) is per-tenant, and the "repo-resident data-lineage graph" the dossier infers as future moat accrues to Inth-the-company, not across customers. This is the dimension where Inth structurally cannot score well, and pretending otherwise would be the framework violation I correct for. The waiver only holds if dimensions #1 and #3 both clear top-quartile -- which is the load-bearing test.

Burns spent seven years as co-founder/CTO of Everfund building GDPR-regulated UK donation infrastructure processing payments across 33+ countries before he authored c15t -- this is specific knowledge in the **Almanack** sense, the kind society cannot train you for because it requires being the person responsible when a regulator emails about a data-subject request at 11pm. The mechanism compounds: Burns shipped the SDK that solved his own consent problem, devtool companies that respect taste (Vercel, Zed, Infisical) adopted it, and the next product -- repo-resident privacy-risk mapping -- is downstream of the same lived problem. Eighteen months forward, this dimension either compounds into the "Inth DSAR" managed platform converting OSS users into named ARR, or it stalls because the founder is solo and the scan-plus-evidence product is a different muscle from the SDK.

For the market-pivot dimension, the counterfactual sharpens it: Inth's score flips a tier upward if the dossier showed a second adjacent vector already in motion -- AI-agent code-change governance, data-lineage-as-a-service for the EU AI Act, or a SOC-2-style continuous-evidence SKU. The dossier hints at the AI-agents angle ("AI systems and agent-written code changes all move user data inside the codebase") but nothing is shipped against it yet. Compliance is broad enough to absorb 2-3 pivots inside one seed runway -- consent → DSAR → data maps → AI governance is a real ladder -- but it is not platform-shaped the way **Notion** (2013) was, where the same code-leverage extends into note-taking, project management, and database all from one canvas.

Inth's founder trio reads clean as a single-anchor parallel to **Replit**'s Amjad Masad: technical founder with multi-year obsession in a specific developer-adjacent domain, shipping the public artifact before the priced round. Burns wound down Everfund publicly and graciously ("Thank You For Being Part of Our Journey"), podcasts on Heavybit's Scaling DevTools and Open Source Ready about consent management rather than self-promotion, and the integrity leg reads as quiet competence. The friction is that Inth's third hire was Will De Ath as Head of Growth -- a labor-leverage signal I would rather see deferred until after a co-founding CTO, because expanding from a consent SDK to a repo-scanning evidence platform on one technical founder is the kind of bandwidth bet that breaks at Series A.

36-month forecast: Inth reaches $3-8M ARR by converting c15t adopters into paid Inth DSAR / managed-consent contracts, then gets acquired by OneTrust or Transcend in the $40-120M range as a developer-channel bolt-on -- not a defining outcome, a respectable one. The mechanism is straightforward: the OSS funnel is real, the founder-product-market fit is real, the upside ceiling is bounded because compliance-tooling exits cap there absent a network mechanic. This is consistent with the high/low signature -- permissionless leverage gets you to the OSS-acquisition outcome; the missing compounding network is exactly why it does not become **Twitter**.

The bear case stings: Apache 2.0 means a shadcn/c15t fork *already exists* on GitHub, Privado has $17.5M and Transcend has $90M chasing the same code-resident compliance wedge, and a single founder hiring Growth before CTO is the inverse of the **Stack Overflow** developer-pull pattern -- it is labor-leverage signaling in a code-leverage business, which my own framework flags as a category error. The way Inth wins despite this is that OSS forks rarely catch the original maintainer's iteration velocity (the **Replit** fork landscape is the evidence), and Burns's seven-year Everfund context is a moat no Transcend PM can replicate by reading the spec. The verifiable condition that flips me from Invest to Pass: if 12 months from now Inth has not shipped a paid SKU with at least one named devtool logo (Vercel, Expo, Linear-tier) as a paying customer, the OSS-to-paid conversion thesis is broken and the fork risk wins. Until then, I take the allocation -- smaller than I would on a network bet, sized for the **Perplexity**-exception shape.
