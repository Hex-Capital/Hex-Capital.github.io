# Ontora -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The pitch tells me everything I need to know in one sentence: "We help managers regain control of their organisations." That is a mission statement optimized for enterprise procurement, not for changing the world. When I evaluate a company, I start with the explosion question — if this fully works, what happens? If Ontora succeeds completely, organizational diagnostics become faster and cheaper for mid-to-large enterprises. Managers get better reports. Consulting engagements shrink from months to hours. And then what? The world looks roughly the same. Nobody's life is fundamentally different. This is redistribution of existing consulting spend — taking dollars from McKinsey and BCG and routing them through software instead. My highest-conviction bets create entirely new categories of human capability. Ontora optimizes an existing one.

The technology concern compounds the consequence problem. LLM-based interviewing, RAG over enterprise documents, integrating CRM and email into a knowledge base — this is the standard 2025-2026 AI application stack. The dossier itself flags it: "The underlying technology uses broadly available foundation models." No patents, no IP filings, no proprietary model technology. When I backed Impossible Foods, Pat Brown had synthesized heme protein — a genuine scientific breakthrough that competitors couldn't replicate for years. When I backed Rocket Lab, Peter Beck built electric turbopump engines with 3D-printed combustion chambers. Ontora's technical differentiation from Celonis is that it handles unstructured data instead of structured event logs. That's a product decision, not a technology discontinuity. Any well-resourced team can make the same product decision, and Celonis — sitting on $1.77 billion in funding and $771 million in revenue — has every incentive to.

I'll give credit where it's due. Maximilian Arnold built something real at Porsche — CoAnalyst, an agentic RAG assistant for SAP procurement data projected to save millions across VW Group. That's directly relevant experience. The Vertiv testimonial from a strategy manager at a public company claiming what took months was delivered in an afternoon — that's a genuine 100x time compression signal, and at pre-seed that's meaningful validation. If I were evaluating Arnold's capability to build enterprise AI products, I'd rate him as competent and experienced. But competent and experienced is exactly what I'm not looking for. I want naive outsiders with extraordinary learning rates who don't understand what can't be done. Arnold understands perfectly well what can be done — he's done the adjacent thing before. There's no evidence of first-principles rethinking or rapid pivoting during the YC batch. And the third co-founder, David Korn, is essentially invisible — no confirmed LinkedIn, no public background. When I talk about gene pool engineering in the founding team, I mean every early person should be visible and exceptional.

The strongest bull case is this: if Ontora makes McKinsey-quality organizational diagnostics available for $500 a month instead of $500,000 an engagement, then every 50-person company in the world suddenly has access to intelligence that was previously reserved for the Fortune 500. That's a democratization story, and it rhymes with things I care about — making the expensive radically affordable. But the problem is that organizational consulting is not healthcare, energy, food, education, or legal access. It's not a basic human need being held hostage by legacy cost structures. It's a professional service consumed by businesses. Even the affordability angle is B2B cost optimization, not the kind of radical affordability that gets me out of bed. And the path to that affordability runs through a long enterprise sales cycle with a three-person team that has zero job postings and no visible GTM function. Enterprise procurement doesn't care that your AI is fast — they care about security reviews, compliance, and reference customers. One Vertiv testimonial is a start, but it's a long road.

This is a capable team building a reasonable product in a real market. It will probably generate returns for someone. But I don't invest to generate reasonable returns — I invest to either lose my money or make 50 times my money. The consequence of Ontora's success is a well-run SaaS company worth a few hundred million dollars. The technology has no moat against commoditization. The ambition level is appropriate for a Series A pitch to a growth fund, not for the kind of improbable bet I write checks for. I'd rather fund the company that's trying to replace all management entirely with AI agents — something that makes people laugh — than the company making management consulting 10x cheaper for enterprises that already function.
