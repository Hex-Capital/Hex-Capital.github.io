# Velum Labs -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

The most striking thing about Velum Labs isn't what they're building today -- it's the trail of what they've already abandoned. In their pre-launch phase, this two-person team has publicly presented as a homomorphic-encryption firewall for content-level access control, then an "operating system for data quality," and now "the ontology engine for enterprise AI." Their GitHub organization is populated with forks of Microsoft's SEAL homomorphic encryption library from the prior product direction, while the flagship "Firewall" repo sits at one star. Three identities before shipping a product isn't iteration -- it's a search party. And here's the painful irony: the homomorphic encryption direction was actually the most interesting version of this company. Privacy-preserving computation as a foundational layer had genuine protocol potential. Instead, they pivoted toward the most consensus enterprise software category imaginable.

Let me run the founder-idea pairing. Alen Rubilar-Mu?oz has legitimate technical chops -- CalHacks overall winner, principal software engineer at Dataroot managing an ML platform, research in topological neural networks and privacy-preserving inference. His pinned GitHub repo is literally called "ontology." Benjamin Mu?oz-Cerro brings Stanford physics and quantum computing research. These are sharp technical minds. But "sane" in my framework doesn't mean smart -- it means credibly experienced in the specific domain where the idea lives. Neither founder has sold enterprise software, run data infrastructure at scale, or navigated the procurement cycles at hospitals, banks, and government agencies they're targeting. They appear to still be students. And the idea -- automated data quality through LLM-powered ontology derivation -- is not remotely insane. Monte Carlo has $236M in funding. Atlan has $206M. Bigeye has $73M. Great Expectations raised $65M. Every data team on earth wants better data quality. This is the opposite of non-consensus. It's one of the most crowded, well-capitalized categories in enterprise infrastructure.

Now the AI kill test, and this is where I get genuinely uncomfortable. Velum's entire value proposition IS AI capabilities -- zero-shot NER, LLM-powered relationship extraction, automatic schema mapping. The dossier states it plainly: "The core technical components use broadly available ML techniques." If I ask my standard question -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? -- the answer is definitively no. There is no business without the LLM. That makes this a feature waiting to be absorbed by Snowflake, Databricks, or dbt Labs, all of whom are actively building data quality and governance into their platforms. Snowflake's Horizon governance suite and Databricks' Unity Catalog already include data quality monitoring. The moment ontology-based contract derivation proves valuable, it becomes a checkbox feature for platforms with existing distribution to every data team that matters. The 50+ pre-built enterprise extractors represent real integration work, but integration is grindable -- any well-funded team can build connectors. There is no physical-world operation here, no regulatory position, no protocol lock-in. The moat is software, and I've spent two years explaining why software moats are dissolving faster than founders realize.

The bull case deserves a genuine hearing. If enterprise AI adoption stalls because organizations can't trust their data -- and there's real evidence this is happening -- then whoever solves automated data quality becomes critical infrastructure. Velum's ontology-first architecture is architecturally distinct from Monte Carlo's anomaly detection or Atlan's metadata catalog; retrofitting those products with live ontology derivation from data traffic would require fundamental redesigns. If Velum nails the accuracy problem, gets embedded in a few regulated enterprise environments early, and the ontology definitions create genuine switching costs, this could become the semantic layer that every enterprise AI deployment depends on. That's a real story. But it requires believing that two students with $500K can out-execute $600M+ in incumbent funding while simultaneously selling into the longest procurement cycles in enterprise software. It requires believing that Snowflake and Databricks won't build this natively. And it requires believing that LLM-powered ontology derivation works reliably enough across 50+ heterogeneous enterprise systems to generate trustworthy data contracts -- something for which zero public benchmarks exist. I don't find any of those bets compelling at these odds.

The "ontology engine" framing gestures at primitive status -- as if Velum is building a foundational layer that other applications will integrate with. But a proprietary ontology derived by one vendor's LLM pipeline isn't a standard. It doesn't create the compounding network effects I see in actual protocol investments. When Aleo builds zero-knowledge privacy infrastructure, adoption by one party makes the protocol more valuable for every other party. When Velum maps one customer's data contracts, no other customer benefits. This is an enterprise tool wearing a protocol costume. The narrative can theoretically expand from "data quality" to "enterprise AI infrastructure," but the competitive landscape and the fragmentation of this market cap the infinity story before it begins.
