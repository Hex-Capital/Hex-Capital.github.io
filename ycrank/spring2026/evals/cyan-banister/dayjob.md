# Dayjob -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

George Postlethwaite spent five years at Deliveroo launching the UK grocery business and three years building Gaea ERP for e-waste recyclers with actual depot ride-alongs before pivoting Dayjob in January 2025 -- and that lived-in-the-depot biography is the highest thing Dayjob has going for it, while the lowest is that Dayjob's artifact is a back-office scheduling agent buried inside someone else's ERP that no normal human can ride, scan, photograph, or hand to a friend. The signature here is "real operators in an unsexy vertical shipping back-office software" -- a solid B2B vertical SaaS profile pulling $496K ARR in seven months, not a "magically weird" frontier swing.

Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus from his parents' garage to scale before building Anduril in 2017 -- the load-bearing pattern is "founder shipped a comparable product in an adjacent domain BEFORE the current company," and Dayjob does carry a softer version of that template. Postlethwaite and Fooks co-built Gaea ERP for waste/e-waste recyclers from 2022, which is not garage VR hardware but it is three years of depot exposure to the exact schedulers Dayjob is now selling agents to. Dayjob diverges from the Luckey rung in two ways that matter: the prior product was vertical ERP software rather than a category-defining tactile object, and the pivot inside the same customer base is more "evolved the wedge" than "earned the right to attack defense." Still, Postlethwaite riding shotgun with planners scheduling ~100 daily skip jobs is autobiographical contact with the problem, not a memo.

The pure dev-tools API rung -- the one I cannot install or photograph in my own life inside 48 hours -- is where products go to die my attention, and Dayjob sits closer to that rung than I want. Dayjob plugs into existing waste-operator ERPs as an autonomous scheduling agent; I cannot open an app, I cannot watch a viral pull happen, I cannot read a Wi-Fi network name at a Four Seasons café and feel the founder is shipping. The closest tactile artifact Dayjob produces is a skip truck arriving in a customer's time window 50% more often, and that signal lives inside Allstone's and Coastal Recycling's dispatch logs, not in my hand. HQ Trivia broke the co-founder rule but compensated with explosive tactile virality I could literally feel -- Dayjob has no equivalent override on this axis.

"AI Agents for Industrial Logistics" as the slide-one framing for Dayjob is the most YC-consensus 2026-vintage wrapper imaginable, and over the next 18 months that framing compounds against Dayjob rather than for it. AMCS at $273.1M revenue and Routeware sitting on 1,000+ haulers will both ship native agentic re-optimization modules -- they already ship AI routing -- and the moment they do, Dayjob's overlay positioning collapses into a feature inside the license the operator already pays for. Waste/skip-hire as a vertical IS genuinely unsexy and that partially offsets the consensus framing, but "unsexy vertical wrapped in AI agent" is itself a consensus YC 2026 motion, and I do not see the Crusoe-grade weirdness that would let Dayjob be ignored long enough to compound underpriced.

What would have to be true for Dayjob to clear the capital-intensive / unsexy-regulated bar rather than read as software overlay? Dayjob would need to layer in EPA e-Manifest compliance reporting, weighbridge integration, or a hardware telematics artifact on the truck itself -- something where the regulatory or physical moat compounds with each new customer rather than recompetes. Waste IS a regulated category, and the dossier hints at depot-level workflow data captured since 2022, but Dayjob today is a scheduling algorithm overlay, and route-optimisation algorithms are well-known across OptimoRoute, Route4Me, and Samsara. Without a compliance or hardware lock-in to compound, Dayjob's defensibility is workflow integration depth -- which is exactly the moat AMCS and Routeware already own at scale.

Postlethwaite is the named CEO and Fooks is the CTO; they met at Oxford thirteen years ago, lived in five houses together, were ushers at each other's weddings, and co-built Gaea before pivoting to Dayjob. This is the structural inverse of the Yusupov/Kroll Trivia partnership that collapsed into feud -- clear title delineation, deep pre-company relationship, and no hint in the dossier of equity ambiguity or shared-CEO language. Dayjob passes this test cleanly enough that I will not penalize it; it is the absence of weirdness elsewhere, not the founder governance, that constrains Dayjob.

Thirty-six months out, Dayjob lands between $4-9M ARR across UK and US waste/skip-hire operators and gets acquired by AMCS or Routeware for $40-90M as a bolt-on agent module -- a clean base hit for the founders, not a fund-returner for me. The mechanism: vertical depth and ERP-integration stickiness produce real revenue inside a narrow buyer set, but the same incumbents whose licenses Dayjob avoids cannibalising have every reason to acquire rather than compete with Dayjob once the wedge proves the agent thesis.

The bull case I have to take seriously is Flock Safety, which I sourced from a Wi-Fi network name and which also looked like "software-ish overlay in an unsexy vertical with founder authenticity" before compounding to $8.4B -- the Gaea-to-Dayjob pivot inside the same depot relationships is genuinely "of the place, not visiting it." But Flock had a camera on a pole I could photograph and a founder who personally felt neighborhood crime, while Dayjob's artifact lives inside someone else's ERP and the moat -- workflow integration depth -- is precisely what AMCS and Routeware already own across thousands of haulers. The verifiable condition that would flip my verdict: Dayjob signs a US waste hauler at Republic Services / Waste Connections scale (>100 trucks) on a multi-year contract before the next round, demonstrating Dayjob can displace incumbent routing modules at depth rather than ride alongside small UK fleets.
