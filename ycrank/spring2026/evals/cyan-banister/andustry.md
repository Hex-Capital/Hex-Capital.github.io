# Andustry -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Andustry is an unsexy-industrial wrapper around an unresolved governance question, and that pairing is what registers first. Johann Störken's family runs a trading-goods business and Het Dave was at AppliedAI working on FINN's agent evaluation -- one authentic lived thread into industrial procurement, glued to a McKinsey resume and a dossier that cannot decide whether there are two founders or three. The dimension Andustry scores highest on (a genuinely unsexy cross-border sourcing category for CNC machines, automotive castings, and 2,000 tonnes/month of raw materials to smelters) is the same dimension that disguises the dimension it scores lowest on (no named CEO, a phantom "Nikolas Keller" in the search snippets that the YC page does not corroborate). The inverse of HQ Trivia, where tactile virality bought me an override on co-founder governance -- here the governance risk is the same, but the artifact is a B2B broker workflow, not a thing my friends are screaming about at 9 p.m.

Anduril sits at the top of my lived-experience ladder because Palmer Luckey shipped Oculus out of his garage before he ever filed defense paperwork; the biography put him in front of the problem. Andustry's Störken is the closest analogue here -- a family in the trading-goods business is exactly the kind of dinner-table contact with industrial procurement pain that I weight heavily, and it is the single most interesting line in the dossier. Where Andustry diverges from the Anduril rung is the partner: Dave's path is CDTM, TUM, AppliedAI -- a polymath-credentialed AI-agent tourist, not someone who has felt the cost of waiting six weeks for a quote on a $1.2M CNC machine. One foot of the founding team is *of* the place; the other is visiting.

HQ Trivia is the bottom rung on the governance ladder I cannot stop staring at -- Yusupov and Kroll's later feud is the post-mortem I cite every time I see a deck with no clear tiebreaker, and Andustry triggers it cleanly. Two co-founders, no CEO named on the YC page or in the dossier, equity not disclosed, a third name floating in a search snippet, and a transatlantic operating split between a US +1 415 line and a German +49 172 line on a 2-person team. The HQ Trivia failure mechanism was that explosive growth amplifies every unresolved co-founder authority gap; the Andustry failure mechanism would be the inverse -- modest growth in a brokerage with margin pressure, where the unresolved Keller question becomes the fracture line when the first $1.2M order goes wrong on QC.

Magical weirdness compounds over 18 months only if the wrapper outlasts the trend, and Andustry sits inside the most consensus AI sleeve of the YC P26 batch: "AI agents doing X." The $400K saved on a $1.2M CNC machine and $800K saved on automotive castings are real customer love signals -- but Xometry already booked $687M in FY2025 revenue with AI quoting, and MISUMI just paid $350M for Fictiv in April 2025. The incumbents have capital, supplier networks, and AI teams; Andustry's "AI-native broker" framing is a sentence, not a moat. Eighteen months from now the weirdness either matured into a defensible cross-border specialty (smelters, customs, freight) or it dissolved into the same agent-procurement noise every YC batch will be shipping by P27.

For the capital-intensity dimension to flip a tier, Andustry would need to own something physical -- a bonded warehouse in Rotterdam, a customs-broker license stack, an inspection team on the ground in Shenzhen or Pune. The dossier shows none of that; the moat description literally reads "no public data found" and the future moat is inferred as a quote dataset. RapidDirect owns production capacity in Shenzhen; Fictiv embedded QC teams in-region; Andustry is asset-light commission-only with no rate disclosed and the same product that creates price transparency also erodes its own take rate. The category is unsexy enough to clear my filter, but the company's posture inside it is software-broker-light, not industrial-deep.

Postmates is the single-anchor parallel on physical verifiability -- I bet because the delivery happened in front of me, a deli sandwich in a stranger's hand at my door. Andustry passes a weaker version of the same test: I can email sourcing@andustry.com, request a quote on a CNC machine, and within 24-48 hours something real arrives in my inbox that I can compare against a Xometry instant quote. That is more observable than a dev-tools API but less observable than a Pokémon trainer in a park, and the customer testimonials -- $400K saved, 2,000 tonnes/month, 2-day castings turnaround -- are at least specific dollar figures rather than logos.

My 36-month forecast: Andustry reaches $1-3M in commission revenue on a long tail of SME industrial sourcing deals, the unresolved founder-count question or the US/Germany operating split fractures into a CEO conflict somewhere around Series A, and the company either pivots into a narrow vertical (most likely CNC machines for US reshoring buyers) or quietly winds down without an acquirer, because Xometry and Fictiv/MISUMI have no reason to pay for a broker layer they can build in a quarter. Not a flameout, not a return-the-fund -- the dead middle where commission brokerages go when the founder-market fit was thin to begin with.

The strongest bull case stings: Störken's family trading background is exactly the lived-experience signal I claim to value, Tom Blomfield as YC partner is meaningful, the $400K-saved testimonial is the kind of price-discovery story that compounds into word-of-mouth in SME manufacturing, and US/EU reshoring away from China is a real macro tailwind that could make a cross-border AI broker the right wrapper at the right moment. The HandUp override could apply -- a founder whose biography mirrors the problem domain can buy a check from me even when the commercial defensibility is thin. What dismantles it is the McKinsey line on Störken plus the AppliedAI line on Dave plus the missing CEO plus the AI-agent consensus framing -- four marks against, one genuine mark for. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: Andustry publicly names a single CEO with majority operating authority, discloses the Keller question, and ships one signed enterprise procurement contract with a named mid-market manufacturer above $500K commission -- then I will take a second call. Until then, it is a rebuttal, not a hard no, but I am not writing the check.
