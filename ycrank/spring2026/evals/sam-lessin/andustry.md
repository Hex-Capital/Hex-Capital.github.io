# Andustry -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Xometry is a $2.5B public company doing $687M in annual revenue, and they just acquired Thomasnet for $300M, giving them 500k+ suppliers with distribution already locked in. Andustry claims 500k+ verified companies in its own network — the exact same number, which should make anyone suspicious — but Xometry has the revenue, the manufacturing-of-record relationships, and the engineering team to bolt on AI agents to their existing platform in a quarter. This is the Adobe problem I keep talking about. Every time someone pitches me "AI for [established workflow]," I ask: who already owns the customer relationship and the data? In industrial procurement, that's Xometry in North America and increasingly in Europe. Andustry is pitching AI-driven sourcing and negotiation agents for SME procurement — and my immediate reaction is that this is a feature Xometry ships, not a company Xometry fears.

Andustry's €1M+ transaction volume in beta with a two-person team is, I'll admit, a real number at pre-seed. That's not nothing. Johann Stürken brings RWTH Aachen mechanical engineering, McKinsey consulting, Airbus experience, and a family background in trading goods — that's legitimate domain density for industrial sourcing. Het Dave from IIT Jodhpur provides the technical layer. The founder-market fit on Johann's side is genuine. But here's my problem with Andustry: the idea is completely sensible. "AI to source industrial parts faster and cheaper" is the kind of pitch where every LP in the room nods along. Nobody thinks this is crazy. Nobody's uncomfortable. And when nobody's uncomfortable, the price already reflects the upside. There are at least three other funded AI procurement startups — Leverage AI, Zip, Fairmarkit — all attacking variations of this same thesis. Andustry is a sane team with a sane idea, and that's precisely the combination that bores me.

The primitive test kills Andustry for me. Venmo created peer-to-peer mobile payments as a behavior — it became a verb. Andustry is not creating a new behavior. Procurement sourcing exists. Brokers exist. Marketplaces exist. What Andustry does is automate an existing multi-step workflow (supplier discovery, quote collection, comparison) with AI agents. That's an efficiency gain on an existing process, not a new category. Five years from now, nobody will say "I Andustried that part" — they'll say "I used AI to source that part," and the AI will be embedded in whatever platform already has the supplier relationships. The pay-on-delivery, zero-upfront-fee model is clever positioning against traditional brokers who charge 2-5% upfront, but clever pricing is not a primitive. It's a GTM tactic.

The strongest bull case for Andustry runs through physical-world moats: industrial parts cross borders, require quality verification, involve tariffs and regulatory compliance across 100+ countries, and cannot be digitized away by an LLM. This echoes Metropolis, where I seeded AI-powered parking management that's now worth ~$5B — software amplifies the business, but the physical infrastructure can't be replicated by an app. But the parallel breaks down on a critical point: Metropolis operates physical parking assets. Andustry operates a matching layer between buyers and suppliers it doesn't control. The physical moat belongs to the suppliers, not to Andustry. If a supplier decides to go direct or switch to Xometry's platform, Andustry has no structural lock-in. The two-sided network effect the dossier flags is "nascent and unproven" — their own description — and at pre-seed with two people managing 100+ countries of cross-border regulatory complexity, execution risk is enormous.

I'll grant Andustry one dimension where it scores well: capital discipline. Two founders, no hiring, €1M+ transacted in beta, pay-on-delivery model that doesn't require inventory financing. Johann's consulting background probably means he knows how to run lean. If this were the last money they ever raised, they could probably survive on transaction margins for a while. That's real. But capital discipline alone doesn't overcome the fundamental pricing problem — Andustry is in a consensus category (AI procurement) building an incremental improvement (faster sourcing) in a market where the dominant incumbent (Xometry at $687M revenue) can trivially add the same capability. The risk isn't in the price; the risk IS the price. What would flip me to Invest: if Andustry demonstrated that its supplier network generates proprietary transaction data that produces materially better matching accuracy than anything Xometry or Alibaba can achieve — not claimed 500k+ verified companies, but proven, measurable supplier quality differentiation on completed transactions at scale. Show me the data moat is real, not aspirational.
