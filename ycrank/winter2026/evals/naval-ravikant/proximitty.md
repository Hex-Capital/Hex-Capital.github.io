# Proximitty -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The central tension in this company is architectural. Proximitty's primary differentiator -- computer vision that operates on top of existing browser-based loan management systems without API integration -- sits on a technology layer that Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are actively commoditizing as foundational model capabilities. When I invested in Alchemy, the blockchain developer platform was infrastructure that the protocol layer couldn't easily replicate upward. Here, the infrastructure layer is replicating downward into Proximitty's territory. A company whose moat depends on proprietary browser automation in a world where "computer use" is shipping as a standard model feature is building on sand that's being washed away in real time. That doesn't mean the company fails -- it means the differentiation has to come from somewhere other than the browser automation itself.

The specific knowledge question is where I get stuck. Ho advised on credit risk at McKinsey and scaled Taptap Send from $75M to $200M ARR. The scaling credential is operationally impressive -- that's not slide-making, that's execution under pressure. But McKinsey credit risk advisory is structured, transferable knowledge. It's the kind of thing they train you to do, which means eventually they'll train a machine to do it. Remittance and loan servicing are adjacent, not identical. I don't see evidence that Ho has spent years inside the collections workflow understanding why specific borrowers respond to specific contact strategies at specific moments -- the kind of pattern recognition that emerges from obsessive engagement with the problem, not from consulting engagements. Zhang is more interesting. Five years architecting security infrastructure at Bloomberg serving 300,000+ terminals, then building ACI.dev to 4.7k GitHub stars as an open-source AI agent tooling platform. That's genuine technical specific knowledge for the agent-building layer -- he's been thinking about how AI agents interact with existing systems long enough that his intuitions are non-trivially ahead of someone starting fresh. The team's specific knowledge is lopsided: strong on the agent engineering side, thinner on the lending domain side.

The leverage architecture here is automation, not creation. Proximitty replaces human collections agents with AI agents performing the same tasks -- skip tracing, right-party contact, payment negotiation, compliance checking. That's cost reduction leverage: do the same work with fewer people. It's valuable, but it's categorically different from what I look for. When Uber launched, it didn't make taxi dispatchers more efficient -- it created a new category of transportation worker. When Replit shipped, it didn't help existing engineers code faster -- it made programming accessible to people who would never have opened a terminal. Proximitty makes existing collections operations cheaper. The no-code, no-API deployment model does create genuine deployment leverage -- bypassing the 6-18 month enterprise integration cycle that kills most fintech sales -- and that tactical insight is clever. But the core product is "your current collections workflow, automated," not "a new form of capability that didn't exist before."

The bull case I want to engage with honestly: if the no-API browser overlay deploys in weeks while competitors spend months on API integration, Proximitty could accumulate borrower-level performance data -- which contact strategies work, which negotiation approaches yield payment -- before better-funded competitors even finish onboarding their first customer. That data feedback loop is the real potential moat, not the browser automation itself. The compliance monitoring layer across FDCPA, TCPA, CFPB, and state regulations is genuinely non-trivial and creates trust-based switching costs in a market where regulatory failure is existential for banks. And if the "trusted by publicly traded fintechs and banks globally" claim is real at pre-seed, that's meaningful enterprise pull. The path to a strong outcome exists: fast deployment creates data advantage, data advantage creates better models, better models create switching costs, switching costs compound. That's a real compounding theory. But it requires winning the deployment race before the foundation model providers make browser automation a commodity feature any competitor can use.

The timing is consensus. AI transforming debt collections is not a contrarian bet -- five funded competitors with a combined $275M+ in capital already operate here. Prodigal has been building in this exact space since 2018 with $14.2M raised. Skit.ai generated $22.4M in revenue in 2024. The market has already decided this is a good idea. The no-API deployment approach is somewhat contrarian within the space -- most players are API-first -- and that architectural orthogonality to incumbents is interesting. But "AI for collections" is the definition of a hot market where everyone agrees the transformation is coming. The asymmetric upside I look for comes from being non-consensus and right. Being consensus and right produces modest returns because the pricing already reflects the optimism.

I respect the founding team's complementary skills and the tactical intelligence of the no-API deployment model. But this is a competent team building an automation layer in a consensus market on top of a technology substrate that's being commoditized by the largest AI companies in the world. The specific knowledge isn't deep enough on the domain side, the leverage is cost reduction rather than capability creation, and the timing is consensus. I'd want to see evidence that Ho has non-obvious insights about borrower behavior that couldn't be replicated by any smart person with access to the same data -- the kind of domain intuition that takes years to develop and can't be articulated in a pitch deck.
