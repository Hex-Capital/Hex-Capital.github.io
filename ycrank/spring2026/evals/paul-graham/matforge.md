# matforge -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Matforge is something that almost never happens at pre-seed: the CEO has already delivered the core value proposition to his target customers. Akash Ramdas didn't read about the semiconductor materials bottleneck in a trade publication and decide to build an AI platform. He spent years at Stanford screening 15,000+ compounds for interconnect applications and discovered novel metals that Intel and TSMC put on their roadmaps. The idea didn't come from a brainstorming session. It grew organically from thousands of hours of tedious computational screening and materials characterization — the kind of painful, unglamorous scientific work that most founders building "AI for science" companies have never actually done.

The schlep here is massive, and it's precisely the kind of schlep that explains why over a billion dollars of AI-for-materials funding in 2025-2026 went to broad, horizontal platforms instead. Periodic Labs raised $300M and went broad. Lila Sciences raised $550M and went broad. CuspAI raised $154M and went broad. Not one of these well-funded companies focused exclusively on semiconductor materials. That pattern is diagnostic. Semiconductor materials discovery isn't just hard computationally — it requires understanding fab-compatible process integration, navigating qualification cycles that take years, and building trust with the most conservative R&D organizations on the planet. Smart investors and founders look at that schlep and unconsciously decide to build a general-purpose AI science platform instead, the way smart programmers looked at payment processing and decided to build something — anything — else. The fact that nobody with $100M+ is doing what Matforge wants to do isn't a market signal that the opportunity is small. It's a schlep signal that the opportunity is painful.

The founding team maps well to the problem. Akash owns the domain — semiconductor materials science from IIT Madras through Stanford, with published work screening thousands of candidates and direct relationships with the fabs. Advaith owns the AI systems layer — founding applied scientist at Persona AI building autonomous agents, foundation model work at Luma Labs, CMU AI degree. They've known each other since their IIT Madras days in 2019 and co-authored a book together. This isn't a team that met at a co-founder matching event. The technical fit is unusually precise: one person who deeply understands the materials science, one person who deeply understands agentic AI architectures. For this specific product — autonomous AI agents that screen semiconductor materials — you'd be hard-pressed to design a better two-person team.

What I can't see yet is any evidence of the product. Zero revenue, zero users, no public prototype, no customer announcements. Akash's GitHub has 8 repos and 2 followers. The company has no job postings, no social media presence, no community. At pre-seed I don't penalize this heavily — many of the best YC companies had nothing visible before batch. But combined with the prediction-to-synthesis gap that MIT Technology Review flagged in December 2025, it creates genuine uncertainty about whether computational prediction alone delivers enough value to semiconductor companies, or whether Matforge needs wet-lab capabilities they don't have and can't afford. If the answer is that AI prediction without physical validation is insufficient, this two-person team with ~$500K faces a structurally harder problem than their software-only approach can solve.

The bull case is strong enough that I'd write a check. If Akash's Intel and TSMC relationships are real working relationships and not just academic citations, this company has a warm path into the two largest logic foundries on earth. One contract with either would transform the business. The concentrated customer base that looks like a risk is also a feature: you don't need a million users. You need three. And the domain moat is genuine — you can't hire semiconductor materials expertise off the street; it takes years of PhD-level work to develop the intuition for which materials will actually survive fab integration. That expertise, combined with the structural schlep that keeps well-funded competitors away from this specific vertical, creates the conditions for a company that could own semiconductor materials discovery the way Stripe owns payment infrastructure — not by being the most technically sophisticated, but by being the only one willing to do the unglamorous work.

I'd invest a small personal check. The organic problem-founder fit is among the strongest I've seen at this stage — Akash didn't decide to work on semiconductor materials, he already was working on semiconductor materials when he realized the discovery process needed to be automated. The schlep is real and structural. But I'm investing on conviction about the founders and the problem, not on evidence of a working business. Everything between here and a real company is unproven.
