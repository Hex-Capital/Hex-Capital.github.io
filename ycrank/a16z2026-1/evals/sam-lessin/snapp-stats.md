# Snapp Stats -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Shawn Tsao built Caviar and sold it twice -- once to Square for north of $100M, then Square flipped it to DoorDash for $410M. That's a real founder with a real exit, and I respect the hell out of that track record. But here's my problem: Tsao took that credibility and aimed it at the single most consensus-obvious application of multimodal AI you could dream up. "AI agent for sports video analysis." Every hackathon team in San Francisco is building this. Every incumbent with a sports data contract is adding this. Tsao is a sane person with a sane idea, and in my framework, that's the most boring configuration possible.

The mispricing test kills Snapp Stats before I get to anything else. AI in sports is a $10B market that Grand View Research projects to $50B by 2033 -- which means every analyst, every fund, every corporate dev team at Microsoft and Google has already seen these slides. The English Premier League partnered with Microsoft in 2025 for AI features. WSC Sports added generative AI capabilities in 2024. Genius Sports launched GeniusIQ. When I seeded Solana at four cents, every smart crypto person had written off alternative L1s -- that's mispricing. When I looked at Eaze, every mainstream VC was running from cannabis. Snapp Stats is the opposite: it's in a category where capital is flowing freely and incumbents are actively building. There's no gap between market pricing and reality here. The market has this one roughly right.

The AI kill test is where Snapp Stats falls apart structurally. Their tech stack per Built In SF is LangChain, Crew.AI, TensorFlow on AWS/GCP/Azure. That's not infrastructure -- that's an application layer built on someone else's primitives. Genius Sports has $670M in annual revenue and existing relationships with every major league. Sportradar has exclusive MLB data through 2032 and over a billion in revenue. When those companies decide to offer agentic video Q&A to coaches -- and they will, because the tooling is commoditizing monthly -- Snapp Stats is competing against distribution machines with a feature they can build in a quarter. I don't invest in dapps, I invest in protocols. Snapp Stats is a dapp. The "Large Visual Memory Model" branding is marketing for what amounts to multimodal retrieval-augmented generation over video, which is rapidly becoming a commodity capability in the foundation model layer.

The primitive test reveals nothing new being created here. Coaches already analyze game film. Athletes already review performance footage. CoachNow and SportsVisio already offer video analysis platforms for this segment. Snapp Stats claims to do it with "persistent, searchable memory" and "multi-video analysis" -- that's a feature improvement on an existing workflow, not a new behavioral category. When Venmo succeeded, it created a verb. When Patreon succeeded, it created the behavior of directly paying creators. Nobody is going to say "Snapp me that highlight." The company is competing in an existing market on execution, which caps the upside to incremental market share capture rather than category creation.

The strongest bull case for Snapp Stats is this: Tsao's consumer product instincts from Caviar combined with Alex Marshall's AI chops from Genentech and Pentagon applications could produce a product so good that coaches and athletes adopt it before incumbents like WSC Sports retool their sales channels away from broadcasters. The business model conflict is real -- WSC Sports and Sportradar monetize through media rights holders and sportsbooks, not through coaching staffs. If Snapp Stats nails the college athletics wedge, especially with NIL economics creating new demand for performance analytics, they could build workflow lock-in before the big data companies decide to serve that segment directly. For this to work, Snapp Stats would need to become the operating system for college coaching workflows within 18-24 months -- deep enough integration that switching costs compound faster than incumbent expansion. The dual B2C/B2B positioning described in their materials suggests they haven't yet decided whether that's even the bet they're making, which is a problem with a six-person team.

The capital discipline dimension lacks data, but the signals are mixed. Hiring a Founding AI Engineer at $200K-$250K in San Francisco is market rate, not lean. Running two customer segments simultaneously -- consumer sports feed and B2B coaching tool -- with six people means neither gets full attention. Tsao knows how to build and sell companies, which suggests he won't burn stupidly, but the strategic ambiguity of serving both B2C and B2B at pre-seed looks like a company that hasn't yet found its wind. And I believe you need to be patient finding the right wind before you sail fast -- Snapp Stats is trying to sail in two directions at once.

Multiple red flags from my framework converge here: the AI is the product rather than a feature, the idea is consensus-exciting among other investors (a16z Speedrun participation confirms mainstream appetite), the business is pure software with no physical moats or protocol-level lock-in, and the product is a better version of existing workflows rather than a new category. Tsao's Caviar pedigree earns respect but doesn't override the structural problems. Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability are the two dimensions that lock this into Pass territory -- Snapp Stats is priced like an AI opportunity in a hot category, and there's nothing in the architecture that prevents Genius Sports or a foundation model provider from eating this market within two years.
