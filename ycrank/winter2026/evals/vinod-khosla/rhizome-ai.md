# Rhizome AI -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Here's a founder whose career arc tells me he's capable of attempting something that scares people — IBM Research, employee #16 at Instabase where he closed $7M in enterprise deals solo, then founding product engineer at EvolutionaryScale where he co-authored the ESM3 protein language model paper published in *Science*. That trajectory shows the kind of acceleration I look for: someone who keeps moving toward harder problems. And yet what he's chosen to build is a tool that helps regulatory affairs professionals answer FDA questions faster. I look at Chetan Mishra and see a mind that could be working on something where the consequence of success reshapes an industry. Instead, he's built a very good search engine for regulatory documents.

Let me be direct about what bothers me. If Rhizome succeeds spectacularly — captures the entire addressable market, displaces Cortellis and IQVIA's regulatory intelligence products — the pharmaceutical regulatory system still works exactly the same way. The FDA still reviews drugs the same way. Drugs still take a decade to approve. What's changed is that the people filling out the paperwork do it in days instead of weeks. That's valuable — six-figure revenue in the first year with 20+ customers proves real demand — but the consequence of success is a productive vertical SaaS company worth somewhere between $500M and $2B. Nobody's life changes at scale. The drug approval timeline doesn't fundamentally compress. I've seen this pattern hundreds of times: smart founders who've reduced risk to the point where the outcome, even in the best case, is inconsequential relative to their capability.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement, because there's a version of this story that's more interesting than what I just described. Chetan came from EvolutionaryScale, which means he had a front-row seat watching AI-designed proteins generate an explosion of novel drug candidates. If AI drug discovery delivers on its trajectory — and I believe it will, which is why I backed companies in that space — the regulatory pipeline will be overwhelmed. Every one of those AI-designed molecules needs to navigate FDA, EMA, PMDA, and the others. The volume of regulatory submissions could grow 5-10x in the next decade, and the current system of human regulatory affairs professionals manually reading documents simply won't scale. In that scenario, Rhizome isn't a productivity tool — it's critical infrastructure for a new era of pharmaceutical development. The 2.5 TB normalized corpus across 30 datasets and 6 markets is real engineering work that creates a meaningful time advantage over competitors, and the business model conflict with incumbents — Clarivate and IQVIA profit from selling document access, while Rhizome profits from making those documents unnecessary to read — is a genuine structural opening. If Chetan articulated a vision where Rhizome becomes the intelligence layer that enables the regulatory system itself to handle 10x the throughput, I'd lean in.

But that's not the company I see in the dossier. What I see is published pricing tiers at $400/month and $20K/year, a land-and-expand model targeting individual regulatory professionals, and a YC launch page asking for introductions to "regulatory affairs, quality, or R&D leaders." This is a well-executed enterprise SaaS playbook. The zero-hallucination track record through 2025 is a real trust-building achievement in a regulated industry, and the testimonials — "I would cry if I lost access to Rhizome" — signal genuine product-market fit. But the technology itself — retrieval-augmented generation over structured document corpora with citation tracking — is architecturally well-understood. Any well-funded competitor with domain expertise and a strong data engineering team could replicate this approach within 12-18 months. The moat is time-to-market and trust, not a fundamental technology breakthrough that makes the existing approach economically unviable.

The solo founder question compounds my concern. Enterprise SaaS serving regulated life sciences companies requires simultaneous execution on product development, data pipeline maintenance, enterprise sales cycles with IT security reviews and compliance approvals, and customer support from teams that expect dedicated attention. One person cannot do all of this and also build the vision I described above — the infrastructure that transforms regulatory throughput at scale. The absence of a co-founder or any hiring plans suggests Chetan is building a manageable business, not assembling a team to attack a transformational problem. I've invested in single-person situations before — Impossible Foods was one person — but in those cases the magnitude of the problem demanded the bet regardless. Here, the problem magnitude doesn't override the execution constraint.

I respect what Chetan has built. Real revenue, real customers, real technical achievement. But this is the kind of company I tell founders about when I say: you're too talented to spend your career optimizing a workflow within a system that itself needs reinventing. The regulatory intelligence market is real but bounded. The technology is capable but not structurally disruptive. The founder has the raw material for something much more ambitious. I'd want to have a conversation with Chetan about what he thinks Rhizome looks like at a trillion-dollar scale — and if the answer is "a better Cortellis," that confirms my read.
