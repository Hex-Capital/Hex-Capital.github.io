# Arzana -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Manufacturing front-office automation is exactly the kind of market that makes other investors' eyes glaze over. Parsing RFQs from handwritten notes and PDFs for Midwestern manufacturers — nobody is pitching this at AI dinners in San Francisco. That's the signal I look for. When I backed PagerDuty, the reaction was "operations alerting, really?" When I backed Gusto, people said "payroll seems solved." The markets that trigger dismissive reactions from other investors are where the asymmetric returns concentrate, because you're not competing with twenty other term sheets for the same deal. Arzana sits squarely in the "too boring" archetype. The global manufacturing operations management software market is $17.5 billion growing at 19% CAGR, and the front-office slice — quoting, order entry, AP/AR, customer service — has been a manual process at most manufacturers for decades. Something structural had to change to unlock it.

What changed is LLM capability crossing the threshold for reliably parsing unstructured manufacturing documents at production quality. This is the same pattern I mapped with Harvey in legal AI: before GPT-4, legal document analysis was a science project. Before 2023-2025 model improvements, manufacturing RFQ parsing across handwritten notes, varied PDF formats, and email threads was similarly impractical — OCR and rule-based systems couldn't handle the format diversity. The structural moment is real. And there's a second structural force: SAP, Oracle, and Epicor own the ERP systems of record, but their front-office automation layers are structured-data tools. Building lightweight AI layers that parse unstructured inputs would cannibalize the professional services revenue their implementation partners depend on. This is the same incumbent business model conflict that gave Stripe room to grow next to PayPal — the margin structure creates a blind spot that a specialized entrant can exploit.

The traction is what elevates this above the dozens of "AI for X industry" pitches I see weekly. Nine named customer logos including Schneider National — a Fortune 500 company — and Zeiss, with a four-person team at pre-seed stage. Twenty-plus plants and distribution centers. A customer testimonial citing time-to-quote dropping from five days to one. These are hard to fabricate at named-logo level, and getting Fortune 500 manufacturers to deploy new software in their operations stack is a non-trivial sales accomplishment at any team size. The product-to-distribution trajectory is structurally sound: they're entering through quoting automation, the sharpest pain point, but the platform already spans purchasing, AP/AR, CRM, and outbound sales. Eleven-plus ERP integrations create the switching cost that makes expansion natural — once you're embedded in a manufacturer's operational stack processing their RFQs through their Epicor or SAP instance, you become infrastructure. That's the wedge-to-platform motion. The Iowa presence, which looks like a liability to coastal investors, is actually a distribution advantage — a Founding Field Engineer based in Okoboji means they're doing implementations on manufacturing floors, not running demos over Zoom.

The concerns are real and I won't minimize them. The CEO has taken "only one coding class" — in an AI company, that's a meaningful risk factor. The entire technical capability sits on two founding engineers with limited public track records. Endeavor AI raised $7 million from Craft Ventures targeting the same market, which means a better-funded competitor exists. And the pivot from Brilla, a campus meal delivery startup, to manufacturing AI raises the question of whether this is genuine domain pull or late-cycle pattern-matching on AI trends. The multiple-miracle concern is worth examining: do they need reliable LLM parsing AND deep ERP integration AND enterprise sales to conservative manufacturers? My read is that LLM parsing is being solved by the foundation model labs — the same dynamic as Harvey, where the technical miracle is outsourced — which collapses the miracle count. The real single miracle is implementation and distribution: can they deploy and scale inside manufacturing environments fast enough to build an integration moat before Endeavor or a horizontal AI platform catches up?

The bull case that would make this a generational bet: manufacturing is a $2.3 trillion sector in the U.S. alone where virtually all front-office work is still manual. If Arzana becomes the "office operating system" for manufacturers the way Gusto became the HR platform for small businesses, the market is 10x larger than it appears from static TAM analysis. The ERP integration library becomes a compounding moat — every new connector makes them more deployable, every deployment generates manufacturing-specific workflow data that improves their parsing models, and every happy customer becomes a reference sale in an industry that runs on peer referrals. The 21-year-old CEO from Iowa who landed Schneider National and Zeiss at pre-seed would be an extraordinary founder signal if verified. The "boring market + authentic distribution access + technology inflection" combination is exactly the pattern behind my best-performing investments.

Where I net out: this fits my framework on market archetype, technology timing, and product-to-distribution structure. The traction signals are genuinely impressive for pre-seed. The team risk and competitive risk are real but manageable — the non-technical CEO is concerning but can be addressed with senior engineering hires, and Endeavor's $7 million seed is notable but doesn't constitute a market-ending move at this early stage. I'd take the meeting and push hard on customer verification and the technical team's depth, but the structural setup is right.
