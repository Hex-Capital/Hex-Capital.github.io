# Arctic Health -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Arctic Health is the unsexy-regulated artifact I'd usually lean toward — credentialing across 50 states + DC + 300 payer portals is exactly the kind of NCQA-shaped paperwork plumbing where a moat compounds in domains other investors won't enter — except Sam Slezek, Raghav Mehrish, and Michael Clark arrive at this problem from Morgan Stanley insurance coverage, Airbnb engineering, and a boutique hedge fund, which means Arctic Health is unsexy *in the dossier* but not unsexy *in the founder origin*, and that mismatch is the whole evaluation.

Anduril is my reference rung on the founder-origin dimension because Palmer Luckey shipped VR from his parents' garage before he ever touched defense, and the prior-product trajectory is what made me tattoo conviction on it in 2017. The mechanism is autobiographical contact with the problem — Luckey understood headsets in his hands before he understood DoD procurement on a deck. Arctic Health's closest analogue inside the team is Mehrish's Zenefits tenure, and Zenefits is at least *adjacent* to the multi-state compliance hairball — Zenefits famously got mauled by state licensing in 2016, so somebody who lived inside that engine has touched the per-state-edge-case grind that Arctic Health's "300+ insurance networks" claim now depends on. But Slezek covering insurance companies as a Morgan Stanley analyst is not the same as running credentialing inside a 12-provider OB/GYN practice in Bismarck, and the dossier gives me no founder moment of *feeling* this problem — no spouse who waited 90 days, no family practice they tried to credential. Arctic Health discovered credentialing the way the consensus YC AI-agent darling discovers back-office automation: from a memo.

The lowest rung on the magically-weird axis belongs to the ex-FAANG-PM pitch with six funds chasing, and the failure mechanism is that the moat is the model, not the founder — a polymath generalist rebuilds it in a quarter. Arctic Health's competitive frame matches that mechanism more than I'd like: Assured raised $6M doing the same AI-native credentialing wedge (Fierce Healthcare), Medallion sits at $130M, Verifiable at $50.1M, Axuall at $41M, Certify at $40M Series B in 2025. That is not a category nobody is looking at — it's a category five funded teams are racing inside, with incumbents who can rationally go downmarket the moment Arctic Health's SMB wedge becomes interesting. Arctic Health's $500K seed from Menlo, Fortitude, and YC is institutionally real but it is the opposite of "credible VCs passed on aesthetic grounds." Nobody is passing on aesthetic grounds. Everybody is funding their version of this.

On the unsexy-regulated dimension — my highest placement for Arctic Health — the mechanism is that NCQA standards, per-payer portal idiosyncrasy, and 50-state licensure stratification do compound into a process-data moat over 18 months *if* you accumulate enough labeled approved/rejected submissions to teach the agent the edge cases. Arctic Health's claim of "specialist-reviewed applications on every submission" is the right shape; the question is whether a four-person team with $500K outruns Medallion's $130M warchest down-market. The compounding works only if Arctic Health locks specialty verticals (behavioral health, allied health) where the incumbents' unit economics genuinely cannot follow.

On the CEO-tiebreaker dimension, what would have to be true for Arctic Health to clear a top-quartile score is a documented operating agreement naming Slezek as the unambiguous decision-maker with majority operating authority and a vesting/exit-conflict protocol. The dossier gives me three co-founders with three differentiated titles (CEO, CTO, COO), which is structurally better than the HQ Trivia / Yusupov-Kroll horror, but absent equity splits or a stated tiebreaker, Arctic Health sits in the "they haven't had the conversation yet" zone. Three-cofounder configurations are the 33/33/33 risk I screen hard against.

On physical-world verifiability, Niantic anchored my whole framework because I played Ingress for three years before I wrote the check — the artifact was in my hand. Arctic Health is a B2B browser-automation backend that submits payer applications. I cannot install it, I cannot ride it, I cannot scan it at a Four Seasons café. The only people who can observe it are credentialing managers at small practices, and even they are observing PDF outputs of a workflow they previously ran by hand. The product is verifiable only through founder testimony, which is precisely the abstraction I score below midpoint by default.

My 36-month forecast: Arctic Health is acquired by Medallion or Verifiable for a $20-50M aqui-hire by mid-2028, or — if the SMB wedge holds — stalls at sub-$3M ARR squeezed between Assured-class peers below and Medallion going down-market above, with a possible pivot into a single specialty vertical like behavioral health credentialing where the regulatory surface area is genuinely too narrow for the $130M incumbent to chase. The mechanism is that Arctic Health's moat thesis (per-payer process data) requires more volume than $500K buys against five funded competitors.

The bull case that stings: Zenefits is a lived lesson, not a line on a resume — Mehrish watched a $4.5B HR-compliance company nearly die from per-state licensure failures, and that is exactly the failure mode credentialing automation must avoid. If Mehrish is the technical conscience that makes Arctic Health's portal automation *actually* survive Tennessee Medicaid's portal redesign in Q3 while Assured's breaks, the data moat starts compounding for real, and Menlo follows on. The dossier evidence that wins against this bull case is the founder backstory section itself: no founder has stood inside a small practice fighting a 90-day Aetna enrollment cycle. Arctic Health is solving the problem from the payer-and-platform side, which is where the incumbents already are. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict: a named design-partner practice or health system publicly attesting to a measured reduction from 60-90 days to under 14 days on a specific payer panel, with the founder's origin story revised to include who in their life actually waited.
