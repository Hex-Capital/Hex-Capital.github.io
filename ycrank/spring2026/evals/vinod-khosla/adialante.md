# Adialante -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

MRI technology has been fundamentally unchanged for forty years. That's the kind of sentence that makes me sit up. When an entire industry calcifies around a single engineering paradigm -- superconducting magnets, gradient coils, shielded rooms, $3 million price tags -- and someone comes along with a genuinely different physics approach that eliminates a third of the hardware, I want to understand what happens if they're right. Adialante's FREE technology isn't an engineering tweak; it's a published, peer-reviewed departure from how magnetic resonance imaging has been done since its invention. If it achieves clinical-grade imaging at one-tenth the cost and size of conventional systems, MRI stops being a hospital-only technology and becomes something you encounter in a parking lot outside your urologist's office. The consequence of success here isn't just a better MRI machine -- it's making the most powerful diagnostic imaging modality on the planet accessible to the billion-plus people who currently can't reach one. That's a consequence worth taking seriously. But -- and this matters -- it's bounded. The global MRI market is $7 billion, not $700 billion. Even expanding into all cancer screening applications, the world doesn't reorganize around this the way it would around fusion energy or artificial general intelligence. It's a meaningful disruption of a calcified medical technology market, not a civilizational transformation.

What concerns me is that this is no longer a contrarian bet. Five years ago, building low-cost portable MRI would have been dismissed by every incumbent. Today, Hyperfine has FDA clearance and $13.6 million in revenue. Promaxo has FDA clearance with 69 installed systems specifically for prostate MRI -- Adialante's exact target market. Chipiron just raised $17 million for quantum-based low-cost MRI. Siemens launched the Free.Max at 0.55 Tesla. The experts and incumbents aren't dismissing accessible MRI anymore -- they're building it. When I invested in Juniper, Cisco's CTO personally told me TCP/IP routing in telecom would never happen. That active dismissal by the establishment was part of the signal. Adialante is entering a space where the establishment has already validated the thesis. The timing isn't ahead of the curve; it's concurrent with a wave of well-capitalized competitors who have multi-year regulatory head starts and commercial traction that Adialante has yet to begin generating.

The founding team presents a tension I've seen before. Torres and his colleagues come from UMN's Center for Magnetic Resonance Research -- one of the world's premier MRI labs. The ISMRM Rabi Award on the team (Srinivas) signals elite technical capability. Five PhDs across MRI physics, image reconstruction, and computational modeling. The scientific bench strength is exceptional. But they're all insiders. Every member comes from academic MRI research. My portfolio's biggest wins came from outsiders who didn't know what the industry considered impossible -- Pat Brown the biochemist who didn't know you "couldn't" make meat from plants, Peter Beck from New Zealand who didn't know you "needed" an aerospace pedigree to build rockets. Torres invented FREE technology inside the very institution that defines what MRI is. That doesn't disqualify him -- the invention itself is genuinely novel -- but it raises the question of whether the team can execute the commercial pivot this technology demands. The mobile fleet model requires operational logistics, regulatory navigation across jurisdictions, staffing, scheduling, and healthcare sales -- none of which map to the skill set of a world-class MRI physics lab. I see no evidence of anyone on the team with commercial healthcare operations experience.

The strongest bull case runs like this: FREE technology is a platform, not a product. If the physics works at clinical quality for prostate, it works for breast, brain, musculoskeletal, everything. The mobile per-scan model creates an innovator's dilemma that Siemens, GE, and Philips cannot touch without cannibalizing their $1-3 million capital equipment business and recurring service contracts -- the same structural conflict that made Square lethal to traditional payment processors. Promaxo's capital-sale model actually reinforces Adialante's advantage: clinics that can't afford $1 million for a Promaxo system will pay per scan for a mobile unit. The FDA clearance gap closes with time, and once cleared, the per-scan model scales faster than any hardware-sale competitor because it eliminates the customer's capital expenditure decision entirely. If all of that materializes, this is a multi-billion-dollar medical device platform company with a business model moat that technology alone can't replicate. That's a real upside case.

But I keep returning to the risk stack. This company needs the physics to work at clinical quality (unproven at scale), FDA clearance for a novel MRI approach (uncertain timeline, with no submission yet), and successful mobile fleet operations (a completely different business from hardware development) -- all simultaneously. When I see a plan that requires the technology, the regulation, and the operations to all break right at once, I worry the founder hasn't prioritized which risk to eliminate first. The NSF SBIR Phase II grant of $1.19 million through November 2026 funds continued technology development, which is the right first risk to attack. But $2.43 million in total funding against well-funded competitors with FDA clearances and commercial traction means the clock is ticking. This is a genuine technology innovation wrapped in significant execution and timing risk. The problem matters, the physics is real, and the team has deep capability in their domain -- but the consequence magnitude doesn't reach the threshold where I'd override the red flags, and the competitive landscape tells me the window for a first-mover advantage in low-cost MRI has already closed.
