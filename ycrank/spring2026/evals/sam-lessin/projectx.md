# ProjectX -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Two of three original co-founders listed on Crunchbase — Ronit Banerjee and Suvrakamal Das — are gone before ProjectX even gets to YC. That's the first thing I see in this dossier, and it's not a minor detail. Co-founder turnover at pre-seed, before you've shipped anything meaningful, before you've raised real money — that's a signal about the foundation. I've said repeatedly that the best investments come from founders I've watched across multiple ventures, people with deep shared history. Rounak Adhikary, Bishal Karmakar, and Sourya Majumder are all from Kalyani, West Bengal — geographic proximity, not years of battle-tested collaboration. Compare that to Andrew Kortina and Iqram at Venmo, who'd been building together since college. Synthetic co-founder relationships at the seed stage correlate with synthetic commitment, and ProjectX has already burned through one set of co-founders. That's not fatal, but it's a red flag that demands an extraordinary thesis to overcome.

ProjectX is pitching "a cloud-based operating system accessible via a single URL that runs every application in its own independent virtual computer." Let me run the mispricing test: is the DaaS market a category the consensus has dismissed? No. It's a $4.5-9.82 billion market growing at double-digit CAGR, with Microsoft Azure Virtual Desktop, Amazon WorkSpaces, and Citrix all actively competing. Every enterprise CIO knows what desktop virtualization is. The per-app GPU isolation angle is technically interesting — I'll give ProjectX that — but it's a feature differentiation within a well-understood market, not a contrarian category bet. Nobody has "written off" cloud desktops. The market prices this space accurately: it's dominated by hyperscalers with infinite infrastructure budgets, and a startup with $204K in Google Cloud funding is bringing a butter knife to a tank fight. There's no narrative mispricing here. ProjectX isn't Solana at four cents where every smart person said "Ethereum won" and they were wrong. ProjectX is in a space where every smart person says "Microsoft and Amazon win" and they're... probably right.

Rounak Adhikary has been building ProjectX since 2022, age 20, funding early development from his web agency Woldix. That's four years of sustained commitment, and the scrappiness — bootstrapping from agency revenue, winning the Tiger Launch Global Finals at Princeton, landing a Google Cloud grant — shows real hustle. But hustle is table stakes. The primitive test asks whether ProjectX creates a genuinely new behavior that didn't exist before. "Per-app isolated cloud computing" is not a new primitive. It's better virtualization. VMware has been isolating workloads since 1998. Kubernetes added GPU scheduling support years ago. Neverinstall and Vagon are building adjacent browser-based cloud compute with $1.21M and $542K respectively. ProjectX's "run Windows and Linux apps in the same session" is clever engineering, but nobody's going to use "ProjectX" as a verb. Nobody's going to say "I ProjectX'd my workflow." Venmo created a behavior — peer-to-peer mobile payments — that became a cultural primitive. ProjectX is offering a better version of something that already exists, and I have a specific red flag for that: the upside is capped by the existing market's willingness to switch.

Here's where I have to steel-man the bull case, because intellectual honesty demands it. ProjectX could be building the "operating system of the cloud" — a genuine paradigm shift from "I have a computer" to "I have infinite parallel computers, each purpose-built for every task." The AI agent co-working angle — human and AI agent workflows side by side — is genuinely timely in 2026. If ProjectX nails the orchestration layer for mixed human-agent computing environments with sub-second cold starts and cross-OS support, they could define a new category that incumbents' existing architectures can't easily serve. Microsoft's Windows 365 is architecturally committed to streaming a full Windows desktop; rebuilding it for per-app Linux+Windows compute would cannibalize Azure Virtual Desktop's positioning. And Rounak at 23 with four years of obsessive focus on this problem has the missionary commitment I respect. The Slack exception in my framework says I'll pay consensus prices when a company is genuinely creating a new behavioral primitive. Does ProjectX qualify? I don't think so — not yet. The website returned a loading page at time of research, nearly two years after the June 2024 public beta announcement. No user counts, no customer logos, no revenue signal. If you're creating a new computing primitive, someone somewhere should be using it and telling their friends. The absence of any traction evidence two years post-beta is the evidence. The condition that flips me: show me 500+ daily active users who can't go back to their old workflow. That's primitive creation. Everything else is a feature demo.

ProjectX fails my AI kill test in a specific and damaging way. Google Cloud is simultaneously ProjectX's investor ($204K seed grant) and its most dangerous potential competitor. The product runs entirely on hyperscaler infrastructure — AWS, GCP, Azure — with no physical-world moat, no protocol-level lock-in, and no network effects. When I backed Metropolis at seed, the thesis was AI amplifying physical parking infrastructure that no software could replicate. When I backed Solana, the thesis was a new consensus protocol with network effects that compound over time. ProjectX has neither. It's a software orchestration layer on rented GPUs. Microsoft could ship per-app isolation in Windows 365 as a feature update. The $60K in government grants and $204K from Google Cloud become particularly concerning in this light — they're infrastructure credits with expiration dates, not revenue. When those credits burn off, ProjectX hits full hyperscaler compute costs with no demonstrated unit economics to support per-app dedicated GPU allocation at scale. Capital discipline looks reasonable today — the team has been scrappy with minimal funding — but the business model structurally requires massive GPU infrastructure spend that a $3M seed round won't sustain against incumbents spending billions.

ProjectX sits at an intersection I find structurally unattractive: a consensus market (cloud desktops), an incremental product improvement (per-app isolation rather than per-desktop), platform dependency on potential competitors (hyperscalers), and no evidence of the kind of category creation that would override those concerns. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Consensus Mispricing and Structural Durability. On mispricing: DaaS is not a dismissed category — it's an incumbent-dominated category with no narrative gap to exploit, scoring well below my default of 15/30. On durability: pure software on rented infrastructure with a hyperscaler investor-competitor is the opposite of what survives in my framework, scoring 5/15. Pass.
