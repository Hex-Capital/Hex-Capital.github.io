# Cajal -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

The first thing I'd want to know — the thing I always want to know — is how Luke and Pedro ended up here. Not "here" as in formal verification, but here as in: what happened in your life that makes you the person who has to prove mathematical theorems with AI agents? And from everything I can find, I can't locate that thread. Luke studies computational neuroscience at Cambridge — meta-reinforcement learning in orienting behavior. Pedro won a hackathon in Spain and gave a talk about AI paradigms. They met through Entrepreneurs First's Bridge program in a castle in Germany, which is a delightful detail but tells me about the program, not about them. The company is named after Santiago Ramón y Cajal, the father of neuroscience — which connects to Luke's academic field, not to formal verification or Lean or proof assistants. That naming choice actually raises a question for me: did this company start as something neuroscience-adjacent and pivot toward formal verification because the timing is right? Because if so, that's a flag — I'm always watching for founders chasing a hot category rather than solving a problem that's been burning in their chest for years.

And formal verification IS getting hot. Harmonic AI just raised $295 million at nearly a billion and a half valuation doing something closely related — AI-driven theorem proving in Lean 4. When I see a well-funded competitor that large in what's essentially the same technical domain, I don't automatically run away. I backed SpaceX when Boeing and Lockheed had every conceivable advantage. But the reason I could write that check was because Elon's personal obsession with making humanity multi-planetary was so transparent, so obviously the center of his identity, that I knew he'd run through walls that would stop a rational person. With Cajal, I'm looking for that same irrational commitment and I can't find it in the public record. Neither founder has documented prior experience in formal verification, theorem proving, or Lean programming. That gap between the technical ambition — autonomously discovering and formalizing mathematical proofs — and the founders' visible expertise makes me uncomfortable, even accounting for the Cambridge affiliation providing access to people who do have that depth.

The bull case is real and I want to be honest about it. Formal verification for applied science is genuinely one of those spaces that's hard to assess, unpopular with most early-stage investors, and sitting at a technological inflection point — exactly the profile I've learned to trust. LLMs hallucinate. They can't guarantee correctness. And in quantum computing, aerospace, and cryptography, a hallucinated answer isn't just wrong, it's dangerous. If Cajal actually cracks autonomous proof discovery for these applied domains, they're building the kind of infrastructure layer I've repeatedly backed — the Crusoe Energy play where something that sounds absurd today becomes critical infrastructure tomorrow. The differentiation from Harmonic into applied science verticals rather than pure competition math is smart positioning. And the data moat from verified mathematical corpora could compound in exactly the way that excites me. If I could sit with these founders and hear them tell me about a moment in their lives when the absence of mathematical certainty cost them something personal — when they saw a system fail because nobody could prove it was correct — then maybe I'd feel that jolt of energy. But I don't have that story yet.

What I also notice is that this company doesn't create economic access for anyone who currently lacks it. Their customers are frontier AI labs, quantum computing researchers, and quantitative finance firms — the most elite institutions in the world. When I backed Uber, it created income for drivers. When I backed Affirm, it gave transparent credit to people who'd been exploited by hidden fees. When I backed Flock Safety, it brought public safety data to communities that couldn't afford it. Cajal makes powerful institutions more powerful. That's fine as a business, but it doesn't activate the part of my thesis that connects capitalism to poverty alleviation, and it doesn't make invisible systems visible to people who need transparency. It's an infrastructure play for the already-resourced.

The co-founder dynamic is hard to read with limited information. They met through EF Bridge, which is at least a structured context for founder matching — better than a random conference introduction. But I don't see evidence of a shared history that's been stress-tested. Two people, no documented working relationship before the program, different academic backgrounds in different countries. After what happened with HQ Trivia, I probe co-founder dynamics hard, and here I simply don't have enough signal to evaluate whether these two can survive the kind of crisis that every startup eventually faces. The 2-person team size is normal for pre-seed, but given the technical depth required — dependent type theory, proof automation, multi-agent systems, AND domain expertise in quantum computing and finance — two people feels stretched thin for the scope of the ambition.

I'm passing on this one. The space is intellectually fascinating and sits at a real inflection point, but my framework is weighted toward founders whose personal narrative is inseparable from the problem they're solving, and I can't find that connection here. Smart people identifying a smart opportunity is different from the biographical inevitability I look for.
