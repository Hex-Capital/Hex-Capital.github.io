# Gikl, Inc -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

"Coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers — we're doing the same for science." That tagline tells me nearly everything I need to know, and not in the way the founders intend. This is explicitly analogical thinking: take a pattern that worked in domain A, apply it to domain B. That is the definition of going from 1 to n. The next great company in science will not be built by copying the Copilot playbook — just as the next great search engine was not built by copying Yahoo's directory model. When a founder's primary thesis is "X but for Y," they have revealed that they do not possess a secret. They possess an analogy.

So let me ask the question directly: what is the secret here? The implied claim is that non-experts, augmented by AI agents, can make meaningful contributions to unsolved scientific problems across mathematics, biology, physics, computer science, engineering, and chemistry simultaneously. Is this actually contrarian? Most working scientists would reject it — they would say that real breakthroughs require years of accumulated intuition, that domain expertise is irreplaceable, that citizen science mostly produces noise. So there is a surface-level contrarian quality. But look closer. The historical examples cited — Foldit, the Polymath Project, Marjorie Rice — are not evidence for this thesis. Foldit worked because protein folding was gamifiable; the Polymath Project was organized by Fields Medalists coordinating professional mathematicians, not non-experts; Marjorie Rice is a single individual from 1977. The claim that AI bridges the expertise gap sufficiently for crowds to solve hard science is not a secret anyone has validated — it is a hope. And a hope is not a secret. A secret is something you have reason to believe is true that others have reason to believe is false. I see no evidence these founders possess knowledge that makes this hope more credible than it appears.

The competitive landscape compounds the problem. Google has deployed AI Co-Scientist at all 17 DOE National Labs. OpenAI is building a fully automated researcher targeting AI research intern capability by September 2026. Sakana AI has raised $379 million at a $2.65 billion valuation for automated end-to-end research. FutureHouse raised a $70 million seed. Elicit has roughly $20 million in ARR. When I look at this landscape, I see a Girardian convergence — multiple well-capitalized players all rushing toward "AI for science," imitating each other's thesis with slightly different product shapes. ScienceSwarm's defenders will argue it occupies a "third category" — not literature retrieval, not autonomous AI, but crowdsourced community problem-solving. But this distinction is fragile. Google can add community features to AI Co-Scientist in a quarter. The underlying AI capabilities are available via foundation model APIs, which the dossier itself acknowledges. When your differentiation is a product design choice rather than a proprietary technology or structural moat, you are one product update away from being absorbed.

The strongest bull case requires me to take the founders seriously, and I do. Vajda directed Media Generation at Meta for eleven years, shipping text-to-image and Movie Gen at production scale, with 77+ publications and thousands of citations. Yamamoto is a Senior Staff researcher from Meta's core Llama group, holds a physics Ph.D. from Rice, published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, and co-authored with a Fields Medalist. These are not consultants or MBAs — they are genuine researchers who have operated at the frontier of both AI systems and scientific inquiry. Yamamoto's two years at Palantir are a small but notable data point. If these founders possess a specific technical insight — perhaps about how AI agents can formally verify scientific claims in a way that existing tools cannot, something derived from their work on Llama or Meta's generative systems — then the thesis could be much stronger than the public positioning suggests. A community platform where AI verification creates a trustless validation layer for scientific contributions would be structurally different from anything Google or Sakana is building. But I see no evidence this technical insight exists. The website describes a platform for browsing challenges and submitting approaches. That is a community management problem, not a technology problem, and nothing in these founders' backgrounds suggests deep expertise in incentive design, community cold-start dynamics, or marketplace economics.

The market entry strategy is precisely what I warn against. Six domains on day one — all of science, simultaneously. Facebook started at Harvard. PayPal started with eBay power sellers. Palantir started with the CIA. ScienceSwarm starts with everything. When your initial target is "anyone interested in any unsolved problem in any scientific field," you have not chosen a beachhead — you have chosen a battleground. There is no small market here to monopolize, no concentrated user base whose needs you can serve 10x better than any alternative, no mechanism by which early dominance in one niche creates structural advantages for the next. The platform lists six broad scientific domains without apparent prioritization. This is not a strategy; it is an aspiration.

I am passing. The founders are technically exceptional, and in a different configuration — perhaps building proprietary AI verification infrastructure for a specific scientific domain where they have deep expertise, starting with a narrow problem where Yamamoto's physics background gives them an unfair advantage — I could imagine a company worth backing. But what I see here is a consensus thesis (AI will accelerate science), wrapped in mimetic thinking (do what coding assistants did), targeting a market where massively capitalized incumbents are converging, with no proprietary technology, no traction, no beachhead, and a community-dependent business model that has not demonstrated that anyone will contribute. The founders have not answered the secret question. They have answered the analogy question, and those are different things entirely.
