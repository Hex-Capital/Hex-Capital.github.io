# Autumn AI -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Autumn AI is that it's entering a market where the schlep is visible to everyone. ZoomInfo is a public company doing $1.25 billion in revenue. 6sense has raised $426 million. Apollo, Demandbase, and a dozen smaller players are all fighting over sales intelligence. When I talk about schlep blindness — the unconscious avoidance of hard problems — I mean problems like payment processing before Stripe, where thousands of programmers knew it was broken but nobody wanted to deal with the banks. The sales signal aggregation problem is the opposite: everyone can see it, everyone is already working on it, and ZoomInfo has already shipped an AI copilot for prospecting. This isn't a problem hiding in plain sight. It's a problem standing in the middle of the road with a neon sign.

Vishnu built sourcing technology at Sierra Ventures, which gives him real exposure to the problem of tracking prospects and surfacing signals. That's better than a brainstorming session. He wasn't reading TechCrunch and thinking "AI is hot, sales tools are hot, let's combine them." He was actually in the weeds of VC deal sourcing, which involves exactly the kind of signal monitoring Autumn does for sales teams. But there's a difference between adjacency and obsession. The strongest founder-problem connections I've seen — Drew Houston forgetting his USB drive, the Airbnb founders not making rent — involve a founder who couldn't escape the problem. Vishnu saw the problem while building tools for someone else's workflow. That's a weaker bond than I'd like.

The technical credentials here are genuinely strong. Two Columbia CS graduates, one with a NeurIPS presentation and an AI residency at SandboxAQ (an Alphabet spinout), the other with published ML research and contributions to LMCache. They met on the first day of freshman orientation and spent four years together before founding this company — that's exactly the kind of co-founder relationship that survives the crucible. As a two-person team, they're clearly building the product themselves. If I were evaluating the founders in isolation, I'd be interested. These are real hackers.

The bull case: LLMs have crossed a threshold that makes real-time extraction of buying signals from unstructured public text newly feasible at low cost. The incumbents are structured around selling proprietary contact databases — their revenue models depend on data licensing, which creates a structural conflict with building an open-signal aggregation product. A fast, lightweight product focused on real-time public signals could carve out the SMB segment that ZoomInfo and 6sense largely ignore. If Autumn builds the product that makes a three-person sales team as informed as an enterprise team with a six-figure ZoomInfo contract, there's a real wedge there. The SMB adoption rate is supposedly growing 200%+ over the next two years. And these founders have the technical ability to build something elegant while the incumbents build something bloated.

But here's what concerns me. The core technology — monitoring public APIs, applying NLP to extract intent signals from social posts and filings — is technically reproducible by any competent engineering team. The dossier itself notes that "LLM-based signal extraction is increasingly commoditized." When the schlep is just engineering effort rather than structural barriers like regulation, logistics, or trust, the moat has to come from something else: network effects, switching costs, proprietary data loops. Autumn has none of these yet, and at this stage I see no structural reason they'd develop them before a larger competitor adds the same feature. API fragility is another real problem — LinkedIn, Twitter, and GitHub have all progressively restricted access. Building your core product on data sources that could disappear at the platform's discretion is a foundation made of sand.

There's no evidence of user pull. No named customers, no revenue, no usage metrics. The YC LinkedIn launch post got 977 reactions, but LinkedIn engagement on a launch announcement tells me about social media dynamics, not product demand. At pre-seed I don't require revenue, but I want to see something — a user who found the product and won't let go, a sales team that's already built their workflow around the early access product, any signal that people actually want this specific thing. Total absence of that evidence, combined with the crowded competitive landscape, means I can't distinguish Autumn from the other companies building LLM-powered sales signal tools right now.

These founders are technically talented and they have a solid co-founder relationship. I wouldn't be surprised if they build something important eventually. But the current idea — aggregating public signals for sales teams using AI — is exactly the kind of problem where being smart and technical isn't enough to win, because everyone else building in this space is also smart and technical, and many of them have hundreds of millions in funding. The problem isn't hidden. The schlep isn't deep. The technology isn't defensible. I'd want to see these founders working on something where their technical ability creates more structural advantage.
