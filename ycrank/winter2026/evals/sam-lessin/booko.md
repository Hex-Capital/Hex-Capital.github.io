# Booko -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Booko is the distilled version of the bet I tell people to stop making. Dynamic pricing for yoga studio time slots using ML demand prediction is not an insane idea -- it's an extremely sane idea, which is precisely the problem. Airlines have done this for forty years. Hotels have done it for thirty. The entire revenue management industry exists. Applying it to fitness classes and medspa appointments is an incremental extension of a well-understood concept into an adjacent vertical. When I look at the founder-idea pairing, I see an unknowable founder with a known idea -- the opposite of the Coinbase pattern where you had a credentialed engineer proposing something genuinely crazy. Neither founder has any publicly verifiable background in dynamic pricing, ML systems, booking platforms, or the wellness industry. The product demonstrates technical competence -- SOC 2, HIPAA, per-client model training, API integrations -- but I can't trace that competence to the founders' histories because those histories don't exist in the public record. I need the "sane" part to be visible for the framework to function.

The structural moat problem here is severe, and it maps directly onto the lesson Birchbox taught me. Booko's core value proposition is an ML model that predicts which time slots will go unfilled and adjusts pricing to fill them. The dossier itself states the quiet part: "The core technology -- demand prediction for time slots using historical booking data -- is not novel at an algorithmic level. Any ML team with access to booking data could build a similar model." That's the entire business. The software IS the product, and the software is commoditizable. As models get cheaper and more capable -- which they are, rapidly -- this becomes a feature that Mindbody, Zenoti, or any booking platform can ship natively. Mindbody already has dynamic pricing. They charge 20% plus 3.5% today, and the bull case is that this fee creates an opening for a cheaper third-party optimizer. But that opening exists only as long as Mindbody chooses not to close it. The moment Booko proves the market is real and worth capturing, Mindbody can cut its take rate, bundle pricing optimization into its core subscription, or restrict API access. You're building on a platform that has both the incentive and the capability to absorb your functionality. This is the "feature waiting to be absorbed by an incumbent" pattern in its clearest form.

The bull case deserves genuine engagement because the perishable inventory insight is real. An empty 5pm Tuesday yoga class is gone forever -- unlike a product on a shelf, there is no storage, no markdown, no second chance. That creates genuine merchant urgency, and the claimed 20% revenue uplift is a compelling ROI if it holds across a broad customer base. The Mindbody take-rate conflict is real structural analysis: Mindbody profits from the 20% fee on each dynamically priced booking, which creates a genuine disincentive to reduce that fee and match a standalone optimizer. If Booko could execute fast enough to establish itself as the default pricing layer across multiple booking platforms -- not just Mindbody but Zenoti, WellnessLiving, GlossGenius, and the long tail -- before any single platform absorbs the feature, there's a version of this that becomes the "pricing primitive" for all time-slot businesses. Tax accountants, consultants, event venues, tutoring centers -- the addressable surface area is broad. But that expansion requires building and maintaining integrations with every vertical's dominant platform, which is operational slog without compounding returns. Each new vertical is a new integration lift, not a network effect that accelerates the next one.

What I notice that a generic analyst might not: this company sits in the exact dead zone of my framework. It's a software business where the AI capability is the pitch. The website leads with "94.2% prediction accuracy" and "ML models trained on per-organization historical booking data." When the first thing you tell me is how good your AI is, you've told me that every competitor will have equivalent AI capabilities within twelve to eighteen months. The question I always ask -- would this business be good if AI didn't exist? -- produces a clear answer: without the ML model, there is no Booko. There's no physical operation, no regulatory position, no protocol-layer lock-in, no network effect. It's pure software optimization running on someone else's data, accessed through someone else's API, serving customers who belong to someone else's platform. Compare this to Craftwork, where the software layer makes a painting company more efficient but the moat is operational -- crews, scheduling, quality control, the physical reality of showing up with ladders and brushes. AI can't paint your house. AI absolutely can predict whether your 5pm yoga class will fill up.

The narrative ceiling is visible. "Dynamic pricing for time-slot businesses" is a real category but not an infinity story. It doesn't expand into a new economic primitive. It doesn't become infrastructure that other businesses build on. It's a feature layer -- a useful one, potentially a profitable one for a while -- but not one that compounds into something where each milestone makes the next narrative expansion more credible. The company's tags on its own YC page tell the story: "Machine Learning, SaaS, B2B, Analytics." Every word in that list is a word I use as a filter to pass.
