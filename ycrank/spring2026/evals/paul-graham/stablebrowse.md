# StableBrowse -- Paul Graham Evaluation

StableBrowse is a schlep-aware infrastructure pitch wrapped around a founder team that left no animal tracks before the check — four cofounders, an "execution graph" for insurance and mortgage portals, and zero cereal boxes. The dossier hands me the regulated verticals I like (HIPAA, healthcare prior auth, mortgage processing per stablebrowse.com) and withholds the one thing I read for first: what did Sarthak Awasthi, Jay Mehta, Deepit Shah, and Somansh Shah ship in public before they pitched? A self-reported "98% success rate" with no third-party benchmark is not an artifact; it is a deck slide. That mismatch — schlep present, animal absent — is the whole evaluation.

**Coinbase** (2012) is the right ladder rung to put next to StableBrowse, and the comparison is exactly where StableBrowse breaks. Brian Armstrong won the schlep nobody wanted because he personally welcomed the bitcoin compliance grind that scared the rest of the YC batch into pure-software bets; the unpleasantness *was* the moat. StableBrowse's listed targets — insurance quote binding, prior authorization, mortgage processing — are real grunt work in the same family, and "SOC 2 Type I and Type II certifications in progress" is the kind of paperwork sentence Armstrong would have written. But where Armstrong rescued his rejected application with a hand-built Android wallet, StableBrowse offers a marketing line about "engineers at Meta, Google, Amazon, Citi" using the product — affiliation logos are not artifacts. The schlep is named; the appetite for it is asserted, not demonstrated.

The pass example in my own file — the MBA team with a glossy deck and no shipped demo — is the failure pattern I have to test StableBrowse against on animal resourcefulness, and the pattern partly fits. StableBrowse's dossier contains no GitHub repos for any of the four founders, no Hacker News thread, no Product Hunt launch, no pre-funding stunt, no scrappy revenue improvisation, no Show HN of the execution graph engine that would be trivial to ship as an open-source teaser. The "70-80% fewer tokens" and "3-4x faster" claims on stablebrowse.com are self-reported and unaudited; Drew Houston posted a demo video at **Dropbox** (2007) that strangers could run, not a metric table. Animals leave tracks. I am looking at fresh snow.

Organic-idea fit on StableBrowse is the dimension I want to score generously and cannot quite. The snippet that founders "started StableBrowse after frustrations themselves while building browser agents" is the right shape of sentence — it echoes Drew Houston's USB stick — but it is one attributed line in a search result, not the lived backstory Patrick Collison had hacking commerce flows at **Auctomatic** (2007) before **Stripe** (2010). Jay Mehta's eight years in distributed systems is adjacent to the problem, not inside it. The product is plausibly something Mehta wished existed; it is not obviously something he had been hand-rolling for two years.

Hacker genealogy on StableBrowse is where the dossier compounds against itself over the next eighteen months. Deepit Shah co-authored a paper on adaptive RBF numerical methods and did time at Amazon — that is technical credentialing, not hacker tribal markers. Patrick Collison won Ireland's Young Scientist with Lisp work before he was twenty; Aaron Swartz co-wrote RSS before **Reddit** (2005). The StableBrowse dossier surfaces zero open-source contributions across four founders, zero competitive-programming history, zero recognized HN presence. In a category where Browser Use exists as an open-source project the founders could have contributed to publicly, the silence is the signal. Hackers building browser infrastructure leave commits.

Cofounder configuration is the dimension where StableBrowse would flip a tier if one fact were true. Counterfactual: if the dossier said Mehta and the Shahs roomed together at UIUC or worked the same Amazon team for three years, StableBrowse moves from a four-person team with no described prior relationship — my explicit low-rung anchor from "What We Look for in Founders" (October 2010) — into **Airbnb** (2009) trio territory. The dossier says neither. Four cofounders with undisclosed chemistry is the configuration the YC interview is designed to surface and I am not in the room.

Thirty-six month forecast on StableBrowse: acquihired by Browserbase or Kernel under $30M, or pivoted into a vertical-specific RPA tool sold to one insurance carrier and one health system, with ARR under $2M. The mechanism is the high/low signature: a real schlep without an animal team is exactly the profile that gets absorbed by the better-funded incumbent that *can* hire animals — Browserbase already has $67.5M raised, $300M valuation, and $3M revenue per Upstarts Media 2025, which means the channel and the capital are sitting on the other side of the table. The semantic-graph layer is buildable by any of them in a quarter.

The bull case on StableBrowse that actually stings is the **Coinbase** (2012)-as-moat argument: if StableBrowse's per-site knowledge graphs for insurance carrier portals and EHR systems compound into verified execution patterns no incumbent will rebuild from scratch, the regulated-vertical schlep becomes the same kind of unpleasant-work asset Armstrong built. Tom Blomfield picked this. That is real signal. But Coinbase had Armstrong's rejection-and-prototype animal moment on the record before the check; StableBrowse has marketing copy and four engineers without GitHub trails. The pattern I lose money on is clever idea, soft founders — and a semantic execution graph for regulated browser automation is a clever idea. I pass. The verifiable condition that flips me: a signed paid pilot with a named top-ten US insurance carrier or a named health system within six months, disclosed publicly. That is the cereal-box-equivalent for this company. Until then, the schlep is theoretical and the founders are unproven.
