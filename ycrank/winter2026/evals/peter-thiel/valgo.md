# Valgo -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The most striking thing about Valgo is not the idea but the provenance. Robert Moss spent seven years at MIT Lincoln Laboratory on the core team that built ACAS Xa — the next-generation collision avoidance system now certified by the FAA as a worldwide standard for aircraft. He, Sydney Katz, and Harrison Delecki then co-authored what will be the MIT Press textbook on algorithmic safety validation, and they teach the corresponding Stanford course. This is not a team that read about a market opportunity and decided to build a startup. These are the people who created the intellectual foundations of the field. When I co-founded Palantir, the insight was that PayPal's fraud algorithms could be repurposed for intelligence analysis — a specific technical capability being redirected toward a new domain. Valgo has a structurally similar origin: safety validation algorithms developed for aviation and autonomous systems, redirected toward insurance risk quantification. The question is whether the insight underneath the capability constitutes a genuine secret.

The implicit thesis is that insurance for autonomous systems — the bottleneck preventing commercial deployment of self-driving trucks, delivery robots, and physical AI broadly — can be unlocked by converting simulation data into actuarially credible loss estimates. The conventional insurance world insists on historical claims data; the autonomous vehicle world insists on better technology. Valgo's contrarian claim is that neither side sees the real bottleneck: the translation layer between simulation and underwriting. This is a meaningful insight, but I would not call it a secret in the strongest sense. Most thoughtful people in the autonomy space would agree, once you explain it, that simulation data should be usable for risk pricing. The disagreement is less about the truth of the thesis and more about whether it can be executed — whether insurers will trust it, whether regulators will accept it, whether autonomy companies will share their data. That makes this more of an execution bet on a structural gap than an investment in a truth the world rejects.

The positioning as infrastructure rather than carrier is the smartest strategic choice in the dossier. Koop Technologies has raised $7 million operating as a vertically integrated MGA — writing policies through Lloyd's. Valgo positions as the risk quantification layer underneath all insurers, the way Stripe positioned as payments infrastructure rather than competing with banks. If Valgo's probabilistic models become the accepted standard — the ACAS Xa of autonomous insurance — then every insurer entering the space would build on top of them. Standards are the deepest form of monopoly, and Moss's biography proves he knows what it takes to get a safety system from research to FAA certification. The path from Stanford lab to regulatory standard is not hypothetical for this team; they have walked it before.

The bull case, stated as strongly as I can: Valgo occupies a position where neither side of the market (autonomy companies nor insurers) has the expertise or incentive to build what Valgo builds. The founders are arguably the three most qualified people on Earth for this specific problem. If autonomous systems deployment accelerates — and every signal suggests it will — someone must solve the insurance infrastructure problem, and Valgo starts with an insurmountable knowledge advantage. Each customer's simulation data improves the model, creating data network effects that compound. The addition of an insurance executive with $5 billion in M&A experience (if the credentials verify) addresses the domain gap on the underwriting side. And the expansion path is elegant: start with autonomous trucks, expand to robotaxis, then delivery robots, then drones, then any autonomous system. The risk quantification layer for physical AI is a potentially enormous category.

But several structural concerns prevent me from reaching full conviction. First, the dual-sided market problem is genuine and severe. Autonomy companies must share proprietary simulation data — information they consider competitively sensitive — while insurers must accept a fundamentally new paradigm for rate-filing. Neither transition has established norms or regulatory precedent. Second, the market timing is hostage to autonomous vehicle deployment timelines, which have consistently disappointed. Valgo could be right about everything and still face a five-year wait before the market urgency matches the product readiness. Third, while the competitive landscape is thin, Koop Technologies has a multi-year head start in building relationships with both autonomy companies and Lloyd's. Fourth — and this is what holds me back most — the thesis, while sound, is not the kind of truth that most intelligent people actively reject. It is a hard problem that no one has solved, not a secret that the consensus denies. The distinction matters because a hard problem invites many solvers once the market becomes large enough, while a genuine secret provides structural protection through disbelief.

The founders are exceptional — perhaps the best founder-market fit I have seen at a YC pre-seed in this cohort. The technical moat from academic authority is real and would take a competing team years to replicate. But exceptional founders building on a structural market gap, without a deeply contrarian thesis that repels competitors, produces a strong company — not necessarily a monopoly. I would want to meet this team. If Moss and Katz demonstrate the kind of definite, specific vision for how their risk models become the regulatory standard for all physical AI — not just a product but an infrastructure layer that makes the old actuarial paradigm obsolete — I could be persuaded. As it stands in the dossier, I see extraordinary capability deployed against a real problem, but not yet the kind of civilizational conviction that turns a very good startup into a defining company.
