# Laurence -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Laurence is the domain transfer: Leo Gierhake spent years at Jump Trading overseeing billions in live trading volume, and now he's applying that stochastic optimization intuition to Amazon PPC bidding. This is the kind of specific knowledge I look for -- you can't learn to manage billions in live trading volume from a course or a book. It's knowledge forged under real-money pressure, where being wrong costs you immediately and being right compounds. The poker background ($250K in lifetime earnings) reinforces this: someone who has internalized probabilistic thinking at the felt, where every decision is a bet under uncertainty with imperfect information. That's non-teachable. Matthew Chen's ML engineering at Google and Meta is strong but more replicable -- any top-tier ML engineer at a FAANG company accumulates similar skills. The combination works: one founder who understands how to make decisions under stochastic uncertainty, one who knows how to build ML systems at scale. But here's what gives me pause -- neither founder has deep, lived-in Amazon advertising domain expertise. That knowledge lives in the advisor, Aaron Apple. When the domain insight is in an advisor rather than a founder, the specific knowledge architecture has a structural gap.

The leverage mechanics are real but bounded. Software automating manual agency work -- recalculating bids hourly instead of weekly across tens of thousands of keywords -- is genuine leverage compression. Two founders managing $10M in live ad spend that would normally require a team of analysts and agency workers is exactly the kind of output-per-input ratio that makes software powerful. The data flywheel is there: more managed ad spend trains better models, which produce better results, which attract more ad spend. But this is automating an existing workflow more efficiently, not creating a new category of capability that didn't exist before. When I invested in AngelList, it wasn't making fundraising faster -- it was making a new kind of fundraising possible. Laurence makes ad bidding better. That's valuable. It's not the same thing as creating leverage that didn't previously exist.

The contrarian case is weak, and that's my primary concern. "AI for advertising optimization" is about as consensus as it gets in 2026. Yes, the specific application of reinforcement learning rather than rule-based automation is technically differentiated -- most incumbents use simpler approaches. But the category itself is crowded: Pacvue, Quartile, Teikametrics, Perpetua, Skai, plus every new entrant with an ML background. Five funded competitors with meaningful scale. When I backed Uber, ride-sharing was illegal in most cities -- smart people thought it was a terrible idea. When I backed Bitcoin, serious technologists dismissed it as nerd money. What smart, informed person is currently dismissing the idea that AI can optimize ad bidding? Nobody. They're building competing versions of it. The traction is solid for pre-seed -- growing from $2.5M to $10M in managed ad spend, 15-20% profit improvements, 40% ACoS reduction, named clients -- but strong traction in a consensus category doesn't produce asymmetric returns. It produces a good business that gets competed down.

The bull case I have to take seriously: if RL genuinely outperforms rule-based optimization by 15-40% consistently, the incumbents can't easily replicate it. Pacvue has 462 employees generating $50.8M in revenue -- that's an organization optimized for its current architecture, not for fundamental R&D pivots. Quartile manages $2B+ in ad spend with simpler ML. If Laurence's approach delivers measurably and consistently better outcomes, the data flywheel could compound faster than incumbents can respond, and the Amazon PPC TAM ($60B+ in ad revenue) is large enough to support a massive outcome. The quant trading background genuinely is rare in this market -- most ad-tech engineers come from e-commerce, not from managing billions in live trading volume. If I squint, this looks like the early days of quantitative trading itself: a small team with superior mathematical models entering a market dominated by manual, rule-based approaches. Renaissance Technologies didn't need a contrarian thesis about whether markets could be modeled -- they just needed better models than everyone else. But the analogy breaks down because Amazon controls the platform. In financial markets, the exchange is neutral infrastructure. Amazon is a competitor, a platform, and a regulator simultaneously. That platform dependency -- Amazon can change API access, auction mechanics, or data availability at any time -- puts a ceiling on the compounding potential that makes me uncomfortable for a long-term bet.

The founding team passes the intelligence-energy filter. Cornell Math/CS, Google, Meta, Jump Trading -- these are high-signal credentials. The advisory board with Aaron Apple (top-75 Amazon 3P seller at exit, 8-figure PPC consultancy) provides genuine distribution knowledge and channel access. But I keep returning to the structural question: is this a company where the technology compounds and gets harder to replicate over time, or is it an execution race where the RL approach can be reproduced by well-funded competitors or new entrants from the same quant finance talent pool? The approach is technically reproducible. The data advantage compounds, but it compounds slowly relative to incumbents who already manage orders of magnitude more ad spend. I'd want to see evidence that the RL models exhibit nonlinear improvement with data -- that there's a phase transition in model quality, not just incremental gains -- before I'd be convinced the moat is durable.

This is a competent team applying genuine quantitative expertise to a large market with real early traction. It's a good company. But it's not my kind of bet. The specific knowledge is real but domain-transferred rather than domain-native, the leverage is incremental rather than categorical, and the positioning is consensus rather than contrarian. My best returns have come from companies where the world had to change its mind about whether the idea was even viable. Nobody needs to change their mind about AI optimizing ad bids.
