# Gikl, Inc -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's what catches my attention first: two senior Meta AI researchers who could have built another autonomous AI agent -- another company trying to replace scientists with machines -- instead chose to build a platform that lets ordinary people participate in science alongside AI. That choice is the most interesting thing about ScienceSwarm. Every well-funded competitor in this space, from FutureHouse at $70 million to Sakana at nearly $400 million, is betting on removing humans from the loop entirely. Google has already deployed its AI Co-Scientist at 17 DOE National Labs. And Peter Vajda and Seiji Yamamoto, who spent a combined twenty years building frontier AI at Meta -- who have the technical depth to compete head-to-head on the autonomous track -- chose the opposite direction. They're augmenting humans, not replacing them. That's a meaningful contrarian signal within a category that is otherwise extremely consensus.

But I need to be honest about what's missing for me, and what's missing is the story. If these founders were sitting across from me, my first question would be: how did you get here? Not your résumé -- I can read that. I want to know what moment made you feel that science is broken, that 99.9% of humanity being locked out of discovery is something you personally cannot accept. Seiji has a physics PhD, published in PNAS and Physical Review Letters, co-authored with a Fields Medalist -- so he's lived inside the machine. He knows what twelve years of training feels like because he did it. That's something real. But knowing the system from the inside is different from having been broken by it or excluded from it. These are accomplished insiders at one of the most powerful companies in the world. Their biographies read like success stories, not survival stories. I don't hear the biographical thread that tells me they'll persist through the inevitable crisis -- the moment Google launches a free version, the moment the community stalls at two hundred contributors, the moment the money runs out. I've learned from my own path that the founders who survive those moments are the ones who literally cannot imagine doing anything else because the problem is written into their bones.

The vision itself, though, pulls at something I care about deeply. "Coding assistants turned millions of people into software developers -- we're doing the same for science." When I backed Uber, I didn't see it as a logistics company. I saw it as a tool that gave drivers economic agency they didn't have before. The taxi medallion system was a lock on who could participate. Science has its own medallion system -- the PhD, the tenure track, the peer review gatekeepers, the institutional affiliation. If ScienceSwarm actually works, it takes one of the most opaque, exclusive systems in civilization and makes it transparent and participatory. That pattern -- making invisible systems visible, opening doors that were locked -- is exactly what drew me to Carta, to Affirm, to Flock Safety. The 0.1% statistic they cite, that only one in a thousand people actively does science, is the kind of number that should make everyone uncomfortable. The question is whether AI has genuinely crossed the threshold where a motivated non-expert with the right tools can contribute something verifiable. Foldit and the Polymath Project showed glimmers of this years ago, but those were narrow demonstrations. Whether the model scales is the fundamental bet here, and it remains completely unproven.

What gives me pause beyond the founder-story gap is that "AI for science" is one of the hottest categories in technology right now. This is not an unpopular bet. Hundreds of millions of dollars are flowing into this space. When I backed SpaceX, everyone thought private space was delusional. When I invested in Crusoe Energy, people looked at me like I was out of my mind -- mining Bitcoin on gas flares in North Dakota. The beauty of those investments was that the consensus was wrong and we could see it. Here, the consensus is that AI will transform science, and ScienceSwarm is betting on a specific twist within that consensus -- the community angle rather than the automation angle. That's a more nuanced contrarian position, but it's not the kind of deeply unpopular, "this is insane" bet that activates my strongest conviction. The competitive landscape is also genuinely threatening: Google is already in 17 DOE National Labs with a free tool, and OpenAI is targeting research-intern capability by September of this year. If those tools become sufficiently good and free, the "why do I need a community platform?" question becomes very hard to answer.

The strongest bull case would require a few things to be true simultaneously: that AI-augmented non-experts can produce verifiable scientific contributions (not just noise), that the community network effects create real switching costs before big tech replicates the model, and that the founders' deep AI expertise from Meta gives them a durable edge in building the verification and synthesis layer. If all three hold, this could become the GitHub of scientific discovery -- the place where contributions accumulate, reputations form, and the community itself becomes the moat. The nine years these founders spent working alongside each other at Meta is actually a meaningful signal on team resilience; they've navigated reorgs, product pivots, and the pressures of operating inside a company that changes strategic direction frequently. That's more stress-testing than most co-founder pairs get before starting a company together. But I would need to sit with them, hear their personal stories, understand why they left the comfort of Meta for this specific mission, and feel that jolt of energy I felt with Niantic -- that visceral sense that the opportunity is bigger than anyone realizes. Right now, on paper, I see strong credentials and an inspiring vision, but I don't feel the pull that makes me write a check from my own money.
