# Arzule -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most striking thing about Arzule is that they pivoted in the wrong direction. Their CTO Jeffrey Lin built multi-agent arbitrage systems. Their initial product was observability tooling for multi-agent workflows. That was at least organically connected to something they knew -- Lin had lived inside multi-agent coordination problems. Then during or right after the YC batch started, they abandoned that and jumped to B2B partnership intelligence. When founders pivot, the good pivots move toward deeper knowledge. Emmett Shear pivoted Justin.tv toward game streaming because he was obsessed with gaming. These founders pivoted away from the thing they actually understood and into a domain where neither of them has any documented experience managing partnerships, running SaaS go-to-market, or selling to enterprise BD teams. That's the pattern I worry about most.

The first thing I always ask is: how did they find this problem? Did it grow out of their own experience, or did they sit down and generate ideas? There is zero evidence in the dossier that either Nikhil or Jeffrey has ever managed a B2B partnership, worked in a partnerships role, or experienced the pain of partner discovery firsthand. Their backgrounds are quant research, AWS internships, multi-agent systems. The connection between those backgrounds and "AI-native revenue engine for B2B partnerships" is invisible. This has the signature of a category picked from market analysis -- they noticed PRM is a growing market, incumbents look slow, and AI can be applied to the discovery layer. That's the kind of idea that sounds smart in a brainstorming session but lacks the organic foundation that sustains founders through years of painful execution. When Stripe's founders tackled payments, they were developers who had personally cursed at payment APIs. I don't see an equivalent story here.

The bull case deserves serious consideration. The B2B partnership space does contain genuine schlep -- messy ecosystem data scattered across press releases, integration pages, and product changelogs, combined with the tedium of partner coordination and revenue attribution. That's the kind of unsexy, fragmented problem that incumbents like Crossbeam and PartnerStack haven't fully addressed because they built their platforms around managing existing relationships, not discovering new ones. If Arzule's AI-driven monitoring layer genuinely works, the upstream discovery wedge is a real gap. And the founders' multi-agent systems expertise could be a legitimate technical edge for building the kind of coordinated agent infrastructure that continuous ecosystem monitoring requires. The pivot also shows they can adapt -- they recognized the multi-agent observability market wasn't right and moved. That's one half of relentlessly resourceful. But adaptability without domain conviction is just searching, and searching is different from building something you can't stop thinking about.

The traction signals are thin even by pre-seed standards. "Working with companies in DevTools, HR, and Finance verticals" is the kind of phrase that could mean three paying customers or three introductory calls. The $400M partnership revenue figure describes their customers' existing revenue, not anything Arzule generates. No pricing page, no revenue signal, no waitlist data. The Twitter account is restricted. With a recent pivot, this product may be weeks old in its current form. I'd want to see a single customer who says they'd be genuinely hurt if Arzule disappeared -- any evidence of real economic dependency. I don't see that.

On the technical side, both founders have CS degrees and software engineering backgrounds, and they clearly can build things. Lin's multi-agent arbitrage system is a real technical project. But I can't find any public GitHub repos, open-source work, or visible code from either founder. At pre-seed I don't require a finished product, but I want to see evidence that these are the kinds of hackers who ship things because they can't help themselves. The absence of any visible building artifacts is a mild concern, not a dealbreaker. They're technical, but the evidence is inferential rather than demonstrated.

The competitive landscape also gives me pause. Crossbeam has $117M in funding and sits on top of partnership account data that would be a natural foundation for building exactly the discovery layer Arzule is targeting. PartnerStack has $40M and deep relationships with partnership teams. These aren't sleepy incumbents in a market that technology has passed by -- they're actively evolving platforms with the data assets and customer relationships to add AI-driven discovery as a feature rather than cede it to a startup. This isn't a market where nobody wants to do the work. It's a market where well-funded companies are already doing adjacent work and could expand.

I don't think this is my kind of bet. The absence of organic problem discovery is the primary issue. The pivot from their area of genuine expertise into an unfamiliar domain, the lack of any founder-problem backstory, and the competitive positioning against well-resourced incumbents all point toward a company that was conceived through market analysis rather than lived experience. Smart founders, real problem, wrong origin story.
