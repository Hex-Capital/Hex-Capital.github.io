# Confluence Labs -- Elad Gil Evaluation

The most striking thing about Confluence Labs isn't the 97.9% ARC-AGI-2 score -- it's the inferential leap between what they've demonstrated and what they're selling. ARC-AGI-2 tests the ability to solve abstract grid transformation puzzles from a few examples. Drug design, materials science, and physics research involve entirely different problem structures -- continuous optimization spaces, noisy experimental data, complex physical constraints. The claim is essentially: "We can learn patterns in colored grids efficiently, therefore we can design molecules efficiently." That's not a product thesis, it's a research hypothesis. And it's a hypothesis that needs to be validated before anything else matters -- which means this company requires multiple sequential miracles, not one. First, prove the approach transfers from abstract benchmarks to scientific domains. Second, build domain-specific integrations for each vertical. Third, sell into slow-moving, risk-averse enterprise research organizations. Fourth, do all of this with two people against Imbue ($232M, 10K H100s) and Isomorphic Labs ($600M, DeepMind lineage). That's four miracles, and I've written explicitly about why compounding small-probability events makes a venture very likely to fail.

The market question compounds the concern. "AI for scientific discovery" is not a non-obvious market -- it's one of the most consensus-hot narratives in venture capital right now. Every major AI lab has a science initiative. Isomorphic Labs raised $600M. Recursion is public. Insilico has a drug candidate in Phase 2a. When I see a $4.8B-to-$34.8B TAM slide citing Precedence Research, that's exactly the static market sizing I warn against -- it tells you nothing about where this specific company captures value or why now is different from two years ago. The "why now" they implicitly offer -- that LLM program synthesis has crossed a capability threshold on ARC-AGI-2 -- is shared by every competitor. Imbue hit 95.1% within days of Confluence's 97.9%. This is a fast-moving leaderboard, not a structural moat. And they've open-sourced their solver under MIT license, which means their primary demonstrated advantage is freely replicable. Compare this to Harvey, where the single miracle -- model fidelity for legal work -- was being delivered by the foundation model labs, freeing Harvey to focus on workflow and distribution. Confluence Labs has to deliver their own miracle AND build the application layer AND sell into enterprise science.

The strongest bull case: if "sample-efficient AI" genuinely represents a distinct technology paradigm from data-scaling AI, and if the big labs are structurally misaligned because their business models incentivize breadth over efficiency, then Confluence could be building a foundational layer that large labs won't build themselves. Data-sparse scientific domains might be structurally underserved by the scaling paradigm in the same way that small business payroll was underserved by enterprise HR vendors before Gusto. The Paul Graham personal backing is a real signal -- Graham is selective and presumably evaluated the founders directly. And Niranjan's reported prior startup at $5M ARR (if accurate) suggests this isn't his first time building something commercial. If you believe the ARC-AGI-2 result is evidence of a generalizable capability rather than benchmark optimization, and if you believe the transfer to scientific domains will happen within 12-18 months, this could look like a prescient early bet. But that's a lot of "ifs," and the structural evidence for the transfer thesis is absent from the dossier entirely.

The founders have demonstrated genuine technical execution speed -- achieving SOTA on a competitive benchmark within weeks of its release is real. But I see no evidence they've thought about distribution beyond "developing partnerships with researchers." The company's only public distribution signal is 94 GitHub stars on an open-source solver. There's no articulated path from benchmark result to paying customers, no indication of how the initial product becomes a channel for additional products, and no evidence of distribution thinking at the earliest stage. When I ask "can this become a distribution channel?" the honest answer is: I can't even identify what the initial product is, let alone how it distributes follow-on products. The Atlassian Confluence brand confusion is a real GTM headwind that suggests the founders haven't yet prioritized the commercial side of the business.

The technology cycle timing is partially right -- LLM reasoning capabilities have clearly crossed an inflection point, with ARC-AGI-2 scores jumping from ~54% to 97%+ in months. But being right about the capability curve and being right about the commercial application are different things. The pharmaceutical and materials science industries have notoriously long adoption cycles for new computational tools. Being technically early is valuable only if you're commercially positioned to capture the market when it arrives -- and I see no evidence of commercial positioning here. This is a research project looking for a product, not a product positioned at a structural moment.
