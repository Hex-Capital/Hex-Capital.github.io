# Oddpool -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Oddpool is a strong-window company with a thin operator profile: the highest-scoring dimension is the post-Dome structural opening in prediction-market data infrastructure, and the lowest is a two-person team that pivoted from Benchspan inside the same P26 batch. That pairing tells me what kind of bet Oddpool is — a real nonobvious window that consensus has not yet reframed since Polymarket absorbed Dome in early 2026, sitting in the hands of two competent ICs (Avi Arora ex-Microsoft Prompt Shields, Ritesh Malpani ex-Bloomberg) with no scaling history and a six-month-old product. The window is wider than most YC P26 companies I've looked at this batch; the team is not yet built to run through it.

Market structure timing is where Oddpool earns its check-worthiness. The high rung on this ladder is Coinbase in 2012 — I read a structural shift in digital asset infrastructure before consensus formed. The low rung is the consumer social apps I pass on every batch because no exogenous platform shift has occurred. Oddpool sits closer to Harvey than to a consumer pass: Kalshi crossing CFTC clearance and hitting ~40% institutional flow (trade-ideas.com, Apr 2026), $44B+ in 2025 trading volume crossing the threshold where institutional desks need professional data tooling, and Polymarket's acquisition of Dome removing the only YC-backed neutral aggregator from the market within the last six months. That last fact is the timestamp — the structural opening is dated, specific, and not yet consensus. Most investors are still looking at prediction markets as a consumer betting story, not as a data infrastructure category that just lost its incumbent.

Operator-scaler founder profile is where Oddpool's ladder placement is weakest, and this is the dimension that decides whether the window gets converted. The high rung is Parker Conrad rebuilding Rippling after operating Zenefits through scale — someone who has personally lived the 1-to-100 transition. The low rung is the equal co-founder structure with no decisive CEO, which I treat as a hard red flag. Oddpool sits between these but closer to the bottom: Avi Arora's Microsoft Prompt Shields work (20B classifications/year) and Ritesh Malpani's "100K+ TPS trading infrastructure" at Bloomberg are real IC credentials, but neither founder has scaled an organization, and the LinkedIn headlines for both still reference "Co-founder @ Benchspan (YC P26)" — Oddpool is a within-batch pivot, not an accumulated thesis. No designated CEO is disclosed in the dossier. Strong markets compensate for some execution gaps, but they don't compensate for indecision when a venue tries to acquire you.

Infrastructure-layer positioning lands mid-rung. Stripe is the high anchor — payments API that thousands of businesses built on top of. Wish is the low anchor — pure consumer marketplace with no API layer, which is exactly why it collapsed 99% post-IPO. Oddpool's S3 dumps, WebSocket feeds, unified REST across Kalshi and Polymarket, and the 700K+ contracts indexed put it on the infrastructure side of that line, but the two-venue coverage versus Prediction Hunt v2's five-venue surface (Kalshi, Polymarket, PredictIt, ProphetX, Opinion) means Oddpool is not yet the default layer. The historical orderbook archive from March 2026 with 1-5 minute granularity is the most defensible asset Oddpool owns — that is a forward-only dataset a later entrant cannot rebuild — and it's the reason this dimension scores meaningfully above zero.

Single-miracle viability is mixed for Oddpool. Harvey was the canonical single-miracle case: the technology miracle was happening exogenously on the foundation-model curve. Anduril was my known exception that violated this test on the strength of overwhelming market structure. Oddpool needs no technology miracle (the venue APIs are public), but it does need three things to align: continued venue API access without ToS revocation, institutional buyers selecting a neutral aggregator over native venue tools, and outpacing Prediction Hunt and FinFeedAPI on coverage. The Dome precedent — Polymarket acquiring its data layer — is the specific mechanism that compresses the miracle count: the venues are demonstrably hostile to neutral aggregators at scale. Margin architecture is the cleanest dimension: Oddpool's tiered pricing ($30 Pro / $100 Premium / Enterprise custom) is usage-based per API call, scales with customer value the way Abridge's per-encounter pricing does, and carries software gross margins. No retention data is disclosed, so the dimension cannot score high, but the architecture is correctly designed.

My 36-month forecast for Oddpool: acquired by Kalshi or Polymarket for $20-60M within 18-24 months, mirroring the Dome-to-Polymarket path almost exactly. The mechanism is the same one that closed Dome — the venues are structurally incentivized to absorb neutral aggregators rather than let them aggregate institutional flow across competitors, and Oddpool's historical orderbook archive is the specific asset that makes it acquisition-attractive rather than ignorable. The acquihire ceiling caps the venture outcome well below the 10x-1000x range I require.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is that Oddpool's March-2026 historical orderbook archive becomes the Bloomberg-for-prediction-markets dataset over 36 months, and the post-Dome neutrality gap is structurally permanent because Kalshi and Polymarket cannot acquire each other's aggregators without antitrust friction — meaning Oddpool plays both sides into a real $50M+ ARR institutional data business. That case stings because the dataset is genuinely non-replicable forward-only, and I've seen historical-data archives compound into Bloomberg-class moats. The dossier evidence that wins for the bear case is specific: Polymarket already acquired Dome rather than tolerating it, the YC-backed precedent is six months old, and Oddpool is currently a two-venue product against Prediction Hunt's five-venue coverage. The verifiable condition that would flip me to Invest: signed institutional pilots at $50K+ ACV with two named quant desks (Citadel, Jane Street, Susquehanna, Jump) — that proves the data moat is winning the institutional sale fast enough to escape the acquihire ceiling. Without that signal, Oddpool is a high-probability Dome rerun.
