# Amboras -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Amin and Imad Mokadem built an e-commerce brand to $400K+/month revenue before deciding the tools they were using were broken. Imad started even earlier — designing board games at ETH Zurich and building a Shopify store to sell them, then getting frustrated enough with the developer dependency and plugin sprawl that he built EcomCoder, a Shopify plugin, to scratch his own itch. That chain — board games to Shopify frustration to plugin to standalone platform — is the Dropbox pattern. Drew Houston forgot his USB drive and built file sync. Imad Mokadem hated managing his Shopify store and built Amboras. The problem discovery is authentically organic, and it's Amboras's strongest asset. These founders lived in the e-commerce future and built what was missing from it. They didn't sit in a conference room and brainstorm TAM slides.

Brothers who've already operated a six-figure MRR business together have something most pre-seed co-founders lack: a stress-tested relationship. I've seen too many founding teams fracture under pressure because they never had pressure before. Amin (CS, ETH Zurich) and Imad (Mechanical Engineering, ETH Zurich) have done the hard thing of building real revenue together, and Amboras represents their second act. The hire of Abde Aboulas — an ex-UCI cyclist who scaled growth at Rapha and Carhartt — shows they're thinking about commercial execution, not just product. What I can't see in this dossier is what I most want to see: the stories of creative workarounds, the moments where Amboras hit a wall and the founders did something nobody expected. The $400K+/month prior business is evidence of competence, but competence and resourcefulness aren't the same thing. I don't have Amboras's cereal-box moment.

"Let AI sell your stuff on autopilot" is the kind of tagline that makes everyone nod. "The Shopify of the AI era" sounds great in any pitch meeting. And that's exactly what worries me. When I wrote about Black Swan farming, the core insight was that the best investments initially look wrong to almost everyone — because if they looked right, the opportunity would already be competed away. Amboras is building in a space where Durable has raised $26.5M, Swap has raised $149M, Shopify is aggressively shipping AI features through its Renaissance update, and every developer with access to an LLM API can see the same opportunity. The "1,000+ stores" claim on amboras.com would be a useful signal if it were independently verified, but the testimonials from Milan, London, Amsterdam, Mumbai, Barcelona, and Rome for a San Francisco pre-seed company raise more questions than they answer. Amboras doesn't have the repulsion factor I look for — nobody hears this pitch and says "that's insane." They say "oh, that's smart," which in my experience is the more dangerous response.

The pivot from EcomCoder to Amboras does show learning rate. Moving from a Shopify plugin (#14 day rank, 97 upvotes on Product Hunt) to a standalone platform is not a cosmetic rebrand — it reflects a genuine insight that being a parasite on Shopify's ecosystem limits your ceiling. That's the kind of strategic flexibility I associate with the "running back" quality. But the underlying technology — LLM-powered store generation and A/B testing, reportedly powered by Anthropic's Claude — is built on generally available infrastructure. Amboras doesn't have the kind of proprietary technical moat that Stripe built through years of PCI compliance work and bank integrations. Stripe succeeded because the Collisons were willing to do the regulatory schlep that repelled every other developer. The schlep in e-commerce platform building is real but not structural — it's product complexity, not regulatory complexity. Any well-funded team can replicate Amboras's feature set.

The bull case an advocate would make goes like this: Amboras has the most organic founder-market fit in its batch. Two ETH-educated brothers who built a $400K+/month e-commerce brand, felt the pain of Shopify's fragmented ecosystem firsthand, and now have 1,000+ stores on their AI-native alternative — all before a proper fundraise. The bundling advantage is real: Shopify's app marketplace revenue model creates a structural conflict that prevents Shopify from building a true all-in-one solution, and Amboras can move faster without that constraint. The 3.9% average conversion rate, if real, is roughly double the e-commerce industry average. And the ClassDojo exception from my own portfolio proves I'll invest outside my "hackers building for hackers" pattern when schlep blindness and execution quality are high enough. That's a reasonable argument. But it breaks down on inspection: the traction is unverified, the conversion rate is self-reported, the ClassDojo comparison doesn't hold because e-commerce platforms don't have the sales-cycle schlep of K-12 education, and the structural conflict at Shopify can be resolved the moment Shopify decides to ship an autopilot tier — which their Renaissance update suggests they're already doing. The condition that would flip me: show me independently verified revenue growing at 7%+ weekly with merchant cohort retention above 80% at 6 months. That would prove the autonomous optimization actually works and that Amboras merchants don't churn back to Shopify.

Amboras scores highest on organic problem discovery — the Mokadem brothers genuinely lived this problem — and lowest on idea contrarianism, which in my framework is the dimension that separates good-sounding bets from great-returning ones. The founders are competent, ETH-educated, and domain-experienced. But competent founders pursuing a consensus idea in a market where Shopify has 4.8M+ merchants and is actively shipping AI features is not the kind of bet I write checks for with my own money. I need the idea to repel before it attracts. Amboras attracts from the first sentence.
