# CentralComs -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

CentralComs is a vertical AI agent layer running *inside* AppFolio, Buildium, and Yardi -- which means Kyle Chang and Kevin Wong have built a business whose distribution is leased from three gatekeepers who are each shipping their own first-party agents. That single sentence is the whole evaluation. The founder has adjacent specific knowledge (Chang shipped facilities AI to NASA JPL and NVIDIA at Indoor Finders) and zero network compounding, which is the analytical signature: a competent vertical SaaS sitting on permissioned rails.

**Twitter** (2007) is the high rung on leverage architecture -- anyone posts, the platform charges nothing, marginal cost of replication is zero, no gatekeeper can revoke your distribution. CentralComs is the structural inverse. The YC page describes the product as operating "directly inside AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi," and AppFolio Realm-X is already publishing "10.3 hrs/week saved per user" numbers on the same workflows in 2026. That is not permissionless code leverage compounding the founder's specific knowledge -- that is a permission slip from three vendors who can change an API term, raise a partner-tier fee, or ship the feature natively. Anchorage Digital is the only portfolio piece where I funded permission-as-moat, and that override required pre-consensus crypto-category timing from a 2014 **MetaStable** seat. EliseAI's $250M Series E at $2.2B post-money is the opposite of pre-consensus. CentralComs does not get the Anchorage exception.

The **Pinterest** miss is what I think about on dimension two, but Pinterest at least *had* a network -- I passed because I didn't get the aesthetic, not because the architecture lacked compounding. CentralComs has the opposite problem: the architecture genuinely lacks compounding. Each property manager customer is economically independent of the next. Adding the 100th property management firm does not make the 99th's experience better, and no cross-firm graph forms inside AppFolio's walled tenancy. This is single-sided B2B SaaS, and "on the upside you can make 10,000 X" requires upside geometry that single-sided subscription economics cannot produce. The dossier offers no usage data, no waitlist, no Discord -- which is fine at pre-seed and not the issue. The issue is structural.

Kyle Chang's three years at Indoor Finders shipping facility-management AI to NVIDIA, NASA JPL, Cushman & Wakefield, and Samsung is the single dossier fact that registers as real specific knowledge. It is adjacent, not direct -- enterprise occupier facilities is not residential property management -- but it is shipped artifact in a domain a Stanford-CS-clone cannot replicate from a blog post. Mechanism over 18 months: if Chang ports the cross-vendor workflow vocabulary from corporate facilities into PMS-land faster than AppFolio's product org ships native equivalents, that knowledge gap holds; if AppFolio Realm-X and Buildium Lumina ship the same multi-step ticket flows by Q4 2026 (and the public roadmaps suggest they will), the specific-knowledge lead decays inside one product cycle. Kevin Wong's "Technical" LinkedIn line is too thin to score either way.

The market dimension is a counterfactual exercise. For CentralComs's TAM placement to flip a tier, the pitch would need to credibly describe property management as a *platform-shaped* market with at least two adjacent pivot vectors -- say, expanding from PMS-integrated agents into the insurance-claims workflow, or into HOA operations, or into cross-vertical facilities (which Chang's Indoor Finders background actually enables). The dossier does not name any of those. "AI agents for property management" inside three named PMS stacks is one vertical with one motion. Twitter pivoted out of Odeo because media-as-protocol absorbs any pivot; CentralComs's seed runway will not absorb two.

Single-anchor parallel on founder trio: **Stack Overflow** in 2008, Atwood and Spolsky, the developer-product-by-developers template. The integrity read on CentralComs is clean, the intelligence read is fine, the energy read is unmeasurable from a YC page with team size listed as "0." No partner litigation, no self-promotion about ethics, no contested prior exit -- I apply the absent-evidence default and move on. This is not where the bet breaks.

CentralComs in 36 months is either acquired by AppFolio or Buildium for $15-40M as an acqui-hire after reaching $1-3M ARR, or it winds down quietly when Realm-X and Lumina ship the same agent loops natively by mid-2027. The mechanism is the leverage-architecture failure compounding the network-absence failure: a vertical SaaS with no compounding network needs a permissionless distribution channel to outrun incumbents, and CentralComs is sitting on the incumbents' channel.

The bull case I have to sting myself with is **Perplexity** (2022), the Known Exception where a single-sided product passed because criterion #1 (permissionless API distribution), criterion #3 (Aravind Srinivas's Berkeley retrieval PhD), and pre-consensus AI-cycle timing all cleared at top quartile and reset the math. Could CentralComs be the property-management Perplexity? No. Srinivas had a PhD specifically in retrieval and entered before the category had a narrative; Chang has three years of adjacent enterprise facilities work and enters *after* EliseAI's $2.2B Series E and AppFolio's public Realm-X launch have already crystallized the narrative. Two of the three Perplexity-exception gates fail. The verdict flips only if Chang ships an open-source cross-PMS agent framework with organic developer adoption that bypasses AppFolio/Buildium/Yardi as distribution channels -- the **Stack Overflow** (2008) pattern applied to property ops. The dossier shows no GitHub repos for either founder. Until that artifact exists, CentralComs is a pass.
