# Foaster -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Foaster's LLM benchmarks — 12M+ combined views, shared by OpenAI's president, engagement from DeepMind, Mistral, and NVIDIA researchers — are a genuinely impressive credibility signal for two 23-year-old ENSAE Paris students. Raphaël Dabadie built 400k-member communities at age 11 and managed professional relationships for Rafael Nadal at 18. Alexandre Combes built Répondia, handling 60k+ restaurant calls with AI agents before pivoting to enterprise consulting. These are clearly fast learners operating from first principles, not following a playbook. The pivot itself from restaurant call handling to enterprise AI transformation is evidence of rapid adaptation — they recognized a bigger opportunity and moved. I score founders on learning rate, not résumés, and Foaster's founders register well on that dimension. But impressive founders do not override a fundamental problem with what they're building.

Foaster's consequence of success is this: enterprises adopt AI three months faster, and consulting fees drop. That is a fine business outcome, and I do not invest in fine business outcomes. When I ask "what happens to the world if Foaster fully succeeds?" the answer is that McKinsey loses some market share and Fortune 500 companies spend their AI budgets more efficiently. Nobody's life fundamentally changes. The TAM projections — $11B growing to $91B by 2035 — describe an expanding market for enterprise AI services, but Foaster is competing for redistribution of corporate consulting spend, not creating new demand. My red flags explicitly identify this pattern: "targeting redistribution of existing spend rather than creation of new demand." OpenAI created a new category. Impossible Foods created a new category. Foaster optimizes an existing one. The 350-employee case study — 280 interviews in 5 days, 1,200+ processes mapped — demonstrates execution capability, but the consequence of scaling that capability is a moderately profitable enterprise software company, not a transformed world.

Foaster's core technology — LLM-based 45-minute interviews producing operational maps — uses generally available foundation models with no evidence of proprietary model training. The dossier states this directly: "Any well-resourced team with consulting domain expertise could build a competing product." That is my definition of an execution play, not a technology play. Celonis has $1.77B in funding, $771M in 2023 revenue, and adjacent process-intelligence capabilities — adding AI interview functionality is a feature addition, not a company. McKinsey already partnered with Wonderful AI for enterprise agent deployment. The Big Four are all building AI practices with client relationships that dwarf Foaster's 1,194 LinkedIn followers. Foaster's interview methodology and prompt engineering represent accumulated know-how, but accumulated know-how in applying commodity LLMs is a narrow and temporary moat. I prefer technology risk to market risk. Foaster has both.

The bull case deserves honest construction. If AI genuinely transforms 80% of jobs — and I believe it will — then the bottleneck shifts from model capability to organizational deployment. Foaster could be the bridge every enterprise needs, the "picks and shovels" of the AI transformation wave. The founders' AI agent experience (Répondia's 60k+ calls) gives them real operational knowledge of deploying AI at scale, and their research credibility (xAI's co-founder personally requested Grok 4 be added to their benchmark) opens doors with enterprise buyers who need to trust the team's AI expertise. LIPTON Teas and Infusions as a client reference suggests Foaster is already selling to large organizations. At 23, with a demonstrated pivot and research output that caught the attention of the world's top AI labs, these founders have the trajectory to build something significant. Could a Known Exception apply? DoorDash violated my non-linear technical approach criterion because the consequence magnitude was planetary — transforming food access for hundreds of millions of consumers. But Foaster's end customer is the enterprise, not the individual. The consequence chain (Foaster helps enterprises deploy AI, which eventually helps consumers) is too indirect and too dependent on what each enterprise does with the transformation. DoorDash's exception does not apply here.

Foaster does deliver real cost reduction — a 10-day AI-driven assessment replacing months of traditional consulting at presumably a fraction of the price. That maps to my affordability lens. But the beneficiary is corporate procurement departments, not the billions of people who need cheaper healthcare, energy, food, or education. When I backed Curai Health, the affordability target was primary care for individuals who couldn't access it. When I backed Impossible Foods, it was protein for the planet. Foaster makes consulting cheaper for companies that can already afford consulting. The affordability is real but the human impact is narrow.

One condition would flip me: if Foaster demonstrated that its operational mapping technology could be turned outward — not just diagnosing where enterprises should deploy AI, but actually deploying it, becoming the execution layer that replaces the work itself — that would change the consequence magnitude from "better consulting" to "automated operations at scale." That is a different company with a different ceiling. Right now, Foaster maps the problem and prescribes solutions. The company that builds and deploys the solutions has the consequential outcome. Foaster's founders have the learning rate to get there, but the current product is diagnostic, not transformative. I would need to see evidence that Foaster is building toward replacing operational work, not just recommending where to replace it.

Foaster has strong founders building an incremental product in a commoditizing market. The decisive dimensions are consequence magnitude — which is moderate at best, bounded by the fact that this is enterprise consulting optimization rather than a new category — and non-linear technical approach, where the absence of proprietary technology and the explicit commoditization risk in the dossier place Foaster squarely in the "well-executed wrapper" category I consistently pass on. The founders earn the highest dimension score, but two out of five is not enough when the two weakest dimensions are my two most heavily weighted criteria.
