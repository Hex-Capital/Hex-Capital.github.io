# Robocurve -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Jay Chooi and Aris Zhu are building a scoreboard for someone else's revolution, and that sentence tells me almost everything I need to know about **Robocurve** through my framework. The dossier's own framing gives it away: "we measure how well robots can do real-world jobs." When I wrote the $50M check into **OpenAI** (2019), the success-state was a civilizational shift in what cognition costs; when I led **Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) at $115M, the success-state was the end of hydrocarbon baseload. **Robocurve**'s maximum-ambition success-state is that Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, and Figure all cite a Robocurve number in their pitch decks. That is a real business. It is not my business.

**OpenAI** (2019) is the HIGH rung on consequence, and the analogy loads because both **Robocurve** and OpenAI sit on top of the same wave -- physical AI, foundation models, the $15T labor thesis I made explicit to Fortune (2026-03-06). The mechanism that made OpenAI a consequence-first bet was that the *primary* artifact -- the model -- was the thing that displaced labor. **Robocurve** is the *secondary* artifact: an evals harness scoring the primary artifacts. The dossier says "benchmarking-as-a-service to frontier labs" and names **Inspect Robots** as the framework -- that is a picks-and-shovels position on top of my own **Physical Intelligence** (2024) thesis. Picks and shovels made real money in California in 1849, but I am not the picks-and-shovels investor; my portfolio is the shovel-maker's customer.

The pass example in my own playbook -- the "GPT-4 wrapper that summarizes sales calls" I dismiss in 30 seconds -- is not literally what **Robocurve** is, but it rhymes on the axis of technical and capital risk. The failure mechanism on that ladder rung is that any Tier-1 generalist can fund it, because the required miracles are zero. **Robocurve** is one notch above that: operating real robot hardware for reproducible benchmarks does carry capex and integration risk, and the dossier flags "operating and maintaining physical hardware and real-world setups" as the crux with no unit-cost disclosed. But this is not **KiOR** (~2008) catalyst chemistry or **QuantumScape** (early, IPO 2020) solid-state ion transport -- it is systems integration on commodity robots. Consensus VCs will fund this. My edge does not activate.

Jay Chooi is a Rhodes Scholar Class of 2026, Harvard BA in CS+Math with a concurrent MA in Statistics, MATS Research Fellow, and a top-7% contributor to the UK AISI's Inspect Evals repo -- a paradigmatic consensus-credential profile, and my TED Talk framework treats that as a soft negative on the "defies documented expert consensus" axis. The dossier gives me zero evidence of Chooi having publicly contradicted his field, pivoted against orthodoxy, or held a "too important not to do" position that experts rejected. Aris Zhu is a co-author on GPC in IEEE RAL 2025 and a prior Amazon SWE -- again, straight-line credentialed. Compare this to the **Physical Intelligence** (2024) known exception on my ladder: Sergey Levine and Chelsea Finn are also credentialed, but their consequence-magnitude was so large that credential-continuity was acceptable. **Robocurve** does not clear that consequence bar, so the credential profile is not overridden.

Learning rate would flip a tier for **Robocurve** only if the dossier disclosed a real pivot -- Chooi walking away from an AI-safety-evals career into robot-evals is the closest thing on offer, and the **Slash** (2024) known exception tells me I do reward documented pivots. But the shift from Inspect Evals to Inspect Robots is thematic continuity, not the Kanye-moment pivot that justified my $100M Series C into Slash. If in eighteen months either founder publishes "we were wrong about third-party benchmarking and here is what we pivoted to," I upgrade the placement; today the dossier reads as one linear plan since inception.

**Commonwealth Fusion** (2019) at $115M is the anchor for how required-capital determines whether I can even be the right investor, and **Robocurve** fails that test cleanly. Two-person team, pre-seed, benchmarking-as-a-service economics, framework already shipped -- this rounds to a $5-10M seed and a $15-25M Series A. I am over-resourced. Even if I loved every other dimension, my check size does not fit this cap table, and the Public Benefit Corporation registration in the dossier footer plus the "Forecasting Physical Automation for Societal Preparedness" mission framing further signals that **Robocurve** is not architected for venture-scale outcomes.

My 36-month forecast for **Robocurve**: acquired by a frontier robotics lab or foundation-model buyer -- most likely Physical Intelligence, Skild, or Figure -- for $15-40M as an internal evals team, or absorbed into a standards consortium along the MLPerf model. The mechanism is that the same conflict-of-interest argument that opens the market for **Robocurve** ("labs cannot self-score credibly") also caps its ceiling: once one major lab acquires **Robocurve**, every other lab discounts the score, so the equilibrium is either neutral non-profit or captive acqui-hire. Neither outcome returns a Khosla Ventures fund.

The strongest bull case against my forecast is MLPerf-as-natural-monopoly: measurement layers in fast-moving AI markets have historically compounded into de facto standards, and if **Robocurve**'s **Inspect Robots** framework becomes the reference the way MLPerf became for ML inference, the network effects the dossier hand-waves about ("more labs submit, more benchmark authors publish") could actually accrue -- and Chooi's top-7% Inspect Evals contributor position gives him framework fluency that ManipulationNet and RobotPerf lack. The bear counter: MLPerf is a nonprofit consortium precisely because customers refuse to pay a for-profit third party to grade them, and **Robocurve**'s PBC registration signals the founders half-know this. The verifiable condition that flips my verdict is a signed, disclosed, multi-year paid contract with any of Physical Intelligence, Skild, 1X, or Figure by month 18 -- not a pilot, a purchase order -- because that single data point falsifies the "labs will not pay third parties to score them" thesis that drives my pass.
