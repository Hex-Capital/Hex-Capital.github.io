# Stilta -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumps out at me before anything else: five direct competitors have raised a combined $115 million-plus in the last twelve months building AI for patent attorneys. Solve Intelligence alone has $55M, 400+ customers, and claims eight-figure ARR. Patlytics has $21M. There's literally another YC-backed company -- &AI, funded by First Round -- doing the same agentic patent search thing. When I look at a market where this many founders and investors have independently identified the identical timing window and the identical thesis, I'm not looking at a non-consensus opportunity. I'm looking at a consensus trade. And consensus trades, almost by definition, produce mediocre venture returns because the "insane idea" premium has been fully arbitraged away. The founders here are sane -- no question -- but four McKinsey engineers building AI tools for a category where every serious VC has already placed a bet? That's a sane person with a sane idea, which is actually my least favorite configuration.

The AI kill test is where this really falls apart for me. Stilta IS an AI company. Remove the AI and there is literally nothing left -- no physical operations, no protocol layer, no proprietary data asset, no regulatory moat. The company's own pitch says their approach "has not been technically possible until a few months ago," which is actually a devastating admission from my framework's perspective: if the enabling technology just became available to everyone simultaneously, then every competent team in the world can build the same thing. This is the precise opposite of what I look for. My test is simple -- would this business be compelling if AI capabilities froze at today's level and never improved? The answer is maybe, but then you're competing with Solve Intelligence's 400+ customer head start. Would this business be compelling if the next generation of foundation models made agentic orchestration trivially easy for incumbents like Clarivate or Questel to bolt onto their proprietary patent databases? Almost certainly not. The company is building an application layer on data infrastructure it doesn't own, using AI capabilities it doesn't control. That's a fragile sandwich.

The strongest bull case -- and I want to be honest about it -- is that patent work has unique auditability and traceability requirements that create genuine domain depth. Legal-grade AI is harder than most people think. The McKinsey/QuantumBlack team has real enterprise deployment experience, and Oskar Block's track record bootstrapping ventures to $1M+ ARR suggests he's not just a fundraising optimizer. If they can embed deeply enough into major law firm workflows before the better-funded competitors lock those relationships down, the enterprise switching costs could compound. Patent attorneys are conservative buyers, which means whoever earns their trust first might keep it. And the agentic architecture -- autonomous multi-step workflows rather than chatbot-style Q&A -- could represent a genuine workflow shift that stickier than typical SaaS. If all of that hits, you have a real business in a growing $5B+ market. But that bull case requires winning a foot race against teams with 20-100x more capital, which is not the structural advantage I invest behind. It's an execution bet in a crowded field, and I've seen that movie enough times to know the odds.

There's no new primitive being created here. Compare this to my Venmo bet -- peer-to-peer payments were a new financial primitive that other businesses built on top of, creating compounding network effects. Or Aleo -- zero-knowledge proofs as privacy infrastructure. Stilta is an application built on someone else's models, someone else's patent databases, and someone else's agentic frameworks. Nothing in this architecture becomes a layer that other companies integrate with or build upon. The "Cursor for patent practitioners" framing is revealing -- Cursor itself is an application, not a protocol, and it already faces mounting competition from every IDE vendor building AI features. Being the "Cursor for X" is a feature description, not a moat description.

I respect the founders' credentials and I believe they can build a functional product. But this is exactly the kind of company I've been publicly warning about -- an AI-first SaaS play in a hot market where the software IS the business and the moat dissolves the moment a better-funded competitor or an incumbent with proprietary data decides to compete seriously. My Birchbox lesson applies directly: narrative magnitude without structural moat produces the worst possible venture outcome. I'm passing.
