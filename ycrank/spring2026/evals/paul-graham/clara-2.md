# Clara -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The most interesting thing about Clara is not the AI, not the market, and not the team's credentials. It's the origin of the idea. George Favvas spent nearly a decade running Circle Medical, a telehealth company he co-founded in YC's S15 batch, and grew it to $100M in revenue with profitability. Caitlin Swift was a nurse practitioner inside that same system. Zeeshan Ahmed designed the clinician-facing EHR interfaces. These three people didn't read about physician shortages in a report and decide to start a company. They ran a telehealth practice, hit the ceiling of the human-clinician bottleneck, and are now building the system they wished they'd had. When I talk about organic problem discovery, this is close to the ideal case. The idea grew from years of operational frustration, not from a brainstorming session about "AI in healthcare."

The schlep here is one of the largest I've seen in any category. State-by-state medical licensing. Insurance billing. Clinical liability. HIPAA compliance. Medical records integration from 150,000+ facilities. Prescription authority. Lab ordering workflows. This is the kind of regulatory and operational ugliness that makes smart engineers unconsciously look away. Most AI healthcare companies stay safely in the "copilot" or "documentation tool" lane precisely because they don't want to touch the actual delivery of care. Clara is doing the opposite -- inverting the model so AI does the clinical reasoning and humans verify. That inversion requires navigating every piece of the schlep that everyone else avoids. This is structurally similar to what Stripe did with payments: the problem was obvious for years, but the schlep of dealing with banks and regulators kept everyone away. Favvas already navigated this schlep once at Circle Medical. He knows where the mines are buried.

The bull case is genuinely compelling. If you've already built the clinical operations infrastructure once -- if you know how to license providers across states, bill insurance, manage clinical workflows, handle regulatory compliance -- then you have a playbook that takes years for competitors to replicate. K Health has $441M but they're AI-for-triage, not AI-first care delivery. Hippocratic AI has $3.5B valuation but they sell to health systems, not consumers. Forward spent $546M on hardware pods. Nobody well-funded is doing exactly what Clara is doing: AI-first DTC primary care with human verification, built by people who already operated the human-first version and understand its limits. Carbon Health's bankruptcy in February 2026 is also a timing signal -- the capital-intensive, clinic-heavy model is breaking, which opens space for asset-light alternatives. If Clara executes, the structural advantage of having done this before could compound rapidly.

But I have two concerns that keep my conviction below where I'd need it to write a check. The first is the missing technical founder. Favvas is operations and business. Swift is clinical. Ahmed is design. Nobody on the founding team, as far as I can tell, is a hacker who builds the core AI system. The job postings for "Founding Data Engineer" and "Founding Product Engineer" suggest the people who will write the most important code aren't on the team yet. The team is nine people, so presumably some engineers exist, but none are named co-founders with visible technical profiles. In my experience, the best companies at this stage are built by founders who wrote the first version themselves. Stripe was coded by the Collisons. Dropbox was coded by Drew Houston. When the core product is an AI system doing clinical reasoning, and nobody on the founding team appears to be the person building that system, it creates a dependency on people not yet fully in place. This isn't fatal -- Favvas proved at Circle Medical that he can build and lead technical teams -- but it's a real gap in the founding DNA.

The second concern is simpler: there is zero evidence of user pull. No user counts, no revenue, no app downloads, no waitlist numbers, no press, no organic sharing signals. Nothing. At pre-seed this isn't unusual, and I don't penalize it heavily. But combined with the technical founder gap, it means I'm being asked to bet entirely on the strength of the organic discovery and the team's operational track record. That's a strong foundation, but it's not enough for me to feel the kind of conviction I need to write a personal check. I'd want to see even a small signal -- a few hundred users on a prototype, clinicians requesting access, something that shows the AI-first model actually changes patient behavior.

If Favvas were also a programmer, or if there were any early traction signal at all, this would likely cross my threshold. The organic problem discovery is exceptional, the schlep is enormous, and the founding team's operational knowledge of healthcare delivery is genuinely rare. But the absence of a technical hacker-founder building the core AI -- in a company whose entire thesis depends on AI clinical reasoning -- keeps this in the territory of a strong idea with the right operational team but incomplete founding DNA.
