# Minicor -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's what jumped out at me before anything else: Anthropic shipped Computer Use as a public beta in October 2024. The core capability that Minicor is productizing — an AI model that can see a screen and click on things — is being handed out by the model provider as an API feature. That's not "an incumbent might add AI to compete with you." That's "the supplier of your key ingredient is also your potential replacement." Every time I see a company whose entire technical moat depends on a frontier model capability that the model maker is actively shipping as a product, I get deeply skeptical. It's the AI kill test in its most lethal form — not Adobe adding a filter, but the model layer itself eating your value proposition from above while UiPath eats it from below with their AI Healing Agent. You're in a sandwich, and both slices of bread are moving toward each other.

And the category isn't mispriced. Look at the landscape: Automat raised $19.25 million, Decisional is in YC too, UiPath is a $13 billion public company actively adding self-healing. Half the AI infrastructure pitches I see right now are some version of "we automate legacy workflows with computer vision." This is consensus. When your YC page is tagged "Generative AI" and your competitive set includes a $13B public company and a YC batchmate with $19M in the bank, the upside is already in the price. There's no narrative mispricing here — nobody is dismissing RPA as a category. They're pouring money into it. The specific sub-niche of "legacy desktop apps with no API" is narrower, sure, but it's not the kind of category that makes sophisticated investors uncomfortable. It's the kind that makes them nod and say "makes sense."

The bull case — and I'll give it its due because the traction numbers are surprisingly good — is that Minicor becomes the Twilio of legacy system access. Twenty-five thousand patients a day flowing through their architecture at pre-seed with a five-person team is genuinely impressive execution. SOC 2 Type II and HIPAA compliance before you've even raised a real round? That signals founders who understand enterprise selling isn't about demos, it's about procurement. If every AI health-tech company needs to write to Epic or Athena and there's no API, and Minicor is the only reliable bridge with compliance certs already in hand, you could squint and see a platform play. The MCP interface for coding agents is the right abstraction — you're not selling to end users, you're selling picks and shovels to the AI gold rush in healthcare. That's infrastructure thinking, and I respect infrastructure thinking. But here's the thing: picks and shovels only work when the gold rush happens and when nobody else is selling the same picks. The gold rush in AI-for-legacy-healthcare is real, but UiPath has been selling enterprise picks for fifteen years and just added the same AI healing capability to their toolkit.

On the primitive test: this doesn't create a new behavior. People have been doing desktop automation since the first macro was recorded in Excel. RPA is a thirty-year-old category. Minicor does it better — AI-assisted recovery, natural language definition, managed VMs — but "better" is execution, not creation. Nobody will say "Minicor me" the way they say "Venmo me." Compare this to what Teamshares did: employee-owned small business rollups as a venture-backable category literally did not exist before they created it. Minicor is a better mousetrap in a well-understood mousetrap market.

The founders check some boxes but not the ones that light me up. Faizaan and Saheed met in college, built a non-profit together through the pandemic, and now they're building a real company. That co-founder relationship history is genuine — four-plus years of shared context before starting. Saheed's time at Ford and Morgan Stanley means he's lived inside the legacy enterprise nightmare firsthand. That's founder-market fit. But the idea itself is sensible, not insane. "Make legacy desktop automation work better with AI" is what a reasonable person pitches to a reasonable audience. There's no moment where I think "that's fucking crazy" — which means there's no moment where the sane-insane ratio generates the kind of asymmetric upside I'm looking for. A reasonable person with a reasonable idea in a well-funded category is, frankly, the definition of what I pass on.

Capital discipline is the one bright spot in my framework. Five people, production scale, compliance certs, no open job postings, no evidence of burn-it-down hiring plans. These founders are building like this might be the last money they ever raise, which is exactly how you should build. If everything else lined up, this would be the signal that tilts me. But capital discipline alone doesn't overcome the fact that the market is consensus, the primitive doesn't exist, and the AI model providers are shipping your capability as a feature.
