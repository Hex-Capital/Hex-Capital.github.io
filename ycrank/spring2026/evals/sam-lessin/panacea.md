# Panacea -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Panacea is the kind of company my framework reads as a fixed-fee boutique services firm wearing AI clothes — its highest dimension is a genuinely non-standard milestone pricing model (payment due only on milestone completion, no upfront fees per withpanacea.com), and its lowest dimension is a zero-community, zero-public-writing founder profile where Haris Javed-Akhtar has no Twitter, Yaman Ziadeh has ~2K LinkedIn followers, and the only public touchpoint is a cal.com/haris-panacea/call link. That high/low pairing tells me everything: this is an execution-machine services shop that might run profitable, but it does not have the regatta dynamics, distribution alpha, or asset-class mispricing that makes me write a check rather than wish them well.

On capital structure — Panacea's highest dimension — the ladder runs Teamshares at the top (employee-ownership rollup, an instrument no other VC could underwrite), Ro in the middle (capital-intensive but the regulatory rails were the moat), and the canonical YC SaaS cap table needing five rounds to reach default-alive at the bottom. Panacea's outcome-based, milestone-priced revenue model is meaningfully more interesting than a $25K MRR ARR-template — it inverts the hourly-billing economics MDI Consultants and EAS have run since 1978, and if AI actually delivers the "12x faster" claim on withpanacea.com it expands margin rather than getting commoditized. But it sits well below Teamshares because the cap table itself is standard equity, and the talent moat — exactly one named principal consultant, Harun Sinha — is one departure away from collapse. Mid-low on the ladder.

On founder-as-brand — Panacea's lowest dimension — John Fish/Bookshelved anchors the top (a YouTuber-built literary hub I underwrote on owned distribution), Marina Mogilko sits in the middle (millions of subscribers, but I had to structure 5% of 30-year earnings to get venture-shaped exposure), and the bottom rung is the pitch with no community footprint and zero pre-funding writing. Panacea is the bottom rung verbatim: Haris and Yaman have no Twitter accounts, no GitHub, no public essays on FDA pathway reform, no podcast, no newsletter, no Discord, no Product Hunt. The customer testimonial on withpanacea.com is unattributed. This is pure outbound founder-sales to Bay Area biotech, which means every dollar of revenue requires a dollar of CAC — the opposite of what the Slow Creator Fund was built to back.

On round pricing, Solana at $0.05 anchors the high rung and the canonical AI-agent-for-vertical-X seed at $30M post with four tier-1s sits at the bottom — Panacea slots near that bottom rung because the dossier is silent on round size but Weave Bio just closed a $20M Series A in October 2025 with USVP and Innovation Endeavors in the syndicate, which means the category is now visibly consensus and Panacea is selling into the consolation-prize end of the trade. On asymmetric founder conviction, Drop.io's missionary arc anchors the top and the sanitized YC pitch anchors the bottom; Yaman's prior role as AI Platform Engineer at Anterior (YC-backed healthcare AI) is real founder-market fit but neither founder has the 3+ year visible obsession with FDA reform that the criterion demands, so Panacea sits low-mid.

On sleeve fit, my Ro/PillPack/Truepill prescription-rail cluster anchors the top, the canonical foundation-model AI infrastructure seed at consensus pricing anchors the bottom, and Panacea sits awkwardly between them — it touches the unstated healthcare-DTC pattern through the FDA pathway connection, but the actual product shape ("AI-native FDA regulatory services") is exactly the "AI is just more internet" vertical-SaaS-on-top-of-LLM category I de-rate. There is no on-chain economic activity, no DTC margin profile, no rollup structure — just consulting hours augmented by document generation. Mid-low.

Panacea's 36-month forecast: reaches $2–5M ARR as a boutique fixed-fee FDA consultancy serving sub-$50M YC-alum biotech and device companies, gets systematically out-distributed by Weave Bio in any enterprise pharma deal where USVP can open the door, never raises a Series A, and either holds as a profitable two-founder services business or sells to a CRO like IQVIA or ICON for sub-$50M as a tuck-in. The mechanism is the talent-concentration ceiling (one named ex-FDA consultant cannot scale fixed-fee delivery beyond a handful of concurrent engagements) running headlong into a capitalized AI-only competitor with naming-VC distribution into the buyer base.

The strongest objection is that Panacea is the Ro known exception in disguise: 50-state FDA regulatory complexity is exactly the kind of "venture moment" only certain teams can clear, Haris's MD credential plus Yaman's ex-Anterior healthcare-AI engineering is the rare labor layer, fixed-fee pricing compounds margin if the 12x AI claim holds, and the prescription-rail sleeve already houses Ro, PillPack, Truepill, Hinge Health, and Livongo without being publicly framed as a thesis. That case stings because the unstated pattern is real. It loses on the dossier facts: Ro had a regulatory schlep that *required* venture capital to clear, while Panacea's fixed-fee model implies it should be bootstrappable; the entire moat hinges on one named consultant (Harun Sinha) where Ro had built proprietary prescription rails; and there are zero signed LOIs with named biotech counterparties in the dossier. The condition that flips me: 3+ named ex-FDA principals on the team plus $500K+ ARR from named biotech customers within 12 months, at a round priced below the Weave Bio comp. Until then, I pass.
