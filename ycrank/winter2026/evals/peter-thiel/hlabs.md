# Hlabs -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The first thing I notice about Hlabs is that its thesis is indistinguishable from a Congressional floor speech. "Build an industrial supply chain for robots in the USA" is not a secret -- it is the loudest consensus in American industrial policy right now. Every defense contractor, every CHIPS Act advocate, every tariff proponent, and every reshoring consultant agrees that the United States needs domestic manufacturing of critical technology components. When the government, the media, and the investment community all share your thesis, you do not have a secret. You have a newspaper headline. The question I always ask -- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" -- and I cannot identify the answer here that would make a skeptic uncomfortable.

The more interesting implicit bet is that the robotics industry will fragment into hundreds of small builders rather than consolidating around a handful of vertically integrated giants. Tesla builds its own actuators for Optimus. Figure AI will almost certainly do the same. Boston Dynamics has been vertically integrated for decades. The sophisticated consensus in robotics may actually be that the winners will own their full stack, top to bottom, the way Apple owns its silicon. Hlabs is wagering on the PC architecture pattern -- many assemblers, standardized components -- rather than the Apple pattern. That is a genuine fork in the road, and being right about it would matter. But the dossier gives no evidence that the founder has articulated this as a deliberate, reasoned thesis about how the robotics market will develop. It reads more like a product decision than a worldview.

The technology does not clear the 10x threshold. FOC motor control is a well-understood technique -- textbooks cover it, open-source reference designs exist on GitHub (Open Dynamic Robot Initiative, OpenTorque Actuator), and any competent electrical engineer can implement it. What Hlabs offers is integration convenience: a developer-friendly board with open-source software that saves weeks of custom work. That is valuable, but it is a 3x improvement in prototyping speed, not a 10x leap in capability. Nothing about the product makes the prior approach structurally obsolete. A well-resourced competitor -- HEBI Robotics with its Carnegie Mellon pedigree, or RobStride with Sequoia's backing and Chinese manufacturing costs -- could offer equivalent products. The open-source software, while smart as a distribution strategy, simultaneously eliminates the proprietary technology moat. You cannot simultaneously be open-source and claim proprietary defensibility.

Paul Hetherington deserves credit for real attributes: a relevant engineering degree combining mechanical and electrical systems, genuine embedded systems research experience at Bath, and the grit of a second-time YC founder. But Mystic AI -- six years as CEO, $1.31M raised total, no visible exit or scale -- is not a signal of definite optimism. It is a signal of persistence, which is different. The pivot from ML infrastructure to hardware robotics components does not suggest the kind of singular, obsessive vision I look for. The founder's background in UAV control and embedded systems provides genuine technical credibility, but solo-founding a hardware company with $500K is a structural challenge that conviction alone cannot resolve. Hardware demands capital, manufacturing partnerships, supply chain management, quality control, and customer support simultaneously. Forty-plus orders is an encouraging demand signal for pre-seed, but scaling from forty boards to an industrial supply chain requires an entirely different operation.

The strongest bull case would go something like this: if the humanoid robotics market explodes the way smartphones did in 2008-2012, and if it fragments into dozens of viable builders (the way the Android ecosystem produced Samsung, HTC, LG, Xiaomi, and many others), then the company that owns the standardized component layer captures extraordinary value. Hlabs would become the Qualcomm of robotics -- the reference platform everyone builds on. The defense angle strengthens this: ITAR restrictions and Buy American requirements create a regulatory moat that Chinese competitors cannot easily breach, and defense robotics is a market I take seriously. If Hetherington focused exclusively on becoming the sole-source provider of standardized actuator control systems for US defense robotics programs, built the clearances and certifications, and made his platform the default for every DARPA-funded robot prototype, there would be a genuine monopoly path rooted in regulatory capture rather than pure technology. That company would interest me. But that is not what the dossier describes -- serving household robotics, humanoids, quadrupeds, robot arms, and defense simultaneously from day one is the opposite of monopoly strategy. It is competitive thinking applied to five markets at once.

I do not see the path to a power-law return here. The company is solving a real problem for a real customer base, and the early traction is respectable. But a hardware components business without proprietary technology, without network effects, without a concentrated beachhead strategy, and without a thesis that would make experts argue with you -- that is a company destined to compete. And we know what happens to companies that compete.
