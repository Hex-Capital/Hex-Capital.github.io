# Taiga -- Elad Gil Evaluation

Candid Health has raised $99.5M with ~250% revenue growth in 2024. AKASA has raised $205M. LunaBill, just one YC batch ahead, reports $764K contracted ARR. AI-enabled healthcare startups captured 62% of all digital health venture funding in H1 2025. When I look at Taiga entering AI medical billing in Spring 2026, the first thing I see is not a nonobvious market -- I see one of the most obvious AI application categories in venture capital right now. The structural catalyst is real: GPT-4-class models crossed the accuracy threshold for parsing unstructured clinical notes into ICD-10 and CPT codes around 2023-2024. But that shift has been consensus for over two years. Every major RCM investor has already deployed capital against it. When I backed Coinbase in 2012, crypto infrastructure was dismissed by mainstream investors -- the structural shift was invisible to consensus. When I wrote the seed check for Perplexity before ChatGPT launched, AI search was considered a dead category. Taiga enters a market where the structural window is not only recognized but actively crowded with well-capitalized competitors. That's the core problem with market timing here.

Taiga's positioning as a full-service billing agency rather than a SaaS tool is a deliberate choice, and it has some tactical merit for the small-practice segment. Independent practices with 1-5 physicians don't want a software tool that helps them bill better -- they want someone to handle billing entirely. Taiga's seven EHR integrations (Athenahealth, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, AdvancedMD, Tebra, Elation, Epic) and two-week onboarding create real switching costs once a practice is live. But the agency model fundamentally works against infrastructure-layer positioning. Compare Taiga to Stripe: Stripe built a payments API that thousands of companies integrated into their core stack, creating compounding network effects and developer ecosystem lock-in. Taiga is a vendor, not a platform. There's no API other businesses build on, no developer ecosystem, no compounding dynamics beyond individual practice-level retention. Each new customer is a linear addition, not a network node. When I evaluate whether a company is building a layer that OTHER businesses depend on, Taiga fails that test clearly.

The bull case deserves honest construction. Taiga's target segment -- roughly 200,000+ physician-owned practices in the U.S. -- is structurally underserved because large incumbents like Waystar (~$8B valuation) and R1 RCM have cost structures built around enterprise contracts with hospital systems. Serving a 3-physician family medicine practice is unprofitable under their model. Candid Health and AKASA also target larger provider organizations. Taiga's AI-native cost structure could make small practices economically viable for the first time -- an analogy to how Gusto made modern payroll viable for SMBs when ADP focused exclusively on enterprise. Both founders' parents are physicians with independent practices, providing authentic exposure to the pain point. The percentage-of-collections model (likely 4-10%) aligns with my "pricing captures value" principle -- Taiga earns more when the practice collects more, meaning revenue scales with demonstrated ROI. If AI replaces human billing teams entirely, gross margins could reach software-like levels (70%+) on what appears to be a services offering. And the single-miracle test is actually favorable: the technology capability is arriving exogenously via foundation models, small practices are proven payers for billing services (they already spend on this), and HIPAA compliance is standard table stakes. Taiga needs distribution to work -- one miracle, not three.

But that bull case doesn't survive scrutiny against the competitive landscape. LunaBill, from just one YC batch prior, already reports $764K contracted ARR in a narrow slice of the same market (AI voice agents for insurance follow-up). Candid Health grew revenue ~250% in 2024 with $99.5M in backing. The Gusto analogy breaks down at a critical point: when I invested in Gusto, nobody prestigious wanted to build payroll software. Taiga enters a category where prestige-seeking AI founders and hundreds of millions in venture capital are already deployed. The "boring but critical" pattern only generates alpha when the market is actually boring to other investors. AI medical billing in 2026 is anything but boring to the venture community. The commoditization risk is real -- LLM-based coding and denial prediction rely on foundation models accessible to any well-funded competitor, and Taiga's potential data flywheel is precisely what Candid Health and AKASA are already building with far more claims volume.

Nanda Guntupalli (Penn CS) and Adam Wax (Lehigh CS+Math) appear to be recent or current university students. Neither has prior startup founding experience, prior exits, or direct healthcare industry work experience found in public records. Adam's background includes internship experience and hackathon wins (Best Visual Design at Lehigh Valley Hackathon, AI study assistant using AWS Bedrock), which demonstrate technical capability but not scaling capability. The "friends since 3rd grade" relationship provides cohesion, but I see no evidence of the decisive leadership hierarchy I look for -- both are listed as co-founders with no designated CEO. When I evaluate whether founders can navigate the 1-to-100 transition, I need evidence of decisiveness, team-building capability, and ideally direct experience operating inside a company during hypergrowth. Taiga's founders bring authentic problem exposure through their families but none of the operator-scaler signals I weight heavily.

The condition that would flip me to Invest: if Taiga demonstrated that their AI-native cost structure enables profitably serving practices under 5 physicians -- a segment with verifiable evidence that Candid Health and AKASA cannot economically reach -- with at least $50K MRR and net revenue retention above 110%, I'd revisit. That would prove the structural unit-economics gap that makes the Gusto parallel real rather than aspirational. Right now, with zero disclosed traction, zero public revenue, and a consensus market with $300M+ already deployed to competitors, Taiga doesn't pass my market timing test. The single-miracle viability is the strongest dimension here -- the technology works and the payer exists -- but a favorable miracle test in an obvious, crowded market with inexperienced founders and no infrastructure-layer positioning isn't enough. The dimensions that lock this as a Pass are Nonobvious Market Timing (the market is definitively consensus) and Operator-Scaler Founder Profile (no evidence of scaling capability in a category where well-funded competitors demand rapid execution).
