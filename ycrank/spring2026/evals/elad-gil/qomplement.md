# qomplement -- Elad Gil Evaluation

$200,000 spent training a proprietary language model for desktop automation is the number that immediately tells me what kind of bet qomplement is making -- and it's the wrong one. When I backed Harvey, the entire thesis rested on the fact that the technology miracle was happening exogenously: foundation model capabilities were improving on their own curve, and Harvey was building a platform that rode that curve rather than competing with it. qomplement is doing the opposite. They spent $200K and 30+ days training a custom CUA model (Expansión, Sep 2025) at the exact moment OpenAI, Anthropic, and open-source projects like BrowserUse (50K+ GitHub stars, $17M seed) are all building computer-use capabilities into their frontier offerings. qomplement is training a model to do what the major labs are making a commodity feature. That $200K isn't a moat -- it's a depreciating asset.

qomplement's market timing fails my primary screen in a specific way: the structural shift is real but fully recognized by consensus. CUA capabilities crossing a practical threshold in 2024-2025 is a genuine technology inflection -- but it's one of the most visible, most discussed developments in AI right now. BrowserUse raised $17M on exactly this thesis. Extend AI raised $17M and is already cash-flow positive serving Brex, Square, and Checkr. Rossum has $44.9M ARR serving 450+ enterprises including PepsiCo and Siemens. When I invested in Coinbase in 2012 or Perplexity in 2022, those markets looked dead to other investors. Desktop automation in 2026 looks alive to everyone. qomplement's LatAm origin and local-first architecture add a geographic wedge -- the statistic that 50% of Mexican companies use AI agents but only 17% have them "deeply integrated" (Google Cloud, cited in Expansión) points to a real gap -- but geographic arbitrage in a commoditizing technology category is a fragile edge.

qomplement triggers one of my explicit red flags: they're building an application on a contested infrastructure layer. The CUA/computer-use capability layer hasn't been won yet. Frontier model providers are actively competing to own it. qomplement sits on top of this layer as an application -- they automate document workflows inside legacy desktop software, but they don't become a platform that other businesses build on. Compare this to Stripe, which didn't just process payments but became the payments API that thousands of companies integrated. qomplement's product records user routines and replays them autonomously (Expansión, Sep 2025). That's a workflow automation tool, not infrastructure. The switching costs are moderate at best -- re-recording routines is friction, not the deep integration dependency that creates compounding lock-in. When the underlying CUA layer gets commoditized by frontier models, qomplement's differentiation collapses to domain-specific workflow templates, which are replicable.

Kerim Taray (CEO) and Andrés Fernando Garza García (CTO) both attended Tecnológico de Monterrey and are described as "former CTOs from high-growth startups" by Semilla Ventures, but no specific companies are named anywhere in the dossier. The CTO is described as a "recently graduated engineer at time of founding" by Milenio -- which contradicts the "high-growth startup CTO" framing and suggests limited scaling experience. qomplement has a 4-person team with zero job postings on their YC page, 16 Twitter followers, and a website that showed only a company name at research time. The $60K/month AWS infrastructure spend (Expansión, Sep 2025) against undisclosed pre-seed funding creates a runway problem: on a standard YC $500K deal, qomplement has roughly 8 months of infrastructure-only runway before accounting for salaries or any other costs.

The strongest bull case for qomplement centers on the transportation customer automating 10,000+ monthly invoices with $15,000/month in cost savings (Expansión, Sep 2025; Startups Latam) -- that's real pre-seed traction in a painful, measurable workflow. The local-first architecture genuinely addresses a regulatory need in healthcare and financial services where cloud data transfer is restricted. And the claimed 15x cost advantage over cloud-based alternatives could win price-sensitive LatAm enterprise buyers. If qomplement could build a category-leading position in LatAm legacy-system automation before the frontier models catch up, the geographic-plus-regulatory positioning might create a durable wedge. But this bull case requires qomplement to win a race against OpenAI and Anthropic's computer-use roadmaps, which is a race where a 4-person team with a $200K model is structurally disadvantaged. The single verifiable condition that would flip me: if qomplement demonstrated 20+ paying enterprise customers with sub-5% monthly churn across multiple verticals, proving that the workflow-specific layer -- not the underlying CUA capability -- is where the value accrues. That would be evidence of an application-layer moat I'm not currently seeing.

qomplement's margin architecture is opaque -- no pricing page, no revenue figures, no public business model beyond an inferred per-seat or per-agent SaaS subscription. Competing on a "15x cheaper" positioning (Expansión, Sep 2025) signals cost competition rather than pricing power, which is the opposite of what I look for. The best businesses can raise prices repeatedly without losing customers; qomplement is leading with being cheap, which compresses margins and attracts customers who will churn to the next cheaper option. This is a pass. The two dimensions most decisive: Nonobvious Market Timing scores low because desktop automation is one of the most consensus AI categories in 2026, with well-funded competitors already at scale; Infrastructure-Layer Positioning scores low because qomplement is an application sitting on a contested CUA infrastructure layer that frontier model providers are actively commoditizing. Neither dimension reaches the threshold where my framework finds alpha.
