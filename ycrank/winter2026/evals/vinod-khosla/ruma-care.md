# Ruma Care -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

The most telling thing about Ruma Care is that everyone agrees the problem is real. Hundreds of practice managers told the founders prior authorization is broken. Physicians rank administrative automation as their top investment priority. CMS is mandating FHIR-based PA APIs by 2027. When every stakeholder in the ecosystem validates your problem, you're not contrarian — you're consensus. And when I see consensus, I see a crowded room: Cohere Health with $200M raised, Develop Health at $17.6M, Waystar as a public company doing a billion in revenue, Basys.ai training on Mayo Clinic data. This is not a space where experts are dismissing the opportunity. This is a space where capital and attention have already arrived.

Let me start where I always start: the consequence of success. If Ruma Care fully succeeds — dominates biologics PA automation for every infusion clinic in America — what changes? Prior authorizations process faster. Denial rates drop. More patients access their biologics. That matters to patients, genuinely. But the insurance system still exists. The buy-and-bill model persists. Payers still require prior authorization. Ruma makes a broken system function more smoothly; it doesn't replace the system. The TAM for PA software is $3-6 billion depending on whose estimate you use. That's a fine market for a SaaS company. It is not the kind of consequence that justifies 90% failure probability. When I backed Commonwealth Fusion Systems after losing $160 million on KiOR, I did it because fusion energy, if it works, makes fossil fuels obsolete — the entire energy infrastructure operates differently. Ruma's success means clinics fill out forms faster. The magnitude doesn't match the ambition I look for.

The strongest case for Ruma is founder-problem fit, and I want to take it seriously. Christina Huang has been a biologics patient for seven years, experiencing insurance denials personally. That's not abstract empathy — that's lived pain. Meng Fei Shen didn't just do customer discovery interviews; he worked as a medical assistant at a rheumatology clinic, sitting in the chair, doing the faxes, navigating the portals. And the early results are real: 50% denial rate reduction and 20x staff time reduction per authorization. Those are strong operational metrics. The Stanford symposium, the free Formulary Navigator as a lead-gen tool — this is a team that knows how to execute. If what I cared about was probability of building a successful $100M revenue healthcare SaaS company, I'd be interested. But I don't optimize for probability. I optimize for what happens if everything goes right. And what goes right here is a valuable vertical SaaS business in a competitive market, not an industry transformation.

The technology itself — extracting payer rules, auto-filling forms, learning from denial patterns — is applied AI doing workflow automation. It's useful. It creates a data flywheel that improves over time. But the CMS FHIR mandate arriving in 2027 could actually commoditize the integration layer that gives Ruma its technical moat today. When regulation standardizes the APIs you're building against, every EHR vendor — Epic, Oracle Health — can build native PA automation. The data asset around denial patterns has real value, but it's a time-based advantage, not a structural one. A well-funded competitor willing to specialize can replicate this in 18 months. The technology doesn't make the incumbent approach economically unviable; it makes the incumbent approach slightly less painful to navigate.

What I keep coming back to is the calibration of ambition to problem. $60 billion in unaccessed care. Patients suffering for months waiting for medications that exist and that their doctors prescribed. That problem genuinely matters — it's the kind of problem I might say is too important not to work on. But the solution here isn't attacking the root cause. A founder who wanted to truly disrupt this system would ask: why does prior authorization exist at all? Can we build a model where payer approval is instantaneous and algorithmic? Can we restructure biologics economics so buy-and-bill risk disappears? Can we eliminate the insurance intermediary for specialty medications entirely? Those would be consequence-magnitude questions. Instead, Ruma navigates the existing maze more efficiently. That's the difference between building Juniper — replacing Cisco's entire architecture with TCP/IP routing — and building a tool that helps people configure Cisco routers faster.

I pass on this, but I want to be clear about what would change my mind. If Ruma demonstrated that their denial pattern data could be turned into a pricing and risk engine that fundamentally restructured how payers approve biologics — not just filling forms better but changing the approval paradigm — that would escalate the consequence magnitude substantially. Or if they showed evidence that their platform could expand to control the entire specialty medication access workflow end-to-end, becoming the operating system for biologics delivery rather than an authorization tool, the ambition would match the problem. The founders clearly have the execution capability and domain immersion to build something significant. I just need the "something" to be bigger.
