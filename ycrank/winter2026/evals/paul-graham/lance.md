# Lance -- Paul Graham Evaluation

The thing that jumped out at me about Lance is that Gavin Brennen grew up around Marriott operations. That phrase — "grew up around" — is doing a lot of work, and I want to unpack it. When someone grows up inside an industry, they don't see it as a "market opportunity." They see it as the water they swim in. They know which problems are real and which are imaginary. They know the specific software the front desk runs, the specific moment when calls pile up and nobody can answer them, the specific reason hotels haven't fixed this yet. That last part matters most. Hotels haven't automated phone handling not because nobody thought of it, but because the PMS systems are legacy nightmares that resist integration. Oracle Hospitality's interfaces were designed in a different era. Getting data in and out requires either painful API partnerships that a three-person startup will never get, or something much uglier. Lance chose the ugly path: vision-based agents that literally look at the screen and navigate it like a human would. That's the schlep. Every smart engineer who thinks about hotel automation eventually encounters the PMS integration wall and unconsciously walks away. Lance walked toward it.

The Computer Use Agent approach is what I'd call "schlep as moat." It's fragile — when Oracle redesigns their UI, things break. It requires constant maintenance. It's the kind of work that makes talented engineers cringe. That's precisely why it works as a strategy. HiJiffy built a chatbot because chatbots are pleasant engineering problems. Duve built a messaging platform because messaging APIs are well-documented. Lance chose voice calls handled by agents that navigate hotel software visually. Nobody at a hackathon would pick this approach. The combination of voice-first (harder than text) plus operational execution through legacy systems (harder than API calls) plus multi-step task completion (harder than answering questions) creates a product that's genuinely difficult to replicate not because of any single breakthrough but because the total schlep load is so high that competitors would rather solve easier problems.

Fifty hotels at pre-seed is a striking traction signal. These aren't small independent properties — they're across Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt brands, which means navigating enterprise procurement, brand compliance, and the particular conservatism of hotel management companies. A named customer testimonial claiming 30% reduction in front desk call volume is specific enough to be credible. Priya Sharma saying they're "recovering revenue we used to miss — from restaurant bookings to reservation changes" points to real economic value, not hypothetical benefit. When your early customers describe your product in terms of recovered revenue, that's the kind of pull signal I look for — not "we like using it" but "we're making money we weren't making before."

The team composition maps well to the problem, which is rarer than it sounds. You have the domain person who grew up in hotel operations, the technical product person who built voice and AI agent technology at Google and Salesforce and was a founding engineer at a company with a 9-figure exit, and the CTO who researched speech and language at Stanford and built agent infrastructure at scale. That's not three generalists who brainstormed their way into hospitality. Caleb Chan's prior exit as a founding engineer is a meaningful signal — not because exits guarantee future success, but because founding engineers at 9-figure outcomes have been through the full lifecycle of building something real. Gatik Trivedi founding and exiting a company while still a student, plus getting accepted to Berkeley MET at a 0.7% acceptance rate, suggests the kind of early intensity that correlates with determination. All three attended Berkeley, which means the co-founder relationships have a natural history rather than being manufactured for this venture.

The competitive landscape is my biggest concern. Canary Technologies has $180 million in funding and 20,000 hotels. Duve has $85 million. These aren't hypothetical threats — they're actively building AI voice features. But I've seen this pattern before where the startup with the ugly technical approach beats the well-funded incumbent with the clean architecture. The incumbents are structured around text-based messaging and broad-suite strategies. Adding voice AI as a module within a larger platform is different from building voice-first with deep operational execution. Canary's incentive is to keep selling their existing suite; Lance's incentive is to make the voice agent extraordinarily good. That focus difference matters. Still, the capital asymmetry is real — a single lost hotel group relationship could devastate Lance's customer base at this stage.

What would make this a great investment? If the Computer Use Agent approach turns out to be more robust than it appears — if the maintenance burden of tracking PMS UI changes is manageable at scale — then Lance has built an integration method that bypasses the API gatekeepers entirely. Every other competitor needs Oracle's or Amadeus's cooperation. Lance doesn't. That's a structural advantage disguised as a technical liability. If hotels are as desperate for voice automation as the 65% staffing shortage figure suggests, and if Lance's early deployments generate the kind of operational data that makes the system measurably better at each new property, then the 50-hotel base could grow quickly through hotel group expansion. The unit economics of SaaS replacing labor costs are inherently favorable. This is a company where the schlep is real, the organic discovery is genuine, and the early evidence of user pull is above average for pre-seed. It's not Airbnb-level conviction — the origin story is organic for one founder rather than all three — but it's a solid bet.
