# Inth -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Christopher Burns built c15t to 2.6M npm downloads and got it installed at Vercel, Expo, Zed, Infisical, and Unkey before raising a YC round, which makes Inth a mission-driven OSS operator's second act stuck inside a category -- privacy compliance dev tools -- that Transcend just raised a $40M Series B in (Axios, May 2024) and that I read as fully-funded consensus plumbing, not a venture moment. The dimensional signature here is high founder-conviction-via-prior-art paired with low category-sleeve fit, which is the analytical profile of a respectable open-core dev tool that will get bought, not a Slow bet.

On founder conviction -- the dimension where Inth scores best -- the load-bearing analogy is my own Drop.io arc: bootstrapping a primitive against free incumbents because you literally cannot stop building it. Burns is recognizably in that mode. He ran GDPR-regulated donation infrastructure across 33+ countries at Everfund for years, then authored the OSS consent SDK that 6.5K daily downloads and shadcn forks now validate, then went on Heavybit's Scaling DevTools and Open Source Ready Ep. 18 to talk consent architecture before Inth even existed. Where Inth diverges from the Drop.io rung is that Burns is solo on the YC page with a Head of Growth (Will De Ath) instead of a co-founder CTO, expanding from a shipped consent SDK to an unshipped repo-scanning evidence platform -- exactly the moment a single technical founder is most exposed.

The lowest-dimension paragraph belongs to the canonical pass I wrote in my own thesis file: the AI-agent-for-enterprise-vertical YC seed at $30M post with four tier-1s already in. The failure mechanism there is category, not team -- the founders are sharp but the sleeve is consensus and consensus pricing is automatic deselection. Inth's category sits adjacent: B2B privacy compliance infra is exactly where Transcend ($90M raised), DataGrail ($84M), Ketch ($43M) and Privado ($17.5M) have spent the last 24 months pattern-matching. Inth's pitch -- "AI agents write code that moves user data, regulators care" -- is verbatim the thesis Transcend's Series B deck used. That's not contrarian; that's the same narrative being underwritten everywhere, just at a smaller check size.

Round dynamics for Inth are silent in the dossier -- no tier-1 names visibly in or passing, no priced round disclosed beyond YC standard -- and silence is what my Value Seed essay punishes. The mechanism over 18 months is that an open-core dev tool with real OSS traction and a clean YC stamp will attract a priced seed extension at consensus pricing once Inth runs Launch Week through demo day, and the alpha I would have gotten from being early evaporates into a $15-25M post-money round that prices in the c15t download chart. I am not the only bid here and the structure isn't unusual enough to make me one.

Capital structure for Inth is the most ordinary part of the dossier -- Apache 2.0 OSS funneling to a managed paid SKU ("Inth DSAR -- managed platform -- in development" per LinkedIn) is the textbook open-core SaaS cap table, indistinguishable from fifty other dev-tool seeds. The counterfactual that would flip this dimension a full tier: if Burns had structured a revenue share against managed DSAR processing volume, or carved a royalty on hosted consent traffic with a buyback at Series A, the instrument itself would be the reason to write the check the way Teamshares' employee-equity structure was. Nothing in the dossier suggests Inth has been creative on structure; pricing page exists, tiers not even surfaced, no exotic instrument referenced.

For founder-as-brand, the single-anchor parallel is John Fish / Bookshelved -- and Inth is actually the inverse shape of that bet. Fish had a personal YouTube audience and the company was downstream of the human. Burns has 1,252 X followers and is barely a personal brand, but c15t has 1,691 GitHub stars and is installed in production at devtool tastemakers (Zed, Vercel, Expo) -- so the *project* owns developer attention even though the *founder* doesn't. That asymmetry is interesting but it's not Slow Creator Fund material; it's standard OSS-led growth, which the dev tools VC crowd (Heavybit, Boldstart, CRV) already underwrites professionally and at prices I won't pay.

My 36-month forecast for Inth: acqui-hired by Transcend or DataGrail in the $20-50M range to absorb the c15t community into an enterprise privacy-ops suite, or alternatively grinding to $2-5M ARR as a standalone open-core tool that cannot break the OneTrust enterprise channel without raising another $20M Inth's current shape doesn't justify. The producing mechanism is exactly the high/low pairing -- Burns has the conviction and the OSS distribution to make Inth a credible bolt-on for a privacy-ops incumbent, but the category economics push toward consolidation rather than category-defining independence, because the buyers are legal/compliance org and they already buy from OneTrust.

The strongest opposing case for Inth is the Sublime Security pattern -- I bought into enterprise security before the next consolidation wave because the architectural bet (email security rebuilt around detection-as-code) was non-obvious and the team was missionary. The bull on Inth says: repo-resident compliance with auditable evidence for AI-written code touching user data is exactly that kind of architectural rewrite, Burns is exactly that kind of missionary, and c15t's adoption at Vercel/Zed/Expo is the leading indicator that the dev-side of the privacy stack is genuinely repartitioning. That case stings because Sublime is in my recent-check list and the structural parallel is real. It fails on the contrarian-belief test: Sublime had a thesis the SOC SaaS crowd actively disagreed with, while Inth's thesis is verbatim what Transcend just raised $40M on -- "AI agents move user data, engineering teams need governance" is the most-consensus privacy pitch of 2026. The flip condition: if Burns publishes an opinionated long-form essay arguing precisely why scan-only vendors (Privado, Relyance) and dashboard-only vendors (OneTrust, Ketch) are structurally incapable of solving the agent-written-code case, AND Inth offers me a non-standard instrument -- a royalty against managed-DSAR processing volume rather than a SAFE -- I'll take another meeting. As priced and structured today, I pass.
