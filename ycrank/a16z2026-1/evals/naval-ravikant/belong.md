# Belong -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

Nick Holmstén spent seven years as Global Head of Music at Spotify shaping the discovery infrastructure that defines how hundreds of millions of fans interact with artists. Ash Pournouri managed Avicii through a career that generated billions in economic value across tickets, merch, and brand deals. Together they represent specific knowledge from both sides of the fan-artist equation — platform mechanics and artist monetization — accumulated over decades in the Swedish music/tech ecosystem that produced Spotify itself. If you asked me to assemble the ideal founding team for a company that productizes the fan-artist economic relationship, you'd draw something close to this pair. The specific knowledge is real, deep, and non-transferable. A competitor could hire a music executive; they cannot hire two people who collectively built the infrastructure and managed the talent that defined modern streaming-era fandom.

The leverage audit is where Belong loses me. The core product is a Mastercard debit card issued through Middlesex Federal Savings. Debit interchange is linear revenue — every dollar requires a customer to physically swipe. Compare this to Notion, where a small team ships software to millions at zero marginal cost. Belong requires ongoing banking compliance (SOC 2 achieved March 2026, but compliance is a perpetual cost, not a one-time achievement), customer support for a financial product, and artist-by-artist partnership deals that are pure labor leverage. They have 11 employees in alpha with no announced users or revenue. The FanScore and KYF systems could theoretically become a permissionless data platform — a fan identity layer that other businesses build on — but today they're features of a card product, not independent leverage. The structural parallel in my portfolio is Poshmark, not Notion: a marketplace requiring operational teams to scale, which underperformed my highest-conviction bets for exactly this reason.

The principal-agent question splits between the two founders. Holmstén left Spotify — one of the most prestigious positions in global music — and his LinkedIn identifies him as CEO of Belong. That's a principal signal. Pournouri's LinkedIn still reads "Founder at UNLTD Group," he manages a portfolio of 40+ companies, and he carries the title "President" rather than co-CEO. Is Belong his principal bet or one investment in a diversified portfolio of agent-like commitments? The distinction matters. A co-founder who is actually an advisor with a co-founder title creates the internal principal-agent friction I specifically screen for. I'd need to sit across from Pournouri and understand whether Belong is the thing he'd be building without funding or one of forty things competing for his attention.

The strongest bull case: Belong sits in a structural gap that incumbents cannot close. Spotify is an agent of its subscription revenue — embedding a banking product would require regulatory infrastructure outside its competency and cannibalize its platform-neutral positioning with artists. Major banks lack the cultural credibility and artist relationships needed for fan engagement. Goldman Sachs projects $4.2B in superfan-driven music revenue by 2030. Holmstén and Pournouri's combined rolodex — Troy Carter as an angel investor confirms the network extends to artist management at the highest level — could lock up exclusive artist partnerships that become Belong's real moat. If five marquee artists go exclusive on Belong, the two-sided marketplace ignites and the fan identity graph compounds. Banking-as-a-Service infrastructure has made the card product viable for a startup for the first time, so the timing argument is legitimate. The access democratization angle is also real: fans currently generate billions in economic activity with zero recognition or reward flowing back — Belong's Passport and FanScore systems give fans status and access previously reserved for industry insiders.

But here's what I keep returning to: can Belong be a billion-dollar business with fifteen people? No. The fintech operational requirements — compliance, card operations, customer service, artist relations — demand proportional headcount growth. This is the structural test that separates my highest-conviction investments from competent companies in real markets. Cursor achieves $500M ARR with fewer than fifty people because code leverage is permissionless. Belong's revenue scales with card swipes and partnership deals, not with code. The announced competitors confirm this is an operationally intensive category: Fanatics ONE already has 10M FanCash members and the full weight of a $33.5B ecosystem behind it. Belong's compliance barrier (SOC 2, Mastercard BIN) creates perhaps a 6-12 month lead time, but that's a speed advantage, not a structural moat.

The specific knowledge is the best I've seen in this space. The leverage architecture is the wrong kind. Wealth compounds through permissionless leverage — code, media, network effects that work while you sleep. A branded debit card works while your compliance team works. I respect the founders enormously and the market timing is right, but my framework requires the marriage of irreplaceable knowledge and exponential leverage. Belong has the first without the second. If the FanScore data layer evolves into a standalone platform — a fan identity API that every ticketing company, merch vendor, and streaming service pays to access — the leverage profile transforms entirely. That's the version of Belong I'd invest in. The current card-first architecture constrains it.
