# Trope -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

Matthew Chow and Victor Vannara are pitching Trope as "AI agents for ERP implementations," and the shape of that sentence is the entire problem. The dimensional signature here is a competent operator pair - Chow shipped production code for Tesla Cybertruck and Zipline - executing a pitch whose contrarian-secret slot is empty, aimed at a channel (implementation partners billing $14.45B of ERP integration services per Mordor 2026) whose gross margin depends on the very hours Trope proposes to compress. Trope is the archetypal 2026 YC AI-for-vertical bet with an above-median team welded to a below-median thesis.

**Stripe** (2010) is the load-bearing analogy on the secret dimension, because Patrick Collison named a specific incumbent failure mode - PayPal had "won" payments but had structurally under-built for developers, and no incumbent could re-architect around a seven-line integration without cannibalizing enterprise sales. Trope's homepage cites "70% of ERP implementations fail to meet budget," which is a McKinsey slide, not a secret; every SI partner and every ERP vendor already agrees with that number. The named incumbent in Trope's dossier is Microsoft Copilot in ERP (GA October 2025 per Microsoft's own blog), and Trope offers no articulated reason Microsoft, SAP Joule, or Oracle cannot ship partner-side tooling inside their existing licensing envelope. Where Collison could say "Braintree and PayPal cannot ship a developer-first API because their org charts are sales-led," Chow and Vannara are saying "we build agents for SOWs and cutover plans." That is consensus restated in agentic vocabulary, and it fails my opening question inside sixty seconds.

**Anduril** (2017) is the load-bearing anti-analogy on monopoly trajectory, and it clarifies why Trope's channel choice is worse than the dossier admits. Anduril's regulated-customer wedge produced a moat because Lockheed's cost-plus incentive structure rewarded complexity - the customer *wanted* the incumbent to fail. Trope's customer is the implementation partner whose revenue model is billable hours; a tool that compresses a nine-month SAP implementation into six months attacks that partner's P&L, not the ERP vendor's. The dossier flags this explicitly as "channel dependence on implementation partners... whose gross margin comes from billable hours," and no pricing structure addresses it. Where Anduril's regulated customer was structurally starving for the product, Trope's partner customer is structurally ambivalent about it.

On definite-plan versus indefinite iteration, Trope's public surface area is a single "Get a demo" CTA and marketing copy about four agent categories - meeting intelligence, Scan & Fix, data migration, SOW drafting. Over an 18-month compounding window, this optionality is expensive: each agent category is a different reliability problem (ABAP config edits are not SOW drafts are not cutover plans), and hedged product scope at pre-seed compounds into a support-cost trap by Series A. **SpaceX** (2008) at the equivalent stage had one committed sentence about reusability; Trope has four hedged sentences about lifecycle coverage. That is the indefinite-optimism failure mode Zero to One (2014) was written against.

Chow's Tesla Cybertruck and Zipline autonomous delivery credits are the strongest signal in the dossier - production hardware/robotics software shipped at two credentialed companies before graduating UBC. The counterfactual for a tier flip on this dimension would be either Vannara carrying a comparable scale-shipped credit (the LinkedIn field is unretrievable, so the dossier cannot resolve this) or a disclosed ex-SAP/NetSuite/Accenture partner-side operator on the founding team who owns the domain wedge. Neither is present. Chow is a real technical operator; he is not yet a Levchin, a Moskovitz, or a Luckey, and the domain he is entering is one where buyer credibility comes from having sat inside an implementation partner, not from having shipped robotics firmware.

**Palantir** (2003) is the reference point on atoms-heavy or regulated moat, and Trope has neither leg. The customer is a commercial SI, the product is a software overlay on ERP artifacts, and the regulatory perimeter is whatever SOC 2 the customer already demands. There is no capex barrier, no certification depth, no atoms. In the Founders Fund manifesto's terms, this is 140-character territory, not flying-car territory, and the dossier gives me no signal to override the software-only default.

My 36-month forecast on Trope: reaches $800K-$2.5M ARR selling to two dozen mid-market NetSuite and D365 implementation partners, then either gets acqui-hired by a services-tier consolidator (Cognizant, Infosys, or a NetSuite-adjacent tooling vendor like Celigo) at a modest revenue multiple, or pivots off the partner channel to sell directly to end-customer IT organizations for post-go-live ERP operations - which puts Trope into direct combat with CoPlane ($14M raised, PYMNTS 2025) and inside the blast radius of Microsoft Finance Copilot. The mechanism is the partner-incentive misalignment compounding into a low-ceiling ACV curve well before Series B.

The strongest bull case invokes the **Asana** (2010) exception: operator pedigree overrode a missing structural moat because Moskovitz had shipped Facebook to scale, and workflow tools were consensus territory that a great operator team could still monopolize. The parallel to Trope would argue that Chow's Tesla/Zipline execution plus a real 10.92% CAGR services market equals enough. It doesn't - Moskovitz had shipped a category-defining consumer network to a billion users, not a firmware module inside a truck, and Asana's buyer was not structurally hostile to the product's core value proposition. The verifiable condition that would flip me: a signed multi-year contract with a tier-one SI (Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant, or Infosys) at $500K+ ACV disclosed in the next 12 months, demonstrating that the partner-incentive problem is solvable by pricing structure. Absent that, Trope is a pass - the operator-lineage-plus-market-tailwind stack does not clear my gate when the contrarian-secret slot is empty and the channel economics are backwards.
