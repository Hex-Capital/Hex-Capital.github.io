# StableBrowse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

$96 million in aggregate competitor funding — Browserbase at $40M and a $300M valuation, Browser Use at $17M with 50K GitHub stars, Steel.dev at $17M, Kernel at $22M — all targeting the same developer building AI agents that interact with the web. StableBrowse enters this field with standard YC-level capital against five established competitors with production customers. When I look at a market and every serious venture firm is already writing checks into it, I know I'm not looking at a contrarian bet. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "browser infrastructure for AI agents" in 2026. This is the hottest consensus in developer tooling. The technical differentiation — stripping the rendering engine instead of wrapping Chromium — is genuinely interesting engineering. But architectural cleverness in a crowded, well-capitalized space is not the same thing as being the first check into SpaceX when rockets were blowing up. StableBrowse is solving a problem that dozens of well-funded teams are racing to solve, and that OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are building directly into their own agent infrastructure.

Somansh Shah graduated from Purdue in 2025 with a data science major and a Lenovo internship. Deepit Shah holds a BS/MS in CS from UIUC with stints at Amazon and Google. When I sit across from founders, I ask them to tell me their story — not the business, the story. What I see in StableBrowse's founding team is credentials and technical competence, but no biographical crucible that makes these four people the inevitable builders of a machine-native web engine. Where is the personal encounter with the brokenness of browser infrastructure that kept someone up at night? Where is the survival under constraint, the pivot through adversity, the evidence that this team would be building StableBrowse even if no one gave them money? The dossier reveals certifications (IBM Full Stack, Stanford ML), hackathon wins, and internship experience — the résumé of talented recent graduates who spotted a market gap, not people forged by a problem that found them. Two of the four founders — Sarthak Awasthi and Jay Mehta — have common names with no confirmed professional profiles linked to StableBrowse. Deepit Shah's LinkedIn suggested he was returning to Google full-time. These are not just gaps in public data; they raise real questions about team commitment and coherence that I cannot resolve from the outside.

StableBrowse claims 130.6 MB per instance, 4ms cold start, and 85% token reduction versus conventional browsers — metrics that, if they hold across the modern web at scale, would represent a structural advantage. Browserbase and its peers would need to re-architect their core product to match a rendering-free approach. I recognize the infrastructure-at-inflection pattern here: the AI agent market is projected at $182.97B by 2033 at 49.6% CAGR, and StableBrowse is building at the engine layer rather than wrapping existing engines. This is where the bull case gets interesting — the parallel to Crusoe Energy, which looked like a crypto gimmick but was secretly building energy infrastructure. But Crusoe's founders had walked through North Dakota oil fields and seen fire being thrown away. They lived inside an invisible system — wasted energy that nobody in Silicon Valley even knew existed — and built portable data centers to capture it. StableBrowse is optimizing a technical layer in a market that every developer and every LLM provider already understands perfectly well. There is no hidden system here. The headless browser market is transparent, well-mapped, and crowded. No information asymmetry is being disrupted. No extractive structure is being exposed. No ordinary person is being freed from a system they didn't know was exploiting them. StableBrowse serves developers serving AI agents — it is picks-and-shovels infrastructure for the already-technical, in a market where the picks-and-shovels vendors are already raising Series B rounds.

The strongest argument for StableBrowse is that its rendering-free architecture is not a wrapper — it is a fundamentally different approach to the browser engine itself, and that difference could compound into a structural moat as agent sessions scale. CDP compatibility as a drop-in migration path is smart product design. The AI agent inflection is real and undeniable. YC acceptance and Tom Blomfield's partnership carry signal. If StableBrowse's approach proves reliably compatible across the modern web — handling anti-bot detection, dynamic layouts, canvas elements — it could become the infrastructure layer that Browserbase and Browser Use cannot replicate without starting over. That is a real bull case. But I have to weigh it against what I know about how infrastructure moats actually form at this stage: StableBrowse has no public GitHub repos, no disclosed customers, no revenue signals, and no evidence of production usage. The web compatibility claim is entirely unproven at scale. And the LLM provider platform risk is existential — if OpenAI or Google bundles browsing infrastructure natively, the standalone layer shrinks overnight. My known exceptions do not apply here: StableBrowse is not at a cultural inflection point (RTFKT), not "magically weird" in a genre-defying sense (Superplastic), and not a product with such overwhelming cultural timing that governance concerns should be overridden (HQ Trivia).

One verifiable condition would flip me: if a founder sat across from me and told me the biographical story — the specific, personal experience with broken web infrastructure — that makes StableBrowse their life's work rather than their market opportunity, and if they showed me production agent sessions demonstrating reliable compatibility across a meaningful slice of the web, with data moats beginning to form from those sessions, I would revisit. But right now, StableBrowse sits in a consensus-attractive AI infrastructure market with well-funded competitors, a founding team whose biographical connection to the problem is invisible, and no evidence of the opaque-system disruption that drives my highest-conviction investments. The two dimensions that lock this verdict are Founder's Biographical Forge — where the absence of narrative is compounded by founder verification gaps and a possible commitment question around Deepit Shah — and Contrarian Conviction, where >$96M in competitor funding makes this one of the most consensus-funded pre-seed categories I have seen in the current YC batch.
