# ProjectX -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Here's the thing that jumps off the page before I even get to the product: three out of four original co-founders are gone. The July 2024 press lists Adhikari, Das, Banerjee, and Kuncham. By the time the YC application lands, it's Adhikary, Karmakar, and Majumder. That's 75% co-founder turnover before you've raised a real round. I've seen a lot of early-stage teams restructure -- sometimes it's healthy pruning, sometimes it's the canary in the coal mine. But when I'm evaluating the "sane person" half of my framework, I need to see stability under pressure, and swapping out three quarters of your founding team during the hardest phase of company building doesn't inspire confidence that the interpersonal dynamics are solid. The dossier shows no shared employer or university overlap between the current co-founders either. I backed Andrew Kortina from Venmo through Fin because I'd watched the relationship hold up across years of grinding. Here I see the opposite pattern.

Now let me run the pricing test, because that's always my first question: is the market wrong about this category? The answer is no. Not even close. There are 95 companies in the cloud GPU platforms space, 41 funded, $20.8 billion raised collectively. Google bought Cameyo last year for app virtualization. DigitalOcean bought Paperspace for $111M. Shadow has $105-169M. CoreWeave has billions. This is one of the hottest infrastructure categories in tech right now. When I'm looking for mispriced bets, I'm looking for categories where smart people have actively dismissed the opportunity -- where the consensus narrative is wrong and the price reflects that wrongness. Cloud GPU access is the exact opposite. Every sophisticated investor on the planet knows this market is growing at 35% CAGR. The upside is already in the price. ProjectX at a YC valuation is not a contrarian bet -- it's a consensus bet with a team that has a fraction of the resources of their competitors.

The primitive question is where I'd want to get excited, and I can't quite get there. "Any GPU app in a browser tab with sub-3-second cold starts" is legitimately cool engineering. I respect the technical ambition. But is it a new primitive or is it a better mousetrap? When Venmo launched, there was no behavior called "Venmo-ing someone." The category didn't exist. What ProjectX is doing is making an existing behavior -- running GPU-intensive applications -- more accessible via a new delivery mechanism. That's a UX improvement on cloud computing, not a new behavioral primitive. It's the difference between creating peer-to-peer mobile payments and building a faster ATM. Both are useful. Only one births a category. And the "cloud OS" framing is telling -- they're reaching for a grander narrative than the product supports. This is DaaS with better cold starts, not a new operating system paradigm.

The bull case I have to honestly wrestle with: what if per-app GPU streaming becomes as fundamental as Jio was to Indian mobile data? 75% of Indian students lack access to high-quality computing devices. If Infinity nails the India education GTM -- they have $2.4M in signed LOIs and an IIT Bombay pilot -- and this becomes the default way a billion people access professional software, that's not incremental. That's infrastructure for a country. The sub-3-second cold start is a genuine technical achievement that would be hard to replicate casually. And the usage-based billing model means they could theoretically grow revenue with usage rather than burning cash on customer acquisition. If I squint, there's a version of this story where the India-first angle IS the mispricing -- where US VCs are all chasing the same AI infrastructure plays and nobody's looking at democratized GPU access for emerging markets. But I don't think that version survives contact with reality. AWS, Azure, and GCP all have massive Indian operations. The moment this market is proven, a hyperscaler adds a "per-app streaming" feature to their existing GPU fleet and distributes it through relationships with every university system in India. The structural durability just isn't there.

And that's really the kill shot. Not the AI kill test specifically, but the incumbent kill test. Every hyperscaler already has the GPU infrastructure, the low-latency streaming capabilities, and the enterprise relationships. Building a consumer-friendly per-app streaming OS on top of someone else's GPUs means your entire cost structure is controlled by potential competitors. When I backed Metropolis, the parking infrastructure was the moat -- you can't replicate 4,000 parking facilities with software. When I look at ProjectX, the "infrastructure" is rented GPU time from cloud providers who could eat their lunch any Tuesday. A software abstraction layer over commodity compute is exactly the kind of thing that software companies are learning isn't a moat anymore. The $204K in total funding against competitors with hundreds of millions just makes the structural problem more acute -- but even with infinite capital, I'm not sure the moat exists.

The accelerator-hopping pattern also triggers my factory farm alarm. Draper University, IIM Bangalore Launchpad, Stanford ASES, now YC. The founder is 23-24, which is fine -- I don't discriminate on age. But collecting accelerator stamps is the conveyor belt model I think is dying. The best founders I've backed don't need the system. They build in the dark until they have something undeniable, then they raise capital as a tool, not as validation. This feels like a team that's been optimizing for the fundraising game rather than proving the business works without it.
