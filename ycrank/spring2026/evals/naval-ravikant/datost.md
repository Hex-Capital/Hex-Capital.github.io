# Datost -- Naval Ravikant Evaluation

The first thing I notice is the competitive landscape, and it tells the whole story about timing. Julius AI has $11M and two million users. Scoop Analytics raised $3.5M and is building the exact same thing -- AI analyst in Slack. Zenlytic has $15.4M. Chata.ai has $20M and $6.3M in actual revenue. When I see five funded companies occupying the same conceptual space, I'm not looking at a contrarian opportunity -- I'm looking at a consensus category where the pricing already reflects optimism. "AI data analyst" is the 2026 version of "Salesforce for X" -- a category so obviously appealing that every smart person has already arrived. You don't get paid for being right when everyone else is also right.

Now the founder question: what do these founders know that can't be taught? Maceo is 21, with engineering stints at Traba, SimonComputing, and Performant Software. Jason Wang worked at Confluent on the Data Governance team and at Traba alongside Maceo. Jason's Confluent experience puts him adjacent to data infrastructure -- he's seen how data moves and gets governed at scale, which is relevant. But adjacent isn't the same as deep. Neither founder spent years as a data analyst drowning in the bottleneck they're trying to solve. Neither built BI tools before. Neither has a documented obsession with the relationship between data access and organizational decision-making. Their knowledge is competent software engineering -- the kind you acquire at any mid-stage startup. If someone can train you to do it at Confluent's data governance team, that knowledge is transferable by definition. Compare this to the founder-problem fit I look for: Ivan Zhao's years of thinking about the architecture of thought before building Notion, or Amjad Masad's lifelong obsession with making coding accessible before Replit. I don't see that depth here.

The leverage architecture is where this breaks down structurally. Datost is an application layer sitting on top of two platforms it doesn't control -- Slack's API and whatever LLM provider powers the natural language to SQL. This is the opposite of infrastructure. It's the opposite of picks-and-shovels. My portfolio is dominated by platforms: AngelList, CoinList, Alchemy, Stack Overflow, Replit -- companies where one engineer's work serves millions and where the platform itself becomes the substrate other people build on. Datost is a feature built on someone else's substrate. Salesforce owns Slack and already deploys Einstein AI across its product suite. A Slack-native analytics bot is a natural extension for them, not a new company. Microsoft has Power BI and Teams -- they'll build the equivalent without blinking. The leverage ratio here -- output per unit of founder input -- is capped by platform dependency and per-workspace customization requirements. Every new customer needs schema mapping, permission configuration, and context training. That's closer to linear scaling than the nonlinear leverage I look for.

The bull case deserves honest consideration. If Datost nails reliability across diverse database schemas -- if the agent genuinely becomes the data team member that every 50-person company can't afford to hire -- the Slack-native distribution could be powerful. Non-technical users sharing AI-generated charts in channels creates organic pull. The accumulated organizational context -- learned terminology, query patterns, schema relationships -- could generate real switching costs over time. And the "why now" is legitimate: LLM reasoning capabilities have crossed a threshold where multi-step SQL generation and iterative debugging are production-viable rather than demo-ware. If the founders can ship faster and learn faster than Scoop Analytics and the incumbents, the Slack workplace is a wedge into enterprise data infrastructure. But "if they can ship faster" isn't specific knowledge -- it's execution speed, which is table-stakes, not a moat.

The technology doesn't compound in a way that creates defensibility. Natural language to SQL is an active area of open-source research -- DIN-SQL, DAIL-SQL, and dozens of academic projects are publishing better approaches quarterly. The Slack integration is API work, not novel engineering. The dossier explicitly flags this: "any team with LLM API access and Slack API knowledge could build a basic version." The barrier is supposedly reliability at scale across diverse schemas, but that's an engineering challenge, not a physics breakthrough. It's the kind of problem that gets solved by better foundation models, which benefits everyone equally. This is not Extropic building thermodynamic computing chips. This is not Stack Overflow accumulating an irreplaceable knowledge graph over fifteen years. This is a well-executed wrapper around capabilities that are commoditizing rapidly.

I like that both founders have a prior working relationship at Traba -- co-founder trust matters, and shared context reduces communication overhead. Getting into YC at 21 shows initiative. But initiative without specific knowledge produces a smart team chasing a hot market, and that's not the asymmetric bet I'm looking for. I want founders where the knowledge precedes the company, not where the company is the first contact with the domain. Pass.
