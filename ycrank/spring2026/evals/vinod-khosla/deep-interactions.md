# Deep Interactions -- Vinod Khosla Evaluation

Deep Interactions is the inverse of what my framework registers as signal: a meta-tool that helps other people ship AI apps faster, sold via a founder Calendly link to enterprise pilot owners. The dimensional signature is unambiguous -- the highest placement is founder learning rate (Beni Ngwamah pivoted from running WEKA in African impact fintech to Virgin Media O2 Moonshots to this; Sruthi Viswanathan ran "The Spaceship AI" as CTO before Deep Interactions), and the lowest is technical/capital risk repelling consensus VCs. That pairing names the company: a competent Oxford HCI research team shipping a Lovable-adjacent collaboration wrapper into a category where Lovable already booked $400M ARR by February 2026 with 146 employees. This is the McKinsey-1986-cellphone problem in reverse -- the experts here are extrapolating Lovable's curve, not defying it.

**OpenAI (2019)** is my anchor on consequence magnitude because the success-state was civilizational: a world where machines do 80% of economically valuable work, the $15T labor figure I quoted in Fortune 2026-03-06. Deep Interactions's stated consequence is that "95% of businesses abandon their AI pilots" gets fixed -- but fixing pilot-abandonment is not a civilizational variable, it is a middleware efficiency gain on top of the actual AI displacement that companies like Cognition (Series B April 2024) are doing at the model layer. Deep Interactions sells to the PMs and designers coordinating the pilot; the value created is faster shipping of someone else's AI product. No incumbent regime collapses if Deep Interactions hits maximum ambition -- Lovable, Replit ($9B Series D March 2026), Bolt, and v0 all continue. The dossier even concedes the moat thesis as "described as the product thesis, not a deployed asset." That is the **KiOR (~2008)** inverse: KiOR's success-state redrew global liquid fuels and justified 70-80% failure odds; Deep Interactions's success-state redraws a Miro board.

The pass example I keep returning to -- "GPT-4 summarizing sales calls into HubSpot" -- failed my Technical/Capital Risk screen because it was a wrapper on a commodity model with no scientific miracle. Deep Interactions's integration list (Slack, Gmail, Chrome, Miro, Figma, GitHub, Vercel, Supabase, CLI) is exactly the wrapper-on-foundation-model pattern my Fortune 2025-07-01 framing warns against. No named physics, no foundation model trained, no fleet, no plant. Replit Agent 4 already supports parallel agents on a single project (Replit blog, March 2026) -- Deep Interactions's collaboration thesis sits one product release away from being a Replit feature. The required-capital profile here is $5-10M to $1M ARR SaaS economics, which is precisely the regime where I am over-resourced and any Tier-1 generalist can fund the deal.

Founder-defies-expert mechanism analysis: Sruthi Viswanathan and Neil Natarajan are both Oxford DPhil candidates in Human-Centred AI -- Natarajan advised by Reuben Binns and Sir Nigel Shadbolt. That is paradigmatic domain-expert continuity, not defiance. The **Glean** exception applies when capital scale and consequence magnitude override the domain-expert profile (Arvind Jain ex-Google Search); Deep Interactions has neither override. Over 18 months this dimension compounds against the company because HCI researchers tend to optimize for usability metrics, which is the exact "20-30% better" pattern I flag as a red flag -- not the "instigator" pattern from my TED Talk on climate.

Counterfactual on founder learning rate: for Deep Interactions to flip this dimension from a mid score to a Slash-tier override, the dossier would need a documented public pivot moment with stated reasoning -- not "Sruthi was previously CTO of The Spaceship AI." The dossier instead lists three founders with adjacent prior roles and no narrative of "we were wrong about X." Beni Ngwamah's WEKA-to-Virgin-Media-to-Deep-Interactions arc is the closest thing to a pivot signal, but it reads as career progression, not a thesis update of the kind that justified my $100M Series C into Slash in 2024.

**Commonwealth Fusion (2019)** is my single-anchor parallel for capital profile because the $115M Series A was sized for a roadmap consensus VCs would not underwrite. Deep Interactions's funnel is a Typeform waitlist and a founder Calendly link -- the capital depth required is the inverse: small, commodity, fundable by any seed shop. 12 businesses live with 50K usage hits is real but does not change the capital-intensity character of the business.

Forecast, 36 months: Deep Interactions either acqui-hires into Vercel, Replit, or Lovable for under $30M as a "team collaboration" feature module, or shuts down by Q2 2028 having never crossed $3M ARR -- the mechanism is that the multi-stakeholder workflow Deep Interactions sells is one Replit Agent release away from being a checkbox feature inside an incumbent at 100x the distribution, exactly the commoditization risk the dossier already names.

The strongest bull case stings: Lovable went from zero to $400M ARR in roughly 24 months, the category is expanding 10x faster than any SaaS wave I have priced, and the multi-stakeholder coordination problem is a genuine gap that Oxford HCI researchers are credibly positioned to solve -- and my Slash (2024) exception proves I write checks into YC-pedigreed companies that violate my consequence-magnitude prior when founder signal justifies it. The dossier wins the argument against itself: Slash had a publicly told Kanye-pivot moment that demonstrated learning rate above the consequence-magnitude floor; Deep Interactions has Oxford credentials and a 100 Women in AI 2025 recognition, which is consensus-credential signal, not defiance signal. One verifiable condition flips the verdict: a signed contract at $250K+ ACV with a named Fortune 500 customer for the multi-stakeholder workflow specifically, on a 12-month exclusivity that Replit cannot replicate by next product release. Absent that, this scores like the sales-call-summarizer pass.
