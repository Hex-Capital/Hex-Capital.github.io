# Adialante -- Peter Thiel Evaluation

The competitive landscape tells you everything you need to know about whether a secret exists here. Promaxo has raised $63-81 million and already has FDA clearance for in-office prostate MRI with 69 systems deployed. Hyperfine is a public company generating $13.6 million in annual revenue on portable MRI. Chipiron just raised $17 million for a SQUID-based quantum approach to the same problem. Siemens has the Free.Max at 0.55T. Voxelgrids is building full 1.5T systems at 40% lower cost. When I count five funded companies and a major incumbent all converging on the same thesis -- "MRI needs to be cheaper and more accessible" -- I am looking at a market with no secret. The insight is consensus. Everyone in medical imaging has agreed for a decade that MRI is overpriced and undersupplied. Agreement is the enemy of monopoly returns.

The strongest counterargument is the physics. FREE -- Frequency-modulated Rabi-Encoded Echoes -- eliminates gradient coils entirely, removing roughly a third of conventional MRI hardware. This is not a marginal refinement; it is an architectural rethinking of how spatial encoding works in magnetic resonance. The peer-reviewed publications, the NSF SBIR Phase II award, and the presence of an ISMRM I.I. Rabi Award winner on the team all suggest this is serious science, not vaporware. If FREE achieves diagnostic-quality imaging at one-tenth the cost and size of conventional systems, it would make the approaches of every competitor listed above structurally obsolete -- including Promaxo's capital-sale model, Hyperfine's low-field approach, and Siemens' merely-smaller-but-still-expensive Free.Max. The SpaceX analogy tempts: reusable rockets didn't just reduce launch costs incrementally, they made expendable rockets economically irrational. A genuine 10x cost reduction in MRI hardware would have the same structural effect.

But the analogy breaks down on a critical dimension: Musk had a definite plan and the iron conviction to execute it through three consecutive failures. What I see in Torres and Jenkins is academic excellence, not entrepreneurial conviction tested under fire. Torres developed FREE during his PhD at Minnesota's CMRR; Jenkins completed his MS in the same lab. The personal motivation -- Torres grew up on the south side of Chicago and wants to bring imaging to underserved communities -- is genuine and admirable. But I have seen hundreds of academic founders with genuine missions who could not navigate the translation from lab to market. FDA clearance for a novel MRI physics approach is a multi-year gauntlet requiring regulatory strategy, clinical trial design, reimbursement coding, and manufacturing scale-up. The mobile "clinic on wheels" model adds fleet operations, multi-state regulatory compliance, and staffing logistics on top of the hardware challenge. I see no evidence that anyone on this team has built a company, managed a regulatory submission, or operated a mobile healthcare service. The team is five PhDs and an MS -- this is a research group, not yet a company.

The business model innovation -- per-scan pricing with no capital expenditure for clinics -- is the most interesting strategic element, and the one a generic analyst might underweight. It creates genuine business model conflict for Siemens, GE, and Philips, whose economics depend on selling $1-3 million machines with recurring service contracts. They cannot easily offer a per-scan mobile service without cannibalizing their core revenue. This is the kind of asymmetric positioning that enabled Palantir's early growth: government agencies wanted integrated intelligence platforms, but defense contractors couldn't build them without undermining their existing consulting models. If Adialante can execute, the mobile model makes it structurally difficult for incumbents to respond. But execution on this model is precisely what the team is least equipped to deliver.

For this to be a great investment, two things would need to be true simultaneously. First, FREE must achieve diagnostic-quality prostate imaging at its claimed cost and size points -- not in a lab, but in a mobile clinical setting with reproducible results. Second, the team must either acquire or develop commercial and operational capability at a pace that outstrips Promaxo's installed-base advantage. Promaxo already has clinics using their system, generating clinical data, building switching costs. Every month Adialante spends in pre-FDA development is a month Promaxo deepens its position in the exact niche Adialante is targeting. The physics may be superior, but physics alone does not create a monopoly -- execution, timing, and market capture do. I am not persuaded that a team of academic researchers, however brilliant, can close a multi-year gap against a competitor with ten to twenty times their capital and a regulatory head start.

The prostate MRI starting point is the right niche -- specific, growing (guidelines now mandate MRI before biopsy), underserved by existing radiology infrastructure. This is the kind of small-market beachhead I look for. But a good niche selection does not compensate for the fundamental problem: this is a competitive market where the thesis is consensus, the strongest competitor is already entrenched, and the founding team has not yet demonstrated the capacity to build a commercial enterprise around their scientific achievement. I respect the science. I do not see the monopoly.
