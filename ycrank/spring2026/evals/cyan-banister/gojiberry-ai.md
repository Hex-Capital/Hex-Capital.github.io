# Gojiberry AI -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Here's the thing that stopped me cold in this dossier: the company was listed for sale on TrustMRR for $11.15 million on March 9, 2026 — one week after hitting #1 on Product Hunt and while simultaneously participating in YC S26. I've backed founders through rocket explosions and near-bankruptcy, people who would sooner live in their cars than let go of the thing they're building. When I see a sale listing concurrent with a YC batch, I'm not seeing a founder who would work on this problem without funding. I'm seeing someone optimizing for the exit before the journey has even started. And when I trace the pattern back — CoCo AI sold, Edusign sold, MechaChain acquired — I see three talented builders who are exceptionally good at constructing and flipping software companies. That's a real skill. It's just not the skill I invest on.

When I ask my opening question — tell me their story, not the business — what I hear is: "We sold our WhatsApp marketing Shopify app, then we tried building a CRM auto-fill tool, nobody would pay for it, so we pivoted to LinkedIn intent signals because it converted." That's a perfectly rational business decision. But where's the biographical inevitability? Pierre-Eliott has built everything from YouTube channels to e-commerce to WhatsApp bots to CRM tools to sales automation. Roman went from Web3 gaming to WhatsApp marketing to LinkedIn outreach. Dylan built attendance tracking software. The common thread isn't a problem that found them — it's a team that finds problems to build around, ships fast, and sells. I have enormous respect for that execution muscle — the $33K revenue in their first four months, the 100+ paying customers within 60 days of pivot, the documented $380/month GTM playbook that scaled to $175K MRR. These founders can clearly build. But they could be building anything, and next year they might be.

The market itself is the opposite of what makes me lean in. LinkedIn sales automation tools — I can name six funded competitors off the top of my head from this dossier alone, and there are probably thirty more. Apollo just acquired Pocus to move into exactly this signal-to-sequence workflow. Clay hit $100M ARR at a $3.1B valuation. Instantly, Lemlist, HeyReach — bootstrapped competitors clearing $10M-$40M ARR in adjacent categories. Nobody shifts uncomfortably in their chair when you pitch "AI-powered LinkedIn outreach for small sales teams." This is the most consensus play in the most consensus market I can imagine. When I invested in Flock Safety, people worried about surveillance ethics. When I backed Contraline, VCs literally couldn't bring themselves to discuss male birth control. This company would get enthusiastic head-nods at any demo day — and that's exactly the signal that tells me it's not a Long Journey bet.

Let me give the bull case its due, because the numbers are genuinely remarkable for this stage. $175K MRR with 56% month-over-month growth, 75% profit margins, sub-5% churn, $127 CAC with 1.3-month payback — these are metrics most Series A companies would celebrate. If the question is "can this team build a profitable $5-10M ARR business?" the answer is almost certainly yes. And there's a world where the data flywheel from 1,600+ customers creates enough signal-to-conversion mapping that the product becomes measurably better than competitors for SMB use cases. Price democratization of intent data is a real wedge — $99/month versus $25K+/year for 6sense is a structural pricing gap that enterprise incumbents can't close without cannibalizing their own sales motion. For a different investor who evaluates execution velocity and unit economics as primary signals, this is a compelling deal.

But I don't invest in execution velocity — I invest in people whose life stories made them incapable of ignoring a hidden structure that extracts value from those who can't see it. There's no invisible system here. Sales intelligence is one of the most transparent, well-understood markets in B2B software. The "problem" is that enterprise tools are expensive and small teams can't afford them. That's price segmentation, not opacity. Nobody is being exploited by information asymmetry — small sales teams know exactly what they need, they just couldn't afford the enterprise version until now. And the entire product sits on top of LinkedIn's platform, which means a single terms-of-service enforcement wave could disable core functionality overnight. When I think about where this technology goes in ten years, I don't see infrastructure becoming harder to displace — I see a feature that Apollo or HubSpot absorbs into their existing suite. The founders will have moved on to their next build by then, and honestly, that might be the right outcome for them. It's just not the kind of conviction I can hold through a valley of death, because the thing underneath the surface isn't infrastructure — it's a well-executed tool in a commoditizing category.
