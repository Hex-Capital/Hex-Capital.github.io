# Lab0 -- Sam Lessin Evaluation

Rocketlane, a $105M-funded PSA vendor with 750+ customers, barred Lab0 from their Propel conference on April 19, 2026. People cite this as proof that incumbents are scared. I see the opposite signal. When three major PSA platforms -- Rocketlane Nitro, Certinia Veda, and Kantata Expertise Engine -- all ship AI agent products within a six-month window of each other, that's not fear. That's a category racing to absorb a feature. Lab0 isn't being excluded because it's disruptive. Lab0 is being excluded because it's redundant. The incumbents already have distribution, customer data, and 750+ enterprise accounts. They're adding AI to their installed base. That's not disruption, that's sustaining innovation, and sustaining innovation is perfect for the incumbents.

Lab0 fails the AI kill test in the most straightforward way possible. ServiceNow acquired Moveworks in late 2025 specifically to build enterprise automation agents. Salesforce can wire implementation agents into its own platform tomorrow. SAP, Oracle, Microsoft -- every enterprise vendor with a product that requires implementation can build "0-day implementation" natively and bundle it for free. Lab0's entire value proposition -- agentic AI that automates configuration, integration, and testing -- is a feature these platforms should be adding to their own products. When I hear "we automate enterprise software implementation with AI agents," I hear "we fine-tuned the LLM for enterprise config." OK. Who else can fine-tune an LLM for enterprise config? Everyone with the customer relationship and the workflow data. Lab0 is building the thing that's perfect for the incumbent to build.

Lakshya Gupta's World of Workflows paper (arXiv, January 2026) is genuinely interesting research -- 234 evaluation tasks benchmarking LLM performance on ServiceNow, identifying "dynamics blindness" as a frontier problem. That shows real intellectual depth. Sujay Srivastava previously founded Ravvio in BFSI AI, and Onkar Borade brings production engineering chops from Oracle and Mudrex. Lab0 has a technically capable team. But the sanity-insanity ratio is inverted here. These are reasonable people building a reasonable thing. Automating enterprise implementation is exactly what every smart enterprise software person thinks should happen next. Nobody hears "AI-powered forward-deployed engineers" and says "that's fucking crazy." They say "yeah, obviously." When the idea is consensus, the return is consensus. The $451.6 billion system integration TAM sounds enormous, but that number is the bull case for Accenture and Deloitte adding AI tools to their consultants, not for a 3-person startup displacing them.

The bull case an advocate would make: consulting firms face the innovator's dilemma. Accenture cannot automate away its own billable hours. Lab0 has no legacy services revenue to protect, so it can build the fully autonomous implementation agent that incumbents structurally can't. The data flywheel from "continuous learning from every rollout" compounds over time, creating an asset no consultant can replicate. And Lab0's research pedigree -- that World of Workflows paper specifically identifying the dynamics blindness problem -- suggests the team understands the technical frontier better than any PSA vendor's AI team. If Lab0 cracks the cascading-side-effects problem that Gupta's paper identifies as the core blocker, it could leapfrog every incumbent simultaneously. That's a real argument. But it collapses when you recognize that the consulting firms aren't the relevant competitors here. The software platforms are. Rocketlane, Certinia, Kantata, ServiceNow -- these are software companies that absolutely can and are building AI agents for implementation. They don't have the innovator's dilemma. They have the innovator's opportunity. Lab0's data flywheel is theoretical and unproven; Rocketlane's 750+ customers and doubled revenue are real. The one condition that flips me: if Lab0 demonstrated a working product that compresses a specific enterprise implementation from months to days with a named customer and a signed contract, proving that the technical gap Gupta identified in the paper is actually solved and monetizable -- that's a different conversation. But that evidence doesn't exist.

Lab0 as a pre-seed company is lean -- three founders, zero job postings, no visible burn. That's fine. But enterprise sales to CIOs require SOC 2 compliance, proof-of-concept periods, and reference customers, which means Lab0's runway gets eaten by sales cycles before the product can prove itself. The brand collision with lab0.com (the NVIDIA-backed robotics company) compounds the go-to-market friction. On capital discipline specifically, I give Lab0 credit for being small and tight, but the mismatch between a 3-person team and enterprise sales motion is a structural problem, not a virtue.

The dimension that kills Lab0 for me is structural durability. This is a pure software AI application competing against well-funded incumbents who are already shipping the same capability into their installed bases. No physical moat, no protocol layer, no network effect that survives an incumbent building it natively. The second killer is consensus mispricing -- there is no mispricing here. The entire enterprise AI agent category is consensus-hot in 2026. Every PSA vendor launched AI products in the same window. Lab0 is priced as a contrarian bet in a category where there are no contrarians left.
