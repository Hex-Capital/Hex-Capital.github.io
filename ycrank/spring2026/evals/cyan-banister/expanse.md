# Expanse -- Cyan Banister Evaluation

Ismaeel Bashir's MSc thesis at EPCC, "Multi-modal HPC Resource and Failure Prediction," is the entire product Expanse is selling — and that's both the most interesting thing about Expanse and the most limiting. It's a credentialed-team productized-thesis play in a consensus AIOps category, where the strongest dimension is a real but bloodless invisible-waste tax (30% over-allocation, 37% utilization on the example 32-GPU cluster), and the weakest is contrarian conviction — Expanse sits inside a category that NVIDIA just paid $700M to enter. No room shifts uncomfortably when you describe a GPU optimization startup.

The contrarian-conviction ladder is where Expanse fares worst, so I'll go there first. **Anduril** is the high rung — defense tech when Silicon Valley considered it politically toxic, founders who took the rejection as confirmation. **Crusoe** is the middle rung — weird enough that crypto-skeptics dismissed it, but the underlying physics was clear. The low rung is the "I was at Google and saw the opportunity" pass — consensus AI in a consensus market, where no investor in the room flinches. Expanse sits between Crusoe and that pass, much closer to the pass: GPU scheduling optimization is the most-funded adjacency in 2026, with Run:ai ($700M, NVIDIA, Dec 2024), WoolyAI, hosted.ai, SchedMD, and the academic literature on arXiv all crowding in. Bashir's HPC/Slurm-native angle is a real positioning wedge — Run:ai is Kubernetes-native and hyperscaler-aligned — but a positioning wedge is differentiation, not contrarianism. No VC is going to feel brave writing this check.

The invisible-system ladder is where Expanse fares best, and it's the reason this isn't a hard pass. **Affirm** is the high rung — credit card opacity weaponized against people who didn't know APR math. **Carta** is the middle rung — cap table mystery, real but felt mostly by founders, not vulnerable strangers. The low rung is **HQ Trivia** — no system disrupted, just attention captured. Expanse sits between Carta and the low rung. There IS a real hidden tax: researchers over-provision 2-3x because they have no way to predict failures, and the cost surfaces as a 37% utilization number nobody on the buying side ever sees rendered as dollars. But this isn't power asymmetry against the powerless — it's procurement slop inside national supercomputing centres and quant funds. The hidden system is real; the moral charge is absent. Affirm made cards transparent for people drowning in fees. Expanse makes Slurm jobs transparent for people running quant strategies at G-Research.

Bashir, Melake, and Mendi all trace through the University of Edinburgh, with Melake and Mendi at G-Research — the founder-market fit is tight, but the **forge** ladder pins them well below my high rung. **Substrate**'s James Proud is the high rung — "forged in fire and came out unbroken," a repeat founder challenging ASML's monopoly. **Affirm**'s Levchin is the middle — credentialed serial founder with real problem-fit. The low rung is the pass-example "I was at Google and saw the opportunity." Expanse's team is between Levchin and the pass, closer to the pass: a 2021-2026 Edinburgh undergrad-master's with an 86% bachelor's grade and Bloomberg/Amazon/Goldman/G-Research internships across the cap table. Excellent students. Not survivors. When I ask "tell me their story," the answer is "we wrote good code at quant funds." That's career optimization with a thesis attached, not biography demanding the company exist.

On defensibility, **Flock Safety**'s camera network is the high rung — each install raises switching costs for the next; **Carta**'s cap table lock-in is the middle; **HQ Trivia**'s zero-moat attention game is the low rung. Expanse is between Carta and HQ Trivia, leaning toward HQ Trivia: Slurm is open-source, the predictors are publishable (arXiv 2604.02158), Run:ai is being open-sourced by NVIDIA, and Bashir's "beat every published baseline" claim is, per the dossier, unverified by any peer-reviewed citation. The data-flywheel argument requires multi-cluster aggregation that doesn't exist yet. On impact, the ladder runs from **Uber** (transportation for people who couldn't hail a cab) to **Postmates** (delivery work flexibility) to enterprise margin tools that serve the already-served. Expanse sits at the bottom — its named buyers are quant funds and national HPC centres. Cheaper FLOPs may eventually trickle to AI access, but that's a third-order argument and it's not what Expanse is.

My 36-month forecast: Expanse reaches $1-3M ARR across 5-10 logos split between G-Research-adjacent quant funds and one or two national HPC centres, then gets acquired by SchedMD or rolled into NVIDIA's open-sourced Run:ai distribution as the Slurm-side bolt-on, in the $30-80M range. Mechanism: the HPC-native positioning is real and underserved, so Expanse will land logos; the lack of structural moat against open-source Run:ai plus the slow government procurement cycle for national centres caps the outcome before it becomes a fund-returning bet. This is the signature playing out — modest invisible-system fit gets you to a real product and a clean acqui-exit; weak contrarian conviction prevents the asymmetric upside that justifies an early Long Journey check.

The bull case stings here, and I want to name it honestly. Substrate is the comparable I should worry about: James Proud's technical depth in a category VCs underweight produced exactly the kind of infrastructure bet I tattooed on my body. Bashir at EPCC is a top-tier HPC research environment, the multimodal predictor is genuinely novel work, and HPC/Slurm is structurally ignored by hyperscaler-aligned tooling because hyperscalers have no incentive to optimize away their own billable hours. If Expanse becomes "the prediction layer for HPC" with per-cluster telemetry compounding across multi-site deployments, the Carta-style switching cost emerges and the acqui-exit forecast looks small. The dossier evidence that wins for me anyway: Substrate's Proud was forged in fire across multiple companies before Substrate; Expanse's CEO is a 2026 graduate whose master's thesis is the company, with a CTO (Bieniek) for whom no public footprint exists at all — that asymmetry on the technical co-founder is exactly the validation gap I learned to take seriously after IRL. The condition that flips my verdict: three paying national supercomputing centre contracts (not pilots, signed paid contracts) within 12 months, with named procurement counterparties — that would prove the GTM pain Expanse is solving is real enough that bureaucratic cycles bend around it, and I'd write the next round.
