# Kinect -- Paul Graham Evaluation

Rep AI has raised $8.2 million and signed 500+ merchants. Alhena AI has Tatcha and Unilever as customers. Tolstoy has 2,000+ brand partners. OpenAI and Perplexity both launched AI shopping assistants in late 2025. And now Kinect wants to enter this exact space — AI-powered e-commerce personalization — with zero verifiable customers and a demo dashboard that literally shows "0%" for conversion lift. When I wrote about Black Swan Farming, the core insight was that the best investments look wrong to most people. Kinect has the opposite problem: it looks right to everyone. "Personalized storefronts powered by AI" is the kind of idea that gets enthusiastic nods from every investor, every conference audience, every TechCrunch commenter. That consensus enthusiasm is precisely the signal that the returns have already been competed away.

Kratik Agrawal and Varun Kandula both co-led conversational intelligence at Reevo, which raised $80M — and that's legitimately relevant experience. Kratik worked at Google Commerce, Anduril, and Verkada. Varun built consulting projects with Sephora and Ralph Lauren through NextGen Consulting, sourcing "$200K+ contracted ARR across 10+ brands." These are capable people. But when I look for the animal quality — the founders who are slightly too intense, who've hacked their way through walls — I don't find stories. I find resumes. Google, MongoDB, Capital One, a consulting club presidency. No prior startup shipped, no side project that caught fire (combined 32 GitHub repos, maximum 1 star), no narrative of creative desperation. The Airbnb founders were selling $40 cereal boxes when no one would fund them. Patrick Collison shipped and sold Auctomatic as a teenager. Kinect's founders have impressive credentials from impressive companies, but credentials from impressive companies are exactly what you accumulate when things have generally gone well for you. I've never been able to evaluate resourcefulness without evidence of constraint.

Varun Kandula's path to Kinect is the part that concerns me most. He "led projects with Sephora and Ralph Lauren" through a consulting group, then co-led conversational AI at Reevo, then started Kinect to sell conversational AI to e-commerce brands. The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas — it's to look for problems you have yourself. Kinect's origin reads like the opposite: a market opportunity identified through client exposure and professional experience. Nobody describes a moment of personal frustration here. Nobody kept forgetting their USB drive. The problem statement on Kinect's website — "the product detail page is static and undifferentiated" — is accurate, but it's the kind of observation you make from the outside looking in, not from the inside living with the pain. Compare this to Drew Houston, who built Dropbox because he personally couldn't stand forgetting his USB stick, or the Octopart founders who were physics grad students who couldn't find electronic parts. Kinect's founders understood e-commerce professionally; they didn't live in the future of e-commerce and build what was missing.

The bull case is real and worth stating honestly. Kratik and Varun have directly relevant technical experience from Reevo — they built conversational intelligence systems for a company that raised $80M, which is more domain depth than most pre-seed founders bring. Kinect's combination of chat plus adaptive page rendering is genuinely differentiated from Rep AI (chat only), Alhena AI (chat only), and Tolstoy (video only). The "white-glove service" model could be interpreted as a deliberate "do things that don't scale" strategy — getting deep inside each merchant's workflow to learn what actually drives conversion. The e-commerce personalization market at $7.12B growing to $30B by 2035 is enormous. And their co-founder relationship is real — they worked together at Reevo before starting Kinect, which is exactly the kind of tested partnership I value. If you squint, Kinect could be the team that synthesizes the category while the incumbents each own one piece. But I've seen this pattern hundreds of times: competent founders from good companies identify a well-validated market, build a reasonable product, and discover that "reasonable" isn't enough when three funded competitors and two platform incumbents are already there. The combination of chat plus page adaptation is a feature set, not a moat. Rep AI could add page rendering in a quarter. Shopify could build this natively without channel conflict concerns — they already power ChatGPT in Shop.app. Kinect's "white-glove" approach suggests managed services economics that scale linearly, not the superlinear returns I need to see in a startup.

Kinect's competitive position reveals the deeper structural problem. Stripe succeeded because payments integration was a schlep — PCI compliance, bank partnerships, regulatory complexity — that repelled other developers. The schlep was the moat. E-commerce AI chat is the opposite of a schlep. It's a category that attracted $16M+ in startup funding before Kinect existed, that Amazon and Shopify are building internally, and that OpenAI and Perplexity validated with their own shopping products in late 2025. When LLM inference costs drop and the category is covered in TechCrunch, there's no schlep blindness left to exploit. Kinect's adaptive page rendering adds a real technical dimension, but it's an incremental differentiation, not a fundamental insight that others can't see. The name collision with Microsoft Kinect — forcing them to use "trykinect.ai" — is a small thing, but it's the kind of avoidable mistake that makes me wonder about the founders' taste and attention to non-obvious details.

One verifiable condition would flip me to Invest: evidence that even 5-10 merchants are using Kinect obsessively — not as an experiment, but as infrastructure they can't remove without seeing conversion drop. That would prove "make something people want" despite the crowded market, and it would tell me the founders learned something from their white-glove work that isn't visible in the dossier. Right now, the website's own metrics show "0%" for the value proposition Kinect is selling. The decisive dimensions are Organic Problem Discovery — this idea originated from consulting exposure rather than lived frustration — and Idea That Repels Before It Attracts — Kinect is building in a consensus-validated category where multiple funded competitors and platform incumbents have already arrived. Both dimensions score low, and neither has a Known Exception that applies.
